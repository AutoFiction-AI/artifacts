# Chapter 10

Draco made tea at six in the morning because the enchanted kettle spat lukewarm water twice and because explaining that to Granger felt unreasonably close to admitting that the Manor was failing under his hands one ordinary domestic charm at a time.

He set the Muggle kettle on the hob instead. The flame answered at once. That, lately, counted as cooperation.

He didn't think about the drawing room.

He spooned tea into the pot, poured the water when it boiled, and kept his eyes on the steam.

He thought about the drawing room.

What he'd said in it. What he had not said. The way Granger's voice had gone precise enough to cut, as if precision were the only thing standing between her and something much worse. He had stood there with every possible sentence available to him and chosen a defence so thin it had shamed him while he was still speaking.

On the stairs the third tread complained, then the seventh. She came into the kitchen in a jumper that swallowed one shoulder and hair that had lost the argument with sleep.

She should not have been awake. She worked until one or two most nights and treated dawn like a private insult.

"Morning," she said.

"Morning."

Her gaze went to the kettle on the hob. Then to the dead enchanted one cooling uselessly beside it.

"Right," she said.

He reached for the teapot before she could reach for the question. "There's enough for two."

"Thanks."

She took down a mug, found the milk, and stood beside him while he poured. Not close enough to touch. Close enough that the sleeve of her jumper brushed his wrist when she reached for the sugar she wasn't going to use.

Neither of them mentioned it.

She wrapped both hands around the mug and leaned back against the counter. He took his tea to the table. After a second she came over and sat opposite him instead of carrying hers back upstairs.

"I didn't sleep," she said. It came out flat, like weather given under protest.

"No."

"Did you?"

"Hardly."

She drank. He watched her over the rim of his cup and then looked away before it could become obvious. The window over the sink showed a strip of winter hedge and a sky the colour of old pewter. The house had that early-morning stillness that made every small sound carry: spoon against mug, kettle metal cooling, the boards settling under old weight.

"I'm not going near that corridor today," she said.

He put his cup down carefully. "No."

"I know that isn't exactly strategy."

"It doesn't have to be."

She glanced up at that, quick and unreadable, and then back to the tea.

"Your heating charm," she said after a moment. "Or whatever's left of it."

"Unreliable."

"Let me see."

He nearly said he was managing. The lie had become reflex so long ago that it barely counted as speech anymore. But she was already holding out her hand for the kettle, and there did not seem to be much point in pretending the thing on the hob was a lifestyle preference.

He pushed it across the table.

She turned the kettle over, thumb at the seam, wand in her right hand. The diagnostic she cast was small and quick, almost absent-minded, but her whole attention went into it. He had started cataloguing what concentration did to her face without meaning to: the way her eyebrows drew together, the tiny set of her mouth when a problem proved itself worth the irritation.

"The enchantment's intact," she said. "That's almost more annoying. It's not failing on its own. There's just not enough ambient power feeding the household layer to hold anything fussy for long."

"General decay."

"General decay," she agreed. "And old house magic that's used to being overfed." She tapped the copper base with her wand, murmured a second spell, and waited. The dormant charm inside the kettle woke with a soft hum. Not strong. Enough. "That should hold for a few weeks."

"Thank you."

"Mm-hm."

He set it back on the counter. She was still watching him.

"What?"

"Nothing. I'm just trying to work out how long you've been using the Muggle kettle rather than admit this needed fixing."

"Not long."

"How long."

"A week."

Her mouth did something that was not approval and not quite amusement. "That's ridiculous."

"It still boiled water."

"Eventually."

He might have defended himself if she had sounded annoyed. Instead she sounded tired in a way that made defence feel adolescent.

She stood and crossed to the bread box. "Have you got anything to eat that isn't tea?"

"Probably."

"That's not encouraging."

"It's the best I can do before sunrise."

The loaf had gone hard at the edges but not mouldy yet. He cut away the worst of it, set two slices in the pan because the toaster had died three days ago, and burned the first side while watching her re-check the kettle charm for no reason except habit.

"You're staring," she said without looking up.

"At the bread."

"You should look at it more usefully."

He rescued the toast. She took the less-burned slice without ceremony, buttered it, and ate half of it standing before sitting down again. The domestic plainness of it landed harder than the drawing room had any right to leave them vulnerable to. No negotiation. No apology. Just her in his kitchen at dawn in a jumper that did not fit, eating ruined toast as if the house had always contained two people.

When he set a second cup beside her, made with the repaired kettle, she looked at it and then at him.

"This is better," she said.

He did not ask which part.

---

For the next few days they learned, badly and without discussing it, how to move around the edges of what the drawing room had broken open.

Breakfast happened in the kitchen because she was awake too early twice more and because after the second time he stopped pretending surprise. She worked later. He listened for her on the stairs. Sometimes she came down with her hair tied back properly and sometimes with it half-fallen out. Once she sat at the table in her slippers reading notes over tea as if she'd been doing it there for years.

The work did not stop. It only changed temperature. She was building an anchoring model out of rot, war damage, and centuries of Malfoy engineering written by men who believed explanation was for inferiors. Septimus Malfoy's notation continued to behave like a personal insult. The magical decay did not care whether either of them had slept. The library door started staying open. He stopped closing it behind him when he left to make tea.

On the fourth evening he carried a pot of soup into the kitchen and found her already there, seated at the table with three folded sheets beside one elbow and her wand tucked behind the other like a pencil.

She looked up at the pot. "Is that dinner?"

"If we're being generous."

"I can be generous about soup."

He ladled one bowl, then paused. She was still looking at him, waiting with the sort of patience that made refusal look theatrical.

"You can sit down," she said. "Eating alone makes food boring."

It was so nearly an absent remark that he might have let it pass if the room had not gone very still around it.

"Does it."

"Yes." She unfolded one of the sheets, checked a line, and added, "Unless you're deeply committed to boredom."

"I've had practice."

"I'm aware."

He set out the second bowl.

The soup was potatoes, leeks, and the last respectable bit of cream before the cold-box enchantment gave up entirely. Not good. Hot. They ate with bread from the same degrading loaf and the kind of silence that did not require defence. She read over a notation block between spoonfuls. He watched the page travel steadily toward a smear of soup and moved it farther from her elbow without comment.

"You've been doing that all week," she said.

"Saving the archive from you?"

"Putting food just outside the range where I can ruin it accidentally. It's patronising."

"And yet effective."

She made a face at him and tore another piece of bread. "Septimus is using property shorthand for anchor load."

"He'd say he wasn't."

"He'd be wrong."

"He'd say the distinction is modern and therefore vulgar."

That got the smallest breath of laughter. Not much. Enough that he missed the next line of his own thought.

She pushed one of the sheets across the table. "Look."

The page was full of her fast, ugly annotations and Septimus's older, meaner script underneath. Draco read the margin twice.

"He's saying French theorists mistake decorative reinforcement for structural work."

"I got that much."

"No, you got the polite version. The word there isn't reinforcement. It's trimming. As in lace on a petticoat."

She stared at the line, then at him. "He called an entire school of magical theory petticoat lace."

"In fairness, he was usually drunk when writing in the margins."

"That's not fair."

"Family tradition, then."

She laughed. Properly this time. Sudden. Unchecked. It hit the ceiling hard enough that he looked up before he could stop himself, as if something in the room had actually altered.

She caught him looking.

"What."

"Nothing."

"You're very bad at nothing."

He set his spoon down. "We narrowed our historical grievances to the Normans. The French were just standing nearby."

That got him the second laugh, shorter and rougher and somehow worse for him than the first.

He looked back at his soup because there did not seem to be any safe way to look directly at the fact of her laughing in his kitchen.

"Your family sounds exhausting," she said.

"Historically, yes."

When they finished, he gathered the bowls and stood. She did not move.

"I'm going back to the library," she said. "If you want to look at the rest of that section, leave the washing up and come with me. If you don't, I'll keep swearing at Septimus alone."

"You swear at him?"

"Frequently."

"He'd be delighted."

"That's not a reason to stop."

He washed the bowls anyway because he did not trust the house elves to exist usefully anymore, and because needing a minute with his back to the sink felt safer than going straight from shared dinner to shared library. The hot water charm on the tap faltered twice. He ignored it. When he walked in ten minutes later carrying tea, the library door was propped open with one of the architectural survey volumes from the lower shelves.

The sight of it stopped him in the doorway.

She had claimed the floor by the south window, pages spread around her knees. His chair at the table held a stack of books and one loose slip of parchment in her handwriting. He set the cups down and picked it up.

Page 214. Margin. He's insulting France again.

The handwriting was atrocious. The fact of the note was worse.

"Your chair's occupied," she said without looking up.

"I noticed."

"You can move the stack."

"Magnanimous."

"I'm in an excellent mood. Septimus is a misogynist, but at least he's a consistent one."

He moved the books and sat. The chair was still warm from the fire. Or from her being near it all afternoon. He did not examine that distinction.

For an hour they worked in the easy broken rhythm that had become theirs without permission. She would mutter at a passage, hand it over, and he would translate the family shorthand or the nastier bits of Latin while she copied out the usable structure underneath. He made tea when the pot cooled. She forgot to drink hers until it had gone tepid. He replaced it with a fresh cup and took the cold one away before she noticed.

At some point the ache in his forearm started climbing.

It began low, like pressure at the base of the bone, and then spread outward until each line of the Mark felt separately awake. He kept his hand flat on the arm of the chair and read the same sentence three times without meaning touching it.

"You're doing the thing with your jaw," she said.

He looked up too quickly. "What thing."

"The thing where you decide pain is a private hobby." She set her quill down. "How bad."

"Manageable."

"That's not a scale."

"Six."

Her expression did not change.

"Seven," he said.

"Arm."

He considered refusing on principle. Then the skin over the Mark gave a hot, ugly throb, and principle began to look embarrassingly expensive.

He rolled his sleeve above the elbow and crossed to the chair opposite hers. She pulled the lamp closer and set the ledger beside her, already open to the last reading. Her left hand closed around his wrist with clinical steadiness. Her right lifted the wand.

"Tell me if it spikes," she said.

"You'll know."

"Tell me anyway."

The first spell skimmed the surface layer. The Mark sharpened under his skin, black lines standing out like fresh ink pressed through paper. Heat flashed up his arm so fast his fingers jerked.

"That's a spike," she said.

"Thank you."

She ignored that. Another spell, slower this time, tracing the outer lattice from wrist to elbow. The pain peaked, held, and then changed. Not vanished. Tilted. Her thumb stayed on the inside of his wrist where she was measuring his pulse. He could feel the exact place of it more clearly than he could feel the chair under him.

"Again," she murmured, more to herself than to him.

She set the wand down. The next part she did with her hand.

Her palm covered the Mark from the underside of his forearm, skin to skin over the raised, ugly thing. The relief was immediate enough to be humiliating. The burning dropped out from under him so abruptly that his breath went ragged on the way back in. His shoulder, which had been tightening upward for the better part of an hour, let go. The fingers of his left hand uncurled one by one against the chair arm.

Granger's eyes flicked to his face and then back to the Mark.

"There," she said quietly.

The black did not disappear. It dulled. The wet, fresh look of it sank toward something older, more like soot rubbed into old scar tissue. The angry flush at the edges receded. Under her thumb his pulse had stopped battering itself stupid.

"Your pulse has dropped," she said. "And the skin temperature's down."

"I can tell."

"Good. I don't want this to be subjective."

He nearly laughed. Nothing about her hand on his arm felt even faintly objective.

She shifted her hold, fingers spreading farther toward the inside of his elbow, and closed her eyes for a second in concentration. He watched the movement in her throat when she swallowed. The library had gone very quiet. Fire. Parchment. Her breathing. His trying not to be obvious about the fact that the only stable point in his body was where she was touching him.

"The surface agitation is easing," she said. "That's observable. The outer curse lattice is less active when I keep contact on it."

"And the part that isn't observable."

Her mouth tightened. "There's something lower down that I still can't separate cleanly. I can feel it resisting the diagnostic. I don't know whether the relief means I'm damping the Mark or reaching a second structure under it."

"A second structure."

"Maybe." She opened her eyes. "I'm not calling it fact until I can replicate it."

He held her gaze. "Do you need to?"

"Yes."

"Tonight?"

Her thumb moved once against his wrist, almost absent. "Not for long. I don't know enough yet."

She took her hand away.

The ache returned at once. Not as badly. Enough to make the loss legible.

Neither of them commented on that either.

She picked the wand up again and ran one final confirming spell. The lines of the Mark flared faintly and settled.

"Duration?" she asked.

"Less than three minutes."

"Two and a half." She wrote it in the ledger. Her notation was compact and furious, as if the page had personally failed her by not already containing the answer. "Immediate response: heat reduction, decreased pulse, visible dulling of surface saturation. Cause unknown."

"You make everything sound aggressive."

"Only the things that deserve it."

He tugged his sleeve back down. The fabric against the newly cooled skin felt almost wrong.

"Does it always do that?" she asked, still looking at the page.

"No."

"With anyone else."

"No."

That made her pause. Not long. Enough.

"All right," she said, and wrote something else he could not see.

He went back to his chair. She went back to Septimus. The library did not return to what it had been ten minutes earlier. The change was too physical for that. He could still feel the print of her hand on his arm as if she had left heat behind instead of taking it away.

After that, the house learned their habits faster than either of them admitted.

Dinner happened in the kitchen more often than not. If she was in the library at seven, he took a plate up and set it far enough from the papers to protect both. If he was late, she eventually appeared in the doorway and looked annoyed in a way that turned out to mean hungry. Books accumulated on his chair with slips of parchment tucked between the pages: translate this line, is this notation legal, I think your ancestors were idiots. The library door stayed open. She stopped resetting her voice into formality every time something ordinary passed between them. He stopped noticing the moment he chose to answer her like a person instead of a host.

On Tuesday night she fell asleep over a translation.

The quill dropped first. Then her head followed, cheek on her forearm, breath gone slow and even over a sheet half-covered in structural notes and an argument with Septimus written in the margin in her own hand.

He should have woken her. The desk was an act of hostility on the neck. The fire had burned low. She had worked through supper and then eaten standing by the mantel while reading. Any sensible person would have sent her upstairs.

Instead he crossed the room quietly, lifted the quill before it could roll into the crease of the ledger, and read the line her sleeping body had pinned under one elbow.

Load transfer impossible without paired access.

She had underlined paired access so hard the nib had nearly gone through the parchment.

He looked at her for a long moment. At the smudge of ink along the side of her hand. At the hair coming loose again. At the tiredness she was not getting away with in sleep.

The old wool throw still hung over the back of his chair. He shook it out and laid it over her shoulders as carefully as if care itself might wake her.

She stirred under it, not enough to surface. Her hand twitched once and settled closer to her face. He stayed standing there until her breathing evened again.

Then he rebuilt the fire and sat down beside her with a book open in front of him that he did not read.

He had spent years reducing his life to whatever could be survived. Smaller rooms. Smaller wants. Fewer habits that could turn into losses. It had worked because the world had been obliging enough to keep confirming the mathematics.

Now there was a woman asleep in his library under his mother's throw, with his research notes on the floor and his tea gone cold beside her elbow, and survivable no longer felt like the same thing as safe.

After twenty minutes he stood, doused the lamp by his own chair, and left her the fire.

The corridor to his rooms shortened as he walked it, the Manor quietly making its opinions known in stone and geometry. He ignored that too.

* * *

Theo arrived on a Thursday, because Theo arrived on whichever day suited Theo and expected architecture to adjust.

The Floo stuttered hard enough to spit ash into the entrance hall before depositing him upright, irritated, and well-dressed in a coat too new to be accidental. Theo had always understood the defensive uses of presentation. Post-war Slytherin life, he'd once said, ran on looking like you belonged in rooms that did not want you until the rooms got tired of checking.

"You've got a guest," he said, brushing soot from one sleeve.

"Granger's working in the library."

"Granger," Theo repeated. "As in Hermione Granger. The Hermione Granger who has apparently become a minor cottage industry in the gossip columns."

Draco shut the Floo grate with more force than necessary. "Has she."

"Three this month." Theo followed him toward the kitchen. "One thinks she's auditing your finances, one thinks she's writing legislation in your library, and one is boring enough to assume you're having a breakdown and she's the nearest war hero."

"How thoughtful of them."

"They're also all very interested in why she's spending so much time in Wiltshire."

"She's researching the decay."

"Right." Theo glanced at him. "Are you going to feed me, or am I meant to live on scandal."

He was staying for lunch. He was staying long enough to make sure Draco had been eating and sleeping something adjacent to enough, and both of them knew it before the kettle had boiled.

By the time Draco handed him tea, Theo was leaning against the counter with the ease of someone who had stood in this kitchen through school holidays, probation visits, and the first starved year after the war when most of their old acquaintances had treated the Manor like a contagious disease.

"The network's worse," Theo said. "I tried to come on Tuesday and landed in a pub in Berkshire."

"Were you confused."

"Briefly. Then I had lunch." He took a drink. "My contact in Floo Regulation says they're running half capacity and still losing ground. The Muggle rail lines are suddenly full of witches pretending to enjoy inconvenience."

"Granger thinks it's structural. War damage to the anchoring."

"Granger thinks." Theo said it mildly. "You talk about what she thinks a lot."

Draco ignored that. "How's the business."

"Still theoretically a business." Theo set his cup down. "A few consulting contracts. Two families pretending they aren't paying me to fix wards they neglected for twenty years. One old estate in Somerset where the attics are trying to bite people. Everything documented six different ways because apparently the Nott name now triggers administrative curiosity."

"And the shop."

Theo's mouth flattened. "Rejected again."

"The space was let."

"No. The space is still empty, the windows are still papered, and the committee is still very sorry for the delay while it considers whether I'm fit to sell legal goods in Diagon Alley."

He said it lightly enough that Draco knew he was furious.

Theo carried the Nott name the way Draco carried Malfoy, with constant awareness of what it cost to place it anywhere public. The difference was that Theo kept walking out into the world and paying the cost anyway. Draco had mostly arranged his life to avoid the invoice.

"You should speak to Granger," he said. "She'd know people."

"I'm not asking Hermione Granger to fix my commercial tenancy."

"Why not."

"Because I'd prefer not to become a charitable project."

"She doesn't do charity."

"Exactly."

The kitchen door opened before Draco could answer that properly.

Granger stopped on the threshold with a folded page in one hand and the distracted expression of someone who had come downstairs to ask a question and found extra variables instead.

"Oh," she said. "I didn't know you had company."

"Granger, Theodore Nott. Theo, Granger."

"We were in the same year," she said, recovering quickly. She crossed the room and offered her hand. "I don't think we ever actually spoke."

"We didn't." Theo shook it. "Different social circles. Mine being the socially compromised ones."

"I remember your Potions marks."

"I choose to hear that as admiration."

"It isn't."

That made Theo smile. Draco, irritatingly, was not the only person in the room capable of getting one out of her.

"I hear you're solving the magical decay from Draco's library," Theo said.

"Trying to understand it," she corrected. "If I were solving it, I wouldn't still be arguing with men who've been dead for two hundred years."

"The dead do paperwork badly," Theo said. "The living aren't doing better."

"You've seen the licensing issue," she said at once, interest sharpening.

"I've seen my last three applications acquire extra forms every time a clerk notices the surname at the bottom."

"For a shop."

"For a very dull shop, yes."

She came the rest of the way into the kitchen and set the folded page on the table. "What extra forms."

Theo blinked once. "That sounds dangerously like interest."

"It is."

"Financial disclosures. Liability declarations. Two separate character attestations, which feels a little pointed at twenty-five."

"For a tenancy board?" She looked at Draco as if he might somehow confirm the absurdity of the state. "That isn't standard."

"No," Theo said. "It's just current."

She pushed the page she'd brought down toward him. "That's the emergency procurement guidance for decaying infrastructure permits. Half of it was drafted by people at Enforcement because no one else could move quickly enough. If they're applying similar standards to private licensing, there'll be a paper trail."

"You're carrying Ministry paperwork around the Manor now," Theo said.

"I'm carrying everything around the Manor now. That's different."

Draco put a knife on the table with perhaps more force than the bread required. "You can interrogate the Ministry over sandwiches."

"Can we," Theo said, "or is that one of the new house rules."

"It's lunch."

"He does this," Granger said to Theo, as if Draco were temporarily not present. "He pretends a meal is not happening until food actually appears, as though that changes the administrative burden."

"You sound settled," Theo said.

The words were neutral. The effect was not.

Granger's fingers paused on the edge of the page. Draco was absurdly relieved when she only said, "I sound hungry."

"Good," Draco said. "Everyone stay where you are and let me make lunch before the bureaucracy gets sentient."

Lunch was sandwiches because the cold-box enchantment had become aspirational and because anything warm required charms the house no longer held reliably. He set out bread, cheese, mustard, and the last of the pickled onions. Granger cut apples with the concentration of someone still thinking about permits while holding a knife. Theo perched on the counter and provided commentary nobody needed.

"The Prophet piece with the finances," Theo said while Draco sliced cheese, "also suggested the Ministry had stationed Granger here to keep you from selling cursed heirlooms to desperate pure-bloods."

"Have I missed a lucrative opportunity," Draco asked.

"Apparently."

Granger rolled her eyes. "There isn't a Ministry budget line for babysitting former Death Eaters in country houses."

"There should be," Theo said. "Think of the employment numbers."

"I wouldn't qualify," Draco said.

"No," Theo said. "You'd be the pilot programme."

Granger snorted into her tea.

They ate at the kitchen table with paperwork pushed between the plates. The conversation moved oddly well. Floo failures, old estates, Ministry bottlenecks, anchoring theory, the cost of surnames that made clerks sit up straighter. Theo had a practised ease in mixed company, the sort of social calibration Draco had once mistaken for effortless and later understood was work. With Granger he adjusted fast, matching her speed without flattening himself into politeness. She interrupted him twice to correct a procedural detail. He interrupted back once to stop her turning a question into a lecture.

"No," he said, tapping the page she'd brought down, "that's not what I'm asking. I'm asking whether it's stupidity or policy."

"Those aren't mutually exclusive."

"Nothing in Britain is ever only one bad thing at a time," Draco said.

Both of them looked at him.

"What," he said.

"You do talk when required," Granger said.

"Only at meals."

"We'll note the miracle."

He ate the rest of his sandwich to avoid answering that.

By the second cup of tea the papers had migrated almost entirely to Theo's side of the table. Granger had started listing departments and names on the back of an envelope in the compact angry script she reserved for institutions.

"Licensing won't help you," she said. "Not if the obstruction isn't — it's not formal enough to challenge formally. It's procedural. People adding steps." She pushed her plate aside. "Enforcement might. I know three people there who still push back when scrutiny turns into something else."

"And they owe you favours," Theo said.

"They don't owe me anything. They just — they do their jobs. I happen to keep showing up."

"That's somehow more frightening."

"Do you want the names or not."

Theo leaned back in his chair. The joke fell out of him by degrees.

"I don't know," he said.

It was the first fully honest thing he had said since arriving.

Granger didn't fill the silence. Draco appreciated her for that more than was sensible.

"All right," she said after a moment. "Then think about it. Actually think about it."

Theo let out a short breath through his nose. "That's an incredibly irritating instruction."

"Usually is."

The corner of his mouth went. "Write the names down anyway."

She did.

That changed something in the room. Not dramatically. No revelation. Just the quiet fact of Theo, who hated needing anything visible, accepting a piece of help in Draco's kitchen from Hermione Granger as if the war had not only happened but continued rearranging the furniture years after everyone had sworn it over.

After lunch she gathered the pages, the envelope, and her tea.

"It was good to meet you properly," she said to Theo. "Even if your administrative life is appalling."

"Likewise."

"And the Manor seems to stabilise the Floo better from this end, so if Berkshire gets you next time, try again."

"That's both encouraging and menacing."

"Most house magic is."

She left with her notes under one arm, stepping around the loose board by the door without looking down. She had learned the kitchen's bad habits. The knowledge of that sat oddly in Draco's chest.

Theo watched the doorway until her footsteps faded up the corridor.

"Don't," Draco said.

"I haven't said anything."

"You're about to."

Theo considered him over the rim of his cup. Then, to Draco's surprise, he took a different route entirely.

"How's the Mark."

Draco looked down at the untouched half of his lunch. This was not the subject he had expected and, for that reason, probably the one Theo had meant to choose from the start.

"Worse in some ways," he said. "Better in others."

"Christ."

"Helpful, I know."

"Try again."

Draco rubbed his thumb once over the seam in the table and made himself answer plainly.

"The ache is stronger now. More consistent. It sits there all day and then spikes without much warning." He kept his voice even. "During examinations it changes."

"Changes how."

"She runs the diagnostic, and if she keeps contact on it the heat drops. The swelling goes down. My pulse settles." He hated the flatness of his own voice and kept going anyway. "I can breathe properly. Then it comes back when she stops."

Theo did not move for a second.

"And you're classifying that as better in others."

"It's better in others."

"It's also not a footnote."

Draco looked up.

Theo's expression had lost most of its polish. "Are you measuring it."

"She is."

"No, you're — you're both stopping short." He stood and reached for his coat from the back of the chair. "Quick readings, two minutes, done. Neither of you wants to stay in it long enough to actually —" He pulled the coat off the chair. "You know what I mean."

Draco's spine went rigid. "We don't know what the effect means."

"Then stop guessing from a distance."

Theo shrugged into the coat and buttoned it with unnecessary care.

"I'm going to say one more thing," he said, "and then I'm leaving before you decide to be insufferable."

"A threat to our friendship."

"You look different." He adjusted one cuff. "Like you're —" He stopped. Pulled the collar straight. "Seven years of walking through this house like you were waiting for it to ask you to leave. You don't look like that."

"Theo."

He picked up the envelope Granger had left for him, slipped it into an inside pocket, and headed for the hearth.

"If the Floo drops me in Berkshire again," he said, "I'm drinking before I owl."

"That seems wise."

Green flame took him. Ash drifted down across the grate.

Draco stood for a moment in the quiet that followed, one hand flat on the mantel. Theo had taken the names. Theo had been right often enough over the years to make the rare occasions worse, not better. And the examinations were what they were, whether or not anyone had said it plainly yet: not routine, not incidental, and increasingly not brief.

He carried a fresh cup of tea to the library.

She was at the long table this time instead of the floor, sleeves rolled, three books open and the ledger pulled close enough that she could write without standing. She looked up as he came in and held out a hand for the tea automatically.

"Did he make it out," she asked.

"Probably to Berkshire first."

That got the small breath of laughter it deserved. Then she looked at his face properly and the laughter went away.

"What."

He set the cup down near her elbow. "He says there've been gossip columns."

Her hand stopped over the ledger. "About what."

"About why you're here."

"Of course there have."

The flatness in her voice was not surprise. It was fatigue. Something in him disliked that instantly.

"He also asked about the Mark," Draco said.

Her eyes came back to him, sharper now. "What did you tell him."

He could have evaded. With Granger he had grown used to discovering, a beat too late, that evasion only created extra work.

"That it settles during examinations." He heard Theo's accusation in his own head and went further because stopping there had started to feel childish. "That if you keep your hand on it, the heat drops. My pulse slows. It comes back when you stop."

She was very still.

"And what did he say."

"That we're flinching."

The word landed. Not because Theo had said it. Because it was true enough to irritate her.

She shoved one of the books aside and stood.

"He's right," she said.

He stared at her. "You don't even like him."

"I don't have to like him to object to bad method." She cleared a space at the table with quick, efficient violence. "We've been treating the effect as a side note because neither of us wanted to deal with what it implies. Sit down."

"You said longer contact might make it worse."

"It might. That's why we're going to measure it properly instead of pretending uncertainty is caution." She looked at him over the table. "Do you want this handled like data or not."

There were twelve wrong answers available. He sat.

She pulled the ledger closer and flipped to a clean page. "We're recording duration, immediate physical response, and any change in the lower resistance layer. If you feel anything odd, say it when it happens."

"My feelings about odd have degraded."

"Then be descriptive."

She came around to his left side. The movement was familiar now in a way that did not make it less dangerous. She rolled his sleeve above the elbow, exposing the Mark to firelight and the weak spill of winter afternoon at the windows. Dark lines, old poison, the brand that had no right to keep behaving like a fresh wound at twenty-five.

Her left hand settled around his wrist. Right hand with the wand. Clinical, exact. He had never been able to decide whether that made it easier.

"Baseline first," she said.

The opening diagnostic passed through the Mark like cold wire. The skin tightened. Heat rose fast enough to make his teeth clench.

"There."

She watched the curse react, then wrote one line in the ledger without letting go of him. He could not see the words from this angle.

"Pulse elevated," she said under her breath. "Surface saturation increased."

"You narrate very aggressively."

"Quiet."

The second spell ran lower, probing under the outer lattice instead of skating over it. Pain lanced up the inside of his arm and into the shoulder. He sucked in breath through his nose. Her grip on his wrist tightened in answer.

"That's deeper," he said.

"I know."

The wand went down on the table. She shifted closer and laid her palm flat over the Mark.

It was not gentle. It was direct. Full contact over the whole ruined shape of it, her hand warm and steady, the heel of it braced near his elbow while her fingers spread toward his wrist. Relief hit so hard it was nearly a second injury. The brand lost heat in brutal increments. His spine unlocked against the chair. His left hand, which had been starting toward a fist again, fell open over his thigh.

He let out a breath he had not known he was holding. The sound of it filled the table space between them.

Granger's eyes lifted briefly to his face, then dropped back to the Mark as if seeing too much there.

"Immediate drop," she said, quieter now. "Surface response same as before. Stay with it."

"I'm not moving."

"That wasn't instruction for you."

He almost smiled. It died quickly.

She kept her hand where it was and closed her eyes. No wand now. Only contact and whatever brutal concentration let her feel through cursed tissue without flinching. Under her palm the Mark changed again, not just cooling but drawing inward, the raised lines flattening until they felt less like active magic and more like old scar. At the very base of the skull-and-serpent, near the place where the ink darkened toward his inner arm, something gave a small, ugly twist.

He flinched.

Her eyes snapped open. "What."

"Lower. Right at the bottom. Not pain exactly." He swallowed. "Like something pulling tight."

"There," she said at once, and shifted her hand half an inch.

The pull sharpened. Then it steadied.

She inhaled once, sharp enough to hear. "I can feel resistance giving way."

"Is that good."

"I have no idea."

Which, from her, counted as honesty severe enough to trust.

She stayed there. One minute. Two. Long enough that he stopped tracking time and started tracking the details he should have been above noticing: the pressure of her thumb at the inside of his wrist, the smell of parchment and tea in her sleeve, the fact that every time she breathed his own body seemed embarrassingly interested in following suit.

The Mark held quiet under her hand.

When she finally lifted her palm, the loss was immediate and not complete. The ache returned only halfway. The heat did not rush back with it.

She stared at the skin.

"Well," she said.

"That sounds promising."

"It sounds repeatable." She reached for the quill without looking away from his arm. "Five minutes, forty seconds. Significant surface calming. Persistent reduction after contact ends. New response at lower layer, nature unknown."

She wrote fast. Then slower. Then put the quill down and touched the very bottom edge of the Mark with two fingers only, testing the residual magic there. He felt that too, differently. Sharper. More precise.

"Did it spike again," she asked.

"No."

"Same location."

"Yes."

She made another note.

"We're doing this again tomorrow," she said.

He looked at her.

"Longer?"

"Same duration first." She capped the ink, finally looked up, and there it was again, the expression she wore when a problem had just become more interesting than was socially healthy. "If the lower response repeats, then longer."

"You're pleased."

"I'm angry," she said. "At myself."

"For what."

"For wasting a week being cautious in a way that looked sensible and wasn't." Her mouth tightened. "Theo can be right offstage. I don't have to enjoy it."

The line was alive enough that he almost laughed. What came out was smaller than that but still counted.

She heard it. Something in her shoulders eased.

He pulled his sleeve down over the arm and felt, with a kind of offended gratitude, that the fabric no longer burned. Across the table the new page in the ledger lay open between them, three fresh headings where there had been none this morning.

Duration.
Immediate physical response.
Lower-layer change.

"After dinner," she said, as if answering a question he had not yet asked.

"All right."

She picked up the tea he had brought and finally drank it. He stayed where he was while she turned back to the ledger.

Tomorrow had been altered into method.
