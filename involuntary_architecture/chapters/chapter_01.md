# Chapter 1

The filing charm collapsed on a Tuesday evening, and three years of sorted correspondence slid from its shelves and pooled across Hermione's office floor.

Hermione stared. The charm was hers, a standard administrative enchantment she'd anchored to the oak cabinet in her first week in the Department of Mysteries, the kind of spell that should have sat in the wood and worked without attention for years. She'd refreshed it once, maybe twice, in all that time. It was not supposed to fail.

Kneeling, she picked up the nearest scroll, an internal memo from August with the wax seal still intact, and pushed magic back into the cabinet's grain. The spell caught, wavered, and held, but only thinly. The difference registered at once: same wand, same intention, same clean structure, but the enchantment sat in the wood like something balanced there, not grown through it.

So she filed the memo by hand, then the next one, then the next. By the time the last scroll was back in place, it was past eight and her knees ached from the stone floor. After hours the Department of Mysteries did not sound empty so much as stoppered; the soundproofing charms sealed the silence until her own breathing was the loudest thing in the room.

In the corridor, the lights were thinning again. They weren't flickering. They were just dimming so gradually it was impossible to call the moment of failure. For weeks she'd been logging small degradations: the east archive's preservation charms developing a stutter in September, Section G's diagnostic arrays returning readings she was no longer willing to call anomalous, her office temperature holding two degrees low since mid-October. Anyone else could have ignored it under a jumper. Hermione Granger measured things.

In September she'd written the report: detailed, thorough, exhaustive enough to irritate anyone assigned to read it. Croaker had read it once, asked her to keep monitoring, and said nothing further. The Department of Mysteries did not acknowledge crises. It acknowledged data.

The data said Britain's ambient magical field was declining. The decline was not dramatic. No buildings were collapsing. The Prophet was not issuing special editions. There was just accumulation. A charm that should have lasted three years failed after two and a half. Floo connections dropped mid-sentence. A Healer at St. Mungo's had to cast twice where once used to suffice. Each failure was small enough to dismiss; together they formed a pattern she had stopped pretending not to see. By mid-October her private log ran to thirty-seven pages. People who understood the graph got quiet. People who didn't argued with it.

Every archive available to her had already been searched: the Department's restricted collection, Hogwarts under a special dispensation from McGonagall that came with no questions and no deadline, two private collections, one in Devon and one owned by a Ravenclaw academic who owed her a favor, and everything the Ministry had ever published on ambient-field theory. None of it reached far enough back. The decay was older than the Ministry, older than Hogwarts, rooted in infrastructure that predated modern magical governance, and the records that mattered had mostly stayed in family hands.

Most collections had been catalogued after the war.

One had not.

Three weeks of cross-referencing Devon notes against Hogwarts frameworks had gotten her only this: somewhere in the deep structure of British magic sat an anchoring system no surviving public text described adequately. Its outline showed up in margins and footnotes, in references to anchoring lines and foundational nodes and a network underneath the visible spellwork. The primary sources were missing. Every trail ended at old families. Most had died out, cooperated, or yielded nothing new. One was named Malfoy.

Before leaving, she took the latest incident summaries and went down to Section G because going home on a fresh failure without checking the larger trend would only have meant spending the walk inventing worse numbers. The diagnostic room sat behind three wards that had started requiring a second attempt every few days. Tonight the first recognition charm skimmed over her badge and failed to catch. The second one admitted her with a reluctant click.

The main array looked intact until she started it. Then the brass needles shivered out of sequence and the rune ring on the outer plate lit in patches instead of cleanly around the circumference. Hermione recalibrated by hand, fed the last week's figures into the basin, and waited while the silver surface thickened, steadied, and finally gave her a line graph she disliked on sight. The drop was not catastrophic. It was worse than that. It was consistent.

She checked the incident summaries against the same dates. A Floo relay in Leeds had spat a woman into the wrong grate with her shopping half-charred around her ankles. A charms apprentice in Bath had snapped a levitation sequence halfway through and dropped a crate of bezoar phials hard enough to shatter nine of them. Two accidental-magic reports from Muggle homes had come in that morning, both flagged for unusual force. One six-year-old in Croydon had burst every lightbulb in the kitchen and driven the cutlery into the plaster. Another child in Luton had tried to call a toy broom to hand and blown the sitting-room window outward instead. The Ministry language for these things was restrained to the point of dishonesty. *Volatility event.* *Unexpected magical surge.* Hermione read the lines and pictured broken glass in a terraced house, parents bleeding from their hands while they tried to understand what their child had done.

She copied the figures into her private log. Halfway through, the self-inking quill dragged dry across the page. Hermione swore softly, shook more ink into the reservoir herself, and finished the entry by hand.

That was what the crisis looked like, most days. Nothing grand. Nothing anyone in authority could stand up and point at in a briefing room. Just enough failure, scattered widely enough, that responsibility could always be passed one desk farther down the corridor.

Down in the Atrium, a maintenance wizard was trying to fill a cleaning bucket from the fountain. On his third attempt the water vanished again before the mop touched the floor. He swore at the bucket, refilled it, and swore harder when the charm stripped the water away a second time. Hermione watched long enough to confirm it wasn't a one-off, then walked out through the visitor entrance into the October night.

London was cold, damp, and entirely itself. Muggle infrastructure had its own forms of failure, but tonight the streetlights worked, the buses ran, and the pavement held steady under her feet. She passed the Tube station and kept walking. The choice had started in January after a Knight Bus ride ended with her sick on the pavement outside a Tesco and the simple thought, humiliatingly useful, that she could just use her legs. The habit had stayed. Forty minutes through Holborn and Camden, past steamed chip-shop windows, a man swearing into his mobile, a woman hauling a sleeping child out of a minicab. None of it relied on magic. Reliability had become more valuable than convenience, which was not a preference she'd expected to acquire.

---

Her flat in Kentish Town sat above a shuttered dry cleaner's. It was one bedroom on a cheap lease, with cream walls she'd never bothered to cover. Books were everywhere: shelves, table, kitchen counter, the armchair by the window that had become storage after the cushion's warming charm started bleeding out by midnight. A blanket still lay folded over one arm, unused. In the bathroom, a Muggle umbrella occupied the tub because a drying charm had once held long enough to become invisible and then failed, leaving the object dry, purposeless, and somehow permanent. No one had been invited over since the move. The place was for sleeping, reading, eating, and leaving again.

By the door, the sticking charm she used as a coat hook had started to peel from the wall. Hermione pressed it back with her thumb until it caught, barely. In the kitchen the kettle charm was unreliable, so water went on over the gas hob instead. The hob was Muggle. The flame did what fire did every time.

Dinner was toast and the last of the cheddar because the refrigerator's expansion charm had collapsed the week before, shoving everything back to actual size and cracking a jar of chutney against the rear shelf. She'd cleaned the glass out of the grooves with a butter knife, bought another jar, put the milk back, kept going. The flat still functioned. So did the divorce, if functioning meant nobody shouted and the paperwork stayed where she'd filed it.

Two months earlier Ron had called to say he was seeing someone from the second shop at Weasleys' Wizard Wheezes. He'd handled it carefully, with the decency he reached for when he knew he was crossing old ground, and she'd said the right things and meant them. Afterwards she'd stood in the kitchen and waited for hurt, jealousy, anything with edges. Nothing came. Just the flat remainder of a marriage that had subtracted itself so slowly there was nothing left to divide.

At the counter she read a journal article on her tablet while she ate. The subject was ambient-field theory, a fresh defense of a conjecture she'd already dismissed twice. The author called magical fluctuation cyclical, self-correcting, ultimately benign; the data stopped in 2001. Thoroughness made her finish the piece anyway. Then she opened the folder of copied notes she'd been carrying between office and flat for weeks: Devon references, one battered Hogwarts index, three pages of names of old families whose records had either burned, been seized, or produced nothing except elegant variations on *not here*. The Malfoy collection sat at the bottom of the last page with no mark beside it. She looked at the name, closed the folder, and washed the plate.

Her left forearm itched. Automatically, she tugged the sleeve lower and pressed two fingertips through the cotton to the place beneath. The charm felt smooth. No seam caught. She did it every morning before work and sometimes again by evening without noticing, in the same sequence as checking the flat wards and patting for her keys.

The Floo coughed.

Green flame rose to her knees and died. Hermione waited. The network tried again with a wheeze and a brief unsteady flare, then dropped. A voice cut through the fire for a second and vanished.

Kneeling at the grate, she cast a diagnostic. The relay was still there. She could feel the north London node humming under the failure, serving four thousand households and barely holding together. Harry's fireplace at Grimmauld Place was trying to reach her.

Apparition was an option. An owl was an option. Instead she wrote, *Floo dropped again. I'm all right. Tomorrow?* and sent the note through the flat's mail charm, which still mostly worked unless it decided to deposit Ministry correspondence in a Muggle postbox three streets over.

Harry would ask whether she'd slept. She'd say yes. He'd make that face where his mouth stayed neutral and his eyes said he'd known her too long for this. Then she'd redirect because the truthful version snagged on stupid details. Yes, she slept sometimes. Yes, she ate. Yes, she kept arriving at work dressed and mostly on time. Once she started explaining why that was not the same as being all right, she would have to keep going.

On the sofa between two journal stacks, she listened to the Floo cough once more. Green fire flickered; Harry's voice made it through this time, just long enough for "Hermione?" before the network failed again.

She left it there.

---

Wednesday passed in refusals and administrative archaeology.

By ten that morning she had three replies waiting on her desk. The Devon collector's niece had gone through the remaining family trunks and found nothing earlier than 1794. The Ravenclaw academic wrote four dense paragraphs to say, apologetically, that his uncatalogued papers turned out to be estate ledgers rather than magical theory. Hogwarts sent a note from the librarian's office confirming that no additional access restrictions had been placed on her research and no additional relevant texts had appeared by miracle overnight.

By lunch Hermione had stopped glancing up at every owl tap. She took her notebook and went three floors down to the Records Annex, where the Ministry stored the paper skeleton of every post-war inventory, seizure, pardon review, and heritage dispute no one had managed to turn into a tidy public history. The room smelled of dust, damp stone, and preservation charms running too hot. One of the catalogue cabinets stood open because its indexing spell had begun shelving cards by date one day, color the next, and alphabet only when threatened.

She braced a knee against the cabinet and pulled drawers by hand instead.

The post-war heritage files filled two and a half cabinets. Families cooperated, obstructed, bribed, lied, appealed, and occasionally discovered the usefulness of suddenly claiming that generations of highly valuable magical material had already been lost to mildew, moths, or youthful carelessness. Hermione read through entry after entry. Black family papers: partial seizure, private settlement, library dispersed. Greengrass: inspected, catalogued, judged irrelevant. Lestrange properties: sealed, structurally unsafe, nothing retrievable without specialist curse-breakers the Ministry had not been willing to fund in 1999 and would be even less willing to fund now. Half the relevant records ended in the dry language institutions used when they wanted history to sound procedural instead of violent.

The cabinet light dimmed while she was reading. She tapped it with her wand. It brightened for five seconds and died again. Hermione took the lamp off a neighboring desk and carried on.

An hour later she found the Malfoy file.

It was thinner than it should have been. Much thinner. The original intake form from May 1998 listed the existence of a substantial private archive, heavily warded, not made available for emergency wartime survey. A later sheet from August 1999 recorded that full Ministry cataloguing had been deferred pending criminal proceedings against the head of household, unresolved property claims, and "resident ward volatility incompatible with standard archival handling." Another note, unsigned, had been attached at an angle as if added in irritation: *Collection remains in situ. No external inventory completed. Review postponed.*

That was all. No catalogue. No follow-up. No later assessment. Hermione stood there holding four useless pages that nonetheless told her exactly what she needed to know. The library had not been absorbed into the post-war cleanup. It had not been opened. It had not even been properly touched.

She copied the reference numbers into her notebook because the duplicating charm on the public desk had fused two sheets together that morning and she did not trust it not to ruin the original. By the time she climbed back to her office, her fingers were blackened with archive dust and there was a smear of it on the side of her hand where she'd pushed hair out of her face.

Croaker's memo arrived Thursday morning on pale lavender parchment, interdepartmental seal, and language so carefully imprecise it might as well have had *routine* stamped over it in red. His margin note was smaller and more direct: *Granger. Relevant to your research. Last uncatalogued major collection. Authorize at your discretion.*

The substance only confirmed what the Records Annex had implied. The Malfoy family library in Wiltshire remained the last major private collection in Britain still uncatalogued. It was known to contain pre-Ministry records, foundational magical theory, and archive material unavailable in any public institution. A qualified researcher was requested to assess the collection for texts relevant to current departmental concerns.

Hermione read it once for content, once because *Malfoy family library* landed in her chest like dropped weight, and once more for the omissions. Croaker did not mention magical decay. He did not mention the stabilization indices she'd been tracking for months. He did not put in writing that the books in that house might be the only surviving record of the system now failing across Britain.

So Croaker was being careful. The memo could sit in a file as routine heritage work. The margin note could not. Croaker was telling her, as discreetly as the institution allowed, that every conventional route had run out.

She flattened the parchment on her desk and pressed both palms to the wood. The Ministry-standard oak felt solid under her hands. It did not move.

Seven years had passed since she'd last set foot on the property. Draco Malfoy she had not spoken to since the trial in the spring of 1999, six years ago, when she'd still been nineteen and angry enough to believe survival ought to buy some authority over how the war was remembered. The Wizengamot had been ready to send him to Azkaban. He had been sixteen when he took the Mark and seventeen at the battle. The prosecution called that choice, as if any child in that house had ever possessed the kind of freedom the word implied.

So she'd testified. She had sat with both hands knotted in her lap, thumbnail biting into the heel of the other hand, and answered as narrowly as she could. The record showed what had happened in the drawing room, and she had no intention of saying the word on her arm aloud for a chamber full of quills. What she gave them instead was the part that mattered for judgment: Draco Malfoy had been a frightened boy in a war, and when the Snatchers dragged them into that room and demanded identification, he had looked at Harry's swollen face and lied. Fear was one thing. Mercy was another. Sitting there, she had not been willing to pretend either of them erased the other.

The verdict was acquittal with conditions and two years of wand restriction. He had stood when it was read, looked at the bench, looked at his mother, looked at the door, and left. From the gallery Hermione had waited anyway, furious with herself for waiting. A glance would have done it. Even a nod would have done it. He had given her nothing.

Six years later she could still feel that stupid pause in the gallery if the memory caught her tired.

The library was still the right lead. For weeks she'd known the gap in her research was historical. The anchoring infrastructure was older than the Ministry and partly built by old families; the Malfoys were one of them, and their archive had stayed private through war, scandal, and every post-war cleanup effort that had pried open the rest. Bathilda Bagshot mentioned the collection in a footnote. Heritage Division whispered about it over lunch. Lucius had shown off selected texts to scholars when it suited him and kept the foundational records locked away. That left Hermione with one remaining option and no clean feelings about it.

She authorized the assignment. Then she drafted the access request to the current head of the Malfoy household, outlining scope, departmental interest, handling protocols, and the compromise she could just barely imagine tolerating: scheduled access in daylight hours, no unnecessary escort, return to London each evening if practical. The first sentence came out too stiff. She struck through *required* and wrote *requested*. She started a line about accommodations and crossed that out entirely. By the time she had a version she could send, the page looked clean enough to pass for calm. It mentioned neither the trial nor the marble floor nor the room's dead cold pressing through her back.

Her best external hand made the letter look like correspondence between strangers. That, at least, was true. Whatever Draco Malfoy had become at twenty-five, she didn't know him. Whatever she had become, all work and exhaustion and a flat full of half-failed charms, he didn't know her either.

By the time she signed, sealed, and pushed back from the desk, her hands were shaking. For a few seconds she only watched them where they rested on the chair arms. The decision had reached her body later than her handwriting. Eventually the shaking eased. The corridor lights outside her office dimmed, steadied, dimmed again. She sat there until her hands were still enough to send the owl.

That should have been the end of the matter for the day, but it wasn't. Once the letter had gone, every ordinary task in front of her became faintly absurd. She still had a stack of stabilization tables to reconcile against the previous quarter. She still had to note the failed corridor lights in the maintenance ledger because pretending not to see them would not make them work. She still had to answer a junior researcher's request for her September field equations. She did all of it. The figures aligned under her hand. The equations packet went out with the right annotations. When she finally looked up, the office had emptied around her and the light had gone the dull color of late afternoon.

Only then did she realize she had missed lunch and most of the afternoon.

On the way out, she stopped by Section G again. The overnight basin had updated itself with new reports. A pediatric Healer in Birmingham had logged three wandless surges in children who had never shown that degree of force before. An apothecary in York had reported preservation charms dropping on regulated ingredients. Hermione wrote the reference numbers into the margin of Croaker's memo because her notebook was in her bag and she did not trust herself to go searching for it without rereading the copy of her own signature still ghosting the back of her mind.

By the time she left the Ministry, the letter to Wiltshire was already beyond recall.

---

Harry's note came Friday evening on a Ministry owl that looked nearly as frayed as the network.

The Floo had been down most of the day. North London's relay had failed again and the public explanation in the morning *Prophet* had used the phrase *scheduled maintenance*. Hermione had read it over tea at her desk and felt heat rise behind her eyes. Lying was infuriating on its own. Being lied to about data she'd gathered herself was worse.

His handwriting remained tight and slightly crooked, the trace of learning to write in a cupboard and never fully losing the angle of it.

*Floo's out again. Ginny says come for dinner Sunday. Don't bring wine, we've still got the six bottles from your birthday because you abandoned us with them. Have you slept at all this week?*

The note made her smile. Harry had known her since she was eleven, bossy, certain, and unbearable in very specific ways. He had stayed through every version since. He no longer asked the kind of question that left room for a graceful lie. He went after the practical failure and waited.

Back came her answer in a hand only slightly neater than his.

*I'm sleeping. I slept eight hours last night. I've taken up watercolours. I have a robust social calendar and many leisure interests. Tell Ginny yes to dinner. And I left early because Robards trapped me in a thirty-minute story about ward calibration and I could feel parts of my brain shutting down in self-defence.*

The quill hovered after that. She could tell him about the Manor now or wait for the Auror grapevine to do it for her within the week. She wrote *I have to go to Wiltshire on Monday* in her head, saw at once how Harry's face would change, and set the quill down without touching paper.

She told him nothing. Sunday would do. In person she could keep it factual for at least the first thirty seconds, which was usually long enough for Harry to hear *handled* and not yet hear the rest.

The second owl arrived after dark.

It hit the outer sill hard enough to make her look up from the kettle. Bigger than the Ministry bird and glossier, with rain slicking the feathers dark as ink, it waited at the window with the kind of patience that suggested it had been taught not to leave until answered. Hermione crossed the room, unlatched the pane, and took the envelope from its leg.

Cream paper. Green wax. The Malfoy crest pressed so sharply into the seal it looked cut instead of stamped.

For a second she only stood there with it in her hand. Then she put the kettle aside, broke the wax, and unfolded the letter.

The hand was not the one she remembered from school assignments thrown down in an irritated slant. This was narrower, more controlled, the lines held almost too tightly as if looseness itself had become a risk.

*Ms. Granger,*

*Your request is acceptable on a provisional basis. No volume from the library leaves the house. Given the scale of the collection and the state of several warded rooms, day access would be inefficient. If the Department intends a proper survey, the researcher will need to remain on site for the preliminary review. Monday morning is workable. Scope and conditions can be settled on arrival.*

*D. Malfoy*

That was all. No apology for the terms. No pleasantry. No acknowledgement of anything except the work and the fact of her name on the page.

Hermione read the letter again. The first request she'd made of herself after authorizing the assignment had been small and cowardly and understandable: go there, do the work, come home at the end of the day, keep London between herself and the house. Draco had just removed that option with six lines and a signature.

He was right. She knew he was right before the flare of anger had finished rising. Wiltshire was too far to commute sensibly without depending on networks already failing under strain. No private archive that old would allow books to move cleanly into Ministry custody, not with family wards laid through the collection and half the country's magic going unreliable around the edges. The house had kept the library private for generations. Of course it would insist on being consulted on its own ground.

She hated that he was right. She hated more that the letter made the practicalities look plain. There was not even room in it for the illusion that this might be a controlled visit.

The kettle screamed on the hob. Hermione snatched it off the flame, poured water without looking, and set the mug down untouched. Steam curled through the kitchen. The letter stayed open under her hand.

If she declined now, the Department would send someone else less qualified and probably less careful, or no one at all. The research would stall where it had already stalled. The corridor lights would keep dimming. Another child somewhere would put a window out with accidental magic because the country's field was misfiring under their feet and no one in authority wanted to say so aloud.

So there was only the next decision.

She sat at the table, pulled a clean sheet toward her, and wrote back.

*Mr. Malfoy,*

*Monday morning will be acceptable. I will require working access to the library, any associated catalogues or family reference systems, and a secure surface for Ministry notes. If there are house conditions relevant to handling the collection, please state them on arrival.*

*H. Granger*

She sanded the ink, sealed it, and only then noticed that she'd signed with an initial instead of her first name, as if parity could be arranged through stationery.

The owl waited while she tied the reply to its leg. When it had gone, the flat looked altered in a way that had nothing to do with magic. She stood in the kitchen with both hands on the table until the new fact settled into something solid. These would not be visits. There would be no trains back to London at dusk. She would sleep there.

The suitcase lived on top of the wardrobe because after the divorce there had been nowhere she particularly wanted to go. Hermione dragged a chair over, climbed up, and brought it down herself. The old extension charm on it had frayed two summers ago and she no longer trusted any repair she'd done under current field conditions, so she dug out the plain Muggle case from under the bed instead: hard-sided, black, inelegant, purchased in January when she'd started replacing household enchantments one appliance at a time with objects that did not depend on the atmosphere of British magic behaving itself.

Packing gave her hands something to do. She started with work because work was sortable. Devon notes. Hogwarts copies. Her current graphs. Three blank notebooks. The Ministry letters. A stack of sharpened pencils because self-inking quills had become temperamental and she was damned if she was going to arrive at Malfoy Manor at the mercy of unreliable nib charms. Then clothes. Wool jumpers. Dark skirts. Trousers practical enough for archive dust. Shirts that would survive being packed badly.

Halfway through, she found herself holding a pale blue blouse she had not worn since June. The fabric was too soft, too easily creased, wrong for ladders and old shelves and long hours bent over a desk. She folded it back into the drawer and chose a stiffer white one instead.

The motion should have felt ordinary. It did not. Each item placed into the case made the trip less hypothetical. Wand. Charger for the tablet. Hairpins. The tin of headache draught she kept in the bathroom cabinet. A Muggle torch. The small jar of ink that did not answer to refilling charms because it had been bought in a stationer's. By midnight the bed was buried in sorted piles and the case was only half shut because she'd packed books first and left herself nowhere for softness.

She stood back and looked at it with a disgust that had no useful object. The case itself was not the point, obviously, which only made the feeling more annoying. The trip kept becoming real in fresh, stupid increments. She had signed the memo, sent the owl, answered the letter, decided which pencils to trust. Authorizing the assignment had been one threshold. Agreeing to sleep under that roof was another.

On the kitchen table, Draco's letter sat beside Harry's note. One told her to come to dinner. The other told her to report to a house she had spent seven years treating as a location she could keep in the past if she was disciplined enough about not approaching it.

She should have written to Harry again. She should have said, *Actually, Sunday will have to be brief. I'm leaving for Wiltshire on Monday and I don't want to explain by owl.* She did not. He would arrive already worried. He would look at the case. He would ask the direct question. Hermione wanted one more day before that happened.

Past one, she carried the suitcase to the door and left it there because putting it back on the bed would have been a child's version of undoing the choice.

Sleep did not come.

She lay in the dark and listened to the pipes knock in the wall, the television through plaster next door, a siren thinning itself out somewhere toward Camden Road. Twice she got up to check that Malfoy's letter still said what she remembered it saying, as though the line about remaining on site might soften if read at two in the morning instead of eleven. It did not.

When lying still stopped even pretending to be rest, she went back to the kitchen and opened the incident copies she'd brought home from work. The newest report had arrived too late to make the day's briefing ledger. A child in Birmingham had panicked during a burst of accidental levitation and pulled every metal object in the room toward himself hard enough to split his mother's lip. The responding Obliviator noted household damage, neighbour exposure risk, and "parental distress significant." Hermione read that last phrase twice. Parental distress significant. Somewhere a Muggle mother was holding a tea towel to her mouth, looking at forks embedded in the wall and trying to comfort a child who thought he'd done something monstrous.

She copied the file reference into her notebook because if she was going to walk into the Manor on Monday she wanted the freshest figures in front of her, not the cleaner, older set she might have preferred to carry. The numbers were starting to outrun discretion. That was the whole point. The crisis did not wait for anyone's nerves to settle.

Near three, she showered because the flat had gone cold enough that standing under hot water felt simpler than continuing not to sleep. Steam fogged the mirror. When she came back into the bedroom in her dressing gown, the suitcase by the door was the first thing she saw.

Her body knew the route to panic well enough to begin without permission. Not a scream, not a collapse. Just the fast mean clarity of the drawing room snapping into place inside her head: marble under her spine, chandelier light in her eyes, Bellatrix's hand hard on her wrist, the knife point finding skin. Hermione pressed both palms to the edge of the dresser and made herself stay where she was until the room in front of her was only the room in front of her.

No one was here. No one was touching her. The walls of the flat stayed fixed. The window held. The suitcase remained a suitcase.

Eventually she could move again.

She dressed for bed a second time, checked the latch, checked it again, and lay down with one hand under the pillow and the other clamped over her left forearm. The charm was still there, slick and even under her palm, and beneath it the old raised letters seemed suddenly too close to the surface. She kept her hand there until her breathing evened out enough to pretend at sleep.

Sleep finally took her sometime after three, and even that felt closer to collapse than rest. At six the alarm went off to a cold room: the warming charm had bled out during the night. She showered, dressed, and snapped the suitcase shut.

Before leaving for work, she slid Croaker's memo and Draco Malfoy's letter into the front pocket of the suitcase by the door. The case looked ugly, practical, undeniable. Every time she crossed the flat, she saw it. Every time she saw it, the same fact waited there: on Monday she was going to Wiltshire, and she was not coming back at the end of the day.
