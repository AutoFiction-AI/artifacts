# Chapter 17

The flat hadn't changed.

Three days back in London, Hermione could cross it in the dark without surprise: six steps to the kitchen, four to the bathroom, radiator knocking in the same place every time. The predictability she'd chosen after the divorce should have felt like safety. Instead it felt sealed.

The Mark still found her from Wiltshire, but only as blunted data. A dull ache while she brushed her teeth. A flare at four in the morning sharp enough to wake her. The pain came loose from place. It hit while she stood at the sink or lay in the dark and told her only that somewhere in Wiltshire he was hurting. She could not tell whether he was standing or sitting or folded over the library table with his head in his hand. Whatever sustained contact had been doing to the curse, leaving had stripped it away.

By the third night sleep had reduced itself to one usable conclusion. Draco had not been angry that the house might be amplifying them. He had been angry that she had known the terms might be false and let him go on inside them anyway.

---

Thursday morning the Floo failed outright.

The Floo skipped the warning signs. At seven forty-three Hermione stepped into her fireplace, threw the powder, said, "Ministry of Magic," and got back an ordinary orange burn, fit for heat and useless for travel. Two more tries left soot on the knees of her trousers and a dark streak across one cuff.

Apparition took her to an Atrium already sliding toward panic. Technicians crowded the grates. Voices bounced off tile in that clipped professional register people used when fear would only slow them down. The outage had spread across London, parts of Bristol, most of the West Country.

Dennis Creevey intercepted her near the lifts with a stack of incident reports hugged to his chest. Muggle trainers showed under his robes. One sleeve was scorched. He looked about nineteen until he opened his mouth.

"We've had six accidental-magic incidents since last night," he said. "Children. The youngest is four."

"Six?"

"Ambient field went strange around three. We don't know if the collapse caused the cascade or the cascade took the Floo with it." He shifted the files higher. "Two kids are in St. Mungo's. One of them set her bedroom on fire. Infrastructure wants you downstairs after you've seen these."

Her hands were steady when she took the reports. That part still worked: intake, triage, sequence, action.

The reports were not bureaucratic-bad. They were frightened-bad. A nine-year-old in Dorset had shattered every window in his house during a nightmare. Twin girls in Camden had levitated the kitchen table for forty minutes and then started breaking plates with their crying. The four-year-old in Bristol was named Ruby. She had been crying in her crib when ambient magic superheated the air around her. Ruby was in the burn ward. Her parents were Muggles. The Obliviators had told them it was an electrical fault.

Reading that on a Ministry corridor wall made the crisis leave abstraction behind. Stabilization curves and node charts were one thing. A four-year-old with burns across her body because the field meant to buffer undeclared magic had slipped its bounds was another.

Dennis led her into a windowless workroom below the Atrium where half a dozen people had already colonized the table. A charmed map of southern Britain hovered over the center, lit with weak green points and angry red ones. The red had started clustering. A woman from Magical Infrastructure Hermione knew by sight, Patel, stood with her sleeves rolled up and both hands braced on the back of a chair. Croaker occupied the corner nearest the blackboard, coat still on, eyes giving away nothing except that he had not slept.

"Tell me you brought a miracle," Patel said without preamble.

"I brought soot and no breakfast."

"Close enough." Patel pointed at the map. "London relay degradation doubled between midnight and six. Bristol's worse. The West Country is a mess. Your Manor work is the only lead anybody keeps using the word promising about, so I need you to tell the room what we actually lose if that work stays stalled."

Hermione put the incident reports down and looked at the chart. It was uglier than the overnight version she had left on her desk. The red bands around London had widened and started feathering west. Bristol was almost solid. Somewhere between Birmingham and the Severn valley, one of the filum lines had begun blinking in and out of existence so fast the map charm kept losing the edge.

She took out the notebook she had carried back from Wiltshire on the night Draco told her to leave. It contained her own transcriptions, field numbers, copied diagrams, and just enough Malfoy shorthand in the margins to be infuriating. The cover was bent where she'd shoved it into her case too hard.

"The short answer," she said, "is that the Manor work had finally moved beyond identifying the damage. We were starting to sort out how the original network was meant to be repaired."

No one interrupted. Croaker's stillness had that effect on rooms.

"Tiberius's ledger gave us the broad structure," Hermione said. "Twelve anchor sites, filum threads, pressure routes between them. The Levett manuscript gave us the repair grammar: how damaged threads are plaited back toward a knot-point, how much pressure an anchor house can take before it starts shedding the excess into the field, what order the stabilizing work has to happen in if you don't want to tear the junction wider. What it didn't give cleanly were the Manor-specific maintenance clauses. Those are buried in family shorthand and property notation in the older archive material."

Patel frowned. "And the missing clauses matter because."

"Because a general repair model is not the same thing as a usable sequence. If you know a bridge needs support and not where the support sits, you still have a river and dead people in it."

"Fine. The archive."

"The archive only holds when both of us are there."

That landed harder than she'd expected in a Ministry room. People glanced at one another the way they did when a magical condition sounded too personal to be official.

Croaker said, "Explain."

"The hidden room is threshold-sensitive. The wall in the library does not open for me alone. It opens and stays open when Malfoy and I are both in it. We tested that weeks ago. We also discovered that the useful pages require both kinds of literacy. He reads the family notation and the property abbreviations. I read the runic construction text and the structural theory. One without the other is partial at best."

"Can he copy the material for you?" Patel asked.

"Not reliably enough. Some of it is too damaged. Some of it only makes sense against other books the house chooses to surface at the same time. And even with copies, we were still cross-checking live conditions at the Manor against the national field data. The house is one of the anchors. Working there was not just archival access. It was a pilot site."

Croaker lifted a hand slightly. "Observable results."

There it was. The part she had not yet managed to carry cleanly even in her own head. She opened the notebook to the pages she had already started wearing soft with her thumb.

"For the four days before the rupture," she said, "we were matching the Levett repair sequence to the Manor's local stress points and then tracking what the national monitors did after each successful pass. We were still testing, not repairing. But every time we managed a full run in the archive and I came back with a coherent set of clauses, the field numbers stopped worsening for a while. The west Atrium grate went from dropping twice an hour to holding for most of a morning. Two Bristol relays that had been shedding travellers into the wrong fireplaces stabilized for thirteen hours. Accidental-magic reports flattened for a little over a day instead of climbing."

Patel's head came up. "You're certain that correlation tracks the Manor work and not a temporary easing in the field?"

"No," Hermione said. "I'm not certain. That's the point. We had four days of improvement in a crisis that's been getting worse for months. That is not proof. It might have been the house protecting its own anchor once it sensed we were close to the right material. It might have been coincidence in a small sample. But it was the first bend in the numbers that wasn't random noise."

Croaker said, "And now."

Hermione looked at the red bands. "Now it's gone."

The workroom stayed quiet for a beat too long.

Patel dragged a hand over her mouth. "Could the stalled component be the archive access, or you?"

"Both," Hermione said before she could dress the answer up. "The room itself requires paired presence. And the one useful pattern we had depended on both kinds of reading happening in the room while the Manor was under live load. We weren't just deciphering texts. We were comparing them against an anchor while it was actively straining."

"So if you resume, the improvement might resume."

"Might."

"And if you don't?"

"Then we have my copies, which are incomplete, and a national field collapse that no longer has even a provisional brake on it."

Patel looked at Croaker. Croaker looked at Hermione. There were more comfortable ways to be cornered.

"What prevents resumption?" he asked.

There was no usable professional answer to that question. Not one that would survive cross-examination and still belong to her.

Hermione kept her face level. "Access I don't currently have."

Patel's mouth flattened. "Can the Ministry compel it?"

The answer that came first was logistical. The real answer arrived half a breath later and stayed behind her teeth.

"You can compel people," Hermione said. "You cannot compel a sentient house not to be itself. You can put ten curse-breakers in that library and the wall will stay wall if the condition isn't met. And if you try to muscle an anchor house while the field is already unstable, you may do more than waste your time."

Croaker said, "Can we test anything from what you brought back?"

"Yes." She looked down at the notebook. "Not enough. But yes."

They moved downstairs again, deeper into the Ministry, to a service chamber off the relay maintenance corridor where one of the Atrium grates had been taken apart for inspection. The bricks behind it had been stripped back to reveal the braided channels in the wall. Under diagnostic light the channels looked like veins lit the wrong colour, most of them a tired amber, one of them spitting sick orange sparks at irregular intervals.

Patel crouched by the open panel. "This is the same stretch that's been dropping to ordinary fire mid-transit."

"Because the load is backing up at the knot-point," Hermione said.

"You can tell that from looking."

"No. I can tell it because the last eight hours of monitor data and three hundred years of dead Malfoys are all saying the same thing."

Dennis, who had followed carrying two instrument cases and a face that suggested he had not heard the word breakfast since childhood, set the cases down and popped them open. Inside were relay calipers, chalk, a brass pressure dial, and a narrow box of warding nails. He handed Hermione the chalk without asking whether she wanted it. This was why she liked him.

She knelt by the wall. Heat beat at her cheeks. The failing channel had exactly the wrong rhythm: pressure, stall, spill; pressure, stall, spill. Not random after all. That at least was something.

"If the Levett sequence is right," she said, more to herself than to the room, "the knot should take pressure again if the damaged thread is braced before the spill point instead of after. We've been trying to catch it too late."

Patel said, "We."

"Humanity."

Hermione copied the provisional sequence from her notes onto the brick with chalk. The symbols looked inadequate the moment they left her hand, too clean and recent against old Ministry stone. She adjusted the third marker, added the pressure notation Levett used for partial rethreading, then stopped over the final space.

That was where the Manor clauses had started forking. One family annotation said to bind the anchor to its own memory before feeding it load. Another, later hand had contradicted it in shorthand she could only half read without Draco's impatient translations across the table. The difference between the two had been one of the things they were supposed to settle this week.

Patel noticed her stop. "Problem."

"Missing instruction."

"Can you infer it?"

"I can guess."

Croaker said, "Do it."

Hermione drew the final sigil from her best approximation and put her palm flat to the stone. "Dennis."

He passed her the dial and fed controlled magic into the side channel. The orange flare sharpened, went white for one alarming second, then thinned. The heat in the brick dropped. One of the upper channels brightened from amber to green. Somewhere overhead a bell chimed twice as the monitor room registered the change.

Nobody in the service corridor breathed.

Hermione counted under her breath. Five. Six. Seven.

Dennis looked from the wall to the dial. "Pressure's leveling."

"For now," Hermione said.

At eleven seconds the orange came back hard enough to crack two of the chalk marks. At fourteen the side channel spat sparks into Dennis's sleeve. At sixteen the whole brace failed with a sound like a kettle screaming. Patel threw up a shield before the hottest of it hit the floor. The orange light collapsed back to its original sick pulse.

Nobody had lost skin. That made the attempt a success by current national standards.

Patel exhaled first. "That was better than we've had all week."

"That was a brace," Hermione said. "A bad one. It bought us sixteen seconds on a controlled channel and told us the failure point really is where the Manor texts say it is. That's useful. It's not repair."

Croaker asked, "What would make it repair."

Hermione was still kneeling. Her palm stung from the heat. "The actual Manor clause instead of my guess. The rest of the sequence that tells the anchor where to send the pressure once you've got its attention. And the paired work that let us identify which clauses were functional and which were ceremonial rubbish."

Patel leaned against the opposite wall and tipped her head back. "I hate that your answer keeps being the same."

"So do I."

They ran the test twice more. The second try failed faster. The third bought twenty-two seconds and scorched one of Dennis's instruments beyond recovery. Twenty-two seconds was still not a repair. It was also twenty-two seconds longer than the relay had stayed coherent that morning. By noon three departments were calling down for updates and nobody had anything fit to give them except not enough and maybe later.

Hermione spent the next three hours turning the service-corridor results into something the Ministry could use without mistaking it for hope. The brief she wrote reduced itself to a list of limits: provisional brace on degraded local channel, confirms knot-point theory, not field-ready, requires missing Manor sequence, requires in situ verification. She took out one sentence about paired presence and put it back in because it was true. She took out one sentence about the house being an active participant because there was no bureaucratic language that didn't make it sound either childish or insane. In the end she left in threshold condition, pilot-site relevance, and nothing helpful.

At half three the Mark flared so sharply she had to brace both hands against her desk. White went across her vision and receded. By the time she could straighten, the spike had flattened into the usual dull pull. He was alive. He was in pain. He was still there.

Dennis looked up from the opposite table. "You all right?"

"Fine."

"That didn't look fine."

"It wasn't dramatic enough to be anything else."

He stared another second and, to his credit, did not push. Instead he came over and dropped two new reports on the desk.

"Three more since noon," he said. "One in Croydon, two in Bath. No burns this time. One levitation event, one burst of transfiguration, one household concealment failure that took half a street with it."

Hermione closed her eyes for one second, no more. "Thank you."

"Do you want tea?"

"No."

"Do you want me to get you tea anyway."

She opened her eyes. "Yes."

He left. She looked at the reports and did not open them immediately. Across the room, two junior researchers were arguing over whether Floo instability should still be modelled as a transport problem or formally reclassified as field collapse. Somewhere above them a grate coughed green, failed, and went orange again. The Ministry was full of sounds like people pretending systems still counted as systems if you shouted the proper nouns hard enough.

By six the Floo was technically back and practically untrustworthy. Green fire kept guttering orange mid-transit. Croaker signed the brief with the expression of a man authorizing something worse than failure because failure at least ended. Patel sent three copies to Infrastructure and one to St. Mungo's so the Healers could stop asking whether the relay damage was a separate emergency.

The bottom drawer of Hermione's desk held a jumper and a pair of trainers for nights she slept on the office sofa. She looked at them for a while and shut the drawer again.

Home was colder than she remembered. The warming charm on the radiator failed twice before it held. She did not bother with dinner at first. She spread the Manor notebook, loose Ministry charts, and three copied vellum pages across the kitchen counter and then onto the floor when the counter proved too small.

At the Manor the work had looked almost respectable. One table for Tiberius's map. One for Levett's repair tables. One for the family material that only made sense after Draco read the shorthand aloud in the clipped, irritated voice he used when old relatives had been idiots on parchment. She had hated how easily the division of labour fit. He knew what every symbol had once been meant to conceal. She knew which concealments mattered to the network and which were family vanity. Together, for a few hours at a time, they had been able to turn old damage into solvable sequences.

On her kitchen floor in London, all of that coherence vanished.

She could still read the runic body text. She could still follow the plaiting instructions in the Levett pages. What she could not do was move from those abstractions into the ugly practical clauses in the margins, the bits where a Malfoy two centuries dead had noted which wall warmed first under strain, which west-facing seam took pressure badly in winter, which signs meant an anchor was answering and which meant it was about to bite. Draco had translated those pieces without ceremony, one hand flat on the page, the other on his tea. She had written them down fast enough to keep up and never once really understood how much of the bridge between theory and use was his.

She sat cross-legged in the middle of the papers and tried anyway.

By nine she had recreated three different versions of the missing relay clause from memory. One contradicted Levett. One contradicted the service-corridor result. One might have been right if she had transcribed a single abbreviation correctly the night before the rupture, which she could no longer swear she had. The notebook acquired new slashes, circles, and sidebars. Nothing in it became trustworthy enough to hand back to Croaker with her name attached.

The Mark pulled low and mean in her left forearm.

She pushed her sleeve to the elbow and looked at the skin. The glamour held: winter-pale forearm, a vein faintly blue at the wrist, no silver-white lettering breaking through. The ache worked deeper than skin, a buried hook dragging slow.

What unsettled her was not only the pain. It was the shape of it. The spikes had changed since she'd left. At the Manor, after the later examinations, the curse had started doing something almost rational under her hands. It quieted. Not fully, not cleanly, but enough that she could separate baseline pain from stress response, the old command magic from whatever new feedback had begun to sit between them. In London the old distinctions had blurred. The Mark now yanked like something half-trained and then abandoned.

That, too, was data. Miserable data, but data.

She took out a clean sheet and wrote the only version of the problem that did not lie.

Without direct contact, the Mark reverts.

Without paired archive access, the repair sequence stalls.

Without both, field variance resumes acceleration.

The three sentences sat there in her hand, bleak and plain. They made the rupture sound like mechanics. They turned his body and his house into inputs and left out the part where he had never agreed to be either. She stared at the page until the omission became impossible to tolerate.

She turned the sheet over and wrote nothing on the back.

Dinner eventually came from a tin and was eaten standing up at the counter because sitting at the table alone felt like accepting a permanence she wasn't ready for. The lentil soup tasted faintly of metal under the salt. Draco had made risotto once, late in the third week, stirring with the same patience he gave to ruined marginalia and broken spells. She had grated cheese over both bowls while he shoved books out of splash range with the back of his wrist. They had eaten with the books shoved aside and argued over a fourteenth-century annotation until she forgot to be careful for almost an hour.

She set the bowl in the rack and still had him in the kitchen with her, wrist shoving books out of splash range while the rice thickened in the pan.

Friday was worse.

The first memo hit her desk before eight. Two more outages overnight. Seven additional accidental-magic events, three of them in houses whose wards should have contained a child's surge without anyone outside the family noticing. An internal note from Infrastructure asked whether Malfoy Manor could be formally designated a strategic repair site. A second note, from someone more junior and less careful, asked whether the "paired threshold condition" could be replicated with blood, objects, or coercive magic.

Hermione read that one twice before folding it in half so sharply it tore.

By nine she was back in Croaker's office with the torn memo on his desk between them. The room smelled of dust, parchment, and the old iron filing cabinets he refused to replace with anything charmed.

"No," she said.

Croaker looked at the memo. "I assume that is the answer to the proposal, not a general statement of mood."

"Both."

"Sit down."

She sat.

He took off his glasses, cleaned them with the edge of his sleeve, and put them back on. It was one of the few indulgently human things he did.

"I need the practical version," he said. "Not the outraged one."

"The practical version is still no. We do not know enough to start substituting bodies and force for a condition we barely understand. The house is already under strain. If we try to cheat a threshold-sensitive anchor in the middle of a national collapse, we may not get a neat refusal. We may get rupture."

"And the outraged version."

Hermione looked at the memo again. "The outraged version is that Malfoy is not a reagent."

Croaker's face did not change. "I assumed you understood that before this morning."

So had she. That was the problem.

"I did," she said.

He let the answer sit. He was very good at silence when silence was the sharpest available instrument.

"Can the work continue here?" he asked after a moment.

"Not in any way that matters quickly enough."

"Then if the only serious lead remains in Wiltshire, I need to know whether there is any professional obstacle to your resuming it."

Professional obstacle. The phrase was almost kind. It gave her one last chance to lie in a language both of them could share.

Hermione said, "No."

Croaker nodded once, as if the answer confirmed a calculation he had already made. "Then the obstacle is personal."

"Yes."

"Is it likely to remain so for long."

Her laugh had no amusement in it at all. "That depends how patient you think betrayal should make somebody."

Croaker folded the memo and set it aside. "I am not asking you for confession, Granger."

"Good."

"I am asking whether I should continue making plans that assume the Manor work is paused indefinitely."

The truthful answer was I don't know. The more truthful answer was I do not know because I no longer trust my own motives near that house, and I have already proved what damage that can do. She gave him the part that could survive office air.

"No," she said. "Do not assume indefinitely."

His eyes stayed on her for another second. "All right."

That should have been the end of it. Instead he said, "The people in Infrastructure are going to keep trying to make this simpler than it is. Do not let them."

"I won't."

"And if the answer is that you need to go back, then go back before the crisis decides for you."

There were half a dozen replies available to that. Hermione used none of them.

The rest of Friday passed in the particular misery of being the person nearest a solution and not near enough. The relay brace from Thursday could be reproduced now that she knew where it failed. That made it useful to technicians and useless to the country. She spent the morning training two Infrastructure teams to buy themselves twenty seconds, thirty if the brick was kind, at degraded local knot-points. Enough to stop a transit chamber from fully collapsing while they cleared it. Enough to move a burned child from Bristol to St. Mungo's without losing the healer halfway through. Not enough to call anything stable.

At noon Dennis brought her a fresh stack of intake reports and a sandwich she had not asked for.

"You have to stop doing that," she said, looking at the sandwich.

"No, I don't."

"You are twenty-three."

"And you're impossible."

He said it without heat. Hermione almost smiled.

"Ruby's still stable," he added. "Parents are asking different questions now, which is better than the first kind of questions. There's a team with them."

"Good."

"And the west grate held twenty-eight seconds on the last run."

"That's not nothing."

"No." Dennis hesitated. "Do you think the Manor work would still do what it was doing if you went back today."

Because he was Dennis, the question contained no gossip at all. Only need.

Hermione set the sandwich down. "I think the archive would open. I think the texts would still be the texts. I think the field might ease again for a little while if we resumed the sequence we were on."

"But."

"But none of that is the only question anymore."

Dennis nodded as if he had not understood and had still, somehow, understood enough. "Right."

He left her with the sandwich. She ate half of it because he was annoying enough to make disobedience childish.

By half five the whole building had the stretched, over-bright quality institutions got when everyone in them knew the weekend would not mean rest. Green flames in the Atrium were back, but only in the temperamental way old machinery returned after being slapped. People were taking stairs rather than trust them. Three different departments had started drafting public language that all meant we have not lost control yet.

Hermione took her notebook, the relay brief, and the half-smashed remains of Dennis's scorched pressure dial back to her flat because leaving any of it at the office felt like tempting arson by bureaucracy.

Harry knocked at half six with takeaway from the Thai place near the Ministry and the look he wore when he had decided to keep showing up until the job was done.

---

"I brought food," he said. "The Floo's being strange again, so you're stuck with me till it settles."

Relief was immediate enough to irritate her. Even that she was too tired to hide.

"If you've come to stage an intervention, at least tell me whether it's the edible kind."

He lifted the paper bag. "Depends what they did to the spring rolls."

They ate on the sofa. The pad see ew was overcooked. The spring rolls had gone cold. Harry noticed she was actually eating and was decent enough not to mention it. He also noticed the papers spread over half the coffee table and one dining chair dragged in beside the sofa because she'd needed the extra flat surface.

"You've colonized the flat," he said.

"It's temporary."

"That sounds like something people say just before they end up living inside a pile of evidence."

"I already live inside a pile of evidence."

"You really do."

He took another bite, chewed, and looked at her over the container. "You look like shit."

"Thanks."

"I mean it. When did you last sleep properly?"

"Define properly."

"Hermione."

"I'm sleeping some. It's been a bad week."

"It's been three bad days and one catastrophic one. Dennis has started using the tone people use about bomb disposal."

"Dennis is twenty-three and covering three jobs. He doesn't need my sleeping habits on his list."

"He was worried." Harry set his container down. "The girl in Bristol is stable, apparently. Team's with the parents. But thirteen kids in less than two days, Hermione."

"I know."

"Kingsley's getting pressure from the Wizengamot. Infrastructure's losing their minds. Croaker looked like he was considering murder by memo." He tipped his head toward the papers. "And you brought the whole office home."

"Only the bits that aren't setting things on fire."

"That's not as reassuring as you think."

She put her own container down before she dropped it. "The decay's accelerating."

"Because."

Because the answer had spent the whole day demanding clearer language, she gave it to him in clearer language.

"Because the Manor work had finally become operational instead of theoretical," she said. "We weren't only finding old descriptions anymore. We were laying Tiberius's anchor map over current failure points and using the Levett manuscript to work out how the damaged threads are supposed to be fed back toward a live knot-point. The hidden archive only stays open when Malfoy and I are both in it. He reads the family shorthand. I read the construction text. The house only gave us the useful material when both of those things were happening at once."

Harry listened without interrupting. That alone meant he was worried.

"And?" he said.

"And for four days the numbers stopped getting worse every hour. Not fixed. Not even close. But the west Atrium grate held longer. Two Bristol relays stopped misrouting travellers for half a day. Accidental-magic reports flattened instead of climbing. Yesterday I could only reproduce a brace that bought a damaged channel twenty-two seconds."

"So the work was helping."

"It looked like it was helping."

"What's the difference."

"The difference is that one is evidence and the other is hope. We had evidence of improvement. We did not have proof of cause. It could have been the Manor protecting its own anchor once it sensed we were getting close. It could have been a temporary lull. It could have been both."

"But it stopped when you left."

"Yes."

"Because you're not there."

"Because I'm not there."

Harry looked at her for a beat. "So go back."

"I can't."

"Because of what happened."

"Yes."

He took another bite. The casualness was deliberate. It gave her somewhere to put her eyes that wasn't his face.

"Do you want to tell me what actually happened?" he said. "Because the story about disagreement over methodology is bollocks."

"Harry."

"I'm asking. That's not the same as pushing."

Hermione tucked her feet under her and tried, briefly, to think of a cleaner version. None existed.

"I found something in the archive," she said. "About the house. It amplifies emotional connections between the people living in it. It was built to do that. I found the text weeks ago, and I didn't tell Malfoy."

"How long did you sit on it."

"Weeks."

He nodded once. "And he found out himself."

"He found the same folio. Worked out when I'd read it. Worked out what changed after." Her grip tightened around the cardboard container. "I stayed. I kept working. I kept being close to him without telling him what I knew that closeness might mean."

"To you."

The word sat there and did not soften.

Harry put his dinner down. "What are you angry about?"

"What?"

"Right now. What keeps you up."

The first answer rose fast enough to feel true. He hadn't let her explain. He had decided what she'd meant. He had made her into something colder than she was.

Except he hadn't done that. He had named what she'd done.

"I'm angry that he decided I'd been manipulating him," she said. "That I'd been studying him like he was a subject."

"Hadn't you been."

"Not like that."

"What would 'like that' be."

The radiator ticked. Outside, the parked car across the road blinked its hazards into the window glass.

"The research required proximity. It required the examinations. It required both of us in the archive. That doesn't mean I was using him."

"I'm not asking about the research."

She stopped.

"So go tell him that," Harry said. "You screwed up, you know you screwed up. Go say so."

"You make that sound simple."

"Isn't it?"

"No. Because he's not entirely wrong about me." She pressed her thumbnail into the side of her index finger. "He got it partly right. And he didn't let me explain."

"Explain what."

"That I was going to tell him."

"When."

"When I'd verified."

"Right. When you were ready."

The car alarm outside gave one thin warning chirp and cut off again.

"That's not the same thing," she said.

"Yeah, it is."

She said nothing.

"He found out before you were ready," Harry said. "And that's— I mean, that's the bit, isn't it."

"That's not." The denial started and she heard it for what it was.

"You were scared," Harry said. "That makes sense. The house might be messing with your feelings, and that's, yeah. Of course you were scared."

"But."

"But you sat on it. And while you were sitting on it, he was still in it. Doing the whole thing. Not knowing."

Her face had ended up against her knees without her quite noticing. The fabric smelled faintly of soy sauce and paper.

"I was trying to be sure."

"I know." He put the fork down. "I'm just saying what it looks like from his end."

The connection in her arm pulled low and steady.

"It could all be the Manor," she said into the cloth. "Everything I feel. Everything he feels."

"Could be."

"You're not going to tell me it's real."

"How would I know? I'm not inside that house."

That almost made her laugh. Not quite.

"I know you, though," he said. "That wasn't thorough. That was scared."

"That's unfair."

"It's a bit unfair. It's also true."

Wet-eyed and too tired to care, Hermione lifted her head. Harry had seen her cry in a girl's bathroom at twelve, in a forest at seventeen, and in plenty of smaller years since. He had never once made a spectacle of it.

"Croaker asked this morning whether there was any professional obstacle to my going back," she said.

"And."

"And I said no, because professionally there isn't one. The work's there. The texts are there. The house will probably open. The country is still coming apart." She rubbed at one eye with the heel of her hand. "But if I go back for the research, then I'm doing it again. I am. I'm making it about the thing I can solve instead of the thing I can't."

Harry nodded.

"Then don't go back for the research," he said.

The sentence didn't solve anything. It only stripped the lie out of the problem. If she went back, it couldn't be with field charts under her arm and national emergency in her mouth. The first thing she said had to be about what she'd hidden. The archive could wait to learn whether he wanted her in the house at all.

After a while she said, "The pad see ew was terrible."

"I know. The spring rolls were worse."

"I ate two."

"I saw. That's how bad this is."

Later, when the conversation had loosened by a notch, Harry asked how Dennis was holding up and said he reminded him a little of Colin at that age, all stubborn elbows and accidental competence. Hermione told him Dennis was covering three desks and pretending not to be furious about it. Harry said that sounded exactly right for the Creevey line. She told him she'd be in on Monday. He told her to tell Dennis herself because he wasn't her secretary. Familiar bickering returned by reflex, thin but real.

At nine the Floo was still unreliable, so Harry Apparated from the doorstep. Cold rushed into the flat before she shut the door again.

The takeaway containers stayed on the table until the noodles stuck to the cardboard. She should have binned them, washed the forks, done one ordinary thing before bed. Instead she carried the coffee-table papers to the desk, separated the Ministry work from the Manor notes, and sat down with a clean sheet.

The first line she wrote was: The Floo failed across half the country yesterday.

She stared at it, then crossed it out so hard the nib tore the paper.

The second line was: Ruby Melling is four years old and in the burn ward because the field slipped again.

She crossed that out too.

The third attempt began as an explanation of the relay brace, of the missing clauses in the Levett sequence, of how the knot-point theory had survived testing even if the brace itself had not. Halfway through the sentence she put the pen down. It was almost impressive, the speed with which her brain kept offering the same evasion in new technical clothes.

When she picked the pen up again, she did not try for a letter. She wrote down what the first conversation would have to do if it happened at all.

Do not ask for the archive first.

Do not put field charts first.

Do not say "the country needs" before "I knew."

The bluntness of it made her wince. It also looked more honest than anything else she had put on paper since leaving Wiltshire.

She added one more line beneath the others.

Do not ask him to help before I tell him what I did without dressing it up as method.

That was ugly enough to be close to true.

Her hand hovered over the page. For a moment she considered writing the rest of it out properly, sentence by sentence, and stopped before the page could turn into a case brief. She was too practiced at cases.

Instead she folded the sheet once and slid it into the front of the Manor notebook where the provisional relay brief had been. It was not a letter or a plan. It was a condition.

The Mark tugged again, low and stubborn. She pressed her thumb to the inside of her left wrist and sat with it without trying to turn it into data for once. Somewhere in Wiltshire he was in pain. Somewhere in London Ruby slept under burn spells and St. Mungo's wards. Somewhere below the Ministry, technicians were buying half-minutes from failing brickwork because she had brought them twenty-two seconds and not more.

All of it kept pressing on the same point. If there was going to be an ask, it had to begin with what she'd hidden.

She turned out the desk lamp and went to bed before she could start writing the wrong draft again. Sleep was poor when it finally came, but it came.
