# Chapter 13

The pub was a Muggle place near Charing Cross Road, a narrow room with uncomfortable chairs and middling beer and no associations. She and Ron had started using it after the divorce because neither of them had ever kissed or argued or decided anything in this building, which was exactly why it worked. There was no memory to navigate around. It was just a room.

She'd Apparated into the alley behind the used bookshop three doors down and walked the rest, her coat pulled close against the December cold. The Floo would have been easier, but the Floo was unreliable now. There had been three failures in the last week alone, connections dropping mid-transit, and she didn't fancy rematerializing in someone's kitchen in Birmingham because the network had lost her routing. So she had Apparated, with the familiar squeeze and the ringing in her ears afterward, and then London came back at her all at once: damp pavement under her boots, diesel from a bus taking the corner too fast, wet cardboard in the bookshop bin, a cyclist swearing at a van. After weeks of controlled quiet, the city felt almost aggressively public. It wasn't bad. It was only more than she was used to.

Ron was already there. These days he was always early, a habit he'd acquired only after the separation. At the sight of her, he stood, which was new too, some politeness he'd adopted lately that she hadn't asked for and didn't correct because correcting it would mean discussing it, and discussing it would mean admitting the shape of their interactions had changed.

"Hey." He kissed her cheek. His face was cold. "You look — you look good."

It came out slightly wrong, the compliment carrying too much weight because he'd had to decide to say it, and the decision was audible. It wasn't worth rescuing.

"Got you a cider. Is that still right?"

"That's perfect."

They sat. The pub was half-empty at two in the afternoon: a few regulars nursing pints, a woman reading a paperback in the corner, a football match on the mounted television with the sound off. Ron glanced at it. Since the divorce he'd become genuinely interested in football, one of the small expansions his life had made without her in it. He'd explained the offside rule to her twice and she still didn't understand it, which he found funny in a way that wasn't mean. The fact that he could find it funny instead of irritating was one of the ways she knew they'd done the right thing.

"How's work?" she asked.

"Good. Weird. George has got me managing the books for the second shop and I'm completely rubbish at it, but it turns out that when I'm rubbish at the books, George does them himself, and then they're actually done right. So it's a system."

Her laugh brought out a grin, pleased with itself for getting it and then slightly embarrassed by the pleasure. A second later his ears went pink and he looked down at his beer, and the familiarity of it pulled at the old, muted place where their marriage used to live. It wasn't loss. It was fondness for the thing they'd been that had become the thing they were now, which was different and less and also, in ways she still couldn't fully articulate, more honest.

"You?"

"Still at the Manor. The collection keeps turning up material no one's catalogued properly. It's useful." The professional frame arrived automatically, the one that cost her nothing. "More useful than anything else we've got."

Ron drank his beer. Across from her he looked broader than she remembered, which happened every time she saw him after a gap. Her mental image of him was still nineteen and lanky; the man in front of her was twenty-five, solid, and slightly sunburned from the running Harry had mentioned. He'd taken it up in the summer and kept going through autumn. Now he ran along the canal near his flat three mornings a week, and the idea of Ron voluntarily running, Ron, who at school would have considered a brisk walk to the Great Hall excessive exertion, was one of the small surprises of their post-marriage life that she found genuinely, uncomplexly good.

"So you're there full-time," he said. It wasn't a question.

"Mostly. I come back for meetings."

"And Malfoy's there."

"It's his house, Ron."

"Yeah, I know. I know how it sounds." He stopped, rubbed the back of his neck, then started again with the visible effort of someone rerouting a sentence away from where it was headed. "I mean the house. How's the house? Like, is it... fine? As a place to be?"

There were several ways she could answer that. The house was extraordinary, terrifying, beautiful, sentient, responsive, and currently adjusting its internal geography to accommodate her preferences in ways she hadn't asked for. The house had shown her the room where she'd been tortured and then, weeks later, started warming her bathwater to the exact temperature she preferred. The house knew things about her that she hadn't told it.

"It's unusual," she said. "But manageable."

Ron gave her the look reserved for obvious evasions, the one that said he'd heard the gap and was still deciding whether to push on it. He'd gotten better at that since the divorce. During the marriage he'd either pushed or he hadn't, with almost no calibration. Now there was a middle range, a pause where he weighed it.

"Hermione."

"What?"

"You said manageable. You didn't say fine."

She took a swallow of cider. The pub was warm with the blunt warmth of a radiator on a December afternoon. From the corner came the dry sound of a page turning, and from the regulars' end of the bar came the muted commentary of the match. Beneath all of it, like a radio signal from very far away, ran the low hum of the Mark resonance. It was faint in London and thinned further by distance, but it was there. Draco's pain, that restless ache the Mark carried now, magic searching for a channel that no longer existed, travelled through miles of December air and settled in her forearm like an echo.

Pressure helped, so she pressed her wrist against the edge of the table.

"I don't have a neat answer for you," she said. "The house is complicated. The research is real and it matters. And I'm handling it."

A strip of label was already coming loose beneath Ron's thumb, a nervous habit she'd forgotten about and now remembered with the sharpness of an old object found at the back of a drawer. The recognition was almost physical. All through their engagement he'd done that in restaurants, peeling labels while they talked about the wedding or didn't talk about the things they should have been talking about instead.

"George and Angelina are getting serious," he said after a while.

"Really?"

"Yeah. She's been at Sunday dinners three weeks running. Mum's broken out the good china. You know what that means."

"Molly Weasley pulling out the good china is practically a betrothal announcement."

"Don't say that to George, he'll bolt." Ron smiled. The smile was genuine and easy and had nothing to do with Hermione, which was the kind of smile she liked best from him now, the ones that were about his life, not about their shared past. "Anyway. She's good for him. She's got this way of just not taking his shit, you know? Like, he'll do the George thing where he deflects with a joke and she just... waits. Doesn't laugh. Waits until he says the real thing."

"Good."

"Yeah."

He drank, set the bottle down, and looked at her over the rim of it. "She's also made me start buying actual food."

"Ron."

"I know. Deeply embarrassing. There's coriander in my fridge right now."

"What are you doing with coriander?"

"Letting it go bad, mostly." A quick, crooked grin. "Anyway. I've got a routine now, is the thing. It's boring. It's good."

Her fingers tightened once around the glass.

"I do eat," she said.

"I didn't say you didn't."

"You meant it."

"I meant—" He rubbed at the ragged strip of label with his thumb. "Yeah. I meant it a bit."

The woman in the corner turned another page. Someone at the bar laughed. Hermione sat in the middle of those ordinary human sounds and felt the resonance pulse, faint but continuous, a thread that connected her to a house in Wiltshire and the man inside it. Ron, peeling his label across from her, had no idea that she could feel another person's pain in her own arm. He didn't know that a house might be engineering half the comfort it offered, or that the reason she couldn't say fine was stranger than anything he was probably imagining.

"Listen," Ron said. He put the beer bottle down. The label was half off, a ragged strip of wet paper. "I know it's not my — I know I don't get to say this. But I'm going to say it once and then shut up."

"Okay."

"You do this thing." He wasn't looking at her. "Where you find a problem and it becomes — look, you did it with Buckbeak's appeal. Third year. You did it with the Horcruxes. You did it with us, our marriage, when it was ending, you had a whole — you researched the divorce like a project, Hermione, and it was the most organized breakup in history and also the saddest thing I've ever been part of."

"Ron—"

"I'm not — that's not a dig. I don't even know if I'm saying it right." He pressed his thumb into the shredded label. "Just that with us I kept thinking I'd say something, and then I didn't, and by the time I did it was—" He stopped. "You know."

She didn't say anything. The cider had gone warm in her glass.

"That house," he said. "That family. The research and the decay and whatever else is happening that you're not telling me. I don't mean Malfoy — or, I don't know, maybe I do, I don't actually care about that bit. I just—" He stopped. Started again, quieter. "I just don't want to ring you in three months and find out you haven't left the building. All right?"

She turned her glass on the table. Once. Back again.

"You're not wrong," she said.

He blinked. "That's new. You admitting I'm not wrong."

"Don't get used to it."

He smiled. The rueful, self-knowing one she'd always liked best on him. "Wouldn't dream of it."

By the time they were putting on coats, he was telling her about the flat. He'd finally hung the shelves in the bedroom, badly, and they'd already fallen down once. This weekend he was going to try again with a level because Harry had pointed out that eyeballing it wasn't working. In return she gave him a translation problem she'd been stuck on, kept vague and professional. As ever, he listened without tracking every technical detail and still caught the shape of it, the frustration and the fascination, nodding in the right places.

"Oh," he said, as they headed for the door. "The other thing. Mum went to St. Mungo's for her knee, the old jinx, the Moody one, and they turned her away. Said they're rationing healing magic. Prioritizing critical cases."

"I know about the rationing."

"I figured you would." He wound his scarf around his neck, a Chudley Cannons scarf, orange and faded, that she'd given him three Christmases ago when they were still married. He still wore it. That left her with no idea what to do. "The Floo dropped again this morning. Two hours. And a kid in Diagon Alley, six maybe, set a rubbish bin on fire. Accidental magic, but the kind that shouldn't happen that young, not that strongly."

"I know."

"Right." He looked at her. "If the work matters, the stuff you're doing at the Manor, then I suppose it matters. I just want you to come back from it. That's all."

At the door she hugged him. He was warm and solid and smelled like beer, cold air, damp wool, and the soap he'd used since school. His return hug was careful, his arms finding her the old way and then stopping short, leaving space between her ribs and his as if he were measuring it by feel.

"Take care of yourself," he said.

"You too."

He left. She watched him walk up Charing Cross Road toward the Leaky, hunching into his coat, and felt the ache of watching someone you loved walk away from you in the most ordinary way, not leaving, just going, back to his flat and his badly hung shelves and his Sunday dinners and the life he was building that didn't need her in it. It wasn't loss. Loss had already had its turn. This was the healed place afterward, skin closed over, only a faint pull when an old bruise got pressed.

On the pavement she felt the December cold on her face and the Mark resonance in her arm. When she pressed her wrist against the brick wall beside her, the wall was only a wall, cold and rough and utterly indifferent to her touch.

---

Harry's office at the Department of Magical Law Enforcement was the same corner room they'd given him two promotions ago. He'd done nothing with it except accumulate files and add a photograph of Ginny and James that sat on the desk facing outward. James was nine months old in the picture, chewing on a toy broomstick with the focused commitment of a child who'd located the centre of his universe.

"You look tired," Harry said.

"Hello to you too."

He stood and hugged her, a real hug with both arms, the kind of contact that required no negotiation because the friendship had survived a war and everything after. It could take the weight. He smelled like cedar soap. He'd used the same brand since fourth year.

"Tea?"

"Please."

Harry made tea badly. It was too strong, there wasn't enough milk, and the cup had been repaired so many times the handle sat slightly crooked. Hermione drank it anyway because Harry's tea was one of the constants of her life, and she wasn't prepared to lose constants right now.

"Ron said you saw him," Harry said as he settled behind his desk.

"An hour ago."

"How was it?"

"Fine. Good, mostly. He told me about Molly and St. Mungo's."

Years of absorbing bad news professionally had reorganized the muscles around Harry's eyes. He didn't look haggard. He looked set, alert in a low-frequency way that never quite switched off.

"It's worse than the rationing," he said. "The Floo's been intermittent for three weeks. We've had four accidental magic incidents in the last ten days, all kids under seven, all discharging way beyond what their age should produce. One of them blew the windows out of a flat in Camden. Muggle flat. We had to send a team for the glass, the neighbours, the parents, all of it. Two Aurors spent half the afternoon explaining to a terrified mother that her kid wasn't possessed."

Hermione set her cup down. "Faster than we projected. Once it starts breaking through in children, it isn't staying in the Floo or hospital wards anymore. The Manor records are the only place we've found that says how the old anchors were weighted. Without that, we're patching blind."

"I got the Mysteries briefing. It said that with more diagrams."

She almost smiled, but not quite.

"How close are you?" he asked.

"Close enough to know we're reading the right material. Not close enough to promise a fix."

There was a knock. The door opened on a young Auror with wind-burned cheeks and a stack of incident forms hugged to her chest.

"Sorry," she said. "They need your signature on the Camden follow-up. Muggle Liaison wants a second sweep in the morning."

Harry took the top parchment, scanned it, and swore under his breath.

"What now?" Hermione asked when the door shut again.

"A neighbour found robe fabric in the window frame and thinks it's from a theatre costume. Which would be funny if the kid who blew the window out hadn't done it because the magic came up too hard and too fast and no one could stop it." He signed the form with quick, irritated strokes and shoved it aside. "That's what it looks like here now. Paperwork stacked over actual fires."

Through the office wall she could hear someone arguing with a filing cabinet. The cabinet was making a grinding, petulant sound, the kind of noise an enchanted object made when its underlying magic was misbehaving. The wizard outside swore. A drawer slammed hard enough to rattle Harry's framed photograph.

"And that's before the part where people start looking for someone to blame," Harry said.

"You mean the Ministry."

"I mean everyone." He opened the top folder on his desk and turned it toward her. Paper-clipped together were a Prophet cutting with her name in it, a request for clarification from the Minister's correspondence office, and a brief note from Department of Mysteries administration asking whether her Malfoy travel authorizations should remain unrestricted while the story was circulating. "Croaker had to answer for you this morning."

She looked down at the clipping. Skeeter's paragraph was only a few lines. It didn't need more. *Extended residence*. *Historic estate*. *Questionable private access during a national emergency.* Her name was there once. Malfoy's twice.

"That's absurd," she said.

"It is, and it still happened." Harry tapped the second parchment. "The correspondence office wants to know why a Department of Mysteries researcher is effectively living at Malfoy Manor while St. Mungo's is turning people away. Administration wants to know if your travel needs reviewing because once a name hits the paper everyone suddenly remembers procedures."

"So what did Croaker say?"

"That if they pulled you now, they could explain it themselves to the parents in Camden and the healers at St. Mungo's. It bought you time. That's all."

Hermione stared at the papers. A Prophet mention was cheap trouble. Internal review meant forms, signatures, someone deciding how many nights a week she was allowed at the Manor. It meant hours shaved off the archive and handed to people who cared more about appearances than whether St. Mungo's kept turning patients away.

"People are watching," Harry said. "Not because they care about — look. The country's frightened. And you're in the paper next to Malfoy."

She pushed the folder back toward him. "The research is real. The archive is the only source."

"I believe you." He leaned back in his chair. "But Croaker had to answer for you this morning, and a month ago none of this was on anyone's desk. That's—" He gestured at the folder. "That's what's different."

She knew that. The trouble was that hearing it in Harry's office, with incident forms still wet from Camden on his desk, made it sound less like theory and more like a clock starting.

"Ginny read the piece too," Harry said.

Hermione closed her eyes briefly. "Of course she did."

"She said to tell you that if you're going to keep sleeping at Malfoy Manor, you'd better decide what story you're prepared to let people tell about it, because if you don't, Rita bloody Skeeter will do it for you."

"That does sound like Ginny."

"Mm." His mouth twitched, then flattened again. "She also said—" He ran a hand through his hair. "You know what she said."

Hermione looked at the desk instead of at him.

"Harry—"

"I'm not asking." He was quiet for a moment. "I don't even know what I'd—" He stopped. "Just don't let it get ahead of you."

The tea tasted terrible. Outside the office, the filing cabinet had apparently lost its fight; a second drawer banged open and a wizard muttered, "For fuck's sake," with the exhausted venom of a man arguing with furniture in the middle of a public emergency.

"How bad is it going to get?" Harry asked, quieter now.

"Bad." Her answer came too fast. She forced herself to slow down. "Hogwarts, Azkaban, this building. Same old work under all of them. If they pull me out of the Manor before I get the weighting tables, we're improvising with wards we can't afford to improvise with. That's why I'm there. Every line I get out of that archive matters."

"And Malfoy?"

It was too direct, even for Harry, and because it was Harry he got to ask it.

"Malfoy reads the family notation," she said. "Without him I lose half the material."

Harry looked at her for a beat too long. He knew that wasn't the whole answer. He also knew he wasn't going to get the rest by pushing.

"Right," he said.

He let it go. She recognized the move from Ron earlier, the same deliberate step back from a door he could force and wouldn't.

They stood. His second hug was briefer, an arm around her shoulders and a squeeze.

"If you need anything," he said. "You know."

"I know."

When she left, the folder with her name in it was still on the edge of his desk.

The visitors' entrance took her out through the red telephone box and deposited her on a grey London street in December. It was four o'clock. The light was already failing. In mid-December the sun was gone or going by then, and the sky had flattened into a steel colour that would be black within the hour. On the pavement around her London kept moving, commuters and shoppers and a man selling roasted chestnuts from a cart on the corner, smoke rising into the cold air and dispersing.

She'd meant to go back to the flat. There were clean jumpers in the wardrobe, notes she'd left spread over the kitchen table, a sink full of mugs she hadn't dealt with before returning to Wiltshire three days ago. One quiet evening in Bloomsbury had seemed, that morning, like a reasonable plan. She'd wash her hair, eat toast standing at the counter, and sleep in a bed no one else had ever touched.

Instead she kept walking.

She passed the bookshop where she'd bought her first non-magical novel after the war and the cafe where she and Ron had sat on a Tuesday afternoon and agreed, over lukewarm coffee, that the marriage was done, not in those words and not dramatically, but in the accumulation of small honest sentences that added up to the same conclusion. At the Tube entrance a busker was playing a song she nearly recognized, his guitar case open on the pavement, and a woman dropped a coin in while he nodded without breaking the melody.

On Shaftesbury Avenue she passed a shop whose display window should have held a concealed advertisement for Madam Malkin's, one of those charmed billboards that Muggles saw as a shoe advert, and the magic was flickering. The image stuttered between dress robes and brogues, two frames overlaid. A passing Muggle glanced at it and kept walking, registering the glitch as bad wiring. Hermione stopped dead on the pavement.

The image jerked again: dress robes, brogues, dress robes. Hermione's hand went into her coat pocket and closed around her wand. She could have propped the concealment for an hour, maybe two, but not from a public pavement without drawing exactly the kind of attention the failing charm was meant to avoid.

A bus breathed to a stop at the kerb. Cold air pushed exhaust and roasted chestnuts down the street. Two schoolgirls in green blazers shoved past her, arguing about homework. The city went on around the failing magic with the blank, practical indifference of a place too large to stop for one person's realization.

She could still go to Bloomsbury. She could let herself into the flat, put the kettle on, spend one night away from the Manor. The thought lasted less than a minute. Croaker was already burning time on her behalf, and the work waiting in Wiltshire had just followed her into a shop window.

She turned away from Bloomsbury.

From the alley behind the telephone box she Apparated, and the world compressed to a needle-point and expanded again until she was standing on the frosted gravel of the Manor drive with the last grey light dying over Wiltshire and her ears ringing and her breath coming out in clouds.

---

The Floo failed twice before it finally deposited her at the Manor gates instead of the front hall. The walk up the drive took four minutes. Once, early on, she'd timed it, when the Manor had still been a professional assignment and she'd still been pretending measurement could make this manageable. She didn't bother anymore. The cold bit at her face. Her boots crunched on frozen gravel. The bare trees along the drive stood like structural drawings of themselves, all line and no softness, black against the darkening sky.

With each step the Mark resonance strengthened. The faint signal she'd been carrying all day in London filled in and gained resolution, the way a radio station clarifies as you drive toward its tower. His pain and wakefulness came through with it, plus a quality she couldn't name but had learned to recognize, agitation edging toward expectation. The resonance put him two floors up, though she was still outside and the information shouldn't have narrowed that sharply at this distance. Either the bond was learning her or she was learning it.

Halfway up the drive she made herself a small, stupid promise: she'd go to her room first. She'd wash the London out of her hair, change her jumper, eat, and write Croaker before the guilt of not doing it turned into avoidance. Only then would she go to the library. Only then would she deal with Draco if he was there. The order mattered.

The front door opened before she reached it.

It didn't wait for a hand on the latch. Heavy oak swung inward, smooth and unhurried, and the entrance hall beyond was lit with that golden light the Manor made when it seemed almost pleased.

Then she stepped inside.

Warmth met her at once. It wasn't the blunt heat of a radiator or a fire. It seemed to know the cold spots in her coat, the numbness in her fingers, the ribbon of December cold that had slipped down the gap at her collar while she'd stood on a London pavement thinking about things she didn't want to think about. It found all of it and answered with an attention that felt less like a heating charm and more like being noticed by something enormous and careful.

The air smelled of old paper, preservation spells, and the faint mineral tang of the Manor's stone. Beneath that was another quality, not a smell but a settling, a sense of the house easing around her return as if the building itself had been holding tension that her presence released.

For a moment she stood in the entrance hall with her coat still on and her hand still wrapped around the strap of her satchel.

Ron's voice, rough with restraint: I just don't want to ring you in three months and find out you haven't left the building.

Harry's voice: just don't let it get ahead of you.

Ginny's voice, carried by Harry and clean as glass: decide what story you're prepared to let people tell.

She was supposed to be able to see the main staircase from here.

Tonight the corridor to the library was short. One left turn opened straight onto the hall outside the library doors, gold light laid down across the runner as neat as an invitation. The staircase was still there somewhere to the right, past a stretch of shadow and a doorway that had not been there that morning. The house wasn't blocking her. It was making one option easy.

The back of her neck prickled.

On the wall beside her, the hook the Manor had made for her waited at exactly the right height. It was plain iron, not decorative, and it had been there since the third night, as if it had always belonged there. She'd never asked for it. Tonight, with the corridor shortened and the library already lit, taking her coat off on that hook felt absurdly closer to consent than hanging wool on iron had any right to feel.

She kept it on another fifteen seconds, long enough for the cold to leach out of the fabric and the warmth underneath to start feeling good.

Then she took it off anyway.

The library door was already open. Inside, the room glowed warm and gold, the lamps lit, the shelves steady. Her desk sat where she'd left it, notes stacked in order, a cup of tea beside them that she hadn't made and that was, she knew without touching it, exactly the temperature she preferred. Across from her chair, Draco's stood pulled slightly back from the long table, as if someone had just risen from it or had been expected to.

She stopped on the threshold.

She'd told herself on the drive that her room came first. She'd deal with her hair, find food, write the letter, and only then work.

The library had cut all of that out of the evening and left only the part it wanted.

Her stomach went hollow with it, not fear exactly, not even surprise. It was recognition. The house had learned the shape of the shortest path through her resistance and laid it under her feet like carpet.

When she crossed the room the chair at her desk held the last of the day's warmth. She did not sit, not immediately. She set her satchel down instead and put two fingers against the untouched teacup. The tea was at the perfect temperature. Of course it was. The fact of it made irritation rise so fast it almost steadied her.

"No," she said aloud, very quietly, to the room or the house or herself.

The word went nowhere useful.

The Mark resonance was stronger here. It wasn't sharper with pain this time. It was closer. It was present. He was awake above her, restless in a way she could feel without understanding. When her fingertips touched the edge of the desk, the ache under Draco's Mark eased by a fraction, enough that she felt the release in her own arm before she let herself think what it meant.

A soft click sounded across the table. A second cup had appeared beside Draco's place, steam lifting from it in a straight pale line. He wasn't there. The house was still making room for him.

That, more than the first cup, made her want to leave. The second cup turned hospitality into staging. It made her feel watched in a newly practical way, as if the Manor had moved from preference to arrangement while she was out in London pretending the problem was still theoretical.

She looked back toward the hall. The staircase remained out of sight. It wasn't gone. It was merely inconvenient.

If she walked out now, found the long way round, and went upstairs, the house would not stop her. She knew that. It would let her have her room, her washbasin, her dry jumper, perhaps even the letter to Croaker. It would only have shown her, first, how much easier it could make the other choice.

Her hand stayed on the back of the chair.

She thought of the folder on Harry's desk, the flickering concealment charm on Shaftesbury Avenue, and Ron talking about coriander dying in his fridge because at least the fridge proved he was going home every night. The house had opened the door before she'd reached it. It had shortened the corridor. It had laid out the work and the tea and the seat across from hers as if her evening were already settled.

The ease of it disturbed her so badly that for one clear second she wanted her flat with physical force: the bad radiators, the stack of unpaid post on the sideboard, the kitchen tap that dripped if you didn't wrench it hard left. She wanted a place indifferent enough to let her come and go without comment.

She sat down.

She did not sit because she was convinced. She did not sit because the warning voices had gone quiet. She sat because the notes were here, and the archive was here, and Draco was somewhere above her carrying that low, constant pulse under his skin, and the house had made every step away from the room feel like extra work she no longer trusted herself to choose.

She did not touch the tea.

For nearly a minute she only looked at the second cup across the table, steam rising steady into the warm air. Then she pulled her notes closer, still in the clothes she'd worn through London, and set her quill to the line they hadn't solved that morning.

The library door stayed open behind her. She left it that way, and she did not go upstairs.
