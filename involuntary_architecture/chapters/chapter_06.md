# Chapter 6

Granger checked her notes twice before she looked at him. That alone would have told him the protocol had changed.

By now Draco knew the shape of her examinations: instruments set in strict order, his chair angled toward the window, the quick correction at her left cuff that came a beat after she realized he'd noticed it. He'd learned to sit through the first passes of diagnostic magic and to keep stiller for the deeper ones. During the work, her face emptied of everything except concentration. The library silence between them had started to feel companionable. The examination silence still belonged to rules, and rules were easier to trust.

Today's variation required sustained contact. She'd explained the method while laying out a fresh notepad, two sharpened quills, a timing charm, and a stack of clean linen squares she would ordinarily have considered irrelevant to anything worth studying. Wand work blurred the Mark's raw signature, she'd said. A palm laid directly over it would not. Neither of them mentioned the third session, when an accidental brush of her hand had made the Mark surge toward her like a compass needle finding north.

They used the small sitting room off the library's east wall. Natural light mattered to the diagnostics, she'd said once, and by the next morning the Manor had stripped the heavy drapes and cleaned the windows on its own. By midmorning the day had already thinned to grey. Frost feathered the lower panes. Outside, bare branches moved against a white sky with the dry impatient sound of wood on glass. The room held the ordinary chill of a neglected corner until the low fire remembered itself and woke properly.

She stood at the table, her hair tied back too tightly, as if restraint could be made physical by force of will. She had rolled her right sleeve once to keep the cuff from dragging through the ink. She did not look at him until she absolutely had to.

"Ready?"

He rolled his left sleeve to the shoulder. The Mark stood dark against his skin, the lines raised enough to catch what light there was.

"Any change since yesterday afternoon?" she asked. "Not just pain level. Pattern, heat, spread, frequency."

"Not beyond the usual."

"Sleep?"

"Not especially."

That earned him a brief flat look. "That isn't an answer."

"It is the only one I've got."

Her mouth made a small unimpressed shape. "Fine. Baseline first. Then direct contact. If anything changes, you tell me immediately. Not after you've decided whether it's interesting."

"Have I done that before?"

"Yes."

A nod was all she got. She took the first reading with her wand, cool light moving under the skin while he watched the pale reflection slide over the window. The sensation was familiar now: being examined by magic cleverer than most Healers he'd ever met. Years ago a specialist had lasted less than two minutes before the Mark snapped his diagnostic frame apart. Granger's spells went in cleanly. The curse let them.

She worked in layers, quiet and exact. First the surface response. Then the subdermal flux. Then the old dead channels beneath that, which had no business still carrying anything and yet did. At intervals she asked short questions without looking up.

"Pins and needles?"

"No."

"Pressure at the shoulder?"

"A little."

"More than Tuesday?"

"Maybe."

"That's not a scale."

"You didn't ask for a scale."

"I thought I was speaking to someone with basic inferential capacity."

He could have answered that. He did not. Her focus had narrowed to the Mark itself, to the black shape under his skin and whatever it was telling her through the wandlight. When she worked like this, teasing her stopped being sport and turned into something closer to interference.

"Baseline stable," she murmured at last. "Still more reactive than Tuesday. Less surface flare than yesterday, though. Hold still."

The wand went down on the table. Her hand replaced it.

Prepared or not, he felt the contact everywhere. Her palm was warm and the pressure firm. Her fingers curved around his forearm until the Mark sat beneath the center of her hand. A thumb settled at the inside of his wrist, exactly over his pulse. The effect was not dramatic at first. It was simply another person's skin on his, which should not have registered as intensely as it did.

"Starting the deeper pass," she said. "Don't move."

Eyes closing, she sent the diagnostic through her hand instead of the wand. Most practitioners could manage that for a few seconds. Granger could hold it. Her breathing slowed. Concentration tightened her mouth. Magic moved from her palm into the Mark in a low steady current, and the restless pressure that had lived in his arm for months cut out so abruptly he forgot to breathe.

His hand opened on the chair arm. His shoulder dropped. His jaw unclenched without permission. For one clean astonished second he noticed all the small adjustments his body had stopped making.

Her eyes opened at once. "What?"

"It went quiet."

"Quiet how?"

"Completely."

She frowned hard enough to mean she thought he'd misstated the result. "Describe it."

"There isn't anything to describe. It's gone."

"Gone isn't precise."

"It's the precise word I've got."

That answer landed somewhere under her skin. He saw it. She did not take her hand away. Under her palm the Mark lay flat, stripped of its usual shimmer. There was no defensive flare. Dark magic did not twitch along the old channels. The skull and serpent looked like old ink and scar tissue, nothing more.

She shifted her focus inward again and then, with visible care, stopped the spell.

"I've dropped the diagnostic," she said. "Tell me the instant it changes."

He waited for the familiar wrongness to thread back under the skin. It did not.

Three seconds.

Five.

Eight.

"Well?"

"Still quiet."

That got the first genuine break in her composure. Not much. Just a sharp intake of breath and a muttered, "Right."

Her free hand groped for the quill. With the other still braced over his arm, she began writing left-handed in a cramped slant that would have horrified her under calmer circumstances.

"Direct contact persists after diagnostic withdrawal," she muttered. "Suppression maintained absent active spellwork."

"That handwriting is criminal."

"Don't start."

The answer should have sounded clinical. With her hand still around his arm, it did not.

She watched the Mark for another count of ten, then looked up. "I'm lifting my hand. Count for me."

Warmth left his skin.

"One," he said.

She kept her gaze fixed on the Mark.

"Two. Three. Four."

At five the skin was still flat.

"Six."

At seven a faint pressure returned, the smallest live thread under the skin.

"There," he said.

"Keep counting."

"Eight."

By nine the familiar wrongness had fully reasserted itself.

Her palm returned. Silence dropped over the arm again, immediate and entire. A violent kind of relief moved through him, so quick and complete he hated that she must have felt his pulse jump under her thumb.

For a moment neither of them spoke. Ice ticked against the window. A branch scraped somewhere above them.

"That's not the diagnostic," she said at last. Thought was already outrunning speech. "The technique is observational. It shouldn't alter the curse itself, let alone this much. So either the method is interacting in a way no one's documented, or the Mark is responding to direct skin contact, or-" She broke off, corrected. "No. Those aren't mutually exclusive."

"It responded before."

Her gaze snapped to his face. "When?"

"Third session. You brushed it by accident."

"That lasted less than a second."

"It was enough to notice."

Something in her expression acknowledged the point against her will. She looked back down at the Mark. "Fine. Then we stop pretending the earlier result was meaningless."

She shifted her hand a fraction, testing pressure. The Mark remained flat. She eased the contact until only the heel of her palm covered the skull. Still flat.

"Any change?"

"No."

She narrowed further, until only two fingers rested across the serpent's body.

"Now?"

"A little. Not much."

Her mouth tightened. Another left-handed scrawl. "Area matters."

She reduced again to one fingertip at the tail of the serpent. The Mark stirred once, weakly, like something testing whether it was allowed to wake.

"There," he said.

"Pressure as well as contact area," she said. "Fine. Annoying, but fine."

"Annoying."

"I prefer results that obey theory."

"That seems unwise in this house."

The answer nearly got a smile out of her. Not quite. She was already reaching for one of the linen squares with her free hand.

He watched her for a beat. "What is that for?"

"Barrier control."

"On the basis that this wasn't strange enough?"

"On the basis that if I don't test the obvious variable now, I'll spend the rest of the day angry with myself."

She laid the linen between her palm and the Mark and pressed down through it.

The effect broke at once.

Not fully. Not violently. But the silence gave way. The old live pressure leaked back under the skin, low and unmistakable.

She felt it when he did. Her eyes sharpened. "Changed?"

"Yes."

"How much?"

"Enough."

She stripped the linen away. Quiet returned in a rush.

"Direct skin," she said softly. "Not mere pressure. Direct skin."

The note she made after that went crooked halfway through. He had the absurd impulse to reach over and steady the page for her. He kept both hands where they were.

She ran through the next set more carefully. Palm centered over the Mark with spellwork. Palm centered without spellwork. Fingers spanning the skull and lower jaw. Thumb alone at his pulse with the rest of her hand lifted. That one changed nothing at all except his ability to think straight.

"Result?" she said.

He forced his voice level. "The arm's worse. The rest is the same."

She drew her thumb away fast enough to admit she'd heard what she'd nearly done. "Right."

Another note. Another reset.

Then she placed her palm over the Mark again without casting and said, in the voice she used when forcing herself through a list she did not enjoy, "I'm going to hold for two minutes. No questions. I need duration."

He nodded.

The silence that followed no longer belonged only to rules. That was the problem.

Her hand stayed where it was. The pressure on his pulse settled into something steady enough to count by. Without the curse needling under his skin, his awareness redistributed itself in ways he did not trust. He noticed the weight of his own arm on the chair. The low fire. The smell of ink and wool and the faint clean trace of whatever soap she used. The tiny line between her brows when she was keeping herself focused by force.

At forty seconds, she opened her eyes as if startled by something she had not expected to feel.

"What?" he said quietly.

"Nothing."

"That wasn't nothing."

"It wasn't data yet."

She almost looked away from him, then did not. "Can you feel the effect beyond the arm?"

The question was clinical. The answer was not.

"My shoulder isn't braced anymore," he said. "My hand's not trying to grip things. My teeth-" He stopped, irritated by the fact he was having to inventory himself this closely. "It's not just the arm."

Her gaze dropped to where her palm covered the Mark. He saw the thought arrive in her and set itself against whatever professional structure she'd been trying to hold.

"Systemic relaxation secondary to localized contact," she said, as if dictating to a room with no occupants. "Possible sympathetic response. Possibly broader."

"Broader is a useful word."

"I know."

At the end of the two minutes she did not remove her hand immediately. He felt the hesitation before she acted on it. When she finally lifted away, the absence was worse for having become familiar.

"Count," she said.

He did.

The return lagged longer this time.

"Seven," he said.

Nothing.

"Eight."

A faint stir.

"Nine."

By ten the wrongness had woken again, though not all the way.

Her head came up sharply. "Longer recovery."

"It seems so."

"Don't sound pleased."

"I don't know that pleased is the word."

That made her go still for one beat too long.

She set her hand back over the Mark and resumed the quieting before either of them had to do anything about the line she'd nearly walked into. This time she kept her eyes on his arm, not his face.

"One more variable," she said. "Then we're done."

"You said that two variables ago."

"Yes. It turns out the experiment disagreed with me."

She shifted her chair closer. Their knees nearly touched. He watched her realize it and refuse to back away from it because that would have acknowledged the thing itself. Very Granger.

"I'm going to move off the Mark by half an inch," she said. "Tell me if the suppression changes."

Her palm slid to the side, still skin to skin but no longer centered over the skull. The quiet held for a second, then frayed.

"Less," he said.

"How much less?"

"Enough that I notice it."

She put her hand back. Silence again. Her face tightened, not with embarrassment now but with calculation.

"So it's local and contact-dependent and stronger with direct coverage," she said. "And the spell isn't necessary, though it may intensify the effect when layered in. Which is not a sentence I should be able to say."

"You say worse things all the time."

"Not in my notes."

He looked toward the page she'd been assaulting left-handed. "Do those still qualify as notes?"

That time she did laugh, once and against her will. The sound was small, disbelieving, gone almost as soon as it happened. It did not help.

She took one breath and shut it down. "I'm ending the session."

The moment the words were out, she realized she still had her hand on him. She withdrew at once, set the quill down too carefully, and began stacking papers that did not need stacking.

The Mark woke slowly this time, like something dragged out of deep water against its will. He closed his fingers around the chair arm and waited it out.

"That's enough for today," she said, eyes on the notes. "I need resonance texts. Threshold theory, if there is any worth having. Probably something on curse response under sympathetic contact. And I need to compare this against the last three sessions while I still remember the timings."

"You remembered them while your handwriting committed treason. I think you're safe."

She ignored that. "Tomorrow we repeat the controls first."

"You're already sure there will be a tomorrow."

Her hands stopped on the papers. "Of course there'll be a tomorrow."

It came out too fast. Too definite. The answer of someone correcting not him but herself.

She gathered the notes against her hip and headed for the door.

"Granger."

At the threshold she stopped without turning.

He had nothing workable to say about what had happened. He could not speak about the fact that her hand had done what no Healer, potion, or spell had managed. He could say even less about the worse fact: when she'd lifted it, his first clear thought had been put it back.

"Your notes are upside down."

Her eyes dropped to the top page. A quick muttered curse. A sharp correction of the stack.

"Right," she said, without turning. "Thank you."

Then she was gone.

For a while he stayed where he was. The room had darkened another degree. The branches outside knocked once, twice, then settled. When he set his own palm over the Mark, nothing changed. His hand was only his hand. The distinction mattered more than he wanted it to.

Somewhere beyond the east wall, something in the stone gave a low internal knock, as if an old mechanism had shifted weight after lying still a very long time.

He told himself it was the house settling.

Eventually he rolled the sleeve down and went after her.

***

The library, as usual, had more of Granger in it than the guest wing ever did. Color-coded tabs, charmed index cards, stacks of books breeding across three reading tables, a clean sheet pinned over an older shelf diagram with new annotations packed into the margins: all of it had spread with brisk inevitability. On his table by the south window waited a cup of cold tea and a note charmed to the handle: *Preservation charm on the Avery folio is failing. Handle with clean cotton.*

The note disappeared into his pocket with the others. They were useful research artifacts. That was the reason.

Across the room she sat on the floor between two shelves with four books open around her and a clean notebook in place of the disgraced one. She did not glance up or mention the sitting room. That was fine. He opened an estate-binding text at his usual seat and let work close over the morning.

Silence suited them. It held page turns, the scratch of quills, an occasional request for a translation or date. An hour might pass on three remarks and no one needed to pretend it was awkward. The quiet had weight now. It felt built.

It also felt different than it had yesterday.

He noticed it in stupid places first. The way she no longer startled when he crossed behind her table. The way she pushed the ink bottle toward the center without looking when she heard him cap his own. The way his body, traitorous thing that it was, had started expecting a certain kind of relief whenever she moved into arm's reach.

At one point she rose to drag a book from the upper east shelf, failed to reach it cleanly, and said without turning, "That one. Dark green spine."

He was already there by the time the last word left her mouth.

Their hands missed each other by less than an inch on the book's edge. She took it without comment. He returned to his table. Nothing in the room altered visibly. It still felt as if something had taken notice.

Half an hour later she broke the quiet.

"I'm not sending the Mark notes to Croaker yet."

He looked up. "Was anyone under the impression you would?"

"I thought I'd save us both the conversation."

"Very kind."

She bent back over her page. "I need another controlled pass first. At least one. Probably three."

"You always need three."

"Because one result is an anecdote."

"And four?"

"Four is irritatingly persuasive."

He let that sit for a moment. "If you do tell him eventually, what exactly are you going to say?"

Her quill stilled.

"That sustained direct contact alters the curse response," she said after a beat. "That the effect is immediate, skin-dependent, and persists briefly after separation. That I don't yet know why. That I'm not interested in letting the Department turn you into a test case before I do."

The sentence was brisk enough to pretend the last clause had been administrative rather than personal. He knew better than to say so.

"Generous of you."

"Practical."

"Of course."

She gave him a look that said she knew exactly what he was doing and had no intention of helping. Then the look broke because she'd already half-turned toward the east wall.

"Malfoy."

The tone was different. He was on his feet before he knew he'd moved.

She stood near the point where Victorian shelving gave way to bare stone. A draft moved there that had not existed a minute before, carrying dust and the dry packed smell of long-sealed parchment.

A door had appeared.

No memory in him accommodated it. He'd grown up against that wall, reading in the window seats as a child, crossing the room daily under Lucius's eye, learning where each shelf bowed and which floorboard complained under weight. There had never been a door. Now dark wood sat flush in the stone, older than the library fittings, its iron handle black with age. The frame looked less installed than uncovered, as though the wall had decided to remember something it had been pretending not to have.

"That's new," he said.

"I had gathered."

The remark barely touched it. Her voice had gone thin with concentration. She was staring not at the handle but at the join between wood and stone, as if the fit itself might explain the mechanism.

"You want to open it."

"Don't you?"

He did. That was part of the problem.

Reaching past her close enough to feel her warmth through wool, he pulled the handle.

The room beyond was small and windowless. Old stone enclosed it beneath a low ceiling, and alcoves cut straight into the walls served in place of shelving. Scrolls, codices, and loose folios filled those alcoves in packed vertical rows. The material looked older than anything in the visible library, older perhaps than the present house. The air carried stasis rather than decay. Time had not so much passed in there as waited.

Nearest the door sat a narrow stand built into the wall, just wide enough for a ledger and a lamp. Farther in, the floor had been worn smooth where generations of feet must have crossed and recrossed it. Except that line of thinking ran straight into the obvious question of who, exactly, had ever been allowed in.

She made a small sound and was inside before he'd finished taking stock. Not random movement. Purpose. Her eyes were already moving over the alcoves for ordering logic, catalogues, shelf marks. A scroll came out of the nearest niche with reverent efficiency. By the time he crossed the threshold she was reading.

"Anchoring theory," she said. "Original documentation. Pre-Statute, at least. Look at the notation."

The scroll opened farther under careful hands.

"This is primary literature. Not references to it. Not later copies. Actual source material."

On his side of the room, a folio in familiar family notation offered estate-binding protocols in an ancestral hand. The sight of it turned his stomach a little. Lucius had spent half his life in the main library and had never once mentioned a hidden archive. Either his father hadn't known, or he had and said nothing. Both possibilities were ugly.

Another codex came down into her lap. Excitement sharpened her voice. "Ambient field measurements. They knew the substrate could degrade. Malfoy, this room is everything."

"I need the mapping kit," he said. "If we're cataloguing any of this properly, it starts now."

"Yes. Also gloves. And the large notebook, not the blue one, the-"

"I know which notebook you mean."

The kit waited in the main stacks. He stepped back into the library to fetch it.

Three paces later, stone ground against stone.

Turning, he saw the doorway narrowing. The frame contracted inward with patient force. Through the shrinking gap, Granger stood rigid with the codex in her hands.

"Malfoy-"

He stepped back inside at once. The movement stopped as if it had never begun.

Silence held for one beat.

"Again," she said. "We test it again."

He stepped into the library. The grinding started immediately. He stepped back in, and the frame held.

She set the codex down with visible care and came to the threshold. "Wait."

He looked at her.

"Don't come back in yet."

"You want to prove the obvious more slowly?"

"Yes."

There was no point arguing with her when she sounded like that.

She caught his sleeve with two fingers and then, apparently deciding that was too little to mean anything, took hold properly at his wrist. "Now step out."

He did.

The doorway began to close anyway.

Her grip tightened once, hard. "Not contact, then."

"It seems the room is unimpressed by hand-holding."

"This isn't the moment."

She released him and the gap had narrowed another inch before she stepped fully out after him. The door vanished in a single smooth draw of wood and stone. Where it had been was now only wall.

For a second neither of them moved.

Then she put her palm flat to the stone.

Nothing.

He did the same beside her. Still nothing. The rock was cold through the skin. He could feel the blankness of it and, under that, something waiting.

"Again," she said quietly. "At the same time."

Their hands went back together.

Warmth spread under both palms, slow and deliberate. It started under her hand first or he only imagined that because he could not stop imagining her as the changed variable in every problem now. Either way, the stone answered in stages. Grain surfaced where there had been none. Iron darkened into shape. The outline of the door drew itself back into the wall as if the house wanted them to feel each separate click of recognition.

The Mark gave a low inward pull under his sleeve, not pain exactly, but the same unmistakable sense of a lock finding its fit.

She heard him inhale. Her gaze cut briefly to his arm, then back to the reforming door.

"You felt that," she said.

"Yes."

"So did I."

That was new information in more ways than one. He had no time to ask what exactly she meant by it before the handle had fully emerged.

Inside, the archive waited unchanged.

"Both present to open," she said. "Both present to keep it open."

"Apparently."

She did not step in immediately this time. Instead she looked from the door to him and back again, the pieces arranging themselves behind her face with unpleasant speed.

"We've been working in this library together for days," she said. "The room wasn't here yesterday. It wasn't here this morning before the session."

"Your point."

"The changed variable is obvious."

He knew what she meant before she said it. He hated that he knew.

"This morning direct skin contact on the Mark altered the curse itself," she said. "Not for a second. Not by accident. Sustained contact. An hour later the wall produces a room that responds only if both of us are present. I'm not calling that coincidence."

He looked at the dark gap of the archive, at the shelves just visible past the threshold, then back at the blank stone to either side. "You're saying the house needed proof."

"I'm saying it may have been waiting for whatever threshold we hit this morning."

"And what threshold is that, exactly?"

"I don't know." The answer came faster than she would usually have allowed. "Paired attunement. A sympathetic circuit. Some estate-binding condition no one's named properly in three hundred years. Pick a term. The point is that it didn't have it before today."

"It had me."

"Yes. It did."

The agreement was too immediate to be kind.

"It never showed my father this room," he said. "Or me."

Her gaze stayed on him now. "If this threshold is paired, blood won't satisfy it alone."

"You don't know that."

"No." She drew a short breath through her nose. "I don't. But we can test the obvious pieces. You alone didn't hold the room. I alone didn't open it. Both of us together did. That already tells us more than we knew three minutes ago."

The calmness of that answer made him want to put a fist through something. "So that's it? The house decides the matter is shared and suddenly the one room we actually need appears."

"That is, inconveniently, what seems to have happened."

He stared at her. "You say that as if it isn't deranged."

"It is deranged." Some of her own strain broke through then, visible at last. "I'm not saying it isn't. I'm saying the room is still full of source material and I'd like to know why it's decided to be deranged now before it changes its mind."

That was closer to the truth.

He bent and picked up the folio he'd noticed first. The inside cover carried a note in a sharper, later hand than the main text. He read it once, then again.

Once the house had judged a matter shared, the inward records refused a solitary claimant. Heir or not.

The note was brief. Irritated, even. Not prophecy. Not mystery. Administrative annoyance written by some long-dead Malfoy who had apparently suffered the same indignity of being told no by his own walls.

Granger saw his expression change. "What?"

He turned the folio so she could see.

Her eyes moved quickly over the page. "There."

"There," he repeated flatly.

"That's why your father didn't simply claim it. Or you." Her finger hovered over the old note without touching the page. "Not inheritance. Not ownership. A shared matter."

"And today it became one."

She was quiet for a beat too long. "It looks that way."

Neither of them said what was obviously sitting underneath the phrase.

She straightened first. "We still need the mapping kit. And gloves. And all the things we'd be idiots to work without."

"You're already past the part where that's a problem."

"I'm not past it." The words came out faster than her usual precision. "It's invasive and it's-" She cut herself off. Started again. "But it's a constraint. We work with constraints or we get nothing done."

"That's your answer. Logistics."

"That's my answer right now." Her jaw set. "Unless you've got a better one."

He didn't. What he had was the house making proximity a rule before either of them had decided what proximity meant.

"Fine," he said.

"Good. Both of us out. Both of us back."

They stepped into the library together. The wall sealed over at once.

Retrieval, under ordinary conditions, would have taken him a minute and a half. Under these ones it turned into a choreography neither of them wanted to acknowledge. She veered toward her east-stack references, stopped when she realized he had gone the other direction, and corrected course with visible irritation. He fetched the mapping kit from beneath the south table and found her already there, arms full of notebooks, gloves, parchment, and a small stack of texts on custodial wards. The narrowed set of her mouth said she was pretending not to notice how often they nearly collided.

"Lamp," she said.

"Already have one."

"A second."

"Of course."

When they turned back, the Manor had placed a tea tray on the nearest table. Pot, two cups, a plate of butter biscuits, clean cloths folded with unnatural neatness. Neither of them had asked for it.

He met her eyes across the tray.

"No," she said.

"I hadn't spoken."

"You didn't have to."

He took the tray anyway. She gathered the books. Together they went back to the wall.

The door re-formed more quickly this time. Warmth answered at once beneath their hands, and the stone gave way before either of them had finished exhaling.

Inside, the archive felt smaller now that it had declared terms.

She claimed the floor beside the central alcove. He took the opposite wall with the folio he'd found first. The room was small enough that their knees nearly touched once they both settled.

"Start with shelf order," she said, already halfway back inside the codex she'd abandoned. "Don't trust apparent chronology. The house may be grouping by use instead of date."

"Comforting."

"It wasn't meant to be."

The mapping grid went down between them. Notes began. Within minutes she was muttering in medieval Latin under her breath and crossing out her own translations with irritated precision. He answered when she asked for a family abbreviation. She corrected him once, checked the margin, then admitted he was right with a brief nod that cost her more than it should have.

The work settled into them almost immediately. That was the second problem.

The first shelf he mapped held estate-binding manuals, maintenance logs, and household protocols older than the visible library's cataloguing system. The second held architectural diagrams so dense with later annotations they looked less written than overgrown. Granger's side of the room yielded anchoring theory, substrate measurements, and one damaged table of field failures organized by region rather than date. None of it answered everything. All of it mattered.

Half an hour in, he found the note again in another form. Not the inside-cover complaint this time, but a line buried in a maintenance protocol: records touching shared work were to remain in the inward room until both keepers had reviewed them. The handwriting had been copied twice over the centuries, the wording shifting slightly each time, but the sense held.

Shared work.

He looked up before he meant to. Granger was already looking at him from across the mapping board, as if she'd reached the same phrase somewhere else and was waiting to see whether he would say it first.

"There it is again," he said.

"On my side as well."

"Shared."

"Yes."

He let the word sit there between them like something left on the table by a careless third party.

She broke eye contact first and bent back over the page. "Whatever the exact mechanism is, the archive seems to distinguish between solitary study and..." Her pen paused. "This."

"How flattering for us."

"I didn't say it was flattering."

"No. Only structurally invasive."

"Accurate, though."

The room held on that for a little too long. He returned to the folio before he had to decide whether the answer had contained more honesty than she intended.

An hour later she sat back hard enough to make the lamp wobble.

"What?"

"I need the Byrne cipher lexicon." She pressed the heel of her hand to one eye, leaving an ink smear at the knuckle. "There's a marginal family shorthand here I can nearly parse, which is the worst possible state for a text to be in."

"I'll get it."

The words were out before he had thought them through.

Her head came up. Neither of them moved.

Then, almost together:

"No."

The room stayed still. It did not need to react. The point had already been made.

He set the folio down with more care than he felt. "Right."

"Right," she echoed, and sounded angrier at the fact of having to say it than at him.

They rose together in silence. The movement was awkward in the small space. His knee knocked the mapping board. She caught the inkwell before it tipped and swore under her breath. By the time they reached the threshold they had regained the bare minimum of composure.

The door sealed the moment both of them stepped out. On the library table beside the tea tray sat the Byrne lexicon.

Of course it did.

He stopped dead. So did she.

"I hate this house," he said.

"That makes two of us."

She snatched the lexicon up, then paused with it pressed against her chest. "No, that's not true. I don't hate the house. I hate being anticipated by it."

"Comforting distinction."

"It's an important one."

"Is it."

"Yes."

There was enough force in the answer to keep him from saying what he had been about to say next. He shifted the tea tray to his other hand instead.

Back at the wall, the door did not reappear immediately.

They both knew why before either spoke. His hands were full with the tray. Hers were full with the lexicon and two loose folios she'd grabbed on reflex.

She blew out a breath. "For God's sake."

He set the tray down on the floor. She stacked the books beside it. They put their hands to the stone together. Warmth spread. Grain surfaced. The door opened.

There was no missing the implication this time.

Inside again, they set the tray on the floor between their stations. She reached first for the lexicon, then stopped and shoved one cup toward him without looking.

"Drink that before it goes cold."

"You are issuing orders now."

"I'm trying not to have you collapse into the archival material. There are distinctions."

He took the cup because arguing would have meant not taking it.

The tea was strong and over-brewed. She had not added milk to either cup. Of course she hadn't. He said nothing and drank it anyway.

For the next stretch of time the work tightened rather than spread. The lexicon gave her one family cipher, which opened two more, which led to a cluster of maintenance references neither of them had known existed. He traced a recurring set of location marks through three centuries of copied diagrams and realized with a slow unpleasant certainty that the house's damage had never been discussed as isolated failures. The old notes treated breakage the way a body might treat a torn tendon or a damaged rib. Structural. Connected. Repairable if addressed soon enough. Dangerous if left to calcify.

"Here," he said, sliding a folio across.

She took it and scanned the page. "That's not a room map."

"No. It's a reinforcement sequence."

"For what?"

"I haven't decided yet."

Her eyes moved farther down the page. The line between her brows deepened. "No. Wait. These marks recur. Not by date. By load."

"Yes."

She sat forward so fast their knees touched and stayed there. Neither of them moved to correct it.

"If these are load-bearing sites," she said, already reaching for her notebook, "then the failures aren't random either. They'd propagate along connected structures, not just place by place."

"Which would be irritatingly relevant."

"Deeply irritating."

The exchange lasted seconds. Then he turned the next folio and found maintenance copied beside inheritance and the same shared-work notation again. On her side of the board, anchoring diagrams kept landing beside repair tables. The room was not random. It had been sorted this way on purpose.

He hated that enough to trust it.

At some point she reached for a biscuit without looking, bit into it, and kept reading as if food were just another tool to be deployed when concentration threatened to fail. A crumb landed on the margin of a fourteenth-century copy. Draco took the page from her hand before he had fully decided to.

She blinked. "What are you doing?"

"Saving the document from you."

"I wasn't going to drop anything on it."

He looked pointedly at the crumb.

Her gaze followed his. "That is sabotage."

"By butter, perhaps."

She huffed a laugh through her nose, brushed the page clean with ridiculous care, and took the folio back. The intimacy of the exchange arrived late enough to be useless.

Later still, while he was cross-referencing one shelf mark against another, she said without looking up, "You were right about the note."

"Which one. There have been several."

"The one on the inside cover. The house judged the matter shared. That's why today." Her pen slowed. "Not because the archive somehow knew we were researching ambient-field decay in the abstract. Because something about the morning changed how the house was reading us in relation to the work."

He watched her hand move over the page. "You sound almost comfortable saying that now."

"I am not comfortable." The answer was immediate. "I am following the evidence."

"And the evidence says?"

She put the pen down at last and met his eyes across the tray and the mapping board and the small, claustrophobic space that required them both to remain in it.

"That the Mark session altered more than the Mark," she said. "Whatever the house measures, we crossed some threshold this morning that we had not crossed before. That's why the archive opened today and not last week. That's why it didn't open for you alone. That's why it wouldn't have opened for your father if he was standing at the wall by himself with every title he ever thought mattered."

He had been ready for the first half of that. Not the second.

"Careful," he said quietly.

Some shadow of the thing beneath her words crossed her face. "I'm not trying to be cruel."

"No. You're trying to be correct."

"Yes."

The honesty of that answer left very little room to stand in.

He looked back down at the page before she could say anything else. The old family hand in front of him remained old family hand no matter how many times he translated it. The note on the inside cover remained what it was. Heir or not.

Lucius could live and die in this house and still not be the person it answered.

The thought should have pleased him more than it did.

It did not please him at all.

The hours inside the archive did not pass cleanly. Without windows the day lost shape. Light changed only because the lamp had to be turned higher, then because the house bled a dim sourceless glow into the seams of the ceiling when the lamp stopped being enough. The floor warmed gradually under them. The tea went cold. They worked through it.

Once, shifting position, she nudged his ankle with her socked foot and kept reading as if she had not noticed. Perhaps she had not. He had.

Once he stood to stretch without thinking and the ceiling gave a soft warning creak, not lowering, just reminding. He sat back down before she could look up.

Once she opened her mouth to ask for a text from the visible library, shut it again, and rewrote the sentence on the page instead.

The door had started deciding the size of ordinary inconveniences: a stretch abandoned, a reference left unfetched, a question scratched into the margin instead of asked aloud.

By what must have been late afternoon, copies of anchoring diagrams covered the mapping board. Half a page of correlations linked war-damaged sites to older reinforcement nodes. A maintenance table from one century echoed a repair notation in another. On Granger's side, a stack of transcriptions had grown dense enough to matter. On his, family protocols had begun to resolve into something uglier and more useful than simple household lore.

He found a line in a later commentary describing the house as *attendant*, a word that came closer to listening than watching. The passage should have clarified matters. It did not. Six hundred years earlier, someone in his line had already accepted that the Manor tracked its inhabitants, sorted work into solitary and shared forms, and withheld access accordingly. Precedent existed. Documentation existed. None of it made the present less unnerving.

Across from him, Granger had tucked both feet under herself and forgotten they belonged to a body at all. Her hair had started to come loose around the temples. There was ink on the side of her hand, a grey smear at the heel of one palm where she'd wiped it without noticing. Every so often she would stop, stare at a line as if willing it into obedience, then write twice as fast.

Eventually she said, "You should tell me if your arm changes."

"It hasn't."

"That wasn't the question. The question was whether you'd tell me."

He considered lying. "Probably."

"That is not reassuring."

"I wasn't aware reassurance had become part of the protocol."

She pressed her lips together. "It's not. It's part of basic competence."

"You seem to be managing quite a lot of that for both of us."

That got her attention more cleanly than he expected. She sat back and looked at him, really looked, as if evaluating whether he'd meant the line as mockery. He hadn't. Not entirely.

"You found the access note," she said.

"Yes."

"And the shared-work references."

"Yes."

"And you didn't leave."

There was no clean answer to that. He chose the least bad one.

"Neither did you."

Her expression altered by a degree. Not softened. Something more complicated and less useful than that.

"No," she said. "I didn't."

The conversation went no farther. It could not have without becoming another kind of conversation altogether.

By full dark, the biscuits were reduced to crumbs, the tea had gone metallic with tannin, and the notes between them had grown into a recognizable first day's haul rather than a pile of academic hope. Granger passed him a scroll when she stalled at a family cipher. He translated it, slid her the next folio, and marked the shelf number before her quill lifted from the page. When she reached blindly for the tea tray and found only crumbs, he pushed the last intact biscuit across the board with two fingers. She took it without looking up.

A few minutes later she said, "One more shelf and then we stop."

He glanced once at the black iron handle, still there in the lamplight.

"Fine," he said.

He got to his feet, reached into the upper alcove she could not reach from the floor, and brought down the next bundle of folios. The archive remained open around them, patient and implacable, as if two people inside it were the rule and anything else the exception.
