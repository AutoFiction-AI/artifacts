# Chapter 18

The seventh day was the worst.

It was not because the Mark hurt more, though it did. The magic in it kept reaching, stupidly and repeatedly, for the diagnostic touch that was no longer there. For weeks the pain had at least possessed a direction. Hermione's hands on his forearm could quiet it. Her spells could force it to reveal its uglier habits. Without that, the ache turned feral. It burned under the skin, ran up toward his elbow, then settled into a deep grinding pull that made his left hand flex against blankets, chair arms, tabletops, air. By the seventh day even his body seemed embarrassed by how often it looked for her.

The seventh day was worst because by then the house had stopped pretending.

For six days the Manor had managed a few involuntary gestures. It had not offered the responsive architecture he had grown used to when she was here. No shortcuts surfaced. No helpful doors appeared where walls had been. No library shelves rearranged themselves under his hands. There had only been twitches: a sconce in the east wing flaring once at noon and dying by evening, a corridor outside the library tightening by three paces and then lengthening again, a door near the conservatory sticking and releasing as if the house could not decide whether to be obstinate or merely exhausted. If he was feeling generous, he could call those signs of life. If he was honest, they were last autonomic functions.

On the seventh day there was nothing.

The Manor held its shape. The library stayed at the end of the same corridor. The guest room stayed in the same place along the east wing. The walk between them measured forty-one paces every time, where during the better weeks it had sometimes been twenty-three. He counted because there was nothing else to do with the arithmetic of loss except make it literal.

A house that moved was a house with opinions. This was only a house.

At ten in the morning Draco stood at the library entrance, dressed because dressing still divided one hour from the next. He had put on trousers, a shirt, and shoes like a man with business to conduct. The performance had satisfied no one. Beyond the open library door the windows let in what December offered, which was barely anything: a flat grey wash of light and an unbroken sheet of cloud. He had lost track of whether it was the second or third week of the month. The calendar in the morning room still existed. He had stopped consulting it three days ago.

The archive wall gave him blank stone. He put his palm to it anyway, part habit and part test. Cold met skin. It was only winter in old stone, not the sharper refusal of a house choosing to lock him out. He kept his hand there until the chill reached the heel of his palm and made the bones ache. Nothing changed. The seam did not appear. The wall did not warm. There was no answering pressure from the room behind it, no sense of a held breath on the other side.

He had tried at dawn. He had tried again an hour later. He had tried after not trying for a full afternoon yesterday because pride had briefly suggested restraint might preserve dignity. The wall had been stone every time.

Warming charms would have helped in the library. He had not bothered. Lighting the grate would have helped too. He had not done that either. The room felt wrong when it was warm without her in it, as if comfort under those conditions were a trick or an insult.

What he missed was not only her touch on his arm, though his body kept arranging the week around that absence. He missed the sustained fact of her attention. For six weeks she had moved through the library and changed its use by occupying it. For six weeks she had left books open at unreasonable angles, wedged notes between volumes, abandoned her scarf over chair backs, and interrupted silence with translation or argument or annoyance. For six weeks he had been looked at closely and not treated as a problem to manage, a relic to avoid, or a name to lower one's voice around. The withdrawal from that had its own humiliating clarity. He had not known how much of his day had started to depend on hearing her swear at marginalia.

In the kitchen the tea cooperated because the kitchen always cooperated. Water boiled. Steam ghosted up from the kettle. A cream-coloured cup waited by the sink, slightly too wide and with the handle turned wrong for his grip. It was hers. He had been using it for three days before he noticed, because the kitchen had apparently developed preferences while he was busy failing at austerity. Once he saw it, he kept using it. Switching to another cup would have required him to admit that he was, in fact, choosing around her absence instead of merely enduring it.

He carried the tea back through the east corridor rather than the nearer west passage, which he told himself had been a practical decision. The east wing gave him the guest room she had used, the narrow turn toward the conservatory that neither of them had ever had patience for, the morning room his mother preferred, and halfway down the corridor, the drawing room door.

It was open.

It had been open all week. The house had not shortened the walk there or thrown the room in his path with the crude theatricality it had once shown. It had simply left the door ajar, the way a house might leave a cracked tile visible under a carpet edge. He had not gone in. He had not looked directly through the doorway either. Still, the room pressed against his awareness each time he passed, as precise and irritating as the awareness of a loose tooth.

He kept walking. The tea rippled against porcelain. The bandaged skin under his sleeve gave a small ugly throb as if it objected to the corridor, the room, the morning, or all three.

Back in the library, a seventeenth-century genealogical record sat open on the table. It was one of the accessible texts and one of the useless ones. He had spent most of yesterday in company with family trees, marriage entries, steward appointments, property notes, and the sort of smug domestic record-keeping old pure-blood houses mistook for history. A few marginal marks had helped him confirm dates on earlier bonding episodes inside the house. None of it had moved the actual work forward. None of it had answered the only question that still carried any heat.

The harder knowledge was not in the book. It lived in the fact that Hermione had found the relevant folio first, read it without him, and gone on standing close enough to touch.

For two days anger had made everything clean. She had withheld material information while examining his body. She had decided what he could bear to know. She had kissed him with knowledge in her pocket that changed the terms of the kiss. The neatness of that story had been almost a relief. By the third day the neatness had started to rot. Doubt did not make her innocent, but it made her recognizably human. Of course she had been frightened. Of course she had wanted proof before bringing him the ugliest possibility either of them had found. Of course she had told herself she was buying time for rigor when in fact she had been buying time for fear.

Understanding any of that did not reduce the damage. It only stripped away the simpler villain.

He sat, stood again, crossed to the window, and came back to the table without remembering deciding to move. His tea went untouched beside the book. By noon the skin under the gauze felt hot enough that he loosened the wrap one-handed and checked whether the old curse had finally done the courtesy of splitting him open. It had not. The Mark had simply gone redder along the outer curls, the black in its center glossy as wet ink. He tightened the bandage badly, swore under his breath when the knot slipped, and did it again with his teeth.

The house gave him no witness to the indignity. No floorboard creaked in sympathy. No corridor shortened in mockery. Even the usual settling noises seemed to have withdrawn from the walls.

He tried the archive wall once more at half past one and got only stone. He opened the genealogical record again. He read the same three lines on a Malfoy marriage from 1784 until the words blurred into shape without meaning. One of Hermione's translation notes still lay under his left hand where he had found it seven days ago, a strip of parchment with her cramped writing correcting some ancestor's Latin and then, because she had been tired, insulting him in the margin for spelling a name three different ways within the same page. He had left the note exactly where it was and resented himself for the tenderness of that.

Measured, unhurried footsteps came first: not a servant's and not Hermione's. It was his mother.

Narcissa paused in the doorway in a dark coat and gloves. One glance took in the whole room: the dead grate, the blanket over the chair arm, the untouched cup, the record left open at the same place for too long, Hermione's note still on the table, and him in the middle of it all looking exactly as bad as he felt.

"You look dreadful," she said.

That almost made him laugh. The sound reached his throat and died there. "I'm fine."

"You are not fine." She removed her gloves with precise fingers. "Has Mipsy been coming?"

"I told her not to."

"That was not what I asked."

"She tried. I sent her away."

Her gaze moved over the room again. He could see her calculating the neglect in layers. The grate had not been lit. Dust had started to gather under the library window where Hermione usually opened it for air by midafternoon. One of the side tables still held a stack of books Hermione had meant to return to a shelf and plainly never had. Narcissa draped her coat over the chair by the door and, by that act alone, made it clear she intended to stay.

"Tea?" she said.

He poured what he had already made into a clean cup and went back to the kitchen for more water. By the time he returned, she stood at the window with one hand resting on the frame, her head tilted very slightly as though listening for a pulse inside the walls. Nothing answered. After a moment she sat in Hermione's usual chair, the one with the worn velvet and the ink stain on the arm, and lifted the cup without drinking.

Silence held for a while. His mother knew how to use silence. So did he. In better years that had passed between them as breeding. In worse years it had functioned more like a weapon. Today it felt like the only form in which either of them could afford honesty.

"I did not come to ask what happened," Narcissa said at last. "I know what happened. The house told me before it stopped telling anyone anything."

He looked at her. "The house told you."

"In its way."

She said it without mysticism. That was one of the few mercies of his mother's relationship to the Manor. She had never spoken about the house as if it were enchanted beyond comprehension or somehow holy. It was only old, powerful, temperamental, and woven through too much family history to ignore.

"I have not been fully honest with you, Draco," she said.

The sentence did not sound strategic. It sounded expensive.

He set his tea down carefully. "About the house."

"About the house. About what it does and what it cannot do." Her gaze lowered to the cup and returned to him. "I told myself I was protecting you from knowledge you were not ready for. That was partly true. It was also convenient."

"Then say it now."

She met his eyes. The resemblance was most evident when she was deciding whether to wound someone for their own good.

"You have read the bonding texts," she said.

"I've read them."

"Then you know the mechanism in outline. What you do not know, because the damaged pages assume any Malfoy child worth educating would already have heard it at table, is the limitation." Her thumb moved once around the rim of the cup. "The house amplifies. It gates. It opens certain passages and withholds others. It rewards concordance. All of that is true."

"I know."

"What the house cannot do is fabricate."

He said nothing.

She went on because she had decided, at last, to be direct. "It cannot create a connection where none exists. It can make an existing bond louder. It can alter conditions around that bond. It can do for attachment what glass and warmth do for a winter plant. It cannot put a seed into bare stone."

The words took a moment to arrange themselves into meaning. He was still staring at her when she added, more quietly, "What the house responded to between you and Ms. Granger was already there before the first corridor shortened."

Furniture shifted in his head, heavy and slow. Hermione's hands around his forearm during the first examination. Her face bending over the Mark with clinical concentration that had never once been cold. The library late at night with only two lamps lit because she said the rest made the room impossible to think in. Her mouth on his in the dim space between chair and table, frightened and clumsy and better than anything he had expected to have again. The house had turned up the volume. The sound had been there first.

"How do I know what belongs to the base level?" he asked. The question came out harsher than he intended. "How do I separate it?"

"You do not."

"That is not an answer."

"It is the only one." She held his gaze. "There is no untouched version available to you, Draco. Not in this house and not outside it either. We all love under conditions. Family is a condition. War is a condition. A house like this is only a more vulgar one."

He let out a breath that did nothing useful for his lungs.

"You are asking for a control specimen," Narcissa said, and almost smiled at the expression that crossed his face. "I know Ms. Granger's language has infected this house. But even in her terms, you cannot have one. The question is not whether the Manor altered what you felt. Of course it altered it. The question is whether it invented it. It did not."

That should have felt like relief. It did not. Relief implied closure. This was only rearrangement, the floor plan of his uncertainty altered under his feet.

"You knew this the whole time."

"Yes."

"You let us both go on without saying it."

"Yes."

He looked at her a long time. She did not flinch. The cup in her hand stayed level, but he could see the work of composure at her wrist.

"Why?"

The answer did not come at once. That, more than the content, told him she was done with easy evasions.

"Because I knew what saying it would cost me," she said finally. "And because I was not certain I could bear paying it while I was still deciding whether I had the right to interfere."

He frowned. "That is not clearer."

"No," she said. "It is simply truer."

He might have snapped at her for that on another day. Today the effort required to be annoyed felt larger than the reward. He leaned back in the chair and felt the worn wood press into his shoulders.

"Explain," he said.

Narcissa set the cup down. Her hand went, almost unconsciously, to the edge of her cuff and then away again.

"When I came to this house as a bride," she said, "the architecture altered. Not dramatically. A few rooms reopened. Some doors ceased sticking. The east corridor shortened when I was expected in the dining room. The temperature changed in winter. It was enough for me to understand that the house had taken notice."

She paused, and he understood with a cold prickling clarity that he was being invited into a part of her life she had never previously considered his business.

"I assumed it would deepen," she said. "The way family accounts suggested it sometimes had for earlier generations. I assumed that if I learned the rhythms of the house, if I stayed, if the marriage settled into itself, more would follow."

He did not interrupt.

"It did not." She folded her hands, then unfolded them again. "The house respected your father. He was a Malfoy. It obeyed bloodline rules around him. But the deeper resonance never came. Not once in twenty-five years. The paired awareness, the gated rooms that would only hold for two, the way it has been reading your body through hers and hers through yours. None of that happened for us."

The room did not have enough air in it.

"Mother."

"This is not a complaint," she said, and her voice sharpened just enough to make the distinction stand. "Your father and I had a real marriage. We chose it. We survived with it. There were years when surviving with it required all the skill either of us possessed, and we managed that too. I am not speaking against what I had. I am telling you the house never answered us the way it answers what it sees between you and her."

The phrase between you and her landed as fact, not speculation.

He looked down at the table because there was nowhere else to look. Hermione's note sat under his fingertips. The strip of parchment had absorbed a faint brown stain from old tea. He had not even realized he was touching it until then.

"Lucius knew?" he asked.

"Enough to know that the house was more alive with me than without me. Not enough to name the absence." Narcissa's expression shifted by a degree and became, for one brief second, nakedly tired. "He was not interested in absences he could not command."

He almost asked whether that had hurt. The question stopped at his teeth. Of course it had hurt. The form of the hurt was what she had just spent ten minutes laying at his feet.

"Why tell me now?" he said instead.

"Because you are old enough to do your own damage with the truth." The dry edge in the sentence softened before it could become cruel. "And because I have had a week to consider whether I wished to go on being a woman who watched her son repeat a confusion she recognized and said nothing because silence was more comfortable."

The library settled around them. Outside, wind moved against the windowpanes without enough force to become a storm.

"She kept the folio from you," Narcissa said. "That was her error, and I will not excuse it. But she did not create the thing that frightened her. She found it already in progress and handled it badly. People do."

People do. The phrase had the shape of a maxim and the fatigue of one learned the expensive way.

"It still matters that she hid it."

"Yes."

"It still matters that the house is involved."

"Yes."

"That does not tell me what to do."

"I did not come to tell you what to do." She stood, smoothing her skirt beneath careful palms. "Your choices remain your own. The house will have opinions whether invited or not."

He stayed seated. "Why did you not tell me sooner?"

The pause at the doorway was real. He could see her deciding how much of her private life she was willing to hand over and in what state.

"Because explaining how I knew meant admitting that the house gave you a form of answer it never gave me," she said. "I needed time with that."

The honesty in it struck harder than the revelation itself. His mother, who had spent half her life converting pain into manners, had just told him she envied him.

By the time her coat was buttoned, the room felt rearranged even though nothing had moved.

"Your arm," she said, glancing at the gauze.

He looked at it too. "It'll be fine."

"It will not." She pulled on the right glove, finger by finger. "You may survive that. It is not the same claim."

Then she was gone, footsteps receding through the entrance hall with the soft authority of someone who never hurried even when wounded. The front door opened and closed. A pipe knocked somewhere upstairs. The house offered no further commentary.

Draco sat with what she had given him: the grief of a woman telling her son that her marriage, real and chosen and lived, had never once moved the house the way this messy, unverified, damaged attachment had. She had carried that knowledge for years. Today she had laid it in front of him and declined to tidy it.

He picked up the genealogical record and saw none of it. He put it down again. He crossed to the archive wall and pressed his palm against the stone not because he expected it to open but because he suddenly needed an argument with matter. The wall stayed shut. For a foolish second he was almost offended. Then he saw the absurdity of asking the house for confirmation while trying to reclaim his life from its testimony.

He laughed once, without humor, and let his forehead rest against the cold stone.

If the house had not invented it, then the week had not been a cleanse. It had not burned away falsehood and left truth behind. It had only removed proximity and left him alone with the parts that remained when no one touched him, no one argued with him, no one moved through the corridor with a satchel banging lightly against her hip.

He missed her.

The clarity of it annoyed him at once. Missing her did not absolve her. Wanting her did not answer the influence question. Narcissa had not handed him certainty. She had only removed one lie from the center of the room. Everything else still stood around it, solid and awkward and impossible to ignore.

He went back to the table and finally drank the tea. It had gone cold. By the time the knock came, dusk had thickened at the windows and the library had sunk into that late-afternoon half-light in which every object looked a little farther away than it was.

---

It was the side entrance off the garden, not the front door.

That registered before the knock had quite stopped echoing. Hermione knew which door opened closer to the library. She would know which one spared them the entrance hall and his mother's portraits and the long performance of hospitality. The knowledge landed somewhere under his ribs before he had time to decide what it meant.

He crossed the corridor at a pace that wanted to be a run and arrived trying not to look as if he had. Outside stood Hermione in a heavy dark coat with the collar turned up against the cold, a scarf wound high at her throat, and a leather satchel hanging from one shoulder with enough weight in it to drag the strap across her coat. The satchel came first in his notice because of course it did. She would bring work even to an apology. Then the rest of her assembled: hair coming loose around the temples, cheeks raw from the weather, mouth set too carefully, one gloved hand still lifted from knocking.

Her gaze moved over his face, the poor bandaging at his wrist, and the general state of him. She did not comment on any of it. The restraint was probably mercy.

"Hi," she said.

She did not say Malfoy. She only said hi, as if the week between them had not invented a new form of distance.

"Hi."

Cold air held between them for one beat too long. He stood back. She stepped inside. The house did not warm the corridor, shorten the distance to the library, or shut the door behind her with any self-important flourish. He closed it himself and listened to the latch catch.

Hermione stayed where she was, just inside the threshold. He could feel the effort it cost her not to start with the satchel. Her hand remained on the strap as if work might keep trying to climb into her mouth first.

"I didn't come back to ask for the archive," she said, confirming that he had not imagined the struggle. "I know that's what the bag suggests. The work is in there because I didn't stop being me in London, not because I thought I could use it as an excuse to get through the door."

He said nothing. Silence had been the coin of the house all week. Let it work for once on her behalf rather than his.

"I should've told you," she said. "About the folio. About the damaged clause. About all of it. I should've told you the day I found it, or at worst the next morning when I still had time to pretend I had simply been slow rather than cowardly."

"You should have."

The plainness of that made her flinch. She accepted the hit.

"I told myself I needed to verify before I said anything," she went on. "Some of that was true. I did need more context, and the page really was damaged, and I still don't know the full mechanics. All of that is true." Her mouth tightened. "It wasn't the main truth. The main truth was that if I said it out loud too soon, I'd have to hear it out loud too. I'd have to stand there with you not knowing whether any of this was ours or the house's, and I kept deciding that was a burden I could manage better on my own."

She looked at him directly then, perhaps because the sentence had finally reached the point where looking elsewhere would have been too obvious.

"That was the ugliest part," she said. "Not the research. Not caution. The managing."

The corridor remained obstinately ordinary around them. Stone lay underfoot, bad light came through the windows, and a cold draft ran along the skirting from some old crack in the frame. He had half expected the house to perform a reaction to her return and hated himself for the expectation.

"My mother was here," he said.

Hermione's grip on the satchel tightened. "Did she say why the house has been dead?"

"She said why it was never alive in the way we thought."

That made her stillness sharpen. It was a familiar kind of stillness, the one that meant she was taking in information too quickly to move while she did it.

"What did she tell you?"

"That the house cannot fabricate." Saying it aloud altered the shape of the words in his own mouth. "It can amplify. It can gate rooms. It can make a nuisance of itself whenever two people are trying to ignore facts. It cannot manufacture a bond from nothing."

A long second passed.

Hermione let out a breath that clouded faintly in the cold corridor. "All right," she said, and then, because one sentence was not enough for her when the ground moved, "All right. That matters."

"Yes."

"It doesn't make what I did less wrong."

"No."

"And it doesn't settle the influence question."

"No. It does not."

Her chin dipped once in acknowledgment. "No clean control condition," she muttered, almost to herself.

Against his will, a little of the week's bitterness turned wry. "That was almost word for word my mother's objection."

Brown eyes lifted again, surprised despite herself. "Your mother said control condition."

"She said you have infected the house with your language."

For the first time since crossing the threshold Hermione looked as if a laugh might exist somewhere in the same world as this conversation. It did not quite make it out, but the possibility was there. Then it went.

"Did she tell you anything else?"

He could have delayed. He could have parceled the information out for effect, made her wait inside the same uncertainty she had given him. The impulse arrived and shamed him so quickly that he almost missed it.

"She told me my father never resonated with the house," he said. "Not the way this has. She said the architecture noticed her when she arrived, but it never did any of the deeper work for them. No paired rooms. No mutual awareness. Nothing like the archive."

Hermione's face changed in a way he could not have named if pressed. It was not pity or relief. It looked more like recognition that the price of Narcissa's silence had been larger than they knew.

"That must have cost her," she said.

"Yes."

The satchel strap creaked under her hand.

"Why are you here?" he asked.

He did not mean the corridor. He did not mean Wiltshire. She heard the question correctly.

Hermione answered more slowly than she had apologized, and that slowness made the answer feel truer.

"Because being apart wasn't neutral either," she said. "Because I kept acting as if uncertainty was the only dangerous condition and it wasn't. London is worse. The Ministry is worse. Your arm is worse. The work is stalled. And I spent seven days finding new ways to tell myself that I was being principled when really I was just trying not to stand beside the mess I'd made." She stopped, restarted. "That's not me saying the practical stakes mattered more than you. It's me saying I was still trying to split those categories apart in my own head, and they're not separate anymore."

The words were not tidy. They arrived with their own corrections still attached. That, more than eloquence would have, convinced him she had not scripted them on the train.

"Harry said the part I was avoiding, though not in those terms," she added. "He was less polite."

"He usually is."

"Yes."

Another silence opened, but it was not the same one as before. The first had been a waiting room. This one contained actual material.

He could have let them sink back into analysis, into the old endless argument about whether the house had contaminated each touch, each look, each late-night conversation in the library. He knew how to do that. Hermione looked ready to help. Thinking was where she went when the ground was bad. A week ago he might have followed her into it until both of them disappeared.

Instead he said, "If you stay, it can't be like before."

That stopped her. For one beat she looked almost blank, as if she had prepared herself for anger and not for terms.

He had not planned the sentence. The rest came rough, without the polish fear sometimes gave him.

"No more finding something that changes the terms and deciding alone how much of it I can—" He stopped. The sentence had been about to arrive polished, and he didn't trust it that way. "What you did with the folio. That can't happen again."

Hermione did not interrupt. That alone told him she was listening with her whole body.

"And I don't get to do my version either." He heard how careful he sounded and hated it, but the precision mattered. "I don't get to go silent for a week and call it principle."

She swallowed. The movement of her throat was visible above the scarf. "Together, then."

"If the archive opens, we read whatever it gives us in the same room. If one of us finds something—"

"That day," she said, and then caught herself finishing his sentence. "Sorry. Yes. That day. Not after I've—" She stopped. Restarted, more carefully. "Not after I've already built a case in private and decided what you can handle."

He heard her arriving at her own accusation without his having to deliver it.

"No managing," he said.

Her answer took a second because she was checking it for weakness before she let it out. "And if one of us wants out, or can't do it, we say that too. Out loud."

He looked at her. "Fine."

A breath went out of her that was not relief exactly. It sounded more like surviving the first cut without pretending it had not happened.

"Good," she said.

There was still more between them. Damage remained. Want remained. Doubt remained. The corridor held all of it and offered no assistance. Yet the terms had changed in one important way. The damage was no longer happening in parallel private rooms.

"How bad is it outside?" he asked.

Hermione closed her eyes briefly, perhaps because the answer forced the world back into the hall with them. "Bad enough that no one at the Ministry is using the word temporary anymore. The Floo was down for eight hours yesterday. St. Mungo's is rationing restorative draughts again. Accidental magic reports keep coming in from all over. People are pretending not to panic because panic is inconvenient."

That was enough to set the scale again. Britain was still failing, and the work was still waiting.

He glanced at the satchel. "You really did bring work."

"I told you. I didn't stop being me in London."

That answer was almost dry and almost ordinary. The shape of their old exchanges flickered in it and then steadied.

He nodded once. "I want to show you the room first."

Her face lost what little warmth had returned. She knew which room before he turned. Still, she followed without asking him to say it.

They walked the east corridor in full length. The house did not fold the distance for them. The guest room door stayed where it had always been. The morning room stood closed. The conservatory passage remained narrow and underused. Their footsteps answered cleanly off stone.

The drawing room door stood open where it had stood all week.

No stagecraft attended it now, and no pressure came from the threshold. No false warmth leaked from within. The room simply waited beyond the frame, ajar and cold and ordinary in the most offensive way.

He stopped beside the doorway. Hermione stopped too.

Dim corridor light reached the marble floor and the lower arms of the chandelier. Dust showed at the baseboards. A crack ran through one tile near the far wall, hairline but undeniable. The fire had not been lit in days, perhaps longer. Nothing in the room declared itself sacred or cursed. It was only a room in an old house. That had always been part of the obscenity.

"It has been open all week," he said. "I kept walking past it and not going in."

Hermione looked through the doorway without moving. He could not know all of what she saw, but he knew enough of the room's older contents to understand her stillness. He saw them too: the chandelier from floor level, the marble under her back, Bellatrix's bracelets clicking softly each time she reset her grip on the knife, the first red line opening on Hermione's forearm and the blood running toward her wrist because the floor sloped slightly there, and his own seventeen-year-old body against the far wall, every muscle locked so hard he had come away shaking.

His mouth filled abruptly with saliva. He swallowed against it. Beside him Hermione's right hand tightened on the satchel strap until the knuckles blanched under wool.

"I didn't go in alone," he said. The admission scraped on the way out. "I wasn't going to let the house choose that for me again."

For a moment she did not answer. Her gaze remained fixed past the threshold. Then her left hand lifted and adjusted the sleeve over the scar in the small automatic motion he had started noticing every time she was frightened enough to forget she was doing it.

"All right," she said.

They entered together.

The first thing he had to fight was the instinct to take up his old place. His body remembered the war more quickly than his mind did. The far wall exerted its pull at once, offering the same witness position it had assigned him years ago: stand here, stay here, count and fail. He felt himself angling toward it and stopped. Then he made himself walk past that line of sight, past the angle that would have given him the chandelier and not the floor, and into the middle of the room where Hermione had already stopped.

That alone changed the air.

The room remained smaller than memory, dustier too, with nothing in it except furniture pushed back toward the edges and the particular cold of unused marble. Yet stepping into the center rather than the wall felt like wrenching a nail out through flesh. He had not understood until that second how faithfully his body had preserved the old arrangement.

Hermione stood over one patch of floor as if her feet had found it without consulting her. When she spoke, her voice was nearly level.

"It was here."

She set the satchel down by the nearest chair. Then, before caution could reassert itself, she crouched and touched the marble with her gloved fingertips. She held them there a second, two, three. When she straightened, she pulled off the glove, pushed her sleeve back, and exposed the silver-white letters on her left forearm to the room.

The sight of the scar in here landed differently than it had anywhere else.

Bellatrix had cut the word in this room with a cursed knife while laughing. Draco remembered that laugh less as sound than as rhythm, the easy delighted rhythm of someone enjoying her own hands. He remembered Hermione's breath catching when the blade snagged on bone at the start of the second stroke. He remembered being unable to do anything except say her name once and hear how useless it sounded.

Hermione lowered the scarred forearm until the letters met the marble.

The stone bit instantly. He could see it in the tightening of her mouth. She left her arm there anyway, not as theatre and not because she thought pain was a virtue. She left it there like a woman verifying an equation against the original terms.

"This is the temperature I kept dreaming," she said. "Not blood. Not the knife. The floor. The floor under my shoulder blades and the cold coming through my jumper while she was talking."

Her throat worked. She kept her arm down.

"I remember her hand in my hair too," she said after a moment. "That is a stupid detail to keep, but it is what stayed. She kept yanking my head back between words. I could smell her perfume every time she did it. And when the knife caught..." She shook her head once, impatient with herself. "The first cut dragged because she hit bone. I felt the hitch of it before I felt the pain."

There it was. The room answered rendered fact with rendered fact, not abstraction.

He had no comfort fit for that level of detail. Anything he said that reached toward solace would lie on contact. So he answered with the only honest currency available.

"I remember the bracelets," he said.

Hermione looked at him.

"The ones at her wrist." He forced himself to keep looking back. "They clicked every time she moved her hand. I kept hearing that and the knife and thinking if I moved now I would be too late, and if I stayed still I was already too late." His gaze flicked, unwillingly, to the far wall. "I stood over there counting chandelier points because if I looked at your arm the whole time I was going to be sick, and being sick felt like another useless thing."

The words came badly, but badly was probably right. There was no clean speech available for the ugliest room in the house.

Hermione lifted her arm from the floor. The letters had gone red around the edges from the cold.

"I used to dream the marble was holding me down," she said. "That was the logic of the dream. Not that she was here again. That the floor was."

Acid climbed into the back of his throat. He swallowed it.

"It is not," he said.

"No." She looked down at the tile under her shoes, then around the room, taking in walls, fireplace, chandelier, doorway, all of it. "No. It is not. It is stone, and it is filthy, and it has had years to become part of the furniture again while I was still carrying it around like weather."

She rubbed her thumb once over the raised scar. The gesture held no vanity in it, only assessment.

"I'm not saying it means nothing," she said. "I'm not saying I can stand in here now and feel cured, because that would be nonsense. My stomach is trying to leave through my mouth and my arm feels hot enough to strip skin." Her eyes went to him, steady despite that. "But it is a room. She cut me in it. She doesn't get to keep cutting me with it every time I picture the floor."

The sentence did not sound triumphant. It sounded like work. That made it more believable.

He looked once more at the far wall where seventeen-year-old him had braced himself and counted light instead of blood. Then he crossed the last small distance and stood beside her, not opposite, not back in witness position. His bandaged arm brushed her bare wrist. The contact sent a flare of pain through the Mark and then, just as quickly, a lessening.

Hermione noticed. Her gaze dropped to the gauze.

"It's worse," she said quietly.

"Yes."

She hesitated only a fraction before taking his hand.

Her palm was cold from the marble. The calluses at her fingers felt exactly as he remembered. She did not lace their hands romantically or delicately. She took hold the way she took hold during examinations, with full attention and no room for uncertainty about whether contact had been chosen. He closed his fingers around hers.

That was the concrete alteration. It was not the room shrinking or light blooming or the house congratulating them. Two marked bodies stood in the center of the drawing room by choice, with her scar uncovered, his arm bandaged badly, and neither of them pretending the room held more authority than they gave it.

This time the house answered differently.

No warmth flooded the air. No false golden comfort spilled down the walls. Instead a small pressure eased under his skin, as if the room had unclenched around them by one degree. The Mark stopped demanding the whole center of his attention. Somewhere outside the doorway a sconce in the corridor lit itself with a dry click.

The room did not become kind. It merely ceased being the only active force inside itself.

Hermione let out a breath she had plainly been holding. "There," she said, almost under it.

He nodded because his throat had closed.

They stayed another half minute, maybe more. Time in the room refused good measurement. Then Hermione pulled her sleeve back down over the scar, not to hide it from him but because the air had become unbearable on newly chilled skin. Draco picked up her glove and handed it to her. She took it, put it on, then bent for the satchel.

"I don't want to shut the door," she said.

"Neither do I."

So they left it open.

At the threshold she had to release his hand to step over the stone lip, but she took it again in the corridor without making a question of the gesture. The east-wing sconces were lit now, though only faintly. Their light spread in thin yellow pools along the wall and did not attempt enchantment.

"How bad is the Mark?" Hermione asked as they started back.

"Bad enough that I considered asking my mother to hex it off."

That earned him a quick sideways look. "You would never ask your mother for help in a useful tone."

"No. I would phrase it badly and regret it."

"That at least is consistent."

The exchange would have been almost ordinary anywhere else. Here, coming out of the drawing room with their hands still linked, it felt stranger and better.

"Can you look at it?" he asked after a few more steps. "In the library. If you still want to."

Hermione did not rush to answer. She considered the request in the exact spirit he had just demanded of them both.

"Yes," she said. "If you want full honesty with it."

"I do."

"Then yes."

They reached the library door in the same full corridor the house had denied them all week.

---

Hermione paused on the threshold and looked inside as if reacquainting herself with a room that had gone half feral in her absence. The reading table still held two chairs pulled toward each other by use. The genealogical record lay open where he had abandoned it. Her strip of parchment sat beside the text, accusatory and domestic at once. The grate remained cold. Two stacks of books leaned dangerously near the edge of a side table because she had always promised to reshelve them properly tomorrow.

"Is that my note?" she asked.

"You left it."

"I can see that."

He let the rest stay unsaid. So did she. The note remained where it was.

Hermione set down the satchel, unwound the scarf from her neck, and shrugged out of the coat. Underneath she wore a dark blue jumper and the same practical skirt she had apparently taken to the Ministry all week. The familiarity of that clothing in this room hit him harder than her arrival at the door had. She rolled the stiffness out of one shoulder, drew her wand, and aimed it at the grate.

A warming charm ran through the wood in stages. The logs took the spell reluctantly, crackled, smoked, and then caught for good. Heat spread by degrees. It came from Hermione's wand and patience, not from any sudden generosity in the house.

She crouched to adjust one fallen log with a poker. Strands of hair slipped loose around her face. When she straightened, her cheeks had already started to lose some of the rawness the cold had drawn into them.

"Do you want more tea?" he said.

"Please."

He had taken three steps toward the door when the next sentence arrived before he could stop it.

"Hermione."

She turned.

Her name in his mouth felt new enough to alter the room. Four syllables he had kept locked behind Granger for months because the surname was safer, less revealing, easier to position between them like a table. The first name carried more body and more history in it. He had been able to think it in private without damage. Speaking it was different.

Brown eyes lifted to him and stayed there.

"More tea would be good," she said. Then, after a beat so small it would have vanished if he had not been waiting for it, "Draco."

Neither of them smiled. The moment was too narrow and too serious for triumph. The names simply stood there between them, impossible to take back and too private to perform for anyone else.

He went to the kitchen before the room could ask him to account for the reaction in his face.

The tea took less time than his hands wanted it to. He used the cream-coloured cup for her and a blue-chipped one for himself because the blue cup had survived a crack in the handle three winters ago and seemed, today, like the sort of damaged object that earned continued use on principle. When he carried them back, Hermione had taken a notebook out of the satchel along with a folded packet of Ministry reports and a narrow reference text bound in green. She had also set the genealogical record upright and moved his mother's empty cup aside as if reclaiming the table in small practical motions.

He placed her tea by her elbow. She murmured thanks without looking up from the page she was scanning.

"You brought reports," he said, taking his chair.

"I brought enough to know how fast things are worsening and not enough to pretend I could solve it alone in London."

"A surprisingly self-aware packing strategy."

"I had a week to improve."

The answer would have been defensive a fortnight ago. Tonight it was merely tired.

He opened the genealogical record again. A line he had skimmed twelve times earlier finally resolved into utility once Hermione sat across from him. The habit of private interpretation had not yet died, but it was at least weak enough for him to fight.

"Here," he said, turning the book toward her at once. "I don't know whether it matters or whether I'm seeing patterns because I've looked at this damned page too long."

Hermione set down her cup and leaned across the space between their chairs. Her finger followed the cramped notation in the margin. The fire cracked behind them. One lock of hair fell forward; she tucked it back with an absent movement and kept reading.

"This isn't genealogy," she said. "It's household assignment shorthand buried in the genealogy because your ancestors couldn't bear the thought of keeping one category of record in one place."

"That sounds like us."

"That sounds like people with estates and no editors." Her mouth twitched, then she went serious again. "It is noting which marriage contracts produced changes in house access. Look. This mark here is not issue. It is threshold variance."

He leaned in. "You are sure."

"Fairly. The hand is atrocious, but the notation matches the west-gallery repairs from the Levett manuscript."

"You brought Levett notes?"

"Of course I brought Levett notes."

The easy certainty of it hit him with the same strange pleasure as hearing his own name from her had a few minutes earlier. Some parts of her had not been lost to the week. They had only been somewhere else, waiting for reentry.

She slid one of her notebooks out of the satchel and flipped to a page dense with copied symbols. "The shorthand here is indicating paired permission and altered route behaviour after formal household binding. It is not romantic. It is administrative in the most depressing possible way. But it does confirm that earlier generations tracked this in family records, which means your folio was never the only source."

"That would have been useful a week ago."

She went still. "Yes."

He could have let the sentence cut and move on. Instead he added, because they were supposed to be doing this differently now, "I'm not saying it for sport."

"I know." She kept her eyes on the page for one moment more, then looked at him. "And I am not pretending that because I am back in the library I get to start at the second sentence. I know exactly where the first one was."

The honesty of that left no satisfying reply. He nodded and looked down at the notebook.

Hermione traced another line. "This phrase here is closer to maintained by concordance than activated by passion, if that makes sense."

"It does, unpleasantly."

"Yes. There is nothing in these houses that cannot be made sound bureaucratic eventually." She sat back, thinking. "If this cross-reference is right, there should be a parallel note in the domestic register for the east wing. Or in the archive, if the family started hiding the more interesting material."

At the word archive both of them glanced, involuntarily, toward the wall.

The wall was still only stone.

For a second the old pattern tugged at them: wonder what the house wants, wonder whether it is listening, wonder whether waiting for it counts as passivity or participation. Hermione caught herself first.

"No," she said.

He looked back at her. "No?"

"No sitting here staring at architecture as if it's going to settle the argument. We work first."

The answer was so precisely her that it nearly hurt.

"Fine," he said.

She looked at the bandage instead of the books. "Before or after the work?"

He flexed his fingers. The Mark answered with a hard pull under the gauze. "Before, probably. I'm having trouble pretending it isn't dictating my attention."

Hermione nodded once. "All right. Tell me exactly how much."

The question was not clinical. It was part of the new contract. He answered in the same spirit.

"It has been worse each day since you left," he said. "By the fourth day it was waking me up. By yesterday the outer lines felt hot all the way to the elbow. Today it is less a sharp pain than a constant drag, except when it spikes. It did that in the drawing room when you took my hand, and then it eased."

"Did the resonance at distance change while I was in London?"

"If you mean did I know when you were upset, not clearly. If you mean did it get worse every evening around the hour you would usually still be in the library, yes." He glanced at her. "I assume you had your own version."

"Yes." She did not soften it. "I knew when it was especially bad. I also knew when you were in the east wing, which I was trying very hard not to know."

He leaned back in the chair. "That would have been useful information."

"Yes."

At least now the word did not come with evasion attached.

Hermione set aside the notebook. "May I?"

He held out the left arm.

She did not touch the gauze immediately. First she moved her chair around the corner of the table until she sat beside him rather than opposite. Then she reached for the knot he had tied badly and paused there, eyes on his face instead of the bandage.

"If I see a change I don't understand," she said, "I tell you that's what it is. If I think I know and turn out to be wrong later, you hear the revision as soon as I do. If I need to speculate, I say I am speculating."

"Good."

"And if you want me to stop, say stop."

"I know how that works."

"I'm aware," she said, dry enough to acknowledge the history of the sentence. "I'm saying it anyway."

Then she untied the knot.

The outer layer came away cleanly. The inner strip had stuck where the salve dried against raw skin. Hermione saw the pull in his jaw and looked up.

"Sorry."

"Take it off."

She did, carefully but without the false gentleness that would only have prolonged it. Air hit the exposed skin and made him hiss through his teeth.

The Mark looked worse than it had at noon. The old black lines along the skull and serpent had gone bright at the edges, inflamed red bleeding out into the surrounding skin. Near the wrist the smallest branches had raised slightly, glossy as if the curse sat nearer the surface than flesh liked permitting. The center of it remained the familiar deep black that no healing spell had ever touched. Around that center the damage now looked irritated, hungry.

Hermione inhaled once through her nose. "All right."

"That is not a diagnosis."

"No. That is me not lying and saying it looks better than it does." She bent closer. "The outer channels are overactive. See here and here? This branch should not be carrying that much heat unless the resonance loop is trying to complete and failing."

"That means what."

"That means the curse has gotten used to a pattern of suppression under contact and does not have it." She glanced at him. "That's not me flattering myself. It's the behaviour we've observed."

"So yes, my week was making it worse."

"Yes." She did not delay the answer. "Also yes, the ambient field worsening could be aggravating it more generally. Both can be true."

There it was. Direct uncertainty, not hidden.

"And long term?" he asked.

Hermione's mouth tightened, not because she meant to dodge but because honesty here still hurt her. "I don't know long term. I know sustained direct contact quiets it. I know distance worsens it. I know the entire magical environment is under strain, which means the Mark is receiving less ambient stability than usual. I don't know whether the resonance will keep scaling up or whether it plateaus if we stop letting the house write around us. That's the part I still can't prove."

He looked at her bent over the Mark he had once thought no one would ever touch without revulsion.

"You should have said that weeks ago," he said.

"Yes."

The old answer again, plain and unadorned. He had not realized how much of the week he had spent angry not merely because she hid the folio, but because he assumed that if she ever came back the apology would arrive padded with defenses. It had not.

Hermione laid two bare fingers very lightly against the inside of his wrist, just below the Mark, taking pulse first the way she always had. Her focus narrowed. "Tell me if this changes."

Then her other hand settled over the lower curve of the skull.

The effect was immediate enough to be obscene.

Heat pulled inward. The constant drag up his arm loosened by one clean degree. The sensation was not pleasure, exactly. It was relief too physical to stay morally simple. Draco let out a breath he had not intended to let her hear.

Hermione's eyelids lowered briefly, concentration or resonance or both. "There," she said. "The outer branches are settling already."

"Yes."

"That is still happening this fast."

"Yes."

She nodded as if forcing herself to catalogue rather than feel. Her thumb shifted half an inch over the raised line of the curse, and his whole forearm answered. The room changed around them in the same second, though not dramatically. A shelf near the archive wall gave a small internal click as books resettled. Somewhere behind the stone a mechanism or spell line released with the quiet sound of pressure equalizing.

Both of them turned their heads toward the wall.

The seam had appeared.

It was only a hairline at first, a pale vertical stroke in the stone where there had been none. Then the line widened to the thickness of a finger, stopped, and waited.

Hermione's hand was still on his arm. He could feel the instinct in her to pull away and the equal instinct to stay exactly where she was until the present task was complete.

"Finish the bandage," he said.

Her gaze came back to him at once. That might have been the most significant act in the room.

"All right." She did not move her hand from the Mark until the worst of the heat had gone. Then she reached for the clean roll of gauze he kept in the table drawer and began winding it properly, the way he had never managed one-handed. The contact withdrew in stages. The pain returned only partially.

"Better?" she asked.

"Yes."

"It will flare again if we lose contact for too long, I think."

"You think."

"I think," she confirmed, tightening the wrap with efficient fingers. "I'm not promoting it to certainty because the wall decided to dramatize itself."

That pulled a short breath of laughter out of him. It hurt less than expected.

"There is the Hermione I know," he said.

Her hands paused once on the bandage. Then they resumed. "She's been here the whole time. She was just behaving badly."

He could not argue with that. He also could not stop looking at her bent over his arm in the firelight with her hair half loose and her expression narrowed into work. Narcissa had said the house had not invented it. The proof of that seemed suddenly and unmanageably visible in the simple fact that even now, after everything, watching Hermione concentrate on tying gauze made his chest go tight.

She tucked the end of the wrap neatly and sat back. "There."

He flexed his fingers again. The pain remained, but it had dropped below the threshold of total dominance. "Better."

Hermione followed his glance to the wall. The seam still waited, narrow and pale, as if the house had learned enough not to fling itself open like an overeager host.

"You were right to make us finish first," she said.

"I was right for once."

"I would not go that far."

The line was dry rather than playful. He found he liked dry much better than comfort right now.

He rose. So did she. The library fire had settled into a steady burn behind them. The tea on the table still steamed faintly. Her notebook lay open to Levett shorthand. His mother's cup remained abandoned at the corner of the table, a relic from a different revelation earlier in the day.

Neither of them hurried to the wall.

"Do you want to wait?" he asked.

Hermione considered. "No. I want to go in knowing that if the archive hands us another ugly fact, we don't pocket it this time."

"Agreed."

She stepped closer to the seam. He came with her. Up near the wall the stone smelled faintly of dust and old cold. The opening had widened another fraction, just enough that he could see darkness inside and the edge of the inner threshold beyond.

"It does not get to be the only thing with initiative in this house," Hermione said.

"No."

He set his right palm against the seam.

For one second he thought the wall might open under his hand alone, repeating the unstable access it had granted him before the rupture. It did not. The stone stayed fixed.

Then Hermione put her hand beside his.

The response was immediate and quiet. No fanfare attended it. The seam widened. Hidden mechanisms released within the stone. The wall gave way at once, not like an order and not like absolution, but like a threshold yielding to a choice they had made together.
