# Chapter 9

The lamb was overcooked.

Hermione noticed this the way she noticed most things about Malfoy — sideways, while thinking about something else. He'd been in the kitchen for forty minutes, which was longer than usual, and when he set the plates down in the library she saw the edges of the chops gone grey and tight. He didn't mention it. She didn't mention it. They ate at the long table between stacks of Abelard Malfoy's correspondence and a genealogical chart she'd been cross-referencing against anchoring point locations, and the lamb was overcooked and fine and she ate all of it.

Three weeks. She'd been in the Manor for three weeks and she was eating overcooked lamb in a library that rearranged its own shelves and not finding this remarkable.

He was reading the Abelard letters while he ate, one hand holding a chop and the other turning pages. He'd switched to the originals after she'd found a transcription error in the copies — a single diacritical mark that changed "to anchor" to "to sever," which was the kind of mistake that could have sent them down the wrong research path for days. He'd been quietly furious about it. Not at her. At the transcription, at whoever had made the copies three hundred years ago, at the accumulated carelessness of his own family's record-keeping. She'd watched him go through the first twenty letters checking every mark against the originals, his handwriting in the margins getting smaller and more precise as he went, and she'd felt something that was not professional satisfaction.

Now he was eating with one hand and annotating with the other, his fingers stained with ink at the tips, and she was looking at his hands.

She was looking at his hands.

The realization arrived late, the way these things did — she'd been doing it for several minutes before the watching became conscious. His fingers were long and thin and there was a smudge of blue-black ink on the side of his right index finger where he'd been pressing too hard on the nib. He turned a page and his thumb caught the edge and he licked it, absently, to get a better grip. She followed the motion of his thumb to his mouth and back to the page and her stomach did something that had nothing to do with lamb.

She looked down at her plate. There was a bone on it and a scrape of mint jelly and her knife was at an angle she didn't remember leaving it at.

The library was warm. It had been warm for days — since the examination that went wrong, or right, since the memory that had poured through the Mark and left them both wrecked and silent in this room. The Manor had responded to whatever had shifted between them by becoming almost generous. The corridors from the guest wing to the library had stabilized into a consistent route. The shelves surfaced texts she needed before she asked for them. The fires lit themselves when she entered a room and banked when she left. She was being tended to by a building, and the building was getting better at it.

She should have found this more disturbing than she did.

"The third letter references a node at Glastonbury," Malfoy said, without looking up. "But it's using a locational cipher I haven't seen before. The first glyph might be directional."

"Or temporal. Abelard's notation system uses the same base glyph for spatial and temporal orientation — the modifier's in the subscript."

He turned the letter toward her and she leaned across the table to look, and for a moment their heads were close enough that she could smell the ink on his fingers and something underneath it, soap or skin, something warm and plain. She pointed to the subscript. Her sleeve brushed his wrist. Neither of them moved.

"Temporal," he said, after a pause that was slightly too long.

"Temporal," she agreed, and sat back, and picked up her water, and drank it, and the water was room temperature and tasted like nothing and her hand was steady.

Outside, it was already dark. Early December in Wiltshire meant the light went by half three, and they'd been working since morning. The frost on the library windows had melted from the inside, leaving tracks of condensation that caught the firelight. She could feel him — the low, constant awareness the Mark resonance had settled into, his presence two feet away registering as a kind of warmth in her peripheral attention that was distinct from the fire, distinct from the library. She'd stopped trying to rationalize it as diagnostic residue. She hadn't started calling it what it was.

She was comfortable. That was the word she'd been avoiding. Not safe, exactly — the Manor didn't permit safe, not with its corridors shifting and its rooms appearing according to logic she couldn't map. But comfortable. The work was absorbing. The library was extraordinary. The company was—

She put the thought down before she finished it. Picked up the genealogical chart instead.

"I think the Glastonbury node was one of the original twelve," she said. "The distribution pattern makes more sense as a dodecahedral lattice than a grid. Your family didn't build in straight lines."

"My family didn't build anything in straight lines." He set down the chop bone and wiped his fingers on a cloth, methodically, one finger at a time. "Including their record-keeping, their politics, and most of their marriages."

She almost laughed. She caught it behind the water glass but he saw it anyway, and something in his expression shifted — not a smile, he didn't smile easily, but a loosening around the jaw, a settling. He looked less careful for a moment. He looked like someone who had just made another person almost laugh and was pleased about it in a way he wouldn't admit.

She bent back to the chart and the evening continued. They worked for another two hours. At some point he got up and made tea without asking, and he brought it back with milk in hers and nothing in his and the milk was the right amount — not because he'd asked, not because she'd told him. He'd learned it the way the house learned corridors: through repetition, through attention, through the specific dailiness of being in the same place as someone for three weeks.

She wrapped her hands around the cup and the warmth of it went through her palms. He sat back down and pulled the next letter toward him and they worked in the quiet of the library while the fire popped and the frost thickened on the windows, and outside the Manor the English winter was doing its ordinary work of making everything dark and cold, and inside the Manor the light was warm and steady and the silence between them was not empty. It was full of turning pages and the scratch of his pen and the occasional murmur when one of them found something worth sharing.

At one point she stretched and her back popped, three vertebrae in quick succession, and he looked up from the letters and said "That sounded structural."

"It's fine."

"You've been hunched over that chart for four hours."

"I hunch. It's a professional hazard."

"It's going to be a medical hazard if you don't move your neck occasionally."

She rolled her neck, deliberately, and something else popped, and he winced on her behalf and she said "See? Fine," and went back to the chart, and the exchange was nothing — was the kind of thing that happened between people who spent time together, the flat unremarkable texture of shared hours — and it was so ordinary and so easy that she didn't register it as significant until later, when it was too late for the recognition to be anything but painful.

The fire burned and the library held them in its generous warmth, and Hermione Granger was comfortable in Malfoy Manor.

---

She left the library at half eleven.

He'd already gone up twenty minutes earlier, stacking the Abelard letters in the order he'd reach for them in the morning — a particular kind of optimism, organizing for a tomorrow he assumed they'd both be here for. She'd said goodnight and he'd said goodnight and neither of them had paused on it, and that was new too. For the first week, the goodnights had been stiff little negotiations. Now they were just the end of the day.

The corridor from the library to the guest wing was the route she'd walked most often, and the Manor had learned her habits or decided to accommodate them — the same sequence of turns, the same portraits in the same order, the same stretch of stone floor where the third flagstone from the left had a crack she used as a landmark. She walked it without thinking. Her mind was still in the Abelard letters, turning over the Glastonbury cipher, and underneath that, not quite submerged, the shape of his hands on the page. The way he'd winced when her neck popped. The milk in her tea.

She was thinking about the milk in her tea when she turned the corner where the corridor jogged left toward the guest wing staircase.

The door was open.

It was a room she had never seen in three weeks of living in this house, and she recognized it instantly.

The chandelier. Massive, ornate, hanging from a ceiling so high the chain disappeared into shadow. Crystal drops catching light from somewhere — there were no candles lit, no fire, but the chandelier held a pale luminescence that might have been magical residue or might have been the moonlight coming through the tall windows at the room's far end. The windows were uncurtained. The glass was dark.

The marble floor. White, veined with grey, polished to a depth that reflected the chandelier above it like still water. Cold — she could feel the cold coming off it through the open doorway, a different cold than the Manor's usual winter chill. This was preserved cold. The temperature of a room that had not been warm in seven years.

She knew this floor. She had lain on this floor.

Her feet stopped moving. Not a decision — the information bypassed the part of her brain that made decisions and arrived directly at her legs, her spine, the soles of her feet on the flagstone. She was standing in the corridor looking into the drawing room of Malfoy Manor and her body had remembered it before she had.

The room was exactly as it had been. That was the worst thing — not a detail changed, not a piece of furniture moved. The same high-backed chairs arranged along the wall. The same dark fireplace, massive, unlit, the mantel carved with serpents. The same expanse of marble floor between the door and the far wall, and she knew how long that expanse was because she had been dragged across it.

Bellatrix's hand in her hair. That was the first sensory memory to arrive — the grip, the pull, the specific angle of her neck as she was hauled from the entrance hall into this room. The others were behind it, pressing forward in a sequence her body had filed with merciless precision while her conscious mind had been doing its best to leave.

The knife.

The sound it made on her skin was not the sound she'd expected. She'd expected a cutting sound, something sharp and clean. What it had sounded like was tearing — the cursed blade didn't cut so much as separate the skin, and the sound was wet and wrong and Bellatrix had been talking the whole time. Not shouting. Talking. Conversational. Almost cozy. "Tell me," she'd said, and the knife had pressed into the inside of Hermione's left forearm, and Hermione had screamed and Bellatrix had said "oh, don't do that yet, we've barely started," as if Hermione were being rude.

The M first. The blade starting at the inside of the wrist and carving upward. Two humps. Bellatrix's hand was steady. That was a detail Hermione had never told anyone — how steady Bellatrix's hand had been, how precise, how she'd carved the letters like someone writing a note. No haste. No trembling. She had all the time she wanted and she knew it and the knowledge was part of the cruelty, the way she'd paused between letters to examine her work, tilting Hermione's arm in the light.

Hermione's left forearm ached under the glamour. The scar tissue was responding to the room or to the memory or to some residual curse-magic in the marble and it didn't matter which because the effect was the same — a hot, deep throb along each letter, as if the wound were opening in sympathy with its own origin.

U-D-B-L-O-O-D.

She had counted the letters as they were being carved because counting was the only thing she could do. Seven letters. Seven separate cuts. She'd gotten through the first four before her vision had gone white and she'd screamed in a way she'd never screamed before — not pain exactly, or not only pain, but the particular horror of being written on, of having someone else's word put into her skin by force. The M and the U and the D and the B and then the white-out and then she was back and Bellatrix was on the L and Hermione had looked to her right—

There had been people in the room. The Snatchers. Greyback, massive and breathing too loud. Peter Pettigrew somewhere behind her. Ron and Harry shouting from below, from the cellar, their voices muffled by stone. And against the far wall, standing very still, a boy with white-blond hair and grey eyes who was looking at her and then was not looking at her.

She had seen him look away.

She was still standing in the corridor. She hadn't moved. The door was open and the room was there and her body would not cross the threshold and would not retreat and she was stuck in the doorway of the worst room in the world and the chandelier above her was the same chandelier she had pulled down with a blasting spell to create the chaos that let them escape. She could see the repair — a hairline seam in the plaster where the ceiling had been mended, the chain reattached. Someone had fixed the damage she'd done on her way out. Of course someone had. This was the Malfoy house. Damage was repaired and preserved and maintained. Everything was kept.

She could hear her own breathing. It was too fast. Her hands were at her sides and they were fists and her fingernails were cutting into her palms and she knew this was a trauma response, she had read about trauma responses, she had discussed trauma responses with a Mind Healer in the year after the war. She knew what was happening to her body and the knowledge was useless, absolutely useless, because knowing the name for freezing did not unfreeze you.

The marble floor stretched out before her, pale and depthless in the chandelier's cold light.

She had bled on that floor. She remembered that too. The blood had been bright on the white marble, brighter than blood should have been — shock made it look vivid, oversaturated, like a colour she'd never seen before. It had pooled in the veining of the stone. She'd watched it from the floor, her cheek pressed against the cold marble, while Bellatrix leaned over her and said something she couldn't hear because the pain had become a sound of its own, a high ringing that filled her skull and replaced all other input.

The Manor had opened this room for her.

She understood that with the same certainty she understood the anchoring lattice or the Mark's resonance — not through proof but through the sudden, total clarity of something she'd been refusing to see. The house had opened this room because she had been getting comfortable. Because she had been eating lamb in the library and looking at his hands and feeling something that approached safety, and the house had decided — or the house's architecture had reached a condition where — or the house did not decide anything and the room had simply manifested because the conditions aligned, the way weather aligned, the way magic aligned—

It didn't matter. The room was here. She was here. The house had put them together.

She could not feel Malfoy through the resonance. She tried and there was nothing — the low background awareness she'd grown accustomed to was gone, replaced by something dense and opaque, as if the Manor's attention were focused so narrowly on this corridor and this room that it had no bandwidth left for anything else. She was alone with the drawing room and whatever the drawing room was.

The cold crept from the marble across the threshold and found her feet. She was wearing the wool socks she'd brought from her flat, thick and practical, and the cold went through them as if they were nothing. As if the room had been saving its temperature for her. As if seven years of sealed doors had preserved this cold the way a vault preserves its contents — not because the cold served a purpose but because the room did not know how to be anything other than what it had been on the night that defined it.

An hour ago she'd been drinking tea with the right amount of milk. An hour ago the library had been warm and the fire had been steady and she'd been thinking about a cipher and a pair of ink-stained hands. That woman was still somewhere inside her, behind the freeze response, and she could not reach her.

---

She didn't know how long she'd been standing there when she heard him.

Footsteps on stone, approaching from the direction of the library. His particular footsteps — she knew them now, the way she knew the third flagstone's crack — measured, even, the sound of someone who'd been walking these corridors since childhood. They slowed as they reached the jog in the corridor. They stopped.

She didn't turn around. She couldn't. She was looking at the chandelier and the floor and the windows with their dark glass and the chairs along the wall and the fireplace with its serpents and she could not turn around.

He was quiet for a long time. Long enough that she thought he might leave. Long enough that she started to want him to leave and then to fear that he would and then to hate herself for both.

"Granger."

His voice was very careful. She recognized the register from the first day she'd arrived, from the negotiation in his study — controlled, precise, every word checked before it was released. He was being careful because he was standing behind a woman who was frozen in the doorway of the room where she'd been tortured and he didn't know what to do and careful was his default when he didn't know what to do.

"I didn't know it was here," he said. "This corridor — the room hasn't been accessible. I thought—" He stopped. Started again, and the restart was audible, effortful. "I thought I'd sealed it."

She laughed. It came out wrong — too high, too short, not really a laugh. More like the sound a laugh would make if you stripped all the humour out of it and left only the breath.

"You thought you'd sealed it." Her voice was flat and strange in her own ears. "You sealed the room."

"Years ago. After the war. I had the house seal it. The corridor was supposed to — the Manor was supposed to keep it inaccessible."

"Well," she said. "It didn't."

She heard him step closer. Not close — he stopped several feet behind her, still in the corridor, not crossing the line where the flagstone met the marble threshold. She could feel him now, faintly, the resonance stuttering back to life as if his proximity had broken through whatever the Manor had been doing. He was afraid. She could feel that through the bond — his fear, distinct from hers, a different temperature and texture, sharper and more controlled.

"Come away from the door," he said. Very quiet. "Please."

"I can't."

"You can. It's just a room. The house is—"

"Don't." The word came out hard and fast and she felt him go still behind her. "Don't tell me it's just a room."

Silence. The chandelier above them held its pale, residual light. Somewhere deep in the Manor a clock struck midnight, or something that sounded like a clock — the house produced sounds sometimes that had no visible source.

"No," he said. "You're right. I'm sorry."

She closed her eyes. The marble floor was still there behind her eyelids, white and veined, and Bellatrix's voice was there too, that conversational tone, that patience. She opened her eyes because open was better than closed, because at least with her eyes open the room was a room and not a memory.

"I'm going in," she said.

"Granger—"

"I'm going in." She said it to the room, not to him. Her voice had levelled out into something she recognized — the register she used when giving testimony, when presenting findings to the Wizengamot, when she needed to sound like a person who made decisions. "If the house opened it, then I'm going in. I won't stand in a doorway."

She made her feet move. The first step was the worst — the transition from flagstone to marble, from corridor temperature to the drawing room's preserved cold. Her shoes on the marble made a sound she remembered. Flat, hard, echoless. Marble ate sound. She had screamed in this room and the marble had eaten the screams.

She walked to the centre of the room and stopped. She was standing on the floor she had bled on. The blood was gone — cleaned, of course, long ago — but she looked down and she could see exactly where it had been, could map the stain on the stone like a chart she'd memorized. Here was where her head had been. Here was where her arm had been stretched out for the knife. Here was where she'd curled into herself after Bellatrix finished and stepped back to admire.

Behind her, she heard him enter.

His footsteps were wrong — uneven, halting, the careful stride broken. He got three steps onto the marble and stopped. She turned around and looked at him.

He was grey. Not pale the way he usually was — grey, the colour of someone who has been hit. His eyes were on the floor, not on her, and they were tracking across the marble the same way hers had, reading the room's geography, and she realized he could see it too. He knew where the blood had been. He knew the layout of this room from the other side — not from the floor looking up but from the wall looking down, and the knowledge was on his face.

"You stood over there," she said. She pointed to the far wall, between the second and third chairs. "Against the wall. Your mother was next to you."

His jaw worked. She watched the muscle in the side of his face move and it was not enough. It was a reaction and it was not enough.

"You were wearing your school robes," she said. "Easter holidays. You'd been home a week. I remember because I spent a month afterward thinking about how you'd been home a week and this was what your home looked like."

"Granger, I—"

"You looked at me." She hadn't planned to say this. The words were coming from the place where the memory lived, not from the part of her that decided what to say. "When she started on the M. You looked at me and I looked at you and you looked away. Do you remember that?"

He was standing very still. His hands were at his sides. She noticed they were shaking — a fine tremor, barely visible, the kind of shaking you could hide in pockets if you had pockets. He didn't have pockets. He was in shirtsleeves.

"Yes."

"I don't know what I wanted from you." She heard her own voice, analytical, almost conversational, and recognized the register as dangerous — this was her dissociation voice, the one that sounded calm because it had disconnected from everything underneath. "I was seventeen and I was on your floor and your aunt was carving a slur into my arm and I looked at you and I think I wanted — I don't know. Something. Some sign that you—"

She couldn't finish the sentence. Not because of emotion but because there was no honest way to end it. What had she wanted? For him to save her? He was sixteen. For him to protest? He'd have been killed. For him to look and keep looking? He'd looked. He'd seen it. And then he'd turned his face to the wall.

"I remember everything about that night," Malfoy said. His voice had gone flat, stripped of its usual careful modulation. This was a new register — not controlled, not careful, just levelled by the weight of what it was carrying. "I remember the sound she made when she laughed. I remember Pettigrew's breathing. I remember the — the sound the knife made. I didn't know a knife could make that sound."

"It was a cursed blade," she said, because precision was the only thing she had left. "It wasn't cutting. It was separating the skin. Bellatrix's knife was designed—"

"Stop." He closed his eyes. "Please."

"Why? You were there. You heard it."

"I know I heard it."

"Then why does it bother you to hear me describe it?"

His eyes opened. They were bright and she understood he was close to something — tears or rage, she couldn't tell, and she didn't think he could either. "Because you're describing it like a research finding. Like it's data. And it's not — Granger, it happened to you. It happened to you on my floor."

"I know whose floor it was." The dissociation cracked. She heard it in her own voice — the flat register splitting, something raw underneath pressing through. "I know whose house this is. I know whose family she was. I know you were sixteen and scared and there was nothing you could have done that wouldn't have gotten you killed. I know all of that. I've known it for seven years. I testified at your trial and I meant every word. I believe you were not in a position to intervene. I believe it. Do you understand? I believe it and it doesn't help."

The last word echoed off the marble. Flat, hard, swallowed.

He stood there. He looked at her across the distance of the room — the same distance that had been between them that night, wall to floor, except now they were both standing and neither of them was screaming and the aunt who had done it was dead and none of that made the distance smaller.

"What do you want me to say?"

She almost said something cruel. She could feel it in her mouth — the shape of a sentence that would have been true and vicious and entirely unfair, something about how he'd had seven years to figure out what to say and if he hadn't managed it by now then maybe there wasn't anything. She didn't say it. She held it behind her teeth and it tasted like metal and shame.

"I don't know," she said instead. "I don't know what there is to say."

He took a breath. She watched him take it — watched his chest expand, watched the effort of pulling air into a body that clearly didn't want to be in this room. He walked forward. Not toward her — toward the wall. His wall. The spot between the second and third chairs where he had stood at sixteen and watched Bellatrix Lestrange torture Hermione Granger on the marble floor.

He put his back against the wall and stood there.

"I didn't look away because I didn't care," he said. He was speaking to the room, to the floor, to the spot where she'd been. "I looked away because I was — I couldn't—" He stopped. The sentence restructured itself behind his expression. "I was afraid that if I watched I'd do something. Try to stop her. And she'd have killed me and then she'd have killed you and it would have been a gesture, not a rescue, and I couldn't—" His hand moved, a sharp abortive motion. "I did the math. In my head. While it was happening. I stood there and I did the math on whether I could reach my wand before she cursed me and I couldn't. The numbers didn't work. And so I stood here. And I turned my face to the wall. And I listened to it."

The room held the words the way it had held the blood — absorbed them, offered no resonance, no echo, no comfort. Marble ate sound.

"That's supposed to make me feel better?" She said it before she could stop herself, and she heard how it sounded — ugly, unkind, the voice of someone who was being given an honest account of someone else's worst moment and responding with bitterness. She heard it and she couldn't take it back and part of her didn't want to.

"No." He didn't flinch. His back was against the wall and his hands were still shaking and his voice was the flat, levelled thing it had become. "Nothing about this is supposed to make you feel better. I'm telling you what happened because you asked what I remember and I'm not going to lie to you in this room."

"You did the math." She said it back to him like a stone she was turning over, looking at from different angles. "You stood against that wall and you calculated whether you could intervene and the numbers didn't work and so you watched."

"I listened. I turned my face away and I listened. It was worse."

"It was worse for you. Listening."

Something crossed his face. "That's not what I—"

"Because I want to be very clear about what it was like for me." The dissociation was gone now. What was in its place was older and simpler and it ran under her ribs like current. "I was on this floor. I was seventeen. A woman I'd never met held me down and carved a word into my arm and I screamed so hard I tore something in my throat and I looked at you — the only person in this room close to my age, the boy I'd gone to school with for six years — and you turned away. You did the math and you turned away and I was alone on this floor."

He pressed his shoulders harder against the wall. His eyes were fixed on her and they were wet and he wasn't blinking. "Yes."

"And I have to live with that. I have to walk through this house — your house, the house where that happened — and I have to work with you and eat with you and sit across from you in the library and I have to carry that. Every day. The knowledge that you were in this room and you turned away."

"I know."

"You don't know." It came out harder than she meant. "You don't — you know your version. You know the wall and the math and the decision. You don't know what the floor felt like. You don't know what the marble sounded like from down there, or what her breath smelled like, or what it's like to be held still while someone writes on you. You know you were in this room and I know I was in this room and those are not the same room."

The silence that followed was not the silence they'd shared in the library after the Mark memory. That silence had been connective — the quiet of two people inside the same pain. This silence was the opposite. This was the silence of two people discovering that their versions of the same event were not reconcilable, that the room they'd shared at seventeen had been two rooms all along, and the distance between wall and floor was not a distance that could be closed by standing in the same space seven years later.

Malfoy slid down the wall. Not dramatically — his legs went out from under him in a slow controlled folding, knees bending, back sliding against the stone, until he was sitting on the marble with his arms on his knees. He was sitting on the floor. The same floor. He put his head in his hands and she watched his shoulders move and she felt nothing, or she felt everything and couldn't sort it, and the resonance between them was doing something new and unbearable — transmitting his grief into her awareness while she was trying to sustain her anger, mixing the channels, making it impossible to feel only one thing at a time.

"I know it's not the same," he said from behind his hands. His voice was muffled. "I've never thought it was the same."

She looked at him on the floor. The boy from the wall, seven years later, on the floor. It was not enough that he was sitting there. It was not an adequate gesture. But she recognized what it was — not performance, not strategy, not the Malfoy precision she'd learned to expect. His legs had given out and he was on the floor because the floor was where he'd ended up and he hadn't tried to make it look like anything.

She didn't go to him. She couldn't. The anger was still there, and the grief, and the ugly unkind thing she'd said about it being worse for him was still in the room and she wasn't sure she was wrong.

"I testified for you because it was right," she said. Her voice was rough. She'd been talking too much or too loudly in a room that ate sound and her throat was raw with the effort. "I testified because you were a child and the system was going to destroy you for choices you didn't fully make. I believed that. I still believe it. But I need you to understand that believing it doesn't mean I've forgiven it, and forgiving it — if I ever do — won't mean it didn't happen, and the fact that it happened here, in this house, in this room, in the house I'm now — in the house where I—"

She ran out. The sentence was too big and too honest and she couldn't finish it. The part she couldn't say was the part about the lamb and the library and his hands on the page and the way the Manor felt like something she could come home to, and how all of that existed simultaneously with this floor and this chandelier and this cold, and how she did not know how to hold both, and how she was furious that she had to try.

He raised his head. His face was wet. He didn't wipe it. He looked at her from the floor and his expression was the most undone she had ever seen him — not the careful mask, not the brittle formality, not even the rawness of the Mark memory. This was underneath all of that. This was the version of Draco Malfoy who had stood against this wall at sixteen and listened to a girl scream and done the math and turned away and had never, in seven years, found a position from which to look at it straight.

"I should have done something," he said. "The math didn't matter. I should have tried."

"You'd have died."

"Maybe."

"You'd have died and it wouldn't have stopped her. She'd have killed you and finished the word and it would have been the same except you'd be dead."

"Then I should be dead." He said it flat. Not theatrical, not self-pitying. The voice of someone who had done the long division on his own survival and come up short. "If the choices were watch or die trying, then I chose wrong."

"That's not—" She stopped. Breathed. The marble cold was in her feet, climbing her shins. "That's not what I'm saying. I'm not saying you should have died. I would never — Malfoy, I testified—"

"I know you testified. You keep saying that like it settles something."

The words landed and she felt them land — not as an attack but as a truth she didn't want. She did keep saying it. She kept reaching for the testimony the way she reached for data, for evidence, for the solid ground of something she could prove. I testified for you. I believed in your defense. As if saying so were a shield against the uglier, less defensible feeling underneath, which was: I testified for you and you were in this room and I was on this floor and believing you were a victim too did not change the fact that you were standing and I was not.

"I don't know how to do this," she said. She was not talking about the conversation. She was talking about all of it — the Manor, the research, the Mark, the lamb, the library, the growing thing between them that she could not name and could not verify and could not, apparently, stop. "I don't know how to be in this house and know this room exists. I don't know how to look at you and not see the wall."

He didn't answer. He sat on the floor with his back against the wall and his arms on his knees and his face wet and he didn't answer because there was no answer, because the room was the room and the floor was the floor and they had been in it together seven years ago and they were in it together now and both times the distance between them was the same unbridgeable measure.

The chandelier above them held its pale light. The marble floor was cold and clean. The room kept everything.

She waited for something else. For him to stand, to argue, to offer the thing — apology, justification, the word that would make any of this navigable. He stayed on the floor. She stayed standing. The chandelier watched them both with its cold, repaired light, and the distance between standing and sitting was a different measure than the distance between standing and lying down, but it was still a distance, and she didn't know how to cross it and didn't know if she wanted to.

She walked out.

She didn't look back. She crossed the threshold from marble to flagstone and the corridor's ordinary temperature hit her like bathwater, warm after the drawing room's preserved cold, and she kept walking. Down the corridor, past the portraits, past the flagstone with the crack. The crack she'd used as a landmark for three weeks, the small domestic familiarity of knowing a floor, and the floor she'd just been standing on was also a floor she knew and the knowledge was different and the difference was the whole problem.

She heard the door close behind her — the Manor closing the room or Malfoy closing it or the room closing itself, and she didn't turn to see which.

The route back to the guest wing was the same route it had been two hours ago, when she'd left the library thinking about the milk in her tea. The portraits were the same. The turns were the same. The Manor had not rearranged anything. It had not needed to. It had already done the only rearrangement that mattered.

In the guest wing, in her room, she sat on the bed and held her left forearm in her right hand. The scar was throbbing under the glamour. She could feel each letter, a hot pulse that followed the sequence — M-U-D-B-L-O-O-D — as if the wound were reciting itself. She pressed her thumb against the inside of her wrist, hard, over the start of the M, and held it there until the throbbing compressed into a single point of pressure, sharp and specific, and then she let go and the throbbing spread back along the letters like water filling a channel.

The teacup from earlier was on her bedside table. She'd carried it up from the library two nights ago and hadn't brought it back down. The milk residue had dried to a skin on the inside. He'd made her that cup of tea. He'd known the milk. He'd sat across from her in the warm library and winced when her neck popped and made a dry joke about straight lines and cooked her overcooked lamb and none of it, none of it, cancelled the drawing room. She wanted it to. She wanted the ordinary hours to weigh more than the worst one. She wanted the arithmetic to come out differently and it didn't, and wanting it to was not the same as it being so, and she was so tired of knowing the difference.

The Manor was quiet around her. No shifting corridors, no adjusting temperature, no surfaces tending to her needs. The house was still, and whether the stillness was guilt or indifference or the simple mechanical absence of the conditions that made it move, she couldn't tell.

Through the resonance, faint and bruised, she felt Malfoy two floors below. Still in the drawing room. Still on the floor.

She pulled the blanket over her shoulders and sat in the dark and did not sleep.
