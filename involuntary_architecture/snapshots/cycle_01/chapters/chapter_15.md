# Chapter 15

The Levett manuscript was proving resistant.

They'd been at it since dinner — or since what had passed for dinner, which was tea and the remnants of a bread-and-cheese plate that the house-elves left in the library around seven each evening. Hermione had eaten most of the cheese without noticing. Malfoy had eaten the bread. They'd divided the meal without discussing it, the way they divided most things now: by habit, by proximity, by whatever silent negotiation had replaced the careful formality of their first weeks.

The text was a construction manual for anchoring infrastructure, written in the fourteenth century by a Malfoy who had either invented a personal notation system or been actively hostile to future readers. Possibly both. The marginal annotations were in three languages and at least two different hands, and the primary text used runic modifiers that neither of them had encountered independently but that, when she cross-referenced her theoretical framework with his family knowledge of the symbols, resolved into something almost legible.

Almost. The current passage had been refusing resolution for forty minutes.

"It's not 'binding,'" she said, frowning at the glyph cluster at the center of the page. "The radical is wrong. It's more like — threading? Weaving through?"

"Plaiting," he said. He was sitting close enough that his shoulder was near hers when they both leaned over the text, which was often. She could smell the cedar of his soap and the undertone of something else — ink, or the specific mineral tang that clung to everything in the deep archive, the preservation charms layered so thick down there the air tasted like chalk. He'd been writing in the margins of their working translation all evening, his handwriting getting smaller and denser as the hours passed. "The secondary radical modifies it. It's a textile metaphor. My great-great-however-many-times grandmother was apparently a weaver before she married into the name."

"How do you know that?"

"There's a tapestry. Third floor. She wove it herself, and my mother made sure I knew, because—" He stopped. The rest of the sentence would have been something about Narcissa's opinions on Malfoy women's contributions to the family legacy, and he seemed to decide it was too much to offer at nearly midnight. "It's a textile metaphor."

She looked at the glyph again. Plaiting. The anchoring magic was described as threads drawn through a substrate — the land itself, the ambient field — and plaited into a structure that held. Not bound, not hammered. Woven. The image made the decay suddenly more visceral: not a wall crumbling but a fabric unraveling, threads pulling loose one by one.

"That works," she said. "That actually works. Look — if you read it as plaiting, then this passage here—" She turned the page and found the corresponding section, her finger tracing the line of glyphs until they resolved. "The whole anchoring system is a textile structure. The ley-line connections aren't channels, they're threads. And war damage would be—"

"Cuts," he said. "Tears in the fabric."

"Which means the Ministry's been trying to mend a tapestry by building scaffolding around it. No wonder nothing's held."

"Mm." He pulled a second text toward him — a later manuscript, this one in Latin with French glosses. The page he wanted was bookmarked with a strip of parchment in his handwriting from two days ago. She watched him scan the Latin without thinking about it; his reading speed in Latin was faster than hers, one of the few areas where the pureblood education outpaced hers, and she had stopped resenting this and started relying on it sometime in the third week. "This passage references a repair method. Rethreading. Not patching — actually running new thread through the existing warp."

"You'd need access to the original structure. The loom, essentially."

"Which would be the anchoring points themselves."

"Which are—"

"Under the house. Among other places."

They looked at each other. The implication settled between them the way implications had been settling between them for weeks — with weight, with the quiet understanding that what they were finding in this library was going to matter beyond the two of them and this room.

"We should map them," she said.

"Mm." He pushed his chair back slightly, not standing but stretching his spine. His neck cracked. "Not tonight."

"No. Not tonight."

They worked on the maintenance protocol for another half hour. Malfoy found a cross-reference in the Latin manuscript — a passage describing the location of what the text called *nodi*, knot-points where the plaited threads converged — and she pulled the Levett folio back toward her to check whether the construction manual corroborated the locations. It did, approximately. The notation was different enough that the correspondence wasn't exact, and they spent fifteen minutes arguing about whether a particular glyph indicated compass direction or depth below surface. He was wrong, she was almost certain, but he made his case well enough that she wrote both interpretations in the margin and bracketed them.

"We'll need the estate maps," she said. "The old ones. Pre-war."

"Second gallery. Third shelf from the end. There's a case with the surveyor's records going back to the sixteenth century." He paused. "I think. I haven't opened it since I was twelve."

"What were you doing with surveyor's records at twelve?"

"Hiding from a dinner party."

She almost laughed. The image was specific enough to be true: a twelve-year-old Malfoy, bored and trapped, opening a case of old maps in the upper gallery while his parents entertained downstairs. She could see the boy clearly and the seeing was dangerous in the way all these moments were dangerous — the small, unintended disclosures that accumulated between them like sediment, building a picture of a person she hadn't meant to know this well.

He got up to make tea. She heard him at the side table where the kettle sat, the small sounds of the preparation — the tap of the caddy, the pour of water, the clink of the spoon — and she kept her eyes on the manuscript and listened to him the way she listened to everything he did when he wasn't watching: with an attention she did not want to name.

He set a cup beside her elbow. She didn't look up. "Thanks."

"Mm."

The tea was right. It had been right for weeks — the amount of milk, the brewing time, the specific way she took it — and he had never asked. He'd simply observed and adjusted until the cup he made her matched the cup she made herself, and the care in that, the quiet precision of it, was the kind of thing that could undo a person if she let it.

She drank. He sat back down. The work continued.

The library at this hour had a quality she'd come to think of as its real self — not the grand sunlit room she'd first catalogued in early November, but this smaller, darker version with the lamplight making islands on the desk and the shelves drawn close and the silence not empty but populated, as if the books themselves settled differently when the people reading them stopped pretending to be professional about it. The upper galleries were dark. The windows showed nothing but the reflection of the room and, behind it, the black of a winter night without a moon.

He rubbed the back of his neck with one hand and she watched the gesture — the long fingers, the wrist where the Mark sat under his rolled sleeve. The lamplight caught the edge of the Mark and she looked away. She knew it by touch better than by sight anyway. She knew, from the resonance, that the Mark was quiet tonight. She could feel it the way she felt the temperature of the room: a low-level awareness, not quite pain, just the fact of his body's condition seeping into hers like damp through stone.

She knew what she knew. That was the problem.

The bonding texts she'd found in the deeper archive — four weeks ago, or close to it; she'd lost the exact date, which bothered her in a way she didn't have time to examine — described this sensation with clinical precision. The resonance. The growing inability to distinguish between your own physical state and the other person's. The Manor's architecture doing what it was designed to do: amplifying what existed between its inhabitants, creating conditions for connection, gating access behind the strength of the bond. She had read the texts twice and understood the mechanism and she had not told him. Every evening since, they sat like this, close over a manuscript, their elbows almost touching, and the knowledge sat in her like swallowed glass — present always, cutting only when she moved wrong.

"You've gone quiet," he said.

"I'm thinking about loom mechanics."

"You're a terrible liar."

"I'm an excellent liar, actually." She said it before she thought about it, and the truth of it — the weight-bearing truth, given what she was concealing — made her hand tighten on the pen she was holding until the barrel dug into her fingers. She looked back at the manuscript. "I'm thinking about how you'd access an anchoring point that's been buried under a house for six hundred years without destabilizing the house."

He didn't press. He rarely did. It was one of the things about him that made the silence between them bearable: he asked once, accepted the deflection, and let the question exist unanswered without making it a wound. Other people — Harry, Ron in the years before — would have circled back. Malfoy let things sit, and the absence of pressure was its own kind of closeness. He trusted her to speak when she was ready. He was wrong to trust her about this specific silence, but he didn't know that.

The library had settled into its night configuration — quieter, the shelves drawn closer, the shadows deeper in the upper galleries where the lamplight didn't reach. The fire in the grate had burned low and neither of them had moved to rebuild it because the Manor itself was warm, had been warm for days now, the stone walls holding heat with the steady generosity of a house that wanted its inhabitants comfortable. Wanted them here. Outside, December was doing what December did in Wiltshire: frost on the grounds, darkness since four o'clock, the wind finding the old window joins and making a thin sound that Hermione had come to associate with these late working hours, this specific quality of time.

She turned a page in the Levett manuscript. Her handwriting in the margins had gotten worse over the weeks — faster, less careful, abbreviated in ways that only made sense if you'd been following the work from the beginning. His annotations were neater but increasingly dense, and she'd started reading his marginal notes the way she read the primary text itself: as a record of a mind working on the page, showing its reasoning, trusting her to follow.

She reached for her tea and found it cold. The cup had been sitting there for an hour at least. She drank it anyway — cold tea was still tea, and the bitterness was clarifying.

"This section," he said. He angled the text toward her with one hand, his index finger under a line of archaic notation. "I think this is a maintenance protocol. The language shifts — imperative, not descriptive. Instructions rather than theory."

She leaned in. His finger was on the page and her gaze followed it and the notation was dense, the glyphs small, and she tilted her head to read them and his face was right there. Closer than it should have been, closer than the text required. The lamplight was on his jaw and the shadow of his eyelashes on his cheek and the notation she was supposed to be reading had become entirely illegible because he had gone very still.

He was looking at her. Not at the page. His finger was still under the line of text but his eyes were on her face, and the expression in them was something she had been pretending not to see for weeks — the unguarded thing that appeared when he forgot to be guarded, the look that was neither careful nor dry nor precise but simply, nakedly, want.

She should have leaned back. She should have said something about the notation, about the hour, about anything that would have rebuilt the distance they'd been maintaining like a bandage over a wound neither of them wanted to examine. She knew what the Manor did. She knew what the resonance was. She had a month of concealed knowledge telling her that every moment of closeness was suspect, that the warmth she felt might be architecture rather than desire, that the pull between them could be the house's amplification rather than the simple, devastating fact of wanting someone.

She didn't lean back.

His mouth was slightly open. She could see the small dry place on his lower lip where he bit it when he was concentrating, a detail she had catalogued weeks ago without examining why she was cataloguing it, and his breath came shallow and she could hear it because the library was that quiet, the fire that low, the world outside that dark.

"Granger," he said, and his voice was quieter than usual, slightly rough, and the roughness was not from talking — they'd been mostly silent for the past hour. The roughness was something else.

"Yeah."

"I'm not—" He stopped. The self-correction, the retreat from honesty that was so characteristic she could have predicted the pause before it arrived. Except this time there was nowhere to retreat to because she was right there, her face inches from his, the page between them the only barrier and neither of them looking at the page. "Never mind."

She put her hand on his.

On the manuscript page, his finger still under the line of archaic text, and her hand covered his and neither of them breathed. His hand was warm — warmer than she expected, warmer than her own fingers that had been turning stiff manuscript pages for hours. His knuckles under her palm. The texture of his skin, dry from the chalk-dust of preservation charms that coated everything in this library. The slight catch of his breath, audible because the room was that quiet.

He turned his hand under hers. Not pulling away — turning it over, so his palm met hers and his fingers closed around hers and the manuscript page crumpled under their combined weight and neither of them cared about the fourteenth-century parchment they were ruining.

"I don't know what I'm doing," she said. Her voice was not the voice she used for research, for arguments, for the precise and measured delivery that had organized her entire adult life. It was smaller than that. "I don't — I should—"

He kissed her.

It was not smooth. His mouth found hers at a wrong angle and she felt the bump of his teeth against her lip and the startled inhale through his nose and his hand tightened on hers and his other hand came up and hovered near her jaw without quite landing, as though he was waiting for permission or for his own nerve to catch up with his body. She made a sound — not a word, something pulled from the center of her chest — and she leaned into it and the angle corrected itself and his mouth was on hers properly and it was clumsy, it was genuinely clumsy, his nose bumped hers and she could feel the tension in his jaw under her palm when her free hand came up without her deciding it should and the whole thing was nothing like what she'd imagined in the sleepless hours when she let herself imagine.

She'd imagined composure. She'd imagined his precision extending to this. It didn't. His hand finally landed on her jaw, too firm, his fingers pressing into the soft skin below her ear with a grip that was closer to holding on than to tenderness, and she didn't mind — she actively didn't mind, the force of it was the most honest thing she'd felt in months, his body telling her something his speech never would. Her free hand went to his shirt — not unbuttoning, just holding, the fabric bunched in her fist against his chest, and she could feel his heartbeat through the cotton and it was fast, hammering, and the resonance between them —

The resonance. The thing she could feel along the frequency of the Mark, the connection that ran between them like a wire pulled taut, and it was more now, not louder but more total, the awareness of him flooding through her like water through a split in a dam. She could feel his heartbeat in her own chest. She could feel the adrenaline in his blood as though it were hers. The Mark on his forearm was doing something — not hurting, not for either of them, but resonating, vibrating at a pitch that matched the frequency of her own pulse, and the sensation was the most intimate thing she had ever felt and the thing she trusted least in the world.

*Is this me?* The thought came like a fracture in the surface of the moment. *Is this me or is this the house?*

She kissed him harder. That was her answer — not an answer, a refusal to let the doubt have the moment when the moment was already happening. His hand slid from her jaw to the back of her neck, into her hair, which had come loose from its tie hours ago, and his fingers tangled there and she felt the tug at her scalp and her body responded before her mind could categorize the response. She opened her mouth against his and tasted tea and the faint mineral air of the library and beneath that something that was just him, particular and impossible to attribute to architecture.

He pulled her closer. The desk was between them and the motion was graceless — a stack of reference texts toppled sideways, one hit the floor with a flat heavy sound that should have broken the moment but didn't. She was half out of her chair, her hip jammed against the desk's edge, his hand at the back of her neck and her hand still fisted in his shirt and their joined hands still on the manuscript, fingers interlaced over the Levett folio's ruined page about maintenance protocols for magic that had been woven into the English earth six centuries ago.

She could feel the doubt and the want occupying the same space in her body, like two instruments playing different melodies in the same key. The want was specific: his mouth, his hands, the way his breath came short and warm against her upper lip when they broke apart for a half-second and came back together. The doubt was equally specific: the resonance was real, the amplification was real, the house around them was warm as a held breath and she could not, even now, even with his mouth on hers, separate what she felt from what the Manor was helping her feel.

She pulled back. Not far. An inch. His forehead rested against hers. His breathing was ragged and his eyes were closed and the hand in her hair had gone still.

"Is this—" he started.

"Don't," she said. She wasn't sure what she was refusing — the question, the doubt, the conversation that would follow if either of them tried to name what had just happened. "Don't. Not yet."

She kissed him again. Slower this time. His mouth was softer when he wasn't startled by it, and he kissed her back with a deliberateness that was nothing like the careful restraint he'd maintained in every other interaction since she'd arrived at the Manor. This was deliberate in the other direction — choosing to be present, choosing to be thorough. His lips against hers and then against the corner of her mouth and then her jaw, and she tilted her head and felt his breath against her neck and the sensation traveled the length of her spine and she thought *I know what the house does and I am here anyway and I don't know what that makes me*.

He said her name against her neck. "Granger." Not her first name — the surname they'd maintained like a last piece of formality, except his mouth was on her throat and the name was a vibration against her skin and it sounded nothing like distance.

She let go of his shirt. Moved her hand to his face, his jaw, the angular line of it under her palm. He was thinner than anyone she'd touched in years — Ron had been solid, broad, a comfort of scale — and the difference was not better or worse, it was simply this body, this specific person, the faint scratch where he hadn't shaved since morning. She ran her thumb along the hinge of his jaw and he turned into the touch, eyes still closed, and the gesture was so unguarded that she felt something in her chest fold in on itself — not pain, not quite tenderness, something closer to the shock of seeing an animal let itself be handled after months of flinching.

They stayed like that. His face against her neck, her hand on his jaw, the desk still between them and the library around them and the fire almost dead and the lamp guttering to its last half-inch of oil. The Manor, warm and present around them like a third body in the room. She could feel the house's response the way you feel a pressure change before a storm: not as a thought or a sound but as a shift in the quality of the air, a deepening of the warmth that was already too warm, the stone walls holding heat with an enthusiasm that had nothing to do with weather.

When they separated — fully, leaning back into their own chairs — the absence of his hands felt like the removal of something structural. The air between them was cooler by comparison. He was looking at her with an expression she'd never seen on his face: open, stunned, the dryness and the precision stripped away, and underneath it a fear that was recognizable because it matched her own.

"I should go," she said.

"Yeah." He ran his hand through his hair and it stood up wrong and he looked, for a moment, like somebody she hadn't met yet — not the boy who'd watched her scream on his drawing room floor, not the careful man who weighed every word, but someone between those, newer, still forming. "Yeah."

She gathered her notes. The motion was automatic — straightening papers, closing the manuscript, recapping the inkwell. Her hands were shaking. She didn't try to stop it. He watched her tidy the desk and said nothing about the shaking and the kindness of that silence, the deliberate not-noticing, sat in her throat.

"Tomorrow," she said. "The anchoring points. We should map them."

"Tomorrow."

She stood. He stood. The library settled around them with a sound like an exhalation — the old wood, the old stone, the old magic in the walls doing whatever it was the magic did when the people inside the walls did what it wanted them to do.

"Goodnight, Granger."

"Goodnight."

She left the library. The corridor outside was lit by wall sconces that burned low, the flames dipping as she passed and then rising again behind her, as if the house were tracking her passage through its body. The stone floor was warm under her shoes. It shouldn't have been — stone floors in December should have been cold, should have made her hurry — but the Manor had been heating its floors for weeks, for her, and she'd stopped being startled by it. That was the worst part: how quickly the abnormal had become baseline. How easily she'd adapted to living inside something that was adapting to her.

The corridor to the guest wing was shorter than she remembered. It had been this way since she'd returned after the weeks away — the house compressing the distance between the library and her room, trimming the walk from five minutes to three as if it couldn't bear for her to be in transit any longer than necessary. She hated that she noticed it. She hated more that the shortened walk felt like consideration rather than manipulation, the same way a person might walk you to a closer door because they could see you were tired.

She was tired. She was also vibrating at a frequency that was incompatible with sleep, her lips still tender from the pressure of his mouth and her hand still holding the ghost of his hand and her body carrying the imprint of the last hour like a photograph developing in reverse — the image getting sharper, not fainter, with every step she took away from him.

---

Her room was warm.

She stood in the doorway and felt it on her face. The fireplace was lit — she hadn't left it lit. The bedcovers had been turned down and the lamp beside the bed was on at its lowest setting and the room smelled faintly of lavender, which it had never smelled of before, and the whole arrangement was so pointed, so blatantly tending, that she pressed her hand against the doorframe and stood there for ten seconds fighting the urge to laugh or to scream or both.

She closed the door. Locked it with her wand, then with her hand, the deadbolt a physical reassurance that a locked door still meant something in a house that rearranged its own corridors at will.

She sat on the bed. Pressed her hands to her face and breathed.

The resonance was still there. Quieter now, attenuated by the distance between them, but steady, humming along the connection like a plucked string losing amplitude in slow degrees. She could feel him — not his thoughts, never his thoughts, but the register of his wakefulness, the pace of his heart closer to normal now but not normal, the fact that he was still in the library standing where she'd left him and he was awake and his body was running the same circuit of shock and want and fear that hers was, and she knew all of this because the house had built a wire between them and the wire carried everything.

She pulled off her shoes and dropped them on the floor. Her feet ached in the specific way feet ache after a long day of not moving — not exertion but stiffness, the body's complaint at being held still too long. She pulled her jumper over her head and sat in her vest top and jeans, her arms exposed to the warm air. She looked at her left forearm. The glamour was intact; it held better when she wasn't casting. Underneath it the scar was there, the letters she'd carried for seven years, the word carved into her by a dead woman in the room downstairs. Malfoy had traced those letters with his thumb three weeks ago and tonight she had kissed him in his library and neither of them had mentioned the scar. She didn't know if that meant they were past it or if it meant they were building something on top of it without looking down.

She lay back on the bed. The mattress held her with the particular generosity the Manor extended to her sleeping arrangements — not a generic comfort but a specific, attentive one, adjusting to her weight and her position in a way that better mattresses simply did not do. The sheets warmed under her body heat within seconds.

She should have been thinking about the research. The plaiting model changed everything — the repair methodology, the approach to the anchoring points, the entire framework the Ministry had been using was wrong. She had the shape of a solution for the first time since she'd arrived at the Manor. It was the kind of breakthrough that would normally have kept her up for hours with excitement, filling parchment, drawing diagrams, building the argument.

Instead she was lying on her back staring at the ceiling with her fingers pressed to her mouth and the taste of him still on her lips.

The bonding texts had been specific. Not metaphorical, not oblique in the way of most Dark Arts theory. They described the mechanism in the neutral, instructional language of builders, of craftsmen recording process for future generations: the Manor reads the emotional frequency between its inhabitants and amplifies it. The amplification is not invention. The house cannot fabricate feeling from nothing. But it can take a thread and thicken it, a warmth and deepen it, a pull and strengthen it, until the inhabitants cannot distinguish the organic signal from the architectural one. It can make the proximity more electric. It can make the distance more painful. It can make the quiet evenings in the library feel like the only real hours in a person's day, and the accidental brush of a hand feel like being touched for the first time.

This was the problem. Not that the Manor was lying to her — it might not be lying at all. The feelings might be entirely real. She thought they were real. In her worse moments, in the small hours when the analytical framework crumbled and she was just a woman lying in a bed in a house that knew her too well, she was certain they were real. But they had been grown in a greenhouse. She would never know what size they'd have reached in open air.

She had kissed a man tonight. The kiss had been clumsy and desperate and specific and it had tasted like cold tea and chalk-dust and his particular skin and none of those things could be faked by a house, surely, a building could not manufacture the way his teeth had bumped her lip or the way his hand had shaken when it found her jaw. But the intensity — the need — the sense that the distance between them had become physically unbearable, that not kissing him was a kind of pain — was that hers? Was the volume of the want hers, or was the house turning the dial?

She thought about the way he had looked at her in the seconds before. The unguarded expression that he couldn't hold for long, that appeared in flashes and then disappeared behind the dryness and the precision the way the sun disappears behind fast-moving clouds. He had been looking at her with an honesty that cost him something — she could see the cost, could feel it through the resonance, the vulnerability of wanting something when your entire post-war life had been organized around the practice of not wanting.

He didn't know what the house was doing. He didn't know about the texts. He had kissed her believing that whatever was between them was between them, and the difference between his belief and her knowledge was the specific, unbridgeable distance she could not bring herself to close.

She rolled onto her side and pulled the blanket over herself without getting under the covers properly, still in her jeans and her socks, too hollowed out to finish undressing. The pillow was cool against her cheek — the one surface in the room the Manor hadn't warmed — and she pressed her face into it and closed her eyes.

Behind her eyelids: his face in the lamplight, the manuscript between them, the moment before. The expression. The way he had started to say something and stopped. *I'm not—* What? Not what? She would never know what the end of that sentence was because she had put her hand on his before he could finish and now the sentence would stay incomplete, a piece of syntax permanently suspended between intention and utterance.

The resonance carried his state to her across two floors of stone. He was lying down now. Not sleeping — his heart rate was too fast for sleep, the pattern of his wakefulness too irregular. The Mark on his forearm was quiet, quieter than she'd felt it in weeks, the dark magic in it settled into something close to calm. As if the Mark, too, was satisfied. As if the curse-brand that Voldemort had burned into his skin at sixteen was responding to the fact that someone had touched him with her mouth and her hands and her willing body, and she couldn't tell if that was beautiful or horrifying and possibly it was both.

She lay still and the Manor breathed around her. She could feel it — not hear it, feel it, the way you feel the motion of water when you're in it. The house was settling into something she could only describe as satisfaction, if architecture could be satisfied. The warmth was its warmest since she'd arrived five weeks ago. The corridor outside her door was still and short. The walls gave off heat like sunned stone. The whole wing, the whole house, felt like a body curled around her in sleep, protective and encompassing and impossible to refuse without getting up and walking out the door.

She should tell him. Tomorrow. She should sit across from him in the library and open the bonding folio and say: I found these four weeks ago and I didn't tell you and I should have and here is what they say. The house has been amplifying everything between us. The resonance is part of its design. Every moment of closeness — the evenings in the library, the examinations, the way the archive only opens for us together — has been occurring under conditions I understood and you didn't. And tonight when I put my hand on yours and when you kissed me and when I kissed you back I did all of it carrying a knowledge I hadn't shared and you deserved to have.

She should tell him tomorrow.

She would not tell him tomorrow.

She knew this with the specific clarity that arrives at one in the morning after a kiss that has rearranged the architecture of your nervous system. She would not tell him because telling him would mean watching his face close — watching the openness collapse, watching him retreat behind the precision and the dryness and the careful, defended distance that had taken her five weeks to erode. She would not tell him because she had seen his face when it was unguarded and she could not make herself be the reason it closed again. She would not tell him because she was selfish in the way of a person who has been alone for a long time and has finally been handed something warm and cannot make herself put it down, even though she knows — she knows, she knows — that the warmth may not be entirely hers.

The fire crackled. The Manor held her. The lavender smell in the room was new and deliberate and almost certainly the house's idea of comfort and she breathed it in anyway because what else was she going to do, stop breathing.

She lay on her side in a warm room in a house that wanted her and she could feel, two floors up, the boy she had kissed lying awake in his own warm room, and the distance between them was the width of the house and the width of what she knew and the width of what she couldn't bring herself to say, and all three distances were exactly the same.

She did not sleep for a long time.
