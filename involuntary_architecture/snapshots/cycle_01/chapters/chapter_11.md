# Chapter 11

They had developed a routine for the examinations, and the routine itself was a kind of intimacy neither of them acknowledged.

She would set out her diagnostic instruments on the library's long table — the resonance crystals, the notation ledger, the stabilization framework she'd designed specifically for the Mark's magical signature. He would roll up his left sleeve and lay his forearm on the padded surface they'd improvised from a folded cloak, and then he would pick up whatever he was reading with his free hand, as if the examination were no more notable than having his pulse taken at a routine physical.

It hadn't always been like this. The first examinations had been rigid, formal — his jaw set, her instructions precise, both of them maintaining the clinical frame with an effort that was itself a kind of confession. Now he read during examinations. She hummed sometimes, without meaning to, when the diagnostic results were interesting. He'd told her once that the humming was how he knew she'd found something, and she'd been self-conscious about it for two days before deciding that self-consciousness about humming was its own pointless category of intimacy she didn't need.

This morning the library was doing its usual thing — fire lit, lamps adjusted to the grey December light, the room's configuration subtly rearranged since yesterday so that the working table sat closer to the hearth and the windows showed a slightly different angle of the grounds. Frost on the lawns. The last leaves gone from the elms. She'd stopped trying to catalogue the Manor's daily spatial adjustments. The changes were always minor, always oriented toward comfort, and paying too much attention to them meant thinking about why the house was so invested in their comfort, which was a question she'd been skirting since Narcissa's visit.

"Ready?" She placed her hands on either side of his forearm, fingers bracketing the Mark.

"Mm." He didn't look up from his book.

She cast the diagnostic. The spell settled over the Mark like a second skin, translucent, mapping the magical layers underneath. The top layer — Voldemort's binding spell, degraded, its network anchor dissolved — registered as a fading lattice, dark and intricate, losing coherence at the edges. Below that, the self-perpetuating Dark magic that fed on Draco's own core, parasitic and adaptive. And below everything, the layer she still couldn't classify — older than the casting, deeper than the curse, strengthening where everything above it was decaying.

"Third layer's shifted again," she said, adjusting her grip. "Hold still."

"I am still."

"You just —" She stopped. He hadn't moved. What she'd felt was his pulse, which had always been there under her thumb, and the diagnostic's sensitivity had increased to the point where his circulatory system registered as movement in her spell field. She was calibrated too finely. "Sorry. Never mind."

He glanced at her over the top of his book. A brief, assessing look — deciding whether to ask. He decided not to. Looked back down.

She pushed the diagnostic deeper. The Mark's magic resisted at the second-layer boundary — it always resisted, the Dark magic protective of its own infrastructure — and then the resistance gave way in the specific reluctant unfurling she'd come to recognize. Like a fist releasing one finger at a time. The deeper layers exposed themselves to her diagnostic, and she mapped them with the precision that was the one thing about these examinations she still fully trusted.

The base layer was stronger this week. She noted it in the ledger without commentary, recording the measurement alongside the previous week's readings. A clear upward trend. The unidentified magic at the bottom of the Mark was growing, and its growth correlated with — she checked the dates — every sustained examination session. Every hour of her diagnostic contact with his arm.

She didn't write that correlation in the ledger.

"I'm going to probe the base layer directly," she said. "You might feel pressure."

"I always feel pressure. That's what the Mark does."

"Different pressure. This is the diagnostic interacting with the deeper —"

"Just do it, Granger."

He said it without irritation. It was the register they'd found after the drawing room — shorter with each other, less padded, but not unkind. She'd stopped noticing when "Granger" had shifted from a wall to something else. Something he used the way you'd use a name you'd gotten comfortable saying.

She pushed the diagnostic into the base layer.

The Mark opened.

And the room doubled.

She was in the library. She could see the table, her own hands, his forearm, the ledger, the fire. And simultaneously, overlaid like a transparency laid over a photograph, she was somewhere else — the same library, the same table, but from the other side. She was seeing the room from his angle. She could feel the book's edge where his right hand held it — the leather binding, slightly cracked, a ridge pressing into his middle finger. She could feel the padded surface under his forearm, warm from the hours of his body heat. She could feel the place where her thumb rested against his wrist and it was — the sensation of being touched, registered from his side, specific and present in a way that told her his attention had not been on the book for some time.

He was aware of her. Not as a diagnostician or a researcher or a colleague. Aware of her the way a body is aware of another body in close proximity — the weight of her, the heat of her, the ink on her fingers and the wool of the jumper she was wearing. From inside his experience, she understood that he'd been letting these details accumulate — not studying them, nothing so deliberate, but the way a room gathers the scent of whatever's been burning in its hearth. Slowly. Without having decided to.

And underneath that — she almost lost it, almost couldn't hold the doubled awareness, but for one bright terrible second she felt his awareness of the Mark under her hand. Not the pain of it, though the pain was there, a constant low-grade burn he'd stopped bothering to register. The shame. The old sick shame of the thing branded into his arm, and laid against it, pressing down on it like a hand on a wound, the precise relief of her touch. How the Mark quieted when she touched it. How he'd come to associate the absence of pain with the specific pressure of her fingers. How the two had become, in his body, the same thing — her presence and the absence of hurt — tangled together below the level of language, where the body kept its own accounts.

Three seconds. Maybe five.

She pulled her hand off his arm as if the Mark had burned her.

The library snapped back to a single perspective. Her own. The fire, the table, the frost outside. Her hands in her lap, the tips of her fingers buzzing with residual sensation — not pain, but the disorientation of having been in two places at once and now being in only one.

Draco put the book down. The studied casualness was gone. He was looking at her and his face had the expression she'd come to associate with his most careful management — controlled, attentive, a slight tension at the corners of his mouth that meant he was working to keep something off his face.

"What just happened."

Not a question. Or a question that had already arrived at its own answer and was only asking for confirmation.

"Resonance feedback," she said. Her voice was even. She was good at even. "The diagnostic created a sympathetic channel — it happens with sustained —"

"You flinched."

"I didn't —"

"You pulled your hand away like I'd burned you. That wasn't resonance feedback. What happened?"

She looked at him. His grey eyes, lighter near the fire. The sharp angles of his face, the jaw she'd watched soften over weeks of meals he no longer ate alone. His forearm on the table, the Mark exposed, black against pale skin, and the memory of what it had felt like to be inside his experience — the accumulated attention, the specific quiet want that he kept pressed down so far inside himself that she wasn't sure he fully recognized it as want.

She could not tell him what she'd felt. Not because it was clinical data she needed to process, but because what she'd experienced from inside his body was something private he had not chosen to show her, and she had no right to it. The shame and the relief — those were his. The way her touch had become synonymous with the absence of pain in whatever subconscious ledger his body was keeping — that was his, and he didn't know she'd read it, and telling him would be a violation she couldn't dress up as science.

"The resonance is bidirectional now," she said. "That's new. I could feel sensory input from your — from your perspective. Temperature. Pressure. It lasted a few seconds."

"What did you feel?"

The question was quiet. He asked it the way he asked things that mattered to him — directly, without the usual deflection, the effort of it visible in the stillness of his hands on the table.

"Peripheral sensation," she said. "Your hand on the book. The table surface. It was disorienting, that's all. I wasn't expecting it."

He looked at her for a long moment. She could feel him deciding whether to push — she could actually feel it, the faint deliberation that the resonance was now broadcasting whether she wanted to receive it or not — and she watched him decide to let it go. The decision cost him something. She could feel that too.

"If the channel's opening both ways," he said, "you should probably note that in the — whatever it is you're recording."

"The diagnostic ledger. Yes."

"Right." He picked up his book. Put it down again. "Should we — is it safe to continue the examinations, if the resonance is —"

"It's manageable."

"You just flinched hard enough to —"

"It's manageable, Malfoy. I was surprised. I won't be next time."

He looked at her. She looked back. The fire cracked and resettled and the sound was loud in the library's quiet.

"Okay," he said. And then, after a pause that almost wasn't there: "If this is getting worse, we can — I'll figure something else out."

"I know." She started packing the diagnostic instruments. The crystals went into their velvet-lined case, the stabilization framework collapsed into its compact form. Her hands had stopped buzzing. "I know."

She didn't say: the problem is that I want to continue, and I can't tell anymore whether the wanting is mine.

---

She spent the rest of the afternoon in the main library while Draco worked in the gated archive. They'd discovered that the archive room would hold for him alone now in short stretches — an hour, sometimes two, the walls going vague at the edges as if the room were a held breath slowly releasing. She'd been grateful for the intervals apart, though she wouldn't have used that word. The separation let the resonance dim to a low background hum — his location in the house, his wakefulness, the general texture of his attention — manageable, something she could nearly fold into the peripheral awareness where it didn't require examination.

Today the hum wouldn't settle. Three rooms away and a floor above, she could feel him in the archive with a clarity that was new. Not just the fact of his presence but the quality of his concentration — the focused steadiness when a text engaged him, a slight cooler shift when something confused or frustrated him, a thing she could only describe as brightness when he found a connection, a pattern, an answer.

She put down her quill and sat with it. She was carrying another person's cognitive weather inside her body. This was not resonance echo. This was not a known artifact of diagnostic contact with Dark objects. The literature she'd cited to herself for weeks described fading echoes, not amplification. Not this slow steady deepening where each examination left the channel wider, more detailed, more precise.

She couldn't ignore what the resonance had become. She was holding the peripheral awareness of another person inside her body, and the other person was beginning to hold hers, and neither of them had consented to this and neither of them was stopping it. The examinations continued because the research required them. The research required them because the Mark was degrading in ways that needed monitoring. The monitoring required her hands on his arm, which required proximity, which deepened the resonance, which made the next examination more intimate, which — she could see the spiral. She'd been able to see it for weeks. She kept choosing to step onto it anyway.

That was the part she really couldn't attribute to diagnostic artifact.

Work. The folios on the table in front of her were from the lower shelves of the main library — not the gated archive, which required his presence, but the general collection. Lesser texts. Malfoy family administrative records from the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries. Property surveys. Warding logs. Correspondence about ley-line routing with the Wizarding Infrastructure Authority, which had apparently existed in some form since the 1600s and was just as bureaucratically constipated then as its modern descendant.

She'd been working through these for a week, building a baseline picture of the Manor's magical engineering from the ground up. Foundation spells. Warding architecture. The structural magic that kept the house standing for centuries and maintained the ambient enchantments — heating, preservation, wards, and whatever else the house was doing — all functioning simultaneously. Genuinely useful work. Genuinely boring. The combination she found most restful because useful meant it advanced the research and boring meant it didn't engage the parts of her mind that had been circling the question she couldn't answer.

She was three folios deep in a surveyor's report on ley-line connections — dated 1743, meticulous, the magical notation standard for the period — when the text changed.

Not abruptly. The surveyor had been noting anomalies in the Manor's warding structure, places where the standard protective enchantments intersected with something older and more complex. A marginal annotation, in a different hand: *see Binding Folio, shelf 14-C, re: the resonance architecture and its maintenance.* Cross-referenced to a catalogue number that didn't match the library's current system.

She followed the reference. Shelf 14-C was behind the property records, a lower section she'd catalogued but hadn't fully examined — the folios there had been filed under Estate Administration, and she'd assumed they were more of the same. She pulled the volumes until she found one whose catalogue mark matched: a plain black folio, unadorned leather, the binding old enough that the stitching had been replaced at least twice. The kind of plain binding that usually signified either genuine modesty or deliberately hidden value.

She carried it back to the table, cleared a space between the surveyor's folios, and opened it.

The text was archaic magical notation — dense, semi-pictographic, the script that predated modern standard by several centuries. She could read it. Slowly. Word by word, building each sentence in her mind, the meaning assembling itself after the grammar like a photograph developing in solution.

The title page:

*On the Binding Architecture of the Manor and Its Proper Maintenance for the Perpetuation of the Line.*

She didn't close the folio. Her hands on the leather were steady. The quill she'd put down lay exactly where she'd left it, the ink drying on the nib. She read.

The first section described the Manor's purpose. Not as shelter. Not as fortress. As instrument. The house had been designed — the text used the word *wrought*, in a construction that implied magical engineering rather than physical construction — to sustain the Malfoy magical line across generations. The mechanism was bonding architecture: the house's magical infrastructure was built to read the emotional resonance between its inhabitants, to identify connections with potential for deep magical bonding, and to amplify those connections through environmental response.

She translated the next passage three times to be certain.

*The architecture shall respond to the resonance between inhabitants by adjusting its conditions to favor the bond's development. Warmth shall attend proximity. Cold shall attend separation. The deeper chambers shall open to those whose bond has achieved sufficient strength, that the work of the line may proceed under the most favorable influence. The resonance shall be measured through the quality and frequency of sustained contact, through the depth of emotional proximity, and through the willingness of both parties to remain within the architecture.*

The gated archive. The room that held only when both of them were present. The door that hadn't existed until the examination where his pulse was under her thumb and the Mark quieted under her touch and the diagnostic reached a layer of magic that shouldn't have been there.

She read on.

The bonding architecture worked on genuine emotional resonance. This was stated clearly, almost as a limitation — the house could not fabricate feeling from nothing. *Where no seed exists, the architecture shall find no purchase.* But where resonance existed, the house could amplify it. Temperature. Spatial configuration. Access gating. The comfort of proximity and the discomfort of distance — all of it calibrated by the house's magic, reading the emotional state of its inhabitants and adjusting conditions to encourage and deepen the bond.

A section on the amplification mechanism described the process in terms that were technical but legible: the architecture created feedback loops. Positive feelings between inhabitants were reflected back through the house's environmental response — warmth, comfort, open corridors, accessible rooms — which reinforced the positive feelings, which the house then amplified further. The feedback was designed to be invisible. The text used a phrase she translated as *seamless integration*: the amplified feelings should be indistinguishable from organic ones. The inhabitants should not be able to tell where their genuine resonance ended and the house's amplification began.

She pressed her palms flat against the table on either side of the folio and looked at the ceiling. The library ceiling was high, dark, the beams lost in shadow above the lamplight. She looked at it for a full minute before she could make herself look back down.

The section on channels of awareness was next. When inhabitants of the Manor were in the process of forming a deep bond, the architecture created mutual awareness between them — a channel through which each could sense the other's proximity, emotional state, and physical condition. The channels deepened with sustained contact. They were the house's monitoring system, its way of measuring the bond's progress, and also its way of accelerating it: the awareness itself became a bonding mechanism, intimacy imposed through involuntary knowledge of the other person's inner weather.

The Mark resonance. Every week of sensing his proximity, his pain, his wakefulness. The way she knew which floor he was on without thinking about it. The way she could feel his concentration from three rooms away, right now, as if the distance between them were tissue-thin. The examination that morning — feeling him feel her — was the channel widening, the architecture opening more bandwidth between them, and the mutual sensation she'd flinched from was the house showing her how far the bond had already progressed.

She turned another page. The text described maintenance protocols — how the bonding architecture should be renewed, what to do if the resonance stalled or reversed, the conditions under which the house might redirect its amplification from one pair of inhabitants to another. A passage near the end caught her and held:

*The architecture shall prove most efficacious when the inhabitants are unaware of its influence. Should the bonded pair come to understand the mechanism, the architecture cannot compel continuation. The bond must, in its final stages, become a matter of will. The house can cultivate. It cannot command.*

An architect's manual. Written by someone who understood the house as an instrument and the people inside it as material. Raw material for the line the house was built to perpetuate. And someone who had been honest enough, or arrogant enough, to document the system's single limitation: it worked best in the dark. Awareness was the one thing the architecture couldn't compensate for.

She closed the folio.

The library was silent except for the fire, which burned the way the fire always burned — evenly, steadily, the house maintaining the precise temperature that made staying comfortable and leaving feel like loss. Her tea sat cold at her elbow. Outside the tall windows, full dark. She'd been reading for hours.

Above her, in the archive, Draco shifted. She felt it the way she'd felt it all afternoon — the quality of his attention changing, a transition from deep focus to the vaguer register of someone surfacing from concentrated work. He'd be coming down soon. He usually did around this time, when the archive's hold started to weaken and the walls went soft at the edges.

She had known his rhythms for weeks. She'd called this familiarity — the natural product of sharing space with someone day after day. The way you learn the sounds of a housemate, their patterns, the shape of their day. She'd let herself find comfort in it.

It was the house.

It had been the house the entire time. The resonance she'd attributed to diagnostic artifact. The corridors that routed her past his door. The fire that lit itself when they were together. The comfort she'd been letting herself feel in this place where she'd once been held down and carved open — all of it the bonding architecture doing exactly what it was designed to do. Reading whatever existed between them and amplifying it. Making proximity feel natural. Making the thought of leaving feel like cost.

Every moment of genuine connection was now suspect.

The intellectual intimacy in the library — was that them, or the house creating conditions where their minds worked better together? The post-drawing-room tenderness — real grief and real gentleness, or the architecture accelerating emotional recovery to preserve the bond? The way she'd felt when she caught him watching her with something careful and unguarded in his face — was that want, or was it the house's interpretation of what want should feel like, fed back to her through a channel she hadn't known existed?

The examination that morning. Feeling him feel her. The accumulated attention, the quiet desire he carried so deep inside himself. Was that the channel widening because the house wanted it wider? Had she been reaching for the deeper layers of the Mark, or had the house been guiding her diagnostic spells toward the exact contact that would create maximum resonance?

She didn't know. That was the thing she kept arriving at, the wall at the end of every line of reasoning. She could not tell. The folio described the mechanism but did not distinguish between what the house amplified and what the inhabitants felt on their own. The architecture was designed to make the distinction impossible. *Seamless integration.* The whole point was that you couldn't tell. The architect had built the house so that its manipulation would be indistinguishable from the inhabitants' own feelings, and that design was working exactly as intended, and she was inside it, and she could not find the seam.

She thought about the morning's examination. The shame she'd felt from inside his body — the old, ground-in shame of the Mark. That had been real. She was sure of that. The house couldn't fabricate a sixteen-year-old boy's horror at what was being branded into his arm. But the relief — his association of her touch with the absence of pain — had that developed on its own, through weeks of her diagnostic contact genuinely quieting the Mark's restless magic? Or had the house been amplifying the relief, deepening the association, making her hands feel more soothing than they actually were so that his body would learn to need her specifically?

She couldn't tell. She could not tell.

She slid the folio back behind the property records where she'd found it. She stacked the surveyor's reports neatly on top, aligning the edges. She carried her cold tea to the sideboard and set it down with the other used cups.

She should tell him. The obligation was immediate and structural — a load-bearing requirement she could feel the way she felt all obligations, as a thing that, if left unmet, would compromise everything built on top of it. He was inside this architecture. His feelings were being shaped by the same mechanism. He lived here. This was his family's house, his family's magic, his inheritance manipulating his emotional life without his knowledge or consent. He had a right to the information she was holding.

She should tell him. She was going to tell him.

His footsteps in the corridor above — the archive releasing him, the house rearranging to accommodate his movement toward the library. The resonance brightened as he got closer. She could feel the specific quality of his approach — unhurried, a slight stiffness in his left shoulder from hours of leaning over texts, the particular looseness that came after a productive session when he'd found something that engaged him enough to forget his own discomfort.

He appeared in the library doorway. Pale, angular, his sleeves still rolled from archive work, the Mark visible on his forearm. He looked tired in the way he always looked tired — not dramatically, just the permanent slight depletion of someone who slept badly and had stopped expecting otherwise. But under the tiredness, something else. An openness he'd been allowing more since the drawing room, since the quiet days after it, since they'd stopped pretending that shared meals were a matter of scheduling convenience.

"The archive's fighting me again," he said. "The back wall went soft around four. I had to transcribe the last three pages from memory before the text dissolved." He crossed to the sideboard, saw her cup among the others, glanced at her. "Have you eaten?"

"Not yet."

"It's past seven."

"I know."

He looked at her. The look was — she understood now what the look was. She had a folio's worth of technical vocabulary for it. The architecture reading his emotional state and amplifying it, the house reflecting his concern back through environmental cues that encouraged him to act on it, the whole system designed so that his impulse to feed her would feel natural and uncomplicated and his own.

Or it was just him, worried about her because she forgot to eat when she was working and he'd noticed this about her because he'd been paying attention and that was what people did when they cared.

"I'll get something from the kitchen," he said. "You look like you've been staring at the same page for three hours."

"Different pages," she said. "Many different pages."

"Right." He almost smiled — not quite, the near-miss that was his default when she said something that wasn't funny enough to earn a real one but familiar enough to register. "Anything useful?"

She thought of the folio behind the property records. The bonding architecture. The feedback loops and the seamless integration and the channels of mutual awareness that she could feel right now, broadcasting his proximity and his tiredness and his genuine, uncomplicated desire to make sure she ate something.

"Property records," she said. "Administrative stuff. Foundation engineering. Nothing exciting."

He waited a beat, as if he expected her to add something. She didn't. She held his gaze and felt the resonance between them carrying the texture of his mild curiosity — not suspicion, nothing as sharp as that, just the attentiveness of someone who'd learned to read her and was registering that today's reading didn't quite parse.

"Right," he said. "I'll be back."

He went to the kitchen.

She stood in the library and listened to his footsteps down the corridor and she felt the distance between them stretch in the resonance — his presence dimming slightly, not gone, never gone now, but reduced to the low hum that was her new permanent baseline. She'd called this normal. She'd called this the inevitable result of diagnostic contact with Dark artifacts, a known phenomenon, well-documented. Every name she'd given it had been a way of not reading the folio she'd just read.

He came back with sandwiches, the bread cut at an angle because he'd apparently decided at some point that this was how bread should be cut. A fresh cup of tea for her, properly hot. He set the plate on the table between her folios and sat across from her and picked up his book and the quiet domestic fact of it — two people eating in a library, the fire going, the night dark outside — was so precisely the thing she'd been letting herself want that the wanting sat in her chest like a stone.

She ate a sandwich. It was good. He'd put too much butter on it, which was how she liked it, and he knew this because they'd been sharing meals for weeks and he'd been paying the kind of attention she now understood might be the house's attention routed through his nervous system. Or the attention of a man who lived alone and had suddenly found himself feeding someone and was quietly, carefully good at it. The two explanations sat on top of each other like the doubled perspective of the morning's examination, and she could not peel them apart.

Or his attention. His own. There was no way to tell. The folio had been very clear about that.

"You're quiet," he said, not looking up.

"I'm eating."

"You're usually quiet while eating. This is a different quiet."

She looked at him across the table. The firelight catching the angles of his face. The book propped in his hand. The Mark on his forearm, exposed because he'd stopped rolling his sleeve down around her — a trust so small and so specific that she'd been thinking about it for days, the decision to leave uncovered the thing he was most ashamed of because he'd decided she was safe.

"Tired," she said. "Long day with the property records."

"The property records that were nothing exciting."

"They're still nothing exciting. I'm just thorough."

"I know." He said it simply, without weight, and the absence of weight was the weight — the ease of knowing someone, the unremarkable fact of having learned their patterns. "Try to sleep before midnight for once."

"I'll try."

She went to her room. The corridors were lit and well-heated, the house routing her through the shared spaces, past the kitchen, past the wing where he slept. She noticed the route. She'd always noticed. She'd called it the Manor's odd sense of direction, or efficient architecture, or nothing at all, and every name she'd given it had been a way of not naming the thing the folio named.

She closed her door. The bed had been turned down — the house had been doing this for weeks, and she'd told herself it was residual enchantment and she'd told herself it was just a well-maintained old house and she'd told herself it was nothing, and none of those thoughts had been quite dishonest enough to qualify as lies but none of them had been straight either.

She sat on the bed in her clothes and felt Draco three floors above settling into his room, the resonance transmitting the slow dimming of his attention as he moved toward sleep. The house held them both. The house measured the space between them and found it, even now, insufficient for its purposes, and she could feel the subtle architectural pressure — a warmth, a pull, a corridor-length adjustment she could sense rather than see — encouraging her to be closer.

She should tell him.

She was going to tell him, after she verified the text. The notation was archaic. Her translation might be inaccurate. She needed to cross-reference the folio against other sources, confirm that the bonding architecture it described was still active, check whether centuries of modification had altered or disabled the mechanism. She was a researcher. Verification was not avoidance. Verification was the responsible course of action before raising an alarm about something that might, on closer examination, turn out to be—

She stopped.

She sat on the bed and listened to herself think and she heard what she was doing. She was building a structure — careful, logical, brick by brick — a framework of reasonable justification that would let her keep the knowledge locked inside her own head, where it wouldn't require action, where it wouldn't force a conversation she wasn't ready to have.

She wasn't going to verify. Not soon, not urgently, not the way verification required — which was telling him what she'd found and working through it together, subjecting the text to the collaborative rigor that was the only honest approach to a finding this significant. She was going to keep the knowledge because keeping it preserved the framework where she was the researcher and this was a problem and her feelings — the tenderness she'd been allowing, the want she'd been not-examining, the specific warmth she felt when he brought her tea without asking how she took it and got it right — could remain in the category of data rather than something she had to decide about.

If she told him, they would have to name what existed between them. She was not ready to name it. She was especially not ready to name it now that the naming itself was compromised. Was the pull toward naming it hers or the house's? Was her reluctance to name it a genuine scruple or the architecture prolonging the bonding process, keeping feelings unexamined because unexamined feelings were easier to amplify?

She couldn't tell. The house had been designed so she couldn't tell. And the recursion — I can't tell whether my feelings are real, and I can't tell whether my inability to tell is a product of the architecture — fit so precisely into the shape of her own specific weakness that it felt almost personal. She'd spent her entire life needing to understand before she acted. Needing to verify before she committed. Needing certainty before she let herself feel anything she couldn't take back. And the house had built a trap that was her exact shape: a doubt that could never be fully resolved because the instrument of resolution was itself compromised.

She pulled the blanket over her legs without undressing. Above her, through the resonance, Draco's awareness had gone quiet — the slow, diffuse texture of sleep. He was dreaming, or close to it. She could feel the edges of his unconscious attention the way you could feel the heat of a banked fire from across a room — faint, ambient, impossible to ignore once you knew it was there.

Tomorrow she would wake up and examine his Mark. She would eat his sandwiches. She would sit across from him in the library and feel his presence through the resonance and she would do all of it under knowledge she was not sharing. And the word for that was not verification. The word for that was not prudence or due diligence or responsible scholarship.

She knew the word. She was precise enough, and honest enough with herself if with no one else, to name it.

The house was warm around her. The house was always warm. She lay in the dark and let the lie stand and she did not sleep.
