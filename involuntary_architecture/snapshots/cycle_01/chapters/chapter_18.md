# Chapter 18

The seventh day was the worst.

Not because the Mark hurt more — though it did, the magic in it grinding against a frequency that had gone silent, reaching for a diagnostic touch that was no longer there. The seventh day was worst because the house had finally stopped trying.

For six days the Manor had managed something. Not the responsive architecture he'd grown used to when she was here — no shortcuts offered, no corridors generously rearranging themselves, no rooms surfacing like suggestions. But there had been small signs: a sconce flickering in the east wing that might have been intentional, a door that stuck and then released as though the house had changed its mind, the faintest contraction in the hallway outside the library like the building clearing its throat. Signs of life, if he was generous. Last autonomic functions, if he was honest.

On the seventh day, nothing. The Manor held its shape, and the stillness was worse than any rearrangement. A house that moved was a house with opinions. This was just a house.

Draco stood at the library entrance at ten in the morning. He'd been getting dressed each day because it was the one structural act that still organized his time — trousers, a shirt, even shoes, as though he were expecting someone or had somewhere to go. The library door was open. It had been open all week, no longer bothering to close. Beyond it, the tall windows let in what December offered, which was barely anything: flat grey light, the sky an unbroken sheet of cloud that hadn't cracked in days. He'd lost track of whether it was the second or the third of the month. The distinction between morning and afternoon had become academic, the light the same exhausted grey at noon as at three.

He walked to the wall where the gated archive appeared. Blank stone. He put his hand against it — not expecting anything, just repeating the morning ritual, testing whether the house remembered. The stone was cold. Not the pointed cold of a house choosing to withhold, just the temperature of stone in deep winter when nobody had bothered with a warming charm.

He hadn't had the energy for warming charms. He hadn't had the energy for much.

The Mark was a grinding pressure from his wrist to his elbow. He'd started wrapping it on the third day — gauze, a Muggle solution that would have made his father's mouth go flat, but the physical compression helped marginally and the wrapping meant he didn't have to watch the snake moving under his skin. It moved constantly now, the dark magic restless and searching, the same low-grade ache he'd lived with for years but worse — sharper, more insistent — because now the magic had known her diagnostic spells, had known the specific frequency of her touch, and it was reaching for that frequency the way a hand reaches for a door handle that's been removed.

He knew what this was. Not withdrawal from a substance — the Malfoy vices ran colder — but from the specific addiction of someone paying sustained attention to you with care. He'd survived this kind of withdrawal once, after the war, when even Theo kept a measured distance and his mother's visits carried the weight of monitoring. He'd survived by contracting his world until needing anyone was no longer a variable. The Manor and him. Neither requiring anything further.

That had been more convincing before Granger spent six weeks inside his walls. Before she reorganized his library shelves and argued translation theory with him until past midnight and fell asleep over a text while he sat across from her for twenty minutes inventing reasons not to leave. Before she pressed her fingers to his forearm and the curse in his skin went quiet for the first time in seven years, and he'd felt something so close to peace that the absence of it now was its own kind of injury.

He made tea in the kitchen, which cooperated because the kitchen always cooperated — the one room in the house that ran on pure function, no architectural opinions, no editorial commentary. The water boiled. The kettle steamed. The cup was where he'd left it: cream-coloured, slightly too wide, the handle turned wrong for his grip. Her cup. He'd been using it for three days before he noticed, and then he'd kept using it because switching cups would have meant acknowledging something he preferred to leave unexamined.

The tea tasted fine. He drank it standing at the counter because sitting at the table required a performance of normalcy he couldn't manage. His reflection in the kitchen window was grim. He'd assessed it the day before with a clinical detachment his mother would have recognized: the skin beneath his eyes gone bruise-dark, his colour worse than its usual pallor, his hair unwashed past dishevelled and into something genuinely unpleasant. He looked like someone who should be checked on. He looked, specifically, like someone whose house had stopped caring about him, which was accurate and also the least of what was wrong.

He walked back through the east wing corridor because it was the most direct route and because the house was no longer offering alternatives. Every door along the corridor was open or closed and stayed that way — the guest wing where she'd slept, the morning room his mother used for tea, the narrow passage to the conservatory that neither of them had ever bothered with. He passed the drawing room. Its door was open too, had been open since the house stopped closing anything, standing wide on its hinges like a mouth mid-sentence. He didn't look in. He hadn't looked in all week, but he knew the room was there the way you know a loose tooth — by the shape of the gap, by the involuntary clench every time he walked past it.

He carried the tea back to the library and sat in the reading chair. The text he'd been staring at for four days was open on the table — a genealogical record of seventeenth-century Malfoy bonding customs, found in the accessible stacks, the ones that didn't require the gated archive or a second person. It told him nothing he didn't already know from the bonding folio. The mechanism. The amplification. The terms. She had found those texts first, weeks before him, and kept them, and continued standing close enough to touch, and kissed him, and let him want her while carrying knowledge that made every moment of wanting suspect.

She had kissed him knowing.

The anger had been clean for the first two days. Precise, justified, the kind of cold certainty he'd been raised to wield. She had lied by omission. She had maintained an informational advantage over someone whose body she was examining. She had treated their connection as a hypothesis to verify before sharing, and he had been — again, always — the subject of someone else's calculation, the thing studied rather than the person consulted.

By the third day the anger had thinned. Not gone, but worn through in places, and underneath it the thing he didn't want to examine: she had been right to doubt. The bonding texts were not wrong. The Manor was amplifying something between them — their proximity, their awareness of each other, the resonance that built with every examination. He could not prove that what he felt when her fingers were on his forearm was entirely his own feeling, unaided, unaugmented. The proof would require her absence, and her absence was its own distortion, its own variable, and he was living inside it, and it was making him worse.

He sat in the chair. His tea went cold. The winter light shifted from grey-white to grey-dark, the December afternoon surrendering to evening without bothering to distinguish the two. The Mark ground in his forearm and he pressed it against the chair's arm and the compression helped for ten seconds and then it didn't.

On the table, next to the genealogical text, sat a scrap of parchment covered in her handwriting — a translation note she'd left behind, cramped and precise, her lowercase g's with the distinctive curl she gave them. He'd found it on the third day and hadn't moved it. He'd considered throwing it in the grate and then he'd considered putting it in a drawer and then he'd left it where it was, because both extremes would have been an admission.

He sat in the cold library and the house sat around him and neither of them moved.

---

The footsteps reached him before the knock. Measured, unhurried, the sound of someone who knew this house well enough to navigate it without looking. He knew who it was before she appeared. His mother's walk was as distinctive as her voice — the precise heel placement, the pause at thresholds that was not hesitation but acknowledgment, the way she moved through the Manor as though the house were a dialect she'd been speaking for thirty years.

Narcissa appeared in the library doorway. She was wearing a dark coat, still buttoned, and her gloves were on, which meant she had come straight through the house without stopping. She looked at him and he watched her take in the full picture — his appearance, the cold room, the single teacup, the dead grate, the blanket draped over the chair arm — and make the calculation of what to say about what she saw.

"You look dreadful," she said.

He hadn't expected that. She usually began with something softer, or with nothing at all, letting the visit find its own shape.

"I'm fine."

"You are not fine." She pulled off one glove, then the other, folding them together with practiced precision. "Has Mipsy been coming?"

"I told her not to."

His mother looked at the unlit grate, the blanket, the translation note in someone else's handwriting on the table. She unbuttoned her coat and draped it over the back of the chair by the door with the careful motions of someone who treated every room she entered as a space she was choosing to inhabit.

"Tea?" he said, because the ritual survived everything.

"Please."

He went to the kitchen and made it the way she took it — black, no sugar, the cup from the set she preferred because she noticed which set and he'd been raised to notice that she noticed. When he came back she was standing at the library window, one hand resting on the frame, her head tilted in the way she had when she was listening to the house. But the house was not speaking. After a moment she withdrew her hand and sat in the chair opposite his — the one Granger had used, with the worn velvet and the ink stain on the right arm from a spill three weeks ago that he hadn't cleaned.

If Narcissa noticed whose chair it was, she did not say.

They drank tea. For several minutes, neither spoke. His mother had always used silence deliberately, as a medium rather than an absence, and Draco had inherited the capacity and was grateful for it, though at the moment the silence felt larger than the room could comfortably hold.

"I did not come to ask what happened," Narcissa said. "I know what happened. The house told me before it stopped telling anyone anything."

"The house told you."

"In its way." She turned the cup in her hands, a minute adjustment. "I have not been fully honest with you, Draco."

The weight of the sentence landed somewhere he hadn't expected. It was not the preamble to a strategic disclosure. It sounded like a confession.

He set his cup down. The gauze on his forearm caught the cup's edge and he adjusted, an awkward motion he would have hidden if he'd had more energy for hiding.

"About the house," he said.

"About the house. About what it does and what it cannot do." She looked at her tea, then at him. "I told myself I was protecting you from knowledge you were not ready for, and that was partly true and partly an excuse for not having to say it aloud."

"Then say it now."

She met his eyes. Hers were the same grey — the Malfoy inheritance that had skipped nothing, not colour, not bone structure, not the habit of watching before speaking.

"The Manor's bonding architecture," she said. "You have read the texts."

"I've read them."

"Then you know the mechanism. What you do not know — what the texts do not say plainly, because the people who wrote them took it for granted — is the mechanism's limitation." She paused. Her thumb traced the rim of the cup. "The house amplifies. This is true. It takes what exists between its inhabitants and increases the resonance, the awareness, the felt presence. It gates access based on the strength of that connection. It creates conditions that encourage closeness. All of this is documented and all of it is accurate."

"I know this."

"What the house cannot do," Narcissa said, "is fabricate."

The word sat in the room.

"The Manor cannot create a connection where none exists. It cannot manufacture resonance from nothing." She stopped, and the stop was notable, because his mother did not usually need to gather herself mid-sentence. "Think of a greenhouse. A greenhouse controls light, warmth, humidity. It creates optimal conditions for growth. But a greenhouse cannot produce a seed. If nothing has been planted, nothing will grow. The conditions are irrelevant without the material."

He stared at her. The Mark pulsed under the gauze and he let it.

"You're telling me—"

"I am telling you that what the house amplified between you and Ms. Granger existed before the amplification." Her voice was steady, precise, the register she used for things she had considered carefully before speaking. "The resonance, the mutual awareness, what you felt during the examinations — all of it. The house took something that was already present between you and made it louder and stronger and harder to ignore. But it did not put it there."

He was quiet for a long time. The information reorganized itself in his head with the slow heaviness of furniture being moved in a room too small for it. He thought of her hands on his forearm, the Mark going still under her diagnostic touch. He thought of her in the library at midnight, pushing hair off her face, mouthing a translation. He thought of the kiss — clumsy, terrified, the best thing that had happened to him in years — and he thought of it now with the knowledge that the wanting underneath it had been his. The house had turned up the volume. But the sound had been there.

"How do I know what the base level is?" The question was harder to form than he'd expected. "How do I separate what I'd feel without the house from what the house added?"

"You do not."

"That's not—"

"You do not," she repeated, and her voice shifted into the register she reserved for things that cost her. "No one does. No person in any relationship can say with certainty: this feeling is entirely my own, untouched by proximity, by habit, by circumstance, by loneliness, by the particular chemistry of another person's presence. The house makes the question visible. It does not invent the question."

She drank her tea. He watched her and understood, for the first time in this conversation, that she was not delivering a prepared briefing. She was speaking from inside something she had lived with for a very long time.

"Your father," she said.

Draco went still.

"Your father never resonated with the house." She said it plainly, without inflection, as though the sentence were a document she'd read many times and decided, finally, to share. "The bonding architecture activated when I moved in. Mildly. The house acknowledged us — it was warmer, it opened a few rooms that had been closed for years. But the deep resonance, the mutual awareness, the gated access — that never came. Not in twenty-five years of marriage."

The silence that followed was not comfortable.

"The house respected him," she said. "He was a Malfoy. It was his by inheritance and it honoured that. But what the Manor has been doing with you and Ms. Granger — what it did in the weeks she was here — it never did that for us." Her hand went to her cuff, adjusted it, returned to the cup. Her composure held, but the effort of the composure was visible, a slight tightness at the corners of her mouth. "Not once."

"Mother."

"This is not a complaint." Her voice was steady and the steadiness was achieved, not natural, and he could hear the work beneath it. "Your father and I had a real marriage. We chose each other. We raised you. We survived a war inside this house. That marriage was shaped by forces the house had no part in — politics, family obligation, his choices during the war, and mine. I am not telling you we were lesser." She paused. "I am telling you the house never treated us the way it treats what it sees between you and her."

"It's not—" He stopped. Between you and her. She'd said it as settled fact, and the words were doing something he was not prepared for.

"The seed was there," Narcissa said, and her voice was quieter now, closer to the private register she rarely used outside their home. "Whatever grew between you, it was real before the house touched it. The house cannot grow what does not exist. What it adds is volume. Intensity. It takes a thing you might have ignored or talked yourself out of or buried under enough distance and formality and makes it impossible to overlook."

She finished her tea and placed the cup on the side table with the faint click that meant she was approaching the end of what she'd come to say.

"She kept the texts from you. That was her error, and I will not minimise it. But she did not invent what she found. She discovered the truth about something that was already happening to both of you, and she was frightened by it." A pause. "She handled it badly. People do."

She stood. She smoothed her skirt with precise hands.

"I did not come to tell you what to do. You are an adult. Your choices are your own." She almost smiled, the expression so faint he might have imagined it. "The house will have opinions regardless of what you decide."

"Why didn't you tell me sooner?"

She paused in the doorway. The pause was long enough that he understood it was not strategic. She was deciding how much honesty she owed her grown son about the private arithmetic of her own marriage.

"Because telling you meant explaining why I knew," she said. "And explaining why I knew meant admitting that the house gave you something it never gave me." She picked up her gloves from the table by the door and pulled them on with careful, practised motions. "That was difficult to say. I have had some time with it now."

She buttoned her coat.

"Your arm," she said.

"It'll be fine."

"It will not." She adjusted a glove. "But you know that."

He heard her footsteps recede down the entrance hall, measured and unhurried. The front door opened and closed. The house did not adjust — no corridor shifting, no temperature change. The house did nothing at all.

He sat in the cold library with what she had given him. The specific grief of a woman telling her son that her marriage — real, chosen, survived — had never moved the house the way his messy, broken, possibly-ended thing had moved it. She'd carried that knowledge for years. She'd carried it alone, and today she'd set it down in front of him, and the generosity of that was the kind of thing he didn't have language for.

The Mark ground against the bone. He pressed his wrapped forearm to the chair and closed his eyes and sat with the question, which was the same question it had always been but shaped differently now. Not: was this fabricated or real? The house couldn't fabricate. His mother had said so, and his mother did not say things she had not verified. The question was: how much of what he felt was his own and how much was the house's amplification? And the answer she'd given him, which was not an answer at all, was that this was the question every person in every relationship had to sit inside, and the house only made it impossible to look away.

---

She came at dusk.

There was no warning. No message, no fire-call — the Floo was down again, the decay making even basic infrastructure unreliable — no owl. He had not reached out because he didn't know what he would say and because reaching out required a certainty he hadn't found, and because some part of him understood that this was a thing she needed to decide to do or not do without his interference.

But his mother's visit had loosened something. Not certainty. Something closer to permission — permission to stop converting the ache of missing her into the cleaner, more manageable shape of fury. Permission to sit with the fact that he wanted her back and that wanting her back was terrifying and that the want was his, had been his before the house touched it, and the house had only made it louder.

He was still in the library when he heard the knock.

It was not the front door. It was the side entrance, off the garden, the one the house used to keep locked except when it chose to let someone in. The lock was disengaged now — the house had stopped locking anything, stopped having preferences — but the choice of entrance meant something. She knew the side door. She'd used it on mornings when she came back from a walk in the grounds, her coat smelling of frost and grass. She'd used it because it was closer to the library.

He went to the door. His footsteps echoed on the stone corridor, the sound flat and dead, the acoustics of a house that had stopped modulating. The corridor was the shortest route from the library to the garden entrance and it was exactly as long as architecture required, no shorter, no longer. No opinions.

She was standing on the step. December dark behind her, full and featureless, the grounds invisible beyond the weak sphere of the entrance lamp. She wore a heavy coat and a scarf wound tight against the cold, and her hair was escaping whatever she'd done with it, the curls winning their perpetual argument with order. She carried the leather satchel she used for texts.

She'd brought work. That registered before anything else did. She hadn't come to deliver a speech or an ultimatum or a condition. She'd come with her work bag.

She looked at him and he watched her eyes move over his face and the gauze on his arm and the general state of him, and he watched her decide not to comment.

"Hi," she said.

Not Malfoy. Not a formal address. Just hi, the word hanging in the cold air between them.

"Hi."

She didn't ask to come in. She stood on the step and he stood in the doorway and the gap between them was three feet of cold air and six weeks of proximity and a week of absence and the specific damage of his last words to her, which had been precise and cruel, and her silence before walking out, which had been its own precision.

He stepped back from the doorway. She came in.

The house did nothing. No warmth, no welcoming shift. The entrance hall was dim, the lamp by the door casting its small circle. She unwound the scarf and he could see that her hands were shaking, very slightly, in the way that could have been the cold.

"I should have told you," she said. She was looking somewhere near his collar, not at his face. "The texts. The bonding architecture. I should've told you the day I found them."

"Yeah."

"I told myself I needed to verify, and some of that was — some of it was true. But mostly I was scared." She stopped. Restarted, which he recognized because he did the same thing — the restart that was an attempt at more precise honesty. "I was scared that if I told you, you'd pull back, and I'd lose what we'd been building. And I knew that was wrong. I knew I was making your decision for you by keeping you in the dark. I did it anyway."

He didn't respond immediately. The Mark ached. The corridor was dark behind him and the house was offering nothing — no atmospheric softening, no environmental assistance. They were on their own with this.

"I know," he said.

She looked up. Her eyes were red-rimmed but dry. She looked like someone who had done her crying elsewhere and arrived at the far end of it, wrung out and present.

"My mother was here today," he said. He hadn't planned to say this, but the information needed to go somewhere. "She told me what the house can and can't do."

She waited. The same quality of waiting she brought to the examinations — holding the space open, gathering what she could before reaching for conclusions.

"It can't fabricate," he said. "The house. It amplifies — it makes things louder, stronger — but it can't make something from nothing." He paused. "She's known the whole time. She had her own reasons for not telling me."

"What reasons?"

"It would've meant explaining that my father — that the house never—" The sentence wouldn't complete itself. He let it go. The details could come later, or not. What mattered was the shape of the thing: the house hadn't planted the seed. It had only turned up the light.

She processed this. He could see the intelligence working behind her eyes — not cold, not clinical, just the mind she had, doing what it did with information.

"That doesn't resolve it entirely," she said. "Amplification is still influence. We still can't know—"

"I know."

"—what's baseline and what's—"

"I know." He hadn't meant to interrupt, or maybe he had. "My mother said the same thing. She said no one in any relationship can say for certain what's their own feeling and what's proximity or habit or circumstance. She said the house makes the question visible. It doesn't invent it."

She was quiet. The entrance hall was cold around them. His arm throbbed. She was standing four feet away in her heavy coat with her hair loose and her work bag over her shoulder, and he wanted to touch her with a clarity that was either entirely his own or partly the house's or both, and the distinction mattered and also it didn't, and he was exhausted by the distinction mattering.

"I can't separate it," he said. The honest thing, and it came out rougher than the careful version would have. "What I feel from what the house did to what I feel. I've been trying all week and I can't get underneath it." He stopped. Started again, and the restart cost him. "I just know the house has been dead since you left. And the Mark's the worst it's been. And I've been sitting in that library drinking tea from your cup and I can't make myself stop."

The quality of her attention changed.

"You've been using my cup?"

"Shut up."

She almost smiled. He almost let her see that he wanted her to.

They stood in the cold corridor. The house was quiet around them. The question was the same question — was this real, was this theirs, was this the architecture or the feeling they'd brought into it — and the answer was still both, still unknowable, still unsorted. But standing here with her it felt different from how it had felt across a week's silence.

"I want to show you something," he said.

He walked. She followed. Through the ground-floor corridor toward the east wing, their footsteps on the stone — two sets now, slightly out of rhythm, hers quicker than his. The house offered nothing. No guidance, no shortcuts. They walked the full distance.

The drawing room door was where it had been all week. Open. Not opened-by-the-house, not staged or produced. Just a door standing open because the house had stopped closing anything, one of many doors in a house that had lost the will to curate. It was the worst of them.

He stopped. She stopped beside him.

The room beyond was dim. The chandelier caught the faint light from the corridor — a glitter of crystal and dust. The marble floor was grey in the darkness. The room was cold, but the whole house was cold, and this cold was not specific, was not pointed, was not the house making a statement. It was December in an unheated room.

"It's been open all week," he said. "I've walked past it every day."

She looked at the room. He watched her look at it and he knew what she was seeing — the same things she'd seen months ago when the house had pushed this room at her when her guard was at its lowest. The chandelier she'd stared at from the floor. The marble she'd lain on. The wall where he had stood, seventeen, and done nothing.

"I haven't gone in," he said. "It felt like something I shouldn't do alone."

She was quiet for several breaths. Her hand went to her left forearm and adjusted the sleeve — the habitual gesture he'd been oblivious to for weeks and then seen, and now couldn't unsee. The scar underneath. The one he'd traced with his thumb while neither of them breathed.

"Okay," she said.

They went in together.

The room was just a room. That was the thing that undid him. The chandelier was smaller than his memory had made it, or the room itself had changed scale, or seven years had simply revised his recollection. The marble floor was veined and uneven. A crack ran through one tile near the far wall that he'd never noticed before. The room smelled like cold stone and plaster dust and nothing else — no residue, no history-scent, just a room in a house in winter.

She walked to the centre of the floor and stopped. He followed. They stood on the marble. It was cold through his shoes. The chandelier above them hung motionless — no drafts, no wind in the sealed room, the crystal still and dusty. He remembered standing against the far wall. He remembered the specific angle: Bellatrix's back to him, Granger on the floor, and the way the chandelier had caught the firelight and thrown small moving patterns on the ceiling while she screamed. He had watched those patterns because he couldn't watch what was happening on the floor. He had counted the points of light. He'd got to fourteen before his aunt finished.

"I used to dream about this floor," she said. Her voice was close to steady. "The feel of it against my back. The cold. I could always feel the cold in the dreams, even in summer."

He said nothing. There was nothing to say that would be adequate, and he had learned — over months, at cost — that his inadequacy in this room was more honest than any attempt at adequacy would have been.

She looked at the ceiling. The chandelier. Then at him.

"It's also just a room," she said. "It's the room where she—" She stopped. Breathed. "But it's a room in your house and we're standing in it and the floor is cold and the light's terrible and it doesn't have any power I don't give it."

She was not saying this for him. She was saying it because she was standing in the room and saying it, and the act of being here and speaking was the point. It was not a ceremony, not closure — just two people in the worst room choosing to be in it.

He reached for her hand. The motion was graceless — his wrapped forearm bumped her wrist and he adjusted and his fingers found hers and they were cold, both of them, the cold of this house in this season, of this room that held a war inside it, and her hand closed around his and held.

They stood in the drawing room. The house did nothing.

Then — so faint he nearly missed it — a shift. A current in the air, the slightest change in pressure. The cold in the room eased by a single degree. Not warmth. Not the seductive golden heat the house had produced at the height of whatever it had been building between them. Just the faintest indication that the house was still there, still aware, still responsive to two people standing in the same room with their hands touching.

They left the drawing room together. Neither of them closed the door behind them.

She let go of his hand to step over the threshold and he felt the absence of her grip like a draught. In the corridor she took his hand again, and the second taking was different from the first — not a reaching but a resuming, as though the connection had only been paused for the practical business of stepping through a doorway.

In the corridor, the light was marginally better. The sconces along the east wing had come on — dim, barely more than the darkness they replaced, the first autonomous thing the house had done in days. A suggestion rather than a declaration.

She was still holding his hand. He could feel the calluses on her fingers from years of wand-work, the specific architecture of her grip. The Mark under the gauze had eased. Not gone quiet — it was far from quiet — but the grinding had softened to something he could carry, the way a noise you've been straining against loses its edge when another sound enters the room.

"The research," she said.

"Still there."

"The archive?"

"Sealed. Since you—" He didn't finish.

"It might open. If we're both—"

"Maybe." He paused. "The decay's getting worse. I can feel it in the Mark."

"I know. The Floo was down eight hours yesterday. St. Mungo's is rationing again."

They walked toward the library. The corridor didn't help them, didn't rearrange, didn't shorten itself. They walked the full distance and the ordinariness of the walk — two people moving through a hallway in a house in winter — was the most unremarkable and remarkable thing that had happened to him in a week.

She let go of his hand again at the library end of the corridor. This time neither of them reached back. They had established something in the drawing room that didn't require continuous contact to persist, and the knowledge of that was enough.

In the library doorway she paused. She looked at the room — the reading table, the two chairs, the stacks of texts he hadn't moved, the dead grate, the cold teacup, the scrap of parchment in her handwriting.

"Is that my translation note?"

"You left it."

"I know I left it."

He didn't explain why he hadn't moved it. She didn't ask.

She set her satchel down by the reading chair and went to the grate and cast a warming charm. The fire caught slowly, the logs accepting the spell with the reluctance of wood that had been cold for too long. Heat crept outward. Not the Manor's architectural warmth, not the house providing — just a fire in a fireplace, lit by a witch, the warmth of it specific and earned and ordinary.

She sat in her chair. He sat in his. The fire grew. The library warmed by degrees.

"I'm going to make more tea," he said, and stood, and then stopped, because a sentence arrived in his mouth before he'd decided to let it.

"Hermione."

Her name. Not Granger. Her name — four syllables he'd been holding at arm's length for months, keeping behind the surname's safe formality, treating the distance of it as a wall he could maintain. Her name in his voice in this library, and it sounded different from anything else he'd said in this room.

She looked at him. Brown eyes, tired, specific to her and no one else.

"More tea would be good," she said. "Draco."

He went to the kitchen. The kettle boiled. He poured two cups — the cream-coloured one and the blue one with the chip on the rim — and carried them back. He handed her the cream-coloured one and sat down with the blue one and the fire was warm and the sconces along the library walls had brightened by a degree, the Manor's slow return to awareness, tentative and cautious, the way a thing wakes when it doesn't know yet whether waking is safe.

She opened a text from her satchel. He opened the genealogical record, though now, with her in the next chair, the words on the page had reorganized into something he might actually be able to use.

The magic was still dying across Britain. The decay was worsening — she'd told him about the Floo, and he could feel it in the Mark, and the anchoring infrastructure they'd been studying was still crumbling and still unfinished and still urgently, critically needed. The bond between them was real and amplified and they could not separate one from the other and might never be able to. The doubt was not a problem to be solved. It was a condition they would live inside, the way they lived inside this house — involuntarily at first, then by something that looked, from the inside, like choice.

She made a note in the margin of a text and frowned at what she'd written and crossed it out and tried again. He read a passage and couldn't parse a phrase — archaic notation, the Malfoy shorthand that his ancestors had used to hide their most important knowledge in plain sight — and he turned the book toward her so she could see the line, and she leaned across the gap between their chairs to look, her finger tracing the symbols, and the fire burned, and the house held steady around them, and outside the windows the December dark was full and close and the work continued.
