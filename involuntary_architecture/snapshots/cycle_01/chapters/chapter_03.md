# Chapter 3

She'd prepared for this the way she prepared for everything — overprepared, really, three parchments of diagnostic protocols she'd adapted from published literature on curse-mark analysis, two standard Dark object assessment frameworks, and a sequence of probing charms she'd modified herself to account for the specific layering described in Baruffio's monograph on voluntary curse-binding. The protocols assumed a compliant subject. They assumed adequate workspace and controlled light. They assumed the examiner would not need to hold the subject's arm for sustained periods.

They did not assume Draco Malfoy, sitting across from her at a table in the study adjoining the library, his left sleeve rolled to the elbow, his forearm laid flat on the oak surface between them with the careful precision of someone placing evidence into the record.

The room had good morning light. That was what she'd told herself when she chose it — the east-facing windows, the sturdy table, the neutral territory between his private rooms and the library she'd claimed as her workspace. Good light for detailed diagnostic work. Practical. She'd set out her notes, her wand, a quill for annotations. Everything arranged.

"I'll need to touch your arm," she said. "For sustained contact during the diagnostic sequence. Some of the probing charms require a physical anchor point."

"I assumed."

"I'm telling you so you're not startled."

"I won't be startled." He said it evenly, looking at the far wall.

She pulled her chair closer. The legs scraped against stone and the sound was too loud in the quiet room. She could see the Mark from here — it was not what she'd expected from wartime photographs or Ministry documentation. The Dark Mark in those images had been vivid, almost aggressive: the skull, the serpent, the ink-black lines that moved like something alive. What she saw on his forearm was faded. Not gone — nothing about a Dark Lord's curse-brand would simply fade — but diminished, the black softened to a dark grey, the lines less sharp, the serpent's movement sluggish rather than sinuous. It looked like a bruise that had been healing for years and then stopped healing.

She took his wrist.

His skin was warm. That was the first thing she registered, and she registered it with the clinical detachment she'd been practicing since the night she agreed to this — warm, dry, the tendons of his wrist taut under her fingers. The last time she'd touched Draco Malfoy — she tried to remember. She couldn't. The trial, perhaps, a handshake that didn't happen, a distance maintained across a courtroom. Before that, nothing she could call contact. They'd existed in the same rooms, the same corridors, the same war, without touching. Seven years of shared history and she was counting the instances of physical contact on no fingers.

She turned his arm in the light, positioning it for the first diagnostic pass. He was rigid. Not just his arm — his whole body had locked, shoulders drawn back, jaw set, breathing controlled in the way that meant it was being controlled. She could feel the effort of his stillness through the point of contact, the muscles in his forearm refusing to relax under her hands. His wrist was narrow in her grip. She could feel the radius and ulna beneath the skin, the architecture of him, and the Mark sat on top of it all like a stain on a map — the skull and serpent rendered on the specific topography of his forearm, following the line of muscle, curving slightly where the arm narrowed toward the wrist.

"First pass is a general curse-structure analysis," she said, because talking through the procedure was what the protocols called for and because the silence in the room was becoming its own kind of pressure. "It shouldn't be painful. You might feel warmth or a mild buzzing."

He didn't respond. She hadn't expected him to.

She cast the first diagnostic charm. The familiar amber light settled over his forearm, and she watched through the diagnostic overlay as the curse's outer layer resolved into legible structure — standard Dark magic signatures, consistent with what she'd read. Voldemort's work had a recognizable magical fingerprint: cold, layered, designed to bind loyalty through pain. The surface reading was unremarkable. She could feel the curse-magic through the contact point, a low wrongness against her fingertips, like holding something that vibrated at a frequency just below perception.

She cast the second charm, probing deeper. The Mark's middle layers were more complex — not just a brand but a communication architecture, the remnants of a network that had once connected every Death Eater to its maker. The network was dead. Voldemort was dead. But the infrastructure remained in the curse-mark itself, channels that had carried signal and now carried nothing, like veins in a body after the heart has stopped. This was consistent with published research. She annotated her notes without looking up.

The third charm was one she'd modified herself, designed to interact with the curse's deepest layer — the binding that connected the Mark to its bearer's magical core. She cast it, and the amber light shifted, probing down into the structure where the curse met Draco's own magic.

The Mark moved.

Not the sluggish serpentine drift she'd been observing on the surface. The dark magic in the Mark's deep layer shifted toward her probing charm like a thing orienting. The lines on his forearm darkened by a shade. The serpent, which had been barely twitching, raised its head.

"Granger."

"I see it." Her voice came out steady, which was a triumph of training over instinct. "Hold still."

She adjusted her grip on his arm, fingers tightening fractionally around his wrist, and pushed the diagnostic charm deeper. The Mark's response intensified. The dark magic wasn't resisting her probe — Dark curse-marks resisted, that was their most basic defensive characteristic, they deflected or lashed out or went inert under examination. This one was reaching. The magic in the Mark flowed toward her charm the way iron filings orient to a magnet, and under her fingers his arm — which had been rigid since she first touched him — went slack.

His breathing changed. She could hear it, the controlled rhythm faltering into something less managed. The Mark's lines were sharper now, the faded grey deepening toward black under her sustained contact, and the restless ache he'd described during their negotiation — the pain without signal, the magic trying to move through channels that connected to nothing — had stopped. She couldn't see pain stopping, but she could feel the tension drain from his forearm, the muscles releasing under her hands in a way that was involuntary and obvious.

The Mark was quiet. Quiet in a way it apparently hadn't been in months, and the quieting had begun when she touched him and intensified when her diagnostic magic reached its deeper layers. She held the charm steady and watched the dark magic settle into something that resembled, if she was going to use a word she wasn't ready to use, rest.

She catalogued this. She set it aside. She ran three more diagnostic passes, each one confirming what the first had shown: the Mark's curse structure was more layered than any published analysis suggested, the deep binding between the curse and Draco's magical core was active in ways that should have been impossible seven years after the caster's death, and the Mark responded to her magic specifically — not just to diagnostic probing in general but to her probing, her magical signature, with a differential response she could measure and could not yet explain.

She released his arm. Drew back. Picked up her quill.

Her hand was steady. She had decided it would be steady and it was. She wrote three lines of annotation and then stopped because the quill nib had caught on the parchment and she needed to look at something that wasn't his forearm, wasn't the Mark, wasn't the place where her thumb had been resting against the inside of his wrist for the past fifteen minutes.

"The curse structure is more layered than the literature accounts for," she said. "I'll need multiple sessions. The response pattern is — " She paused. Chose a word. "Unusual."

"Unusual how?"

"The deep layer reacted to the diagnostic probe in a way I haven't seen documented. It's not hostile. It's more — responsive. I need to review my readings before I can say more than that."

He rolled his sleeve down. The fabric covered the Mark and something in the room loosened, some held breath that wasn't hers or his but belonged to the space between them. He stood. She noticed that he moved his arm carefully, the way someone moves a limb that has stopped hurting and doesn't trust the absence of pain.

"Same time tomorrow?" she asked, because the protocols called for daily diagnostic sessions during the initial assessment phase, and because filling the silence with logistics was the closest thing she had to a plan.

"If you need it."

"I need it."

He left the study. His footsteps echoed in the corridor outside and then didn't — the stone swallowing the sound at a distance she couldn't judge, or Malfoy walking in the particular way he had that she was noticing without wanting to, deliberate, each step placed as though the floor might not be trusted.

She sat with her notes and her quill and the east-facing light moving across the table. The oak surface still held the faint impression of warmth where his arm had rested. She hadn't anticipated how physical it would be — not the touching itself, which she'd prepared for, but the accumulating awareness of another person's body at close range for sustained minutes. The fine blond hair on his forearm. The scar tissue at the edge of the Mark where the curse-brand met unbranded skin, a faintly raised border she could feel under her fingertips. The way his pulse had been running fast when she first took his wrist and had slowed, gradually, as the Mark's pain eased — as if his whole body were recalibrating to the absence of something it had been carrying for months.

She did not think about the fact that the Mark had reached for her. She thought about it anyway. The dark magic had oriented to her with something that, in any other context and any other subject, she would have called recognition. She wrote this word in the margin of her notes and then crossed it out and replaced it with *anomalous differential response* and then stared at the crossed-out word underneath because the clinical language was a lie and she knew it.

She'd examined Dark objects before. Cursed jewelry, hexed artifacts, a boggart-infested wardrobe that had required a week of sustained diagnostic work. None of them had reached for her. Dark magic was hostile or inert; it did not welcome examination. What the Mark had done was something she had no precedent for, and the absence of precedent in her carefully accumulated knowledge was its own kind of disorientation, quieter than the corridors would be later but no less real.

---

The library saved her.

Not from anything dramatic — from the residue of the examination, the sensory memory of his wrist under her fingers, the anomalous thing the Mark had done that she didn't have a framework for yet. The library gave her a problem large enough to occupy every available cognitive resource, and she fell into it the way she'd been falling into problems since she was eleven years old.

It was extraordinary. She'd known the Malfoy collection was significant — every serious researcher in Britain knew, the way you know about a country you've never visited, by reputation and rumor and the occasional smuggled artifact that surfaced in other collections with a Malfoy provenance stamp. But knowing it was significant and standing inside it were different orders of experience.

The room was vast. Two stories of shelved walls with a mezzanine gallery running around the upper level, rolling ladders fixed to brass tracks, reading tables positioned under windows that admitted long shafts of autumn light. The air smelled of old paper and dust and something underneath both — the faint mineral tang of preservation charms that had been working for so long they'd become part of the room's atmosphere. The books went back centuries. She could see it in the shelf nearest the door without touching anything: a run of household accounts from the early 1700s bound in cracked calfskin, next to a grimoire with a spine that hummed faintly when she passed — not threatening, just present, the way a sleeping cat might purr if you brushed against its chair.

She started at the southeast corner, which was arbitrary but she had to start somewhere and arbitrary was better than standing in the middle of the room trying to absorb the whole thing at once. She pulled her cataloguing framework from her Department of Mysteries work: classification by subject, subclassification by language, notation for preservation state, age, and provenance where identifiable.

Within an hour she'd added two new categories. *Resistance to diagnostic magic* — for the texts that wouldn't be sorted, whose pages deflected her identification charms with the patient stubbornness of objects that had opinions about being organized. And *unknown script* — for the alcove she found on the mezzanine where three full shelves of texts were written in a magical notation she'd never encountered, angular and precise, the characters laid down in ink that still looked wet though the parchment underneath was brittle with age. She photographed the symbols with a copying charm and set the images aside for later analysis.

The collection had no organizing logic she could detect. Someone — generations of someones — had simply acquired. A shelf of astronomical observation journals sat next to a crate of loose letters in French. A section of alchemical texts shared space with what appeared to be a complete run of a seventeenth-century magical periodical she'd thought was lost entirely. One shelf held nothing but bestiaries, illustrated with moving drawings of creatures that pressed themselves against the pages as she passed — a Graphorn raised its head, a Demiguise flickered visible and invisible, something she didn't recognize clawed at the margin with tiny painted talons. She found a first-generation copy of the Flamel notation on anchor-point theory wedged between two cookbooks, and she had to sit down on the floor with it because her hands were shaking — not from the morning's examination, from the specific vertigo of holding a text she'd been told no longer existed.

The Flamel text was water-stained in one corner and someone had annotated the margins in a cramped hand she couldn't read, but the primary notation was intact — the anchor-point diagrams, the mathematical proofs for ley-line stabilization, the material Flamel had considered too theoretical to publish in his lifetime. She sat on the cold stone floor and turned pages and forgot, briefly and completely, where she was and why she was there and what had happened that morning with the Mark and the man it was branded on. This was the thing about her that nobody seemed to find charming and she could not help: a sufficiently interesting problem could erase everything else.

She worked through the morning and into the afternoon without stopping. Somewhere around midday she became aware that the library's upper gallery had changed — a section of shelving she hadn't approached yet had shifted forward, angling itself toward the light from the nearest window as if presenting its contents for easier reading. She noted this. She set it aside the way she was setting aside everything about this house that she couldn't process while maintaining the pace of cataloguing.

The floor around her accumulated sorted piles — texts she could read, texts she couldn't, texts that resisted classification, texts that needed immediate preservation work before they could be handled further. Her knees ached from sitting cross-legged on the stone floor. Her fingers were grey with dust. She'd filled eleven pages of notes and hadn't covered a quarter of the southeast corner.

The scope settled over her like weather. She'd estimated weeks. This was months, possibly longer. The collection was too large, too disordered, too multilingual for one person to process in any reasonable timeframe, and the texts that resisted her cataloguing spells would need individual attention — each one a small puzzle, a negotiation between her magic and whatever protective charms the original owners had laid down centuries ago.

She didn't mind. She adjusted her mental timeline without drama, the way she adjusted everything — practically, efficiently, with the faint irritation she directed at herself for having underestimated the problem in the first place. She kept working.

The light changed. The library's west-facing windows caught the late afternoon sun, which came in low and gold through old glass and made the dust visible in the air — not dirt, just the particulate evidence of a room full of very old paper, each mote a tiny fragment of something that had once been a page or a binding or a margin note. The room became warm. Not the Manor's ambient warmth, which she was learning had its own logic, but simple sunlight on stone and wood and leather, and for a stretch of minutes that she did not count, the library felt like nothing more complicated than a very good place to work.

She was sitting on the floor between two shoulder-high stacks of sorted texts when she realized she hadn't eaten since the tea and toast she'd managed that morning before the examination. Her stomach hadn't told her. Her stomach was an unreliable reporter when she was deep in a problem, a fact Ron had observed approximately two hundred times during their marriage and which she had never once conceded was a character flaw rather than a feature.

She stood. Her back informed her it had been holding this opinion for several hours. She stretched, shelved the current stack with placement notes, pocketed her wand, and went to find tea.

She passed Malfoy in the corridor outside the library. He was carrying a cup of something and a book and he looked like he'd been somewhere else in the house for hours — his hair was slightly disordered, which she suspected was as disordered as it got, and there was dust on his sleeve that he hadn't noticed or hadn't bothered with.

"The Flamel notation," she said, because she was still holding it and the excitement was stronger than the awkwardness. "You have a first-generation copy. Do you know what that is?"

He looked at the book in her hands. "It's been there since before I was born. Possibly before my father was."

"It's one of maybe three copies in existence. The Ministry's been looking for one for decades."

"Then I suppose it's fortunate you checked between the cookbooks."

She couldn't tell if he was joking. His face gave her nothing. She held the book against her chest and went to find the kitchen, or whatever passed for a kitchen in a house that produced food trays from nowhere, and behind her she heard him go into the library and the door close and the house was quiet again.

---

The house was quiet at half past nine. She'd eaten — a tray that appeared on the small table in the guest wing sitting room, warm and correctly proportioned, which raised questions she was not yet addressing about who or what was doing the cooking. She'd worked in the library until the light went and then by wand-light for another hour, sorting the unknown-script texts into subgroups by character set and ink composition. Now she wanted her room, her bed, her notes for tomorrow's examination protocol revisions.

The route was simple. She'd walked it that morning: left out of the library, down the long corridor with the tall windows and the portrait of a witch in green robes — a Malfoy ancestor, she assumed, from the pale coloring and the expression of amused disapproval — then right at the staircase, up one flight, and the guest wing opened before her. Fifteen minutes of walking at most, in a straight line with two turns.

She turned left out of the library. The corridor stretched ahead of her, properly, the windows letting in thin moonlight that laid silver rectangles across the stone floor. The portrait was there. But it wasn't the witch in green. It was a man — tall, bearded, dark-haired, in robes of a cut she associated with the late sixteenth century. He watched her pass with the specific stillness of a painted figure that could move and was choosing not to.

She'd noted the wrong portrait. She must have, that morning — tired from a night of poor sleep, distracted by the examination ahead of her. She was not someone who misremembered visual details, but she allowed the possibility because the alternative required engaging with the house and she was walking to her room, not conducting an investigation.

The staircase was where she expected it. She climbed, counting — one flight, as she'd done that morning. Except the landing she arrived at had a window facing south. Her room's window faced east. She was on the wrong side of the house, which should not have been possible from a single flight of stairs that she had climbed in a straight line.

She stood on the landing and looked at the south-facing window. Outside, the Manor's grounds were silver-grey in the moonlight — a formal garden she hadn't seen from this angle, hedges trimmed to geometric precision, a stone bench, the dark line of trees beyond. The air up here was cooler than the corridor below, and the stone under her feet had a slightly different texture, rougher, as though this section of the house was older or less maintained.

She went back down the staircase. The corridor at the bottom was not the corridor she'd left. It was narrower, with a vaulted ceiling that belonged to a different architectural period than the rest of the floor, and it ran in a direction she couldn't reconcile with her mental map of the house. The portrait was gone. The windows were gone. The corridor was lit by wall sconces that burned with a bluish flame she hadn't seen elsewhere in the Manor, and her footsteps didn't echo the way they had been echoing — the sound was absorbed rather than returned, as though the stone were softer here, or listening.

She stopped walking.

The house was not hostile. She could feel this the way she could feel weather — not a conscious assessment but an ambient read, something in the quality of the air and the light and the silence that communicated *not danger.* But not-danger was not the same thing as safe, and the corridor ahead of her went on further than her wand-light reached, and she did not know where she was.

She cast Point Me. The spell worked cleanly — her wand swung north and held, and when she specified her room, the wand pointed left and slightly up. Twenty meters, by her estimation. She walked twenty meters left, found a door, opened it. A broom cupboard. Mops, buckets, a bottle of something that smelled of lemon and old magic. Her room was not here.

She closed the cupboard. The lemon smell followed her out, sharp and incongruous, and she stood in the corridor with it fading around her and felt the first real pulse of something that wasn't annoyance — something colder and less useful.

She tried another direction. Found a corridor that looked like the one from that morning — the windows, the moonlight — but the staircase at its end led down instead of up, and the floor below was a hall she'd never entered, vast and dim and smelling of cold stone and disuse. A tapestry on the far wall showed a hunt scene, the horses and hounds frozen mid-stride, and she could have sworn that as she turned away the nearest hound moved its head to watch her go. She retraced her steps. The corridor behind her was longer now than it had been thirty seconds ago, and the door she'd come through opened onto a different hallway entirely — wider, warmer, with a carpet she hadn't walked on before.

She checked her watch. It had been eleven minutes since she left the library.

She kept walking. Not frantically — she was methodical about it, trying each corridor, each staircase, marking her path with small illumination charms that she could track, except the markers kept appearing behind her when she hadn't doubled back, as though the house were folding the space she'd already crossed into the space ahead. She passed through a gallery of windows overlooking a courtyard that should have been the central courtyard but was smaller, planted with something dark and thorned that she couldn't identify by moonlight. She climbed a staircase that deposited her on a landing with three doors, none of which led anywhere she recognized. She found a corridor that she was almost certain she'd walked down twice already, except the sconces were different and the ceiling was higher and there was a smell she couldn't place — not unpleasant, just wrong, something vegetal and slightly sweet, like a greenhouse at night.

Twenty minutes. She checked her watch again when the guest wing materialized around her — between one step and the next, without transition, without the sense of having turned a corner or opened a door. She was simply there. The corridor was exactly as she'd left it that morning: the rug with the faded blue pattern, the sconce with the slightly crooked shade on the left wall, the window at the far end showing the grounds under a sky that had clouded over while she was lost. Her door was the second on the left. She could see the light she'd left burning inside, the warm yellow line under the door.

She stood in the hallway for a while. Her breathing was even. Her hands were not shaking. She was not frightened — or she was, but not in a way that felt immediate. The fear was analytical, which was the worst kind, because analytical fear meant she understood the thing she was afraid of well enough to be afraid precisely and she couldn't yet find its edges.

She went into her room and sat on the bed and didn't undress. The room was the same as she'd left it — her notes stacked on the small desk, her overnight bag on the chair by the window, the bed made with the austere precision of house-elf work. The walls were solid. The window faced east. Everything was where it should be.

She thought about Point Me. The spell had worked. It had pointed accurately to her room, given her a correct distance — twenty meters — and the twenty meters had led to a broom cupboard. The spell hadn't failed. The house had simply declined to let the space between her and her destination remain the space the spell described. The navigation was correct. The territory was unreliable.

She thought about the portrait that wasn't the portrait she'd passed that morning. She thought about the staircase that went up one flight and arrived on the wrong side of the house. She thought about the corridor with the blue-flame sconces and the sound-absorbing stone and the twenty meters that ended in mops.

She thought about the Mark. The way the dark magic had oriented to her diagnostic charm with what she'd crossed out and replaced with *anomalous differential response.* The way Draco's arm had gone slack under her hands. The way the room had felt, afterward, like something in it had been settled by her presence. The way he'd moved his arm when he left, careful, as if the painlessness were fragile. As if she might have left something behind in the curse that could be broken by rough handling.

She thought about the library shelves that had angled themselves toward the light while she worked, presenting their contents like a host opening a cabinet for a guest. She thought about the food tray that appeared without being summoned, warm and correct, and about the guest wing room with its too-good mattress and its east-facing window that showed her exactly what the grounds looked like in the morning light, as if the house wanted her to find the view pleasing.

She pulled her legs up onto the bed and leaned against the headboard and looked at the room that was behaving, for now, like a room. The Manor was not a house with old magic and eccentric architecture. She'd been telling herself that version — the rational version, the version where centuries of accumulated charms produced occasional spatial anomalies, the version where a house could be strange without being aware. She'd been telling herself that version because it was the version that allowed her to stay here as a researcher studying a problem, not as a person living inside one.

The house was aware. She didn't have proof in any framework that would satisfy a Department of Mysteries review, but she had twenty minutes of corridors that rearranged themselves around her, a portrait that changed faces, a navigation spell that worked perfectly in a space that didn't hold still, and the specific quality of the silence in this room right now — the deep quiet of a large building at night, which could be emptiness but which she could no longer make herself believe was emptiness, because emptiness didn't have this weight, this attention, this patient held quality of something waiting to see what she would do.

She was living inside something that watched her. She'd agreed to a research arrangement. She'd negotiated terms and access and brought her cataloguing framework and her diagnostic protocols and her professional composure, and she was inside a sentient structure that had rearranged itself around her for twenty minutes and then let her go, and she didn't know why it had done either thing.

She turned off the light with her wand and lay in the dark. The sheets were cool. The mattress was better than the one in her London flat, which was a detail she resented noticing. Through the window the sky was fully clouded now, the last moonlight gone, and the room was dark in the way that only countryside dark achieves — total, soft, the kind of dark that has texture.

The house settled around her. She could hear it, or feel it — a sound below hearing, a vibration in the stone, the particular quality of a very large building shifting into its nighttime configuration. She'd heard houses settle before. Old buildings moved. Timber and stone expanded and contracted with temperature, and magical construction added its own layer of ambient adjustment.

This wasn't that. This was the house settling the way a living thing settles — deliberately, attentively, with the weight of awareness distributed through the walls and the floors and the corridors that had rearranged themselves around her and then, at the precise moment she'd been about to stop being methodical and start being frightened, had delivered her to her door.

She lay in the dark and didn't sleep and tried to rebuild her assessment of the situation from the ground up. She was here to research the magical decay. She was here to examine the Dark Mark. She had negotiated access to the library and residential accommodation in exchange for diagnostic work. These were the terms. These were the professional boundaries.

The boundaries did not account for a house that moved. They did not account for a curse-mark that reached for her magic. They did not account for the fifteen minutes she'd spent holding Draco Malfoy's wrist while the dark magic in his arm settled under her touch like something that had been waiting for her.

She'd have to account for all of it. She'd have to add the Manor to her variables, alongside the Mark and the library and the decaying magical field and the man whose arm she would hold again tomorrow morning because the diagnostic sequence required it and because the Mark's anomalous response demanded further investigation and because — this was the part she was not examining — under her hands, for the first time in what was clearly months, it had stopped hurting him.

She turned onto her side. The pillow was better than her London pillow. She added this to the list of things she resented about the Manor and closed her eyes and did not sleep for a long time, and the house around her was quiet in the specific way of something that is choosing not to make noise.
