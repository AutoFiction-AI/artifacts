# Chapter 13

The pub was a Muggle place near Charing Cross Road, a narrow room with uncomfortable chairs and middling beer and no associations. She and Ron had started using it after the divorce because neither of them had ever kissed or argued or decided anything in this building, which was exactly why it worked. No memory to navigate around. Just a room.

She'd Apparated into the alley behind the used bookshop three doors down and walked the rest, her coat pulled close against the December cold. The Floo would have been easier but the Floo was unreliable now — three failures in the last week alone, connections dropping mid-transit, and she didn't fancy rematerializing in someone's kitchen in Birmingham because the network had lost her routing. So Apparition, with its familiar squeeze and the ringing in her ears afterward, and then ordinary London pavement under her boots, and the simple solidity of it — pavement that stayed where it was, walls that didn't rearrange themselves when she wasn't looking — felt strange. Not bad. Just strange, the way any normal thing feels after weeks inside the abnormal.

Ron was already there. Always early now, the way he'd never been when they were married. He stood when he saw her, which was new too — some politeness he'd adopted after the separation that she hadn't asked for and didn't correct because correcting it would mean discussing it, and discussing it would mean admitting the shape of their interactions had changed.

"Hey." He kissed her cheek. His face was cold. "You look — you look good."

It came out slightly wrong, the compliment landing with too much weight because he'd had to decide to say it, and the decision was audible. She let it pass.

"Got you a cider. Is that still right?"

"That's perfect."

They sat. The pub was half-empty at two in the afternoon — a few regulars nursing pints, a woman reading a paperback in the corner, a football match on the mounted television with the sound off. Ron glanced at it. He'd become genuinely interested in football since the divorce, one of the small expansions his life had made without her in it. He'd explained the offside rule to her twice and she still didn't understand it, which he found funny in a way that wasn't mean, and the fact that he could find it funny instead of irritating was one of the ways she knew they'd done the right thing.

"How's work?" she asked.

"Good. Weird. George has got me managing the books for the second shop and I'm completely rubbish at it, but it turns out that when I'm rubbish at the books, George does them himself, and then they're actually done right. So it's a system."

She laughed. He grinned — pleased with himself for getting the laugh, then slightly embarrassed by the pleasure, which was so precisely Ron that the familiarity of it ached in a specific, low-grade way. Not loss. Fondness for the thing they'd been that had become the thing they were now, which was different and less and also, in some ways she couldn't fully articulate, more honest.

"You?"

"Still at the Manor. The research is — it's significant. Material in the collection that nobody's documented in centuries." She reached for the professional frame, the one that cost her nothing. "It could be important for the decay."

"Right." Ron drank his beer. He was broader than she remembered, which happened every time she saw him after a gap — her mental image of him still nineteen and lanky, the actual Ron twenty-five and solid and slightly sunburned from the running Harry had mentioned. He'd taken it up in the summer and kept going through the autumn, and now apparently he ran along the canal near his flat three mornings a week, and the idea of Ron voluntarily running — Ron, who at school would have considered a brisk walk to the Great Hall excessive exertion — was one of the small surprises of their post-marriage life that she found genuinely, uncomplexly good.

"So you're there full-time," he said. Not a question.

"Mostly. I come back for meetings."

"And Malfoy's there."

"It's his house, Ron."

"Yeah, I know. I know that's — look, I'm not." He stopped. Rubbed the back of his neck. Started again with the visible effort of someone rerouting a sentence away from where it was headed. "I mean, the house. How's the house? Like, is it... fine? As a place to be?"

She could answer this several ways. The house was extraordinary, terrifying, beautiful, sentient, responsive, and currently adjusting its internal geography to accommodate her preferences in ways she hadn't asked for. The house had shown her the room where she'd been tortured and then, weeks later, started warming her bathwater to the exact temperature she preferred. The house knew things about her that she hadn't told it.

"It's unusual," she said. "But manageable."

Ron looked at her the way he looked at her when she was being evasive and he could tell and was deciding whether to push. He'd gotten better at this since the divorce — the deciding. During the marriage he'd pushed or he hadn't, binarily, without much calibration. Now there was a middle range. A pause where he weighed it.

"Hermione."

"What?"

"You said manageable. You didn't say fine."

She drank her cider. It was warm in the pub — the ordinary warmth of a radiator working on a December afternoon, not the intelligent, adjusting warmth of a house that noticed her. She could hear the woman in the corner turn a page of her paperback, and the muted commentary of the football match coming from the regulars' end of the bar, and somewhere below all of it, like a radio signal from very far away, the low hum of the Mark resonance. Faint from London. Degraded by distance. But there. Draco's pain — the restless ache that the Mark carried now, the magic in it searching for a channel that no longer existed — transmitted through miles of December air and settled in her forearm like an echo.

She pressed her wrist against the edge of the table. The pressure helped.

"I don't have a neat answer for you," she said. "The house is complicated. The research is real and it matters. And I'm handling it."

Ron nodded slowly. He was peeling the label off his beer bottle — a nervous habit she'd forgotten about and now remembered with the sharpness of something found at the back of a drawer, the recognition almost physical. He'd done that all through their engagement, sitting in restaurants, peeling labels while they talked about the wedding or didn't talk about the things they should have been talking about instead.

"George and Angelina are getting serious," he said after a while.

"Really?"

"Yeah. She's been at Sunday dinners three weeks running. Mum's broken out the good china. You know what that means."

"Molly Weasley pulling out the good china is practically a betrothal announcement."

"Don't say that to George, he'll bolt." Ron smiled. The smile was genuine and easy and had nothing to do with Hermione, which was the kind of smile she liked best from him now — the ones that were about his life, not about their shared past. "Anyway. She's good for him. She's got this way of just not taking his shit, you know? Like, he'll do the George thing where he deflects with a joke and she just... waits. Doesn't laugh. Waits until he says the real thing."

"Good."

"Yeah."

They were quiet. The football match continued its muted drama. Someone at the bar laughed at something. The woman turned another page. Normal human sounds in a normal human room, and Hermione sat in the middle of them and felt the resonance pulse — faint, faint, but continuous, a thread that connected her to a house in Wiltshire and the man inside it, and she thought about how Ron, sitting across from her peeling his label, had no idea that she could feel another person's pain in her own arm, that she'd been touched in places that weren't physical by a house that might be engineering everything she felt, that the reason she couldn't say "fine" was so much stranger than anything he was probably imagining.

"Listen," Ron said. He put the beer bottle down. The label was half off, a ragged strip of wet paper. "I know it's not my — I know I don't get to say this. But I'm going to say it once and then shut up."

"Okay."

"You do this thing." He wasn't looking at her. He was looking at his hands on the table, the way he did when he was being honest about something that embarrassed him. "Where you find a problem and the problem becomes the whole world and you forget that there was a world before the problem. You did it with Buckbeak's appeal third year. You did it with SPEW. You did it with the Horcruxes. You did it with the marriage — our marriage — when it was ending, you researched divorce like it was a thesis project, and it was the most organized breakup in history and also somehow the saddest thing I've ever been part of."

"Ron—"

"I'm not — that's not a complaint. That's just you. It's how you work. And I used to think it was brilliant and then I thought it was the thing that ruined us and now I think it's just — it's the thing about you that's never going to change, and it's not good or bad, it's just the way your brain works when it's scared."

She didn't say anything. The cider was going warm in her glass.

"That house," he said. "That family. It's the kind of problem that could swallow you. And I don't mean Malfoy — or I don't only mean Malfoy — I mean the whole thing. The research and the library and the decay and whatever's happening with the magic. You'll make it the whole world because that's what you do with the things that scare you, and by the time you come up for air—" He stopped. "I don't know. I just. Be careful."

The phrase landed. Be careful with that house. And she didn't know if he meant the architecture or the heir or the research or the pattern she couldn't see in herself because she was inside it, and the ambiguity was its own kind of precision because Ron, who people had always underestimated, was saying something true about all of those things at once.

"You're not wrong," she said.

He blinked. "That's new. You admitting I'm not wrong."

"Don't get used to it."

He smiled. The rueful, self-knowing one she'd always liked best on him. "Wouldn't dream of it."

He finished his beer. She finished her cider. He told her about the flat — he'd finally hung the shelves in the bedroom, badly, and they'd fallen down once already, and he was going to try again this weekend with a level this time because Harry had pointed out that eyeballing it wasn't working. She told him about a translation problem she'd been stuck on, keeping it vague, keeping it professional, and he listened the way he always listened — not tracking every detail but getting the emotional shape of it, the frustration and the fascination, and nodding in the right places.

"Oh," he said, when they were putting coats on. "The other thing. Mum went to St. Mungo's for her knee — the old jinx, the Moody one — and they turned her away. Said they're rationing healing magic. Prioritizing critical cases."

"I know about the rationing."

"I figured you would." He wound his scarf around his neck — a Chudley Cannons scarf, orange and faded, that she'd given him three Christmases ago when they were still married. He still wore it. She didn't know what to do with that. "The Floo dropped again this morning. Two hours. And a kid in Diagon Alley — six, maybe — set a rubbish bin on fire. Accidental magic, but the kind that shouldn't happen that young, not that strongly."

"I know."

"Right." He looked at her. "If the research matters — the stuff you're doing at the Manor — then I suppose it matters. I just want you to come back from it. That's all."

She hugged him at the door. He was warm and solid and smelled like beer and the cold and the specific Ron-smell that had been the first thing she'd noticed about him when she was twelve and wouldn't be able to identify if asked. He hugged her back carefully, his arms settling in the specific configuration of someone who knew her body from years of holding it and was being precise about the distance between memory and the present.

"Take care of yourself," he said.

"You too."

He left. She watched him walk up Charing Cross Road toward the Leaky, hunching into his coat, and felt the particular ache of watching someone you loved walk away from you in the most ordinary way — not leaving, just going, back to his flat and his badly hung shelves and his Sunday dinners and the life he was building that didn't need her in it. The ache wasn't loss. It was the specific sadness of something that had been finished properly, all the seams closed, the wound healed clean enough that what remained wasn't pain but the memory of where pain had been.

She stood on the pavement and felt the December cold on her face and the Mark resonance in her arm and pressed her wrist against the brick wall beside her, and the wall was just a wall, cold and rough and utterly indifferent to her touch.

---

Harry's office at the Department of Magical Law Enforcement was the same corner room they'd given him two promotions ago. He'd done nothing with it except accumulate files and add a photograph of Ginny and James that sat on the desk facing outward. James was nine months old in the picture, chewing on a toy broomstick with the focused commitment of someone who'd found his purpose.

"You look tired," Harry said.

"Hello to you too."

He stood and hugged her — a real hug, both arms, the kind of contact that required no negotiation because they'd known each other since they were eleven and the friendship had survived a war and everything after and could take the weight. He smelled like cedar soap. He'd used the same brand since fourth year.

"Tea?"

"Please."

He made it badly. Too strong, not enough milk, the cup repaired so many times the handle was slightly crooked. She drank it anyway because Harry's tea was one of the constants of her life and she wasn't prepared to lose constants right now.

"Ron said you saw him," Harry said, settling behind his desk.

"An hour ago."

"How was it?"

"Fine. Good. He told me about Molly and St. Mungo's."

Harry nodded. He had the particular look of someone who'd been absorbing bad news professionally for long enough that it had reorganized the muscles around his eyes — not haggard, exactly, but set. Alert in a low-frequency way that didn't switch off.

"It's not just the rationing," he said. "The Floo's been intermittent for three weeks. We've had four accidental magic incidents in the last ten days, all kids under seven, all discharging way beyond what their age should produce. One of them blew the windows out of a flat in Camden. Muggle flat. We had to send a team — Obliviators, the glass, the structural damage, the neighbors who'd heard the explosion. And the parents, the magical parents — they were terrified, Hermione. Because their child can't control what's happening and neither can they, and no one at the Ministry can explain why a five-year-old is producing magic that should be beyond a third-year."

"The ambient field's destabilizing," she said. "The infrastructure that regulates baseline magical pressure — it's degraded to the point where young wizards can't discharge safely. They're getting surges because the field isn't absorbing the excess."

"I got the Mysteries briefing. They said roughly that, with more words."

She would have smiled but the information was too specific and too bad. The decay that had been background noise in her professional life — the thing she'd been studying, tracking, watching worsen from the clean distance of data — was now a public emergency. Families with children who couldn't trust their own magic. Hospitals turning away patients because the healing spells drew on the same failing infrastructure. Transportation collapsing. The entire substrate of wizarding life in Britain coming apart, not in a dramatic catastrophe but in a slow, grinding degradation that was somehow worse because you couldn't point to the moment it broke.

"How's the research going?" Harry asked.

"There's material in the Malfoy library that documents the original magical infrastructure. The anchoring points, the ley-line connections — the deep structure that the ambient field depends on. It was built centuries ago, partly by the Malfoy family."

"And the war damaged it."

"Yes. That's what's failing. The anchoring points were designed to be permanent, but the scale of Dark magic during the war — the curses, the protections, the sheer volume of hostile magic passing through the field — it degraded them. Not all at once. Slowly. And the degradation has been compounding for seven years."

"Can you fix it?"

"I don't know yet. The texts are in archaic notation that takes both the Malfoy family's inherited knowledge and formal theory to decode. We've made progress but there's more to do."

Harry looked at her for a moment. "We," he said.

"Malfoy and I. He reads the family notation. I handle the theory. Neither of us can do it alone."

"Right."

Through the office wall she could hear someone arguing with a filing cabinet. The cabinet was making a grinding, petulant sound, the kind of noise an enchanted object makes when its underlying magic is misbehaving — another symptom of the ambient instability, institutional magic fraying at the seams, the small enchantments that kept the Ministry running starting to stutter and fail. The wizard outside swore. Something metal fell.

"People are talking," Harry said.

"About the decay?"

"About you. Where you've been." He leaned forward, elbows on the desk, the posture of someone delivering information he'd rather not deliver. "You've spent most of the last month at the Manor. People notice, Hermione. You're not someone who disappears into a Malfoy property for weeks without generating opinions."

"I'm doing critical research in the only archive that—"

"I know. The Prophet had a paragraph last week. Skeeter's column. 'Ministry researcher spending extended time at the Malfoy estate.' Nothing direct, just the implication. She doesn't have to say it outright."

"I don't read Skeeter."

"Ginny does." He paused. His hand went to his hair, the gesture so automatic he probably didn't register it. "Ginny said to tell you — and this is Ginny, not me — that if you're going to set up camp at Malfoy Manor, you should have a cover story better than 'the library is very interesting,' because no library is that interesting."

"I'm not setting up—"

"Her words. I told her it's about the research and the decay and the infrastructure. She said she believes you about the research. She also said the research isn't the only reason you're still there, and she's not going to insult your intelligence by pretending she doesn't notice."

Ginny Potter. Who'd been possessed by a Horcrux at eleven, watched her brother die at seventeen, married the most famous wizard alive, and come out the other side with a clarity of perception that none of them could match. Ginny didn't soften things. She didn't hedge. She'd survived enough to stop performing diplomacy and the result was a woman who said what she saw and didn't apologize for the seeing.

Hermione wanted to be angry about it and couldn't quite get there, because Ginny was right in a way that bypassed argument — not right about the specifics, which Ginny didn't know, but right about the shape of it, the outline of a situation that looked, from the outside, like more than what it claimed to be.

"The research is real," she said. "The decay is getting worse. The archive is the only source."

"I know. I believe you." Harry looked at the photograph of James and the broomstick. Looked back. "I'm not asking you to justify being there. I'm telling you other people are watching and not all of them are going to be generous with their assumptions. The Malfoy name still means what it means. Your name means what yours means. The two of those in proximity gets people's attention."

"Since when do I care what people assume?"

"Since always. You just pretend you don't."

He said it without edge. The particular Harry directness that was too kind for anger and too honest for comfort — the thing about him that had made her want to strangle him since she was twelve and also the thing that made him irreplaceable.

She drank the terrible tea. Outside the office, the filing cabinet had apparently won its argument with the wizard, because something heavy slammed and footsteps retreated. The ambient hum of the Ministry felt different than it had six months ago — thinner, less stable, the way a building feels when the power is fluctuating and the lights aren't quite steady.

"How bad is it going to get?" Harry asked. Quieter now.

"Bad. If we don't understand the anchoring infrastructure well enough to repair it — or at least stabilize it — it'll keep degrading. The Floo is just the beginning. Healing magic draws on the same field. So do the wards at Hogwarts, the protections at Azkaban, the enchantments on the Ministry itself." She gestured at the room around them. "Everything magical in Britain sits on top of the same foundation, and the foundation is cracked."

Harry absorbed this. He was good at absorbing bad information — a skill learned the hard way, over years of bad information arriving at inconvenient times in escalating quantities.

"And the Malfoy texts are the only way to understand it."

"The Malfoys helped build the original infrastructure. Their records are the only documentation of how the anchoring points work. There's nothing in the Ministry archives. Nothing at Hogwarts. Nothing anywhere I've looked. The original builders kept the knowledge in the family."

"Convenient."

"It was designed to be."

He didn't say anything for a moment. Then: "Look. Do what you need to do. I'll deal with Skeeter if it escalates. But, Hermione—" He met her eyes with the particular steadiness that meant he was about to say something she wouldn't enjoy. "Be honest with yourself about why you're there. Not with me. I don't need to know. But with yourself."

"I'm being honest."

He looked at her with the expression he used on suspects who were almost but not quite convincing, and she felt a flare of irritation because he was right and she was lying and they both knew it, and the specific lie was not that the research was fake but that the research was sufficient explanation, when the truth included a sentient house and a resonance she could feel in her arm right now, sitting in an office at the Ministry of Magic, the low pulse of Draco Malfoy's pain transmitted across sixty miles of English winter.

"Okay," Harry said.

He let it go. She could see him letting it go, the same way Ron had — the deliberate decision not to push, which both of them had learned to do with her over years of pushing and watching her shut down. It was possible that the two men who knew her best in the world had independently arrived at the same strategy for handling her, which was to say the honest thing once and then retreat, and the fact that she had taught them both to do this by being difficult about honesty was not lost on her.

She stood. He stood. He hugged her again — briefer this time, an arm around her shoulders, a squeeze.

"If you need anything," he said. "You know."

"I know."

She left the Ministry through the visitors' entrance, the red telephone box that rattled and rose and deposited her on a grey London street in December. Four o'clock. The light was already failing — mid-December, the sun gone or going, the sky a flat steel colour that would be black within the hour. She stood on the pavement and London moved around her, commuters and shoppers and a man selling roasted chestnuts from a cart on the corner, the smoke rising into the cold air and dispersing.

She walked. Not toward anything. Just through the city, the way she used to walk when she needed to think and the flat was too small and the office was too full of people who wanted her to have answers. Past the bookshop where she'd bought her first non-magical novel after the war. Past the cafe where she and Ron had sat on a Tuesday afternoon and agreed, over lukewarm coffee, that the marriage was done — not in those words, not dramatically, but in the accumulation of small honest sentences that added up to the same conclusion. Past the entrance to the Tube where a busker was playing something she almost recognized, his guitar case open on the pavement, and a woman dropped a coin in and the busker nodded without breaking the melody.

Ordinary. All of it ordinary. Walls that stayed where they were. Buildings that didn't rearrange themselves to suit their inhabitants' moods. People who didn't know that the magic holding their world together — the magic they didn't even know existed — was degrading underneath them like rot in the joists of a house that looked fine from the street.

She passed a shop on Shaftesbury Avenue where the display window should have held a concealed advertisement for Madam Malkin's — one of those charmed billboards that Muggles saw as a shoe advert — and the magic was flickering. The image stuttered between dress robes and brogues, two frames overlaid, and a passing Muggle glanced at it and kept walking, registering the glitch as a faulty display. But Hermione saw what it was: the concealment charm drawing on the same failing ambient field, the same cracked infrastructure, the same damage that was making children's magic spike and Floo connections drop. The same damage she'd spent a month studying in a library in Wiltshire, getting closer to understanding, getting closer to the man whose family had built the thing that was now breaking.

She Apparated from the alley behind the telephone box, and the world compressed to a needle-point and expanded again, and she was standing on the frosted gravel of the Manor drive with the last grey light dying over Wiltshire and her ears ringing and her breath coming out in clouds.

---

The walk up the drive took four minutes. She'd timed it once, early on, when the Manor had still been a professional assignment and she'd still been pretending that measurement could make this manageable. She didn't time it anymore. She walked. The cold bit at her face and her boots crunched on frozen gravel and the bare trees along the drive stood like structural drawings of themselves — all line and no softness, black against the darkening sky.

The Mark resonance strengthened with each step. The faint signal she'd been carrying all day in London was filling in, gaining resolution, the way a radio station clarifies as you drive toward its tower. His pain. His wakefulness. A quality she couldn't name but had learned to recognize — something like agitation, something like waiting. Two floors up, the resonance said, though she was still outside and the information shouldn't have been that precise at this distance. The bond was learning her. Or she was learning it.

The front door opened before she reached it.

Not the way doors open when you turn a handle. The way a door opens when the building behind it has decided. The heavy oak swung inward, smooth and unhurried, and the entrance hall beyond was lit with that golden light the Manor made when it was — she didn't want to finish the thought. When it was pleased.

She stepped inside.

The warmth was immediate and specific. Not the blunt heat of a radiator or a fire but something that seemed to know her — the cold spots in her coat, the numbness in her fingers, the particular chill that December air leaves on the skin of someone who's been standing on a London pavement thinking about things she doesn't want to think about. The warmth found all of it and addressed it with an attention that felt less like a heating charm and more like being noticed by something enormous and careful.

The air smelled of the library. Old paper, preservation spells, the faint mineral tang of the Manor's stone. And beneath that, the other quality — not a smell but a settling, the way the house exhaled when she came back, as if the building itself had been holding some tension that her presence released.

She stood in the entrance hall with her coat still on.

Ron's voice: be careful with that house.

Harry's voice: be honest about why you're there.

Ginny's voice, through Harry, clean as glass: the research isn't the only reason.

The corridor to the library was short tonight. This morning, when she'd left, it had been longer — she'd noticed, the way she noticed everything about this house now, the way the path between her room and the entrance stretched when she was leaving, as if the house were reluctant. Coming back, the corridors offered themselves generously. A left turn that usually passed two empty rooms opened instead directly into the hall outside the library doors, the house editing its own geography to shorten her walk.

She took off her coat and hung it on the hook the Manor had made for her. Iron, plain, at exactly the right height. She'd never asked for it. It had appeared the third night, and she'd used it without comment because commenting would mean acknowledging that the house was accommodating her, which would mean acknowledging that she was being accommodated, which would mean acknowledging that she liked it.

The library door was already open. The room beyond was warm and golden, the lamps lit, the shelves steady — the archive stable tonight, not shifting, the house in its most generous configuration, as if it were showing her the best version of the life it could provide. Her desk was where she'd left it, her notes stacked, a cup of tea sitting beside them that she hadn't made and that was, she knew without touching it, exactly the temperature she preferred.

She'd spent an afternoon in the ordinary world. She'd sat in a Muggle pub with a man who'd loved her for years and worried about her in the specific, informed way of someone who knew her worst patterns. She'd sat in a Ministry office with her best friend, who'd told her the world was watching and asked her to be honest. She'd walked through London where the walls didn't move and the rooms didn't respond and the silence was the regular silence of a city full of people who didn't know her name or her scar or the weight of the research she was carrying.

And she'd come back here. To the house that warmed for her, shortened its corridors for her, made her tea and opened its doors and arranged its light to please her. The house that knew her better than was reasonable and wanted her here with a persistence that couldn't be called desire because buildings don't desire, except that this one did, and the specificity of its wanting was more unsettling than anything it had ever done that frightened her.

She sat down at her desk. The chair was warm. The tea was perfect.

She picked up her notes and the resonance settled into its familiar register — Draco, two floors above, awake, the Mark's pain a low hum underneath her own pulse. She could feel the quality of his wakefulness, though she couldn't have said how. Not thoughts. Not words. Just a texture, like knowing whether rain is heavy or light without looking out the window. He knew she was back. She was sure of this without evidence — the resonance didn't carry that kind of information, or it shouldn't — but the quality of his pain had shifted when she came through the door, a fractional easing, the Mark quieting by the smallest degree, and she'd felt it the way you feel a held breath released in the next room.

She worked. The texts were where she'd left them, the translation half-finished, the notation dense and archaic and yielding slowly to the combined pressure of her theory and his family knowledge. Tomorrow they'd work on it together. Tomorrow she'd sit across from him in this library and their hands might not touch and the clinical frame would hold or it wouldn't and the house would be warm and watchful around them, and she'd know, because she'd spent an afternoon in the world where no one watched her and no house wanted her, exactly what she was choosing by being here.

The house dimmed the lamps when her eyes got tired. She didn't ask it to. The tea stayed warm long past when it should have cooled.

She kept working.
