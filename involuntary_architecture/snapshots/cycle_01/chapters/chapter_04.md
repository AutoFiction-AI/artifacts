# Chapter 4

The east corridor had a draught problem. It had always had a draught problem — a thin persistent breath of cold air that seeped between the stones near the third window, the one with the cracked leading that had been cracked since before Draco's father was born. The draught was a known quantity. Draco dressed for it in winter, carried warming charms through the passage in October, and had stopped noticing it the way people stop noticing the sound of their own house settling at night.

Granger had been in the Manor for four days, and the draught was gone.

He discovered this on a Tuesday morning, walking from his rooms to the main staircase with his hands in his pockets and his collar turned up out of habit. He was halfway down the corridor before he registered what was missing — the cold, the slight pulling sensation along the left side of his neck. He stopped. The air was warm. Not heated-warm, not spelled — the particular warmth of stone that has absorbed heat from the inside, the way the Manor felt in late summer when the walls gave back the day's light.

It was November.

He pressed his palm to the wall. The stone was warm. Not radiating but holding, the way a living body held heat, and under his hand there was something that was not quite a pulse but was not quite the absence of one either. He stood there for a long moment with his hand flat against the corridor wall, feeling whatever it was he was feeling, and then he took his hand away and walked the rest of the passage without touching anything.

She was already in the library when he came down. He could tell before he reached the doors because the doors were open — both of them, the heavy oak panels that he had to push with his shoulder and that had required a specific knocking pattern since he was twelve. Open. Not ajar, not cracked. Open wide, the way they had opened for his grandfather, who the house had loved.

Granger was at the long table with her sleeves pushed up and three texts spread in a semicircle around her, the organizational system she'd built in a day and a half doing more to make the library navigable than anything Draco had attempted in years. She had a quill in her left hand and a wand in her right, cross-referencing something between a catalogue entry and a passage that was giving her trouble. She didn't look up when he came in.

"The Alderic folio was on the third shelf yesterday," she said, not looking up. "The one with the red binding, the preservation charms on the spine. It's moved."

"Moved where?"

"Here." She pointed. The folio was on the table next to her, propped against a stack of Draco's great-aunt's astronomical journals. "I came in this morning and it was here. I didn't move it."

He looked at the folio. He looked at the shelf where it had been — he could see the gap, the dark rectangle on the wood where the binding had sat for years, the dust line broken. The shelf was across the room and up a rolling ladder. The book was now eighteen inches from her right hand.

"The house does that," he said. "It rearranges."

"It rearranges for me." She said it precisely, the way she said everything — not an accusation, just a correction. The distinction between the house moves things and the house moves things to where I can reach them. She looked up then, and her expression was the one she'd been wearing since the corridors had shifted on her two nights ago: carefully interested, carefully not frightened. "Three other texts I'd been planning to pull today are on this table. I had them on a list. I hadn't told anyone the list."

He didn't say anything to that. There was nothing to say that wouldn't sound like he was defending the house or agreeing that its behavior was alarming, and both of those felt wrong. The house moved things. It had always moved things. His mother's reading glasses had traveled from room to room throughout his childhood, appearing wherever she sat down, which she had never once commented on aloud.

But the glasses had been his mother's, and the house had known her for twenty-five years.

"I'll make tea," he said, which was what he said when he didn't know what to say, and he went to the small service alcove off the library that the house had decided to keep in the same place two days running, which was new.

While the kettle heated he stood in the alcove and listened. The library had particular sounds — the creak of the upper gallery, the faint ticking of preservation charms cycling through their work, the occasional papery sigh of a text settling on its shelf. He had grown up with these sounds. They were the house breathing, his father had told him once, which at five had been comforting and at fifteen had been horrifying and at twenty-five was just the fact of the place he lived.

The sounds were different now. There was a hum he didn't recognize — low, almost subsonic, the kind of sound you feel in your sternum before you hear it in your ears. It had started the day Granger arrived or possibly the day after; he wasn't sure because it was the sort of thing you only noticed when you tried to locate when you first noticed it. The hum was present when she was in the library and absent when she wasn't. He had checked.

He made the tea. Two cups, because she was there, and he'd been making two cups for three days now without having decided to.

When he brought the tea back she was standing at the far wall where the genealogical section was shelved — the family histories, the marriage records, the blood-status documentation that made his skin crawl every time he walked past it. She was reaching for a volume on the top shelf, stretching on her toes, and the shelf lowered.

Not dramatically. Not a visible movement — more as though the proportions of the wall had quietly adjusted so that the top shelf was reachable where it hadn't been before. She got the volume, pulled it down, and didn't appear to notice that the architecture had rearranged itself to accommodate her height.

Draco set her tea on the table and sat down with his and did not comment. He had spent twenty-five years in this house. He had never seen a shelf lower for anyone.

---

He spent the morning in a different part of the Manor, in the study his father had used and that Draco used now for correspondence and for the particular kind of sitting that was not quite brooding and not quite rest. The Dark Mark ached — a low, restless pulse that had nothing to do with the old signal and everything to do with whatever the magic in it was becoming. It had been worse in the night, worse enough to wake him, and his forearm had that hot-wire feeling along the lines of the brand that meant the skin was inflamed again even though the surface looked the same as always.

He rolled his sleeve up and looked at it. The skull and serpent, grey-black against his pale skin, the lines still sharp after all these years. Curse marks didn't blur. They didn't fade or soften at the edges the way a tattoo would. The Dark Mark looked the same as it had the night Voldemort pressed his wand to Draco's forearm in the study at Riddle House while Draco's father held his shoulder and Draco bit through the inside of his cheek hard enough to taste blood and didn't scream because his father was watching and his father did not raise sons who screamed.

He rolled his sleeve back down. The fabric was cool against the inflammation and it helped slightly and he sat in his father's chair in his father's study and didn't think about anything useful.

The house was different. He had been aware of it before Granger arrived — a restlessness in the corridors, rooms he hadn't visited in months opening unexprompted, the library reorganizing with more frequency than usual. He had attributed it to the Mark's reactivation, or to the general magical decay that was making everything in Britain slightly wrong, or to the fact that houses, even sentient ones, had moods.

But the changes since her arrival were specific. The warming of the east corridor. The library doors. The shelf that lowered. The hum he could feel in his chest when she was in the room. These were not the Manor's general restlessness; they were directed, oriented toward a particular person who had been in the house for less than a week and who the house had apparently decided to accommodate with a thoroughness it had never shown its own heir.

He resented this. The resentment was familiar — it had the same flavor as his resentment of the name, the legacy, the assumption that the house was his inheritance when the house had always felt more like a custodial arrangement in which he was the least important party. He had grown up being told the Manor was his, would be his, was built for him and his children and their children. But the Manor had never once opened both library doors for him at the same time.

There was a knock at the study door. He recognized the knock — careful, rhythmic, the way Granger knocked on everything, three evenly spaced raps like a diagnostic. She was thorough even in the way she announced herself.

"Come in."

She pushed the door open — she had to push it; it didn't open for her here, in his father's study, which was something — and stood in the doorway holding a text she'd found.

"I think this is a diagnostic manual," she said. "For curse marks. It's old, but the methodology section is detailed. It references examination techniques I haven't seen in the standard literature." She paused. "Your great-great-grandmother wrote it, apparently."

"Cassiopeia."

"There's an inscription. She was a Healer?"

"Of a kind. She was — " He stopped, because the answer involved too much family history and because Granger was looking at him with an expression of genuine curiosity that wasn't about the text. She was looking at the study. At him in the study. He realized she hadn't been in this room before and she was registering his father's desk, his father's shelves, the weight of a room arranged by someone who was in prison and maintained by someone who had changed nothing.

"She didn't practice publicly," he said. "She was a woman in the 1870s. She researched privately."

"Of course she did." Granger said it without bitterness, which surprised him. She looked at the text she was holding with more tenderness than the observation warranted. "She was good. The methodology is rigorous. It's going to be useful for the Mark work."

"Good."

She nodded and left, pulling the door closed behind her. It latched with the ordinary click of an ordinary latch. No special treatment, no warm stone, no humming. His father's study, at least, was still his.

He sat with that small, pathetic consolation and then got up because sitting with it felt worse than moving.

The afternoon was a series of small observations that accumulated into something he couldn't ignore. He went to the west gallery and the lights came up a beat late, the way they always did — the gallery had been temperamental since his mother left, responsive but grudging, as though it couldn't be bothered. He walked back through the central hall and the temperature was neutral, neither warm nor cold, the Manor's baseline indifference to his passage.

Then Granger crossed the central hall on her way from the library to the kitchen — she'd started using the kitchen, making sandwiches from the stores the house-elves kept stocked, eating at the long table because the dining room was too formal for one person eating a sandwich and because she was Hermione Granger and she would eat where it was practical or not at all — and the hall warmed. He felt it. A slow bloom of heat in the stone under his feet, radiating upward through his shoes, and the light shifted, the November grey in the tall windows softening into something almost golden. The change lasted the thirty seconds it took her to cross the hall and then it faded, not back to cold but to that flat neutral that now felt, by contrast, like being ignored.

She hadn't noticed. She was reading as she walked — a habit he was beginning to catalogue, along with the way she pushed her hair behind her left ear when she was concentrating and the fact that she took her tea with milk and no sugar and that she sometimes mouthed words to herself when she was working through a difficult translation. He was cataloguing her and he knew he was cataloguing her and the knowing didn't make him stop.

The house was cataloguing her too. That was the difference. Whatever the Manor was doing — warming for her, rearranging for her, opening and adjusting and humming in its stones when she walked through — it was the behavior of a house that had found something it wanted. Draco had lived in this house his entire life and the house had never wanted him. Tolerated him, maintained him, provided the specific tepid reliability of an institution that would not let its heir freeze or starve but would not go out of its way to make him feel welcome either.

He thought about this while he pretended to read in the smaller sitting room, which was the room he actually used because the formal sitting rooms felt like display cases. He thought about how the house had always been warmer in his mother's wing, how it had closed off his father's wing entirely after the arrest, how the corridors to the drawing room —

He did not think about the drawing room.

The smaller sitting room was cool and the light was grey and outside the window the grounds were going brown with autumn's last work. A good day for self-pity, if he'd been the type, which he was not. He was the type who sat in reasonable rooms and noticed things and felt faintly resentful of a house and did nothing about any of it.

---

His mother arrived at half three, which was her habit on Tuesdays. She came by Floo — the connection was unreliable lately but she treated its intermittent failures as a personal affront rather than a systemic crisis, which was the most Narcissa Malfoy response to institutional collapse that Draco could imagine. She stepped out of the fireplace in the small parlour, brushed a non-existent speck of soot from her sleeve, and looked at the room the way she always looked at the Manor: the specific survey of someone who had lived in this house for a quarter century and still checked on it the way you'd check on a large animal you'd once been responsible for.

"The parlour fire is higher than usual," she said. It was her version of hello.

"I hadn't noticed."

"You wouldn't." She touched the mantelpiece, her fingertips trailing along the stone. It was something she'd always done — touching the house, making contact with its surfaces the way another person might touch a wall for balance. But it wasn't for balance. Draco had understood since he was old enough to understand anything about his parents' marriage that his mother's relationship with the Manor was its own separate thing, older and more complicated than whatever she and his father had built together. She had lived in this house from the age of twenty and the house knew her hands.

"Tea?" he said.

"Please."

He went to make it and she walked. This was also habit — she didn't sit down immediately when she arrived, she circled. Through the parlour, into the corridor, her heels precise on the stone. He could hear her moving while he waited for the kettle, that particular rhythm of a woman who knew every room and was checking on all of them.

When he brought the tea back she was standing in the doorway of the corridor that led to the central hall, her hand resting on the doorframe. She was very still.

"You have a guest," she said.

"I told you about the arrangement."

"You told me about the arrangement." She turned from the doorway and took the tea from him and her face was composed in the specific way that meant she was choosing what to show. "I didn't expect the house to know about it quite so thoroughly."

"What do you mean?"

She sipped her tea instead of answering, which was her preferred method of not answering, and walked into the parlour and sat in the chair she'd sat in for twenty-five years and that the house had kept in the same position despite rearranging everything around it. The chair was a fixed point. Draco had asked his father about it once and his father had said something about the Manor respecting tenured occupants, which had been both true and insufficient.

"The corridor is different," Narcissa said, after a pause long enough to establish that she would answer on her terms. "The one from here to the central hall. It used to turn left at the portrait of your great-uncle Septimus. Now it goes straight."

"It does that. It rearranges."

"It rearranges," she repeated, and the repetition was not agreement. She looked at him over her teacup. "It has rearranged to make the path from the guest wing to the library shorter. The path from this parlour to the library is also shorter. The path from your study to the library is shorter." She paused. "You see the pattern."

He did. The house was reorganizing around the library, pulling its rooms closer, tightening its geography around the space where Granger worked. The corridors that connected to the library were straightening, shortening, losing the deliberate indirection that the Manor usually maintained between its spaces. The house was making it easier for everyone to get to where she was.

"The house likes company," Narcissa said. She said it lightly, the way she said things she didn't mean lightly. "It has been a long time since anyone used the library with real purpose."

"I use the library."

"You sit in the library." She said this without cruelty, or at least without what she would consider cruelty. It was an observation. He sat in the library; Granger worked in the library. The distinction was real and he felt it like a thumb on a bruise.

He drank his tea. The parlour was warmer than it had been when he'd walked in twenty minutes ago, which he attributed to the fire and not to his mother's presence, because attributing it to his mother's presence meant thinking about the house responding to people and he was already thinking about that too much.

"She has the Mark work well in hand," he said, because it was the kind of thing you said. "The first examination was — thorough."

Narcissa's face did something brief and controlled that he couldn't fully read. His mother had opinions about the Mark that she had never stated directly — that it was an obscenity, that it was his father's fault, that she had watched them brand her sixteen-year-old son and done nothing because doing nothing was how she survived and she would carry that weight forever without discussing it. He knew all of this without her having said it, which was how most of what he knew about his mother worked.

"I'm glad," she said, and then she stood, which meant the tea conversation was ending and the walking-through-the-house portion of the visit was beginning. This was also habit. She came, they sat, she walked, she left. The middle portion — the walking — was the actual purpose. She was checking on the Manor the way a retired keeper checks on a facility. Making sure the walls were where they should be. Making sure the doors opened correctly. Making sure the thing she'd lived with for so long was behaving itself.

He followed her, because she expected him to and because he wanted to see what she saw.

She moved through the central hall without comment — he watched her note the temperature, the quality of the light, the stone's warmth, in a series of quick proprioceptive checks that looked like walking but were actually a full diagnostic. She paused at the bottom of the main staircase and put her hand on the newel post. Her thumb moved against the carved stone. She tilted her head slightly, the way she did when she was listening for something he couldn't hear.

"Hmm," she said, and continued up the stairs.

The first floor corridor. The gallery. She touched the wall at the junction where the gallery met the east wing and her hand stilled.

"The draught," she said.

"It's gone."

"Yes." She pressed her palm flat against the stone. She was quiet for several seconds. "Since when?"

"A few days."

She didn't ask what had changed a few days ago. She didn't need to.

They turned the corner toward the library wing and Narcissa stopped. There was a door in the corridor that should not have been there — a narrow dark-wood door, set into the stone between two sconces, in a stretch of wall that had been blank stone for as long as Draco could remember. The door had a brass handle, slightly tarnished, and the wood was old — older than the surrounding stonework, as though the door had been here first and the wall had grown around it.

Narcissa looked at the door. And Draco, watching his mother's face, saw it: not surprise. Recognition. The brief, controlled settling of features that meant she was seeing something she had expected to see eventually, something she had perhaps hoped not to see, something she understood.

"Mother."

"Leave it," she said. Her voice was steady but her hand was on the wall and her fingertips were white where they pressed the stone.

"That door wasn't here this morning."

"No." She removed her hand from the wall. She turned away from the door. Her composure reassembled itself in the time it took her to blink, so completely that if he hadn't been watching her face at the precise moment she'd seen the door he would have thought she was simply uninterested. "It was not."

"You've seen it before."

"The house has rooms that open when conditions change," she said, and this was an answer to a different question than the one he'd asked. "When I first came to live here, after the wedding, rooms appeared that had been closed for years. Your grandmother told me it was the house welcoming me. I found that explanation charming at the time."

"And now?"

"And now I find it insufficient." She walked past the door without touching it, which was notable because she had touched every other surface in the house. "The house has preferences. I would not call them opinions."

She said this with finality — the single declarative that ended a line of inquiry, the sentence that was a closed door of its own. He knew this mode. Pushing against it produced nothing except his mother's silence and the particular quality of her displeasure that felt worse than being shouted at.

They continued past the library — the doors were closed now, Granger presumably still inside — and Narcissa's route took them through the guest wing corridor, which she walked slowly, trailing her hand along the wall. She stopped again, this time at a window alcove where the stone had a different quality, warmer and smoother, almost polished.

"She is comfortable here?" Narcissa asked.

"She's working."

"That isn't what I asked."

He thought about it. He thought about Granger in the library that morning, unsurprised by a book that had moved to her table overnight, correcting him with the easy precision of someone who felt secure enough in a space to argue about it. He thought about the sandwich she'd made at lunch, moving through the kitchen with an ease that had taken him years to develop and that she'd managed in days. He thought about the way she'd looked at his father's study — registering but not judging, taking in the room as data rather than spectacle.

"I think so," he said. "As comfortable as anyone could be, given — " He stopped. Given what. Given that she'd been tortured in this house. Given that the person who'd tortured her was his aunt and that he'd stood against the wall and watched. Given that she was sleeping in a guest wing down the corridor from a room whose existence he could not think about without his lungs tightening.

"Yes," Narcissa said, and the way she said it suggested she had heard the rest of the sentence in his silence. "Given."

They had circled back to the small parlour. His mother collected her cloak from the stand where the house had placed it — Draco hadn't hung it there, the stand had been empty when she arrived and then it was there with the cloak draped neatly and who knew if the house had done that or if she had and he simply hadn't seen.

"The house is not malicious," Narcissa said, fastening the cloak at her throat with the silver clasp that had been her mother's. She said it the way she said important things: once, quietly, without emphasis.

"I know."

"But it is not neutral." She looked at him. Her eyes, which were his eyes — the grey, the specific grey that ran through the Black line and the Malfoy line and met in his face — were steady and serious and giving him nothing he could use. "Those are not the same comfort."

She kissed his forehead, which she had done since he was small and which he endured because refusing it would have been worse than the brief, embarrassing tenderness. Then she stepped into the Floo, said her destination with the crisp certainty of someone who expected the network to function through sheer force of will, and disappeared in green flame.

He stood in the parlour for a while after she left. The fire subsided to its normal level — lower, now, without her presence to justify the display. The room cooled. The house was settling around him with the familiar ambient indifference that was not exactly neglect and not exactly comfort — the tolerance of a large old structure for the small creature it was obligated to shelter.

He went back to the corridor where the door had appeared. It was still there. Dark wood, brass handle, the faint smell of old varnish and something underneath it that might have been cedar or might have been nothing. He put his hand on the handle and turned it. The handle gave slightly and then stopped, not locked but resistant, the way a door resists when the room behind it isn't ready or isn't sure.

He let go. The handle settled back to its resting position. The door remained, neither opening nor closing, neither welcoming nor refusing. It simply existed in a space where it hadn't existed that morning, and the fact of its existence was a question to which his mother had the answer and would not give it.

He went downstairs. The library doors were open again. From the corridor he could see Granger at the long table, lit by the amber glow of the reading lamps that the house had quietly adjusted to the warm end of the spectrum — the temperature of light she worked best in, apparently, which the Manor had determined independently and implemented without consultation. She was writing notes in the margin of a scroll with a self-inking quill she'd brought from home, her handwriting small and dense and certain.

He didn't go in. He stood in the corridor and watched the doors that had opened for her and the light that had warmed for her and the house that had reorganized its corridors and lowered its shelves and surfaced its texts and spent the warmth of its old stones to make itself ready for her, and he thought about what it meant that the thing he'd inherited — the house, the name, the weight of all of it — had recognized something in her before he had been willing to look closely enough to see it himself.

Not that he'd recognized her. That was the wrong word. She was Granger: precise, relentless, too thorough, impossible to argue with about matters of accuracy. She was the woman who'd been tortured on his drawing room floor and who had come back to his house anyway because there was work to do and she would always choose work over self-preservation and that was either admirable or pathological and he wasn't sure it mattered which.

He didn't go in. He went back upstairs, to his rooms, where the corridors were their normal length and the stone was its normal temperature and the house was its normal self — present, indifferent, his. The Mark ached on his forearm, the restless pulse that had no signal and no source, and he lay on his bed in the grey November light and felt the house settle around him like something waiting.

Below him, two floors down, the library hummed.
