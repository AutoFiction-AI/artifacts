# Chapter 10

Draco made tea at six in the morning because the alternative was his ceiling and the running inventory of things he'd said wrong in the drawing room.

The kitchen was the oldest part of the Manor that still worked like a kitchen: stone floor, scarred counters, a kettle that had sat on the same stretch of counter since before his grandfather's time. He used the Muggle one now. The enchanted kettle needed a heating charm. His had been unreliable for weeks, stuttering mid-boil or leaving the water lukewarm in the cup. Better to manage a manual flame than explain to Granger that his household magic was failing along with everyone else's.

He didn't think about the drawing room.

He poured water over the tea bag, counted, removed it, and added milk, less than he wanted, because the cold-box enchantment was struggling and they were running low.

He thought about the drawing room.

What he'd said. What she'd said. Her voice when she'd told him what she remembered, precise enough to make every word feel chosen with tweezers, while her hands shook around the teacup. He'd stood in the doorway with every possible sentence available to him and chosen a defence so thin it had embarrassed them both.

On the stairs, the wood complained at the third step, then the seventh, then the landing. She came in wearing a jumper that didn't fit and hair that had lost the argument with sleep.

She should not have been awake. She worked until one or two most nights and treated dawn like a personal insult. He had never seen her in the kitchen before seven.

"Morning," she said.

"Morning."

Her glance went to the kettle. His followed hers. She took down a mug from the hook.

"There's water," he said.

"Thanks."

Rather than ask, she made her own tea. He already knew how she took it: milk, no sugar, steeped too long because she forgot about it while thinking about something else. She sat at the kitchen table, scarred oak older than most of the doors in the house, and wrapped both hands around the mug. He took the chair across from her.

"I didn't sleep," she said. It came out like weather.

"No."

"You?"

"Hardly."

A nod. She drank and looked toward the window, where December was beginning to show itself along the tree line.

"Your heating charm," she said, glancing back at the Muggle kettle.

"Unreliable."

"I can look at it. If you want."

He nearly said no. He nearly told her he was managing.

"All right," he said.

She pulled it toward her, turned it over in her hands, and ran a diagnostic with the quick economy of someone whose wand hand knew its work too well to think about it. He looked at the window while she worked.

"It's not degrading on its own," she said. "The enchantment's solid. It just isn't getting enough ambient throughput. General decay." A tap of the wand, a short murmur, then she set it back down. "That'll hold for a month. Maybe six weeks."

"Thank you."

"Mm-hm."

He made another pot and brought her a cup without asking. She took it without comment. They drank with the kitchen settling around them: old wood, the faint whistle from the gap above the window, the tick of the cooling kettle.

"This is better," she said, lifting the mug.

---

After that, breakfast happened in the kitchen. Dinner did too. The first evening she said, almost absently, that eating alone made food boring, and after that he started turning up at seven without waiting to see whether she'd meant anything by it. When she forgot meals in the library, he brought her a plate and set it far enough from the texts to keep her from ruining both the food and the research. When his heating charm failed again, she fixed it again.

The work kept moving. She was building an anchoring theory out of rot, war damage, and centuries of Malfoy engineering. Septimus's notation still refused to behave. The magical decay still did not care whether they were getting better at living in the same house. Sentences ran longer before he cut them off. She stopped pulling herself back into formality every time one of them said something that belonged to actual speech instead of professional exchange.

Soon there were books left on his chair with notes in her atrocious handwriting. Eating at seven became routine. The library door stayed open. Concentration pulled her eyebrows together until strangers would have mistaken it for anger. Frustration made her tap the page with one finger as if the text were being stubborn on purpose. When she found a line that mattered, she went still for one held breath before writing it down hard enough to scar the parchment.

Septimus Malfoy's opinion of French magical theorists had dragged a laugh out of her one afternoon, sudden and unguarded, and the sound hit the ceiling hard enough to make him look up as if he'd heard a spell misfire. She caught him looking. He said, "Family tradition. We narrowed it down to the Normans," and got a second, shorter laugh for his trouble. He kept both.

During the examinations the Mark went quiet under her hands. Outside them it went back to its restless ache. He was not going to think very hard about the difference.

On Tuesday night she fell asleep over a translation.

The quill dropped first. A minute later her head followed, cheek on her forearm, breathing gone slow and even over the desk. The fire had burned low. He should have woken her. He should have told her the desk would destroy her neck and sent her upstairs. Instead he set the quill in the rest so it wouldn't bleed across three days of work, rebuilt the fire, and took the old wool throw from the back of his chair.

She stirred when he laid it over her shoulders, not enough to wake. He stayed standing beside the desk until her breathing settled again. Then he sat down and remained there for twenty minutes without reading a word.

He had spent years learning not to want. Not company. Not absolution. Not a future large enough to be taken away again. The lesson had held because the world had made it easy. A life built around wanting less was a life with fewer exits for disaster. It was small and quiet and survivable.

Now there was a woman asleep in his library with his mother's throw over her shoulders, and survivable did not feel like the same thing as safe.

At last he left. The house shortened the corridor to his rooms as if it had opinions about any of this. He ignored that too.

* * *

Theo arrived on a Thursday, because Theo arrived on whichever day suited Theo.

The Floo stuttered and then deposited him in the entrance hall with ash on his coat and irritation arranged into something elegant. The coat was new, dark and well-cut. Theo had always been good at presentation. Post-war Slytherin life, he'd once said, ran on the principle of looking like you belonged in rooms that did not want you until the rooms forgot to check.

"You have a guest," he said.

"Granger's working in the library. The archive has texts no one else can access."

"Granger." Theo brushed ash from his lapel. "As in Hermione Granger. The Hermione Granger who's been the subject of three gossip columns this month about why she's spending all her time in Wiltshire."

"She's researching the decay."

"Right. Are you going to feed me, or should I have eaten first?"

He was staying for lunch. He was staying long enough to make sure Draco had been eating, and both of them knew it. By the time Draco put the kettle on, Theo was leaning against the counter with the ease of someone who had stood in this kitchen through school holidays, house arrest, and the long humiliating year after the war when almost everyone else had decided the Manor was contagious.

"The network's worse," Theo said. "I tried to visit on Tuesday and ended up in a pub in Berkshire."

"Were you confused?"

"I adapted. The bitter was decent." He took the tea Draco handed over. "My contact at Floo Regulation says they're running half capacity and still losing ground."

"Granger thinks it's structural. War damage to the anchoring."

"Granger thinks," Theo said mildly. "You talk about what she thinks."

Draco ignored that. "How's the business?"

"Slowly." Theo blew across the tea. "A few consulting contracts. Everything documented six ways because my surname still makes people reach for extra paperwork. Diagon Alley rejected the storefront application again. Polite letter this time."

"The space was let?"

"The space was not let. The windows are still papered over."

He said it evenly, which meant he was angrier than the tone allowed. Theo carried the Nott name the way Draco carried Malfoy: with constant awareness of what it cost to sign it at the bottom of anything public. The difference was that Theo kept walking into the world with it. Draco had mostly stopped walking anywhere.

"You should talk to Granger," he said. "She'd know people."

"I'm not asking Hermione Granger to solve my commercial tenancy."

"She'd do it."

"I'm sure she would. That's not the point."

The kitchen door opened.

Granger stopped in the doorway, surprised to find voices. She had ink on her fingers and a page half-folded in one hand.

"Oh," she said. "I didn't realise you had company."

"Granger, Theodore Nott. Theo, Granger."

"We were in the same year," she said, recovering quickly. She crossed the kitchen and offered her hand. "I don't think we ever actually spoke."

"We didn't." Theo shook it. "Different social circles. Mine being the socially untenable ones."

Her mouth twitched. "I remember your Potions marks. They were better than mine in sixth year."

"Finally. A woman of accuracy."

Draco watched her laugh at that and discovered, with more irritation than the moment justified, that Theo could get there in under a minute.

"I hear you're solving the magical decay from Draco's library," Theo said.

"Trying to understand it. Solving it would be ambitious."

"She's being modest," Draco said.

Both of them looked at him. So he said the first practical thing he could find.

"I was going to make lunch. Stay."

They stayed.

Lunch was sandwiches, because the cold-box was failing and the kitchen had stopped pretending otherwise. The conversation moved from Floo failures to anchoring theory to Theo's storefront search to the fourteenth-century text Granger had been fighting all morning. Theo interrupted her twice. She interrupted him back three times.

She was sharper with Theo than she was with Draco. Not unkind. Just quicker to test him. Theo met her there, dry and alert, and between them Draco got to watch two very intelligent people refuse to let the other get away with vagueness.

"The licensing issue is systemic," Granger said at last, setting down half a sandwich. "Not anecdotal. If they're asking for extra documentation from old Slytherin names, there'll be a pattern in the files."

"There's a pattern," Theo said. "There's also no appetite for fixing it while the children of convicted Death Eaters are the ones being inconvenienced."

"That doesn't make it acceptable."

"No, it doesn't." Theo set his mug down. "But being right about it hasn't helped me get a lease."

A beat, then she said, "I know three people at Enforcement. If you want names."

Theo looked at her for a moment. "I'll think about it."

After lunch Granger gathered her pages and her tea. At the door she looked back at Theo and said, "It was good to meet you. Properly."

"Likewise."

"Come back whenever you want. The Manor seems to stabilise the Floo better from this end."

Then she was gone, leaving the kitchen quieter than it had been ten seconds earlier.

Theo waited until her footsteps had faded.

"Don't," Draco said.

"I haven't said anything."

"You're about to."

Theo let that pass. "How's the Mark?"

This was not what he had been about to say. Draco took the escape anyway.

"Worse in some ways. Better in others."

"Helpful."

"It aches more. More consistently. But during the examinations, when she's doing the diagnostic work, it settles." He looked at his untouched tea. "She doesn't know why. That bothers her more than it bothers me."

Theo was quiet for a moment. With Theo, silence usually meant calibration. This one did not.

"I'm going to say something," Theo said, "and then I'm getting in the fireplace."

"Go on, then."

Theo stood and reached for his coat. "You look different."

"Different how?"

"Like you —" He stopped. Buttoned the coat. "You've been walking through this house for seven years like you were waiting to be told to leave." He pulled the collar straight. "You don't look like that today."

"Theo."

"The Floo should hold if I go now. If I end up in Berkshire I'll owl you from the pub."

He stepped into the hearth and vanished in green flame.

Ash drifted down across the grate. Draco took the empty mugs into the kitchen, washed them, set the kettle on, and made a fresh cup without asking whether she wanted one.

In the library she was back on the floor by the south window, pages spread around her knees. She glanced up when he came in, looked once at his face, and held out a hand for the tea before he had fully crossed the room.

"Did he make it out?" she asked.

"Probably to Berkshire first."

That got a breath of laughter. She moved a stack of books off his chair with her foot and bent back over the text.

He sat.
