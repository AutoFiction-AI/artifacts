# Chapter 3

For this, she'd overprepared. Of course she had. Three parchments of diagnostic protocols lay beside two standard Dark-object assessment frameworks and a sequence of probing charms she'd modified to account for the layered binding described in Baruffio's monograph on voluntary curse-binding. The literature assumed a compliant subject, adequate workspace, controlled light. It did not assume sustained contact with Draco Malfoy.

Across from her, at the oak table in the study adjoining the library, he sat with his left sleeve rolled to the elbow and his forearm laid flat between them like evidence entered into a file.

Morning light made the room usable. That was why she'd chosen it: east-facing windows, a sturdy table, neutral ground between his rooms and the library she'd claimed as workspace. Notes, wand, and annotation quill waited in strict order.

"I'll need to touch your arm," she said. "Sustained contact. Some of the probing charms need a physical anchor."

"I assumed."

"I'm telling you so you're not startled."

"I won't be startled." His gaze stayed on the far wall.

A chair leg scraped when she moved closer. From this distance the Mark looked nothing like the wartime photographs or Ministry records. In those images it had been vivid, almost aggressive: skull, serpent, ink-black lines that moved as if they had appetite. On his forearm it had faded toward dark grey. Not gone. Nothing about a Dark Lord's brand simply went away. But the black had softened, the edges had blurred, the serpent's motion had slowed to a tired twitch. It looked like a bruise that had been healing for years and then stopped.

At her touch, the useful fact was tension. Shoulders locked, jaw set, breath measured too carefully to pass for natural. Under her hand his forearm held itself rigid while she turned it into the light, every muscle braced against stillness. The skull and serpent curved with the arm's actual lines, ink laid over tendon and bone like a stain that had learned the shape of him.

"First pass is a general curse-structure analysis," she said, because protocol required explanation and silence was starting to gather mass. "It shouldn't hurt. You might feel warmth or a mild buzzing."

No answer came. None was necessary.

Amber light settled over his forearm as the first diagnostic charm resolved the curse's outer structure into something legible. Voldemort's magic carried the fingerprint she'd expected: cold, layered, built to bind loyalty through pain. The surface reading was unremarkable. Through the contact point came the low wrongness of the curse itself, a vibration just below perception.

The second probe reached the middle layers. Those were stranger. Beneath the brand sat the remnants of a communication architecture, a dead network that had once connected every Death Eater to its maker. Voldemort was dead. The infrastructure remained, channels that had carried signal and now carried nothing. Without looking up, Hermione annotated the page.

For the third pass she used her own modification, designed to touch the deepest layer: the binding between curse and bearer, where the Mark met Draco's magical core.

The Mark moved.

Not the sluggish serpentine drift of the surface. The dark magic in the deep layer turned toward her probe like iron drawn to a magnet. The lines on his forearm deepened by a shade. The serpent lifted its head.

"Granger."

"I see it. Hold still."

Her grip tightened by a fraction as she sent the charm deeper. The response intensified. Dark curse-marks resisted examination. They deflected, lashed out, or went inert. This one reached. Magic flowed toward her wandwork instead of away from it, and beneath her hand his arm, rigid from the first touch, went slack.

His breathing broke pattern. The controlled rhythm gave way to something rougher, less managed. Under sustained contact the faded grey of the Mark darkened toward black, and the restless ache he'd described the day before, pain without signal, channels trying to carry magic nowhere, stopped. Pain could not be seen, but release could. It went through his forearm at once, obvious as a door unlatching.

Quiet settled over the Mark. Months, perhaps, since it had been this still. The change had started when she touched him and deepened when her magic reached the bottom layer. For one brief, inadmissible instant, the nearest word was rest.

That word did not go into the notes. Three more passes did. Each confirmed the same impossible pattern: a curse structure more layered than the literature allowed for, a deep binding still active seven years after the caster's death, and a measurable differential response to her magic alone.

When she finally let go, the absence of contact felt too visible. Her quill scratched three lines across the parchment and caught on the fourth because looking at the page was easier than looking at his arm, or the place inside her hand that still remembered it.

"The curse structure is more layered than the literature accounts for," she said. "I'll need multiple sessions. The response pattern isn't documented."

"Meaning?"

"The deep layer reacted to my probe. It wasn't hostile. It was responsive." For a beat she considered giving him the crossed-out word from the margin and chose against it. "I need to review the readings before I say more."

His sleeve came down. With the Mark covered, the room seemed to exhale. He rose carefully and flexed the arm once, testing whether the pain had truly gone or was only waiting.

"Same time tomorrow?" she asked. Logistics were safer than interpretation.

"If you need it."

"I do."

Footsteps carried him into the corridor and then vanished before the distance made sense. The house had been doing that with echoes since her arrival. So had her attention.

East light moved across the table. On the page before her, *recognition* appeared in the margin, got crossed out at once, and became *anomalous differential response.* Defensible, certainly. A lie too. No, not a lie exactly. A dodge.

Dark magic, in her experience, was hostile or inert. Cursed jewelry did not welcome examination. Hexed artifacts did not lean toward a wand. A boggart-infested wardrobe did not quiet under touch. No framework she trusted covered what the Mark had just done. No framework she knew at all, perhaps, but she wasn't prepared to write *perhaps* in the margin either.

---

The library saved her from dwelling on it.

Reputation had undersold the Malfoy collection. Every serious researcher in Britain knew it existed through rumor, provenance stamps, and the occasional item that surfaced elsewhere and clearly had not begun there. Standing inside it was something else entirely.

Two stories of shelved walls rose around a mezzanine gallery. Rolling ladders clung to brass tracks. Reading tables sat in long shafts of autumn light. Old paper and dust held the air, with preservation charms underneath, mineral and faintly metallic after centuries of use. On the nearest shelf, cracked household accounts from the early eighteenth century leaned against a grimoire whose spine hummed softly when she passed.

The southeast corner was arbitrary, but arbitrary beat paralysis. Out came the Department of Mysteries framework: classification by subject, subclassification by language, notation for preservation state, age, and provenance where identifiable.

Within an hour, the framework had grown two new headings. *Resistance to diagnostic magic* for texts that refused to be sorted and deflected her identification charms with patient stubbornness. *Unknown script* for the mezzanine alcove where three full shelves were written in a notation she'd never seen, angular and precise, ink still wet-looking on parchment gone brittle with age. A copying charm captured the symbols for later.

No sane organizing logic held the room together. Generations of Malfoys had simply acquired. Astronomical journals sat beside a crate of loose letters in French. Alchemical texts shared a shelf with what looked like a complete run of a seventeenth-century magical periodical she'd thought lost. An entire row held bestiaries, moving illustrations pressing against the page as she passed: Graphorn, Demiguise, a tiny clawed creature she couldn't identify in that brief glance. Between two cookbooks sat a first-generation copy of Flamel's notation on anchor-point theory, and the shock of it sent her to the floor hard enough that dust rose around her skirts. Her hands were shaking, not from the morning's examination but from the specific vertigo of holding a text she'd been told no longer existed.

Water had touched one corner of the Flamel manuscript sometime in the last century, and a cramped marginal hand had annotated half the pages in writing she couldn't yet read. None of that diminished the prize. The anchor diagrams were intact. So were the proofs for ley-line stabilization and the sections Flamel had judged too theoretical to publish in his lifetime. For several stolen minutes the world narrowed to ink, parchment, and theorem. Manor, Mark, Draco, all of it vanished behind the problem.

By midday the upper gallery had changed. A section of shelving she hadn't touched shifted several inches toward the nearest window, angling its spines into the light as if presenting them. The fact went into memory and nowhere else. Cataloguing mattered more than interpretation.

Dust greyed her fingers. Sorted piles ringed the floor: readable texts, unreadable texts, resistant texts, texts that needed preservation before handling. Eleven pages of notes later, the southeast corner was still mostly untouched.

Weeks had been absurd. Months was closer. One researcher wasn't going to process a collection this large, this disordered, this multilingual, especially not with every resistant volume demanding its own negotiation. She adjusted the timetable in her head and felt a quick, sour flare of irritation at herself for not seeing the scale sooner.

Late sun turned the west windows gold. In that light the library felt briefly simple: stone, wood, leather, dust hanging visible in the air, a room built for work and very good at making work the center of a life.

Only when she stood for tea did her body object. Hunger first, then the pull in her back. With the current stack reshelved and placement notes tucked into the relevant gaps, she pocketed her wand and stepped into the corridor.

Malfoy was coming the other direction with a book in one hand and a cup in the other. Dust streaked one sleeve. His hair was untidier than she'd yet seen it.

"The Flamel notation," she said, still carrying the volume against her chest. Excitement outran awkwardness. "Do you know what you've got there?"

His eyes dropped to the cover. "It's been there since before I was born. Possibly before my father was."

"It's one of maybe three copies in existence. The Ministry's been looking for one for decades."

"Then it's fortunate you checked between the cookbooks."

A joke, perhaps. His face declined to help. She moved on toward the kitchen, or whatever served as one in a house that produced meals without visible staff, and heard the library door close behind him.

---

At half past nine the Manor was quiet.

Dinner had appeared on the small table in the guest-wing sitting room, warm and proportioned with irritating accuracy. More hours in the library had followed, first by the last of the daylight, then by wandlight while she sorted the unknown-script texts by character set and ink composition. What she wanted now was her room, her bed, and the notes waiting for tomorrow's protocol revisions.

The route should have been simple. Left out of the library, down the corridor with the tall windows and the portrait of the witch in green robes, right at the staircase, up one flight, guest wing. Fifteen minutes. Two turns.

Moonlight silvered the corridor when she turned left. The tall windows were where they belonged. So was the portrait. The witch was not. In her place stood a bearded man in late-sixteenth-century robes, dark-haired and still, watching with the calm reserve of a painted figure choosing not to move.

Possible explanation: she'd misremembered. No, not misremembered. Mis-sequenced, perhaps. Tiredness. Distraction. The morning examination. Unlikely, still, but safer than the alternative, and she was trying to get to bed, not start an argument with the house.

The staircase appeared where it should. One flight up brought her to a landing with a south-facing window. Her room faced east. No straight ascent should have done that.

Outside the south window lay a formal garden she had not seen from any other angle: hedges clipped to geometric severity, a stone bench, tree line beyond. Up here the air was cooler. Underfoot, the stone felt rougher, older.

Back down the stairs, then. At the bottom waited an entirely different corridor, narrow and vaulted, belonging to another architectural century. No portrait. No windows. Blue flame burned in wall sconces she'd not seen elsewhere in the Manor, and her steps disappeared into the stone instead of echoing back.

Hostile wasn't the word. Not yet. Weather came closer: pressure in the air, in the light, in the silence. Not-danger, if she insisted on classifying it. Which was not remotely the same as safe.

Point Me spun her wand north and held. Asked for her room, it pointed left and slightly up. Twenty meters, by estimate. Twenty meters later she opened a door onto a broom cupboard full of mops, buckets, and lemon-cleaner magic old enough to have turned sharp.

That was when fear stopped being abstract.

Another corridor offered the tall windows again, but the staircase at its end led down into a hall she'd never seen, vast and cold and unused. A hunt tapestry covered one wall. As she turned away, the nearest painted hound seemed to shift its head. Backtracking produced a hallway wider than the one she'd entered and carpeted in a pattern new to her.

Eleven minutes had passed when she checked her watch.

Method survived longer than comfort. Illumination charms marked her path, except the markers kept reappearing behind her when she knew she had not doubled back. A gallery of windows overlooked a courtyard too small to be the central one. A staircase ended at three doors, none familiar. One corridor felt almost known until the sconces changed, the ceiling lifted, and the air picked up the damp vegetal sweetness of a greenhouse after dark.

Twenty minutes in, the guest wing arrived all at once. No corner turned. No door opened. One step landed in strangeness and the next in the corridor outside her room, complete with faded blue rug, crooked sconce shade, and the thin line of yellow light under her door.

By then fear had narrowed into something ugly and usable. Point Me had worked. That was the problem. The distance had been true. Or true enough. It was the space between points that wouldn't hold still.

Inside, the room presented itself exactly as she'd left it: notes on the desk, overnight bag on the chair, bed made with house-elf severity, east window intact. Once she let herself count the rest, the evidence came too quickly. The portrait. The staircase. The blue-flame corridor. The shelves angling toward light earlier. Dinner arriving unsummoned. Corridors stretching, then folding back. She reached for simpler explanations and discarded them almost as fast. Old wards. Responsive architecture. Some inherited defensive system keyed badly to guests. No. Not enough. None of that took a person for twenty minutes and then set her down outside the correct door.

It had let her out when it was finished. The Manor, then. The house. Whatever name she gave it, she was sleeping inside something that paid attention.

At the desk, tomorrow's examination notes gained a second heading beneath Dark Mark protocol revisions: MANOR VARIABLES. Under it she wrote three lines in ink pressed hard enough to score the page. No movement through the house alone after dark. Do not trust distance spells without fixed landmarks. Mark and Manor both active conditions of study.
