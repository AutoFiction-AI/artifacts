# Chapter 12

The corridor to the library was twenty-three paces on Tuesday. By Thursday it was forty-one.

Counting gave the front of Draco's mind a job while the rest of it worked itself raw. Forty-one paces. Same grey stone. Same amber sconces. Same runner underfoot. Only the distances had altered. Twice that morning he had nearly turned toward the guest wing with some excuse about a reference text or the archive. Twice he had kept walking. If Hermione wanted distance, the least he could do was stop presenting himself at her door and calling it work.

Wednesday morning sent him to the gated archive before she arrived. Testing. Pointless, because the room had always required both of them and he knew it, but he went anyway. The alcove in the library's south wall gave him blank stone. No door. No seam. Cold mineral under his palm. He stood there long enough to build and discard three opening lines: *The archive won't open.* *We need the folios.* *Are you avoiding me on purpose.* By nine he was back at his desk with a book open and his face arranged, as if the wall had told him anything useful.

Thursday produced the same result. The research they needed was still inside. So was the condition for opening it, and the condition had stopped standing next to him.

Cold followed. He knew ordinary winter in a stone house: the chill lodged in corners, climbed the legs if you stood still too long, and lifted once you got near a grate. This was not that. Fires still burned. Heating charms still held. By Thursday he had stopped asking the elves to lay fires anywhere except the rooms he meant to use and started shutting doors behind himself, as if a smaller route through the house might feel less empty. It did not.

None of it got mentioned to her.

Hermione was still in the house. She still arrived at the library at nine and left by five. Her notes sat in squared stacks now, quill cleaned, chair pushed in. A week earlier she had left books open and cardigans slung over furniture, had let herself spill into a room and remain there after she was gone. Now every trace of her disappeared at day's end. She looked less like a woman living in the Manor than a guest determined not to overstay.

He went back through every recent conversation and found nothing he could use. Three days ago they had been bent over a sixteenth-century text, shoulders nearly touching, arguing about a word that meant both *to anchor* and *to bind*. She had looked at him then with that quick bright concentration that made him feel, briefly and absurdly, adequate to her. By the next morning she was brisk, professional, present only when the work required it.

The drawing room was the obvious explanation, though not the confrontation itself. That wound had scabbed over enough to touch. What came after it might have scared her more: the week of shared meals, of books left on his chair, of tea made without asking, of ease. Perhaps she had looked at whatever had started growing between them and decided the soil in this house was too poisoned to trust.

Or perhaps it was simpler. He was a Malfoy. She was Hermione Granger. Distance had been the truest thing between those facts for years.

So he matched her retreat with one of his own. Matching rooms, tempers, and expectations was one of the few talents his family had bothered to cultivate in him. He hated every second of using it here. Saying *what happened* or *why have you gone* would have required breaking first, and Draco Malfoy had been trained too well for that.

Wednesday afternoon put them in the same corridor. An armful of books rode against her chest, her chin braced on the top volume to keep it from sliding. He had been heading for the library and stopped at the window instead, making it look as though he had something to study beyond the glass. They nodded like colleagues.

"I'll be in the library until four," she said.

"Right."

She passed him, warmth briefly following in her wake. He stayed where he was until her footsteps disappeared. Following her after that would have looked too much like asking.

Meals were worse. Without ever discussing it, they had turned the small dining room into a habit. Research talk. Talk about nothing. Hermione reading while she ate. Draco learning to judge her attention by the angle of her head and the stillness of her fork. On Thursday he told the elf not to lay a second place and hated himself for making the adjustment before she had even missed dinner. He sat alone at Narcissa's table, swallowed food the elves had made well enough, and looked at the empty chair across from him until the idea of whisky started to feel ancestral. He left the bottle where it was.

At least the Mark offered a measurable problem. The interval between examinations had stretched too far. Without her hands and her spellwork, the curse had grown restless again. It was not the old summoning burn. This was pressure building in the bones of his forearm and keeping on because it had nowhere useful to go. He started noting the hour of each flare in the margin of a catalogue sheet, as if precision could compensate for not asking her to touch it. By dinner it felt as if the Mark were remembering an argument his body had lost.

Sleep never arrived. He rebuilt the fire twice, lay down, sat up, and finally gave up on the bed altogether. The Mark had learned her touch. It knew the cadence of her spells. Three days without either had left it angry.

Above him, the ceiling gave nothing back. He lay there thinking about the last unguarded look she had given him on Monday evening, the instant before the professional mask snapped into place. Underneath it had been guilt, or fear, or both.

After midnight the curse flared again, not with the old emergency heat but with a long slow burn that spread from forearm to shoulder and stayed. The window glass was cold under his palm. Frost silvered the grounds. Around him the Manor held itself in a listening stillness that made the urge to speak to the walls feel almost reasonable. He had done that after the war, when the house was his and no one else lived in it. He had asked corridors to shorten and doors to open. He had bargained with architecture because there had been no one safer to bargain with. The impulse returning now told him enough about what Hermione's presence had changed.

Morning came without sleep. Friday found him in the library before her. She stopped in the doorway with her coat still buttoned, as if she needed an extra layer of outside between herself and the Manor, and for one suspended second the space between them was only space.

"The diagnostic series," she said. "We've let the interval run too long."

She was right. The examinations had found their own rhythm and then lost it.

"I know."

"After lunch. The usual setup."

"Fine."

Once the coat came off, she crossed to the separate table she had claimed earlier in the week and unpacked her satchel with precise, indifferent efficiency. Quill. Ink. Notes. By the time he looked back down at his text, the conversation had already been dismissed.

---

After lunch they used the worktable by the tall windows. The instruments went down in their familiar order: wand, diagnostic crystals tuned to the Mark, the probe resonator she had built herself, a clean sheet of parchment for notes. When Hermione rolled her sleeves up, the right stopped at the elbow. The left stayed lower, cuff arranged with one extra fold and more care than the task required. He had noticed the asymmetry before without understanding it.

"Sleeve."

His own left sleeve went back. The Mark looked vicious today, skull and snake cut sharp under inflamed skin. She would see the change at once.

Cold fingers closed around his wrist. Three days without contact had made even that baseline touch a jolt. He kept his face still.

"Your hands are cold."

"The library's cold."

"It is."

Her thumb settled over his pulse. Outside the windows the grounds had gone iron-grey under low cloud, hedge lines black with frost. The daylight already looked tired.

"Standard series first," she said. "Then I want to run the deeper mapping again. I've been through last week's data and the secondary decay pattern isn't... I want to confirm something."

"All right."

Blue light netted over the Mark. The curse surged once against the diagnostic charm and then went obediently still. Relief came so fast his hand unclenched on the tabletop before he could stop it.

Her eyes flicked up. "The pain's been worse."

"Manageable."

"Your knuckles were white, Malfoy."

No answer came. She had already shifted into the second spell, projecting the deeper architecture of the Mark above his skin in pale rotating layers. One hand held his wrist steady while the other guided the wand. Beside them, the self-scribing quill scratched her murmured readings across parchment.

Watching her work was still permitted. That was what the arrangement gave him. Attention narrowed her face into its own severe beauty: the line between her brows when a number came back wrong, the lower lip caught briefly between her teeth, the silent movement of translated phrases across her mouth. Sweat gathered at her temples as she pushed deeper than usual, mapping the older magic under Voldemort's brand and whatever had rooted it into the Manor's failing field.

The work made a scaffold they could sit inside. Within it they were examiner and subject again, researcher and problem, two capable people occupied by a technical difficulty. Her hand on his arm, his pulse under her thumb, the relief moving through the Mark when she touched it: all of that could pass as apparatus if he kept his thinking narrow enough.

For a few minutes he managed it.

Then the glamour on her left forearm wavered.

It was not light but surface. Air above the skin shivered as if heat had passed through it. The even tone blurred, thinned, and gave way. What came clear beneath it did not arrive all at once. It resolved by degrees, like a bottom seen through water when the current eases.

The letters had been carved, not cut. Bellatrix had taken her time. The scar tissue lay raised and silver-white against Hermione's skin, edges ragged where the cursed blade had torn instead of slicing cleanly. The word had been made to last.

MUDBLOOD.

Five letters and five more, spanning the inside of her forearm. The opening strokes bit deepest. The M still carried the hardest pressure, the blade driven down into skin pinned flat against marble. The final D ran shallower. By then Bellatrix had been laughing, the arm slick, the knife slipping. He knew that sequence because he had watched it happen from twelve feet away and failed to move.

Before thought caught up, his hand left the table and landed over the scar. Warm. Raised. Real.

Hermione flinched hard enough to break every spell she was holding. The mapping collapsed into sparks. The resonator whined and went dark. The quill scored one wild line across the parchment and stopped. Her whole body had drawn inward around the exposure, and her forearm went rigid under his palm.

"Granger."

Her gaze stayed on his hand.

"Granger, I..."

"Don't." The word came flat and over-controlled. "Don't say you're sorry."

Her grip around his wrist tightened once. He expected her to wrench away. Instead she set the wand down, caught the cuff of her left sleeve with her right hand, and dragged it higher until the last letters cleared the fabric. Then she turned the arm fully upward on the table and left it there between them, pulse hammering.

"Don't do it halfway," she said.

Only then did he move his thumb. Slowly, across the first groove of the M. The scar tissue had its own map under his skin, dense and ridged, healed only in the technical sense that the wound had stopped bleeding. He traced the U. The D. Under his thumb the letters felt like a record no spell could revise.

Her breathing changed. It did not quicken; it turned deliberate.

By then the fire and a few guttering candles carried the room. What remained of the December afternoon had gone blue at the windows and was nearly finished. In the dimness he read by touch. B. L. O. O. D.

Back at the beginning, he traced the M again. This time she made a small sound into her closed mouth, half swallowed before it became anything he could name.

"I see it," he said. His voice came out scraped raw. "Every time I close my eyes, that room is there. The floor. The chandelier. You on the floor and..." The rest stuck. Some memory doors were too heavy to open from this side. "I see it."

"I know."

"I didn't know it was still there. On you. I thought..." The assumption revealed itself only while he was making it. Seven years of believing a Healer or a spell or Hermione's own brutal competence must have erased it. "I didn't know."

She finally looked at his face instead of his hand. Tiredness made her expression almost plain.

"Where would it go?"

The question dropped straight through him. Where would it go. Where did Dark magic go after the trials, after the obituaries, after the public decided the war belonged to the past. Nowhere. It stayed in the arm Bellatrix had cut and in the body that still woke up inside it every morning.

"Dark magic doesn't heal clean," she said. "You know that."

"I know. I just wasn't..." His thumb rested on the L. "I wasn't thinking about yours."

"No. People don't."

That, more than the scar itself, carried the charge. People saw competence when they looked at Hermione Granger. Brilliance. Endurance. They did not see the system built to hide the worst thing anyone had ever done to her.

"You cover it every day."

"Every day."

"Why."

He knew the answer and asked anyway. Her mouth tightened.

"Because when people see it, they—" She stopped. Her eyes went to his hand on the word. "They do what you're doing right now. Look at it, then look at me, and their whole face changes. And it's always pity. Every time, it's pity, and I can't—" Her jaw set. "I can't be that in a room. The person it happened to. I can't think past it."

"So you hide it."

"So I hide it."

His thumb went back over the M. She let it.

"I watched," he said. The admission came out heavy and badly shaped. "I stood against the wall and watched her do it. I was sixteen. I was terrified. My father was in the room. None of that is enough. I know it isn't."

"No." Her voice held no mercy and no cruelty either. "It isn't."

Fire ticked in the grate. Wind pressed a little harder at the windows. The gap after her answer stayed open, and no better language appeared to bridge it.

"I'm not going to apologize."

Her eyes narrowed.

"Not because I don't—" His thumb pressed into the L. "If I say sorry, then you have to answer it. And then we've—it's supposed to be smaller after that. Settled. And this—" He looked at the word under his hand. "This doesn't get smaller. I don't have anything that makes it—" The sentence stalled. "There isn't a word. I'm not going to pretend there is."

The hammering in her pulse eased by degrees under his hand. Across the table her other hand still circled his wrist where it had held him for the examination. He only registered it fully when her thumb shifted once against the skin below the Mark.

"That's the first honest thing anyone's said to me about it," she said. "In seven years."

No register he possessed offered a useful reply. He left the air between them unfilled, and for once the omission did not feel like cowardice.

She moved first again. Her hand turned on his arm and settled flat over the skull and snake. The touch had nothing to do with diagnosis. Warm palm. Warm fingers. His Mark quieted under it with humiliating speed.

They stayed like that until the fire dropped to coals and the room gave itself over to dusk.

Eventually she drew back and looked at her forearm, at the word still silver against her skin.

"I should recast it," she said.

"Should you."

His two words came out level, but she heard the rest anyway. Her gaze lifted to his, then dropped once more to the scar. The glamour did not return.

"The diagnostic readings," she said after a moment. "Some of the deep-layer data came through before the spell broke. I should transcribe it while it's fresh."

"All right."

At her own table she bent over the parchment and began to write. The left sleeve stayed where she had shoved it. Each pass of the quill carried the silver letters through the dying firelight, and across the room his Mark stayed quiet.
