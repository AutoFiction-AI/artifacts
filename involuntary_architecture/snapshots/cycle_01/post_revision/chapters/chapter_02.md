# Chapter 2

The Mark woke him at four again.

Not with the old burn. Not with the fishhook drag behind his navel that had once meant summons, movement, whatever room the Dark Lord had chosen for that night's audience. That signal died with the man who cast it. What remained was slower and less useful: an arrhythmic ache deep in the bones of his forearm, magic trying to force itself through a route that no longer led anywhere, heating the sealed channel until the whole arm felt inhabited by failed intent.

In the dark, Draco lay still and waited for it to pass. The bedroom was too large for one person. Three years ago he'd moved into his parents' suite, not out of appetite for grandeur but because the alternative was the room he'd slept in at sixteen, and that room still carried the ghost of the boy who had knelt on the carpet and dry-heaved after taking the Mark. Airing it out had not helped. Redecorating had not helped. Eventually he'd shut the door and left it shut.

Around five the ache eased. He got up because there was no reason not to.

At the far end of the ground floor sat the servants' kitchen, the one he actually used. The formal kitchen belonged to a vanished household staff and made a single person feel absurd. Tea first. Then toast, eaten standing at the counter with no plate because choosing one chair at the table meant seeing the other three stay empty.

From the kitchen to the library, his body took the same route it always took: east corridor, back stair, portrait gallery, narrow passage, south entrance. The reception rooms would have been faster. He had not used them in years. Thought rarely entered into it now. His feet knew which turns kept him out of certain rooms and made them before the rest of him caught up.

Other rooms stayed closed as well. The drawing room where his father had held meetings. The conservatory where Bellatrix had once sat humming in the evenings. The second-floor study where — no. Before the second cup of tea, he was not going to start making an inventory of absences.

The library justified the house. Two stories of shelves climbed the walls. A mezzanine ran the perimeter, fitted with rolling ladders and iron rails polished by hands long dead. Afternoon light usually flooded the west-facing tables; this early, the room held only a dim grey wash and the smell it always held, old paper, dust, preservation charms worked so deeply into the bindings that the air itself seemed lacquered with them.

By the south window waited his chair, shaped to him by repetition, and on the table beside it lay the treatise he'd been worrying through for a week. Seventeenth-century sympathetic magic, dry as old plaster and no more useful than the rest. For eight months he'd looked for anything that might explain why a dead man's brand had begun to wake in his arm. St. Mungo's had refused to touch it. Two private practitioners had tried. One recoiled outright. The other finished the examination and admitted the layering was beyond her training. She'd suggested the Unspeakables with a straight face. He had laughed.

So he read. Somewhere in the family's collection there might be a reference, or a precedent, or at least a theory that did not end in shrugging helplessness. Morning after morning he pulled books, made notes, followed citations into older shelves, and found nothing.

All morning the Mark kept up its low pulse. Pressing his thumb hard against the brand sometimes blunted it for a minute or two. Lately even that had started to fail.

Winter altered the Manor in plain ways before it did anything stranger. In summer the place loosened. Curtains lifted in draughts with no source. Doors swelled in their frames. Whole wings seemed to exhale. Winter drew the walls close. Corridors narrowed by inches he could never prove. Rooms at the far ends of the house went hard with cold. By November the east wing whistled at the windows and the upper halls held onto silence long after his footsteps had passed. He did not call the house alive. Call it alive and every shutting door became an answer. He did not call it listening. Call it listening and every pause on the stair had to belong to someone. Before breakfast he preferred timber, stone, old magic, and the possibility that he was tired. This morning, with the Mark beating in his arm and the windows black, that preference held less firmly than usual.

His mother had not visited in two weeks. That was deliberate. Narcissa Malfoy did nothing by accident. Three years earlier she had moved into a townhouse in Bath, tasteful and proportionate, rooms that stayed where they were put. She'd been graceful about it. *The Manor is yours, darling. It needs someone in residence.* She had never said *I can't live there anymore*. She had only paused on the threshold, set two fingers against the doorframe, and left them there a beat too long before stepping out. Twenty-five years in this house had worn at her in ways she did not discuss. Draco had not asked for details. Asking would mean admitting the house could lay hands on people at all.

By noon the treatise had yielded nothing. Lunch was soup, bread, and an apple he forgot on a side table until the flesh began to brown against the wood. After that came the east lawn, walked because his body needed movement and the grounds were the one place large enough to cross without brushing past doors he preferred closed. At half three, the usual circuit broke.

An owl struck the library window hard enough to rattle the panes and dropped Ministry stationery onto the desk.

Department of Mysteries. Departmental seal bitten deep into the wax. Paper thick enough to feel official before he read a word of it. At the library desk, where all mail got opened, he broke the seal and found Granger's name at the foot of the page.

First reading: content. She requested access to the Malfoy library for work related to the ongoing investigation into systemic magical decay. The Ministry would provide a standard stipend for room and board. She anticipated a stay of several weeks at minimum. She required unrestricted access to all holdings, including restricted sections.

Second reading: the rest of it. She had not entered this house since the night she was dragged across the floor of a room he no longer used. The last time they had spoken was at his trial seven years earlier, when she sat in a courtroom and described, with measured accuracy, what his family had done and what he had failed to prevent. No word he might have offered then would have survived the distance between them. Silence had seemed less insulting than an inadequate apology, so silence was what he'd given her.

Beneath the formal request sat the real fact. Hermione Granger would not have written to him unless every other door had failed to open. He was the last one.

At Granger's name the Mark kicked once under his sleeve, sharp enough to make his hand jerk. His thumb went to it before he'd decided to move.

She needed the library. In return, he needed a witness who could look at the brand on his arm and not flinch.

That evening he wrote back. He accepted her terms and added one of his own.

---

On the morning she was due, Draco cleaned.

Not the whole Manor. That project belonged to someone with staff or delusions. The library got done, the guest wing got done, the kitchen got done, and so did the corridors between them. Dust lifted from shelves under his wand. Stone floors lost their film of disuse. Fresh candles replaced the stubs in the library sconces. Somewhere in the middle of it he recognized that this was the most attention he'd paid the house in over a year, and the recognition annoyed him enough that he kept working harder.

In the east arm waited the guest rooms: a bedroom, a sitting room, a private bath, all held under preservation charms and the hush of space that had gone too long unoccupied. November air came in through the window he forced open and corrected the stale stillness a little.

By eleven he was in the entrance hall because that was where guests were received, and whatever else this arrangement was, it would be conducted correctly. The hall was one of the few formal spaces he had kept up. Black-and-white marble. A ceiling built to dwarf arrivals. A staircase wide enough for four people abreast. The family had always understood entrances better than what followed them.

From the drive came the crack of Apparition. She had not used the Floo. That could have meant the network was misbehaving again, or it could have meant she had chosen the method that left a front door between them. He suspected the second.

Before she could knock, he opened it.

On the step stood Hermione Granger in a dark coat with a leather bag over one shoulder, hair pulled back and already escaping. The years had changed her and failed to change her. She was smaller than memory had made her, more contained, less mythic than the Ministry clippings and courtroom recollections he'd allowed to accumulate in her place. What remained most familiar was the expression. She had prepared for this. She knew how the first half minute was supposed to go and had come armed for it. Across the courtroom seven years ago he had watched that same preparation carry her through testimony that spared him nothing and still kept him out of Azkaban.

"Malfoy," she said.

"Granger."

No hand offered itself. The absence stood between them as plainly as any contract.

"Come in."

Past him she went, and the moment her body crossed the threshold her breath caught. Her shoulders rose under the coat, then dropped. By the time the door latched she had flattened the flinch almost out of sight. He knew the labor of that. He'd spent years smoothing his own reactions before anyone else could look at them.

Formal hush closed around them in the entrance hall. Her gaze moved over the marble floor, the sweep of the stairs, the height of the ceiling, measuring what had altered and what had not.

"The library's through the east corridor," he said. "I'll show you once we've discussed terms."

"Your letter mentioned additional conditions."

"It did."

To the morning room he led her, the closest thing the house had to neutral ground. South-facing windows. A desk used for household business by three generations of Malfoys. Tea already set out, whether from hospitality or habit he could not have said. Granger took the chair opposite without waiting to be placed. He sat behind the desk because oak between them was easier than open space.

"You'll have full access to the library," he said. "All holdings, including the restricted sections."

"Fine."

"The guest wing's in order. You've got your own rooms and a private bath. Use the kitchen whenever you like. If you need to move through the rest of the house for research, do it. I'd only ask for notice before you enter private rooms."

"That won't be a problem."

So far they were talking about rooms, keys, plumbing. He could do rooms, keys, plumbing.

"My additional condition concerns the Mark."

Across from him, she waited without moving. Her left sleeve was buttoned tight at the wrist. Habit, then. Most people would have missed why. He did not. The room seemed briefly crowded with memory anyway: her on a floor not far from here, his own uselessness standing over it, the fact that she had still come.

Without ceremony, he pushed up his left sleeve and laid his forearm on the desk.

Darkening had made the skull and serpent look newly inked. The surrounding skin was faintly raised. Along the edges, in this light, a shimmer showed where residual magic sat close to the surface.

Granger leaned forward. She did not touch him.

"Turn your wrist a little," she said.

He did.

Her gaze tracked the skull first, then the serpent's tail, then the raised skin at the edges, quick and level and unsentimental.

"It's been waking up," he said. "Not the summoning. That's gone. This is different. Pain without a signal. The magic in it is restless. It keeps trying to move and there's nowhere for it to go. St. Mungo's refused it. Two private practitioners had a look. Neither helped."

"When did it start?"

"Eight months ago. At first it was mostly at night. Now it's constant enough to be tiresome."

Another second passed while she kept looking.

"From the surface alone, I'd say the structure isn't simple residual curse activity," she said. "There are at least two layers visible here, maybe more. But that's only surface reading. If you want an actual map of the curse, I'll need proper light, time, and sustained contact."

Not now hung in the room without her needing to say it twice.

"What are you proposing?" she asked.

"You get the library for as long as you need it. In return, you examine the Mark. Properly. Diagnostic work, records, whatever you need in order to tell me what it's doing and whether it can be managed."

"You want me to study a curse two Healers wouldn't."

"I want you to study it because you're the most qualified person likely to say yes."

That landed harder than he'd intended. The real clause sat underneath it, plain enough without being spoken all the way out.

"We keep it to the work," she said.

"Of course."

"Last names. Clear boundaries around the examination work. I keep records and you see them. If I find a treatment path, I tell you. If I find a risk to you or anyone else in the house, I tell you that directly too."

"Agreed."

"And the research comes first. The library is why I'm here. The Mark work gets scheduled. No improvised diagnostics because we're already in the room together."

"Understood."

Neither of them reached for the tea. Steam climbed between them and thinned. At length he poured anyway, because the sound of liquid in porcelain gave his hands something to do. She accepted the cup and wrapped both palms around it without drinking.

"I'll show you the library first," he said. "Then the guest wing."

---

In the library doorway she stopped with one hand still curled around the brass. For one clean second she forgot to guard her face.

His usual route brought them there by the east corridor and the back passage rather than the reception rooms. If she noticed the detour she said nothing. Through the south entrance she went, then stopped just enough for the pause to register: shelves rising to the ceiling, mezzanine circling overhead, centuries of collection and theft and scholarship compressed into one room.

"Your catalogue," she said after a moment.

"Mostly nonexistent. My grandfather kept a ledger. It covers about a third of the holdings and stops in 1971."

"Restricted section?"

"North wall behind the screen. It's warded, but I'll key you in. Most of it is Dark Arts adjacent rather than outright Dark. Theory, history, magical taxonomy. Some of it is genuinely dangerous."

Already she was moving, fingertips gliding along shelf spines, head tipped to read titles. A folio came off one shelf, got opened, dismissed. Another replaced it. Then three more landed in a neat stack on the nearest table: French, Latin, English. She was not looking around anymore. She was sorting the room into use.

For a while he let her have that. Leaning against the doorframe, arms folded, he watched her forget him in favor of the books.

"This will take longer than several weeks," she said.

"I'm not going anywhere."

"No," she said. "I don't suppose you are."

The rest of the ground floor tour covered only what he meant it to cover. The morning room again. The servants' kitchen with its uneven stone floor and grey basin light. Her gaze flicked once to the single mug on the rack, once to the lone plate drying beside it, then moved on without comment.

Through the back corridor he showed her the gardens, then the passage linking kitchen wing to library. The west wing stayed unmentioned. So did the shut doors along the way. She did not ask for names.

Up the main stair, across the landing, into the east arm: the guest corridor panelled in dark wood, sconces brightening as they passed. At the end waited the suite itself. November light came through the windows thin and unforgiving, laying the room bare in a manner he found unexpectedly reassuring.

"It's adequate," she said.

"There are preservation charms on the bedding. The bath plumbing is temperamental. Sometimes the hot tap develops opinions."

A near-smile touched her mouth and went away.

"If you need anything adjusted, let me know. I'll be —" His hand took in too much house and too little life. "Around."

"Thank you, Malfoy."

"Granger."

He left her there to settle.

Ordinarily the walk back to the library went east corridor, landing, main stair, back passage, portrait gallery. At the foot of the stairs, where the passage should have bent left, a corridor stood open that had not been there that morning.

Not new. Old stone, same grey limestone as the rest of the ground floor. Not impossible either, because the Manor moved when it pleased. Still, recognition took a second. The passage had existed once. As a child he had run through it often enough to remember his mother's voice calling after him. Then, sometime in adolescence or shortly after, the corridor had gone. The house did that. Doors sealed over. Routes lengthened. Spaces dropped out of use and were absorbed.

Now the shortcut had returned, linking the base of the east stair directly to the library's north entrance.

Warm sconces lit the length of it. Air moved inside it as though the passage had just taken a breath. No dust lay on the floor. The smell waiting there was the smell of the library itself, paper and preservation charms, as if the house had swept the corridor and opened it only moments before he arrived.

This morning there had been wall. By afternoon there was access.

Within an hour of Granger crossing the threshold, the Manor had given back a route it had kept from him for years.

At the corridor mouth he stood very still. The skin at the back of his neck tightened with the old animal warning of being noticed in the dark, of something larger than himself having just swung its head toward him.

One step took him into the passage. The sconces brightened. His own footfall came back at him fuller than the echoes on his usual route, as though the stone were relearning sound.

Three steps from the reading tables, four from his chair, the corridor delivered him to the north library door and left him there.

He did not mention it.

The book on the table opened under his hand. Above him, boards in the guest wing answered her movements once, twice, then fell quiet. Behind his chair the north door stayed ajar, and the corridor beyond it remained lit as if the house expected traffic.
