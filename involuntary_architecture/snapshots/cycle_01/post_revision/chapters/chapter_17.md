# Chapter 17

The flat hadn't changed.

Five and a half weeks was long enough for London to feel blunt. Dust filmed the bedroom windows. A mug by the sink had gone chalky at the rim. The stack of post on the hall table leaned against the lamp in a pose of slow neglect rather than crisis. Her flat had waited for her without curiosity.

Three days back, what Hermione noticed most was obedience. Walls stayed where walls belonged. The corridor to the kitchen was the same length at midnight as it had been that morning. The bathroom door opened onto the bathroom every time. Nothing listened. Nothing withheld. Nothing decided which rooms she had earned.

She had chosen the place for that. After the divorce, she had wanted something she could hold in her head all at once: fourteen hundred square feet, no hidden corners, no surprises, no architecture with moods. Back then it had felt sane.

Now it felt dead.

At the Manor the library alone had been larger. Her room in the guest wing opened onto a house that shortened corridors when she worked too late and warmed the stone near her door until the cold receded from it. Here the sofa stayed grey twill and nothing more. No surface met her halfway. No wall gave anything back.

Across her left forearm the Mark still reached her. Distance had flattened the resonance into a blunt ache, surfacing at four in the morning or in the middle of brushing her teeth. In Wiltshire she could tell when Draco turned in bed upstairs. In London she got pain without detail. Whatever her sustained contact had done for the curse, leaving had undone it. Now the magic in his arm pulled against dead instructions, a binding with no master left to serve, and some degraded piece of that strain still found her from forty miles away.

From her flat in London there was nothing to do with that knowledge.

Sleep had gone useless. Not the productive sleeplessness of the library, with a text open in her lap and a live awareness of him elsewhere in the house. This was the other kind. Darkness remained darkness. Silence remained silence. Her brain only kept returning to the same loop until the words lost shape and not force.

_You knew the house was doing this and you stayed._

_After the examination, after the drawing room, after the scar._

_You knew and you didn't think I deserved to know._

He had deserved to know. She had meant to tell him. Half a dozen versions of that conversation had been built and dismantled in her head, each one collapsing at the same place: she'd kissed him while knowing there might be architecture inside what she felt, and the not wanting to stop had been the part she could not frame cleanly enough to survive saying aloud.

But his anger had not really been about the bond.

Lying awake beneath a ceiling that never changed, Hermione kept arriving at the same hard point. He had been angry about the asymmetry. She had kept one fact that altered the terms of every examination, every hour in the library, every minute after the kiss. He had been moving inside one set of terms while she already knew they might be another. Reasons existed. Verification mattered. None of that changed the shape of it.

---

Thursday morning the Floo failed outright.

There was no stutter, no dropped call, no half-functioning relay people could still bully into working. At seven forty-three Hermione stepped into her fireplace, threw the powder, said "Ministry of Magic," and watched the flames remain ordinary orange. Two more tries got her soot on the knees of her trousers and nothing else.

Apparition took her to an Atrium already sliding toward panic. Technicians crowded the grates. Voices bounced off tile in that clipped professional register people used when fear would only slow them down. The outage had spread across London, parts of Bristol, most of the West Country.

Dennis Creevey intercepted her near the lifts with a stack of incident reports hugged to his chest. Muggle trainers showed under his robes. One sleeve was scorched. He looked about nineteen until he opened his mouth.

"We've had six accidental-magic incidents since last night," he said. "Children. The youngest is four."

"Six?"

"Ambient field went strange around three. We don't know if the collapse caused the cascade or the cascade took the Floo with it." He shifted the files higher. "Two kids are in St. Mungo's. One of them set her bedroom on fire."

Her hands were steady when she took the reports. That part still worked: intake, triage, sequence, action.

The reports were not bureaucratic-bad. They were frightened-bad. A nine-year-old in Dorset had shattered every window in his house during a nightmare. Twin girls in Camden had levitated the kitchen table for forty minutes and then started breaking plates with their crying. The four-year-old in Bristol was named Ruby. She had been crying in her crib when ambient magic superheated the air around her. Ruby was in the burn ward. Her parents were Muggles. The Obliviators had told them it was an electrical fault.

Reading that on a Ministry corridor wall made the crisis leave abstraction behind. Stabilization indexes and cascade models were one thing. A four-year-old with burns across her body because the field meant to buffer undeclared magic had slipped its bounds was another.

By half one the Floo was technically back and practically untrustworthy. Green fire kept guttering orange mid-transit. Hermione spent the day with Accidental Magic, reviewed overnight field data, and sat through a meeting where a man from Magical Infrastructure asked if the decay could be reversed.

"I don't know," she said. "Not with what I have."

"Your research into the anchoring structure..."

"Is incomplete."

He let the sentence hang after that. So did she.

The missing part was not hard to name. Original construction records. Deep archive material. The old Malfoy texts that sat behind a room which only held when both she and Draco were present.

Ruby in a burn ward. The Floo failing across half the country. And the work that might help sat in a house she'd walked out of because the man who owned it had found out she had lied to him about something that mattered.

At half three the Mark flared so sharply she had to brace both hands against her desk. White went across her vision and receded. By the time she could straighten, the spike had flattened into the usual dull pull. He was alive. He was in pain. He was still there.

The bottom drawer of her desk held a jumper and a pair of trainers for nights she slept on the office sofa. Hermione looked at them for a while and shut the drawer again.

Home was colder than she remembered. The warming charm on the radiator failed twice before it held. Dinner came from a tin and was eaten standing up at the counter because sitting at the table alone felt like accepting a permanence she wasn't ready for. The lentil soup tasted faintly of metal under the salt. Draco had made risotto once, late in the third week, stirring with the same patience he gave to ruined marginalia and broken spells. She had grated cheese over both bowls while he shoved books out of splash range with the back of his wrist. They had eaten with the books shoved aside and argued over a fourteenth-century annotation until she forgot to be careful for almost an hour.

The bowl went into the rack. The memory did not.

---

Harry knocked on Friday at half six with takeaway from the Thai place near the Ministry and the look he wore when he had decided to keep showing up until the job was done.

"I brought food," he said. "The Floo's being strange again, so you're stuck with me till it settles."

Relief was immediate enough to irritate her. Even that she was too tired to hide.

They ate on the sofa. The pad see ew was overcooked. The spring rolls had gone cold. Harry noticed she was actually eating and was decent enough not to mention it.

"You look like shit," he said.

"Thanks."

"I mean it. When did you last sleep properly?"

"Define properly."

"Hermione."

"I'm sleeping some. It's been a bad week."

"Yeah." He twirled more noodles onto the fork. "The Floo's been down half the day. Kingsley's getting pressure from the Wizengamot. People want a public statement."

"They should give one. The Prophet's going to get the accidental-magic cases sooner or later."

"Dennis said you were in this morning."

"I was."

"He also said you looked wrecked."

"Dennis is twenty-three and covering three jobs. He doesn't need my sleeping habits on his list."

"He was worried." Harry set his container down. "The girl in Bristol is stable, apparently. Team's with the parents. But six kids in one night, Hermione."

"I know. The decay's accelerating."

"Because?"

Because the answer had one shape, she gave it to him whole. "Because the work at the Manor was starting to apply pressure in the right direction. Not enough. Not close to enough. But it was something, and now it's stopped."

"Because you're not there."

"Because I'm not there."

Harry looked at her for a beat. "So go back."

"I can't."

"Because of what happened."

"Yes."

He took another bite. The casualness was deliberate.

"Do you want to tell me what actually happened?" he said. "Because the story about disagreement over methodology is bollocks."

"Harry."

"I'm asking. That's not the same as pushing."

Hermione tucked her feet under her and tried, briefly, to think of a cleaner version. None existed.

"I found something in the archive," she said. "About the house. It amplifies emotional connections between the people living in it. It was built to do that. I found the texts, and I didn't tell Malfoy."

"How long?"

"Weeks."

He nodded once. "And he found out himself."

"He found the same folio. Worked out when I'd read it. Worked out what changed after." Her grip tightened around the cardboard container. "I stayed. I kept working. I kept being close to him without telling him what I knew that closeness might mean."

"To you."

The word sat there and did not soften.

Harry put his dinner down. "What are you angry about?"

"What?"

"Right now. What keeps you up."

The first answer rose up fast enough to feel true. He hadn't let her explain. He had decided what she'd meant. He had made her into something colder than she was.

Except he hadn't done that. He had named what she'd done.

"I'm angry that he decided I'd been manipulating him," she said. "That I'd been studying him like he was a subject."

"Hadn't you been?"

"Not like that."

"What would 'like that' be?"

The radiator ticked. Outside, the parked car across the road blinked its hazards into the window glass.

"The research required proximity. It required the examinations. That doesn't mean—"

"I'm not asking about the research."

She stopped.

"You're angry at him," Harry said. "Not at the situation. At him. So what did he get wrong?"

"He didn't—" She pressed her thumbnail into the side of her index finger. "He got it partly right. But he didn't let me explain."

"Explain what?"

"That I was going to tell him."

"When?"

"When I'd verified—"

"Right. When you were ready."

The car alarm outside gave one thin warning chirp and cut off again.

"That's not the same thing," she said.

"Yeah, it is."

She said nothing.

"He found out before you had it sorted," Harry said. "That's the part."

"That's not—" The denial started and she heard it for what it was.

"You were scared," Harry said. "That makes sense. The house might be messing with your feelings, and that's— yeah. Of course you were scared."

"But."

"But you sat on it. And while you were sitting on it, he was still—" Harry gestured vaguely with his fork. "In it. Doing the whole thing. Not knowing."

Her face had ended up against her knees without her quite noticing. The fabric smelled faintly of soup.

"I was trying to be sure."

"I know." He put the fork down. "I'm just saying what it looks like from his end."

The connection in her arm pulled low and steady.

"It could all be the Manor," she said into the cloth. "Everything I feel. Everything he feels."

"Could be."

"You're not going to tell me it's real?"

"How would I know? I'm not inside that house."

That almost made her laugh. Not quite.

"I know you, though," he said. "And you weren't being thorough. You were being scared."

"That's unfair."

"It's a bit unfair. It's also true."

Wet-eyed and too tired to care, Hermione lifted her head. Harry had seen her cry in a girl's bathroom at twelve, in a forest at seventeen, and in plenty of smaller years since. He'd never once made a spectacle of it.

"I don't know how to go back," she said.

"Yeah," he said. "I know."

"The research matters. Ruby matters. The whole country is coming apart. But if I go back for the research, then I'm doing it again. I am. I'm making it about the thing I can solve instead of the thing I can't."

He nodded.

"Then don't go back for the research," he said.

After a while she said, "The pad see ew was terrible."

"I know. The spring rolls were worse."

"I ate two."

"I saw. That's how bad this is."

Later, when the conversation had loosened by a notch, Harry mentioned Dennis holding the department together and said he reminded him a little of Colin. Hermione told him she'd be in on Monday. He told her to tell Dennis herself because he wasn't her secretary. Familiar bickering returned by reflex, thin but real.

At nine the Floo was still unreliable, so Harry Apparated from the doorstep. Cold rushed into the flat before she shut the door again.

The takeaway containers stayed on the table until the noodles stuck to the cardboard. She should have binned them, washed the forks, done one ordinary thing before bed. Instead she sat with her sleeve shoved to the elbow while the connection kept time in her arm.

She saw the folio first.

Not the whole night. Just the moment in the archive when the last line locked into place and the room seemed to drop away under her. Draco asleep somewhere above her. Candlelight on old vellum. Her own hands already starting to shake as she read the passage again: the house amplifying emotional connection between its inhabitants, deepening access in proportion to the bond between them. Then the clean, immediate decision that had followed. Close the book. Say nothing tonight. Verify first.

At the time it had felt responsible.

Now she could see the scene from the other side. He had let her into the rooms he avoided, the old texts, his arm under her hands, his body going rigid and then letting her proceed anyway. He had kissed her without knowing. She had kissed him knowing there was a question she had not let him near.

Even after the drawing room. Even after he had seen the scar for what it was and touched the letters as though touch itself could be careful enough to undo them. Even then she had kept the folio to herself.

That was the part Harry had named and she hadn't. Not that she loved work more than people. Not that she was secretly cold. The uglier thing was simpler. Faced with uncertainty, she had decided she could bear his not knowing better than she could bear her own not knowing beside him.

Across Britain children were burning, levitating furniture, blowing out windows. The archive remained in Wiltshire. The work remained urgent.

None of that changed the constraint.

She could not go back with professional necessity in front of her like a shield. Could not arrive asking for the archive, or for help, or for access, before she told the truth without dressing it up as method. If she went back, it could not be to finish the research first. It had to be as the person who had done the damage.

The thought sat with her in the dark while the pull in her forearm kept its stubborn measure. Sleep was poor when it finally came, but it came.
