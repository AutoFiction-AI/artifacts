# Chapter 5

On her eleventh day at the Manor, mornings had settled into a rhythm she hadn't asked for and didn't resist.

At six, grey light came through the east-facing window the house had angled to catch dawn. Tea appeared on the side table in a small silver pot. After a quick pass of her wand over the glamour on her left forearm, smoothing scar tissue into anonymous skin, Hermione dressed and took the walk to the library. The house kept that route at roughly four minutes even when it changed the corridor sequence.

Settling in was the phrase for it, and she disliked it. The words implied duration, implied a provisional domesticity that had not been part of the arrangement. Her job here was to research the library and examine the Mark. London still held a functional kitchen, a bed that didn't rearrange its own headboard, and a life she intended to resume when the work was done.

Yet the mornings had a rhythm. The library was extraordinary. More dangerously, the work made her feel like herself in a way she hadn't felt in years. The thought did not get to finish. So she went to the library.

Most mornings, Malfoy was there first. For the first few days that had irritated her because she was used to being the earliest person in any room and because some childish part of her still experienced competence as a contest. By the second week the irritation had altered into something else. Not comfort, exactly. Reliable presence.

The argument started over one word.

*Deperdere*, in a 1643 text written in the cramped hand of Abraxas Malfoy's great-great-grandfather. Standard Latin gave them *to lose, to destroy*. Malfoy insisted the family usage had narrowed over time into something more precise, closer to *to disperse irretrievably*, and he thought the distinction mattered for the magical-decay model they were building.

"It's not a synonym for *amittere*," he said, turning the book toward her. One fingertip rested on the line. "My grandfather's journals use the same construction. It's about dissipation, not loss. The magic isn't being taken or destroyed. It's bleeding out."

Her own parallel text, a 1790s theoretical treatise in standard Latin, supported part of his reading and not the rest. "The distinction only holds if the original builders imagined ambient magic as a contained system. These texts don't." She tugged her volume closer, found the place she wanted, and tapped it. "Here. *Fundamentum magiae est fluvialis, non lacustris.* The foundation of magic is riverine, not lacustrine. They're describing flow, not containment. In context, *deperdere* means dissipation from something already in motion, not leakage from a sealed system."

"I'm not arguing it was sealed. I'm arguing there's a difference between natural flow loss and structural hemorrhage, and this is the word they used when the damage was structural."

Late afternoon had gone golden without either of them noticing. Dust crossed the high windows in bright slants. Books, scrolls, her notes, his annotations, and two dead cups of tea had taken over the largest reading table by the east wall.

On a second reading he was right, at least in part. Her framework still held. So did his. The people who built the system had used one vocabulary for attrition and another for failure, and the distinction was not ornamental.

"Fine," she said. "You're right about the word. The riverine model still holds for the broader system."

"Obviously."

"Then we need both frameworks. Macro flow, structural failure mode. The decay we're seeing now isn't the system running down. It's the system breaking."

Across from her, he was already writing. His hand stayed small and even on the parchment, nothing like the erratic family scrawl in the older texts. Watching him work had become its own distraction. When he was thinking, stillness took him over in layers: pen paused, eyes gone slightly unfocused, body quiet without going rigid. Then the pen moved again and whatever problem had been under consideration was already solved.

It had been years since she'd worked beside someone who could keep up like this. Harry was brilliant in ways that had nothing to do with archival research. Ron had always been better at strategy than anyone gave him credit for and had never cared about the puzzle for its own sake. Her colleagues at the Ministry were competent. A few were good.

Malfoy was good. Fast too, and precise, and unusually willing to be wrong. That last quality mattered more than brilliance. Twice this week he'd caught things she'd missed. Three times she'd corrected him. Neither of them pretended the score didn't matter.

"There's a third instance," she said, pulling another volume from the stack near her elbow. "Seventeen twelve. Different author. Same usage."

Without looking up, he held out his hand for the book. The handoff left an inch of air between his skin and hers. A week ago that would have been nothing. Now it wasn't.

They stayed in the library until the light failed. As darkness gathered outside, the lamps brightened so gradually that the room never seemed to change, not until she glanced up and found black glass at the windows and Malfoy still across from her as if neither of them had anywhere else to be.

Which, she supposed, they didn't.

"The Floo in the east parlor has been unreliable," he said. His eyes stayed on the page. "Use the study fireplace if you need the Ministry."

"Thanks."

He nodded, already back in the text.

The translation chain kept lengthening under her hand. Each text opened three more. The pattern emerging from the cross-references felt close enough to touch now, not visible yet but waiting under the surface.

"Are you hungry?" he asked.

Only then did she check. "Yeah. Probably."

"The kitchen puts out supper at seven." He glanced at the wall clock. "It's twenty past."

"I can eat late."

"You could also eat on time."

Nothing in his tone made it an order. That almost made it worse. Hunger had caught up with her. It was past seven. The text would still exist in the morning.

"Fine."

He waited until her book closed before he stood. She noticed that and declined to examine it.

---

Supper was simple: roast chicken, potatoes, greens, bread still warm enough to steam when torn. The house-elves worked at a level of quiet competence Hermione found both impressive and politically uncomfortable, an old tension she had never managed to resolve. They ate in the smaller dining room near the kitchen rather than the formal one. At this hour the windows showed only their own reflection and the dark beyond it.

"The seventeen-twelve text mentions something called the Tethys Working," she said once the silence had settled. "Have you seen it before?"

"Once. In my grandfather's personal library, not here. Handwritten copy. No attribution. Probably older than the manuscript itself."

"Can you get it?"

"I can look. He kept that collection at the townhouse. My mother may know where it ended up."

Bread, butter, and a piece of potato occupied her for a minute. Somewhere during the meal the butter dish slid closer to her side of the table. She noticed. She did not comment.

"How's the Mark?"

"The same."

"As yesterday?"

"As yesterday. Active. Not worsening."

A flicker of grey amusement crossed his face when she looked up. "It's true. I'd tell you if it changed. You'd need to alter the diagnostic approach."

"Right. Good."

"Granger."

"Mm."

"Eat the greens."

She ate the greens. They were fine.

Clearing the table happened without agreement. Plates stacked under her hands. With a flick of his wand he sent them back to the kitchen, household magic so casual it bordered on Muggle. The bread basket remained. She ate the last piece standing up, which felt uncomfortably like the gesture of a person who lived there.

"I'm writing up the model tonight," she said. "The two-framework distinction. I want the terminology fixed before we go further into the seventeen-twelve material."

"I'll recheck the notation in the morning. If I've got the family usage wrong, better to know before you build a reference chain around it."

"You're not wrong."

At the door he paused. Almost smiled, or came as close to smiling as he allowed himself in rooms where she could see it. "I might be."

"Not about this."

"Well." His gaze shifted to the doorframe. "Goodnight, Granger."

"Goodnight."

Footsteps rang sharp on the corridor stone and then softened as the house swallowed the sound. In the warm room he left behind, rosemary and bread lingered over the cleared table.

---

It happened on the walk back to the guest wing.

Tonight the Manor gave her a straight corridor from the kitchen end of the house to her room, portraits lining the walls with the bored silence of people who had given up trying to speak to her. The Tethys Working occupied most of her attention. So did the structural-hemorrhage distinction. Malfoy did not. Even so, halfway down the corridor she became aware that he was on the third floor.

She stopped.

It was not a guess. It was not inference. His position in the house arrived in her mind with the same clean certainty as the weight of her wand in her pocket. Third floor, east side. His rooms.

Under that knowledge sat a second sensation: a dull ache in her left forearm, separate from the scar and wrong in a different way. Scar pain had a hot, scratchy texture, like a burn that had healed badly and never fully finished. This was deeper. Borrowed. A slow pulse that belonged elsewhere and had somehow found its way into her arm.

Her palm flattened over the spot through her sleeve. Pressure changed nothing. The ache wasn't in skin or muscle. It sat inside the diagnostic trace, maybe, in the residue left by three examinations and too much sustained contact with curse magic. She had read about that. Cursed-object examiners sometimes developed temporary trace bonds: sensory artifacts, usually minor, usually brief. Standard recommendation: reduce contact frequency and duration.

A harder press sharpened it for an instant into something recognizably his. Restless magic. The Mark trying to move through a channel that no longer went anywhere.

By the time the sensation fell back again, she was standing in a quiet corridor with her hand clamped around her own forearm and her breathing too fast. One lamp at the far end flickered, recovered, and left the shadows in a slightly different arrangement.

Diagnostic residue. That was the explanation available to her, so it became the explanation she used. Sustained spell contact with an unusually active dark artifact had produced an expected side effect. It would degrade. If it didn't, she could alter the protocol.

Inside her room, the literature argued with the evidence. Belby's monograph and the St. Mungo's examiner protocols described sensory aftereffects as minor, transient, and non-directional. A faint echo of a curse's quality. Hours, sometimes days. What she had felt had a location. It had pointed to him through walls. Either the Mark's reactivation behaved unlike anything in the literature, or the literature had no category for what had just happened.

She chose the first model because the first model let her keep working.

Notes came out. For two more hours, she worked at the desk under the window. At some point the ache returned, faint as a radio signal at the edge of reach. At some point it told her, without mechanism and without permission, that he was still awake.

By eleven the lamp was out. Midnight found her flat on her back in the dark, fingertips resting against the inside of her left forearm, feeling something that was not hers pulse beside the scar that was.

If it persisted, she would tell him. Clinically relevant data belonged in the protocol. Keeping it to herself would compromise the work.

The mattress beneath her was better than the one in London. Lavender and cedar preservation charms clung to the sheets. Tomorrow, she told herself.

That lie lasted less than a minute.

Not telling him was not a decision so much as a fact that had already settled inside her. The information felt private in a way that had nothing to do with science and everything to do with the intimacy of it. She could feel him in his own rooms. She could track the Mark's ache as it thinned toward whatever counted as rest in cursed tissue. Being inside the peripheral weather of his body without his knowledge or consent felt like a trespass. Handing that trespass over out loud felt worse.

It was residue. It would fade.

In the dark, with the ache still there, she let it keep her company and refused to call that wanting.

---

Three days later she met Harry for lunch at a pub in Bethnal Green, a Muggle place he had found when he and Ginny first moved east. The food was mediocre, the chips were excellent, and nobody from the Ministry would have thought to look for either of them there.

London hit her as soon as she rounded from the alley to the front door: noise, diesel, bodies moving with purpose in every direction. After the Manor's watchful quiet, the city felt like being thrown back into a current.

Harry had already claimed a table near the back. He stood when he saw her, hugged her, and for a moment the ordinary things did what ordinary things sometimes still could. His jacket. His hand on her shoulder. His familiar shape.

"You look all right," he said as they sat. "Bit tired."

"Thanks."

"I could've said you look terrible."

"I look fine."

"Yeah, you do." He took a drink. "How's the library?"

The research was easy to describe because it was the cleanest part of all this. She told him about the scale of the collection, the anchoring texts, the emerging picture of war damage to the system beneath British magic. Not the resonance. Not the moving house. Not the pleasure of arguing Latin with Malfoy until both of them forgot the hour.

"So it's real," Harry said. "The decay has a cause."

"It has a cause. Reversing it is the problem."

"But the information exists."

"Some of it. The Malfoys kept the best records. Or the only surviving records, which is close enough."

He nodded. Nobody listened like Harry when he chose to listen. Fifteen years had made him very good at hearing the part she left out.

"Getting enough sleep?"

"Yeah."

"Hermione."

"I'm sleeping. The room's quiet, the bed's comfortable, I'm getting six hours."

Five of those hours sometimes involved lying awake with a pain that didn't belong to her, but he had asked a narrower question than the truth deserved.

"You look tired," he said again, still not accusing.

While she reached for her water, her sleeve started to ride up. Instinct caught it before thought did. She pulled the cuff back down with her other hand and wrapped both around the glass. Seven years had turned that motion automatic. The scar was something the body managed before the mind got involved.

"Ginny says hi," Harry said. "She also says you're coming for dinner the next time you're in London for more than an afternoon. She's got opinions about the Floo."

"The Floo's been bad?"

"Three failures on our line last week. One of them dumped me in Birmingham."

"Birmingham."

"I'm not joking. The Midlands hub is going. The Ministry keeps calling it maintenance, but Ginny spoke to someone at the Prophet and they're sitting on two more infrastructure stories. It's getting worse."

The research reassembled itself around that information and came out uglier. If the hubs were failing, then the decay pattern in the texts was not historical curiosity. It was a live map.

"I need those Prophet reports."

"I'll get them. Ginny thinks the Ministry's going to have to say something public within the month. She's furious they didn't admit the pattern earlier. Someone in her department thought she'd broken her own fireplace and cried over it."

"That fits. Individual users would blame local causes first. If the anchoring points are damaged, the failures would look personal until they didn't."

"You sound like you've been waiting for someone to confirm it."

She had. Harry's anecdotal data was the first field confirmation she'd gotten that matched the old texts cleanly enough to trust. He did not need the full theory. "The research matters more than I thought it did. It's diagnostic now, not historical."

"Right." His expression said he was following as far as he could without asking her to slow down for his benefit. "Good."

A quieter question came next. "So the Manor. Is it all right? The house treating you all right?"

"The house is a house, Harry."

"That's not what you told me last week."

Last week's fire-call had included the variable corridors and not much else. No mention of shelves surfacing the books she wanted. No mention of a butter dish migrating toward her at supper. No mention of a building that seemed to know when she was hurrying to get back to work.

"It's strange," she said. "The corridors shift. Rooms rearrange. It's not dangerous. Just variable."

"Variable."

"Like a building with preferences."

That earned a long look.

"And Malfoy? How is he with all of it?"

He asked carefully, which meant he had been waiting to ask at all.

"Fine. Helpful with the translations. His family notation isn't standard and he can actually read it." Precision came into her voice too fast. She forced it slower. "He's a good researcher."

"Okay."

"I mean it. He's rigorous. He catches things I miss."

"I believe you."

"You're making a face."

"I'm not."

"The one where you're deciding not to say something."

Harry put down his chip. "I'm glad the research is going well. I'm glad the library's useful. I'm asking if you're all right because you look tired and you're living in a house that moves its walls, and I know you, and I know what that house is."

Her hand went to the sleeve again, this time only tugging it lower over the wrist while she reached for a chip. Same gesture, different reason. The first one had protected the scar. This one gave her hands something to do while she lied to someone who knew her too well.

"I'm all right," she said. "Promise."

The green of his eyes made lying feel adolescent and clumsy. He let it go anyway. Not because he believed her. Because he knew the difference between not ready and never, and he was granting her room to decide which this was.

For now, it was neither. It was a lie.

"Okay," he said. "Come for dinner. Ginny'll do that chicken thing you like."

"The lemon one."

"The lemon one."

"I'll try."

"Don't try. Come."

A laugh came later, over a case involving a cursed postbox in Croydon and three weeks of the Royal Mail blaming ghosts. Harry noticed that too, of course he did. He paid because he always paid. She let him because arguing about it had been pointless since they were teenagers.

Outside, November sat low and grey over Bethnal Green. She pulled her coat tighter.

"Come back soon," Harry said. "Not in another two weeks."

"It hasn't been that long."

"It's been fourteen days."

He was right. Fourteen days at the Manor and it already felt like longer, not because time had dragged but because it had thickened.

"I'll come for dinner," she said. "Tell Ginny I'll bring wine."

"She'll say you don't have to."

"I know."

The second hug lasted longer than usual. Then he was gone toward the Apparition point, and she was left on the pavement with London all around her and a sharp absence in her left forearm. No echo. No borrowed ache. No sense of direction pointing her toward another body. Out here her arm was only her arm, marked by one scar and nothing else.

That absence was information. The fact went into the file with the rest, and then she went home and returned to Wiltshire.

---

Half three found the grounds cold and the formal garden brown with the season. The Manor rose behind the hedgerow in pale stone and dark windows, looking as if it had been aware of the road long before she turned onto it.

The front door opened before she touched it.

Warmth met her first, then the smell of wood polish and old stone. Near the entrance a hook had appeared for her coat sometime in the last several days. It had not been there when she arrived, or had not chosen to be visible.

By the third stair the resonance was back.

A pulse moved through her forearm, low and immediate. The directional certainty came with it. Library. Active Mark. Not severe, but not quiet either.

So London had stripped the signal away and the house had given it back. That was new data, not a repetition of the old. Distance mattered. Proximity mattered. The Manor mattered. Whatever this was, it obeyed conditions she could test.

Upstairs, she found him at the reading table with a fresh stack of texts, flagged pages, and a pot of tea still giving off steam. He had spent the afternoon working. The evidence of it was everywhere.

He looked up. "London?"

"Lunch with Harry. The Floo network's degrading at the hub level. Three failures on the Potter line last week."

"I know. The east parlor took four attempts this morning."

There it was: independent confirmation, field data from the man in front of her and the man in London lining up too neatly to dismiss. Across from him, she sat, opened her notebook, and tried to read while the ache in her arm kept time with the man across from her.

A sharp flare crossed the trace before he reacted outwardly, and the question left her mouth before she could stop it.

"Was it worse around noon?"

His eyes lifted from the page. "My arm?"

"Yes."

"You weren't here at noon."

No good answer arrived in time. The one she used was only half false. "I'm trying to map the Mark spikes against the Floo instability. If the same damage is feeding both failures, timing matters."

His expression did not change much. That was usually worse than an obvious reaction. "Around noon," he said after a beat, "it was worse after the second Floo attempt. Then it settled."

"Right."

Ink touched parchment a little too hard. Across from her, he turned another page.

A few minutes later she said, "I want to change tomorrow's exam."

That got his attention more cleanly than the noon question had. "How?"

"No wand buffer. Sustained direct contact on the Mark itself."

"That's a change."

"So is the data."

It was not the whole truth. The whole truth was sitting live in her arm, warm and invasive and impossible to explain without saying far more than she intended to say. Direct contact might damp it. Direct contact might clarify it. Direct contact might make it worse. She needed to know which, and she needed to know before this thing turned from anomaly into habit.

He studied her for a moment, weighing the request, perhaps weighing her. "All right," he said. "Tell me what you need me to do."

Relief arrived with enough force to be irritating. "Tomorrow morning. Library. I'll bring the revised notes."

"Fine."

Work resumed, though not easily. Every turn of his hand registered. Every quieter pulse of the Mark dragged at the trace in her arm. By nine he stood, closed the book he'd been using, and said he was going upstairs.

The resonance moved before he did. That was the part she hated most.

After his footsteps faded into the upper corridors, Hermione pulled the protocol sheet toward her. Wand-mediated pass went under one firm black line. Beneath it she wrote: Session IV. Direct palm contact. Full duration.
