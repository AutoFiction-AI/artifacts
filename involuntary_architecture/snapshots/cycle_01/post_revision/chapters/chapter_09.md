# Chapter 9

The lamb was overcooked.

Hermione noticed it the way she'd started noticing most things about Malfoy: sideways, while pretending her attention was on the work. Forty minutes in the kitchen had left the chops grey at the edges and a little dry in the middle. He set the plates down in the library without apology. She ate all of hers without comment.

Three weeks ago, eating overcooked lamb in Malfoy Manor would have registered as absurd. Tonight the absurdity was that it didn't.

Abelard Malfoy's letters lay spread between them beside a genealogical chart and two stacks of reference books the shelves had produced before either of them asked. Malfoy read while he ate. Ink darkened the tips of his fingers where he'd been annotating the originals, ever since she'd found the transcription error that turned "to anchor" into "to sever." Her attention kept catching on his hands.

Only after several minutes did she admit what she was doing. His thumb snagged on a page. He put it to his mouth for better grip, absent-minded, and heat moved through her low and fast. Looking down helped very little. Her plate held a bare bone, a smear of mint jelly, a knife at the wrong angle. None of it explained the shift under her ribs.

Since the examination that had gone wrong, or right, the Manor had turned almost accommodating. The route from the guest wing to the library stayed stable. Shelves surfaced the right texts. Fires lit when she entered and banked when she left. By increments so small they might have passed for coincidence, comfort had become part of the house.

"The third letter mentions Glastonbury," he said, eyes still on the page. "Locational cipher, I think. First glyph's unfamiliar."

"Could be temporal. Abelard uses the same base glyph for both. The modifier's in the subscript."

At his turn of the page she leaned in, close enough to smell ink and soap and the clean warm note of skin. Her sleeve touched his wrist. Neither of them moved until she'd pointed out the mark.

"Temporal," he said after a beat.

"Temporal."

Outside, early December had gone dark hours ago. Frost filmed the windows. The library held steady warmth and the low, constant awareness that now came with sitting near him: not thought, not language, only the blunt fact of Malfoy two feet away.

"I think the Glastonbury node was one of the original twelve," she said. "The pattern works better as a dodecahedral lattice than a grid. Your family never built in straight lines."

"My family never built anything in straight lines. Not their houses. Not their politics. Not most of their marriages."

The laugh escaped before she could stop it. Across the table his mouth didn't quite smile, but the line of it loosened. For a second he looked less guarded and more pleased than he would ever admit.

Work took the next two hours. At some point he rose, made tea without asking, and returned with milk already in hers. He'd learned the amount by repetition. That, more than the tea itself, unsettled her.

Later, when three vertebrae cracked in quick succession as she stretched over the chart, his head came up from the letters. "That sounded structural."

"It's fine."

"You've been folded over that page since dusk."

"I hunch. Professional hazard."

"It'll become a medical one."

A roll of her neck produced another pop. He winced. "See?" she said. "Fine."

Ordinary talk. Ordinary attention. By half eleven he was stacking the Abelard letters in the order he meant to reach for them tomorrow, assuming tomorrow would look much like today. She watched him do it and let herself name the thing she'd been dodging all evening.

For the first time since arriving, the fact landed cleanly: she was comfortable in Malfoy Manor.

---

The corridor to the guest wing was the one route the house no longer bothered to change. Same portraits. Same cracked flagstone. Same left turn toward the stairs.

At the turn, a door stood open.

Recognition came before decision. Chandelier. Marble floor. Tall windows black with winter. Preserved cold rolling through the doorway like cellar air. The drawing room.

Her body stopped first. Memory followed at speed.

Bellatrix's fist in her hair. The jerk of her neck. Marble against her cheek. The knife on the inside of Hermione's left forearm, not cutting so much as parting the skin with that wet tearing sound she'd hated more than the pain because it sounded like matter being rearranged. Bellatrix talking the whole time in that soft, conversational voice. Tell me. Oh, don't do that yet, we've barely started.

Under the glamour, the scar went hot. M first. Then U. Then D. By the time Bellatrix began the B, the room had narrowed to the blade and Bellatrix's breath and the knowledge that this was being done slowly on purpose. When sight came back properly, Draco Malfoy was against the far wall, white-blond head turned away.

Seven years of distance meant nothing to the body. Cold reached through the threshold, through wool socks, through stone, and pinned her there. A Mind Healer had once given her the language for this. Freeze response. Knowing the term did nothing.

The house had opened the room at the precise moment she'd stopped bracing for it. Lamb. Tea. The exact amount of milk. Whether that counted as intention or architecture did not matter. The room was here. So was she.

Usually she could feel him somewhere in the Manor, a faint warmth at the edge of attention. Now there was nothing. The house had narrowed around the doorway until even that thread went dead.

Footsteps sounded on stone behind her. Measured, familiar, slowing as they reached the turn.

"Granger."

His careful voice. The one he used when every word had been checked for sharp edges.

"I didn't know it was open," he said. "This corridor wasn't supposed to connect. I thought I'd sealed it."

The laugh that came out of her had no humour in it. "You sealed the room."

"Years ago. I asked the house to keep it inaccessible."

"Well." Her throat worked once. "It didn't."

A few seconds passed. Then, quieter, "Come away from the threshold. Please."

"I can't."

"Then don't stand there alone."

Open was better than closed. Closed turned the room back into memory. She kept her eyes on the marble. "I'm not standing in a doorway."

Before he could stop her again, she stepped across the threshold.

Marble flattened the sound of her shoes exactly the way it had that night. In the centre of the room she stopped and looked down. The blood had been cleaned years ago. Memory laid it back in place without effort: where her cheek had hit, where Bellatrix had stretched the arm, where the red had run into the veining of the stone.

Behind her, his steps came wrong. Three across the marble and then a halt. Turning showed her a face gone grey, his eyes reading the floor the way hers had.

"You stood there," she said, pointing between the second and third chairs. "Your mother was beside you."

His jaw tightened. "Yes."

"Easter hols. School robes. You'd been home a week. I remember looking at you when she started on the M. You looked at me and then you looked away. Do you remember that?"

Another beat. Then: "Yes."

What had she wanted from him at seventeen? Not a rescue. Maybe not even movement. Just a sign that someone in the room understood this as horror and not spectacle, family business, collateral damage. The old want made her feel ill even now.

"I still don't know what I wanted from you," she said. "Only that I wanted a sign."

Malfoy's attention dropped to the floor. "I remember everything about that night. Her laugh. Pettigrew breathing. The sound the knife made. I didn't know a knife could sound like that."

"It was cursed," Hermione said automatically. "It wasn't cutting. It was separating the skin. Bellatrix liked precision."

His eyes shut. "Please stop."

"Why? You were there."

His gaze came up bright and furious with effort. "Because you're describing it like evidence."

"It is evidence. It happened on your floor."

For the first time since he'd entered, he crossed the room on purpose. He walked past her to the wall where he'd stood at sixteen, put his back against the paneling, and looked at the marble instead of at her.

"I didn't look away because I didn't care," he said. "I looked away because if I kept watching, I thought I'd move. Try to stop her. I did the arithmetic in my head. Wand, distance, how fast she'd curse me, whether I'd reach you before she did worse. The numbers were hopeless. So I stayed where I was and turned my face to the wall and listened."

"It was worse for you," Hermione said. "Listening."

A brief recoil crossed his face. "That isn't what I'm saying."

"I know exactly what you're saying." Her voice lost the dissociative flatness. Rawness got through. "And I need you to hear mine. I was seventeen. She was writing on me. I looked at the only person in the room close to my age and you turned away. I've known for seven years that you were scared and that you probably couldn't have stopped her. I testified to that. I believe it. It doesn't help."

A long pause sat between wall and floor.

"I should've done it anyway," he said at last. "Tried. Even if it killed me."

"You'd have died."

"Probably."

"And Bellatrix would've finished the word." Anger made the sentence merciless. "I'd still have been here. You'd just be dead with me."

"Then maybe dead was the right answer."

The flatness of it stopped her. No drama. No plea. Just arithmetic carried forward seven years. "Don't," she said, though she wasn't sure whether she meant don't say that or don't make me answer it. "I'm not telling you you should've died."

"No. You're telling me you can explain why I lived." His mouth worked once. "You keep saying you testified for me as if it closes the account."

That landed because it was true. She did keep reaching for the trial, for evidence, for the clean legal version of the night. None of that had altered the room.

"I don't know how to do this," she said. Not the conversation. The house. The work. The library upstairs still warm from tea. "I don't know how to stay here and know this room exists. I don't know how to look at you and not see the wall."

That was the point at which his legs failed him. No gesture in it, no strategy. He slid down the paneling until he was sitting on the marble with his forearms braced over his knees, head bowed like the body had simply run out of instructions.

No apology followed. None would have been enough. The room had already taken everything it could take.

She turned and walked out.

Crossing from marble to flagstone felt like stepping out of a cellar and into ordinary weather. The corridor stayed fixed around her: portraits, stone, cracked flagstone, guest wing ahead. Nothing shifted. The house had no need. It had already shown her what it wanted shown.

At the crack in the floor, the resonance caught again, altered beyond recognition. For weeks it had lived at the edge of her attention as warmth. Now it came through as cold stone, held breath, a grief dense enough to feel like weight. He was still in the drawing room. She kept walking.
