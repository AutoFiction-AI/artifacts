# Chapter 6

Today's protocol was different.

By now Draco knew the shape of her examinations: instruments set in strict order, his chair angled toward the window, the quick correction at her left cuff that came a beat after she realized he'd noticed it. He'd learned to sit through the first passes of diagnostic magic and to keep stiller for the deeper ones. During the work, her face emptied of everything except concentration. The library silence between them had started to feel companionable. The examination silence still belonged to rules, and rules were easier to trust.

Today's variation required sustained contact. She'd explained the method without looking directly at him. Wand work blurred the Mark's raw signature; a palm laid over it would not.

The theory was simple enough. So was the implication: her hand on his arm for as long as the reading took. Neither of them mentioned the third session, when an accidental brush of her palm had made the Mark surge toward her like a compass needle finding north.

They used the small sitting room off the library's east wall. Natural light mattered to the diagnostics, she'd said once, and by the next morning the Manor had stripped the heavy drapes and cleaned the windows on its own. December had thinned the day to grey. Frost feathered the lower panes. The room held the ordinary chill of a neglected corner, not hostility, merely lack of attention.

"Ready?"

He rolled his left sleeve up. The Mark stood dark against his skin, the lines raised enough to catch the light.

"Any change since Tuesday?"

"Not beyond the usual."

Her mouth made a brief unimpressed shape. "Fine. Baseline first. Then direct contact. Tell me if anything changes."

A nod was all she got. She took the first reading with her wand, cool light moving under the skin while he watched frost spread at the edge of the glass. The sensation was familiar now: being examined by magic cleverer than most Healers he'd ever met. Years ago a specialist had lasted less than two minutes before the Mark snapped his diagnostic frame apart. Granger's spells went in cleanly. The curse let them.

"Baseline stable," she murmured. "Slightly more reactive than Tuesday. Hold still."

The wand went down on the table. Her hand replaced it.

Prepared or not, he felt the contact everywhere. Her palm was warm and the pressure firm. Her fingers curved around his forearm until the Mark sat beneath the center of her hand. A thumb settled at the inside of his wrist, exactly over his pulse. The effect was not dramatic at first. It was simply another person's skin on his, which should not have registered as intensely as it did.

"Starting the deeper pass," she said. "Don't move."

Eyes closing, she sent the diagnostic through her hand instead of the wand. Most practitioners could manage that for a few seconds. Granger could hold it. Her breathing slowed. Concentration tightened her mouth. Magic moved from her palm into the Mark in a low steady current, and the restless pressure that had lived in his arm for months cut out so abruptly he forgot to breathe.

His hand opened on the chair arm. His shoulder dropped. For one clean astonished second he noticed all the small adjustments his body had stopped making.

Her eyes opened at once. "What?"

"It went quiet."

"Quiet how?"

"Completely."

The answer landed harder than he'd expected. She didn't take her hand away. Under her palm the Mark lay flat, stripped of its usual shimmer. There was no defensive flare. Dark magic did not twitch along the dead channels. The skull and serpent looked like old ink and scar tissue, nothing more.

A slow stroke of her thumb across the edge of the Mark drew no reaction. Her voice came out lower. "That shouldn't happen."

"No."

"I'm lifting my hand. Count."

Warmth left his skin. Six seconds passed, then seven. By eight the Mark woke again, low and insistent, the familiar wrongness threading back under the skin.

"Now," he said.

Her palm returned. Silence dropped over the arm again, immediate and entire.

For a moment neither of them spoke. Frost clicked against the window. A branch scraped somewhere above them.

"That's not the diagnostic," she said. Thought was already outrunning speech. "The technique is observational. It shouldn't alter the curse itself, let alone this much. So either the method is interacting in a way no one's documented, or the Mark is responding to-"

"To you."

Her thumb shifted against his wrist. Whether she meant to do it or not, he felt the movement as clearly as the first touch.

"I don't have an explanation yet."

"Yet," he repeated.

"I need better data." The sentence arrived faster, sharper. "Duration, threshold, whether pressure matters, whether the effect lingers after contact breaks. I also need to compare it to the last three baselines, which means I need my notes and I've managed this badly."

He looked toward the notepad stranded beyond her reach. "You do usually like a quill."

That got a flicker out of her, nearly a laugh and not quite. With her free hand unavailable, she took the quill in the other and began writing left-handed in a cramped slant that would have horrified her under calmer circumstances.

"Onset immediate," she muttered, writing. "Observable suppression of surface activity. Subject reports complete quieting."

"Subject?"

Her attention stayed on the page. "Don't start."

The answer should have sounded clinical. With her hand still braced around his arm, it did not.

She tested in increments. She tried full-palm contact, then fingertips, then one finger at the tail of the serpent. The Mark stayed inert until the last reduction. Under a single point of contact it stirred once, weakly.

"There," he said.

"Pressure as well as contact area." Another left-handed scrawl. "Fine. Annoying, but fine."

"Annoying."

"I prefer results that obey theory."

He looked at her bent over the notepad, hair loosening from its tie, one finger still pressed to his skin because removing it would change the result. His body had not been this calm in years. There was nowhere useful to put that fact.

By the time she seemed to register the posture they'd arrived at, her pen had stopped moving. Her gaze flicked from the Mark to his face and away again. She took one shallow breath, then withdrew her hand at once and set the quill down too carefully.

A few breaths later the effect was gone. He closed his fingers around the chair arm and waited it out.

"Thank you," she said, already stacking papers. "I need the resonance texts. There's one in the east stacks and possibly a commentary in the-"

"Granger."

At the door she stopped, one hand still pinning the papers against her hip.

He had nothing workable to say about what had happened. He could not speak about the fact that her hand had done what no Healer, potion, or spell had managed. He could say even less about the worse fact: when she'd lifted it, his first clear thought had been put it back.

"Your notes are upside down."

Her eyes dropped to the page. A quick correction, then she was gone.

For a while he stayed where he was. The room had darkened another degree. Frost thickened at the edges of the glass. When he set his own palm over the Mark, nothing changed. His hand was only his hand. The distinction mattered more than he wanted it to.

Eventually he rolled the sleeve down and went after her.

***

The library, as usual, had more of Granger in it than the guest wing ever did. Color-coded tabs, charmed index cards, stacks of books breeding across three reading tables: all of it had spread with brisk inevitability. On his table by the south window waited a cup of cold tea and a note charmed to the handle: *Preservation charm on the Avery folio is failing. Handle with clean cotton.*

The note disappeared into his pocket with the others. They were useful research artifacts. That was the reason.

Across the room, she sat cross-legged on the floor between two shelves with three books open around her and a clean notebook in place of the disgraced one. She did not glance up or mention the sitting room. That was fine. He opened an estate-binding text at his usual seat and let work close over the morning.

Silence suited them. It held page turns, the scratch of quills, a stray question when translation snagged. An hour might pass on two remarks and no one needed to pretend it was awkward. The quiet had weight now. It felt built.

Halfway through a genealogical supplement, her voice cut across the room.

"Malfoy."

The tone was different. He looked up at once.

She stood near the east wall where Victorian shelving gave way to bare stone. A draft moved there that had not existed a minute before, carrying dust and the dry packed smell of long-sealed parchment.

A door had appeared.

No memory in him accommodated it. He'd grown up against that wall, reading in the window seats as a child, crossing the room daily under Lucius's eye. There had never been a door. Now dark wood sat flush in the stone, older than the library fittings, its iron handle black with age.

"That's new," he said.

"I had gathered."

The remark barely touched it. She had already leaned in. Only the hand hovering an inch from the iron showed she still knew better.

"You want to open it."

"Don't you?"

He did. Reaching past her close enough to feel her warmth through wool, he pulled the handle.

The room beyond was small and windowless. Old stone enclosed it beneath a low ceiling, and alcoves cut straight into the walls served in place of shelving. Scrolls, codices, and loose folios filled those alcoves; the material looked older than anything in the visible library, older perhaps than the present house. The air carried stasis rather than decay. Time had not so much passed in there as waited.

She made a small sound and was inside before he'd finished taking stock. A scroll came out of the nearest alcove with reverent efficiency. By the time he crossed the threshold she was already reading.

"Anchoring theory," she said. "Original documentation. Pre-Statute, at least. Look at the notation."

The scroll opened farther under careful hands.

"This is primary literature. Not references to it. Not later copies. Actual source material."

On his side of the room, a folio in familiar family notation offered estate-binding protocols in an ancestral hand. The sight of it turned his stomach a little. Lucius had spent half his life in the main library and had never once mentioned a hidden archive. Either his father hadn't known, or he had and said nothing. Both possibilities were ugly.

Another codex came down into her lap. Excitement sharpened her voice. "Ambient field measurements. They knew the substrate could degrade. Malfoy, this room is everything."

"I need the mapping kit," he said. "If we're cataloguing any of this properly, it starts now."

The kit waited in the main stacks. He stepped back into the library to fetch it.

Three paces later, stone ground against stone.

Turning, he saw the doorway narrowing. The frame contracted inward with patient force. Through the shrinking gap, Granger stood rigid with the codex in her hands.

"Malfoy-"

He stepped back inside at once. The movement stopped as if it had never begun.

Silence held for one beat.

"Again," she said. "We test it again."

He stepped into the library. The grinding started immediately. He stepped back in, and the frame held.

Her turn produced the same result. Three seconds after she crossed the threshold the ceiling began to lower and the alcoves compressed by an inch. Once she returned, the room steadied.

"Both of us," she said.

"Together, then. One more."

This time they left side by side. Behind them the archive closed fast, the doorway narrowing to a slit before the wall sealed over it completely. Bare stone met them where the entrance had been. There was no crack and no trace.

Cold rock met his palm when he touched it. Her hand came up beside his. Nothing happened at first. Then warmth spread under both their palms, slow and deliberate. Grain surfaced in the stone. Iron darkened into shape. The door drew itself back into the wall as if the house wanted them to feel each stage of the process.

Inside, the archive waited unchanged.

"Both present to open," she said. "Both present to keep it open."

The rule was so plain it insulted him.

"It never showed my father this room," he said. "Or me."

Granger set the codex on the floor with visible care. "I know."

"No, you don't." His voice came out flatter than he meant it to. "I've been in this library my whole life. Today it opens the one room we actually need, and it—" He stopped. Started again, tighter. "There's a condition on it."

"Yes." Her gaze stayed on him. "If either of us leaves, the room goes."

"You're already past the part where that's a problem."

"I'm not past it." The words came out faster than her usual precision. "It's invasive and it's—" She shook her head once. "But it's a constraint. We don't leave separately during a session. We bring what we need in together. If one of us forgets a reference text, both of us go out and both of us come back."

"That's your answer. Logistics."

"That's my answer right now." She glanced at the sealed wall behind them. "Unless you've got a better one."

He didn't. Her jaw had set so hard he could see it at the hinge. What he had was the house making proximity a rule before either of them had decided what proximity meant.

"You don't mind the house deciding when we're in the same room."

"I mind it very much." The answer was immediate. "I also mind the decay."

She was already scanning the room as she spoke. "The mapping kit's still outside. So are my spare notebooks, cotton gloves, and most of the references cross-cited in this scroll. We fetch them now, both of us, and we come straight back. After that we only leave if we have to."

Draco looked at the shelves, then at the blank stretch of wall where the door would vanish the moment they both stepped away. The house had taken proximity and made it procedural. He hated the neatness of that. He hated, more quietly, the way her mouth had tightened around both of us, as if saying it had cost her a swallow.

"Fine," he said.

"Good. Move."

They stepped out together. The wall sealed at once. Ten minutes later they returned with the mapping kit, two additional notebooks, gloves, parchment, a reading lamp, and the tea tray the Manor had already placed on the nearest library table before either of them asked for it. That last detail earned the wall a hard look from both of them and no comment.

Warmth answered when their hands touched the stone. The door re-formed more quickly this time.

Inside, she claimed the floor beside the central alcove. He took the opposite wall with the folio he'd found first. The room was small enough that their knees nearly touched once they both settled.

"Start with shelf order," she said, already halfway into the codex. "Don't trust apparent chronology. The house may be grouping by use instead of date."

"Comforting."

"It wasn't meant to be."

The mapping grid went down between them. Notes began. Within minutes she was muttering in medieval Latin under her breath and crossing out her own translations with irritated precision. He answered when she asked for a family abbreviation. She corrected him once, then checked the margin and admitted he was right. The exchange lasted less than a minute. Work took them again.

A Malfoy ancestor whose name he didn't recognize had described the house as *attendant*, a word that came closer to listening than watching. The passage should have clarified matters. It did not. Six hundred years earlier, someone in his line had already accepted that the Manor tracked its inhabitants and altered conditions accordingly. Precedent existed. Documentation existed. None of it made the present less unnerving.

Across from him, Granger had tucked both feet under herself and forgotten they belonged to a body at all. When she reached for a second scroll, her shoulder brushed the stone, then stayed there. Once, shifting position, she nudged his ankle with her socked foot and kept reading as if she had not noticed. Perhaps she had not. He had.

The archive held. Heat worked slowly up through the floor. No daylight marked the passage of time in that room, but eventually the lamp became necessary and then insufficient on its own until the house answered with a sourceless glow in the seams of the ceiling.

They kept reading.

By full dark, copies of anchoring diagrams covered the mapping board. Half a page of correlations linked war-damaged sites to older reinforcement nodes. On the floor between them sat a teapot gone cold and a pile of notes dense enough to matter. She passed him a scroll when she stalled at a family cipher. He translated it, slid her the next folio, and marked the shelf number before her quill lifted from the page. The house had forced a condition.

That did not make him trust the house.

It did not make him leave.
