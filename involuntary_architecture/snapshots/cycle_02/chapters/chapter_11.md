# Chapter 11

By the second reading, the pattern was too clean to dismiss. The base layer at the bottom of the Mark had strengthened again. Hermione wrote the measurement in the ledger, checked last week's entry, then the week before. The rise was steady. Every long examination moved it.

That correlation stayed out of the ledger.

"I'm going to push lower," she said.

Draco kept his eyes on the book propped beside his hand. "Go on."

Magic slid under her fingers, through the degraded lattice of Voldemort's binding, past the defensive dark matter that still fed on his core, down to the buried layer that did not belong with the rest. The contact gave way. The room went wrong.

There was the library, the fire, the table, her own hand on his wrist.

There was also the room from his side of it: the crease in the page under his thumb, the weight of his arm on the padding, the exact place where her touch rested. It was not just physical sensation. Her nearness, registered from inside him with humiliating clarity. The ink on her fingers. The wool at her wrist. The fact that his attention had been on her for longer than the book in front of him.

Under that sat something worse. Shame, old and ground deep, wrapped around the black thing on his arm. Then came the answering ease when her palm held it down, when the brand quieted and his body, traitorous and simple, took the relief as hers.

Hermione tore her hand away.

The doubled view collapsed. Her fingertips buzzed. Across the table Draco had already set the book down.

"What happened."

"I caught a spillover." Even now her voice came out level. "The resonance went both ways for a few seconds. Sensory information, that's all."

His gaze held on her face. "That's not all."

No point denying the flinch. Hermione closed the ledger, buying herself a breath. "I had your angle for a moment. The table. The book. Pressure. It was disorienting."

"What else?"

What else was private. What else belonged to the part of him that had not chosen to put itself in her hands. She looked at the exposed Mark, then at his mouth, gone flat with effort.

"Nothing I can use," she said. "Nothing you need reported back to you."

Silence settled. He almost pushed. She saw the moment he decided not to.

"If this is getting less safe," he said, "we stop."

"It's still useful."

"That wasn't my question."

"I know." Her hands had steadied. "I said what I meant."

He nodded once, unhappy with the answer and accepting it anyway. That, more than the question, unsettled her.

The rest of the examination produced no second breach. She logged the measurements, closed the case, and packed away the crystals while he turned a page he had not been reading.

---

The south wall alcove was open that afternoon, which meant the archive was holding. Draco worked in the deeper room beyond it, somewhere among the oldest shelves, and every so often his concentration brushed the edge of her attention hard enough to break her own. It was not words or thoughts. It was just the fact of him reading, pausing, finding something, moving again.

Work sat in safer territory. The main-library folios spread across her end of the table were the ordinary bones of the estate: surveys, warding logs, repair orders, correspondence with long-dead bureaucrats. They were useful because they were dull, and dull because they were useful. She had spent a week building the Manor from its foundations upward, line by line, hoping structure would keep her away from the other question.

A surveyor's report from 1743 finally slipped. Halfway down the margin, in a later hand, someone had added a cross-reference in cramped notation: *Binding Folio, archive 14-C. See resonance architecture.*

Hermione read it twice. The catalogue mark was older than the current library system and wrong for the public shelves.

The archive door stood ten feet away.

Past the alcove, the air was cooler and drier. The smell of old preservation charms sat heavier there, with iron under it. Shelves ran higher and closer together than in the main room. Another grille, already unlatched because the archive was open for both of them, separated the working stacks from a narrower run of older texts. At the far end Draco stood with his back to her, copying from a crumbling ledger onto loose parchment before the page gave up under the archive's usual instability. He did not turn.

Shelf 14-C held plain bindings and no titles on the spines. The black folio sat shoved between a genealogy register and a book of foundation seals, unadorned except for a catalogue number impressed so shallowly she had to tip it toward the light to read it.

She took it out and carried it back to the main table.

The notation on the first page was old enough to fight her. Meaning came slowly, after grammar, after three passes through each clause. The title yielded first.

*On the Binding Architecture of the Manor and Its Proper Maintenance for the Perpetuation of the Line.*

Her hands stayed very still on the leather.

The opening pages were not about shelter or defensive wards. They described the house as an instrument. The Manor had been built to sustain the Malfoy line by detecting attachment between inhabitants, adjusting itself to favor that attachment, and deepening it through repeated environmental response. Warmth came with proximity, cold with distance, corridors shortened or stretched, and chambers opened only when the bond reached the necessary strength.

The next section was worse because it was explicit. The house worked only where there was already something to work with. *Where no seed exists, the architecture shall find no purchase.* But once the seed existed, the Manor amplified it until the altered feeling and the original one could no longer be told apart.

Hermione translated that passage three times, hoping the third would say something else. It did not.

Another section described the awareness between paired inhabitants: proximity, emotional weather, physical condition. The house used that channel to measure progress and then widened the channel to accelerate it. Weeks of knowing which floor he was on, and the sudden sideways glimpse into his body that morning, were all documented in a dead man's manual as if they were plumbing.

The line that finally stopped her sat near the back.

*The architecture shall prove most efficacious when the pair are ignorant of its influence. Once the mechanism is known, continuation cannot be compelled. The house may cultivate. It cannot command.*

Hermione shut the folio and stared at the fire until her eyes watered.

Then the last weeks reassembled themselves under uglier headings. The guest wing bent so she kept crossing shared rooms to reach anything useful. Doors opening only when both of them stood there. Doors staying shut when one of them did not. Tea at her elbow. Him knowing how much butter she liked on bread. The channel between them. His attention. Her own.

Every genuine thing between them was now suspect. Maybe the house had found a seed and leaned on it until she could not tell where the first feeling ended and the pressure began.

Footsteps shifted in the archive. Draco was moving toward the front room.

He had to know. The obligation landed at once and with full weight. This was his house, his family's machinery, his life being shaped without his consent.

The notation was archaic. She should verify. No: she could verify after she told him, and she knew it. What she wanted was an hour before she had to watch his face change.

By the time he came through the alcove she had already reshelved the folio in archive 14-C. The surveyor's report was still on the table. She slid her notes over the margin reference and left her hand there as if the paper needed holding down.

"The archive's fighting me again," he said. "The back wall went soft around four. I had to copy the last pages from memory." His eyes moved over her hand pinning the papers, then to her face. "Have you eaten?"

"No."

"It's past seven."

"I know."

Concern showed plainly enough to be embarrassing. He glanced at the cold tea beside her elbow. "I'll get something."

"Malfoy."

He stopped in the doorway to the corridor.

"Did you find anything useful?" he asked.

He wasn't even suspicious. He was just asking. That made it harder to miss what she was doing.

Tomorrow she would put her hands on his arm again, knowing what the Manor did with touch. She would take the tea he brought her. She would keep sitting in this room as if nothing underneath it had changed.

The word for that was not verification.

"Administrative rubbish," she said. "Foundation records. Nothing that matters."

He watched her for a second longer than the answer deserved. Then he nodded and left for the kitchen.

Hermione sat very still at the table until his footsteps were gone.

Nothing had forced it out of her. Not fear for him. Not uncertainty in the text. She had chosen it cleanly and let it stand.
