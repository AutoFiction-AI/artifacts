# Chapter 15

The Levett manuscript was proving resistant.

They'd been at it since dinner, or since what had passed for dinner: tea and the remnants of a bread-and-cheese plate the house-elves left in the library around seven. Hermione had eaten most of the cheese without noticing. Malfoy had eaten the bread. They'd divided the meal without discussion, by habit, by proximity, by whatever silent negotiation had replaced the careful formality of the first weeks.

The text was a construction manual for anchoring infrastructure, written in the fourteenth century by a Malfoy who had either invented a private notation system or been actively hostile to future readers. Possibly both. Marginal annotations ran in three languages and at least two hands. The primary text used runic modifiers neither of them had seen independently, but cross-reference between her theoretical framework and his family knowledge of the symbols had made them almost legible.

Almost. For the past forty minutes, one passage had refused to yield.

"It's not 'binding,'" she said, frowning at the glyph cluster in the center of the page. "The radical is wrong. It's more like threading. Weaving through?"

"Plaiting." He sat close enough that their shoulders nearly met every time they leaned over the text, which was often. Cedar soap clung to him, and beneath it the mineral tang that lived in the deep archive, preservation charms layered so thick in the air that everything tasted faintly of chalk. All evening his handwriting had kept shrinking in the margins of their working translation, tighter and denser with each hour. "The secondary radical modifies it. It's a textile metaphor. My great-great-however-many-times grandmother was apparently a weaver before she married into the name."

"How do you know that?"

"There's a tapestry. Third floor. She wove it herself, and my mother made sure I knew, because—" The sentence stopped there. Nearly midnight, and whatever had almost followed was more than he meant to hand over. "It's a textile metaphor."

Plaiting.

Under that reading, the anchoring magic described threads drawn through substrate, through land and ambient field, then braided into something that held. Not bound. Not hammered. Woven. At once the decay looked different: not masonry failing but fabric coming apart, thread after thread slipping loose.

"That works," she said. "Look. If you read it as plaiting, then this section follows. The whole anchoring system is textile structure. The ley-line connections aren't channels, they're threads. And war damage would be..."

"Cuts. Tears in the fabric."

"Which means the Ministry's been trying to mend a tapestry by building scaffolding around it. No wonder nothing's held."

He pulled a second text toward him, a later manuscript in Latin with French glosses. The page he wanted had been marked with a strip of parchment in his own hand two days ago. Without thinking about it, she watched him scan the Latin. His reading speed there beat hers, one of the few places pureblood education had outpaced her own. Sometime in the third week, she'd stopped resenting that and started relying on it. "This passage references a repair method. Rethreading. Not patching. Actually running new thread through the existing warp."

"You'd need access to the original structure. The loom, essentially."

"Which would be the anchoring points themselves."

"Which are..."

"Under the house. Among other places."

Across the desk their eyes met. The implication settled with the same weight so many things had been carrying between them lately. What they found in this library was going to matter beyond the two of them and beyond this room.

"We should map them."

"Not tonight." He pushed his chair back a fraction and stretched until his neck cracked.

"No. Not tonight."

Another half hour went to the maintenance protocol. Malfoy found a cross-reference in the Latin manuscript, a passage locating what the text called *nodi*, knot-points where plaited threads converged. Hermione pulled the Levett folio closer to see whether the construction manual corroborated the locations. It did, approximately. The notation differed enough that the match wasn't exact, and fifteen minutes disappeared into an argument over whether one glyph marked compass direction or depth below surface. On that point he was almost certainly wrong, but his case was good enough that she wrote both interpretations in the margin and bracketed them.

"We'll need the estate maps. The old ones. Pre-war."

"Second gallery. Third shelf from the end. There's a case with the surveyor's records going back to the sixteenth century." A pause. "I think. I haven't opened it since I was twelve."

"What were you doing with surveyor's records at twelve?"

"Hiding from a dinner party."

A laugh almost escaped her. The image was too specific to be invented: twelve-year-old Malfoy, bored past endurance, opening a case of old maps while his parents entertained downstairs. It was dangerous, how easily those scraps accumulated. Given enough of them, a person took shape.

At the side table, the kettle gave its small working sounds, caddy tap and water pour and spoon against ceramic. Without looking up from the manuscript, she listened anyway. Her attention kept finding him when he wasn't watching. She did not want to name what that meant.

A cup appeared beside her elbow.

"Thanks."

The only reply was a slight tilt of his chin before he sat again.

For weeks now the tea had been exactly right: milk, brew time, the small bitter strength she preferred late at night. He had never asked. He'd simply observed until the cup he made matched the cup she made herself, and there was something quietly devastating in that level of care.

Cold tea an hour later was still tea. She drank it anyway.

Around them the library had settled into what she'd started thinking of as its real self. Not the sunlit room she'd catalogued in early November, but this smaller version under lamplight, shelves closer, silence populated instead of empty. Dark pooled in the upper galleries. The windows reflected the room back at them, and beyond the reflection was winter black without a moon.

Across from her, his hand went to the back of his neck. Lamplight caught the edge of the Mark below his rolled sleeve, and she looked away. By touch she knew it better than by sight. Tonight it sat in its quiet register, not pain exactly, just a low awareness under her skin of his body's condition.

That was the problem. She knew what she knew.

Weeks ago, in the deeper archive, she'd found the bonding texts that named this sensation with clinical precision. Resonance. The growing inability to keep your own physical state cleanly separate from the other person's. The Manor's architecture amplifying what already existed between its inhabitants, gating access behind the strength of the bond. She had read the folio twice. She understood the mechanism. She had not told him. Since then they had sat like this every evening, close over manuscripts, elbows almost touching, while the knowledge stayed in her like swallowed glass, present always and sharp whenever she moved wrong.

"You've gone quiet," he said.

"I'm thinking about loom mechanics."

"You're a terrible liar."

"I'm an excellent liar, actually." The answer was out before she could stop it, and the truth inside it made her grip tighten on the pen until the barrel bit into her fingers. Back to the manuscript. Back to the page. "I'm thinking about how you'd reach an anchoring point buried under a house for six hundred years without destabilizing the house."

On that subject he did what he almost always did: asked once, accepted the deflection, let the silence stand without worrying it into injury. Harry would have circled back. Ron, once upon a time, would have circled back. Malfoy let unanswered things remain unanswered. It made the space between them more bearable. It made concealment easier. Both facts felt terrible.

In the grate the fire had burned low. Neither of them bothered rebuilding it because the Manor had been warm for days now, walls holding heat with the steady generosity of a house that wanted its inhabitants comfortable. Outside, December was doing what December did in Wiltshire: frost on the grounds, dark since four, wind finding old window joins and making that thin winter sound she'd started associating with these late hours.

Her own marginal notes had deteriorated over the past weeks, faster and smaller and full of abbreviations that only made sense if you'd been following the work from the beginning. His were neater, just as dense, and she had started reading them with the same attention she gave the primary text itself: another mind recording how it moved, trusting her to keep up.

"This section," he said at last, angling the text toward her with one hand. His index finger rested under a line of archaic notation. "I think it's maintenance protocol. The language shifts. Imperative, not descriptive. Instructions rather than theory."

Leaning in should have been nothing. His finger was on the page. The notation was dense, the glyphs cramped, and she tilted her head to catch them properly. Instead his face was there, closer than the text required. Lamplight traced his jaw. Shadow lay under his lashes. The notation blurred because he had gone very still.

He was looking at her.

Not the page. Not the text. Her.

For weeks she had been pretending not to see that expression when it flashed across his face and vanished again: the unguarded thing, neither dry nor careful nor precise, only want.

Any sensible version of herself should have leaned back. A comment about the notation would have done it. So would a remark about the hour, the maps, the tea, anything that could have rebuilt the distance they'd been maintaining like a bandage over a wound neither of them wanted to inspect. She knew what the Manor did. She knew what the connection between them had become. A few weeks of concealed knowledge sat behind her eyes telling her that every moment of closeness was suspect, that the warmth might be architecture rather than desire, that the pull between them might be amplification rather than the simple fact of wanting someone.

Still, she didn't lean back.

His mouth was slightly open. A dry place marked his lower lip where he bit it while concentrating. Weeks ago she'd catalogued that detail without asking why. In the quiet she could hear his breathing.

"Granger." His voice came out lower than usual, roughened by something that had nothing to do with speech.

"What?"

"I'm not—" He stopped. Of course he stopped. Self-correction lived in him too, except his always pulled toward less. There was nowhere to retreat now, though. Her face was inches from his. The page lay between them, ignored by both of them. "Never mind."

Her hand went over his on the manuscript.

For a second neither of them breathed. Under her palm his skin felt warm and dry with chalk dust from the preservation charms. Then he turned his hand beneath hers, not away but over, until his palm met hers and his fingers closed around hers. Parchment crumpled under the joined weight of their hands. Neither of them cared.

"I don't know what I'm doing." No research voice in that. No competent, measured version of herself. "I don't—I should—"

He kissed her.

Nothing about it was smooth. His mouth found hers at the wrong angle. Teeth caught her lip. He sucked in a startled breath through his nose. One hand tightened around hers while the other came up and hovered at her jaw, not quite touching, as if he were still waiting for permission or for his body to catch up with what it had already done. Some small sound left her, not a word, something lower and less manageable. She leaned in. The angle corrected. Then his mouth was on hers properly, clumsy still, honestly clumsy, nose knocking hers, tension hard in his jaw when her free hand rose to him without conscious decision.

She had imagined composure. She had imagined his precision extending here too. It didn't. When his hand finally landed on her jaw it gripped too firmly, fingers pressing into the soft skin below her ear with more need than tenderness, and she liked that immediately, liked the plain honesty of his body speaking before the rest of him could interfere. Her free hand caught in the front of his shirt. Through the cotton his heart hammered.

At the same moment the resonance along the Mark went taut.

Not louder. Total. His heartbeat seemed to strike inside her own chest. Adrenaline moved through him and some part of her answered as if it were hers. Under his sleeve the Mark did something strange, not pain, not for either of them, but a live vibrating register that matched her pulse so exactly it made her dizzy. It was the most intimate thing she had ever felt. It was also the thing she trusted least.

*Is this me?* The thought split the surface of the moment. *Is this me or is this the house?*

So she kissed him harder.

That wasn't an answer. It was refusal. His hand slid from her jaw to the back of her neck and into her hair, which had come loose from its tie hours ago. Fingers tightened there. Her scalp tugged. By the time her mind caught up, her body had already opened to him. Tea and library dust touched his mouth, and under both was something unmistakably his.

The desk made the whole thing graceless. A stack of reference texts toppled sideways. One hit the floor with a flat, heavy thud that ought to have broken the moment and didn't. Half out of her chair now, hip jammed against the desk edge, she held his shirt in one hand and his other hand in the other while maintenance protocols for ancient English magic crumpled beneath their fingers.

Want and doubt occupied the same place in her body without canceling each other out. The want was specific: his mouth, his hands, the short hot breath against her upper lip when they broke apart for half a second and came together again. The doubt was just as specific: the house was warm around them, the Mark alive between them, and she could not separate what she felt from what the Manor might be intensifying.

An inch of distance, no more. He stayed close, forehead to hers, breathing rough, eyes closed.

"Is this—" he started.

"Don't." She did not know whether she meant the question or the whole conversation waiting behind it. "Don't. Not yet."

This time the kiss slowed. Startle had gone out of it. What replaced it was worse, or better, depending on how honest she wanted to be. Deliberate now, his mouth moved over hers with a care he had denied himself everywhere else. Then the corner of her mouth. Then her jaw. Head tipped back, throat exposed, she felt his breath at her neck and thought, with a clarity that made her feel briefly sick, *I know what the house does and I'm here anyway.*

Against her throat he said, "Granger."

Not her first name. The surname they had kept between them like the last intact formality. With his mouth at her skin it sounded like no distance at all.

Up at his face her hand went next, thumb along the sharp hinge of his jaw, over the faint scratch of evening stubble. He turned into the touch with his eyes still closed, and that small unguarded movement hit her harder than the kiss had. Before tonight she had seen him careful, dry, polite, angry, locked down. This was something else. This was want stripped of technique. Fear inside it. Relief too.

They stayed there longer than they should have, desk between them, dying fire at their backs, lamp nearly guttered out. The fallen book lay open on the floor. His hand stayed at the back of her neck, not moving, as if either of them might break the moment by pretending it was over.

Eventually they broke apart and sat back in their separate chairs. The absence of his hands felt structural. Across the desk he stared at her with an expression she had never seen on his face before: open, stunned, and frightened in exactly the same register she felt in herself.

"I should go."

"Right." His hand went through his hair and left it standing up wrong. For a moment he looked younger, or maybe just less assembled.

Automatically she started gathering notes, closing the manuscript, recapping the inkwell, aligning loose pages that did not need aligning. A tremor ran through both hands. He noticed. He did not comment. Even that silence lodged in her throat.

"Tomorrow," she said. "The anchoring points. We should map them."

"Tomorrow."

They stood. The library answered with a small settling sound from the wood and stone around them, like the house taking a breath.

"Goodnight, Granger."

"Goodnight."

Outside, corridor sconces burned low. Flames dipped as she passed and rose again behind her. Lately the walk to the guest wing had been doing this, compressing itself, making five minutes into three. Tonight the turns arrived almost before she reached them. The house could tell she was tired, apparently, or wanted her back in bed as quickly as possible. The thought should have made her furious. Instead it landed with a sickening trace of gratitude.

No chance of sleep, though. Her lips still felt bruised from him. One hand carried the ghost of his hand inside it. With every step away from the library, the last hour sharpened instead of fading.

---

Her room had been prepared for her.

Fire burned in the grate though she hadn't left it lit. Bedcovers had been turned down. The lamp beside the bed glowed at its lowest setting. Lavender threaded the air, new and unmistakably deliberate. For ten long seconds she stood inside the doorway with her palm flat to the frame, caught between laughter and the urge to scream.

The door locked under her wand. After that came the deadbolt, because a piece of metal sliding into place still felt reassuring even in a house that rearranged its own corridors.

The mattress dipped beneath her and she sat, hands over her face, breathing through her fingers.

The connection between them had quieted with the distance but not disappeared. One floor above, he was still awake. Not his thoughts, never that. Only register: heart not steady yet, body still lit up with the same shock and want and fear moving through her. The house carried that much between them and no more.

Shoes landed on the floor. Her feet ached with the stiffness that comes from sitting still too long. A jumper followed, dragged over her head and dropped at the bed's edge. Under the glamour her left forearm still held the scar, letters carved there seven years ago by a dead woman in the room downstairs. Three weeks earlier his thumb had traced that word. Tonight both of them had kissed around it, over it, past it, and she had no idea whether that wound had been accounted for or merely covered.

A sane version of her should have been thinking about the research. Plaiting changed everything. Repair methodology, anchoring points, the Ministry's entire approach, all of it looked different now. Normally a breakthrough like that would have kept her upright for hours, scratching diagrams across parchment until dawn.

Instead she lay back with her fingers pressed to her mouth.

What the kiss had shown her was not abstract. Not the same question the folio had set in front of her weeks ago. Not merely house or not-house. It had shown her how little control he had once he crossed the line into honesty. The wrong angle of his mouth. The shake in his hand before it found her jaw. The way he had turned into her palm as if touch itself had become difficult to bear and impossible to refuse. None of that felt theoretical. None of it felt borrowed from architecture.

The house could lay a fire, shorten corridors, turn down her bedcovers. It could thicken whatever already existed until want felt like necessity. What it could not explain away was the startled look on his face before he kissed her, or the hoarse break in his voice, or the fact that she'd known, with a certainty too quick to trust and too deep to dismiss, that he was as frightened as she was.

That was what had changed. Before tonight she could still tell herself the wanting belonged mostly to quiet evenings and proximity and habit, to accumulation. The kiss had proved otherwise. He wanted her badly enough to lose precision. She wanted him badly enough to answer doubt with her mouth.

And he did not know.

He had kissed her believing that whatever sat between them sat there in the open. Tomorrow, she could bring the folio to the library and put it on the desk between them. She could say: I found this weeks ago. I should have told you. The house has been amplifying what's already here, and every hour we've spent together since then has been shaped by something I understood before you did.

She could say it.

She would not say it.

The knowledge arrived with the exhausted clarity that only comes after midnight and after the body has crossed a line it cannot uncross. Telling him would mean watching his face close. It would mean seeing the openness from tonight collapse back into precision, dryness, caution, all the defended distance that had taken five weeks to wear down. More selfish than she wanted to admit, she could not make herself be the person who put those walls back up.

All around her the Manor had reached its warmest point yet. Heat held in the walls, in the blankets, in the air above her face. One floor above, he had finally lain down, but sleep had not found him yet. She could feel that much of his wakefulness through the bond, no more than weather and still far too much.

If she told him tomorrow, he would go formal. The library would fill with the old careful space again. Every small thing between them would go back to having another explanation. After the sound he had made against her mouth and the way his fingers had tightened in her hair, that future felt unbearable.

So she lay on her side in a room the Manor had made too comfortable, breathing lavender it had put there on purpose, and let herself want what she wanted even while she distrusted every condition in which the wanting had grown.

One floor up, the boy she'd kissed lay awake in his own room. Between them sat stone, house, secrecy. All three distances measured exactly the same.

She did not sleep for a long time.
