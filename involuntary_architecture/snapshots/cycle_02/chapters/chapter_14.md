# Chapter 14

The texts were in a script he had never seen his ancestors use.

Draco sat with the folio open across his knees. It was too large for the desk, bound in a hide he couldn't name, the pages thick as card stock and warm under his thumbs. The notation ran in cramped vertical columns: runic shorthand braided with drafting marks, measurements dropped straight into the language as if the writer had never separated words from stone. He could pick out fragments. Family names. Compass bearings. *Fundament*, repeated often enough to matter. Read as a whole, the page refused him.

Not alone.

Across from him, Granger was bent over a second manuscript in the gated archive. The room had opened that morning with suspicious ease. No pause at the threshold, no demand that they prove anything first. The door had simply been there after breakfast, wide as an invitation. If the house meant welcome, fine. If it meant to make togetherness feel ordinary, that fit too.

She had noticed. Her gaze had touched the doorframe, paused, moved on. She did that more now, storing away whatever the Manor offered instead of naming it aloud. Four days earlier, after the Floo failed twice and finally sent her back from London, he'd told her the archive had not opened once in her absence. True, and not the thing he'd meant. Since then they had fallen back into the work with a dangerous ease. Where the room had once snapped shut when one of them crossed the threshold, it now held steady as long as they stayed in it, all gold winter light and well-timed warmth. The house had stopped shoving. It waited them out.

"This column here," Granger said. A quill sat behind her ear. The other half of a biscuit rested with criminal calm on the spine of a fourteenth-century manuscript. Her hair, which had started the day tied back, was now mostly being governed by a pencil. "The notation shifts. It's not the same hand as the first three pages."

Closer made sense. The table was wide, the script tiny, and her finger had already found the line in question. Draco moved his chair until their sleeves brushed.

"Different register," he said. "The earlier pages are formal. This is rougher."

"Rougher how?"

Thicker strokes. Uneven verticals. Abbreviations shoved into the margins with no concern for elegance.

"Field notes. Written on site, I'd say. While the work was being done."

Her attention sharpened. "Original construction?"

"Fourteen-thirties. Braccus Malfoy oversaw the anchoring work here, but he didn't do it himself. He designed it. Other families handled the field construction. Lineal artisans, my father called them. Families older than ours in England, older in this kind of magic too." Lucius's voice came back with uncomfortable clarity, one more inheritance Draco had not asked to keep. "The script must be theirs, or partly theirs."

"So neither of us can read it alone."

"No."

A small nod, more to herself than to him. "Your family knowledge gives us the names and geography. I can work the structural theory. If this is a trade language, that may be enough."

It was not the first time she'd said *enough* and meant *us*.

Work lowered her guard by inches. Concentration put color in her face. It also made her careless in oddly specific ways. The biscuit on the manuscript. The pencil losing its war with her hair. She kept reading under her breath and never seemed to notice. He had been watching her for weeks. Today he stopped bothering to pretend otherwise.

The Mark ached low in his left arm with that newer, stranger responsiveness that had followed her sustained examinations. Near her, the ache pulled instead of bit. He had quit pretending the Mark and his own body were separate systems.

"Look at this," she said, drawing the folio toward the candle. "These aren't repair instructions. They're readings."

Her finger traced a recurring cluster: the root rune, a simplified property sigil, then two parallel strokes tied together by a short bar.

"Inspection?" he asked.

"Assessment. The year mark changes here, here, and here. So do the figures in the margin." She tapped the paired strokes. "This cluster only shows up beside the stable entries."

A cramped note sat under the third mark, written in the mixed hand they were starting to recognize: family shorthand overlaid on trade notation.

Draco sounded it out. "*Concordia incolarum.* Or near enough."

"Concordance of inhabitants," she said. "Household accord, maybe. That's direct textual evidence, not me speculating. And this hooked sign beside it is a resonance mark. I've seen the same form in older anchoring theory."

"Meaning the readings improve when the people in the house are aligned."

"Meaning the record-keepers thought inhabitant concordance affected anchor stability." Her quill hovered above the page. "What I'm inferring is the next part. The folio doesn't tell us whether that concordance means blood, marriage, oath, or any two people the house can use. It also doesn't prove the Manor is doing this to us on purpose. But it does show that the house was designed to route stabilizing force through its inhabitants."

"Connection meaning the bond."

"Connection meaning some magical relation between house and resident. I'm being careful on purpose." She flipped back a page. "This much is solid: the Manor wasn't only built on the ley-lines. It was built into them. The house is one of the anchors."

That settled between them with real weight. Britain's failing magic. The unstable Floo. Children's accidental surges. St. Mungo's rationing. The damage was here too, in the stones around them.

"And the house knows it's failing," he said.

"I think it reacts to strain," she said. "Whether that's knowing in any human sense, I don't know."

He pointed to the paired strokes again. "And that?"

"That's — it says accord matters." She tapped the page. "Not romance, not necessarily. Not even affection in how you'd normally — just a usable alignment between inhabitants."

"So when the lattice weakens—"

"The house leans harder on whatever local stabilizing mechanism it's got left. Yeah." Her mouth tightened, then eased. "The archive opening, the rooms behaving better when we're in step — I think it all fits. I'm not saying it's deliberate. I'm saying the design does this."

"So we're load-bearing."

A laugh escaped her before she could suppress it. "That's one way to put it."

"The bleakest available way."

"Usually your specialty."

He should have answered. Instead his gaze stayed on her mouth a fraction too long. She noticed. Of course she noticed. A quiet had grown between them since her return, different from the earlier careful distance. They kept reaching the same edge and stopping there, each of them waiting for the other to say it first.

"We should keep going," he said.

"Yes." She pulled a clean sheet of parchment toward her. "If these are maintenance records, there should be baseline readings from before the damage. Once we find those, we can compare them to the current decay maps."

The script yielded in fragments. Not quickly. Trade notation assumed a body of shared practice neither of them had. Still, bits began to lock together when his family knowledge met her theory at the correct angle. A place name from Malfoy land records matched to her ley-line geometry. A rune cluster he'd seen in Lucius's study matched to a structural spell described in a text that no longer existed outside this room. The process was slow and absorbing enough that hours went by without anyone naming them.

"Draco Malfoy, sixteenth of his name," he murmured at one point, touching a genealogical marker in the margin. "Cousin line, not mine. They split after Braccus. Normandy and Wiltshire."

"Site supervisor?" she asked.

"I think so. Look. His notes sit between the artisans' marks and the family script. He's translating both directions."

She leaned across the table. Shoulder to shoulder, they studied the same line.

"If he was mediating," she said, "then this whole document isn't — it's not a pure family register. And it's not a pure trade register either. It's a working hybrid."

"A creole."

"Yes." Her eyes stayed on the page, though her voice had gone softer. "A project language. Temporary, practical, and probably uncodified anywhere else."

"So the only way to rebuild it is to do what they did."

"Put both knowledge sets in the same room and see what sticks."

Her face turned toward his at the last sentence. Too close for comfort if comfort had still been the relevant standard. Candlelight warmed her skin. He could have counted the freckles across her nose. Instead he looked back down when she did.

"This mark," she said, tapping the text, "means root or source. I'm fairly sure."

"And this is a property variant. Braccus's branch used the crest without the serpent."

"So root-of-Malfoy."

"The family was one anchor point."

Silence again. The house pressed at the walls, warm and alert. Whether that was pleasure, hunger, or simple pattern recognition, he could not tell. Seduction and leverage were close cousins. The Manor had always known that better than he had.

Fresh candles were stacked on a shelf just inside the archive door. By the time he turned back from lighting them, she'd shifted the desk arrangement: his folio beside hers, reference texts arced between the chairs, space cleared in the middle for a shared key. She had done it absentmindedly, probably. It still knocked him off balance.

"What?" she asked when she caught him looking.

"You reorganized me."

"I made room for the other manuscript."

"Very noble of you."

A corner of her mouth moved, close enough to a smile.

Evening came down hard outside the windows. Inside, the archive narrowed to candlelight, open texts, her knee occasionally brushing his under the table. They argued for twenty minutes over a glyph that turned out to mark seasonal flow rather than direction, and when he conceded, she only said, "It does both, actually," in the tone of someone offering correction, not victory. By then he was close enough to smell candle wax in her hair.

Later, while she reread a passage they'd finally cracked, the pencil gave up and her curls fell loose. One foot was tucked under her. A hole showed at the toe of her sock.

The name for it arrived cleanly: love.

Not falling. Not the start of anything. A fact. The plainest one in the room once he let himself see it. He had spent years building a life narrow enough to survive in: meals alone, corridors kept empty, friendships reduced to the few people who could bear his silences. Need had always been the dangerous part. Need was leverage. Need made a person visible.

Now he wanted ridiculous, ordinary things. Tea brought without ceremony. Her notes left in piles on his table. Her shoulder against his when the script turned ugly. The argument that lasted twenty minutes because neither of them would let the other get away with being half right. He wanted to touch her outside the sanctioned brutality of the Mark examinations. A hand at her back as she passed. His fingers in her hair. Contact that belonged to a life rather than to damage.

"Malfoy."

He looked up.

The folio sat open in her lap, forgotten for the moment. Attention had replaced concentration on her face, alert and careful both. She had caught him staring.

"You're doing it again," she said.

"I know."

That should have been the point where he looked away and made a joke durable enough to hide behind. Instead he kept his eyes on hers. The air between them went very thin.

"You've got ink on your chin," he said.

Her hand went to the wrong side of her face. Before he could reconsider, he reached out and brushed the smudge away with his thumb.

Warm skin. She stayed still.

"There," he said, quieter than he meant to.

"Thanks." Her voice was careful. Her eyes didn't leave his.

His hand dropped back to the table. Neither of them pretended it had been accidental. She studied him for a beat longer, then looked down at the folio and asked, "Can you check the property markers on the northeast section? I think this entry matches the inspection sequence."

"Yes."

Work resumed. The change was slight but undeniable. Her chair stayed where it was. When their shoulders touched again, no one shifted away. The archive held its warmth around them with unnerving patience.

By the time the last candle had burned halfway down, they had enough of the notation to build a real working key. Granger stacked her pages, flexed her ink-stiff fingers, and said, "Tomorrow we can start on the southern anchoring records. If the pattern holds, the early entries should give us the prewar baseline."

"Granger."

Her notes paused in her hands.

"This is the best work I've ever done," he said. The admission came out rougher than he'd planned. "I wanted you to know that."

For a moment she only looked at him. Then, very quietly, "Yeah. Me too."

He should have let her go with that. Instead he heard himself add, "Leave the southern folio here. I'll have dinner sent down tomorrow and we'll keep going."

That got a fuller look. She held his gaze for a beat, weighing the offer.

"All right," she said at last.

She took her notes and went. After the door shut behind her, the archive stayed open a few seconds too long, warm and watchful in a manner he no longer trusted to be innocent. Draco snuffed the candles, left both folios spread on the table, and did not move her chair back across from his.
