# Chapter 3

For this, she'd overprepared. Of course she had. Three parchments of diagnostic protocols lay beside two standard Dark-object assessment frameworks, a stack of control questions, and a sequence of probing charms she'd modified after half the night with Baruffio's monograph on voluntary curse-binding spread across the bed. The literature assumed a compliant subject, adequate workspace, controlled light. It did not assume sustained contact with Draco Malfoy, and it definitely did not assume a house that had started paying attention the hour she arrived.

Morning light made the study usable. That was why she'd chosen it: east-facing windows, a table large enough for notes and wandwork, and the library two doors away in case the morning produced something she needed chased at once. If she had to think after touching him, she wanted books within reach and no intervening corridor.

Across from her, he sat with his left sleeve rolled to the elbow and his forearm laid flat between them like evidence entered into a file.

"I'll need to touch your arm," she said. "Sustained contact. Some of the probing charms need a physical anchor."

"I assumed."

"I'm telling you so you're not startled."

"I won't be startled."

His gaze stayed on the far wall. The control in it was so deliberate it made its own noise.

She moved closer. The chair leg scraped once over stone. From this distance the Mark looked nothing like the wartime photographs or Ministry records. In those images it had been vivid, almost aggressive: skull, serpent, ink-black lines that moved as if they had appetite. On his forearm it had faded toward dark grey. Not gone. Nothing about a Dark Lord's brand simply went away. But the black had softened, the edges had blurred, the serpent's motion had slowed to a tired twitch. It looked less like a symbol than an injury that had stopped healing because it had never been permitted to become an ordinary wound.

At her first touch, the useful fact was tension. Shoulders locked, jaw set, breath measured too carefully to pass for natural. Under her hand his forearm held itself rigid while she turned it toward the light, every muscle braced against stillness. The skull and serpent curved with the arm's actual lines, ink laid over tendon and bone like a stain that had learned the shape of him. His skin was cooler than hers. Pulse steady at the wrist, too steady. He was managing it. Of course he was.

"First pass is a general curse-structure analysis," she said, because protocol required explanation and silence was starting to gather weight. "It shouldn't hurt. You might feel warmth or a mild buzzing."

"Those are meant to be reassuring?"

"They're meant to be accurate."

That got the smallest movement at one corner of his mouth. Not quite a smile. More an acknowledgement that she'd sounded exactly like herself.

Amber light settled over his forearm as the first diagnostic charm resolved the curse's outer structure into something legible. Voldemort's magic carried the fingerprint she'd expected: cold, layered, built to bind loyalty through pain. The surface reading was ugly but conventional. A constriction matrix. Old allegiance channels. Embedded punitive feedback. Through the contact point came the low wrongness of the curse itself, a vibration just below perception, like hearing machinery in another room and knowing from the rhythm that something inside it had worn out badly.

She noted the lattice, angle of response, preservation of surface motion after caster death. On the page her quill stayed exact. Under her hand the Mark stayed still enough to pass for dormant if she had not been close enough to feel the faint, stubborn activity inside it.

The second probe reached the middle layers. Those were stranger. Beneath the brand sat the remnants of a communication architecture, a dead network that had once connected every Death Eater to its maker. Voldemort was dead. The infrastructure remained. Channels that had carried signal now carried nothing. Or tried to. The absence was not clean. Magic still moved down those old paths and met blankness, then circled back on itself. No wonder the ache had no pattern. No wonder it woke him at night.

"Anything?" he asked.

"The middle layer's trying to use channels that don't exist anymore."

"That sounds irritating."

"That's one word for it."

She annotated fast and set the page aside. For the third pass she used her own modification, designed to touch the deepest layer: the binding between curse and bearer, where the Mark met Draco's magical core. She had built the charm on a theoretical model and two badly preserved case studies. It should have done almost nothing if the literature had been right about post-caster collapse.

The Mark moved.

Not the sluggish serpentine drift of the surface. The dark magic in the deep layer turned toward her probe like iron drawn to a magnet. The lines on his forearm deepened by a shade. The serpent lifted its head.

"Granger."

"I see it. Hold still."

Her grip tightened by a fraction as she sent the charm deeper. The response intensified. Dark curse-marks resisted examination. They deflected, lashed out, or went inert. This one reached. Magic flowed toward her wandwork instead of away from it, and beneath her hand his arm, rigid from the first touch, went abruptly slack.

His breathing broke pattern. The careful rhythm gave way to something rougher, less managed. Under sustained contact the faded grey of the Mark darkened toward black, and the restless pull in the old channels stopped all at once.

She looked up. "Tell me exactly what changed."

For a second he didn't answer. Then, more quietly than before, "It stopped."

"The pain?"

"All of it."

The words sat between them. She had expected resistance or flare. Not this. Not immediate relief, and not the nakedness in his face after he said it, as if the answer had slipped out before he could make it smaller.

"Don't move," she said, softer now because the moment required it. "I need a control."

His eyes shut once. Opened. "Fine."

She reduced the charm output while keeping her hand where it was. The deep layer stayed quiet. Then, very carefully, she lifted her palm a bare half inch from his skin.

The effect was immediate. The lines of the Mark jerked dark. His fingers curled hard against the table. Breath caught high and sharp.

Hermione put her hand back at once.

Quiet returned just as fast. Not complete. Not cure. But the frantic, useless churn inside the middle channels dropped away, and the muscles in his forearm loosened under her touch with a relief too obvious to mistake for coincidence.

He let out a breath through his nose and stared at the tabletop. "That's not your spell."

"No," she said.

The answer came out flatter than she'd intended. She did not apologize for the control test. Data mattered. But a pulse of shame still went through her at how quickly his body had betrayed the return of pain.

She ran three more passes under sustained contact, varying pressure, charm depth, and wand angle. Each confirmed the same impossible pattern. Outer layer unchanged. Middle architecture still damaged. Deep binding active seven years after the caster's death and measurably responsive to her magic alone. Not to the diagnostic sequence in abstract. To her. Break contact and the Mark reactivated. Restore it and the agitation quieted again, as if some part of the curse had recognized what was touching it and turned to listen.

That word did not go into the notes.

By the fifth reading his shoulders had lost their first rigid line. Not relaxed. She did not think that was a state he let himself fall into easily, least of all with her hands on him. But the effort of holding absolute control had thinned. A pulse beat steadier at his wrist. Once, when she adjusted the angle of his arm to catch the light, his fingers twitched against the heel of her hand and did not immediately pull away.

Her own body was being unhelpful. The clinical frame had not failed. It was still there, intact, necessary, and she held to it with all the discipline she had. But the contact remained contact. Skin. Warmth returned under his skin while the Mark darkened. Tendon shifting under her thumb when he tried not to react. There was no decent way to turn a forearm into abstraction when it belonged to a man breathing like that across a table from her.

When she finally let go, the absence of contact felt too visible. Her quill scratched three lines across the parchment and caught on the fourth because looking at the page was easier than looking at his arm, or the place inside her hand that still remembered it.

"The curse structure is more layered than the literature accounts for," she said. "I'll need multiple sessions. The response pattern isn't documented."

"Meaning?"

"Meaning the deep layer reacted to my probe. It wasn't hostile. It was responsive."

"You've said that twice."

"Because it's true twice." She looked back at the notes. "And because I don't have a better word yet."

He reached for his sleeve, then stopped with the fabric in his hand. "How long did it stop for?"

She understood the correction a second late. Not how long had the examination taken. How long would the quiet last after her hand was off him.

"I don't know," she said. "We only just ended."

"Useful."

"I'm aware."

He dragged the sleeve down over the Mark and flexed the arm once, testing. His mouth tightened, then eased, then tightened again, as if he were trying to locate a pain that had not yet decided whether to come back. The motion made the answer plain enough.

"It's already returning?" she asked.

"A little." His tone made clear that a little was not nothing. "Not like before. Yet."

That landed harder than she wanted it to. Temporary relief meant two problems where she had thought there might be one. The Mark answered her, and the answer faded.

"I need you to keep track," she said. "When it worsens, when it quiets, what you're doing at the time, whether you're alone."

"You're assigning homework."

"Observation log."

"That's much less insulting."

Again that nearly-smile, there and gone. The dryness was easier for him than sincerity. Easier for her too, probably.

"Same time tomorrow?" she asked. Logistics were safer than interpretation.

"If you need it."

"I do."

He rose carefully, not because she had hurt him but because careful had become habit. At the door he paused long enough to say, without turning, "It hasn't done that for anyone else."

Then he left.

Footsteps carried him into the corridor and vanished before the distance made sense. The house had been doing that with echoes since her arrival. So had her attention.

East light moved across the table. On the page before her, *recognition* appeared in the margin, got crossed out at once, and became *anomalous differential response.* Defensible, certainly. A dodge too.

Dark magic, in her experience, was hostile or inert. Cursed jewelry did not welcome examination. Hexed artifacts did not lean toward a wand. A boggart-infested wardrobe did not quiet under touch. No framework she trusted covered what the Mark had just done. Worse, no framework she trusted covered the control result either. The reaction had not only been magical. It had been conditional. Contact present: quiet. Contact absent: return.

She sorted the morning into what she knew and what she refused, for the moment, to know. Knew: the Mark's old network was damaged but not dead. Knew: some deeper binding remained active. Knew: her touch altered the curse's behavior in a way no protocol accounted for. Refused: the thought that had tried to form when she lifted her hand and watched the pain come back. Refused: the possibility that the study, the house, any of it might matter.

Which was exactly why she went straight to the library. If the answer was somewhere ugly, technical work was how she preferred to meet it.

---

The library did not save her from dwelling on the examination. It merely gave the dwelling something precise to do.

She claimed the southeast corner because it was nearest the long worktable and because choosing a corner was simpler than choosing a system for a collection that did not appear to believe in systems. The first shelf held household ledgers in three hands, then a tract on medieval warding, then a box of unbound French correspondence, then a bestiary whose illustrations pressed faint noses and claws against the page whenever she walked by. Shelf labels contradicted contents. Provenance slips referred to rooms that no longer existed. Several volumes had clearly been stacked behind other volumes years ago and forgotten there. The Malfoys had not built a library so much as kept acquiring until acquisition itself became the organizing principle.

Out came the Department framework: classification by subject, subclassification by language, notation for preservation state, age, and provenance where identifiable. Within an hour the framework had grown three new headings. *Resistance to diagnostic magic* for texts that refused to be sorted and deflected her identification charms with patient stubbornness. *Unknown script* for the shelves of angular notation she had never seen, ink still wet-looking on parchment gone brittle with age. *Misplaced by any sane person* for the volume on blood ward inheritance tucked between household receipts and a child's primer on lunar tides.

She worked quickly at first, because speed let her outrun the memory of his forearm loosening under her hand. Dust greyed her fingers. Notes spread. Books formed rings around her chair: readable texts, unreadable texts, resistant texts, texts that needed preservation before handling, and texts she meant to put back at once but had already started mining for something more useful than their titles suggested.

A copy charm caught the unknown symbols from one brittle sheaf. A preservation veil settled over a cracked binding. A hostile little tract on funerary wards snapped at her knuckles when she tried to shelve it beside a family register and had to be wrapped in cloth before it would consent to remain there. That would have been enough work for a morning. It was not enough to keep her from revisiting the data.

She brought one page of Mark notes into the library with her. She told herself it was ordinary workflow. It was ordinary workflow. The problem was that the library seemed to notice.

The Flamel manuscript arrived in her hands by accident, or what should have been accident. Between two cookbooks sat a first-generation copy of his notation on anchor-point theory, and the shock of it sent her to the floor hard enough that dust rose around her skirts. The cover was warped by old water damage. A cramped marginal hand had annotated half the pages in writing she couldn't yet read. None of that diminished the prize. The anchor diagrams were intact. So were the proofs for ley-line stabilization and the sections Flamel had judged too theoretical to publish in his lifetime.

For several stolen minutes the world narrowed to ink, parchment, and theorem. Manor, Mark, Draco, all of it vanished behind the problem in front of her. Then the problem opened wider.

Flamel's late diagrams distinguished between a dead network and an unloaded one. A magical structure could survive the loss of its maker if the load had migrated elsewhere before failure. Vessel, secondary anchor, or proximate architecture: the phrasing in the margin was half-erased, but the model held. A network built to take signal from one source might continue in damaged form if another structure bore strain or kept the shape of the channels intact.

Hermione read that page three times.

Voldemort was dead. The Mark's middle architecture still existed. Its deep layer still answered. If Flamel's model applied at all, the old network was not simply rotting in place. Something had carried or preserved enough of it to keep it active.

She wrote *secondary anchor?* in the margin before she could stop herself.

Then she stared at the words until she added beneath them: *Could be local. Could be environmental. Test before theorizing.*

That was the moment the shelf above her shoulder moved.

Not much. A dry wooden sound and a drag of weight. She turned in time to see three books ease themselves forward until their spines sat proud of the shelf by an inch each, like fingers raised in a room where no one had asked a question aloud.

Hermione stayed very still.

The first was an early tract on inherited ward structures. The second was a field manual on curse containment copied sometime in the 1820s. The third was a narrow black folio with no title on the spine and a tarnished silver clasp.

She stood, crossed the half step to the shelf, and checked the wood beneath them as if that might produce an ordinary explanation. No hidden rail. No mechanism. No gap large enough for house-elf mischief, if house-elves were even involved in whatever the Manor considered its own business.

She took down the first two volumes. Both made immediate, inconvenient sense beside Flamel. She took down the black folio last.

It was heavier than it looked. The leather had gone smooth with age. No shelf label on the inside cover. No title page. Only a crest stamped blind into the front board and, beneath that, a line of property notation she could not read. Family shorthand, almost certainly. The clasp did not yield to fingers, unlocking charm, or a modest preservation override. When she set it on the table beside her Mark notes, the silver warmed under her thumb and clicked once.

Not open. Merely acknowledged.

She pulled her hand back.

That, too, went into memory before it went into notes.

Work gave her the only decent way to think. She mined Flamel for structural language, cross-referenced the ward tract, and discovered by noon that the collection's real problem was not only scale but intention. Once she started tagging texts that dealt with inhabited structures, responsive wards, or long-lived magical vessels, those texts began finding her faster than her catalogue could justify. A household inventory with nothing relevant in its title opened by itself to a sketch of boundary wards. A volume she had shelved two cases away on legacy enchantments turned up on the table when she came back from the copy stand. The black folio, which she had moved twice into a pile labeled *later, probably impossible,* reappeared on top of Flamel as if it had always belonged there.

The effect was not theatrical. No flying books. No slamming shelves. Just small revisions to location, each one deniable on its own. Taken together, not deniable at all.

She tested it.

First she tucked the Mark notes under a stack of irrelevant household accounts and left the table for ten minutes to examine the unknown-script shelf in the alcove. When she came back, the top account book was where she'd left it. Beneath it, no notes. Those sat beside the black folio at the far end of the table, neatly aligned with a tract on inhabited wards she had not taken down.

Second test: she reshelved the folio herself, memorized the exact gap, and marked the shelf edge in pencil. Forty minutes later the pencil mark remained. The gap remained. The folio waited on her chair.

Third test: she closed her notes entirely and spent a full half hour on the unknown-script material without letting herself glance toward Flamel or the black cover. The half hour produced useful copies and nothing else. When at last she turned, the shelf opposite had angled several inches toward the nearest window, presenting a row of bindings on estate magic that had not been there when she last looked.

By then she no longer had the luxury of calling it coincidence.

If the Manor's anchoring field was participating in the Mark, then the study adjoining the library had not been neutral ground this morning. Every reading she took inside these walls might already be contaminated by the place itself. That did not invalidate the data. It made the data worse. The Mark might not be a sealed problem on Draco's arm at all. It might be an interaction. Mark, bearer, house. Three variables where she had planned for one.

Her stomach gave a sharp, irritated reminder that the body remained part of scholarship whether scholarship liked it or not. She ignored it and kept working.

By midafternoon the table had disappeared under diagrams, transliterations, and competing hypotheses. Flamel gave her structure but not application. The ward tract confirmed that old houses could retain magical shapes long after their makers died. The curse containment manual was useless for anything subtler than a prison hulk. The black folio remained shut and infuriating.

At some point she realized the room had gone quiet around her in the specific way large rooms did when another person entered and did not speak.

Malfoy stood on the opposite side of the table with a cup in one hand and a book under the other arm. Dust streaked one sleeve. His hair was untidier than she'd yet seen it.

"You look terrible," he said.

"I've had a productive day."

"Those are not mutually exclusive."

She took the cup because it was there and because her hands had started to shake from hunger some time ago. Tea. Strong. No sugar. Annoyingly exact.

"The Flamel notation," she said, still half inside the page. "Do you know what you've got there?"

His eyes dropped to the manuscript open under her hand. "It's been there since before I was born. Possibly before my father was."

"It's one of maybe three copies in existence. The Ministry's been looking for one for decades."

"Then it's fortunate you checked between the cookbooks."

This time the joke was clear enough to count. It faded as his gaze settled on the rest of the table. On the black folio. On the stack of estate-magic texts she had definitely not fetched in any rational order.

"Did you put that there?" she asked.

"Which disaster?"

She touched the folio's cover. "This one."

He set his own book down and leaned far enough to see without crowding her. Up close the tiredness in him was less abstract. There was colour high in his cheeks that had not been there in the morning, the sort that usually meant pain was chewing at someone and they were refusing it the dignity of mention.

"No," he said after a moment. "I didn't."

"Has it been in the library long?"

"I don't know. I know the notation." His eyes narrowed at the line under the crest. "Or pieces of it. Very old family shorthand. Pre-reform property script."

"Can you read the title?"

"If it has one, it's not on the outside." He held out a hand. "May I?"

She passed it over.

He turned the folio once, thumb against the clasp, and went still in the way she was starting to recognize as actual attention rather than politeness. "That's odd."

"Because?"

"Because I should be able to open it."

He pressed the clasp. Nothing. Tried a smaller, more precise movement of magic through his fingertips. Still nothing. No click. No warmth. Only old silver under skin.

"Maybe the preservation work is keyed to bloodline," she said.

"I am the bloodline."

"I was attempting tact."

"Then you should stop. It doesn't suit you."

Dry again, but the dryness was stretched thinner now. He handed the folio back. The moment it touched her palm, the clasp gave that same single click.

Neither of them spoke for a beat.

"It did that earlier?" he asked.

"Yes."

"And you didn't lead with that?"

"I was trying to establish whether I was losing my mind in a reproducible way."

"Were you successful?"

"Annoyingly."

His mouth almost moved. Not amusement exactly. Something closer to recognition.

She set the folio down between them. "How long did the quiet last?"

He knew at once which quiet she meant. His gaze shifted from the book to her face and then away again. "A little over an hour."

That was shorter than she'd hoped and longer than she'd feared.

"Then it started again?"

"Yes."

"As before?"

"No." He considered, rejecting whatever easier answer had first presented itself. "Less sharp. More... restless."

Her quill was in her hand before she'd chosen to pick it up. "Any change in location? Did it worsen in a particular room?"

"Granger."

She looked up.

"I've spent eight months in this house trying not to notice where it hurts," he said. His voice stayed level, which made the sentence worse. "You'll have to give me a day to start keeping notes like a good patient."

Heat went across the back of her neck. "Right. Sorry. That wasn't..."

"No, it was exactly what it was." He reached for the cup she'd already emptied and set it a little farther from the papers. "Are you eating at any point, or is this the sort of scholarship where you collapse on a seventeenth-century manuscript and call it method?"

"I've eaten."

"Tea is not food."

"I didn't say tea."

"I don't believe you."

That should have annoyed her. It did, a little. It also landed with the unwelcome texture of care.

She tapped the folio instead. "Does the library usually bring you things?"

His expression changed at that. Not much. Enough. "Not where I can see it."

"That's a remarkable answer."

"It's the accurate one."

He picked up his book. At the door he stopped and glanced back at the spread of texts, the notes, the black cover sitting exactly in the center as if it had placed itself there for emphasis.

"If that book follows you again," he said, "don't tell yourself it's accidental."

"You say that as though I'd be tempted."

"You say that as though you haven't been doing it all day."

Then he was gone, leaving the tea and the dry fact of his absence behind him.

The conversation improved nothing. It only sharpened the problem. The relief from contact had lasted a little over an hour. The house was selecting material relevant to the Mark the instant she started asking the right questions. And Draco, who had grown up here, had given her the sort of answer people gave when they did not want to put a thing into firm language because language made it harder to ignore.

She should have stopped there. Eaten properly. Left the folio for daylight and Malfoy's partial translation. Gone to bed while the corridors still belonged to ordinary architecture.

Instead she kept working, because the pattern was there now and she could not bear to set it down.

Late light drained from the windows. Wandlight took over. She copied Flamel's diagrams, built a comparison grid for the Mark's middle channels against old inhabited-structure models, and finally, after another failed opening sequence on the folio, forced herself to shelve it in a temporary holding gap two cases over from the estate-magic shelf. She marked the location in her notes. If it turned up again, that would be data as well.

Dinner appeared on the end of the long table without servant or summons, warm and proportioned with irritating accuracy. Soup, bread, sliced pears. She ate half of it standing up and noticed only when the bowl was empty that she had not tasted much beyond salt.

By half past nine the library had narrowed to islands of lamplight and paper. Her back ached. Ink had dried on the side of her hand. She found the temporary holding gap where she'd shelved the black folio and saw at once that it was empty.

The note beside the shelf remained in her own hand: *black folio, clasped, old property script, hold here until daylight.*

The folio itself was gone.

She stood looking at the gap longer than the fact deserved if the fact had been ordinary. Then she shut her notebook hard enough to make dust jump from the table.

Bed. Tomorrow. There were limits to how much anomaly one day could productively hold.

---

At half past nine the Manor was quiet in the way places went quiet when they had not actually settled, only withdrawn from immediate display.

What she wanted now was her room, her bed, and the morning's notes spread in sane order under better light. The route should have been simple. Left out of the library, down the corridor with the tall windows and the portrait of the witch in green robes, right at the staircase, up one flight, guest wing. Fifteen minutes. Two turns.

Moonlight silvered the corridor when she turned left. The tall windows were where they belonged. So was the portrait frame. The witch was not. In her place stood a bearded man in late-sixteenth-century robes, dark-haired and still, watching with the calm reserve of a painted figure choosing not to move.

Possible explanation: she'd misremembered. No, not misremembered. Mis-sequenced, perhaps. Tiredness. Distraction. Ten hours of archival work after a morning spent with her hand on Draco Malfoy's arm. Unlikely, still, but safer than the alternative, and she was trying to get to bed, not start an argument with the house.

The staircase appeared where it should. One flight up brought her to a landing with a south-facing window. Her room faced east. No straight ascent should have done that.

Outside the window lay a formal garden she had not seen from any other angle: hedges clipped to geometric severity, a stone bench, tree line beyond. Up here the air was cooler. Underfoot, the stone felt rougher, older, worn in a different pattern than the guest wing. She put a hand to the wall and found the mortar colder too, as if she had climbed not a floor but a century.

Back down the stairs, then. At the bottom waited an entirely different corridor, narrow and vaulted, belonging to another architectural century. No portrait. No windows. Blue flame burned in sconces she'd not seen elsewhere in the Manor, and her steps disappeared into the stone instead of echoing back.

Hostile wasn't the word. Not yet. Pressure came closer: in the air, in the light, in the silence. Not-danger, if she insisted on classifying it. Which was not remotely the same as safe.

Point Me spun her wand north and held. Asked for her room, it pointed left and slightly up. Twenty meters, by estimate. Twenty meters later she opened a door onto a broom cupboard full of mops, buckets, and lemon-cleaner magic old enough to have turned sharp.

That was when fear stopped being abstract.

She shut the door on the cupboard and took two slow breaths. Then she started behaving like herself.

Illumination charms marked the floor at each junction. A chalking spell laid arrows on the stone. A simple tally went into the margin of her notes: left, straight, stair, landing, narrow hall, cupboard. She checked her watch. Eleven minutes since leaving the library. Long enough to be annoyed. Not yet long enough to be frightened into stupidity.

Another corridor offered the tall windows again, but the staircase at its end led down into a hall she'd never seen, vast and cold and unused. A hunt tapestry covered one wall. As she turned away, the nearest painted hound shifted its head by the breadth of a blink.

She went back at once.

The chalk arrow she had left at the last junction waited on the wall, except it now pointed toward her instead of away.

Hermione stopped so abruptly her shoes scuffed stone.

No trick of angle accounted for that. She had drawn the arrow herself. She knew the drag of her wand on the air. She knew where the stroke had gone.

"Right," she said into the corridor. The sound did not travel properly. It seemed to hit the walls and stay there.

She tried a structural revealing charm next. The spell mapped immediate load-bearing lines in pale blue over the stone and should have shown a clean corridor with side branches. Instead the lines layered over one another until three mutually impossible passageways occupied the same space. Old wall through new arch through blocked opening through something that was not exactly a room but had once meant to become one. The spell collapsed under its own contradictions and fizzed out.

Not ordinary wards, then. Or not only wards.

She kept moving. Method survived longer than comfort. A gallery of windows overlooked a courtyard too small to be the central one. A staircase ended at three doors, none familiar. One corridor felt almost known until the sconces changed, the ceiling lifted, and the air picked up the damp vegetal sweetness of a greenhouse after dark. Somewhere water moved in pipes or roots. Somewhere else a door closed far enough away that the sound reached her thinned and wrong.

Seventeen minutes. Nineteen. Twenty-one.

The real problem was not distance. Point Me kept finding it. When she paused to cast again, the needle always settled with infuriating certainty: there. Slightly up. Slightly west. Not far. The distance was true. Or true enough. It was the space between points that would not hold still.

She came back to the blue-flame corridor twice without meaning to. The first time she only recognized the chipped stone at the base of the third sconce. The second time she noticed something else. On the wall beneath the nearest flame, just above waist height, lay a streak of dark polish worn into the stone as if generations of hands had touched there without thinking.

She touched it herself.

The wall was warmer than it had any business being.

Not warm like sunlight on stone. Warm like a body under blankets. Under her palm ran the same low wrongness she had felt through Draco's skin that morning when the deepest layer of the Mark turned toward her probe. Not the same magic. Not identical. But adjacent enough to make the back of her neck prickle.

She took her hand away.

The blue flame to her right brightened.

At the far end of the corridor, where she would have sworn there had been only stone a second earlier, stood a narrow door with no handle.

She did not like the speed of her own understanding. All afternoon the library had revised her worktable a fraction at a time until she noticed. All evening the house had taken one turn, then another, then another, until she admitted she was not walking through neutral space.

Still, understanding wasn't compliance.

She ignored the new door and turned left instead. The corridor narrowed, bent twice, and delivered her to the same blue-flame passage from the opposite direction.

Fine.

She tried the right-hand stair. Up two landings, down one, past a cracked bust and a locked music room, through an antechamber with a ceiling painted as open sky. Blue flame again. Same warm streak on the wall. Same door now standing open by an inch.

The house could repeat itself all night if it liked. So could she. She backed away a third time, jaw set, and followed a long paneled hallway she had not yet seen. At its end waited a dead wall, a blind turn, and then, with a sort of patient inevitability, the blue-flame corridor once more.

By then the refusal itself had become data.

Hermione stood in the middle of the passage and looked straight at the open door. "You want something."

The blue flames held steady. No answer. No movement. Of course not. The Manor had not suddenly become conversational because she was tired enough to try addressing it aloud.

She approached anyway, wand raised.

Beyond the door lay a room barely larger than a confession box. No window. Stone on all four sides. One narrow lectern in the center, black wood polished by age. Resting on it, exactly where no human servant could plausibly have placed it without passing her three times in impossible corridors, sat the black folio from the library.

She knew it at once from the scuffed lower corner and the dulled silver clasp.

For a few seconds she only listened to herself breathe.

Then she stepped in.

Nothing attacked. No ward struck. The air inside the room was close and still, holding dry vellum, cold metal, and the mineral smell of deep stone. The folio sat shut. Patient. Infuriating.

She extended her wand and cast a detection sequence over lectern, floor, walls. Age. Preservation. Containment. No active curse she could isolate cleanly from the rest of the house. The readings tangled with the architecture and went soft at the edges, as though the room objected to being treated as separable from the Manor around it.

Of course it did.

She considered leaving the folio where it was and sleeping in the room itself out of sheer stubbornness. The thought was absurd. Useful only because it clarified the alternatives.

She backed toward the corridor to test one last time. The threshold held. The blue flames beyond it dimmed. The hallway outside did not become her guest wing. It remained the same narrow passage she had been circling for the better part of half an hour.

Not released.

She went to the lectern and put both hands on the folio.

The silver clasp clicked open.

That stopped her more thoroughly than any trap would have. During the day it had yielded nothing. Under Draco's hand, nothing. Under hers now, in the center of a room the house had apparently built out of refusal, it opened without resistance.

The first page showed no title, only a diagram: nested architectural lines overlaid with a second pattern in darker ink, curves and nodes that looked uncomfortably like a magical circulation map. A later hand had annotated the margin in property shorthand dense enough to defeat her at first pass.

She stared at the overlay until the lines stopped behaving like ink and became instruction.

Not proof. Not yet. Not enough. But enough to make her heart kick hard once against her ribs.

She closed the folio and tucked it under her arm.

The response was immediate and almost insultingly plain. The blue flames outside the room guttered down from cobalt to ordinary gold. Air moved through the corridor as if a window had opened somewhere ahead. When she stepped across the threshold and cast Point Me again, the wand snapped toward the right and held so straight it might as well have been pointing down a drawn line.

She followed it.

No wrong stairs. No repeated junctions. The chalk arrow she'd drawn twenty minutes ago appeared once on the proper wall, pointing the proper direction. Her illumination markers remained where she'd left them. One turn, then another, and the guest wing arrived without spectacle: faded blue rug, crooked sconce shade, thin line of yellow light under her door.

She stopped there with the folio still tucked hard against her ribs and let the shape of the thing settle.

The Manor had not tired of frightening her. It had not decided she had suffered enough. It had routed her through itself until she took the book it wanted in her hands. The release was not mercy. It was transaction.

Inside, the room presented itself exactly as she'd left it: notes on the desk, overnight bag on the chair, bed made with house-elf severity, east window intact. Her body only started shaking once the door was shut.

She set the folio on the desk and discovered that her right thumb was streaked grey-black where the clasp had rested against her skin. Not soot. Not ink. Some older metallic residue that did not wipe off on the first pass of her handkerchief. The mark stayed in the whorls of her thumbprint like a tiny bruise made of tarnish.

Observable consequence, then. Useful. She hated that part of herself for noticing.

Once she let herself count the rest, the evidence came too quickly. The portrait. The wrong staircase. The blue-flame corridor. The warm stone under her hand. The shelf shifts earlier. The folio abandoning the place she'd shelved it. Corridors stretching, then folding back, until she accepted the object the house had been steering her toward since afternoon.

She reached for simpler explanations and discarded them almost as fast. Old wards. Responsive architecture. Some inherited defensive system keyed badly to guests. No. Not enough. None of that chose relevant research material. None of that put a specific book in a sealed room and kept a person moving until she picked it up.

It had let her out when it got what it wanted.

The Manor, then. The house. Whatever name she gave it, she was sleeping inside something that paid attention and had preferences about her work.

At the desk, tomorrow's examination notes gained a second heading beneath Dark Mark protocol revisions: MANOR VARIABLES.

Under it she wrote, in ink pressed hard enough to score the page:

No movement through the house alone after dark.

Release tonight followed retrieval of black folio relocated from library.

Possible interaction: Manor conditions may have affected Mark readings. Retest before theorizing.

Thumb contamination from folio clasp; test whether removable in daylight.

Return black folio to library in daylight. Ask Malfoy for property-script translation before further opening.

The folio sat to the left of the page while she wrote, heavy and shut and no longer willing to pretend it was an ordinary book.

Only after the notes were finished did she open it again.

The diagram on the first page had not changed. Nested house-lines, dark circulation pattern, margin note in a hand newer than the rest. On the facing page the old property script continued in narrow, disciplined strokes. Most of it stayed beyond her. The few repeated marks she could sort into pattern looked more like room references than argument, and that was not a distinction she trusted herself to make after midnight.

She closed the folio on that and left it on the desk where she could see it from bed.

Sleep took a long time. When it came, it came shallow, with the sense of walls holding themselves very still around her.
