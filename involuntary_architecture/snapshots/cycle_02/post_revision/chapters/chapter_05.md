# Chapter 5

The work had narrowed to a bottleneck with one Latin verb at its center. If they got the verb wrong, every note she'd made about the decay so far leaned the wrong way: toward attrition instead of damage, toward a tired system winding down instead of a damaged one bleeding out through its seams.

On her eleventh morning at the Manor, grey light hit the east-facing window the house had angled toward dawn. Tea waited on the side table in the usual silver pot. After the usual morning sequence, Hermione dressed, gathered yesterday's notes, and took the short walk to the library. Today the Manor kept it to twenty-three paces.

Settling in was still not the word for any of it. Her job was to use the archive, examine the Mark, and go back to London when both were done. The fact that the house had learned where she liked the light did not alter the arrangement. It only made the arrangement harder to keep impersonal.

Most mornings Malfoy was there first. The childish irritation that had caused in the first week had worn down into a steadier fact: he was where the work was. This morning he stood at the east table with one family journal open, another stacked on top of it, sleeves rolled to the forearm on both sides as if he'd already been at it an hour.

"You were right about the 1712 tract," he said without preamble when she came through the door. "It's the same term in the maintenance rolls. Not similar. The same."

Hermione set her notebook down. "Which term?"

He turned the book toward her and tapped a line written in a cramped, mean seventeenth-century hand.

*Deperdere.*

The margin note beside it was worse. Malfoy family shorthand layered over Latin, half code and half private arrogance. Hermione bent over the page. Under the notation sat a short heading she recognized from the previous afternoon's work: *Ad Tethyn Laboris.*

"No," she said after a moment. "That can't be right."

"Excellent. We're in agreement already."

She ignored that. "If this is attached to the Tethys Working, it can't mean ordinary loss. That procedure only appears in emergency maintenance contexts."

"Yes."

"Then why did you tell me yesterday it might still be standard dissipation?"

"Because yesterday you were treating every family usage as self-mythologizing nonsense and I wanted you to slow down enough to read the line."

Her head came up. "I was not doing that."

One eyebrow lifted a fraction. "You were doing it elegantly."

She opened her mouth, shut it, and reached for the parallel text she'd left flagged overnight. By the time she'd found the place, he had already moved a second volume beside the first.

The argument started there and refused to stop.

*Deperdere*, in standard Latin, could serve for loss, ruin, wasting, destruction. In the Malfoy records the word had narrowed. Not poetically. Functionally. One set of texts used it for ambient decline, another for pressure escaping damaged infrastructure, another for what happened when a working that should have held in one place started leaking into the surrounding field.

"It's not a synonym for *amittere*," Malfoy said, one fingertip resting on the line. "My grandfather's journals make the distinction cleanly. *Amittere* is misplacement, forfeiture, natural expenditure. *Deperdere* is what they wrote when a structure failed and magic went where it wasn't meant to."

Her own source, an anonymous 1790s theoretical treatise in standard Latin, resisted him and supported him at once. "The distinction only holds if the original builders imagined the field as a bounded system."

"They did."

"Not universally." She found the place she wanted and pushed the book across. "Here. *Fundamentum magiae est fluvialis, non lacustris.* The foundation of magic is riverine, not lacustrine. Flow, not reservoir. If the field is movement rather than storage, then structural damage doesn't create leakage from a sealed vessel. It changes the course of an existing current."

He read it, jaw set in concentration rather than resistance. Hermione had started recognizing the difference. Resistance sat higher in him. Concentration narrowed him.

"You're making the same point differently," he said at last. "If a riverbank breaks, the water still goes where it shouldn't. That's the family usage. Not loss in the abstract. Structural hemorrhage."

The word landed harder than she wanted because it was precise, not dramatic.

"Hemorrhage implies active pressure," she said.

"Yes."

"Which means the recent failures aren't the result of gradual depletion."

"Yes."

She looked back down at the page. If he was right, then the Floo failures and concealment slippages and household charm collapses she'd been tracking were not symptoms of a nation slowly running out of power. They were breaches. War damage in old infrastructure kept under strain until the strain found a way out. That model was worse. It was also cleaner.

The quiet that followed lasted just long enough to become useful.

Hermione crossed to the adjacent stack and pulled down the 1712 text again. Malfoy moved aside without looking as she leaned over the table, which meant he had started anticipating her body in space. That fact had become hard to ignore lately. Not alarming exactly. Not harmless either.

"If the Tethys Working is an emergency rerouting procedure," she said, flipping pages until the brittle paper threatened to protest, "then this line might not describe the damage. It might describe the purpose of the working. Not stop the loss. Disperse the pressure before a damaged anchor shears."

"Read it."

She did, first in Latin and then in English because thinking aloud made the syntax visible. "'When the knot refuses binding and the channel takes heat beyond its measure, the field must be...' bloody hell."

"Yes."

"Not restored. Divided."

His mouth bent, not into a smile exactly, but into the shape he made when she arrived where he'd been waiting. "Divided irretrievably," he said. "Deliberately."

Hermione read the line again. Then a third time, slower. The ugly logic of it clarified under repetition. The Tethys Working had not been written to heal damaged infrastructure. It was triage. Bleed off excess pressure. Preserve the surrounding system. Sacrifice local integrity to save the larger network.

"If that's right," she said, "the people who built this expected anchor damage to cascade."

"Of course they did. They weren't idiots."

"No. But they were optimists in other places. Half the repair language assumes competent stewardship over centuries."

"There was your mistake."

She almost said *your family wrote half this* and didn't. It wasn't the point, and he had already done his share of not turning every disagreement into inherited guilt.

Instead she said, "If this model holds, the Ministry's current numbers are worse than useless. They're averaging national instability as if it were fluctuation. If breaches are regional and structural, the average hides the failures that matter."

"Can you prove that from what we have?"

"Maybe not prove. Support hard enough to make Croaker take it seriously."

"That wasn't the question."

It irritated her that he had learned where to press. It irritated her more that he was right to press there.

"Not yet," she said. "But if the maintenance rolls and the theoretical texts converge, then yes, I think I can."

He nodded once and reached for a fresh sheet of parchment. "Then we stop arguing over whether the term matters and start mapping where it appears."

"We've only been arguing because it matters."

"You've noticed."

The next two hours stripped the room of anything except work.

They built a columned list across three sheets: family usage, formal usage, context, implied mechanism, probable modern symptom. Hermione translated and categorized. Malfoy read the shorthand she still could not look at without wanting to set fire to several generations of dead men. The work between them had found a rhythm that was not effortless and not easy. It was better than either of those. It was exact. She would pull a term apart until only one defensible meaning remained. He would catch the place where an apparent contradiction was really a house-specific variation or a family assumption disguised as universal law. Three times she told him he was overgeneralizing from Malfoy sources. Twice he told her she was flattening usage that had technical consequences. Both times they were both partly right.

At one point she reached past him for the maintenance roll and he shifted the parchment toward her before her fingers got there.

"You anticipated that," she said without thinking.

"You were leaning."

"That's not an answer."

"It's the only one you're getting."

The room held the line between them: not quite banter, not quite avoidance. Her pulse did an irritating little step anyway.

By noon the table had vanished under evidence. Books, maintenance rolls, family notebooks, her translation sheets, his smaller cleaner annotations. Dust moved through the high light in slow gold bands. The library smelled of paper, mineral preservative, and the tea he kept forgetting to drink while it was still hot.

On a fourth pass through the 1643 journal, Hermione stopped with her finger braced against the margin.

"Here," she said. "This isn't just family shorthand. It's a distinction between field attrition and anchor rupture."

Malfoy came around the table instead of making her pass the journal across. He bent beside her shoulder, close enough that she caught cold air and starch and the faint bitter trace of black tea. Too close to pretend not to notice. Not close enough to justify reacting.

His hand landed just above the line, pale against the yellowed page. "Read the sentence before."

She did. Then the one after.

There. Not ambiguous this time. Not even written well. The writer had been documenting a damaged site and, halfway through the entry, had stopped generalizing and started naming parts.

"Fine," she said. "You're right about the word."

"I know."

"Don't be insufferable. You're right about the word, and I'm right about the underlying model. The broader system is still flow-based. The failure isn't depletion. It's a break in how the flow is held."

"Macro flow, structural failure mode."

She looked over at him. "Yes."

"You do realize that's exactly what I said yesterday."

"Yesterday you said it like a Malfoy."

"And today?"

"Today you said it with citations."

For a second his mouth moved toward a smile. Gone before it settled, but she'd seen it.

Not winning. Not that. Just the stupid little jolt of getting through to him.

They spent the early afternoon testing the model against recent failures. Hermione pulled the Ministry incident summaries she had copied before moving into the Manor. Concealment charms slipping in pockets across Kent. A Floo hub in Manchester dropping travelers into secondary routes. Two healer reports describing routine tissue-knitting spells refusing to hold at full strength. Malfoy found corresponding phrases in old maintenance notes: heat in the channels, forced dispersion, local rupture preceding regional instability.

"If the Tethys Working was emergency rerouting," Hermione said, "then the absence of any current equivalent is catastrophic."

"That assumes it can still be done."

"It was done then."

"Then they had trained anchor keepers and a network that wasn't half blasted by two wars."

She hated that too. The realism of it. The way it trimmed her instinct toward solvable.

"The procedure may not require the full original network," she said. "There are references to partial field capture."

"There are also references to three deaths in one week at the Kent site."

"I saw that."

"I'm glad one of us is capable of being alarmed by it."

"I'm alarmed by all of it."

"You're excited by it first."

That was unfair enough to make her go still.

His pen stopped. He saw it immediately. Good. Let him.

"That's not what I meant," he said after a beat, quieter now.

Hermione kept her eyes on the page a moment longer than the pause required. When she answered, her voice came out level enough to count as mercy. "I know what you meant. And yes. The work matters before my feelings about the work do. That's how research works."

"For you, perhaps."

"For anyone serious."

The retort was automatic. So was the regret.

He leaned back against the table edge and folded his arms, not defensive, just farther away. "There it is."

"What."

"The part where you decide competence gives you the right to be rude."

She stared at him. The accuracy of it made rebuttal impossible and apology irritating. Both showed.

"I wasn't being rude."

"You were. Elegantly."

There was no graceful answer to having one's own line returned intact. She looked back down at the text because looking at him offered no tactical advantage whatsoever.

After a moment she said, "I'm sorry."

The reply came fast enough that he'd been waiting for it. "Fine."

"And for the record, you were being rude first."

"For the record, I was right first."

That eased the room by a degree. Not enough to call it comfortable. Enough to keep working.

By late afternoon they had the bones of a usable framework. Attrition for ordinary expenditure. Structural hemorrhage for breaches. Emergency dispersion under the Tethys model when damaged sites threatened to tear the wider field with them. Hermione wrote a clean summary while Malfoy checked each term back against the original hand. Twice he changed a single word in her phrasing and both times the sentence got sharper.

It had been years since she'd worked beside someone who could keep up at this speed without turning collaboration into a contest of ego. Harry was brilliant in ways that had nothing to do with archival theory. Ron had always understood pressure and people better than either of them and had never pretended to enjoy research for its own sake. Her colleagues at the Ministry were competent. A few were very good. Most of them, if handed this pile of fractured texts and contradictory notation, would have wanted a week and a team meeting.

Malfoy wanted another source.

"There's a third instance," she said, dragging a volume from the lower stack. "Seventeen twelve. Different author. Same narrowing of meaning."

Without looking up, he held out his hand for the book. She passed it over. Their fingers still did not touch. The inch of air left between skin and skin had stopped being incidental.

He read, turned two pages, then gave a low sound from the back of his throat. Not surprise. Recognition.

"What."

"It's mentioned here as well." He angled the book so she could see. "The Tethys Working. No attribution. Just a note that the manuscript copy in Grandfather's private library was less damaged than the archive copy."

Hermione looked from the line to him. "There was a second copy."

"Apparently."

"At the townhouse?"

"Probably. Unless my mother moved the private collection after the trial and didn't tell me where it went, which is also possible."

"Can you ask her?"

"I can look before I ask. Asking invites interpretation."

"You say that as if interpretation is a finite resource in this family."

"It's not. That's the problem."

Light had gone amber by then. The lamps had not turned on yet, but the windows had started giving back less than they took. Hermione only noticed because the words blurred and she realized she'd been staring at the same sentence for long enough to miss an entire shift in the room.

Malfoy looked at the clock. "You've forgotten food again."

"I haven't forgotten it. I've postponed it."

"With the zeal of a convert."

"It's half past seven already?"

"Nearly."

The text under her hand kept snagging. So did the framework, and the question of whether emergency dispersion could be forced into modern infrastructure. Her stomach settled the argument with one hard cramp.

He heard it; of course he did. One brief look, no triumph in it at all. That made it worse.

"Kitchen," he said.

"I can eat later."

"You can. You shouldn't."

"That's not actually your decision."

"No." He closed his journal. "But if you fall over on my library floor, the house will sulk and I'll never hear the end of it."

That would have been less persuasive if it hadn't sounded partly sincere.

She shut her notebook. "Fine."

He waited until she had marked the page before he stood. Again, he did it as if he wasn't doing it for her. Again, she noticed.

---

Supper was simple and hot and appeared the moment they entered the smaller dining room near the servants' kitchen. Roast chicken, potatoes glossed with butter, greens with too much garlic to count as accidental, bread that still released steam when torn. The windows showed only darkness and the reflection of two people sitting across from each other with the table between them like a rule they were both obeying.

Hermione had never solved the problem of house-elves to her own satisfaction. The old guilt of that sat in her every time one of them appeared and disappeared in efficient silence. Tonight it had to share space with hunger and notes and the fact that Malfoy looked less grey when he was eating than when he wasn't.

"The seventeen-twelve text mentions the Tethys Working as if the writer assumed the reader already knew it," she said once plates had settled them both into the business of using forks. "Not a definition. A reference."

"Which means it circulated beyond one house." He cut into the chicken with neat, economical motions. "Or within several houses that treated each other's records as private enough."

"You say that as if 'private enough' is a category of scholarship."

"In this library it is."

She could not argue with that and did not try.

"If your grandfather's copy still exists," she said, "it may be less damaged where the archive text is water-marked. The missing lines are all in the operative clauses."

"I know."

"Can you get to the townhouse tomorrow?"

"Maybe. The Floo's unreliable, and Apparating there and back aggravates the Mark less, but not enough to call it nothing."

Hermione's fork paused halfway down. "Floo attempts make it worse?"

"Lately, yes."

"Since when?"

He shrugged one shoulder. "The last week, perhaps a little longer. Hard to say. The house absorbs enough of the background noise that I don't always notice the smaller changes until I leave it."

Too neat. Too ugly. Anchor houses buffering local instability. Of course the Manor would blunt what it couldn't stop. She shoved the fact onto the stack in her head marked relevant, unpleasant, not ready for theory.

"How much worse."

"Enough to be annoying. Not enough to justify your face."

"What face."

"That one."

She made herself keep eating. "I'm gathering data."

"You're glaring at a potato."

"The potato is in the blast radius."

That drew the ghost of an actual laugh out of him. Brief. Low. Gone almost immediately, but unmistakable.

Hermione's hand tightened once on her fork and loosened again.

"Can you ask your mother about the manuscript if the townhouse doesn't turn it up?" she said, because if she sat with the way that laugh had gone through her she'd lose the thread. Or not the thread. Something more useful.

"I can." He tore bread, then added, "If I ask, she'll know it matters."

"Does that concern you?"

"No. It concerns me that once she knows it matters, she'll decide which fragment of truth is the useful one."

"That's a very diplomatic way to say your mother withholds information for sport."

"For strategy," he said. "Sport is incidental."

The butter dish shifted a few inches closer to Hermione's side of the table.

She stopped looking at it as soon as she noticed. Pretending the house wasn't listening had not become convincing. But acknowledging each small domestic adjustment made the room feel even more like a place with opinions about them, and she did not have the bandwidth for that tonight.

"How's the Mark otherwise?" she asked.

"The same."

"As yesterday?"

"As yesterday. Active. Not worsening."

He said it with the calm patience of someone reciting a protocol line he'd had to repeat before. A flash of grey amusement crossed his face when she looked up from her plate.

"It's true," he said. "I'd tell you if it changed. You'd alter the exam sequence."

"I know."

"Then stop looking as if I'm lying to annoy you."

"I'm not."

"Granger."

"What."

"Eat the greens."

She looked at him.

He looked back, perfectly grave.

She ate the greens. They were fine. That somehow made the exchange worse.

Afterward the table cleared in stages without either of them formally deciding to clear it. She stacked plates under her hands. He sent them kitchenward with a flick of his wand so casual it bordered on Muggle. The bread basket remained. Hermione ate the last piece standing up, which felt disconcertingly like the gesture of a person who lived here and knew there would be more tomorrow.

"I'm writing up the terminology tonight," she said. "If we keep using two or three words for the same mechanism, I'll start mistranslating our own notes."

"I'll recheck the family usage against the later journals in the morning," he said. "If I've overstated the distinction, better to know before you build a model around it."

"You haven't."

His gaze shifted to her, quick and unreadable. "Confidence from you is such a rare gift. I ought to preserve it in glass."

"Don't be dramatic."

"That's rich."

At the door he paused. The almost-smile threatened again and did not quite form. "Goodnight, Granger."

"Goodnight."

His footsteps rang sharp along the corridor stone and then softened as the house took them. Rosemary and warm bread lingered in the room after he was gone.

The resonance started on the walk back to the guest wing.

Tonight the Manor gave her a straight corridor from the kitchen end of the house to her rooms, portraits lining the walls in bored silence. The Tethys Working occupied most of her attention. So did the structural-hemorrhage model. Malfoy did not. He wasn't supposed to. Even so, halfway down the corridor she stopped so abruptly her skirt brushed the wall.

He was on the third floor.

Not by guess. Not by inference. His position in the house arrived in her mind with the same clean certainty as the weight of her wand in her pocket. Third floor, east side. His rooms.

Under that knowledge sat a second sensation: a dull ache in her left forearm, separate from the scar and wrong in a different register. Scar pain had a hot, scratching texture, like a burn that had healed badly and never finished healing. This was deeper. Borrowed. A pulse moving under the skin with a timing her body did not recognize as its own.

Her hand went over the place through her sleeve. Pressure changed nothing.

The ache was not in skin or muscle. It sat under both. In the diagnostic trace left by three examinations, perhaps. In residue from sustained contact with curse magic. There was precedent for that. Cursed-object examiners sometimes developed temporary sensory artifacts after repeated exposure. Minor, transient, directionless. St. Mungo's recommended reduced contact duration and a review of shielding sequences if the examiner started reporting spillover.

Directionless.

This had a direction.

Hermione took three paces toward the west gallery.

The ache thinned.

She went still, then turned back east. It sharpened again, not dramatically, just enough to make her feel stupid for trying denial in the first place.

"No," she said aloud to the corridor, very softly.

No answer came. The portraits watched with the infinite discretion of painted aristocrats.

She tested it again because testing was what made fear sortable. Five paces west. Fainter. Back to center. Stronger. Two paces toward the stair that led to the upper east rooms. Stronger again, and under the ache now a shape she recognized with appalling certainty: restless magic pushing against a dead command channel, the particular ugly movement of the Mark when it wanted to go somewhere it could no longer go.

By the time she made herself continue to her rooms, her breathing had gone too fast.

Inside, the literature argued with the evidence and lost.

Belby's monograph on dark-object examination described spillover as static residue, a trace impression of the object's magical quality. The St. Mungo's protocols described aftereffects lasting hours, sometimes days, but they were all non-specific: heat, nausea, a phantom itch, temporary arm pain in the casting limb. Nothing in the literature covered knowing which floor another person occupied through old stone and closed doors. Nothing in the literature covered recognizing the quality of another person's pain from inside one's own body.

She took the nearest explanation because the others were worse.

Repeated examination of an unusually active dark curse could have produced an anomalous sympathetic trace. The trace could have latched onto the diagnostic sequence. The Manor's ambient magic, already unstable, could have amplified the artifact. Ugly. Makeshift. Still inside the borders of science if she held it far enough away.

She wrote it down in those terms, neat as a protocol note, and managed to believe it for nearly a minute.

Then the ache moved again.

Not stronger. Different. A short hard pulse that made her flinch and glance toward the door as if the corridor beyond it had changed. She checked the clock. Nine twenty-two. The pulse subsided by the time she uncapped her ink again.

At half ten she gave in and turned the observation into a test.

She opened her bedroom door, stepped into the guest-wing corridor, and stood there with her notebook in one hand like an idiot conducting an experiment on her own loss of composure. The house had dimmed the sconces. Carpet muffled her steps. Far off, stone ticked once as if the Manor were settling deeper into itself.

Third floor, east side. Still there.

She took the nearer stair. One landing. Two. The ache sharpened with altitude.

Any honest professional would have turned back there. She knew it while her hand was already on the rail. Knew the test had tipped. Not into theory now. Into the ugly pull of being able to feel him without being seen. She climbed one more half-flight anyway, then stopped hard enough to catch the banister.

Above her, beyond another turn in the stair, waited his corridor.

She did not go that far.

From where she stood, she could feel the resonance as a low line under her skin, no longer only pain. Wakefulness. Tension held tight enough to count as posture even through the link. The fact of another body not resting. It made no sense. Or rather it made too much. The intimacy of it hit a beat later and hit harder.

Not clinical residue. Clinical residue did not tempt people up stairs.

Hermione backed down the steps, one at a time, furious with the house, with the Mark, with herself, with the exact order in which those angers presented. Back in her room she locked the door, though nothing in the Manor suggested locks mattered except as performance.

By eleven she had three pages of notes and no theory she trusted.

Possible variables:

Repeated direct examination.

Active dark curse in environmental proximity to anchor-house instability.

House amplification.

Unknown sympathetic transfer.

She crossed out the last phrase and wrote *resonance* in its place, then stared at the word until it started looking melodramatic and underdescriptive at once.

The lamp went out. Midnight found her flat on her back in the dark, fingertips resting against the inside of her left forearm, counting a pulse that was not hers and trying, idiotically, to make it mean nothing but exhaustion.

If it persisted, she would tell him. Clinically relevant data belonged in the protocol. Keeping it to herself compromised the work.

She lasted perhaps thirty seconds with that plan.

The conversation arrived whole and impossible the moment she tried to picture it. *I can feel where you are in the house. I can tell when your arm hurts. I followed it halfway to your rooms and then came to my senses.* No way to say any of that aloud without making it sound as intimate as it was. No way to make it procedural once it entered air.

It could still fade. Tomorrow, maybe. The day after. The literature had no category for it, but literature lagged real magic all the time. The trace would weaken. The directional pull would burn out. She would be left with a useful anomaly instead of a private trespass.

In the dark, with the pulse still there, she let it keep her company and hated the small ugly part of herself that didn't want it gone at once.

---

Three days later she met Harry for lunch at a pub in Bethnal Green, a Muggle place he had found when he and Ginny first moved east. The food was mediocre, the chips were excellent, and nobody from the Ministry would have thought to look for either of them there.

The resonance thinned on the road out of Wiltshire and kept thinning all the way to London. By the time she crossed from the side street to the pub's front door, it had gone from a live line in her arm to the faintest residue, a trace she could almost dismiss as her own nerves. Almost.

London hit hard after the Manor. Diesel. Damp concrete. People moving at speed because the city demanded it. A siren somewhere three streets over. The late-November air smelled like buses and rain held back for an hour or two more.

Harry had already claimed a table near the back. He stood when he saw her, crossed the distance before she was fully out of her coat, and hugged her with the same unselfconscious certainty he had at seventeen and twenty-five and every age between. His jacket smelled faintly of smoke from the Auror training grounds.

"You look all right," he said when they sat. "Bit tired."

"Thanks."

"I could've gone with terrible."

"I look fine."

"Yeah, you do." He picked up his pint. "How's the library?"

The research was the safest part to tell. She gave him that first. The scale of the collection. The anchoring texts. The emerging picture of war damage to the infrastructure under British magic. Not the house adjusting itself around her. Not the resonance. Not the increasingly specific pleasure of arguing with Malfoy until both of them forgot meals and time and the part of the world not made of old paper and winter light.

"So it's real," Harry said when she finished the cleaner version. "The decay has a cause."

"It has a cause. Reversing it is the problem."

"But there is a cause."

"Yes."

"Good."

He said it like a man relieved to find the disaster had at least chosen the courtesy of being legible.

The server dropped plates and vanished again. Harry salted his chips. Hermione reached automatically for vinegar.

"What's the latest?" she asked. "Outside the library. Actual failures, not Ministry statements."

"Floo's getting worse." He shoved his plate toward the center without comment, sharing as naturally as breathing. "Three failures on our line last week. One dumped me in Birmingham."

"Birmingham."

"Don't sound so betrayed."

"I sound appropriately horrified."

"Good. Keep that. It was freezing and the reception witch thought I was drunk."

Against her will, Hermione laughed.

Harry's expression softened at the edges. He'd been aiming for that and knew he'd hit it.

"Ginny spoke to someone at the Prophet," he said. "They're sitting on two infrastructure stories because the Ministry keeps insisting it's isolated maintenance. It isn't. We've had bad Apparition splinches too. Low-grade, mostly. Not enough to hit the front page yet. Enough that the healers are swearing."

The clean lines of theory in Hermione's head shifted and locked around that information. Floo hubs, Apparition strain, concealment failures. Different systems, one pressure source. The old maintenance language started lining up in her mind faster than she could write it.

"I need whatever those Prophet reporters have," she said.

"I'll get it."

"And the DMLE incidents if you can."

"I said I'll get it."

He wasn't offended. Just steadying the pace of her. He had spent enough years watching her run at problems as if speed itself were a form of ethics.

"Sorry."

"You are not sorry."

"No."

"Good. Saves time."

For a few minutes they ate, and the ordinary business of chips and bad pub cutlery did what ordinary business sometimes still could. Harry told her James had learned to say the word *broom* in a tone that suggested he believed all objects ought to be called broom if he wanted them badly enough. Hermione told him about a Ministry clerk who had filed six separate reports under six different spellings of *filum fracture* because he did not believe the phrase ought to exist in English. Harry nearly choked laughing. She had to pass him water. The server looked over as if deciding whether intervention was required and then thought better of it.

When the moment settled, Harry asked, "Getting enough sleep?"

"Yes."

"Hermione."

"I'm sleeping. The room's quiet, the bed's comfortable, I'm getting six hours."

Five of those hours sometimes involved lying awake with another person's pain feathering through her arm so faintly she could almost think it memory. The residue was weak here, but not gone. She held her wrist under the table and let the pulse disappear beneath movement and noise.

"You look tired," Harry said again, not accusing. Just placing evidence on the table.

While she reached for her water, her sleeve started to ride up. Instinct caught it before thought. She pulled the cuff down with her other hand and wrapped both around the glass. Seven years had turned that motion automatic. The scar was a fact the body managed before the mind needed briefing.

Harry saw the movement. Of course he did. He said nothing about it.

"Ginny says hi," he said instead. "She also says you're coming to dinner the next time you're in London for more than an afternoon. She's got opinions about the Floo and she'd like to deliver them at volume."

"That sounds like a threat."

"You know Ginny. It's an invitation with structural integrity."

"How bad's the Floo on your end, really?"

"Bad enough that she doesn't want James using it at all this week. One of the lines at the Prophet spat three people into the wrong atrium and singed a fourth bloke's eyebrows clean off. He apparently looked better after, which isn't relevant but is what Ginny led with."

"Useful of her."

"She contains multitudes."

Hermione cut into the chicken pie and did not taste it. Harry's field anecdotes slotted into the structural model with ugly ease. If the hubs were going irregular at the same time old anchor houses were reporting instability, then the repair problem was no longer theoretical. It was public waiting to happen.

"You sound like you've been waiting for someone to confirm it," Harry said.

She had. He heard the answer before she gave it.

"The research matters more than I thought it did," she said. "It's diagnostic now, not historical."

"Right."

"And if the old maintenance procedures were triage rather than repair, then it may be worse than diagnostic. We may not have a clean restoration model left."

"That sounds bad."

"It is bad."

"All right." He took a drink, eyes on her over the rim. "And the Manor?"

The change in subject was gentle enough to count as kindness and direct enough to count as Harry.

"What about it."

"Is it all right?"

"The house is a house."

"That's not an answer."

"It's the only one you're getting."

"I learned that move from you," he said. "Still an answer."

Last week's fireplace conversation had included variable corridors and the fact that certain rooms appeared closer together some days than others. It had not included the shelves surfacing the books she wanted before she asked for them. It had not included the hook now waiting for her coat in the entrance hall. It had not included the way silence in the library had started to feel less like absence and more like a third presence with opinions about pace and proximity.

"It's strange," she said finally. "The corridors change. The rooms rearrange. The house pays attention."

"To you."

Not a question.

Hermione took a bite she did not want and chewed before answering. "Apparently."

"And you can still work in there?"

He asked it practically, the same tone as any other question.

"I can. Better than I expected."

"Good."

She almost said more. Didn't. Harry saw that and let it pass, which meant the thing he wouldn't let pass was coming.

"You're not sleeping," he said.

"I told you—"

"You told me six hours. You don't look like six hours." He put down his fork. "You look knackered, and you're living in a house that moves its own walls. That's what I'm asking about."

The residue in her arm gave one faint pulse and disappeared again under the noise of the room. London made concealment easy. That should have felt like relief.

Her hand went to the sleeve a second time, this time only tugging the cuff lower over the wrist while she reached for a chip. Same gesture, different reason. The first one had protected the scar. This one gave her hands a task while she lied to someone who had known her too long for most lies to pass.

"I'm all right," she said. "Promise."

The green of his eyes made lying feel adolescent and clumsy. He let it go anyway. Belief had nothing to do with it. He knew the difference between not ready and never, and after all these years he had learned that pushing her too hard only made her turn fact into argument.

For now, it was neither. It was a lie.

"All right," he said. "Then come for dinner next week. Ginny'll do that lemon chicken you like and take the piss out of me in front of you, which seems healthy."

"That does sound healthy."

"Don't dodge."

"I'm not dodging."

"You are. You're doing it politely."

She looked down at her plate. "I'll come if I can."

"Come if you can't. I'll Apparate you back myself."

"You're impossible."

"And yet."

The rest of lunch turned easier by degrees. Harry told her about a cursed postbox in Croydon and three weeks of the Royal Mail blaming ghosts. She told him about a Ministry undersecretary who had tried to solve a stabilizing-charm collapse by banning the word collapse from all written communications. Harry paid because he always paid. Hermione let him because they had exhausted that argument before they were old enough to drink legally.

Outside, the sky over Bethnal Green sat low and grey. Harry shoved his hands into his coat pockets and looked at her with that steady older-brother seriousness he only ever deployed by accident.

"Come back soon," he said. "Not in another two weeks."

"It hasn't been that long."

"It's been fourteen days."

He was right. Fourteen days at the Manor and time had done the odd compression trick it did in intense places. The days had not dragged. They had thickened.

"I'll come for dinner," she said. "Tell Ginny I'll bring wine."

"She'll say you don't have to."

"I know."

The second hug lasted longer than usual. Then Harry headed toward the Apparition point and she was left on the pavement with the city all around her and only the thinnest residue at her forearm, a weak line that barely counted as connection until she paid attention to it. Out here her arm was mostly her own. Mostly.

That, too, was information.

She held onto it all the way back to Wiltshire.

---

Half three found the grounds cold and the formal garden stripped brown by the season. The Manor rose beyond the hedgerow in pale stone and dark windows, looking as if it had been aware of the road long before she turned onto it.

The front door opened before she touched it.

Warmth met her first, then wood polish and old stone. Near the entrance a hook had appeared for her coat sometime in the last several days. It had not been there when she arrived. Or it had not chosen to be visible.

The residue in her arm sharpened before she reached the main stair.

By the third step it was unmistakable. A low pulse under the skin, immediate and directional. Library. Active Mark. Not severe, but not quiet either.

So the signal did reach London, only weakly. Distance thinned it. The house strengthened it. Proximity sharpened it into location. That was ugly. It was also testable.

Hermione paused at the first landing and turned deliberately toward the west corridor.

Fainter.

Back to center. Stronger.

Up another flight. Stronger again, enough that she could tell the difference between the ordinary restless pressure of the Mark and the sharper catches that meant it had been aggravated recently.

Whatever this was, it obeyed conditions. Distance. Architecture. House state. Perhaps his own nearness. Perhaps her own. The scientific part of her took hold of those variables and started naming them before the rest of her could decide whether naming them helped.

She found him in the library with a fresh stack of texts, three flagged journals, and a pot of tea still steaming at his elbow. He had spent the afternoon working. The evidence of it lay everywhere: shorthand sheets translated into partial English, a map of southern England sketched from memory and crosshatched with question marks, a second pile of notes written in the smaller tighter hand he used when the pain was bad.

He looked up. "London?"

"Lunch with Harry. The Floo network's degrading at the hub level. Three failures on the Potter line last week, including Birmingham."

His mouth shifted. "That is a failure, yes."

"I thought you'd appreciate the taxonomy."

"Always."

He set down his pen and pushed one of the journals across the table. "I spent the afternoon checking the later family records. Two more references to Tethys. Both in relation to failed travel routes."

Hermione shrugged out of her coat and sat. The pulse in her arm kept time with him across the table, low and invasive.

"Travel routes as in local transit enchantments," she said, reaching for the journal, "or travel routes as in long-distance corridor work."

"Both, I think. The notation's muddy." He turned a page with the side of one hand as if the skin of the other forearm had gone sensitive again. "Read that line."

She did. Then the one under it.

"It's not just route failure," she said. "It's rerouting strain into domestic wards to keep the line from breaking."

"Which sounds familiar."

"Because the house has been doing exactly that."

"And the Floo hubs may be doing it badly."

Harry's account, the old text, the pulse under her skin, all of it assembled into a pattern she disliked more with each piece added. She pulled her notebook toward her and started laying the data in order: public travel failures, house-specific shielding, Mark spikes after transit strain, old emergency dispersion model.

Across from her, Malfoy watched for a second too long. "You look like you've found a body."

"I may have found the shape of one."

"Comforting."

"Harry says the Prophet's sitting on two infrastructure stories. If they're right about the hubs going, then the failures aren't random. They're clustered in systems that rely on anchored routing."

"That's not good."

"No."

He sat back. The movement sent a sharper flare through her forearm before his expression changed. A beat later his jaw tightened and his right hand went almost casually to the edge of the table, fingers pressing once against the wood.

The question left her before she could stop it.

"Was it worse around noon?"

His eyes lifted to her face. "My arm?"

"Yes."

"You weren't here at noon."

No good answer arrived. The one she used was only partly false. "I'm trying to map Mark spikes against transit failures. If the same structural damage is feeding both, timing matters."

His expression changed less than most people's would have. That was usually worse.

"Around noon," he said after a moment, "it was worse after the second Floo attempt. Then it settled."

The pulse in her own arm answered as if in confirmation. Hermione wrote the note down too hard and hated that he could probably see the force of it.

"And before noon."

"Manageable."

"After."

"I said manageable."

"That's not a quantity."

"You should try living with it. The quantities get vague."

The correction landed exactly where it ought to. She dipped her head, conceding it. "Right."

He went back to the journal. She made herself do the same.

For twenty minutes they worked in rough quiet. Hermione translated a maintenance note from 1734 that treated field leakage as if it were weather. Malfoy cross-checked an inventory list against what had survived the postwar seizures. The pulse in her arm did not stop. It registered every subtle shift in his body before the rest of the room caught up. When he flexed his left hand under the table, she knew. When the ache eased, she felt that too.

This was not sustainable.

The concealment had already changed the quality of the work. She was listening for him while pretending not to. Filtering her own observations before they reached speech. Testing hypotheses with her skin and then translating them back into respectable language. If she kept going like this, the protocol would rot around the lie even if the lie never left her mouth.

She set down her pen.

"I want to change tomorrow's exam."

That got his attention more cleanly than the noon question had. "How."

"Up to now I've been reading it wand-first." She kept her tone clinical because anything else would have been fatal. "The wand buffer gives me surface activity, heat, flare pattern, responsiveness to stimulus. It keeps my own magic from sitting directly on the curse and it stops the Mark from using my hand as a second conductor."

His gaze stayed on her, intent and unsparing.

"It's safer," he said.

"Yes. It's also blurring the deeper signature."

"Blurring what, exactly."

Hermione made herself answer the question he had actually asked. "Whether the recent surges belong to the Mark itself or to the damaged field around it. The wand lattice smooths both into the same reading. If the Mark is reacting to local structural failures, I need the raw pattern, not the buffered one."

"And the change."

"No wand buffer. Sustained direct contact on the Mark itself."

Silence.

The silence was practical, not dramatic. Two people running the same calculation from different sides.

When he spoke, his voice had flattened into the careful register he used when the subject had turned from unpleasant to specific. "Direct contact for how long."

"Long enough for the flare pattern to settle. Several minutes, probably."

"Your hand over it."

"Yes."

"No spell insulation."

"No wand between us."

He leaned back a fraction. That was the pause. Not prudery. Not embarrassment. Risk.

"The buffer exists for a reason," he said.

"I know."

"If it spikes, it goes into you."

"If it spikes hard, I break contact and terminate the session."

"And if it doesn't spike hard. If it just stays there."

He cut the sentence off before the real end of it. That, too, she had started recognizing.

"Then I get a clearer reading," Hermione said.

It was only part of the truth. The rest sat live in her arm, warm and invasive and impossible to explain without telling him far more than she intended to tell anyone. Direct contact might damp the resonance. It might sharpen it into certainty. It might tell her whether the link was a diagnostic artifact, a function of the house, or a property of the Mark itself. It might also be unbearable. She needed to know before the anomaly hardened into protocol by neglect.

He looked at her for a long time.

"What do you think it'll show," he said finally.

Hermione chose with care. "The current exam can tell me when the Mark is active. It can't tell me what it's responding to. Direct palm contact should separate the curse's own pulse from the field disturbance around it. If the Mark's reacting to local network strain, I need to see that without the buffer smearing the trace."

It was true. Not complete. True enough to survive scrutiny.

"And you need that tomorrow."

"Yes."

"Because of the Floo."

"Because of the Floo, because of the house, because the last three sessions have been getting less stable." Another truth, if not the one at the center. "And because if the old texts are right, we're already late."

His fingers tapped once against the tabletop and stopped. The pulse in her arm echoed it a beat later, a private obscenity.

"Where," he asked.

She blinked. "Where what."

"Where are you doing it. Not here, I assume."

She looked around at the main table. Open books. Draft notes. Too much space to make the contact feel technical, oddly enough. Too public. Too exposed.

"The small sitting room off the east wall," she said. "Better light. Fewer interruptions. I want the instruments set already so I'm not breaking contact to reach for anything."

"Natural light matters to the diagnostics."

"It helps with color shift and skin response."

"Right."

He was still weighing it. She could see that in the stillness more than the face. He did not interrupt when he was distressed. He got quieter.

"You understand what you're asking for," he said at last. "Not just the reading."

There it was. Not said fully. Audible anyway.

Hermione kept her eyes on the notebook because looking directly at him would have risked honesty. "Yes."

That was a lie too, at least in part. She understood the method. She understood the risk to the exam sequence. She did not understand what prolonged contact would do to the link already running under her skin, and he knew enough to hear that uncertainty even if he could not name it.

For a second she thought he would refuse. The refusal would have been reasonable. Easier, perhaps. Easier for the part of her still pretending the buffered method could continue indefinitely while she quietly used his body as an instrument and said nothing.

Instead he said, "All right."

Relief hit so hard it bordered on anger.

"Tomorrow morning," she said, too quickly. "Before we start the library work. I'll revise the sequence tonight."

"Tell me what you need me to do."

"Sit still. Same as always."

One corner of his mouth moved. "You do ask the impossible."

The line was dry enough to count as normal. The fact that normal had returned at all after that exchange felt dangerously like a gift.

"I also need you not to try the Floo again before the exam," she said, back to notes because notes were safer. "If transit strain aggravates the Mark, I want a clean baseline."

"Fine."

"And no analgesic charms on the arm beforehand."

"I wasn't planning any."

"Good."

He rose a few minutes later to pull another text from the west shelf. The resonance moved before he did. Hermione kept her face down over the protocol sheet and wrote as if that private advance warning were not currently ruining her life.

Session IV.

Environmental conditions: morning light, east sitting room, instruments pre-laid.

Baseline wand pass limited to initial surface reading only.

Primary diagnostic method: sustained direct palm contact over Mark. No buffer.

Observe:

Mark heat shift.

Pulse differentiation.

Response to sustained contact.

Transfer effects in examiner.

The last line sat on the page in clean black ink. Too clean. Too calm. She nearly scratched it out, then left it there.

Across the room, Malfoy had gone still again in that layered way he did when he was reading hard enough for the rest of himself to quiet. Watching him work had become its own form of trouble. Pen paused. Eyes slightly unfocused. Body perfectly under control until the moment an answer arrived and his hand moved.

It had been years since anyone had made the work feel this alive.

That was not the same thing as safety.

By nine he closed the book he'd been using and said, "I'm going upstairs."

The pulse shifted before the sound of his chair did. What she hated most was the precedence, not the sensation itself.

"Tomorrow, then," he said.

"Tomorrow."

He left.

His footsteps faded into the upper corridors. The line in her arm went with him, thinning by degrees as distance and walls asserted themselves. Hermione sat alone at the table until the lamps had to brighten around her and the protocol page dried under her hand.

Then she drew one firm line through *wand-mediated sequence* and wrote beneath it:

Session IV. Direct palm contact. Full duration.
