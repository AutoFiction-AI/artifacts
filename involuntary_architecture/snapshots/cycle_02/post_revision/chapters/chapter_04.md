# Chapter 4

The east corridor had a draught problem. Thin cold leaked between the stones near the third window, the one with cracked leading older than Draco's father. By late autumn he dressed for that passage without thinking, collar up, warming charm ready, body already braced for the bite.

Granger had been in the Manor for four days, and the draught was gone.

On Tuesday morning, halfway to the main staircase, Draco realized what was missing: no cold on the left side of his neck, no thin pull of air under his collar. Warmth held in the wall instead, deep and stored, summer caught in the stone.

It was November.

His palm went to the wall before he had decided to touch it. Something under the stone answered him, pulse-like and faintly alive where there should have been none. After a long second he took his hand away and finished the walk without brushing the wall again.

The library doors were already open. Both panels stood wide. Usually he had to shoulder one aside and rap the old pattern on the other if the latch had settled badly; that morning the oak waited for him like a welcome he had never once been given.

At the long table, Granger had three texts spread in a neat half-circle, the black folio from the night before shut beside her elbow, quill in her left hand, wand in her right. Without looking up, she said, "The Alderic folio was on the third shelf yesterday. Red binding. Preservation charms on the spine. It's moved."

"Moved where?"

The tip of her quill indicated the table beside her. "There. I came in and it was waiting."

Across the room, a black rectangle gaped on the shelf where the book had sat for years. Dust still marked its outline. Eighteen inches from her hand, the folio leaned against a stack of astronomical journals.

"The house does that," Draco said. "It rearranges."

"For me." Only then did Hermione glance up. Interest sat on her face more clearly than alarm. "Three other books I meant to pull today are here as well. I wrote the list last night. I didn't mention it to anyone."

Nothing useful came to mind. Agreement would sound absurd. Denial would sound worse. Throughout his childhood the Manor had ferried Narcissa's reading glasses from room to room without comment, but the house had known his mother for twenty-five years. Hermione Granger had been sleeping here since Friday.

Tea gave him something to do with his hands. In the service alcove off the library, the kettle took up its ordinary muttering while he listened past it. Preservation charms ticked in the shelves. Somewhere overhead a gallery board creaked. Under both sounds sat a lower hum that had not existed last week and now seemed to live in his sternum whenever Hermione was nearby.

By the time he returned with two cups, she was at the genealogical shelves with one arm stretched overhead, fingertips grazing a volume she could not quite reach. The wall altered so subtly that it took him a second to register it. Stone, shelf, and distance shifted just enough. The top row lowered into her reach. Hermione took the book down as if nothing in the room had moved.

His tea landed beside her untouched cup hard enough to send a line over the saucer. He said nothing.

Hermione set the book on the table and finally looked at him properly. "You've got that expression again."

"What expression."

"The one that suggests the building has insulted you."

"That would imply novelty."

A small line appeared between her brows. "If you know what it's doing, you might say so. I can work with strange. I don't work especially well with everyone else pretending not to see it."

Draco looked from her to the lowered shelf and back again. "I'm still deciding whether 'strange' is the useful word."

"Hm." She took her cup at last. "When you find one, let me know."

She went back to the page as if the room had not just re-ordered itself to suit her reach. Draco took his own tea and left before the temptation to stand there being offended by woodwork turned childish even in his own head.

---

Morning work in his father's study went nowhere. The Mark had been bad overnight and worse at dawn, a hot wired ache threading the old brand under his skin. By noon the pain had settled into pulses that caught whenever his wrist turned or his fingers closed too fast. Each catch brought the same small nausea with it. Riddle House rose behind his eyes in pieces too intact to be called memory gone soft: polish and old fire in the air, Lucius's hand digging into his shoulder, the copper taste from where he had bitten his mouth to keep from making a sound too soon.

Paperwork did not compete well with that. He read the same estate letter three times, answered none of it, and finally shoved the page aside hard enough to smear the ink.

The study offered no relief. Too much of Lucius remained in the room: the chair, the desk, the arrangement of shelves maintained for years out of inertia and cowardice and the practical difficulty of deciding where else anything should go. Even the air felt curated by someone else's habits.

A measured knock broke across the quiet. Three even raps announced Granger.

"Come in."

In the doorway she held an old manual open against one palm, two ribbon markers hanging from the bottom edge. "I think this is a diagnostic text on curse marks. It's outdated, but the methodology isn't useless. Your great-great-grandmother wrote it."

"Cassiopeia."

"Yes." Hermione came in only far enough to close the door behind her with her heel. "I don't think it's only a diagnostic text."

Draco looked at the manual, then at her face. "Meaning?"

"Meaning half the marginalia is property notation. Corridor names. Room temperatures. Structural shorthand, I think. She was comparing curse flares to changes in the house."

He let out a short breath that was not quite a laugh. "That sounds like her."

"You say that as if it explains anything."

"It explains that she disliked being told one problem was separate from another." His mouth twitched without humour. "Malfoys do tend to treat categories as a suggestion."

Hermione absorbed that, eyes flicking back to the page. "This was waiting on the library table this morning with two east-wing repair ledgers and a wall survey."

"The house brought you estate records."

"It brought me titles I wrote down before bed." She lifted the book a fraction. "I also wrote down three controls, because I was starting to feel ridiculous. Those stayed where they were."

"Controls."

"Yes."

"You set the library an experiment."

"I set myself one." Her tone remained dry, but the quickness under it was interest, not irritation. "The texts about your Mark and the east corridor surfaced. A botanical index, a book on French inheritance law, and a household inventory did not. If this place is going to be selective, I'd prefer to know the principle."

The principle sat under his skin already, hot and unwelcome. He said, "It may not have one."

Hermione's glance lifted in a clean, brief challenge. "Everything with a pattern has one."

She was usually careful not to sound triumphant when the world presented her with a puzzle. Today she managed something close. The effect on his temper was mixed.

"If you can spare ten minutes," she said, "I'd like to see whether the pattern holds when you're there. If more family shorthand turns up, I want someone who can read it without guessing."

It would have been easy to refuse. He could have said the study required him. He could have said the Mark was bad. He could have said he was not prepared to spend the day assisting her in an argument with the furniture. All three would have been true.

Instead he stood, because the alternative was staying in Lucius's chair with his own hand throbbing on the desk like a threat he had invited to sit down.

The library table had changed while he was away. Hermione's orderly half-circle of texts was still in place, but now three loose sheets lay weighted under a paperknife, lines of her narrow handwriting split into two columns. One column carried check marks. The other did not.

She slid the pages toward him. "Last night's list."

The checked titles were exacting and ugly in their relevance: curse persistence, inherited sigils, east-wing repair summaries, internal wall surveys, Cassiopeia Black Malfoy's private diagnostic notes. The unchecked titles looked chosen to be undeniably irrelevant: herbals, eighteenth-century French legal commentary, a volume on comet charts, household silver inventories.

"You really did do controls."

Hermione gave him a flat look. "Yes."

He took one of the unchecked titles and crossed to the indicated shelf. The book sat exactly where the catalogue said it should, wedged behind a dust line so clean that moving it felt like breaking evidence. He held it up between two fingers.

"Still there."

"Go on." Hermione was already pulling one of the surfaced ledgers toward herself. "Now look at this."

The repair ledger smelled of old vellum and cedar oil. The page she had marked showed maintenance on the east corridor from decades before he was born: repeated patching along the outer wall, references to a stubborn winter leak, a notation in sharper darker ink that had been added years later in family shorthand he had not seen since schoolroom exercises with Lucius.

He translated automatically. "'Buffer line unstable. Reinforce before frost.'"

"Buffer line," Hermione repeated. "That's the same phrase Cassiopeia uses in the margin here."

She turned the manual so he could see. Tiny notes ran up the edge of the page, precise and impatient. Wall temperatures. Flare intervals. Room locations. A small cluster of symbols he recognized from estate records sat beside a paragraph on curse wakefulness.

"She was comparing a body to a corridor," Hermione said.

"Or comparing a corridor to a body."

Her mouth moved as if she had started to answer and thought better of the first version. "Either way, it isn't hospitality."

The sentence sat between them longer than it should have. Draco set the ledger down more carefully than the motion deserved.

"What do you need?" he asked.

Hermione blinked once, surprised perhaps by the directness of it. Then she was moving again, fast and precise. "The actual structural surveys for the east passage. Not the summary ledgers. If the wall is holding heat now, something in it changed. I need to know what sat there before, and whether Cassiopeia altered it for a reason that had anything to do with what she was treating."

"Those records weren't kept in the open stacks."

"Can you find them?"

"Possibly."

He led her to the genealogical shelves because old Malfoy logic had always preferred burying practical matter under ancestral vanity. Estate records tended to hide behind marriage contracts, dowries, and land disputes, all the things a family liked telling itself were nobler than repairs. Hermione followed with her notebook open and her wand tucked behind one ear in a gesture so utilitarian it should not have been as distracting as it was.

The expected compartment was empty. So was the one two cases down. Draco crouched to check the lower panel. Nothing.

"They may be in the archive room Lucius used for seizure inventories," he said.

"You sound as if you would rather set it on fire than go and see."

"I'd rather let it rot on principle."

Hermione leaned against the shelf, not looking at him while she wrote. "Fine. Then before we go and indulge your principles, I want the east passage structural survey, anything Cassiopeia attached to it, and any notes that connect the Mark to room conditions."

For one unimpressed second, nothing happened.

Then a plain mechanical click came from below his knee.

He froze. Hermione did not move either. Another click followed, deeper in the wood. A narrow seam, invisible a moment before, opened in the carved skirting beneath the genealogical shelves. A shallow drawer pushed itself outward by three inches and stopped.

Neither of them spoke.

Draco reached first. The drawer edge bit his knuckles before Hermione had even shifted. The drawer ran further than the visible shelf suggested. Inside lay three rolled surveys bound in faded green tape, a calfskin notebook stamped only with C.B.M. in blind gold, and a packet of parchment forms ruled with the outline of an arm from wrist to shoulder.

Hermione let out a slow breath through her nose. "Well."

Each survey roll carried an old tag in family shorthand. Draco read them off one by one. "'East passage outer wall.' 'Winter buffer adjustment.' 'North draft line.'"

"The corridor," Hermione said.

He lifted the notebook. The leather was dry and warm, as if it had been sitting in a pocket near skin. When he opened it, Cassiopeia's hand looked back at him immediately: strong, narrow, and ruthless about space. The first page contained no introduction, no title, only a date, a room name, and columns laid out with the severe faith that pain became governable if one recorded it properly enough.

Hour. Flare interval. Room occupied. Wall condition. Patient response. Modification.

The next pages did not improve in mood. Cassiopeia never once named the patient. She reduced them to initials and symptoms, as if privacy or cruelty required the same economy. One entry noted a night without sleep and three successive flares in the east study. Another marked relocation to the lower library and improvement held for two hours. A third had only one line in the margin: house over-attentive today; reduced corridor pressure before noon.

Hermione leaned over his shoulder to read, fast enough that her breath moved the paper. "She tracked response to rooms before she tracked potions."

"That sounds like something my family would call efficient."

"Your family should have tried not being appalling for variety."

The line was brisk enough to count as work rather than insult. Draco let it pass because he had no defense worth the effort.

"Who was she treating?" Hermione asked.

"No idea."

"There must have been a name."

"Not if there was shame attached." He turned another page. "Malfoys record scandal either in obscene detail or not at all. It depends which version flatters them more."

Hermione made a quiet dissatisfied sound. "I hate dead people who assume later readers can read their minds."

"You hate everyone who leaves poor notes."

"Yes."

She said it with such offended certainty that he almost smiled. The expression failed halfway but did not humiliate him by being seen.

Hermione reached for the ruled packet and spread the top form flat on the table. "If the Mark is bad now, we can test whether anything in her notes matches what you're feeling. Ten minutes. Fifteen."

No clean refusal presented itself. Pain made honesty easier and pride more foolish.

"All right," he said.

Hermione cleared a space at the long table with brisk competence. The ruled forms fit the page size precisely, as if the drawer had been stocked for the use of that table and no other. She set the notebook open to Cassiopeia's columns, uncapped her ink, and looked at him with professional attention that would have been easier to bear if he did not know how physical professionalism could feel in her hands.

"Sit," she said.

He sat.

"Tell me when the flare peaked last."

"About an hour ago."

"Before or after I knocked?"

"Before."

Her quill moved. "Location."

"Forearm. Up the inside. Sometimes shoulder."

"Heat or pressure?"

"Both."

Hermione glanced up. "That's not terribly useful."

"It's what it is."

"Fine. Worse with movement?"

"Sometimes. Closing the hand. Turning the wrist."

"Show me."

He did both before he could stop himself, testing against pain as stupidly as a child testing a bruise. The ache answered at once. He drew breath through his teeth.

Hermione's eyes flicked to his hand. "That one hurt."

"Yes."

She set the quill down and put two fingers against the inside of his wrist, just below the place where pulse ran strongest. Clinical. Steady. He ought to have been used to it after the first examination. He was not used to it. He was only better at looking as if he were.

The change came at once. Not relief exactly. Relief suggested generosity. This was more exact than that. The pain widened, lost its sharp braided edge, became something he could think alongside instead of around.

Hermione felt it happen, or perhaps only felt the pulse settle under her fingers. "Again," she said.

He turned the wrist a fraction. The ache answered, but duller.

"Lower," she murmured, already writing. "Not gone."

"No."

"Worse in the study?"

He looked at her.

"I am allowed pattern recognition, Malfoy."

"Yes. Worse there."

She wrote that down too. "Sleep?"

"Poorly."

"How poor."

"Three hours, perhaps four in pieces."

"Nausea."

"Some."

"Spell use this morning."

"Minimal."

"Because it hurt or because you had nothing worth casting at?"

"Both."

That earned him the briefest glance, almost approval and not quite. Hermione shifted her grip. Her thumb came down just above the old brand and the pain stepped back again like an animal acknowledging rank.

She felt that too. This time she did not apologise. "There."

"Yes."

"Specific enough to map?"

"Yes."

"Good."

The word came clipped, not unkind. She leaned over the chart and marked small shaded bands up the outline of the arm. When she spoke again, the energy had turned from bedside precision to problem-solving speed.

"Cassiopeia's notes track room conditions across flare intervals. Look." She slid the notebook toward him without letting go of his wrist. "This entry. The flare worsened in a west room with poor wall retention, eased in the library, and changed again with direct contact. She doesn't say contact with whom. I hate that she doesn't say."

"Malfoys assume later readers can infer the obvious."

"The obvious to them is usually buried under three layers of pure-blood nonsense."

"Yes."

She blinked at the easy agreement, then went back to the page. "And here. Same hand, different week. She notes that the house began surfacing relevant records before symptoms stabilized. She doesn't describe that as unusual."

"Relevant to what?"

"To the condition being treated." Hermione met his eyes squarely. "It isn't reacting to me because I'm a guest. It's moving work toward whatever it thinks the work is."

The phrasing hit somewhere tender and unhelpful. Draco pulled his hand back too fast, out of reflex more than decision. Pain answered the movement with a quick bright hook under the skin.

Hermione noticed that too, because of course she did. "Sorry. I wasn't trying to make a speech out of it."

"You didn't."

"I did a bit."

"Only a bit."

Some small tension in her mouth eased. She gathered the papers into a neater pile, then stopped and let one hand rest flat on Cassiopeia's notebook as if anchoring herself back into the intellectual frame.

"Look at the selection pattern," she said. "It isn't offering random comforts. It brought the repair ledgers, the survey, these charts. It didn't bring anything ornamental. It didn't bring anything merely convenient. It keeps serving the same line of inquiry."

Draco looked down at the page again. Hour. Flare interval. Room occupied. Wall condition. Patient response. Modification. The columns made his skin crawl because they were too tidy and because they were useful.

"It is assembling evidence," Hermione said.

"For what?"

She gave a one-shouldered movement that admitted uncertainty without softening it. "For itself. For the Mark. For whatever intersection it thinks matters. I don't know yet." She shut the notebook more gently than he would have. "But I don't think the house is trying to make me comfortable. I think it's building a workspace."

Draco's fingers tightened on the table edge. He made them let go.

Hermione read enough of that from his face to add, not gently, "I know. I don't like that answer either."

Silence held for a moment. Not empty silence. Working silence, full of page edges and old leather and the sound of them both refusing easier interpretations.

At last she said, "Did your mother know what Cassiopeia was doing?"

"If she does, she hasn't said."

"That isn't the same as not knowing."

"No."

Hermione tapped the closed notebook once with the back of her fingernail. "Then ask her."

The order in it was light enough to pass for practicality. The fact that he heard it as an order anyway did not improve his temper.

"My mother does not become more informative when summoned."

"Perhaps not." Hermione slid the body chart to one side and began copying the margin notation at speed. "But if this house is altering walls and handing us diagnostic papers, I'd prefer not to be the last person in it to realise why."

Her tone made the statement about method rather than fear. The fear was there anyway, thin and well hidden. Draco saw it because he knew what room in the house she had not yet spoken of. He said, more abruptly than he intended, "You're not."

Hermione's quill slowed. "Not what?"

"Last to realise." He did not know how to say the rest without saying too much. The Manor is answering you. The Manor is choosing around you. The Manor notices your work because it notices you inside it, and I cannot tell whether that is safer or worse. What came out instead was, "I noticed before you did."

She studied him for a beat, perhaps deciding whether to press. Then she chose mercy or tact or simple concentration and returned to the notes. "All right."

He stood because sitting still had become impossible.

"Where are you going?" she asked.

"To find lunch."

"You don't have to."

"I know."

The service stairs to the old kitchen were colder than the library and blessedly ordinary. Draco cut bread, carved cheese, and found himself angrier with each useful movement, which at least made the activity productive. By the time he brought the tray back, Hermione had filled two pages of notes and forgotten entirely that bodies required feeding unless directly reminded.

He set the tray down within her line of sight.

She looked up as if surfacing. "Oh."

"An eloquent response."

"I was not aware I was giving one." She pulled the plate toward herself with absent gratitude and immediately kept reading while she took the first bite. "Thank you."

It was an ordinary phrase. It still landed oddly. He sat opposite with his own plate and the east-passage survey unrolled between them.

The structural drawing was less a plan than an argument with previous repairs. Old lines had been redrawn over newer ones, wall-thickness notes corrected in three different hands, one narrow channel through the east passage marked as buffer in Cassiopeia's sharper script.

Hermione swallowed and pointed with the crust of bread. "There."

"Try not to annotate the survey with mustard."

She ignored that. "This line. It sits exactly where you said the draught used to be."

"It has been there all my life."

"Not now."

"No."

She frowned at the page. "If this was a buffer line and the house has sealed it, that isn't cosmetic. That's structural adjustment."

"Helpful distinction."

"I mean it isn't fluff. It isn't trying to stop me being chilly." Hermione took another bite because if she did not, she would plainly forget again. "It's changing the building around the work."

Draco said nothing.

Hermione glanced up. "What."

"Nothing."

"That was visibly not nothing."

He considered lying. It felt wasteful in the face of her expression. "I had almost the same thought five minutes ago, and I resent you for getting there independently."

To his annoyance, she laughed. It was quick and low and gone at once. "That sounds difficult for you."

"It is a trial."

"You should be brave about it."

The exchange was stupid enough to feel dangerously close to ease. Draco returned his attention to the survey.

Hermione's voice, when she spoke again, had gone quieter. "Predictability would have been enough."

The line came out so matter-of-factly that it took a second to hear the pressure inside it. She did not look up from the page. Her left sleeve had ridden back half an inch when she reached for the notebook, and the glamour on her forearm held cleanly where it always held. He stared at the fabric, not the arm, and saw anyway a marble floor, a knife, Bellatrix's bright avid mouth.

He made himself look back to the survey before the memory finished arranging itself.

"Will you ask her?" Hermione said after a moment.

"Yes."

The answer surprised both of them. It showed in the brief lift of her brows and in the private irritation that followed in him, because now he actually had to do it.

Hermione only nodded and went back to copying Cassiopeia's notes into a clearer hand. "Good."

By the time the plates were empty, Draco had sent word to Narcissa and regretted it twice. The library table held the notebook, the east-passage survey, and three blank body charts squared to the table edge.

---

Narcissa arrived at half three by Floo, stepping out of the parlour hearth with the contained irritation of a woman who expected a failing network to work properly out of respect for her inconvenience. She gave the room one glance, brushed soot from her sleeve, and looked at him with cool appraisal.

"Your message was unhelpful."

"It was brief."

"That is what made it unhelpful."

Before he could answer, her hand went to the mantel. She closed her eyes for one beat, not theatrically, simply listening through her palm. When she opened them again, something in her face had flattened into attention.

"The fire is running too high," she said.

"I hadn't noticed."

"No." Her fingers remained on the stone another second. "You would not."

Tea was implied. While the kettle heated, Draco told her as much as he could without sounding deranged: books surfaced from closed stacks, Cassiopeia's manual appearing on Hermione's table, a hidden drawer under the genealogical shelves, old body charts that matched the Mark too neatly to be coincidence.

At the notebook, Narcissa went very still.

"You found Cassiopeia's papers."

"The house found them."

Her gaze sharpened, not on him but through him, as though the distinction mattered in a way he had not yet earned. "Have you read them?"

"Some."

"Has Ms. Granger?"

"Enough to know Cassiopeia was tracking the Mark against room changes."

Narcissa took her cup from him and set it down untasted. "You should have started there."

"I started where you might answer."

A less disciplined woman might have smiled. Narcissa only said, "Show me the rest."

He took her first to the library. The hidden drawer beneath the genealogical shelves stood closed again, its seam gone so completely that he would have doubted it had ever moved if the notebook and survey had not been sitting on the table in plain view. Narcissa did not waste time touching the wood. Her attention went straight to the papers.

For several long moments she read Cassiopeia's hand without comment. The silence acquired weight. Draco watched his mother's face the way one watched the sky over difficult weather, not expecting honesty but hoping for legible warning.

At last she closed the notebook.

"I had hoped those were lost," she said.

That was more than he had expected and less than he wanted. "Why?"

Narcissa laid the notebook down precisely on the table edge. "Because recovery is not the same thing as usefulness."

"That is not an answer."

"It is the one you have."

He could have pushed then. The familiar failure of that impulse, repeated over twenty-five years, was that it almost never improved her generosity. So he let the question sit and watched the place where she had set her hand on the leather. Her fingers had tightened only once: at a page he had not yet translated.

"Did the pain change while you were working with her?" Narcissa asked.

He looked up sharply. "That is a very particular question."

"And one you can answer."

He thought of Hermione's fingers at his wrist, the sharp braid of pain loosening under her touch, the lesser improvement that had come simply from being in the library with her while she worked. "Yes," he said.

"By contact or proximity?"

He was quiet long enough for the answer to become its own confession. "Both, I think."

Whatever Narcissa had expected, the confirmation did not surprise her. That was the worst part.

"Show me the corridors," she said.

The route from the library to the east wing felt wrong in a way Draco could not have named until he watched Narcissa move through it. She counted turns with her body, not her eyes, slight pauses registering absences he could not feel until she made them visible. Outside the old morning room she stopped and looked back toward the library.

"It has redrawn the working routes," she said.

"The what."

"The routes that matter to the work being done." She resumed walking before he could do more than absorb the phrasing. "Guest wing to library. Your study to library. This room to both. It is wasting less distance."

"You say that as if the house has a filing system."

"It has habits." Her voice stayed even. "The passage from the morning room to the library is gone. I used it last winter."

They turned toward the library again. The new door appeared just past the bend.

Dark wood stood where blank stone had been when they had passed ten minutes earlier. Brass handled the late light with a subdued old shine. The grain ran deep into the wall itself, not fitted there but grown. Narcissa stopped so abruptly that Draco nearly walked into her.

Recognition crossed her face before composure sealed over it.

"Mother."

"Leave it."

The words came level, but colour had gone from her fingertips where they pressed the stone.

"That door wasn't there a minute ago."

"No."

"You know it."

"Not that door." Her gaze stayed fixed on the handle. "The habit."

He waited. Narcissa did not like being stared into speech. Sometimes, very rarely, that was enough to make her continue just to end the waiting on her own terms.

"After my wedding," she said, "sealed rooms began presenting themselves. Not often. Not dramatically. A music room off a corridor that did not lead there. A winter sitting room with windows onto grounds the wall did not face. Your grandmother called it welcome. I was young enough to find that flattering."

"And later?"

"Later your father decided the house should make itself intelligible." Her mouth sharpened around the word should. "He had hinges measured. Locks catalogued. He turned a notebook on it the way he turned one on everything he meant to own. The rooms stopped presenting themselves for weeks at a time."

Draco kept his eyes on the brass handle. "So it dislikes force."

"Everything dislikes force. The house is only better positioned than most things to object."

That was near enough to dry humour to feel almost intimate. It vanished at once.

"Listen carefully," Narcissa said. "When this house is pressed, it does not yield. It withdraws something else."

"What does it withdraw?"

Her silence was immediate and deliberate. Then, because the door remained there and because some old calculation had altered behind her eyes, she chose a narrower truth.

"Convenience first. Access after that. Entire rooms, if it is in the mood." She did not take her hand off the wall. "Once, when Lucius tried to force an archive room that would not open for him, the west stair gave him a plaster wall for six days. He called it a warding fault. Your grandmother did not correct him."

The image was ridiculous enough that Draco nearly smiled. It died before completion. "Was it like this for him, then?"

Narcissa looked at him at last. "Your father mistook access for victory."

It was an answer and not one. He heard the missing territory around it as clearly as if she had named it.

"And you didn't."

"I learned faster."

"From what."

"From living here." Her voice had gone thinner. "The rooms I mentioned. They came back once Lucius stopped measuring them. I only needed that to happen once."

He said, more softly, "What is it doing with Granger?"

Narcissa's gaze returned to the door. "If I knew that fully, I would already be telling you to send her away or to keep her here. I am doing neither." She drew one careful breath. "I think it means to involve her in work it considers unfinished. That is not the same thing as kindness."

Cassiopeia's notebook flashed back through his mind. Hour. Flare interval. Room occupied. Wall condition.

"The Mark," he said.

"Possibly." Narcissa's hand came away from the wall. "Possibly both. Cassiopeia did not separate them in her notes."

Before he could ask the next question, she said, "Do not put your hand on that handle."

The instruction came too quickly, which made disobedience feel already anticipated. "You knew I would."

"I know you were raised by a man who mistook force for competence." The sentence stayed cool, but the effort of coolness showed in it. "Do not prove him right out of habit."

He wanted to argue on principle. The desire lasted only until he looked at her hand. Her fingers had not quite stopped trembling.

Narcissa resumed walking. This time he followed without pretending it was not obedience.

Near the guest wing corridor she slowed again.

"Is she comfortable here?" Narcissa asked.

"She's working."

"That is not what I asked."

The answer arrived in pieces he did not want. Hermione at the long table as if the room had admitted her before he had. Hermione eating bread over Cassiopeia's notebook because she would not have stopped otherwise. Hermione saying predictability would have been enough in a tone so flat it had cut cleaner than complaint. Under those newer impressions sat the older one he kept circling and never quite touching: the drawing room floor, marble and blood, Bellatrix's knife flashing wet under chandelier light, Hermione on her side with hair stuck to her cheek and the carved word still fresh enough not to be a scar because it was still an open wound. He had stood there with a wand in his hand and done nothing that altered anything.

"As comfortable as anyone could be," he said, and then, because leaving the sentence there felt like cowardice even by his standards, "given."

Narcissa heard the unfinished part without help. Her face did not change, but her eyes did, some inward movement of grief or guilt or merely recognition that passed too fast to name.

"Yes," she said quietly. "Given."

They walked the rest of the corridor without speaking. At the parlour door Narcissa stopped once more and turned to him with the full weight of her attention finally set where he could feel it.

"The Manor does not act out of spite," she said. "That should not tempt you to trust its intentions."

"I don't."

"See that you continue not to."

He fetched her cloak from the stand by the hearth. She fastened it with her mother's silver clasp and looked at him not as son or child or disappointment but as something closer to an equal than she usually permitted, which was almost worse.

"If another door appears," she said, "send for me. Do not test it. Do not mention it to Ms. Granger unless it opens to her first."

Then green fire took her.

The parlour seemed flatter without her in it. Less informed. Draco stood staring at the grate until the last of the Floo flare went out, then swore once under his breath at nothing useful and went to the corridor anyway.

The new door remained where it had been. Varnish and brass met his hand. A faint dry scent of cedar lingered around the jamb. Narcissa's warning sat in his head and did not improve his judgment.

After a moment's stillness his hand closed on the handle.

Resistance came at once, not the catch of a lock but the unmistakable pressure of another hand closing over the handle from the other side.

Draco let go so fast his knuckles knocked the brass. The corridor stayed still. No footstep, no latch, nothing. Under his shoes, stone answered stone in a run of movement so low it might have been imagined if imagination had ever been that inventive in his favour.

The library proved he had not imagined it.

Both oak doors were shut. Since Hermione's second day the room had not closed on her while she worked, not even once. Now the handles refused him completely. There was no give, no rattle, nothing. Through the wood came the scratch of her quill, one scrape of chair legs, and beneath both sounds the low hum threaded through the house like breath.

He left his hand on the handle and did not knock.

Inside, a page turned.

He could picture the exact motion without wanting to: Hermione bent over Cassiopeia's notebook, quill in her left hand, wand somewhere near her elbow, the east-passage survey spread under one palm.

At last he stepped back.

The Manor kept the room from him with quiet certainty. Somewhere beyond the oak, Granger shifted in her chair and went on with the work. Draco looked at the pale crescent the brass had pressed into his palm until it faded.
