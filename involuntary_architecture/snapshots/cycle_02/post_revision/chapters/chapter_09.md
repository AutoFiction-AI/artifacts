# Chapter 9

The lamb was overcooked.

Hermione noticed it the way she'd started noticing most things about Malfoy: sideways, while pretending her attention was on the work. After the examination, neither of them had suggested calling it a night. Forty minutes in the kitchen had left the chops grey at the edges and dry through the middle. The rosemary had blackened. The mint jelly had gone skin-thick in the dish by the time he carried everything upstairs and set the plates down between Abelard Malfoy's letters and a family chart wide enough to cover half the table.

He said, "It was meant to be ten minutes."

"It was," Hermione said, cutting into the chop anyway. "And then you found another letter."

"You found another letter."

"I was in the room."

"A dangerous condition around old paper."

That nearly counted as flirting. Or it would've, if either of them were stupid enough to let it stand. She took a bite before she could answer, chewed the dry lamb, swallowed, and decided not to point out that he'd spent the last hour with his sleeves shoved to the elbow and a quill tucked behind one ear like a clerk in a cheap archive. If she named that, she would also have to name the fact that she'd been watching the inside of his forearm every time he turned a page.

Three weeks ago, eating overcooked lamb in Malfoy Manor would have registered as absurd. Tonight the absurdity was that it didn't.

Abelard's letters lay open in a fan between them, originals weighted at the corners by two brass candlesticks the house had placed there without being asked. A shelf to Hermione's left had started its own opinionated participation an hour ago, pushing out books in soft, deliberate knocks whenever she or Malfoy got stuck. The most recent offering sat by her plate: a cracked Latin index of pilgrimage wards and seasonal line maintenance. She had not asked for it. It had appeared three breaths after she'd said the Glastonbury reference might be a mask instead of a place.

Malfoy read while he ate. Ink darkened the ends of his fingers where he'd been annotating the transcriptions ever since she'd found the error that turned to anchor into to sever. He still looked faintly offended by that mistake, as if the copyist had done it to him personally, centuries after the fact.

Only after several minutes did she admit what she was doing. His thumb snagged on a page. He put it to his mouth for grip, absent-minded, and heat moved through her low and fast. Looking down helped very little. Her plate held a bare bone, a streak of mint, a knife laid crooked across the china. None of it explained the shift under her ribs.

He tipped his chin toward the third letter. "Glastonbury again. Locational cipher, I think. Same as the second mention."

"Could be temporal."

"It's in the margin beside a transport notation."

"That doesn't make it locational. Abelard uses the same base glyph for both." She reached across the spread and tapped the letter with the handle of her knife. "The modifier's in the subscript."

He leaned in. His shoulder brushed hers for an instant and stayed there a moment longer than balance required. "That's not a subscript. That's blotting."

"No, this is blotting." She pointed at the dark smear lower on the page. "This is deliberate. See the hook?"

He looked, then reached for the cracked index. Before his hand got there, the shelf at Hermione's left gave a sharp wooden click. Another book shoved itself out two inches.

They both looked at it.

Malfoy said, "Subtle."

Hermione set down her fork and pulled the volume free. It was a survey of pre-Reformation abbey holdings, stitched back together at some point with green thread. A page near the middle was already lifting itself in the stale air.

"The house is showing off," she said.

"It thinks we're slow."

"We are slow."

"Speak for yourself."

The page that had lifted contained a table of copied glyphs from twelfth- and thirteenth-century dedication stones. The mark in Abelard's margin appeared twice, both times indicating winter use rather than place.

Hermione held up the book. "Temporal."

He took it from her, scanned the table, and exhaled through his nose. "Fine. Temporal."

"You could sound less aggrieved when I'm right."

"That would suggest growth."

She laughed before she could stop herself. It came out cleaner than anything that had reached her throat in days. Across from her, a line eased out of his shoulders. Not much. Just enough that he looked less like a man braced for impact and more like a tired one allowing himself the luxury of hearing a laugh in his own library.

That did something dangerous to her concentration.

The letter in question was one of six Abelard had written to a cousin who, judging by the hand and the volume of complaint, had been charged with the impossible work of keeping three failing estates solvent while also maintaining some portion of the western anchor lines. Hermione had spent most of the afternoon tracing the repeated references to holy ground, winter roads, and something called the bridle-path accounts. Malfoy had spent it translating a family shorthand that seemed built expressly to conceal useful information beneath a layer of inheritance disputes and petty malice.

He put the survey down and took up the genealogical chart instead. "If it's temporal, not locational, then Abelard isn't telling him where to send the stewards. He's telling him when to cycle them."

"Or when to rotate which site gets the stronger support." Hermione reached for the chart and flattened the curling corner with her palm. "Look at the dates on these marriage settlements. February. November. December. Nearly all of the west-country alliances happen in winter."

"You think that's on purpose."

"I think your family did not make decisions without three layers of secondary purpose."

"That's charitable."

"It's not meant kindly."

He looked down the chart as if seeing it from outside the bloodline for the first time. The Malfoy names marched through two centuries in careful black ink, threaded to wives from families Hermione knew mostly from footnotes and sealed Ministry inventories. Beside some of the marriages, a later hand had added estate obligations in abbreviated property notation. She'd thought at first they marked dowries or repair obligations. The more she cross-referenced them, the less domestic they looked.

"That symbol there," she said, touching a notation beside Euphemia Rosier's name. "Same base glyph as the Glastonbury line. Different modifier."

Malfoy bent over the page. His hair fell forward across his forehead. "Autumn."

"Exactly. The obligation moved with the marriage."

He went still. "No."

"Yes."

"No. That mark isn't obligation. It's custodianship." He reached for a ledger at his elbow and flipped through three pages with infuriating speed. "My grandfather used it when he transferred tenant oversight to an estate manager."

"And Abelard used it here in relation to anchors."

"That's because my family filed everything like a property dispute. It doesn't mean the marriage carried the work."

"It means they understood the work as domestic." Hermione heard her own voice sharpen and made no effort to soften it. "Which, frankly, tracks."

He set down the ledger. "You say that as if I should be embarrassed on behalf of fourteenth-century strangers."

"You should be embarrassed on behalf of all your ancestors. It builds character."

"Then I'm overdeveloped."

The shelf behind him gave another opinionated knock. A drawer low in the wall slid open three inches.

Hermione and Malfoy looked at each other, then at the drawer.

"That wasn't open before," she said.

"I am aware."

"Would you like to do the aristocratic thing and pretend not to notice?"

"I think the aristocratic thing would be to call a servant. We haven't had those in a while."

"Pity."

He rose and crouched by the drawer. Inside lay a roll of vellum tied with faded blue ribbon and a packet of folded papers sealed in dark wax. Malfoy lifted the packet first, checked the seal, and made a face.

"Marriage contracts," he said.

"Of course they are."

"The house has no sense of irony."

"I don't think irony is what it's going for."

That sat between them for a second. Not accusation. Not even quite suspicion. Just the open drawer in the wall, the packet of contracts in his hands, and the map the house had chosen to put under their noses.

Hermione hated that thought on principle. Tonight she let it stay for half a breath longer than usual before setting it aside. The chapter of work in front of her was too interesting. So was the man undoing wax with the tip of a paper knife while pretending not to notice she'd been looking too long.

He brought the packet back to the table and spread the contracts beside the chart. The ribboned vellum unrolled into an estate map overpainted three different times. Lines in iron gall ink had been reinforced in vermilion and then, a century later, in a thin wash of metallic blue that caught candlelight in hard little flashes. Hermione leaned over it at once.

"Careful," Malfoy said. "The blue layer flakes."

"Then stop breathing on it."

"Cruel."

She ignored that and traced a fingertip one inch above the vellum without touching. "These aren't roads."

"No."

"They're not boundary lines either."

"No."

"Malfoy."

"I know." His voice had changed. Not bigger. Just flatter with concentration. "Those are line-bearing routes. Or the old version of them. See there? The abbey mark, then the river crossing, then the house-sign. Abelard's writing to a cousin because the cousin's marriage shifted responsibility for the western maintenance run."

"Which is what I said."

"You said domestic."

"That is domestic."

"For my family, perhaps."

She looked up. "Do you hear yourself?"

He looked blank for two seconds. Then he did hear it, because color moved high into his cheekbones and vanished again. "Unfortunately."

"Good."

He sat back, one hand over his eyes for a moment. "You do realize this means half the 'inheritance settlements' in the western files are probably maintenance records."

"Yes."

"And the Ministry indexed them as family property disputes."

"Yes."

"Which means Croaker has been basing his repair projections on the wrong archive category."

"Yes."

"I dislike when your entire face says yes before I finish the sentence."

"You should work faster, then."

He dropped his hand. There it was again, the small structural failure in his guard when she said something that amused him. Not a smile, exactly. More the absence of one carefully withheld.

Hermione looked back down at the map before her own face did something unwise.

They worked through the contracts while the lamb cooled on their plates and the candles burned lower. The earliest western marriage the house had produced joined a Malfoy daughter to a steward family outside Glastonbury. The settlement language dressed the arrangement in the usual property terms, orchards and rights of passage and a dowry in silver, but every practical clause concerned winter maintenance: which route had to remain open, which chapel cellar held reserve stones, whose obligation it was to check the line-bearing markers after flooding. The later contracts did the same work with better euphemisms.

At one point Hermione said, "Your ancestors really did build infrastructure through other people's marriages."

Malfoy, who had gone back for the now-cold tea and was drinking it anyway, said, "My ancestors did most things through other people's marriages."

"And here I thought old pureblood houses valued sentiment."

"We valued leverage. Sentiment occasionally turned up by accident."

"You say we and my family in the same breath."

"Occupational hazard."

"Of being an accomplice to the dead?"

"Of living in the filing system."

That was good enough that she let it have a laugh. He let it have one too, shorter and rougher, and the sound of it in the library did something almost indecent to the room. The fire gave a brief crack. One of the shelves settled. Outside the windows, frost had gone opaque on the leaded panes.

By nine the plates were empty except for bones and rosemary needles. Hermione had forgotten the lamb entirely. The work had sharpened into one of those collaborative stretches where time stopped moving in ordinary increments and started advancing by insight instead. She made a note. He pulled a second ledger. She found the same glyph in a margin three pages later. He swore under his breath when a date failed to align, then corrected it when she pointed out that a scribe had used old-style year count through Lady Day. She said, "You cannot possibly still be offended by March existing," and he said, "I'm offended by all calendars equally," which was not funny enough to justify how hard it made her laugh.

The shelf behind her produced the next reference with a thud heavy enough to rattle her empty cup.

"That one was aggressive," she said.

"It's impatient."

"It has no right."

"It has every right. We're standing in its idea of efficiency."

She turned in her chair and lifted the book from the shelf. It was a register of household obligations copied in a neat eighteenth-century hand from much older originals. Tucked inside was a narrow key of symbols cross-referenced to months, solstice points, and what looked irritatingly like domestic roles.

Hermione stared at the key.

Malfoy came around the table to look over her shoulder. "What?"

She held the page up between them. "The modifiers. They aren't just seasons. They're keyed to which household member carries oversight."

He went very still behind her. She was aware of him in fragments: his sleeve brushing the chair back, the warmth of him at her shoulder, the clean smell of soap cut by ink and the kitchen smoke that still clung faintly to his jumper.

"Steward," he said, tapping the first symbol.

"Wife."

"Heir."

"Guest."

They both stopped.

The room remained room. Fire. Shelves. Frost on the glass. Her own hand holding the register. Nothing in it changed except the fact that the word guest sat under her thumb in a hand copied from material hundreds of years dead.

Malfoy stepped back first. Not dramatically. Just enough to put air where there had been none.

"That may mean nothing," he said.

"It probably means a procedural category."

"Yes."

"Yes."

Her pulse had sped anyway.

She turned the page. The symbol list continued into child, widow, and contracted ally. Useful, appalling people had always needed precise names for their useful, appalling structures.

"You can stop looking like that," she said, still reading.

"Like what?"

"As if the house has just caught us doing something indecent with a ledger."

"I think the indecency is historical."

"And current, if this notation survived unchanged for six centuries."

"That's fair."

She set the register down. "We need the paired archive."

He glanced at the clock on the mantel. "We need sleep."

"We need the paired archive, and then perhaps in a morally improved world we would also need sleep."

"That is not how causality works."

"It is tonight."

He looked at the spread table, the chart, the map, the symbol key. She could see him making the exact sensible argument against another hour's work. She could also see him losing it to the same thing losing it had done to her: the sense that the answer was one room over, one ledger down, one cross-reference away from becoming legible enough to touch.

"One hour," he said.

"Ninety minutes."

"You negotiate like a threat."

"You've noticed."

He collected the letters into a stack careful enough to border on tenderness and handed half of them to her. "Come on, then."

They left the plates where they were. The house took that as permission. By the time Hermione rose with the letters in her arms, a side table had already rolled itself out from the wall to receive the dishes.

She looked at it. "That's unnerving."

"The house disapproves of clutter."

"It lives inside clutter."

"Those aren't the same thing."

He held the library door for her. She went through first, and the corridor beyond was already the short one.

---

The route to the paired archive had been twenty-three paces all week.

Hermione knew because for the first several days the house had kept changing the count. Twenty-seven in the morning. Thirty-one after tea. Forty-one once, when she'd left the library angry with herself for noticing that Malfoy had started setting out a second cup before she asked. Lately the path had held steady: out of the library, left at the dark portrait of the woman with pearls at her throat, down the narrow passage where the runner never lay straight, then the concealed door behind the cracked green panel.

She did not count tonight. She only noticed, after they had already turned, that she had stopped bracing for the walls to move.

Malfoy put his hand against the green panel. The concealed door unlatched at once and swung inward on a breath of colder air. Beyond it lay the smaller archive the Manor only allowed to hold if both of them crossed together. Unlike the main library, it did not pretend to hospitality. Low ceiling. narrow iron shelves. drawers labeled in at least four different hands. One long central table marked with generations of knife scratches. The room felt less like scholarship than storage with opinions.

Hermione stepped inside. The air changed behind her as Malfoy followed and the door closed.

"There," she said, setting the Abelard letters on the table. "Efficient."

"You mean trapped."

"Only if one of us leaves."

"Comforting."

She took off her cardigan and draped it over the back of the nearest chair. The archive kept a workman's chill no matter what the rest of the house was doing, and her skin tightened at once across her arms. Malfoy noticed. He shrugged out of his jumper without comment and held it out.

She stared at it. "No."

"You're cold."

"So are you."

"I've lived here longer."

"That isn't insulation."

"It is adaptation."

"Put your jumper back on."

He kept holding it out. Not insistent. Just there, as if the exchange had already become practical rather than charged.

Hermione could have refused and been entirely within her rights. Instead she took it, because the room was cold and because not taking it would've meant saying aloud that she did not trust herself in his clothes, which was ridiculous and also true.

"Thank you," she said, more formal than the moment required.

"Try not to insult it," he said. "It's only one wash away from collapse."

"An astonishing inducement."

The jumper was still warm from him. That was the problem. Not the wool itself, which smelled faintly of cedar from whatever warded drawer it had come from, but the body heat left in the elbows and collar as if warmth could remain shaped to a person after he'd stepped out of it. She shoved her arms through fast and made herself focus on the table.

The archive had already begun rearranging around their task. Three shallow drawers stood open at the far wall. Two ledgers had slid halfway out of the shelf nearest the table. On the central surface, beside the letters they'd brought in, lay a stack of bound estate inventories no one had fetched.

Malfoy followed her gaze. "Again," he said.

"If it produces the answer, I'm willing to be patronized."

"You say that now."

Hermione untied the first inventory and flipped to the index. "I said it because I'm right."

He gave her a look that acknowledged both the claim and the futility of arguing with it.

For a while they worked without much speech. The archive encouraged that. Sound went flat in the low room, swallowed by vellum and wood. Hermione took the symbol key and the marriage contracts. Malfoy translated estate shorthand from a box of steward reports whose labels had gone brown at the edges. Every so often one of them would say a place name or a year and the other would answer with a page number, and the rhythm of it became so natural she stopped noticing how often they anticipated the same move.

At ten-thirty she said, "Look at this."

He crossed to her side. The table was not built for two people to stand shoulder to shoulder. It required compromise. Or intimacy. She suspected the room did not distinguish.

He leaned in over the open contract. "That's the Rosier line again."

"No. Read the witness signatures."

He bent lower. His sleeve brushed her wrist. "That's not Rosier. That's Abbot."

"Exactly. The marriage wasn't to secure the estate. It was to secure access through abbey land."

"Which means Abelard's cousin couldn't maintain the western route without the wife's rights of passage."

"Which means the household role in the notation matters materially, not ceremonially."

"And you were right."

She turned her head to look at him. "I was."

"I am confirming it because apparently you require witnesses."

"Only in cases of chronic stubbornness."

"I prefer historical consistency."

She ought to have stepped back. Instead she stayed where she was long enough to notice that his pulse had picked up in the side of his throat. Her own did something answering and unhelpful.

He stepped away first and reached for another ledger. "There's a steward inventory from 1462 missing."

"How can you tell?"

"Gap in the numbering."

"You count inventory gaps for fun."

"I count them because when one of my ancestors hid something, he usually did it in the same room as a numbered system he thought no one else would bother learning."

"And did they?"

"No. That's why it worked."

The missing volume turned out to be on the top shelf beyond the short rolling ladder near the corner. Hermione found it by following the run of the numbers with her wandlight. She was already halfway up the ladder when Malfoy said, "That rung catches."

"You could've said that before I climbed it."

"I assumed competence."

"Dangerous again."

She reached for the ledger. The ladder rolled two inches sideways with a rusting shriek.

Her balance went with it.

There was time to know she was not going to fall far, only awkwardly, perhaps onto the table corner. There was time to swear. There was not time to fix it herself before Malfoy's hands were at her waist, hard and immediate, arresting the drop before it became impact.

For a second the world narrowed to the exact grip of him. Not polite contact. Not the clinical contact of the examinations. His hands spanned more of her than they should have. Her body reacted before thought, every muscle going tight and then not tight at all, as if it couldn't decide between flinch and lean.

The ladder settled.

He was still holding her.

"Got you," he said, and his voice sounded wrong, lower than usual and stripped of the dry layer he used for almost everything.

"Yes," Hermione said. Then, because that was not enough language for the moment and too much language for it at once, "I noticed."

His hands left her at once.

She climbed down the rest of the way with the ledger tucked under one arm, face hot in a room cold enough to bite. When she turned, he was already back at the table pretending to read a shelf label with minute seriousness.

"The rung did catch," she said.

"It did."

"Useful warning."

"I am trying not to say I told you so while you are armed with a book heavy enough to qualify as assault."

Hermione put the ledger on the table. "That is tactless and, unfortunately, sensible."

He looked at her then. Not long. Just enough to confirm she was unhurt.

What passed between them in that look was not simple. Gratitude was in it. Embarrassment, both of theirs. Desire, which she refused to name and named immediately by refusing. Also the practical fact that he had caught her without hesitation, and she had let him.

He broke eye contact and opened the ledger.

The missing steward inventory contained no explanation for why it had been misplaced. It did contain, folded between two pages of river tariffs, a narrow maintenance rota listing the winter western lines by household rather than geography. Hermione spread it flat beneath a paperweight and read aloud while Malfoy translated the compressed property marks in the margin.

"Abbey crossing to be checked by bride-house in first frost."

"That'll be the Abbot marriage."

"Stone reserve under east chapel floor, key held by widow in residence."

"Not useful unless we know which widow."

"Glaston route to be reinforced by guest under ward-safe condition." Hermione stopped.

Malfoy looked up from the margin. "What?"

She tapped the line.

He read it twice. "Guest."

"Again."

"It could still be a procedural category."

"Yes."

He was too careful on the word. That made her look at him harder. His expression gave away nothing obvious. It was the small things instead: the slight whitening at the base of his thumb where he held the page down, the stillness too exact to be natural.

"You've seen that category before," she said.

"No."

"That wasn't a question."

"I know."

Hermione waited.

He did not like being waited at. Most people with any sense became less articulate under the kind of silence she had learned from Ministry hearings and long nights in archives where the answer turned up only after stubbornness outlasted pride. Malfoy, infuriatingly, usually became more so. Tonight the silence worked anyway.

"Not in a way that meant anything to me," he said at last. "Old household instructions. Room access notes. Things I assumed were to do with bloodline residency."

"And now you don't."

"Now I think the house is editorializing again."

"That's not an answer."

"It's the only one I have."

He did not sound evasive. He sounded annoyed at himself for being unable to make the thing cleaner than it was. That mattered. She did not know why it mattered as much as it did.

"Fine," she said, though of course it wasn't.

They kept working. The atmosphere shifted after that, not worse exactly, but more charged at the edges, as if the room had listened and adjusted its posture. Hermione refused to indulge the thought. Instead she lined the maintenance rota against Abelard's letter, then against the map from the library, and felt the answer begin to take shape under her hands.

"It's not only the sites," she said, more to the papers than to him. "It's the routes between them. The Ministry records the anchors as fixed points. Your family tracked them as moving obligations."

"Because the path matters as much as the destination."

"Exactly. If the route goes unmaintained, the site fails even if the stone itself survives."

"Which means the collapse isn't twelve broken nodes. It's a damaged weave."

She looked up sharply. "Yes."

He was already reaching for the map. Together they laid it beside the rota, then added the symbol key, then the Glastonbury contract, then the letter with the winter notation in the margin. The table disappeared beneath the accretion. Hermione moved the candle closer. Malfoy flattened a curled edge with the side of his hand. Their shoulders knocked and stayed in contact while they traced the western route from house-sign to abbey mark to river crossing.

"There," he said.

"That's not a line break. It's an interchange."

"To the London route?"

"No, southwest first. Then London." She followed the wash of blue across the vellum and felt her pulse jump for a different reason entirely. "Malfoy, this isn't a grid at all."

"You said that upstairs."

"No, I said the pattern worked better as a dodecahedral lattice than a grid. I thought I was approximating. I wasn't. Look."

She took a loose scrap and started sketching quickly, twelve points, interlinking paths, the western route turning not toward the nearest site but toward the one necessary to maintain tension across the whole structure. Malfoy watched for three lines, then started adding notations of his own in the margins, estate sigils where her points met known houses. When he understood what she was building, the whole mood of him changed. The tiredness remained. So did the old vigilance. But under it came something she had seen only in flashes during the last weeks: excitement stripped clean of defensiveness.

"God," he said quietly. "Croaker's model assumes local repair."

"Because he inherited Ministry schematics copied after the dissolution."

"And my family kept the maintenance version in private files because naturally they did."

"Naturally."

"If you're right, every failed route is overstraining the next one in sequence."

"I am right."

"I know. I am making conversation while being appalled."

That would have been funny enough already. What made it dangerous was the way he said I know. No sting in it. No performance of reluctance. Just fact.

Hermione stared at the sketch between them and had the unstable thought that this was what the house kept doing to her: not magic exactly, not even manipulation in any simple sense, but the repeated construction of circumstances in which being with Malfoy felt less like the compromise she had agreed to and more like the condition under which work, thought, breath, appetite, and time itself made a new and startling kind of sense.

That was an intolerable thought. She pushed it away at once.

He had gone quiet beside her. Not normal quiet. More the kind that indicated he was holding himself carefully inside his own skin. A familiar ache brushed the edge of her attention then, distant and intimate at once, the low dark flare she had learned to recognize as the Mark acting up when he was tired or strained.

She did not react. She had spent weeks teaching her face not to reveal the strange extension of his body that now occasionally lived in hers.

Instead she said, because it was at least half true on visible evidence alone, "You should sit down."

His head turned. "Why?"

"Because you've gone pale."

"That does tend to be my general look."

"You've gone paler than usual."

"What a privilege to be known so well."

The line was dry enough to count as cover. The hand braced on the table gave him away. Not trembling. Just fixed too hard, as if he were holding himself in place by grip alone.

"Malfoy."

"I'm fine."

"That's almost never persuasive."

"It's also still true."

He straightened, deliberately easing some of the pressure out of his fingers. The ache at the edge of her awareness thinned with the movement. She hated the secret knowledge of that. Hated more the reflex that wanted to reach across the table and prove the pain would quiet under her hands.

Not here. Not now. Not unless she wanted to break whatever tenuous shape this night had taken and say too much by the saying.

She looked back at the sketch. "If this holds, we need the eastern files tomorrow."

"Tomorrow?" he said. "You optimist."

"You think I'm sleeping after this?"

"I think you're biologically required to, yes."

"That's a broad claim from someone who looks half-haunted."

"Only half?"

"At present."

That got the closest thing to a grin she'd seen on him all evening. It transformed him only enough to make the absence of it in ordinary life feel greedy and unfair.

They spent another forty minutes proving themselves right and wrong by turns. The rota matched three of the contracts cleanly and contradicted two Ministry copies Hermione had made by hand the week before. One of the drawers offered up a box of estate seals whose back panel, once lifted, concealed a second copy of the western maintenance list with later annotations in Narcissa's grandfather's hand. Those annotations confirmed what Hermione's sketch already suggested: reinforcement had once been managed through households, through who could move where and in what season, not through isolated buildings serviced as if magic traveled by bureaucratic district.

By eleven-fifteen the exhilaration had started to fray into fatigue. Hermione miscopied a date. Malfoy spent thirty entire seconds looking for a ledger that was under his elbow. When she pointed to it, he shut his eyes briefly and said, "I resent being witnessed in decline."

"Then decline privately."

"I am trying. The house keeps involving you."

The sentence landed harder than either of them seemed prepared for.

Hermione looked down at the table. Her hand rested inches from his, both of them flattened on the same map over lines laid down before either family had the shape it wore now. She said, because she could not let the other sentence stand untouched and also could not bear to answer it directly, "We should stop before we ruin something important."

"Probably."

"You say that as if probability still governs this house."

"That's fair."

Neither moved.

Then the archive door clicked.

Not opening. Unlocking. A clear mechanical sound from the wall behind them, as if the room had decided the hour was over and would not negotiate.

Malfoy looked at the door, then at the piles of paper. "We're being thrown out."

"Rude."

"Consistent, though."

She gathered the Abelard letters while he retied the maintenance rota and rolled the map with care that made his tiredness look, suddenly, very young. Not boyish. Not innocent. Just the exhausted concentration of someone who had been managing far too much house and far too little sleep for so long that competence had become posture.

Hermione realized, with a start sharp enough to feel physical, that she trusted him not to let the papers tear. Trusted him to remember the order of them. Trusted him to find the same place tomorrow. Trusted him, more generally than that, with the work that had brought her here in the first place.

The thought should not have felt intimate.

It did.

He waited by the door until she had her cardigan and the borrowed jumper off. She handed it back carefully folded. His fingers brushed hers as he took it. He looked down at the fold, perhaps to avoid looking at her, perhaps because the contact had done to him some fraction of what it had done to her.

"Thanks," she said.

"For catching you or the wool?"

"Dealer's choice."

"Then you're welcome for both."

He put the jumper back on and held the door. When they stepped into the corridor, the house had already shortened the route to the library again.

By the time they returned upstairs, the plates were gone. Fresh tea waited on the side table in cups that did not match.

"That's sinister," Hermione said.

"You say that every time it anticipates a basic need."

"Because basic needs should require a person, not architecture."

"You've met architecture."

"I dislike when you're funny by accident."

"I dislike when you imply accident."

They took the tea back to the table anyway.

The library now wore the late hour plainly. Candle stubs. Shadows higher on the shelves. A colder black at the windows. Hermione drank half her cup standing up, then sat because sitting became easier than holding the full day upright in her spine.

Malfoy restacked the letters in tomorrow order, separating confirmed routes from speculative ones, weighing the map beneath two reference books so it could not curl overnight. He did it with the automatic neatness of someone who had spent years making order in a house that preferred mood to system. The domesticity of that hit her harder than the tea.

He looked up when three vertebrae cracked in quick succession as she stretched over the chart. "That sounded structural."

"It's fine."

"You've been folded over paper since dusk."

"Occupational hazard."

"So is dragon pox for some people. I wouldn't recommend that either."

She rolled her neck until it popped again. He flinched.

"See?" she said. "Fine."

"I don't think your definition of fine can be trusted."

"My definitions are impeccable."

"Your habits are criminal."

"And yet you continue to feed me."

The line came out before she had decided to say it. It was not flirtation exactly. Not safe enough to qualify. More the kind of ordinary observation that became dangerous only because the room had grown so quiet around them.

Malfoy set down the last letter. "The lamb was mostly an act of negligence."

"You plated it."

"I regretted that immediately."

"Still counts."

He glanced at her cup. "You haven't finished the tea."

"It's hot."

"It's tea."

"Those are not opposites."

"With you they often are."

She narrowed her eyes. "You monitor my beverage habits now?"

"You prefer to let tea cool until it reaches an interesting level of neglect. Then you forget it entirely in favor of annotations. Then you complain it's cold."

"That's an outrageous invasion of my privacy."

"It's my library."

"That does not make my tea a public matter."

"The evidence suggests otherwise."

She should have been irritated. Instead she heard herself laugh again, quieter this time. The room took the sound and held it without strain.

He bent his head over the letters to hide what might have been his own smile or simple fatigue. Either way, a looseness had entered the air between them that would've been unthinkable even a week ago. Not ease exactly. Ease implied innocence. This had too much history in it for that. But something adjacent. A way of occupying the same room without each silence needing an argument behind it.

Hermione looked at him and let herself say the thing she'd been refusing to say even in the privacy of her own head.

She was comfortable here.

Not safe. She knew the difference. Safety belonged to places that did not remember what had been done in them. To institutions with clean corridors and predictable locks. To her flat with its fixed walls and uninteresting kettle. Malfoy Manor was not safe. It was alive, withholding, too pleased with itself, too willing to arrange the world on terms she did not understand. It had rooms she had not seen and answers it would not give except sideways.

And still.

Still there was this: the overcooked lamb and the absurd matched cups and Malfoy watching her bad habits closely enough to mock them. The work turning bright between them instead of heavy. His hands at her waist on the ladder, immediate and then gone. The fact that after the memory flood in the examination room they had, somehow, continued. Not as if nothing had happened. Worse and stranger than that. As if something had happened and they were now living in the altered shape left by it.

By half eleven he had finished stacking the papers. He put the top letter square to the others and rested his fingertips there for a second, eyes unfocused with tiredness.

"Tomorrow," he said.

"Tomorrow," Hermione agreed.

She picked up her cardigan and empty cup and left before the scene could become anything else.

---

The corridor to the guest wing held steady tonight.

Same portraits with their hostile old faces. Same crack in the fifth flagstone. Same left turn toward the stairs.

At the turn, a door stood open.

Recognition came before decision. Chandelier. Marble floor. Tall windows black with winter. Preserved cold rolling through the doorway like cellar air. The drawing room.

Her body stopped first. Memory followed at speed.

Bellatrix's fist in her hair. The wrench in her neck. Marble against her cheek so cold it registered as heat. The knife on the inside of Hermione's left forearm, not cutting so much as parting the skin with that wet tearing sound she'd hated more than the pain because it sounded like matter being rearranged. Bellatrix talking the whole time in that soft, conversational voice. Tell me. Not yet. We've barely started.

Under the glamour, the scar went hot. M first. Then U. Then D. By the time Bellatrix began the B, the room had narrowed to the blade and Bellatrix's breath and the grinding certainty that this was being done slowly on purpose. When sight came back properly, Draco Malfoy was against the far wall, white-blond head turned away.

Seven years of distance meant nothing to the body. Cold reached through wool socks, through stone, through bone, and pinned her where she stood. A Mind Healer had once given her the language for this. Freeze response. State-dependent recall. Trauma trigger. Knowing the terms had never once altered the event.

The house had opened the room at the precise moment she'd stopped expecting punishment. Overcooked lamb. Tea in the wrong cups. His jumper warm from his body in the archive. The exact amount of milk. Whether that counted as intention or architecture did not matter. The room was here. So was she.

Usually she could feel him somewhere in the Manor, a faint warmth at the edge of attention, not thought, not even emotion exactly, only the fact of Malfoy in another part of the house. Now there was nothing. The corridor had narrowed around the doorway until even that thread went dead.

Footsteps sounded on stone behind her. Measured, familiar, slowing as they reached the turn.

"Granger."

His careful voice. The one that meant every word had been checked for sharp edges first.

"I didn't know it was open," he said. "This corridor wasn't supposed to connect. I asked it not to use this door."

The laugh that came out of her had no humor in it at all. "You asked the house."

"Years ago."

"And it listened for years."

"Apparently."

She kept her eyes on the marble. "Until tonight."

He was silent for a moment. When he spoke again, the care in his tone had hardened into strain. "Come away from the threshold."

"I can't."

"Then at least step back."

"From what?"

"From this."

She turned her head just enough to look at him. He had gone very pale, paler even than the archive under-candle had made him. The line of his body had changed. Not braced to argue. Braced to withstand.

"You don't get to manage the room for me," she said.

"I'm not trying to manage it."

"Aren't you?"

He looked at the open door, then at her. "Probably, yes."

That honesty was too late and too clean to help.

Open was better than closed. Closed would turn the room back into a sealed idea in the walls, something the house could choose to hold over her whenever it liked. Open at least meant present tense. Open meant the evidence was in front of her and not inside her head alone.

"I'm not standing in a doorway," she said.

Before he could answer, she stepped across the threshold.

Marble flattened the sound of her shoes exactly the way it had that night. The room did not smell of blood anymore. Time had taken that, and the smoke, and the sharp medicinal edge of Bellatrix's perfume. What remained was stone, stale winter, beeswax in the sconces, and the impossible preservation of space itself. In the center of the floor Hermione stopped and looked down. The blood had been cleaned years ago. Memory laid it back in place without effort: where her cheek had hit, where Bellatrix had stretched the arm, where the red had run into the grey veining of the stone, darkening it until everything looked already old.

Behind her, his steps came wrong. Three across the marble and then a halt, as if the floor itself had objected.

She turned.

He had stopped just inside the room. His eyes were on the floor, reading it the way hers had.

"You stood there," she said, pointing between the second and third chairs along the wall. "Your mother was beside you. Pettigrew nearer the fire. I don't remember where your father was at first. Then I do. Then I don't again."

"Yes," he said.

"Easter hols. School robes. You'd been home a week." The dates came to her with nauseating uselessness. "I looked at you when she started on the M. I remember that because I thought, absurdly, there was still time for the room to become something else. You looked at me and then you looked away. Do you remember that?"

Another beat. Then, "Yes."

What had she wanted from him at seventeen? Not a rescue. Rescue belonged to stories where adults intervened before damage became permanent. She had known too much even then for that. Maybe not even movement. Maybe only a sign that someone else in the room understood this as horror and not spectacle or family business or the rough handling of enemy property. The old want made her feel sick and young at once.

"I still don't know what I wanted from you," she said. "Only that I wanted a sign."

His gaze dropped again to the marble. "I remember everything about that night. Her laugh. Pettigrew breathing through his mouth. The chandelier moving when someone slammed the door. The sound the knife made. I didn't know a knife could sound like that."

"It was cursed," Hermione said automatically. "It wasn't cutting. It was separating the skin. Bellatrix liked precision."

His eyes shut. "Please don't."

"Why? You were there."

"Because I know. Because I know exactly what you're describing and hearing you do it like this..." He opened his eyes again, bright with effort and anger at that effort. "You're reciting evidence."

"It is evidence."

"No. It's you being back in it."

"It happened on your floor."

"I know where it happened."

"Do you?"

That finally moved him. Not toward her. Toward the wall. He crossed the room with deliberate steps and stopped in the place where he'd stood at sixteen, then put his back to the paneling and looked at the marble instead of at her.

"I know it happened here," he said. "I know because I've spent seven years not coming in this room and still being able to count the tiles from memory."

"Not coming in here?" Hermione let out another short, ugly laugh. "That's meant to comfort me?"

"No." His voice thinned. "Nothing about this is meant to comfort you."

"Good. I'd hate for us to misunderstand each other now."

He took that without flinching. Or rather he did flinch, very slightly, and made no attempt to disguise it.

"I didn't look away because I didn't care," he said after a moment. "I looked away because if I kept watching, I thought I would move. Try to stop her. I did the arithmetic in my head. Wand, distance, how fast she'd curse me, whether I could reach you before she did worse, whether my mother would get hit too if I missed. The numbers were hopeless. So I stayed where I was and turned my face to the wall and listened."

Hermione stared at him. "You make it sound strategic."

"It wasn't. It was cowardly and strategic. Those things do coexist."

"I know they do." The flatness had left her voice. Rawness was in now, hot enough to make the room tilt. "And I need you to hear mine. I was seventeen. She was writing on me. I looked at the only person in the room even vaguely my age and you turned away. I've known for seven years that you were scared and that you probably couldn't have stopped her. I testified to that. I believe it. It doesn't help."

The old paneling at his back looked darker than the rest of the room, as if oil from generations of hands had sunk into it and stayed. His own hands hung useless at his sides.

"I know," he said.

"No, you don't. That's the point."

"Then tell me the point."

She made a helpless sound, half anger, half disgust at herself. "The point is that I can explain your behavior and still hate that you did it. The point is that I know all the mitigating facts. I sat in a courtroom and gave them to other people in careful order. I did that because it was true and because I didn't want you sent to Azkaban for being sixteen and trapped in a house with monsters. None of that changes what happened in this room."

His head tipped back against the wall with a soft thud. "You think I don't know that?"

"I think you know it in a way that still leaves me doing the explanatory work. That's what I think."

"What explanatory work?"

"This." She spread a hand between them, the room, the history, his careful voice, her own body refusing the present tense. "Saying yes, you were a child too. Yes, you were trapped too. Yes, you were under threat. Yes, the war made cowards of children and called it policy. I know all of that. I've known it for years. I still walked around with your wall in my head."

That landed. She could see the exact second it landed because the carefulness dropped out of his posture and left him looking stripped, not of defense exactly, but of any useful fiction about what this conversation was going to permit.

"I should have done it anyway," he said. "Tried. Even if it killed me."

"You'd have died."

"Probably."

"And Bellatrix would have finished the word." Anger made the sentence merciless and she did not call it back. "I'd still have been here. You'd just be dead with me."

"Then maybe dead was the right answer."

The flatness of it stopped her. No drama. No performance of guilt. Just arithmetic carried forward seven years and set down on the marble between them like a fact.

"Don't," she said. She wasn't sure whether she meant don't say that or don't make me answer it. "I'm not telling you you should have died."

"No." His eyes had gone to the floor again. "You're telling me you can explain why I lived."

"That isn't the same thing."

"Isn't it?"

He pushed off the wall and took two steps toward her, then stopped as if he had reached the limit of what his body could tolerate. "You keep saying you testified for me as if that closes the account. As if because you were generous in public, we don't have to stand here now."

That hurt because it was true.

Hermione folded her arms hard across herself and immediately regretted it when her left forearm lit with scar heat under the glamour. "I keep saying it because the trial had rules."

He looked up.

She heard how inadequate the sentence was and kept going anyway because inadequacy was at least honest. "It had charges and statements and sequence. It had places to put things. I could say this happened, then this, then this, and it all went into record. I knew what to call you there. Defendant. Witness. Child coerced under threat. Here you're just..." She made an angry gesture at the room, at him, at herself. "You're here. And so am I."

"And that's worse."

"Yes."

The answer came too fast to soften. They both heard that.

He looked away. "Fair enough."

"Stop doing that."

His eyes snapped back to hers. "Doing what?"

"Agreeing in that quiet voice as if politeness is the problem. It isn't."

"What would you like instead?"

"I don't know." The confession came out rough. "I don't know. I don't know how to do this without turning it into a hearing or a row or..." She swallowed. The room felt airless now, despite the cold. "Or one more thing the house has arranged for us because it wanted the dramatic reveal after tea."

His attention sharpened. "You think it did this on purpose."

"Don't you?"

"I think the house does almost nothing without purpose."

"That isn't an answer."

"Then yes." The word was dragged out of him, reluctant not because he disagreed but because agreement made the thing uglier. "Yes, I think it waited."

"Until what?"

"Until you were less likely to leave."

The room went very still around them.

Hermione stared at him. "You say that as if it's obvious."

"It is to me."

"Because you've been expecting this."

"Because I know this house." He laughed once, a sound so dry it barely qualified. "Or I know enough to fear the parts I don't."

"You sealed the room."

"I asked for a sealed corridor."

"That is not the same thing."

"No." He scrubbed a hand over his face. "No. It isn't."

"Why this corridor?"

"Because it leads to your rooms."

The words hit low and hard.

"I didn't want this to be your last sight every night," he said, and there was no elegance in the sentence at all. That made it worse. "I didn't want the house being able to put you through it on command. So I asked it to stop using this door."

"And you thought that was enough."

"I thought it was what I could do."

"You keep saying that."

"Because it's still true."

"That was Bellatrix's logic too." Hermione heard the sentence as she said it and did not withdraw it, though some part of her winced at its cruelty. "This is what I can do. This is what I can take. This is what I can get away with."

For the first time that night, actual anger crossed his face, not at her exactly but at the line as it entered him. "Do not compare me to her."

"Then stop offering me the size of your options as if that changes what the options were."

"I didn't say it changed anything."

"No. You just keep setting it down in front of me like evidence that I should be able to live with."

"I don't expect you to live with it."

"You expect me to live here."

That shut him up.

Her own words shut her up too, because they were bigger than the argument they had been inside a second before. Live here. The phrase opened up the whole last month at once. The library table. The archive door. The matched pace in the corridor. His hands at her waist on the ladder. The exact amount of milk. The house turning some grotesque old intelligence on the smallest details of her routine until ordinary care had begun to grow in the cracks around terror.

"Do you know what the worst part is?" she asked, then answered before he could. "Not this room. Not even you in it. It's that twenty minutes ago I was thinking about tomorrow. The eastern files. Tea. Whether the archive would give us the south-coast ledgers if we asked nicely enough. I was thinking..." She laughed once, disbelieving and furious. "I was thinking I was comfortable."

He went completely still.

"I know," he said.

"Of course you know. You've known this place could do that to me since before I arrived."

"Not this. Not specifically this."

"No, just the general category of being softened up by architecture."

"Granger."

"Don't Granger me like that."

"How exactly am I permitted to say your name in a room where every option is wrong?"

The question was unfair. That was what made it real.

"You're not," she said.

He looked at her as if the answer had landed somewhere physical.

"I don't know how to stay here and know this room exists," she said, quieter now because shouting required more certainty than she had. "I don't know how to go back upstairs and pick up where we left off. I don't know how to look at you over breakfast or a chart or your bloody family papers and not see the wall. Not tonight. Maybe not tomorrow either. I don't know."

He took that like a blow he had been braced for and still could not absorb. When he answered, the brittle care had gone. What remained was bare enough to be ugly.

"I never stopped seeing it," he said. "The wall."

Hermione said nothing.

"You think I forgot because I could walk past the corridor." His voice had dropped almost to nothing. "I didn't. I just learned the routes that didn't force me to look at it every day."

"That's called forgetting with better furniture."

"Maybe." He swallowed. "Maybe that's all living here is."

She hated how much that sounded like the truth.

He moved then, not toward her this time but sideways, until he was once again in the exact place where his younger self had stood. It was not theatrical. The room itself almost seemed to take him there, or perhaps memory did. He looked at the opposite wall while he spoke.

"When she started cutting you, I counted. Did I tell you that?"

Hermione's breath caught. "No."

"I counted between sounds. Not because it helped. Because it was a thing I could do that wasn't nothing. Eight seconds between the first scream and the point where she laughed. Twelve before Pettigrew shifted his weight. Four before my father told her not to ruin the blood on the stone because it stained. I remember that too." He shut his eyes. "I remember thinking if I moved on the next count, maybe I'd get three steps. Then I started the count again because I didn't move. That's what I did in this room. Arithmetic and stillness."

The words left her cold in a new place.

"Why are you telling me this now?"

"Because you asked for a sign."

She stared at him.

"Not then," he said. "I know. I know that. But now. You said you wanted to know someone else in the room understood it as horror. I did. I do. I have every day since. It doesn't repair anything. It doesn't make me less useless in your memory. But I am not going to stand here and let you think I watched her like entertainment."

Hermione opened her mouth and found nothing. Not because she believed the room was repaired by the speech. It wasn't. The wall was still there between them. Bellatrix was still there. The word was still in her arm. But the thing he'd just offered was not defense either. It was worse and more useful than defense. It was witness, too late to save the girl she'd been and just in time to make the woman she was feel newly stranded.

"I didn't think entertainment," she said at last. "I thought... I don't know what I thought. Family. Training. Survival. I thought you went where the house had already put you and stayed there."

"I did."

"Yes." She dragged a hand through her hair. "That's the problem. That you did, and that I understand why, and that understanding it does absolutely nothing for my body when I walk into this room."

"I know."

"Stop saying you know when what you mean is you believe me."

He looked almost startled. Then he nodded once. "Fine. I believe you."

It was such a small correction. It changed the air anyway.

Hermione looked away from him at last and down at the marble between them. She could see, overlaid on the clean floor, the exact angle of her own arm on Bellatrix's knee. The way the blood had tracked toward the grout. The ridiculous detail of a stray thread from her sleeve sticking to the wet skin at her wrist.

"I hate that the room appeared now," she said.

"I know."

She threw him a look.

"I believe you," he corrected at once, and there was the faintest shred of exhausted humor in it. It died almost immediately. "I hate it too."

"Do you?"

"Yes."

"Because of me or because now you have to look at it?"

That one hit clean. He didn't dodge.

"Both," he said.

She nodded once. The answer was inadequate and the only honest one available. That seemed to be the standard this room enforced.

"I don't want your death," she said after a while. "For the record. Since apparently we are still doing records."

"All right."

"I don't want your apology either."

"That's fortunate. I haven't got one that isn't obscene."

"Good."

He lowered himself onto the nearest chair as if his legs had finally admitted how badly the last ten minutes had gone. He did not sit back. He leaned forward, elbows on his knees, hands loose between them, gaze on the floor.

"What do you want, then?" he asked, not looking at her.

The question was soft. Not a challenge. That made it harder.

Hermione considered lying. I want sleep. I want the room shut. I want to never have come here. None of it was false and none of it answered.

"I think," she said slowly, because she could not say it fast and stay precise, "I want this not to be the only room in the house that tells the truth."

He looked up.

She kept going before courage could collapse. "Upstairs everything is manageable. Work. Tea. Routes. The archive unlocking. The house being helpful in ways that are probably not help at all. And I let myself live in that because it was easier than asking what kind of place gives with one hand and keeps this at the end of the corridor." Her voice thinned. "I don't know what to do with both versions at once."

He was quiet for a long time.

"Neither do I," he said.

That, more than anything else he'd said, undid the last of her ability to remain in the room.

Not because it fixed anything. Because it didn't. Because there was no expertise here, no plan, no version of him or her adult enough to drag the younger ones safely out by making better sentences over the site.

She nodded once, not to him exactly but to the fact.

"I can't stay in here," she said.

"You shouldn't."

"And I can't promise tomorrow looks normal."

"I wasn't going to ask."

"Good."

She turned toward the door. Behind her she heard the scrape of chair legs against marble.

"Granger."

She stopped but did not turn.

His voice, when it came, was stripped so clean it was nearly toneless. "I am sorry that when you looked at me, the wall was what you got."

The sentence hit with more force than apology had any right to. Because it was not an absolution-seeking apology. It was a statement of damage, finally named.

Hermione kept her back to him. "Yes," she said, and hated how unsteady it sounded. "So am I."

She heard nothing after that for several seconds. No footsteps. No movement. When she turned despite herself, he was no longer standing. He had slid down from the chair to the edge of the wall behind it, forearms braced on his knees, head bowed as if the body had simply run out of instructions.

No apology followed. None would have been enough. The room had already taken everything it could take.

Hermione walked out.

Crossing from marble to flagstone felt like stepping out of a cellar and into ordinary weather. The corridor stayed fixed around her: portraits, stone, cracked flagstone, guest wing ahead. Nothing shifted. The house had no need. It had already shown her what it wanted shown.

At the crack in the floor, the resonance caught again, altered beyond recognition. For weeks it had lived at the edge of her attention as warmth. Now it came through as cold stone, held breath, and a grief dense enough to feel like weight. He was still in the drawing room.

She kept walking.
