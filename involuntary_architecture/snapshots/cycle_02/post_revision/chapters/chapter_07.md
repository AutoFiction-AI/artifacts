# Chapter 7

The room had not been there yesterday.

Hermione was certain of that. She'd walked this corridor twice the previous evening, once to the library and once back, and the door on the left had been a linen closet. She'd opened it by mistake, looking for the bathroom, and found shelves of folded towels and a smell of cedar. Now the door was taller by a foot, dark-paneled, and opening onto a sitting room she had never seen.

South-facing windows. Two chairs angled toward a low fire. A tea service on the table between them. Grey winter light lay over the garden outside, frost visible through glass that held no condensation. Heating charms, then. Preparation. The woman already occupying one of the chairs answered the question of for whom.

"Ms. Granger." Narcissa Malfoy did not rise. "Do sit down."

In the doorway, Hermione took one beat to catalogue what had been arranged: tea already poured, milk in neither cup, fire steady and low, Narcissa still in her winter cloak though the room was warm. The room itself looked old enough to have grown there. Tall windows. Slightly off-center fireplace. Proportions too settled to feel newly assembled out of the house's private reserves.

She sat and picked up the cup waiting for her. The tea was strong, black, unfamiliar in the way that suggested a blend kept for one person's use rather than general hospitality.

"I didn't know you were coming today," Hermione said.

"I did not announce myself." Narcissa held her own cup with both hands. Her fingers were long and bare of jewelry except for a plain gold ring on the left hand, worn smooth by decades. The hands were the only part of her that showed age plainly: faint veins, knuckles a little swollen, a worker's evidence under the polish. "Draco is addressing the ventilation charms in the east wing. He will be occupied for some time."

Narcissa had timed this. Hermione could admire the workmanship and still know she was being managed.

"You want to talk to me alone."

"I thought that would be more productive."

The tea was better than Hermione had expected. Clean bitterness. No stale edge. Outside, the hedges were dark with frost, the gravel paths white-grey under a sky that would blacken by half past three. Late November had gone hard toward winter while she wasn't paying attention. The days had shortened around her, and the Manor's warmth had absorbed the change until the cold outside felt like weather visiting other buildings.

"I want to ask you about the house," she said.

"I assumed you would."

Narcissa's gaze flicked once to the window and came back. "The house has been busy since my last visit. The corridor outside your guest room has shortened by a third. It used to run the length of the east facade. And there is a door near the main staircase that was not there when I came for tea in October."

"I've been keeping a log of the changes."

"Of course you have." No condescension in it, only brief recognition from one careful woman to another. "I kept one as well, when I first moved in. A full inventory of every shift, every new door, every corridor that changed direction between morning and evening. It lasted eight months before the record became longer than was useful and I admitted the log served my sense of control more than accuracy."

She smoothed her cloak over one knee. Difficult things came easier when her hands had work. Outside, a bird landed on the frozen hedge and left again at once.

"Last week we found a room in the library that opens only when both of us are present. It destabilizes if one of us leaves. The house is gating access to research material based on who's in the room." Hermione paused to make the point plain. "Based on both of us being in the room together."

"Yes. Draco mentioned as much."

"He told you?"

"He told me very little. I asked questions he could not avoid answering." Narcissa's mouth altered by a fraction, the nearest she came to a smile. "He has not yet learned how transparent he becomes when he is trying to be opaque."

Warmth, there. Specific and unhidden. Hermione noted it and set it aside. Narcissa's love for Draco set the outer limit of her honesty.

"Has the house done this before?" Hermione asked. "Gated rooms based on who's present?"

Narcissa drank, set the cup down with the handle turned toward Hermione, and folded her hands in her lap.

"The Manor was built by Aurelius Malfoy in 1247," she said. "Though built is generous. Initiated, perhaps. The original construction has been revised, expanded, damaged, and rebuilt so many times that I doubt a single stone from Aurelius's work remains. But the foundational enchantments are his, or derive from his."

"And those enchantments are what's behind the gating. The rooms that appear."

Narcissa's hands resettled in her lap. She had been heading somewhere more slowly.

"The house was built to sustain the Malfoy line," she said. "That did not mean merely sheltering it. It meant steering it. Marriages. Heirs. The private loyalties on which old houses depend. When the Manor approved of a direction, it ceased behaving like architecture and began arranging the people inside it."

"Cultivates what?"

"Whatever it determines the line requires. Magical strength. Useful alliances. Attachments it considers useful." The last phrase had displaced a sharper one. Hermione could hear the refusal in the choice. "The house has a mandate, Ms. Granger. It reads the people inside it. It responds to what it finds between them. It creates circumstances in which one cannot go on pretending indifference."

"And gating the archive room is part of those conditions."

"The house rewards what serves its mandate. It withdraws access from what does not."

Hermione set her cup down. Through the china she could feel the tea cooling in real time. Narcissa watched her hands and did not look away.

"You lived in this house for twenty-five years."

"I did."

"And the house cultivated things. Between you and Lucius."

Narcissa's posture shifted by a degree. Her weight settled differently. Her cup touched the saucer with a small, clean click before she steadied it.

"When I first arrived, nineteen years old and married three weeks, the Manor was very attentive." Her voice dropped out of lecture and into memory. "Lucius and I quarreled over where I was to receive my mother when she visited. It was absurd. That did not make it unimportant. I left him in the west corridor, opened the next door expecting the main staircase, and walked into a morning room I had never seen before."

"Lucius was already there. The door behind me had gone. One fire. One chair. Tea for two. I had come through the conservatory because the corridor bent on me; sleet had soaked through my shoes, and the back of one heel was bleeding where the leather had rubbed it raw. He was furious with the house. I could tell because he was too angry with the walls to be angry with me. He called the room vulgar. Then he looked down, saw the blood on my stocking, and knelt on the carpet to take my shoes off himself."

Hermione said nothing.

"We had been speaking to each other like negotiators since the wedding," Narcissa went on. "In that room he stopped. He held my foot near the grate until feeling came back into it and asked, with real irritation, whether I intended to own a sensible pair of boots before spring. I laughed. He looked startled by that. The first ordinary conversation we had as husband and wife happened because the house barred the door and would not let us leave."

"Invested how?"

"Invested in the way a hostess is invested in a match she has decided ought to succeed. Whether the parties wish to be handled is secondary."

"That's a metaphor."

"Yes. The reality is less tidy." She lifted her cup, found it empty, and returned it to the saucer without a sound. "For a time it kept doing that. If we went too many days speaking only through etiquette, a corridor would shorten until one of us was delivered to the other. If he worked past midnight, a tray would appear in whatever room I had taken my book to, with two cups and a second knife for the envelopes because he never remembered his. If I tried to sleep in the east suite after a row, I would wake to find the only exit opened into his dressing room. Not every intervention was kind. They were simply effective. And eventually, it stopped."

"Stopped."

"Stopped arranging us. The rooms returned to fixed positions. The library sealed the sections it had opened. The corridors stopped correcting for distance. I spent twelve nights in the east suite after a worse quarrel, years later, and the path to his study ran straight every time. His rooms stayed his. Mine stayed mine. I could hear him moving two floors away and the walls did nothing with the sound." One turn of the ring on her finger, slow and deliberate. "Lucius preferred the house that way. Predictable. He said the shifting made him irritable. I do not think he noticed when it ended. Or, if he did, that what he felt was relief."

"Mrs. Malfoy," Hermione began.

"I am telling you this because the house has not stopped for you." Narcissa's voice had thinned to something plainer. "In twenty-five years, the Manor never gated a room behind both my presence and Lucius's together. Not once. Whatever this house is doing with you and my son, it is pursuing a pattern it did not pursue with us."

"What does that mean?" No qualifiers left on it.

"I do not know." For the first time, Narcissa sounded as if she were telling the whole truth. "I know the house's mandate. I know it reads the people inside it, responds to what it finds — "

"But you don't know the mechanism."

"No." Narcissa let her gaze rest on the window for a moment. "Nor the limits. And I do not know whether what the house cultivates would exist without the cultivation."

"The house is not malicious," Narcissa said. "I lived here for twenty-five years. It did not harm me. It did not harm Draco. But it is not neutral."

"Meaning it's working toward an end."

"It has preferences. And what you feel inside these walls may be threaded through the walls themselves." She straightened. "You live inside that condition, Ms. Granger. That is simply the fact of it."

"What would you advise?"

Narcissa stood. Her gloves lay on the arm of the chair, grey leather lined against the cold, and she picked them up with the kind of deliberation that closed conversations for her.

"I do not advise. I do not have the standing to advise you. I am neither pardoned nor convicted. I live in a rented house in Knightsbridge because the Ministry cannot decide whether my war service was treason or not, and until they decide, I am provisional." She pulled on one glove and then the other, smoothing the leather over each finger. "But my son has been alone in this house for three years, and whatever the Manor is doing, he is less alone than he was. I prefer that to the alternative. I prefer it considerably."

"One more thing," Hermione said.

Narcissa waited with one hand on the doorframe. Her nails were short and unpainted, practical in a way that still managed to look deliberate.

"When the house stopped with you and Lucius. What was that like afterward?"

The question crossed into private ground, and Hermione knew it. Narcissa had come to be honest inside selected limits. This one, barely, still sat within them.

"The house became ordinary architecture," Narcissa said. "Not metaphorically. If Lucius chose the west study, he remained in the west study. If I shut a door, it stayed shut. Fires died when the coals burned through. Doors led where they had led the day before. Once, after it had gone still, I stood at the top of the north stairs waiting for the turn that used to send him toward me. It did not." She looked into the corridor beyond Hermione's shoulder. "Lucius preferred it."

"Did you?"

Narcissa did not answer at once.

"No," she said. Quieter than the rest of the conversation. "But I understood why he did."

She left. Hermione listened to her footsteps on the stone corridor, even and unhurried, the stride of someone who knew these halls in the dark. The sitting room remained. The fire kept burning. Two cups sat between the chairs, one empty and one cooling.

---

For several minutes Hermione stayed where she was, cold tea in her hands.

Narcissa had given her precedent and a warning. She had not given her an answer.

On the walk to the library one sentence kept rising anyway: Your mother said the house never did this with your father. Hermione kept it to herself. By the time she reached the door, the omission had become a decision.

Draco was at their shared worktable, the ventilation charms apparently handled. A stack of texts from the gated archive sat open around him, items carried out before the room sealed again. Cotton gloves on, pencil in hand, he was making notes in cramped, careful script.

"Your mother was here," Hermione said.

He did not look up. "I know."

"She wanted to talk to me alone."

"I know that too." Another note went into the margin. "She does that. You don't have to report."

That made the concealment easier. The ease of it felt ugly.

He did not ask what Narcissa had said. Hermione did not offer it. This was the register they had found over the past weeks: functional, workmanlike, a dry undercurrent that sometimes passed for warmth. Research remained the one territory where he stopped guarding himself quite so hard. The shift in him was obvious enough that she had begun to accommodate it without meaning to, steering toward texts and notation and anything that let them stand near each other without naming what they were doing there.

For a moment she watched him write. Left-handed. The pull under her sternum answered before she let her eyes fall anywhere near his forearm, a low familiar pressure that told her the Mark was active without requiring the sight of it. His handwriting looked nothing like the spell that fed on him. The mismatch caught at her more than it should have.

"The archive?" she asked.

"Whenever you're ready."

They walked there together through the stacks. Hermione had begun taking the quickest route to any book, the kind of recklessness that would once have earned a student a lecture. The archive door appeared before they reached the far wall. That had stopped startling her. The room opened with the peculiar impression of having been waiting for them.

Warm air met them, carrying old vellum and the faint mineral scent of preservation charms stretched over material far older than the spells maintaining it. The Tiberius ledger lay where she'd left it on the broad oak table, open to field notes from 1364. Draco took his usual seat. Hermione sat opposite him, close enough for pages to pass between them without either standing. He kept both gloves on. She wore one, right hand covered against the vellum, left hand bare for the pencil. At some point that arrangement had become habit.

Tiberius's notation system was dense: Latin prose altered by runic modifiers that behaved less like ornament than mathematical operators. Draco read the Latin. Hermione worked the runic syntax. Between them the text became legible.

He passed her a page where a runic compound sat in the margin of a construction entry, base form familiar, one added stroke changing the root.

"I don't know this one," she said.

"Nor do I." Draco leaned closer and tapped the inner margin. "But that mark isn't Tiberius's."

Half hidden by age-darkened glue in the gutter sat a tiny hooked sigil and a faded A. Hermione had taken it for damage.

"Abraxas?"

"He used that mark for later glosses." Draco looked at the ledger's spine instead of the page. "I've never opened these far enough to go looking for them. Too much chance of cracking the binding, and without the runes I wouldn't have known which entries needed one."

Hermione braced the vellum with her gloved hand and cast the smallest loosening charm she could manage. The old leather gave by a fraction. A folded vellum sheet slipped from the inner hinge onto the table, covered in Abraxas Malfoy's annotations.

"Family glosses aren't neutral," she said.

"No." He set the sheet between them. "So we test it before we trust it."

They did. One modifier they had already worked out from context matched Abraxas's note exactly. A second came close enough to be useful and far enough off to make both of them wary.

"This one means sustained duration," Draco said at last. "Not preserved as in static. Sustained as in actively fed. If he's not lying, the magic required regular renewal."

"Useful," Hermione said.

"Provisionally."

Treating the sheet as witness rather than authority, they kept going.

The next page described a specific site where Tiberius had performed sustaining rituals on what he called a radix, a root-point, a place where magic had been fixed into the landscape and bound there to hold for centuries.

"There's another one here," Draco said, three pages later. "Severn valley. And one near what would be the Devon coast."

"How many?"

"At least twelve. He's documenting a circuit."

They mapped the locations as they found them. A rough network spread across southern and central England: Salisbury Plain, Glastonbury, the Welsh marches, a point in London close to where the Ministry now stood. Then Godric's Hollow appeared on the page, and Hermione's pencil stopped.

"Godric's Hollow."

"I see it."

"The battle sites. Both wars. If these are anchors, the amount of magical combat at or near them would have damaged the whole network."

"If they're real," Draco said. His voice had tightened into the careful register strain pulled out of him. "If they're still functional."

"They were functional when Tiberius wrote this. The question is whether anyone maintained them after him."

"Given that no one seems to know they exist, I would guess not."

They looked down at the map. Twelve points, linked by what Tiberius called filum, threads of sustaining magic running from anchor to anchor and holding the ambient field across the territory between them. Infrastructure under all of British wizarding magic. Built, or at least documented, by a Malfoy ancestor six centuries earlier. Left to take war damage with no repair.

Abraxas had not annotated every page. When he had, the notes were either vanity or use. A cramped gloss beside the next maintenance table identified a renewal cycle Tiberius had not bothered to explain: quarter-turn reinforcements at the solstices and equinoxes, full circuit inspection every seventh year, keyed wards refreshed at each point by paired practitioners carrying linked permissions from the previous site.

"That isn't ceremonial," Hermione said.

"No." Draco scanned the line again. "It is upkeep."

Two pages later Abraxas had corrected a later hand in the margin with enough force to score the vellum: neglect at one anchor compromises the span.

"God help us," Hermione said softly.

"My family appears to have hidden national infrastructure in a private room and then mislaid the maintenance." Draco's tone stayed flat. It did not disguise the strain in it. "That feels depressingly consistent."

They went back through the ledger and marked every instruction tied to renewal. Materials, intervals, keyed sequences, signs of local failure. None of it read as myth. This was labor. Difficult, technical, expensive labor, but still the kind people could perform if they knew it needed doing. The revelation made the disaster smaller and larger at once. Smaller, because this might be repairable. Larger, because repair implied negligence, and negligence implied human choices.

Hermione drew her satchel closer and took out the Ministry incident summaries she had copied before leaving London: Floo outage clusters, ward-shear reports, county memoranda on failed warming nets and transport instability. She spread them beside Tiberius's map and began matching locations.

Salisbury district failures aligned with the Plain anchor. A run of apparition accidents in the Welsh marches clustered around another point on the circuit. London's recent transport instability, the long slow sickness in the Ministry's internal warding, sat almost exactly over the London anchor Tiberius had marked in a careful square hand six centuries earlier.

Draco leaned over the map until his shoulder nearly touched hers. "That cannot be coincidence."

"No." Hermione circled the London point hard enough to dent the page beneath. "It is a pattern. The damage is propagating outward from the old support structure."

"And the battle sites did the rest."

"Probably in waves." She flipped back through her notes, mind running faster now that the shape of it had declared itself. "The first war damages the network. The second war hits many of the same regions again. No one repairs the original anchors because no one knows the anchors exist, and the field goes on trying to route power through broken lines until the breaks become systemic."

He was still looking at the map. "If this is right, the decay is not only magical exhaustion. It is infrastructure collapse."

"Yes."

He sat back. The chair complained softly against the stone. "That will not improve the Ministry's mood."

"The Ministry's mood is not really the governing issue."

"It will become one the moment you tell them the answer was sitting in a Malfoy archive." He did not sound defensive. Only tired. "They will hear my father's name before they hear the word anchor."

Hermione knew he was right. She hated that it mattered. She hated more that it had to matter to him first. "Croaker still needs this."

"Eventually."

"Soon."

He met her gaze. "Then we bring him copies, not originals. We verify as much as we can before the room becomes evidence instead of workspace."

The instinct to argue rose fast and died just as quickly. He was not trying to withhold the truth. He was trying to keep it from being buried under the family name that had hidden it in the first place.

"Fine," she said. "Copies first. Then Croaker."

That, too, changed the air between them. Agreement had become easier in the archive than anywhere else in the Manor. Perhaps because the room demanded collaboration in such blunt terms. Perhaps because the work left less room for performance.

By late afternoon the oak table had vanished under copied tables, transliterated glosses, and Hermione's increasingly ruthless map notes. Draco translated site descriptions while she turned them into coordinates the Department could actually use. When he stalled on a runic modifier she took the page without comment; when she hit an archaic property cipher he took it back. The handoff had become so practiced that neither of them remarked on it anymore.

At some point the house sent in a tray without being asked. Bread, thick soup, two apples, tea already poured. Draco looked at it for a beat as if considering whether refusal still counted as refusal when the walls had made the choice for you.

"Eat," Hermione said, already taking the bowl nearest her.

"Very authoritative."

"You are pale."

"So are you."

"That's not a counterargument."

His mouth shifted, not quite into humor. He picked up the second bowl. "You spend enough time here and you start issuing orders as if the room answers to you."

"The room does answer to us. That is the problem."

They ate in the narrow space between one text and the next. Outside the archive there would have been weather and hour and all the ordinary ways a day announced it was ending. In here time reduced itself to lamp glow and cooling tea. The bread was still warm. Hermione resented how domestic that felt.

When Draco set his spoon down he rubbed absently at his left forearm. The answering pressure moved under Hermione's sternum at once, a live wire pulled taut and then released. She looked back at the ledger before the reaction could become visible.

"There is another note here," he said after a moment, voice flatter now. "Not Abraxas. Later than that."

He turned the book toward her. In a sharper, less elegant hand, someone had added a brief line beside the Salisbury entry: outer stone fractured after hostile casting; reinforcement deferred.

"Deferred by whom?"

"No signature."

"Convenient."

"Family records often are."

They found three more notations like it in the next hour. Damage named. Repair delayed. Then silence. Whatever chain of stewardship had once existed had frayed long before the present collapse became impossible to ignore. Perhaps during the first war. Perhaps earlier. The paperwork did not bother to defend itself.

By the time they finished the first pass through Tiberius and the glosses, dusk had gone to full dark beyond the hidden room's sense of it. Hermione's fingers ached from note-taking. Draco's careful script had become smaller and more compressed, the sign of strain in him that replaced any spoken complaint.

"Read this back to me," Hermione said, pushing the map toward him. "All twelve."

He did. Salisbury Plain. Glastonbury. The Welsh marches. London. Godric's Hollow. Severn valley. Devon coast. The remaining points pulled a rough sustaining circuit through southern and central England, not elegant on paper until one started laying present failures over it. Then the pattern became ugly enough to trust.

Hermione wrote at the top of a clean sheet: Anchor Network, probable structural cause of ambient-field decay.

Draco watched the words appear. "That sounds official."

"It needs to."

"No," he said. "It needs to be correct."

She glanced up. "Those are not opposite conditions."

"They often are at the Ministry."

The dryness in it was familiar now. So was the impulse to answer it instead of stepping around it. "Then we had better manage both."

He looked down again, but not before she caught the brief change in his expression. Fatigue, certainly. Alarm. And under both, an unwilling recognition that she had just included him in the work as naturally as breathing.

They spent another hour making the copies they would need if the archive denied them entry tomorrow. The room held steady while both were bent to the table, then gave one soft shiver whenever either of them rose far enough to disturb the balance. By the end Hermione could feel the house attending, not hostile, not kind, simply interested in whether they would go on satisfying its terms.

When they carried the copied pages back out to the main library table, the archive door blurred at once and folded itself into shelving.

"Still dramatic," Draco said.

"Still impossible," Hermione said.

The main library felt colder after the enclosed heat of the archive. She spread the fresh copies on their shared table while he weighted the corners with old folios. Under the lamps the map looked less like scholarship and more like triage.

"Will you owl Croaker tonight?" he asked.

Hermione thought of Narcissa's careful face. The house steering. The question she had kept for herself. "No. Not until I can give him more than a theory and a family scandal."

Draco's hands stilled over the papers. "That is unexpectedly considerate."

"Don't make me regret it."

"I will do my best."

That should have been lighter than it was. Instead it landed with the exhausted sincerity of two people too tired to hide properly.

He gathered the translated notes into a precise stack. "Tomorrow, then."

"Tomorrow."

When they finally left the library, the corridor to the east wing stayed mercifully straight. They walked it together anyway. At her door he paused, one hand still full of copied pages.

"Try to sleep," she said, because the alternative was to acknowledge that she knew he would not.

He looked at her for one unreadable beat. "You too."

He kept walking. She waited until his footsteps had taken the turn above the main stair before she went inside.

---

That night Hermione lay in bed and listened to the house.

After midnight the Manor breathed. Beneath the settling timber and old pipes, a slower rhythm moved through stone and plaster, rising and falling as if the building had lungs hidden somewhere in the walls.

Her room stayed fixed. Bed. Bathroom down the corridor. Route to the library. Small mercies, if that was what they were.

Two floors above and to the east, Draco was awake.

For the past week and a half the resonance had lived at the edge of her attention like background noise. It no longer belonged there. Pacing registered as a drag behind her sternum. Stillness had weight. The Mark's active flares came through sharper than its baseline unease, and each examination made the whole signal cleaner instead of fainter.

Narcissa's warning altered the interpretation. What Hermione carried from his sleepless nights might be the house, or the Mark, or both together. It was still there. It was still getting stronger.

And it was measurable.

That was the part she disliked most. His worst nights ran past three. The Mark usually settled closer to four. She knew the difference now between a restless circuit of the room and the heavier stillness when he sat down and failed to sleep in place. None of that knowledge had been offered to her. She had it anyway.

At half past one the resonance sharpened. He was pacing again.

Hermione lay still for another count of ten, then got out of bed, crossed to the desk, and pulled her notebook from the drawer. Diagnostic Residue: Mark-Adjacent Sensory Artifacts stared up at her from the top of the page.

At 1:32 a.m. she crossed out Residue and began a record she wasn't sure she had the right to keep.

1:32. Pacing. East-west, regular turn.

She stared at the line for a moment, then added a second.

1:39. Stop near window. Mark pressure elevated.

The shame of writing it down sat hot in her throat. She knew this because the connection delivered it to her, not because he had told her, not because she had asked, certainly not because he had consented to being observed in his insomnia by someone two floors away.

At 1:44 the pressure sharpened hard enough to make her set the pen down. Not pain, exactly. A brief live pull, then the drag of him moving again. She wrote that too. If she was going to violate the boundary, she could at least do it honestly.

2:07. Acute flare. Possible Mark spike rather than pacing.

She was halfway to her feet before she understood she had moved. The impulse to go to him arrived whole-bodied and immediate. Knock on the study door. Ask nothing. Pretend she was awake for water or for a book or because the house had shifted again. Any of those lies would have been easy.

Narcissa's voice stopped her. What you feel inside these walls may be threaded through the walls themselves.

Hermione sat back down hard enough to jar the inkpot. If she went now, on the strength of a signal she did not understand, she would be surrendering to the exact uncertainty Narcissa had placed in her hands that morning. Worse, she would be doing it while keeping Draco ignorant of the warning that made the impulse suspect.

Upstairs the pacing stopped.

The stillness that followed had weight. She knew by now the difference between him pausing and him sitting down because sitting down always pulled the resonance lower, closer to the center of her chest. It happened now. A held line. Weariness. Then a faint jagged flare that made her grip the edge of the desk.

2:16. Seated. Short spike immediately after.

Her notebook no longer looked like a record of diagnostic residue. It looked like surveillance, and the distinction mattered less on paper than it did to her conscience.

She turned to a clean page and wrote a new heading.

Mark Resonance: Correlation With Examination Depth

Beneath it, after a longer pause, she added the first question that could survive both the archive and Narcissa's warning.

If the original bind retained lived trace, could the present damage force it upward?

The line sat there waiting for her. On the next page she began sketching the array in rough layers, already seeing how much of it would have to be rebuilt from the ground up before she could safely put it on his arm.
