# Chapter 2

Ten miles after the skyline dropped out of his mirrors, the green document folder thumped into the passenger footwell when Eli took an exit ramp too fast. He left it there and convinced himself of a rule.

He could stay gone for one day if he treated it like a weather delay and not a decision.

One day meant no speeches, no declarations, no new life. One day meant coffee from paper cups with lids that never snapped right, one more stretch of interstate, one motel room paid in cash if he had to, then he would call Nora and say what he could say without detonating everything at once. He said the word delay in his head the way airport agents said it over loudspeakers: neutral voice, no fault assigned, passengers advised to remain near the gate.

The car smelled like stale fries and the lemon cleaner from the dashboard wipes he kept in the glove box. June had spilled cold brew in the passenger cup holder on move-in day. He had mopped at it with a dorm T-shirt she said he could throw out, but now the dried sugar came up when the sun hit it and made the whole front seat smell faintly fermented, like fruit left too long in a bowl.

Interstate 70 hummed under him. Trucks sat in the right lane like moving walls. He had put the folder back once at a stoplight in Wheeling and then let it stay down there after. He did not need to see June’s tuition statement again this hour. He knew the dates. He knew every due date in his house better than anyone in it.

His phone started ringing outside Washington, Pennsylvania, and did not stop for the next three hours.

NORA (HOME)

He let it go to voicemail, then tapped out a text at the next red light.

Bad reception in the hills. Driving. I’ll call when I can.

He watched the three dots appear, disappear, reappear.

Then her message arrived.

What hills. You left campus Friday, August 28, at 3:40.

The typing bubble came back almost immediately.

I’m post shift and my wrist is lit up. I need one actual answer from you.

That did it, the wrist line. He pictured the gray brace she wore some mornings, drugstore Velcro already fuzzing at the edges, and the way she still opened the pickle jar with that hand because she hated asking anybody for help with small things. He could hear her in the kitchen drawer with one hand fishing for ibuprofen and the other hand balancing her phone on speaker while she changed out of scrubs.

He put the phone face down in the center console and drove another thirty miles without looking at it.

When he finally did, the call log looked like an accusation typed by machine.

NORA (HOME) x4

CAL BRISTOW x2

UNKNOWN NUMBER

ALLEGHENY BANK ALERT

ALLEGHENY BANK ALERT

Cal's call was inevitable. Monday's compliance audit sat on the calendar two days ahead, the kind of meeting that punished absence by multiplying it. If you missed the room, you still had to answer for every line item in the packet, except now you did it one on one with people who were already irritated and trying not to say lawsuit in plain language.

Cal's voicemail could wait. He bought himself tasks instead: coffee first, then a gas-station sink, cold water, maybe a face that looked less like a man who had slept in his car. He kept stacking errands between himself and the message.

A service plaza west of Columbus gave him a slot between a horse trailer and a charter bus with college kids asleep against the windows. Heat rose off the asphalt in visible shimmers. It was not yet nine and his shirt stuck to the middle of his back.

Inside, the fluorescent lights flattened everybody to the same exhausted color. A man in a Bengals cap argued with a vending machine that had eaten his five. A mother crouched next to a toddler at the fountain and counted to three while the child screamed no at the metal button as if it were surgery.

Eli stood at the coffee station and watched his hand tremble while he tore sugar packets. He had not slept more than two hours in the car outside a rest stop and the back of his neck felt sunburned and greasy at the same time.

His phone buzzed again on the counter.

ALLEGHENY BANK ALERT: Unusual purchase attempt, card ending 4418, QUICKFUEL #117, Belmont OH. Reply YES if authorized.

Before he could type, a second alert landed.

ALLEGHENY BANK ALERT: New device sign-in to joint account detected. If this was not you, tap to secure.

"Sir?" the coffee clerk said. "Card got declined."

He looked down at the reader. TRANSACTION BLOCKED flashed in red.

"Run it again."

She did. Same result.

Heat climbed his neck. He peeled a five and a fist of quarters from his pocket, counted wrong once, then slapped the coins down hard enough to slosh coffee over his knuckles. The burn made him swear under his breath.

The same unknown number called before he got his lid on the cup.

"Mr. Mercer, this is Allegheny fraud prevention. We placed a temporary hold after out-of-pattern fuel charges and an unrecognized login. Can you verify your last transfer amount?"

His mouth went dry. "I can verify the fuel charge."

"I need the transfer amount, sir."

He ended the call and stared at the wet skin on his hand until the sting sharpened, then dulled.

When he stepped out of the plaza with his coffee, the phone started ringing with Cal’s name again. He watched it ring to the last pulse and then to silence.

The voicemail arrived six seconds later.

He sat in the driver’s seat, shut the door, and hit play.

"Eli, it's Cal. Call me back as soon as you get this. We're scheduled in Conference C Monday at nine for external audit prep, and you're still listed as lead for corrective actions and pharmacy diversion controls. I need you on today's noon prep call so we're not building this blind. HR's copied because you've been unreachable since Friday. If you're delayed, I need a real ETA, not 'on my way.' Call me back."

Cal paused, and Eli heard the familiar throat-clear, that dry little reset he did before saying anything he might need quoted later.

"Also, separate thing, you left your green legal pad in my office, the one with next week's handoff notes. I locked it in my top drawer so nobody walks off with it. Call me, Eli."

The message ended.

Eli sat with the phone in his palm while the air conditioner coughed warm breath at his knees. The legal pad detail landed harder than the threat. Of course Cal would secure a legal pad as if it were evidence. Of course he would make room in a voicemail for a small act of procedural kindness and then tighten the noose in the same minute.

He had worked under Cal for nine years. Cal ironed his shirts so sharply that the sleeves looked folded from cardboard and carried peppermint tins in his jacket pocket because he thought chewing gum looked juvenile in meetings. He called everybody by their first name and never once forgot to copy the right person on a warning email.

Eli deleted the voicemail and immediately pulled it back from trash because that looked too final, even to himself.

He checked Nora’s thread.

Bank flagged Ohio charges and a security hold. Are you driving west, yes or no.
June asked if you made it home. I covered once. Don’t make me do it twice.

He typed back: Westbound. Not hurt. I’ll call in ten.

She called before he could put the phone down.

"Say it once and say it clean," she said. "I can handle a sentence. I can't handle static."

"I needed a day to clear my head."

"Then clear it with facts. I moved payroll and mortgage into a separate view so I can keep this week from imploding. Text me your location by three Eastern or I call Theo and your office."

He opened his mouth to argue and heard himself say, "Okay."

He crossed into Indiana by noon with his jaw aching from clenching it through truck traffic. The sky turned the hard white-blue of late August, no clouds, just glare. Every overpass threw a brief square of shade and then took it back. He kept reaching for the radio and then turning it off after two songs, as if music required a version of himself he had left somewhere at the Pennsylvania line.

One day, he reminded himself.

He would get to Indianapolis outskirts, sleep, eat something real, call Nora from a quieter head, say he needed space and he had taken a drive and he would be back in forty-eight hours. He would leave out the part where he did not know yet if forty-eight hours was a promise or a wish.

He filled the tank at a QuickFuel off Route 40 where the concrete islands were patched with darker circles like old wounds. A hornet kept ricocheting against the inside of the station window, trapped between glass and neon lottery stickers. He paid cash because the card hold had not cleared.

He called Nora from the pump while the numbers climbed.

"You said ten minutes," she said.

"I know. I'm on Route 40. I'm not hurt."

"Geography isn't a reason, Eli."

"I needed to drive before I said something I can't unsay."

"Cal's assistant called me asking if you were in an ER. That's where we're at."

The pump clicked off. Gas fumes sat sweet and thick in his throat.

"I'll call you before three," he said.

"You call me with a reason, not another weather report."

She hung up first.

Another call came from Cal while he passed a billboard for personal injury attorneys. This time he answered.

"Cal."

"Good. Listen carefully. You missed the noon prep block and you are now flagged unreachable. If HR doesn't hear from you by five Eastern Monday, they open an abandonment review. If this is medical, call HR today and document it. If it isn't, stop making me chase you."

Eli tasted old coffee and metal.

"I hear you."

"Hearing me is not the same as answering me," Cal said, and the line went dead.

Eli looked at his own reflection in the rearview mirror while the call timer vanished from the screen. Sweat had dried to salt at his temples. Stubble shadowed his jaw in uneven dark patches. He looked like a man who had slept in his car because he had slept in his car.

Nora sent a screenshot of the shared account dashboard at two fifteen Eastern: yellow warning banners, pending tuition, mortgage draft, three circled fuel charges in Ohio and Indiana.

Before he could lock the screen, she called.

"Item one, tuition date is still live. Item two, mortgage draft is still pending. Item three, your out-of-state fuel trail is now six charges. Item four, where are you sleeping tonight."

He glanced down at the screenshot while rolling through a green light and missed the brake lights ahead until a pickup laid on the horn. He stomped the pedal, and hot coffee splashed his thigh through his jeans.

"Richmond by evening," he said through his teeth.

"Send me the address when you have it," she said. "Not vibes. An address."

She disconnected.

Three thirty found him outside a BP station near Richmond, Indiana, the kind with a cracked ice machine and a hand-painted sign that read WE DO NOT TAKE CHECKS. A row of dead moths lined the inside edge of the window where the lights burned all night.

This was where the idea that had been flickering all day turned solid.

It came to him as logistics first, because logistics were easier than guilt.

Smartphone meant location history, shared app logins, cloud backups, family plan records, face and name tied to every ping. Smartphone meant Nora could watch him move across a map in ten-mile increments if she wanted. Smartphone meant HR could prove he had not been anywhere near Pittsburgh when he claimed signal trouble. Smartphone meant every lie would carry metadata.

He sat there with the engine idling until he remembered he had already authorized pump four and still had not moved.

Inside the store, the refrigerated doors wheezed every time they opened. The cashier was a woman in her sixties with silver braids pinned up under a red visor and a gold Saints pin on the lanyard around her neck. The pin looked polished by years of thumb rubs. She had a spiral notebook on the counter where she wrote down every pump number in tidy block letters even though the register tracked it automatically.

"Pump four already authorized," she said without looking up. "You left your card in the reader."

He checked his back pocket. It was empty.

"Shit." He went back out, found the card still jutting from the reader, and stood there a moment with it between two fingers like it was a piece of someone else’s property.

When he came back in, the woman was scratching numbers in her notebook.

"Long day?" she asked.

"Yeah."

"You got that interstate face."

The phrase meant nothing to him, but he nodded like it did.

He bought a bottle of warm water, a pack of stale peanut crackers, and a prepaid flip phone blister pack hanging near the charging cables. The packaging said ninety days with activation and showed a smiling couple on a picnic blanket as if burner phones were for romance.

The cashier raised an eyebrow at the box.

"Those things still sell," she said. "Mostly truckers and men trying not to be found."

He gave a short laugh he did not feel.

"Which one am I?"

"You tell me, honey."

She rang it up and gave the total. He paid in cash, bills damp from his wallet.

"Name for the activation receipt," she said, tapping the keyboard.

The name came out before he had measured it.

"Ian."

She looked up then, finally, and held his face for a second. She did not look suspicious. She did not look sentimental either. She looked like a woman filing one more stranger under maybe.

"Ian what?"

"Just Ian."

"Suit yourself." She typed without comment and tore the receipt.

He carried the old phone and the new box to the side of the building where a dented metal trash can sat under an overhang. Rainwater had collected in the lid groove and gone green.

The old phone was warm from his hand. The screen was smudged. A hairline crack ran from when June had dropped it on the kitchen tile in March and apologized three times. The family-plan wallpaper still sat on the lock screen: June between him and Nora on move-in morning, all three of them squinting in August light, pretending nobody was counting losses yet.

He stood there long enough for a mosquito to find his ankle through the sock.

One day, he said to himself again, but the sentence had changed shape. It sounded thin and rehearsed now, like something delivered to a mirror.

The phone buzzed with an incoming call from Nora just as he lifted the trash-can lid.

He watched her name pulse across the screen.

He answered this one.

"Hey," he said.

The line crackled. He could hear hospital noise behind her, the overhead paging system and a cart wheel that needed grease.

"Where are you," she said.

She did not say hello. She did not accuse him. She asked for coordinates.

"Indiana. Near Richmond."

He heard her exhale through her nose, not relief, not exactly. It sounded like triage.

"Okay," she said. "You're alive and not upside down in a ditch. Good. Next: why are you in Indiana on a Saturday afternoon when you were supposed to be in Pittsburgh prepping for Monday."

A semi rolled by close enough to shake the overhang. He moved his hand over his free ear to hear better.

"I needed to drive," he said. "I needed one day where nobody was asking me for anything before I came back and started saying things I can’t unsay."

Silence on her end for three beats.

Then: "Eli, you don’t get to disappear into poetry right now. I need plain language. Are you coming home tonight."

He should have said yes. He knew the exact yes that would buy him twelve quiet hours.

Instead he said, "I don’t know about tonight."

Something metal clanged on her end, maybe a bed rail, maybe a dropped tray.

"Then tell me this," she said, voice flatter now. "Should I call your office and tell them you had a family emergency, or should I stop covering for you and let the consequences land where they land."

He closed his eyes. The mosquito buzzed his other ankle. The trash can smelled like beer and hot plastic.

"Don’t lie for me," he said.

"I already did this morning for June." Her voice thinned on June’s name and then steadied. "I told her your phone died on the turnpike. She asked why that would keep you from using a charger. She’s not a child."

He pressed his thumb hard into the cracked corner of the phone.

"I’ll call her tonight."

"No," Nora said. "You’ll text first and ask if she wants a call. You don’t get to dump your timing on her too."

He almost argued and then did not.

"Okay."

"And Eli?"

"Yeah."

"The bank flagged out-of-state charges. If anything else moved through our accounts, tell me what it was, when it moved, and where you put it. I'm in the employee lot after twelve hours, and June keeps asking me questions I can't answer."

At 11:43 last night, he had moved fourteen thousand dollars out of the emergency subaccount ending 9031 into a new checking account in his name only, with a transfer memo that said BRIDGE. It was still pending batch close, which meant she could see the balance dip but not the destination line yet.

"Nothing else today," he said, and heard himself lean on one word like a crutch.

Silence, then the snap of plastic on her end, maybe the keyboard tray in her car folding shut.

"Today isn't what I asked. If you moved money, say it now."

He kept breathing into the phone and said nothing.

"All right," she said. "I'm done covering this. I'm not calling your office again, and I'm not feeding June another story while I drag myself into another shift. You want trust back, start with numbers."

"Text me where you're sleeping tonight," she said. "Street address, not vibes."

The line clicked dead.

He stood under the overhang with the silent phone in his hand until the screen dimmed to black.

Then he lifted the trash-can lid and dropped it in.

The sound was smaller than he expected, just plastic against plastic, then a thud as it hit old bottles under the liner.

There was no thunder. There was no cinematic relief. There was only garbage smell and traffic.

He sat in the car with the new flip phone box in his lap and peeled the cardboard seam with his thumbnail until it split. The cheap plastic phone inside looked like something from fifteen years ago, tiny screen, rubber keypad, battery wrapped in static-crinkled film.

He assembled it in order because order still worked: battery, back cover, SIM, hold power key, wait for bars.

A startup chime played thin and cheerful.

He went through activation prompts one by one.

Preferred name?

IAN

Backup email?

SKIP

Emergency contacts?

SKIP

Voicemail greeting?

He stared at the blinking cursor and said, "You’ve reached Ian. Leave it," then stopped recording before his own voice could fully settle into the lie.

He copied three numbers from memory and the old call log he had photographed in the parking lot before throwing the smartphone away: Nora, Theo, the hospital HR main line. He hovered over June’s number and then put the phone down.

A storm smell rose from the west, hot dust turning metallic before rain. The first drops ticked against the windshield and spread dark circles in the dust.

He texted Nora from the new number.

This is me. Old phone broke. I’m at Richmond BP now and headed toward Indy. I’ll send motel address tonight.

The dots appeared quickly, then stopped, then came back.

If this is really you, I sent this number to Theo. I'm not calling HR again. Text June first if you contact her.

Another message landed before he could answer.

Is this actually you? Mom said your phone "broke." Did it break or did you throw it away.

He read June's line twice and bit the inside of his cheek until he tasted iron.

Rain came harder as he started the engine and pulled back onto the road, loud on the roof, wipers fighting to clear a windshield coated in dead bugs and road film. The flip phone sat in the cup holder like a toy, like proof, like an exit ramp he had already taken.

Mile marker 149 slipped past. He clicked his signal and let it blink three times before he canceled it and stayed westbound.

Mile marker 162 rose at another interchange; wet asphalt and hot brakes bled through the vents, and he kept straight.

Evening bruised over Indiana cornfields by mile marker 177, June's message still open in the cup holder, and he quit pretending there was a schedule for turning back.
By the time midnight rolled across the dash clock, he was still westbound.
