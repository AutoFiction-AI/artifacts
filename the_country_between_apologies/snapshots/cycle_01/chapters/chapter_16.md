# Chapter 16

The roomette looked smaller after dark than it had on the platform in Reno, like the walls had inched inward once the train left the station lights. Eli sat sideways on the narrow seat with his bad rib turned toward the window and the green folder open across his knees. The fold-down table trembled with every joint in the track. The plastic cup of water on it kept rippling as if someone were tapping a finger against the rim.

He had labeled three envelopes in block letters before boarding: `CASH`, `RECEIPTS`, `DO NOT SAY THIS`. The last one already had five torn pages in it. He had started three different versions of the same sentence in his notebook and crossed all of them out hard enough to scar the paper. The train swayed left. His pen ran off the line. He wrote one more opening anyway.

Nora, I know this sounds rehearsed.

He tore that page out too.

If he started with how it sounded, he was already acting. If he started with why he left, he was already bargaining. He pressed his thumb into the old crack in the folder spine and looked at the receipts instead: two MoneyGram stubs from the grocery counter on South Virginia, one payroll cash-out slip from the casino office, and one handwritten list of dates Theo had made him keep. Each slip carried a date, amount, method, and confirmation number. Nothing lyrical fit there, and no room for mood remained.

A knock came against the sliding door. The car attendant, Randi, leaned in with the step stool tucked under one arm. She wore a denim vest over the railroad shirt and a brass pin shaped like a trout on the collar. Her left thumbnail was painted electric blue, the others plain, and she spoke with the calm patience of somebody who had explained the same five things for twenty years.

"You want me to make this down now, or you waiting till after Denver?"

"After Denver," Eli said. "If that's okay."

"It's your room. I'm just the person with the blankets." She glanced at the folder and then back at him, not nosy, just clocking. "Dinner call's in twenty. Don't skip it. People skip and then buy stale chips at midnight and get mad at life."

He almost smiled. "Yes, ma'am."

"Don't ma'am me, you'll age me. Randi."

She slid the door shut with her hip and moved on, keys clicking softly at her belt.

He put the folder back in his duffel and tested the flip phone. The signal showed one bar, dropped to none, and then climbed back to one. It came and went with the cuts in the canyon wall. By the time the train bent along the Truckee River and the sky turned a bruised purple over the ridges, he had drafted and deleted six texts to Theo that all sounded like he wanted a medal for doing arithmetic.

In the dining car he sat across from a retired school superintendent from Sacramento who had brought her own tea bags in a sandwich bag and kept apologizing for the way the car rocked when she reached for salt. Her name was Lenore. She had thick white braids coiled at the base of her neck and a small notebook full of birds she'd seen from train windows over the years, each one dated and written in tiny block script.

"People say fly and get it over with," she said, stirring tea with a wrapped straw. "But then you miss where one landscape turns into another. That's the whole point, isn't it?"

Eli cut into chicken that tasted mostly of steam and pepper and said, "Maybe."

"You're heading home or away?"

The question was gentle and direct enough to hurt. He lifted his water glass and watched it shiver in his hand with the rhythm of the wheels.

"Home," he said. "This time for real."

Lenore nodded as if she'd heard the weight under the sentence and wasn't going to make him unpack it for a stranger over train chicken. She told him about a grandson in Boise who refused to wear coats because he said coats made him slower in kickball. She worried aloud that winter was going to teach him a lesson the hard way, then laughed at herself for worrying in advance. By the time dessert came she had listed three kinds of hawks she hoped to spot crossing the plains.

The ordinary talk steadied him for an hour. Then it left him worse.

Back in the roomette he climbed into the upper berth because the lower one made his hip cramp, and then immediately regretted it when his rib scraped the ladder edge. He lay on his back with the curtain half-open and listened to the rail seam beat beneath him: ta-tunk, ta-tunk, ta-tunk. Every impact moved through the thin mattress and into bone. Sleep came in shallow pieces and dropped out whenever the train slowed for a siding.

Near midnight, outside Salt Lake, the signal finally held long enough for a call. Theo picked up on the second ring.

"You all right?" Theo asked. He sounded winded, as if he'd just come up stairs.

"Yeah. On the train. I got bars for maybe three minutes if we're lucky."

"Then don't do preamble. What moved?"

Eli sat up, knees knocking the berth wall, and read from the receipt like he was taking inventory in a storeroom. "First transfer was one thousand four hundred at sixteen-oh-nine Nevada time. Reference ends in three-six-nine-one. Second was eight hundred sixty at sixteen-forty-two. Ends in two-two-seven-four. Plus four hundred from the payroll card cash-out. That one's pending till morning."

Theo was quiet long enough for Eli to hear the click of a lighter in the background, then the scrape of it closing without lighting. Theo had quit smoking three years ago and still carried the same red Bic in his pocket like a superstition.

"Okay," Theo said. "I can pick the first two tonight if the desk guy doesn't act like a cop. Third one I'll grab before shift. You keeping anything on you besides ticket money?"

"Two hundred cash. Maybe one-eighty by Chicago if meals run weird."

"Keep it. Don't get noble on me and show up broke."

"I'm not trying to be noble."

"Good. Because nobody asked for noble."

The train dipped; the call crackled.

Theo went on, voice tighter now, businesslike in the way he got when he was trying not to slide into old fights. "Once I pick up, I move all of it to Mom. Same night. I'll send her confirmations and cc you the numbers. You don't need to narrate over it. Let the receipts talk."

"Yeah."

"And Eli?"

He had not called him Dad in months. Eli understood that was not punishment now. It was accuracy.

"Yeah," he said again.

"When you walk in that house, don't walk in with a speech. Walk in with facts and shut up long enough for people to decide what they can do with you."

The line hissed and then cleared.

"I know," Eli said.

"No, you know it in theory. I need you to know it in your mouth."

Eli closed his eyes. The curtain smelled faintly of bleach and old dust.

"I hear you."

"All right. Train dies on me, I'm not calling back till morning. Text when you can."

Theo hung up first.

Eli stayed in the berth and listened to the wheels until they blurred into a low metallic roar, like somebody dragging chains across concrete far away. He had spent weeks telling himself he would return with proof. The proof was coming together: timestamps, transfer IDs, balance remaining. The part that made his palms sweat was the other ledger, the one no app could print.

He climbed down, opened the folder again, and wrote a new page without crossing out anything.

What I did:

I took $14,000 from emergency funds before I left.

I disappeared for ten weeks.

I had sex with someone while still married.

What I moved back before arrival:

$2,660 tonight through Theo, with confirmations.

Then he stopped because the number looked small next to the first line, and because writing someone while still married made his stomach turn at how neat it sounded. He scratched out someone and wrote Marisol. Then he capped the pen and pressed his forehead against the cool window until the vibration numbed his skin.

At dawn the train climbed into a stretch of snow-dusted cut banks and pale grass. He washed at the metal sink down the hall, bracing one hand against the wall when the car pitched. In the mirror his healing scar made a pink hook near his temple, and the bruise-shadow under his left eye still rose when he was tired. He looked like a man who had been in a fight he started and then lost halfway through.

Randi was in the corridor folding used towels into a blue cart with geometric precision, corners lined up like she was setting bricks.

"You sleep any?" she asked.

"Some," Eli said.

"That's all anybody gets out here. Coffee's fresh for another fifteen minutes, then it turns into a punishment."

He took the warning and followed the smell to the service alcove.

By midday, crossing into open country where the horizon flattened and refused to move no matter how long he stared, the phone held signal long enough for texts.

Theo: Picked up both. Third cleared. Sending all now.

Then, a minute later:

Theo: Sent 2660 to Mom from my account. Receipt numbers 88417 and 88421. Emailed PDFs to her + me.

Another minute:

Theo: She replied "received." That's the word. Nothing else.

Eli read the three messages twice, then copied the receipt numbers by hand into the folder. He did not answer for a full ten minutes because every response he drafted sounded like either apology theater or a request for emotional labor Theo had not offered.

Finally he sent: Thank you. Keep me on calendar. I land tomorrow evening.

Theo wrote back: Already did.

There was no heart emoji, no good luck, and no father-son script. The clean edges of it made Eli trust it.

The harder call sat under all of that like a live wire.

He put it off through Nebraska and into Iowa, through a late lunch where he could not taste the tomato soup, through a station stop where people smoked in tight coats under a hard blue sky and checked their watches as if time might be bargained with. By sunset he had opened Marisol's contact eight times and closed it eight times.

In the motel office near Glenwood Springs she had kept a chipped yellow bowl on the desk for loose keys and aspirin packets, and she always turned the deadbolt with two sharp clicks before counting the cash drawer. Those were practical habits, and none of them were mystical. The first night he slept there he woke to sulfur from the springs and bleach from the linen room and heard her in the hall laughing once, low and rough, at some story from the grounds crew. The second month she stopped laughing that way around him.

When the train hit a long straight run after dark and the car settled into a steadier roll, he took the phone to the vestibule between cars, where the metal plates flexed under his boots and cold air leaked through the seam in a thin knife.

She answered on the fourth ring.

"Hey," she said. Her voice was flatter than usual, tired. He could hear a television in the background and the soft hollow thunk of an ice machine dumping cubes. "You still moving east?"

"Yeah. Somewhere in Iowa."

"I hate that state on principle. Too many billboards for God and fireworks."

He almost laughed and didn't. "You at the desk?"

"Laundry room. Night clerk called out. I'm doing both. What do you need, Eli?"

There it was, clean and unsentimental. No invitation to warm up. He gripped the metal rail. The cold went straight into his palm.

"I wanted to tell you the money went through Theo tonight. Two transfers and a payroll cash-out. He sent receipts to Nora."

"That's logistics. I asked what you need."

The train lurched. He widened his stance and felt his rib protest like a thumb driven into a bruise.

"I needed to tell you I wasn't dodging," he said. "About leaving. About us."

"You're still doing the long runway," Marisol said. She was quiet, which was how she got when she was done tolerating spin. "Say the sentence."

He stared through the dark glass at his own reflection, a ghost in a cracked window.

"I'm not coming back," he said. "I'm ending this. I'm not asking you to wait, and I'm not going to call like we're half-together in different states."

In the pause he heard the squeal of a laundry cart wheel through her side of the line, then her breath moving once, deliberate.

"Okay," she said.

The word landed harder than if she had cursed him.

"Okay," he echoed, because he had nothing better.

"Don't mirror me," she said. "Talk like yourself."

He let out air and tried again. "I care about you. I did then and I do now. But I used this to hide from what I blew up at home, and that's on me. I can't keep taking from you because it feels better than the truth."

"That's closer," she said. "Still polished, but closer."

He waited. The train roared over a bridge. Wind hammered the vestibule door.

"You want me to make you feel clean before you get there?" she asked.

"No."

"Good, because I won't."

He pressed his knuckles to the rail until the skin blanched.

"Are you okay?" he asked, then hated himself for it as soon as the words left.

"No," she said. "I'm pissed and tired and my forearm's on fire because the press jammed again and I pulled a sheet wrong. But I'll be okay. Those are different answers."

She had always been precise that way, specific before comfort.

"You going to tell her about me?" Marisol asked.

"Yes."

"When?"

"Before I start pretending I'm fixed."

"That's not a date."

"When I get in."

"Good." Another pause, softer now. "I didn't ask for forever from you. I asked for plain. We got there late, but we got there."

A door banged somewhere behind her. She muffled the phone and cursed at somebody in Spanish, sharp and efficient, then came back.

"I gotta go reset a machine before it floods."

"Marisol-"

"No," she said. Not cruel, just final. "Don't do a last speech. You already did the part that mattered."

He closed his mouth.

"Take that sulfur smell out of your jacket before you walk into your house," she said. "Nora will clock it in one second."

Then she was gone.

He stayed in the vestibule until his fingers went numb from the leaking cold and the phone screen dimmed to black. He had expected relief and got something heavier, not dramatic, just weight: the weight of no longer being able to pretend the affair was a weather system that happened to him. He had chosen it. He had ended it. Both sat in the same hand.

Back in the roomette he lowered himself onto the seat, careful with the rib, and opened the notebook to a clean page.

Do not give speeches.

State facts.

Answer direct questions directly.

Do not ask for immediate forgiveness.

Do not ask June to manage your guilt.

Disclose Marisol without euphemism.

He read the list once and left it unadorned.

Outside the window the dark plains carried scattered farm lights like dropped screws on black felt. The train horn blew long and low at crossings, a sound that seemed too big for the narrow track it came from.

He slept better after that, not deep, but in longer stretches. In one dream he was back in the casino laundry with lint stuck to sweat on his forearms, shoving hot sheets into a press that kept spitting them out folded wrong. Mateo stood at the far table laughing at something Eli could not hear. Then the laugh cut off and the machine alarm started, and he woke with his hand clenched around the blanket seam.

Morning outside Chicago came gray and damp, freight yards and brick backsides of warehouses streaked with old rain. Passengers lined up in the aisle with backpacks and neck pillows, everybody impatient in polite American ways. Randi moved through them with her key ring and her trout pin and said, "We'll all get there if we let each other breathe," to no one in particular and everyone at once.

At Union Station he had forty minutes before the eastbound connection. He bought ibuprofen, two bananas, and a bottle of water that cost more than it should have. In the waiting area he sat beneath a mural of steel workers and opened his email at a kiosk terminal with a temporary code Theo had texted.

He had three new messages.

From Theo: PDF attached, transfer confirmations. Timestamped 9:14 p.m. and 9:22 p.m. CST.

From Nora: Received.

From Theo again: I put both receipts in the shared folder too. Don't lose your paper copies.

That was it. Nobody asked where he was. Nobody welcomed him. Nobody threatened him either. It was a narrow bridge with nothing underneath.

On the second train, somewhere east of South Bend, he finally texted Nora with arrival information and no framing.

Train 43 due 6:40 p.m. tomorrow if on time. I have the receipts in hand and numbers logged. I can wait at station or get a motel if that works better.

The typing dots appeared, disappeared, appeared again.

Nora: Come to the house after 8. June has class and shift. We'll talk when I'm back from work.

Then, after a pause:

Nora: Bring every document.

He typed yes and changed it to Okay.

The rest of Indiana and Ohio passed in rain bands that blurred the glass and turned field drainage ditches into strips of tin. He watched wipers on passing trucks beat frantic time and thought about how long he had spent trying to find a sentence that could make damage feel smaller. There wasn't one. There was only sequence. First he would arrive. Then he would answer what was asked. Then he would keep sending what he could send, every week, whether anyone thanked him or not.

In the dining car that evening a kid in a Notre Dame hoodie dropped a fork and swore softly; his mother laughed and handed him a napkin, and the kid pretended he was too old to need help. Eli looked away because the moment was ordinary and unbearable in equal measure.

After lights-out he lay in the lower berth this time and let the ache in his side spread where it wanted. He did not rehearse. He followed the route in his head instead: old steel towns, river bends, the tunnel before Pittsburgh where the windows turned to mirrors and every passenger had to look at themselves for thirty seconds whether they wanted to or not.

Late the next afternoon the train slid along the Monongahela under a low white sky. He saw warehouses, graffitied retaining walls, church spires, and the familiar ragged edges of home. His phone buzzed once.

Theo: You in yet?

Eli: Ten minutes out.

Theo: Remember what I said.

Eli: I do.

He zipped the green folder into his duffel, checked for the receipts one more time, then stopped himself from checking again. On the platform at Pittsburgh the cold was wetter than out west, a river cold that climbed through shoes. People surged toward pickups and ride shares, toward hugs, toward nobody.

Eli stood still long enough to let the crowd pass around him. The train door folded shut behind his back with a pneumatic sigh. He adjusted the duffel strap off his sore shoulder and walked toward the station exit without calling anyone to come get him.
