# Chapter 7

Eli sat at Theo's kitchen table before sunrise with the atlas spread flat under both forearms, as if the thing might curl up and run if he let it breathe. The cover had worn bald at the corners. The spine crackled every time he pushed it open to the middle. Theo had marked pages with torn strips of painter's tape that looked like little blue tongues, and each strip carried a block-letter label in orange pencil: `CHEAP SLEEP`, `DAY LOT`, `DO NOT STOP HERE AFTER DARK`. Eli kept running his thumb over the groove the pencil had cut in the paper. It felt less like directions and more like a record of somebody else's mistakes.

The apartment still held last night's garlic and dish soap. In the next room, a radiator knocked as if someone was tapping a spoon inside it. Theo stood at the stove in a gray T-shirt and work pants, his sleeve tattoos half hidden in steam from the kettle. He had a peppermint toothpick tucked at the corner of his mouth, and he worried it to splinters when he was thinking. He did not look at Eli while he talked.

"I called Darnell on break," he said. "His cousin in Rifle says the framing crews still pick up guys in the lot behind the Sinclair. They pay same day if you don't screw around. You show up at five-thirty, not six. At six you're furniture."

Eli had a pen in his hand and the green folder open by his elbow. The folder held a tuition statement, a mortgage notice, and a printout of the transfer he had made out of the emergency account before he left, fourteen thousand in a clean row of digits that looked less criminal because they were typed. The numbers had not changed when he stared at them. He had $1,186 in cash left in an envelope under the passenger seat and a checking account he did not trust himself to open because each debit line read like a petty confession: gas at 11:42 p.m., motel ice fee, radiator flush, diner tip too high because he did not know what else to do with shame.

"Rifle's almost another thousand miles," he said.

"Nine-sixty if you don't wander." Theo set a mug near him. The coffee was black enough to shine. "You can hit Kansas City before lunch if you get out now. Then it's just highway and bad ideas."

Eli tried a smile and abandoned it. "I'm already in the bad-ideas phase."

Theo pointed with two fingers to the route he'd circled across Missouri and Kansas. "Then use one of mine for a change."

Imani came down the hallway tying her scarf around her neck, museum badge clipped to her bag, dark hair shaved close on one side and still wet on the other. She moved with the blunt efficiency of someone always five minutes behind and still calmer than everyone else. She slid a sandwich bag onto the table by Eli's hand. The bag held two hard-boiled eggs, a packet of salt, and half an apple wrapped in paper towel.

"You forget to eat when you're panicking," she said. "Don't do that and then call people."

He looked up at her. "Thank you."

"That was not a kindness speech," she said, already halfway to the door. "That was instructions."

Theo snorted through the toothpick. Imani kissed his cheek without breaking stride and left. A minute later the stairwell door banged, and then the apartment went quiet.

Eli turned back to the atlas. Theo's penciled circles hopped west in uneven loops: Columbia, Kansas City, Salina, Hays, Limon, then a heavy ring around Glenwood Springs with `HOTEL LEADS / SPRINGS TRAFFIC` written in the margin. Somebody had once spilled soda on the Colorado pages. The sugar had dried into a faint tack that made the paper drag against his wrist. He traced the line from St. Louis to the mountains and felt his chest sink the way it did in courtrooms and principal offices, rooms where men with clipboards told him what came next and called it choice.

"Call Mom before noon," Theo said. "Don't text. Call."

Eli nodded.

"And don't machine-gun June's phone. One call, maybe two. She'll answer when she wants." Theo finally faced him. His eyes were bloodshot from an overnight shift, but his voice stayed flat, almost gentle. "You don't get to flood her because you're scared. That's not repair. That's pressure."

Eli gripped the pen tighter. "I know."

"You say that a lot."

"I know that too." He looked down at the map because he could not hold Theo's face and his own history in one frame. "Nora said seventy-two hours."

"Then make a plan with a number on it. Not a paragraph." Theo tapped the edge of the folder. "What can you move in seventy-two hours?"

Eli heard himself answer before he had decided whether it was true. "Three grand."

Theo stared. The toothpick clicked against his teeth. "From where?"

"From work. From what I pick up in Colorado."

"That is not seventy-two-hour money and you know it." Theo pulled the toothpick out and snapped it in half. "Don't call her with bullshit math. If you can send four hundred, say four hundred. If you can send zero, say zero. She can survive bad numbers. She can't survive fiction."

Eli wanted to argue, because arguing still felt like agency, but nothing came up except a dry sound in his throat. Theo reached into the bowl by the door and handed him the flip phone charger wrapped with black tape where the wire had split.

"You left this in the outlet," Theo said. Then, quieter: "Check your brakes when you fuel up. I heard them whining when you parked."

Eli held the charger, nodded again, and hated himself for how often gratitude and humiliation arrived as the same sensation.

He carried his duffel down the stairs and out to the street where his sedan sat under a sycamore tree shedding bark in curled strips. The trunk smelled like old rubber and laundry detergent from a burst pod he had never cleaned out. He slid the atlas onto the passenger seat, tucked the green folder beside it, and sat for a second with both hands on the wheel before turning the key. The engine caught on the second try and settled into a rough idle. When he pulled away, Theo was at the second-floor window, arms folded, watching the car until it turned the corner.

By the time Eli hit the Poplar Street Bridge, sunlight had climbed onto the steel trusses and made each beam flare white. The Mississippi below looked thick enough to hold tire tracks. He merged west and called June on speaker with the phone balanced against the dash vent. The ring count rolled to voicemail.

"Hey, June. It's Dad." He tried to keep his voice casual, the tone he used when he asked whether she'd remembered shin guards or a lab notebook. "I'm heading out this morning. I wanted to hear your voice before class if you're up. Call me when you can, okay? No rush. I just... yeah. Love you."

He ended the message and let the silence flood back in. Truck tires drummed the bridge seams. A paper cup rolled in the passenger footwell and hit the seat rail every few seconds like a metronome built for irritation.

At the first fuel stop outside Columbia, he filled the tank to the nearest dollar and watched the numbers climb faster than he could tolerate: 48.00, 49.00, 50.00. The pump clicked off at 51.27. Inside, the station's fluorescent lights flattened everyone's face to the same tired color. A toddler in a dinosaur hoodie slapped both palms on the slush machine and laughed every time his mother pulled him back. Eli bought stale peanut-butter crackers, black coffee that tasted like scorched wire, and a yellow legal pad because the receipt paper in his pocket already had too many false starts on it.

He sat on the curb by the air pump and wrote columns.

`cash on hand`
`fuel`
`food`
`possible deposit`

The math tightened and then collapsed. If he slept in the car and stopped eating like a normal adult and landed work in less than two days, he could maybe send twelve hundred by Thursday. Maybe. The number looked obscene and tiny at the same time.

His phone buzzed.

`June: i can't do phone right now`

Another bubble arrived before he could type.

`please stop calling back-to-back. i heard your message.`

The lowercase, the period at the end of message two and not message one, the way she called him back-to-back when it had been a single call. It all carried the same blunt precision she used as a kid when she caught him cheating at cards and laid the evidence out on the coffee table by suit and number. He typed and erased three replies, then sent: `Okay. I hear you. I'm here when you want.`

He got no response.

Back on the highway, he told himself he would leave it there. Twenty miles later he called again anyway, this time with both hands tight on the wheel and his eyes on the lane paint as if staying centered physically might keep him from slipping verbally.

The call went to voicemail.

"Hi, June, it's me again." His voice went formal without permission. "I just wanted to update you that I'm making progress westbound and looking at work leads. You don't need to call back immediately. I wanted you to have that information."

He heard himself sounding like a hospital memo and nearly hung up mid-sentence. "I'm sorry. That sounded weird. Ignore that part. Have a good class."

He ended the call and laughed once, a single hard bark that startled him more than the cars around him.

Past Boonville, rain swept across the interstate in thin diagonal sheets. Wipers squealed, skipped, caught. A lifted pickup drifted into his lane then corrected. Eli drove with his jaw set and the atlas half open on the passenger seat to Missouri, page edge fluttering in the fan air like it wanted to turn itself. He could feel the call to Nora waiting in the phone the way you could feel a tooth you needed pulled.

He took the Kansas City bypass and stopped at a truck plaza where diesel hung in the air like wet wool. The lot was full of idling semis, cabs vibrating in place. Inside, a bank of showers sat behind a counter with handwritten numbers on dry-erase squares. A man in steel-toe boots stood at the microwaves stirring canned chili with a plastic fork. Eli carried his coffee to a corner near the lottery machine and stared at Nora's contact until the screen dimmed.

When he called, she answered on the second ring.

"Where are you." She did not offer a greeting, and her tone did not even lift at the end.

"Outside Kansas City. Truck stop near the bypass."

He heard a metallic clank through her end, then the muffled rush of air that came when she pushed through a door. Hospital ventilation, he thought. Or maybe a stairwell. She had once told him the only place to cry at work was between floors five and six because the camera there had been broken for years.

"I've got nine minutes," she said. "I start meds at one. Let's do this clean."

Eli swallowed hot coffee and burned his tongue. "Okay."

"I need funds in the joint account within seventy-two hours. Seventy-two from now, not from yesterday's text. Thursday, three p.m. Eastern. If there is no transfer, my attorney files on Friday morning and asks for immediate temporary controls. Do you understand me."

"Yes."

"Say the number you're sending."

The station's PA squawked something about pump seven. Eli walked outside, into the truck noise, because he could not stand hearing himself lie under fluorescent light.

"Three thousand," he said.

She did not answer right away. The pause was small and absolute.

"From what job," she asked.

"Day labor in Colorado. Theo has leads in Rifle and Glenwood. Framing crews, cleanup, maybe hotel work. Cash same day if I can get on."

"If."

"Yeah."

"Then what I heard is not a plan. I heard weather and luck." Her voice stayed level, nurse voice, charting voice. "I'm not asking you to explain yourself to me emotionally right now. I'm asking for dates and amounts I can rely on."

"I can send something before Thursday."

"Something is not an amount."

He stared at a puddle stippled with diesel rainbow and watched trucks throw ripples across it as they rolled past. "Twelve hundred by Thursday," he said, each word feeling like a chipped tooth. "Then more once I get work."

"How much more and by when."

He pressed two fingers into the bridge of his nose. "I don't know yet."

"Then say you don't know. Don't sell me confidence you can't fund." A scrape on her end; maybe she shifted the phone against a locker door. "June has a tuition installment due. Mortgage is already bleeding fees. I can't keep patching this with overtime and grace periods while you improvise a pilgrimage."

The word hit harder than yelling would have.

"I'm not on a pilgrimage," he said.

"Then stop acting like one." For the first time her voice cracked around the edges, then steadied. "Have you talked to her."

"I called. She doesn't want to talk yet."

"Then stop leaving speeches. It scares her when she hears you sounding..." Nora exhaled. "It sounds like a goodbye every time."

Eli leaned against the cinder-block wall by the ice freezer and felt the cold from inside the machine through his shirt. "I'm trying to keep her in the loop."

"She's eighteen, not your project status report." Nora's words came quicker now. "You two can repair or not repair later. Right now I need money in the account and a text that confirms exact amount. Thursday by three."

"Okay."

"And Eli." She waited until he answered.

"Yeah."

"Don't disappear again if this fails. I can survive broke. I can survive furious. I'm done surviving unknown."

The line clicked dead before he could say anything that would not have sounded like theater.

He stood there with the phone still to his ear while a reefer unit throbbed behind him and spat warm air onto his calves. Somebody laughed by the pumps. A dog barked inside a cab. His coffee had gone cold enough to drink; he swallowed it anyway and tasted paper and burnt metal.

He took out the legal pad and wrote:

`thurs 3pm ET - 1200 minimum`
`text proof`
`no story`

He underlined `no story` twice until the pen tore the page.

An hour west of Kansas City, traffic thinned and the sky opened into a hard blue lid with clouds smeared flat near the horizon. Grain silos rose out of fields like concrete chess pieces. The highway sound settled into one long vowel under the tires. Eli called June again because he had promised himself not to and promises to himself had become the cheapest currency he had.

The call went to voicemail.

"Hey. It's Dad. You don't have to answer. I heard what you said." He kept his eyes on a billboard for fireworks and prayed for language that did not smell like damage control. "I talked to Mom. I'm working on getting money moved by Thursday afternoon. I'm headed to Colorado for day work. That's all I wanted to say."

He paused, listening to wind noise leak through the cracked window.

"I'm not asking you to fix anything," he added. "I know I broke enough."

He ended the call before he could keep going.

By late afternoon he crossed into Kansas under a state-line sign pocked with dead bugs. He pulled into a rest area outside Topeka where the vending machines hummed against a wall painted with a mural of sunflowers and bison. A retired couple sat on a bench sharing a bag of grapes, each one torn in half first so the other could have the larger side. Eli ate one egg from Imani's bag and read the atlas again.

Theo's handwriting in the margins got tighter the farther west the pages went, as if he had pressed harder trying to force clarity into a map. Next to Limon he had written `WIND WILL KICK YOUR ASS`. Beside a small circle around a town called Parachute: `Ask for union board postings at library.` Near Glenwood Springs: `Motel district / hot springs maintenance / roof crews after hail.` At the top of the page, in smaller print: `If it feels like punishment, do it anyway. Just don't call it purpose.`

Eli stared at that sentence until the letters blurred. Theo had written it for him or for himself years ago; either way it landed like a sentence handed down in a room with a flag and no windows.

His phone buzzed again.

`June: stop narrating your route to me`

Then another line arrived.

`if u want to talk, ask a question and wait`

He sat with the message lit in his hand. Ask a question and wait. She was still teaching him, even now.

He typed: `Okay. Are you eating enough?`

He deleted it.

He typed: `What class was today?`

He deleted that one too.

He finally sent: `Do you want me to stop calling for now?`

No answer came after two minutes, then ten, then fifteen. He watched a thunderhead build in the distance and throw shadow over the parking lot.

When he got back on the road, he kept the phone face down on the passenger seat and did not touch it for almost an hour. Then it vibrated once, skittering toward the atlas.

`June: yes. for now. text only if money/timeline changes`

He felt the message like a hand on the center of his chest, firm and not gentle. He called anyway before his better self could intervene. Straight to voicemail.

"Hi, June. This is Dad." The phrase sounded absurd and bureaucratic, and he hated that he kept saying it. "Received your text. I will stop calling. I will update only on money and timeline changes."

He heard his own breath catch and forced the rest out in one line. "I love you and I'll shut up now."

He hung up and smacked the heel of his hand against the steering wheel once, hard enough to sting. It was not rage, not exactly. It felt more like trying to reset a machine that kept jamming.

Dusk gathered in layers, orange leaking down behind feedlots and water towers, then gray taking everything back. He passed a sign for a motel with WEEKLY RATES painted in red on white plywood and considered stopping. If he slept six hours he could still reach eastern Colorado by late morning and hit any labor lot before dawn the next day. He took the exit, rolled to the stop sign, then turned right back onto the interstate without looking at the motel office.

Motion still felt safer than rooms.

By nine-thirty he was west of Salina with truck traffic rising again, convoys of headlights and spray. The radio gave weather in clipped county names and warned of high crosswinds along I-70 into Colorado. Eli drove with the window cracked so cold air could keep him sharp. It smelled of cut hay, hot asphalt, and diesel. His lower back ached. The old stiffness in his right knee throbbed every time he eased off the gas.

At a service plaza near Hays, he filled up and stepped out into wind that yanked his shirt against his ribs. A blue sign on the ramp listed the next rest area in twenty-six miles, then another in eighty-four. Another sign, smaller, warned `TRUCK BRAKE CHECK RECOMMENDED - MOUNTAIN CORRIDOR AHEAD.` Eli read it, nodded as if he'd agreed to something, and kept walking.

Inside, he bought two energy drinks, jerky he could barely afford, and a prepaid phone card even though he did not need one yet. The cashier wore a Cardinals cap and had nicotine patches visible under each sleeve cuff. He slid Eli's card back and said, "You look beat, man. There's a sleep lot two exits up."

"Yeah," Eli said. "I'll get it later."

The man shrugged. "Everybody says that right before they start talking to road signs."

Back in the car, Eli opened the atlas across the steering wheel. Under the dome light the paper looked almost pink. The route from where he sat to Glenwood Springs cut in a thick westbound line through places he had never meant to know by name. It did not fork toward home anywhere until after the mountains, after weather, after whatever cash he could scrape out of strangers. He ran a finger along it and thought of handcuffs, then corrected himself because that was melodramatic and he knew better. It was not a cuff. It was a bill due.

He wrote one more note on the legal pad and wedged it above the instrument cluster.

`THURSDAY 3 PM ET`
`SEND 1200`
`TEXT PROOF`

He pulled out, merged, and pointed the hood toward the dark.

Near midnight, the road emptied enough that his headlights seemed to float in their own tunnel. Wind shoved the car sideways in long slow pulses. He drank warm energy drink and tasted fake cherry and aluminum. Twice he drifted onto the rumble strip and jerked back. He told himself he would stop at the next rest area, then the next, then after one more song.

His phone lit up with Nora's name but no call came through, only a text.

`Need confirmation by noon tomorrow that you have a job lead with start time. No speeches.`

He could have answered honest: no lead, no start time, just highway. Instead he typed, `Working on it now. Will confirm by noon.` He hit send before he could edit the sentence into truth.

Fifteen minutes later the brake pedal gave a soft, spongy inch before it caught. The change was small, but it was there. He pumped it once at an exit ramp and felt it firm up. On the shoulder a reflective sign flashed past: `STEEP GRADES WEST OF LIMON - CHECK BRAKES.` He registered the words one by one, too late to do anything with them. The sign was in the mirror before his brain filed it under urgent.

He gripped the wheel tighter and rolled his shoulders to wake up. The atlas had slid open to Colorado on the passenger seat. Theo's penciled circles glowed pale under the dash light, each one a little target he had promised to hit and did not yet know how to survive.

At 12:37 a.m. he crossed the state line with the tank half full, forty-eight dollars in his wallet, just over eight hundred folded in the envelope under his seat, a legal deadline in his windshield, and a promise already starting to crack. The highway rose almost imperceptibly toward black country he could not see. Behind him Missouri and Kansas flattened into memory. Ahead, the grade steepened.

He did not take the next rest exit.
