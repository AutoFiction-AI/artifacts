# Chapter 5

By the time Eli crossed the river and came down into south St. Louis, the patched radiator had started its thin kettle whine again, a high note under the engine that he could feel through the steering wheel. The sedan smelled like old coffee and the sweet chemical edge of coolant. He drove with both windows cracked because he did not trust the gauge and because the air inside the car had turned stale with his own shirt and fear.

Theo's block sat under a row of flat-roof brick buildings with narrow porches and dented mailboxes bolted crooked at shoulder height. Somebody had parked a rusting grill half on the sidewalk, half in the dirt strip where grass used to be. A television thumped through an open downstairs window in quick bursts of crowd noise and ad jingles. Eli rolled past once, then circled again because he had missed the number and because he had still not decided what words he was going to use.

He parked in front of a fire hydrant anyway. He told himself it was two minutes. He shut the engine off and sat with his hands on the wheel while the metal clicked and cooled. Nora's message thread was open on his burner. The last text from her had come at three in the afternoon.

`You do not get to disappear and then call this confusion. Return funds today.`

He could have answered. He had answered versions of that message in his head for two days, each version sounding calm and practical until he looked at the numbers in his green folder and watched calm collapse into arithmetic. Mortgage arrears. June's tuition bill. The transfer he had made from emergency savings before he left, fourteen thousand dollars moved in twelve taps while Nora was on an overnight ICU shift and June was packing dish soap and twin XL sheets.

He locked the car, took the folder, and left his duffel in the trunk. If Theo shut the door in his face, Eli did not want to stand on the sidewalk holding everything he had like a man evicted before he even asked.

The stairwell smelled like hot dust and someone else's fried fish. Paint peeled from the handrail in curled strips that lifted against his palm. By the second landing his right knee had started its familiar complaint, the old stiffness from years of pretending office chairs and conference rooms did not count as injury. He paused at apartment 2B and listened. He heard a sink running, then a cabinet closing hard.

He knocked once. He waited. He knocked again, louder.

The door opened to the chain stop. Theo looked through the gap first, one eye and half his face, then unlatched the chain and pulled it wider with his bare foot braced against the jamb.

Theo had gotten wider across the chest since Eli had seen him last spring at Nora's birthday dinner. The sleeve tattoos climbed both forearms in dense black and muted green, florals tangled with block lettering Eli had never asked about. His hair was clipped short on the sides. He wore a faded Local 304 shirt and gray work pants with something dark ground into one knee. A square of nicotine gum bulged his cheek while he looked Eli over from shoes to hairline like he was checking a pallet before accepting delivery.

"You lost?" Theo said.

Eli tried to smile and felt it miss his face. "No. I came to see you."

"At nine-thirty on a Tuesday with no call?" Theo glanced past him to the stairwell. "Where's your car?"

"Out front."

"You drove here from Pittsburgh."

"I drove here from Indiana." Eli shifted the folder from one hand to the other. "Can I come in for a minute?"

Theo kept his hand on the doorknob. He did not move. "Mom said you vanished four days ago. She also said you took fourteen grand before you bailed."

Eli looked down at the threshold strip, the brass worn dull in the center where shoes hit. "I know what she told you."

"Yeah?"

"Most of it's true." He forced himself to look back up. "I need a place to sleep for two nights. That's what I'm asking."

Theo did not answer. The sink in the apartment shut off. A woman called from deeper inside, "T, who is it?"

Theo said, without taking his eyes off Eli, "It's my dad."

The pause from inside stretched long enough for Eli to hear the refrigerator compressor kick on. Then the woman said, "Do you want me out here?"

"Yeah."

Theo opened the door another two inches but still blocked most of it with his body. "You don't get to walk in and do a reunion scene. You hear me?"

"I'm not here for that."

"Good." Theo stepped back one pace. "Then come in and tell the truth fast."

The apartment was narrow and clean in the way clean gets when two people have jobs and no spare time. Shoes lined one wall in matched pairs under a shelf of books and folded grocery totes. A fan clicked in the living room window. The couch was low and gray, one arm patched with black thread in tidy stitches. A wooden crate beside it held a stack of library books, three coasters, and a mason jar full of loose change arranged by coin type. Quarters in one row, dimes in another.

Imani stood at the edge of the kitchen with a dish towel in one hand. Her sidecut was grown out a little, tight curls clipped back with a copper barrette. She wore wire-frame glasses that kept sliding down her nose, and every time they dropped she pushed them up with the back of her wrist so the dishwater would not smear the lenses. A legal pad lay open on the counter under a mug. Eli could see three highlighted lines and a sticky note that said `fellowship app - submit Fri 11:59 PM` in block capitals.

"Hi, Mr. Mercer," she said. Her tone was polite and level, but she did not smile. "Do you need water?"

He nodded once. "Thanks. Eli is fine."

She filled a glass from the tap and set it on the table. Theo stayed standing while Eli sat, which made Eli feel like he had asked for a bank loan and was waiting for terms.

Theo said, "Start with where you've been. Then tell us why you came here before going home."

Eli drank half the water too fast and put the glass down carefully. "I dropped June off. I started back. I couldn't do it. I kept driving. I had a radiator blow on the west side of Indianapolis. I worked a kitchen shift for cash to cover part of the repair."

"You worked a kitchen shift," Theo repeated.

"At a diner by the tow yard."

"Okay. Keep going."

Eli pressed his thumb into the wet ring under the glass. "I thought if I kept moving I could get my head straight before I talked to anybody. That was dumb. I know that."

Theo gave one short laugh that had no humor in it. "You thought disappearing was strategy."

"I thought I had twenty-four hours. Then it turned into more." He looked at Imani. "I did buy a burner. I didn't want location apps on me."

"You didn't want accountability on you," Theo said.

"That too." Eli rubbed his forehead and stopped when he felt sweat and road grit come away on his fingers. "I took money before I left. I planned to put it back quick."

Theo's jaw worked. He spit the gum into a napkin, folded it, and set it down with precise little presses of his thumb as if neatness could keep him from yelling.

"How much do you have right now?" Theo asked.

"On me?"

"Total."

"Cash and bank, about twelve-six." Eli corrected himself. "Twelve thousand six hundred."

Imani spoke for the first time since the water. "The transfer was fourteen."

"I know." Eli looked at the legal pad and then away. "I covered repairs and gas and motel nights." He pulled the green folder out and put it on the table. "I've got receipts."

Theo leaned one hand on the chair back across from Eli and looked down at the folder but did not touch it. "You brought proof before you brought an apology."

"I brought both."

"I don't hear one yet."

Eli kept his hands flat on the table. He had rehearsed this part in parking lots and truck-stop bathrooms and while staring at motel ceilings that hummed with cheap fluorescent light. Every rehearsal had him sounding composed. Sitting in Theo's kitchen, he heard the manager voice come out anyway, the one that lined facts in neat rows to keep panic from showing.

"I'm sorry I left without telling anybody. I'm sorry I moved the money. I'm sorry I put Mom in this position with tuition and the mortgage due." He stopped and swallowed. "I'm asking for two nights. I need to sleep somewhere and figure out how to put the cash back by Friday."

Theo crossed his arms. "Don't use her deadlines to make this sound noble."

"I'm not."

"You did this."

"I know I did."

"Do you?"

The word landed harder than the shout Eli had braced for. He looked at his son, at the scar under Theo's chin from a bike crash at ten, at the fresh burn mark on Theo's forearm that looked like a forklift battery incident, at the five-year sobriety medallion hanging from a nail by the fridge with his keys. Theo noticed where he was looking and stepped so his shoulder blocked the medallion from view.

"Don't do that," Theo said.

"Do what?"

"Don't look at that chip like it proves you're part of it."

Imani lifted the dish towel and folded it over once. "Theo."

"No, let him hear it." Theo looked back at Eli. "You want to know what this feels like? It feels familiar."

Eli's neck tightened against his collar. "Theo."

"No. You're going to listen now." Theo pulled out the chair and sat down hard, knees wide, hands flat. "You keep saying this week got away from you. Like weather. Like traffic. Like a calendar accident. But I've got a date for this exact move." He pointed at Eli with two fingers. "Saturday, July seventeenth, 2021. Family session at St. Bart's Recovery, South County, ten a.m."

Eli stared at him. The room narrowed, fan click and refrigerator hum growing louder while everything else dropped out.

"I was twenty-two," Theo said. "Three weeks sober and still shaking in group because my body hadn't decided what to do without vodka. Counselor says family session, bring one parent minimum. Mom had a double at Barnes and couldn't get off. You said you'd come. You texted at nine-forty that you were parking."

Eli remembered typing in the hospital garage with his hazard lights blinking, remembered deciding he had fifteen minutes to fix one compliance report before he walked into the room where his son would ask him for things Eli did not know how to give. He remembered opening the report, then another email, then another.

Theo kept going, voice flat now, almost gentle in the way hard facts can be when anger gets tired of carrying itself. "At ten-thirty, counselor asks if we should start without you. I said no, he's coming. At eleven, she asks again. I said traffic's bad. At eleven-thirty I went to the vending machine and called you from the hallway because my phone got locked in meds storage. You answered. I heard your office printer in the background."

Imani looked down at the table. Eli did not move.

"You said, and I remember this word for word, that you'd had a work emergency and we'd do a longer talk tomorrow. Tomorrow never happened. You sent me forty bucks on Venmo with a thumbs-up emoji at seven that night." Theo laughed again, short and bitter. "That was your fatherhood for the month. Forty dollars and a thumb."

Eli breathed in through his nose and smelled dish soap, hot onion from somebody else's dinner downstairs, and the metallic tang of his own sweat dried into his shirt. He felt every inch of the chair under him, the press of cheap plywood through his jeans, the wobble in one back leg.

"I remember the Venmo," he said.

"You don't get points for remembering the worst part."

"I remember all of it." Eli's voice roughened on the last word. "I remember I sat in my car for twenty minutes outside that building and couldn't make myself come in because I knew you were going to tell the room what I already knew. That I'd spent years calling you lazy and dramatic when you were sick. I was a coward."

Theo's eyes narrowed, suspicious of any sentence that sounded too tidy.

Eli kept going before he could retreat into explanation. "I told myself work was urgent. It wasn't. I told myself if I missed one session it wouldn't matter. It mattered. I made your rehab about my embarrassment."

Silence settled across the table. The fan clicked, clicked, clicked.

Imani took her glasses off and polished one lens with the hem of her T-shirt, buying everyone ten seconds. "Thank you for saying it straight," she said, putting the glasses back on. "We still need to decide what happens tonight."

Theo looked at her, then at Eli, then at the folder. "How many drinks have you had today?"

"None."

"Yesterday?"

"None."

Theo waited.

"I had two beers Sunday night in Indiana," Eli said. "I haven't had anything since."

Theo nodded once. "I'm not running a breathalyzer checkpoint in my own living room. But I'm not doing surprises either."

"Understood."

Imani pulled the legal pad closer, flipped to a clean page, and wrote the date in the top right corner. Her handwriting was small and square, each letter separate as if she had trained herself against ambiguity. She clicked her pen twice.

"If you stay," she said, "it's two nights. Tonight and tomorrow. You leave by eight a.m. Thursday unless we all agree on something else."

Eli looked from her to Theo. Theo did not object.

"Okay," Eli said.

"Rule two," Imani said. "No alcohol in this apartment. No showing up smelling like a bar. No hidden bottles in the car."

"Agreed."

"Rule three. Quiet hours after ten-thirty because Theo clocks in at five and I've got class at eight. If you come in late, you text first and keep it silent."

"I can do that."

"Rule four. Chores are not symbolic." She tapped the pen against the pad. "You do dinner dishes tonight and tomorrow. You take trash and recycling down in the morning before seven-thirty. If laundry is in the basket by the bathroom, you fold it instead of stepping over it."

Eli blinked. "Laundry."

Imani met his eyes. "Yes. Laundry."

He gave one nod. "Okay."

Theo leaned back and watched Eli with that same intake expression, the way he used to inspect used tires when he was sixteen and spending weekends at the shop two blocks from Nora's cousin's place. "Rule five," Theo said. "No father speeches. No fixing me. No postmortem on my life. You answer direct questions direct. You don't perform remorse to get comfort."

"I won't."

"And you don't talk to Mom from here unless it's money logistics. If you want to do tears and history, take that outside." Theo looked toward the bedroom door. "I'm not making her hear you beg."

"Fair."

"Say it like a sentence," Theo said.

"It's fair."

Imani wrote while they talked, then turned the pad around so both men could see. She had listed each rule with checkboxes. At the bottom she had written, `If any rule breaks, stay ends immediately.`

Eli stared at the page and felt an old reflex rise in him, hot and stupid, the reflex that said parent means authority, father means exception, this is my son and I don't sign terms in his kitchen. The reflex that had ruined him in smaller installments for years before the interstate made the damage obvious.

He looked at Theo's face and saw no malice there, only a practiced caution Theo had paid dearly to learn.

"Do you want me to sign it?" Eli asked.

Imani said, "Yes."

He took the pen. His hand trembled once on the downstroke of the E. He signed and passed it to Theo.

Theo hesitated, then signed under him.

"I'm not doing this to humiliate you," Theo said.

Eli set the pen down. "I know."

"I'm doing it because the old version of you shows up when things get uncomfortable, and he breaks stuff."

"I know that too."

Imani folded the page and tucked it under the sugar canister by the stove. She checked the time on her phone and exhaled through her nose. "I've got to finish a grant statement in forty minutes and then read two articles I should've read yesterday," she said. "Theo, are we doing eggs or just toast?"

"Eggs," he said.

She moved to the fridge and opened it. The practical shift in her voice was so clean it startled Eli. Crisis to groceries in one breath. He realized that was what adulthood looked like in this apartment: not forgiveness, not melodrama, just sequence.

"You can shower," Theo told him. "Towel closet by the bathroom. Clean shirt if you need one is in the hall bin. If your knee's bad, take the nonslip mat off the rack and put it down."

The detail hit Eli harder than any insult had. He nodded and stood.

In the bathroom he turned the water on and watched it brown for two seconds before running clear. He peeled his shirt off and saw his own body in the mirror under yellow light: broad still, but softened at the waist; shoulders slumped; road grit at his collarbone; a bruise on his hip from crawling under the car two days ago to check the radiator hose clamp. He looked like a man who had slept in four different beds in as many nights and called that mobility instead of panic.

He stepped into the spray and let the water pound the back of his neck until the water heater started to fade and he had to turn it hotter. Through the vent he could hear Theo and Imani talking in low voices over clatter.

"You okay?" Imani asked.

"Yeah."

"That was a lot."

"I know."

"You don't owe him collapse to prove this matters."

"I'm not collapsing."

"Good. Salt's on your left."

Eli shut his eyes. He wanted to disappear into the tile and grout. Instead he rinsed, dried off, and put on one of Theo's extra T-shirts from the hall bin, a black shirt with a faded print for a long-closed punk venue on Cherokee Street. The cotton smelled like detergent and someone else's clean skin.

When he came back to the kitchen, Theo had scrambled eggs in a cast-iron pan and toast standing in a wire rack. Imani had her laptop open at one end of the table, reference articles spread around it with sticky tabs sticking out like yellow grass. She wore over-ear headphones around her neck but not on her ears, ready to put them on the second conversation turned circular.

Theo slid a plate toward Eli. "Eat. Then dishes."

"Thanks," Eli said.

He ate slower than he wanted to, forcing himself not to shovel food the way he had at the diner after his shift in Indiana. The eggs were soft and pepper-heavy. The toast had one burnt edge each slice. He swallowed and felt food settle into the hollow under his ribs where anxiety had been rattling all day.

Theo stood at the sink rinsing the pan. "What exactly is your plan for Thursday morning?"

Eli wiped his fingers and reached for the folder. "I can transfer five grand back by tomorrow afternoon if I keep enough to keep moving. Another three by end of week if I find day labor." He opened to a page of scribbled numbers and a gas receipt stapled in the corner. "I was thinking Kansas City first, then maybe Denver."

Theo did not turn around. "That sounds like drifting with a spreadsheet."

"Maybe." Eli set the paper down. "I don't have better yet."

"Then don't dress it up." Theo set the pan on the drying rack and faced him. "You can say you don't know."

"I don't know," Eli said.

"Better."

Imani looked up from her laptop. "If you call Nora tonight, keep it to what money moves when. She does not need your emotional weather report at ten p.m."

"I wasn't going to call tonight."

"Good." She returned to her screen, then paused. "Also, I know this is obvious, but I'm saying it anyway. Don't open my office door for any reason. That room is where my thesis notes are, and one dropped coffee would cost me six months."

"I won't touch it." He glanced at the closed door off the hall, a handwritten sign taped to it: `Please knock even if open.`

Theo sat down finally and started eating his own eggs cold. He kept glancing at the time on his phone between bites. Eli recognized the rhythm from years ago when Theo used to check basketball scores under the dinner table. Now the checks were for alarms and shift start and sponsor call windows.

"You still talking to Marcus?" Eli asked before he could stop himself.

Theo put his fork down. "That's close to father speech."

"It's a yes-no question."

"Yes. Thursday nights. He still pisses me off and he's still right most of the time." Theo lifted his plate. "Anything else that isn't about me?"

"No."

"Great. Dishes."

Eli stood and took plates to the sink. Hot water stung a split in the skin beside his thumbnail. He scrubbed the pan until the burnt egg released in black curls. Behind him, Imani's keyboard clicked in fast bursts. Theo disappeared into the bedroom and came back with a thin blanket and one flat pillow.

"Couch opens if you pull from the bar under the middle cushion," Theo said. "It gets stuck on the left side. Lift before you drag."

"Okay."

Theo set the blanket on the couch arm. "I leave at four-forty-five. If you're awake, fine. If not, don't fake it."

"I won't."

"And Eli?"

Eli looked up from the sink.

Theo leaned against the doorway, thumb rubbing the edge of his phone case, an old habit from when he was trying not to pick a fight in high school. "When you came to my graduation and left before the ceremony because you said parking was impossible, I believed you for years. Last month Nora told me you left because you had a panic attack in the lot and didn't want anyone to see."

Eli shut the faucet off.

Theo shrugged once, as if the motion itself embarrassed him. "I'm not saying that to make you small. I'm saying I can deal with ugly facts. I can't deal with sanitized ones."

Eli felt the towel rough against his knuckles as he dried his hands. "I hear you."

Theo held his gaze for one beat longer, then nodded and went to brush his teeth.

The apartment went into night mode in stages. Imani closed her laptop at eleven twelve and stretched both arms overhead until her shoulder popped. She said good night to Eli with a nod that was not warm but not hostile either, then carried her notes into the office and shut the door softly. Theo set three alarms on his phone, plugged it in on the floor, and disappeared into the bedroom with a final "Lock's automatic. Don't deadbolt unless you're sure I'm in." The bedroom door clicked.

Eli pulled the couch bed out halfway, got it stuck, remembered Theo's instruction, lifted left then dragged, and heard the metal frame settle with a clank that sounded too loud in the small room. He laid the blanket down and sat on the edge without taking his socks off.

Outside, a freight train wailed low and long from somewhere near the yards. He could hear a couple arguing on the sidewalk below, phrases rising clear then blurring as they moved down the block. The refrigerator cycled. Pipes ticked behind the wall. The apartment had no empty silence in it, only layers of other people's ongoing lives.

He took out his burner and opened Nora's thread again. He typed:

`I am in St. Louis with Theo. I can transfer 5000 tomorrow and more Friday.`

He stared at `I am` and deleted it.

`I'm in St. Louis at Theo's. I can transfer 5000 tomorrow and more Friday. I'll send confirmation when it clears.`

He read it twice, sent it, and set the phone face down.

Sleep did not come in one block. It came in narrow strips between noise: the upstairs toilet flushing at one a.m., a motorcycle revving too hard in second gear at one-thirty, Theo getting up before dawn and moving through the kitchen with practiced quiet that still sounded loud to Eli because every cabinet close announced another failure. At four-forty, Eli opened his eyes to the smell of coffee and saw Theo in the hall light, pulling on steel-toe boots.

"You up?" Theo whispered.

"Yeah."

"Trash goes down before seven-thirty. Bags are by the back door." Theo paused, then added, "Meeting tonight at six if you want one. I'm not babysitting you there, but it's open."

"I'll think about it," Eli said.

Theo nodded once. "Think faster than usual."

The door shut behind him. Eli lay back down and watched the ceiling fan shadow turn slowly across cracked plaster. He remembered Theo at eleven in a Cardinals cap too big for his head, asking Eli to stay and throw two more pitches in the alley because he'd finally started hitting the curve. Eli had said tomorrow, always tomorrow, because his phone kept buzzing and he believed urgency belonged to every outside demand before it belonged to the boy in front of him.

At six-thirty, Imani emerged in leggings and an oversized museum sweatshirt, hair damp at the temples from a rushed shower. She measured coffee grounds on a digital scale and frowned when the number overshot by two grams, scooping a little back into the jar with exact care.

"Morning," she said.

"Morning."

"Did you sleep?"

"Enough."

She glanced at the tied trash bags by the back door, then at the sink where Eli had already washed his mug. "Thank you for doing that without being reminded twice."

The line was dry but not cruel. Eli almost laughed. "Low bar."

"Bars exist for a reason." She poured water into the kettle and set it on the burner. "Theo texted me that he offered you a meeting."

"He did."

"You should go." She pulled her glasses from the counter and put them on. "Not because meetings are magic. Because your pattern is motion. Sit still somewhere with witnesses for one hour."

Eli watched steam begin to rise from the kettle spout. "You always this direct before seven?"

"Only with houseguests and grant deadlines." She checked her phone. "I've got a seminar in thirty and a desk shift at the museum at noon. I left pasta in the fridge. Use it. Don't buy takeout to avoid folding laundry."

He nodded. "Yes, ma'am."

She gave him a flat look. "Don't call me ma'am."

"Right. Sorry."

Imani packed her bag with mechanical efficiency: laptop sleeve, two highlighters, wallet, transit card, folded umbrella though the sky looked clear. At the door she stopped and turned back.

"One more thing," she said. "If Nora calls me, I'm not lying for you."

"You shouldn't."

"Good." She shouldered the bag. "Then we understand each other."

After she left, the apartment felt larger and less forgiving. Eli stripped the couch, folded blanket and pillow, and set them exactly where Theo had left them. He carried trash down the back stairs to a corral of dented bins that smelled of beer and rainwater. On the way up he paused at the second landing window and looked out over the alley.

Two kids were drawing with chalk on cracked concrete near a garage door. One had written `NO PARKING` in huge letters and then decorated the O with petals so it looked like a flower. A man in a paint-splattered hoodie smoked on a milk crate and watched them with the severe concentration of someone guarding a temporary peace.

Eli stood there longer than he meant to, one hand on the window frame where paint had bubbled from humidity. He did not feel absolved. He felt tired and cornered and, for the first time in days, locatable.

Back upstairs he opened the green folder and spread papers across the table the way he used to spread compliance binders before an audit. Mortgage notice. Tuition statement. Repair invoice from Indiana. ATM receipt showing a cash withdrawal he had convinced himself was a buffer and now read like theft. He wrote three transfer scenarios on the back of a grocery circular and crossed out two when the gas estimate did not hold.

At eight fifty-three his phone buzzed.

Nora: `Funds by 3 PM today. Confirm exact amount.`

No hello. No accusation. Just a deadline with a clock inside it.

Eli typed, deleted, typed again.

`I can send 5000 by 3. Confirmation screenshot immediately after.`

Nora: `Do it.`

He stared at that two-word reply and thought of every year he had mistaken Nora's efficiency for coldness when it had mostly been labor. Keeping everyone fed, schooled, insured, scheduled, and upright while he took comfort in being called dependable. Dependable until he was not.

He transferred the money. The app spun for fourteen seconds that felt like a mechanical test of character. When confirmation appeared, he sent the screenshot to Nora and another to Theo, then put the phone down before either could answer.

Near noon he folded laundry from the bathroom basket: Theo's work socks paired by thickness, Imani's T-shirts turned right side out, a towel with a frayed edge he smoothed flat three times because it kept bunching. The domesticity was humiliating only because he had spent years treating this kind of labor as invisible background instead of the thing that kept a house from becoming a pile.

At four-thirty Theo came home with forklift dust on his boots and a sweat-darkened collar. He dropped his lunch container in the sink and checked the sugar canister where Imani had stashed the rule sheet. It was still there.

"Mom got the transfer," Theo said.

"Yeah."

"She texted me `received` and a thumbs-up."

Eli let out a breath that might have been a laugh. "Runs in the family."

Theo's mouth twitched before flattening again. "Meeting is in forty-five. You going?"

Eli looked at his hands, at detergent dryness around his knuckles, at a nick from opening the trash bin lid. "Yeah," he said. "I'm going."

Theo nodded once. "Shoes by the door. We leave in ten."

Eli stood to get them. As he bent, his knee clicked and complained, and he put one hand on the crate table to steady himself. On the shelf above it sat Theo's keys, his union badge, and the five-year chip. Eli did not reach for the chip this time. He only noted its weight in the room, like a small coin-sized planet with its own gravity, and kept moving.

He had one night left on the couch under signed rules and a son who still watched him like a risk assessment. It was not mercy. It was structure. It was more than he had earned, and exactly what he had asked for.
