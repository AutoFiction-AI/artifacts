# Chapter 3

By noon the next day the frontage road had gone white with heat. The asphalt looked skinned, a wavering strip beside the interstate where semis blew past and shook the gravel shoulder. Eli drove with both windows down because the sedan's air had started coughing warm thirty miles back, and now the fan pushed nothing but breath from a hair dryer. He watched the temperature needle creep above center, then a little higher, then settle as if it might behave. A minute later it climbed again and stayed there.

He eased off the gas. He rolled the cabin heater to full and took the blast straight in the face. It smelled like old pennies and wet rubber. The needle held for half a mile. Then it jumped hard to red.

"Come on," he said to the dash, low and polite, the voice he used with auditors and angry surgeons. "Just give me one exit."

Steam punched out from the seam of the hood, fast and white, and the engine answered with a knock that sounded like a hammer dropped inside a sink. "Fuck, no," he said, put on his signal, and coasted to the shoulder where the frontage road widened near an abandoned fireworks stand. The sign still promised BUY ONE GET TWO through weather-faded rockets and cartoon eagles.

He shut the car off before the knock could become something final. The silence that followed was worse than noise. Heat climbed out of the engine bay and wrapped him. Cicadas drilled from weeds taller than the hood. Somewhere behind him, a loose road sign clanged in a wind that never reached skin.

He sat with both hands on the wheel and waited like waiting might undo physics. Sweat ran down his ribs, soaked his waistband, then cooled and turned sticky in the artificial breeze from trucks screaming past. He had two hundred eighty-seven dollars in his wallet, folded by twenties and fives, and another sixty-three in quarters and singles in the center console from toll leftovers and bad vending machines. The rest was numbers in accounts. Numbers left footprints.

He told himself he could risk one card swipe and keep moving. He told himself one swipe at a random repair shop in Indiana was nothing in a country this big. Then he pictured Nora in the kitchen, phone in one hand, June's tuition portal open on the laptop, little map pins appearing where her husband bought coolant and bad coffee while pretending he had lost reception. He pulled his own wallet out, counted once more in the shade of the steering wheel, and put it back.

The prepaid flip phone sat in the cup holder, hinge loose, outer screen scratched to a cloudy square. It had two stored contacts, neither named. He had kept it that way on purpose, as if an unnamed number could protect him from himself. He flipped it open and called roadside service from a laminated card zip-tied to his key ring, gave the dispatcher the frontage road marker, and said yes, cash. No, no membership. Yes, he understood the nonmember rate.

The tow truck took forty-three minutes. He measured the time by the shrinking shadow of the fireworks stand and by the way his shirt dried in strips. When the truck finally backed toward him, the driver leaned out with his left arm while his right hand stayed at his ear, tapping a beige hearing aid like he was tuning a radio.

"You the Buick?" he called.

"Yeah." Eli stood too fast and felt a hot pulse behind his eyes.

The driver climbed down. He was maybe sixty, wiry under a stained gray shirt that read HANK'S RECOVERY in red block letters, and there was a youth softball glove on the passenger seat with pink laces half rethreaded. He caught Eli looking and shrugged once. "Grandkid left it in my truck last night. If I don't fix it, she writes me a report." He grinned without waiting for approval. "Pop the hood if it will pop."

Eli released the latch, then stepped back from the plume that rolled up when Hank lifted the metal. Coolant had sprayed in a green fan across the shroud. Hank whistled through his teeth.

"Radiator split and maybe fan trouble," he said. "Don't start it again. Where're you trying to go?"

"Indianapolis. Cheap motel near the interstate." The details came out in ranked order before Hank could answer. "I just need enough work to reach St. Louis. I can deal with full repair there."

Hank closed the hood with both palms. "Tow and intake at Pike Yard is three fifty cash up front today. Sunday rules."

Eli almost laughed. Not because it was funny. Because the number landed exactly in the space where his plan had no material. "I've got two eighty-seven."

"Then you've got two eighty-seven." Hank scratched his jaw, looked at the interstate, looked back at Eli. "I can't burn diesel for free, man."

"I'm not asking for free." Eli took the money out and held it where Hank could see all of it. "Take this now. Let me settle the rest with the shop. I can pay the difference tonight if they can do partial."

Hank's mouth tightened. He tapped his hearing aid again, a quick anxious habit that had nothing to do with sound. "Owner doesn't like paper promises."

"Then call him. Tell him you have my license. Tell him you keep the car if I don't pay."

"He already keeps the car if you don't pay."

Eli forced himself not to fill the pause with more words. He stood in the heat with his money in his hand while Hank weighed labor against trouble. A semi blasted by and the wind shoved dust into both their faces.

Hank sighed and took the cash. "Two eighty-seven now. You owe sixty-three before the car leaves the fence. Not a penny less. And if the mechanic says it's dead, don't yell at me."

"I won't yell at you."

"People say that like a vow." Hank stuffed the bills into a metal clipboard and jerked his chin at the cab. "Get in. Seat belt sticks. Pull hard."

The truck smelled like stale spearmint and grease. A tiny paper calendar hung from the dash with hand-drawn stars on Tuesdays. Eli noticed it because he needed to notice something besides the phone in his pocket buzzing once, then stopping. Unknown incoming from a Pennsylvania area code.

He did not answer.

Pike Yard sat behind a chain-link fence between a truck wash and a diner with a tilted sign that said YARD BIRD CAFE. Three service bays stood open to the heat. A woman in black coveralls leaned over an engine block with a trouble light hanging from her neck like a pendant. Her coveralls were half unzipped at the throat, and under them she wore a faded T-shirt with a print of old hummingbirds. A pencil was stuck through the knot of her hair. She did not look up when Hank killed the engine.

"Darlene," Hank called. "Frontage-road boilover. Buick sedan."

"Put it on two," she said. Then she looked up at Eli, took him in once, and wiped her hands on a rag already black. "You paying cash or telling stories?"

"Both," Eli said.

"I can only invoice one of those." She set her trouble light down and walked him to the bay while Hank dropped the sedan from the flatbed. Up close she was younger than he first thought, maybe mid-forties, with a crescent scar crossing one eyebrow and a nicotine patch curling off her upper arm. "What happened before it boiled?"

He gave her the sequence. Warm air from vents, rising needle, steam at shoulder, one knock. She listened without interruption, head tilted, eyes on the hood latch.

"Don't start it," she said when he finished. "That's me saying it in church language. Don't start it, not even to roll it ten feet."

She lifted the hood, pressure-tested the system, and found the crack within a minute: upper tank split near the seam, coolant tracing dried in bright green veins down to the frame. The fan clutch had enough wobble to rattle a tooth. She spoke the numbers while writing them on the back of an old invoice.

"New radiator, new fan clutch, flush, belts check, labor: seven hundred sixty if nothing else is bent. If head gasket cooked while you were driving red, this car belongs to God."

Eli stared at the invoice. Seven hundred sixty meant card. Card meant timestamp, city, trail. Also seven hundred sixty meant hotel, food, fuel, and tomorrow collapsing at once.

"What's the cheapest way this runs long enough to get me west?" he asked.

"Define long enough."

"Three hundred miles. Maybe four."

Darlene let out a short sound between a laugh and a cough. "You want me to give your problem to Missouri."

"I want a partial fix. Used radiator, if you have one. No warranty. I sign whatever says this is my bad decision."

She flipped to a fresh line and wrote again. "Used radiator from salvage row if the neck is clean. Hose patch. Flush enough to get rust chunks out. I can do that for four forty-five, cash, and I still hate it."

"I've got sixty-three left after tow balance." He held her gaze, because looking away made him look like a liar. "I can work off part. I can pay the rest tomorrow morning."

"It's a shop, not a charity calendar."

"I'm not asking for charity."

"You're asking for credit from people who've known you three minutes." She handed him the invoice. "No."

A screen door banged from the diner. A woman came out carrying two glasses of iced tea with lemon wedges and a cigarette tucked above one ear, unlit. She was maybe late fifties, broad in the shoulders, hair in a silver braid thick as rope down her back. Her left shoe had a homemade red felt insert peeking at the heel, and she walked with the careful weight of somebody managing plantar pain without admitting it.

"Are you asking Darlene to get religious about an IOU?" she said, setting one tea by the toolbox and keeping the other for herself. "That's brave."

"Cora, mind your table count," Darlene said, but there was no heat in it.

Cora ignored her, looked at Eli, and took a sip through a straw. "You don't have card money? Or you don't want your card in Indiana?"

He should have lied. Something easy, harmless, weather-shaped. Instead he heard himself say, "I'm staying cash."

"That second one, then." Cora nodded like she had expected it. "Miguel's dishwasher called from county lockup an hour ago. He's not making tonight's shift."

Darlene snorted. "Your staffing problem isn't my accounts receivable."

"No, but your sister owns this diner and keeps borrowing your impact wrench, so we can all pretend family is relevant." Cora turned back to Eli. "You can run the dish pit?"

"I can run anything with water and racks."

"Confident answer from a man in loafers." Her eyes dropped to his shoes, then to his hands. "You ever done line work?"

"College kitchens. A hospital cafeteria in my twenties."

"That was the Carter administration." Cora tapped her glass with one fingernail. "Tonight's five to midnight, maybe later if the truckers linger. Eighty cash plus a meal, and twenty from my mercy slush if Vera signs off. He pays Hank the sixty-three tow shortfall now. I front you one thirty-seven as deposit, and he starts paying me back out of tonight's shift before he sees a pillow. You start at dawn. He does breakfast prep after if he still owes your side."

Darlene drank her tea and watched him over the rim, weighing how much disaster she could tolerate touching her bay. "One condition," she said. "I don't turn a wrench until the tow shortfall is paid and that one thirty-seven deposit is in my hand."

"I don't have that." Eli spread his empty palms.

Cora's voice stayed mild. "He has sixty-three now and a body. I can front one thirty-seven from my tip envelope if he signs me a note and gives me his license copy. If he runs, I'm out grocery money and he keeps being exactly who he already is."

The last sentence landed blade-flat on skin. Not a cut yet. A warning that a cut was available.

Eli swallowed. "I'll sign anything. I'll pay you back tonight."

Cora took the unlit cigarette from behind her ear and pointed it at him. "Don't make me chase you. I don't run anymore unless a fryer catches."

"I won't run."

"Everybody says that today." She looked at Darlene. "Deal?"

Darlene folded the invoice in half and tucked it into her breast pocket. "Deal until dawn. If he no-shows the shift, I push the sedan into overflow and start charging lot rent at eight a.m."

"Fair," Cora said.

Nothing about it felt fair, but Eli nodded anyway, paid the last sixty-three to Hank, signed his name on two clipboard forms, and watched Cora peel one hundred thirty-seven dollars from a flowered envelope she kept zipped inside her apron. The bills were worn thin at the folds. She counted them twice before handing them to Darlene.

"My nephew's braces fund," she said to Eli without meeting his eyes. "Now your problem."

By four-thirty he was in the diner stockroom wearing an apron that said YARD BIRD CAFE over a cartoon chicken with an angry eye. The apron strings barely met behind his back. His loafers had been replaced by rubber clogs one size too small from a crate marked LOST & FOUND. The kitchen manager, Vera Ortiz, was a short woman with a buzz cut and a Notre Dame sticker on her water bottle. She kept a stopwatch clipped to her belt because she trained for half-marathons between doubles.

"Listen once," Vera said, moving fast as she spoke. "Scrape, spray, rack, run, stack. Glass on blue rack, silver on gray, plates on green. If a rack jams, kill power before you touch it unless you enjoy getting lit up. Don't block the floor drain. Don't disappear for smoke. If you break a mug, you tell me now, not when blood hits the salsa."

"Got it."

"Say it like you mean it."

"I got it."

"Good. Gloves there. Earplugs there. If Raul asks for clean sauté pans, move like your rent depends on it because his temper does." She pointed at the back line where a man with a forearm tattoo of a lighthouse was whisking batter and humming under his breath in perfect baritone. "That's Raul. He sings old gospel when he gets stressed, then cusses in three languages. Don't take it personal."

The shift started before he could take inventory of fear. Tickets spat from the printer in relentless white curls. A bus from a tractor auction came through first, then a wave of truckers, then local men in work boots who ate pork tenderloin sandwiches with extra pickles and left rings of tobacco in the parking lot. Plates arrived in towers slick with egg yolk, biscuit gravy skinning over at the edges, ketchup darkening where it had started to dry. The dish machine roared and coughed steam into his face every forty seconds.

By six his shoulders burned. By seven a bar of pain rode his spine. At seven-thirty he dropped a tray of heavy white mugs because sanitizer made the plastic rack slick, and one handle skittered under the prep table, white and curved as a tooth.

Vera turned, saw the shards, and shouted over the hood fan, "You cut?"

"No."

"Then move. Sweep after rush." She slid him a bus tub with her hip. "You're not paid to stare at your mistakes."

He worked faster and got worse. He stacked green racks too high. He forgot the flatware soak and had to run two extra cycles while Raul yelled for spoons. He bent wrong lifting a pot and felt something sharp grab under his right shoulder blade. He kept going.

A young prep cook named Lena passed him diced onions and a damp towel without being asked. She wore one glittery blue nail on each hand because her nine-year-old daughter painted them every Wednesday and got offended if she removed them before Saturday. "Breathe out when you lift, sir," Lena said. "You're trying to deadlift dishes."

"I don't usually do this," he said.

"Nobody does usually. Usually is fake." She grinned and vanished back to her board.

At eight-ten the dish machine belched, stalled, and threw a code. Water pooled around the drain because somebody had kicked a potato peel into the grate. Eli reached in barehanded, felt the clog slimy against his fingers, yanked it free, and restarted the cycle while Vera reset the breaker. Hot gray water sloshed into his clogs and stayed there. The skin on his heels softened and rubbed raw.

"You're doing fine," Cora said when she came through with a tub of pie plates. She had traded her server apron for a black cardigan against the kitchen vent draft. The cigarette still sat behind her ear, still unlit. "Fine here means not dead and not crying in the freezer."

"That's a narrow definition."

"Narrow saves time." She set the pie plates down and lowered her voice. "Darlene found a radiator in salvage that isn't complete garbage. She starts at five a.m. if you make midnight upright."

"Thank you."

"Don't thank me till your car moves." She looked at his hands, now puckered and red from sanitizer. "Use the cream by the first-aid kit before your skin cracks open. That stuff stings like sin, but it works."

He nodded and kept spraying plates.

The hardest hour came after ten, when the dining room thinned but the backlog from late orders hit at once: cast-iron skillets with baked cheese welded to the bottom, fryer baskets furred with carbon, hotel pans glazed in sugar from peach cobbler. Every muscle in his forearms buzzed. His palms went numb around the spray nozzle, then woke with needles when he let go. The dishwasher door hissed with the pressure-release note of an ICU line, and for one stupid second he was back in hospital corridors, badge clipped straight, talking about compliance windows and corrective action plans while men with stronger arms than his rolled linen carts past him.

Now he was elbow-deep in congealed gravy, and a teenager from table service was calling him "sir" because she had no better word for someone old enough to be her father scraping half-chewed meatloaf into a rubber bin.

Humility was too soft a word for it. Humility sounded voluntary.

At eleven-fifty-five Vera killed the order window and blew out a long breath. "Breakdown," she said, clapping once. "Line cool-down, dish close, then side work."

Eli stood still and almost tipped over. His shirt clung to him. His wedding band had trapped a pale clean stripe under the skin while everything around it went red and wrinkled. He twisted the ring once and left it where it was.

Vera counted cash from the office drawer and came back with a folded set of bills held by a rubber band. "Eighty for shift, twenty from Cora's pity reserve," she said. "That's one hundred. You still owe Cora thirty-seven on the deposit loan and Darlene three oh eight on the repair. Do the math before sleep."

He took the money. "I appreciate this."

"Show it by showing up if she needs breakfast help." Vera unscrewed her water bottle and drank in three hard swallows. "You didn't quit. That's enough for day one."

He paid Cora at the pass-through while she entered tips in a battered ledger with tiny neat handwriting. She peeled his hundred into her envelope, then waved away the remaining thirty-seven.

"Keep it till morning," she said. "I'm not worried about getting rich off you."

"You should take it now."

"You still need a room and probably antibiotics for your feet. Sit down. Pie on me." She jerked her chin toward a back booth near the coffee station. "Blueberry or pecan. No speeches."

He sat because standing had become theoretical. The booth vinyl was cracked and warm. A jukebox near the restrooms clicked through old tracks at low volume, and someone had written NO NICKEL SHORT in black marker above the coin slot. Cora brought him a wedge of blueberry pie and coffee so dark it carried the sheen of used motor oil. She set down two forks and slid into the opposite bench without asking.

Up close, he saw she had small white crescent scars across two knuckles, old burns maybe, and reading glasses hanging from a beaded cord patterned with little plastic corn kernels.

"Where are you from?" she asked.

"Pittsburgh area."

"That's not a where. That's a shield. Try again."

"Monroeville."

"Better." She broke the pie crust with her fork and ate like she had done this at two in the morning for twenty years. "Who is looking for you?"

He stared at his coffee. Heat rolled up from it and fogged his glasses. "I didn't say anyone was looking."

"You didn't have to. Men passing through with cash-only rules usually fall in one of three buckets. Divorce, warrant, or self-help pilgrimage with bad podcasts." She pointed her fork at him. "You don't look like a warrant. You definitely don't look like podcasts."

He laughed despite himself, a short cracked sound that hurt his throat. "What does podcasts look like?"

"Brand-new boots and a notebook for thoughts."

He looked at his swollen hands, the chemical redness, the half-moon indent from his ring. He could still choose a story. Temporary travel. Card fraud scare. Family emergency. He had built a career on controlled disclosures, giving just enough to satisfy policy while protecting institutional skin.

Cora waited him out in silence, drinking coffee that had gone cold.

He said, "I dropped my daughter at school two days ago. First year. Dorm move-in, all the plastic bins, parents pretending nobody's wrecked. I told my wife I was driving straight home."

"And?"

"I took the west ramp instead." He set his fork down. "I kept telling myself I was taking a day. One day to clear my head. Then another. Then the phone felt like a trap, so I threw it out."

Cora did not react right away. She looked toward the kitchen where Vera was yelling at somebody about unlabeled mayo, then back to him. "That's not an accident, honey. That's a choice with luggage."

"I know."

"Do they know you're alive?"

"I've sent short messages. Not enough."

"That answer means no." She leaned back and stretched one foot under the table, wincing when the arch caught. "My ex disappeared for nine days when my boy was seven. Came back with a sunburn and a story about finding himself at a bass tournament. We found him. He was at his cousin's trailer drinking and crying at daytime TV."

Eli looked up.

"Point isn't that your situation is his," she said. "Point is the people waiting on you keep doing arithmetic you don't even see. Grocery arithmetic. Tuition arithmetic. Sleep arithmetic." She tapped the table twice. "You can run your body out here all you want. Your absence still sits in their kitchen chair every morning."

He let the words land. They did not feel like judgment. They felt like inventory.

"I'm trying to get west, make money, then fix what I broke," he said.

"You haven't even named what you broke." Cora took another bite of pie. "But you're not lying to yourself tonight, and that counts for about one inch."

He almost reached for the safe version again, stress and static and bad timing. Instead he said, "I moved fourteen thousand dollars from our emergency account before I left. It was mortgage money. It was tuition money."

Cora set her fork down. "Then hear me clear: no more pretty words about contingency. You text your wife that exact number before you sleep, or I tell Darlene to stop at sunrise and keep the car behind the fence."

"I'm not a drunk," he said instead, and heard how defensive it sounded.

"I didn't say you were."

"I'm not trying to find myself."

"Nobody's ever trying to find themselves. They're trying to avoid somebody seeing them plain." She pushed the second fork toward him. "Eat. You look like a folded map."

He finished the pie because she kept watching until he did. Sugar and blueberry paste sat heavy in his stomach, but the shaking in his hands eased. Around one-fifteen she stood, stretched her back with both fists pressed against her hips, and told him the room upstairs was open.

"Thirty-five tonight," she said. "Cash. Towel if you ask, sheet if you ask twice. The AC works when it feels respected."

He paid and climbed the narrow stairs over the diner with every step telegraphing itself through his calves. The room held a twin bed, a window unit rattling with loose-change clatter, and wallpaper patterned with small faded sailboats gone yellow at the seams. The sink tap coughed rust before clearing. He washed dish chemical off his arms until the water ran cold, then stood barefoot on a bathmat thin enough that he felt tile through it.

His feet were a mess: skin at both heels rubbed open, edges white and raw from soaking in hot gray water. He dabbed antibiotic cream from a plastic first-aid packet Cora had handed him at the stairs. It burned exactly as promised.

He sat on the bed with the prepaid flip phone, snapped it open, and clicked to a blank message for the Pennsylvania number he had memorized, the one tied to Nora's line even though he had not saved the name.

He typed: `Car trouble in Indiana. I'm safe.`

He deleted Indiana.

He typed: `Car trouble. I'm safe. Need one more day.`

He deleted one more day.

He typed: `I'm sorry.`

He stared at those two words until the tiny inner screen dimmed.

From the lot below came the metallic bark of a trailer hitch dropping, then male laughter, then the long reverse beep of a truck backing blind. Someone fought at the far edge of the fence, voices rising and collapsing into a slap of silence. The window unit gave off a trapped-in-plastic buzz. He could smell fryer oil in the sheet when he pulled it up, old and sweet.

He opened the text thread again and thumbed in each word on the rubber keypad: `I left after move-in. I took $14,000 from our emergency account before I left. I'm in Indiana with a dead radiator.`

That was closer to true and still not enough.

If he sent it, she could call. If she called, he would hear the clock in their kitchen behind her, the one that clicked too loud at night, and he would open with the radiator crack, the tow receipt, the cash shortage, the temporary labor. By the time he got to the fourteen thousand, the call would already be bleeding out.

He set the phone on his chest and listened to trucks shift gears on the interstate. Sleep did not come. Around two he drafted another text to June and erased it before the first line finished. Around three he got up, sat at the tiny laminate desk under the mirror, and wrote figures on the back of a diner receipt.

Tow shortfall: paid. Repair total: four forty-five. Deposit on file with Darlene from Cora: one thirty-seven. Remaining repair balance: three oh eight if Darlene kept her estimate. Room: thirty-five. Fuel to St. Louis plus food: maybe one forty if he drove gentle and skipped meals that were not free. Cash on hand after room: two dollars plus loose change.

The numbers were clean. His life was not.

At four-ten he opened Nora's thread one last time, added `I'll call at six and answer whatever you ask.`, and hit SEND before he could edit himself clean.

Outside, dawn had started to thin the black above the tow-yard fence. A forklift coughed awake. Darlene's laugh carried up from below, sharp and brief. Eli lay back in clothes that smelled like bleach and fry oil, eyes open, the sent message hot in his pocket, live and mean.

He did not sleep before his alarm.
