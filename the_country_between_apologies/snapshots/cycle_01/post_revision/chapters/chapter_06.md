# Chapter 6

The night after he sat through closing prayer beside Theo, Eli heard the knife before he reached the kitchen: quick taps from Theo's short chef's knife, onions piling past the edge of a cutting board too small for the job.

Eli stood at the stove with a wooden spoon in one hand and a bag of frozen peas sweating onto his wrist. The apartment had the late-evening smell of cumin and laundry detergent and that burnt-dust radiator smell that showed up the first cool nights of fall, even before the building turned the heat on for real.

Imani came in from the bedroom tying her sidecut back with a black satin scarf, wire-frame glasses low on her nose, one sock still in her hand. She hopped twice toward the kitchen island, slid her foot into the sock without touching the floor, and said, "Somebody tell me if we're doing rice in a pot or rice in that cursed little rice cooker before I commit to either timeline."

"Pot," Theo said. "Cursed little rice cooker makes glue."

"That thing has one mood," Imani said. "And that mood is kindergarten paste."

Eli turned down the burner. "I can run to the store if you're short on anything."

Theo scraped onions into a bowl. "We're good. Sit down if your knee's barking."

"It's fine."

His knee was not fine. It had a weather report all its own and had been issuing warnings since they carried June's mini fridge up three dorm flights in August. He shifted his weight anyway, like he could bluff his body the way he used to bluff auditors.

Theo opened a spice tin, pinched in paprika, then handed Eli a raw chicken thigh without looking at him. "Salt both sides first. Don't bury it in the pan wet." He said it the same way he'd say, Buckle that pallet before it tips, no lecture in it, just sequence.

Eli salted. He patted with paper towel. He listened for the first clean hiss when the chicken touched hot oil and felt a ridiculous pulse of relief at getting one small thing right.

Across from him, Imani lined up three chipped bowls in a row and dropped measured scoops of yogurt sauce into each one. She tasted a spoonful, frowned, and added lemon. Then she reached behind the toaster and pulled out a folded museum brochure, opened it flat, and started writing her Tuesday seminar notes on the blank margin in tiny block letters.

Eli said, "You still doing that master's program and the desk job?"

Imani looked up. "Yep. Two classes this term. I work admissions at the museum Wednesday through Saturday, ten to two. Then reading till my eyes mutiny."

"She's writing eighty pages on how rich people stole half of Europe," Theo said.

"I'm writing eighty pages on how institutions rename theft until donors can applaud it," she said.

Theo barked a laugh. "See? That's why she gets the A's."

Eli smiled before he could stop himself. The sound of Theo laughing was lower than it had been as a teenager, less sharp around the edges. In high school it had always come with a dare attached. Now it sounded like something earned and rationed.

Imani pointed at the pan. "Flip those before they turn into roof shingles."

He flipped them. The skin had gone amber and spotted in places where the heat had run hotter. It looked good enough.

On the little speaker by the sink, an old soul track came up with a bass line Eli knew but couldn't place. Theo named it in the first two notes. Imani shouted from the pantry, "No, wrong year, that's the remaster."

Theo held up a finger. "Original pressing, seventy-five."

"Try seventy-three, forklift boy."

They were both wrong. The singer announced the song on a live clip before the first verse, date and venue included, and Theo slapped his own forehead with a wet hand.

"I stand corrected by a dead man," he said.

Imani grinned. "That's your best kind of correction."

Eli laughed. It surprised all three of them, including him. The sound came out loud and unplanned, not the conference-call courtesy breath, and it hitched once in his chest. Theo glanced at him and shook his head.

"Careful," Theo said. "Next you'll be arguing liner notes."

"I can barely read the back of a cereal box without my glasses now," Eli said.

"Welcome to the club," Imani said. "My bag is basically glass cases and chargers."

She said it and reached into her tote and produced two pairs of folded readers, one in a cracked green case, one wrapped in a sock. Theo pointed at the sock pair.

"Those are your emergency emergency glasses?"

"Those are my emergency emergency emergency glasses," she said.

Theo put the back of his hand to his forehead like fainting. "She's unstoppable."

The timer on Eli's burner beeped. He turned off the heat, moved the chicken to a plate, and watched steam cloud the kitchen window over the sink. Outside was the parking lot and then the alley, both lit the same tired sodium orange as every lot he'd slept in for the last week. Inside, Theo had magnets on the refrigerator from places Eli had never heard him mention: a state park in Arkansas, a cat cafe in Chicago, an auto parts sponsor picnic in Alton. There were two dry-erase calendars side by side. One held Imani's class deadlines in blue marker, each reading assignment boxed and checked. The other held Theo's shift bids, overtime blocks, and Tuesday/Thursday marks that read MEETING 7:30 in neat print.

Eli had been in this apartment for thirty-six hours and only now clocked that the meetings were always at the same time, always in the same handwriting, no flourish. He had known Theo was sober. The dry-erase blocks on the fridge showed the labor of staying that way.

Warehouse work, he'd known. He hadn't pictured shift bids, union steward calls, and a text thread that could blow up over forklift tire policies. He'd clocked the tattoos too, but only now noticed one date in Roman numerals climbing from Theo's wrist, plus the initials of a man Eli had never met.

"Rice," Imani said.

He snapped back and moved to drain it. He almost poured the water into the sink without the colander. Theo caught his wrist light and quick.

"Not straight down," Theo said. "You'll make wallpaper paste in the pipes. Use the strainer."

"Right."

Imani kept her eyes on the sauce. "Apartment plumbing is like emotional plumbing. It'll take one weird decision and punish everybody."

Theo snorted. "That why the upstairs guy flooded his bathroom again?"

"That was not emotional," she said. "That was tequila plus bad grout."

Before he sat, he pulled the flip phone from his pocket and killed the vibration. Nine missed calls stacked under the lock-screen clock, all from hospital numbers, and one voicemail transcript from Cal still glowed at the top: FINAL REQUEST FOR IMMEDIATE CONTACT TODAY TO AVOID ABANDONMENT. He turned the phone face down by his water glass and told himself he'd open the inbox after dishes.

They ate at the small table that was really a repurposed workbench with one leg shimmed by a folded coaster. Theo sat with his back to the window. Imani tucked one foot under her and used the edge of naan to wipe up sauce. Eli kept meaning to slow down and kept eating faster, as if someone might clear his plate and send him back to the couch before he had tasted enough.

"This is good," he said.

Theo shrugged. "It's chicken and rice."

"It's good chicken and rice."

Imani pointed her fork at him. "Accept the compliment. We're trying to build healthy pathways in this home."

Theo accepted with a tiny nod and a side glance at Eli that was half joke, half caution. They had become fluent in each other while he was out of frame, and the fact sat heavy at the table.

A phone buzzed on the table. It wasn't his. His own cheap flip phone sat face down and silent, the red voicemail dot still needling the corner of the screen. This was Theo's, faceup by his plate.

Theo read the screen and said, "Manny wants to swap Thursday. Says his kid has croup."

Imani said, "Take it if it gives you Friday night. You said Friday's the chip night."

Theo nodded. "Yeah."

Chip night meant a five-year sobriety chip at the South Grand meeting, then a few guys from Theo's group here for coffee and grocery-store cake. Eli had heard the phrase this morning and guessed poker. He learned better when he reached into the freezer for ice and found three unfrosted sheet cakes stacked on frozen spinach, each with a sticky note in Imani's round handwriting: NOT FOR RANDOM SADNESS.

He swallowed and asked, too late not to sound like an outsider, "Five years this Friday?"

Theo's fork paused halfway to his mouth.

"Yeah," Theo said.

"That's... that's big."

"Yeah," Imani said, softer than before. "We're proud of him."

Theo looked back down. "It's a day."

"It's five years of days," she said.

Silence moved across the table, not hostile, just full.

Eli said, "I didn't know the date."

Theo took a breath through his nose and set his fork down. "No. You didn't."

There was no sting in the words. That made it worse. Eli reached for his water glass and found it empty. He stood, filled it from the tap, watched the bubbles turn silver under the kitchen light, and stayed at the sink one beat too long before he sat again.

After dinner he washed dishes because those were the rules: cook, clean, no debate. Theo dried. Imani packed tomorrow's lunches into plastic containers with painter's tape labels and dates written in marker. Their kitchen sounded like domestic percussion, faucet running, drawer slide, snap-lid click, ceramic on rack.

Eli set a glass in the sink too hard and chipped the rim. He sucked air through his teeth.

"My bad."

Theo held out his hand. "Give it here before you fillet your thumb."

Eli gave it over. Theo ran a fingertip around the chip, then put the glass in the trash and wrapped it in a grocery bag so nobody would cut themselves reaching for coffee grounds.

It was a small action, done without speech. It was competence as care.

He dried his hands and went to the hall closet for his duffel because he told himself he needed clean socks. Really he needed to check his work email before midnight because once he slept he would tell himself it could wait one more morning and then one more after that.

In the bedroom they had given him, which was technically Theo's office with a futon and stacked bins of winter coats, the air was cooler. The building's pipes knocked twice inside the wall. Eli sat on the futon edge and opened the old laptop he had left in his trunk for months.

The hospital VPN took three tries. The system gave him a password reset prompt, then a security question, then another prompt. He typed too fast, backspaced, typed again. The screen finally loaded his inbox with the same corporate blue banner he'd looked at every weekday for twelve years. There were forty-two unread messages, most of them automated reminders and one from Cal flagged urgent from three days ago.

He opened Cal's message first.

Subject: Attendance / Required Contact - Final Notice

Cal's writing sounded exactly like his voice: neutral until you learned where the trapdoor sat.
He usually ended even hard messages with "Take care," two spaces before his name, but this one cut off cold after the time demand.

Eli,

You have missed scheduled work obligations since 8/29 and have not responded to direct calls or documented reporting channels. HR has attempted contact at your file addresses and by phone. This is a final request for immediate contact today to avoid abandonment designation under policy 4.7.

Call me before 5:00 p.m. Central.

Cal Bristow
Director of Compliance

The message had been sent at 4:12 p.m. two days ago.

The next message was from Human Resources Shared Services, no name in the signature, only a ticket number and two links to policy PDFs.

Subject: Employment Separation Notice

Mr. Mercer,

As of 09/07/2026, your employment with Allegheny Regional Hospital has been terminated under Job Abandonment Policy 4.7 due to unreported absence and failure to respond to required contact attempts.

Your medical, dental, and vision benefits remain active through 09/30/2026. Continuation options (COBRA) will be sent to your mailing address on file within 14 business days. PTO payout details will appear in your final pay statement.

If this determination is in error, submit written appeal within five business days of notice. Appeals received after deadline will not be reviewed.

He checked the timestamp. Sent yesterday morning. Appeal deadline: tomorrow at 11:59 p.m. Eastern.

He opened the link to continuation rates. His existing premium deduction had been $242 a month. COBRA for family coverage: $1,186.48.

He read the number three times, each time the decimal points snapping into place harder than the last, like staples.

The room got narrower, and not metaphorically. The futon mattress edge cut behind his knees. The desk corner pressed his forearm where he leaned. He put both feet flat on the rug and listened to the apartment outside the half-closed door: Theo laughing once at something from the living room, Imani's voice reading from a textbook in a mock-dramatic accent, page turns, then the soft whump of somebody dropping onto the couch.

He could still appeal. He could write a paragraph about family emergency, acute distress, phone loss, route confusion, no charger, whatever pieces might fit. He knew policy language. He could stack terms until they looked reasonable.

He also knew policy 4.7. He had enforced versions of it on people who stopped showing up and thought one good apology email would make payroll rewind.

A sound came out of him before he meant it to, not a word, just a rough exhale that scraped his throat.

He opened a blank reply to HR, typed three lines, deleted them, typed again:

I request reconsideration based on personal crisis and communication failure.

He stopped. Communication failure sounded like a dropped call, not ten days of disappearance with a burner phone in his duffel.

He deleted it.

He shut the laptop and sat with his hands on his thighs until the skin cooled where the computer had been.

There was a knock on the door frame, though it was not really a knock. Theo tapped twice with his knuckles and stayed in the hall.

"You good?" he asked.

Eli rubbed his face. "No." He looked up. "Come in."

Theo stepped into the room, leaned his shoulder against the bookcase, and waited.

"I got terminated," Eli said. He kept his voice level for the first clause because that was old muscle memory. "As of yesterday. Job abandonment."

Theo did not ask if Eli had misunderstood the email. He did not offer fake shock.

"Okay," he said.

"Insurance runs through end of month. After that it's COBRA at" - Eli gave a short laugh that sounded wrong in his own ears - "it's twelve hundred a month for family coverage."

Theo whistled once under his breath. "Jesus."

"Yeah."

"You telling Mom tonight?"

"I should."

"That's not what I asked."

Eli stared at the stacked storage bins by the futon, each labeled in black marker: WINTER BOOTS, TAXES, CORDS WE MIGHT NEED. "No," he said. "Not tonight. I need to think through what I can actually pay first."

Theo's jaw moved once. "You can think. You can't hide."

"I know."

"Do you?"

The word landed flat. There was no shout and no speech. There was just the question and all the years under it.

Eli said, "I know enough to hear how that sounds." He spread his hands, then dropped them. "I'm not trying to spin you. I'm trying not to call her with half a sentence and no plan."

Theo folded his arms. Ink crossed his forearms, black lines and dates and one faded sparrow Eli had never asked about. "Then make a plan in hours, not days. You have till tomorrow night on that appeal, right?"

"Yeah."

"You filing it?"

"I don't know if I should."

"Would it be true?"

Eli looked at him and said nothing.

Theo nodded once. "Then don't dress it up." He pushed off the bookcase. "Come out when you're ready. Imani's making tea."

He left the door open behind him.

Eli stayed seated another minute, maybe three. He could hear water kettle hiss rising in the kitchen. The smell of ginger drifted down the hall where Imani had sliced it with the side of a knife and smashed it before steeping. He knew that because she had shown him at breakfast and said, "If you only slice it, it's polite. If you smash it, it actually helps."

He reopened the laptop, typed a note in his personal draft folder:

Terminated 9/7. Benefits end 9/30. COBRA 1186.48.

Tell Nora tomorrow morning before noon.

No story version.

He shut it again and went to the kitchen.

Imani was at the counter in an old museum T-shirt with a paint stain on the hem, pouring tea through a mesh strainer into three mugs that did not match. She set one in front of him and one in front of Theo and kept her own between both palms for a second before sitting.

"You look like somebody unplugged you," she said.

Eli wrapped his hands around the mug. The ceramic was almost too hot to hold. "Work's done."

"Done like shift done or done done?" she asked.

"Done done."

She nodded, no pity face. "Okay."

Theo looked at her, then at Eli. "He got the separation notice. Insurance till month end."

Imani inhaled through her teeth. "Okay. What did HR call it exactly?"

"Job abandonment."

"In writing?"

"Yeah. Second paragraph."

She counted on her fingers as she spoke, looking at Eli directly, not Theo, not the table.

"Then sequence," she said. "Tomorrow before noon, you call Nora with the exact wording and the COBRA number. After that, you decide on the appeal before the deadline."

She tapped the table with one fingernail. "If you leave this week, tell us whether you need the hall-drawer charger and the blue cooler. I don't want to guess what you'll pretend you can do without."

Eli almost smiled because the line landed like Imani: practical, a little sharp, and impossible to perform against.

"That's fair," he said.

"I'm not scoring fair," she said. "I'm trying to keep this place from running on panic."

Theo pointed at her mug. "You stole that from me."

"I improved it," she said. "You're welcome."

Theo turned to Eli. "I got something. Hold on."

He disappeared into the bedroom he and Imani shared, came back with a road atlas thick as a brick, spiral binding frayed, cover creased white along the fold. It was a Rand McNally, two editions out of date. Eli recognized it as the one that had lived on the back shelf of Theo's first beater pickup in high school, next to jumper cables and a jug of coolant.

Theo set it on the table and flipped to Missouri. Yellow highlighter marks crossed interstate routes in thin careful strokes. Red pen circles marked towns with notes in block print: DAY LABOR LOT 5AM, CASH DAY SHIFT / WEEKLY MOTEL 42 + TAX / TRUCK STOP W/ SHOWERS.

He turned pages west through Kansas and into eastern Colorado. A corner by Salina had a coffee ring and the faint outline of a phone number written, then scratched out. In the margin near Grand Junction, Theo had taped a narrow strip of paper torn from a receipt with two names and a union local contact.

"I started this in rehab year," Theo said. "Guys would pass route tips around. Where not to sleep, where cops run people off, where the church lot opens early if you keep it clean. I added stuff when I was driving for deliveries after I got out. It's old, but most of it's still close."

Eli touched the page edge. The paper was soft from use. "You carried this around all that time?"

"Yeah."

"I thought you just used your phone."

Theo gave him a look that was not unkind. "Phones die. Service dies. People shut them off when bills hit. Paper keeps working."

Imani sipped tea and said, "There's also a tiny joy in writing on maps like you're planning a heist."

"She color-coded my routes once," Theo said. "Almost got me killed in Omaha because yellow looked like orange at night."

"You're alive and dramatic," she said.

Eli ran his thumb over a penciled note near Denver: IF YOU'RE BROKE, EAT FIRST, THEN PLAN. Under it, smaller, a second line in different handwriting: AND SLEEP. BAD DECISIONS HAPPEN AT 2AM.

"That's yours?" he asked.

Imani shook her head. "Nope. That's a guy named Reggie from Theo's meeting. He writes on everything. Your paper bag from takeout would have life advice by dessert."

Theo closed the atlas halfway and pushed it toward Eli.

"Take it when you go," he said.

The words caught Eli harder than the termination notice had. He looked up. "You sure?"

"Yes."

"It's yours."

"It's a tool," Theo said. "Tools are for using."

Eli swallowed tea that was still too hot and felt it burn down the center of his chest.

"Thanks," he said. "For this. For... all of it."

Theo shrugged one shoulder. "Don't do the speech. Use it."

Imani leaned back, cradling her mug, eyes on Eli over the rim of her glasses. "And don't vanish between updates. Text city, sleep plan, and whether you ate something besides gas-station peanuts. That's house policy."

"I won't."

She tipped her head. "You hear the full sentence you just said?"

He did. He did hear it. He heard the promise form in his mouth before the plan did, same as always.

He corrected himself. "I'll send where I'm each night while I'm on the road. If I change route, I text before I turn." He looked at Theo. "And I'll call Nora tomorrow before noon with the numbers."

Theo nodded. "That's better."

They sat with tea and the atlas open between them like an extra plate at the table.

Later, after dishes and showers and the apartment settling into night sounds, Eli lay on the futon and traced tomorrow in his head by sequence: wake early, draft bullet points, call Nora, tell facts, no excuses, decide appeal, then pack the car. Through the wall he could hear Theo and Imani in their room talking low, not arguing, just the murmur of two people who knew how to share a day down to the dumb details, the museum donor who smelled like mothballs, the forklift horn that stuck on in aisle twelve, whose turn it was to buy detergent.

He remembered Theo at sixteen slamming his bedroom door hard enough to crack the cheap frame. He remembered Theo at twenty-two on a rehab intake call, voice flattened to one syllable answers while Eli stood in a hospital parking lot pretending he could not leave a meeting. He remembered, with nausea that had nowhere to go, how many ordinary facts he had not known when they happened and how quickly you could call that ignorance busyness and get away with it for years.

The atlas sat on the desk by the laptop, a thick square shadow in the dark.

He got up, opened it again under the desk lamp, and copied three notes onto an index card in his blockiest handwriting so he would not claim he could not read it at dawn:

ST. LOUIS -> SALINA -> DENVER CORRIDOR (CHEAP SLEEP OPTIONS)
TEMP AGENCIES / DAY LABOR LOTS (ARRIVE BEFORE 5:30)
CALL HOME BEFORE SPENDING CASH

He stared at the last line. That one was Theo's handwriting in the atlas, not his. The letters leaned forward, impatient.

He set the pen down, left the lamp on, and took the flip phone from his pocket before he could build another cleaner version.

Nora answered on the fourth ring with monitor chirps behind her and somebody calling for respiratory.

"What is it?"

"It's me," he said. "I got terminated yesterday. Job abandonment. Benefits end September thirtieth. COBRA is eleven-eighty-six-forty-eight for family."

Silence, then a clipped inhale.

"Are you safe right now?" she asked.

"Yeah. At Theo's."

"Good. I can't do this from a med-room doorway." A metal cart clattered in the background. "Call me at eight-fifteen Eastern. Have exact cash on hand, appeal deadline, and where you're sleeping this week. Don't freestyle."

"Okay."

"And Eli?"

"Yeah?"

"Don't disappear before that call."

The line clicked dead.

He stood with the phone still at his ear, listening to nothing, then set an alarm for 5:05, carried the atlas to the kitchen table, and wrote 8:15 CALL NORA at the top of the index card in all caps.
