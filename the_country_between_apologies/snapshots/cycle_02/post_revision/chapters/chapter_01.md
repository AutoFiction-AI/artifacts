# Chapter 1

At 9:30, Eli had the move-in passes damp in his hand and nowhere to go. The dorm loading lane sat jammed with hatchbacks and rental SUVs under a heat that felt sticky and metallic. A volunteer in a neon vest kept lifting both palms and calling, "Blue pass, curb lane. Green pass, garage lane. Hazards on, trunks open, fire lane stays clear," like he was directing aircraft. Eli kept one hand on the steering wheel. The paper edges had softened from sweat.

June had one knee up on the seat, lacing and unlacing the same running shoe while she watched the chaos through the windshield. Her dark hair was braided tight today, practical, and the little nick through her left eyebrow caught when she turned into the light. She looked like she was about to warm up for a meet, not move into a brick building where she did not need anyone to wake her up ever again.

"You still want me carrying the fridge?" Eli asked.

"I want you not throwing your knee out trying to prove anything," she said. "You can do the lighter bins and the fan. I'll take the fridge with whoever's nearest and not seventy."

"Fifty-two is not seventy."

"Right. Sorry. Very youthful fifty-two."

She smiled when she said it, quick and affectionate, already halfway somewhere else. Eli smiled back and heard himself doing the tone he used at board meetings, dry and easy, like nothing was harder than finding parking.

"For the record," he said, "I'm in peak moving shape. I stretched this morning."

"That's terrifying."

The line lurched. He eased the sedan forward six feet and reached back to steady the green document folder where it sat buckled under a bag of extra towels. Nora's paper stack bulged the zipper: tuition statement, immunization records, housing contract. The mortgage arrears notice rode in the back sleeve, sharp-edged and patient. He kept glancing at the folder, then forcing his eyes back to the brake lights.

At 7:12 that morning, before they left the house, he had moved fourteen thousand dollars out of the family emergency account into an old personal checking account. He had done it in bare feet on cold kitchen tile with the refrigerator humming and the app timer threatening to log him out. June's bursar balance was due by September 2 at 4:00 p.m. and the mortgage was already short. He had still moved the money. He had told himself it was a forty-eight-hour buffer. He had not told Nora.

A student volunteer with silver stars under her eyes tapped his window. "One cart per family, thirty minutes on dock, then garage. If your timer expires, we rotate you out." Inside, June and a sweaty freshman named Len wrestled the mini fridge onto a dolly while Len's mother barked spice-storage policy like she was briefing a field crew.

"You're doing great," Eli told her, and he sounded convincing enough that she laughed.

Up on the fourth floor, June's roommate Sloane had pens lined in three exact rows across her desk. "Sorry, I know this looks unhinged," she said, snapping caps back on with her thumbnail. "0.38 for planner boxes, 0.5 for lecture notes, 0.7 for headers. If one gets recapped wrong, my whole week slides off the rails." Eli followed June's pointing finger like an order sheet: mattress pad first, fan by the window, lamp clipped to the headboard, power strip taped where nobody would trip. By the time the milk crate and running shoes were squared under the bed, he had found the role he could still play: hold tape, lift bins, stay useful, stay out of the way.

At noon an RA named Tasha cut through with a practiced script. "Fourth floor, quick run: hall meeting at six, quiet hours start Sunday at ten, and the garage clock says twelve minutes to ticket-then-tow. Flashers don't buy extra time."

The word tow hit him harder than it should have. Everything today came with a timer, and he was already failing one nobody else could see.

By one o'clock the room looked inhabited. June's phone buzzed every thirty seconds with group chats. Horns and rolling carts leaked through the open window.

Nora texted while Eli flattened empty boxes in the hallway.

`How's room setup?`

He sent back a photo of June and Sloane wrestling a duvet into a cover, both of them cursing and laughing.

`Good. Organized chaos.`

Three dots appeared, vanished, returned.

`Thanks. Need ETA later. I start at 7 tomorrow and June's med forms are in your folder.`

Eli felt the folder's weight even from the hallway floor. The med forms sat in the same sleeve as the notice he had not mentioned; one errand would solve one problem and expose the other.

Then, after a pause:

`Also, mortgage letter is on the counter. We need to plan it tonight.`

The phone warmed in Eli's palm while his jaw locked on that last line. He pictured the kitchen at home exactly as it would look tonight and felt the route narrowing: white envelope from the mortgage servicer on the counter, reusable grocery bag hanging from the pantry knob, June's cereal still on the top shelf because she said dining hall food made her feel like she was eating inside a fluorescent bulb. Ordinary objects, all of them waiting for him to tell the truth or make a second lie.

He typed, deleted, typed again.

`I'll be back before dark.`

It was a lie smooth enough to send in two seconds.

He slipped the phone into his pocket and kept breaking down boxes until the cardboard edges burned his thumb.

At two, June checked the orientation app and announced that she had exactly forty-five minutes before her hall welcome. "There's a hall thing on conflict stuff, consent stuff, and where the free printers hide," she said. "You don't have to sit through it. Parents get their own session where everybody pretends they're chill and panic-cries into branded tote bags."

"I'll pass," Eli said.

"Coward."

"Strategist."

She shoved a roll of tape into his chest. "Can you take this and the trash down? We have no floor space left and I'm trying not to start college in a landfill."

He took the tape because it was a task with an end point. He could go downstairs, dump the bag, and come back. Nobody asked for confessions while he was carrying trash.

"On it."

When he came back, she was standing in the center of the room with her hands on her hips, scanning everything one last time. She nodded to herself in a way that was so Nora it made his throat hurt. Then she grabbed her lanyard, checked for her ID card, and turned to him.

"Okay. So."

"So."

"You don't have to do the weird drawn-out goodbye," she said. "I'm not dying."

"I had a speech prepared."

"Please don't."

"It was very moving. People in the hall were going to clap."

"Dad."

She stepped in and hugged him hard, quickly, one squeeze and out. It was not little-kid clinging, and it was not performative either. It was exact.

"Thank you for all this," she said into his shirt. "For real."

"You're welcome."

He opened his mouth on a sentence he could not hand her in a hallway full of strangers and open doors and somebody's speaker blasting old Kendrick down the corridor. What came out was smaller and still too honest.

"I'm not okay today," he said.

She pulled back and looked up at him, eyes sharp now. "What kind of not okay? Move-in sad, or don't-get-in-the-car not okay?"

"The kind where I keep thinking about getting on the highway and not taking my exit."

June went still. "Then don't freelance it. Go straight home and call Mom before you hit the bridge."

"Yeah," he said.

"I'm serious."

"I know."

"You're going straight home, right? Mom said tomorrow's a mess and she needs the forms tonight."

The question came out casual, but she watched him like she watched race splits on her phone, waiting for the number that told the truth.

"Yeah," he said. "I'll head out as soon as I get the car."

"Text both of us when you're back so she doesn't spiral and I don't spiral."

"I will."

"And don't do that thing where you stop for gas and accidentally buy, like, twelve jerky sticks and call it dinner."

"I hate that you know me this well."

"I know your sins." She kissed his cheek, already moving toward the door. "Love you."

"Love you too, Junie."

She winced at the nickname but did not argue, which felt like mercy.

He watched her join a cluster of freshmen in the hall. She slid into their rhythm in under ten steps, matching their pace and jokes, already half turned toward a girl in a thrifted denim jacket who was explaining where to find the cheapest coffee before eight a.m. June did not look back.

Eli stood in the doorway a little too long until Sloane's father came in carrying an armful of hangers and said, "You all set?"

"Yeah," Eli said, stepping aside. "All set."

The elevator down was full of parents holding empty bins like props from a finished play. A woman in scrubs with a tote bag that read NURSES ARE A FORCE OF NATURE kept dabbing under her eyes with the back of her wrist and laughing at herself each time. A man in golf clothes was trying to set up autopay for his son's meal card and swearing quietly at the app because it kept rejecting a security code by one digit. Eli recognized the panic in both of them and gave neither of them anything except a small nod.

In the garage, the air was ten degrees cooler and smelled like hot rubber and damp concrete. He loaded the empty bins into the trunk, slid behind the wheel, and closed the door. The sound in the car changed immediately. Outside noise flattened into a hum. He could hear his own breathing and the faint rattle in the passenger door that Nora had been telling him to fix for six months.

He turned the key to accessory but not ignition and sat there.

A shuttle bus hissed to a stop two levels up. Tires chirped. Someone dropped a metal water bottle and cursed. Time moved in chunks. He watched people leave with blank carts and return with children and lamps and rolled rugs, then leave again alone.

On the floor behind the passenger seat he spotted a crushed granola-bar wrapper from June's last track meet, still wedged under the mat despite two vacuum runs. He hooked it with his shoe and left it there. A ponytail elastic looped around the seat-belt buckle, stretched translucent from sun, and a yellow highlighter cap had fused to the cup-holder liner in August heat. He had spent years driving this car to school pickups, urgent-care visits, weekend grocery runs where June raided the cart for cherries before checkout. He had cursed the crumbs and receipts and sock lint. Now the mess looked like evidence, and the thought of cleaning it all out tonight felt like scraping her out of the car.

His phone lit up.

`Nora: You rolling?`

His thumb hovered over the keyboard, then dropped. By the time he finally exhaled, the screen had dimmed.

The transfer sat in him like swallowed metal. Fourteen thousand moved at 7:12 a.m., hidden before coffee, hidden through hugs and boxes and dorm jokes. He had told himself it was temporary, a forty-eight-hour buffer. In plain language, he had moved family emergency cash, lied to Nora about leaving campus, and was preparing to skip Monday's audit call while tuition and mortgage deadlines sat in the seat beside him. His shirt stuck to his back. His pulse thudded high in his throat.

The green folder was on the passenger seat now, buckled in where June had been. A corner of the mortgage envelope stuck out of the sleeve. He pulled it free, read the due date he already knew, and refolded it on the old crease. Then he worked through the rest: June's med forms, the tuition bill, a printout of his own calendar from work with Monday's audit call circled at 8:00 a.m. in Nora's neat handwriting. Every page pushed him toward the same decision he kept refusing.

He set everything back where it had been and did not start the car.

Nora texted at 3:18.

`Need a real ETA, Eli. I've got to pack lunch and chart before sleep.`

He could hear her saying it, not angry yet, just narrowing the frame to tasks because that was how she kept anxiety from turning feral. He typed,

`Just leaving campus now. Traffic was locked up.`

That was the first deception that felt like an action and not a sentence. He sent it and sat another twenty minutes in the same parking space.

A family in matching orientation T-shirts loaded back into a minivan three spots over. The father had a sunburn line around his wedding ring where he must have taken it off for golf all summer. He waved at Eli through the windshield with that reflexive driver-to-driver politeness, then backed out, careful and patient, and disappeared.

Eli took his own ring in his fingers and turned it once. The silver edge pressed a pale groove into skin that had shaped itself around metal for twenty-two years.

He started the engine at 3:47.

He drove out of the garage and followed signs through campus traffic that moved like clogged blood. Students crossed against lights in packs. A city bus cut in front of him and stalled half in the lane while the driver lowered a ramp for a passenger with a rolling walker. Eli waited. Nobody honked. He drummed the steering wheel with his thumb and watched heat shimmer above the hood.

At a red light near the bridge, his phone buzzed with a new message from June.

`Forgot to ask - did you leave the Brita pitcher in my desk crate or on the floor by bed?`

He typed, `Left side of the desk crate.`

`hero`

Then:

`Text mom when you're home pls. She's pretending she's chill and she's not chill.`

His throat tightened on the three words, gratitude and accusation braided together so tightly he could not separate them.

`Will do.`

He did not put the phone back into the cup holder right away. He held it in his palm until the light changed and the car behind him gave one polite chirp.

By the time he reached the interstate split, the sky had gone that late-afternoon white that made every sign too bright. Eastbound lanes rose to the right toward home: hospital badges, garbage bins at the curb, the couch indentation where Nora always fell asleep on her side after a double shift. Westbound carried him toward the airport and then farther, names on signs he had never needed for anything. He held the middle lane one beat too long.

His phone lit up in the cup holder: Nora calling.

He answered on speaker.

"Where are you?" Nora said.

"Near the bridge. Traffic's bad."

"Then listen. June's bursar payment is six thousand four hundred eighty by September second at four p.m. If we miss it, they slap a hold on her account. I need you home in forty minutes with that folder on the table. No errands."

He watched the eastbound lanes climb away to the right. "I'm on my way."

"Say your exit."

He could not do it. "Tunnel's breaking up. I'll be there soon."

He killed the call before she answered.

He had taken this interchange hundreds of times. His body knew the right lane by memory. This time he stayed middle through the first decision point, then left as if he were avoiding a slow truck, then left again.

A tanker truck boxed him in on the left and an on-ramp dumped a line of cars into his lane on the right. He checked his mirror, signaled, and eased onto the wider westbound artery.

The eastbound exit slid past in his side mirror and disappeared.

Nora called right back. He muted it and kept both hands on the wheel.

He had still not said the sentence that mattered: I took fourteen thousand from emergency, and I'm leaving you with mortgage letters and tuition deadlines while I drive away.

He kept going.

Outside the city the lanes widened and the noise changed from stop-and-go brakes to steady interstate roar, tires on old seams in the asphalt thudding a rhythm into his chest. Billboards replaced campus brick: injury lawyers, casino buses, a church with SERVICE TIMES in red LEDs missing one segment so S looked like 5. On the shoulder, a shredded retread twitched like a dead snake whenever trucks pushed air over it.

On the first long curve, the green folder skated toward the edge of the passenger seat, and he shoved it back with two fingers without taking his eyes off the road.

He drove with both hands on the wheel, back straight, speed exactly at the limit. If anyone looked in, they would see a competent middle-aged man in a clean polo, a green folder on the passenger seat, and nothing visibly wrong.

By the time her second call dropped to voicemail, the city skyline was gone behind him. The car pointed west and held there.

Eli Mercer kept driving.
