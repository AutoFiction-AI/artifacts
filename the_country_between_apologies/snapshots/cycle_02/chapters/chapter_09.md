# Chapter 9

By 4:47, Eli had already checked out and pulled under the gas-station canopy by the on-ramp. He had been awake before the motel alarm finished its second chirp because he had never really slept. The room’s high-country dry cold still clung to him, along with the heater smell of hot dust and old pennies.

A woman on the local station kept saying words like *advisory* and *deteriorating corridor* while color maps bloomed red over the mountains on the TV above the coffee urn. The map didn’t care about scale. It made the pass look the size of a thumbnail, a quick pinch between one town and the next. He knew better. He had driven enough winters in western Pennsylvania to respect snow, but this wasn’t Pennsylvania snow. This was wind lifting entire shoulders of it and throwing it sideways at steel.

He had pulled on his shirt slowly because the left side of his chest still remembered the awkward hunch from last night, kneeling in sleet to check the front wheel after a Kansas mechanic had done the cheapest brake adjustment he would agree to. The work had bought him less squeal, not confidence. The pedal still felt soft at the end. The mechanic had called it "serviceable if you keep your speed down and your luck up."

Luck was what people named bad math after the fact.

His burner had sat on the sink with one bar left. He had plugged it in while he shaved with hotel soap and looked at himself in the mirror that made everyone look jaundiced. He was fifty-two, with cheeks rough with gray, eyes bloodshot, and a bare ring finger pale where the band had sat for twenty-two years. He touched that line without thinking and then forced his hand down.

The plan in his head stayed simple because any complicated plan would have exposed how little control he had. He needed to cross the pass early and get west of the weather. He needed to find the day-labor listing Theo had circled near Glenwood Springs. He needed to start earning before Nora’s next deadline message landed and turned into one more number he couldn’t meet.

In the motel lot, his sedan had worn a crust of blown snow like powder sugar. He scraped the windshield with a plastic loyalty card from a grocery chain he didn’t remember signing up for. The card cracked at the corner. He kept scraping.

By then, half the pumps were blocked by pickups with chain boxes open. Men in gloves knelt in slush, feeding steel links behind tires. A county plow idled near the curb, amber lights stuttering against the predawn dark. Above all of it, the electronic sign pulsed in blunt capitals:

**I-70 MOUNTAIN CORRIDOR**  
**TRACTION LAW IN EFFECT**  
**COMMERCIAL CHAINS REQUIRED**

Inside, he paid cash for coffee and a stale breakfast burrito he never unwrapped. The clerk rang the little brass bell by the register, snapped his change onto the counter, and said, "You heading up now?"

"Trying to beat the worst of it," he said.

She gave him the look people gave strangers they would never see again. "Everybody says that. Mountain still makes its own schedule."

Outside, a kid in an orange highway jacket waved cars toward a chain-up turnout. He could not have been more than twenty. His nose was red from the cold and from constant wiping. He kept a folded paper in his chest pocket and checked it between interactions like a student cramming flashcards. When Eli rolled down the window, the kid leaned in and said, "Passenger vehicles need snow tires or approved traction devices past mile marker one-forty-four if this holds. You carrying cables?"

"Yeah. Trunk." Eli lied without pause. He had looked at cables inside the gas station and then put them back when he saw the price.

The kid glanced at Eli’s front tire, then back at him. "Tread’s getting thin. If they flip this to passenger chain law, patrol will pull you over up top."

"How long before that happens?"

"Could be twenty minutes. Could be never. Radar says bands are stacking."

Eli nodded like he had absorbed advice instead of dodging it. "Appreciate it."

"I mean it, man," the kid said, voice sharpening. "Turnout’s right there. If you don’t have gear, wait till daylight and reevaluate."

Eli put the car in drive. "I’ll keep it slow."

The kid slapped the side of the door twice, frustrated and already moving to the next truck.

He merged behind a fuel tanker and felt the familiar relief of motion, the cheap false relief that had carried him across four states. On straight interstate it could pass for strategy. In the foothills it was just denial with momentum.

The first miles stayed manageable. Light snow fell on wet pavement, and his wipers stayed on intermittent. The tanker’s taillights glowed steady red ahead of him, two points he could lock onto while the sky went from black to bruised gray. He breathed with the engine noise. He breathed in for four counts and out for four. He did not call Nora. He did not call June. He told himself he would call once he was through.

By the time the grade steepened, the lane shoulders disappeared under wind-drifted powder and the red lights ahead began to blur at the edges. Trucks shifted down and the whole highway changed pitch. You could hear strain in it: diesel groan, chains clattering from trailers, tires hissing on a slurry of melt and freeze.

His hands tightened on the wheel not from fear exactly but from concentration, that managerial focus he used in conference rooms when audits turned hostile. He told himself to keep variables visible, preserve options, and not panic in front of people.

The mountain took visibility first.

It happened in less than a minute. One gust pushed loose snow across the lanes. Then another. Then the windshield became a static screen of white shards moving sideways at impossible speed. The tanker’s lights vanished as if someone had unplugged them. His high beams only made it worse, a bright wall two feet from the hood. He snapped them off. The world narrowed to the faint shadow of the dashed line appearing and disappearing under the left edge of the car.

He backed off the gas. He dropped to thirty-five, then thirty. There was no shoulder visible. There was no guardrail visible. There was only slope and blind depth somewhere beyond the white.

A shape loomed suddenly on his right: a box truck crawling with hazards on. He had no memory of seeing it approach. He had drifted left without feeling it and corrected too fast, tires biting then floating. The rear of his car stepped out half a foot and came back with an ABS chatter that traveled up the brake pedal into his shin.

"Come on," he said to the wheel. "Come on, come on."

There were no graceful decisions left, only tiny corrections.

Then the line of brake lights ignited ahead all at once, red through white like a string of flares.

He hit the brake pedal and felt the first inch go soft. He pumped. The second push grabbed, released, grabbed again. The sedan shuddered. Somewhere up front metal hit metal with a sound like a dropped dumpster and then three more impacts stacked behind it. Horns began and did not stop.

He aimed for what he thought was the right shoulder and found nothing but churned snow and hidden rut. The steering wheel went light in his hands. The rear swung. He countersteered and overcorrected. The passenger side kissed a guardrail hard enough to fling him against the belt. He heard the crack in his chest before he felt pain, a green-stick snap under the left nipple, and then pain arrived in a white sheet hotter than the cold outside.

Airbags blew, powder and chemical stink filling the cabin. The windshield starred from the passenger side inward. His forehead hit something despite the bag, maybe visor, maybe frame. There was a bright flash, then dark specks swimming.

Another vehicle struck his rear quarter and spun him the rest of the way sideways until the front bumper jammed into snowpack against the barrier. Engine stalled. Wipers froze mid-sweep. The radio kept playing one bar of a country song, cut out, restarted, cut out.

For a few seconds he could not tell if he was breathing because every breath came in broken segments. He tasted salt and copper. Warmth ran down between his eyebrows and dripped from his nose onto the deployed airbag.

Outside, silhouettes moved in and out of white. A man shouted, "Is anyone trapped in the gray sedan?" It took Eli too long to recognize the sedan was his.

"I’m here," he said, but his voice stayed inside the car.

He tried the door. It opened six inches and stopped where the frame had buckled. Snow and exhaust blew in through the gap. Somewhere near his rear bumper a woman was crying in short, furious bursts, as if she was angry at her own fear. A child coughed, wet and repetitive.

He fumbled for the phone in his coat pocket. The screen stayed black. He held the power button with a shaking thumb until nothing changed and the battery icon finally appeared with a red line and then died again.

He laughed once, sharp, more air than sound. It hurt enough to make him stop.

A state trooper braced at the door gap and shouted over the wind. "Sir, eyes on me. Stay seated. We’re locking this lane down. Can you move your legs?"

"Yeah. I can move. My bag’s in back. Green folder. I need—"

"Paper waits." Her voice had that practiced no-argument tone nurses and cops shared. "Body first. Any neck pain?"

"No. Rib. Left side. I can’t get a full breath."

"You’re moving enough air to talk. Good. Don’t twist. EMTs are coming car by car. Name?"

"Eli Mercer."

"Okay, Eli. I’m Trooper Chen. Look at me." He did. "Seatbelt stays on until we clear this side. Stay with me. You understand?"

He nodded.

She moved on to the next car, disappearing into blowing snow. Through the cracked windshield he saw her stop at a minivan, then kneel to eye level with someone in the back seat. She kept one hand on the window frame while she talked, steadying herself against wind and against whatever she was seeing inside.

The wait lasted ten minutes or an hour; time in the whiteout had no unit. EMTs arrived with orange sled bags and moved with clipped efficiency that looked rude until you understood it was mercy. The paramedic assigned to Eli leaned in through the bent door. "I’m Dario, Summit EMS. I’m gonna palpate ribs, check lungs, then move you. You’re gonna hate me for thirty seconds. Ready?"

"No."

"Fair answer. Still have to do it."

Dario pressed along Eli’s left side, counting under his breath in Spanish. At the sixth rib Eli sucked air and swore hard enough to fog the inside of the windshield.

"There it is," Dario said. "Could be a fracture, could be a brutal contusion, but chest gets treated like chest. Any blood thinners?"

"No."

"Lose consciousness?"

"Don’t think so."

"That doesn’t chart. Did you black out?"

"Then no."

"Good enough for roadside. We’re getting you out on my count. Keep your left arm tight. Don’t hero this."

They eased him through the door gap sideways. The first step onto packed snow sent pain from rib to spine so fast his vision tunneled. Dario’s hand locked on his belt and hauled him upright before he pitched forward. Wind took his breath and replaced it with diesel and burnt rubber.

The crash scene resolved in jagged pieces as they moved him between vehicles. A jackknifed semi blocked two lanes, trailer angled like a snapped bone. A Subaru sat with its hood folded into a V, steam rising through snow. The woman he had heard crying was now cursing at no one in particular while she held a teenager in a hockey jacket against her side. The kid kept saying, "I’m fine, Mom, I’m fine," then vomited yellow Gatorade onto the shoulder.

Somebody’s suitcase had burst open in the slush. Sweaters and a child’s stuffed octopus lay half buried, one plastic eye staring up through ice crystals.

Eli watched the octopus as they loaded him into the ambulance because it gave him something fixed to look at.

Inside, heat hit him hard and made him nauseous. Dario cut away Eli’s shirt at the left seam and taped leads to his chest. Another medic cleaned the blood on his forehead with saline that felt like needles. The wound was shallow but long enough to need closure.

"Rate your pain one to ten," Dario said.

"Seven till I breathe. Nine when I do."

Dario almost smiled. "That math checks out." He clicked something on the monitor. "Any allergies?"

"Penicillin rash as a kid."

"Copy. Insurance card with you?"

Eli stared at the ceiling panel. "Maybe. Wallet’s in my coat."

"We’ll hand it to registration."

He turned his head toward the rear windows. Through whited glass he could see Trooper Chen directing cars backward down the on-ramp, one gloved hand up, one hand on her radio. She looked smaller than her voice had sounded.

The ambulance dropped through switchbacks toward lower elevation. Every curve rolled pain through his left side in a fresh arc. He kept trying to count turns and losing track at four. His body wanted stillness and there was none available.

At High Valley Medical Center, they parked him under a bay light that buzzed like a trapped wasp. The automatic doors opened and closed on cold air and warm disinfectant. Triage moved fast because crash days did that; everyone in the building had the same weather in their bones.

A nurse caught his wrist for a cuff reading while steering the gurney toward triage. "You got lucky," she said, not unkindly. "No flail chest from what I can feel. Don’t spend that luck all at once."

In the curtained bay she set a clear plastic spirometer in his hand and tapped the yellow marker. "Seal your lips and pull to fifteen hundred."

He drew in. The marker jumped to six hundred and stopped. Pain bit under his left nipple and ran straight through to his spine. Air snapped out of him in a barked cough that bent him sideways and lit his vision white.

She caught his shoulder, folded the warmed blanket into a hard pad, and shoved it against his ribs. "Brace here. Short pulls, stacked. Do not try to win on the first breath."

He tried again in three smaller draws, jaw tight, eyes watering. He hit nine hundred, then one thousand. It was not enough to feel proud, but it kept panic from taking over.

"Good," she said. "Ten reps every hour while you’re awake. You stop doing that, you buy yourself pneumonia." She hung a small dose cup over his tray table. "I can page for IV pain meds before imaging, but they’ll fog you."

He thought about forms, transport, and the way time kept charging interest. "Give me pills. I need my head."

X-ray came first, then a quick CT for the head strike, then glue on the forehead cut that stung like salt in an open knuckle. A physician assistant came in afterward with an iPad and a voice trained to project calm. "Non-displaced fracture, left eighth rib," she said. "Concussion symptoms are mild right now. We can keep you for observation through late afternoon, or discharge now with meds, spirometer, and warning signs. Either way, no driving."

He gave a short laugh that turned into a cough. "That part solved itself."

"Observation adds another facility block on billing," she said, reading his face before he asked. "I’m not steering you. I’m giving you facts."

He stared at the fluorescent seam in the ceiling and made himself choose. "Discharge me now. I need whatever transport still exists before dark."

She nodded once. "Then keep your lungs expanded even though it hurts. Broken ribs punish people who guard."

The curtain opened again and a registrar stepped in with a rolling cart. She flexed her left hand once, then set her fingers on the keys.

"I know this is bad timing," she said, "but discharge won’t print until I lock demographics and payer."

Eli gave her his license. She typed fast, stopped, typed again. "Your employer plan through Allegheny Regional is returning inactive effective twelve-oh-one a.m. yesterday. Does that track?"

He watched her screen reflected in the plastic face shield clipped to the cart. "Yeah."

"I’m sorry. Then this visit probably has to process as self-pay unless you have alternate coverage."

"No alternate."

The registrar nodded once, no pity performance. "Today’s estimate after uninsured adjustment is two thousand seven hundred forty. That includes ED facility, imaging, provider, and meds dispensed here. First statement date projects November twentieth." She turned the monitor slightly so he could see line items scrolling in clean corporate font. "If you set terms now, minimum is one hundred fifteen monthly. I can set auto-debit or paper billing."

The number landed with more force than the rib had. He had expected ugly. He had not expected precise.

"Set it now," he said. "Paper billing. No auto-debit."

"Got it. Mailing address still Pittsburgh?"

The question made his throat close for a second. "Yes."

"Preferred phone?"

He gave her the burner number, then corrected one digit because pain meds had blurred his memory. She read it back. He nodded.

"I can flag hardship review with this packet if you sign today," she said, sliding a form onto the tray. "That usually pauses first-cycle late fees while documents process."

He signed. The pen dragged where his hand trembled.

When she left, he lay with the billing printout on his chest and watched the paper rise and fall in short jerks. He had moved fourteen thousand dollars before he left home and still managed to hit this place with less than four hundred cash left and no vehicle. That was the cleanest summary of him any spreadsheet had ever produced.

Near noon, a hospital social worker in a puffer vest found him with a clipboard and a bus timetable open on her phone. She set a paper cup of vending-machine hot chocolate on the tray and admitted it was terrible but warm.

"Tow lot confirmed your sedan is non-drivable," she said. "Front and side frame damage. They moved it to Continental Recovery off county road seven. Storage clocks at sixty-five a day starting midnight."

"Can I get my stuff?"

"You can, with ID, if they deem it safe to access. If airbags blew and there’s glass, they may escort you." She handed him a card. "Here’s the fork in transit terms: I can put you in a voucher cab to the lot now, but you’ll miss the four-thirty clinic shuttle west. Or you take the shuttle, keep moving, and eat one storage day."

"Any county bus after the van?"

"If roads reopen. Best case, county gets you as far as Glenwood tonight. Worst case, you’re stranded in Frisco." She scanned him with professional skepticism. "You have somewhere specific to go?"

He thought of Theo’s atlas folded in his duffel, of the circled listing, of how thin the plan sounded when spoken aloud. "A motel job lead. Not confirmed."

"Then choose the option that buys tomorrow."

He looked at the tow-lot card, then at the timetable in her hand. "Book me on the four-thirty van. I can eat one storage day. I can’t eat getting stuck here overnight."

She tapped his name into the manifest. "Done. I’ll have a tech bring what came in with you. If you need to contact family, courtesy phone is in the lobby."

He asked for his belongings. A tech brought a clear plastic bag with his wallet, keys bent at the ring, wet atlas, green document folder, and a coat stiff with dried blood at the collar. The phone was gone.

"Burner was dead in the vehicle," the tech said. "Tow crew logged electronics as unrecovered. Could still be under the seat."

Eli nodded and didn’t trust himself to speak. He wiped road grit from the atlas with a hospital towel and watched blue ink from Theo’s route notes feather slightly where moisture touched it.

He stepped outside into afternoon glare. Sky had opened to a harsh, polished blue as if the storm had never happened. Plows hissed past in wet arcs. The cold was clean now, almost cheerful, which felt like insult.

He sat on a bench by the smoking area sign and tried to inhale without stabbing pain. He had the billing packet, the tow-lot card, and one granola bar crushed flat in his coat pocket. He had no car, no stable phone, and no story he could tell in a single call without sounding like he was auditioning for forgiveness.

Inside the lobby, there was a public courtesy phone mounted near the volunteer desk. It had a beige plastic handset, a coiled cord, and tape over one crack. He stared at it for a full minute before he stood.

At the desk he asked to use it. The volunteer, an older man with tremor-soft hands and immaculate Denver Broncos socks visible under his scrub pants, passed him a notepad for numbers. "Line drops sometimes," the man said. "Hit nine first if you need outside."

Eli dialed Nora from memory and missed one digit, then redialed. Voicemail. He hung up before the tone. He dialed June and got voicemail again, hers still the old recording with music in the background and a rushed "leave it, I’ll call back" from before she had reasons not to.

He looked at the notepad, at his own handwriting cramped and uneven from pain. He could not build the whole bridge from this hallway. He could barely stand up straight.

He asked the volunteer, "Can I send one text from your desk computer? Just through web message? Mine died."

The man looked around, lowered his voice like they were planning a heist. "Officially no. Unofficially if you keep it short and don’t log into anything weird, Marcie won’t yell at me." He slid the keyboard over.

Eli typed with two fingers because his left side burned when he reached.

*Alive. Crash in the pass. At High Valley clinic. Rib cracked. Phone dead. I’m alive.*

He sent it to Nora’s number and, after a pause long enough to hurt, to June’s.

He offered no explanation, no timeline, and no apology script. It was only proof of pulse.

Replies came before he left the chair.

From Nora: *Where exactly are you? Are you admitted?*

From June: *What pass? Are you serious right now?*

He typed to Nora first.

*High Valley Medical Center, Silverthorne side. ER discharge, not admitted. Cracked left rib and a forehead cut. I’m taking the 4:30 clinic van to Frisco, then county west if roads reopen.*

Then to June:

*Eisenhower pass on I-70. I’m serious. Pileup at dawn. I’m banged up, but I’m on my feet.*

Nora answered before he could flex his fingers loose.

*Then no disappearing. Text when you board. Text when you hit Frisco. If county cancels, you stay in the station and call me from that courtesy phone. I’m not guessing anymore.*

He sent back one line.

*Okay.*

He copied the van departure and transfer times from the social worker’s timetable into the thread exactly as Nora demanded, then printed the schedule and folded it into the green folder with the bill.

By four o’clock, pain medication had dulled the edge of the fracture into a heavy, mean throb that synchronized with each step. He boarded the clinic van with six other people carrying plastic bags of discharge supplies. An older rancher in a back brace argued cheerfully with the driver about whether green chile counted as breakfast food. A teenage snowboarder with a splinted wrist kept tapping video edits on his phone, replaying the same trick and crash loop with detached fascination.

Eli sat by the window, folder on his lap, and watched the mountain corridor slide past in cleaned-up afternoon light: tow trucks dragging wrecks onto flatbeds, sand trucks laying fresh grit, abandoned tire chains glittering at the shoulder like shed snakeskin.

When the van climbed toward the transit station, they passed the turnout where the orange-jacket kid had warned him at dawn. The place was empty now except for one crushed coffee cup and black streaks where tires had spun uselessly in slush. He could still hear the kid’s voice: *If you don’t have gear, wait till daylight and reevaluate.*

He had heard it clearly. He had chosen motion anyway.

The bus heater rattled overhead. Somewhere behind him, the rancher laughed until he coughed and swore. Eli pressed two fingers to the edge of the taped wound on his forehead, then to the pale ring mark on his hand, as if checking that both absences were still there.

They were.
