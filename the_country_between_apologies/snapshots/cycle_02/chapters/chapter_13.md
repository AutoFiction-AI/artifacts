# Chapter 13

The impound office sat behind two layers of chain-link and a strip of razor wire that glinted like fishing line in the late-afternoon sun. The trailer was beige, dented around the doorframe, and cold from a wall unit that rattled as if somebody had dropped loose bolts in it. Eli stood at the counter with his green folder open to the insurance estimate and his thumb pressed over the number he had kept reading all week: **$5,110 less deductible**.

Renee, the clerk behind the scratched plexiglass, wore a denim vest over a thermal shirt and kept a peppermint tucked in her cheek. She had silver roots showing under drugstore red, and every time the printer jammed she tapped the side panel twice with the back of her knuckles before opening it. The move looked practiced, the way somebody knocks on old pipes before turning a faucet.

After finishing the dawn linen run in Glenwood, Eli had spent six hours on a shuttle to this Utah yard Mountain West used for western salvage, just to sign forms and collect what he thought was his only usable money.

"You're Eli Mercer?" she asked.

"Yeah. I talked to Mountain West this morning. They said the check was ready once I signed the release."

She scanned his license, frowned at her screen, and pushed the card back through the slot. "The check went to the lienholder three business days ago."

Eli blinked and stayed still long enough to feel the ache in his left rib sharpen against the edge of the counter. "No. The car was totaled. This was settlement money."

"Right," Renee said. "Settlement money. There is an active title claim. RidgeLine Title Finance. They filed first-position lien rights on the VIN. We paid lienholder direct."

He laughed once, thin and sharp. "Paid them what? All of it?"

Renee slid two stapled packets under the slot. "Net payout was four thousand nine hundred eighty after deductible and processing. It all went to RidgeLine. You still have a deficiency balance with them."

The word hit him harder than she said it. **Deficiency.** Like he had turned up short as a math result and that was the whole story.

"You told me on the phone I'd get something," he said.

"I said if there was excess, we'd issue excess. There was not excess."

He opened the top packet. The paragraphs were full of little boxed terms in bold type: **secured interest, priority claimant, proceeds application, deficiency carry-forward**. His eyes kept snagging on legal phrases and sliding off the line before he finished the sentence. He had managed compliance audits for sixteen years. He knew this language in conference rooms, clean shirts, and catered sandwiches. Here it came on thin paper that smelled like hot toner and glue, and it ate his name whole.

"I need transportation," he said. "I need to get east. I've got deadlines. Tuition deadlines. Court stuff."

Renee gave him a look that was not unkind and not useful. "I can print your accounting breakdown. That is what I can do."

"I had wages come in from a motel job. I've got some cash. If I pay something right now, can they release part of it?"

"Release part of what?"

"The payout."

She did not sigh, but her jaw tightened. "There is nothing to release. RidgeLine held title, RidgeLine got paid, the rest is between you and them."

He flipped to the second page and found the line that mattered because it looked like mercy from a distance: **Any excess proceeds, if any, shall be remitted to registered owner.** He held that line with his finger as if he could drag it into a different paragraph by force.

"It says here—"

"If any," she said, already pointing. "There was not any."

His voice dropped before he could stop it. "I need enough for a car that runs. I don't need good, I need running."

Renee reached for her keyboard, stopped, and looked at him over the top of her glasses. "Mr. Mercer, I can call RidgeLine and put you on speaker. That is all I can offer."

He nodded because the alternative was punching the counter and breaking a hand he needed.

She dialed with two fingers. While it rang, she took a black notebook from under the desk and wrote down the time of call in careful block letters. Her ring finger was bare except for a pale white band where a ring had been for years.

A recorded voice thanked them for choosing RidgeLine Title Finance, then listed five options before giving them hold music that sounded like an electric piano in an empty mall. Eli stared at the poster taped beside the window: **NO VERBAL ABUSE OF STAFF. NO EXCEPTIONS.** Somebody had drawn a frown face in ballpoint at the corner.

A woman finally picked up. Her name was Brielle. Her voice had that call-center brightness that wore thin after each sentence. There was a television or a tablet somewhere near her, cartoon voices bouncing in and out behind her words.

Renee gave account number, VIN, claimant code, date of loss.

Brielle said, "I am showing the insurance disbursement applied this week. Current deficiency stands at one thousand six hundred ninety-three dollars and eleven cents."

Eli leaned toward the speakerphone. "You took the whole payout and I still owe you almost seventeen hundred?"

"I am showing principal, accumulated finance charges, and late fees through default date," she said. "Would you like me to read transaction detail?"

"No, I would like a car."

"Sir, the collateral unit was totaled. There is no collateral release available."

"I know it's totaled. I needed that money to buy something else so I could work." He heard himself speeding up and tried to slow down. "I can send you payments. I can start this week. You can keep a piece and give me enough to move."

"That is not an option under your contract."

"Then tell me what the option is."

"Payment in full on deficiency or approved monthly arrangement. I can offer a hardship plan with automatic withdrawal."

He barked a laugh and covered his mouth too late. "Automatic withdrawal from what account?"

Brielle paused. The cartoon voice in the background said something about a dragon egg. "If you are currently unbanked, we can mail payment coupons."

He almost asked where she lived, what rent cost there, whether she ever stood in a trailer office and listened to somebody explain she was still in debt after losing the only useful thing she owned. He did not ask. He swallowed metal taste and looked at the poster again.

"Mail the coupons," he said.

Brielle read a disclosure so long that Eli forgot the beginning before she finished the end. He said yes at the points where she needed yes. Renee circled signature lines while the disclosure rolled on. Institutional language overran everything human in the room. His deadlines, Nora's voice on voicemail, June's meal plan cuts, his rib that still clicked on deep breaths. None of it fit in the boxes. The boxes won anyway.

When the call ended, Renee pushed the packets back. "I still need one signature to release personal property."

"I can get my things?"

"Anything not attached. Ten-minute escort. You cannot remove plates, battery, or installed equipment."

He signed where she pointed. The signature looked like somebody else's, the loops too wide, the last name collapsing into a line. Renee stamped both copies and called for yard escort.

A man named Luis came in from outside with gravel in the tread of his boots and a white medical tape strip wrapped around his right wrist. He smelled like diesel and cheap soap. When he saw Eli pressing his side, he jerked his chin toward the door and said, "Take it slow. Nobody times you unless you run."

Outside, the wind shoved cold under Eli's jacket and set every loose chain on the fence chattering. Rows of wrecked cars sat packed tight, windshields starred, airbags limp as old pillows. Eli followed Luis down lane C, past a pickup burned black at the front end and a minivan with a car seat still buckled in back. He told himself not to look at the seat. He looked anyway.

His sedan sat angled against a curb of broken asphalt. The front end was folded high and off-center, hood kinked like bent cardboard. The driver-side door had a yellow X sprayed on it. The back window was gone and somebody had taped clear plastic over the gap that snapped in the wind.

Luis checked his watch. It had a cracked crystal and an Elmo sticker under the face where the numbers should have been plain. "Start now," he said. "I will stay by the gate. Holler if glass gets you."

Eli opened the passenger door because the driver side stuck. He had to brace one foot on the tire and pull with both hands. Pain ran from his rib into his spine in a hot line that made his vision gray at the edges for one second, then clear again. The car smelled like antifreeze, wet fabric, and that spent-airbag chemical that never quite left.

He reached into the footwell and found the green folder wedged under the seat rail, corners damp but pages intact. He found Theo's marked atlas in the backseat with a crescent of dried blood on the cover that might have been his from the crash night. He found two socks, one work glove, a phone charger with copper wire exposed at the tip, and a plastic grocery bag tied around a pair of boots.

He did not find the small flashlight June had thrown in his cup holder on move-in day. He crouched and checked under the front seat anyway, then remembered seeing it roll under the brake pedal during the whiteout right before impact. A stupid object to miss. He missed it.

He stood too fast and saw stars. He put one palm on the roof and waited for the yard to stop tilting.

Luis called from the gate. "You good?"

"Yeah," Eli said, though his breath came short and ragged. "I'm done."

On the walk back, they passed the office trailer where a radio played classic country through static. Luis pointed at Eli's folder and said, "Keep copies on your phone too. Paper walks away."

"Burner phone," Eli said. "No scan app."

Luis nodded as if that fit a category he recognized. "Then take photos and text to somebody who keeps his shit organized. Everybody has one cousin like that."

Eli gave a dry smile. "I've got one son like that."

Luis grinned, and for the first time Eli noticed the missing tooth at the side of his smile. "Even better. Sons hold grudges and spreadsheets forever."

Back in the trailer, Renee handed him a receipt for released property and another for storage charges he still owed if he wanted additional access later. She had switched from peppermint to a cinnamon stick and chewed it hard enough to splinter the end.

"You need a ride to transit?" she asked without looking up.

He nodded.

"City bus stop is half a mile north. Route 9 gets you to the intermodal depot. Comes every forty minutes if the driver is in a good mood."

He thanked her. She shrugged once, then tapped the printer again before it jammed.

The walk to Route 9 cut along a frontage road where semis coughed up dust and brake heat. Eli carried the duffel in one hand and the folder under his arm. He had learned to hold his left side rigid when he walked. If he let it swing naturally, the rib pain turned sharp and wet, like something tearing under tape.

By the time the bus came, his shirt stuck cold to his back. The driver wore a navy knit cap and drove with one hand while eating sunflower seeds from a paper cup. He let Eli board without comment, but when Eli dropped exact fare in the box and turned for a seat, the driver called after him.

"You headed to the depot, keep your bag looped through your arm if you sleep."

"Not tonight."

The driver snorted. "Everybody says that." He tipped the cup of shells toward the windshield. "Depot makes liars out of all of us."

The intermodal terminal in Salt Lake looked newer outside than it felt inside. Glass facade, brushed steel signs, then fluorescent glare and a ceiling too low for the sound it held. Announcements bounced off tile and concrete until every sentence arrived with an echo half a beat late. Diesel exhaust leaked in each time the automatic doors opened. There was a coffee stand closed behind a gate and a vending row where one machine hummed but did not light up.

Eli sat on a molded plastic bench near Gate D and spread his money inside the folder where people passing could not count it. Two hundreds. One twenty. Three tens. A five. Six ones. Four quarters, three dimes, a nickel. **$271.35.**

He wrote the number on a sticky tab from the back of the atlas and stuck it to the inside cover. Then he listed what he needed before morning.

Ticket west to Reno or east toward Pittsburgh.

Food.

Phone minutes.

Pain pills if he could find cheap over-the-counter.

A place to lie flat for two hours without getting his bag lifted.

Wire transfer to Nora.

He stared at the list until the order stopped shifting. Food moved up when his stomach cramped. Phone minutes moved up when he pictured missing a call from Theo or a message from Nora with a court date. The wire stayed on the page like a dare.

He checked eastbound first because that had been the point since Nora's voicemail. The self-serve kiosk froze twice on the destination screen, then finally printed options in pale gray ink.

Salt Lake to Pittsburgh, next departure 7:10 p.m., forty-seven hours with transfers: **$412.50**.

Cheaper fare seventy-two hours out with overnight layover in Omaha: **$289.00**.

He laughed without sound. If he bought the cheap ticket, he could eat and keep maybe ten dollars. He would arrive with ten dollars and a folder full of proof he had failed in three states.

Reno showed up at **$68.00** if he left at 10:25 p.m., six and a half hours with one stop. Theo had marked Reno in the atlas margin months ago with a blunt pencil and one sentence: **Casinos hire fast, chew people up faster. Still money.**

Money mattered more than speed now. Speed without money was another stunt.

He stood, folded the list into his pocket, and went looking for the wire window before he let himself bargain with hunger.

The money services counter sat beside the snack shop, separated by a waist-high rail and a sign that read **WIRE / BILL PAY / MONEY ORDERS**. A woman in her twenties worked behind glass with a stack of index cards propped under the monitor. She wore a lavender scrub top under her hoodie and had blue nitrile gloves tucked in her back pocket like she had come from another shift. A tiny enamel pin of a cartoon kidney sat on her lanyard.

When Eli stepped up, she flipped one of the index cards facedown. It read **EDTA tube = purple top** in neat block print.

"Sending or receiving?" she asked.

"Sending."

"Domestic?"

"Yeah. Pennsylvania."

She slid the form and a pen through the slot. "Fill every line. If your handwriting gets weird in section four, start over. Corporate kicks it back for less than that."

He wrote Nora's full name slowly. He had not written it by hand in years. The pen scratched over cheap paper. He wrote the amount line blank and held there.

"Fee depends on amount?" he asked.

"Starts at nine ninety-five."

"For how much?"

"Up to two hundred."

He did the math twice in his head and once on the back of the form.

If he sent one hundred fifty, fee nine ninety-five, remaining cash would be one hundred eleven and change after a Reno ticket.

One hundred fifty was tiny against what he owed. One hundred fifty was also not zero.

He wrote **150.00** and slid the form back.

The clerk typed, frowned at the screen, and looked up. "You put relationship as spouse. Keep it that way or change it to legal spouse?"

He almost asked what legal spouse meant here, then heard how tired he sounded in his own head. "Just spouse."

She nodded and kept typing. Her nails were short and chewed flat, the skin around her thumbs cracked and reddened from sanitizer. "Cash only today. Card system is down till six."

He passed bills through the slot. She counted aloud without performative cheer: "Twenty, forty, sixty..." Her pace never changed.

"You want text notification to recipient?" she asked.

"No."

"You sure? It can save calls."

"I need to send the receipt myself."

She printed two slips, stamped one, and circled the tracking number with a green highlighter. "Keep this dry," she said. "Thermal ink disappears if you leave it in a hot car."

He let out one short breath that might have been a laugh. "Not a problem anymore."

She looked up, caught something in his face, and did not ask follow-ups. Instead she tapped her index cards and said, "Good luck, Mr. Mercer. I'm gonna pass phlebotomy if this place kills me first."

"You will," he said, and meant it.

At the bench he photographed the receipt with the burner phone, then typed Nora's number from memory. His thumbs hovered over the keypad longer than the message took.

**Sent $150 wire today. Tracking number follows. More Monday if Reno lead works.**

He added the number and hit send before he could sand it down into a speech.

The message delivered. No reply.

He looked at the food options anyway, because bodies did not care about moral theater. The snack shop had wrapped sandwiches sweating under plastic and a roller grill with hot dogs turning too slowly. He bought a turkey sandwich that smelled mostly like refrigerator and a bottle of water. Nine dollars and twelve cents gone.

He sat with the sandwich and took small bites because his stomach cramped if he ate fast after a day on coffee. At the far end of the bench, a woman in a puffer coat slept with her purse strap braided twice around her wrist while a man in paint-splattered work pants argued into a headset mic about weekend custody pickup. Nobody looked at anybody for long. Everyone checked their bags when an announcement cracked overhead.

His phone buzzed while he was capping the water.

Nora: **Received 150. Tuition grace ends Friday 5 p.m. Send dates and amounts, not estimates.**

No greeting. No anger marks. Just the lane she had chosen because that lane did not collapse under him.

He typed back: **Understood. Reno tonight. Will send next transfer Monday or Tuesday latest.**

He read it once. Too many words. He erased **latest** and sent.

Then he opened the atlas to Theo's notes. Reno page had three circles and arrows in pencil: **El Dorado laundry hires same week. Ask for night supervisor.** Another note underlined twice: **Do not front pain and then crash. Tell people what you can lift.** Theo's handwriting was blocky and slanted left, built by years of filling out forms in treatment centers and union classes and DMV offices. Practical penmanship from a man who had decided he would never again sign something without reading it.

Eli touched the margin note with one finger and felt the old shame rise with the same speed as always. It came with memory, not abstraction: Theo at nineteen in a county rehab waiting room, Eli standing by a vending machine pretending to answer work emails because he could not bear the intake counselor asking what support plan they had at home. He had called it triage then. The truth was he bailed on intake and left Theo to answer alone when they asked who would lock up pills and watch him nights. Heat climbed Eli's neck and his stomach clenched so hard he had to breathe through his teeth.

He bought more phone minutes from the kiosk because he would need them in Reno. Another twenty-two dollars. The receipt paper stuck to his damp fingers.

By seven the terminal had gone louder and colder, but he ignored it and dialed the number Theo had penciled beside **El Dorado laundry hires same week**. A woman answered on the third ring, her voice clipped over warehouse echo.

"Employment trailer."

"I'm coming in from Salt Lake tonight," Eli said. "Can I walk in tomorrow?"

"You can if you're in line by seven-thirty. Clipboard caps at twenty names."

"If I miss it?"

"Next intake window is Thursday. No holds."

He wrote it on the sticky tab in block letters: **7:30 a.m. / trailer behind garage / 20-name cap / Thursday if missed.** Missing one morning now meant pushing his first Reno check and blowing up Monday's promised transfer.

At eight-thirty he went to the ticket counter with his duffel over one shoulder and the folder under his arm. The agent wore a pressed white shirt and a tie with tiny trout on it. His badge said **Sandeep**. He had one thumbnail wrapped in beige tape and spoke with careful patience that felt learned, not natural.

"Destination?"

"Reno. Ten-twenty-five if there is a seat."

Sandeep typed, nodded, and printed boarding slip. "Sixty-eight dollars even. You have one checked bag included if under fifty pounds."

"Carry-on only."

"Boarding starts forty minutes prior. Keep ticket visible and ID ready."

Eli paid cash. Sandeep counted twice and tucked the bills under a clip weighted with a smooth river stone. A photo of two kids in soccer uniforms sat taped to his monitor. One had shin guards over jeans like he had rushed from school straight to practice.

As Sandeep handed over the ticket, he said, "You look like you need horizontal time. Quiet room upstairs is twelve dollars for two hours. Reclinable chairs. Not private, but safer than gate seats."

Eli looked at the remaining money in his folder tab. **$71.23.**

Twelve dollars for two hours flat on his back sounded like oxygen.

He pictured Nora's text. He pictured the wire receipt clipped behind the tuition statement, amount too small and still undeniable.

"I'll stay down here," he said.

Sandeep's mouth moved like he wanted to argue and thought better of it. "Then set an alarm. Last call does not wait for sleepers."

"I'm staying up," Eli said.

Sandeep gave him a look that said he heard that sentence ten times a shift.

Eli went back to Gate D and called Theo before he could lose his nerve.

Theo picked up on the fourth ring. Forklift backup beeps sounded in the background, then a door shut and the noise dropped.

"You all right?" Theo asked. No greeting, straight to triage.

"Yeah." Eli shifted on the bench and felt the rib catch. "I'm in Salt Lake. Heading Reno tonight."

"Why Salt Lake?"

"Impound and lender stuff. Insurance payout got swallowed by RidgeLine. All of it." He stared at the floor tiles while he said it. "Still owe them sixteen ninety-three."

Theo was quiet long enough for Eli to hear his own breathing through the phone speaker. Then Theo said, "Jesus."

"Yeah."

"You got cash for ticket and food?"

"Ticket bought. Sent one-fifty to Mom already."

Another pause. This one changed shape, less shock and more measurement. "You sent it before eating?"

"Before decent food. I had a sandwich."

Theo exhaled hard through his nose. "Okay. That is movement."

Eli waited for the lecture. It did not come.

Theo said, "Where are you sleeping when you hit Reno?"

"I've got an atlas note from you and a name at a casino laundry supervisor. If hiring is same-day, I can get a week-by-week room."

"If hiring is not same-day?"

"Then I make calls and take day labor until somebody gives me a shift. If nothing hits by Tuesday night, I call you before I spend my last forty."

"Don't skip that part," Theo said, voice sharpening. "You always jump from plan to catastrophe. Do the ugly middle in order."

Eli rubbed a thumb over the cracked edge of his ticket. "I'm trying."

"I know. Listen." Theo's tone flattened into the practical rhythm Eli remembered from the house rules speech in St. Louis. "Text me every transfer receipt, no exceptions. I'm not running your money yet. I'm your paper trail if somebody decides your payment never happened. Keep one thread in order: time sent, confirmation code, dollar figure. Skip the essays."

"Okay."

"And if you get tempted to ghost because it feels cleaner, call me first and say that exact sentence. I can work with ugly. I can't work with silence."

Eli looked through the glass doors at the loading bay where buses idled in clouds of white exhaust. "I almost didn't call tonight."

"I figured." Theo cleared his throat. "I'm glad you did anyway."

A voice in the background called Theo's name.

"Got to go," Theo said. "Eat something with salt before boarding. Your voice sounds like shit."

"Love you," Eli said, the words out before he could decide whether he deserved them.

Theo took one beat and answered, "Love you too. Keep moving the right direction this time."

The line clicked dead.

Eli sat with the phone in his hand until the screen went dark. He felt both better and worse, which seemed to be the chapter he was living now. Better because there was a line back to somebody who knew him in real time. Worse because that line came with terms, and he had run out of talent for pretending terms were insults.

He bought a packet of salted peanuts for two dollars and ate them slowly. The salt cut through the metallic taste in his mouth. He took two ibuprofen from a travel bottle and drank the rest of his water. His rib stayed loud anyway.

At ten the boarding area crowded fast with blankets, neck pillows, paper sacks from fast-food counters, plastic grocery bags tied in knots, and one cardboard cat carrier with key-poked air holes.

Eli opened the folder on his knees and checked the stack by touch: wire receipt dry in the sleeve, bus ticket folded to the barcode, insurance accounting flattened behind the tuition statement so no corner stuck out.

He had become a man who traveled with paper proof of each small step, the way probationers carried stamped cards. He understood the humiliation and the use. If he lost the feeling, he still had the facts.

When boarding began, Sandeep scanned tickets and sent people down the ramp in a steady rhythm. As Eli reached the scanner, Sandeep glanced at his face and asked quietly, "You ate?"

"Enough," Eli said.

Sandeep nodded once, like that was the answer he expected from men in Eli's condition. "Seat 18A, left side. Outlet is loose; hold the plug up with your shoe."

The bus smelled like vinyl, old coffee, and whatever cleaner they used between runs when they had time to use cleaner at all. Eli stowed the duffel overhead and took the window seat. The glass was cold enough to sting when he leaned his temple against it.

Outside, terminal lights smeared across the pane in yellow streaks.

Eli took out the wire receipt, unfolded it, and read Nora's name printed in block capitals above the amount. One hundred fifty dollars. Too small to fix anything. Large enough to remove one excuse.

The bus merged onto the freeway. Tire noise settled into a steady hum, low and relentless, like a machine set to keep counting whether he watched it or not. He slid the receipt back into the folder, zipped the folder shut, and looped the strap around his forearm the way the city driver had told him.

Sleep never came. He watched Utah thin into black desert and kept his hand on the folder until his fingers cramped.
