# Chapter 9

The needle went in wrong the first time. Kem felt the resistance of Dren's skin, the rubbery give of it, and his wrist jerked sideways. Dren hissed through his teeth.

"Sorry. I'm sorry."

"Just do it," Dren said. "Don't talk about it."

Kem set the needle back over the split in Dren's left forearm. Silo stone had opened it from mid-forearm nearly to the elbow. Vel had washed the grit out with creek water and spirits, but the flesh still gaped dark and wet around the edges.

A few feet away, Vel sat against a trunk with one arm locked over her cracked ribs. She had tried the first stitch herself, gotten the needle in, and stopped when the pain took her breath away. Now she watched Kem do the part she could talk him through and not do herself.

He knew what the job was supposed to be. He needed to bring the split edges together without tearing them worse and close the wound well enough that Dren kept the arm.

The second pass was better. It caught both edges of the wound, and the waxed thread dragged through in a wet pull that made his stomach jump. The line it made looked nothing like a clean seam. It looked like a child sewing up a split doll.

"Christ," Dren said.

"I know."

"Keep going."

Kem kept going. The third stitch went crooked. On the fourth he bit too deep on one side and barely caught the other, bunching the skin into a ridge that would scar ugly. Dren's forearm was tanned and ropey from years of hauling cargo. Every time the needle punched through, the muscle jumped under the skin.

The fifth stitch finally sat right, even and snug, close enough to clean that Kem stared at it for a beat before moving on. Then the sixth hit something fibrous under the skin, Dren grunted, and the small flare of competence vanished. By the seventh, his grip was cramping. Blood and wax slicked it.

Packed dirt pressed hard through Kem's knees. Late light slanted through the trees and turned the blood on Dren's arm almost black. Vel had not changed position. She was still against the trunk with her arms folded carefully over her ribs, breathing shallow and measured. She watched without helping, which was its own kind of instruction.

By the eighth stitch, rhythm started to replace panic. He pushed through, pulled, checked the gap, tied off. He still shook, but the shake belonged to the job now.

"You're pulling them uneven," Vel said.

"I know."

"The last two are going to scar."

"I know."

"Finish."

The ninth stitch pulled the seam mostly closed. It was ugly work. The thread crossed in dark Xs over swelling skin. Some of the twelve stitches were serviceable. Some were a mess. All of them held.

Dren flexed his fingers and then his wrist. The skin tugged. Nothing split.

"That's bad," he said.

"It's ugly."

He looked at the arm again. "Looks like you stitched it in a moving wagon." Another flex. A careful test of grip around the pack strap. "But it'll get me down the road. Thanks."

No one at the Vault had ever thanked him for bad work. There, thanks had marked rank and attendance, the right body in the right room. Dren had looked at the ugly seam and meant it.

The needle went back into its cloth wrap after he cleaned it with the last of the spirits.

The strip Dren had tied over the stitches was already pinking through.

"Too loose," Vel said.

She pushed off the trunk to inspect it, got one knee under her, and stopped there with her teeth set hard. The ribs caught her halfway up. Kem moved before she could tell him not to. He took her good elbow and felt how carefully she was holding the left side of herself together.

"Fold another cloth," she said when she had enough breath for it. "Pad under the seam first, then wrap. If his fingers go cold, you've done it wrong."

Dren watched Kem tear a strip from the cleanest rag they had left and fold it double. "Comforting."

"It's better than bleeding on your boots," Vel said.

Kem slid the pad over the stitches and wrapped again, trying not to look too long at the swollen, uneven line his thread had made. The first turn sat too low. Vel corrected him with a brief motion in the air. The second was better. By the third wrap he could feel the difference between pressure that steadied and pressure that crushed. Dren kept his jaw locked and his arm still, except for the one time the cloth bit wrong and he said, "If you kill the hand after all that, I'm going to be offended."

"Then keep it alive," Vel said. "Do the sling next. Wrist higher than elbow."

Kem tore another strip, longer this time, and looped it under Dren's wrist and around the back of his neck. The first knot slid at once.

"That's decorative," Dren said.

Kem redid it. Vel watched the angle, then nodded once.

"Better. Tomorrow he'll hate you instead of me."

"That feels like a low bar."

"It is."

By the time the arm sat high against Dren's chest, Kem had learned three new ways cloth could fail. Dren had learned to keep his complaints short because each one jarred the stitches. Vel let Kem lower her back against the trunk again, one arm tight across her ribs until the worst passed.

"It'll scar," Vel said.

"Good. Then I'll remember not to let him do the other arm." Dren tested the wrap lightly. Pink seeped through, but slower now. "We staying here?"

"Tonight," Vel said. "We move at first light."

There was no point pretending they could do anything else. Light was going. Dren could walk, but not fast. Vel could stand, but every deep breath sharpened her face. Kem still had the burned-out ache of the Strength Charm in his thighs, as if somebody had packed hot sand into the muscles and left it there.

Vel had him strip the useful things from the torn packs and set them in a pile near the fire lay: bread, dried meat, the cloth wrap, flint, the little cooking tin, the small journal she kept tucked inside her coat instead of stowed where sensible people kept things. The pile looked smaller than it should have after three adults' worth of travel. They had two clean cloths, one heel of bread, a swallow of spirits, and thread enough for maybe one more repair if tomorrow went badly. Every item that made it onto the dirt felt like something rescued from sinking.

Dren tested the pack strap again with the bad arm, let it drop, and adjusted the load with his right arm. The calculation showed in small movements: grip, pull, reach, stop. The arm would stay functional, but slower. It would carry less weight. He had fewer clean days before the joint stiffened. Kem watched him take stock with the blunt efficiency of a man who lived inside margins.

"Arm's fine," Dren said to the trees.

"Then stop advertising it," Vel said.

Blood had dried under Kem's nails. Wax clung near the little puncture the needle had left at the base of his left thumb. He looked used now, marked in ways the Vault never had room for. He stared at himself as if the damage belonged to someone else.

Then he checked his Ledger.

178.

He had been at 183 when they broke from the silo. Two levels had gone on Concealment at the creek. Three had gone on Strength while he dragged Dren up the bank. The subtraction sat clean. It still made his stomach go hollow.

He had walked out of the Vault with 300. Now he had 178. A hundred and twenty-two levels were gone in less than a month. At 245, after the first fight, loss had felt like exhilaration, proof that his body could do something besides sit in ceremonial rooms and decay politely. At 178, the thrill had thinned out. Thirty no longer sounded like a number that belonged to other people. Combat Pulse was gone. Three Utility charms were gone. Sustenance was still there, but it sat in his awareness like a coin he kept touching in his pocket to make sure it had not vanished too.

Dren had been watching him.

"How many?" he asked.

"What?"

"Levels."

"178."

Dren nodded once. He did not whistle or pity him. He heard the number the way a trader heard freight weight or winter grain and put it in the correct column.

"That'll get you somewhere," he said.

He picked once at the sling knot with his teeth, dissatisfied, then let it be. "It's also the kind of number that makes people stupid."

Kem looked up. "How."

"Because it still feels big." Dren settled more carefully against his pack. "Men at thirty know they're at thirty. Men at a hundred and seventy-eight start buying themselves comfort and calling it judgment."

"I spent five levels getting us out."

"Yeah. That's why I'm not insulting you." Dren glanced at the wrapped arm. "I'm telling you the next bad spend won't look heroic. It'll look easy."

Vel's eyes were still closed. "He's right."

Kem wanted to dislike the lesson on principle. Instead he looked down at the Flame Charm still sitting in his roster and understood exactly what Dren meant.

Vel was not asleep. Nobody breathing that carefully was asleep. Dren found a position that kept pressure off the stitches and went still.

Kem scrubbed at the blood with grass, dirt, and water until only faint brown stayed in the creases. The puncture kept stinging. He ignored it.

Evening slid through the clearing. They had not eaten. Tiredness settled into his jaw, forearms, and shoulders, the kind that came from concentration and the long drop after fear. No ability would fix that. It had to burn out on its own.

The Flame Charm sat in his roster like an invitation he no longer trusted. It would cost one level for dry flame, one level to avoid bent knees and scraped knuckles, one level that might matter tomorrow if Dren slipped again or if Vel finally admitted the ribs were worse than she said.

So he went for wood instead. He pulled dry twigs from under a fallen spruce, shaved bark thin with Vel's knife, and dug out a fistful of old grass from beneath a rock where the day's damp had not reached. He stacked fine kindling first, then small sticks, then heavier pieces once the flame could bear them. It was the same order Vel had taught him in the highlands, and the same order he had ignored twice already in his head while imagining a faster way to do it.

It took four tries.

On the second, the spark died before it caught. On the third, he fed the new flame too soon and buried it in its own smoke. The fourth held because he forced himself to wait through the ugly little stage where nothing looked impressive and everything mattered.

"It needs air more than faith," Vel said without opening her eyes.

Dren made a sound that might have been a laugh and might have been pain. "Useful distinction."

By full dark, the flames had settled low and steady. Vel opened her eyes when the fire caught. She looked at it, then at Kem. Something eased for a second around her mouth before she pushed herself closer to the heat and stalled halfway there when the ribs bit.

Kem crossed the space, crouched on her good side, and got an arm under her elbow.

"Not the left," she said through her teeth.

"I gathered that."

"Good."

He lowered her the last two feet. Her coat brushed cold against his wrist with sweat trapped under the wool. She sat by the fire without thanking him or waving him off.

---

They ate dried meat and the stale heel of bread from Dren's pack. Nobody wasted energy pretending it was better than it was. Dren tried to saw the bread apart with his knife braced against his boot and failed twice before Kem took it from him and tore the pieces himself. Dren let him, then let him retie the top of the bandage when the first knot slipped while he reached for the meat.

The second wrapping went faster. Kem knew now where the cloth needed to sit and how much weight the sling could take before Dren's mouth flattened.

"See," Dren said when Kem finished. "Give you ten more wounds and you'll almost be employable."

"Let's not set targets."

"Fine. Five wounds."

Dren chewed one-handed and fell asleep almost as soon as he lay down, turned on his good side with the pack under his head. He could sleep anywhere. Kem watched how he tucked the pack just right under his neck and rolled onto the good side without waking himself fully.

Across the fire, Vel held a tin cup near her mouth without drinking. Every few breaths she stopped and waited out the bite in her side before she took the next one.

"You could spend on the ribs," he said.

Vel looked at him over the rim of the cup.

"Could," she said.

"You're not going to."

"No."

He waited. She did not add anything.

"Why not."

"Because they'll still be ribs tomorrow."

"That's not an answer."

"It's the answer."

Dren, already folding down into sleep, said, "Healing's expensive when the job is pain instead of dying."

Vel glanced at him. "Look at that. He learns."

Dren shut his eyes. "I've paid other people's healers before."

Kem looked back at Vel. "You spent two on Hael."

"Hael had blood in his chest and an hour left if I was generous." She shifted, stopped when the ribs caught, and let the breath out slow. "I've got cracks on the left side and a bad temper. The cracks will mend on their own."

"And if they don't?"

"Then I get meaner for a week." She took another shallow breath. "If I spend now, I still wake up hurt, only with fewer choices."

Kem looked at Dren, already out cold on his good side, then back at Vel with the left side of her coat pulled tight over the ribs. In the Vault a cracked rib would have brought servants, tincture, a healer, a tray. Vel sat still and counted what tomorrow might cost.

"Where does it go?" Kem asked.

She looked up.

"Zero," he said. "When it finally runs out, where do people go."

For a while the fire did all the talking. Vel watched the flames sink around a blackening stick. Then she checked Dren's sleep and looked back at Kem.

"It's not death," she said.

"You've said that before."

"Because that's the first thing people ask."

"Then say the second thing."

A small breath went out of her nose. It was not laughter. It sounded more like irritation at being cornered by a fair question.

"When you hit zero in the right place, you cross," she said. "You go out of here, out of the Ledger."

Kem held still.

"There's a breach in the south where it can happen. It doesn't happen anywhere else. If you spend down to nothing in a field, you stay in the field. If it happens there, with the threshold set right, you go through."

"Through to what."

"A place without the Ledger. There's no number there, no abilities either. It's just your body and whatever you've learned to do with it."

He looked past her at Dren's sleeping shape on the other side of the fire. Dren lived by keeping clear of zero. Dren had a daughter. Dren had route math. Zero was not a destination to him. It was a cliff edge you backed away from all your life.

"How do you know that?" Kem asked.

Vel rubbed at an old burn with her thumb once. "I saw someone cross."

"Who?"

"Someone I was on the road with for years."

The answer hit harder for being that small. Kem waited. This time she kept going.

"It was night," she said. "Cold enough the rocks kept the day's heat underneath and froze your face above it. We'd been arguing about where to bed down. I wanted the north lip. They wanted the cut because the wind broke there. I was still holding their cup because they'd shoved it at me and told me to drink instead of talking. I was close enough to grab their sleeve if I'd known what I was looking at." Her eyes stayed on the fire. "When their number hit nothing, my own Ledger yanked inside me like a hook through the middle. Every insect in the cut went quiet at once. Dust lifted off the stone toward them. Then there was empty air where a body had been."

The fire snapped. Kem realized he had stopped breathing and let the air out slowly.

"There was no body," Vel said. "There was no blood either. The pack was gone too. They didn't fall. They didn't burn. They were here and then they weren't." She flexed once, as if the old burn still hurt. "I stood there long enough for the metal to burn me before I remembered to drop it."

"And that's only there," he said.

She didn't answer.

"And only if it's done right."

"Done wrong, you die in a field and nobody knows the difference." She shifted her weight off the bad ribs. "So yeah."

"What does 'done right' mean?"

"It means zero isn't enough by itself." Vel met his eyes then, flat and direct.

The answer shut one door and opened three others. Kem could feel the questions crowding up behind his teeth, each worse than the last.

"Why tell me now?"

"Because before tonight you'd have heard 'there's a door' and spent half your Ledger trying to slam into it." She glanced toward Dren and then back. "Tonight you spent five getting us out, made a fire yourself when you could've spent for it, tied his sling three times until it held, and then stared at 178 like it was a wound. That's different."

She was right, which irritated him more than if she had been cruel. Three weeks ago he would have heard about a threshold and treated it like a challenge built for him. Some part of him still wanted to. Another part kept seeing the five-level subtraction line up clean inside his head and feeling panic under his ribs.

"So this whole time," he said, "you weren't chasing a story. You saw someone go."

"Yeah."

"And you've been walking after that ever since."

Vel looked into the fire for so long he thought she would leave him with silence again. When she spoke, the voice came out flatter than before.

"I went back at dawn," she said. "I thought maybe I'd missed something because I was scared or stupid, that there'd be a body in a crack, blood under the dust, a boot, anything." Her mouth flattened. "There was the fire ring, two bedroll marks, and the cup where I'd dropped it. That was all, so yes, I kept walking."

Kem pictured the campsite at dawn: the fire ring gone cold, the two bedroll marks still pressed into the dust, one cup beside them and no sign of the person who should have been there.

"What was the place like?" he asked.

"It's a cut in the rock wide enough for three people to stand abreast. The stone's black. There are old scorch marks all through it. The wind doesn't move like the rest of the world there. You feel it before you see it. Your Ledger goes tight."

He tried to picture it: hard stone underfoot, wind pulling the wrong direction, his own number dropping toward zero while the last of what he had narrowed to one choice.

"People live there," he said.

"That's what I was told, and I watched enough to believe it."

"There's no Ledger there," Kem said. "There aren't any abilities either."

Vel pulled her coat tighter.

"And you think the ones who cross stay there."

"I think the ones who cross don't come back north." Vel shifted one shoulder in the smallest possible shrug. "That is not the same thing. It's just what I've got."

"What if it's only another kind of dying?"

"Then it's an organized version." She looked back at the flames. "Bodies usually leave evidence."

Despite himself, he let out a short, startled laugh. Vel's mouth moved at one corner, brief and unwilling.

Heat hit one side of his face. Night cold sat on the other. The thought of losing every ability he had left should have been pure terror. Some of it was. The rest sat lower and stranger. His body still remembered the drag of thread through skin. The fire in front of him existed because he had coaxed it up from bark and patience instead of burning a level on command. Dren's arm was closed because Kem had done clumsy work and kept doing it until it held. He could not tell whether what tugged at him was dread or relief.

"I'm not sure if that sounds better or worse," he said.

"It depends on the day," Vel said.

"What about today?"

She pressed once at her ribs, then let it fall. "Today I'd settle for sleep."

They let the conversation stop there. Dren slept. Insects resumed their work in the trees. Far to the north, the agents they had escaped were probably limping through a story that made them sound less foolish. The black cut Vel had described kept replacing the clearing every time Kem looked into the fire.

Kem looked again at the blood in the cracks of his knuckles, the needle prick near his left thumb, the callus beginning where there had only been softness a month ago.

"I'm going to sleep," he said.

"I'll take first watch."

"You should sleep. Your ribs are bad enough."

"I'll take first watch."

That ended it. Kem lay down with his pack under his head the way Dren had shown him. The ground was cold and uneven. A root worked into his shoulder blade until he found the angle that hurt least and accepted it. Stars showed above the branches. He did not know their names. In the Vault, nobody had ever needed the sky for anything.

Sleep came in pieces. Once he woke to a low cough from the fire and heard Vel swallow the rest of it, quiet enough that Dren did not stir. Later there was only the hiss of wood settling into ash.

Then Vel's voice came across the fire, so low he nearly dreamed it. "Check his fingers."

Kem pushed himself up on an elbow. Cold had settled harder through the clearing. Dren was snoring through his mouth, the sling still riding high. Kem touched the exposed knuckles of the bad arm. They were warm. He pressed higher at the wrist the way Vel had shown him and watched Dren's face tighten without waking.

"Still there?" Vel asked.

"Yeah."

"Then ease the knot. Not enough to drop it."

He did, slower than before, feeling where the cloth wanted to bite and where it would hold. He eased the sling higher and tucked the blanket edge under Dren's elbow so the arm stopped rolling outward. This time he did not have to ask if it was too tight.

"Go back to sleep," Vel said.

Kem fed two sticks into the coals first. Then he lay down again.

In the last piece before dawn, his father sat in the Conservatory with the usual blank eyes and a Ledger at zero. This time the face was not empty. Kem woke before the dream could make sense of that, to gray light, white ash, and Vel still sitting upright where he had left her, watching the trees.
