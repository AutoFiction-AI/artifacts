# Chapter 11

Three days after they left Thessa's settlement, they found the bridge. Kem's shoulders still remembered every borrowed bit of Strength he'd used to drag the pack up bad hills, and Thessa's question had only gotten meaner with distance. What remained of the bridge, gray timbers slumped into the current and iron bolts orange with rust, looked like a bridge the flood had finished after years of neglect. The southern end had been torn open and then left to rot. Northern pilings stood like teeth in a broken jaw, with water rushing between them in brown surges that carried sticks and clods of soil.

At the bank, Kem watched the river. It was wider than he'd expected, twenty meters at least, loud with the kind of sound water made when it was working rather than resting. The surface moved fast and uneven, cresting over submerged rocks, carrying silt from somewhere upstream where it had rained recently. During the three days south from Thessa's place, the terrain had turned barer and less maintained. Fences had thinned. Cultivated ground had given way to rougher scrub. The road itself had narrowed from packed earth to a track that was mostly suggestion. This was the boundary between the Spent lands and whatever lay beyond them.

"How deep?" he asked.

Vel was crouching at the bank, testing the current with a stripped branch. She pulled it out and studied the wet line. "It'll be waist-deep at the edges. Chest-high in the middle, maybe higher. Rain upstream pushed it up."

"So we spend."

"Not first."

"Bridge Conjuration would take three levels. I could span it in twenty seconds."

"Bank shifts under it and you're out three, still in the river."

"I've got Water Walking at two."

"Keep it." She pulled the branch out of the water. The current had already stripped the bark below the waterline, leaving white wood that looked gnawed. "You walk it with your legs unless the river changes the job. If you can plant your feet, you walk. If the bottom goes, if the pack starts dragging you sideways, if you can't make that rock under your own body, then you spend and get yourself there. Not before."

Dren was sitting on a flat rock a few meters back, working his boots off and lashing them to the top of his pack in a configuration Kem had watched him perform half a dozen times, leather soles turned outward, the knot cinched with a trader's twist that left a tail he could pull to release. Dren had used the same knot on cargo bales, saddlebags, and once on a loose tent stake that would not hold in soft ground.

"Current's stronger than it looks," Dren said, without looking up. "Don't aim at the bank. Aim at that flat rock out there. Midstream, closer to the south side. Let the current carry you to it, then cut the last channel. If the shelf disappears under you, spend for the rock. Don't get proud."

"Right." Kem was already assessing options. Bridge Conjuration, three levels. Flight Charm, two, but he'd never used one and the calibration problems from his first weeks still haunted him. Or a Strength boost and just bull through.

"You heard me," Vel said.

Kem looked at her. She was standing now, the branch discarded, her expression the flat practical one she used when she'd already made a decision and considered the discussion administrative.

"The river's dangerous."

"The river's crossable."

More questions were available. How much drag counted as dragging. Whether chest-high on Dren meant chest-high on him. Whether Water Walking on bad current counted as getting himself out or as panicking early. The part of him that might have asked them was still thin and new. The older part wanted a rule he could obey cleanly. Vel knew the road. Dren knew the water. He told himself that should be enough.

"Say it back," Vel said.

Kem looked from her to the rock. "If I can keep my feet, I walk. If the bottom goes or the pack starts pulling me sideways, I spend for the rock."

"Good."

Dren went first. He hoisted his pack and walked to the bank without ceremony. River crossings were part of trade routes the way stairs were part of buildings. Water came to his thighs on the first few steps. Then he took the current at an angle toward the flat rock, moving with the unhurried steadiness of a man reading terrain, each foot planted, tested, weighted. The current pushed against him, but his face did not change. His attention stayed on the water, tracking where pressure eased, where footing held, where cost stayed low.

At the deep middle run, the water reached his chest. He lifted his pack above his head. One boot touched the rock. Three breaths there, a small angle change, then the last eight meters of side channel to the south bank.

Twenty minutes from bank to bank. On the far side, he pulled himself out, shook water from his pack, and sat down to wring out his shirt.

Vel went next. Boots came off. Her knot differed from Dren's and did the same job. She entered the water as if stepping into a room she'd visited before. Being shorter put the current at her ribs sooner than it had on Dren, but a lower stance and decades of practice solved most of that. One hand hit the rock. From there she crossed the side channel without pausing and climbed out on the south bank.

Ten minutes. She climbed out on the south bank, wrung her shirt once, then looked back long enough to make clear the earlier rule had not expired.

Both of them were across. The river ran between Kem and the rest of his group, loud and brown and indifferent to his situation.

He loosened his pack straps, tightened them again, then picked up the stripped branch Vel had left behind and waded one step into the edge current. Gravel held. The second probe found the lip where the bottom pitched away, and the branch shivered in his hand hard enough to carry force all the way up his wrist. From the far bank, Vel pointed first at the outcrop, then at his pack.

"If that starts carrying you instead of the other way around, that's the line," she called. "Cut it loose or spend. Don't stand there deciding whether it counts."

"Keep your hips low," Dren added. "Stand up straight and it'll knock you sideways."

Boots came off.

---

He entered where Dren had told him to, twenty meters upstream of the outcrop. From shore, the line had looked simple: let the current carry him diagonally to the rock, brace there, then cut the last eight meters of side channel to the south bank. Water hit with a cold that felt stored rather than made. His ankles locked. Another step found slick green stone and forced a quick correction.

Waist-deep water pressed at his thighs with flat, steady force. The pack shifted on his shoulders. Both hands locked on the straps. Beneath his feet, gravel gave way to smoother stone in all the wrong places.

A third of the way across, the bottom dropped.

Six inches, maybe eight. Enough. The river rose from waist to chest and changed the problem completely. Force that had been shoving at his legs started driving into his ribs. He staggered, caught himself, took one blind step, then another.

*If I can keep my feet, I walk. If the bottom goes or the pack starts pulling me sideways, I spend for the rock.* Vel had been plain. Kem told himself the bottom had not gone, not fully. It had only shifted. The pack was heavy, not dragging. One more careful step and the rule would stay theoretical.

"Angle more," Dren called from the far bank. "You're going too straight."

Kem angled. A hidden stone rolled under his foot. His knee went sideways and the river made a hard, practical case for lying down. Somehow he stayed upright through a movement that used both arms and no dignity. By then the pack was soaked and his clothes were dragging at him, heavy enough to make those Strength boosts on the road feel less like waste and more like ordinary sense.

Water Walking sat in him at two levels, easy and available and humiliating. He took another ordinary step instead.

Halfway out, his feet found the submerged shelf that fed the rock. For one breath the line seemed recoverable. Water lapped at his shoulders. The current shifted around him in indifferent patterns that had nothing to do with him and would keep going after he was gone.

Vel was watching from the south bank with the same exact attention she gave any situation she was still evaluating. Nearby, Dren sat squeezing water from his shirt.

One more step ended the shelf. His foot found nothing and the current took him.

There was no crash of white water. The river simply moved him sideways until his body stopped being a thing that walked and became a thing being carried. His toes searched for bottom and found none. The pack dragged him backward. Water went down his throat in a hard, accidental gulp, and coughing only pulled more in.

The river pulled him under.

Sound changed first. Then the whole river closed over him, brown and loud and close. Cold became enormous. Kicking got his head back up once. A gasp. Another shove under, casual as a hand pressing a floating stick back beneath the surface just to watch it rise.

His shin cracked against rock. Pain came sharp and particular, the exact wrongness of bone meeting stone through thin skin. He used it. One push off the strike point got his head clear long enough to cough until his ribs hurt.

Ten meters gone, just like that.

"Get your feet downstream!" Vel shouted. Her voice broke against the water and reached him in pieces, but the order was clear enough. "Feet first — don't fight it, ride it to the shallows!"

His feet went downstream. Rocks found him anyway. One ripped his shirt and laid open a hot stripe of skin from shoulder blade to ribs. Another caught the pack and spun him under again, just long enough for cold to fill his ears and certainty to fill everything else. The river could kill him. It was not even making an effort.

When his head came up again, the south side looked close enough to touch. The river had dumped him onto a gravel tongue downstream of the outcrop, a waist-deep shelf where he could stand. Standing did not help much. His legs were shaking from cold and effort. Blood from the cracked shin spread in the water around him in pale pink ribbons.

Vel and Dren were upstream and across from him, on the actual south bank. Between the gravel tongue and that bank ran the side channel Dren had crossed in the last push, dark water folding hard along the shore. The outcrop that anchored the route was now behind him and upstream.

Water Walking came to the front of his teeth. He swallowed it. Spending now would mean admitting the crossing had gone wrong exactly where they'd said it could, and some stupid part of him still wanted to finish it the correct way.

Upstream through the shallows looked possible until he started doing it. A step at waist depth turned chest-deep on the next stride. Gravel gave way to clay that sucked at his feet. One half-submerged branch offered a handhold for a single pull.

The branch snapped.

Down to the neck again. Current shoved him sideways while his hands clawed at mud and roots that came apart in numb fingers. For a moment his feet found purchase. Then the clay bottom gave way and he dropped again.

On the bank across the side channel, Vel took a step into the shallows.

Up again, coughing. Vel's weight had shifted forward. She stood at the lip of the water with the contained focus she used right before acting, close enough to the edge of her own assessment that the sight of her did more than the water in his lungs had.

She had been wrong about the river. They both knew it now.

Some submerged root caught under one heel and let him shove himself upstream. Vel stepped back out.

The next stretch was worse than the first dunking because it went on. False bottoms. Sudden cuts. One feeder channel carrying water even colder than the main river and smelling of raw clay. Neck-deep there, with his hands slipping on green-slick stones and his feet touching only often enough to keep panic from turning final.

Each breath kept arriving a moment before the river took it away. The margin got thinner every time.

By the time he fought his way back to the route, the flat outcrop rose a meter above the waterline exactly where Dren had said it would. Bloody hands slipped twice before finding enough purchase to climb onto it. From up there the map finally held still. Main channel behind him. Side channel in front. South bank eight meters away.

The outcrop had not solved the real problem.

Eight meters of hard water separated the rock from the bank where Vel and Dren waited. Dren had his arms folded. Vel stood at the edge of the shallows with the look of someone trying not to make a second bad decision.

Kem slid off the rock. The side channel took him hard and he fought it badly, with frozen hands, lead legs, and the last reserves of a body that had never been asked for this kind of work. The current spun him once. Then a foot hit gravel. Solid. Shallow enough. He lunged. The bottom held. Another lunge and the water dropped from waist to thigh to knee.

Two hours after he'd entered the river, he crawled onto the far bank on his hands and knees. Face-down in the dirt, he breathed mud and didn't move.

---

Dren said, quietly, "Better than I expected, honestly."

That did not help.

Kem rolled onto his back. The sky was pale and ordinary, with a few thin clouds sliding east. His body had become a list of complaints: the shin split open on rock and still leaking into the dirt, the long scrape on his back where torn shirt stuck to raw skin, hands turning from numbness into a bright painful tingling, thighs and lower back aching from two hours of fighting current. All of it earned. None of it permanent.

When he sat up, Vel was standing three feet away, wringing out the hem of her shirt. Her legs were wet to the knee. So she had gone in far enough to be ready.

"You were going to come in," he said.

"You were struggling."

"You told me not to spend."

"I did."

"That could've killed me."

"Yeah."

Fury came on slowly and cold, matching the rest of him. Brown water closing over his head. The bottom dropping away. That flat, absolute second of understanding that the river could take him and his Ledger would never change.

"Why?" he said.

Vel sat across from him on a rock. Dren moved farther up the bank and started laying out his pack to dry, giving them the distance of a man who had opinions and no interest in billing for them.

"Because I thought you could make it," Vel said. "Wrong call."

"So I nearly drowned because you made a bad call."

"You nearly drowned because I made a bad call and because when it crossed the line I gave you, you kept treating the first half like law."

Kem waited.

"I said walk it while you had bottom," she said. "I said if the bottom went or the pack started dragging, you spent for the rock. The middle came up sooner than I thought. That's mine. Once it did, you still kept trying to finish it without spending."

"Because you told me not to spend."

"I know what I told you. I also told you where the line was."

He'd expected an apology. Some attempt to ease it. Vel was telling him he should've overruled her.

"Rain upstream raised the middle. I should've read that. I should've put a line on you or sent the packs over separate. That's mine. But once you lost the shelf, you had enough to decide for yourself. You had the rock. You had Water Walking. You had the same river I did. You still kept looking at me like I could do the deciding from the bank."

"I was trying to do what you said."

"You were trying to make it my mistake all the way through."

She picked up a stone from the bank and turned it in her hands without looking at him.

"Back in the Vault they told you keeping the number whole made you good," she said. "I tell you not to spend on stupid things. Those are different instructions. You keep cramming them together so you don't have to decide anything."

His father at the Rite came back to him at once. Perfect stillness. Perfect obedience. Same shape, Kem thought. Different furniture.

"That's not the same thing."

"No. It isn't." Vel's voice stayed flat. "That's why you're sitting here half skinned."

The river kept moving behind them, unchanged by the point it had already made. Arguing was possible. Winning felt less so.

"So what do I do?" His voice came out raw from the water he'd swallowed.

"Next crossing, you check depth yourself," she said. "Bottom. Current. What changes where. If it stops being a walking problem and starts being a drowning problem, spend. If I call it wrong, say so before you're under. If you call it wrong, live with the bill. But make the call yourself."

The stone clicked back into the dirt.

"Next river, I make my own call," he said.

"Good," Vel said.

Three days since Thessa's kitchen, and the question still sat where it had landed. Was Vel teaching him to judge for himself, or building the kind of man she needed? Both possibilities fit too neatly to let the matter rest.

"One more thing," Vel said. "Since the Spent Towns."

"What about them?"

"You've bled eighteen levels on convenience," Vel said. "Six before Thessa. Two heating washwater. Two on light because dusk made you nervous. Two in Strength taking that wash in a jump instead of walking the bank."

His mouth opened. Closed.

"Since her settlement, another twelve. Two on cooking fires when the flint would've done. Six in Strength getting your pack over hills your own shoulders could've handled. Four on light when Dren had a torch or we had oil."

He had not been counting.

"Check your Ledger," she said.

The number was there, persistent and precise, the Ledger's one reliable quality. He had not looked at it closely since Thessa's settlement, but the earlier figures were still there in memory now, tied to places more than dates.

One hundred and forty-eight.

Two hundred and twenty-five when they'd entered the Spent Towns. One hundred and eighty-three after the silo fight. One hundred and sixty after the settlement escape. One hundred and forty-eight now. Seventy-seven levels gone since the Towns. Eighteen of them to being warm, lit, clean, and spared the weight of his own pack while he had been congratulating himself for restraint at the river.

"Today the river should've cost you," Vel said. "Hot water, bright camps, hills you can walk under your own weight, those shouldn't. That's the split. Start making it."

Vel stood and began unpacking wet things. Conversation over.

Dren came over with a strip of cloth he'd torn from his pack. "Your shin," he said. "Before it fills with dirt."

Kem took the cloth. "Thanks."

"Wasn't what I signed on for," Dren said. "The motivational drowning."

"She said she was wrong about the river."

"She was." Dren sat on his heels and looked at the water. That particular expression was becoming legible to Kem now, the one that showed up whenever Dren's daughter and his Ledger entered the same calculation. "She's usually not, though. Wrong, I mean. That's the problem with traveling with somebody who's been doing this forty years. When she's right, you learn. When she's wrong, it almost kills you."

Dren stood, brushed dirt from his knees, and went back to his things.

Kem wrapped his shin and sat on the bank looking south. Terrain on this side of the river was different, drier, with thinner vegetation and soil so pale it looked leeched. The road, such as it was, continued as two ruts in dust-colored dirt. Whatever lay ahead did not look like the Spent Towns.

He didn't check again. The number was there whether he looked at it or not.

They kept moving until dusk.

First night south of the river, they made camp on a chalk rise above a dry wash. Kem would have walked past it if Vel had not stopped and said, "Pick it."

He looked at her. "Now?"

"Now."

The wash offered shelter and easier water. The rise offered wind, sightlines, and ground that looked mean but honest. Kem started toward the wash because it was closer and his shin was throbbing in time with his pulse. Then he saw the drift line caught in the reeds: sticks, grass, one dead fish gone to skin and spine, all combed into the same narrow stripe well above ankle height.

"The rise," he said.

"Why?"

He pointed with his chin. "That wash filled recently. If rain hits upstream again, I'm not sleeping in it. And if somebody comes up the road, I'd rather see them before they're on top of us."

Dren glanced at the reeds. "Good enough."

Vel said, "Then set camp."

That turned out to be a different job from choosing the ground. Kem dropped the packs where the rise looked flatest until Dren said, "Runoff," and nudged one bundle six feet over with his boot toward a patch of dirt the last storm had not cut open. Kem fetched scrub for the fire, judged half of it wrong, fetched more. The striker skipped in his numb fingers twice before sparks finally took in the bark.

For a second the old answer came up bright and easy. Flame Charm. Two levels. Hot water in a blink, dry shirt sooner, no need to crouch here shivering over a spit of fire like a fool. He could feel the ability in him, available, almost insulted not to be used.

Vel set the cook pot beside him. "Boil water. Clean the shin."

He looked up, half expecting instruction on whether this counted. She was already spreading blankets to dry and not looking back.

So he made the smaller calls himself. More scrub. Then thicker branches. Pot close enough to heat fast, not close enough to soot the whole thing black. When the water steamed, he carried it off with a folded cloth and sat on a rock above the camp. Mud had dried around the cut in a rusty crust. He poured, and the pain was clean and immediate, nothing abstract about it. River grit washed down his ankle in pink threads.

Dren came over long enough to hand him the rest of the torn cloth. "Again," he said. "You missed some."

Kem cleaned it again.

By full dark the fire held on its own. They ate cold bread, onion, and the last of Thessa's hard cheese because nobody had the patience left to tend a pot after that day. The river was out of sight now, but he could still hear it when the wind shifted, a long low rush that made the back of his neck tighten before he could tell himself where he was.

No one returned to the argument. Vel asked once whether the packs would stay dry if the wind turned. Kem looked at the ground, looked at the wash, and moved them another yard uphill before answering. She said nothing. He kept waiting for correction anyway. It didn't come.

---

Sable built the fire herself.

She used striker and steel from the field equipment kit she'd taken six weeks earlier, back when the kit had been an object of institutional comedy, designed by administrators who had never lit a fire and packed by clerks who could not tell kindling from deadfall. Since then she had learned the difference as she learned everything, systematically, with attention to process, noting which materials caught and which smoked and died.

Sparks caught in the shredded bark. Cupped hands fed the flame dried grass, then twigs, then larger pieces in the order five weeks of trial and revision had established. The fire grew. The care was the same care she had once given quarterly audit reports, and this time she let the comparison stand.

Three weeks of peeling away from the team whenever terrain allowed had made camp into procedure. Tonight the remaining agents were one ridge north with the spare animals and their own fire, close enough for retrieval if something went wrong and far enough that she could think. Two of the five had been unable to continue. Spending had unsettled them in ways decades of Hoarded propriety had not prepared them for. The other three could still function, but growing competence made them harder to predict. They asked fewer procedural questions now. One had started gutting rabbits without looking ill afterward. Another had begun correcting the maps from memory. Unpredictable spenders at level 280 were a liability. Distance suited the work better than she had expected.

Her camp was organized. Boots aligned by the fire. Provisions inventoried, another week at this pace before resupply. An annotated map lay open beside her, margins filled in her own precise hand, and even the language in those notes had changed. The landmark the official map labeled "Terrain Feature 7" was a ridge. In her notes it was a ridge too. That shift felt larger than it should have.

Down the slope, somewhere beyond the scrub, a mule bell knocked once. One of the agents answered it with a curse too faint to make out. She had put enough ground between herself and them to keep the call private, not enough to pretend they had already left her.

Then she activated the Communication ability.

Two levels left her in the familiar precise subtraction, a third sensation between pain and loss that Hoarded vocabulary had never bothered to name. Her Ledger adjusted to 270. Six weeks earlier it had read 300. Thirty levels had gone to pursuit, tracking, travel, and the accumulated expense of being alive outside a building for the first time in her adult life.

The ability connected. Lady Aldraste's voice arrived clean and cold, carrying the authority of a woman who had chaired institutional meetings for three decades and learned to compress command into the pitch of her syllables.

"Report."

"The subjects are moving south," Sable said. "Past the boundary settlements. Direction is consistent with a Scour approach."

"Rate of travel."

"Reduced. Their guide is injured, and that limits their pace." She paused. The next sentence required a choice between the institutional frame and the accurate one. "The Aldraste subject's incidental ability use has declined. He is spending less on routine tasks."

"Meaning?"

"He is being trained. The Ashwick woman is teaching him to function without abilities." Even saying it, Sable could hear the omitted portion. The training was working. The reckless spender she'd watched fight with the exhilaration of a child was becoming more deliberate, and that transformation no longer fit neatly under decline.

"His current level?"

"Approximately 148. He was at 160 after the settlement escape."

"That is a considerable expenditure." Administrative frost. Numbers that meant money, waste, institutional failure. Underneath it ran a different arithmetic, one Sable knew well enough to hear by now. A mother counting how much of her son was left.

"The majority was combat and defensive spending," Sable said. "Recent losses are incidental."

"Your instructions remain unchanged. Retrieve the subject with minimal force and minimal additional expenditure. The Council's patience with this operation is not without limit."

"Understood."

"And Auditor Orm."

"Yes."

"Your current level."

Firelight banked steadily in the structure she had built with her own hands. Six weeks earlier this would have been impossible. So would eight hours on rough terrain, sleep outdoors, navigation by stars, weather read from cloud patterns, or a single operational decision not routed through committee and countersigned by two superiors.

"Two hundred and eighty-five," she said.

At 270, the lie came out in her official voice, the voice that filed reports and presented audit findings, and in that register all official statements sounded true. The institutional reason for it was simple. A thirty-level expenditure on a retrieval would trigger a spending review, and a review would expose the gap between what the Council had authorized and what she had done with that authorization. Underneath that sat the real reason, less presentable and more important. Those hidden fifteen levels represented choices she had made for herself, spent on becoming someone the Council had not approved. They were hers in a way almost nothing in forty-seven years of institutional service had ever been.

"Noted," Lady Aldraste said. "Continue southward. Report at standard intervals."

The connection held for a moment longer than protocol required. Sable waited. In that silence, Lady Aldraste sounded like a woman deciding whether to step outside the institutional frame that had contained her for decades, long enough to watch her husband go still, her son walk out, and her house become a political problem instead of a family.

"Is he well?" Lady Aldraste asked. The words were quiet. They did not sound like a Council directive.

"He is functional," Sable said. "He is adapting to field conditions."

"That is not what I asked."

Sable thought of Kem in the fight, burning levels with the efficiency of a dropped torch, grinning afterward with a happiness nobody in the Vault had displayed in her presence, ever. She thought of his habit of asking questions, interrupting, circling back, genuine every time. She thought of the trader's grudging thanks afterward — a few flat words, nothing ceremonious, plain and real. Kem had looked at that as if someone had handed him a thing he had not known he wanted.

"He seems well," Sable said. "That is not in my report."

The connection ended.

Sable sat by the fire and watched the flames work through the fuel she had arranged. New calluses had formed over skin that had never gripped anything rougher than a pen or the spine of an audit ledger. They were still pink and tender. Her hair was pulled back in a knot copied from a Spent Town woman three weeks earlier, nothing like the formal arrangement she had maintained for thirty years. What she looked like now, she did not know. There had been no mirror since the capital.

For weeks, pressure had been loading into the same hidden place in her thinking, weight and then more weight, waiting for the crack.

Habit reached first for the institutional frame, a report-shaped sentence about expenditure correlating with field capacity. It failed against the fire, the calluses, the knot in her hair, the level she had kept for herself.

*Does spending make you more?*

She made no entry in the field notes. The observation log she had been maintaining out of bureaucratic reflex stayed blank. Instead she sat with the fire she had built, the calluses on her hands, and the number 270 that was hers, and let the question remain where it was, uncategorized, unprocessed, without protocol for what to do next.

The fire cracked. A log shifted and sent a constellation of sparks upward into the dark. She watched them rise and vanish and did not reach for the tracking ability that would have followed them. She let them go.
