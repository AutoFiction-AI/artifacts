# Chapter 13

The road failed under a farmer before noon.

For two days Kem had felt the hollowness in it through his boots. The top layer looked ordinary enough from a distance, packed dirt, wheel ruts, a skin of pale dust, but underneath it the old Shaping had gone soft and ratchety. Every hundred yards the ground gave a little different under his weight, as if the whole route had been assembled out of promises and forgotten the minute the workers were sent on. The Margins were full of that kind of construction. The Hoarded paid for a thing once, named it permanent, then let weather and need chew through it until permanence looked exactly like neglect.

Twenty yards ahead, a farmer in a sweat-dark shirt stepped onto a place that had already gone gray with strain. The surface gave a long dirt sigh and dropped. One side of the lane folded into the drainage channel underneath, and the man sprawled on a slab that kept seesawing under him. His herb pack slid toward the opening. A mule behind him jerked its head up and brayed once, ears flat.

Kem stopped with one arm already half lifted.

He was at forty-three. Seven levels gone in two days, not in one heroic burst, just in the way a road ate you if you let it. Light in a culvert when the roof over it had come down and somebody inside started screaming. A clean bind around a stranger's wrist after a blade slip in a field kitchen. Warmth against basin wind when a night dropped too hard for an old couple sleeping under a cart. Two levels to ease the worst of the pull in Dren's bad arm when the scar seized and his grip stopped working right. Water. Fire. One cracked axle braced long enough for a family to get their grain off it. None of it had been catastrophic. None of it had been part of the route budget either.

Vel hadn't said a word about any of it.

That silence tracked him harder than her old corrections ever had. She had spent the whole journey telling him where he was wasting himself and what kind of fooling around would get him dead by the basin. Now every time he spent on somebody in front of them, she watched once, measured it, and went back to walking. The absence of protest had begun to feel like its own arithmetic.

Dren stopped dead beside him, left arm held a little away from his body the way he carried it when the old wound had started burning. The road kept tearing outward. Dirt sheeted down into the channel in little slides. The farmer reached for the pack because it was worth something, and the grab shifted his weight and took another foot of lane with it.

"Don't move," Vel said.

She was already looking at the breaks in the surface, not the man, counting load and angle the way she counted everything. Kem knew the look. If they hauled him wrong, the rest of the shelf would go with him. If they waited too long, it would go anyway.

The charm came to him before he'd finished choosing it. Stabilization. Expensive for what it did. Stupidly expensive, given where he was.

He threw it.

Twelve levels left him in a burst that hit his sternum and flashed white behind his eyes. The world narrowed to pressure and the taste of old metal. The collapse locked in place mid-failure: broken road frozen at wrong angles over the exposed channel, dirt and old Shaping held together by force and nothing else. The mule screamed and yanked backward on its lead until the farmer let go of it.

"Go," Vel said, and Dren went.

He dropped his own pack, crossed the tilt in eight careful steps, hauled the farmer upright by the belt and arm, then went back for the herb pack while the man made the sound of someone trying not to be sick in public. Kem stayed where he was because if he stood too fast the burst in his chest would turn into a full stagger. The Ledger did its subtraction in the background with obscene calm.

Thirty-one.

The number did not feel abstract. At forty-three he had still been able to sense depth behind him, enough room in the list that a bad decision could be a bad decision and not an amputation. At thirty-one the list felt close enough to count by touch against a wall. A Barrier he had been assuming was there had gone. So had the speed ability he had used twice in the Margins without thinking much about it. Other things were still present, but nearer now, as if all of them had taken a step toward the front of him.

Vel crossed last, weight spread, ready to catch her balance if the road tried something while the charm held. She caught the mule's lead before it bolted, wrapped it once around her wrist, and shoved it at the farmer hard enough to mean take your own damned animal. The man stared at Kem as if he had dropped from the sky by mistake.

"How long?" he asked.

Vel looked at the frozen break, then at the channel, then at the old seams in the far edge. "Not long enough to trust it. Get off the lane and tell the next idiot to do the same."

The farmer nodded too many times. "I can pay a little. Herbs. Dried root. My wife uses them for fever."

"Keep them," Dren said.

Kem tried standing. The first attempt put a black fringe at the edge of his sight. The second worked. His skin had gone cold. The body always knew first.

The farmer was still watching him. There was gratitude in it, yes, but there was something else too, the stunned inventory people made when they saw a spend that large at close range. How much had that cost. What kind of person could afford to do it. What kind of person did it anyway.

Kem looked past him to Vel.

"Thirty-one," he said.

She met his eyes once, sharp and unreadable. "Then you know."

The farmer glanced between them, sensing the thing he had stepped into but not understanding it. Dren had already bent for his pack. Nobody said anything else on the road. They walked south along the field edge until the lane gave way to a scatter of broken stone and dead thistle, then cut toward an abandoned farmstead crouched behind a stand of salt-scrub.

Half a mile on, the place proved real enough to lean against. Four walls, a sagging roof, one room still mostly dry, and a dead well in the courtyard with its crank arm gone. A labor notice had been pasted to the outer wall so long ago the paper had gone leathery with sun. RENEWABLE EXPENDITURE UNIT, the top line read in fading Hoarded block script. Below that, smaller print had blistered away except for PROJECTED ANNUAL OUTPUT and COMPLIANCE REVIEW WINDOW.

Dren found a clay jug in the corner that still held water under a layer of grit. He poured some into a cup, sniffed it, tasted it, waited, then drank the rest in three slow swallows. Across from Kem, Vel sat with Maren's route journal open on her knees, thumb pinning one page flat against the breeze sneaking through the broken shutters. The handwriting on the open spread wasn't hers.

Kem set his pack down and did not sit right away.

Thirty-one stayed with him as a hum in bone and teeth. It was not pain exactly. Pain was easier because it announced itself. This was the body's running count of what it could no longer afford to pretend.

"I want to know what you meant."

Vel did not look up. "What thirty-one feels like."

"Not that."

She closed the journal and kept it pinned under her thumb anyway.

Kem sat because if he stayed standing his anger was going to break loose before he had words to put on it. The wall behind him still held heat from the day. He could feel individual abilities now when he checked for them, not in names but in absences and weights: healing still there, light still there, a few combat uses, not much room left for stupidity. He said, "You told me zero was a threshold. Fine. You didn't tell me how it opens."

"No."

"Tell me now."

For a second it looked like she'd give him one of the smaller adjacent answers she used when she wanted a conversation to end without ending. He could see her deciding which part to trim away. Then she looked at his face, found whatever was there, and said, "It takes a cascade. Spending near the site. One person can hit zero and get through, but the opening barely holds. Two people spending together, it holds longer. More descent means more force."

The room seemed to get hotter on the word together. Kem felt the number he had just become sit between them like a fourth body.

"How much more."

"Two hundred. Two-fifty. Three hundred." Vel's voice stayed level. "The bigger the drop, the stronger the crossing."

There it was. Not the shape of a hope. The shape of a requirement.

Kem's first stupid thought was not betrayal. It was arithmetic. He had left the Vault at three hundred. Two hundred levels of descent. More, now. Enough to make a door for someone else. The thought hit so cleanly he hated himself for how long it took the feeling to catch up.

"And you knew that when you found me."

"I knew a solo crossing wasn't enough."

His pulse kicked once, hard enough that he felt it in his throat. She was narrowing the question again, cleanly enough that on any other day he might have let it pass just to avoid the work of fighting through it.

"That's not the question."

Dren's pouring slowed to almost nothing. Water ticked against the clay cup in slow, separate sounds.

Vel looked at the far wall. "No."

The single syllable left a cold space inside Kem, not because he hadn't expected it, but because some part of him had apparently still been waiting for the denial anyway. He had wanted her to lie. The want embarrassed him so quickly it almost covered the hurt.

"You needed someone high enough to burn down beside you."

"Yes."

Kem went still on the floorboards.

Something Thessa had said in the settlement came back, not the whole speech, just the hard center of it: years ago, Vel asking after defectors still high enough to matter. Kem had pushed it aside because there had always been the next emergency, the next cost, the next mile. On the road everything was urgent enough to postpone any question that threatened the structure holding you upright. Now there was nothing between him and this one except a hot room and a woman who had just confirmed the ugliest part of it.

"How long were you looking?"

"Several years."

His stomach turned over once. Several years. Not a bad choice made in desperation when she met him. Not opportunity. Search.

"How many people."

"Two serious leads. One had already spent too far down. The other wasn't willing."

"So I was the first one who was high enough and foolish enough."

"You were the first one who wanted zero."

It did not help. If anything it made it filthier. Wanting something did not make a trap less of a trap. It just meant he had walked into it under his own power.

The first weeks of the journey shifted in his head, not cleanly, just enough to make him sick. Vel watching him in the forest, correcting his fire, teaching him how not to waste himself. The campfire talk. The restraint lessons. Her face at the labor camp while he burned through stranger after stranger because he couldn't stop. Every memory now had another set of numbers under it. Had she been angry because he was being reckless, or because each reckless act was eating the door she needed him to become. Had there ever been a way to separate one from the other. The answer might have been yes in some scenes and no in others, and that was somehow worse than simple fraud.

He looked at the closed journal under her thumb.

"When were you going to tell me."

Vel was silent long enough that the answer started forming without her. At the site. Once turning back was impossible. Once he had spent too much for refusal to mean anything except dying somewhere less useful.

When she spoke, she only proved the thought right. "Later."

Kem laughed once. The sound scraped on the way out. "That's not a time."

"At the basin. When it had to be dealt with."

The room tilted for a second, not with dizziness this time. With recognition. Every withheld answer, every redirect, every practical correction given exactly when he needed it and not a breath sooner. He had mistaken that control for experience. He had called it competence because he liked having someone in front of him who seemed to know where to put her feet.

"After I'd burned half my life getting there."

Vel did not defend the sequence. She didn't have to. The silence did it for her.

Dren set the cup down. "I'm going outside."

Neither of them looked at him. His boots crossed the courtyard and stopped just beyond the doorway, close enough to hear if something broke, far enough to deny it later.

Kem braced himself against the floorboards because every part of him wanted to do something stupid. The wood was hot and gritty. Thirty-one hummed in him like a trapped insect.

"That's the — you know what the word is." His voice had gone unsteady without him noticing. "Component. That's what I was."

Vel's jaw tightened once. She did not say no.

"I was keeping you alive."

"For what."

She didn't move.

He stood because staying seated had begun to feel like accepting the room's terms. Through the doorway the dead fields flashed white in the heat. The old labor notice on the wall had curled at the edges, but the ink still held where the euphemisms mattered. COMPLIANCE REVIEW WINDOW. RECOVERY YIELD. The same patient administrative obscenity as the camp, bureaucratic enough to hide the body inside it.

His voice came out too polite. He heard his father in it and hated that too.

Kem pointed at the notice. "That's what they do. Here. They look at a body and ask what it can still yield. You found me and started there."

Vel opened her mouth, stopped, then said, "At the start, yes."

The answer landed so cleanly it emptied him out for a second. He had wanted her to narrow the question again, or lie, or do anything that would leave him room to keep fighting the same part of it. Instead she gave him the ugliest piece whole.

"And after."

"I kept you alive."

"For the basin."

"For the road too." She swallowed. "For both. I don't have a cleaner answer than that."

He could feel his mouth trying to shape a laugh and refusing. Cleaner. As if the problem was wording.

"Every time I spent," he said. "The camp. The couple under the cart. The farmer today."

Vel's eyes shut once, briefly. "I counted what was left. I also wanted you alive at the end of the day. Both were true every time."

That hit harder than the first admission. Not because it was worse. Because it was mixed. He wanted a neat fraud. He got the road as it had actually been, every kindness and calculation braided together so tightly he could not pull one free without bringing the rest with it. Vel taking his wrist at the river to show him balance. Vel awake through his fever. Vel watching his remaining levels the same way she watched rotten bridgework.

"And the lessons," he said. "The restraint. All that talk about waste."

"Some of it was budget." Her voice had gone flatter, as if she was forcing each word over something sharp. "Some of it was me watching you throw years away in front of me and not being able to stand it. I can't split those now."

His shoulders tightened so hard he felt the pull down his spine. He had not known until then that he had been saving a childish little piece of the journey from this. Not the practical alliance. Not the first days. The middle of it. The part where he had stopped feeling like cargo and started feeling like company.

He stepped toward her before he had decided to. Not far. One pace. Enough to make the air between them feel chosen.

"Did you ever look at me and forget it."

Vel's eyes went to his face and stayed there. This time the silence was different. Not evasive. Not tactical. Just costly.

Kem understood the answer before she said it, and the understanding came with an immediate second thought that was almost as bad: there had probably been moments when she had forgotten, and those moments did not save either of them.

"Sometimes," she said.

The word should not have hurt more than yes. It did. Sometimes meant she had needed an effort to remember or an effort to forget, and in either case the arithmetic had always still been in the room.

He wanted to leave then. The impulse came whole and immediate: north, away, let her solve her own crossing, let the basin take her plan and choke on it. Thirty-one levels said otherwise. North meant broken road, no journal, no map, Sable somewhere behind them, the Vault at the far end of every retreat whether he ever reached it or not. South meant Vel at eighteen, Dren with one bad arm, and the only route any of them had. Leaving was a plan for people who knew where they were going.

He looked down at the signet instead.

The signet still rode there, silver-dark, the closed-circle crest worn smooth by weeks of sweat and dust. House Aldraste. Preservation by standing still while everyone else paid. Even once he stripped it free, the thing knew how to keep claiming him.

He pulled it off.

Pale flesh showed underneath, soft where the metal had sealed itself against weather and grit. The ring sat against his skin with ugly familiarity. For a second he pictured throwing it over the wall into the scrub and hearing nothing when it landed.

Vel watched him do it and said nothing.

That silence answered in its own way. She did not tell him to keep it. She did not tell him to throw it. She did not pretend the object wasn't part of the room with them.

He curled around it and put it in his pocket.

Dren came back in before either of them spoke again. He took one look at Kem's face, another at the pale strip where the ring had been, and decided correctly not to ask. "South road's passable if we cut the fields for a stretch. Another collapse ahead. Dry channel under it."

"We move now," Vel said.

No one argued with the order. That was its own fresh insult.

Kem shouldered his pack first. The rough seam of a new callus caught on the strap, enough to sting, and then the weight settled where it needed to. Ten feet out into the lane he stopped just long enough for the others to fall in, then kept going.

Dren took the middle without comment. Vel stayed behind him. No one argued the formation either.

They left the farmstead and cut south through fields where grain had gone thin and brittle on the stalk. Once there had been irrigation here. Kem could see the dead channels in the land, straight cuts that had collapsed inward where the lining broke. Survey stakes leaned at odd angles out of the ground, each one branded with a Hoarded lot number and a year old enough to have become mockery. The sun pushed at the back of his neck. Flies worried the corners of his eyes where sweat collected. Thirty-one stayed present in him the whole time, not alarming now, just impossible to forget.

Vel gave directions without looking at him. "Fence line. Then left at the salt stand."

Kem followed them because he wanted south more than he wanted dignity, which was humiliating in a new way.

An hour later they heard shouting ahead and came over a low rise to find six people hauling grain sacks out of a leaning storage shed. The ground under the east wall had opened into a split no wider than a ditch but deep enough that darkness held at the bottom even in full sun. Every time a sack thumped across the packed earth, dust sifted from the rafters. A crudely painted board above the wide door read HARVEST HOLDING ANNEX 6B. Under that, in stenciled Hoarded print too neat for the building around it, was AUTHORIZED SHRINKAGE TOLERANCE: 14%.

The place looked as if someone had told the joke years ago and left the punchline standing in weather ever since.

One of the women saw them first. "You three walking or useful?"

Dren muttered, "Useful, apparently," and turned toward the shed before Kem had answered.

Vel had already scanned the crack, the sag in the roofline, the stacks inside visible through the doorway. She pointed once at the west side. "No one goes under the east beam again. Drag from the back stack only. If that wall starts talking, drop and run."

The woman nodded as if orders from strangers were an ordinary sort of weather out here. Maybe they were. Kem stood one second longer than everyone else, long enough to feel how instantly his body wanted to obey Vel when the subject was not trust but collapse.

The want made him angry enough to move.

Inside the shed it smelled of dust, old grain, mouse piss, and the deep mineral cold that came off ground already deciding to fail. A boy no older than fourteen was trying to drag a sack twice his width by brute pull. Kem took the other end without speaking. Together they got it to the door where Dren shouldered it away one-armed, swearing softly at what that did to his scar.

"How many left?" Vel asked.

"Nine," the woman outside said.

Vel glanced once at the east beam. "Make it six."

"Six gets us three days."

"Then have three days and your bones. Move."

The next minutes ran on body logic. Lift, drag, pass it back, go again. Kem's anger did not leave. It simply had to share space with work. The floor under his boots had a give in it he hated. Every time he crossed near the split wall, the bracing charm rose to the front of his mind on pure reflex. He could do it. He could spend and lock the corner long enough for them to clear the whole shed. He could feel the mechanism already half assembled behind his eyes.

He did not.

Thirty-one. Six levels in the wrong place and chapter by chapter the door at the basin got smaller. That calculation had been Vel's weapon against him. Now it was his too, and hating that did not make it less true.

"Left side," Vel said from the doorway.

He adjusted without answering.

The boy slipped on spilled grain. The sack went sideways. Kem caught it against himself and the rough weave burned across the seam of the new callus at the base of his right thumb. He felt skin split. A hot line opened there under the dust. The pain was stupidly useful. It kept him in the body he actually had instead of the one that could solve everything by tearing more years off itself.

At six sacks the east wall made a low internal pop. Everybody heard it.

"Out," Vel said.

The boy lunged for another sack anyway. Kem grabbed the back of his shirt and hauled him hard enough that the cloth tore at the collar. They hit the yard together just as a ribbon of dirt spilled down the inside of the wall and the beam dropped an inch.

Nobody argued after that.

They stood in the sun, breathing dust. The woman who had first shouted at them braced on her thighs and stared into the doorway with the face of someone doing ruin math in real time. Three days of grain. Six sacks gone. No more safe way to get the rest without a spend too big for any of them to volunteer and still keep living the week.

"Shit," said the boy.

"Yep," said Dren.

The woman straightened and wiped at her mouth with the back of her wrist. "Water's yours if you want it. Out of the cistern, not sweet. Better than the road."

"We'll take it," Dren said.

Vel looked at the crack again, then at the foundation stones on the west corner. "Don't sleep in the house nearest this. The ground's going to keep going tonight."

"You from inspection?" the woman asked.

The question hit Kem like a slap for reasons he could not have fully explained. Maybe it was the word. Maybe it was the fact that Vel, even dusted white and down to eighteen, still looked more like authority than help.

"No," Vel said.

"Good." The woman jerked her chin at the stencil over the door. "Last inspection wrote up the shrinkage tolerance and went home."

No one in the yard laughed. The line did not need help.

They drank from the cistern under a lean-to roof of patched tin. The water was warm and mineral-thick and had a dead beetle floating in the second bucket. Kem drank anyway. The cut kept leaking where the sack had skinned him. He wrapped it once in a strip torn from an old feed cloth. Vel noticed the bandage and looked away before the gesture could become an offer.

That hurt more than if she had tried to help.

The woman told Dren the next intact shelter south was an abandoned weigh station two miles on, stone base, roof mostly sound, pump dead but no one came through enough to contest it. "If you hear ground move in the dark, don't wait to see which direction," she said. "Just run."

"Comforting," Dren said.

"It's free."

They left before the shed came down fully. Kem could hear the family behind them arguing over whether to risk another trip inside. He did not turn around. He knew what it would do to him if he saw them choose the grain anyway.

The fields gave way to waste ground by degrees. Salt brush replaced crop rows. Broken channel walls surfaced and vanished again. Once they passed a toppled marker stone listing permitted maintenance intervals in language so bloodless it might have been funny anywhere less ruined. Vel kept the route. Dren kept the middle. Kem kept his distance and his own head full.

Anger was easier while they were moving. Motion gave it something to lean on. The minute they stopped to climb a fence or edge along a dry wash, other things crowded in behind it. Vel's face in the farmhouse when he said component. The accuracy of her silence. The memory of the first week on the road, when he had thought her impatience with him was the impatience of someone keeping a fool alive. It had been that. It had also been inventory management. The two truths kept refusing to separate.

By late afternoon the weigh station appeared where the road widened around an old stone platform and a rusted scale big enough for wagons. The building attached to it had once been whitewashed. Now the lime had gone patchy and the wood shutters hung crooked on leather-dry hinges. A board over the platform read ROUTE COMPLIANCE POINT 4. Another sign underneath, newer by only a few years, instructed haulers to declare undepleted labor assets before entering basin territory.

Dren stared at that one for a second. "That's a sentence."

"Unfortunately," Kem said.

It was the first thing he had said to either of them in an hour that was not route or warning. The sound of his own voice came out strange, dry from disuse.

Inside, the main room held a counter, a broken bench, and three shelves full of ledgers so warped by damp that their pages had fused. A scale ledger still lay open under a stone paperweight. The last readable line listed grain tonnage, mule count, and a separate column marked HUMAN SUPPORT HOURS with a neat check beside VERIFIED. Somebody had left halfway through writing the next date and never come back.

The place smelled of mouse nests and old paper. It would do.

Dren went through the room with a trader's eye and took the least rotten corner for his bedroll. Vel checked doors, window slits, roofline, then set her pack against the wall nearest the entrance. Kem chose the far side by instinct, as far from her as the room allowed without making a statement so obvious even Dren would have to comment on it.

No one commented.

Dren found a strip of iron to brace the main door and said, "If either of you start another honesty session tonight, do it outside."

Vel's mouth moved as if she might answer. Kem got there first. "I hadn't scheduled one."

"Good," Dren said. "Because I'd like six hours where the only collapse risk is geological."

He built a miserly little fire in the old cook pit out back, using fence splinters and a piece of broken crate. They ate cold grain mash from his stores with dried onion shredded into it and water that tasted faintly of metal from the station's catch barrel. It was not a meal anyone would remember on purpose. That made it useful. Ordinary business still needed doing even when the day had just broken in half.

The cut stung every time he flexed. Dren noticed and tossed him a clean scrap of cloth. "Wrap it right. Dust'll make it ugly."

"I know how to wrap it."

"Then do it."

Kem did. Vel sat two paces away on the station platform, knees up, route journal open in the last light. She read with the concentration she gave everything practical, one thumb following a line down the margin. Now and then the page shifted and he caught the second script in the book, the one that wasn't hers. He looked away before the curiosity could turn into a question. He was too full already.

When darkness finally flattened the fields, Dren took first watch without asking, mostly because his wound made him slowest to settle and fastest to wake anyway. Kem lay on his bedroll with the signet heavy in his pocket and the Ledger light enough now that sleep should have come easier. It didn't.

The station timbers clicked as they cooled. Somewhere out in the fields ground moved with a slow granular hush, not enough to call danger, enough to remind everyone what the land liked to do after dark. Dren coughed once, spat into the yard, and settled again. Vel turned a page in the journal.

Kem closed his eyes. Opened them. Closed them again.

Every time he drifted, some memory of the road came up altered. Vel teaching him how to build a fire low so it would not advertise them at night. Vel standing over him after the fever, not touching him, just staying awake because the body might turn bad too fast if nobody watched. Vel on the labor road, face white with pain in the cracked ribs, still snapping instructions while he flung levels around like a man trying to buy absolution at retail price. It would have been easier if the memories had gone false cleanly. Instead they kept arriving as proof of two incompatible things that had both happened.

He rolled onto one elbow. Dren had his hat over his eyes now, which did not mean he was asleep. Vel was still outside on the platform, journal closed, both arms braced behind her. She was looking south into the dark as if it could be counted.

Kem sat up.

Dren moved the hat a fraction. "Your watch?"

"Need to piss."

"Take a knife."

Kem picked one up from beside the cook pit because refusing would start a conversation. He walked past the scale platform, past the last of the station stones, and kept going even after he had reached a patch of scrub that would have served the stated purpose just fine.

The night air had cooled by a few degrees, enough to make sweat dry tacky on his shirt. Ahead, the road was only a lighter strip through darker ground. He could still turn north from here. The thought came without drama. Just a direction laid over another direction.

He kept walking.

At twenty paces the station fire was a pinprick. At fifty it disappeared behind the rise. The signet was in his fist without him remembering taking it from his pocket. Metal warmed fast there. He imagined following the road back through the farmstead, back past the broken lane, back through the Spent Towns, back into every version of himself he had already left. The image would not stay coherent. Too much had happened in between. There was no clean reverse route. There was only weathering.

The road dipped, and he stopped at the lip of another failed section. Not a full collapse, just a long split where the shoulder had peeled away into darkness. In daylight it would have been a nuisance. At night it was a warning against melodrama. One wrong foot and all the furious clarity in his head would reduce to a snapped leg in a ditch.

He stood there, breathing through his nose, and understood with exhausting precision that leaving now would not be refusal. It would be stupidity. He did not have the map. He did not know the water points. Sable was still somewhere behind them with whatever remained of the pursuit. The north did not mean innocence anymore. It meant choosing different people to use him, or dying on principle in a drainage channel while the world went on being itself.

Thirty-one hummed under his sternum. Not enough to feel invincible. Enough to feel every bad option.

He looked down at the signet in his grip.

In the dark it had no family resemblance at all. Just weight. A circle of metal cut for a finger that no longer matched the body carrying it. He thought of throwing it into the split and losing it to the same ground that had taken so much else. He even drew his arm back a little. But the gesture stalled before release, not because he had forgiven anything about where it came from, and not because he still wanted to belong to it. He kept it because it was evidence. Because the road south kept insisting that his origin mattered even when he hated the fact. Because one day, if he made it to zero or didn't, he wanted something solid to hate without having to reconstruct it from memory.

He put it back in his pocket and turned around.

The station reappeared gradually, a darker block against the dark, then a small amber line where the coals still lived. Dren was awake when Kem came back. He did not lift his head from the rolled blanket under it.

"Any trouble?" Dren asked.

"Road's split fifty paces north."

"Everything's split."

Kem stood there another second. Dren opened one eye.

"If you're going," Dren said, "go in daylight. At least then you'd know which mistake killed you."

There was no accusation in it. No comfort either. Just the sort of practical statement Dren made when emotion threatened to turn into inefficiency.

"I'm not going," Kem said.

Dren's eye closed again. "Then sleep."

Kem looked toward the platform. Vel had not moved while he was gone, or if she had, she was back in the same posture now, elbows on knees, shoulders folded in. Maybe she had watched him leave. Maybe she hadn't. Maybe she had decided that stopping him would be another kind of manipulation she had no right to attempt. He could not tell from this distance, and tonight he distrusted every guess he made about her.

He took second watch anyway, mostly because sleep still felt impossible. Dren went inside without argument. Vel stayed where she was until the moonless dark had fully settled, then came to stand by the platform steps.

"There's a wash east of here," she said. "If the ground talks loud, don't run that way."

Logistics. Nothing else.

"Fine."

She nodded once and started back inside.

The fact that she was still giving him useful instructions made anger surge up so quickly he almost called her back just to start it again. Instead he let her go and sat on the edge of the stone platform with the knife across his knees.

Night watch emptied the head out in strange intervals. For a while all he could think about was the specific pressure of the scale platform under him, the roughness of stone through his trousers, the ache in the cut, the reduced but constant hum of the Ledger. Then thoughts came back in loose clusters. His father in the Conservatory, preserved into absence. The labor notice on the farm wall. The family at the grain shed counting days of food against risk of burial. Vel at eighteen, still moving south with cracked ribs and no room left for error. Himself at thirty-one, furious enough to leave and too altered to do it cleanly.

At some point a dry cough came from inside the station. Vel's. Then another, choked off hard in the middle as if she had trapped it against her own ribs. He could picture the pain of that too well after watching her with the cracked side. Instinct rose before judgment, the old ready motion of getting up, checking, asking if she needed anything.

He stayed where he was.

The staying did not feel like victory. It felt like setting one hurt down on top of another and calling the stack discipline.

When dawn finally started paling the eastern edge of the fields, it came colorless at first, then yellow and merciless. Dren emerged rubbing his face and muttering about his arm. Vel came out last, journal packed, expression blank with the kind of tiredness she wore as function. Nobody mentioned the night walk. Nobody mentioned the cough. Nobody mentioned anything from the farmhouse because there was no version of the conversation that would not either reopen it or fake its closure.

They packed in near silence.

Kem slid the signet into the side pocket of his bag instead of his coat. It belonged off his body for now. He checked the wrap, tightened it, and shouldered the pack. The rough edge of the cloth under the strap pressed into the split skin and reminded him of the grain sacks, the boy's torn collar, the six sacks salvaged instead of nine, the kind of choices that had become normal so gradually he could no longer remember what normal was before them.

They left the weigh station as the sun got its full heat on the land. Dren walked between them again, bad arm close to his ribs. Vel kept the route and Kem kept the silence. The road narrowed, then gave up and became two tracks through broken ground. Somewhere ahead the basin waited. Somewhere behind them the farmer's road had probably finished collapsing. The day already smelled of dust and hot stone.

Kem kept walking.
