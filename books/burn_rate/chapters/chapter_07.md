# Chapter 7

The next morning the wrong pack sat beside Kem's blanket.

Vel had switched them while he slept. He knew it the moment he hauled it upright. Flour, pan, both water skins, something dense and wrapped in canvas at the bottom. The load rode meaner than the one he'd gone to sleep beside. Yesterday's bruises woke as soon as the straps found them.

He built the fire himself. The tinder caught, died, caught again. By the time Vel came back from the trees, there was a low ugly flame chewing through damp twigs and Kem was standing beside it smoke-eyed and pleased in a way he would've denied if asked. She looked at the fire, then at the pack on his shoulders.

"Walk," she said.

He did. By noon the straps had cut hot lines into his collarbones and the base of his spine felt nailed together. The Ledger sat behind all of it, patient and available. A minor Strength Charm would've made the next climb easier. A balance correction would've kept the load from dragging him sideways on the descent. He thought about both. He kept walking anyway, because she'd said he'd do it again and this was plainly what she'd meant.

That first day he spent nothing. He got the evening fire going with wet bark and temper. He carried the heavier load until his shoulders stopped feeling like separate parts of him. When they made camp, Vel lifted the pack off him, pressed a thumb once into the strap dents, and said, "Better."

That night his arms shook when he tried to work the buckles loose. He slept mostly on his back because either shoulder lit up when he rolled. At dawn Vel switched the packs again without comment. The day after that she made him take the heavier one only on climbs. Then she stopped explaining the pattern at all. He quit asking what each variation was meant to prove and started assuming the proof was whichever part of him hurt.

He held to that longer than he would've guessed and less long than he should have. On the third night the flint turned slick in steady drizzle and he burned nearly a level on a Flame Charm because he was cold and tired and had started to hate friction. Vel looked at the clean blue coal, then at the wet flint beside it.

"Warm now?" she asked.

"Yes."

She nodded once and went back to chewing dried meat. That was worse than anger. By the time he lit the cave two days later, he was already explaining the spend to himself before he made it: one level, barely one, dark like that wasn't useful to anybody. The morning before the settlement, he shaved a climb with Strength after waking sore in places he hadn't known to name. None of it was necessary. Each time he found a reason first and the spend second.

By Vel's reckoning, the new Hoarded pursuit had been on the road for six days. On Kem's own count, he'd been gone from the Vault eleven. The road improved before the town appeared. For six days Kem had been walking on dirt tracks, game trails, whatever path Vel chose that kept them below ridgelines and out of sight. Now the ground under his boots was packed and graded, not anything the Vault would recognize as a proper road, but something a Shaper had laid down and the town had kept passable. The surface was compressed and even, rainwater channels cut along both sides. The ditch edges were sharp. Somebody had cleared them recently. The road had been paid for again and again.

Vel didn't comment on it. She adjusted her pack and kept walking, her pace the same efficient tempo she held whether the ground was flat or climbing, and Kem had stopped trying to match it and settled into his own stride, which was longer but less economical. The blisters he'd raised the first two days had hardened into calluses. That counted as progress.

The settlement came into view as they crested a long rise: a cluster of buildings in a shallow valley, sixty or seventy structures, with fields spreading out on three sides and a line of low hills to the south. Early summer light sat heavy on the rooftops, and the heat had a different quality here than in the forested highlands, drier and closer, pressing against exposed skin and staying there. It was the largest thing Kem had seen since the Vault, and it looked nothing like the Vault. The buildings were stone and timber, shaped but not uniform. Some walls curved where a Shaping ability had been applied freehand, others squared off block by block. Repairs showed as visible seams between ability-work and manual labor, and nobody had bothered to make the seams invisible. The Vault's architecture existed to deny that maintenance was necessary. This place wore its maintenance like working clothes.

What struck him was the sound.

The Vault was quiet.

This settlement hummed.

A low, warm vibration ran under everything: Fertility Charms, he realized, active in the fields to the east. Not one but dozens, the combined output of farmers spending levels daily to make soil yield more than it would on its own. The sound was like standing near a hive, a thrumming he felt in his back teeth before he heard it with his ears. Layered over that came the crack and grind of Shaping work, someone reshaping stone on the settlement's far side, a distant rhythm of ability striking material. And closer, another pulse he couldn't identify at first. Rhythmic, gentle, warm. It rose and fell like breathing.

"What's that?" he asked.

Vel had stopped to let him look. She followed his gaze toward a low building on the settlement's southern edge, its door propped open with a stone. "Healing ward. They've got a healer who takes patients. That pulse is a Restoration Charm running."

"Running. As in, continuously?"

"She keeps it going for an hour each morning. Costs her a level every few weeks, depending on how many people she's working on."

A level every few weeks. If the healer was at, say, 180, she'd been feeding that ward for years, maybe decades. Morning after morning, a little of her own body turned into other people's knees and lungs and knitted skin. In the Vault, polite families would have lowered their voices over her name: *she had such potential, but she spent it all.* In the Conservatory Rite, they would have seated her near the door, if they'd admitted her at all.

From where Kem stood, listening to the warm pulse of her ability carrying over the morning air, she sounded like the most useful person in the valley.

They walked in. The settlement's main road was wider than the approach, lined with buildings that served as both homes and businesses: a tanner's workshop with hides stretched on a frame outside, a grain store with its doors open and the smell of dry seed carrying out, a place that sold tools and rope and had been expanded three times in different decades, each addition visible in the stonework. People were out, working, calling to each other across the road, carrying loads without haste. A man with a crate on his shoulder nodded at Vel as he passed. She nodded back. Two women sat on a bench outside a doorway, shelling peas into a bowl between them, talking in voices too low for Kem to catch. One of them looked up at him, registered the way he kept stopping to take things in, and went back to her work.

Nobody nodded at Kem. They looked, though. Briefly. Long enough to sort him: outsider, soft, sheltered. The last one was accurate enough that it landed.

A woman was repairing a section of wall on a building to their left. She had her fingertips flat against the stone and the material was moving under them, bulging outward to fill a crack, settling back to flush. Her face was concentrated, her free arm braced against the wall for balance, and when she finished she stepped back and shook out her wrist like the joint ached. A man on a ladder above her called down a criticism Kem couldn't catch, and she laughed and told him to do it himself if he was going to complain about her pointing.

She moved to the next section and started again. Each repair cost a sliver of level, not much at once but enough to add up. Every time she leaned into the stone, the wall smoothed and a fresh stiffness climbed her wrist. She kept going.

Kem looked down at his own scraped knuckles. His Ledger pulsed at the edge of his awareness: 225. Twenty levels gone in six days, most of them to impatience. More than this woman was spending to keep a wall standing and her neighbors dry.

"We need supplies," Vel said. She'd been watching him watch the settlement, but she didn't say anything about it. "Food for the next stretch south, and I need route conditions. If the pursuit's on the trade road, we stop using the trade road."

She led them to a tavern, broad and low, with a clay-tile roof and a yard where someone had set up benches under a canvas shade. A few chickens scratched in the dirt near the entrance. Inside was dim and cool, smelling of grain beer, cooked onions, and the sour bite of old ale worked into the wood over decades. Half a dozen patrons sat at tables, men and women in working clothes, eating midday meals or drinking slowly. They looked up when Vel and Kem entered. The looks settled on Kem in the same measuring way they had outside, held for a beat, and moved on. One man went back to his food. A woman at the bar turned fully to study him, then turned away.

The tavern keeper came from a back room, a crate of clay bottles braced against her hip. She was perhaps fifty, heavy-set, with her hair pulled back under a cloth and forearms thick from years spent lifting, pouring, and hauling stock. A leather brace wrapped her left knee, dark with old oil where the straps crossed. When she shifted her weight, she did it around that leg. She set the crate on the bar with a practiced thud and looked at them.

"Passing through?" she said to Vel.

"Couple of days. We need dried food for six days' travel south. Whatever you've got."

"I can put that together." The keeper's eyes moved to Kem and took him in once. "Him too?"

"Him too."

She started pulling items from below the bar: wrapped packages of dried meat, hard bread, a pouch of grain that smelled nutty. She moved with quick practiced economy, no abilities, each package found without looking, each bottle set down without a clink. Kem stood back while Vel negotiated quantities and price, and he let his eyes move around the room. The tavern was plain but swept clean. Decorative grooves had been shaped into the stone walls near the ceiling, a small touch of craft that had cost a fraction of a level and was worth it to whoever lived here. The tables were scarred from use. A cat slept on a chair near the window, its tail hanging off the edge.

The keeper set the food packages on the bar and named a price. Vel counted marks from a leather pouch, Kem's marks, technically, converted from the portable valuables he'd taken from the Vault. Coins and small trade goods that amounted to more money than most people in this room earned in a season. Vel paid without comment, and the keeper sorted the coins into a box beneath the counter.

Then her eyes dropped to the ring against the bar.

His House signet was still on him. He'd taken it from the ceremonial stand at the Vault gate eleven days ago, not because he'd decided to bring it but because leaving it would have meant he'd thought the whole thing through. He'd been wearing it since. It was his father's crest and his grandfather's. In the Vault, he'd worn things like that the way he'd worn sleeves.

The keeper's face changed.

It wasn't anger. Her face simply shut a door. The professional courtesy she'd been showing Vel drained out of her expression and what replaced it was flat and careful. She looked at the signet, then at Kem's face, then at the signet again, and her mouth became a line. The crease between her eyebrows deepened. Whatever memory the crest had pulled up, it had already reached its verdict, and Kem's road dust and awkward posture in her tavern did nothing to disturb it.

She finished stacking the food packages. She pushed them across the bar toward Vel, not Kem. "Anything else?" she said, and the temperature of her voice had dropped to a place where *anything else* meant *please leave*.

"No," Vel said. "That's fine."

Kem opened his mouth. He didn't know what he was going to say. An explanation, an apology, some version of *I'm not what you think,* which was absurd because she was looking at the physical proof of exactly what he was. He closed his mouth. The keeper picked up her empty crate and went back through the doorway without looking at him again.

He stood there with his wrist on the bar and the ring still visible in the silence the keeper had left in her wake. The man at the nearest table glanced over, saw it, and looked away, finished with him already.

Kem looked down at the signet. On him, it had always looked ordinary. The keeper had looked at it the way people looked at rot in a grain sack. He turned his wrist inward.

Vel was shouldering her pack. She'd seen the whole thing. She didn't turn toward him or offer commentary. She gave him room to stand in it.

"I didn't know," he said, once they were outside.

Vel squinted against the midday sun. "You didn't know what?"

He started to answer and stopped. The keeper's face came back to him, the neat way it had closed. The ring had done all the talking.

"Any of it," he said. "What any of this looked like from out here."

"Yeah." She adjusted a strap on her pack. "Come on. I need to find someone about the southern routes."

---

They found Dren at the market yard.

Vel had stopped at the settlement's well and asked a man drawing water about a trader who knew the country south of the Spent Towns. The man jerked his chin west, then let his gaze drop to the ring. "Dren. He's loading up over there. Supposed to head east tomorrow. Unless east can wait."

"We need him heading south."

"Then offer enough to make him stupid." The man braced the bucket between his boots and looped the rope over the well post. "Bellwasher came in from the north road at dawn saying a Hoarded party spent silver in Ashmarker for a description of a tall pale man and an older woman traveling together. Somebody mentioned a house-ring." His eyes flicked to the signet again. "That's been the morning's entertainment."

Vel's expression stayed flat. "How far north is Ashmarker?"

"Day and a half by wagon. Less if they're riding light."

Kem tucked the ring against the strap of his pack without meaning to. It pressed cold into his skin.

"Thanks," Vel said.

The market yard was a packed-dirt clearing on the settlement's western edge where carts and pack animals and stacked goods created a working chaos Kem found difficult to parse. People argued over quantities, loaded crates, inspected produce. A boy chased a loose goat between two carts and crashed into a woman carrying a bolt of cloth. She swore at him without surprise and kept walking. A man was using a minor Shaping ability to repair a cracked cart wheel, the wood swelling and sealing under his touch, a job that would've taken a carpenter half a day and here took thirty seconds at the cost of a fraction of a level. The spending was constant and casual, part of the working rhythm, and nobody around treated it as anything other than getting things done.

Dren was loading his cart near the yard's far fence. He was shorter than Kem had assumed, stocky and road-darkened, with a pale rope-burn scar along the outside of his left forearm. He loaded with old-route precision, each item placed where it balanced the load, each tie cinched with a knot Kem couldn't imagine coming loose. His boots were battered, resoled at least once, the leather cracking at the ankles. He wore a belt crowded with gear: a knife, a water flask, a coil of cord, a pouch Kem couldn't identify.

Vel walked up to him directly. "You Dren? I'm told you know the routes south."

He looked up from a strap he was tightening. His eyes went over Vel first, quick and practical: the knife, the road dust, the fact that she came straight to his cart instead of looking for someone else to ask. Then he looked at Kem longer, at the signet, at the half-step of hesitation before he settled where he was standing.

"I know them," Dren said. He went back to his strap. "Southern routes are shit. Towns thin out after two days, then it's hill country, then the Margins." He didn't look up. "Nobody goes there who doesn't have to." His gaze touched the signet. "And unless this yard's produced another pale bastard in the last hour, you're the pair the north road's talking about."

"We're going on purpose," Vel said.

"Figured." He tested the strap with a sharp tug. "You and the lord."

"He's not —"

"He's Hoarded. I can tell by the way he's standing. The ring helps. He's standing in my market yard like he's waiting for someone to bring him a chair." Dren looked at Kem. "No offense."

"I'm not waiting for a chair," Kem said.

"Sure. What are you paying?"

Vel named the sum. Kem watched Dren's face as the number landed — a flicker around the eyes, then a glance down at the cart, as if the marks were already being cut into smaller uses.

"That's a damn double eastern run," Dren said. "Before I lose half of it dumping the cart."

"Yes," Vel said.

He straightened up. Where Vel counted levels, Dren counted marks, sale prices, and days away from home.

"South to where?" Dren asked.

"Past the settlements' southern edge. Through the hill country to the rougher terrain beyond. We need someone who knows the water sources and the safe camps."

"I know the water. Terrain's fine." He spat to one side. "It's the people in it. Bandits. Folks drifting north from the Margins who'll take your pack and maybe feel bad about it." He squinted at Kem. "He know how to handle himself?"

"He's learning," Vel said.

"How fast?"

"Fast enough."

Dren didn't look convinced. He rubbed his jaw with the back of his wrist, leaving a streak of road dust on his cheek. "How long?"

"Five, six days to the edge. Then you're done and you head back."

"I was heading east tomorrow. Callowfield. Reliable buyers, reliable contract." He picked at a rope knot near his hip. "I need that money."

"I'm asking you to take better money for a week's work."

"Yeah, and I know why it's better." He leaned against his cart and crossed his arms. "South routes pay well because south routes are shit." His forearms were thick from years of loading cargo, and the old scar caught the light. "I've got a healer up in Roth I'm trying to get to, and every run's part of that." He looked at the wheel rut by his boot. "My girl. Mira. Her legs are —" He rubbed the back of his wrist across his jaw and looked at his cart. "Point is, your number gets me closer than Callowfield. But Callowfield doesn't go south."

"It's six days," Vel said. "We're not looking for trouble."

"You're paying me double an eastern run to go south fast. I've been hauling cargo twenty years." He looked at Kem. "He's wearing a Hoarded signet in Spent Towns. That's not six days I want to sign up for."

A silence opened. The market yard moved around them. Carts loaded, people argued, the boy chased his goat back the other way. Dren stood with one arm braced on the cart wheel, saying nothing. The market kept clattering around them. He let the silence sit until it started to feel expensive.

"There are people looking for him," Vel said. "They're behind us. If we move smart, they stay behind us."

Dren absorbed this. His expression didn't change. "Hoarded retrieval?"

"Close enough."

"Great." He scratched the back of his neck. The gesture pulled his collar aside and showed more road-darkened skin, shirt salt-stiff at the seam. "So if they catch up, I'm the bastard standing next to him."

"We've handled them before," Vel said.

"How many levels did 'handling them' cost?"

Vel didn't answer immediately, and that was its own kind of answer. Dren caught it.

"Right." He uncrossed his arms. "Half up front. Other half before I turn back." He looked at the cart. "Cart stays here — I take it south and every wheel rut tells them where we went. I need today to dump what I can, stash the rest, and hear whether Bellwasher was drunk or useful."

"We leave tomorrow morning," Vel said.

"Tomorrow morning means I'm selling at a loss. Anything I can't move by tonight sits in someone's shed and I'll be lucky to get two-thirds of it back. That cost comes out of your side."

Vel looked at Kem. Kem looked at the pouch on her belt. His Vault money, converted into the currency of a world where people worked for what they had.

"Agreed," Kem said.

"Agreed," Dren said. He stepped away from the cart and took a fuller measure of Kem, like he was deciding whether the load was worth the road. "You ever carried a pack for more than a day before this?"

"Ten days now."

"How's it feel?"

"My shoulders hurt."

Dren's mouth twitched once, the closest he'd come to approval. "Tomorrow morning. Be ready." He turned back to his cart, then stopped. "And put that ring somewhere it can't catch the light. I don't need the southern settlements deciding I'm running escort for a Hoarded inspection."

He started untying the ropes on his cargo, then glanced back once. "Camp outside the settlement. I'll find you after dark. If Bellwasher was telling the truth, tomorrow changes."

Vel turned and headed for the edge of the market yard. Kem followed, adjusting his pack where the straps bit into the muscle at the base of his neck. The ache ran from there into the shoulder joint. Vel had told him it would eventually become background noise. It hadn't yet.

They found a spot outside the settlement to camp for the night, in a grove of trees where the ground was soft with leaf litter and the air smelled of warm earth and dry grass. Vel built a fire the way she always did, flint and dry tinder, no abilities, each motion practiced. The settlement sat below them in the fading light, its shapes softening as evening came on. The Fertility Charm hum had quieted as the farmers stopped for the day, but the healing ward's pulse still ran, faint and steady, carrying over the distance.

From the rise between the trees, Kem could still see the market yard. Dren worked it until the light thinned, unloading his cart at a pace that made the haggling uglier. A woman in a blue apron talked him down on a stack of cured hides and he let her, jaw tight, because taking the insult cost less than waiting for a fairer buyer. Two boys rolled a barrel toward a shed while Dren argued with a man over storage space and finally counted out marks as if peeling skin. Kem watched until distance turned them into shapes. Every hurried sale was another price attached to escorting him.

Kem sat on a rock near the fire and worked the signet around with his thumb. The metal was warm from his skin and from the long day's sun. He thought about taking it off, putting it in his pocket. Then he pictured the keeper pushing the food toward Vel and left it where it was.

He left it on.

Full dark had not settled yet when footsteps came through the leaf litter. Dren emerged between the trees with a road pack on his shoulders and no cart behind him. He set the pack down by the fire and wiped his forearm across his mouth.

"Bellwasher was useful," he said. "So was a cooper's apprentice who came in late. Your people were in Ashmarker asking smart questions and paying stupid money. They know you're south. They don't know which road we take. I'd like to keep it that way."

Vel looked up. "Cart's dealt with?"

"Sold half. Stashed half. Lost money on both. You'll hear about it if I live long enough." He pointed south. "We leave before the sky's properly light. Ditch line east of the barley, then west once the farms stop watching. No fire after we move. No abilities unless somebody's bleeding or about to stop. And cover the ring."

His eyes settled on Kem for the last part. "I mean it. People here don't love the Hoarded enough to keep their mouths shut for free."

"I heard you," Kem said.

Dren nodded once. "Good." He jerked his chin downslope, toward a darker patch of trees. "I'm bedding down there. Wake me if somebody's dying. Not for anything smaller."

He picked up the pack and went.

Vel watched him disappear between the trees, then ground the fire lower with the end of a branch. "Sleep while you can. We move the minute he wakes."

Kem pulled a strip of cloth from the bottom of his pack, one of the clean scraps Vel kept for binding cuts, and wrapped it over the signet until the crest disappeared under dingy linen. The knot came out ugly. It held.

Below them, the settlement darkened by degrees. A door thudded shut. Someone laughed once and stopped. Farther off, a dog started up and another answered. Kem tried to picture the route Dren had just bought for them: ditch line east of the barley, west once the farms stopped watching, no wheel ruts, no fire.

A day and a half north, people had paid to learn his face. Before dawn, they would be off the road and into the fields before that money could catch up.
