# Chapter 3

She found him by the trail he'd left.

Not footprints — those were everywhere, and half the north road carried traffic this time of year. What Vel followed was the residue. Two days north of the settlement where she'd healed the farmer, she picked up the first sign: a scorch mark in a roadside clearing where someone had used a Flame Charm on a pile of dry kindling. The kindling itself was still there, blackened to powder, the heat signature too uniform and too thorough for any natural fire. Someone who didn't know what a campfire was supposed to look like had pointed an ability at it and held on until everything burned.

A mile further, the food. She smelled it before she saw it — the sick-sweet reek of Sustenance Charm output decomposing in late-spring warmth. She stepped off the road and found a trampled patch of meadow where enough food for forty people had been conjured and left. Bread, fruit, some kind of stew in a conjured basin that had half-collapsed as the ability's residual structure faded. Most of it was three days old and turning to gray mush in the sun. Insects had found it. A fox had dragged something meaty into the brush.

Forty portions. One person. She'd heard the stories already — a Hoarded lord who'd walked out of the Vault, House Aldraste's son, apparently sound of mind and unsound of everything else. The settlements between the Vault and the highlands were mentioning it the way they mentioned anything the Hoarded did: with the flat attention of people who knew it didn't concern them. Vel had been walking hard for two days because it concerned her very much.

She kept going. A stream crossing showed the shimmer of a Purification ability used on water that was already clean — she cupped a handful from the same stream and drank it, tasting nothing but cold and the faint mineral edge of snowmelt. Another clearing, this one with the grass pressed flat in a precise rectangle where a Shelter Projection had been maintained overnight. The rectangle was absurdly large for one person. Temperature-regulated, too, from the way the grass had grown differently inside the projection's footprint — an ability designed for a military bivouac, used as a mattress on a warm spring night.

She did the arithmetic as she walked. The Sustenance Charm — that was the big one, eight to ten levels for a full-company output. The Flame Charm, one level. Shelter Projection, two to three depending on duration. Purification, one. The Projection Shield he'd used to leave the Vault — she'd heard that part of the story, the door blown open in the middle of a Conservatory Rite — was three levels minimum, probably more if he'd overdriven it the way he seemed to overdrive everything. Add whatever she couldn't see, the small spends that left no visible trace, and he was somewhere around —

She stopped on the road and pressed the heels of her hands into her eyes.

Around 286. In three days.

Vel had been 286 once. She'd been twenty years old and already three years into a pattern of spending that would define her life, and at 286 she'd felt the first real awareness that the number went in one direction. She'd spent one level that day — a Binding to secure a collapsed beam in a settlement the Hoarded had abandoned to rain damage — and stood afterward feeling the absence like a chip taken from a tooth. Subtle. Permanent. The beginning of an arithmetic that never stopped.

This man had burned fourteen levels on food and fire and clean water and a warm place to sleep.

She found him in the early afternoon, four hundred meters past the last clearing. He was sitting on a fallen log beside the road with his boots off, examining his feet with the pained concentration of someone encountering a problem he genuinely didn't understand. The boots sat beside him — soft-soled, pale leather, the kind designed for the Vault's polished stone corridors. They were scuffed and dirt-stained and one had split along the sole where road gravel had worked through material that had never been meant to touch ground.

Vel stopped at the edge of the tree line and watched him.

He was tall. Young — mid-twenties, the unlined face and full color of someone whose body had never been asked to do anything. Pale skin going pink at the nose and ears from three days of sun he'd never been exposed to. Vault silks, once cream and gray, now snagged by branches and darkened with sweat and ground-in dirt. His hair was dark and needed cutting, or more likely had always been maintained by servants who no longer existed in his life. On his right hand, catching the afternoon light, a heavy signet ring: the House Aldraste crest.

As she watched, he produced a small shimmer of light — a Glow, the most basic illumination ability — and held it near his foot to examine a blister on the ball of his heel. He was sitting in partial shade. The sun, which was directly available two feet to his right, would have done the same job for free.

One level. To look at his own foot.

Vel stepped out of the trees.

He looked up sharply, and she saw several things move across his face at once: the quick contraction of surprise, then wariness, then — under both of those — a relief he didn't fully succeed in hiding. He'd been alone for three days. He didn't know how to be alone.

"Hello," he said. He dismissed the Glow with a flick of his hand that suggested long habit — abilities as casual as breathing. "I don't suppose you might be able to — that is, I've been trying to determine whether this road leads to the Spent Towns, but the route has been somewhat unclear. The crossroads back there had what I believe was a cairn, or possibly just accumulated rocks, and I wasn't entirely certain which branch it was indicating, and now I suspect I may have —"

"It goes to the highlands," Vel said. "Spent Towns are another week south of those."

"A week." He looked at his feet. The blisters were raw and layered — new ones forming on top of old ones, the kind of damage that came from pushing through pain you didn't understand because you'd never experienced it before. "Right. A week. On these."

"On those, no. You won't make three more days."

He nodded slowly, as if she'd confirmed something he'd been trying not to think about. "I'd considered — I do have a Mending ability. For the skin, I mean. But I suspect you're going to tell me that's not the right approach."

"I'm not going to tell you anything yet." She came closer. She was looking at his hands now. Soft. No calluses, no scars, no evidence of grip or impact or labor. The hands of someone who had held ceremonial objects and nothing else for twenty-six years. "How many levels have you spent since you left?"

The question landed differently than she'd expected. He didn't flinch or deflect. He just went still for a moment, that slight inward focus of someone checking their Ledger — and the casualness of it, the way he consulted the number the way she might check the position of the sun, confirmed something about where he'd come from. At 300, the Ledger was background noise. You didn't ration what you'd never had to count.

"I'm at two hundred and eighty-six," he said. Then, with careful precision: "That is rather more than I intended to spend. I'm aware of that."

Fourteen levels. Vel had spent fourteen levels in the last four months, and each one had been budgeted and planned and weighed against the journey ahead.

"Tell me what you spent them on," she said.

"You already know, don't you." He gestured vaguely north. "The trail. I wasn't — it didn't occur to me that the abilities would leave traces. I suppose that's obvious in retrospect."

"Tell me anyway."

He was quiet for a beat. Then he started listing them, and the formality of his speech — the Hoarded habit of complete, qualified, subordinated sentences — made the inventory worse. Each item arrived fully grammatical and absurd.

"There was the Projection Shield at the Rite, which I will not pretend was calibrated appropriately — it was my first ability use and I was not prepared for the output. Then outside the Vault walls, I produced sustenance, though the quantity was — I'm aware that I made food for approximately forty people. I was attempting a single portion. The interface between intention and result is not as intuitive as I'd been led to believe." He paused. "And then it was dark, and the road at night is genuinely quite dark — I had not expected that — so I used a Light ability. And a fire, because the instructions in the books all say to build fires at camp and I couldn't make the wood — I couldn't get it to catch, so I used a Charm. And the water in the stream had things floating in it —"

"Leaves."

"— which I could not identify at the time, so I cleaned it. And the ground is very hard and very cold at night, so there was a shelter of some kind, which I think was oversized because I didn't know how to — I didn't know the parameters." He stopped. Looked at her. "That's not all of it. There were smaller things. A light when I dropped something in the dark. A warming when the morning was cold. I'm not — I don't have a complete inventory because I wasn't tracking them at the time."

He said this last part with something that might have been embarrassment, though the Hoarded register made it hard to tell — it came out sounding like a formal admission of accounting irregularities rather than a man confessing he'd burned levels on things a child could do with a match and a blanket.

Vel sat down on the log beside him. Not because she wanted his company. Because she'd walked twenty miles in two days to find this man and her legs were telling her to sit or they'd stop cooperating.

"The leaves," she said. "In the water."

"Yes?"

"They were leaves."

"I understand that now."

A bird called from the tree line. The afternoon was warm and still, the road empty in both directions, and the faint stink of rotting conjured food drifted from the north on a breeze that didn't care about either of them.

"You know what a level costs," Vel said. It wasn't a question.

"In the abstract. The Hoarded teach the mechanics. Each use is a permanent reduction. No restoration, no recovery. It's — we learn this. We learn it the way you learn that fire is hot. A principle to be respected and avoided."

"Not the abstract. What it actually costs. What it means to go from 286 to 285 and know you're never getting that back."

He considered this with more seriousness than she'd expected. "I don't think I do. Not yet. At 286 the loss still feels — theoretical. There's so much left. It's like spending from a full purse. You know the coins are gone but the weight hasn't changed."

That was honest. It was also exactly what she'd expected, and it made the disgust worse because it wasn't stupidity — it was the specific ignorance of someone who'd never had to count. He understood the principle. He couldn't feel the arithmetic. At 286, you couldn't. At 31, the arithmetic was the loudest thing in any room.

"Why did you leave," she said.

He looked at the road. At his ruined feet. At the signet ring on his hand, which he turned once, unconsciously, before answering.

"My father is level three hundred. He has been three hundred for sixty-one years. He sits in the Conservatory every week with perfect discipline and perfect composure, and he is —" Kem stopped. When he started again, the formal register cracked just slightly, a simpler sentence pushing through the qualifications. "He's dying. Not — his body is fine. His Ledger is untouched. But he's empty. He's been emptying for years and no one will say it because his number is still perfect. The Hoarded call it discipline. It's not discipline. It's — I don't know what it is. But I watched it happen and I couldn't watch anymore."

"Stillness," Vel said.

"You know the word."

"Everyone outside the Vault does. They just don't care, because it only eats the people who chose to do nothing."

He absorbed the sharpness of that without flinching, which surprised her. "That's fair," he said. "I think that's fair."

They sat. The bird called again. A cart passed on the road — a farmer heading north with a load of early vegetables, who glanced at them with the mild disinterest of someone who saw strangers on roads every day and would continue to see them long after these two were gone.

"I want to reach zero," he said. Not grandly, not as a declaration. He said it the way someone might say they wanted to get home before dark — a practical statement about where they were headed. "I don't know what zero is. I don't know if it's — whether it's an end or a beginning or just nothing. But I'd rather be at nothing than at three hundred and whatever he is."

Zero. He said it the way everyone said it who didn't know what it meant. The end of the count. The bottom. He didn't know it was a door, that she'd seen someone step through it and vanish into somewhere real, that she'd been trying to follow for eight years. He just knew he didn't want to be his father.

Vel looked at him — the ruined boots, the signet, the blisters, the sun-pink face of a man who'd been indoors for twenty-six years. Everything wrong with the Hoarded wrapped in expensive silk and sitting on a log unable to identify a leaf. He was waste made flesh — not the waste of spending, but the waste of everything the Hoarded represented. A system that preserved people until they rotted from the inside out, and a boy who'd walked away from it burning levels on campfires.

He was at 286.

The number was a physical thing in her chest. Two hundred and eighty-six. She'd been searching for this — for someone like this — for years. The mechanism required a sustained cascade, a high-output descent from a spender with enough levels to amplify the threshold's energy as they burned down. One person crossing alone was unstable — she'd seen that. She'd seen what unstable looked like, the threshold flickering, the terrifying half-second when it might not hold. She needed volume. She needed someone who could fall from a great height.

Two hundred and eighty-six. Even after the waste. Even after the food and the fire and the clean water and the warm bed and the light to look at his own goddamn feet. He was still at a number that could fuel the kind of descent the crossing required.

She let the satisfaction settle somewhere underneath the disgust, a quiet warmth she didn't examine. He solved a problem she'd been carrying since the day she'd watched Maren disappear.

---

"I'm heading south," Vel said. "To zero. I know the route, I know the territory, and I know what's there when you arrive."

The shift was immediate — he sat straighter, and the Hoarded formality returned like a reflex, the careful speech of someone trained to evaluate propositions. "You know what zero is?"

"I know what it is."

"And you would — what are you proposing, precisely?"

"That we travel together. I guide you south. I teach you how to survive without burning through your Ledger on every problem you can solve with your hands. We both reach zero."

He was quiet for long enough that she thought he might refuse. Then: "What do you need from me?"

"Your levels. I'm at thirty-one. The route south gets harder and the people in it get less friendly. I can't afford to spend on everything the road's going to throw at us. I need someone with levels to handle what I can't."

That was true. It was also not all of it. The mechanism, the cascade, the specific way his high-level descent would stabilize the threshold — that was the part she kept folded and locked behind practical language. He didn't need to know yet. He didn't need to know until they were deep enough that the alternative to continuing was dying on a road, and by then it would be too late to care about the terms.

She was aware of what she was doing. She was aware that it was the kind of calculation the Hoarded made every day — evaluating a person's usefulness and structuring an arrangement to extract it. She didn't like the parallel. She filed it where she filed most things she didn't like and kept talking.

"What happens at zero?" he said. "You said you know. You said something's there."

"I'll tell you when you need to know."

"That's — when will I need to know?"

"When we're closer."

He leaned forward, and the formal hedging thinned into something more direct. "I'm going to be spending my levels on this journey. Every level I spend is permanent. I think I have a right to know what I'm spending them toward."

"You'd be spending them anyway. You just told me you want to reach zero."

"Wanting to reach zero and agreeing to reach it under your direction with no understanding of what zero contains are — those are not the same commitment."

She waited. He waited. They watched each other across a gap that was wider than the two feet of log between them.

"There's something at zero," she said. "It's not death. It's not the Stillness. It's a real place. I've seen it." She held the rest of it closed — the threshold, Maren, the cascade, the years of searching, all of it — and gave him one true thing. "If you reach zero the right way, in the right place, you don't end. You go somewhere."

"Somewhere."

"Somewhere that isn't this." She gestured at the road, the trees, the general condition of a world governed by a number that only went down. "That's what I'll tell you now. The rest comes later."

He processed this the way he processed everything — aloud, working through it in real time, as if thought only became real when it was spoken. "So you're asking me to trust you with the destination, trust you with the pacing, trust you with my levels —"

"I'm asking you to walk south with someone who knows the road when you don't know what a leaf looks like."

"I know what a leaf looks like."

"You purified a stream because of one."

"That's not —" He stopped. Started again. "All right. Fine. That's a valid point."

"Terms," Vel said. "I guide, you follow. I decide when you spend and on what. I decide the route, the pace, and the stops. I tell you what you need to know when you need to know it."

"And my role in this arrangement is —"

"You walk. You learn. You don't use a single ability without asking me first."

"That's. Those aren't terms. Those are orders."

"Yes."

"You understand that I'm at two hundred and eighty-six and you're at thirty-one. I don't mean that as — I'm not threatening anything, but in any standard assessment of bargaining position —"

"You can't start a fire."

He opened his mouth. Closed it.

"You can't identify clean water," Vel said. "You can't walk six miles without destroying your feet. You can't navigate a road with a cairn on it. You've been outside for three days and you've spent fourteen levels on problems that don't require abilities. At your current rate you'll be at zero in two months and you'll be standing on a road in the middle of nowhere with nothing around you and no one to tell you whether you just ran out or arrived somewhere. Do you want that to be how it ends?"

"No."

"Then my terms are the terms. Because my thirty-one can get us both where we need to go, and your two hundred and eighty-six can't get you to the Spent Towns."

He sat with that. She watched him sit with it — the internal negotiation of a man who'd been raised to command now encountering the specific humiliation of needing someone with a number that wouldn't register as a rounding error in the Vault's accounting.

"I'd like to propose a modification," he said.

Vel waited.

"To the information condition. I accept that you have context I don't have, and I accept that there may be things I shouldn't know yet — I'm not naive enough to think that information shared at the wrong time is the same as information shared at the right time. But I'd like your word that you will tell me. Not never. Not when it's convenient for you. When I genuinely need to know, by your judgment, but with the understanding that you'll tell me you're holding something back. I don't need to know what it is. I need to know there's something I don't know."

That was smart. It was the best thing he'd said, and it was exactly the kind of condition she couldn't fully honor — because the mechanism's specifics, the fact that she'd spent years looking for a high-level defector willing to burn down, the fact that he was a solution to a problem rather than a person she'd happened upon — all of that was information that would make any sane man walk away. And she needed him to keep walking.

"I'll tell you what I can, when I can," she said. "If I'm holding something back, I'll tell you there's something I'm not sharing."

"But not what."

"Not what. Not until you're ready for it."

"'Ready' being determined by —"

"Me."

He exhaled. A long, deliberate breath that she recognized — the Hoarded substitute for the profanity they'd trained out of their children. His hands rested on his knees, the signet ring catching light, and she could see him weighing it. The road south, alone, in split boots, burning levels on everything. Or the road south with a woman who wouldn't tell him where they were going but knew how to get there.

"Fine," he said. "Your terms."

"Good."

"I want to — I'd like to say something."

"Say it."

"I don't trust you."

"Good."

"I want to, though. At some point."

Vel looked at him. Twenty-six years old. Three days free. Blistered, sunburned, sitting on a log in the ruins of his only life with a ring he couldn't stop touching and a number that meant nothing to him yet. He was everything she'd spent four decades opposing — a Hoarded aristocrat, a monument to inaction, a walking argument for the system that had ground down everyone she'd ever cared about. And he'd looked at his father's empty eyes and walked out of a door that only opened once, because he'd rather be at zero than at three hundred and gone.

She almost liked him for that. She pushed it aside.

"First thing," she said.

"Already?"

"Take off those boots."

He looked down. "They are off."

"I mean get rid of them. Throw them in the ditch."

"These are —" He stopped himself. "Yes. Fine. They're terrible for this. I know. But what do I walk in?"

"There's a trading post six miles south. We'll buy you real boots."

"With what? I didn't bring — I have no money. The Vault doesn't use money internally."

"What's the signet worth?"

His hand went to the ring immediately, covering it. The movement was involuntary — deep, reflexive, the kind of gesture that came from the body's memory of what an object meant rather than from a conscious decision to protect it.

"It's not — that's my House seal. It isn't a trade good."

Vel looked at him.

"You're serious," he said.

"You've got two split-soled shoes and bleeding feet. That ring could buy you boots, socks, a pack, and enough dried food for a week."

"I —" He looked at the ring. Turned it on his finger. "I took this from the gate stand when I left. I didn't even think about it. I just — it was there, and I was leaving, and I took it. I'm not sure what that means."

"It means you're still deciding whether you left."

He stared at her. She'd hit something — she could see it land, a specific impact behind his eyes.

"I left," he said. Quieter. The Hoarded hedging stripped back to something flat. "I left."

"Then trade the ring."

A long moment. The bird had stopped calling. The afternoon was tilting toward evening, the light going amber through the trees, and the road stretched south between them and whatever came next.

"I'll keep it," he said. "Not because — it's not attachment. Or it is, but I'm not ready to — I'm not going to pretend I'm finished with it yet." He reached into the neck of his shirt and pulled out a flat piece of metalwork — a clasp, heavy, ornamental, the kind used to fasten formal Vault robes. "This. This is just a fastener. Would it work?"

"Probably."

"And I have a few other — formal pieces, from the Rite. Things I was wearing when I left. They're in my bag." He gestured at a small satchel on the ground — Vault-made, embroidered, absurd for road travel. "Would those buy boots?"

"They'd buy boots and leave change."

"Then that." He looked almost relieved. "Boots. From a trading post. With money I earned by — well. By having been born into the right family and wearing expensive things to a ceremony." The corner of his mouth moved. "I suppose that's a kind of spending too."

Vel stood up. Her back was stiff and her calves were humming from two hard days, and the man on the log was looking at her the way people looked at the first fixed point they found in a storm — not with trust, not yet, but with the specific hope that direction might exist.

"Six miles," she said. "Barefoot, on this road, that's about three hours. Your feet are going to hurt."

"They already hurt."

"They're going to hurt more."

He stood. She watched him put weight on the blisters and saw the wince he tried to control — not performing toughness, just the automatic reflex of someone who didn't want to seem weak in front of a person whose opinion had suddenly started to matter.

"Vel?"

"What."

"Thank you. For offering this. I understand it's not — I realize the arrangement is not ideal for either of us, and I don't want you to think I'm not aware of —"

"Walk."

He picked up his satchel, left the ruined boots on the log, and stepped onto the gravel road. The sharp stones pressed into his blisters immediately — she could see it in the way he shifted his weight, looking for a stride that distributed the pain.

"Don't spend anything," she said.

"Right. No. I know."

They walked.

Vel matched his pace, which was slow and uneven — he favored his right foot where the blisters were worst, and every few steps he made a small involuntary sound, a half-breath that wasn't quite a gasp. He didn't complain. She noted that. Didn't file it yet as trust or character — just observed it.

The road went south through mixed forest, the trees thick with new spring growth, the air warm and smelling of mud and green things coming alive. He walked beside her with the signet ring on his hand and gravel under his bleeding feet, and neither of them spoke for a long time.

The arithmetic ran in Vel's head the way it always ran, the way it had run for forty years — constant, habitual, the background hum of a life lived by subtraction. Him at 286. Her at 31. The route south: highlands first, then the Spent Towns, then the harder country beyond. She'd need to budget his spending — ration it, control it, keep him from draining himself on trivial comforts. She'd need to teach him to live in his body instead of his Ledger. She'd need to get him south with enough levels to matter when they arrived.

And she'd need to not tell him why.

The ugliness of that sat beside the satisfaction, and she held both. He solved a problem she'd been carrying for eight years — the gap in her plan, the variable she couldn't fill alone. A high-level spender willing to burn down. She'd asked about defectors in six settlements. She'd chased three rumors that came to nothing. And here he was, real and inconvenient, walking barefoot because she'd told him to and bleeding on a road he couldn't navigate.

He was going to be furious when he found out. She knew that the way she knew her own level — as a number that sat at the edge of consciousness, constant and unavoidable.

But that was later. Right now he was walking south, and his feet were bleeding, and his Ledger read 286, and it was enough. It had to be enough.

"Vel?"

"What."

"Is it always this far between places?"

"Yes."

"Oh."

He kept walking. She kept walking. The road went south and the sun went west and neither of them looked back.
