# Chapter 8

Vel counted the road south in levels.

The Spent Towns fell behind them over three days — the last of the managed farmland, the last settlements where a Fertility Charm still turned the soil green. The road narrowed from packed trade-route to dirt track, winding through fields that had been good once and were merely adequate now, land where the charm had been used up within its cycle and the ground was doing its own work for a change. Spring had settled into the warm part of itself. The air smelled like cut grain and drying mud.

Twenty-eight. That was the number she carried, the number she'd budgeted from, the number that had to stretch across the southern routes and whatever they found on them. She checked it the way she checked her pack straps — habitual, involuntary, a background process that ran beneath everything else. Twenty-eight.

Dren walked point. He read the road without thinking about it, the way a person reads a language they've spoken since birth — which ruts were fresh, which bootprints were local, where the track was solid and where yesterday's rain had softened it into something that would eat your ankle if you stepped wrong. He'd been doing this for twenty years, and it showed in how he moved: unhurried, efficient, his weight centered, his attention spread across the landscape without concentrating on any one part of it. He had a habit of touching the strap of his satchel when he was thinking — thumb and forefinger working the leather like a merchant counting coins in his pocket.

"There's a depot settlement about an hour ahead," Dren said, not turning around. "Grain storage, maybe an inn. We can top off the water and keep going."

"How big?" Vel asked.

"Small. Thirty, forty people. They service the farms around here."

Kem walked behind Vel, carrying his own pack for the fourth consecutive day without complaint. He'd stopped adjusting the straps every ten minutes, which meant his shoulders had either toughened or gone numb. He was watching the fields with the quiet attention of someone still learning what open land looked like — how light moved across grain, the patterns birds made when they lifted from a hedgerow. He'd started seeing things without needing to name them, and Vel had begun to find this less irritating and more like the beginning of something useful.

"Is that wheat?" Kem asked, nodding at a field to the left.

"Barley," Dren said.

"Right."

"You can tell by the—"

"By the awns. I know. Vel told me yesterday."

"Hm." Dren didn't look back. "She teach you what good barley looks like yet?"

"She told me not to worry about it."

"Smart." A pause. "That's not good barley, by the way. Fertility Charm's worn thin. You can see where it stops — the color changes about fifty meters in."

Vel could see it. A line across the field where the green darkened, the stalks thickened, and then — nothing dramatic, just a gradient — the barley became thinner, paler, working harder for less. The line between charmed land and uncharmed land. Every farmer in the Spent Towns knew that line. It was the border between what you could afford and what you couldn't.

They walked. The road dust settled on Vel's boots and the sun was warm on her neck and the only sounds were their footsteps, the insects, and the distant report of someone using a Shaping ability at one of the farms — a rhythmic crack that meant construction or repair, the workaday sound of levels being spent on things that needed doing.

She was listening to the wrong sounds.

What she expected, if pursuit came, was precision. The Hoarded pursued the way they lived — with minimum expenditure, maximum efficiency, the smallest application of force that could solve the problem. She'd been waiting for a Perception sweep: one clean pulse from a trained tracker, reading the landscape at range, locating them without spending more than a level. That was how the Hoarded hunted people. Quiet. Cheap. Effective.

What came instead was the sound of someone ripping a forest apart.

It wasn't literally fire. Vel's ears parsed the noise before her mind caught up — crashing through heavy undergrowth from the north, multiple sources, branch snaps amplified by residual kinetic discharge, the stale buzz an ability left in the air when it had been used carelessly and the energy hadn't finished dissipating. Then a tree falling. Then another tree falling. Then a voice she couldn't make out saying something that ended in a question mark.

"Off the road," Vel said.

Dren was already moving — a single sideways step into the tall grass bordering the track, his body dropping into a crouch with the economy of a man who'd been avoiding problems since before Kem was born. Kem was slower. His pack caught on a fencepost. He pulled, the post shifted, and the fence wire sang a note that traveled twenty meters in both directions.

Too slow. Too loud. It didn't matter.

They came out of the treeline at the edge of the grain fields in a loose cluster that no one would have mistaken for a formation. Five figures in matching gray cloaks — standardized equipment that said institutional procurement, not personal choice. Three of them were wearing the cloaks wrong: one fastened at the waist with the hem pooling around his boots, one with the hood cinched so tight his face was a red circle of sweat, and one who'd cut armholes in the sides and was using it as a vest. The fourth had abandoned her cloak somewhere in the woods, her bare arms still shimmering with the residue of whatever speed ability she'd been burning to keep pace. The fifth had his cloak on correctly and looked miserable about it.

Level 300. All five of them.

Vel felt it in the displacement — the sheer mass of stored potential radiating off them like heat from a forge. Five Ledgers at maximum capacity, five bodies that had never burned a significant level in their lives, five repositories of power so dense it distorted the Ledger's background hum the way a heavy object warps the surface it sits on. She'd felt level-300 presence before — every Hoarded aristocrat carried that weightiness, the fullness of unused capacity. But five at once, in an open field, moving toward them with the coordinated precision of a herd of startled cattle, was something else entirely.

Sable Orm's retrieval team.

"That's — I think that's — yes." The one with the tight hood pointed at Kem. "That's the Aldraste heir. I'm relatively certain."

He was pointing at the only tall, pale, obviously-Hoarded person standing in a grain field south of the Spent Towns.

Vel calculated. Five at 300, untrained. Herself at 28, four decades of fieldwork. Kem at approximately 225, improving but still wasteful. Dren at 138, a trader, not a combatant, crouched in the grass with an expression that said he'd known this job would go wrong and he'd taken it anyway. The settlement's grain depot was visible ahead — silo, a few structures, people moving between them. Civilians in range.

The math said run. The geography said: flat open ground, grain fields in every direction, the road straight and exposed, the treeline behind the agents and the settlement ahead. No cover worth the name.

"Kem," Vel said. "Behind me. Don't do anything until I tell you."

"I can fight—"

"Behind me."

***

The hooded agent went first.

His hands moved with the careful precision of someone who had studied a technique in a manual and was now attempting it in the field — fingers curved, wrists rotated inward, the Binding ability's somatic components executed with textbook accuracy and zero practical instinct. At level 300, a Binding could have pinned all three of them to the ground like insects on a board. A trained Binder would have spread the effect across multiple targets, calibrated the force, maintained the hold while colleagues closed in.

This agent missed all three of them and hit a boulder.

The Binding was designed for flesh. Applied to stone, it didn't pin — it fused. The agent's palm sealed flat against the rock surface with a sound like wet clay hitting a wall. His body wrenched sideways, his arm going taut, his feet leaving the ground for a moment before gravity and the Binding's hold found an equilibrium that left him half-hanging off the boulder with his face pressed against the stone.

"That's not—" He pulled. The Binding held. He pulled harder. The boulder didn't care. "My hand is — I can't—"

The bare-armed woman went second. A Perception Sweep — Vel recognized the posture, the widening of the eyes, the stillness that preceded the ability's activation. At level 300, a Perception Sweep deployed by a trained scout could read every heartbeat on a battlefield, map structural weaknesses in architecture, track a target through walls. It was one of the most useful abilities in the Ledger.

At level 300, deployed by someone who had never used it before, it read everything. Every insect in the grain field. Every mole in the earth beneath it. Every stress fracture in the silo's mortar. Every grain of pollen suspended in the air. The structural composition of every building in the settlement. The respiration rate of every person within two hundred meters. All of it, simultaneously, at maximum resolution, with no filter and no training in how to shut it off.

The woman made a sound — not a scream, something smaller and more damaged, a sound a mind produced when it received a quantity of information it had no framework for processing — and sat down hard in the dirt. Both hands went to her temples. Her eyes were open and seeing nothing useful.

"Too much," she said. "The — there are seventeen — the silo's third course has a — I can hear the worms — too much too much—"

Two down. Vel felt something that wasn't amusement — closer to the grim recognition that the Hoarded's most expensive field deployment was performing exactly as anyone with field experience would have predicted. She'd started to move, to use the distraction, to get Kem and Dren off the road while three remaining agents processed their colleagues' failures.

The vest agent threw a Combat Pulse.

He threw it at Kem. His aim was that of a person who'd been told where to point but had no practice pointing — the Pulse left his hands headed in approximately the right direction and arrived approximately forty meters to the left, where the settlement's grain silo stood.

The silo came apart.

It wasn't an explosion — Combat Pulses didn't burn. Concussive force hit the structure like a fist hitting a sandcastle. The upper third of the cylinder separated from the lower two-thirds in a spray of mortared stone, the blocks scattering outward in an arc that showered the road and the nearest field with debris. Grain — thousands of kilos of stored barley — erupted from the ruptured cylinder in a cloud that turned the air yellow-brown and smelled like a bakery caught in a windstorm. Stone fragments flew.

A piece hit Dren.

He'd been crouched in the grass six meters from the silo, exactly where a person would be if he'd stepped off a road next to a grain depot because five lunatics had just come out of a treeline. He wasn't in the fight. He wasn't near the fight. He was a trader crouching in grass, and a chunk of mortared stone trailing grain dust caught his left arm between the elbow and the wrist and opened it.

The sound was a wet smack, followed by Dren saying "Shit" in a tone that was more irritation than pain. Then the pain arrived and his voice changed. He looked at his arm. The gash ran from the inside of his elbow halfway to his wrist, deep enough that the edges gaped, deep enough that the meat underneath was visible, pink and then rapidly red as the blood found the opening. Stone dust and grain chaff were already in the wound, ground into the raw tissue by the impact.

"Shit," he said again, quieter, pressing his right hand over the gash. Blood pushed through his fingers immediately.

Vel marked it — Dren's wound, Dren's arm, a civilian hit by debris from an ability nobody had aimed at anything, because this was what happened when level-300 power was wielded by people who'd never hit a target in their lives. It wasn't a combat injury or a tactical outcome. A piece of building had fallen on him.

She couldn't help him now.

The fourth agent — the one who'd been watching his colleagues fail — decided to test his Strength augmentation. He was standing at the back of the group, and he didn't aim it at anyone. He raised his fist and swung at the air, testing the ability the way you'd test a tool before using it.

The air moved — not wind, but displacement. The Strength-augmented fist pushed a wall of compressed air outward at a speed Vel's body registered before her mind caught up — a pressure wave that bent the standing grain flat in a ring around the agent, that lifted the dust and grain particles from the silo explosion and drove them horizontal, that hit the two nearest trees and snapped their trunks at the midpoint. The wood broke with a sound like bone — two cracks, fast, the trees folding and going down in a tangle of branches and torn leaves.

Fifteen meters away, Vel felt the pressure wave in her sternum. It wasn't a hit — it was a preview, a casual demonstration of what that fist would do if it connected with something softer than air.

The comedy was over.

The fifth agent — the one who'd gotten his cloak right — had been standing still, processing. Watching the Binding fail, the Perception overwhelm, the Combat Pulse miss, the Strength concuss the air. He was the one who learned. His hands came up with a deliberate motion, fingers spread, and Vel recognized the Force Push a half-second before it left his hands.

She was already turning. The Force Push at level 300 was a directed compression wave — not the unfocused blast of the Strength test, but a channel of force aimed at a target. She turned sideways to narrow her profile, and the edge of the Push caught her across the ribs on the left side instead of square in the chest. The difference between those two outcomes was the difference between cracked ribs and a stopped heart.

She heard two cracks before she felt them — the same sound the trees had made but closer, inside her, bone giving way under lateral compression. It wasn't a clean snap but a grinding flex, ribs bending past their tolerance, costal cartilage separating from the sternum on the left side where the force concentrated. Her left side went hot. Then it went wrong — a rolling wrongness, the bones rearranging into a configuration that didn't support breathing, which was a problem because she was currently trying to breathe.

She hit the ground on her right shoulder. Drew one breath — shallow, because anything deeper pulled the separated ribs apart and the sensation of bone ends moving against each other whited the edges of her vision — and got up.

Getting up cost more than any ability she'd used in a month. Her left arm went to her side involuntarily, pressing against the fracture site, and the pressure helped and also didn't help because the ribs were wrong under her hand, the contour of her chest altered in a way her fingers could map — two ribs, maybe a third. The cartilage felt torn rather than broken — a softer failure, which meant it would hurt more, heal slower, and she wasn't going to spend on it.

Twenty-eight. She'd walked onto this road at twenty-eight.

She burned a Combat ability. A kinetic strike — focused, narrow, the precise application of force she'd been practicing for forty years — caught the cloak agent in the hip and turned him sideways. At 300, his body absorbed the damage the way a wall absorbed a thrown ball: the physics registered, the structure didn't care. But his stance broke and his second Push went into the dirt.

Cost: one level. Twenty-seven.

She burned an Evasion charm. A half-second of sharpened reflexes — her body reading the Strength agent's wind-up before her eyes finished tracking his arm. She sidestepped the fist's displacement wave by two feet. The air where she'd been standing compressed hard enough to kick up a wall of dust that pelted the back of her neck with grit.

Cost: one level. Twenty-six.

Kem was behind her. She could feel his Ledger displacement shifting — the signature of someone gathering an ability, pulling it from reserves. "Kem. Perimeter. Now."

The perimeter went up. She felt it snap into place — a dome of shimmering force, blue-white at the edges, covering a radius of at least thirty meters. It crackled where the grain dust met the barrier. It was enormous. It was triple what they needed. Kem had pulled it from the same place he pulled everything: the deep well of a level-300 Ledger that was learning to spend but hadn't learned to measure.

The cloak agent's third Push hit the perimeter and dispersed. The barrier hummed, held, shed the force in a ripple that flattened barley in a circle around them.

Vel burned a second Combat ability — aimed at the ground in front of the Strength agent, kicking up a spray of dirt and stone that caught him in the eyes. He staggered back, one hand going to his face, the augmented fist swinging blind. The swing connected with air and the concussion tore a furrow in the field twenty meters long.

Cost: one level. Twenty-five.

She burned a third Combat ability at the cloak agent's knee. Not to injure — at 300, she couldn't injure him with a single strike from 25. But the knee buckled, his weight shifted, and the Push he was building collapsed in his hands, the energy discharging harmlessly into the barley.

Cost: one level. Twenty-four.

She burned a second Evasion as the hooded agent — who had finally freed himself from the boulder, his palm raw and bleeding where the Binding had torn away from skin — threw a second Binding at her. The charm pulled her sideways and the Binding passed through empty air and hit a fencepost, which it consumed.

Cost: one level. Twenty-three.

She burned her last Combat ability and the rotation required to cast it nearly dropped her. The somatic component demanded a twist of the torso that her ribs vetoed — pain flared through her left side, bright and total, her breath catching on the fracture. She forced the rotation anyway. The strike caught the Strength agent in the shoulder, and something in the joint gave under the focused force. His arm went limp. He dropped to one knee, and his face registered the discovery that pain was real and currently interested in him personally.

Cost: one level. Twenty-two.

Twenty-two.

Six levels. She'd burned six levels in under three minutes against five people who couldn't aim, couldn't coordinate, couldn't hold a formation, couldn't do a single thing that a week of field training would have made basic. She'd burned her budget for two weeks because raw power didn't need skill to break things. Raw power just needed to exist in sufficient quantity, and five level-300 agents had quantity enough to rearrange the landscape.

Her ribs were grinding with every breath. She could feel the bone ends moving — a subtle, nauseating shift each time her chest expanded, the cartilage separation making the left side of her ribcage a structure that moved in ways ribcages shouldn't. Breathing was a negotiation between oxygen and structural integrity, and structural integrity was losing.

***

"We move," Vel said. Her voice came out quiet and flat, which was what happened when speaking at normal volume required the ribcage expansion she couldn't currently afford.

Dren was on his feet. His left arm was pressed against his body, his right hand clamped over the wound, and the blood had soaked through his shirt and reached his fingers. His face was the color of old paper. But he was standing, his eyes were clear, and he was looking south with the focus of a man evaluating an exit.

"Tree cover," Dren said, jerking his chin toward the road ahead where the track entered a stretch of mixed woodland. "Half a kilometer. Maybe less."

"Can you run?"

He looked at his arm. Looked at Vel — at the way she held her left side, the shallow breathing, the posture of someone managing damage she wasn't mentioning. "Can you?"

Vel couldn't run. She could walk fast, in a controlled manner that kept her torso rigid and her arms close, movement that minimized every rotation her ribs would punish. It wasn't running — it was the ambulatory equivalent of the shallow breathing she'd adopted: the minimum required for forward progress.

"Kem. Drop the north side. Reinforce south. We're moving."

The perimeter shifted. The northern face dissolved and the energy redistributed to the south, creating a wall of force between them and the agents. Kem was walking backward, his hands out, his face showing the concentration of someone maintaining an ability while in motion — a skill he hadn't had two weeks ago.

They moved. Dren first — his stride shortened, his balance off, but his legs worked and his sense of direction was a thing no arm wound could take. He found the fastest line to the tree cover without being told, the trader's instinct for a clean route asserting itself even with blood running down his hand and dripping off his fingertips into the road dust. Vel followed, each step a negotiation with her ribs. Kem last, walking backward, the barrier rippling as a Binding and a half-formed Combat Pulse hit it and dissipated.

Behind them, the agents were not pursuing. The Perception woman was still on the ground, hands over her ears, her eyes squeezed shut. The boulder agent was examining his torn palm. The vest agent had run out of Combat Pulses — or, more accurately, had realized that his second Pulse had destroyed a fence instead of a person and was staring at the wreckage in what appeared to be vocational crisis. The Strength agent was kneeling in the dirt, cradling his shoulder. The cloak agent was standing still, watching them retreat, his hands at his sides.

He was the only one Vel kept her eyes on.

They reached the tree cover. The road entered the wood and the canopy closed overhead, filtering the light into green shadow. Vel put her back against an oak trunk and let herself breathe — still shallow, still managed, but the adrenaline was dropping and the ribs were presenting their full invoice. The left side of her chest was a topology of wrong. She probed with her fingers and found the two fracture sites — one at the fifth rib, one at the sixth, both in the anterolateral line where the Push had compressed the bone. The cartilage separation was higher, near the sternum, and it shifted when she pressed, a soft grinding that made her hand flinch away before she decided to stop.

She could heal this. A Restoration ability, targeted and minimal, would knit the bone and reattach the cartilage in minutes. Cost: two levels. She'd be at twenty. Twenty, with the Margins ahead, with unknown terrain and unknown threats and the Scour beyond that, and every level between here and zero already spoken for in a budget she'd been building since before Kem Aldraste had ever heard of her.

She pressed her palm flat against the fracture site and breathed through her nose.

Twenty-two. The margin was gone. Not the levels — the buffer. The space between what she'd planned to spend and what she'd actually spent, the cushion that was supposed to absorb exactly this kind of problem. Six levels, burned in a single engagement, against people fumbling their own abilities. She'd accounted for pursuit. She'd accounted for combat. She hadn't accounted for the blunt arithmetic of five level-300 Ledgers wielded by people who couldn't hit what they aimed at but could destroy what they didn't.

"Kem."

He was bent over, hands on his thighs, breathing hard. The perimeter had cost him — not physically, he had the reserves, but maintaining an ability while walking backward across open ground was draining in the way new skills drained a person: concentration, focus, the effort of doing something the body hadn't automated yet.

"How much?" she said.

He checked. The inward focus, the slight defocusing of the eyes. "One-eighty-three."

Forty-two levels. On a perimeter that had been three times the necessary size, maintained for four times the necessary duration, against agents who'd been too disorganized to mount a real pursuit. The perimeter could have been done for fifteen levels by a trained spender. Twenty at the outside. Twenty-two levels of pure waste.

She didn't say this. The perimeter had worked. The Pushes and Bindings had hit the barrier instead of them. They were alive because Kem had thrown up a wall of force that was excessive and expensive and exactly what they'd needed.

"Okay," she said.

Dren sat down with his back against a birch. He moved his right hand from the wound long enough to look at it, and the gash started bleeding freely again — a thick seep that ran down his forearm and dripped from his wrist into the dead leaves. The wound was ragged, the edges dark with stone dust and grain chaff ground into raw tissue by the impact.

"Hold that shut," Vel said. She dug into her torn pack — the Force Push had ripped it open, scattering half her supplies across a grain field she'd never see again — and found a strip of clean cloth. "Press this over it. Hard. We'll clean it when we stop."

Dren took the cloth. Pressed it against the gash. His jaw was set and his breathing was controlled in the way of a man who handled pain by denying it his full attention. He wrapped the cloth around his forearm and pulled it tight with his teeth and his good hand. A knot, practiced, the kind of self-bandaging a trader learned after enough years of loading cargo alone.

"That'll hold for now," he said. He flexed his fingers experimentally. Winced. Stopped flexing. "Some fucking professionals."

It wasn't anger. It was the flat verdict of a man who evaluated goods and services for a living. He'd been hired to guide them through territory he knew, and the service he'd received in return was a chunk of silo in his arm from an ability that hadn't been aimed at him, or at the building, or at anything specific at all. His daughter was waiting for him back in the Spent Towns, and he'd just lost blood and functionality to collateral damage from a fight between people whose combined Ledger readings could have funded a small nation.

Vel leaned her head against the oak bark. The rough surface pressed into her skull, and the pressure was the only comfortable thing about her body. Grain dust had settled into her hair, her clothing, the torn seams of her pack. The stale, floury smell of the destroyed silo clung to everything. In two hours, when the dust dried, it would be a fine grit in every fold and crease. She'd be picking barley out of her ears for days.

Twenty-two levels and cracked ribs and a wounded guide and a partner forty-two levels lighter than he'd been half an hour ago. The budget for two weeks of southern travel, spent in three minutes of chaos.

She opened her eyes and looked back through the trees.

The agents were visible at the edge of the grain field — scattered, useless, the Perception woman still down, the boulder agent sitting next to his boulder, the vest agent having what appeared to be a quiet crisis near the ruined silo. The settlement's people had come out and were standing at a careful distance, watching the agents with the patience of civilians who had seen powerful idiots before and knew the safest response was distance and time.

There was a sixth figure.

Vel had counted five during the fight. Five gray cloaks, five sets of 300, five flailing displays of power that had produced one destroyed silo, two broken ribs, one civilian wound, and zero successful restraints. But there, at the treeline where the agents had first emerged, stood a sixth person — not in gray, standing apart from the group, closer to the trees than the field, positioned as if she'd chosen the spot deliberately — observation range, not engagement range. Dark hair, a formal Hoarded arrangement coming loose. The posture of someone who'd spent decades sitting correctly in official chambers and was only now learning what it felt like to stand in open air for hours.

She was holding a small notebook. She was writing in it.

She had watched the entire fight. Vel's combat — the strikes, the evasion timing, the way Vel moved to minimize exposure. Kem's perimeter — the radius, the energy output, the direction of retreat. Dren going down and Vel not stopping. Five agents failing in five different ways. All of it recorded in a notebook she'd brought from the Vault because she was the kind of person who brought notebooks to field operations.

As Vel watched, the figure finished writing and closed the notebook. She looked south — not at the agents, not at the silo wreckage, south, down the road, into the trees where Vel and Kem and Dren had disappeared.

Then she turned and walked back to the agents — she wasn't hurrying or running, just walking with the unhurried gait of someone on her own schedule, crossing the trampled barley toward the five people she'd been assigned to lead and had apparently decided to observe instead.

Five agents who couldn't fight. That was a storm. Storms passed. Storms burned their own energy and stumbled home.

One person who watched and learned and wrote it down. That was someone building a curriculum from wreckage, cataloging what had worked and what hadn't, studying Vel's patterns and Kem's output and the geometry of their escape. That was someone who would be better next time because she had the resource no one in the Hoarded system was supposed to possess: the willingness to spend her own ignorance.

Vel turned away.

"We need to move," she said. She pushed off the tree. The motion sent a flare through her left side — the ribs shifting, the cartilage grinding, the fracture reasserting itself with the insistence of an injury that had been briefly forgotten and resented the oversight. She breathed through it. Shallow. Controlled. Her left arm found its place against her ribs, holding them in, holding herself together through pressure she applied because the pressure she refused to spend two levels on would have done the job better.

Kem straightened up. He was watching her face, and his eyes had the look of someone who'd recently learned to read pain in other people — not innate, a developing skill. He didn't ask if she was okay. He'd learned that Vel didn't answer that question.

Dren was already on his feet, his left arm held close, the cloth darkening. He picked a direction through the trees and started walking. "There's a creek crossing about three kilometers in. We can clean this up there."

Vel adjusted her pack on her right shoulder. The left side was territory she'd be navigating around rather than through for weeks — the ribs had made that decision for her, and the two levels she wouldn't spend on healing them were a tax she'd be paying in pain for as long as the bone needed to knit on its own. Every step was a small negotiation. Every breath was a line item.

Twenty-two. She was heading south at twenty-two, with fractures she wouldn't heal and a budget she couldn't rebuild and a guide bleeding through improvised bandaging and a partner who'd just burned forty-two levels she'd been counting on. The five agents behind them were a problem that would solve itself — they'd regroup, they'd complain, they'd limp back to the Vault with a report that field operations were harder than the briefing suggested.

But the sixth one. The one with the notebook. The one who'd stood at the treeline and watched five people fail and hadn't failed herself because she hadn't tried anything yet. The one who was already better than she'd been an hour ago, simply from standing still and paying attention.

That one wasn't going home.

Vel walked south. She counted her steps because counting was what she did, and each step cost exactly what it cost, and the ribs kept their own tally alongside hers.
