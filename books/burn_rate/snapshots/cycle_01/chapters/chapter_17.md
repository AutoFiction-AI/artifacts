# Chapter 17

The wind hit first. Not a gust but a condition — a constant, gritty pressure that came off the Scour like breath from a furnace, carrying the taste of powdered rock and the flat mineral smell of geology laid bare. Kem shielded his eyes with one hand and looked south, and the world dropped out from under itself.

The Scour was a wound. Not a valley, not a canyon — a wound, as if something had taken a bite from the earth's surface and exposed everything beneath it. The erosion basin stretched maybe two kilometers across, carved down through layered rock that showed its history in stripes: ochre and pale gray and a deep, oxidized red that looked like old blood in the mid-summer sun. The walls of the basin dropped away in terraces and shelves, carved by wind into shapes that suggested time more than violence — overhangs, columns, narrow fins of harder rock left standing when the softer material around them eroded away over centuries. No vegetation. No shade. No cover. The sun struck the exposed rock and reflected, so the heat came from everywhere at once, and the basin floor shimmered with it.

Vel stood next to him at the ridgeline's edge, one hand resting on her pack where the route journal sat. Her face did something Kem had learned to read over six weeks — not emotion, exactly, but the registration of a cost she hadn't budgeted for. She was looking at the basin floor the way she looked at everything: measuring.

"There it is," she said.

Somewhere down there, eight years ago, Maren had reached zero. The threshold had opened, shown a flash of somewhere else, and closed. Vel had watched. Vel had been watching ever since, from 62 levels, from 31, from 20, from 14, burning herself down across eight years to follow a woman through a door that might not open twice.

Fourteen levels. That's what Vel had left. Kem had eighteen. Thirty-two between them. He'd spent more than that on a single defensive perimeter during the agent fight south of the Spent Towns. A Hoarded lord would spend more than that on a formal dinner if the Hoarded ever ate formally, which they didn't, because eating formally might require effort and effort was the enemy.

"I count four," Vel said. She was looking through a gap in the ridgeline rock toward the basin's navigable descent — a switchback path cut into the wall, the only conventional way down. Two figures stood at the top of the path. Below, on the basin floor near a cluster of small stone structures, two more.

Not the untrained level-300 agents who'd accidentally detonated a grain silo and given themselves migraines. These were Red Reserve. The people who knew what abilities were for and had been permitted to use them.

"Two at the descent," Vel said. "Two on the floor. The descent pair are fresh — they're standing wrong. Too stiff, too visible. They've been posted recently. The floor pair are permanent. Look at the camp."

Kem looked. The structures near the basin floor weren't field shelters. They were buildings — low, stone, with canvas-and-leather roofing, built for years of use. A fire pit with a carbon-black ring that had been burning for a long time. A gear rack. Clothing on a line, bleached by sun and wind. Someone had been living here. The wind-polish on the stone path between the structures showed thousands of footsteps ground into the same route.

"Sable said permanent garrison," Kem said. "I was hoping she was being dramatic."

"Sable doesn't do dramatic."

"No. She does bureaucratic, which is worse."

Vel almost produced a sound that in another person would have been a laugh. "I need to map their positions properly. That's two levels."

Two levels. Out of fourteen. A seventh of everything she had left, spent on looking. But they couldn't plan a route past four trained combatants by squinting from a ridgeline.

"Do it."

Vel closed her eyes and activated a Perception ability. The air around her shifted — a subtle displacement, like the pressure change before a storm, as the Ledger drew from her. When she opened her eyes, they tracked with an inhuman precision, sweeping the basin in systematic arcs.

"The commander is on the floor, near the threshold point. Woman. Compact build. She's been patrolling that same area for years — I can see the wear paths in the rock. Loops back and forth, same route, different times of day. Five years of that." Vel's enhanced eyes moved. "The other permanent agent is running a circuit between the camp and the east side of the basin, covering the tunnel exit. The descent pair are young. One's fidgeting with her gear."

She blinked. The sharpness left her eyes and she sat down heavily on the ridgeline rock. "Twelve."

Kem let the number settle. Twelve and eighteen. Thirty.

"I should mention," he said, "that at my current level, I could perform the Conservatory Rite without anyone noticing. I'm finally discreet enough for a Hoarded ceremony. Took losing two hundred and eighty-two levels, but the form is improving."

Vel's mouth moved. The structural foundation of a smile without the architecture.

A voice rose from the basin floor — not shouted but carried. Projected by ability or by acoustics, or by five years of learning exactly how sound moved in this wound in the earth.

"I can see you on the ridgeline. I've been watching you for thirty minutes."

Vel went still. Kem's hand moved toward nothing — a reflex from when reaching for something meant he had options. He had twelve options left. Thirteen if he counted not using any of them.

"I know what you're here for," the voice called. "I know what you're trying to do. I've seen three people try it. I'd like to talk before we do this the other way."

"She's alone down there," Vel said. The Perception was fading but traces remained. "The circuit agent is at the eastern wall. Descent pair haven't moved."

Kem stood. "I'll go."

"If she—"

"Then you'll have twelve levels and no cascade partner and this will have been a very long walk for nothing." He said it flatly. Six weeks of approaching zero had burned the drama out of his delivery. "But she announced herself. She asked to talk. She's following procedure."

"I hate procedure."

"No you don't. You hate that other people's procedures get in the way of your procedures."

He took the switchback descent. The path was carved into the basin wall in tight turns, the stone underfoot worn smooth — not from erosion but from boots. Thousands of days of patrols. His own boots were held together by friction and optimism, the soles worn so thin he could feel the heat of each step.

The commander met him where the descent path leveled onto the basin floor. She was shorter than the voice had suggested — compact, weathered, somewhere in her forties. Brown skin darkened further by five years of Scour sun. Calloused hands. She wore field clothing that had been patched so many times the patches had patches, and her hair was cut short in a style that had nothing to do with fashion and everything to do with wind. She stood the way Vel stood — feet settled, weight centered, the posture of someone who'd spent enough levels to know what her body could do and couldn't.

And at the edge of her camp, near the gear rack, Kem noticed a row of stones set on a flat shelf of rock. Seven of them, each a different type and color from the basin's geological layers. They were arranged with a care that had nothing to do with function — spaced evenly, selected for something. Shape, maybe. Pattern. The collection of a person who'd been alone with rocks for five years and had started paying attention to them.

"Commander Osen," she said. "Red Reserve. Scour garrison."

"Kem Aldraste."

She looked at him the way a mechanic looks at an engine — assessing condition, not character. "I know. Your retrieval order's been sitting in my dispatch pouch for six weeks." She nodded toward the descent path. "Those two up there are for you. The Council sent them three weeks ago. They're competent enough."

"Just competent enough?"

"Nobody sends their best people to sit in a hole in the ground. The best people are back at the Vault, not spending anything." She said it without bitterness. Just fact. "I've been here five years. I volunteered for the posting and I re-upped twice. People don't do that for the career advancement."

"Why do they do it?"

She looked at the stretch of featureless basin floor behind her. The threshold point — or what Kem assumed was the threshold point, because it looked exactly like every other square meter of rock.

"Because I wanted to know if it was real." She turned back. "It's real. I've watched three people hit zero and go somewhere else. Right there. One breath they're standing, the next breath they're not. Not dead. Just not here." She paused. "I have orders to prevent that from happening to anyone else. So I stand here, and I make sure nobody walks through a door that the people who sign my orders would rather pretend doesn't exist."

She was Sable. The recognition hit Kem with the specificity of a face seen twice in different light. Commander Osen was what Sable Orm would have become with different orders and five more years — competent, spent, loyal to a system she understood was built on a lie, guarding something she knew was real because the alternative was everyone knowing, and everyone knowing would collapse the architecture of inaction the Hoarded had been building for centuries. Sable had been deciding. Osen had decided.

"You came here to talk," Kem said. "Not about my retrieval."

"Your retrieval's a footnote. If you want to go home, the two agents at the descent will escort you north with minimal incident. That's the easy version." She crossed her arms. "But you're not here for that, and the woman on the ridgeline isn't either."

"No."

"Then I need to tell you something, because I follow protocol and because I'd rather you understood the situation before we have to do this the hard way." She uncrossed her arms. "The threshold has degraded."

The wind blew dust across the space between them. Kem felt the ground shift — not literally, but in the way the ground shifts when the map you've been following turns out to describe a different territory.

"Three years ago, the Council sent a specialist. I don't know what she did exactly — spent a lot of levels over two weeks doing something to the cascade site. Dampening, they called it. Weakening the energy signature. Afterward, the readings changed." Osen spoke the way Vel gave field assessments — compressed, specific, no decoration. "Natural erosion was already happening. The geological alignment the mechanism depends on has been shifting — rock layers, water tables, something about the substrate. The dampening accelerated the decay."

"How bad?"

"Bad enough." She looked at him directly. "Eight years ago, two spenders going to zero in proximity would have generated a reliable cascade. Stable threshold, sustained opening, clean crossing. That's what the woman with the journal experienced — I watched it happen. Clean. Now? My readings say a two-person cascade has maybe a thirty percent chance of producing a stable opening. Maybe less. You'll burn everything you have and the door might flicker for two seconds and close, or it might not open at all."

Kem's mouth was dry. Behind him — above him, on the ridgeline — Vel was listening. He was certain of that. Her Perception traces would be fading, but the basin's acoustics carried, and Vel had forty years of listening for the information that would kill her plans.

Eight years of calculations. The cascade mechanism, the proximity requirements, the descent rate, the co-spending ratio — all of it calibrated to a threshold that no longer existed as she'd understood it. Three words to undo nearly a decade: it's not enough.

"What would sustain it?" Kem asked.

"More energy. More contributors. The specialist's report — which I wasn't supposed to read but I've been here five years and reading is what I do between patrols — said a three-person cascade would likely compensate. Three people descending toward zero in proximity, generating overlapping energy." She paused. "The Council didn't share that assessment with me officially. They don't want me thinking about how to open the door. They want me thinking about how to keep it shut."

"But you thought about it."

"I've had five years." She looked at the row of stones on her gear shelf. "You think about everything, out here. Every possible version of the thing you're supposed to prevent."

Kem turned and climbed back up the switchback. The heat rose with him, the basin wall radiating stored sun. By the time he reached the ridgeline, sweat had soaked through his shirt and the dust had turned to paste on his neck.

Vel was where he'd left her, sitting on the hot rock, the route journal open on her knees. She'd heard everything. Her face was the face of someone who'd just been told the house they'd been building for eight years had no foundation.

"Three," she said.

"Yeah."

"We don't have three."

"No."

Vel looked at the journal. Maren's handwriting on the open page — annotations about the basin's wind patterns, the geological layering, the location of the cascade site marked with a small x and the word *here* in handwriting that was not Vel's own. Written eight years ago, when the threshold was intact and two people would have been enough.

"I spent eight years on math that doesn't work anymore." Her voice was flat. Not compressed — empty. The flatness of a vessel that had just been poured out.

Kem sat down beside her. The rock burned through his trousers.

"So we improvise."

Vel looked at him.

"You've been improvising for forty years. I've been improvising for six weeks. Between us that's forty years and six weeks of making it up as we go, which is still about forty years more experience than every Hoarded lord in the Vault combined."

"Improvisation isn't going to generate a third cascade contributor."

"No. But it might get us to the threshold point alive, and a thirty percent chance of the door opening is thirty percent more than we had yesterday."

Vel closed the journal. Her hand lingered on the cover — the specific care she showed nothing else, the tenderness she reserved for a dead woman's handwriting.

"The tunnel," she said. "If we reach the basin floor before they reposition, we'll have eight hundred meters of open ground to the threshold. If we can start the cascade—"

"With two."

"With two. It might work. It might flicker and close. It might hold long enough for one of us and not the other." She stopped. "But it's what we have."

"And if it needs three?"

"Then we're two people with twenty levels standing in front of four trained fighters who have a thousand, and I'll think of something very clever in the time it takes them to end us."

"That's a terrible plan."

"It's the plan we have." Vel looked at the position of the sun. "We go in two hours. The light will be behind us coming out of the tunnel exit — in their eyes, not ours. Buys us thirty seconds of confusion, maybe."

"Thirty seconds and twenty levels."

"I've worked with less."

"When?"

"I'll tell you on the other side."

---

They moved laterally along the ridgeline as the shadows stretched, staying below the lip where rock met sky. The wind was constant, pushing grit into every fold and seam, and Kem's eyes watered from it until the tears cut clean tracks through the dust on his face. The Scour's rim was a different world from the basin floor — up here, the rock was sharper, less wind-polished, with lichens and dried grasses clinging to cracks. Thirty meters below that rim, nothing lived.

The tunnel entrance was a horizontal scar in the rock face: two meters high, three wide, the edges showing the clean bite of Shaping abilities. Miners had cut into the basin wall decades ago, extracting ore or mineral deposits that the Hoarded had found profitable enough to allow spending on. The Hoarded hadn't done the cutting — they'd contracted Margins laborers, people already depleted enough that spending a few more levels on mining made no difference to the system's arithmetic. The tunnel was the kind of place the Hoarded produced without entering: someone else's sweat shaped into someone else's profit.

The ceiling sagged where reinforcement abilities had long since faded. Rubble narrowed the passage from three meters to barely one. Cool air moved out of the darkness, carrying the smell of old stone and water that had been finding its way through cracks for years.

Vel knelt at the entrance, journal open. "Maren mapped the interior. Main shaft runs about two hundred meters in, then angles down and east. Three partial collapses, one full blockage. That was eight years ago."

"So it's probably worse."

"Probably."

"Great."

They entered the tunnel. The darkness was immediate and total — not the darkness of night, which had gradients and stars, but the underground dark that had never seen light and took the concept as a personal affront. Kem activated a Light ability. Two levels. The glow bloomed in his palm, warm and steady, and illuminated rough-cut walls, a floor of broken stone, and a ceiling that looked like it had been asked to hold itself up and had agreed under protest.

Sixteen.

The tunnel smelled like mineral dust and age. The walls were cool against Kem's shoulder when he turned sideways to fit through a narrow section. Behind him, Vel moved the way she always moved in tight spaces — low, efficient, each shift of weight deliberate. She'd been crawling through bad places for four decades and her body knew them the way a river knows its bed.

The first collapse was a slump — the ceiling had sagged and shed a layer of fractured rock, narrowing the passage to a low crawl. Kem went through on his stomach, the Light held in front of him, stone pressing his back. The pressure was steady and indifferent. Rock didn't care whether you got through or didn't. Vel followed, moving faster, her smaller frame fitting where his shoulders caught. She didn't comment on the tight fit. She didn't comment on anything. Underground, she became even more compressed — as if the weight of rock above them matched the weight she always carried.

At the second collapse, the tunnel was blocked by a slab of layered rock resting at an angle, leaving only a triangular gap at the bottom. The gap was body-width, maybe. If the slab shifted while they were under it, the gap would close.

Kem studied it. The slab was fractured along its bedding planes — thin layers of different hardness, the geological record of whatever had deposited this rock a hundred million years ago. The lower layers showed water staining. The upper layers were dry, sun-bleached where they'd once been exposed before the ceiling above collapsed.

"That moves, we're under it," Kem said.

"I see that."

"Reinforcement?"

"Your call."

It was his call because it was his levels. Three of them. A structural Reinforcement would lock the slab in place long enough for them to pass — freeze its fracture planes, hold its weight, prevent the small shifts that would turn a gap into a grave. Three levels. The same cost as a Flame Charm for cooking, a Light for reading, a Strength boost for carrying a heavy pack. Things he'd spent without thinking six weeks ago.

He placed both hands on the slab. The stone was cool and faintly damp. He activated the Reinforcement and felt three levels leave — not a smooth withdrawal but three specific subtractions, each one a point of absence that registered in the Ledger's constant hum. The hum was quieter now. It had been getting quieter for weeks, the background presence of his remaining levels fading as the numbers dropped. At three hundred, the Ledger had been a wall of sound — every ability available, every potential action humming at once. At thirteen, it was a whisper. A few voices left in a room that used to be full.

Thirteen. He held the slab while Vel crawled through, her body fitting neatly into the gap, her movements unhurried. She trusted the Reinforcement. She trusted his ability to hold it. Six weeks of distrust and fracture and repair, and she crawled under a slab of rock held in place by his remaining levels without hesitation.

Kem followed, shoulders scraping stone, the Light bobbing ahead of him. He released the Reinforcement as he cleared the gap, and the slab groaned and settled, and behind him the gap narrowed by half. No going back that way.

The tunnel angled downward. The rock changed — darker, wetter, the mineral smell sharpening. Water had been working through these walls for years, widening joints and softening surfaces. A section of floor had subsided, opening a gap that dropped into blackness below. Kem held his Light over the edge. Four meters down, rubble. Not useful. Not immediately lethal. But the floor around the gap was laced with hairline cracks, radiating outward like a frozen impact.

He tested the floor with his weight. It held. He took three careful steps, feeling each surface before committing. The fourth step found a crack that widened under his foot — a slow, grinding give, the rock preparing to let go. He shifted back, found solid ground, and pressed himself against the wall where the stone was load-bearing.

Behind him, Vel activated a Navigation ability. The cost was two levels. The effect was nearly invisible — no light, no sound, no obvious alteration. But her movement changed. She crossed the unstable section in four steps, each foot landing precisely on solid ground, her body angling through gaps between bad stone as if the path had always been obvious and she was simply walking it. She moved the way water finds the cracks in a system — inevitably, without effort, following the route that was already there.

Ten.

The next section was stable but narrow — body-width, the walls pressing close enough that Kem could feel both at once, his chest on one side and his shoulder blades on the other. The claustrophobia was rational, which made it worse. There was no room to panic. Panicking would wedge him tighter. So he breathed through his mouth and moved inch by inch and thought about the fact that his father, at three hundred levels, had never been in a space smaller than a Vault hallway. Lord Aldraste's entire life had been large rooms and wide corridors and the absolute assurance that nothing would press against him from any direction. Level three hundred and not a scratch. Level three hundred and dying from the inside.

The tunnel opened slightly. Vel used an Evasion ability — two more levels — to pass through a final constriction where the rock had shifted and the ceiling met the floor at an angle that shouldn't have been navigable. The ability didn't make her smaller. It made her luckier — the constriction had a viable route, barely, and the ability found it the way her Navigation had found the solid ground.

Eight.

Vel Ashwick, forty years of spending, the most precise user of abilities Kem had ever seen, was at eight levels. Eight. A Hoarded serving attendant spent more than that in a year maintaining the Vault's temperature. The ambient ceremony at a Conservatory Rite consumed more than that — formal abilities that kept the hall's acoustics perfect and the air at exactly the right temperature, all spent by people whose levels were not tracked because their levels did not count.

Vel's hands were shaking. Not from fear — from the Ledger's diminishing signal. The hum that had been a background constant for fifty-eight years of life was barely audible at eight levels. She was running out of something more fundamental than abilities. She was running out of the sensation of having them.

The exit was ahead. A change in the air pressure, a taste of wind that carried heat instead of mineral cold, a lightening of the darkness from absolute to merely deep. Kem activated his last defensive ability — a Ward, the lightest one he had, barely enough to deflect a thrown punch. He set it at the tunnel exit, a thin shell of protection that would buy them three seconds if someone was waiting outside.

Twelve. He was at twelve levels. Everything he owned in the world.

Between them, twenty. What a Hoarded lord spent on a tantrum. What a Conservatory Rite burned on candles. What a Vault serving staff spent in a month on the maintenance of people who would never spend anything on anyone.

They came out of the tunnel into late-afternoon sun, and the basin floor hit them like a fist made of light and heat. Red-gray rock, wind-scoured to a polish, hot enough to feel through boot soles. The erosion channels carved shallow grooves across the floor — waist-high ridges of harder stone left standing between worn troughs, enough to crouch behind, not enough to hide behind. The air shimmered.

The threshold point was roughly eight hundred meters to the west. Kem couldn't see it. There was nothing to see — no marker, no altar, no monument. Just rock. The most important point in the geography of the Ledger world was an unmarked stretch of basin floor indistinguishable from every other stretch.

The garrison camp was visible to the south-southwest: stone buildings, the fire pit, the gear rack, the clothesline. The circuiting agent was between them and the camp, maybe three hundred meters away, walking his patrol. He hadn't spotted them yet.

The descent agents were out of sight behind the curve of the basin wall.

Commander Osen was at the threshold point. Standing. Waiting. Her row of stones would be behind her, on the gear shelf near the camp, seven rocks she'd collected from the geological layers of a place she'd guarded for five years. The small, irrelevant thing a person does to remain a person in the service of an institution that doesn't require her to be one.

"Eight hundred meters," Vel said, breathing hard. The tunnel's demands, the spending, the heat — all of it compounding in a fifty-eight-year-old body that had been counting for forty years.

"Open ground."

"She'll see us before we're halfway."

"She sees us now."

The circuiting agent spotted them. A shout — sharp, trained, the practiced alarm of someone who'd drilled for exactly this — bounced off the basin walls.

The commander's voice came back, unhurried: "I see them."

They moved.

Two hundred meters across the basin floor. The rock was uneven, each step a negotiation with exposed strata and erosion channels. Kem's boots slipped on smooth stone. The sun was behind them, as Vel had planned — the late-afternoon light in the eyes of anyone looking east toward the tunnel exit. It bought them nothing against the commander, who was west of them at the threshold point, but it might slow the descent agents when they came.

Three hundred meters. The circuiting agent had altered his patrol, moving to intercept. He was younger than Osen, fresher, the movements of someone who hadn't been ground down by five years of desert and doubt. Maybe level 258. Maybe higher. More levels in his warm-up routine than Kem and Vel had in total.

Four hundred meters. The descent agents appeared at the base of the switchback, moving fast. The fidgeting one had stopped fidgeting. They fanned out west, covering the approach to the threshold point, cutting off the direct route.

Five hundred meters. The basin floor was open and bright and offered nothing. No cover. No strategy. Just two people walking toward a point that might not do anything when they reached it, through a field of trained operatives who could end them without breaking a sweat.

Kem checked his Ledger. Twelve. Still twelve. The number sat where it always sat — at the edge of awareness, a presence so constant that noticing it was like noticing your own breathing. Except at three hundred, the Ledger had been a cathedral of sound, every potential action a note in a chord so full it was almost silence. At twelve, it was a stripped thing. A few notes left, each one distinct and separate, each one a specific ability he could name and feel. He knew exactly what he could still do. The list was short enough to recite.

Vel was at eight. She'd know her list too. She'd been memorizing lists like that for forty years — the shrinking catalogue of what she could still afford, measured against the growing catalogue of what she needed. She'd been doing this math since before Kem was born.

At six hundred meters, Commander Osen called out. Her voice carried across the basin with the flat clarity of a woman who had been projecting her voice across this exact distance for five years.

"You can't make it work." She didn't sound angry. She didn't sound triumphant. She sounded like someone stating a measured result. "It's not enough anymore."

Vel didn't stop.

Kem didn't stop either. He looked at the commander across two hundred meters of open ground — a woman who had spent five years guarding a door she knew was real, who had watched three people walk through it and done nothing, who collected rocks and followed procedure and went to sleep every night in a camp built for permanence at the edge of the world's most important secret.

She wasn't a villain. She was a monument. The most expensive monument the Hoarded had ever built — a trained, competent, spent person positioned at the end of everything to make sure nobody found out that the end of everything was actually a beginning. The Hoarded spent her the way they spent everything — other people's lives, exchanged for the preservation of a lie.

She sounded almost sorry.

---

Three hours behind them, on the ridgeline, Sable Orm sat on a rock that had been absorbing the day's heat and looked down.

The basin opened beneath her like an anatomical diagram of the earth — layered, exposed, each stratum visible in its own color and texture. She could see the garrison camp. She could see the four agents. She could see two small figures moving across the basin floor toward a point that looked like every other point, and she could see the commander standing at that unremarkable spot, the unchanging center of a pattern Sable had spent six weeks learning to read.

She'd been tracking for nine days. The first Perception spend — one level — had confirmed their direction from the Margins south. The second — another level, another subtraction from the account she'd maintained at three hundred for forty-seven years — had given her distance and terrain, the shape of two people's passage through depleted country.

Two-thirty-six. Her Ledger hummed at two-thirty-six. The sound was different from what she remembered at three hundred — not quieter, exactly, but thinner. As if the instrument that produced it had lost a few of its strings. She imagined describing this in a report. *The acoustic quality of the Ledger at reduced levels suggests a spectral narrowing consistent with—*

She stopped. She was writing reports in her head again. Filing observations under categories that had stopped applying somewhere around day four, when she'd realized she wasn't tracking Kem Aldraste anymore. She was following. The difference was administrative and also total.

The wind pushed at her hair, which had long since abandoned any formal Hoarded arrangement and now did whatever the weather demanded. Her field clothing was dirty. Her boots were scuffed. She looked, she suspected, like someone who had been outside for six weeks for the first time in her life, which was exactly what she was.

From the ridgeline, she could see the two figures stop at maybe six hundred meters from the threshold point. The commander's voice wouldn't carry this far — the basin swallowed sound below a certain distance — but Sable could see the body language. Osen standing her ground. The two small figures continuing to walk.

Sable had read the classified files. She'd accessed them through her Senior Auditor credentials during the third week of pursuit, because no one had thought to restrict an auditor's access to documents about a place no auditor was supposed to visit. The threshold assessment. The dampening operation. The specialist's report: *Two-contributor cascade insufficient at current degradation parameters. Three-contributor minimum recommended for stable threshold event.*

Three contributors. Vel and Kem were two. They might not know the threshold had degraded — or maybe they did, now, if the commander had followed protocol and disclosed. Either way, two people with twenty levels between them were walking toward a door that probably required three to open and was guarded by four people with a thousand.

Sable did the math. She was not good at field calculations — her expertise was quarterly audits, compliance reviews, budget reconciliation. But even she could see the gap. The gap was the size of a person. A third contributor.

She had two hundred and thirty-six levels. More than enough to stabilize a degraded cascade, if the specialist's report was accurate. More than enough, and she was sitting on a rock three hours away, watching.

She didn't know what she would do when she got down there. She had been not knowing for nine days. Each day she'd made one more decision that wasn't a decision — one more Perception spend on tracking instead of heading north, one more night camped in the direction of the Scour instead of the direction of the Vault, one more morning where she woke up and chose south without examining the choice. The question she couldn't stop asking — *if zero is a door, then what was I locking?* — had become less a question and more a direction, pulling her forward the way the Perception ability had pulled her attention south.

She'd told herself she was observing. Gathering intelligence. Following the retrieval target to confirm his final disposition and file a comprehensive report. The institutional language was comfortable, worn smooth by decades of use, and it fit the shape of what she was doing the way a key fits a lock it was made to open. Except she'd stopped believing it somewhere around day five, when she'd realized she couldn't write the report even in her head without the sentences breaking apart. *The target proceeded south toward the Scour in the company of—* in the company of a woman who had been spending for forty years and had eight levels left and was walking toward a door because someone she loved was on the other side. Sable couldn't file that. There was no form for it. No classification that held.

Below, the two figures kept walking. The descent agents were closing from the west. The circuiting agent was moving to intercept. The commander stood at the threshold point, unmoving, the center of a geometry that was about to collapse into something Sable couldn't predict and couldn't look away from.

She stood up. The rock below was three hours of descent — the switchback, or a secondary route she'd mapped from the ridgeline, a scree slope that was faster and less visible. Three hours. By then it might be over. By then the two people down there might have burned their remaining twenty levels and found out whether thirty percent was enough or whether the door stayed shut.

Or she might arrive and the math might change.

Sable adjusted her pack. She checked her footing. She looked down at the basin floor one more time — the layered rock, the shimmering heat, the four agents and two figures and one unmarked point that the entire architecture of the Hoarded had been built to hide.

She started walking down.
