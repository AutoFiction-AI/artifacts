# Chapter 13

The road had been bad for two days. Not road — packed earth over old Shaping work, the kind of infrastructure the Hoarded contracted fast in the Margins and never maintained. Kem could feel it through his boots: shallow subsidence where structural charms had eroded, cracks running through compacted dirt, root systems pushing up from plants that should have been dead years ago but kept going anyway because nothing in the Margins died cleanly.

He was at forty-three.

Seven levels gone in two days. He could account for each one. A Light ability in a stretch of collapsed culvert on the first evening, where even Dren couldn't navigate the rubble blind. Two levels on a healing touch for a woman they'd found sitting in the brush with a broken wrist, cradling the arm in her lap like something she was carrying for someone else — she'd been there two days, she said, which meant two days of nobody coming, which meant the Margins. A Warmth Charm on a night when the basin wind found every seam in their camp. A minor Strength boost to help Dren shift a fallen crossbeam blocking the only passable track, because Dren's bad arm couldn't take the weight alone and going around would have cost them half a day they didn't have. Two more on a Flame Charm and a Water Purification when they found a silted well and no dry kindling.

Seven levels. Some of them he could have saved. The Warmth Charm, maybe — Vel could have rigged a windbreak. The Strength boost — they could have gone around. But the woman's wrist had been broken and nobody was coming, and the well water would have given them cramps they couldn't afford, and some of the decisions that looked like waste were just the decisions that kept them alive and moving.

Vel hadn't commented on the spending. That was new. In the first weeks she'd have taken each unnecessary spend apart with surgical attention — the cost, the alternative, the principle. But since the labor camp, since she'd watched him burn ninety-eight levels on strangers and couldn't bring herself to tell him to stop, something in her economy of correction had gone quiet. She still tracked each spend with that flat assessment, the look that made his skin prickle because he could never tell what calculation was running behind it. But the lectures had stopped. He didn't know what that meant. He'd stopped assuming he knew what anything about Vel meant.

Dren walked ahead, favoring his left arm — the one Kem had stitched after the silo explosion, weeks ago now. The wound had healed wrong, the scar tissue pulling tight in the heat, and Dren carried the limb slightly off his body as though keeping distance between it and the rest of himself might make it someone else's problem. He hadn't complained about it. That was Dren — you mentioned a thing when something could be done about it, and when nothing could, you adjusted and kept going.

The Margins around them were what the Margins always were. Depleted farmland the color of bone dust, where Fertility Charms had been cast and recast until the soil couldn't hold anything anymore. Low structures with Shaping-smooth walls going to ruin — built straight and cheap by workers on ability-debt contracts, built to last exactly long enough for the Hoarded to extract value and not a season past that. Posted notices on stone pillars in faded ink: PRODUCTIVITY TIER REASSIGNMENT — ALL EXPENDITURE UNITS REPORT TO SECTOR DESIGNATE. Nobody had taken them down. The energy required to peel ink off a stone pillar was energy nobody here could spare.

Mid-summer heat pressed on everything. The air was thick and dry, the sun a flat white weight on pale sky. Kem's skin had changed over the weeks of travel — browned, peeling across his nose and the backs of his hands, nothing like the indoor pallor he'd carried out of the Vault. His body too. Leaner. Harder at the shoulders and hands, where new callus and muscle sat on top of bone they'd never met. He caught himself sometimes in the unfamiliar fit of his own clothes and thought: I don't recognize this person. Then he'd check his Ledger and think the same thing about the number.

Forty-three. Low enough that he could feel each remaining ability as a distinct presence — when he'd been at three hundred, the Ledger had been a wall of potential, vast and undifferentiated, everything available at once. At forty-three, it was a list he could read end to end in a few breaths. A Stabilization Charm. A defensive Barrier. A Healing Touch. A handful of combat applications he'd learned to use with something close to precision. Each one with a cost beside it in his mind, each cost now significant against the total. Spending one level at three hundred had been invisible. Spending one level at forty-three was arithmetic you did in your chest.

The ground gave out twenty yards ahead of them.

No warning — or rather, warnings that had been there for decades, legible to anyone who understood what cheap Shaping work looked like at the end of its lifespan. The structural charms holding this section of road over a shallow drainage channel had been thinning for years, the old magic eroding the way everything in the Margins eroded, and when the last thread gave the road surface folded inward with a sound that was wrong. Not a crack. A long tearing exhalation, as if the ground had been holding together through sheer habit and finally let go.

A section fifteen feet across dropped. The edges kept eating outward — road surface breaking away in bites, the collapse spreading in slow cascading failure.

Dren stopped. Vel stopped.

There was a man on the failing section. A Margins farmer by his clothes and his thinness, a pack of dried herbs on his back that he'd been hauling somewhere to trade. The ground tilted under him and he went to hands and knees and the herb pack slid sideways and he grabbed for it because it was worth something, and the grabbing shifted his weight, and the edge nearest to him gave another foot.

Kem didn't decide. He'd learned that about spending — there was a moment before the arithmetic, a place where the body moved because the situation required it and the numbers ran behind, fast and automatic, like balance or breathing. The decision lived ahead of the math.

He threw a Stabilization Charm at the fracture line.

Twelve levels.

It tore out of him with a heat he felt in his hands and his sternum and the backs of his eyes — the Ledger adjusting, a permanent subtraction, twelve points of himself deleted in the space of a single breath. The energy hit the collapsing road and caught it. Dirt and stone and the remnants of old structural Shaping froze mid-failure, the cascade of collapse stopping along a ragged line, the surface hardening in the exact configuration of its own ruin. It looked like a wave caught mid-break — road surface locked at wrong angles, exposed drainage channel underneath, the farmer stranded on a tilted platform of stabilized earth.

Dren was already moving. He dropped his pack and crossed the stabilized section, testing each step, reaching the man in eight careful strides. "Give me your hand. Leave the — yeah, leave it, we'll get it." He hauled the farmer to solid ground and went back for the herb pack. The farmer came up shaking, his palms bleeding from the broken road surface, and he looked at Kem and looked away. In the Margins, you didn't stare at someone who'd just spent for you. It drew attention to a debt that couldn't be repaid.

Vel stood at the collapse's edge, reading the fracture patterns the way she read everything — as information, as cost. Her eyes tracked the stabilized section, the structural failure, the depth of the drainage channel. She was checking whether the Charm would hold. Whether the road ahead was viable. Whether the twelve levels had been spent wisely or just spent.

Kem sat down on the road's edge.

His hands were shaking. Not from effort — from subtraction. Twelve levels gone and the Ledger had adjusted with the flat permanence it always had, the background hum of his remaining abilities shifting to a thinner key. Things that had been there minutes ago were not there now. A Barrier Charm he'd been holding in reserve — deleted. A speed ability he'd used twice in the Margins — gone. Not diminished, not weakened. Removed, as cleanly as if they'd never existed. The place where they'd been was a gap he could feel, a silence in the chord.

He checked the number.

Thirty-one.

The number settled into him the way all Ledger losses settled — not gradually but all at once, a new baseline his body adjusted to between one breath and the next. He could feel the change in the background hum of his awareness. It was quieter now. Not silent — the Ledger's presence never went fully silent above zero — but thin, a sound at the edge of hearing where it had once been a steady note he'd never needed to listen for. At three hundred the hum had been a room full of noise, too constant to register. At forty-three it had been a conversation he could follow. At thirty-one it was a voice speaking just above a whisper, and he had to hold very still inside himself to hear what it was saying.

What it said was: you have this, and this, and this, and after that there's nothing.

Two hundred and sixty-nine levels gone since he left the Vault. A number so large it felt like someone else's biography. A hundred and thirty of those had gone into other people — healing, defense, infrastructure, the woman's wrist, the labor camp, the road. The rest on his own survival and his own early stupidity. Either way, deleted. Each one a capability removed from a kit that had once held everything. He was down to the last drawer now, and he could see the bottom.

He sat with it. The number was a fact, the same way all Ledger numbers were facts — precise, irreversible, indifferent to what you thought about them. But this one was also someone else's. Thirty-one was the number Vel Ashwick had carried at the start of this journey. Thirty-one was the number she'd been living with for forty years of field medicine and ditch-sleeping and healing people the Hoarded had abandoned. When she'd first told him her level, weeks ago in the forest, he'd thought: that's barely enough to survive on. That's the Ledger of someone who's given almost everything away.

Now it was his Ledger.

"Thirty-one," he said.

Vel had turned from the collapse to look at him. Something moved across her face — a tightening around the eyes, a shift he couldn't read and didn't trust himself to interpret. She looked at him the way you'd look at something you'd been watching approach from a long way off.

"Then you know," she said, and turned back to the road.

* * *

They stopped at an abandoned farmstead half a mile south of the collapse. One of the Margins' standard ruins — Shaping-raised walls crumbling in the heat, a roof that sagged but held, a courtyard with a dead well and a stone bench worn smooth by years of someone sitting on it. The farmer with the herbs had gone his own way without a word, heading east with his bleeding hands and his pack. In the Margins, help was weather. It happened and then it was over and you didn't write about it.

Kem sat against the courtyard wall. The stone was warm through his shirt. His Ledger hummed at thirty-one, a sound he could almost isolate now — each remaining ability a distinct frequency in a chord that had been getting thinner for weeks. He could feel the shape of what he had left. A Healing Touch. A Light ability. A few combat applications. A Stabilization Charm weakened from use. The list was short enough to hold in his mind without effort, and holding it felt like holding a handful of coins and counting them and knowing you'd counted right and wishing you'd counted wrong.

Vel sat across the courtyard with Maren's route journal open on her knees. She traced a line on a page with one finger, her other hand holding the battered cover flat. The handwriting on the visible page wasn't hers — he'd noticed this weeks ago without thinking much of it. Two different hands in the journal, one neater than the other, the margins dense with annotations. Vel held the journal with both hands, with a care she applied to nothing else she owned. Everything about how she handled it was different from how she handled supplies or weapons or her own body — gentler, more specific, as though the pages could be hurt.

"I want to know what you meant," Kem said.

Dren was refilling their water from a clay jug he'd found in the farmstead, testing each mouthful before pouring. He glanced up at Kem, then at Vel. His pouring slowed.

"What thirty-one feels like," Vel said. "Now you know."

"That's not what you meant."

She closed the journal. Didn't put it away — held it in both hands on her lap, thumbs on the cover.

"You've been holding something back about zero," he said. "You told me it's a threshold. A door. Something's on the other side. You were specific about it not being death." He could hear the formal shape of his own speech — the Hoarded cadences surfacing from wherever they lived when he wasn't paying attention, the long clauses and careful diction of a person who'd spent twenty-six years in rooms where language was managed as carefully as levels. He didn't correct it. "You didn't tell me how the door works."

"No."

"Tell me."

She looked at him. The assessment look — the one he recognized from the first day she'd found him, three days out of the Vault at two-eighty-six, burning through levels on problems a child could solve. He'd read it then as contempt. He was no longer sure what else had been in it.

"The threshold doesn't open because you hit a number," she said. "It's not a lock with a key. It requires a cascade. Sustained spending in proximity to the threshold site. One person hitting zero alone can cross, but the threshold barely opens. Barely holds." She paused. Picked a different track than the one she'd started on. "Two spenders approaching zero together — the combined energy output stabilizes the opening. The threshold holds long enough for a real crossing. And if one of the spenders is descending from a high starting level — burning a large range in a compressed span — the cascade amplifies. The amplitude of the descent matters. The bigger the drop, the more energy the threshold gets."

Kem heard the mechanics. He heard the logic — two sources of expenditure reinforcing each other, the way two fires built close together throw more heat than two fires apart. It made a kind of physical sense, the Ledger responding to concentrated output rather than a single number.

He also heard the years.

"How high," he said.

"The higher the starting point, the stronger the cascade. Someone descending from two hundred. Two-fifty. Three hundred." She said the number without inflection. "The energy output scales with the starting differential."

"And you knew this when you found me."

She answered a different question. She always did that — narrowed the frame, offered a smaller adjacent truth, and you might not notice the real one sliding past. "I saw a single crossing, years ago. One person, alone. The threshold opened for a few seconds and it was — wrong. Flickering. Unstable. The person made it through, but I knew it wasn't reliable. I knew I couldn't count on a solo crossing."

"So you needed a partner." He spoke carefully, laying each word down, because the path was leading somewhere and he needed to see it to its end before he reacted. "Someone at a high level. Someone willing to burn all the way down alongside you. Someone who'd start at three hundred and spend everything between there and zero."

"Yes."

"And you found me."

Vel's mouth opened. He saw the beginning of something — a qualifier, a correction, the impulse to redirect. Then she closed her mouth and just looked at him.

Thessa's voice surfaced in his head, clear and precise. It had been sitting there since the settlement — present every time he put weight on it, sharp in the way of things you choose not to examine because the next crisis is always closer. *Vel came through here years ago. Asked about high-level defectors. Whether any Hoarded lords had walked out. Whether anyone at two-fifty or above might be persuaded to burn down.*

He hadn't confronted Vel about it then. There'd been the Hoarded representative, the escape, the eighteen levels he'd burned getting them clear. Then the river. Then the Margins, and the labor camp, and ninety-eight levels poured into strangers who needed them. There had always been the next thing. And under every next thing, Thessa's words had been working deeper, the way a splinter works deeper when you leave it alone.

"Thessa told me," he said. "That you'd been through her settlement before. Years ago. Looking for someone at my level."

"Yes."

One word. No qualification.

"How many years."

"Several."

"How many people did you consider before me."

"I heard about two defectors. One had spent down too far by the time I found him. The other —" She stopped. "She wasn't willing."

"So I was the first one who was high enough and stupid enough."

"You were the first one who was high enough and actually wanted zero."

The distinction landed and sat there between them. It didn't help.

He thought about the forest. The first day. Vel watching him stumble through basic trail navigation with the particular attention of someone taking measurements. He'd thought she was deciding whether he was worth the effort of teaching. He'd thought the assessment in her eyes was: can this person be made competent? Now the assessment read differently. Can this person be guided to spend at a rate I can work with? Does he have enough starting material? Will he last the distance?

Every generous reading of every moment they'd shared had a second reading underneath it, and the second reading was worse. The campfire where she'd told him about the drowning girl — was that trust, or was it investment? Building the emotional architecture that would keep him committed? The restraint lessons — wisdom, or budget management? And the Margins, watching him burn ninety-eight levels on strangers — had she been running the subtraction with every healing he performed, every enforcer he fought, every irrigation ditch he couldn't walk past?

He didn't know. That was the thing that sat heaviest. He couldn't separate what had been real from what had been engineered, because the engineering was good enough that it might have been real all along. Or the real parts were real and the engineering ran beneath them like a drainage channel under a road, and both were true at once, and he was going to have to live inside both.

"So that's what I am," he said. "A battery."

Dren set down the water jug. Slowly, as if the sound of clay on stone might break something.

Vel didn't answer.

"When you found me in the forest," Kem said. "When you looked at me and decided to make the offer. You weren't disgusted by the waste. You were calculating. Whether I had enough left. Whether my rate of spending was something you could work with. Whether I'd last the journey." He was talking faster now, the Hoarded composure cracking along its usual fault lines — he talked more under pressure, always had, filled silence with words because silence left room for the thing he was trying to outrun. "The teaching. The fire-making, the field medicine, the restraint lessons. You were keeping your investment functional."

"I was keeping you alive."

"Which is the same thing."

"It isn't."

"It looks exactly the same from where I'm sitting, Vel."

She put the route journal in her pack. The motion was careful and deliberate — both hands, inner pocket, the way she always stowed it. He watched her do it and thought: that's the thing she protects. That journal. Whatever it means to her. I'm the thing she uses.

* * *

He stood up.

The courtyard was small and hot and the Margins pressed in through every gap — the dead fields visible through the empty doorway, the ruined structures lining what had once been a road, the posted labor notices still legible on the farmstead's outer wall. RENEWABLE EXPENDITURE UNIT. PRODUCTIVITY TIER THREE. PROJECTED ANNUAL OUTPUT: 4.2 LEVELS. He'd read a dozen of these in the labor camp two days ago. They classified human beings by yield.

"I want to understand something." His voice had gone strange — conversational, almost pleasant. He could hear the Hoarded polish in it, the register his father used in Council sessions when he was about to end someone's career without raising his voice. He hated the sound of it coming out of his own mouth. He used it anyway. "You spent forty years healing people. That girl drowning in the canal when you were seventeen. Every farmer and every settlement since. Forty years of jumping in when nobody else would. And then you found me." He turned to face her. "You looked at me and you saw levels. You calculated my output." He gestured — at the faded notices on the wall, at the Margins visible through the door, at the landscape of extraction around them. "You recruited me as a component. That's what they do here. They look at people and see what can be extracted." He paused. His hand was still raised, pointing at the posted notice that classified humans as Renewable Expenditure Units, and the parallel was so clean it made him sick. "You replicated the system you claim to oppose. You saw levels, not a person. You just had a nicer reason for it."

The words landed in the courtyard and stayed.

Vel stood against the far wall with her hands at her sides. Her face was doing the thing it did when she couldn't find the smaller adjacent answer — when the real question was too large for the redirect she usually offered. He watched her cycle through responses. A mouth movement of a thought started and abandoned. A shift in posture that meant she'd found a qualification and discarded it. She was searching for the distinction between what she'd done and what the Hoarded did. Between her purpose and their extraction. Between the way she'd used him and the way the posted notices on the wall behind her described a system for using everyone.

She didn't find it.

The silence went ten seconds. Fifteen. Long enough that Dren, who had been sitting very still by the water jug, stood up carefully and said, "I'll check whether the road south is clear," and walked out through the courtyard entrance. His boots ground on loose stone and then it was the two of them and the heat and the Margins.

"You're right," Vel said.

The words arrived without force. Flat. Quiet voice, the one that surfaced under real pressure — every syllable weighed and placed. They didn't surprise him. The fifteen seconds of silence had already been the answer. This was confirmation.

"Not about all of it," she said. "What grew between us — the river, the camp, the conversations, the labor camp, all of it since — that was real. I didn't plan it. I couldn't have."

"But the starting point."

"The starting point was me needing someone at your level and finding you and making a deal that served my purpose." She said it without inflection. Not apologetic. Not defiant. Just the shape of the thing. "Yes."

"And the spending guidance. The restraint training. That wasn't discipline — it was resource management. You were protecting your cascade budget."

She flinched. Small — a contraction in her expression that most people wouldn't have caught. He caught it because he'd been watching her for weeks and because he'd been trained by twenty-six years in the Vault to read faces that never said what they meant.

"Both," she said. "It was both. I was teaching you because you'd have died without it, and I was managing the cascade because I needed you to have enough left at the end. I can't separate them. I've tried."

"That's convenient."

"It's true."

"Those aren't the same thing."

She didn't answer. She looked at the wall behind him, at the Shaping-smooth surface going to dust, at the heat shimmering through the courtyard entrance where Dren had gone.

"I can't make the origin different," she said. Her voice had dropped to something barely audible — not trailing off, just conserving, rationing even the energy of speech. "I found you because I needed your levels. Everything after happened because you're a person, and I couldn't have predicted what kind. But I can't cut the beginning off and hand you the rest and pretend the rest stands on its own." A pause. "It doesn't."

Kem stood in the courtyard and felt the heat on his skin and the thirty-one humming at the edge of his mind and the specific weight of the word *component* sitting in his mouth like a stone. He looked at the labor classification notice still posted on the farmstead wall. The Hoarded had looked at the Margins and seen energy to extract. Vel had looked at him and seen energy to extract. The distinction was supposed to be that she cared about him and the Hoarded didn't care about anyone. But the structure was the same. The arithmetic was the same. And in the Margins — standing in the wreckage of a system that had ground generations down to nothing through precisely that arithmetic — the caring didn't undo the structure. It just made the structure harder to see.

"No," he said. "It doesn't."

He could leave. The thought surfaced on its own, automatic — the obvious response to discovering you've been used. Walk away. Go north. Find a settlement, offer his remaining levels for food and shelter, live out his days at thirty-one or whatever he burned down to.

Except he couldn't. Thirty-one levels in the deep Margins, with Sable's team somewhere behind them and the territory ahead getting worse, and no route knowledge, no map, no understanding of what lay between here and anywhere safe. Vel had the journal. Vel had the forty years of field experience. Vel had the knowledge of what was south and what was coming from the north. Without her, he was a man with thirty-one levels and no plan in country that would eat him alive.

And she couldn't survive without him either. She was at eighteen — he'd heard her tell Dren two days ago, adjusting a supply calculation. Eighteen levels and the body of a woman who'd been grinding herself down for four decades. She needed what he had left for the crossing and for whatever stood between here and the threshold.

They were stuck with each other. The betrayal and the dependency were the same structure, and pulling out one would collapse the other.

They stood there. The heat pressed on the courtyard walls and neither of them moved. The Margins offered no comfort, because the Margins never had comfort to give — not to the people who'd been spent down here, and not to two people burning through on their way somewhere else.

Kem became aware that he was touching his right hand. The ring finger, specifically. The House signet — heavy gold, the Aldraste crest of three locked towers, the Hoarded symbol of preservation through inaction. He'd worn it since the Vault. Through every mile of this journey. In the Spent Towns, a tavern keeper had seen it and her face had closed, and he'd covered it with his palm. In the Margins, nobody recognized the crest — they only knew the Hoarded's systems, not their symbols. He'd kept the ring on because taking it off would have meant thinking about who he was, and he hadn't been ready to think about that.

He pulled it off.

The finger underneath was pale and slightly grooved from weeks of wear, the skin soft where the gold had pressed. He held the signet in his palm. It caught the courtyard light — warm, heavy, ornate. Three locked towers. The family that chose stillness. The system that extracted from the Margins and preserved at the Vault and treated the distance between those two things as natural order. The ring his father had worn while his mind emptied out behind his eyes.

He thought about throwing it into the scrub past the farmstead wall. Letting it vanish into Margins dirt, one more thing the Margins swallowed.

He didn't.

He closed his hand around it, felt the edges of the towers press into his palm, and put it in his pocket.

Dren came back through the entrance. "Road's passable if we cut through the fields for a stretch. Another collapse a quarter mile on, but the drainage is dry — we can walk it." He looked at Kem. He looked at Vel. Whatever he read in the space between them, he filed without comment. "We should move. That stabilization won't hold past dark."

"Yeah," Kem said.

They gathered their packs. Vel shouldered hers with the automatic efficiency of a person who'd been carrying loads for forty years — her body knew how to distribute weight, settle straps, start moving without wasting the effort of starting. Kem picked up his pack. It was lighter than it had been two days ago. Everything was lighter. He couldn't tell anymore whether that was the pack or the Ledger or the particular weightlessness of knowing that the person he'd trusted most had built their relationship inside a structure designed to use him.

He walked ahead. Ten feet, then fifteen. The distance of a man who couldn't leave — thirty-one levels in the Margins, hostile territory in every direction, neither of them capable of surviving alone — and couldn't stay close. The distance of a man trying to outpace the inside of his own head and aware that it wasn't working.

Vel followed at the gap he'd set. She didn't close it. She didn't try.

Dren walked between them, which was a choice, and a kind one, because it meant neither of them had to look at the back of the other's head.

They walked for an hour without speaking. The road south was worse than what they'd come in on — stretches where the Shaping had failed entirely, leaving raw earth that the summer heat had baked into a surface like broken pottery. Twice they stepped off the road into the dead fields, picking through soil that crunched underfoot. Dren found the dry drainage channel he'd scouted and they walked the bottom of it for a quarter mile, crumbling walls of old earth on either side, the sky a strip of white above them.

Kem thought about his father. The Conservatory Rite — the weekly ritual of sitting perfectly still, demonstrating the discipline of never acting. He thought about how his father's eyes had gone empty behind the composure. The personality thinning year by year. The Stillness eating him from the inside while his Ledger held at three hundred and everyone in the room celebrated the number.

He'd left the Vault because he couldn't watch it anymore. He'd spent his first ability on opening a door because staying still had become impossible. And Vel had been positioned for exactly that kind of person — someone whose revulsion at the Hoarded philosophy would make him willing to burn everything down. She hadn't caused his departure. But she'd been ready for it, the way a channel cut in dry ground is ready for rain, and when he fell she caught him and pointed him south.

The signet pressed against his thigh through the pocket fabric. He didn't take it out.

The Margins stretched south. Depleted earth, crumbling infrastructure, the summer heat rising in waves that made the road shimmer and the distance uncertain. Behind them, the stabilized collapse sat in its frozen ruin — road surface locked at wrong angles, the drainage channel exposed, Kem's twelve levels hardened into the ground. The Charm would hold until dark, maybe longer. Then the Shaping would thin and the road would finish collapsing, and the Margins would swallow another piece of infrastructure the Hoarded had built too cheaply and walked away from, and nobody would come to fix it.

Ahead of them, more road. More Margins. More distance to cover with less of everything — fewer levels, fewer words, fewer of the small exchanges that had once made the walking bearable. Kem kept his eyes forward and his hand out of his pocket and his pace just fast enough that Vel's footsteps behind him stayed at the distance he'd set.

Thirty-one. Vel's number. His number now.

He kept walking.
