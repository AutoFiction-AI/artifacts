# Chapter 4

The ridge trail had been climbing since dawn, and Kem's calves were filing complaints he had no language for.

In the Vault, the longest walk was sixty meters — Conservatory to the east dining hall, always on flat stone, always in soft-soled shoes designed to make no sound. Nobody ran in the Vault. Nobody climbed. The body was a thing you maintained through inaction, like a vase, and it performed accordingly. Now his body was being asked to carry its own weight uphill through tangled forest for six consecutive hours, and it was responding with specific muscular opinions he'd never known it had.

Vel was thirty paces ahead, moving through the undergrowth with the kind of ease that came from having done this since before he was born. She didn't look back. She hadn't looked back since they'd broken camp that morning, and Kem had stopped deciding whether this was trust or indifference because both options were equally dispiriting.

The highland forest closed overhead. The trees were old — Kem didn't know what kind, couldn't tell one from another, an omission in his education he hadn't known existed until two days ago. He'd asked Vel what a particular tree was called and she'd looked at him as if he'd asked her what walking was. So he'd stopped asking about trees. The light filtered through the canopy in shifting columns, warm where it landed, cool where it didn't. The air carried the smell of earth, of something resinous and sharp, of leaves that had fallen last year and were slowly becoming soil. His pack straps had worn through the linen of his shirt and were working on the skin beneath, which would blister by afternoon. He knew this because it had blistered yesterday.

His Ledger read 286, a number he was aware of the way he'd once been aware of the Vault's walls — always there, always at the edge of thought, a quiet hum of remaining capacity. In the Vault that hum had been a deep, steady presence at 300, the fullness of a reservoir never tapped. Now it was slightly thinner. The fourteen levels he'd burned through since leaving — the Projection Shield, the Sustenance Charm, the absurd incidental wastes that Vel catalogued with the precision of someone counting coins she couldn't afford to lose — had changed the hum's quality in a way he was only beginning to understand. Not emptier. Just different. A glass poured from, for the first time.

Vel stopped.

She said nothing. Just stopped, one hand against a tree trunk, her head angled in a way that meant she was listening with forty years of forest behind her ears. Kem stopped too. The pack settled. His calves expressed themselves.

He listened. Wind in the upper canopy. The click-rustle of small things in the undergrowth. A bird somewhere east, making a stuttering call that might have been a warning or a mating display — he had no way to distinguish these. He heard nothing unusual, which he'd learned was often the problem.

"Company," Vel said, still facing the ridge behind them. "Multiple. Coming down our backtrail."

"How far?"

"Close enough that the birds quit about three minutes ago and haven't come back. And there's a spot about two hundred meters behind us where the mud shows boot treads that aren't ours." She was already moving off the trail, angling down the slope toward denser cover. "Stay behind me. Step where I step."

Kem followed. Quiet was a thing he'd been trained for, in a different context — the Vault's code of controlled movement, the ceremonial discipline of footfall that demonstrated conservation of energy. But the Vault's floors were marble and stone. This ground was made of things that cracked and shifted and gave way, and his boots were stiff-soled, heavy, purchased from a trader near the Vault gates who'd seen Kem's clothes and charged accordingly.

They descended through a drainage gully where the ground was slick with accumulated damp. Vel navigated it without breaking stride. Kem navigated it by sitting down involuntarily and sliding three meters on his back, collecting a streak of mud and a gash across his right palm from a sharp stone. He bit the inside of his cheek and kept moving.

Vel led them along a shelf of exposed rock — no tracks on stone — then through a stand of young trees growing so close together that his pack caught on every third trunk. Twenty minutes of careful movement through terrain that was actively trying to undress him.

Then Vel stopped again. Her mouth compressed into a line he was learning to read as arithmetic she didn't like.

"They've adjusted bearing," she said. Quietly, but not whispering — Vel didn't whisper. "They have a tracker. Someone spent a level on a Perception ability."

"They spent a level to find me?"

"That's the least interesting thing about what I just said." She looked at him. "They spent a level. The Hoarded authorized an enforcement agent to spend. That means Council directive. That means this isn't your family sending someone to talk sense."

Kem felt something settle in his stomach — not cold exactly, more like weight, the specific gravity of understanding that people had been dispatched. That somewhere in the Vault, in a room he could picture precisely because he'd spent years passing its doors, an authorization had been signed and sealed and handed to someone whose job it now was to bring him back.

Vel assessed the terrain. Looked uphill, downhill, checked sight lines.

"We can't outrun a Perception tracker in forest this thick," she said. "Not at the pace you walk."

"I could use a Speed—"

"No."

"You don't even know what I was going to—"

"You were going to suggest a Speed enhancement or a Concealment overlay, and either one would cost you eight to twelve levels because you don't know how to calibrate, and I'm not losing a week's budget because you had an idea." She was scanning the slope above them. "We go to ground. See what they bring."

She found a position — a natural depression behind a deadfall, the massive trunk of a tree that had come down seasons ago and was slowly returning to the soil. Good sight lines upslope. An escape route downhill through a ravine. Tactically sound, Kem supposed, though his understanding of tactics was entirely theoretical, sourced from books he'd found abstractly interesting in the Vault's library.

They waited. Three minutes. Five. The forest sounds around them were ordinary, but behind and above, the bird calls hadn't resumed.

Then they came through the trees.

Seven figures in the gray livery of Hoarded enforcement — not soldiers, because the Hoarded didn't have soldiers, which was central to the philosophy's elaborate fiction. These were enforcement agents: young men and women from minor houses, each allocated five levels of combat training, authorized to operate outside the Vault under specific Council directives. They moved in a loose formation, spaced apart, covering approaches. Their posture held the rigid bearing of Hoarded discipline, but their eyes were moving too fast, scanning the unfamiliar forest with the jittery attention of people who had trained for this scenario in a courtyard.

The lead agent was a woman about Kem's age, dark-haired, a minor-house crest stitched on her collar. She stopped twenty meters away and held up one hand, palm forward — the formal signal of Hoarded authority that Kem had seen a thousand times in the Vault's administrative corridors.

"Kem Aldraste." Her voice carried the precise, clipped diction of someone who had rehearsed this. "By directive of the Hoarded Council, under recovery authority granted to the enforcement branch, you are ordered to submit to escort and return to the Vault for—"

"Move," Vel said.

Kem moved.

***

Vel went right, low, fast. Not the explosive speed of an ability — just the efficient movement of someone whose body had been doing this work for four decades. She covered the distance to the nearest agent in the time it took him to register that the woman behind the deadfall was no longer there.

Her first ability looked small. That was the thing Kem would remember afterward — how small it looked. A precise application of force, no wider than two fingers, directed at the soft tissue below the agent's ear. The man's knees gave and he sat down hard in the ferns, his expression shifting from alertness to confusion to nothing. He folded sideways and lay still.

One level. One agent.

The second outrider — a broad-shouldered woman positioned to the first's left — was already activating her shield. It bloomed in front of her, a disc of translucent force roughly a meter across. Textbook deployment, correct technique, exactly as she'd trained. Vel stepped to her unshielded right and pressed two fingers to the woman's neck with a touch so light Kem almost missed it. Whatever the ability did, it was fast — the agent's face went slack, her knees buckled, and her shield dissolved into the air as her concentration went with her balance.

Two levels. Two agents.

The lead agent — the one who'd been delivering the formal retrieval speech — fired her Combat Pulse. It was the standard enforcement variant: a concussive burst aimed to stun and disorient, well-directed, technically sound. It hit the space Vel had occupied a half-second earlier. Vel had read the woman's shoulders before she'd committed to a direction and was already elsewhere — two steps left, pivot, close. She touched the agent's sternum with the heel of her hand, and the woman's voluntary motor control shut down from the ribs out. She collapsed in stages — shoulders, hips, knees — wearing the bewildered expression of someone whose body had just stopped taking orders.

Three levels. Three agents. The whole thing had taken maybe twenty seconds.

Vel straightened. She wasn't breathing hard. She looked at the four remaining outriders, who were standing in a semicircle in the highland forest with the specific look of people for whom the distance between training and reality had just become measurable.

"Walk away," she said.

One of them did. A young man — barely twenty, his enforcement livery still creased from storage — turned and pushed back through the undergrowth without a word. His footsteps crashed through the forest for a long time. Kem watched him go and did not blame him.

Three remained. Something passed between them — not courage exactly, but the stubbornness of people who'd been given five levels and told those five levels made them agents of the most powerful institution in the world. The man nearest Kem dropped into a combat stance and raised his hands, energy already gathering around his fingers. The woman beside him was pulling power for another Pulse. The third — a lean, quiet figure who'd been hanging back, the tracker — shifted position to cover the flank.

Kem was still standing behind the deadfall.

He was standing there watching Vel dismantle three trained enforcement agents the way she did everything — without wasted motion, without excess force, without theatre. He was standing there with his pack still on and his hand still bleeding where the rock had cut it and his heart going so hard he could feel it in his wrists, in his palms, in the pulse of blood against the gash on his right hand. His fingers were tingling. The Ledger was buzzing at the edge of his awareness — not the steady hum he'd carried for twenty-six years but something awake, something that wanted out, the way a held breath pushes against the teeth.

The man fired his Combat Pulse.

It was aimed at Kem — the target, the retrieval objective, the person the Council had authorized spending to bring home. The pulse crossed the distance between them as a fist-sized knot of concussive force, and Kem watched it come with the peculiar clarity of someone experiencing danger for the first time, each second presenting itself for examination.

He didn't decide to use the Combat Pulse. That was not how it happened. His body decided — the same way a body jerks a hand from heat, before thought, before intent. The ability rose through him like a wall of water breaking: up from whatever reservoir the Ledger measured, through his gut, expanding into his chest, flooding his arms, and out through his open hands toward the agent who'd fired.

He'd been trying to match what Vel had done. A small, targeted burst. A precise application.

What came out of his hands was not that.

The concussive wave expanded as it left him — not a thread of force but a wall, a hemisphere of compressed air and raw energy that blew outward in every direction. The outrider's incoming pulse was swallowed by it like a candle flame in a gale. The agent himself was caught by the leading edge and hurled backward off his feet. He traveled six meters through the air and hit a tree trunk with a sound that was part crack, part wet thud, and slid down to the ground trailing bark and a smear of something Kem didn't want to look at.

The woman beside him was caught by the lateral expansion and thrown sideways across the forest floor, rolling through ferns and deadfall. The tracker stumbled back but kept his footing, shielded by a boulder.

And the hillside behind them — the slope they'd been climbing since dawn — came apart. The wave hit the earth at an angle and the ground detonated. Soil, rock, root systems — a section of slope twenty meters across sheared loose and slid downhill in a roaring cascade of debris. Trees that had been growing there for decades tilted, groaned, and fell in a series of impacts that shook the ground hard enough for Kem to feel it through the soles of his expensive, impractical boots. Dust billowed. Stones bounced and cracked against other stones. A tree landed across the trail they'd walked that morning and splintered down its length.

The noise was enormous. It filled the forest, echoed off the opposite ridge, and came back diminished, as if the landscape were exhaling.

Then quiet. The particular quiet of a forest that has just been hit.

Kem looked at his hands. They were shaking. Not from fear — from something that was not fear. The ability had moved through him with a force nothing like the Projection Shield or the Sustenance Charm. Those had been mistakes of calibration, embarrassing excess. This had moved through his body like a river through a split dam, and the physical aftermath was still running: arms buzzing, chest tight, a warm electric prickling in his fingers that wanted more.

His Ledger had dropped. He felt the shift like a step missed on a staircase — a lurch in the background hum, a number that had been one thing and was now another. He checked.

Two hundred and seventy-one.

Fifteen levels. One Combat Pulse. He'd been trying to spend one.

The woman he'd knocked sideways was getting up. She was bleeding from her hairline and her left arm hung at an angle that meant something structural had failed inside it, but she was getting up, which meant five levels of enforcement training included something about continuing. The tracker was emerging from behind his boulder, hands raised, energy gathering.

Two of the three agents Kem was supposed to handle were still functional. And his allies in this fight consisted of one fifty-eight-year-old woman at level 28 who was currently looking at the destroyed hillside with an expression he couldn't read.

He reached for Speed.

The world blurred. The colors of the forest smeared sideways, the trees stretched into vertical lines, the ground turned into a single flowing surface beneath feet that were suddenly moving faster than any feet had business moving. He'd wanted to close the distance to the injured woman before she could fire. He'd wanted to be quick.

He was not quick. He was a projectile.

He crossed twenty meters of forest floor in less than a second, the air concussing around his body, the leaves he passed through stripped from their branches by windshear. He saw the woman's eyes widen — a slow-motion expansion in his accelerated perception, her mouth opening, the beginning of a shout he heard as a bass moan — and then the tree was there.

It was a thick-trunked highland tree, solidly rooted, patiently doing its work in the exact spot where Kem's trajectory met the physical world. He hit it chest-first at a speed that drove every molecule of air from his lungs. His ribs compressed against the trunk. The tree cracked at the point of impact and a slab of bark the size of a table sheared off and fell on his head.

He bounced. Literally bounced — his speed-enhanced body carried enough momentum that the impact redirected rather than stopped him. He spun sideways, hit the ground with his left shoulder, slid through a meter of loam and leaf litter, and came to rest against a boulder with his face in the dirt and a ringing in his ears that sounded like someone had struck a bell inside his skull.

Eight levels. The Speed enhancement had cost him eight levels and delivered him face-first into the oldest tree in the immediate vicinity.

He lay there for two seconds. The dirt tasted like iron and rot. His left ribs were reporting something he was going to have to deal with later. Above him, the canopy was a kaleidoscope of green and light, spinning gently.

He got up. The ground was unsteady, or he was. His Ledger hummed at 263 and the number felt strange — not low, not by any measure, but changed. Different from what it had been five minutes ago. His body had done things in the last five minutes that it had never done in twenty-six years.

The tracker was moving toward him, careful, smart, staying behind cover. The injured woman had managed to raise her good hand and was pulling energy for something — a Pulse, a Binding, whatever she had left in her five trained techniques. Two opponents, both functional, and Kem was on his feet with dirt in his eyes and a cracked rib and the residual momentum of the Speed ability still jangling through his nervous system.

He tried a shield.

He knew what a shield was supposed to look like — he'd deployed the Projection Shield in the Vault, a mistake meant for a doorframe that had spanned a corridor. He was trying for something small. Personal. A defensive screen, maybe two meters across, enough to block whatever was coming.

What he produced was a dome.

It erupted from the ground centered on his body and expanded outward at speed — a hemisphere of shimmering translucent force that grew and grew and did not stop growing. Five meters. Ten. Fifteen. It caught the injured woman and the tracker and shoved them backward like dry leaves before a broom, tumbling them across the forest floor. It caught Vel — she said "Shit" and threw herself behind a boulder as the dome's leading edge swept past, dragging a broken branch across her forearm. It caught the undergrowth and the smaller trees bent under it. It caught the air itself, and the displaced volume produced a pressure wave that stirred the canopy forty meters overhead.

Ten levels. A shield that could have covered a village square.

The dome held for three seconds and collapsed, because Kem didn't know how to sustain it and the energy dissipated as fast as it had bloomed. In its wake, the forest was rearranged. The two agents were on the ground again, farther away than before. The tracker was face-down in a thicket, not moving. The injured woman was caught in a tangle of fallen branches, struggling, bleeding from fresh cuts, her broken arm pinned beneath her at an angle that made Kem's stomach turn.

His Ledger read 253. Thirty-three levels gone in three attempts.

His chest was heaving. The cracked rib sang every time he breathed in. His hands were still tingling and the tingling was not fading — it was growing, as if each use of power primed the body for more, as if the reservoir wanted to empty itself, as if spending were an appetite that fed on its own satisfaction.

The woman was pulling herself free of the branches. Her face was set, grim, the face of someone operating on five levels of training and whatever conviction had gotten her out of the Vault that morning. She got one knee under her. Then the other. She raised her good hand and Kem saw the energy gather around her fingers, dim and flickering because she was hurt and exhausted but still present, still dangerous.

He didn't try for precision this time. He didn't try to match Vel's compact, surgical technique. He reached for the thing his body was already reaching for — whatever the Ledger offered first, whatever was closest to the surface — and shoved it forward.

A blast of force and light left his right hand. Not a Combat Pulse — something cruder, less defined, shaped only by the desperate intention behind it. It hit the ground three meters in front of the woman and the earth erupted. Dirt, stone, a root system that came up like a hand opening. The concussion threw her clear of the branches and deposited her fifteen meters away in a heap. She did not get up.

The tracker, who had been stirring in his thicket, lifted his head just enough to see the crater and the woman in the heap and the man standing at the center of the destruction. He put his hands flat on the earth in front of him. It might have been surrender. It might have been the only position his body could hold.

Eight more levels. Kem's Ledger read 245.

Forty-one levels. The fight had lasted maybe four minutes.

The forest around them looked like something had come through it sideways. The hillside was a scar of raw earth and displaced stone. A crater marked where his last ability had struck. Trees were snapped, leaning, or simply gone. The undergrowth was flattened in concentric rings from the dome's expansion. Dust hung in the filtered afternoon light, turning the air opaque and golden.

Vel emerged from behind her boulder. She had a scratch along her forearm where the dome's edge had whipped the branch past her. She looked at the destroyed clearing. She looked at the collapsed hillside. She looked at Kem.

Her face was the flattest he had ever seen it.

He was grinning.

***

The grin was not something he could help. He stood in the wreckage of the highland forest with dirt in his hair and a bruised rib and a Ledger that had dropped forty-one levels in four minutes, and his face was doing something it had never done before — not the polite arrangement of features the Vault demanded, but an involuntary physical response to the most intense experience of his life.

His body had worked.

That was the thing underneath everything else — the waste, the destruction, the danger. His body, the soft, untested, decorative body that had spent its entire existence walking between rooms and sitting in ceremonial stillness, had done things. Real things. His hands had shaped force and thrown it. His legs had moved at speed. His chest had taken an impact and kept operating. He was bruised and scraped and filthy and his rib hurt when he breathed and there was blood in his mouth from where he'd bitten his tongue hitting the tree, and he was more physically present in this moment than he had been in all twenty-six years before it. The Vault had preserved him in amber. This forest had cracked it open.

Vel was walking among the downed agents, checking each one with efficient, impersonal attention — pulse, breathing, level of consciousness. She did this the way she did everything: quickly, with minimal wasted motion, as if even attention were a resource she rationed.

"They'll live," she said, straightening from the tracker. "Bruised, concussed. Her arm's broken. He's got cracked ribs." A pause. "Same side as yours."

"Good," Kem said. "I mean — not good. I didn't — I wasn't trying to—"

"Which part?" Vel's voice was level in the way that a still lake is level — pressure beneath the surface, invisible, total. "The part where you leveled thirty meters of hillside with a pulse meant to stun? The part where you ran into a tree? Or the part where you threw a shield that nearly put me through a rock?"

"I didn't — the shield was supposed to be small. I was trying—"

"I know what you were trying." She looked at him. The afternoon light came through the disrupted canopy and lit the dust around her, and in that light she looked like what she was: a fifty-eight-year-old woman who had been doing this for four decades, who had spent forty years learning to do the most with the least, watching him do the least with the most. "I spent three levels. One per agent. Targeted, calibrated, controlled. Total cost: three. Three agents out, no collateral damage, no environmental destruction, no broken bones on my own side." She paused. "You spent forty-one."

He'd known the number. He'd felt each drop, each lurch in the Ledger's hum, the background frequency of his life shifting and shifting again. But hearing her say it was different.

"Forty-one levels on three agents — and that's generous, because the tracker just stopped because you cratered the ground near him. Forty-one levels, a collapsed hillside, a hundred meters of wrecked forest, and a dome that nearly killed your only ally." Vel's voice was quiet. She didn't get louder when things were bad; she got quieter. The quieter she got, the worse the arithmetic. "You walked into this at 286. You're at 245. That's more than most people in the Spent Towns spend in a year."

The grin faded. Not entirely — his body still hummed with residual charge, still felt electric and awake in ways the Vault had never hinted were possible — but the edges of it softened.

"I know."

"You don't." She squatted beside the lead agent — the woman who'd been delivering the retrieval speech — and unfastened a document case from the woman's belt. Opened it. Inside were papers bearing the Hoarded Council's formal seal: heavy stock, precise script, the language of institutional authority that Kem had grown up hearing and had never, until this moment, been aimed at him.

"Recovery directive," Vel said, scanning the pages. "Council-authorized. Your mother's name is on it. Lady Aldraste, requesting enforcement retrieval." She turned a page. "Standard terms. Six enforcement agents allocated. Five training levels per agent. Thirty levels of authorized spending — just on the retrieval team. That doesn't count the tracker's Perception spend."

Vel folded the documents and tucked them into her own pack. Not for keepsake — for information. Kem was beginning to understand that Vel kept everything that might be useful and discarded everything that wasn't, and that this principle applied to objects, conversations, and people.

"This is institutional," she said. "The Council weighed your departure against the philosophy of inaction and decided that bringing you back was worth the violation. They authorized spending to retrieve you." She looked at him. "That doesn't happen. The whole system runs on the principle that spending is never worth it. They've just admitted it sometimes is."

Kem heard this and understood. Not abstractly — he understood it in the Vault's specific language of cost and precedent, the way he'd been trained to think since childhood. The Council had spent political capital to authorize physical capital to retrieve one defected lord. Each level those agents had been permitted to spend was a crack in the philosophy that held the Hoarded world together. They'd sent seven people into the forest to bring back one, and the math of that decision — the public cost of admitting that action was sometimes necessary — told Kem more about his departure's impact than any message from home could have.

"They'll send more," Vel said. She shouldered her pack. "Now that these seven have failed, they'll send better. More training, more authorization, more spending. The cost of leaving you out here goes up every day you keep burning."

Kem looked at the wreckage. The collapsed hillside. The shattered trees. The craters. The felled trunk that had been a living tree that morning and was now a splintered ruin because he'd pushed force into the world without knowing how much was too much. He looked at his hands. They were dirty, scraped, trembling faintly with residual energy, and they had twenty minutes ago done things he still could not fully comprehend.

"I should have — I couldn't control it," he said. His voice caught on the Hoarded formality that wanted to frame this as a measured self-assessment, and he let it go. "The pulse was supposed to be small. I was trying to do what you did."

"I've been doing this since I was seventeen."

"I know that."

"Then know this too. What you just did — forty-one levels of uncontrolled output — that's not recklessness. It's not stupidity. It's ignorance, and there's a difference, but the difference doesn't help us. You've never been taught how much force a situation requires, because the Vault taught you that no force was ever required. So when you reach for it, you get everything. No filter. No calibration. No muscle memory." She started walking. "And I can't teach you calibration in a week. Which means you're going to do this again, and every time you do, we lose levels I've budgeted for things that'll actually keep us alive."

She was already moving south, through the destroyed clearing and past the last of the downed agents, heading downslope toward denser cover where the pursuit would be slower and the terrain would work for them instead of against them. Kem followed. He was sore in ways he hadn't known he could be sore — the impact with the tree was asserting itself across his entire left side, a deep hot bruise that would darken overnight. His tongue throbbed where he'd bitten it. The pack was heavy on shoulders already raw from the straps.

But his body was alive.

He could not explain this and didn't try. The pain, the exhaustion, the cost — forty-one levels, forty-one irreversible, permanent subtractions from the total of what he was — all of it sat alongside something he had no framework for. His muscles had engaged with the world. His cardiovascular system had performed under stress. His hands had shaped force and sent it somewhere. For twenty-six years he'd occupied a body the way one occupies a waiting room — patiently, without investment, until the real thing happens elsewhere. And now, with dirt in his teeth and blood on his tongue and a cracked rib reporting with every breath, he understood that the body was not a waiting room. It was a thing you used, and using it — even badly, even wastefully, even with a Combat Pulse that cost fifteen levels instead of one — produced something the Vault had never offered: the specific, irreducible knowledge of being physically real.

He was terrified of what he'd done. The woman with the broken arm, the man who'd hit the tree, the tracker face-down in the thicket. He had done that. His hands, his power, his ignorance. People were hurt behind him who would not have been hurt if he'd been capable of precision, or of restraint, or of anything other than raw detonation.

And underneath the terror, in some part of himself that the Vault's philosophy had never reached, something was singing. Quietly. Without permission. A bodily joy he did not know how to refuse.

Vel was thirty paces ahead and not looking back. The destroyed forest fell behind them. Dust was settling on the collapsed hillside. Somewhere in the wreckage, seven enforcement agents were in various states of consciousness in a landscape that no longer resembled the one they'd tracked their quarry through, and somewhere to the north, the Hoarded Council was waiting for a report that would not arrive, and the absence of that report would tell them more than its contents ever could.

The pursuit was real. The Hoarded had sent people to bring him home, and those people had found him, and now they were lying in a demolished forest watching a collapsed hillside settle. The next group would be better. More trained, more authorized, more willing to spend. The cost of Kem's freedom was rising, measured in the levels the Council was willing to burn to deny him his.

He shifted the pack on his raw shoulders and kept walking south. His rib ached. The gash on his palm had crusted and pulled every time he flexed his hand. The Ledger hummed at 245 — a number that a week ago would have been meaningless to him and now sat in his awareness like a stone in a shoe, present with every step. He'd lost forty-one levels in four minutes of uncontrolled combat, and the woman walking ahead of him had spent three to accomplish the same thing, and the disparity between those numbers contained a lesson he could feel without yet being able to articulate.

But his hands still tingled. And the grin kept trying to come back, pushing up from somewhere below his ribs, and he had to keep telling his face to stop, and his face was not listening.
