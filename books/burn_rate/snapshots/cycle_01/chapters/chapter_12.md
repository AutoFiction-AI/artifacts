# Chapter 12

The soil was the color of chalk. Not clay-pale or drought-faded — chalk-pale, the specific exhaustion of ground that had been asked for everything it could give and then asked again. Vel had seen depleted farmland before, in settlements where Fertility Charms had been overworked for a generation. This was different in scale. The depletion went to the horizon, south and east and west, the fields marked by wooden stakes driven into ground too tired to hold them upright. Half the stakes leaned. Nobody had straightened them. Nobody had the levels to care about stakes.

They'd crossed the river two days ago. Since then the land had changed in stages — first the forest thinning, then the soil lightening, then the structures getting worse. Now there was no pretending they were anywhere but the Margins.

The heat didn't help. Early summer in the south was dry and heavy, the kind that pulled moisture from skin and pack straps and road alike. Dren's canteen was half-empty by midday and he hadn't mentioned it. Kem had stopped reaching for Sustenance Charms weeks ago — one of the economies Vel had hammered into him — but she could see his hand drift toward his belt pouch and stop, drift and stop, the old reflex surfacing and being caught. He drank water and kept walking. She gave him that much credit.

The first settlement appeared around noon: a cluster of low buildings arranged around a central well, the structures showing the seams of cheap Shaping work. Walls that met at angles that weren't quite right. Rooflines that sagged where the original builder had run out of levels mid-construction. The Shaping itself had been competent. The specifications hadn't cared about the people living under them.

A woman sat outside the nearest building on a stool, shelling dried beans into a wooden bowl. She looked up when they approached. Her eyes went to Kem first — his height, his hands, the boots that were battered now but still identifiably Vault-made — and her face arranged itself into something neutral and practiced, the expression of someone who'd learned that strangers with good boots didn't usually bring good news.

"Water?" Vel asked.

"Well's there." The woman nodded toward the cistern. "Thirty-two people drawing from it, so take what you need and move on."

"We're passing through," Vel said.

"That's what they all say."

Vel filled their canteens. Seven people were visible in the settlement. All of them moved with a specific economy she recognized — careful, minimum-effort, the motion of people who'd spent most of what they had and felt the cost of every gesture. She'd moved like that herself for years. The careful walk, the deliberate hands. Before Kem, before she'd had someone else's levels to budget alongside her own.

The notice board was by the well. Proper construction — Hoarded administrative hardwood with iron fittings, the kind of thing a district overseer would have had installed. The notices on it were layered deep, older ones buried under newer ones, the whole thing a geological record of bureaucratic attention paid to a place that had never received any other kind.

The top notice was current. Hoarded administrative stock, cream-colored paper:

NOTICE OF PRODUCTIVITY RECLASSIFICATION — MARGINS DISTRICT 7, SECTOR 14-B

Pursuant to Directive 1172 (amended), all residents of the above-designated sector are hereby classified under the revised Productivity Tier system:

TIER ONE (Levels 40+): Full daily output expected. 0.3 levels-per-annum standard rate. Interest on existing ability-debt accrues per schedule (Appendix C-7, Table 3, subsection ii).

TIER TWO (Levels 15–39): Reduced output. 0.15 levels-per-annum. Supplementary manual labor allocation may be assigned. Interest rate adjustment per Formula 9-B (revised).

TIER THREE (Levels 5–14): Maintenance category. Residents classified as Renewable Expenditure Units and may be reassigned to Development Opportunity programs at District Overseer discretion.

TIER FOUR (Levels 1–4): Non-productive. Account closure processing per Standard Protocol 6.

APPEALS: Reclassification appeals may be submitted to the Bureau of Expenditure Oversight, Northern Administrative Center, during standard business hours (first and third Wedgeday of each month, 10:00–14:00). Filing fee: 2 levels.

Vel read it twice. The first time as language, the second as math.

"Renewable Expenditure Units," Kem said. He was reading over her shoulder. His voice had that careful, too-even quality it took on when something from the world outside the Vault didn't fit any framework he'd brought from home. "That means people."

"Renewable because they have children," Vel said. "Children are born at three hundred."

Kem's hand closed on the edge of the board. "And the filing fee. Two levels. If you're Tier Four, you've got one to four levels left and the fee to appeal your classification is —"

"Half of everything you have."

He stared at the notice. She could see him working the rest of the math — the interest formulas in Appendix C that no one here could read, the daily output quotas, the five-year Development Opportunity programs that reduced your debt by two percent per year so that after thirty years of labor you'd still owe more than forty percent of the original amount. The system wasn't designed to pay off. The system was designed to run.

Dren hadn't stopped at the board. He was standing thirty feet down the road, watching south. His bad arm hung slightly forward, the way he carried it now — Kem's stitches had held but the flesh underneath had healed tight and wrong, and Dren protected the arm like a thing he couldn't afford to break a second time.

"We moving?" he said.

Vel pulled the notice from the board. It tore along a nail hole. Underneath was an older one, its ink fading to brown:

DEVELOPMENT OPPORTUNITY PROGRAM — COHORT 47
Duration: 5-year rotating assignment (renewable)
Daily ability expenditure requirement: Per Tier classification
Compensation: Room, board, and 2% annual reduction in outstanding ability-debt balance

She folded both notices and put them in her pack. She didn't have a reason. She took them anyway.

***

They passed two more settlements by mid-afternoon. Each showed the same marks: cheap Shaping, depleted soil, careful people. The notice boards varied in age but not in content — Productivity Tiers, Expenditure classifications, debt schedules referencing appendices nobody here had ever seen. In one settlement, someone had written across the bottom of a notice in charcoal: *formula doesn't balance — asked district office — they said resubmit next quarter*. The handwriting was shaky. Tier Three, Vel guessed. Or Four.

In the third settlement, they found the labor operation.

It was visible from a quarter mile: a compound surrounded by a wall of Shaped stone, about eight feet high, the Shaping layered thick enough that the stone had that fused, seamless look of serious ability investment. Inside, the tops of low buildings were visible, and people moving between them. Workers. They came and went through a single gate that a woman in her fifties controlled.

The enforcer sat on a chair by the gate with a ledger book in her lap and a clay cup on a crate beside her. When workers passed through, she checked names. When they came back in, she checked again.

"Debt work," Dren said. He'd seen it before. "Hoarded contract. Workers spend their daily quota, she marks it, end of the season the district office processes the reduction. In theory."

"In theory," Vel said.

"In theory."

A child sat against the compound's outer wall, in its shade. Maybe eight years old, small for the age, with the wrists of someone who'd never had enough of anything. His face was flushed — not sun-flushed but fever-flushed, the glassy heat of infection. An older girl sat beside him, twelve or thirteen, with the watchful, used-up face of someone who'd been responsible for too long. She had her hand on the boy's forehead. She'd been sitting like that for a while. You could see it in the set of her back.

Kem saw the child at the same time Vel did. She watched his stride change — the slight hitch, the redirection, his body deciding before his mind had caught up.

"Don't," Vel said. Quietly. To herself or to him — she wasn't certain which.

***

Kem knelt beside the child. The girl pulled her brother closer.

"He's got a fever," Kem said.

"I know he's got a fever." The girl looked at Kem's boots, his hands, the remains of Vault that he still carried. "He's had it three days."

"Three days is too long. Has anyone —"

"Has anyone what? Healed him?" She said it the way you'd say *has anyone flown him to the moon*. "With what?"

Kem looked at Vel. She knew what he was asking. She also knew what the answer cost — not to the boy, but to the cascade, the mechanism, the plan she'd spent eight years building. She didn't say yes. She didn't say no.

He put his hands on the child's forehead and closed his eyes. The Curative ability came as a clean pulse of warmth that Vel could feel from six feet away, like a door opening into a heated room. Kem's calibration had improved — this was targeted, precise, the fever and infection only. Eight levels. Not twenty. Not forty. The boy's face lost its feverish tightness and relaxed into something that looked like ordinary sleep.

The girl stared at Kem. She touched her brother's forehead, then his cheek. Her hand was shaking. "How many was that?"

"Eight," Kem said. He stood up, and Vel could see the change — slight, the way spending always registered as a simultaneous lightening and heaviness. He was at 140.

"Thank you," the girl said. It didn't sound like thank you. It sounded like something much harder — gratitude pressed up against the fury of having needed a stranger's charity in the first place.

Vel did the math. Her cascade projection assumed Kem arriving at the Scour with approximately 90 levels — enough for a sustained descent to stabilize the threshold alongside her own. He was ten below budget. She could adjust. She'd adjusted before.

Then Kem walked toward the compound gate.

"What is this?" he asked the enforcer.

The enforcer looked up from her ledger. "Labor compound. Contract work under District authority."

"And the wall?"

"Compound perimeter. Workers stay on-site during assigned shift. Per regulation."

"How long's a shift?"

"Sunrise to sunset. Per regulation."

"And if someone needs to leave? If their kid's sick?"

The enforcer's face didn't change. She was practiced at this. "Shift completion is required before exit authorization. Per regulation."

Kem looked at the wall. Vel saw his hand flex at his side — the reflex that preceded every ability use. He'd done it before the river, before every fight, before every decision to spend.

"Kem," she said.

He didn't look at her. He put both hands flat against the Shaped stone and pushed.

The Disruption came as a sustained wave — not a single blast but a modulated pressure, weeks of Vel's teaching translating into something close to control. The Shaping cracked along its seams first, the fused layers separating with a sound like ice breaking on a river. Then the whole section — fifteen feet of wall, the gate, its mechanism — dissolved into rubble and pale dust. The enforcer fell off her chair. Workers on the other side stood blinking in the sudden daylight where the wall had been.

Twenty levels. He was at 120.

Vel's cascade math was now deeply, specifically wrong.

"You can leave," Kem told the workers. Some were already moving. Others stood where they were, staring at the gap as if it might rebuild itself. One woman picked up a tool she'd been using and set it down again, carefully, in the exact spot where it had been, as if that mattered. Then she walked through the opening without looking back.

The enforcer got up from the dust, ledger book clutched to her chest. "You've destroyed Hoarded-contracted infrastructure. There'll be — the district office will —"

"Send the invoice to House Aldraste," Kem said.

Vel did not laugh. She wanted to. She was also watching the number 120 settle into her calculations like a stone dropped into water, the ripples spreading through every projection she'd built.

The backup arrived within the hour. Three enforcers from a larger compound to the south — two men and a woman, all in the fifty-to-seventy range by their movement, all with the hard-use build of people who'd spent careers on enforcement rather than labor. They assessed the rubble, the dispersing workers, and Kem sitting on a chunk of fallen wall drinking water, and drew the correct conclusions.

The first one opened with a Combat Pulse aimed at Kem's chest. Kem threw up a shield that caught it — the detonation punched the air and blew dust in a circle around him, and Vel felt the shield's cost like a flinch: four levels, maybe five. The second enforcer was already flanking left, and Kem read it — a Perception flash, two levels, his head snapping toward the movement before the man had committed. The third enforcer hung back, gauging.

Then all three pressed at once. Kem's defensive barrier was rough but solid, a sustained field that held while the enforcers hammered it with everything they had. The air around the barrier rippled with spent energy. Nine levels to hold it. Vel counted them in the pressure fluctuations.

He dropped the barrier and hit the first enforcer with a targeted Force Strike before the man could reset — three levels, clean, the enforcer going down hard and staying down. The second caught a Strike to the shoulder — three more levels, and the man's arm went slack. The third enforcer looked at her two colleagues on the ground and at Kem, who was breathing hard and bleeding from a gash on his forearm where a glancing pulse had opened the skin, and she turned and ran.

Thirty-five levels in combat. He was at 85.

Kem sat down in the rubble. He tore a strip from the hem of his shirt and wrapped his forearm, the blood soaking through immediately — a dark, spreading stain. Two months ago he would have healed the cut with an ability and not thought about the cost. Now he tied the cloth tight with his teeth and flexed the hand to check the tendons. Field triage. Vel had taught him that, in the first week, kneeling over a scraped knee he'd wanted to burn three levels on.

A worker came back through the gap in the wall. An old man, sixty or seventy, stooped and slow. He walked to the enforcer's chair, which was lying on its side in the dust, and he righted it. Then he sat down in it. He didn't do anything else. He just sat there, in the enforcer's chair, and looked out at the road where the enforcers had gone.

Nobody said anything about it. Vel watched the man sit in the chair and thought about power and what it looked like when it shifted — not in a grand way, not in a way anyone would write down, but in a sixty-year-old man sitting in a chair he hadn't been allowed to sit in that morning.

Vel stood at the edge of the rubble and did the arithmetic. Her cascade projection needed Kem at 90. He was at 85. She was five levels below the minimum she'd calculated, and Kem was wrapping his arm with torn cloth and wearing the expression of someone who wasn't finished.

She needed to tell him. She needed to say: *every level you spend on these people is a level we need at the Scour.* She needed to explain that the cascade mechanism wasn't elastic, that the threshold required a minimum sustained descent, that his charity was consuming the only resource that stood between them and failure.

She opened her mouth.

And closed it. Because she looked at the workers walking through the gap in the wall. At the girl sitting with her sleeping brother, his fever broken. At the enforcer's ledger lying open in the rubble, its columns of names and daily ability-debt calculations baking in the sun.

Stop spending on people who need help. That was what the Hoarded said. Preserve. Husband your resources. Let the expendable be expended. She'd built her entire life in opposition to that sentence. She couldn't say it now because the person she'd need to become to say it was the person she'd spent forty years refusing to be.

Kem spent the rest of the day.

The collapsed irrigation channel came first: a section that served four nearby settlements, built by Hoarded contractors twenty years ago and left to rot when the contract expired. Kem knelt in the dry ditch and poured sustained Shaping energy into packed earth and stone, rebuilding the channel with the blunt, functional craftsmanship of someone who'd learned by breaking things for weeks before trying to build them. It wasn't elegant. When upstream water began flowing through for the first time in what the settlement people said was three years, a woman waded into the ditch and stood with the water running over her ankles, touching the rebuilt wall of the channel with her palm. She stood there for a long time. She didn't say anything to Kem. She didn't need to.

Fifteen levels. He was at 70.

Then the smaller things, which weren't smaller to the people who needed them. A man with a broken finger he'd been splinting with sticks for two weeks — three levels of healing, twice what a competent healer would charge but half what it would have cost Kem a month ago. A wall about to collapse onto a house where four people slept — five levels of structural reinforcement, rough but solid. A woman whose vision had been clouding for years, cataracts thickening because nobody in the Margins could afford the Ocular ability. Kem hesitated on that one. He'd never done it before. He did it anyway — six levels, and it wasn't perfect, the woman said she could see shapes now and colors and her daughter's face but the edges were still soft. Kem told her he was sorry it wasn't better. She said he shouldn't be sorry. She said *shapes and colors* like she was holding something she'd expected never to hold again.

A child's infected tooth: two levels. The child cried during the healing — not from pain but from the strangeness of an ability being used on her for the first time in her life, the warm invasive pulse of someone else's energy moving through her jaw. Her father held her and looked at Kem the way the girl with the feverish brother had looked at him — that complicated expression, gratitude and anger occupying the same face.

A man's chronic pain from a years-old injury that had healed crooked and wrong: four levels. Kem asked the man where it hurt. The man showed him — a hip, twisted in a fall from a half-collapsed structure, the kind of workplace accident that would have been healed in ten minutes in the Spent Towns and in thirty seconds in the Vault and that here, in the Margins, had become four years of limping. Kem fixed what he could. The man walked three steps and stopped and walked three more, testing, the way someone tests ice before committing their weight.

Twenty levels in small interventions. He was at 50.

Vel spent one of her own — a Perception sweep, mapping the local terrain and the road south, filing landmarks and distances. A level she could barely afford, but she worked better when she could count things. Nineteen to eighteen.

Dren had been quiet all day. He'd watched Kem heal the child and said nothing. He'd watched the wall come down and said nothing. When Kem was rebuilding the irrigation channel, Dren had walked into the ditch and started carrying rubble out of the way — manually, his bad arm tucked against his body, his good arm doing the lifting. He didn't ask. He just worked.

Now he sat on a stone at the edge of the repaired channel, watching the water run. His face had the specific look of a man doing arithmetic he didn't want to finish. He was at 130. The people around him were at 4, at 8, at 15. The distance between Dren and the floor of the Margins was 126 levels. A month ago that would have felt like wealth. Sitting here, watching a sick boy sleep because a stranger had spent eight levels on him and watching a woman wade in running water because a stranger had spent fifteen on a ditch, the number 130 looked different. It looked like the distance between his daughter and the people in this settlement — a distance measured not in levels but in the presence or absence of someone willing to spend.

***

They found Fallow an hour before dark, at the edge of the furthest settlement.

Fallow's structure was smaller than the others and better maintained, in the particular way of someone who had almost nothing and kept it precise. A patched roof. A swept floor visible through the open door. A wooden chair with one leg shorter than the others and a folded cloth wedged under it, the cloth re-folded and repositioned enough times that it had worn thin in the middle. Outside, a garden held three rows of something stubborn growing in the chalky soil — each plant staked with a twig and tied with strips of rag, the rows dead-straight, the spacing exact. The garden hadn't produced much. But it was tended with a discipline that put most of the Hoarded to shame.

Fallow was sitting in the chair when they arrived. Level 4. Vel didn't need a Perception ability to tell — at that level, the conservation was visible in everything. The way Fallow turned their head to look at the visitors, the rotation itself measured. The way their hands rested on their knees without fidgeting, because fidgeting was wasted motion.

Sixty years old. Gaunt past their years. Skin creased deep by three decades of field sun. Hands that were mostly tendon and callus, the fingers permanently curved from tools they'd held so long the shape had become structural. Fallow wore a shirt mended in seven visible places, each repair in a different-colored thread — brown, white, blue, red, green, yellow, brown again — as if the available thread changed with the years. The effect was almost festive, in a way Fallow clearly didn't intend or notice.

"Sit," Fallow said. There was one chair and they didn't gesture at it. "Ground or wall. Chair's mine."

They sat. Dren leaned in the doorframe.

"Dren said you know the routes south," Vel said.

"I know the routes everywhere around here. I've walked most of them at one point or another." Fallow's eyes moved from Vel to Kem. They lingered the way everyone in the Margins lingered on Kem — assessing, categorizing, placing him in the ledger of who had what and who didn't. "You're the one who broke Danner's wall."

"Danner?"

"The enforcer. She's been running that compound six years. Longest operation in this district." Fallow said it without judgment. The wall was a fact. Its absence was another fact. "She'll file a reconstruction request with the district office. Takes three weeks to process. Four more for a Shaper to come down. Your people get maybe seven weeks before it's back up."

Vel felt that land. Kem had spent twenty levels and bought seven weeks.

"What were you before this?" Kem asked. He was leaning forward, the way he did when he was genuinely working through something — not performing interest, just present.

"I was at a hundred and forty when they put me in the fields. Development Opportunity, they called it." Fallow spread their hands on their knees. The calluses had their own geography — ridges and valleys from decades of tools and soil. "Thirty years of development." They looked at their hands the way someone might look at a receipt for something they hadn't ordered. "Very developed. Look at me."

The line was flat. Not delivered for effect — offered with the weary timing of something said too many times to too few people who could change anything. The comedy was in the distance between the Hoarded's language and the body in the chair. Development Opportunity. A hundred and forty to four.

Kem was quiet for a long time. Vel watched him let go of the impulse to categorize, to respond, to make it mean something. He just sat with it.

"My mother grows the same thing," Dren said. He was looking at the garden. "In her yard back home. Those're ash-beans, right? Alkaline soil?"

Fallow glanced at him. Something shifted in their face — faint, a flicker of the person they'd been before thirty years of development had compressed them down to facts and silence. "They don't produce much."

"Hers either. She says they're stubborn enough to be worth the effort."

"Yeah. They're worth it." Fallow turned back to Vel. The flicker was gone. "You want the south routes."

"I want to know about the Scour," Vel said. She kept her voice even.

"Three days south. The land drops into a basin — big, all rock and wind and nothing growing. Old mining territory. The Hoarded ran Shaping crews down there sixty, seventy years back. Stripped it and left."

"What happens there?"

Fallow was quiet for a moment. Not for drama — the silence of someone conserving the energy of speech. "People go to zero. It isn't common. Couple times a generation, maybe. Someone's so far down there's nothing left, and they end up at the Scour because there's nowhere lower to go. And they hit zero and they're gone."

"Gone," Kem said.

"Not dead. Not a body on the ground." Fallow's voice didn't change register. They could have been describing rain. "They're standing there and then they're not. I've seen it twice. First time was a woman named Sera. She'd been at two for months, just barely holding on. She walked south one morning. I was on a field crew near the basin's edge and saw her from a distance. She was standing on the rock floor, and then she wasn't. Like she stepped through something I couldn't see."

"And the second?"

"Man called Hoster. That was maybe eight years ago." Fallow rubbed the side of their jaw — a gesture that looked habitual, something to do with their hands when a memory required effort. "Same thing. He walked down, stood on the rock, and he was gone. There was a flash of something — light coming through a gap, like a door closing. I was further away that time. Could've been sun on the stone. Wasn't, though."

Eight years ago. Vel's chest did something complicated — not a lurch, not a tightening, but a kind of settling, like a weight she'd been holding shifted to a position she could carry.

"Nobody talks about it," Fallow said. "What's the point? People at zero disappear. It's another thing that happens to people nobody protects. The Hoarded don't come down here to investigate. Margins people don't have the levels to care about mysteries." They looked at the garden through the open door. "Sera and Hoster are just names now. Another thing that happened."

Vel reached into her pack and pulled out Maren's route journal. The leather was soft from years of handling, the pages warped from humidity and sweat and the oils of hands that had held it across hundreds of miles. She opened it to the southern section — the Margins passages, the pages where Maren's handwriting was densest.

The annotations for this region were detailed. Maren had come through here. Vel traced the routes in handwriting she knew better than her own — distances between water sources, terrain notes, settlement names that might not exist anymore. On one page, Maren had drawn a rough map of the basin Fallow was describing. An oval, hash marks for the descent, a small x on the basin floor. Beside the x, in Maren's careful script: *here*.

"Route journal?" Fallow asked.

"Yeah."

"That handwriting's not yours."

"No."

Fallow watched Vel handle the pages. They didn't push. They turned to Kem instead. "You're the one at — what, fifty now? After today?"

"Fifty," Kem said.

"Going to zero on purpose." Not a question.

"Yeah."

"Hm." Fallow's mouth moved — not quite a smile, more the shape a face makes when it doesn't have the energy for the expression it wants. "That's new. Most people around here, zero's what happens when there's nothing left of you to take."

Vel closed the journal. She put it back in her pack with the care she applied to nothing else she owned — the leather settled against the folded notices from the board, and she fastened the buckle.

Three days south. She could feel the distance in her body, the way she felt every distance now — as cost, as arithmetic, as the narrowing space between what she needed and what she had.

***

They left Fallow's settlement in the failing light. Vel wanted hours — three days to the Scour, and every hour mattered now that her cascade math had come apart in her hands. They walked in the formation they'd developed over weeks: Vel reading the terrain ahead, Kem carrying the heavier pack because his body could take it, Dren at the rear watching behind.

The road was empty. A footbridge over a dry creek bed was about to give — Vel reinforced it with a Structural ability, one level, the wood stiffening under their weight. Eighteen now. The cost barely registered against the wreck of everything else.

They walked in silence for a while. The pale soil cooled under their feet as the light faded. Somewhere to the west, a settlement's cooking fires put a thin haze against the sky.

Dren caught up and walked beside Vel. This was unusual. He kept rear guard and didn't seek company unless he had logistics to report.

"My agreement with you was to the edge of the Margins," he said. "We're past that."

"Yeah."

"Payment was for guide work. Routes, getting you through the Spent Towns and pointed south. That's done."

"Yeah, it's done."

He was quiet for another fifty paces. Vel could hear his breathing — steady, a walker's breath, but with a hitch on the left side where his bad arm moved against his ribs.

"I'm not leaving yet," he said.

Vel looked at him. His face was set in the dying light — not resolved exactly, but committed, like a man who'd finished a calculation and didn't like the answer but wasn't going to run it again.

"I'm not asking for more payment," he said. "I'm just — I'm staying on."

"Okay."

"Don't ask me why."

"I won't."

They walked. Behind them, Kem was a dark shape against the darkening road. Vel didn't look back. She could feel his depleted Ledger in the space between them like a change in air pressure — 50 where there had been 148 this morning, 98 levels poured into strangers and structures and suffering that hadn't been part of her plan.

Her plan. She'd built it over eight years, and it had assumed a version of this journey where the human cost of the Ledger system stayed at a distance — a political fact, a moral position, something she could manage alongside the cascade projections. The Margins were not at a distance. The Margins were a girl who couldn't afford to save her brother, and a wall built to keep people productive, and a sixty-year-old at level 4 tending three rows of ash-beans in soil that had nothing left to give.

She'd spent her life treating spending as the brave choice. The right thing. Jump in. Heal the sick. Break what needs breaking. The Hoarded hoarded and Vel spent and that had been the axis her whole life turned on.

But the people here hadn't chosen to spend. They'd been spent. The system had extracted from them the way it extracted from the soil — on schedule, under administrative oversight, with appendices. Fallow hadn't spent bravely. Fallow had been spent productively, for thirty years, under a program the Hoarded called a Development Opportunity.

Vel adjusted her pack. The route journal pressed against her spine through the fabric, Maren's handwriting a weight she carried the way she carried everything Maren had left behind.

Kem's footsteps were steady behind her. Fifty levels. She couldn't fix the math by counting, and she couldn't tell him the math was broken, and she couldn't tell him to stop because stopping was the thing she'd sworn she'd never do.

The Margins stretched ahead of them in the dark. Three days to the Scour. Somewhere south, in a basin of wind-scoured rock, people disappeared when they hit zero — not dead, not gone, but elsewhere. Vel had watched it happen once. Eight years ago. A flash of light like a door closing. Maren there and then not there.

She walked south, and the arithmetic walked with her.
