# Chapter 16

Dren was packing before sunrise.

Vel heard him through the thin wall of the abandoned waystation — the sound of a bedroll being tied wrong, retied, tied wrong again. She lay still and counted the sounds. Buckle. Strap. A muffled curse when the leather caught on something. She knew what she was listening to. She'd been expecting it since the Margins farmhouse, since Sable's warning, since Dren had started looking south with the specific frown of a man calculating days against a child's illness.

She got up and walked outside. The Margins dawn was thin and colorless — summer heat already pressing down before the light had properly arrived, the depleted soil giving off a chalky smell that belonged to no season she'd ever learned. The waystation had been a Hoarded supply post once. You could tell by the stamped signage on the doorframe: PRODUCTIVITY TRANSFER STATION 7-SOUTH, the letters faded to ghosts. Someone had scratched *fuck off* into the wood beneath them in a careful hand, the letters neatly spaced, as if the vandalism were its own form of compliance.

Dren came out a few minutes later. He carried his pack low on his left shoulder because his right arm wouldn't take the weight. The wound from the silo shrapnel had healed into a thick, shiny seam that pulled when he lifted anything above his chest — Kem's stitching from weeks ago, amateur work on a real injury, the kind of healing that held the parts together without restoring the function. Dren had stopped mentioning it around the time they entered the deep Margins. He'd just shifted his grip, his carry, his sleep position, the way working people adjust around damage without discussing it.

"Morning," he said.

"Yeah."

He set his pack down by the crumbling foundation wall and looked at it. Then he looked at her. The look had weight in it — the weight of someone about to say something he'd been composing for days and still didn't have right.

"I'm heading back," Dren said.

Vel nodded. She'd known, but the words still changed the air between them. "When?"

"Now." He rubbed his bad arm with his good hand, the gesture automatic and unconscious, the way you touch a thing that hurts to check whether it still hurts. "Should've gone a week ago, if I'm honest. Two weeks ago. I kept — I don't know. I kept finding reasons."

"You don't owe us an explanation."

"I know I don't." He sat down on the foundation wall. The stone was warm already, the Margins holding yesterday's heat in its bones. "I'm going to give you one anyway because I'd rather say it than carry it home."

Vel leaned against the doorframe and waited. She could hear Kem stirring inside the waystation — the creak of a body that had gotten thinner over six weeks of hard travel, joints that protested the floor more than they used to. Kem at eighteen. She was at sixteen. Numbers that would have been a punchline in the Spent Towns.

"My daughter's sick," Dren said. He looked at his hands. "You know that. But I don't think you know what sick means when you're at a hundred and twenty and the healer in Roth charges forty for the work she needs. That's a third of me, Vel. A third of everything I've got left." He picked at a callus on his palm, the motion small and repetitive. "And I've been out here six weeks watching you two burn toward nothing, and I keep thinking — she doesn't care about thresholds. She doesn't know what a cascade is. She's nine years old and her lungs sound like paper and she's waiting for her father to come home with enough levels to fix it."

"I know."

"You know the number. You don't know what it sounds like when she breathes."

Vel didn't argue. He was right. She knew the arithmetic of Dren's life the way she knew any cost structure — precisely, functionally, without the sound of labored breathing at three in the morning in a room she'd never seen.

Dren was quiet for a moment. A thin wind came across the depleted flats, carrying dust that tasted like chalk and old Fertility residue. Three hundred meters south, the road continued through what had been farmland once — the rows still visible, ghost-lines in exhausted earth. Everything here had been used and left.

"I watched the kid spend ninety-eight levels on strangers," Dren said. "In the Margins. People he'd never met. And I watched you count every one of those levels and not stop him, because you needed them for your thing but you couldn't say that out loud, not in front of people the system already ate." He looked up at her. "I'm not judging. I'm telling you what it looks like from where I'm sitting. Two people burning everything they've got on a door that might not even open, while the world's full of doors that'd open fine if someone had the levels."

"That's a fair description."

"It's a shit description. Doesn't cover any of it." He scratched his jaw. His beard had come in patchy and sun-bleached over the weeks — a trader's face that had been outdoors in the wrong latitudes. "My girl's my zero, Vel. That's the place I'm trying to reach. Same thing you're doing. I just — my threshold's a healer's table in Roth and my cascade's forty levels I can barely spare."

Something in Vel's chest went tight in a way she didn't want to examine. Not her ribs — those had healed badly weeks ago and she'd stopped noticing — but something under them, behind the breastbone, where grief lived when you didn't let it surface. She looked at the ghost-lettered signage on the doorframe instead of at Dren's face.

"You've been good company," she said. The words were inadequate and she knew it.

"I've been paid company," Dren said. Then, quieter: "Some of it was paid. The last bit wasn't." He stood up and shouldered his pack, wincing when the strap settled on his bad arm. He didn't adjust it. He left it where it hurt. "Take care of the kid. He's worth more than his numbers."

"I know that."

"Do you?" The question was direct and without malice, which made it worse. Dren met her eyes and held them. "Because from where I've been standing, you know he's useful. That's not the same thing."

Vel didn't answer. The wind picked up the chalk-dust and threw it sideways.

Kem came out of the waystation, blinking in the raw light. His face was thinner than it had been at the start — the softness of Vault living stripped away by weeks of walking, the bones showing through. He'd gotten quieter since the fracture. Not Hoarded-quiet, the careful silence of people preserving themselves, but a different kind: the quiet of someone deciding, day by day, whether to keep going.

"Dren."

"Yeah." Dren looked at him. "Heading home."

Kem stood in the doorway and Vel watched him process it — the slight tension at the jaw, the way his hands hung at his sides without reaching for anything. He'd learned that from her, she realized. The economy of gesture. Not wasting movement. She didn't know how to feel about that.

"Your arm," Kem said.

"Still on. That's your doing." Dren flexed it, grimaced, and let it fall. "Worst field work I've ever received and I'd take it again."

"I could —" Kem stopped. They both knew he couldn't. A healing ability at his level would cost more than either of them could afford, and the old Kem who threw forty-one levels at a hillside was someone they'd left behind in the forested highlands, weeks and a lifetime ago.

"Don't." Dren said it gently. "You've got eighteen levels and a long walk ahead. Spend them on something that matters."

"Your arm matters."

"My arm works. It just hurts." Dren shrugged with his good shoulder. "I've had worse days on the trade routes. Pulled a cart with a broken wrist once because the mule died outside Garren and I had a delivery that'd feed Mira for a month." He paused, looking at Kem with an expression Vel couldn't quite read — something between assessment and affection, the look of a man who has watched a boy become something else and isn't sure the boy knows it yet. "You spend different now. You know that? In the Spent Towns you were throwing levels around like a drunk lord at a festival. Now you count. That's — that's something."

Kem didn't say anything. He put out his hand. Dren looked at it — the hand of a former Hoarded aristocrat, the soft skin long gone, the knuckles scraped, the calluses new and poorly placed, the hand of someone who'd learned to grip and carry and climb in six weeks of desperate education. Dren took it. They shook once, firm, and Dren let go.

"Tell Mira —" Kem started, and didn't finish.

"Yeah," Dren said. He understood what Kem couldn't articulate, or he chose not to make him try, which amounted to the same thing.

He shifted the pack once, nodded at Vel, and turned north. His boots crunched on the Margins road — that specific sound of feet on depleted ground, a thin gritty compression that was nothing like forest soil or Spent Town cobblestone. Vel watched his back until the dust haze took it. He didn't look back. People who were going home to sick children didn't look back.

The waystation was quieter after that. Two packs where there had been three. Two shadows on the road.

---

They walked for an hour without speaking. The road south was a Hoarded-built supply line, cracked and subsided where the earth had been hollowed by decades of Shaping-ability mining underneath. Vel mapped the fractures automatically — step left, avoid the depression, the subsidence pattern here suggested a gallery collapse thirty meters down. The old instincts, the body's knowledge, running underneath the silence like a river under stone.

Two packs felt wrong. The rhythm of three people walking — Dren's heavier tread, his occasional commentary on terrain quality, the way he'd hum tunelessly through boring stretches of road — had been background noise she'd stopped consciously hearing. Now its absence was loud. She kept catching herself looking right, where Dren had walked, and finding open air and depleted scrub. The landscape had the same problem: things removed leaving shapes you couldn't stop noticing.

They passed a Margins farmstead where someone was still trying. An old woman worked a hand plow through chalky soil, moving down rows that had been Fertility-Charmed to death decades ago and were now producing whatever chalk and subsoil and human stubbornness could manage. The woman didn't look up as they passed. She had the focused inwardness of someone doing something she knew wouldn't work and doing it anyway because the alternative was sitting still.

Kem stopped.

She didn't notice for three steps. Then the absence of his footfalls registered — a gap in the rhythm she'd been unconsciously tracking — and she turned.

He stood in the middle of the road. The sun was fully up now, pressing down on the landscape with the flat weight of deep summer. His shadow was small beneath him. He looked like what he was: a twenty-six-year-old at eighteen levels, burned down from three hundred, standing in the wreckage of a system he'd been born to benefit from.

"No," he said.

"No what?"

"No more steps." He didn't raise his voice. The old Kem would have. The old Kem filled silence with words when he was scared, talked fast, asked questions. This Kem stood still and said the thing once. "Not until you tell me why."

"I told you the mechanism. The cascade —"

"Not the mechanism. You." He took a breath that cost him something. "Why you, specifically, Vel. Why you've been walking toward zero for eight years. Why you carry that journal like it's the last thing in the world that matters. Why Thessa looked at you like you'd killed someone. What happened to make you — this."

The road was silent. No birdsong in the Margins — the trees here were scrub, half-dead things that had survived generations of Fertility depletion by learning to need nothing. Vel could feel the Ledger at the edge of her awareness, that low hum of remaining potential: sixteen. Sixteen things she could still do. Every one of them a door closing behind her.

She could deflect. She'd been deflecting for thirty years — answering the smaller question precisely enough that people didn't notice she'd skipped the real one. She could tell him about the cascade's energy requirements, the geographic specificity, the timing variables. She could bury the answer in technical detail until his urgency ran out.

She looked at his face. The sun was in his eyes and he didn't squint. He just waited.

"Okay," she said. "Sit down."

There was a stretch of collapsed wall along the roadside — the foundation of something that had been a storage facility, or a bunkhouse, or one of the Productivity Transfer Stations the Hoarded had littered across the Margins like bureaucratic droppings. They sat on it. The stone was hot through her clothes. Vel pulled Maren's route journal from the inside pocket of her pack and held it in her lap. The leather was soft from years of handling, the corners worn smooth, the binding repaired twice with different thread.

"Her name was Maren," Vel said. And then she stopped, because the name in her mouth did something to her breathing that she hadn't been prepared for, and she needed a moment before the next part.

Kem waited. He sat with his hands on his knees and he waited.

"We spent together," Vel said. "Eleven years. We ran the deep routes — Margins territory, places the Hoarded had used up and moved on from. Healing jobs, infrastructure, the kind of work nobody pays for because nobody with levels goes there voluntarily. She was —" Vel looked down at the journal. Maren's handwriting on the edges of the pages, the cramped annotations in the margins, the route numbers she'd invented for paths that had no official designation. "She was better at it than me. Faster. Braver. She'd walk into a settlement with nothing but her Ledger and a bag of tools and people would — they'd just let her in. I had to work for that. She just had it."

The wind moved the scrub brush along the roadside. Small, dry sounds. The landscape doing its minimum.

"We'd split healing jobs. She'd take the harder cases because she had the touch for it — steady, warm, she could talk someone through the pain while she was spending on them. I did the structural stuff, the field repair, the boring work." Vel turned a page of the journal without looking at it, the gesture habitual, a thing her hands did when she was thinking about Maren. "She kept this journal. All the routes. Where the settlements were, what they needed, which ones had clean water. She drew maps. They were terrible maps, but they worked." A pause. "We were good at it. At the work. At each other. I don't know how to separate those things."

"You don't have to," Kem said.

Vel looked at him. She hadn't expected that — a sentence that wasn't a question, that wasn't pushing for more, that just offered a flat surface for the next thing to rest on. She took a breath.

"She hit zero first," Vel said. "Eight years ago. It wasn't planned. We were in a fight — not combat, a building collapse, old Shaping-mining damage in a settlement south of here. She was at three. She used a Stabilization on a family's house. That took her to one. Then a piece of ceiling came down on a kid and she threw a Barrier. Half a level, maybe less. But it took the last of it."

Vel's hands were steady on the journal. She noticed that with the detached interest of someone watching their own body perform a competence they didn't feel. Steady hands. Interesting.

"The threshold opened. I saw it. I was six meters from her. She was there and then she was — somewhere else. Not gone. I could see it, for a second. Maybe two seconds. A place. Real place. Buildings. Light. People. She looked back at me." Vel stopped again. The stone was hot under her legs and the sun pressed on the back of her neck and the memory had a specific quality she could not render in words: the visual fact of Maren's face turning toward her from inside an impossible doorway, and the doorway closing, and the air where it had been being just air again.

"Then it closed," Vel said. "And she wasn't anywhere."

She realized her thumb was tracing the spine of the journal, back and forth, the leather worn to a groove where she'd done this before. Hundreds of times, probably. Thousands. In camp, on the road, in settlements, in the dark. The groove her thumb had made was its own kind of record — the physical evidence of eight years of touching the last thing Maren had touched.

Kem was quiet for a long time. Long enough that Vel could hear her own pulse in her ears, which she hated, because it meant the body was doing things she hadn't authorized.

"That was eight years ago," he said.

"Yes."

"And you've been —"

"Yes."

"The whole time. The journal. The routes. Looking for a way to —"

"Yes." She cut him off because hearing him assemble it was worse than saying it herself. "I was at sixty-two when she crossed. I spent thirty-one levels in eight years working out the mechanism, looking for a co-spender, mapping the route. I found the cascade theory in a Margins settlement two years after she crossed — an old woman who'd seen three people vanish over her lifetime and had figured out the proximity effect. I tested it with small spends near a secondary site. And I looked for someone with the levels to make it work."

"Someone with the levels," Kem repeated. His voice was flat. Not angry — or not only angry. He was hearing the thing he already knew but in a new register, the personal register, the one where Vel's manipulation wasn't just tactical but desperate.

"I found you because I needed you," Vel said. "That's true and I'm not going to dress it up. I heard about a Hoarded lord who walked out of the Vault and I went north and I looked at you and I saw two hundred and eighty-six levels and a willingness to burn. I saw a cascade partner. That's what you were to me, at the start."

"At the start."

"Don't make me qualify every sentence, Kem. You know what I mean."

"I don't, actually." He picked a piece of mortar from the collapsed wall and turned it in his fingers. A small, aimless motion. "I know what you've told me, which is always exactly enough to keep me moving. You told me about the threshold when I needed to know. You told me about the cascade when you couldn't hide it anymore. And now Dren's gone and you're telling me about —" He set the mortar fragment down. "About a woman you loved. Because there's nothing left to lose by telling me."

"That's not why."

"Then why now?"

"Because you asked." She looked at him. "Because you stopped walking and asked."

"I've been asking for weeks, Vel. After Thessa. After the mechanism. I've been asking with every silent fucking mile since the farmhouse."

"Asking isn't the same as stopping." Vel turned the journal in her hands. The leather was body-warm. "You were still moving. I could still not answer."

He absorbed that. She watched him do it — the slight shift in his posture, the way his jaw worked on something he chose not to say. He'd gotten better at that. At the start he'd said everything that occurred to him, a river of curiosity and anxiety and observation. Now he held things back. She didn't know if that was growth or damage.

"Tell me the rest," he said.

"The rest is the part that doesn't make me look good."

"None of this makes you look good, Vel."

Fair. She looked at the depleted landscape — the ghost-rows in the soil, the half-dead scrub, the remains of infrastructure that had been built to extract and never to sustain. A Productivity Tier classification sign was still bolted to a post fifty meters south, the text weather-stripped to fragments: RENEWABLE... UNIT... ANNUAL OUTPUT. The Margins' wallpaper. The bureaucratic language of a system that had used people as fuel and documented it with the dispassion of inventory management.

"I'm doing the same thing," Vel said. The words came out flatter than she'd intended, which meant they came out honest. "You said it in the Margins, after the mechanism. That I looked at you the way they look at everyone here. Like a resource." She touched the journal's cover. "You weren't wrong. The Margins make it worse because the pattern's the same — someone with power needing something from someone without it, wrapping the need in a reason that sounds good enough to justify the taking. The Hoarded call it Development Opportunities. I called it saving you the trouble of dying for nothing."

"And the difference?"

"I don't know if there is one." That cost her. She could feel it in her chest — not the old rib injury, which was a known quantity, but the pressure of saying a true thing that she'd been structuring her entire justification around not saying. "The intent's different. I care about you. That's real. But the shape of the thing — finding someone, using their levels, guiding them toward the outcome I need — that's the shape of what they did here." She gestured at the Margins around them. "It doesn't matter that I had a better reason. The people who made Fallow work thirty years in the fields had reasons too."

Kem was very still. Not Hoarded still — not the performed immobility of the Conservatory. A different kind. The stillness of someone hearing something they'd been needing to hear and finding it worse and better than they'd expected.

"Maren wouldn't —" Vel started, and stopped. She opened the journal to a page near the back. The handwriting was smaller here, more urgent, the annotations spilling off the ruled margins into the page's edges. She didn't show it to Kem, but she looked at it while she spoke, the way you hold something solid when the ground shifts. "Maren would've hated what I did. How I did it. She spent for people because she saw them. I spent you because I saw her. Those aren't the same thing, and pretending they are is another kind of lie I've been telling myself."

She closed the journal.

"I'm not sorry I found you. I'm not sorry I used your levels. I'm sorry that I couldn't figure out how to need you without it being the same thing everyone else in this system does to everyone else."

Kem said nothing. The quiet stretched. Vel could hear the creak of the old signpost in the wind, the dry tick of scrub branches rubbing against each other, the sound of two people breathing in depleted air. She had said it. All of it, or as close to all of it as she was capable of. Eight years of reasons and justifications and night-arguments with a woman who wasn't there to argue back, compressed into sentences that felt smaller than the thing they contained. Grief always felt smaller out loud. The interior version had rooms and corridors and years. The spoken version was a story about a woman and a door.

The sun was high now. Their shadows had shrunk to almost nothing beneath them — two dark shapes pressed tight against the base of the wall like animals hiding from the heat. The Margins road stretched south, empty, the subsidence cracks running through it like the landscape's own form of depletion, the ground spending itself downward into old mine galleries nobody maintained.

"What if she's not there?" Kem said.

Vel opened her mouth and closed it. She had rehearsed answers to this question. In the dark, in the weeks since the fracture, lying on hard ground listening to Kem not speaking to her, she had built careful arguments about probability, about what she'd seen, about the cascade theory's reliability. She discarded all of them.

"Then I'll be at zero in a world without the Ledger," she said. "And that's still better than this."

Kem looked at her. The sun was making his eyes water, or something else was, and he didn't wipe it away. She held his gaze because she owed him that — the willingness to be seen while she was most exposed.

"Okay," he said.

"Okay what."

"Okay I'll keep walking." He stood up from the wall. His knees cracked — the sound of a young body that had been used harder than it was built for. "Not because it's fine. It isn't fine. You used me and you'd do it again and we both know that."

"Yes."

"But you said it." He brushed stone dust from his legs. A practical gesture, performed while he found the rest of the sentence. "You didn't — you said the thing. About the shape of it. About the Margins. About what you did being the same as what they did. That's — I needed that more than I needed the story."

"The story's true."

"I know. But the story's information. What you said about using me — that's recognition. That's different." He looked south, down the road. "I'm not forgiving you. I want to be clear about that."

"I know."

"Good." He started walking.

---

They moved south through the afternoon without conversation, but the silence between them had changed. The old silence — the logistics-only quiet that had followed the fracture — had been a wall. Solid, maintained, load-bearing in the way that resentment is load-bearing: holding up a structure by holding two people apart. This silence was different. It was the quiet of two people who had said the hard things and were letting the words settle into the space between them like sediment in still water. Not comfortable. But habitable.

Vel walked and counted. Sixteen. She'd spent nothing today but the number was always there, the Ledger's quiet arithmetic at the edge of awareness. Sixteen things she could still do. Somewhere ahead — two days, maybe three — the Scour waited. A basin of exposed rock and permanent wind and Red Reserve agents who had been guarding a threshold for years.

She thought about Dren. Heading north with a bad arm and a hundred and twenty levels and a nine-year-old daughter whose lungs sounded like paper. He'd said *my daughter is my zero* and the phrase sat in Vel's chest like a stone, hard and precise and irreducible. His zero had a name and an address and a disease. His cascade was forty levels he could barely afford and a healer's table in a town he'd been away from for six weeks.

And he'd looked at her and seen the same thing. The same desperate arithmetic. *Same thing you're doing. Just different math.*

The road bent around a Margins settlement that had been abandoned years ago — roofless structures, the walls still standing because Shaping-built walls outlasted the people who lived inside them. A notice was nailed to what had been a community board: PRODUCTIVITY TIER ASSESSMENT — ALL UNITS REPORT TO SECTOR ADMINISTRATION FOR ANNUAL OUTPUT REVIEW. Below it, in different handwriting, someone had written: *They left. We're still here. The notice is still here too.*

Vel kept walking. Kem walked beside her. Not ten feet ahead, the way he'd been since the fracture. Beside her. Not close enough to touch, and not the careful distance of two people performing separation.

At a water stop — a Margins communal well, still functional, the rope frayed and the bucket dented but the water clean — Kem filled both their canteens without being asked. He handed hers back and their fingers touched on the metal and neither of them made anything of it. That was the repair: not a moment, not a gesture, not a word. Just the ordinary human fact of one person handing another person water.

The journal was in her pack. Maren's handwriting on the pages, the routes marked in a cramped careful hand, the annotations crowded into margins that weren't wide enough for what she'd needed to say. Vel had been carrying it for eight years. She would carry it for two more days. And then she would be at zero, in a place without the Ledger, and Maren would be there or she wouldn't, and either way the journal would have done its work.

The road dipped where the earth had subsided over an old mine gallery. Vel stepped around the depression without thinking. Kem stepped around it after her, matching her line, the unconscious mimicry of two people who had learned each other's gait. He didn't know he was doing it. She did. It was one of the things she'd taught him without teaching him — the body learning from proximity, absorbing another person's economy of movement the way soil absorbs rain, quietly and without permission.

She checked her Ledger. Sixteen.

The Scour was two days south. Red Reserve agents, trained and armed and guarding a threshold that had been there longer than anyone alive. Four of them against two people with thirty-four levels between them. The arithmetic was the worst she'd ever faced, and she'd faced bad arithmetic for forty years.

But she'd told Kem the truth. All of it. And he was still walking.

That would have to be enough.
