# Chapter 2

The Conservatory Rite began, as it always did, with the presentation of objects.

This week the Aldraste household had supplied the table: a cracked ceramic bowl that a single Mending Charm could make whole, a bundle of cut flowers already browning at their edges, an unlit oil lamp with a full reservoir, and a small mechanical clock whose mainspring had wound down three hours ago. The objects were arranged on a stone pedestal at the center of the Conservatory hall — four mundane invitations to act, positioned under excellent lighting so every guest could appreciate them not being addressed.

Kem Aldraste sat in the third row of the Aldraste section and watched a petal fall from the browning bouquet.

Around him, sixty-three Hoarded lords and ladies sat in the same practiced stillness they had brought to the Conservatory every week for as long as there had been a Conservatory. The hall was built for this — high ceilings that stretched sound thin, stone walls that absorbed noise, benches cut from the same quarry so no seat creaked differently from another. Even the air felt managed, still and temperate, neither warm enough to invite fidgeting nor cool enough to prompt a shiver. The architecture of inaction.

The Rite had no officiant. No one spoke. That was the point. For two hours every week, the great families of the Vault sat in proximity to objects that could be improved, repaired, revived, or corrected with a single ability use, and demonstrated their collective commitment to not doing so. The cracked bowl stayed cracked. The lamp stayed dark. The flowers wilted. The clock was silent. The Hoarded watched these small failures accumulate and called the watching a discipline, and the discipline a philosophy, and the philosophy a civilization.

Two weeks ago the Ventrathi family had contributed a wounded songbird, which had chirped weakly for the first ninety minutes and then gone quiet in a way that was distinct from sleep. The Ventrathi matriarch had accepted compliments for the difficulty of her selection for days afterward. The bird, Kem gathered, had represented an exceptional test of resolve. A healing charm for a bird that size would have cost a single level — one three-hundredth of the capacity any person in this room carried — and sixty-odd people had sat in cushioned silence while it died.

Kem had attended the Conservatory Rite every week for twenty-six years. He had missed perhaps a dozen sessions — illness twice, a diplomatic reception once, and once when he was nine and had hidden in the kitchens until a servant found him and carried him back. The servant had been docked a level for the exertion of carrying him. She'd been at two hundred and ninety-eight, doing acceptable work. After that she was at two hundred and ninety-seven, which was not a meaningful difference in any functional sense but mattered enormously in the Vault's social accounting, where the gap between three hundred and anything less than three hundred was the gap between person and instrument.

Kem's mother sat in the row ahead, at the end nearest the aisle. Lady Aldraste's posture was precise without being rigid — a calibration she had maintained for decades, her shoulders in a line that communicated authority rather than effort. Her hair was dark and pinned with small silver clasps in the formal Hoarded style, and she wore a robe the color of good iron that had been pressed that morning by servants who did not have the option of being wrinkled. She was watching the pedestal, or appeared to be watching the pedestal. Kem had understood since he was a teenager that his mother watched everything — the other families' composure, the politics of who fidgeted and who didn't, the slow rearrangement of social capital that happened in this room under the cover of stillness. She missed very little and mentioned less.

There was also the thing she did with Lord Aldraste's collar. Every week, at some point during the first half hour, she reached across without looking and adjusted the fabric at his neck — a fold that had gone wrong, or a clasp that had shifted, something small and particular. She did it with the unhurried precision of a person who had been tending this man's appearance for thirty years, maintaining the surface of something whose interior she could not repair. Kem did not know whether the other families noticed. He noticed every time.

His father sat at the head of the Aldraste section. Lord Aldraste's posture was perfect — better than perfect, the kind of composure that had outlasted the person who had cultivated it, running now on memory alone. His hands rested on his thighs in a symmetry that looked arranged because it had been. His robes were immaculate, the collar recently adjusted by his wife, his face set in an expression that had once been dignity and was now the shape his face held when no one was directing it.

His eyes were open and they were not looking at anything — not closed, not focused on some distant consideration, not deliberately unseeing, just empty. The eyes of a man whose attention had been thinning for years, like cloth held too long in sun, until what remained was the structure without the thread. Lord Aldraste at sixty-one had the same three hundred levels he'd been born with — every point of potential intact, a full reservoir that had never been tapped for any purpose — and the man carrying that reservoir was dissolving by increments so small that most of the family had stopped registering the change. His speech had slowed, not thoughtfully, the way a careful man weighs his words, but mechanically, as if the apparatus that assembled sentences was losing components. He paused mid-phrase now, and what came after the pause did not always connect to what had come before. Servants guided him to meals. His valet dressed him and arranged his limbs in the morning. In the Conservatory, he sat with the flawless composure of a man who had spent six decades perfecting the posture and would likely maintain it for the remaining years of his body's function, regardless of whether the mind behind it continued to attend.

The Hoarded called this Stillness. They said the word with the reverence other cultures reserved for devotion or mastery. Lord Aldraste has achieved remarkable Stillness — a credit to the House. The highest form of the Hoarded discipline: a man so committed to preservation that he had preserved himself into absence.

Kem watched the clock on the pedestal not tick.

He had been watching his father disappear for a long time. It had not always been like this. There were years — Kem could not have named the exact year it started, because the onset had been gradual, a slow erasure of small things before the large things followed — when his father had been present, sharp even. Lord Aldraste had run the House's affairs, negotiated alliances, spoken with a precision the other families respected. He had laughed, rarely and specifically, at things that were genuinely funny. He had put his hand on Kem's shoulder one afternoon when Kem was twelve and said something about patience that Kem could no longer remember the content of but still remembered the weight of — the hand, the pressure, the sense that the person behind the hand was fully there.

That person was not here. Kem watched his father's face for some sign of interior life — a reaction to the falling petal, a flicker toward the unlit lamp, anything suggesting the machinery was still running behind the blank composure — and found nothing. The Ledger read three hundred. The man read empty.

The second petal fell.

Kem stood up.

He did not decide to stand. His legs straightened and his weight shifted and then he was vertical, and the sound of his bench creaking under the redistribution was, in the Conservatory's silence, an event. Sixty-three faces turned toward him. The Ventrathi matriarch's lips parted. The Drassic family's eldest son went rigid in his seat. A minor-house lord in the back rows flinched, actually flinched, as if Kem had thrown something.

His mother said his name.

"Kem."

Not a question, not a command, not an appeal. His name in her voice, held steady by the same composure that held everything else in place, and underneath it something she was not going to show this room — something compressed into that single syllable and kept there by thirty years of Hoarded discipline. She did not stand. She did not reach for him. She looked at him with eyes that were, unlike her husband's, entirely present, and she said his name once, and she let the Conservatory's silence seal around it.

He walked out.

His footsteps on the stone floor were the loudest sound the Conservatory had heard in weeks. Each step was a small detonation of decorum — heel striking stone, the rustle of his robe, the whisper of displaced air. He walked past the Drassic section and the Ventrathi section and the minor families in the back rows who were, he understood, already rehearsing how they would describe this moment to the people who hadn't been here. He kept walking.

The Conservatory doors were shut. They were large, ornamental, carved with patterns that represented the founding of the Vault — a history of deliberate inaction rendered in hardwood by artisans who had been paid in marks and spent levels they would never recover to produce a monument to people who spent nothing. The doors were designed to be opened from the outside, by servants, because the Hoarded did not open their own doors.

Kem did not know how to open a door, and this was not a figure of speech. The doors had handles — brass, polished weekly — and in principle the mechanism was simple. Pull or push, depending on the hinge direction, and the door would move. But Kem had never done it. Twenty-six years in the Vault and every door had been opened for him, closed behind him, held at the appropriate angle for the appropriate duration by people whose job it was to manage doors so that Kem and people like Kem never had to think about them.

He thought about them now. He reached for the handle and what came instead was something from deeper in his mind, a reflex he hadn't known he possessed, an instinct that reached for the three hundred levels sitting untouched at the edge of his awareness — the background hum of the Ledger, the number always there, always full.

A Projection Shield deployed in the corridor with a sound like the air itself splitting. The force wall erupted outward from Kem's extended hand, rippling, crackling with energy so far in excess of the task that the excess was itself a kind of statement — not one he had intended, but the room heard it regardless. The Shield hit the Conservatory doors and blew them both off their hinges. The left door slammed sideways into the corridor wall and cratered the plaster. The right door went further, tumbling end over end down the hallway and coming to rest against a decorative column twenty meters away with a sound that suggested the column had opinions about the impact.

Stone dust drifted in the sudden stillness.

Kem stared at the wreckage. The brass handles were still attached to both doors. One of them had bent.

He checked his Ledger — the number that was always there, the background awareness he had never thought about because it had never changed, the quiet hum of capacity sitting full and ready at the edge of his mind.

Two hundred and ninety-seven.

Three levels. He had spent three levels to open a door that a brass handle and a human arm could have managed for free.

Behind him, the Conservatory was silent, but it was a different silence now — not the practiced silence of discipline but the shocked silence of sixty-three people who had just watched an ability deployed in a room where abilities had not been used in living memory. He could feel the weight of their attention on his back. He stepped through the ruined doorway, his shoes crunching on stone debris, and walked into the Vault's corridors without a plan.

---

The corridors were built for people who did not hurry. Wide, polished stone, lit by ability-sustained lamps that servants maintained at a cost of one level per lamp per year. Portraits of Hoarded lords along the walls, painted by Spent Town artists because painting required effort and the Hoarded would not spend on their own likenesses. A carpet runner, deep blue, that absorbed his footsteps and gave the impression of walking through a building after hours. The Vault was beautiful. Kem had never thought about whether it was beautiful because he had never had another point of comparison, but in the clarity of whatever was happening to him — adrenaline, perhaps, or whatever happened inside a man who had never acted and had suddenly acted — the beauty registered, and it registered as expensive.

His legs were already complaining. The longest walk he normally took inside the Vault was the route from his chambers to the Conservatory, perhaps eighty meters, with a pause halfway in the gallery where a bench invited rest. He'd walked past that distance and the burn in his calves had progressed from complaint to argument. His right shoe had started to rub at the heel. The shoes were handmade, commissioned from a Spent Town cobbler who'd spent two levels on the fitting, and they were perfect for marble floors and wrong for everything else.

He took a wrong turn near the eastern residential wing. Then another. The Vault contained several hundred rooms arranged across multiple wings and levels, connected by corridors that all looked essentially identical — polished stone, blue carpet, portraits, lamps. He had lived here for twenty-six years and he could not find the way out, because he had never needed to find it. Servants appeared when he needed to be somewhere. They walked ahead. He followed. The idea that the building had a layout requiring personal comprehension had never entered his planning, which was consistent with the larger fact that he had never planned anything.

He turned a corner and the corridor changed.

The stone here was a shade darker, the walls slightly thicker, and the doors — there were two of them, heavy, unmarked — sat flush with the wall in a way that suggested reinforcement. No portraits on this stretch. No carpet runner. The lamps were maintained to the same brightness as everywhere else, but the light seemed to illuminate less. Kem had known this wing existed the way he knew the kitchens existed — as a fact without detail, a section of the Vault he had been redirected away from so smoothly as a child that he hadn't realized the redirecting was deliberate until years later. No one discussed it. Not in the way the Hoarded didn't discuss Stillness — with euphemism and careful avoidance — but genuinely didn't discuss it, as if the corridor and its sealed doors occupied a gap in the family's collective attention.

The silence behind those doors had a different quality from the Vault's usual quiet. The Vault's silence was the absence of action — a held breath, a negative space. This was a silence with density, a silence that suggested containment rather than emptiness. The silence of people doing something and being quiet about it.

Kem did not stop. He was aware, dimly, that if he started investigating things he did not understand, the momentum carrying him toward the gate would fail, and momentum was the only resource he had that didn't cost levels.

He found the gate hall by accident — a wrong turn he'd taken out of frustration that deposited him in a wide ceremonial space he half-recognized from childhood memory. High ceiling, vaulted, the Vault's main entrance, rarely used because the Hoarded rarely left. Two guards stood near the gate in the Vault's understated uniform, neither armed. Hoarded guards observed and reported. They did not intervene physically, because physical intervention was action, and action was spending. Actual enforcement, when enforcement was needed, was handled by — Kem did not know who handled actual enforcement. The question had never come up in twenty-six years of never needing to enforce anything.

The guards watched him approach. The older one, a man with the expression of someone who had trained himself to see without reacting, held his position and said nothing. The younger one — a woman, perhaps thirty, with a crease between her eyebrows that suggested she had not expected today to contain anything worth creasing over — opened her mouth and then shut it.

"I'm leaving," Kem said. He heard it come out flat and declarative, the grammar of a person who had practiced formal speech his whole life but had never had to say anything that mattered.

"My lord." The younger guard's eyes flicked to the older guard, who did not look back. "The gate is — I'd need to send word to Lady Aldraste before —"

"Open it, please."

"Protocol requires notification of the household before any —"

The Projection Shield came again. Smaller this time — his mind, apparently, retained some memory of the last deployment and had attempted calibration — but still too much, still a rippling wall of force that crackled with surplus energy. The latch mechanism shattered. The gate groaned and swung outward on its hinges, not violently but firmly, and warm late-spring air flooded into the hall with a complexity of smell and temperature that hit Kem like a change in altitude. The air smelled of soil and growing things and something he couldn't identify — something open and directionless that turned out, he would learn later, to be the smell of distance itself.

Two hundred and ninety-five.

By the gate, recessed into the wall on the left, stood a row of ceremonial pedestals — one per major House. Each held a House signet, a heavy ring of worked metal bearing the family crest, displayed in the gate hall as a symbol of the families' commitment to the Vault. The signets stayed because the families stayed. Nobody wore them. They were polished weekly by servants and admired on formal occasions and otherwise left alone.

The Aldraste signet was silver-dark, heavy, the crest a closed circle. Kem picked it up. The metal was warm from the lamp-heated alcove, heavier than it looked. He put it on his finger, where it fit exactly, because it had been sized for him at birth — because the Hoarded planned generations ahead for eventualities they would never act on.

He did not take it because he wanted it. He took it because leaving it would mean he'd paused to consider whether to take it, and pausing to consider would mean this was a decision, and if this was a decision he would have to weigh it against the consequences, and if he started weighing consequences he would sit back down.

He walked through the gate.

---

The world was louder than the Vault.

This was not an observation but a physical event — sound arriving from multiple directions at once, layered and unmanaged and alive. Birds, several varieties producing several kinds of noise, none of them curated or dampened by architecture. Wind moving through trees that were actual trees, not the Vault's manicured garden specimens but full-grown pines that creaked and shed things and produced a rushing sound when the breeze moved through their crowns. Insects. The distant suggestion of running water. And underneath all of it an ambient texture of sound that was simply the landscape existing without anyone's oversight.

The ground outside the Vault was not level. The gate opened onto a path that descended a gentle slope toward a tree line several hundred meters away, and the path was dirt — packed dirt with loose stones and exposed roots and ruts where rain had channeled. Kem's shoes, designed for polished stone by a cobbler who had reasonably assumed the shoes would never leave a building, made contact with the dirt and communicated immediately that they were not going to be helpful.

He walked. His calves, already burning from the longest interior walk of his life, discovered that downhill was a different kind of work from flat, and that neither set of muscles had been meaningfully developed by twenty-six years of sitting in ceremonial silence. His heel blister announced its presence with each step. A stone worked its way under the ball of his right foot through the thin sole. The sun — the actual sun, unfiltered by walls or ceilings, arriving with a directness he had not prepared for — landed on the back of his neck and began, with the impersonal patience of natural processes, to burn him.

He reached the tree line in what felt like twenty minutes and was probably closer to forty. His legs were trembling. His shirt was damp across the back. The pines gave shade, which was an improvement, and the ground beneath them was cushioned with needles — a surface that gave slightly under his weight, that smelled like something alive and decomposing, that concealed things he could not see. A small brown animal darted across the path ahead of him and he startled so badly he stumbled sideways into a tree trunk. His shoulder hit bark and the bark was not smooth.

He stood in the pine forest, breathing hard, rubbing his shoulder, and took stock.

He was Kem Aldraste, level two hundred and ninety-five. Heir to one of the five major Hoarded Houses, a title that he suspected had been functionally revoked the moment he'd blown the Conservatory doors off their hinges. He was wearing robes designed for sitting and shoes designed for marble. He carried no food, no water, no pack, no map, and no money — he was not entirely certain money existed as a physical object, because the Hoarded didn't handle currency, and the servants who managed the household accounts had never explained the financial system to someone who was never expected to interact with it. He had his clothes, his House signet on his finger, and approximately two hundred and ninety-five levels of raw ability that he had no training in using and that he had so far deployed exclusively for the purpose of destroying doors.

His practical skills included reading, several languages, all theoretical. History, comprehensive, all theoretical. Governance philosophy, extensive, all theoretical. And the ability to sit motionless for two hours while flowers died.

He was hungry.

Not the anticipatory appetite that preceded Vault meals — meals that arrived at precise intervals, prepared by servants, served on plates he had never washed and could not have identified the washing process for. This was hunger as a physical condition: the hollow pull below his sternum, a shakiness in his hands that he hadn't connected to his stomach until his stomach growled loud enough to startle a bird off a low branch. He had walked further in the last hour than he normally walked in a week, and his body had burned through whatever reserves it carried and was now presenting him with a demand he had no manual process for addressing.

He knew the theory. Sustenance Charms were a basic utility ability — one of the first things children in the Spent Towns learned to calibrate, or so his tutors had told him. The principle was straightforward: draw on the Ledger to generate food and water from ambient energy, scaled to need. A single person's meal might cost one or two levels. Efficient, practical, the most common ability use in the world outside the Vault. Something any person at level one hundred and fifty did daily without thinking about it.

Kem had never used a Sustenance Charm. He had never used any ability before this morning. He knew it the way he knew the historical causes of the Spent Town migration: as information, arranged in his mind by tutors, disconnected from the physical reality it described.

He activated the charm.

His intention was modest. Something to eat. Bread, maybe. A portion of food appropriate for one person sitting on a log in a pine forest.

What he got was bread — eight loaves of it, golden-crusted, steaming, materializing on the ground around the log in a rough semicircle. Following the bread came a roast, enormous, glistening, radiating heat and the smell of rendered fat, the size of something you would present to a banquet table on a wheeled cart. Then fruit — apples and pears and things he did not recognize, piled in mounds. Wheels of cheese. A cask of something that smelled alcoholic. Smaller dishes appeared in a widening ring: pastries in stacked rows, cured meats fanned out on wooden boards that had materialized along with the meats, pickled vegetables in ceramic jars, bowls of stew that steamed gently in the cool pine-filtered air.

The food kept coming. Kem stared at it. He had no idea how to make it stop.

A final surge produced a three-tiered cake — elaborately iced, studded with what appeared to be candied fruit — which materialized on a tree stump roughly two meters from the log. The stump was not level. The cake began to slide. Kem lunged for it and caught the bottom tier with both hands. The icing was warm and got on his fingers and his sleeves and the signet ring.

He held the cake and looked at the food. It occupied an area of approximately four meters in every direction, arrayed across the forest floor with the abundance of a banquet prepared for a lord's household — which, in a sense, it had been. The Charm had drawn from his own experience of meals, and the only meals Kem had ever experienced were the ones served in the Vault, where a gathering of thirty was a modest dinner party and the kitchen staff spent levels freely to produce food that matched the family's expectations.

He checked his Ledger.

Two hundred and eighty-eight.

Seven levels. He had spent seven levels on lunch.

He set the cake down on the flattest piece of ground he could find, which was not flat enough. The cake settled at an angle. A line of ants discovered the icing within seconds and began a purposeful advance.

He sat on the log and ate a piece of bread. The bread was excellent — the interior soft, the crust shattering properly, the flavor precisely what he was used to because it had been generated from his palate's only reference point. He ate it and watched a bird land on the roast and begin eating with a directness he found briefly instructive. Then a second bird arrived, and then something larger came out of the trees — a fox, rust-colored and cautious, which approached the roast with its head low and its eyes on Kem and then committed to the meat with the single-mindedness of an animal who had decided to deal with consequences later.

Kem ate bread and watched the fox eat roast and thought about the arithmetic.

Twelve levels gone in the first hour. The Projection Shield in the Conservatory had cost three. The second Shield at the gate, two more. The Sustenance Charm, seven. At this rate — he tried to calculate how long his levels would last and realized the calculation required knowing what daily survival cost, and he did not know what daily survival cost, because he had never survived a day without institutional support. He did not know how to start a fire. He did not know how to find water that was not conjured by ability. He did not know which direction led to anything, or what anything would look like when he arrived, or how far his legs could carry him before they stopped working, which based on the evidence so far was not very far at all.

The fox looked up from the roast, muzzle dark and wet. It regarded Kem for a moment, then returned to eating.

He stood. His calves protested. His blister had soaked through his sock and he could feel the wet fabric sliding against raw skin with each step. He left the banquet where it sat — the bread and the roast and the cheese and the three-tiered cake with its column of ants and its growing audience of birds — and he walked south along the path because the path went south and he did not yet have the experience to know that the direction a path goes is not necessarily the direction you should go.

The pine forest thinned. The trees gave way to scrubby grassland and the shade gave way to full sun and the sun fell on him with a direct, impersonal heat he had not prepared for, because he had not prepared for anything. His neck was already pink. His face felt tight. The air tasted different out here — drier, carrying dust and pollen, nothing like the Vault's managed atmosphere. He could feel the Ledger humming at the edge of his awareness, quieter now by twelve points. It was not a sensation he could have described to anyone who hadn't felt it — a fullness slightly less full, like a room with a few books pulled from the shelf. He barely noticed it.

He walked south. His body did what it could, which was less than he would have liked and more than he had any right to expect from a body that had spent twenty-six years doing nothing. His feet bled. His shoulders ached from holding his arms in the unfamiliar rhythm of walking. The signet ring on his finger caught the sun and flashed, and he looked at it — the Aldraste crest, the closed circle, the symbol of a House built on the principle that the best use of power was no use at all — and he kept walking, because he had started walking, and stopping would require a decision, and the only decision he had made today was to stand up, and everything after that had been momentum.

Behind him the food cooled in the pine forest. The fox ate until it couldn't move and lay on its side near the roast. The ants disassembled the cake tier by tier. The stew thickened in its bowls and the bread went stale and the Vault's gate remained open because no one inside had yet convened a committee to determine whether it should be closed.
