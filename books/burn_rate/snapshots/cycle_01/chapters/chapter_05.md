# Chapter 5

Two days since the outriders and Vel hadn't spent a point. She tracked this the way she tracked everything — as arithmetic, not virtue. Twenty-eight on the Ledger, the number sitting at the back of her awareness like a tooth she kept tonguing. Twenty-eight could get them to the Spent Towns if nothing went wrong. If Kem stopped hemorrhaging levels on problems a lit candle could solve. If the Hoarded were slow to send the next wave.

A lot of ifs. She was used to ifs.

Behind her, Kem Aldraste, level two hundred and forty-something, was losing a fight with the trail.

The forested highlands rolled south in long ridgelines under a canopy of beech and hornbeam, late-spring light coming through in shafts that shifted with the wind. Good cover. Hard walking — the ground climbed and fell in short steep pitches, roots lacing the path, the soil still soft from rain two nights ago. Vel moved through it without thinking, her pack riding high against her shoulders in grooves worn over decades. Kem moved through it like a man who had been personally insulted by the concept of elevation.

"How much further today?" he asked. He'd been asking this every two hours.

"However far we get."

"Right." A pause. "That's — yeah. Right."

She could hear his breathing. Heavy, effortful, with the quality of someone who'd never sustained exertion for more than an hour at a stretch. His boots — good leather, Vault-made, stitched for ceremonial corridors — were doing things to his feet that she could track by the way his stride had shortened since morning. His hands were worse. She'd been watching them for two days. The blisters had come in on his palms first, where the pack straps rubbed, and then his right thumb from the knife she'd lent him to cut their path through a deadfall section yesterday. He'd gripped the knife too tight, the way people hold things when they're afraid of dropping them. She hadn't corrected him. The calluses would teach him faster than she could.

A root caught his boot. He stumbled, grabbed a low branch that bent under his weight and dumped bark dust into his hair, and stood there for a moment looking at the branch like he was going to apologize to it.

"The roots," he said. "Are they always this — "

"Yes."

"Fine."

They walked. The canopy thickened as they dropped into a shallow valley where a stream cut through exposed rock, and the air went cool and green and damp. Late afternoon. Two more hours of useful light. She checked the terrain, the water source, the sightlines through the trees, and stopped.

"Here." She shrugged her pack off. "We're making camp."

Kem shrugged his own pack off and the relief that passed across his face was so naked she almost looked away. He stood straighter. Rolled his shoulders. Winced at something in his lower back that he didn't mention.

"Sit," she said. Then: "No. Don't sit. You'll stiffen."

"I'm already — "

"You'll stiffen worse. Stay moving. We've got work."

She watched him absorb this — the visible negotiation between his body's desire to collapse and his recognition that she hadn't been wrong yet about anything physical. He stayed on his feet.

"What work?"

"Fire. Food. Shelter." She unrolled the fire kit from the top of her pack — a waxed-cloth wrap that held a steel striker, a knapped flint, and a bundle of char cloth she'd made three camps ago. "You're doing all of it."

He looked at the fire kit like it was written in a language he could almost read.

"I've seen fire," he said.

"You've seen Flame Charms."

"I've seen fire. In fireplaces. In the Vault."

"Someone else's fire."

"Yes." He picked up the striker, turned it in his hand. Soft fingers against hard steel. "So I — strike this against the stone?"

Vel gathered tinder without explaining, because he needed to see the result before the process would make sense. A loose nest of dried grass, birch bark she peeled from a fallen log, the smallest twigs she could find. She set the nest on a flat rock near the stream and crouched beside it.

"Char cloth on the flint." She showed him. "Strike down. Spark catches the cloth. Cloth goes into the tinder. You blow — small breaths. Don't blow it out."

"That seems straightforward."

"It's not."

He took the flint and the striker and tried. The first strike produced nothing — wrong angle, not enough force. He adjusted, struck again, and the steel skidded off the flint and caught the meat of his thumb. Blood welled up in a thin line across the blister there, bright against the pale, soft skin that had never gripped anything harder than a pen, and he hissed through his teeth.

"Shit. Okay."

He looked at his thumb. Looked at the striker. She could see him calculating — not the Ledger calculation, the simpler one. He could light this fire with a Flame Charm. One level. Probably less than one, at his current capacity. The temptation was sitting right there in his body, in the hum of two hundred and forty-odd levels of unused potential, and she watched him not reach for it.

He struck again. A spark — small, bright, gone before it touched the char cloth.

"Closer," she said.

He struck. Missed. Struck. The char cloth caught: a tiny orange glow at the edge, alive and improbable. He stared at it.

"Now the tinder. Careful."

He placed the glowing cloth into the nest with both hands, his fingers trembling slightly from the effort of gentleness after the violence of striking. He blew. Too hard. The ember flared orange and died.

He sat back on his heels.

"It was right there."

"It was."

"I had it."

"You did."

"That was — I can feel that that's supposed to work. I can feel the principle of it."

"The principle's fine. Your lungs need practice."

He looked at her. Not the look of an aristocrat expecting service. Not the look that said *fix this* or *I shouldn't have to do this*. She'd been waiting for that look for days. It hadn't come. She was starting to think it wasn't coming.

"Slow breath," she said. "Like you're warming your hands. Not like you're blowing out a candle."

He built a second tinder nest — worse than hers, loose in places, too tight in others, but he built it. Struck again. Spark. Catch. He blew, slow this time, and the char cloth smoked and the tinder smoked and a thread of flame climbed from the center of the nest and took hold of the birch bark, and Kem made a sound she hadn't heard from him before. A quiet, involuntary "oh" that had nothing to do with the Ledger and everything to do with what his hands had just done.

She fed the flame kindling while he watched, building it from thread to finger-width to wrist-width until the fire stood on its own. Then she sat back and pointed at the cookpot.

"Water first. Then there's a grouse in my pack, wrapped in dock leaves. You're cleaning it."

The look he gave her was roughly the look he'd given the striker, but worse.

---

He held the grouse by its feet and it hung there, gray-feathered, already cold, its head at the angle of a neck Vel had wrung that morning while he was still asleep. She'd kept it in the pack wrapped in leaves, waiting for this.

"I need to — what's first?"

"Pluck it."

He plucked it. This took a long time. The feathers stuck to the skin and came out in clumps and single barbs alternately, and he pulled too gently, then too hard, tearing skin along the breast so the pink flesh showed through in ragged strips. His hands, already blistered from the striker, were soon coated in a paste of blood and down that stuck to his raw palms and wouldn't shake free. He wiped them on his trousers. The down transferred to the fabric. He wiped harder and made it worse.

She said nothing. He kept plucking.

"Now gut it."

"I don't know where the — which part do I — "

She talked him through it. Belly cut, short and shallow, don't puncture the intestines. He punctured the intestines. The smell hit him and he turned his head and his jaw worked once and then he turned back and kept cutting. She showed him what to pull out and he pulled it out and his face was a careful blank she recognized from years of watching people meet the physical reality of food for the first time. The intestines came free with a wet sound and he set them on the rock beside him and looked at his hands.

"Everyone does this?" he said.

"Everyone who eats. Or someone does it for them."

"Someone did it for me. For twenty-six years." He said this quietly, without performance. A fact arriving in his body for the first time. "Someone killed the bird. Plucked it. Gutted it. Cooked it. Put it on a plate. Cleaned the plate. Every day. For twenty-six years."

"Yeah."

He rinsed the cavity with stream water. Spitted the bird on a green branch she cut for him — hazel, straight enough, the bark stripped. Set it over the fire on a forked stick. Too close. She didn't tell him. The residual feathers he'd missed singed and curled and the fat began to render and pop, spattering into the coals, and within five minutes the underside was charring black while the top stayed raw and pale.

"Turn it."

He turned it. The other side charred. He moved it higher, but the green branch bowed under the weight and the grouse slipped sideways and landed in the coals. He grabbed for it and the heel of his hand pressed into an ember and he jerked back, swearing — "God — that's — damn" — and she leaned over and fished the bird out with a stick and set it back on the spit.

A blister was already rising on his palm, next to the older ones. He held his hand out and looked at it — the accumulated record of one afternoon's work. Blisters on blisters, the knife cut, the ember burn, dried blood and grouse fat and ash.

"It's ruined," he said, looking at the bird.

"It's dinner."

"But it's — " He gestured. The grouse was blackened on two sides and raw on the third, ash embedded in the skin where it had fallen, the whole thing listing on the spit like a ship that had given up. "Nobody would eat that."

"I'm eating that."

He looked at her. She meant it. He could see that she meant it.

She showed him how to prop the spit at the right height and rotate it slowly, and the third side cooked through to something closer to edible while the charred sides went from black to blacker. When she judged the meat was done — or done enough that it wouldn't make them sick — she pulled it off the fire and set it on the flat rock between them.

---

They ate it. Vel tore the bird apart with her knife, cutting away the worst char, and what remained was tough and smoky and salty with ash. Kem chewed slowly, working through the texture of meat his blistered hands had made, and didn't complain. The stream ran behind them and the fire ticked and popped and the highlands darkened from green to gray to the blue-black of the canopy against the last light.

The food was the worst thing Vel had eaten in recent memory, and recent memory included a week of flatbread and creek water during a flux outbreak south of Greymarsh. She ate it anyway. He'd made it.

She was not going to examine why that mattered.

"In the Vault," Kem said, after a while, "food just appears. I mean — I knew there were kitchens. Somewhere. I knew people prepared it. But it appeared. On plates. With garnish. I never thought about what came before the plate."

"Mhm."

"This is what came before the plate." He held up a piece of charred grouse. Firelight caught the ash on his fingers.

"Every time."

"Every single meal."

"Yep."

He ate another piece. Chewed. The quiet between them was different than it had been — not the tactical silence of their first days, where every pause was Vel calculating and Kem filling space with nervous chatter. This was simpler. Two tired people eating at the end of a hard day.

A nightbird called from the canopy. Two notes, repeated, patient. The stream caught the last of the light and held it.

"Can I ask you something?" Kem said.

"You can ask."

"Why did you start?"

She knew what he meant. Not why she wanted zero — he'd asked that and she'd shut him down. This was different. Why she'd started spending. What made someone go from three hundred to twenty-eight, one level at a time, over forty years, in a world where every point spent was a permanent subtraction.

"That's not about the route."

"No."

"Then it's not your business."

"I know." He said this without resentment. Just acknowledgment. Then he went quiet. He picked at the grouse. The nightbird called again, the two-note phrase carrying in the damp air.

Vel pulled a strip of bark from the ground near her knee and tossed it into the coals. It caught and flared and she watched it burn down to a curl of ash.

"I was seventeen," she said.

She hadn't decided to say it. The words came out the way water finds a crack — not because the pressure was unbearable but because the resistance, for a moment, wasn't there. She was tired. The food was bad. The boy had burned his hands and eaten it anyway.

"There was a canal in Avermouth. South of the Vault, before you hit the Spent Towns. Market town. Big enough for two Hoarded representatives, small enough that everyone knew which streets were theirs. I was there with my mother — she sold cloth. End of market day. Sun going down. People heading home."

She paused. Not for effect. For the specific effort of uncovering something she'd packed away so long it had calcified into fact, and facts were easier than memories.

"A girl fell in the canal. Eight, maybe nine years old. She'd been running on the towpath and the rail was rotted through and she went in. The current runs fast where the canal narrows under the market bridge. Deep enough to pull an adult under. Cold — it was early spring, snowmelt still coming down from the hills."

"Was there — "

"There were forty people on that towpath. Maybe more. Half of them were Hoarded. One was at least two ninety-eight — I could see his House seal. They wore them pinned to their coats back then. He was standing six feet from where she went in."

Kem was very still.

"The Hoarded did nothing. Obviously. The seal-coat watched. Didn't even change his expression. The Spent — most of them were young, two-sixty, two-seventy. A Strength ability would've pulled her out. Three levels, maybe four. But they're looking at the Hoarded, and the Hoarded aren't moving, and there's this — " She could feel the shape of it still, forty-one years later. "This calculation happening on every face. The same one. *If they don't spend, why should I.*"

"And you — "

"I jumped in."

She said it flat. The fact.

"I was seventeen and at three hundred and I'd never spent a point in my life and I jumped into the canal after a girl I didn't know. The current took us both under the bridge and I — I don't even remember deciding. I used some kind of Grip ability. The Ledger calls it that — Grip. Your hands hold harder than a body should. I grabbed a bridge piling with one hand and her with the other and I held on until two men on the bank finally waded in."

"One level."

"One level. Three hundred to two ninety-nine. And I climbed out and I walked back past the seal-coat and he was still standing there and his face hadn't moved. Not once. And the girl's mother was crying — not at me, just crying, the way you cry when your body catches up to what almost happened — and nobody said thank you, and my hands were bleeding from the piling, and I was at two ninety-nine."

She stopped. She'd said more than she planned, and the silence that followed had the specific weight of too much honesty — not regret, but the exposure of having told a true thing to someone she hadn't fully decided to trust.

"I didn't plan the rest of it," she said. "I didn't have a philosophy. I just — once you've done it, you can't unsee the places where someone should do something. And nobody does. And you're standing there with two ninety-nine and the canal's right in front of you."

Kem didn't say anything for a while. He'd stopped eating. His face in the firelight was hard to read — younger than twenty-six, the way it went when something got past his composure and he didn't know what to do with it.

"The Hoarded would say you were reckless," he said.

"They'd say I was wasteful."

"Yes." He looked at the fire. "They'd say the girl would have been retrieved by natural means. Or that she should have been more careful on the towpath. Or that the loss of a level represents a permanent consequence disproportionate to the temporary risk of — " He caught himself. Stopped. "That's what my father would have said. Fifteen years ago, when he could still finish a sentence."

The fire popped. A coal split and the halves fell inward, glowing.

"Tell me about him," Vel said. She meant it, which surprised her.

Kem leaned back. His hands hung between his knees, palms up, and in the firelight she could see the blisters and the striker cut and the ash and the dried grouse blood crusted under his nails. Hands that were trying to learn what work was.

"My father's Ledger reads three hundred," he said. "It has always read three hundred. He's never used a single level on a single thing in sixty-one years of living."

"I know what the Hoarded are."

"You know the principle. You don't — " He stopped. Restarted, lower, less rehearsed. "I sat next to him at the Conservatory Rite every week for twenty-six years. That's the rite where we — where they — sit in a room full of objects that invite use and demonstrate the discipline of not touching them. Every week. My mother, my father, me. The great families. Everyone at three hundred. Everyone perfectly still."

"Sounds like hell."

"It was normal. It was just what you do on Seventhday. What I want to tell you about is what happened to him."

He picked up a twig and turned it in his fingers.

"He was sharp when I was small. I remember that. He had a voice that filled the dining hall. He argued with my mother about trade routes, southern policy, which families were gaining Council influence. He had opinions. Strong ones. He was — " A pause. "He was there. The way a person is when they're alive inside their own life."

"And he stopped."

"Not all at once. It's slow. The Hoarded call it Stillness. They call it the highest form of discipline — when someone achieves perfect nonaction so completely that the self quiets." He said this with the precise diction of something memorized young, and then his voice roughened, dropped into something less polished. "He was about forty-five when I first noticed. I was ten. His sentences got shorter. Not compressed the way yours are, where you're choosing to say less. Empty shorter. Like the words were drying up inside him. And his eyes — they started doing this thing where they'd fix on a point in the room and stay there. Not staring. Just — vacant. Gone somewhere I couldn't follow."

"The Hoarded celebrated it."

"Uncle Aldris — my father's brother — told me he was approaching perfect balance. The family physician called it deepening discipline. My mother — " He stopped. The twig snapped in his fingers. "My mother knew. She's not stupid. She could see what it was. But she's at three hundred too, and the alternative is to name it, and if you name it then the whole — everything the Hoarded have built, everything they've preserved for centuries — "

"Falls apart."

"Falls apart. So you don't name it. You sit next to your husband every Seventhday and watch his eyes go empty and call it virtue. And when your son stands up in the middle of the rite and walks out, you say his name once and let him go. Because stopping him would require action. Or because maybe part of you wanted someone to finally move. I don't know. Both."

He dropped the broken twig into the fire. It caught immediately, a bright flare that threw their shadows long against the trees and was gone.

"I didn't leave because I disagreed with the philosophy." His voice was steady. "I know that's what they'll say. Political dissent. Youthful confusion. A young lord under radical influence." He looked at her, and his eyes were wet, but his voice held. "I left because my father's dying in a room full of people who are calling it a gift. And I sat there for fifteen years watching it happen and doing nothing, which makes me exactly as Hoarded as the rest of them. And I don't — I can't do that anymore."

Vel sat with this. The fire burned. The stream ran. The nightbird went on calling its two notes into the dark.

She thought about the man in the seal-coat, standing six feet from a drowning girl. She thought about Lord Aldraste in the Conservatory, three hundred on the Ledger and the person draining out of him like water through a cracked jug. The same math, both times. Don't spend, don't act, don't reach into the water. The number stays full and the life stays empty.

"Can he still talk?" she asked.

"Some days. Fewer each year. When he does it's — the sentences aren't really sentences anymore. They're shapes. The grammar's there but the meaning's gone. Like he's reading off a page he can't see."

"And his Ledger."

"Three hundred. Still three hundred. Perfect."

She let the word sit between them. Perfect. She'd met people in the high Spent ranges — two-eighties, two-nineties — who had that same hollowed-out look. People who'd preserved almost everything and lost something the Ledger didn't track and didn't name. She'd never met a three hundred that far gone. She'd heard about it. She'd never had to sit next to it every week for twenty-six years.

"Eat your food," she said. It was the only thing she had that wasn't an opinion or a comfort or a lie.

He ate. The charred grouse, cold now, the rendered fat congealing white against the blackened skin. He ate it methodically, his blistered hands tearing the meat from the bone. When he was done he wiped his hands on the grass and stacked the bones in a small neat pile near the fire, the way someone stacks things when they need to do something with their hands and there's nothing else.

"That was the worst meal I've ever had," he said.

"Yeah."

"I made it."

"You did."

He looked at the bone pile. The fire had burned down to a bed of coals, the red glow painting the underside of the canopy in shifting copper light. His face was streaked with smoke and ash and the particular tiredness of someone who'd said a hard thing and wasn't sorry.

"Thank you," he said. "For the fire. And the — all of it. Even though I ruined the bird."

"You didn't ruin it. You cooked it badly. That's different."

"Is it?"

"Ruined is giving up halfway. Bad is finishing. You finished."

He almost smiled at that. Not the bright, manic grin from after the outrider fight, the one that meant he was terrified and papering over it with enthusiasm. This was smaller and quieter and sat on his face like something that belonged there.

"Tomorrow," she said, "you're building the fire again. And cleaning whatever we catch."

"All right."

"And you're carrying the heavier pack."

"I'm already carrying — " He stopped. Looked at her. "You gave me the light one."

"Twenty pounds."

"Twenty. And the heavy one is — "

"Thirty-five."

"You've been carrying thirty-five pounds through the highlands for two days."

"Forty years."

He was quiet for a moment. She could see him recalculating something — not the Ledger arithmetic but the simpler math of who she was. The seventeen-year-old who'd jumped into a canal. The fifty-eight-year-old who'd been carrying the heavy pack and hadn't mentioned it. The person between those two points who'd spent two hundred and seventy-two levels, one at a time, and was still walking.

"Thirty-five pounds," he said. "Yeah. Okay. I'll carry it."

Vel banked the coals. She laid out the bedrolls — his close enough to the fire's residual warmth, hers between him and the trail, habit more than any specific threat. The highlands were quiet. No pursuit. The Hoarded would reorganize, send more people, but not tonight. Tonight the world was the size of a campfire: a stream, two bedrolls, the smell of burned grouse and woodsmoke, and the weight of what they'd said to each other sitting between them like something solid they'd both have to carry from here.

She lay on her back. Through the canopy she could see a few stars in the gaps between leaves, small and far away. The stream sounded like the stream and nothing else. A good sound. One of the free ones.

Twenty-eight. The Ledger's hum was there, the way it was always there — not loud, just present, the awareness of what remained. Two hundred and seventy-two levels spent. Twenty-eight left. Every one of the spent ones had gone to something. Someone. Some moment where the water was rising and nobody was moving and she was standing there with what she had.

Twenty-eight was tight. Twenty-eight was enough.

She closed her eyes. In the morning she'd make him do it all again.
