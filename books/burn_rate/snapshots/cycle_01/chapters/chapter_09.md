# Chapter 9

The needle went in wrong the first time. Kem felt the resistance of Dren's skin, the rubbery give of it, and his hand jerked sideways. Dren hissed through his teeth.

"Sorry. I'm sorry."

"Just do it," Dren said. "Don't talk about it."

Kem repositioned the needle — a curved sewing needle Vel had bent with pliers somewhere in the first week of travel, kept in a cloth wrap in her pack alongside thread she'd waxed with tallow. The wound ran from Dren's forearm nearly to his elbow, a ragged opening where silo shrapnel had torn through the sleeve and the flesh underneath. The blood had slowed to a dark seep. Vel had cleaned it with water and a rag that smelled of spirits, and Dren had sat through that with nothing more than a tightening around his eyes.

Now it was Kem's turn. Vel couldn't do it — her ribs, cracked from the fight, made the work impossible when she had to lean forward and hold the wound edges together. She'd tried and stopped after the first attempt, pressing her elbow against her side with a controlled exhale that was worse than a grimace.

"Straight through," she'd told him, demonstrating the motion in the air. "Both sides of the wound, equal depth. Pull it snug but not tight. You're closing skin, not cinching a bag."

He understood the principle. He'd been educated in principles. The problem was his hands, which wouldn't stop shaking.

The second pass of the needle was better. It caught both edges of the wound and he pulled the waxed thread through, watching the skin draw together in a puckered line. It looked nothing like the neat stitching Vel had described. It looked like a child had sewn a doll shut.

"Christ," Dren said, looking at it.

"I know."

"Keep going."

Kem kept going. The third stitch was crooked. The fourth caught too deep on one side and too shallow on the other, creating a bunched ridge of skin that would scar badly. Dren's forearm was tanned and ropey from years of loading cargo, and the muscle twitched each time the needle went in. Kem felt every flinch through the thread, a tiny telegraph of pain running from Dren's body into his fingers.

The fifth stitch was the first one he got right. Both sides even, the thread snug, the wound edges meeting cleanly. He stared at it for a moment — one decent stitch among bad ones — and moved on. The sixth was worse, the needle catching on something fibrous under the skin that made Dren grunt and turn his head away. Kem's stomach lurched but he pulled through and tied it off. The seventh was rushed because his fingers were cramping and he wanted to be done.

He was kneeling on packed dirt in a clearing half a mile south of where they'd stopped running. The late-afternoon light came through the trees at an angle that made the blood on Dren's arm look almost black. Vel sat against a tree trunk with her arms crossed carefully over her torso, breathing in the measured way she'd been breathing since the fight — shallow, controlled, the kind of breathing that said something was wrong inside her and she was managing it through force of will. She watched Kem work without commenting.

By the eighth stitch, he'd found something like a rhythm. Not skill — nothing close to it — but a sequence he could repeat: push through, pull, check the gap, tie. His hands still trembled, but the trembling had become part of the motion rather than an obstacle to it. He wiped blood and wax residue from his fingers on his trousers and repositioned for the ninth.

"You're pulling them uneven," Vel said from the tree.

"I know."

"The last two are going to scar worse than they need to."

"I know."

"Keep going."

He pulled the ninth stitch snug. The wound was mostly closed now — an ugly seam running up Dren's forearm, raised and irregular, the thread making dark X shapes against skin that was beginning to swell around the punctures. There were twelve stitches when he finished. Some of them were acceptable. Some of them were terrible. All of them held.

Dren turned his arm, examining the work. He flexed his hand experimentally, and the stitches pulled but didn't tear.

"That's bad," he said.

"Yeah."

Dren rotated his wrist again. The gash was closed. The arm was functional. "My arm's still on, though. So." He looked at Kem, and something shifted in his expression — not warmth exactly, but a recognition of effort that wasn't pretending to be expertise. "Thanks."

It was the first time anyone had thanked Kem for something he'd done badly. He'd been thanked at the Vault — for existing, for attending rites, for carrying the Aldraste name with the proper bearing. Social acknowledgment of his function in a system that required his stillness. Nobody had ever looked at work his hands produced and said thanks while the work was visibly, undeniably poor.

He didn't know what to do with the feeling, so he cleaned the needle with the remains of the spirits and wrapped it back in its cloth.

Vel hadn't moved from the tree. "It'll scar," she said.

"Everything scars." Dren wrapped a strip of cloth around his forearm, tucking the end under itself one-handed with the automatic competence of someone who'd bandaged road injuries for twenty years. The cloth turned pink immediately where it pressed against the stitches. He held his arm against his stomach and leaned back against his pack. "We staying here?"

"Tonight," Vel said. "Move at first light."

Dren tested his grip on the pack strap with the bad arm, pulling carefully. The stitches held but his jaw tightened. He let go and used the good hand instead, adjusting the pack behind his back. Kem watched him work through the calculation — not levels but capacity. The arm functioned. The arm would slow him. The arm changed what he could carry and how fast he could move and how many days he could keep doing this before the math stopped working. Dren did that kind of assessment the way other people breathed — constantly, invisibly, with the automatic precision of someone who'd been balancing risk against return since before Kem was born.

"Arm's fine," Dren said, to nobody in particular.

Kem sat back on his heels and looked at his hands. Blood under his nails. Wax on his fingers. A shallow puncture on his left thumb where the needle had skipped. He tried to remember the last time his hands had looked like this — used, marked, involved in something — and couldn't think of a single occasion. In the Vault, his hands had been clean for twenty-six years. Unblemished, uncalloused. Ready for nothing.

He checked his Ledger. The number hung in his awareness the way it always did — not visible exactly, but present, the way you knew the temperature of a room without looking at anything specific. 178.

He'd been 300 when he walked out of the Vault. That was — three weeks ago? Less? The days blurred into each other, separated by terrain and weather rather than any structure he recognized. Three hundred to 178. A hundred and twenty-two levels gone. More than a third of everything he'd had.

The number felt different now than it had at 245, after the first fight, when the drop had been exhilarating — the rush of finally using what he'd hoarded, the revelation that his body could do things besides sit in ceremonial halls. At 245 he'd felt lighter. At 178 the lightness had an edge to it. He could feel the gaps in his roster — abilities that used to sit in the background like furniture in a room he'd memorized. Combat Pulse, gone. Three Utility charms, gone. The Sustenance Charm that had fed forty people on his first night outside — he still had that one, but activating it now would cost a level he could feel the weight of, the way you'd feel the weight of a coin when you were checking what was left in your pocket.

This was what Vel had been living with for forty years. Not the poverty of it — that word was wrong, too abstract — but the arithmetic. The constant background calculation: what can I afford, what will this cost, what do I lose by choosing this over that. He'd understood it as a concept when she'd explained it. He was starting to understand it as a texture that ran through every decision in a day.

Dren was watching him. Kem realized he'd been staring at his hands for too long.

"How many?" Dren asked.

"What?"

"Levels. How many you got left."

"178."

Dren didn't whistle or wince. He just nodded, once, the nod of someone who understood numbers as facts rather than as things to react to. At 138 — or wherever Dren was now after whatever incidentals the road demanded — he lived inside the same arithmetic every day. The difference was that Dren had been doing it for decades and Kem had been doing it for three weeks.

"That'll get you a ways," Dren said. Which was as close to comfort as Dren ever came.

The quiet settled. Vel had closed her eyes against the tree. Not sleeping — her breathing was too careful for sleep, too conscious of the ribs. But resting in the way she rested, which was never fully. Dren adjusted the wrapped arm against his stomach, found a position that didn't press the stitches against anything, and went still.

Kem wiped his hands on the damp grass beside him, and when the blood didn't come out of the creases he rubbed them with dirt and water until his skin was raw and mostly clean. The puncture on his thumb stung. He left it alone.

The light was going. Through the trees he could see the sky turning orange at the western edge and darkening overhead. They hadn't eaten. Kem's stomach felt tight, not from hunger but from the sustained effort of keeping his hands steady for an hour. He was tired in a way that had nothing to do with abilities — a physical tiredness from concentration and the long tail of adrenaline, the kind of fatigue that settled into his forearms and his jaw from clenching it.

He started collecting wood for a fire. Without a Flame Charm — he still had one, tucked in the thinning roster like a single match in an otherwise empty box, but the idea of using it for a campfire now felt wasteful in a way it hadn't a week ago. He'd used Flame Charms for campfires three times in the first week of travel and Vel hadn't said anything, which he now understood was a form of letting him learn the cost on his own schedule. He found dry wood, shaved bark with the knife Vel had given him, and built the fire the way she'd taught him in the highlands — small, contained, starting with the finest shavings and adding sticks in order of thickness.

It took him four tries. The third pile of shavings caught but he smothered it by adding a branch too soon. The fourth time he was patient enough, feeding the flame twig by twig until it could handle the small wood, then the larger pieces. By the time the fire was steady, the sky was fully dark and Dren was dozing against his pack with his injured arm held close to his body.

Vel opened her eyes when the fire caught. She looked at it, then at Kem, and the corner of her mouth moved — not quite a smile, but the acknowledgment of something. She didn't comment on the fire. She just moved, carefully, wincing once at the ribs, until she was closer to the heat.

---

They ate dried meat and a heel of bread from Dren's pack. The meat was tough and oversalted, the bread going stale at the edges. Nobody complained. Dren ate one-handed, chewing slowly, his jaw working with the mechanical patience of someone who'd eaten worse things on worse nights. He went back to sleep almost immediately after, turned on his good side with his pack under his head. His breathing evened out quickly — the sleep of someone who could rest anywhere, a road-earned skill Kem envied with a specificity that surprised him. Three weeks ago he wouldn't have noticed how another person slept.

Vel sat across the fire from Kem. She'd pulled her knees up, arms wrapped around them in a way that was half warmth and half protecting the ribs. The firelight caught the scars on her hands — a topography of forty years of use, small pale lines across her knuckles and fingers from work Kem couldn't catalog but was beginning to understand as accumulated cost. She was fifty-eight. She looked older. She'd looked older since the day he'd met her, but he was starting to read the age differently — not as damage but as evidence.

"Where does it go?" Kem asked.

He hadn't planned the question. It arrived the way his questions arrived lately — not from curiosity in the abstract but from a specific pressure that had been building since the fight, or since the stitching, or since he'd checked his Ledger and felt the weight of 178 settle into him.

Vel looked at him. The firelight moved on her face.

"Zero," he said. "When you hit zero. What happens."

She was quiet for long enough that he thought she wouldn't answer. She did this — let silences run until the other person either repeated the question or gave up. He'd learned not to give up.

"It's not death," she said.

"You've said that. You haven't said what it is."

"No." She looked at the fire. Then at Dren, checking his sleep. Then at Kem. Something was moving behind her expression — a calculation, maybe, about how much to share and when. He recognized it because he'd been watching her make those calculations since the first day of their alliance.

"Zero is a threshold," Vel said. "Not an ending. When you hit it — when you actually reach zero, all of it gone, nothing left in the Ledger — you don't die. You go somewhere."

Kem waited. His hands were resting on his knees and he could feel the dried blood still in the creases of his knuckles.

"There's a place," she said. "On the other side. People live there. Without the Ledger." She said it the way she said most things that mattered — flat, without decoration, as if the words were sufficient and anything added to them would be waste. "No levels. No abilities. No number. They just live."

The fire cracked. A piece of wood shifted and sent a scatter of sparks upward into the dark. Kem watched them rise and die.

"A place without the Ledger," he said.

"Yeah."

"That's real."

"Yeah."

He looked at Dren's sleeping form. The bandaged arm. The steady breathing. Dren was at 138 — or near it, whatever the road had cost him since he'd signed on. A number worn down through years of trade routes and small practical spends. Dren would never reach zero. Dren wasn't trying to. Dren had a daughter and a route and a number he managed the way he managed cargo weight, carefully, fearfully, always knowing the load limit. The idea that zero could be a destination rather than a catastrophe — Kem didn't think that would mean to Dren what it was starting to mean to him.

He sat with it. The concept was enormous and he could feel his mind working at the edges of it, trying to find something to hold on to, a frame of reference. A world without the Ledger. Without the number. Without the constant presence in his awareness that told him what he had and what he could do and what it would cost. He tried to imagine that presence gone — not reduced, not diminished the way it had been diminishing every day, but absent — and couldn't manage it. It was like trying to imagine silence when you'd never been in a room without sound.

"How do you know?" he asked.

"I watched someone cross."

The words landed simply. She didn't elaborate. Kem heard the restraint in it — the careful verb, *watched*, the implication of someone else reaching zero while Vel stood on this side. There was a story in that sentence she wasn't telling him, and he could feel its weight the way you could feel a room's dimensions in the dark before your eyes adjusted. Large. Old. Full of things he hadn't been shown yet.

"Who?"

Vel's jaw moved. Not a tell exactly — she was chewing on the inside of her cheek, a habit he'd noticed only at moments when she was deciding how much truth to let out. "Someone I traveled with. Years ago."

"And they—"

"They reached zero. At a specific place, under specific conditions. And they weren't there anymore. Not dead. Just — gone. Through."

"Through to what?"

"That's what I'm going to find out."

Kem's mind kept circling back to the crossing itself. Someone had been there — alive, present, with a Ledger and a number — and then they were somewhere the Ledger didn't reach. He tried to imagine the moment of it. The last ability gone. The last number winking out. And then just your body. Just your hands and your weight and whatever you'd learned to do without the thing that had defined every choice you'd ever made.

He thought about the stitching. His shaking hands on the needle, the waxed thread pulling through skin, the trembling he couldn't stop but had learned to work around. That was what all of it would be like, on the other side. No fallback. No option to spend your way past a problem you couldn't solve with time and effort and your own two hands.

The thought should have been terrifying. It was, partly. But there was something else underneath the fear — a relief he couldn't fully name, like the prospect of putting down a weight he hadn't realized he was carrying until it began to lighten.

The fire had burned down to a concentrated heat, the flames low and steady now, the surrounding darkness pressing closer. Dren's breathing was a slow rhythm behind him — steady, anchoring, the kind of sound you stopped hearing and then noticed again. Insects in the trees. The night sounds that had terrified Kem his first evening outside the Vault and now registered as the texture of the world settling in around itself.

"A specific place," Kem said. "Specific conditions."

"Yeah."

"So it's not just — reach zero and vanish."

"No. There's a location. A mechanism. It doesn't happen by accident."

He was asking questions because that was what he did under pressure — filled silence with words, tried to assemble understanding from enough small pieces stacked end to end. But the questions were real. He needed to know. Not because the knowledge changed his next step — he'd already committed to the journey in ways he couldn't fully articulate, somewhere between the outrider fight and the stitching of Dren's arm — but because the shape of what they were walking toward had just changed, and he needed his mind to catch up with it.

"Why didn't you tell me before?"

Vel looked at him across the low fire. "Because you didn't need to know before."

That was a Vel answer. Information released on her schedule, in her judgment, according to her assessment of what was necessary and when. He'd accepted it at the beginning because he'd had nothing — no skills, no knowledge, no leverage to demand more. He was starting to accept it differently now. Not because he thought her judgment was wrong but because he'd begun to see what it cost her to carry the information alone. She'd been holding this — the knowledge that zero was a door, that there was a world behind it, that someone she'd traveled with had walked through and not come back — for years. By herself.

"Is that why you keep going?" he asked. "To get to the other side?"

"Yeah."

"Not to prove something. Not a philosophy. You just want to get there."

"I want to get there."

There it was. Under the teaching, the route planning, the careful management of his spending and her budget and the hundred small decisions that kept them moving south each day — a want. Not abstract. Not ideological. Personal. She wanted to reach a specific place for reasons she hadn't told him, and the force of that want had carried her for forty years and brought her here, sitting across a campfire from a twenty-six-year-old former aristocrat she'd recruited because she needed his levels and who was now looking at her with blood under his nails.

He didn't push further. He could see the edge of what she was willing to say, and he'd learned — was still learning — that pushing Vel past that edge produced silence, not answers.

"A world without the Ledger," he said again. Not a question this time. He was turning it over, letting it sit in his mind, looking at it from different sides the way you'd turn an unfamiliar object in your hands. "No levels."

"No levels."

"No abilities."

"No abilities."

"How do they live?"

"With their hands. With whatever they can build and grow and carry. The way people lived before the Ledger, I'd guess, or the way people in the Margins almost live now — except without the system pressing on them. Without the number."

Kem's face was hot on the fire side and cold on the other. He thought about the Vault — the ceremonial halls where nobody did anything, the Conservatory Rite where his father sat with blank eyes, the sealed wing that nobody discussed. He thought about 300, what it had felt like to have everything and do nothing with it. Then he thought about 178, and his hands, and the ugly stitches holding Dren's arm together.

"I can't tell," he said, "if that sounds like the best thing I've ever heard or the worst."

Vel almost laughed. Not quite — a sound through her nose, a small expulsion of air that was as close to amusement as the cracked ribs would allow. "Yeah," she said. "That's about right."

They sat with the fire between them. Dren slept. The insects kept their rhythm in the trees. Somewhere to the north, five disorganized agents were presumably regrouping, and Sable Orm was presumably learning from everything she'd watched, and the Hoarded Council was presumably drafting retrieval protocols in language that classified human beings as logistical concerns, and none of it felt as immediate as the fact that there was a place where the number went silent and people lived without it and Vel had spent forty years of her life trying to get there.

Kem looked at his hands in the firelight. Blood in the creases. A puncture on his thumb. The beginning of calluses in places that had been soft three weeks ago. He closed his fists and opened them.

"I'm going to sleep," he said.

"I'll take first watch."

"You should sleep. Your ribs."

"I'll take first watch," Vel said again, and the repetition closed the subject.

He lay down on the ground with his pack under his head, the way Dren had shown him on the third night of travel. The earth was cold and uneven and a root pressed into his shoulder blade. He shifted, didn't find anything better, and stopped trying. Above him through the tree canopy, stars. He didn't know their names. He'd never needed to — in the Vault, the ceiling was carved stone, and the sky was something that existed in paintings.

He fell asleep thinking about a world where you couldn't do anything except what your body and your hands could manage, and he dreamed about his father sitting in the Conservatory with empty eyes, except in the dream his father's Ledger read zero and his face wasn't empty at all, and Kem woke in the gray light before dawn with the image already dissolving and the fire burned down to white ash and Vel still sitting where he'd left her, watching the dark between the trees.
