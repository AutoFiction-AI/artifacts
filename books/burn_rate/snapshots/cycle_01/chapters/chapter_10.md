# Chapter 10

The settlement looked the same.

That was the first thing Vel noticed from the ridge — the careful placement of it, the stone-and-timber structures arranged around the communal well, the gardens along the south edge where the light was best. Twelve years, and the rooflines hadn't changed. Someone had added a second story to the storehouse near the eastern road, and there was a new fence around what used to be open pasture, but the bones of the place were exactly as she'd left them. Thessa had always been good at maintenance.

"How far?" Dren asked. His left arm hung at his side, the stitches Kem had given him pulling every time he shifted his pack. The wound wasn't infected, which was the best thing Vel could say about it.

"Half an hour down."

"And this person — she's expecting us?"

"No."

Kem was quiet for once. He'd been watching Vel since she'd angled their route west that morning, deviating from the path she'd marked on Maren's journal the night before. He didn't ask why. He was learning when to ask and when to wait, which was either progress or a problem, depending on what was growing in his silence.

The path down from the ridge was switchback gravel, eroded in the runoff channels where spring melt had cut through. Vel's feet remembered the route before her mind agreed to make it — the footing at the second switchback, the place where the gravel thinned and the rock underneath slipped if you hit it wrong. Twelve years, and her body still knew the grade. The heat of early summer sat on the valley like a hand. Dry air, the smell of baked clay, and something growing in the gardens below — beans, maybe, or the low herb rows Thessa had always kept near the south wall.

They reached the settlement's northern edge and Vel saw the cart.

It was parked outside the storehouse, clean-sided, well-built, and entirely wrong for this place. Not a trader's cart — the hardware was too precise, the wood lacquered rather than oiled, the axle bearing Hoarded-standard machining that no local smith could produce. A logistics cart, the kind that showed up when someone institutional wanted to look like they belonged.

She put her hand out and Kem stopped. Dren was already reading the cart the way he read everything — calculating its cost, its origin, its implications for them.

"Hoarded," he said.

"Yeah."

"Representative?"

"Looks like."

They circled wide, approaching from the south along the garden rows where the bean frames gave partial cover. The settlement was small enough that they'd be seen eventually, but Vel wanted to see first. She always wanted to see first. The arithmetic of observation before action — the cheapest intelligence you could buy.

She saw Thessa before Thessa saw her.

Thessa was standing near the well, talking to a woman in Hoarded trade dress — the tailored linen, the clean hemline, the leather document case that screamed institutional authority. The woman was maybe forty-five, average build, dark hair pulled back in a style that looked like it had been set that morning with more care than the rest of her appearance warranted. She was talking about delivery schedules and settlement quota adjustments, her voice carrying in the clear air, professional and unhurried.

Then her voice slowed.

Not like someone choosing words carefully. Not a pause for thought. The deceleration was mechanical, as if the process producing speech had lost a gear somewhere behind her eyes. Her mouth kept moving for a beat after the sound dropped away, and her gaze — which had been fixed on Thessa's face with focused attention — drifted sideways to a point approximately three feet to the left of anything. Not blank. Vacant in the specific, particular way that Vel had seen only once before, in a man at a trade post three years back who'd left the Vault at two-ninety and carried something with him he couldn't name.

Stillness. Walking around in a settlement three weeks south of the Vault, negotiating bean quotas.

The episode lasted maybe four seconds. The woman blinked. Her eyes reseated. She picked up her sentence from roughly where she'd left it, smoothing over the gap with the practiced ease of someone who'd learned to paper over these absences the way you'd patch a crack in a wall — quickly, before anyone looked too closely. Her hand, holding a stylus over her document case, hadn't moved during the whole episode. She made a note and smiled.

Thessa's expression didn't change. She'd seen this before. She'd been watching it happen, visit after visit, and she'd decided something about it that Vel would discover soon enough.

Vel's hands had gone cold. Not from fear — from the particular chill of recognition. She'd spent forty years in the field. She'd seen what spending cost. But this was the other thing, the thing the Hoarded didn't talk about: what preservation cost when you carried it long enough. The condition they called discipline, eating its way through a woman who was out here negotiating settlement quotas because the system needed someone to do it, and the system didn't care that the someone was going intermittently absent behind her own eyes.

Kem was standing close enough behind Vel that she could hear his breathing change. He'd gone very still — not Hoarded still, but the rigid stillness of someone seeing a familiar nightmare in an unfamiliar place. His father's empty eyes. Here, in a dusty settlement, in a woman who was writing notes about bean deliveries.

"That's —" he started.

"I know."

"My father does that. That exact —"

"I know what it is."

The Hoarded representative finished her note, tucked the stylus into her case, and said something about returning next month for the updated figures. Thessa nodded. The representative walked toward her cart with the measured steps of someone conserving something she might not know she was losing.

Thessa watched her go. Then she looked up, past the well, past the garden fence, toward the bean frames where Vel was standing with a Hoarded boy and a wounded trader, and her face changed twice in the space of a breath.

First: recognition. The jolt of it in her body, shoulders pulling back, hands coming away from her sides. Then the training kicked in — the persona she'd built for settlement life, the practiced approachability that had kept her safe and useful for twelve years. Her face rearranged into welcome. She said something to a passing neighbor — something light, something about needing a moment — and walked toward them.

The composure held until she was close enough for Vel to see her eyes. Under the welcome, they were furious.

"Vel." It came out like she'd been storing it. Not rehearsing — Thessa hadn't rehearsed this. She'd spent twelve years not preparing for it, and the unpreparedness was in every syllable. "Well. Come in, I've got — sit down. Let me put water on. You look —" Her eyes cut to Kem, to Dren, to the packs and the road dust and the specific wear of people who'd been moving fast. "Sit down."

"Thessa."

"I said come in." The pleasantness cranked up a notch, which meant the anger underneath had too. She was already turning toward her house — the same stone-fronted house at the northwest corner, the one she'd been living in when Vel left twelve years ago. "Bring your — are they with you?"

"Yeah."

"Bring them. There's food. Do you want bread? The bread here's actually good, we've got a — it doesn't matter. Come in."

Vel followed her. Behind her, Kem's silence had a different quality than the one he'd carried down from the ridge. And Dren's boots scuffed gravel in a rhythm that said he'd decided to reserve judgment until judgment was useful.

---

Thessa's house smelled like dried herbs and warm stone. The front room was the right size for one person who'd decided exactly how much space she needed — a table, four chairs, a cooking alcove, shelves lined with labeled jars. Everything maintained. Everything placed. The geometry of a life calculated for minimum cost and maximum stability. Level seventy-eight, spending maybe one per year. Forty years of arithmetic producing this: a clean house, a reputation, and a front door that stayed shut against the things that couldn't be calculated.

She put a kettle on the fire without asking. Cut bread from a round loaf — thick slices, the crust dark and cracked from a proper stone oven. Found cups. Poured water for the kettle. The hospitality was so practiced it was almost an ability in itself, and it served the same function: it cost something small and produced something useful and kept the larger costs at bay.

Kem sat where she pointed. Dren leaned against the wall near the door, his bad arm held close to his body, and said nothing. Vel stood.

"Sit down," Thessa said.

"I'm fine."

"You're in my house. Sit."

Vel sat.

The kettle worked. Thessa poured — real tea, not the boiled-root approximation they'd been drinking for weeks. She set cups in front of Vel, then Kem, then Dren, and sat down across from them with her hands wrapped around her own, and for a long moment the only sound was the fire popping and the settlement's ambient noise through the window: someone calling a name, the scrape of a tool, a gate swinging.

"Twelve years," Thessa said.

"Yeah."

"Twelve years, and you show up with a Hoarded boy and a man who looks like he lost a fight with a grain silo."

Dren lifted his bad arm. "Grain silo won."

Thessa didn't smile. She was studying Vel with the specific attention of someone who'd spent eight years traveling beside her and twelve years refusing to think about it. Running her own calculations. What this visit cost. What it would require. What was being asked underneath the asking.

"You're heading south."

"I need route information. The paths through to the Margins interior."

"Of course you do." Thessa sipped her tea. Then again. The pauses were structural, the cordiality a wall built of offered cups and good bread. "The southern routes have changed since you were through. The old paths past the river are washed out — flooding three years ago. There's a passable crossing two miles east of where the bridge used to be, but it's rough."

"What about the Margins interior? Past the —"

"Have some bread." The plate pushed toward her. "It's good bread. A woman here bakes it in a stone oven she built herself. No abilities, just — have some."

Vel took a piece. It was good. She chewed and swallowed and the specific cruelty of Thessa's hospitality settled over the table: it was sincere. The bread was offered because Thessa wanted her fed, and also because every cup of tea and slice of bread was a delay, a redirection, a way to fill the room with small kindnesses until there was no space left for the conversation they were both circling.

"I can't stay long," Vel said.

"You never could." The first cut through the pleasantness, so quick Thessa sealed it before the blood showed. "I mean — you've been traveling. You must be tired."

"Almost three weeks on the road."

"With him?" A nod toward Kem. Thessa studied him the way she'd studied Vel — reading the signs, the level arithmetic, the body's testimony. "You're Hoarded."

"I was," Kem said. He was sitting with the old Vault posture, straight-backed, hands on the table — the training mixed with the new directness. "It's — I'm. That's complicated."

"Always is." Thessa's gaze lingered on his hands — the particular smoothness that only Vault hands had, though calluses were roughening the palms now. She looked at his face. Then at Vel. "Vel."

"What."

"What level."

Kem opened his mouth and Vel cut in. "His business."

"I'm at one-seventy-eight," Kem said. He glanced at Vel. "It's fine."

Thessa's cup stopped halfway to her mouth. Seventy-eight herself. She knew what numbers meant. Her face did something complicated — surprise, then calculation, then a settling into exhausted recognition, like watching someone confirm a diagnosis she'd been dreading. "You found one."

"Thessa —"

"You actually found one." She set the cup down. The ceramic hit the wood harder than she'd meant. "After all that."

Vel felt the ground shift underneath the conversation. Not the stone floor, which was solid as it had been for twenty years, but the terrain between them — the accumulated sediment of twelve years cracking along fault lines that had been waiting for exactly this weight.

"I need the southern routes," Vel said. "That's all I came for."

"That's never all you come for." Thessa's voice was still shaped like accommodation, but the structure underneath was thinner now, pulled taut. "You've never once shown up somewhere just for information. There's always the thing past the thing. The next route. The next person who needs you to spend on them, or —"

She stopped herself. Took a breath. Pushed the bread plate two inches to the left — an adjustment that meant nothing except that her hands needed something to do.

"The southern routes," Thessa said, her voice reset to functional. "I can give you what I have. But the information's partial. I haven't gone south in twelve years. Because twelve years ago I decided I wanted to be alive at seventy-eight instead of dead at nothing, and you decided that wasn't brave enough for you."

"I'm not asking you to come."

"No. You just leave, and the leaving does the asking for you."

Kem was watching this with his head tilted slightly, processing. Dren was watching the door — and the window, and the path the Hoarded representative had taken — because Dren always watched exits the way Vel watched numbers, with the attentive pessimism of someone who'd learned that knowing where to run was cheaper than fighting.

Thessa stood and went to a shelf. She pulled down a folded cloth that turned out to be a hand-drawn map — the paths south of the settlement, marked in the careful hand of someone who'd gathered secondhand information from traders and travelers over years without going herself. Trade routes, water sources, danger zones.

"This is what I have. It's secondhand. I haven't verified anything past the river." She spread it on the table. Her hand lingered on the northwest corner, where the route from the settlement was drawn. "The Margins are worse than when you — when you went through before. Hoarded enforcement's heavier. Contracted labor operations that weren't there ten years ago."

Vel leaned forward. The map was useful but incomplete — distances without terrain detail, water sources marked but not confirmed. She needed to cross-reference.

She pulled Maren's journal from the inside pocket of her pack.

The motion was habitual. Eight years of consulting these pages, comparing routes, tracing the handwriting that wasn't hers along margins crowded with annotations in two different hands. She opened to the pages covering the southern approaches — Maren's careful cartography overlapping with Vel's own additions — and started matching coordinates to Thessa's map.

Thessa was saying something about the river crossing when she stopped.

She was staring at the journal. At the battered cover, the bent spine, the pages showing eight years of handling and the weather they'd taken before that. She recognized it the way you'd recognize a face — not piece by piece but whole, the accumulated familiarity of something you'd once known well.

"You're still carrying that."

Vel's hands stilled on the pages.

"Maren's." Thessa said it like a stone she'd been holding in her mouth. "That's her journal. I'd recognize that binding — she repaired it with gut thread from that trader outside Hallson's, the one who couldn't tie a knot to save his life." She let out a breath that was almost a laugh and entirely wasn't. "Twelve years, Vel. Twelve years since you walked out of here, and you're still carrying her journal, still heading south, still —"

"Thessa."

"Still chasing the same thing." The accommodation was gone now. Not cracked — lifted off, like a mask set down carefully on the table next to the bread plate. What was underneath wasn't rage. It was grief so old it had calcified into something load-bearing, something that held up the rest of her life, and Vel was standing in her kitchen pulling it out of the wall. "She crossed. I know she crossed. You told me — no. You didn't tell me. You told a trader who told Barrens who told me because you couldn't walk ten minutes to my door and say it yourself." She pressed her lips together. "She crossed and you've been trying to follow ever since."

Vel didn't answer. The journal was open on the table, Maren's handwriting beside Thessa's map, and the convergence of those two documents in this room was more than she'd been ready for. The herbs drying on the shelf above the alcove filled the air with rosemary and something sharper — thyme, maybe — and the smell was the same as twelve years ago, and Vel's ribs ached where she'd cracked them two weeks before, and she had twenty-two levels left, and the journal's pages were warm from being carried against her body.

"I don't need to explain this to you," Vel said.

"No. You never do." Thessa turned to Kem. The shift in her attention was deliberate — a blade aimed with precision. "Do you know how long she's been at this?"

Kem's eyes moved between them. "The journey started three weeks ago."

"Not the journey." Thessa's voice was calm now, dangerously calm, the kind that came only after the performed kindness had been fully stripped and what was left was bone. "Her. This. Looking for someone like you." She paused. "A Hoarded lord willing to burn down. She's been looking for years."

The room went quiet. The fire popped. Outside, someone's gate swung on its hinge.

"She came through here," Thessa continued. She was looking at Kem, not Vel, and her voice was steady in the way of a support beam under too much load — holding, but the wood grain showed. "Five years ago. Sat in that chair you're sitting in. And she asked me about Hoarded defectors. Whether I'd heard of any. Whether any lords had walked out. Whether anyone at two-fifty or above might be persuaded to spend down."

Kem didn't move.

"Five years ago," Thessa said. "She wasn't asking because she was curious. She was looking for a component. Someone with enough levels to make the crossing work." She looked at Vel. "You didn't find one then. You kept looking. And here you are with a lord at one-seventy-eight, and you expect me to —"

"That's enough." Vel's voice was quiet, which was the wrong kind of quiet — the budgeted kind, the flat practical register that meant the cost of continuing this conversation was higher than she could afford.

"It isn't." Thessa was shaking. Not the tremor of immediate anger — the deep structural vibration of something that had been bearing weight for twelve years and had just been asked to carry this too. "It isn't enough. You left me. You left everyone who loved you because the spending mattered more, and now you're doing it again with him, and he deserves to know what you are."

"What I am."

"Someone who sees levels before she sees people." Thessa's eyes were wet and she wasn't bothering with it. The tears tracked down her face and she let them, because hiding would have been another accommodation and she was done accommodating. "Who's been running the numbers on every spender she's met for five years, looking for someone tall enough to reach the shelf. I loved you. Maren loved you. And you used us both — me to learn the routes, her to find the threshold, and him —" a gesture toward Kem — "to make the crossing. You're the most generous person I've ever met and the cruelest, and you don't even know the difference."

The silence that followed was a physical presence in the room. Vel could feel it pressing against her cracked ribs. The house smelled like rosemary and thyme and bread and warm stone, and Thessa was crying — not the loud kind, not performance — the exhausted, quiet kind that came after years of words held back finally let go, the cost of twelve years of silence paid all at once.

Kem was watching Vel. His face had changed. Not anger — he didn't have enough context for anger, not yet. But something had shifted behind his eyes, a question assembling itself that he wasn't going to voice in this room. Something filed away. Troubling but not yet catastrophic, the way a hairline crack in a load-bearing wall is troubling — you don't know yet whether it's structural or surface, but you know you can't stop looking at it.

"The southern routes," Vel said, and her voice was wrong — flattened, over-controlled, the voice she used when the cost of being in a room had exceeded what she'd budgeted. "You said the information was partial."

"Don't you dare." Thessa wiped her face with the back of her hand. "Don't sit in my house and redirect to logistics after I just —"

"The Hoarded rep." Dren's voice from the wall, carrying the room the way a blunt object carries weight. He hadn't moved, but he was looking through the window with the attention of a man who'd been watching something develop for the last thirty seconds. "She's coming this way."

Thessa went still. A different kind of still — the kind Vel recognized from their years running routes together, when something practical interrupted something emotional and the practical won because the practical had teeth.

"She comes once a month," Thessa said. Her voice was unsteady but operational. "Quota reviews. Settlement reports. And —" She stopped.

"And what," Vel said.

Thessa looked at the table. At the bread she'd cut, the tea she'd poured, the map and the journal lying side by side. Then she said it, the thing she'd been circling: "They offered me a deal. The Hoarded. Three years ago. Economic security for the settlement — guaranteed supply routes, subsidized materials, a standing medical allocation. Enough to stop worrying."

"In exchange for what."

"Route information. Traveler reports. Who comes through, where they're heading, whether anyone asks about the southern paths." She swallowed. "I took it."

The information landed in Vel's chest the way a stone lands in a pack you're already carrying — not surprising, just heavier. She'd stopped being surprised by the Hoarded's reach years ago. But the weight of this was specific: Thessa, who had loved her, who had stayed behind out of a justified and reasonable fear, had been trading information to the same system Vel had spent her whole life pushing against. And the deal wasn't cowardice. The deal was survival math performed by a woman at seventy-eight in a settlement that needed a medical allocation more than it needed principles.

"Does she know we're here," Vel said.

"She saw you from the well. I don't — she's not quick, Vel. Her episodes have gotten worse. Last two visits, she lost a full minute during quota review. Just stood there, eyes gone." Through the window, the representative was crossing the open ground toward Thessa's house, her steps measured, her document case under her arm. "I've been bargaining with a woman the system's eating alive. I know what that looks like. I know what that makes me."

"It makes you someone who kept three families fed through winter."

"Don't." Thessa shook her head. "Don't make it clean. It isn't clean. She sits in my house and her speech stops and her eyes go empty and I hand her reports about people like you because if I don't, the supply routes close and this settlement starves." She pushed back from the table. "That's what stopping gets you. That's the price of being alive at seventy-eight."

The representative was forty yards from the door. She was moving with the deliberate pace of someone whose body ran on schedule even when the person inside it didn't, and there was a communication scroll in her free hand — the short-range kind that could reach a Hoarded relay station within thirty miles. If she'd already sent a message from the well, the response could be close.

"We go now," Vel said. She stood. Folded the journal closed and slid it into her pack, the pages aligned, the cover pressed flat — the habitual care she showed the object, and she could see Thessa watching her do it, watching the tenderness she applied to something that wasn't hers, and Thessa's face did something Vel couldn't afford to look at.

"Back door," Thessa said. Her voice had gone operational — the route-runner's voice, the one Vel remembered from a decade of shared decisions made fast. "Through the kitchen. She parks east — you exit west, take the lane between my place and Garran's, cut to the orchards. There's an irrigation ditch that runs south for a mile. Stay below the lip."

"Thank you," Kem said. Quiet. He was looking at Thessa with an attention that Vel found hard to watch — not because it was wrong, but because she recognized it. Kem looked at people and saw them. It was the thing she'd first noticed about him in the forest weeks ago, and it was the thing that made him irreplaceable in her plans and dangerous to them, and right now he was seeing a woman who'd just dismantled Vel's authority in his eyes and was still, in this moment, helping them escape.

"Don't thank me." Thessa was looking at Vel. "Just go. You were always going to go."

---

They went through Thessa's kitchen, past the shelves of labeled jars and a window box of herbs, through a back door into the narrow lane between houses. The settlement was small and the lane was empty and the sun was white overhead, pressing down.

Vel ran a Perception sweep as they cleared the lane. One level — quick, minimal, the most compressed version of the ability she could manage. The information hit her like a brief headache: the representative was at Thessa's front door, her communication scroll active, a Hoarded relay signal already pulsing east. Two people moving on the eastern road, fast, in the nondescript clothes that settlement enforcers wore when they wanted to blend. Response time: fifteen, maybe twenty minutes before they reached the settlement and got a direction.

Twenty-one. She was at twenty-one.

They cut through the orchards — low fruit trees, not well-tended, the branches overgrown in the way of trees that had been Fertility-Charmed once and left to manage on their own. The irrigation ditch ran along the eastern edge, a channel dug into dry earth, deep enough to walk in without showing above the lip if you kept your head down. Dren dropped in first. Kem followed, his height a problem — he had to hunch, his back curved to keep his head below the ditch edge. Vel went last and the clay under her boots was dry and cracked and the channel smelled like standing water and hot earth.

They moved south. The ditch offered cover for maybe a mile. Vel kept the Perception information running in the back of her awareness — the two enforcers approaching from the east, the representative's signal still pulsing, the general shape of what was coming — and calculated. The enforcers would reach the settlement, get a direction from the representative or from whoever had seen them leave, and follow south. Level two-hundred range, probably. Settlement-grade abilities. Not trained for pursuit, but they'd have numbers and reserves that Vel couldn't match.

"How long?" Dren asked, low.

"Fifteen minutes before they're pointed our way. Maybe less if the rep had a better look than I think."

"The concealment from before won't work again?"

Vel looked at Kem. He was moving through the ditch with his shoulders pulled in, concentrating on the terrain — the footing, the turns, the places where the ditch wall had eroded. His face was the one she'd come to know as his working face: calculating expenditure against need. The arithmetic she'd taught him.

"I can do it," Kem said. "Concealment overlay. Shield if they get close. Evasion shimmer at the edges." He said it without looking back. "Eighteen levels, give or take. Down to one-sixty."

"I know what it costs."

"So do I."

There it was — not defiance, not the exhilarated recklessness of his early spending. Just the flat statement of someone who'd learned to count and was counting. A month ago he'd have thrown power at the problem and worried about the arithmetic afterward. Now he was leading with the numbers. Vel wasn't sure if she'd taught him that or if he'd learned it by losing half of everything he had.

"Do it," she said.

His hands came up. The concealment overlay went first — not the wild, blown-out burst he'd have produced three weeks ago, but something shaped, fitted around the three of them and the ditch like a shell pulled over the lip of the earth. The light bent. The footfalls muffled. The air around them took on the visual quality of empty ground, unremarkable, nothing to see.

Seven levels. Vel felt it settle around them — the blur, the dampened sound, the shimmer at the edges where the effect met unaltered air. Not perfect. A trained eye would catch the warp. But competent, and competence from Kem still had the faint surprise of a new thing.

Vel dropped a small concealment of her own over their trail — a one-level wisp that obscured the boot prints in the ditch clay, the disturbed dust at the channel's lip, the residual signs of three people passing. It wouldn't hold for more than twenty minutes, but twenty minutes was the difference between enforcers following tracks and enforcers following guesses.

Twenty. She was at twenty. The number sat in her awareness like a word she couldn't stop hearing.

They moved faster. The ditch curved away from the settlement road into open ground, angling toward a dry creek bed that ran south through scrubby lowlands. Dren kept pace despite his arm, his face rigid with the effort of not using it for balance. The poorly healed stitches pulled at the wound every time the footing shifted, and Vel could see his jaw working, the specific expression of a man converting pain into motion because the alternative was stopping.

Behind them, the enforcers reached the settlement. Vel felt it through the Perception data still fading in her awareness — the disturbance pattern, the way ability use rippled through ambient fields when someone activated a scan. The scan passed over the ditch. Passed over the concealment. Moved on, either because the overlay held or because they weren't looking in the right place or because Thessa had sent them the wrong direction.

Vel didn't want to think about which one.

Forty minutes south, the concealment flickered. Kem's face showed the strain — not of raw power but of sustained precision, the fatigue of holding something careful rather than throwing something large.

"It's going," he said.

"How much longer?"

"Minutes."

A burst shield went up as the concealment thinned — five levels, reflexive, a defensive shell that would absorb one hit if it came. The transition from stealth to defense was smoother than anything Kem had managed before, the kind of tactical pivot that required thinking two moves ahead instead of reacting to the last one. Then the evasion shimmer — six levels layered at the edges of their movement, blurring their position if anyone used a tracking ability. The shimmer rippled and settled.

Eighteen total. His hands were shaking — not from exhaustion, from the specific fatigue of precision work, the way a carpenter's hands shake after dovetailing rather than hammering.

They cleared the ditch and dropped into the creek bed. Open ground stretched south — low scrub, dry grass, the hills rising ahead toward the river and whatever lay past it. No pursuit visible. The enforcers either hadn't found the trail or were still searching the settlement or were heading the wrong way.

Kem checked his Ledger. The pause was brief, internal, the flicker of attention that came with reading a number that mattered.

"One-sixty," he said.

One-sixty. He'd been near three hundred a month ago. The arithmetic of the descent was written in the flatness of his voice, in his steady hands even as they shook, in the way he reported the number without the frustration that would have been there four weeks ago or the exhilaration that had been there six. Just cost. Just the next number down.

They kept moving south. The creek bed turned into a trail and the trail curved toward low hills that would take them to the river and the Margins' edge beyond it. The afternoon sun was white and merciless on the dry ground, and the heat rose from the earth in visible distortion, and the air tasted like dust.

Dren walked ahead, reading the terrain with the practiced eye of a man who'd been on these roads his whole working life. His arm was bad and getting worse — the gait adjustments more pronounced now, the way he unconsciously shielded the injured side with his body turning the walk into a slow, lopsided march. He needed a healer. He needed his daughter. He was here, moving south through the heat, for reasons that had stopped being purely transactional and that he hadn't, Vel suspected, fully examined.

Vel walked behind Kem and watched the back of his head and thought about Thessa. About the word *component*. About the fact that five years ago she had sat in that chair and asked about Hoarded defectors, and the woman who had loved her — who had spent eight years beside her and twelve years refusing to think about her — had watched her do it and understood exactly what it meant.

Thessa wasn't wrong. That was the weight of it — not that Thessa was bitter, or unfair, or speaking from hurt rather than truth. She was doing all of those things, and she was also right. Vel had been looking for a Kem. For years. Not this specific person — not the particular man walking ahead of her with dust in his hair and shaking hands and a Ledger that would have been an insult in the Vault a month ago. She'd been looking for anyone with enough levels and enough willingness to burn down. A component. She'd found him, and she'd guided him, and she'd taught him to spend with economy and to fight with precision and to value each level as it went. And all of that was real. And all of that was also, underneath, the cultivation of a tool she needed.

Kem didn't turn around. He didn't ask the question Vel could feel forming in the space between them — the question Thessa had planted with the accuracy of someone who knew exactly where the foundation was weakest. *Five years. She's been looking for someone like you.* The question would grow. It would grow through the river crossing, through the Margins, through whatever came after. And when it arrived, Vel didn't know what she'd say, because the truth — that she'd needed him before she'd known him, and that knowing him hadn't changed the need, only made the need into something that could hurt — was the kind of answer that explained everything and repaired nothing.

The trail widened. The hills rose. South, toward the river and the Margins and the place where numbers ran out and something else began. The journal pressed against Vel's spine through the pack, Maren's handwriting carried on her back the way you carry something that is either a promise or a debt and might be both.

Her Ledger read twenty. Her ribs ached. The settlement where Thessa lived was shrinking behind them into the valley's haze, and Vel thought about Thessa standing in her kitchen with her labeled jars and her good bread and her deal with a woman the system was eating alive, and she did not look back, because looking back was an expenditure she had never learned to budget for.
