# Chapter 11

The bridge had been gone a long time. What remained — gray timbers slumped into the current, iron bolts orange with rust — suggested years of neglect rather than sudden collapse. The southern end had rotted away entirely. The northern pilings stood like teeth in a broken jaw, water rushing between them in brown surges that carried sticks and clods of soil.

Kem stood at the bank and watched the river. It was wider than he'd expected, twenty meters at least, loud with the kind of sound that water made when it was working rather than resting. The surface moved fast and uneven, cresting over submerged rocks, carrying silt from somewhere upstream where it had rained recently. South of Thessa's settlement the terrain had changed — less cultivated, fewer fences, the road itself narrowing from packed earth to a track that was mostly suggestion. This was the boundary between the Spent lands and whatever lay beyond them.

"How deep?" he asked.

Vel was crouching at the bank, testing the current with a stripped branch. She pulled it out and studied the wet line. "It'll be waist-deep at the edges. Chest-high in the middle, maybe higher. Rain upstream pushed it up."

"So we spend."

"No."

"Bridge Conjuration would take three levels. I could span it in—"

"No."

"I've got Water Walking at—"

"You're going to cross it." She pulled the branch out of the water. The current had already stripped the bark below the waterline, leaving white wood that looked gnawed. "With your legs."

Dren was sitting on a flat rock a few meters back, working his boots off with his good hand. His left arm moved carefully — the silo wound had healed into a knotted ridge of scar that pulled tight when he extended the elbow, Kem's inexpert stitches leaving puckered seams in the skin like a badly mended seam in leather. The arm worked, but not easily. He tucked the laces inside the boots and lashed them to the top of his pack in a configuration Kem had watched him perform half a dozen times — the leather soles turned outward, the knot cinched with a trader's twist that left a tail he could pull to release. He'd seen Dren do the same knot on cargo bales, saddlebags, and once on a loose tent stake that wouldn't hold in soft ground.

"Current's stronger than it looks," Dren said, without looking up. "Don't try to go straight across. You'll end up half a kilometer downstream. Angle with it."

"Right." Kem was already assessing his options. Bridge Conjuration, three levels. He could span the river in twenty seconds. Flight Charm, two levels, but he'd never used one and the calibration problems from his first weeks still haunted him. Strength boost and just bull through—

"You heard me," Vel said.

He looked at her. She was standing now, the branch discarded, her expression the flat practical one she used when she'd already made a decision and considered the discussion administrative.

"The river's dangerous."

"The river's crossable."

He wanted to say more. He had arguments — specific, reasonable arguments about risk and resource management that she herself had taught him. But the branch of his thinking that might have pressed the point was thin and new, and the branch that said *she knows what she's doing, she's been doing this for forty years* was thick and established. He closed his mouth.

Dren went first. He hoisted his pack, adjusted the strap to favor his bad arm, and walked to the bank without ceremony. He'd done this before — river crossings were part of trade routes the way stairs were part of buildings. He stepped in and the water came to his thighs. He moved with the unhurried steadiness of a man reading terrain, each foot planted, tested, weighted. The current pushed against him and his scarred arm swung for balance, the ridge of tissue pulling visibly, but his face didn't change. He was watching the water the way Vel watched ability-use situations — where the pressure eased, where the footing was solid, where the cost was lowest.

Halfway across, the water reached his chest. He lifted his pack above his head with both arms, the bad one protesting in the set of his jaw but holding. He paused there for three breaths, adjusted his angle, and continued.

Twenty minutes from bank to bank. He pulled himself out on the far side, shook water from his pack, and sat down to wring out his shirt. His hand went to his left arm and ran a thumb along the scar — a gesture Kem had seen him make a dozen times since the healing, touching the ridge the way you'd touch a pocket to check your coin was still there.

Vel went next. She stripped her boots, tied them to her pack with a knot that was not a trader's knot but served the same function, and entered the water as if she were stepping into a room she'd visited before. She was shorter than Dren and the water hit her ribs sooner, but she compensated with a lower stance and a reading of current that came from decades of this kind of work. She didn't pause in the middle. Didn't lift her pack. Just moved across the water with the economy she applied to everything — no wasted motion, no wasted effort, the body doing exactly what was required and not one thing more.

Ten minutes. She climbed out on the far bank and wrung her shirt without looking back.

Both of them were across. The river ran between Kem and the rest of his group, loud and brown and indifferent to his situation.

He took his boots off.

---

He entered the river at the point Dren had recommended, twenty meters upstream of a flat rock on the far bank. The water was colder than he'd prepared for — mountain-fed, carrying something that felt like January even now, a cold that went past skin and settled in the bones of his feet. His ankles went rigid. He took another step. His foot found a rock slick with something green and he adjusted, testing, the way he'd watched Dren do it.

The water reached his waist. The current pressed against his thighs with a flat, steady insistence — not violent, just certain. His pack shifted on his shoulders. He gripped the straps and leaned into the push, his feet working at the riverbed, finding gravel and stone that was uneven and smooth in the wrong places.

He was a third of the way across when the bottom dropped.

It wasn't a dramatic plunge — six inches, maybe eight. But it put the water at his chest and changed the balance of the situation entirely. The current that had been pushing at his legs now pressed against his torso, and the difference between thigh-deep resistance and chest-deep resistance was the difference between leaning into a wind and leaning into a wall. He staggered, caught himself, took a step that found nothing solid and another before the first resolved.

"Angle more," Dren called from the far bank. "You're going too straight."

Kem angled. His foot slipped on something underneath and his knee went sideways and the river took the opportunity to suggest, firmly, that he lie down. He didn't. He got his balance back through a motion that involved both arms and no dignity. The pack was soaked. His clothes pulled at him, heavy in a way that made the Strength boost he'd been using to carry the pack for the last three days seem less like waste and more like basic sense.

He was halfway across. The water was at his shoulders now and the current was a presence — not the metaphorical kind, the literal kind, the way any sustained physical force becomes something you bargain with. He could feel it shifting in patterns that had nothing to do with him and would continue long after he was gone. His feet found a ledge of rock and he stopped, breathing hard, the water lapping at his chin when the current surged.

He could see Vel on the far bank. She was watching with the particular attention she gave to things she was evaluating. Dren was sitting nearby, squeezing water from his shirt.

Kem took another step. The ledge ended and his foot went into nothing and the current took him.

It wasn't dramatic. He didn't go under in a crash of white water. The river simply moved him sideways, his feet losing the bottom, his body becoming a thing being carried rather than a thing walking. He reached for the bottom with his toes and couldn't find it. His pack pulled him backward. He swallowed water — not a mouthful, a gulp he hadn't intended, and it burned in his throat and he coughed and the coughing pulled more in.

He went under.

The sound changed. The river closed over his head and everything became brown and churning and loud in a way that was not the loud of the surface but the loud of being inside something. The cold was enormous. He kicked and his head broke the surface and he gasped and the river put him under again, casually, the way you'd push a floating stick back down to watch it bob.

His shin cracked against a rock. The pain was sharp and particular — not the generalized hurt of exertion but the specific wrongness of bone meeting stone through thin skin. He used it. Pushed off the rock with his battered leg, got his head up, coughed water until his ribs ached from the coughing.

He was ten meters downstream of where he'd been.

"Get your feet downstream!" Vel shouted. Her voice competed with the river and mostly lost, but the shape of the words carried. "Feet first — don't fight it, ride it to the shallows!"

He got his feet downstream. The river dragged him over rocks — his back scraped against something that caught the fabric of his shirt and tore it and scraped the skin underneath in a long hot line from shoulder blade to ribs. Another rock caught his pack and spun him and he went under again, briefly, long enough for the cold to fill his ears and the certainty to fill everything else: the river could kill him and it was not bothering to try.

When he surfaced this time, he was near the far bank — the current had deposited him in a shallow where the water was only waist-deep and he could stand. He stood. His legs shook from cold and effort. His shin was bleeding freely, the water around it going pink.

He was forty meters downstream of where Vel and Dren waited. The crossing point with the flat rock was behind him. Between his position and theirs, the river deepened again — a channel close to the bank where the water moved fast and dark.

He waded upstream through the shallows. They were deceptive — waist-deep becoming chest-deep in two steps, the bottom shifting from gravel to clay that sucked at his feet with each stride. He slipped, caught himself on a half-submerged branch, and pulled forward.

The branch snapped.

He went down to his neck and the current in the shallow pushed him sideways and he grabbed at the bank — mud and roots, nothing solid, his numb fingers sliding off. He got his feet under him and pushed up and the clay bottom gave way and he went down again.

On the far bank, Vel took a step toward the water.

He came up. He saw her standing with one foot in the shallows, her weight shifted forward, and something about the way she was standing — not panicked, not yet, but close to the edge of her own assessment — made the situation more real than the water in his lungs had.

She was wrong. The river was more dangerous than she'd thought, and she knew it, and she was deciding whether to come in after him.

He got his feet under him on a submerged root and pushed upstream. Vel stepped back.

The next thirty minutes were the worst. The shallows grew shallower and then deepened again where a tributary fed in, carrying colder water and a raw clay smell. He crossed the tributary at neck depth, his feet barely touching, his hands grabbing rocks that were slick with green growth and offered nothing. His fingers were so numb he couldn't feel what he was gripping. He crossed by faith — the faith that the next handhold would hold, the next footfall would land, the next breath would come before the water did.

It came before the water did. Every time, by margins that felt less and less comfortable.

When he reached the crossing point, the flat rock Dren had indicated was a meter above the waterline. He climbed onto it, slipping twice on the wet surface, his hands leaving bloody smears from scrapes he hadn't noticed getting. He sat there and breathed. The river ran past him, unchanged.

He was still on the wrong side.

The final section was eight meters of deep water between his rock and the far bank. He could see Dren watching from the shore, arms folded. Vel had moved to the water's edge and was standing with her feet in the shallows, her body angled as if she might wade in at any moment and was holding herself back by conscious decision.

Kem slid off the rock. The water took him immediately and he fought it — badly, with frozen hands and leaden legs and the last reserves of a body that had never been asked to do anything like this. The current spun him. He kicked. His foot hit bottom — gravel, solid, shallow enough — and he lunged forward and the bottom held and he lunged again and the water was at his waist, his thighs, his knees.

He crawled onto the far bank on his hands and knees. Two hours after he'd entered the river. He lay face-down in the dirt and breathed mud and didn't move.

---

Dren said, quietly: "Better than I expected, honestly."

Kem did not find this encouraging.

He rolled onto his back. The sky was pale and ordinary, a few thin clouds sliding east. His body was a collection of specific complaints — the shin where the rock had cracked it, seeping blood into the dirt; the long scrape on his back where the torn shirt stuck to raw skin; the numbness in his hands that was turning to a painful tingling as feeling returned; the deep ache in his thighs and lower back from fighting current for two hours. All of it earned. None of it permanent.

He sat up. Vel was standing three feet away, wringing out the hem of her shirt. Her legs were wet to the knee. She'd been in the water. Ready to come in after him, or at least ready enough that the readiness had gotten her wet.

"You were going to come in," he said.

"You were struggling."

"You told me not to spend."

"I did."

"That could've killed me."

"Yes."

The fury came slowly. Not hot — cold, the way his body was cold, building from the specific memory of brown water closing over his head and the bottom dropping away and the long second where he'd known, with absolute physical certainty, that the river could take him and his Ledger would never change.

"Why?" he said.

Vel sat down. Not next to him — across from him, on a rock, the way she sat when she intended to say something she'd been thinking about for a while. Dren was further up the bank, laying out his pack's contents to dry, giving the conversation the studied distance of a man who had opinions he wasn't being paid to share.

"Because I thought you could make it," Vel said. "I was wrong about how hard it'd be for you."

"So I nearly drowned because you made a bad call."

"You nearly drowned because I made a bad call and you followed it."

He waited.

"You didn't look at the river," she said. "I said don't spend, and you didn't spend. You didn't check the current yourself. You didn't watch how Dren and I crossed and figure out where you'd have trouble. You didn't look at the depth markers and decide I was right. You just did what I told you."

"Because you told me—"

"I know what I told you. I'm telling you I was wrong. And you should've seen it."

He stared at her. This wasn't what he'd expected — not the admission, which was rare enough, but the direction. She wasn't apologizing. She was telling him he should have overruled her.

"The river was too much for a straight physical crossing," she said. "I should've caught that. The rain, the current speed, the width. I miscalled it. But you'd been walking next to water for weeks. You've seen what current does. You had enough to make your own judgment and you didn't use any of it."

"Because you said not to spend."

"Because I said. That's the problem."

She picked up a stone from the bank and turned it in her hands. Not looking at him. The compressed voice she used when she was working through something difficult and practical at the same time.

"Camp was different. I taught you to make a fire and you learned to make a fire. That was practice. This wasn't practice. This was a real river that could've killed you, and I said cross it and you crossed it because I said so. Not because you looked at the situation and decided restraint was the right call. You didn't spend because I told you not to. That's the same thing your father did at the Rite — just from the other direction."

The connection landed in a place that was already tender. He thought of the Conservatory — his father sitting in perfect stillness, preserving as a matter of principle. He thought of himself in the river, not spending as a matter of obedience. The architecture was the same. Only the furniture was different.

"Discipline isn't following a rule," Vel said. "If you turn 'don't spend' into something you obey instead of something you judge, you're hoarding. You're preserving restraint instead of preserving levels, and it's the same thing. It's ideology."

Kem sat with the scraped back and the bleeding shin and the sound of the river behind him, which hadn't changed at all. He wanted to argue. The river had made the argument already, and it was difficult to dispute evidence that had been inside his lungs.

"So what do I do?" His voice came out rough from the water he'd swallowed.

"You look at the situation. You decide. Sometimes spending's right. Sometimes it isn't. I can't give you a rule for which is which. You have to build that yourself."

She dropped the stone. Something in her expression shifted — not warmer, but less compressed. The look of someone who'd made an error and wasn't finished accounting for it.

"I made the wrong call today," she said. "Don't let me do it again."

He held her gaze. There was a time — recent, before Thessa's settlement — when this would have settled something between them. Vel admitting error, trusting him with the right to override her. He would have felt the reciprocity of it, the deepening.

What he felt instead was a question he couldn't resolve. Thessa's voice from five days ago, describing how Vel had come through years earlier, asking about Hoarded defectors, high-level spenders who might be willing to burn down. He didn't know if Vel teaching him to judge for himself was generosity or management. Whether building his independence served him or served her plan. The two possibilities existed side by side, and he didn't yet have the tools to tell them apart.

"Fine," he said.

If Vel noticed the flatness in the word, she didn't address it.

"One more thing." She smoothed a piece of bark flat on her knee — a habit she had when she was organizing a practical argument. "Since the Spent Towns. Your incidentals."

"What about them?"

"Flame Charms for cooking fires when you've got flint in your kit. Strength boosts for carrying your pack uphill when your shoulders can carry your pack. Light abilities in the dark when there's a torch three feet away." She ticked each one without looking at him. "You've been bleeding levels on things a match and a strong back could handle. Since the Spent Towns. Eighteen."

He opened his mouth. Closed it. He hadn't been counting.

"Check your Ledger," she said.

He checked. The number was there — persistent, precise, the Ledger's one reliable quality. He hadn't looked at it closely since Thessa's settlement.

One hundred and forty-eight.

He'd been at 160 after the escape. Twelve levels in the days since, spent on things he hadn't even registered as spending. And Vel had been watching the whole time, keeping a tally he hadn't known she was keeping.

"Eighteen levels since the Spent Towns on things a match, a torch, and a strong shoulder could've handled," Vel said. "You're at 148 not because of the fights. Not because of the escape. Because you still reach for an ability before you reach for anything else."

Eighteen. He thought about each one he could remember — the cooking fire two nights ago when his hands were cold and the flint seemed like so much effort compared to a Flame Charm. The Strength boost on the long hill because his pack straps were cutting grooves in his shoulders. The Light ability in the cave they'd sheltered in because the dark was absolute and a torch smelled bad and three levels seemed like nothing against the relief of sight. Each one small. Each one a permanent subtraction he'd made without conscious decision.

"The river says sometimes you should spend," Vel said. "The eighteen says most of the time you shouldn't. Both of those are true at the same time."

She stood up and began unpacking her wet things. The conversation was finished. She'd said what she had to say — once, as always, expecting it to land.

Dren came over with a strip of cloth he'd torn from something in his pack. "Your shin," he said. "Before it fills with dirt."

Kem took the cloth. "Thanks."

"Wasn't what I signed on for," Dren said. He ran his thumb along the scar on his left arm, the habitual gesture, checking. "The motivational drowning."

"She said she was wrong about the river."

"She was." Dren sat on his heels and looked at the water. His face had the expression Kem was learning to read — the one that surfaced when he was doing math that involved his daughter, his Ledger, the distance between where he was and where she was waiting. "She's usually not, though. Wrong, I mean. That's the thing about traveling with someone who's been doing this forty years. When they're right, you learn something. When they're wrong, it almost kills you."

He stood, brushed dirt from his knees, and went back to his things.

Kem wrapped his shin and sat on the bank and looked south. The terrain on this side of the river was different — drier, the vegetation thinner, the soil lighter in color as if something had been drawn out of it. The road, what there was of it, continued as two ruts in pale dirt. Whatever lay ahead didn't look like the Spent Towns.

He didn't check his Ledger again. The number was there whether he looked at it or not.

---

Sable built the fire herself.

Not with an ability. With a striker and steel she'd taken from the field equipment kit six weeks ago, back when the kit had been an object of institutional comedy — designed by administrators who had never lit a fire, packed by clerks who could not tell kindling from deadfall. She had learned the difference since then. She had learned it the way she learned everything: systematically, with attention to process, noting which materials caught and which smoked and died.

The sparks caught the shredded bark and she cupped her hands around the small flame, feeding it dried grass, then twigs, then larger pieces in the order she had established through five weeks of trial and revision. The fire grew. She fed it with the methodical care she once applied to quarterly audit reports, and she noticed the comparison forming and did not suppress it.

She was alone. She had been alone for three weeks, since she'd sent the remaining agents back toward the capital. Two of the five had been unable to continue — the spending had unsettled something in them that decades of Hoarded propriety had not prepared them for. The other three could still function, but their growing competence was making them harder to predict, and unpredictable spenders at level 280 were a liability she did not need. She tracked better alone. She was better at it than she had expected to be, which was itself information she was still processing.

Her camp was organized. Boots aligned by the fire. Provisions inventoried — another week at her current pace before she would need to resupply. She had a map she'd annotated in her own hand, precise small notes in the margins, and she had stopped using the Council-issued terminology for geographic features. The landmark the official map labeled "Terrain Feature 7" was a ridge. She called it a ridge in her notes now. The shift in language felt disproportionately significant.

She activated the Communication ability.

The cost was two levels. She felt it go — a precise subtraction, the specific sensation of capacity leaving, which she had learned was not pain and not loss but something between the two that the Hoarded vocabulary did not have a word for. Her Ledger adjusted: 270. She'd been 300 six weeks ago. Thirty levels spent on pursuit, on tracking, on travel, on the accumulated expenses of being alive outside a building for the first time in her adult life.

The ability connected. Lady Aldraste's voice arrived clean and cold, carrying the authority of a woman who had chaired institutional meetings for three decades and had learned to compress command into the pitch of her syllables.

"Report."

"The subjects are moving south," Sable said. "Past the boundary settlements. Direction is consistent with a Scour approach."

"Rate of travel."

"Reduced. The guide has an arm injury that limits their pace." She paused. The next sentence required her to choose between the institutional frame and the accurate one. "The Aldraste subject — Kem — his incidental ability use has declined. He is spending less on routine tasks."

"Meaning?"

"He is being trained. The Ashwick woman is teaching him to function without abilities." Sable heard herself say it and heard, also, what she was not saying — that the training was working, that the reckless spender she'd watched fight with the exhilaration of a child was becoming something more deliberate, and that she found the transformation difficult to categorize as decline.

"His current level?"

"Approximately 160. Down from 225 when he left the Spent Towns."

"That is a considerable expenditure." The administrative frost. Numbers that meant money, waste, institutional failure. But underneath it — and Sable had spent twenty years in meetings with Lady Aldraste, long enough to hear what ran beneath the surface like water under ice — there was a different arithmetic. A mother counting how much of her son was left.

"The majority was combat and defensive spending," Sable said. "The incidental losses are decreasing."

"Your instructions remain unchanged. Retrieve the subject with minimal force and minimal additional expenditure. The Council's patience with this operation is not without limit."

"Understood."

"And Auditor Orm."

"Yes."

"Your current level."

Sable looked at the fire she had built with her own hands. The flames were steady, the fuel well-arranged, the heat banking the way she had taught herself to make it bank through weeks of practice. Six weeks ago she could not have built this fire. Could not have walked eight hours on rough terrain. Could not have slept outdoors, navigated by stars, read weather from cloud patterns, or made a single operational decision that was not routed through a committee and countersigned by two superiors.

"Two hundred and eighty-five," she said.

She was at 270. The lie came out in her official voice, the voice that filed reports and presented audit findings, and it sounded true in that voice the way all official statements sounded true. She understood the institutional reason for the lie — a 30-level expenditure on a retrieval would trigger a spending review, and a review would expose the gap between what the Council had authorized and what she had done with the authorization. But the institutional reason felt like a shell around the real one, which she could not have named if pressed: the fifteen levels she had hidden represented choices she had made for herself, spent on becoming someone the Council had not approved, and they were hers in a way that nothing she had done in forty-seven years of institutional service had been hers.

"Noted," Lady Aldraste said. "Continue southward. Report at standard intervals."

The connection held for a moment longer than protocol required. Sable waited. In the silence she could hear the specific quality of a woman deciding whether to step outside the institutional frame — a frame that had contained Lady Aldraste for as long as Sable had known her, as long as Lady Aldraste had watched her husband go still and her son walk out and her house become a political problem rather than a family.

"Is he well?" Lady Aldraste asked. The words were quiet. They did not sound like a Council directive.

"He is functional," Sable said. "He is adapting to field conditions."

"That is not what I asked."

Sable thought of Kem in the fight — burning levels with the efficiency of a dropped torch, grinning afterward with a happiness that nobody in the Vault had displayed in her presence, ever. She thought of the way he asked questions — interrupting, circling back, genuine. She thought of the trader's wound and the ugly stitches and how the trader had said *my arm's still on, so thanks* and how Kem had looked when he heard it, as if someone had handed him a thing he had not known he wanted.

"He seems well," Sable said. "That is not in my report."

The connection ended.

Sable sat by the fire and watched the flames work through the fuel she had arranged. Her hands were calloused now — new calluses, still pink and tender, forming over skin that had never gripped anything rougher than a pen or the spine of an audit ledger. Her hair was pulled back in a knot she had copied from a Spent Town woman three weeks earlier, nothing like the formal arrangement she had maintained for thirty years. She was not sure what she looked like. She had not seen a mirror since the capital.

The question had been forming for weeks. Not arriving whole — building, the way pressure builds in a structure, incremental and invisible until the crack appears.

Every ability she used made her better at something. The Wayfinder had given her directional sense. The tracking abilities had given her fluency with terrain and weather. The Communication ability gave her reach. Each one subtracted from her Ledger and added to her capacity in ways the Ledger did not measure and the Council did not acknowledge.

The Hoarded said spending diminished you. That every level lost was a reduction, a step toward emptiness, toward the condition they named preservation and enforced as virtue. She had believed this for forty-seven years. She had organized her career, her identity, and her understanding of what a person was around the principle that keeping was strength and spending was erosion.

But she was more capable than she had ever been. She could build a fire, read terrain, walk a full day on bad ground, make decisions without a committee, and sleep on the earth. She could track a man across a landscape she had never seen. None of these capacities appeared on a Ledger. All of them were real.

She reached for the institutional frame out of habit, the way she reached for the striker when she needed fire.

*Preliminary finding: expenditure of levels correlates with increased functional capacity in the field. This observation is inconsistent with the Council's preservation framework. Recommend further assessment.*

The language sat on top of the real question like a report cover on an audit. Keeping the contents contained, presentable, deniable.

She let the cover slide.

*Does spending make you more?*

She did not write it in her field notes. She did not file it in the observation log she had been maintaining out of bureaucratic reflex. She sat with the fire she had built and the calluses on her hands and the number 270 that was hers, and she let the question remain where it was — unprocessed, uncategorized, without a protocol for what to do next.

The fire cracked. A log shifted and sent a constellation of sparks upward into the dark. She watched them rise and vanish and did not reach for an ability to track their path, though she had one that would have worked. She let them go.
