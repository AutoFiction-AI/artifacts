# Chapter 1

The farmer's shin was broken in two places. Vel could see the bone through the split skin above his left ankle, and the infection had already started — a hot redness climbing the calf, the wound edges tight with fluid that smelled sweet and wrong. Three days, his wife had said. A wall collapse in the grain shed. He'd been pinned under the rubble for six hours before they dug him out, and nobody in the settlement had anything to offer beyond boiled rags and willow-bark paste.

Vel knelt on the packed-earth floor beside the cot and pressed her palms flat against his leg, one hand above the break and one below. The farmer — Hael, forty or so, hands calloused from decades of soil and timber work, the nails cracked and dark — made a sound through his teeth but didn't pull away.

"Still," she said. "Don't move the leg."

She reached for the ability. The Ledger shifted. She felt it the way she always felt it: not pain, not sound, but a subtraction — something behind the sternum changing weight, the number at the back of her mind moving the way a clock hand moves, noticed only when it clicks. Thirty-three became thirty-two.

Warmth moved through her palms and into the leg. She could feel the shape of the damage now — the two fracture lines running oblique through the tibia, loose splinters floating in the swollen tissue, and under all of it the infection threading deeper than she'd estimated. Not surface rot. The bacteria had gotten into the marrow, settled into the exposed bone. Osteomyelitis. She'd need to push further than she'd planned.

"Shit," she said, quietly.

"What?" Hael's wife was by the window. Maret. She'd been wringing the same cloth for twenty minutes, her knuckles white against the fabric. She was thin in the way of someone who'd missed meals for months — not starving but diminished, the body quietly consuming its own reserves. Her dress had been let out and taken in again, the stitching visible where the seams had been reworked twice. When Vel had arrived that morning, Maret had offered her bread, and the bread had been the best thing in the house.

"Infection's deeper than it looked. I'm going to push the healing again."

"Does that cost you more?"

Vel didn't answer. She pushed the ability into the bone.

The second level went. Thirty-two became thirty-one.

The infection fought back in the way infections do — not with intelligence but with the blind persistence of something that had been growing for three days and didn't want to stop. She drove the healing through it, felt the bacteria die in waves, felt the swelling deflate and the redness retreat down the calf like a tide going out. The fracture lines knit. The splinters pulled back to the bone's surface and fused. The tissue reorganized itself around the repair, and Hael's breathing went from shallow and controlled to deep and surprised.

Vel sat back on her heels. Her hands were shaking — the specific fatigue that follows ability use, the body registering that something permanent has been taken. Not the tremor of exhaustion. The tremor of subtraction. She wiped her palms on her trousers.

The leg was functional. The bone was set, the infection cleared. A wide pale scar where the skin had split, stiffness for a week or two, and he'd need to keep weight off it for a few days. But he'd walk. He'd work the fields. He'd be able to stand at the well and haul water for his wife, and that was the kind of thing this settlement couldn't afford to lose.

"That's it?" Hael said. He was staring at his leg with the expression of someone who'd been in pain for three days and couldn't yet trust that the pain had stopped.

"That's it. Stay off it today. Tomorrow you can move around, but nothing heavy for a week."

He touched the scar on his shin, pressing with his thumb. Vel watched him press too hard and wince and then press again, as if checking. His eyes were brown and tired and grateful in a way that made her want to leave the room. She'd never liked the gratitude. The work was the work; the thanks turned it into something she'd have to carry.

Maret put the cloth down. She crossed the room and stood over her husband's cot and put her hand on his forehead, feeling for the fever that had been climbing since yesterday. Her own hand was still trembling from the wringing. She noticed Vel watching and tucked it behind her back.

"What do we owe you?" Maret asked.

"Whatever you can manage."

Maret went to a tin on the shelf above the stove. She counted the marks with the careful fingers of someone who knew exactly how many were in the tin before she opened it. The sound was thin — a small number in a small container. She came back with seven marks and a jar of preserved cherries, the glass cloudy with age but the fruit inside still a deep, livid red.

"The cherries are good," Maret said. "I put them up last autumn, before the —" She stopped. "They're good."

Before the fever, Vel thought. Before eleven people died and the Hoarded sent no one. Before the grain contract was renegotiated and the payments cut and the settlement started eating itself from the edges in. Maret didn't finish the sentence because she didn't need to. Everyone here knew what last autumn meant.

"Thank you," Vel said. She took the marks and the jar.

She picked up her pack from where she'd set it by the door — canvas worn soft with decades of use, the straps repaired with cord and leather scraps, the buckles tarnished. She checked the main compartment out of a habit forty years deep and stepped outside.

The afternoon sun was warm. Late spring, the air carrying turned earth and the smell of something flowering in the hedgerows along the settlement's southern boundary. Twenty or so houses clustered around a well with a crumbling stone lip and a winch mechanism held together with rope and a bent nail. Beyond the houses, the fields stretched south toward a tree line gone shaggy and untended. Wheat and barley in alternating rows, but patchy — gaps where the soil hadn't responded to the last planting, or where nobody had been strong enough to work the ground after the fever took three of the settlement's best field hands.

Vel walked the settlement's single road. Her knees complained about the packed earth — fifty-eight years of kneeling on bad surfaces, and she'd stopped noticing the stiffness years ago and then started noticing it again recently, which meant it was getting worse. She passed the grain shed where Hael had been hurt — the south wall collapsed inward, the roof sagging, timber beams cracked along the grain. Nobody had the levels to Shape it back together, and nobody had the labor to rebuild it by hand. So the grain was being stored in a lean-to behind the meeting hall, open to weather and rats, and that was how things went in a settlement the Hoarded had decided wasn't worth maintaining.

Two children were playing near the well, throwing a rope back and forth with the listless energy of kids who'd run out of games an hour ago. One of them watched Vel pass and said nothing. The other waved. She waved back. They were thin in the shoulders, the way Maret was thin — not starving, just less than they should have been. A woman she hadn't met was mending a fence post at the far end of the road, hammering with a flat stone because the settlement's only real hammer had broken a month ago and nobody could afford the marks for a new one from a trader.

This place had been functional once. The Hoarded had run a grain program through here: Fertility Charms applied to the soil each season, yields shipped north to Vault storehouses, the settlement paid just enough to keep planting. Standard arrangement. Then the rust-fever swept through two years ago, took eleven people in six weeks, and the Hoarded did nothing. Sent no healers. Spent no levels. Adjusted the contract terms to account for reduced labor capacity and let the settlement shrink. The arithmetic of abandonment — Vel had seen it a dozen times in northern settlements, the same slow calculus: if the output doesn't justify the investment, cut the investment. The people inside the equation were incidental.

She sat on the low stone wall by the well and ate one of the cherries. It was good — tart, preserved in something that might have been brandy, with a sweetness that had deepened over the winter months. Maret had put these up before everything went wrong, and she'd given them to a stranger for healing her husband, and that was the kind of trade this world ran on when the powerful looked away.

Thirty-one.

The number sat behind her sternum the way it always did. Not pressure, not sound — a persistent awareness, like a stone she'd swallowed years ago that had never dissolved. She'd learned to live with it the way you learn to live with tinnitus or a bad joint. It was just there. At three hundred, she hadn't noticed it. At two hundred, it had been background noise. At one hundred, she'd started counting. Below fifty, she'd stopped being able to not count.

Thirty-one levels. Forty-one years of spending. Every ability she'd ever used — healing the sick, fighting when she had to, building shelters, mending bones, pulling people from collapsed buildings and flooded canals and burning rooms — a withdrawal from a total that never went up. She'd watched the number drop from three hundred to this, one ability at a time, each one a trade she'd chosen to make. And she'd make the same trades again, every one of them. That wasn't the problem. The problem was that the account was almost empty and the biggest withdrawal was still ahead.

She'd budgeted one level for this stop. Standard bone-set, infection clear, tissue repair. One level, then back on the road. You budget everything when you're at thirty-three. You plan each spend the way you plan each meal when the food's running low — what you can afford, what you can't, what it costs to be generous.

The infection had been deeper than she'd judged. So she'd spent the second level, and now the plan was off by one, and one was a lot when you were at thirty-one.

She finished the cherry and wiped the juice on her trousers. Her hands were still shaking. They'd stop in an hour. They always did.

---

The settlement had given her a room — a storeroom at the back of the meeting hall, cleared of grain sacks and furnished with a cot that smelled of burlap and old dust. She sat on the cot and spread her supplies on the floor. The light came through a window that didn't close all the way, and she could hear the well pulley creaking as someone drew water outside.

She took stock. Not of the pack — she knew what was in the pack down to the weight. She took stock of what was left of her.

Thirty-one levels. She ran through her remaining abilities the way she'd done every night for the last eight years, the way a miser counts coins, touching each one, confirming it was still there. Healing: she could manage three, maybe four standard applications before the ability degraded below useful output. Below level twenty, it wouldn't have enough force to do more than close a shallow cut. Combat: she had a handful of defensive options — a Redirect that could turn an incoming strike, a Perception boost that sharpened her awareness of threats, a short-range Conceal that could buy her thirty seconds of not-being-seen. Nothing that would stop a real fighter. Nothing that would win a confrontation she couldn't avoid. Utility: an Endurance enhancement that could stretch a day's march into two, a Nightsight, a Weatherskin that blunted rain and cold. Minor abilities she'd relied on for decades, each one now carrying a price she had to weigh against everything else she might need.

This was what thirty-one looked like. Not helpless, but thin. A toolkit with most of the tools removed, and the ones remaining too valuable to use casually.

She'd been planning the route to zero for eight years. Mapping the path, cross-referencing landmarks, confirming distances, working out the mechanism — how the threshold opened, what it required, what she'd need to make it work. She knew the destination: a place in the far south called the Scour, where the land dropped into a vast erosion basin and the earth's bones showed through the surface. She knew the route, more or less. She knew what zero was — not death, not an ending, but a door to a place where people lived without the Ledger, without levels, without the constant arithmetic of what you could afford and what you couldn't.

She'd seen the door open once, eight years ago. The sight had rewritten the architecture of her life.

The problem was the math. She'd studied it, tested what she could test, thought about it until thinking looped back on itself. A single person reaching zero at the threshold could trigger the crossing — she'd seen that. But the crossing had been unstable. The door had flickered, had held for seconds, had barely worked. One person's descent to zero wasn't enough energy to keep it open cleanly. She needed more. She needed a sustained cascade — two people spending down together, the combined output stabilizing the threshold, holding the door long enough for a clean crossing. And for the cascade to generate enough energy, she needed someone with a long way to fall. Someone starting high. Someone with levels to burn — a lot of levels — who was willing to burn them all the way to nothing.

She didn't have a lot of levels. She had thirty-one.

Which meant she needed a partner. Someone high-level, someone willing to spend their way to zero alongside her. And that was the gap in her plan that she'd been unable to close for eight years, because the kind of person who had a high level was the kind of person who'd never deliberately burn it down, and the rare few who might were almost always too depleted to matter.

She'd looked. She'd spent years looking. Traveled through Spent Towns and settlements and the edges of the Margins, asking careful questions. Had anyone heard of a Hoarded defector? Anyone at 250 or above who'd walked away? Anyone with the levels and the willingness to descend? The answers had always been no. The Hoarded didn't leave. That was the whole point of being Hoarded — you stayed, you preserved, you held your three hundred intact until the Stillness hollowed you out from inside and your children pretended it was dignity.

She couldn't keep looking. Each trip cost levels she didn't have. Each healing job cost levels. Each fight, each emergency, each time someone needed help and she was the only one around — levels gone, levels she'd meant to save for the journey. At thirty-three she'd still had room to maneuver. At thirty-one the margin was thin enough to see through. If she dropped below twenty, the cascade wouldn't have enough energy from her side to work. If she kept working, kept traveling, kept spending on other people's emergencies, she'd cross that line within a year. Maybe sooner.

Now. It had to be now.

She packed her supplies. Checked the water skin, the dried food, the knife she'd carried since she was thirty. She was leaving in the morning. She'd head north — there was a trading post two days up the road where she could resupply and listen for news. She was out of time for waiting. She'd find a partner or she'd cross alone and hope the threshold held.

She sat with that for a moment. Eight years of planning, and it came down to leaving a storeroom in a dying settlement with thirty-one levels and a route she still wasn't sure would work.

Outside, the well pulley stopped. The light through the cracked window turned gold.

---

The news found her that evening.

She was in the meeting hall with four of the settlement's remaining adults, eating a meal of boiled grain and root vegetables that tasted like everything else she'd eaten in the northern settlements — adequate, filling, stretched thin. The hall was the largest building in the place, a timber-frame structure with a repaired roof that leaked in two spots and a stone hearth that drew well. Dried herbs hung from the ceiling beams, and the room smelled of rosemary and woodsmoke and the sour tang of grain that had been stored too long.

A trader named Sorren had arrived an hour before sunset, leading a cart pulled by a mule that looked as tired as its owner. Sorren was a compact man in his fifties with a weathered face and the particular lean of someone who'd spent decades walking beside a loaded cart — one shoulder slightly higher than the other from holding the lead rope year after year. He had dust in the creases of his neck and a habit of tapping his spoon against the rim of his bowl between bites, a small percussion that filled the gaps in conversation. He'd been running the northern route for twenty years, he said. Dry goods, tools, once in a while a bolt of fabric or a crate of lamp oil. The settlements needed him and paid him poorly and he came back anyway because the route was his and he didn't know how to be anyone else.

The conversation was the usual. Crop reports. Road conditions. Which settlements had been hit by the rust-fever and which had scraped through. Whether the Hoarded grain contracts would be renewed for the coming season or whether the terms would tighten again. Vel half-listened. She'd heard versions of this talk in a hundred settlement halls. The northerners discussed the Hoarded the way you discuss weather — a force you couldn't control and mostly just endured, with occasional flickers of anger that never quite became anything louder.

Olla, the oldest of the settlement's adults, did most of the talking on the local side. She was white-haired, her hands palsied, but she managed the field rotation for the entire settlement and remembered every planting decision going back thirty years. She had opinions about soil drainage and kept them at conversational volume. She ate her grain in precise, small spoonfuls, and between bites she asked Sorren questions with the patience of someone who'd been extracting useful information from traders since before Vel was born.

"The eastern road still passable?" Olla asked.

"Passable. Muddy past the fork. They had rain last week."

"And Cairn Hill?"

"Standing. They got through the winter better than most. Good leadership there." Sorren tapped his spoon. "Actually — heard a strange one at Cairn Hill. Two days back." He paused in the way people pause when they have a story they've been carrying and have been waiting for the right table to set it on. "A Hoarded lord walked out of the Vault."

The room went quiet. Not the quiet of shock — the quiet of people in a settlement the Hoarded had abandoned, hearing something they didn't know how to place.

"Walked out," Olla said. "What does that mean, walked out?"

"Means what it sounds like. Used an ability to open the gates — first ability the man had ever used in his life, the way the story goes. Some kind of shield or force thing, blew half the gate hall apart. Then he just walked through it. Out into the open. Hasn't come back." Sorren tapped his spoon twice. "House Aldraste. The son."

"The preservation house," Olla said. She set her spoon down with the deliberate motion of someone organizing her reaction. "Three hundred for six generations."

"Until now." Sorren shrugged. "Could be road talk. You know how stories get by the time they've passed through three trading posts. But I heard it from Breck's people at Cairn Hill, and they got it from a Vault courier who'd actually been in the building when it happened. Said the boy dropped ten, twelve levels in the first hour. Using abilities he'd never touched. Burning through them like he'd just discovered his hands worked."

Vel had been eating steadily through the conversation, her face neutral. She'd trained herself out of visible reactions decades ago — a useful skill when you spent your life in settlements where strangers were assessed quickly and remembered long. But she was listening now with an attention that had nothing to do with the food.

"How long ago?" she asked.

Sorren looked at her. He'd been polite to her all evening — the considered politeness you showed a healer who'd just saved a man's leg — but she could see him recalculating. The question had been too quick. Too specific.

"Five, six days," he said. "Give or take. Courier was moving fast."

"And do they know where he went after?"

"North, is what I heard. Or maybe just — out. Away. The Hoarded don't exactly build people who know how to navigate." He tapped the spoon, looked at it, set it down. "They'll be sending people after him, I'd think. Can't have a lord's son burning down in public. Makes the whole philosophy look like what it is."

"Mm," Vel said.

"Why the interest?"

"Just curious. Not often you hear about a Hoarded walking out."

"Not often," Sorren agreed. "Not ever, really. I've been on this route twenty years and that's the first one I've heard of that wasn't secondhand rumor from two centuries back." He picked up his spoon and resumed tapping. "Probably won't last, though. Kid's never been outside walls. He'll burn through half his levels trying to boil water and they'll collect him inside a week."

The conversation moved on. Olla asked about the price of lamp oil. Someone else talked about the well mechanism and whether it could hold through summer. Vel ate her meal and let the talk wash over her.

A Hoarded lord. House Aldraste. Level three hundred — or he had been, before he started burning. If the story was five or six days old, he could be at two-eighty by now. Two-seventy. Depending on how fast he was spending and how reckless he was about it. And he'd be reckless, because the Hoarded never learned efficiency. They went from zero experience to full power and had no concept of calibration. Every defector she'd ever heard of — in the old stories, the settlement legends — had burned levels like they were infinite, because when you'd never spent a single point, you had no understanding of what a point was worth.

But if he was real. If the rumor was solid.

He had what she needed. The levels. A long, long way to fall. And if she could find him, and if she could reach him before the Hoarded collected him, the gap in her plan closed.

She finished her meal. She thanked Olla for the food and the rosemary, which was genuinely good — Olla kept a small herb garden behind the meeting hall, a careful plot she tended with the same attention she gave the field rotations, and the rosemary had made the boiled grain taste like something a person might actually choose to eat. She thanked Sorren for the news and the company. She stepped outside into the cooling evening, the sun below the tree line now, the sky darkening from gold to the blue-gray of late spring twilight.

In the storeroom, she sat on the cot and pulled the journal from her pack.

It was battered. The leather cover had gone soft from years of rain and handling, the edges worn round, the binding cracked along the spine and restitched with heavy thread that didn't match the original. The pages inside were thick — the kind of paper that held up to weather — and crowded with writing. Two handwritings filled the margins and overflowed into every gap the text allowed. The first was small and precise, the ink faded to brown, the annotations methodical: distances measured in day-counts, terrain descriptions, water sources, settlement names with populations and average levels noted in careful parentheses. The second handwriting was looser and faster, a different color ink — additions and corrections and questions written over and around the first, sometimes arguing with it. On a page mapping a southern route, the first hand had written *passable in dry season* and the second had added *barely — took us two days longer than this says, and the creek crossing is worse than it looks.*

The southern routes were the most detailed sections. Page after page — paths through territory Vel had been studying for years, landmarks, warnings, notes about which settlements might shelter travelers and which wouldn't. The first handwriting had mapped these routes with the thoroughness of someone who expected to walk them again. The second handwriting had filled in what the first had missed.

Vel turned the pages slowly. She handled them with a care that didn't match how she handled anything else in her life — her pack, her tools, her own body, all of these she treated with the rough efficiency of long use. The journal was different. She turned each page as if the paper might not survive carelessness.

The route she needed was here. South through the Margins to the Scour. She'd cross-referenced it against every other source she'd found over eight years, and this journal was still the most detailed map she had. The annotations were old now, and the landmarks might have shifted — settlements moved, roads washed out, terrain changed by weather and the slow collapse of overused land. But the bones of the route were sound.

She looked at a page near the back where the two handwritings overlapped most densely, the annotations crowding each other, the ink from different seasons layering into a small record of two people who'd traveled the same ground at different times and paid attention to different things. She let her thumb rest on the margin where the second handwriting had corrected a distance calculation — *not 4 days, closer to 5 with full packs* — and held it there for a moment longer than the information required.

Then she closed the journal. Wrapped it in the square of oilcloth she kept it in. Fit it back into her pack against the flat of her back where it rode best.

A Hoarded lord at three hundred, burning down. Five or six days out of the Vault. The Hoarded would send people after him — that was certain. A lord's son spending levels in public was a political crisis, a repudiation of everything the great families built their authority on. They'd want him back before the story spread, before the Spent Towns heard about it, before anyone started asking what it meant that a preservation house had produced a defector.

She'd need to reach him first. Or reach the area he was in and work from there, because a man who'd never been outside walls wouldn't leave a clean trail — he'd leave a mess of wasted levels and confused settlements and burned-out abilities pointed in every direction.

North. He'd gone north, or maybe not — but the Vault was roughly northeast of here, and a man walking away from the only home he'd ever known would walk in whatever direction felt like away. She'd head north toward the Vault's region and listen. A Hoarded lord dropping levels wasn't subtle. People would notice. People would talk. She'd find the trail.

She didn't know if she could reach him in time. She didn't know if he'd still be free when she did — the Hoarded moved slowly on most things, but a defecting lord was the kind of emergency that cut through institutional inertia. She didn't know if he'd listen to a fifty-eight-year-old woman at thirty-one who wanted to walk him to the bottom of everything he'd ever been.

But she was out of time, and he was the first real possibility in eight years, and thirty-one was already too low for the luxury of waiting for a better one.

She lay back on the cot. The storeroom was dark now, the window a blue rectangle. She could hear Sorren's mule chewing in the yard, the slow grinding of an animal that didn't know or care about Hoarded lords or thresholds or the number thirty-one. The settlement was winding down — a door somewhere, footsteps on packed earth, the well pulley one more time. The child who'd been singing earlier in the afternoon had stopped.

Vel closed her eyes. The number occupied the space behind her sternum, precise and permanent. She thought about the journal in her pack and the route south that it described. She thought about the door she'd seen open once, eight years ago — the flash of somewhere else, buildings and light and people, a world without this world's arithmetic — and the specific ache that lived in the gap between that moment and now. An ache she didn't name, because naming it would make it a thing she carried openly instead of a thing she could fold small and keep inside the practical architecture of planning and movement and what needed to be done next.

She'd leave at first light. North.

The last thought before she slept was subtraction: thirty-one minus whatever the road would cost, minus whatever finding the Aldraste boy would cost, minus whatever it took to convince him. The answer was a number she didn't know yet, and not knowing was what passed for hope at this end of the Ledger.
