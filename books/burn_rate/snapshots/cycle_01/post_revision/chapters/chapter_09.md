# Chapter 9

The needle went in wrong the first time. Kem felt the resistance of Dren's skin, the rubbery give of it, and his hand jerked sideways. Dren hissed through his teeth.

"Sorry. I'm sorry."

"Just do it," Dren said. "Don't talk about it."

Kem repositioned the needle, a curved sewing needle Vel had bent with pliers somewhere in the first week of travel and kept in a cloth wrap with waxed thread. The wound ran from Dren's forearm almost to the elbow, a torn opening where silo shrapnel had ripped through sleeve and flesh. The blood had slowed to a dark seep.

They had made the creek because Vel kept giving orders and Kem kept obeying them fast enough to matter. Two levels went on a Concealment Charm when blood in the shallows turned the crossing into a bright red trail marker. Three more went on a Strength Charm when Dren slipped climbing the far bank and almost tore the wound wider. Kem had hauled him the last stretch with Dren's good arm over his shoulders and the borrowed force burning through his thighs. By the time they'd found this clearing and Vel had washed the gash out with creek water and spirits, five more levels were gone.

Now it was Kem's turn. Vel couldn't do it. The ribs she'd cracked in the fight made the job impossible once she had to lean in and hold the skin together. She had tried, stopped after the first attempt, and braced an elbow against her side while she breathed around the pain.

"Straight through," she'd told him, showing the motion in the air. "Both sides, same depth. Pull it snug, not tight. You're closing skin. Not tying off a sack."

He understood the principle. Principles were familiar territory. His hands were not.

The second pass was better. It caught both edges of the wound, and the waxed thread dragged through in a wet pull that made his stomach jump. The line it made looked nothing like what Vel had described. It looked like a child sewing up a split doll.

"Christ," Dren said.

"I know."

"Keep going."

Kem kept going. The third stitch went crooked. On the fourth he bit too deep on one side and barely caught the other, bunching the skin into a ridge that would scar ugly. Dren's forearm was tanned and ropey from years of hauling cargo. Every time the needle punched through, the muscle jumped under Kem's fingers.

The fifth stitch finally sat right. Even. Snug. Close enough to clean that Kem stared at it for a beat before moving on. Then the sixth hit something fibrous under the skin, Dren grunted, and the small flare of competence vanished. Fingers cramped by the seventh. Blood and wax slicked his grip.

Packed dirt pressed hard through Kem's knees. Late light slanted through the trees and turned the blood on Dren's arm almost black. A few feet away, Vel sat against a trunk with her arms folded carefully over her ribs, breathing shallow and measured. She watched without helping, which was its own kind of instruction.

By the eighth stitch, rhythm started to replace panic. Push through. Pull. Check the gap. Tie. His hands still shook, but the shake belonged to the job now.

"You're pulling them uneven," Vel said.

"I know."

"The last two are going to scar."

"I know."

"Finish."

The ninth stitch pulled the seam mostly closed. It was ugly work. The thread crossed in dark Xs over swelling skin. Some of the twelve stitches were serviceable. Some were a mess. All of them held.

Dren flexed his fingers and then his wrist. The skin tugged. Nothing split.

"That's bad," he said.

"It's ugly."

He looked at the arm again. "Looks like you patched a grain sack." Another flex. A careful test of grip around the pack strap. "But it'll get me down the road. Thanks."

No one had ever thanked Kem for bad work. At the Vault, thanks had meant acknowledgment of rank, of attendance, of standing in the right place with the right name on him. This was different. Blood under his nails, thread uneven, result plainly poor, and still useful enough to matter.

The needle went back into its cloth wrap after he cleaned it with the last of the spirits.

"It'll scar," Vel said.

"Good. Then I'll remember not to let him do the other arm." Dren wrapped a strip of cloth around the forearm one-handed. Pink spread through it at once. "We staying here?"

"Tonight," Vel said. "We move at first light."

Dren tested the pack strap again with the bad arm, let it drop, and adjusted the load with his good hand. The calculation showed in small movements: grip, pull, reach, stop. Functional. Slower. Less carrying weight. Fewer clean days before the arm stiffened. Kem watched him take stock with the blunt efficiency of a man who lived inside margins.

"Arm's fine," Dren said to the trees.

Blood had dried under Kem's nails. Wax clung to his fingertips. The needle had nicked his left thumb and left a shallow puncture there. Used hands. Marked hands. He looked at them as if they belonged to someone else.

Then he checked his Ledger.

178.

He had been at 183 when they broke from the silo. Two levels on Concealment at the creek. Three on Strength dragging Dren up the bank. The subtraction sat clean. It still made his stomach go hollow.

Three hundred when he'd walked out of the Vault. One hundred seventy-eight now. A hundred and twenty-two levels gone in less than a month. At 245, after the first fight, loss had felt like exhilaration. Proof that his body could do something besides sit in ceremonial rooms and decay politely. At 178, the thrill had thinned out. He could feel the empty spaces in his roster. Combat Pulse, gone. Three Utility charms, gone. Sustenance still there, but now it sat in his awareness like a coin you kept touching in your pocket because you weren't sure there were any others left.

This was the texture Vel lived in every day. Not a theory. Not a lesson. Arithmetic. What can I afford. What do I keep. What do I lose if I spend here and need something later.

Dren had been watching him.

"How many?" he asked.

"What?"

"Levels."

"178."

Dren nodded once. No whistle, no pity. Just a trader hearing a number and putting it in the correct column.

"That'll get you somewhere," he said.

Vel had closed her eyes against the tree. She wasn't asleep. Nobody breathing that carefully was asleep. Dren found a position that kept pressure off the stitches and went still.

Kem scrubbed his hands with grass, dirt, and water until the blood left only faint brown in the creases. The puncture in his thumb kept stinging. He ignored it.

Evening slid through the clearing. They had not eaten. Tiredness settled into his jaw, forearms, and shoulders, the kind that came from concentration and the long drop after fear. No ability would fix that. It had to burn out on its own.

So he went for wood instead of the Flame Charm still sitting in his roster. Dry twigs. Bark shaved thin with Vel's knife. A patient little stack, the same order Vel had taught him in the highlands. Fine first, then small sticks, then heavier pieces once the flame could bear them.

It took four tries.

On the third, he smothered the small fire by feeding it too soon. The fourth held. By full dark, the flames had settled low and steady, and Dren was dozing against his pack with the bad arm tucked close.

Vel opened her eyes when the fire caught. She looked at it. Then at Kem. Something eased for a second around her mouth before she pushed herself closer to the heat, moving slowly so the ribs didn't bite.

---

They ate dried meat and the stale heel of bread from Dren's pack. Nobody wasted energy pretending it was better than it was. Dren chewed one-handed and fell asleep almost as soon as he lay down, turned on his good side with the pack under his head. He could sleep anywhere. Three weeks ago Kem would not have noticed that about another person. Now he noticed everything a body had learned to do for survival.

Across the fire, Vel had her knees drawn up and her arms wrapped around them carefully, more guarding than warmth. Firelight showed the small scars on her hands. Forty years of cuts, burns, split knuckles, work he could not name and was only starting to understand.

"Where does it go?" Kem asked.

She looked up.

"Zero," he said. "When it finally runs out. Where do people go."

For a while the fire did all the talking. Vel watched the flames sink around a blackening stick. Then she checked Dren's sleep and looked back at Kem.

"It's not death," she said.

"You've said that before."

"Because that's the first thing people ask."

"Then say the second thing."

A small breath went out of her nose. Not laughter. More like irritation at being cornered by a fair question.

"When you hit zero in the right place, you cross," she said. "Out of here. Out of the Ledger."

Kem held still.

"There's a breach in the south where it can happen. Not anywhere else. If you spend down to nothing in a field, you stay in the field. If it happens there, with the threshold set right, you go through."

"Through to what."

"A place without the Ledger. No number. No abilities. Just your body and whatever you've learned to do with it."

He looked past her at Dren, asleep with the stitched arm tucked against his chest. Dren lived by keeping clear of zero. Dren had a daughter. Dren had route math. Zero was not a destination to him. It was a cliff edge you backed away from all your life.

"How do you know that?" Kem asked.

Vel rubbed a thumb across her palm once, as if checking an old ache. "I saw someone cross."

"Who?"

"Someone I traveled with."

The answer sat there, spare and load-bearing. Kem waited. This time she kept going.

"It was night," she said. "Cold enough the rocks kept the day's heat underneath and froze your face above it. We were standing in a cut of black stone. I was close enough to grab their sleeve if I'd known what I was looking at." Her eyes stayed on the fire. "When their number hit nothing, I felt my own Ledger pull hard inside me. Like a hook had gone through it. Every insect in the cut went quiet at once. Dust lifted off the stone toward them. Then there was empty air where a body had been."

The fire snapped. Kem realized he had stopped breathing and let the air out slow.

"No body," Vel said. "No blood. Pack gone too. They didn't fall. They didn't burn. They were here and then they weren't, and the pull in my Ledger stopped."

That was more real than anything she had said before. Not doctrine. Not rumor. Cold rock. Silent insects. Dust rising the wrong direction.

"And that's only there," he said.

"Only there."

"And only if it's done right."

"If it's done right."

"What does 'done right' mean?"

"It means zero isn't enough by itself." Vel met his eyes then, flat and direct. "That's what it means."

The answer shut one door and opened three others. Kem could feel the questions crowding up behind his teeth, each worse than the last.

"Why tell me now?"

"Because before tonight you'd have heard 'there's a door' and burned twenty levels trying to throw yourself through it." She glanced at his hands, scrubbed mostly clean now except for the blood in the creases. "Tonight you spent five getting us out, stitched his arm with your own hands, and then looked sick over the number. That's different."

She was right, which irritated him more than if she had been cruel. Three weeks ago he would have heard about a threshold and treated it like a challenge built for him. Some part of him still wanted to. Another part kept seeing the five-level subtraction line up clean inside his head and feeling panic under his ribs.

"So this whole time," he said, "you weren't chasing a story."

"No."

"You saw someone go."

"I did."

"And you've been walking after that ever since."

Vel looked back into the fire. Silence, from her, was often the cleanest answer available.

The route, the budgeting, the information rationed out like medicine, the ribs she would not heal, the years. He had been calling it conviction because conviction was easier to look at. This was plainer and harder. She had lost someone to a real place and kept moving toward the last point where that loss had happened.

"What was the place like?" he asked.

"A cut in the rock wide enough for three people to stand abreast. Black stone. Old scorch marks. Wind that doesn't move like the rest of the world. You feel it before you see it. Your Ledger goes tight."

He tried to picture it. Hard stone underfoot. Wind pulling the wrong direction. His own number dropping toward zero while the last of what he had narrowed to one choice.

"People live there," he said.

"That's what I was told, and I watched enough to believe it."

"No Ledger."

"No Ledger."

"No abilities."

"No."

"So if something goes wrong, it just goes wrong."

"That's usually how bodies work."

Despite himself, he let out a short, startled laugh. Vel's mouth moved at one corner, brief and unwilling.

Heat hit one side of his face. Night cold sat on the other. The thought of losing every ability he had left should have been pure terror. Some of it was. The rest was stranger. His hands still remembered the drag of thread through skin. The fire in front of him existed because he'd coaxed it up from bark and patience instead of burning a level on command. Dren's arm was closed because Kem had done clumsy work and kept doing it until it held. A life without the Ledger sounded brutal. It also sounded clean in a way the Vault never had.

"I'm not sure if that sounds better or worse," he said.

"It depends on the day," Vel said.

They let the conversation stop there. Dren slept. Insects resumed their work in the trees. Far to the north, the agents they had escaped were probably limping through a story that made them sound less foolish. None of that pressed as hard as the new fact sitting between him and the fire: there was a place where the number ended and the world kept going.

Kem looked at his hands again. Blood in the cracks of the knuckles. The needle prick in his thumb. Callus beginning where there had only been softness a month ago.

"I'm going to sleep," he said.

"I'll take first watch."

"You should sleep. Your ribs."

"I'll take first watch."

That ended it. Kem lay down with his pack under his head the way Dren had shown him. The ground was cold and uneven. A root worked into his shoulder blade until he found the angle that hurt least and accepted it. Above the branches, stars. He did not know their names. In the Vault, nobody had ever needed the sky for anything.

Sleep came in pieces. In the last one before dawn, his father sat in the Conservatory with the usual blank eyes and a Ledger at zero. This time the face was not empty. Kem woke before the dream could make sense of that, to gray light, white ash, and Vel still sitting upright where he'd left her, watching the trees.
