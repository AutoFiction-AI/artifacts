# Chapter 13

The road failed under a farmer before noon.

For two days Kem had felt the hollowness in it through his boots: packed earth laid over old Shaping, sunk in places, split in others, one more Margins job the Hoarded had paid for once and never touched again. Twenty yards ahead, the surface gave a long dirt sigh and folded into the drainage channel beneath it. The farmer dropped to hands and knees on a slab that kept tipping under him, his herb pack sliding toward the break.

Kem was at forty-three. Seven levels gone in two days: light in a culvert, a stranger's wrist, warmth against basin wind, strength for Dren's bad arm, clean water, fire. Vel hadn't said a word about any of it. That silence tracked him harder than her old corrections ever had.

Dren stopped dead, left arm held a little away from his body, ready to move anyway. The road kept eating outward. Soil cracked. The farmer reached for the pack because it was worth something, and the grab shifted his weight and tore another foot from the edge.

Kem threw the Stabilization Charm before he'd finished choosing it.

Twelve levels left him in a burst that hit hands, sternum, eyes. The collapse locked in place mid-failure: broken road frozen at wrong angles over the exposed channel, dirt and old Shaping held together by force and nothing else. Dren dropped his own pack, crossed the tilt in eight careful steps, hauled the farmer off, then went back for the herbs.

By the time Dren returned, Kem was sitting on the road's edge with both hands braced in the dust. The Ledger had already done the subtraction. A Barrier he'd been counting on was gone. So was the speed ability he'd used twice in the Margins. At forty-three he had been able to read the end of the list. At thirty-one he could feel it.

Thirty-one. Vel's starting number.

"Thirty-one," he said.

Vel looked at him once, sharp and unreadable. "Then you know."

Half a mile south they took shelter in an abandoned farmstead, four walls, a sagging roof, a dead well in the courtyard. Dren found a clay jug that still held water and tested it a mouthful at a time. Across from Kem, Vel had Maren's route journal open on her knees, one thumb pinning the page flat. The handwriting on the open page wasn't hers.

"I want to know what you meant."

"What thirty-one feels like."

"Not that."

She closed the journal but kept a hand on it.

Kem leaned back against the hot wall and listened to the thin hum of what he had left. Healing. Light. A few combat uses. Not much room for mistakes. "You told me zero was a threshold. Fine. You didn't tell me how it opens."

"No."

"Tell me now."

For a second it looked like she'd give him one of the smaller adjacent answers she used when she wanted a conversation to end without ending. Instead she said, "It takes a cascade. Spending near the site. One person can hit zero and get through, but the opening barely holds. Two people spending together, it holds longer. More descent means more force."

"How much more."

"Two hundred. Two-fifty. Three hundred." Vel's voice stayed flat. "The bigger the drop, the stronger the crossing."

The number sat between them.

"And you knew that when you found me."

"I knew a solo crossing wasn't enough."

"That's not the question."

Dren's pouring slowed to almost nothing.

Vel looked at the far wall. "No."

Kem waited. When she didn't go on, he said, "You needed someone high enough to burn down beside you."

"Yes."

The word landed clean and ugly.

Something Thessa had said in the settlement came back, not the whole speech, just the core of it: years ago, Vel asking after defectors still high enough to matter. Kem had shoved it aside because there had always been the next emergency, the next cost, the next road south. Now there was only this.

"How long were you looking?"

"Several years."

"How many people."

"Two serious leads. One had already spent too far down. The other wasn't willing."

"So I was the first one who was high enough and foolish enough."

"You were the first one who wanted zero."

That didn't help either.

The first weeks of the journey shifted in his head, not cleanly, just enough to make him sick. Vel watching him in the forest, correcting his fire, teaching him how not to waste himself. The campfire talk. The restraint lessons. Her face at the labor camp while he burned through stranger after stranger because he couldn't stop. Every memory now had another set of numbers under it.

"That's the word, then," he said. "Component."

Dren set the jug down very carefully.

Vel didn't answer.

"When you found me, you weren't seeing a person first. You were seeing range. Budget. Whether I'd last."

"I was keeping you alive."

"For what."

She didn't move.

Kem stood.

Through the doorway the dead fields flashed white in the heat. A labor notice was still pasted to the outer wall, the ink faded but legible: RENEWABLE EXPENDITURE UNIT. PROJECTED ANNUAL OUTPUT. The same language as the camp, the same patient administrative obscenity.

"I keep trying to make this smaller than it is." His voice had gone polite in the worst possible way. He heard his father in it and hated that too. "You spend forty years pulling people out of rivers and ditches and bad harvests, and then you see me and it's not me. It's the number. It's what I can burn."

Vel said nothing.

Kem pointed at the notice on the wall. "That's what they do. Here. They look at a body and start doing yield. You looked at me and started doing math."

"Kem."

"No. Don't trim it down for me." The words came faster, losing shape as they came. "You needed a high-level spender, you found one, you taught him just enough to keep him functional, and when I thought you were looking at me you were looking at what I'd still have left when we got there. Not me. That. You recruited me as a component. That's what they do here. You replicated the same system. Nicer reason, same thing."

The courtyard held still around it.

Dren rose, took his pack by the strap, and said, "I'll check the south road." No one stopped him. His boots scraped stone and were gone.

Vel stayed where she was, hands loose at her sides. One false start touched her mouth and died there. Another. Silence stretched until it became the answer.

"You're right about the beginning," she said at last.

Kem laughed once without humor. "Only the beginning."

"What happened after was real."

"Was it."

"Yes." She swallowed. "I needed your levels. I needed the drop. I found you because of that. Then the rest happened anyway. It doesn't clean up the start. I know that."

"And the lessons. The restraint. All that talk about waste."

"Both." A small flinch crossed her face. "It was survival and it was budget. I can't split them clean."

"Convenient."

"It's ugly. It's still true."

He wanted to leave. The impulse came whole and immediate: north, away, let her solve her own crossing. Thirty-one levels said otherwise. North meant broken road, no journal, no map, Sable somewhere behind them. South meant Vel at eighteen, Dren with one bad arm, and the only route any of them had. Leaving was a plan for people who knew where they were going.

Kem looked down at his right hand instead. The signet sat on his ring finger, silver-dark, the closed circle crest worn smooth by weeks of sweat and dust. House Aldraste. Preservation by standing still while everyone else paid.

He pulled it off.

Pale skin showed underneath, soft where the metal had sealed itself against the world. The ring fit in his palm with ugly familiarity. For a second he pictured throwing it over the wall into the scrub and hearing nothing when it landed.

Instead he closed his hand and put it in his pocket.

Dren came back before either of them spoke again. "South road's passable if we cut the fields for a stretch. Another collapse ahead. Dry channel under it."

"We move now," Vel said.

Kem shouldered his pack first. A new callus along his palm caught on the strap and made him notice, again, that his hands no longer looked like they belonged to someone from the Vault. Ten feet out into the lane he stopped just long enough for the others to fall in, then kept going.

Dren took the middle without comment. Vel stayed behind him. No one argued the formation.

Another quarter mile and the road broke again. They climbed down into the dry drainage, dirt sliding under their boots, old earth walls close on either side, the sky narrowed to a hard white strip overhead. Thirty-one hummed at the edge of Kem's awareness. Ahead of him, Dren's pack shifted with each step. Behind him, Vel kept the distance he set.

He kept walking.
