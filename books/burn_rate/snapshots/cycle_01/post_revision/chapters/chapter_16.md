# Chapter 16

Two days after Sable walked out of the farmhouse, the three of them were sleeping in an abandoned Hoarded waystation east of the direct road to the Scour. Vel had taken the long loop through old irrigation cuts and relay posts because the straight southern line was open ground, easy to watch, and probably held by people with better levels than theirs if Sable was right. The loop bought them cover, one intact pump, three walls still standing, and about two days before the basin.

The road had cost them anyway. Kem spent seven levels in the stupid little bites that passed for caution when you still had levels left: one for light in a collapsed culvert, two bracing a washout while Dren crossed with his left arm pinned to his side, one forcing water through a pump handle rusted half shut, three more in scraps of strength and reinforcement when Margins infrastructure gave out under their feet. Vel spent two of her own, one on concealment when a dust line to the north moved too evenly to be weather, one keeping the waystation stairs from coming down under Dren's weight. By dusk Kem was at eighteen and she was at fourteen.

Dren was packing before sunrise.

Vel heard him through the thin wall of the waystation, a bedroll tied wrong, retied, tied wrong again. A buckle knocked stone. A strap dragged over wood. A muffled curse when leather caught on stone. She knew what she was listening to. She'd been expecting it since the farmhouse, since Sable's warning, since Dren had started looking south with the face of a man doing sums against a child's pain.

Outside, the dawn was thin and colorless. Summer heat already pressed down before the light had properly arrived, and the soil gave off the chalky smell the deep Margins always had, used past season and weather. The station had been a Hoarded supply post once. The stamped sign on the doorframe still read PRODUCTIVITY TRANSFER STATION 7-SOUTH, though half the paint had gone to powder. Someone had carved *fuck off* under it in neat letters.

Dren came out a few minutes later. He carried his pack low on his right shoulder because his left arm would not take the weight for long. The silo gash had healed into a thick, shiny ridge that pulled whenever he tried to lift above his chest. Kem's stitching had held the arm together and not done much else.

"Morning," he said.

"Yeah."

He set the pack down by the broken foundation wall and looked at it for a moment before looking at her. He had the face of someone about to say a thing he'd been turning over for days and still didn't trust.

"I'm heading back," Dren said.

Vel nodded. She'd known, but the words still altered the air. "When?"

"Now." His right hand went to the left forearm without thought, checking the hurt. "Should've gone a week ago, if I'm honest. Maybe two. I kept finding reasons."

"You don't owe us an explanation."

"I know I don't." He sat on the wall. The stone was already warm. "I'd rather say it than carry it home."

Vel leaned against the doorway and waited. Kem was stirring inside, floorboards creaking under a body that had gotten leaner over six weeks of bad road. Kem at eighteen. Vel at fourteen. Numbers that would have been a punchline in the Spent Towns.

"My daughter's sick," Dren said. "You know that. What you don't know is what it means when the healer in Roth burns forty of her own levels on a child's legs and then hands me the price in marks like she's trying to buy those levels back. The money you put up last time covered about half. I still left home short." He picked at a callus on his palm. "And I've been out here six weeks watching you two burn toward nothing, and I keep thinking she doesn't care about thresholds. She doesn't know what a cascade is. She's eight. Some mornings her legs won't take her when she swings out of bed, and she looks at me like I was supposed to stop the day from doing that to her."

"I know."

"You know the numbers. You don't know what it sounds like when she hits the floor."

Vel didn't argue. He was right. She knew Dren's life as arithmetic, exactly and usefully, without the sound of an eight-year-old trying not to cry.

Wind came over the depleted flats carrying dust and old Fertility residue. Three hundred meters south the road ran through land that had once been farmland, rows still visible as ghost lines in exhausted earth.

"I watched the kid spend ninety-eight levels on strangers," Dren said. "People he'd never met. I watched you count every one of them and not stop him because you needed them for your own road and couldn't say that out loud, not in front of people this system had already eaten." His gaze stayed on hers. "I'm not judging. I'm telling you what it looks like from where I'm sitting. Two people burning everything they've got on a door that might not open while the world's full of plainer doors that'd open fine if someone could afford them."

"That's fair."

"It's not enough." He scratched at his jaw. Sun had bleached his beard in patches over the last weeks. "My girl's my zero, Vel. Same thing you're doing, if you flatten it enough. Mine just looks like a healer's table in Roth and a bill for forty levels of bone work I can barely cover."

Grief tightened under Vel's ribs, not the old injury there, but the other thing behind it. She looked at the ghosted station sign instead of Dren's face.

"You've been good company," she said. The sentence felt thin as paper.

"I've been paid company." His mouth twitched once. "Some of it was paid. The last bit wasn't." He stood, hitched the pack higher on his right shoulder, and winced when the movement pulled across the scarred left arm. "Take care of the kid. He's worth more than his numbers."

"I know that."

"Do you?" There was no malice in it. That made it land harder. "From where I've been standing, you know he's useful. That's not the same thing."

Dust crossed the road in a low sheet. Vel let it pass between them and said nothing.

Kem came out blinking into the raw light. His face was thinner than it had been at the start, the softness of Vault life stripped off by walking and heat. Since the fracture he'd gotten quieter. Not Hoarded-quiet. More deliberate than that.

"Dren."

"Yeah." Dren looked at him. "Heading home."

Kem took that in without moving much at all. "Your arm."

"Still works enough." Dren flexed the left hand once, grimaced, let it hang. "Ugly job. Kept the thing attached."

"I could..." Kem stopped.

"Don't." Dren said it gently. "You've got eighteen levels and a long walk left. Spend them on something that matters."

"Your arm matters."

"Hurts, is all." Dren shrugged with the right shoulder. "I've hauled worse. Pulled a cart with a broken wrist once after the mule dropped outside Garren because the load paid Mira's medicine for a month." He looked at Kem for a second longer. "You spend different now. In the Spent Towns you threw levels around like a drunk lord at a festival. Now you count."

Kem put out his hand. Dren looked at it, then took it. One firm shake and he let go.

"Tell Mira..." Kem started, then stopped.

"Yeah," Dren said.

He nodded once at Vel and turned north. His boots made the thin gritty sound depleted ground always did under a good hard sole. Vel watched him until dust took him. He never looked back.

After that the station felt wrong. Two packs where there had been three. There was too much open road around them.

---

Two sets of footsteps made the road sound wrong.

Vel kept expecting Dren's heavier tread on her right and getting scrub, wind, cracked earth. The southern road had been laid down for Hoarded supply wagons and mined hollow underneath for decades after. Subsidence had broken it into long, tilted plates. She mapped the fractures as she walked, step left here, skirt the dip there, old habit running ahead of thought.

They passed a Margins farmstead where someone was still trying. An old woman pushed a hand plow through chalky soil that had been Fertility-Charmed to death decades ago and now gave back whatever chalk, subsoil, and stubbornness could manage. She never looked up.

Kem stopped.

Vel took three more steps before the missing footfall registered. She turned.

He stood in the middle of the road with the sun straight on him, shadow tucked small under his boots. He was twenty-six years old and down to eighteen levels, burned from three hundred and finally looking like a person who knew it.

"No," he said.

"No what?"

"No more steps. Not until you tell me why."

"I told you the mechanism. The cascade..."

"Not the mechanism. You." He dragged in a breath and let it out slow. "Why you, specifically. Why you've been walking toward zero for eight years. Why you carry that journal like it's the only object in the world. Why Thessa looked at you like you'd killed someone. Why any of this matters enough for you to do what you've done."

The Margins had no birds in this stretch, only scrub and heat and the faint hum of the Ledger at the edge of her awareness. The number there was fourteen. Fourteen things she could still do if it came to that.

She could have given him the small answer. Technical details, route math, the geography of threshold sites. Thirty years of practice had made her good at answering the question next to the real one. He would have heard the dodge this time.

Sunlight was in his eyes. He did not squint. He just waited.

"Okay," Vel said. "Sit down."

They sat on a length of collapsed wall beside the road, maybe an old bunkhouse footing, maybe storage, maybe one more transfer station the Hoarded had built and abandoned. Heat came through the stone into her legs. Vel took Maren's journal from the inner pocket of her pack and held it in her lap.

"Her name was Maren," she said.

The name caught in her chest harder than she'd expected. Kem waited.

"We spent together for eleven years. Deep routes, Margins work, the jobs nobody with sense or money volunteered for. Healing, repairs, pulling people out of structures that should've been condemned twenty years earlier." Vel rubbed a thumb along the leather spine. "She was better at people than I was. Walked into a settlement and half the work was done already because everyone trusted her in five minutes. I had to earn it the long way."

Dry brush clicked against itself in the wind.

"We split the jobs. She took the uglier healing because she had the hands for it. I did structural work, field repair, the dull things that keep roofs up and pumps running. She kept the route journal. Wells, supply caches, places with clean water, places where the road wanted to kill you." Vel opened it without really seeing the page. "Her maps were bad. Still worked."

"You loved her."

"Yes."

That answer was easier than the next ones. Vel kept going before she could stop.

"She hit zero first. Eight years ago. It wasn't planned. Building collapse in a settlement south of here, old mining damage under the foundation. She was at three. Spent to stabilize the house. Spent again when ceiling came down on a kid. That last piece took the rest."

Vel's hands stayed steady on the journal. Her body could keep composure for her even when the rest of her had gone elsewhere.

"The threshold opened. I was close enough to see through it. A place. Real place. Buildings. Light. People moving. Maren looked back at me." Vel swallowed once. "Then it closed."

Heat gathered under the back of her shirt. Her thumb found the worn groove on the journal spine and stayed there.

"That was eight years ago," Kem said.

"Yes."

"And you've been looking for a way to do it again ever since."

"Yes."

"The journal. The routes. The mechanism. All of it."

"Yes." She cut him off before he could build the whole shape out loud. "I was at sixty-two when she crossed. I spent thirty-one levels over the next eight years working out the mechanism, checking other sites, looking for someone who could make the cascade hold. An old Margins woman gave me the first real piece of it two years after Maren crossed. She'd seen three people vanish in her lifetime and had better sense than anybody in the Vault."

"Someone who could make it hold," Kem said.

"Someone with the levels." Vel did not look away. "I heard about a Hoarded lord's son who'd walked out with two hundred and eighty-six and a habit of burning whatever was in front of him. I went north. I found you. I saw a road south."

Kem was quiet for a beat. "At the start."

"At the start."

He rolled a chip of mortar between his fingers and dropped it. "You keep saying that like it's enough."

"It's true."

"It isn't enough."

There was no point fighting that either.

"I've been asking for weeks," he said. "After Thessa. After the mechanism. Every silent mile since the farmhouse."

"Asking isn't the same as stopping." Vel turned the journal over in her hands. The leather was warm, the edges worn, the same object she'd been carrying for eight years. "You were still moving. I could keep the answer broken up."

That landed. He let it sit there.

"Tell me the rest," he said.

Vel looked down the road. A weather-stripped sign still clung to a post fifty meters south, only fragments left on it: RENEWABLE... UNIT... ANNUAL OUTPUT. The Margins kept the paperwork long after it lost the people.

"I'm doing the same thing," she said.

Kem said nothing.

"You told me already, back in the Margins. Resource." Her hand stayed flat on the journal cover. "Two hundred and eighty-six. That's what I went north for. A Hoarded lord's son who'd walked out with a number like that and was already burning through it — I didn't think about you. I thought about what you had."

"And after?"

"After, you kept being a person and I kept needing the number." She moved her thumb along the journal spine. "You spent forty-one on those scouts and I wanted to hit you. You stitched Dren's arm and your hands were shaking and I didn't know what to do with that. Ninety-eight in the Margins and I let you because I couldn't say stop — not to people the system already ate." She looked at the ghost rows in the exhausted soil. "None of that changes what I did first."

Kem stared at the road between his boots. "Maren."

Vel opened the journal to a page near the back. The handwriting there was smaller, the notes crowded into margins that had run out of room years ago. "She'd walk into a place and spend because someone was standing there hurt. I went looking for someone to spend." She shut the journal. "I don't know how to make those the same thing. I kept trying to."

"I'm not sorry I found you. I'm not sorry I used your levels." The next sentence took longer. "I'm sorry I kept dressing it up."

The old signpost creaked in the wind. Dust moved over the road and settled again.

"What if she's not there?" Kem asked.

Vel had rehearsed better answers than the true one. She let them go.

"Then I'll be at zero in a world without the Ledger," she said. "And that's still better than this."

He looked at her for a long moment.

"Okay," he said.

"Okay what."

"Okay I'll keep walking." Kem stood. His knees cracked. "Not because it's fine. You used me. Same road, same number, you'd do it again."

"Yes."

"Good." He brushed dust from his hands. "Then from here to the Scour, no more pieces. I want the route. Distances, bad ground, whatever you think the Reserve has waiting. All of it before we spend another level."

Vel looked at him, then at the journal.

"Okay," she said.

"I'm not forgiving you."

"I know."

"Good."

This time when he started south, he didn't leave her to decide whether the conversation was over. He waited until she stood and matched his pace.

---

They walked through the afternoon heat with the journal out between stops.

At the first stretch of shade worth the name, Vel crouched with it open on a broken culvert wall and showed Kem the last pages: the abandoned settlement west of the basin, the old gallery line that might still offer cover, the descent she thought the Reserve watched least. He asked practical questions now. Distances. Water. How many bad options were still ahead. Vel answered them before he had to ask twice.

The road bent around a dead settlement where Shaping-built walls were still standing without roofs. A notice clung to what had once been a community board: PRODUCTIVITY TIER ASSESSMENT. ALL UNITS REPORT TO SECTOR ADMINISTRATION FOR ANNUAL OUTPUT REVIEW. Someone had written underneath it in a different hand: *They left. We're still here. The notice is still here too.*

By the time they reached a communal well, Kem was still walking beside her instead of ten feet ahead. He filled both canteens without comment and handed hers over. Fingers touched on the metal and moved on.

Vel put the journal back in her pack after that. Maren's cramped handwriting rode against her spine again. There were two more days in it, maybe a little less if the road held. After that either the journal had been a map or it had been a relic.

The road dipped over an old mine gallery. Vel stepped around the depression. Kem matched the line without thinking and kept talking.

"If the Reserve has four," he said, looking ahead, "and we have thirty-two between us, that's bad."

"Yes."

"How bad."

"Bad enough that I don't want the first surprise to be there."

"Fine." He adjusted his pack. "Then no surprises."

Fourteen hummed at the edge of her awareness. Eighteen hummed at his. Thirty-two lay between them and a permanent garrison at the Scour.

Dren was somewhere north with a ruined left arm, a hundred and twenty levels, and an eight-year-old daughter waiting for a healer to make her legs hold. Vel kept seeing his face when he'd said *my girl's my zero.*

The arithmetic ahead was still terrible. It was just honest now.
