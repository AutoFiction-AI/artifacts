# Chapter 6

The Council chamber had seventeen chairs, and Sable Orm knew the occupant of each by the sound they made sitting down. Councilor Breve's knees cracked. Councilor Hain breathed through his mouth when he thought no one was listening. Lady Aldraste settled into her chair without a sound. Around this table, that counted as rank.

Sable had spent twenty-three years in this room. She had organized its agendas, drafted its minutes, revised its protocols, and, on four occasions she did not discuss, corrected the arithmetic of Council members who outranked her. To her, the chamber was a ledger with furniture: every column aligned, every entry predictable.

What she did not know was why she had been summoned to stand before it rather than sit behind the recording desk.

"The Aldraste situation," Councilor Breve began.

Sable waited. Across two decades of administrative service, she had learned that the first sentence of a Council address was rarely the one that mattered.

"Young Lord Aldraste has been outside the walls for approximately five days." Breve checked his notes. The gesture itself was an indictment. "He has spent an estimated twelve levels. In five days. The perimeter enforcement detail authorized under standing recovery protocol made contact yesterday. It did not recover him."

Around the table, hands stopped over paper. Twelve levels. In the Vault, the number was obscene. Sable's nearest colleague had spent four across an entire career, two on a Perception ability during an emergency audit and two on a Light charm during a power failure that threatened the record vaults. She had been spoken to about it.

"The House signet was taken from the ceremonial stand," Lady Aldraste said. Her voice carried the calm of a woman who had decided exactly how much of herself this room was going to get. "My son did not plan this. That is the charitable interpretation, and I will be offering no other."

Sable recognized the structure at once. Lady Aldraste was entering a breach into Council business, not a son into family trouble.

"The question before the Council," Breve said, "is the next method of retrieval."

The enforcement team had gone out under standing recovery authority. This new pursuit would bear the Council's name.

"There has been discussion regarding the deployment of specialized resources."

Specialized resources. Sable had encountered references to them in Council records three times across her career. Each reference had been redacted before filing. The hand changed each time. The secrecy had a procedure.

Her mind went to the sealed wing on the Vault's eastern corridor. Twenty-three years of passing those doors had taught her that some silences were manufactured more carefully than others.

"The Red Reserve," Lady Aldraste said.

"For the benefit of those present," Breve said, looking directly at Sable now, "the Red Reserve is a Council-maintained unit of Hoarded personnel authorized to train in the use of abilities. For security purposes."

Authorized to train. Permitted to spend. Behind the eastern doors, the Vault kept people whose existence contradicted the sermons by continuing to breathe.

"The Reserve is not a viable option for this operation," Councilor Hain said. He said it too quickly.

Lady Aldraste answered in the cadence of someone reading from a prepared document. "Deploying trained spenders for a retrieval operation would constitute a public acknowledgment that the philosophy of preservation cannot address all institutional concerns. The existence of the Reserve is predicated on its non-existence. Using it visibly destroys the premise."

"Agreed," Breve said. "The Reserve is not deployable for this purpose."

The sequence settled into place. Enforcement had already failed in the field. The Reserve could not fail in public. What remained was something House Aldraste and the Council could survive on paper.

"Auditor Orm," Breve said.

Sable's stomach performed a maneuver she did not have an administrative category for.

"The Council would like to discuss your availability for a field assignment."

"I am available for any assignment the Council requires." The correct response came out before the incorrect one finished forming.

"The retrieval of Kem Aldraste requires a lead operative with institutional credibility, administrative competence, and discretion. You have been recommended by multiple Council members." Breve did not look at Lady Aldraste when he said it.

"Councilor Breve. I have not left the capital in forty-seven years." Sable delivered the line as an inventory of relevant data, not a protest. "I have never operated field equipment. I have never navigated terrain. My longest recorded distance on foot in a single day was approximately two kilometers during the record-room relocation of Year Eighteen."

"Which you managed with distinction," Breve said.

"The logistics of document transport across three buildings are not comparable to field pursuit."

"You are precisely what we need." Lady Aldraste met Sable's eyes as if confirming a selection already entered in the record. "Someone who represents what we are. Not what we have hidden."

Nobody contradicted her. Hain cleared his throat and stopped there. Lady Aldraste's hands lay flat on the table, fingers aligned. One thumbnail had a torn edge. In twenty-three years, Sable had never seen that.

The chamber had already decided what Sable was for. She was level 300, visibly unspent, and impossible to mistake for a trained operative. That was apparently an asset.

"I will need a team," Sable said.

"Of course. We have prepared a list of suitable operatives."

"Field-trained?"

Breve looked down at his papers. Hain adjusted a cuff. No one said yes.

"I see," Sable said.

The Council's list of suitable operatives contained eight names. Three declined immediately, one citing a joint condition, one citing a commitment to an ongoing inventory project, and one submitting a written objection that referenced seven separate protocols governing voluntary assignment exemptions. The remaining five reported to Sable's office at the designated hour.

All five were level 300. That narrowed nothing.

Tarrell arrived with three notebooks and a personal seal and announced, before sitting down, that he had been told this was a documentation assignment. Kess had volunteered and looked pleased with herself in the strained, overbright way of someone who had mistaken apprehension for zeal. Lenn came with a field manual memorized and a pack cinched high enough to cut his shoulders raw by noon. Marris kept checking her Ledger as though the 300 might turn out to be a clerical error. Chael, a records porter, brought a compass and the only shoulders in the room that looked as though they had lifted anything heavier than paper.

"I was told this was a documentation assignment," Tarrell said again, perhaps believing repetition might alter classification.

"It is a field retrieval," Sable said.

"Then my classification headers are wrong." Tarrell turned the top notebook over in his hands. "I indexed three tiers of source annotation for documentation-class work. None of that transfers to retrieval."

Tarrell looked down at the notebooks with an expression of quiet betrayal.

Kess studied the route map on Sable's desk. "Will we be walking the entire distance?"

"The target is traveling south on foot. We will need to do the same."

"Right. That should be fine, I've been walking the courtyards every morning." Kess folded her hands on the map. "For how long?"

"The estimate is twelve to fifteen days," Sable said. "That estimate was drafted inside this building, so I advise caution."

Kess placed a parasol on the desk with the care of someone setting down a small hope.

The crate from the Council sat beside the map. Sable had reviewed the manifest the night before. She had not opened it. That omission corrected itself immediately.

Inside were six ration packs sealed in waxed paper, six bedrolls tagged with serial numbers, six Communication charms in lead-lined cases, a medical kit, a hand-drawn map, and a four-page document titled *Standard Field Protocol for Council-Authorized Operations*.

Sable opened the protocol and read aloud. "Total expenditure is not to exceed the pre-authorized operational limit of two levels per operative per day."

No one spoke.

She continued. "All ability use must be pre-authorized by the team leader through completion of Form 7-C: Justification of Level Expenditure. The form requires a written explanation of no fewer than two hundred words per instance of ability deployment."

"What if the situation requires immediate action?" Lenn asked. His voice stayed level by effort.

Sable turned the page. "The emergency provision reduces the written justification to one hundred words in circumstances of direct and immediate physical threat. The form must still be completed prior to deployment."

"Prior to."

"Yes."

No one laughed, which made it worse.

The rest of the kit improved nothing. The medical instructions referenced an Appendix D that did not exist. The map called a river ford twelve kilometers south "manageable" and described the crossing pace as standard administrative. No weapons had been issued. A cluster of settlements farther downroute was marked "cooperation expected" in the same hand that had labeled an exposed pass "navigable."

Chael picked up the suturing needle and held it in the center of his palm, studying it as though it might explain itself.

"Has anyone here stitched a wound?" Sable asked.

Silence, then Lenn: "I have read about the procedure."

"That is now a qualification," Sable said. "You will carry the medical kit."

After that, they stopped discussing the road and started distributing weight. Chael took the spare bedrolls and the extra rations because he could actually bear the weight. Tarrell was permitted one notebook. Marris got the field log because her hands remained steady when everyone else's had started finding interesting new tasks. Kess was instructed to leave the parasol and wrap her heels before dawn. Lenn had to retie every pack in the room, including Sable's. The Council's "manageable" first halt at twelve kilometers was crossed out. Sable marked six instead.

Tarrell watched her alter the map. "The protocol does not authorize retroactive justification."

"Then it will be late," Sable said. "If someone needs to spend to prevent injury, they spend first and report to me after. We are not writing essays at the river."

Nobody argued after that.

"We depart at dawn," Sable said. "Bring only what you can carry on your back. If you cannot carry it, you do not bring it."

Kess's eyes shifted to the parasol.

"You do not bring it," Sable said.

Dawn arrived the way it arrived in the Vault, precisely, light entering through windows engineered to illuminate without warming. Sable had slept two hours. The remaining six hours had gone to rereading the field manual, repacking by her revised assignments, and trying not to imagine the southern road in any detail at all.

The changes were visible at the main gate. Tarrell had one notebook. Kess's heels were wrapped in linen. Marris wore a single pack after Lenn rethreaded the straps for her. Chael carried the medical kit and two extra bedrolls. Lenn had lowered his own pack to a position that suggested recent instruction. It still sat too high.

The gate was where Kem Aldraste had walked out five days earlier. The ceremonial stand where House signets were displayed still held an empty square of velvet in the Aldraste position. A gap in the record.

"Senior Auditor." The gate warden had held the post for thirty years without incident, because incidents did not happen at the Vault's gate. "I am required to log your departure. How many in the party?"

"Six. Council-authorized retrieval operation. The reference number is in the briefing file."

"Estimated return date?"

"Three to four weeks."

He wrote it down with immaculate handwriting. Nothing in his manner suggested that six level-300 Hoarded citizens walking out to pursue a defector across open country was anything unusual. The Vault maintained its composure.

From its case, Sable took the Wayfinder charm. Small disc. Cool metal. Tracking glyph. Cost: two levels.

Two levels. Her mother had died at 296, ashamed of the four she had spent across an entire life. Sable had spent none.

Kem's file supplied the face. Formal. Composed. Twenty-six years of stillness, then the gate.

She activated the glyph.

Her knees went first.

Stone bit her palm as she caught the archway. Direction flooded in, south-southeast, distance, pace, the clean pull of a living line running through her body toward someone moving away. The knowledge had nothing to do with sight or hearing. It entered lower, stranger, as if some buried instrument inside her had been struck hard enough to wake.

300 became 298.

The threshold stone hit the back of her legs when she sat.

"Senior Auditor?" Lenn asked.

"A moment."

Breathing came too fast. The Wayfinder did not care. It kept running at the base of her skull. South-southeast. Roughly nine days at foot pace. Still moving. She could not dismiss it without spending again.

298. Her mind kept returning to the number and dropping against it, the way a foot drops when it expects one more stair and finds only air.

She made herself stand.

"The target is south-southeast," Sable said. Her voice held. "Approximately nine days at our pace, assuming our pace proves measurable."

No one said anything. Five faces watched her with the politeness of people who had just seen something happen for which the Vault had given them no approved vocabulary.

"We proceed south."

They walked through the gate. Sable Orm, forty-seven years old and level 298, stepped over the Vault's threshold and into the world.

Warm air moved across her face. Real birds made noise without permission. The road under her boots was packed earth with roots lifting through it, nothing polished, nothing level, everything asking for attention. Behind her rose the walls that had contained her entire life. She did not look back.

By the first hour, the revised plan was already revising itself. Kess had bled through one heel wrap. Tarrell surrendered the notebook to Chael after ten minutes of pretending he could carry it and his pack at once. Marris could not keep the field log while walking, so the first halt came early and beside a drainage ditch instead of at any Council-approved landmark. Lenn abandoned the pace he had planned in his head. Chael, carrying half the room, was the one who never complained.

Marris opened the log on her knee. Sable dictated the entry while the Wayfinder kept humming south in her bones: first halt advanced, pace reduced, injuries minor, pursuit continuing.

The road went south. She followed it.
