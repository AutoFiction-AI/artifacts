# Chapter 4

The ridge trail had been climbing since dawn, and Kem's calves were filing complaints he had no language for.

In the Vault, the longest walk was sixty meters, Conservatory to east dining hall, flat stone, soft-soled shoes, silence. Nobody ran. Nobody climbed. The body was a thing you maintained through inaction, like a vase, and it performed accordingly. Now his body was being asked to carry its own weight uphill through tangled forest for six consecutive hours, and it had opinions.

Vel was thirty paces ahead, moving through the undergrowth with the ease of someone who'd done this since before he was born. She didn't look back. She hadn't looked back since they'd broken camp, and Kem had stopped deciding whether that meant trust or indifference because both answers felt equally bad.

The highland forest closed overhead. The trees were old. Kem couldn't tell one from another, an omission in his education he had not known existed until two days ago. He'd asked Vel what a particular tree was called, and she'd looked at him as if he'd asked what walking was. So he had stopped asking. Light filtered through the canopy in shifting columns. The air smelled of earth, resin, and leaves that had been falling toward soil since last year. Pack straps had worn through the linen of his shirt and were working on the skin beneath. By afternoon he would have fresh blisters. Yesterday supplied precedent.

His Ledger read 286, a number he felt the way he'd once felt the Vault's walls, always there at the edge of thought, a quiet hum of remaining capacity. Fourteen levels had gone since leaving: the Projection Shield, the Sustenance Charm, the little wastes Vel counted with the precision of someone forced to live by subtraction. The hum was thinner now. Poured from.

Vel stopped.

She put one hand against a tree trunk and angled her head toward the ridge behind them. Kem stopped too. The pack settled. His calves lodged their formal protest.

Kem listened. Wind in the upper canopy. Small things moving through brush. A bird somewhere east making a broken call he could not interpret. Nothing else. That had already taught him enough to be worried.

"Company," Vel said, still facing their backtrail. "More than one. Coming down after us."

"How far?"

"Close enough that the birds quit three minutes ago and haven't come back. I saw boot treads in the mud two hundred meters behind us, not ours." She turned downslope into denser cover. "Stay behind me. Step where I step."

Kem followed. Quiet was not new to him. The Vault trained quiet like theology. But the Vault's floors were marble and stone. This ground cracked, shifted, slid, and resented his boots, which had been sold to him by a trader near the gates who'd seen his clothes and adjusted the price upward.

They descended through a drainage gully slick with damp. Vel moved through it without breaking stride. Kem sat down involuntarily and slid three meters on his back, collecting mud and a gash across his right palm from a sharp stone. He bit the inside of his cheek and kept moving.

Vel took them along exposed rock, then through young trees growing close enough to strip his pack. Twenty minutes of careful movement through terrain that seemed personally offended by his existence.

Then she stopped again. Her mouth tightened. Arithmetic she didn't like.

"They changed bearing," she said. "They have a tracker. Separate specialist, most likely. Someone already burned a level on Perception."

"They spent a level to find me?"

"That's the least interesting part of that sentence." She looked at him. "The Hoarded authorized somebody to spend. That means Council directive. This isn't your family sending a servant to talk sense."

Weight settled in his stomach. Dense and specific. Somewhere in the Vault, in rooms he could picture because he'd spent years passing their doors, paperwork had been signed and sealed and handed to people whose job it now was to bring him home.

Vel checked the terrain uphill and down, measuring sight lines, escape angles, ground.

"We can't outrun a Perception tracker in forest this thick," she said. "Not at the pace you walk."

"I could use a Speed-"

"No."

"You don't even know what I was going to-"

"You were going to suggest Speed or Concealment. Either one would cost you eight to twelve because you don't know how to calibrate, and I'm not losing a week's budget because you had a thought." She pointed. "Down. We see what they brought."

She chose a natural depression behind a deadfall, a trunk that had come down seasons ago and was already sinking back into the soil. Good sight lines upslope. A ravine below if they had to run. Kem understood enough from books to recognize the shape of a sound decision.

They waited. Three minutes. Five. Forest noise carried on around them, ordinary and indifferent. Behind and above, the birds still had not come back.

Then the pursuers came through the trees.

Seven bodies in all. Six wore the gray livery of Hoarded enforcement, the stiff-cut field coats of minor-house agents trained for retrieval work and allocated five combat levels apiece. The seventh hung ten paces behind them in mud-colored field cloth without a crest, lean and watchful, a man built for seeing rather than closing. The tracker.

The lead agent was a woman about Kem's age, dark-haired, a minor-house crest stitched at her collar. She stopped twenty meters away and raised one hand, palm out, the formal sign of Hoarded authority Kem had seen a thousand times in administrative corridors.

"Kem Aldraste. By directive of the Hoarded Council, under recovery authority granted to the enforcement branch, you are ordered to submit to escort and return to the Vault for-"

"Move," Vel said.

Kem moved.

Vel went right, low and fast. Human fast. She covered the distance to the nearest agent in the time it took Kem to register that the woman beside him was no longer beside him.

Her first ability looked trivial. That was what he would remember. Two fingers to the soft place below an ear. The agent's knees unhinged. He sat hard in the ferns, blinked once like a man losing track of the room, and folded sideways.

One level. One agent.

The second agent, a broad-shouldered woman to the first one's left, was already throwing up her shield. It bloomed in front of her, a clean translucent disc the size they'd practiced in courtyards. Vel stepped outside its line and touched the woman's neck. A light touch. The agent's face emptied, her knees gave, and the shield vanished with her concentration.

Two levels. Two agents.

The lead agent fired her Combat Pulse. Standard enforcement variant, technically sound, meant to stun and drop a body without leaving a mark anyone politically important had to read about. It struck empty air. Vel had read the woman's shoulders first. Two steps left, pivot, close. The heel of Vel's hand touched the agent's sternum and the woman lost command of her own body from the ribs down. She collapsed in pieces, bewildered all the way to the ground.

Three levels. Three agents. Maybe twenty seconds.

Vel straightened. She wasn't breathing hard. She looked at the four still standing: two gray-liveried agents in front, one more off to their right, and the tracker outside their line with his attention on the ground instead of the fight.

"Walk away," she said.

One of the agents did. A young man whose livery still carried the fold lines from storage turned and shoved back through the undergrowth without a word. His retreat kept crashing through the forest long after he vanished. Kem did not blame him.

Two agents stayed. The tracker stayed with them.

They did not look brave. They looked cornered by orders and by the fact that turning around had just become an admission. The man nearest Kem dropped into a stance and gathered energy around his fingers. The woman beside him pulled power for another Pulse. The tracker shifted wide to cover the flank, not attacking, just holding the line long enough for the others to do it.

Kem was still behind the deadfall.

He watched Vel dismantle trained enforcement agents without wasted motion or drama. His pack was still on. Blood slid from the cut across his palm. His heart beat in his wrists, in his throat, in the torn skin of his hand. The Ledger at the edge of his awareness was no longer a steady hum. It felt awake. It pushed at him like a held breath with nowhere to go.

The man fired his Combat Pulse.

It was aimed at Kem, the retrieval objective, the political embarrassment with a family name attached to him. The pulse crossed the distance between them as a knot of concussive force. Kem saw it come with the clear, detached attention of a man experiencing real danger for the first time.

The Combat Pulse rose through him before he could choose it. His body chose first, the way a hand jerks from flame. Power came up from whatever reservoir the Ledger measured, broke through his gut, flooded his chest, ran down both arms, and burst from his open hands.

He had meant to do what Vel had done. Small. Precise. Cheap.

What left his body was none of those things.

The wave widened as it traveled. It was a wall. The agent's incoming pulse vanished inside it like a dropped spark in river water. The man himself went backward off his feet, sailed six meters, and hit a tree trunk with a crack followed by the wet second sound Kem did not let himself name.

The woman beside him took the lateral edge and went cartwheeling through ferns and deadfall. The tracker got behind a boulder in time.

The hillside behind them tore loose. Kem's pulse hit the slope at an angle and the earth failed all at once. Soil, stones, and root systems sheared downhill in a roaring sheet. Trees tilted, groaned, and came over hard enough to shake the ground through the soles of his expensive ceremonial boots. Dust billowed. Stones clattered and split. A trunk landed across the trail they'd climbed that morning and burst down its length.

The noise filled the forest, slapped the opposite ridge, and came back thinner.

Then quiet. The mean, suspended quiet after a wound.

Kem looked at his hands. They were shaking with fear and with a rising hunger he did not trust. Projection Shield and Sustenance Charm had felt like clumsy mistakes. This had felt like a dam giving way. The aftercurrent was still in him, arms buzzing, chest tight, fingers prickling with the ugly appetite of wanting to do it again.

His Ledger had changed. The shift felt like missing a step in the dark. Kem checked.

Two hundred and seventy-one.

Fifteen levels for one Combat Pulse. He had tried to spend one.

The woman he'd thrown sideways was getting up. Blood ran from her hairline. Her left arm hung wrong. The tracker came around his boulder with both hands up, gathering power carefully. Two of the three bodies left to him were still in the fight, and his side of this fight consisted of one fifty-eight-year-old woman at level 28 staring at the destroyed hillside like a person checking the price of catastrophe.

Kem reached for Speed.

The world smeared. Trees became vertical streaks. Ground became a single rushing surface. He had meant to close the distance before the injured woman could fire. He had meant to be quick.

Kem became a projectile.

Twenty meters disappeared in less than a second. Air slammed around him. Leaves stripped off branches in his wake. The woman's eyes widened slowly inside his accelerated perception. Her mouth opened on the beginning of a shout he heard as a low dragged note.

Then the tree was there.

A thick highland trunk, rooted and patient and entirely uninterested in his intentions. Kem hit it chest-first hard enough to drive every molecule of air out of his lungs. Bark burst loose. Pain lit his ribs white. A slab of bark fell onto his head with bureaucratic finality.

Momentum threw him off the trunk instead of stopping him. He spun sideways, hit the ground on his left shoulder, slid through wet loam, and came to rest against a boulder with his face in the dirt and a bell ringing inside his skull.

Eight levels. Speed had cost eight levels and delivered him directly into the oldest tree in reach.

For two seconds he lay there tasting iron and rot. His left ribs reported a structural complaint he would be dealing with later. Above him the canopy spun.

Kem got up.

The ground was unsteady, or he was. His Ledger hummed at 263. The number did not feel low. It felt altered. His body had done more in five minutes than it had in twenty-six years.

The tracker was moving toward him, using cover, sensible enough not to come straight. The injured woman had one good hand up and energy gathering around it for whatever technique remained to her. Two opponents, both functional. Dirt in his eyes. A rib that clicked when he breathed. Speed still jangling through his nerves.

A shield, then.

Kem knew what a shield should be. He had already humiliated himself with one in the Vault, a corridor-wide mistake meant for a doorway. This time he went for small. Personal. Two meters, maybe. Enough to block what was coming.

A dome burst out of the ground around him.

It widened fast and did not stop. Five meters. Ten. Fifteen. The tracker and the injured woman went skidding backward through brush and branches. Vel swore and threw herself behind a boulder as the edge swept past, peeling a broken limb across her forearm. Small trees bowed under the pressure. Air shoved outward hard enough to stir the canopy forty meters above them.

Ten levels. A shield large enough for a village square.

The dome held three seconds and vanished because Kem had no idea how to sustain it. The forest it left behind had been rearranged. The tracker was face-down in a thicket. The injured woman was tangled in fallen branches, bleeding from new cuts, her broken arm pinned under her in a position that made his stomach flip.

His Ledger read 253. Thirty-three levels gone in three attempts.

His chest heaved. The cracked rib sang with every inhale. The tingling in his hands was getting worse, as if each use of power sharpened the body's hunger for the next one.

The woman dragged herself half free of the branches. Her face had gone blank with pain and stubbornness. One knee under her. Then the other. She raised her good hand. Light gathered around her fingers, weak now, shaking, but still enough to hurt him if it landed.

Kem did not go looking for precision. He went for whatever sat closest to the surface and shoved.

Force and light blew out of his right hand, rougher than a Pulse, hardly shaped at all beyond the need to stop her before she finished. It struck the ground three meters short. Earth exploded. Dirt, stone, roots wrenched up into daylight. The blast threw her clear and dropped her fifteen meters away in a heap that did not move again.

The tracker lifted his head far enough to see the crater, the ruined woman, and Kem standing in the middle of his own damage. Then he put both hands flat on the ground. Surrender, maybe. Or the only position his body could currently manage.

Eight more levels. Kem's Ledger read 245.

Forty-one levels. The fight had taken maybe four minutes.

The forest looked as if weather had attacked it sideways. One hillside was now a raw scar. A crater marked his last blast. Trees leaned, snapped, or lay broken where they had fallen. Dust hung in the filtered afternoon light and turned it gold.

Vel came out from behind her boulder. A scratch ran along her forearm where the shield's edge had driven the branch across it. She took in the clearing, the slide, the crater, Kem.

Her face went very still.

Kem was grinning.

He could not help it. Dirt in his hair. Tongue bleeding where he'd bitten it. Rib half-cracked. Forty-one levels gone in four minutes, and his mouth kept pulling wide anyway. The soft preserved body he'd spent twenty-six years storing indoors had hit, run, bled, braced, thrown force, stayed upright.

Vel was already checking the agents. Pulse. Breathing. Consciousness. She worked fast and without ceremony.

"They'll live," she said, straightening from the tracker. "Bruised, concussed. Her arm's broken. He's got cracked ribs." She glanced at Kem. "Same side as yours."

"Good," Kem said. "I mean, not good. I didn't mean for that."

"I know you didn't." She jerked her chin toward his pack. "Get it on. We move now. Half the valley heard that hillside go."

Kem bent for the pack. Pain bit clean through his left side. The grin faltered.

Vel looked at the slide again, then at the line of trees his shield had bent and the crater his last blast had gouged.

"Look at this clearing," she said. Her voice was quiet enough that he had to listen. "Forty-one levels bought you this. I've lost three and everybody I put down is still breathing. You broke the ground open and almost took me with the shield. Next team won't need a tracker if you keep leaving this kind of trail."

He swallowed. "I know."

"No, you know the number." She crouched by the lead agent and unfastened a document case from the woman's belt. "You don't know what it buys."

She opened the case. Heavy paper, formal seals, Hoarded script so precise it made Kem's shoulders tighten on sight.

"Recovery directive," Vel said, scanning. "Council-authorized. Your mother's name is on the request." A page turned. "Six enforcement agents allocated. Five training levels apiece. Thirty levels approved for the retrieval team." Another page. "Separate rider for one field tracker. One Perception spend authorized already."

So there it was. Six gray coats in the line. The seventh body hanging back in field cloth, paid for out of a different column. Even the pursuit had bookkeeping.

Vel folded the papers and stowed them in her own pack.

"This is institutional," she said. "The Council weighed your departure against the philosophy and decided you were worth spending on. That admission matters more than the bodies on the ground."

Kem understood it in the Hoarded language that had made him. Six agents. Thirty levels. One tracker hidden in a separate authorization so the official arithmetic could pretend to stay tidy. They had sent seven people into the forest to retrieve one son of one house, and they had signed for every part of it.

"They'll send more," Vel said. "Better ones. More authorization. More willingness to burn. And until you can tell the difference between one level and fifteen, you don't reach unless I'm already losing or I tell you to."

"I should have controlled it. I couldn't. The pulse was supposed to be small. I was trying to do what you did."

"I've been doing this since I was seventeen."

"I know."

"Then learn the useful part. Back there you didn't know what you were doing, and you had forty-one levels to make it everybody's problem." She pulled the pack straps tighter across her shoulders. "I can't teach you calibration in a week. So until your body stops treating every problem like a landslide, you keep your hands off the deep end unless there's no other choice."

She started south through the wreckage and into denser cover where the trees would slow pursuit and hide them better than the ruined clearing could. Kem followed. Pain was declaring itself everywhere now. Rib. Shoulder. Palm. Tongue. Raw skin under the pack straps. Every few steps the split in his palm opened again against the canvas.

The clearing kept flashing behind his eyes. The woman's broken arm. The man bouncing off the tree. The tracker flat in the thicket with both hands on the dirt. Shame came with it, heavy and immediate, and the charge in his hands did not care.

Ahead, Vel kept angling them away from the slide and the crater, into tighter cover. The next team would not need a tracker to find what he'd left behind. Kem shifted the pack higher. His Ledger rubbed at him with every step. 245. Forty-one gone. Vel had spent three. The grin came back anyway. He let it, and kept walking south.
