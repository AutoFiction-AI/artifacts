# Chapter 12

The soil was the color of chalk. Exhausted chalk, the specific pale of ground that had been asked for everything it could give and then asked again. Vel had seen depleted farmland before, in settlements where Fertility Charms had been overworked for a generation. This was different in scale. The depletion went to the horizon, south and east and west, the fields marked by wooden stakes driven into ground too tired to hold them upright. Half the stakes leaned. Nobody had straightened them. Nobody had the levels to care about stakes.

They'd crossed the river two days ago. Since then the land had changed in stages, first the forest thinning, then the soil lightening, then the structures getting worse. Now there was no pretending they were anywhere but the Margins.

The heat didn't help. Early summer in the south was dry and heavy, the kind that pulled moisture from skin and pack straps and road alike. Dren's canteen was half-empty by midday and he hadn't mentioned it. Kem had stopped reaching for Sustenance Charms weeks ago, one of the economies Vel had hammered into him, but she could see his hand drift toward his belt pouch and stop, drift and stop, the old reflex surfacing and being caught. He drank water and kept walking. That much she gave him.

The first settlement appeared around noon: a cluster of low buildings arranged around a central well, the structures showing the seams of cheap Shaping work. Walls that met at angles that weren't quite right. Rooflines that sagged where the original builder had run out of levels mid-construction. The Shaping itself had been competent. The specifications hadn't cared about the people living under them.

A woman sat outside the nearest building on a stool, shelling dried beans into a wooden bowl. She looked up when they approached. Her eyes went to Kem first, to his height, his hands, the boots that were battered now but still identifiably Vault-made, and her face arranged itself into something neutral and practiced, the expression of someone who'd learned that strangers with good boots didn't usually bring good news.

"Water?" Vel asked.

"Well's there." The woman nodded toward the cistern. "Thirty-two people drawing from it, so take what you need and move on."

"We're passing through," Vel said.

"That's what they all say."

Vel filled their canteens. Seven people were visible in the settlement. All of them moved with a specific economy she recognized, careful, minimum-effort, the motion of people who'd spent most of what they had and felt the cost of every gesture. She'd moved like that herself for years. The careful walk, the deliberate hands. Before Kem, before she'd had someone else's levels to budget alongside her own.

The notice board was by the well. Proper construction. Hoarded administrative hardwood with iron fittings, the kind of thing a district overseer would have had installed. The notices on it were layered deep, older ones buried under newer ones, the whole thing a geological record of bureaucratic attention paid to a place that had never received any other kind.

The top notice was current. Hoarded administrative stock, cream-colored paper:

NOTICE OF PRODUCTIVITY RECLASSIFICATION: MARGINS DISTRICT 7, SECTOR 14-B

Pursuant to Directive 1172 (amended), all residents of the above-designated sector are hereby classified under the revised Productivity Tier system:

TIER ONE (Levels 40+): Full daily output expected. 0.3 levels-per-annum standard rate. Interest on existing ability-debt accrues per schedule (Appendix C-7, Table 3, subsection ii).

TIER TWO (Levels 15–39): Reduced output. 0.15 levels-per-annum. Supplementary manual labor allocation may be assigned. Interest rate adjustment per Formula 9-B (revised).

TIER THREE (Levels 5–14): Maintenance category. Residents classified as Renewable Expenditure Units and may be reassigned to Development Opportunity programs at District Overseer discretion.

TIER FOUR (Levels 1–4): Non-productive. Account closure processing per Standard Protocol 6.

APPEALS: Reclassification appeals may be submitted to the Bureau of Expenditure Oversight, Northern Administrative Center, during standard business hours (first and third Wedgeday of each month, 10:00–14:00). Filing fee: 2 levels.

Vel read it twice. Once for the language, once for what it would do to a life.

"Renewable Expenditure Units," Kem said. He was reading over her shoulder. His voice had that careful, too-even quality it took on when something from the world outside the Vault didn't fit any framework he'd brought from home. "That means people."

"Renewable because they have children," Vel said. "Children are born at three hundred."

Kem's hand closed on the edge of the board. "And the filing fee. Two levels. If you're Tier Four, you've got one to four levels left."

"Half of everything you have," Vel said.

He stared at the notice. Appendix C. Table 3. Subsection ii. His thumb dug into the board's edge until the wood gave a dry little squeal.

Dren hadn't stopped at the board. He was standing thirty feet down the road, watching south. His bad arm hung slightly forward, the way he carried it now. Kem's stitches had held, but the flesh underneath had healed tight and wrong, and Dren protected the arm like a thing he couldn't afford to break a second time.

"We moving?" he said.

Vel pulled the notice from the board. It tore along a nail hole. Underneath was an older one, its ink fading to brown:

DEVELOPMENT OPPORTUNITY PROGRAM: COHORT 47
Duration: 5-year rotating assignment (renewable)
Daily ability expenditure requirement: Per Tier classification
Compensation: Room, board, and 2% annual reduction in outstanding ability-debt balance

No reason. Vel folded both notices and put them in her pack anyway.

***

They passed two more settlements by mid-afternoon. Each showed the same marks: cheap Shaping, depleted soil, careful people. The notice boards varied in age but not in content: Productivity Tiers, Expenditure classifications, debt schedules referencing appendices nobody here had ever seen. In one settlement, someone had written across the bottom of a notice in charcoal: *formula doesn't balance; asked district office; they said resubmit next quarter*. The handwriting was shaky. Tier Three, Vel guessed. Or Four.

In the third settlement, they found the labor operation.

It was visible from a quarter mile: a compound surrounded by a wall of Shaped stone, about eight feet high, the Shaping layered thick enough that the stone had that fused, seamless look of serious ability investment. Inside, the tops of low buildings were visible, and people moving between them. Workers. They came and went through a single gate that a woman in her fifties controlled.

The enforcer sat on a chair by the gate with a ledger book in her lap and a clay cup on a crate beside her. When workers passed through, she checked names. When they came back in, she checked again.

"Debt work," Dren said. He'd seen it before. "Hoarded contract. Workers spend their daily quota, she marks it, end of the season the district office processes the reduction. In theory."

"In theory," Vel said.

"In theory."

A child sat against the compound's outer wall, in its shade. Maybe eight years old, small for the age, with the wrists of someone who'd never had enough of anything. His face was flushed, not from the sun but from fever, the glassy heat of infection. An older girl sat beside him, twelve or thirteen, with the watchful, used-up face of someone who'd been responsible for too long. She had her hand on the boy's forehead. She'd been sitting like that for a while. You could see it in the set of her back.

Kem saw the child at the same time Vel did. His stride changed, a slight hitch, a redirection, his body deciding before his mind had caught up.

"Don't," Vel said, quietly enough that she wasn't sure whether she'd said it to him or to herself.

***

Kem knelt beside the child. The girl pulled her brother closer.

"He's got a fever," Kem said.

"I know he's got a fever." The girl looked at Kem's boots, his hands, the remains of Vault that he still carried. "He's had it three days."

"Three days is too long. Has anyone healed him?"

"Has anyone what? Healed him?" She said it the way you'd say *has anyone flown him to the moon*. "With what?"

Kem looked at Vel. She knew what he was asking. She also knew what the answer cost. Not to the boy. To the cascade, the mechanism, the plan she'd spent eight years building. She didn't say yes. She didn't say no.

He put his hands on the child's forehead and closed his eyes. The Curative ability came as a clean pulse of warmth that Vel could feel from six feet away, like a door opening into a heated room. Kem's calibration had improved. This was targeted, precise, the fever and infection only. Eight levels. Not twenty. Not forty. The boy's face lost its feverish tightness and relaxed into something that looked like ordinary sleep.

The girl stared at Kem. She touched her brother's forehead, then his cheek. Her hand was shaking. "How many was that?"

"Eight," Kem said. He stood up, and Vel caught the usual change, slight and wrong at the same time. He was at 140.

"Thank you," the girl said. It didn't sound simple. Gratitude was in it, but so was the fury of having needed a stranger's charity in the first place.

The loss hit her a beat later. She'd built the basin around Kem reaching the Scour with about 90 levels, enough for a sustained descent to stabilize the threshold alongside her own. At 140, he was already ten shy of where she'd wanted him by this point. She could adjust. She'd adjusted before.

Then Kem walked toward the compound gate.

"What is this?" he asked the enforcer.

The enforcer looked up from her ledger. "Labor compound. Contract work under District authority."

"And the wall?"

"Compound perimeter. Workers stay on-site during assigned shift. Per regulation."

"How long's a shift?"

"Sunrise to sunset. Per regulation."

"And if someone needs to leave? If their kid's sick?"

The enforcer's face didn't change. She was practiced at this. "Shift completion is required before exit authorization. Per regulation."

Kem looked at the wall. Vel saw his hand flex at his side. The reflex came before every ability use now.

"Kem," she said.

He didn't look at her. He put both hands flat against the Shaped stone and pushed.

The Disruption came as a sustained wave, not a single blast but modulated pressure, weeks of Vel's teaching turning into something close to control. The Shaping cracked along its seams first. The fused layers split with a sound like ice breaking on a river. Then fifteen feet of wall, the gate, and the mechanism holding it together slumped into rubble and pale dust. The enforcer fell off her chair. Workers on the other side stood blinking in the sudden daylight where the wall had been.

Twenty levels. He was at 120.

The slack she'd built into that route was gone.

"You can leave," Kem told the workers. Some were already moving. Others stood where they were, staring at the gap as if it might rebuild itself. One woman picked up a tool she'd been using and set it down again, carefully, in the exact spot where it had been. Then she walked through the opening without looking back.

The enforcer got up from the dust, ledger book clutched to her chest. "You've destroyed Hoarded-contracted infrastructure. There'll be — the district office will —"

"Send the invoice to House Aldraste," Kem said.

Vel wanted to laugh. Instead she felt 120 pull tight across the next three days.

The backup arrived within the hour. Three enforcers from a larger compound to the south. Two men and a woman, all somewhere in the fifty-to-seventy range by their movement, all built like people who'd spent careers on enforcement rather than labor. They assessed the rubble, the dispersing workers, and Kem sitting on a chunk of fallen wall drinking water, and drew the correct conclusions.

The first one opened with a Combat Pulse aimed at Kem's chest. Kem threw up a shield that caught it. The detonation punched the air and blew dust in a circle around him, and Vel felt the shield's cost like a flinch: four levels, maybe five. The second enforcer was already flanking left, and Kem caught it with a Perception flash, two levels, his head snapping toward the movement before the man had committed. The third enforcer hung back, gauging.

Then all three pressed at once. Kem's defensive barrier was rough but solid, a sustained field that held while the enforcers hammered it with everything they had. The air around the barrier rippled with spent energy. Nine levels to hold it. Vel felt them go in the pressure fluctuations.

He dropped the barrier and hit the first enforcer with a targeted Force Strike before the man could reset. Three levels, clean, the enforcer going down hard and staying down. The second caught a Strike to the shoulder. Three more levels, and the man's arm went slack. The third enforcer looked at her two colleagues on the ground and at Kem, who was breathing hard and bleeding from a gash on his forearm where a glancing pulse had opened the skin, and she turned and ran.

Thirty-five levels in combat. He was at 85.

Kem sat down in the rubble. He tore a strip from the hem of his shirt and wrapped his forearm, the blood soaking through immediately, a dark spreading stain. Two months ago he would have healed the cut with an ability and not thought about the cost. Now he tied the cloth tight with his teeth and flexed the hand to check the tendons. Field triage. Vel had taught him that in the first week, kneeling over a scraped knee he'd wanted to burn three levels on.

A worker came back through the gap in the wall. An old man, sixty or seventy, stooped and slow. He walked to the enforcer's chair, which was lying on its side in the dust, and he righted it. Then he sat down in it. He didn't do anything else. He just sat there and looked out at the road where the enforcers had gone.

Nobody stopped him. Dust kept sliding off the broken wall. The old man sat in Danner's chair and watched the road.

Vel stood at the edge of the rubble and felt the bottom drop out. The Scour needed Kem at 90. He was at 85. Five levels gone past the edge, and Kem was wrapping his arm with torn cloth and wearing the expression of someone who wasn't finished.

She needed to say it plain: *every level you spend on these people is a level we need at the Scour.* She needed to explain that the cascade mechanism wasn't elastic, that the threshold required a minimum sustained descent, that his charity was consuming the only resource that stood between them and failure.

She opened her mouth.

And closed it. Workers kept coming through the gap. The girl stayed by her brother with one palm on his cooling forehead. Danner's ledger lay open in the dust, its neat columns filling with grit.

Stop spending on people who need help. That was what the Hoarded said. Preserve. Husband your resources. Let the expendable be expended.

Vel bit the inside of her cheek until she tasted blood.

Kem spent the rest of the day.

The collapsed irrigation channel came first: a section that served four nearby settlements, built by Hoarded contractors twenty years ago and left to rot when the contract expired. Kem knelt in the dry ditch and poured sustained Shaping energy into packed earth and stone, rebuilding the channel with the blunt, functional craftsmanship of someone who'd learned by breaking things for weeks before trying to build them. It wasn't elegant. When upstream water began flowing through for the first time in what the settlement people said was three years, a woman waded into the ditch and stood with the water running over her ankles, touching the rebuilt wall of the channel with her palm. She stood there for a long time. She didn't say anything to Kem. She didn't need to.

Fifteen levels. He was at 70.

Then came the smaller things, which weren't smaller to the people who needed them. A man with a broken finger he'd been splinting with sticks for two weeks. Three levels of healing, twice what a competent healer would charge but half what it would have cost Kem a month ago. A wall about to collapse onto a house where four people slept. Five levels of structural reinforcement, rough but solid. A woman whose vision had been clouding for years, cataracts thickening because nobody in the Margins could afford the Ocular ability. Kem hesitated on that one. He'd never done it before. He did it anyway. Six levels, and it wasn't perfect. The woman said she could see shapes now, and colors, and her daughter's face, though the edges were still soft. Kem told her he was sorry it wasn't better. She said he shouldn't be sorry. She said *shapes and colors* like she was holding something she'd expected never to touch again.

The child's infected tooth cost two levels. She cried during the healing, not from pain but from the strangeness of an ability being used on her for the first time in her life, the warm invasive pulse of someone else's energy moving through her jaw. Her father held her and looked at Kem with the same complicated expression the girl with the feverish brother had worn before, gratitude and anger occupying the same face.

A man's chronic pain from a years-old injury that had healed crooked and wrong cost four more. Kem asked where it hurt. The man showed him a hip twisted in a fall from a half-collapsed structure, the kind of workplace accident that would have been healed in ten minutes in the Spent Towns and in thirty seconds in the Vault. Here, in the Margins, it had become four years of limping. Kem fixed what he could. The man walked three steps and stopped and walked three more, testing, the way someone tests ice before committing weight.

Twenty levels in small interventions. He was at 50.

Vel spent one of her own on a Perception sweep, mapping the local terrain and the road south, filing landmarks and distances. A level she could barely afford, but she worked better when the ground came clean in her head. Nineteen to eighteen.

Dren had been quiet all day. He'd watched Kem heal the child and said nothing. He'd watched the wall come down and said nothing. When Kem was rebuilding the irrigation channel, Dren had walked into the ditch and started carrying rubble out of the way with his good arm, the bad one tucked against his body. He didn't ask. He just worked.

Now he sat on a stone at the edge of the repaired channel, watching the water run. His face had the look of a man who'd started holding his daughter next to the people around him and couldn't stop. He was at 130. The people around him were at 4, at 8, at 15. A month ago 130 would have felt like wealth. Here it looked like distance, the space between his daughter and the people in this settlement, measured by whether anyone with levels was willing to spend them.

***

They found Fallow an hour before dark, at the edge of the furthest settlement.

Fallow's structure was smaller than the others and better maintained, in the particular way of someone who had almost nothing and kept it precise. A patched roof. A swept floor visible through the open door. A wooden chair with one leg shorter than the others and a folded cloth wedged under it, the cloth re-folded and repositioned enough times that it had worn thin in the middle. Outside, a garden held three rows of something stubborn growing in the chalky soil. Each plant was staked with a twig and tied with strips of rag, the rows dead-straight, the spacing exact. The garden hadn't produced much. But it was tended with a discipline that put most of the Hoarded to shame.

Fallow was sitting in the chair when they arrived. Level 4. Vel didn't need a Perception ability to tell. At that level, conservation was visible in everything. The way Fallow turned their head to look at the visitors, the rotation itself measured. The way their hands rested on their knees without fidgeting, because fidgeting was wasted motion.

Sixty years old. Gaunt past their years. Skin creased deep by three decades of field sun. Hands that were mostly tendon and callus, the fingers permanently curved from tools they'd held so long the shape had become structural. Fallow wore a shirt mended in seven visible places, each repair in a different-colored thread, brown, white, blue, red, green, yellow, brown again, as if the available thread changed with the years. The effect was almost festive, in a way Fallow clearly didn't intend or notice.

"Sit," Fallow said. There was one chair and they didn't gesture at it. "Ground or wall. Chair's mine."

They sat. Dren leaned in the doorframe.

"Dren said you know the routes south," Vel said.

"I know the routes everywhere around here. I've walked most of them at one point or another." Fallow's eyes moved from Vel to Kem. They lingered the way everyone in the Margins lingered on Kem, assessing, categorizing, placing him in the ledger of who had what and who didn't. "You're the one who broke Danner's wall."

"Danner?"

"The enforcer. She's been running that compound six years. Longest operation in this district." Fallow said it without judgment. The wall was a fact. Its absence was another. "She'll file a reconstruction request with the district office. Takes three weeks to process. Four more for a Shaper to come down. Your people get maybe seven weeks before it's back up."

Kem had spent twenty levels and bought seven weeks. Vel let the number sit where it landed.

"What were you before this?" Kem asked. He was leaning forward, the way he did when he was genuinely trying to understand something and not performing interest.

"They sent me out on field debt at a hundred and forty," Fallow said. "Form called it Development Opportunity. Thirty years of development. Very developed. Now I'm four."

Kem sat with that. His hands hung loose between his knees.

"My mother grows the same thing," Dren said. He was looking at the garden. "In her yard back home. Those're ash-beans, right? Alkaline soil?"

Fallow glanced at him. For a second some older alertness came back into their face. "They don't produce much."

"Hers either. She says they're stubborn enough to be worth the effort."

"Yeah. They're worth it." Fallow turned back to Vel. "You want the south routes."

Vel had the route already, inked in Maren's hand. What she needed was everything that had happened there since. "I need the south route," she said. "And I need to know whether zero still takes people there."

"Three days south. The land drops into a basin, big, all rock and wind, nothing growing. Old mining territory. Hoarded ran Shaping crews down there sixty, seventy years back. Stripped it and left." Fallow rubbed a thumb along one knuckle. "And yes. It still takes them."

Kem looked from Fallow to Vel. "Takes them how?"

Fallow was quiet for a moment. Not for effect. Just spending the breath carefully. "Not often. Couple times a generation. Somebody gets down to almost nothing, and there's nowhere lower to go but the Scour. They spend the last of it there, and after that the rock's empty."

"Empty," Kem said.

"No corpse. No body. You look at the basin floor and they're on it, then you look again and they're gone. I've seen it twice. First was a woman named Sera. She'd been sitting at two for months, just barely staying in the world. Walked south one morning. I was on a field crew near the basin edge. Saw her from a distance. One minute she was on the rock. Next minute she wasn't."

"And the second?"

"Man called Hoster. Eight years back, maybe." Fallow rubbed the side of their jaw, a habitual movement, something to do with their hands while the memory passed through. "Same thing. He walked down, stopped on the basin floor, and then there was a strip of white like light through a cracked door. After that it was just stone."

Eight years. Vel kept her face still and felt the old flash come back behind her eyes.

"Nobody talks about it," Fallow said. "What's the point? People at zero disappear. It's one more thing that happens to people nobody protects. Hoarded don't come down here to investigate. Margins people don't have the levels to care about mysteries." They looked out at the garden. "Sera and Hoster are just names now."

Vel reached into her pack and pulled out Maren's route journal. The leather had gone soft from years of handling, the corners dark with sweat and dirt. She opened to the southern pages.

Maren had mapped this stretch in detail. Water sources. Drop-offs. Settlement names that might already be gone. On one page she had drawn the basin: an oval, hash marks for the descent, a small x on the floor. Beside it, in careful script: *here*.

"Route journal?" Fallow asked.

"Yeah."

"That handwriting's not yours."

"No."

Fallow watched Vel handle the pages. They didn't push. They turned to Kem instead. "You're the one at what, fifty now? After today?"

"Fifty," Kem said.

"Going to zero on purpose." Not a question.

"Yeah."

Fallow's mouth moved a little. Not quite a smile. "Doesn't happen much. Around here zero's what gets done to you."

Vel closed the journal. The leather settled against the folded notices from the board when she put it back in her pack.

Three days south. She could feel the distance in her body, in the dry pull under her ribs, in the narrowing room between what she needed and what was left.

***

They left Fallow's settlement in the failing light. Vel wanted hours. Three days to the Scour, and every hour mattered now that the route she'd built in her head no longer fit the body beside her. They walked in the formation they'd developed over weeks: Vel reading the terrain ahead, Kem carrying the heavier pack because his body could take it, Dren at the rear watching behind.

The road was empty. A footbridge over a dry creek bed was about to give, so Vel reinforced it with a Structural ability. One level. The wood stiffened under their weight. Eighteen now.

They walked in silence for a while. The pale soil cooled under their feet as the light faded. Somewhere to the west, a settlement's cooking fires put a thin haze against the sky.

Dren caught up and walked beside Vel. This was unusual. He kept rear guard and didn't seek company unless he had logistics to report.

"My agreement with you was to the edge of the Margins," he said. "We're past that."

"Yeah."

"Payment was for guide work. Routes, getting you through the Spent Towns and pointed south. That's done."

"Yeah, it's done."

He was quiet for another fifty paces. Vel could hear his breathing, steady, a walker's breath, with a hitch on the left side where his bad arm moved against his ribs.

"I'm not leaving yet," he said.

Vel looked at him. His face was set in the dying light, committed the way a man's face gets after he's turned the same choice over too many times already.

"I'm not asking for more payment," he said. "I'm just — I'm staying on."

"Okay."

"Don't ask me why."

"I won't."

They walked. Behind them Kem was a dark shape against the road. Fifty levels. The number sat in Vel like a cracked tooth.

The notices in her pack rasped against the journal buckle every time she moved. Renewable Expenditure Units. Development Opportunity. Fallow at four in a shirt stitched seven times. The Margins hadn't spent themselves.

Tell him to stop, then. Tell him the rest belongs to the Scour.

Vel kept walking. The words stayed where they'd stuck by the broken wall.

Three days south the basin waited. Maren had marked it. Fallow had named two others. Sera. Hoster. When the flash came back to Vel now, it did not come back alone.

She walked south, and every mile pressed harder.
