# Chapter 10

Four days after the grain-silo fight, the settlement looked the same. Dren's left arm had spent those four days swelling around Kem's ugly stitches, Vel's ribs still bit every time the path dropped hard under her boots, and that morning she'd hauled them west off the south road to come here.

From the ridge she could still read the old layout: stone-and-timber houses around the communal well, gardens on the south edge where the light held longest, the storehouse by the eastern road. Twelve years had changed details, not shape. The storehouse had grown a second story. A fence stood where there used to be pasture. The place itself had not moved an inch. Nothing here had been allowed to sag, split, or go to seed.

"How far?" Dren asked.

"Half an hour."

He shifted his pack and winced. "She knows we're coming?"

"No."

Kem said nothing. He'd been watching Vel since sunrise, since she'd scratched a new line in Maren's journal and taken them off the route they'd been riding for three weeks. Usually he'd ask by now. Today he saved it.

The path down was switchback gravel, spring runoff carved into ruts. Vel's feet remembered the bad spots before her head did: the second turn where the stone rolled underfoot, the patch of bare rock lower down that liked to throw a careless ankle. The air was dry enough to chap the inside of her nose. Heat sat on the valley early. Below them the gardens gave off bean leaf, damp soil, bruised herb stems.

At the northern edge she saw the cart.

It sat outside the storehouse, clean-sided and too fine for local work. The hardware was machined clean. The axle had Hoarded precision all over it. A settlement trader would have oiled the wood dark and patched the joinery twice. This one still looked like a policy had signed off on it.

Vel lifted a hand. Kem stopped. Dren had already narrowed his eyes at the cart.

"Hoarded," he said.

"Yeah."

"Collector or messenger?"

"Representative, I'd guess."

They cut wide through the south gardens, keeping the bean frames between themselves and the well. The settlement was too small for stealth to last, but first sight still mattered. It always did.

Thessa stood by the well with a woman in Hoarded trade linen. Tailored sleeves. Clean hem. Leather case tucked under one arm. The woman was talking about delivery schedules and quota adjustments in the calm tone of a person who expected every number in the world to sit still long enough for her to file it.

Then the words thinned.

Her mouth kept moving, but the sound thinned out halfway through the sentence. Her gaze slid past Thessa's shoulder and fixed on the air beside the well post. She didn't blink. Didn't shift her weight. For three beats she stood there with her lips parted, waiting on a thought that wouldn't come back.

Stillness, three weeks south of the Vault, arguing about bean quotas.

The gap lasted only a few seconds. The representative blinked, found her place, and resumed the sentence as if it had only been delayed by weather. She made a note with the stylus in her case. Her hand had not moved during the lapse.

Thessa's face gave nothing away. She had seen it before. Vel could tell from how little surprise there was in her.

Cold ran through Vel's hands. Not fear. Recognition. The Hoarded liked to talk about discipline as if it were a moral achievement. Here it was instead: a woman wearing good linen while the system chewed pieces out of her between one quota and the next.

Kem had gone rigid beside her. His breathing changed.

"That's what my father does," he said quietly. "When he goes away in the middle of a sentence."

"I know."

"He looks exactly like that."

The representative closed her case, said she'd return next month for updated figures, and headed back toward the cart with small measured steps, like she was carrying a tray filled to the lip.

Thessa watched her go. Then she looked toward the bean frames and saw Vel.

Recognition hit first. Then the settlement version of Thessa slid over it: open face, easy hands, a voice made to calm neighbors and send traders away feeling seen. She said a quick word to a woman passing with a bucket and came toward them.

It held until she was close enough for Vel to see her eyes.

"Vel." A beat. "Fine. Inside. All of you. Put the packs down before you fall over."

"Thessa."

"Don't start in the lane." She had already turned toward the stone house at the northwest corner. Same house. Same narrow front step. "Bring the wounded one too. I've got water hot, or near enough. Bread if nobody insults it."

"You're very welcoming."

"I've had twelve years to practice." She kept walking. "Come on."

Vel followed. Kem came after her in silence. Dren's boots dragged gravel in an even scrape that said he was listening hard and filing it away.

---

Thessa's house smelled of dried thyme, old stone, and baking crust. Table, four chairs, cooking alcove, shelves of labeled jars, a broom hung where the bristles could dry straight. No clutter. Nothing in the room that didn't do a job.

She moved through the space without asking what anyone wanted. Kettle on the fire. Cups out. Bread cut in thick slices. The knife landed beside the board, the cups lined up, and by the time she turned they were all waiting on her without meaning to.

Kem sat when she pointed. Dren took the wall by the door. Vel stayed standing until Thessa turned with the kettle in hand.

"Sit down."

"I'm fine."

"You're limping on the left and holding your ribs like a stubborn idiot. Sit down."

Vel sat.

Tea steamed into the cups. Real tea, not boiled roots. Thessa set bread in the middle of the table and finally took the chair across from Vel. For a few breaths the room held only fire crackle, the clink of ceramic, a child shouting somewhere outside.

Then Thessa said, "You vanish for twelve years, and this is what walks through my door."

Dren lifted his stitched arm a little. "Door's still standing. That's a start."

She ignored him and kept her eyes on Vel. "You're heading south."

"I need the routes."

"Of course you do." Thessa took a sip, set the cup down, and reached for the map shelf before she had fully swallowed. "River crossing's gone. Flood took the old bridge three years back. Traders use a ford east of it now when the water's low."

"What about farther in?"

"Eat first." She nudged the bread plate toward Vel. "Mara baked that at dawn and she'll haunt me if I let it go stale on company."

"I'm not hungry."

"Then hold a piece in your hand and make me feel useful."

Vel took the bread.

Thessa unfolded a cloth map onto the table. "This much I know. After the river it gets thin. I hear things. I don't go."

"Why not."

Thessa looked up at her. "Because I live here now." She tapped the western edge of the map with one finger. "I mend roofs. I weed gardens. I haul sacks. I spend little enough that my number lasts. That's the answer you're pretending not to ask."

"I'm not asking you to come."

"No." Thessa's mouth twitched, not close to a smile. "You never ask straight out. You sit at my table with road dust on your cuffs and act like that isn't its own kind of asking."

Kem watched from his cup. Dren watched the window.

Thessa's gaze shifted to Kem. She took in his posture, the pale skin, the hands that no longer looked untouched but still read Hoarded at a glance.

"You're from the Vault."

"I was," Kem said. "I'm not, exactly. It's complicated."

"It usually is." She studied him another second, then looked back at Vel. "What level."

Vel answered first. "Not relevant."

"It is to me."

"I'm at one-seventy-eight," Kem said. "Or I was this morning."

Thessa went still. Her own cup hovered halfway to her mouth and stopped there. Seventy-eight. She knew the scale. Knew what walked into her house when a Hoarded defector said one-seventy-eight in an ordinary voice.

"Oh," she said. Then, flatter: "You went looking again."

"Thessa."

She set the cup down carefully this time. "Don't do that. Don't put my name in your mouth like that'll fix anything."

"I came for route information."

"And a map. And a meal you didn't plan to stay for. And whatever else you dragged in with you." She pressed her thumb against the table grain, once, twice. "Fine. You want the routes, you get the routes. South from here turns worse after the ford. More Hoarded contracts. More field camps. More people with just enough uniform to ruin your day."

She leaned over the map, pointing out water sources, a dry creek bed, the rise before the river. Vel pulled Maren's journal from her pack by habit more than thought and opened it beside the cloth.

Thessa stopped mid-sentence.

She stared at the journal's cover, the bent spine, the gut-thread repair along the corner. Recognition crossed her face so quickly it was almost ugly.

"You're carrying her pages."

Vel's fingers rested on the open spread.

"Maren's journal." Thessa touched the air above it and did not touch it. "Hallson's trader fixed that binding. Couldn't knot a cord to save his life, so she made him do it twice while you laughed at him." Her voice pulled tight. "You still have it."

Vel said nothing.

"You still have it," Thessa repeated, softer now and therefore worse. "You walked out of here twelve years ago. You heard she crossed eight years ago. And you're still dragging her handwriting up and down the south road like if you wear the paper thin enough she'll answer."

"Thessa."

"No, let me say it wrong if I need to." Her eyes stayed on the journal. "I found out she crossed from Barrens. Barrens. Not from you. You were ten minutes away and you sent the news through three people's mouths because that was easier than knocking on my door and watching me hear it."

Vel's ribs pulled hard on the next breath. The room smelled of thyme and fresh crust. Maren's notes lay open beside Thessa's cloth map, cramped handwriting running up against fresh pencil marks and bread crumbs.

"I know whose journal it is," Vel said.

"Do you." Thessa finally looked up. "Because sometimes I wonder. Sometimes I look at you and I can't tell where the grief stops and the route planning starts."

Kem's attention shifted between them. "Vel said Maren found the threshold."

Thessa turned to him so fast her chair scraped stone. "Five years ago she sat where you are. Same table. Same stupid good bread. She asked me if I'd heard of any Hoarded who'd run. Not names first. Numbers. What their Ledgers looked like, whether any of them were angry enough to spend, whether anyone above two-fifty had vanished south."

Kem's hand tightened around his cup.

Vel's voice went flat. "That's enough."

"It isn't." The words came clipped now, the warm mask gone clean off. "He gets to know that wasn't the first time. He gets to know I heard those questions in this room and knew exactly what she wanted and still handed her a map because I was tired and because I loved her and because that made me stupid."

"Thessa."

"Don't." Her breath hitched and she shoved on anyway. "You took my routes. You took Maren's journal. Now you come in with a lord who says one-seventy-eight like it's weather. You want me to call that chance?"

Kem said, "Vel?"

Vel did not answer him. She kept her eyes on Thessa. "You're angry. Fine. Give me the route and keep the rest."

Thessa laughed once, raw and short. "There. That. You always do that. Somebody bleeds all over the floor and you go straight to the next useful fact."

Dren spoke from the wall without moving. "Routes matter."

"Don't help her." Thessa wiped the heel of her hand under one eye, angry at the wetness there. "I'm not saying the road doesn't matter. I'm saying she was counting before she met him. Maybe she cares now. Maybe she doesn't know what to do with caring. I don't know. But five years ago she was here asking what numbers the Hoarded were carrying, and now he walks in."

The room went still.

Kem set his cup down with care. "Did you know," he asked Vel, "that you were looking for this? For somebody who could do whatever happens at the end?"

Vel wanted the smaller question, the route question, the one she could answer. His wasn't that.

"I knew I couldn't cross alone," she said.

Thessa closed her eyes for a moment. "There. That's cleaner than I expected."

"That's not all of it."

"Then say the rest," Kem said.

Vel opened her mouth again, but the journal was lying open between them, spine bent back, Maren's hand plain on the page.

Dren turned his head toward the window. "We've got company."

No one moved at first.

"The Hoarded woman," he said. "Coming here now."

Thessa was on her feet before the sentence finished. She crossed to the window, looked once, and came back with the softness gone out of her face.

"She does quota review once a month," she said. "Settlement reports. Traveler notes."

Vel stood. "Traveler notes."

Thessa looked at the bread, the cups, the map, the journal. "Three years ago they offered grain priority, timber allotments, a healer twice a season, medicine when the children got lung-fever. All I had to do was keep track of who passed through and where they were headed."

"You took it."

"Yes." No softness in it now. "I took it. Winter stopped feeling like a knife at the door for a while. Roofs got fixed. People ate. I took it."

No one argued with her.

Through the window, the representative was crossing the open ground toward the house. The document case was under her arm. In her free hand she carried a short relay scroll, the kind that could wake a station thirty miles away.

Vel asked, "Does she know it's us?"

"She saw you by the well. Whether she understood it fast enough, I don't know." Thessa scrubbed a hand over her face. "Her lapses are worse now. Last month she lost nearly a minute in my kitchen while I was lying to her about barley totals. She stood there with her eyes gone and then asked me for the count again. I've been making bargains with a woman who's disappearing a piece at a time. Don't tidy that up for me."

Vel didn't. She couldn't.

"Back way?" Dren asked.

Thessa nodded at once. "Kitchen door. Out the lane, then west between my place and Garran's shed. Orchard after that. Irrigation ditch runs south a mile. If they scan, stay low under the bank."

Kem rose. "Why are you helping us."

"Because I am. Move."

Vel folded the journal shut and slid it back inside her pack. She felt Thessa watching her do it, saw the look in her face, and let it go untouched.

"Thank you," Vel said.

Thessa's jaw worked once. "Get out before I change my mind."

Vel had no answer that didn't cost more time than they had. She went.

---

They left through the kitchen, past the drying herbs and the chipped basin, into the narrow lane behind the houses. Sun hammered the clay. No one spoke until they cleared the last wall.

Vel spent one level on a tight Perception sweep.

The world snapped sharper for a second and hit her behind the eyes. The representative had reached Thessa's front room. The relay scroll was live. Two settlement enforcers were already coming in fast from the east road. Fifteen minutes, maybe less, before the trail narrowed to directions and guesses.

Twenty-one.

They cut through the orchard. The trees had been charmed hard years ago and then left alone, so the branches sprawled low and messy, fruit not yet ripe, irrigation channels sunk deep enough to hide a crouched body. Dren dropped into the ditch first. Kem followed, stooping to keep below the lip. Vel came last, boots sliding on dry clay cracked by old water lines.

"How long?" Dren asked.

"Quarter hour before they're pointed south."

Kem kept moving. "I can cover us."

"I know."

"Overlay first. Shield if they get close. Edge blur for tracking." He said it to the ditch wall, to the earth in front of him. "Eighteen."

"So do it."

His hands came up. The concealment settled over them in a fitted shell, light bent off the ditch lip, sound thinned, their movement blurred into heat shimmer and dust.

Seven levels.

It wasn't the wild excess he had used at the start of the road. It was shaped work, careful enough to hold.

Vel dropped a second concealment over their tracks, one level only, enough to fog the prints in the clay and blur the broken edge where they had climbed in.

Twenty.

They moved south in a bent half-run. Dren favored the left arm more now, keeping it tight to his ribs. Every bad step showed in his jaw. The ditch curved away from the road toward a dry creek bed.

Behind them, ability use prickled through the Perception residue still fading in Vel's skull. A scan passed over the orchard. Over the ditch. Onward.

Whether the cover held or Thessa pointed them wrong, Vel couldn't tell.

Forty minutes south, Kem's concealment wavered.

"It's thinning," he said.

"How long?"

"Not long."

He shifted without waiting for more. A shield snapped around them so close Vel felt the air press at her skin. Five levels. Then the tracking blur, six more, washed over their movement and turned their outlines watery.

That made eighteen.

When he lowered his hands, his fingers ticked against his palms. He kept them closed after that.

They came out of the ditch into a dry creek bed and kept going. Open ground spread ahead: scrub, brittle grass, low hills rising toward the river. No pursuit behind them. Not yet.

Kem glanced inward at his Ledger. "One-sixty."

Vel nodded. Nothing else.

The trail that picked up from the creek ran south and then split around a wash of broken stone. Dren took the left branch after one quick look. Kem followed him at once. Usually he would have checked with Vel, asked which line matched the map. This time he kept beside Dren.

Thessa's kitchen stayed in Vel's head: bread torn open on the plate, Maren's journal spread wide, Barrens in Thessa's mouth. Five years ago Vel had sat in that same chair and asked the question. Ahead of her, Kem never looked back.

Ahead, Dren said, "Water by nightfall if the map's right."

Kem answered him, not turning. "Good."

When the trail narrowed, he did not slow for Vel to take the lead. The gap was only a few steps. It held.

The hills lifted toward the river. Heat shook above the ground. Vel adjusted her pack and followed them south.
