# Chapter 8

Vel counted the road south in levels.

The Spent Towns fell behind them over three days. South of the last managed farms, the trade road narrowed to dirt and the fields went from rich to merely serviceable. Early summer had settled into warmth, thick enough by noon to press sweat into cloth and leave salt at the collar when it dried. The air smelled like cut grain, drying mud, and the faint mineral bite of old Shaping left in stone too long.

Twenty-eight sat behind everything else.

Dren walked point. He read the track by habit: fresh ruts, local bootprints, a wheel that had listed left under too much weight, last night's soft spots where a wagon had cut deeper after dark. Twenty years on the southern routes showed in how little of his attention looked busy.

"There's a depot settlement about an hour ahead," he said. "Grain storage, one inn if they still call it that, water trough by the road. We fill up there and keep moving."

"How big?" Vel asked.

"Thirty, forty inside the fence. Farms all around it. Folks there count strangers the same way they count sacks."

Kem walked behind her with his own pack and, for once, did not fight the straps. The road had knocked a little of the careful city posture out of him. He still looked at the fields as if open land arrived in pieces he had to learn one by one.

"Is that wheat?" he asked, nodding to the nearest field.

"Barley," Dren said.

"Right."

"You can tell by the awns."

"I know. Vel told me."

Dren glanced back once. "Then start knowing it on sight. South of here, somebody will charge you extra if you make them say it twice."

Kem looked at the field again, harder this time, as though the barley might rearrange itself into a lesson he could pass.

They walked another quarter hour in the heat. Road dust found the wet strip above Vel's boots and stayed there. Somewhere east, a Shaping strike cracked against stone. It was ordinary and therefore useful. She kept listening past it.

What she expected from pursuit was precision. The Hoarded liked the cheapest clean answer. She expected a Perception pulse or a tracker, or a small team that knew how to spend one level exactly where it mattered and then stop spending. They liked neat recovery, neat paperwork, neat cause and effect.

Dren slowed at the rise before the settlement and touched two fingers to the brim of the sun-faded hat he only wore when the road got honest enough to hurt.

"There," he said.

The depot lay in a shallow basin of barley and low stone walls. A square silo lifted above the roofs on the north side, gray and practical, the mortar darker where recent patchwork had failed to match the old. A trough sat by the road with two mules tied beside it. Three people were moving sacks from a wagon into a shed. Another two worked at the field edge with hooked knives, cutting an irrigation clog out of the ditch.

"We stop long enough for water and nothing else," Dren said. "No trading, no questions, and nobody touches the silo wall unless they want a lecture about municipal property."

"You make it sound personal," Kem said.

"I have had the lecture."

"Was it informative?"

"It was long."

Vel studied the silo, the ditch, the tree line west of the road. The depot people moved with the tight economy of a place that could not afford waste and knew it. One of the field hands looked up at them and kept looking. Strangers near grain made every settlement uneasy. Strangers with packs and city cloth made them worse.

"There's creek water south of here," Dren said. "Three kilometers, maybe a little more. Better spot to stop if we make good time."

"We make good time," Vel said.

"Fine by me."

Kem shaded his eyes and looked past the settlement to the darker strip of trees beyond it. "If someone were tracking us, would they wait at the creek or take the road where the cover is worse?"

"If they knew what they were doing," Vel said, "they'd take the road before the creek and leave the water for later."

"And if they don't know what they're doing?"

"Then they make noise first."

The hedge along the west field answered her.

Something caught in it. Cloth tore. A branch snapped. A man's voice said, with deep bureaucratic injury, "This was not in the briefing."

Another voice answered from the trees, female, precise, and pitched to carry without ever sounding raised.

"Tarrell, the notebook is closed during contact."

Dren's hand dropped to the knife at his belt. "Well," he said softly. "They do make noise first."

The woman in the trees kept speaking as if she were correcting minutes at a meeting.

"Lenn, the garment remains a cloak. Kess, if your heels are opening again, you say so now. Chael, hold formation. Marris, do not spend until you have a line."

Kem stared at the hedge. "Those are instructions for an ambush."

"Off the road," Vel said.

Dren moved at once. Kem swung after him, caught his pack on a fencepost, and made the wire sing down the ditch. The warning had come half a second too late, and the road gave them nowhere to disappear before the gray cloaks broke from the trees.

Five figures hit the open ground in matching color and wildly different states of preparedness.

Tarrell came first, because he had tripped out of cover ahead of everyone else. His hood was cinched so tight his face showed through it as a sweating red circle. A notebook smacked against one hip, and a personal seal hung from his belt on a cord that had already wrapped itself twice around his leg.

Lenn came after him in a mutilated cloak he had cut into a vest, the stripped sleeves still trailing from the back like dead things. His pack rode absurdly high between his shoulders. He moved with the stiff concentration of a man repeating instructions to himself.

Kess had lost her cloak somewhere in the woods but had kept the collapsed parasol lashed to her pack with a reverence that defied common sense. White cloth wrapped both heels above the boot line. Her face had the strained, overbright look of someone trying to turn dread into willingness by force.

Chael was the only one who looked as if he had lifted anything heavier than paper in his life. The gray sat badly on him because his shoulders were real shoulders and not an office's idea of them.

Marris came last in a properly closed cloak, her hood down, one hand lifted not to cast but to feel the hum of her own Ledger as if checking it against the air. She looked from the road to the silo to the ditch, and unlike the others she did not speak.

The sixth figure stayed in the shade under the trees: dark hair in a formal Hoarded arrangement, notebook open, no gray cloak, no hurry.

Their Ledgers pressed at the field hard enough to make the background hum feel weighted.

There were five maxed bodies in front of her, five people at 300. That was enough stored force to ruin a small settlement by accident.

Vel counted before any of them spent. She put herself at 28, Kem at about 225 and still unable to measure himself cleanly in motion, and Dren at 138, useful on a road and useless in a stand-up fight. Civilians stood at the trough and the silo. The barley lay open, with no cover worth the name. There was one competent watcher in the trees and one competent agent in the road.

If Kem threw a perimeter now, he would throw it too wide. She could see the shape of the mistake before he made it: a shining dome the size of a meeting hall, anchored in flat ground, with five level-300 idiots outside it trying techniques they did not understand until one of them got lucky or all of them got catastrophic.

She needed movement. She needed the Hoarded to stay stupid for one more minute.

"Kem. Behind me. Don't cast unless I tell you."

"I can fight."

"Not yet. You'll pen us in."

"I can narrow it this time."

"You can hope you can narrow it."

Dren had already left the road and gone low in the ditch grass. "If this turns into running," he said, "the tree line south is the only line that matters."

"I know."

Tarrell lifted both hands for a Binding. He did it with meticulous care, fingers aligned exactly as if he had memorized the sequence from a manual and never once used it in a body that sweated and shook.

From the trees, the notebook woman said, "Tarrell, you anchor to the target, not the nearest mass."

"I know that," Tarrell said.

At 300, a proper Binding could have pinned all three of them to the road and left Vel spending precious levels just to breathe.

Tarrell missed everyone and hit a boulder by the ditch.

His palm sealed to it with a wet slap. He jerked sideways, stumbled, and ended up half hanging from the stone with both boots skidding in the dirt. The notebook flew from his belt and landed open in the barley.

"That is not the target," the woman in the trees said.

"I am aware," Tarrell said, and then, with sudden panic, "My hand is still on it."

Kess, bright with the kind of courage that had not yet survived consequences, stepped into a Perception Sweep before anyone told her to. The parasol knocked against her spine as she opened the sense wide.

Vel knew the posture. At 300 it should have mapped targets, exits, lines of retreat, usable cover, expenditure traces.

Instead it gave Kess everything.

She heard every insect in the barley, every crack in the silo mortar, the pollen moving in the ditch air, the two mules by the trough, the seventeen breathing people in the depot proper, worms under the wet bank, the field hands' knives in the water, the salt drying in Lenn's collar, and the blood already oozing under her own heel wraps where the road had rubbed them raw again.

Kess made a wrecked little sound and sat down hard in the dirt.

"There are seventeen," she said. "The south wall is split. The ditch is full of beetles. I can hear the worms. I can hear all of it."

"Narrow the field," the woman in the trees said.

"I don't know how."

Two of them were useless already.

Vel started angling south. If she could get Kem and Dren off the road before the others figured out which mistake hurt most, the perimeter never had to happen.

Lenn chose violence because he had reached the part of the manual where action felt clearer than thought.

He planted his feet exactly wrong, muttered, "Sightline, release," and threw a Combat Pulse at Kem.

He missed by forty meters and hit the silo.

The upper third sheared off with a sound too large for the morning. Stone blocks split loose, grain burst through the seam, and the whole top section came apart into falling masonry and yellow weather. Barley husks sprayed the road. Dust rolled over the trough. One of the mules screamed and tore free.

The field hands dropped flat. Somebody in the depot shouted for everyone to get away from the wall. The shout arrived after the wall was already coming down.

Dren had gone low in the grass six meters from the road. He did not have to be heroic to be in range. A chunk of mortared stone, no bigger than a cookpot and moving much faster, struck his left forearm and opened it from just below the elbow nearly to the wrist.

The impact made a flat wet sound.

Dren said, "Shit," in the same tone he used for spoiled cargo, and then he looked at his arm.

The cut gaped immediately. Pink meat showed under the blood. Stone grit and barley chaff packed the opening. He clamped his right hand over it, but the blood pushed through his fingers at once and ran black-red over his wrist.

Vel marked the wound and left it there. She could not stop for him yet.

Chael had been the quiet one until that point. Seeing Lenn turn a silo into shrapnel seemed to decide him. He dropped his weight, rolled his shoulder once as if testing the joint, and reached for Strength.

The woman in the trees said, sharper now, "Chael, controlled application only."

Chael swung at open air.

The blow shoved a ring of compressed force across the field. Barley flattened in a widening stripe. Dust went sideways. The trough water lifted and slapped back down. Two trees at the south edge snapped through the middle and dropped together.

The pressure hit Vel in the sternum from fifteen meters away. It was only the edge of it, only a preview, and it still shoved air out of her lungs hard enough to sting her teeth.

If Chael learned control before they reached cover, the fight ended here.

Marris had still not spent. She watched Kess in the dirt, Tarrell stuck to the boulder, Lenn staring at the ruined silo as though destruction had happened to him personally, and Chael blinking at the trees he had not meant to break. Then she looked once toward the treeline.

The woman with the notebook did not raise her voice. "Observe the failures. Spend on what remains."

That was when Vel understood the sixth figure was not there to help. She was there to learn.

"Kem," Vel said. "If I say now, you cast and walk."

"I am ready."

"Be ready faster."

Marris raised both hands for a Force Push.

Vel turned on instinct. The shove caught her across the left ribs instead of square in the chest.

Two ribs cracked with small internal pops. Then something hot and wrong slid under them as cartilage tore loose near the sternum. Breathing stopped being a simple act and became a bad negotiation her body had no patience for.

She hit the ground on her right shoulder, dragged in one shallow breath full of dust and grain husk, and got up again because the alternative was staying there for the next one.

Her left hand clamped the fracture site before she told it to.

She had cracked at least two ribs, maybe three. The cartilage felt loose under the skin. A small Restoration would fix it for two levels. Two levels would take her from twenty-eight to twenty-six before she spent another thing in a fight that had barely started to learn from itself.

She was not spending two on pain.

Vel threw a one-level Combat strike into Marris's hip and knocked her stance sideways before the second Push formed. The expenditure bit cleanly and dropped Vel to twenty-seven.

Marris staggered, caught herself, and looked surprised only for the length of a blink. Even surprised, her hands stayed steadier than anyone else's.

Chael wound up again.

Vel burned an Evasion charm and moved two feet right. The borrowed twist through her ribs nearly dropped her, but the blast hit where she had been standing and sent dust hard enough across her neck to sting. That sidestep cost another level and left her at twenty-six.

Kem's Ledger shifted behind her. He had been waiting for the order since the gray cloaks broke cover, and now Dren was bleeding into the grass, the field was full of falling stone, and the only useful agent had found her range.

The open-ground plan was dead.

"Kem. Perimeter. Now."

He cast too big.

The barrier snapped up around them in a blue-white dome nearly thirty meters wide. It enclosed road, ditch, two fenceposts, half a burst of grain dust, and more useless air than Vel wanted to think about. It crackled where barley husks struck it and slid.

Kem swore under his breath and then louder. "That's not the size I wanted."

"I know."

The next Push hit the shell and ran around it in a white ripple that made the whole dome hum. Marris had aimed at center mass this time. Next time she would aim lower, or at the footing, or at the seam where Kem's concentration wavered.

Vel spent one more level on a Combat strike and cratered the dirt in front of Chael's boots. Stone and hard clods burst into his face. He swung blind, overcorrected, and tore a furrow through the barley long enough to look surveyed. Twenty-five remained.

Tarrell had finally ripped his palm off the boulder. Skin came with the stone. He stared at the raw red mess in disbelief, then tried another Binding with his injured hand because he was too frightened to stop obeying his own training.

The notebook woman said, "Tarrell, do not repeat a failed action unchanged."

"I am adjusting," Tarrell said, and immediately anchored the Binding to a fencepost.

The post sealed upright, humming faintly with borrowed force. Tarrell swore at it with the formal helplessness of a man who had not learned profanity young enough to make it useful.

Kess was on her knees now, both hands pressed over her ears, still talking because the Sweep had not narrowed yet.

"There are nineteen now. No, eighteen. Someone ran from the inn. The mule's in the barley. There's blood by the ditch. I can hear his blood. I can hear the grain pouring out of the upper chamber. I can hear-"

"Stop listening," Dren snapped from the grass, voice flat with pain.

For one second Kess actually did. Her eyes found him, wide and human and terrified.

Lenn recovered from the sight of the silo just in time to become dangerous to everyone again. He had the field-manual look of a man who believed that if the technique failed, the answer was more technique.

"I had the angle right," he said to nobody in particular. "The release point was bad."

"Lenn," the notebook woman said, "do not test revisions on the grain stores."

"Then on what?"

He answered himself by hurling a second Combat Pulse at the perimeter.

The strike hit high. The shell held, but Kem gasped and bent one inch at the waist as the force ran through the dome.

"I'm holding it," Kem said, too fast. "I am holding it. I know I am saying that while holding it. I hear it. I am still holding it."

"Good," Vel said. "Keep talking if it keeps you upright."

Marris moved left. Better angle. Better footing. She had watched the dome ripple once and learned from it.

Vel spent another level and struck her knee before the next Push fully formed. Marris dropped, one leg folding under her, but the half-built force still bled off into the barley and flattened another wide section of field. Twenty-four remained.

Chael wiped mud and blood from one eye with the back of his wrist and looked at the perimeter with slow concentration. Unlike the others, he was not flustered by failing. He was simply recalculating.

That made him the second problem.

Tarrell tore free of the fencepost-binding by abandoning it mid-cast. The backlash flayed more skin off his palm. He made a sound like a man swallowing his own scream and then finally, blessedly, stopped spending.

Kess lurched to her feet with the Perception still partly open. The parasol slipped loose from her pack, fell into the dirt, and rolled until it struck the perimeter in a sad little tap.

"Your wall is thirty-one across," she said to Kem, horrified by what she knew. "No, thirty and a half. South side thicker now. North side weak where the ditch bends. There are three people under the wagon because they think wood matters more than stone. The woman in the shed bit through her lip."

Kem looked at Vel without moving anything but his eyes. "I hate that she knows that."

"So do I."

The notebook woman under the trees had stopped giving broad corrections. She watched Kess's collapse, Tarrell's failures, Chael's wide-force problem, and Marris's aim. Her pen moved constantly. Once, Vel saw her look from the flattened barley to the dome and count the distance between them with her eyes.

Silo dust settled over everybody. Grain kept pouring from the broken upper chamber in a steady pale stream. One of the depot men had crawled from under the wagon and was now dragging the other by the collar, both of them low to the ground.

This was what a fight looked like when nobody aimed at civilians and civilians still bled for it.

Vel drove another one-level strike into Chael's shoulder. The expenditure pulled at her already abused side as if reaching through the break. Chael's arm gave with an ugly hitch and dropped half useless. Twenty-three remained.

He went to one knee, good hand clamped over the bad shoulder, mouth open on shocked breath.

Marris, still down on one leg, looked at Chael's shoulder, then at Vel's left arm clamped over her ribs. She had seen enough to start sorting targets by cost.

That was the true change in the field. The others were still disasters. Marris was becoming an opponent.

Vel leaned toward Dren without taking her eyes off the road. "Can you move?"

"If I'm vertical, I can move."

"When the south side opens, you run for the trees. No heroics."

"Didn't feel heroic."

Kem swallowed and reset his feet. Sweat had soaked his collar dark. "If I drop the north edge and reinforce south, I can walk it. I think. I mean I can try it. I know those are different statements."

"Do it on my mark."

Lenn drew breath for a third pulse. Kess screamed because the sound of it arrived before the cast. Tarrell, pale now and bleeding down his wrist, stooped for his notebook with his good hand as if salvage of the record might justify the rest of him. Chael tried to stand and failed. Marris lifted her hands again from one knee.

Vel spent a final one-level strike and clipped Marris across the forearm before the Push landed. Her hands broke apart. The force went wild and blew a notch out of the road twenty feet short of the dome. Twenty-two remained.

She had spent six levels in less than three minutes.

Her ribs ground every time she dragged air in. Dren's blood had soaked the ditch grass black. Kem's overbuilt dome hummed like a live wire around them. Beyond it stood five people at 300, four of them incompetent and one of them learning fast enough to matter.

"Now," Vel said.

Kem let the north side dissolve and shoved the saved force south. The dome lurched, thinned behind them, and thickened toward the trees in a brighter arc.

"Walk," Vel said.

Dren came up out of the grass and nearly fell before finding his legs. He clamped his left arm to his body and started south at a hard, ugly half-run. Vel followed with her torso locked rigid against the broken ribs. Kem backed after them, both hands out, face white with concentration.

The agents finally did what the notebook woman had wanted all along. They stopped spending blindly and watched them move.

Marris saw first where the south wall had thickened. She pointed with one steady hand. The notebook woman wrote it down.

Tarrell, clutching his skinned palm and rescued notebook together, stared at the path they were taking through the grain and said, to nobody useful, "They favor the injured side."

"Yes," the notebook woman said. "Continue observing."

Kess pressed both hands to her own temples and said, "The barrier's thinner at the ditch seam. The left one has ribs. The trader is losing blood faster when he runs. They are heading to water."

Vel heard that through the shell and hated her for being right.

Lenn looked as if he wanted to prove he was not the silo man forever. Marris said, quiet and practical, "Do not spend high. You'll hit the shell and drain nothing."

The notebook woman's pen paused. "Good. Again."

That, more than the cracked ribs, more than the blood on Dren's arm, was what put cold into Vel's stomach.

They were getting less incompetent.

***

The first line of trees closed over them in a rash of shade and insects.

Kem kept the perimeter moving another thirty paces before Vel said, "Drop it."

He let the dome go all at once and nearly folded with it. The hum vanished from the air, leaving only creekward birdsong, Dren's breathing, and the wet patter of blood off his fingers.

Vel braced herself against an oak and checked the damage with two careful fingertips. The fifth and sixth ribs on the left had cracked. Cartilage peeled loose near the sternum. The place was already starting to swell. A small Restoration would fix it for two levels and leave her at twenty.

Twenty belonged to the Margins, not to this road.

She left the bones where they were.

"Kem."

He bent double, hands on his thighs, sweat dropping off his jaw. "One-eighty-three," he said. "I know that means forty-two for the perimeter. I know that before you say it."

"I wasn't going to say it."

"You were absolutely going to say it later."

Maybe. The barrier had held. Later was not guaranteed.

Dren sat down hard under a birch and made the wound bleed fresh when he checked it. Blood ran over his wrist and into the leaf litter. Vel stripped a clean length of cloth from what remained of her spare shirt and crouched as far as she could without driving the broken ribs white-hot through her side.

"Hold it closed," she said. "Hard."

Dren took the cloth in his teeth long enough to loop it one-handed around his forearm. He cinched it. His face changed color but not expression.

"There's water ahead?" Vel asked.

"Creek crossing. Three kilometers. Maybe a little less if we cut the cart path."

"We cut it."

Kem had turned back toward the field. Through the trees the depot looked broken into separate pieces: half a silo lying where it had landed, gray cloaks spread around the barley, settlement people keeping a distance that was close enough to witness and far enough to live.

The dark-haired woman had stepped clear of the treeline now without a gray cloak. A dark coat cut for office work hung off her instead of anything made for the road. Her hair, arranged in the formal Hoarded way, had started coming loose at the nape. She held the notebook open in one hand.

She had not fought. She had watched.

Vel followed Kem's gaze. "Who is she?"

Kem did not answer at once. His mouth had gone thin.

"I know her by sight," he said. "Sable Orm. Senior Auditor."

"Auditor of what?" Dren asked.

Kem gave a short laugh with no humor in it. "Whatever the Council wants described as procedure instead of violence."

Through the trees, Sable bent to pick Tarrell's fallen notebook out of the barley. She handed it back without looking at his face, then made him show her the ruined palm while she wrote something beside it. When Kess pointed shakily at the south edge of the field, Sable turned and measured the distance to the trees again.

Vel watched Marris limp three steps to Sable's side and gesture with both hands: shell height, line of retreat, the point where the dome had thickened. Sable listened. Chael, one arm hanging wrong, leaned in to hear. Lenn kept looking at the broken silo as if he might will it unbroken by regret alone. Tarrell clutched the notebook and bled on the page. Kess still had no cloak and no business being out here.

"She stayed clean so she could count it," Dren said.

Kem nodded once. "Yes."

"And now she's got measurements."

"Yes."

Vel looked back at Sable. "She's not a field agent."

"No," Kem said. "That's why she stayed out of it. She's an office creature with authority and no business throwing power blind. She was there to see what five people at three hundred could do, what they ruined, and what almost worked."

"Which is worse," Dren said.

"Yes," Kem said again, quieter now.

Sable closed the notebook, spoke to Marris, and pointed not at the road but farther south, where the trees thinned toward the creek lands. Marris looked once in that direction and nodded.

It was not an abstract threat waiting for another chapter. It was a named woman giving the five failures a better use for next time.

Vel pushed off the oak. The movement sent a blade of pain under her left breast and straight through her back.

"We keep moving," she said.

Dren got to his feet with the bad arm clamped in and said, "Yeah."

Vel looked at him. "You can still break west at the creek and take your fee north. We'll manage south without you if we have to."

Kem opened his mouth, startled by the offer, but Dren was already shaking his head.

"No."

"You're hurt."

"I'm not dead."

"That wasn't the question."

Dren tightened the cloth knot with his teeth again, then spat a thread loose into the leaves. When he spoke, he did it without looking at either of them.

"Those people just opened my arm on a grain road and wrote down how long it took me to reach cover. I trade this stretch. I trade north of it. Roth sits off one of my runs. Mira waits there when the healer keeps her overnight." He finally looked up. "I don't peel off now and walk them straight across every stop I use. We break the trail first."

Vel nodded once. "Fine."

Kem looked back through the trees one last time. "Marris is the dangerous one," he said. "The one with the proper cloak."

"I know."

"If Sable is letting her learn from the others-"

"I know that too."

Dren started south before the talk could turn into anything less useful than walking.

The cart path he found was barely a path at all, just twin compressed lines through dry grass where small wagons cut toward the creek in better seasons. He took it at a hard pace anyway, shoulders tucked against the pain. Vel followed, one hand against her ribs, every step a negotiation. Kem came last, turning too often to listen behind them.

No one came through the trees after them.

That did not calm anybody.

The creek announced itself first by temperature. The air dropped a degree, then another. Insects thickened. The grass along the bank went from dry-stemmed yellow to green enough to hide mud under it.

They reached the water where it widened shallow over flat stone. The crossing was only knee deep on Dren, thigh deep on Kem, fast enough to pull at balance and not fast enough to wash anything away cleanly.

Dren stepped in first and hissed when cold water hit the torn arm.

The creek went red around him at once.

Vel saw it the same second Kem did: the bright spreading trail, too clear against the stones, a marker any half-competent Perception sweep could follow even if Kess had finally stopped hearing worms.

"Concealment," Vel said.

"How much?"

"Two."

Kem did not argue. He set one hand over the current, the other over the ring on his finger, and pulled the charm wrong on the first try and right on the second. The water dulled. The red thinned into a general murk of creek silt and moving shadow. The scent lifted and went flat.

Kem swayed when the spend landed.

"One-eighty-one," he said automatically, not because anybody asked.

"Walk," Vel said.

They crossed with Dren leading and Vel close enough behind to shove him if the footing went. Halfway through, her ribs reminded her that twisting was now an expensive luxury she no longer possessed. She almost lost her own balance on a slick stone, recovered, and tasted blood where she had bitten the inside of her cheek.

On the far bank, Dren planted one boot on wet clay and the earth sheared under him. He went down on the wounded side with a strangled sound he did not quite let become a cry.

The fall yanked the cut open wider.

Blood ran free through the cloth. Dren's knees buckled. He caught himself with the good hand and nearly slid back into the water.

"Strength," Vel said.

"I can pull him up without it," Kem said, already reaching.

"You can try. Then you can watch him rip the arm the rest of the way open. Spend."

Kem spent.

The change hit him from feet to shoulders: thighs hardening, breath catching, the jolt of borrowed force moving through a body that still did not trust it. He dropped down the bank, got Dren's good arm over his shoulders, and hauled him the last stretch with brute enhanced leverage while the mud came away under both of them.

"Three," Kem gasped when they were up. "That was three."

"I know."

"I'm at one-seventy-eight."

The number sat in the air for a second, smaller now and permanent.

Dren leaned hard against a sapling until the worst of the shaking passed. "Next time," he said through his teeth, "I vote for the ambush where the grain stays standing."

"Noted," Vel said.

They moved again because stillness was as good as a trail marker. The land south of the creek rose into a stand of birch and scrub oak, then dipped toward a long shallow fold in the ground where the old cart path vanished under leaf litter. Dren found a small clearing there with the reflex of a man who had slept in bad country often enough to judge safety by radius and inconvenience.

"Here," he said.

It was not much of a camp, only a flat patch under birch branches with enough canopy to break sight lines from the path and enough dry ground to lay a wounded man down without soaking him in rot. It was enough.

***

Vel dropped her pack and immediately wished she had not, because bending for it again sent heat through the broken ribs so bright it made the clearing jump.

Kem saw her face tighten. "You should heal that."

"No."

"You can barely stand."

"I can stand enough."

"That's not the same thing."

"No," she said. "It isn't."

Dren lowered himself to the ground with the careful anger of a man offended by his own body. Once he was down he let the control slip for half a second, head tipped back against the birch, jaw gone white.

Vel knelt beside him as far as her ribs would permit and cut away the sleeve with her small knife.

The wound looked worse clean than it had blood-packed. Silo stone had not sliced; it had torn. The edges were ragged where grit and mortar had gone in. Grain husks stuck to the clotting blood. The deepest part near the elbow showed pale tendon under the red.

Kem swallowed hard and did not look away.

"Water," Vel said.

He handed over the skin. She poured creek water through the cut until the mud loosened and the blood ran thinner. Dren's fingers twitched once against his own knee and then locked there.

"Spirits."

Kem rummaged through the half-broken pack and found the squat bottle wrapped in cloth. Vel took it, braced an elbow against her own ribs, and looked at Dren once before she tipped it.

"Bite something if you need to."

"Just do it."

She washed the wound with spirits.

Dren's whole body jumped. He sucked air through his nose so hard it sounded like a blade being drawn.

"That's ugly," Kem said quietly.

"Yes," Vel said.

"Will he keep the arm?"

"If it doesn't rot, yes."

"Helpful," Dren muttered.

"You asked him to look."

"Didn't ask for commentary."

Vel rinsed again, picked out a grain hull with the knife tip, then another. Every forward lean drove the cracked ribs against each other. She kept going until the edge of her vision started to pulse.

Kem crouched opposite her, hands held uselessly open. Under the last of the borrowed Strength, his forearms still looked too tight with stored effort.

"Tell me what to do," he said. "I can hold things. I can hand things. I can do more than that if you tell me exactly what more than that is."

Vel sat back on her heels too fast and had to clamp a hand to her side.

The pain was clean enough to think through, which was almost worse.

"Thread kit," she said.

Kem found it where she told him: cloth wrap, curved needle, waxed thread, the small pliers she had bent years ago and kept because tools that worked did not stop working just because time passed.

She took the needle and tried to settle herself close enough to sew.

The first movement told her no.

Bringing the wound edges together meant leaning over Dren's arm and holding steady with both hands. Her ribs would not let the torso fold. The minute she tried, the left side seized so hard the needle jumped between her fingers.

She stopped before the point touched him.

Dren watched her face, then the hand at her ribs. He did not waste breath pretending not to understand.

"Can't do it?" he asked.

"Not cleanly."

"Good to know we've got standards."

"Shut up."

He shut up.

Kem looked from one of them to the other. "I can do it."

Vel laughed once because the alternative was cursing, and the laugh hurt enough to feel like punishment.

"No," she said.

"I can."

"You've never stitched anything."

"That is true," Kem said. "It is also true that you cannot bend."

He was right and young enough to say it plainly.

Vel closed her eyes for one breath and opened them again. The clearing smelled of spirits, wet cloth, birch bark, and the rank copper stink of fresh blood. Somewhere beyond the trees a night insect started up early, fooled by shade into thinking it had more authority than the sun.

Dren shifted his weight and the wound welled again.

"Fine," Vel said.

Kem blinked. "Fine?"

"Fine. You're doing it."

"Oh."

"Don't sound surprised."

"I am surprised."

"Stop being surprised and listen."

He moved in at once, knees in the leaf litter, needle held with far too much care. His hands were steady for about two seconds and then started to shake.

"That's not encouraging," Dren said.

"You are bleeding on me," Kem said. "I think I am allowed a moment."

"You had a moment."

"I had many moments. None of them involved this."

Vel took the waxed thread back long enough to knot it and pass the needle through the first loop. Even that small motion asked too much from her side. She gave up before the knot was perfect and handed it back.

"You're closing skin, not tying off a grain sack," she said. "Straight through. Both sides, same depth. Pull it snug, not tight. If you strangle the flesh, it tears worse later."

Kem nodded too many times.

"If I do it wrong-"

"Then you do the next one less wrong."

He looked at Dren's arm as if waiting for it to turn into something a library had prepared him for. It stayed an arm split open by silo stone.

Behind him, the last light through the birches had started to thin. Vel thought of the field north of them: Sable's notebook, Marris's better aim, Kess hearing the creek in the wrong direction, Tarrell bleeding on his records, Lenn learning not to throw at silos because he had already done it once.

No one mentioned the western break again.

Dren held his torn forearm out without ceremony. "Do it," he said.

Kem set the point of the needle to skin.
