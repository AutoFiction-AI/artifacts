# Chapter 16

Two days after Sable walked out of the farmhouse, the Scour still was not under them. From the farmhouse the basin had been about two hard days on foot by the direct southern road if they were willing to be seen. Vel had spent those same two days taking them east through dead irrigation cuts and fallen relay posts, then south in crooked pieces, then east again whenever the open road showed fresh dust or mirror-flash. They had not gone backward, but they had barely bought half a day's southing. The direct line belonged to whoever the Red Reserve already had between the farmhouse and the basin. The loop bought them cover, one intact pump, three walls still standing, and proof Sable had not been trying to scare them for sport. It had not bought them time. They were still about two days from the basin because they'd spent two days not going straight at it.

Yesterday, belly-down in a dry canal with reeds turned to wire around their faces, Vel had watched two riders take the direct road south. Not Margins enforcers. Their spacing was too neat, their horses too quiet, and neither man looked around with the loose greed of somebody hoping to extort travelers. One had stopped at an old relay frame and flashed a polished signal plate toward the basin. Ten minutes later another flash answered from farther down. After that she stopped thinking in terms of warnings and started thinking in terms of coverage.

The detour had cost them anyway. Kem spent seven levels in the stupid little bites that passed for caution when you still had levels left: one for light in a collapsed culvert, two bracing a washout while Dren crossed with his left arm pinned to his side, one forcing water through a pump handle rusted half shut, three more in scraps of strength and reinforcement when Margins infrastructure gave out under their feet. Vel spent two of her own, one on concealment when that first dust line swung off the road toward their ditch, one keeping the waystation stairs from coming down under Dren's weight after dark. By dusk Kem was at eighteen. She was at fourteen. Dren was still at one hundred and twenty, which only meant he'd managed not to do anything desperate yet.

He was packing before sunrise.

Vel heard him through the thin wall of the waystation, a bedroll tied wrong, retied, tied wrong again. A buckle knocked stone. A strap dragged over wood. A muffled curse when leather caught on a splintered doorframe. She knew what she was listening to. She had been expecting it since the farmhouse, since Sable's warning, since the ditch yesterday when Dren watched those riders and went quiet in a way that had nothing to do with road discipline.

Outside, dawn was thin and colorless. Summer heat already pressed down before the light had properly arrived. The station had been a Hoarded supply post once, square-built and joyless, with a loading platform facing a road that no longer carried anything worth logging. The stamped sign on the doorframe still read PRODUCTIVITY TRANSFER STATION 7-SOUTH, though half the paint had gone to powder. Someone had carved *fuck off* under it in neat letters. Farther south, on the stump of a relay mast that should have fallen years ago, something flashed once. Then again.

Dren came out a minute later. He carried his pack low on his right shoulder because his left arm would not take the weight for long. The silo gash had healed into a thick shiny ridge from wrist to elbow, and the hand on that side only half closed until he worked it awake.

"Morning," he said.

"Yeah."

He set the pack down by the broken foundation wall and looked toward the relay mast instead of at her. Another flash. Whoever was down there had cleaned old Hoarded signal junk back into use. That alone made the morning feel smaller.

"I'm heading back," Dren said.

Vel nodded. She had known, but the words still altered the air. "When?"

"Now. While they're still watching south." He jerked his chin toward the mast. "By full light yesterday they were pushing east off the road. I'm not walking into that with a third pack on my back."

"You don't owe us an explanation."

"I know." He sat on the wall. The stone was already warm. "I'd rather say it than keep hearing myself think it all the way north."

Vel leaned against the doorway and waited. Kem was stirring inside, floorboards creaking under a body that had gotten leaner over six weeks of bad road. Kem at eighteen. Vel at fourteen. Two numbers that would have been a punchline in the Spent Towns, right up to the part where everybody died.

"My daughter's sick," Dren said. "You know that. What you don't know is what it means when the healer in Roth burns forty of her own levels on a child's legs and then hands me the price in marks like marks are bone and I can just buy some more. The money you put up last time covered half. Maybe half. I still left home short." He rubbed his thumb over a callus in the heel of his palm. "I've got one hundred and twenty left and a girl who's eight and doesn't care about thresholds or the Scour or any of your clever road math. Some mornings her legs won't take her when she swings out of bed. She hits the floor and looks at me like I was meant to keep that from happening."

"I know."

"You know the count." He looked at her then. "I've been picking her up off the kitchen floor since spring."

Vel did not argue. He was right. She knew Dren's life as arithmetic, exactly and usefully, and had never once been in the room when his girl hit the floor.

Wind crossed the old fields carrying dust, pump grease, and the sour after-smell of Fertility work done too often and too long. Three hundred meters south the direct road ran between plots that had once been farmland, rows still visible as ghost lines in exhausted earth.

"I watched the kid spend ninety-eight levels on strangers," Dren said. "People he'd never met. I watched you count every one and say nothing because if you said stop then you had to say why." His gaze stayed on hers. "Yesterday we lay in a ditch because two men on decent horses know this ground better than we do. Maybe that's the garrison. Maybe it's the pair Sable said might be coming. Doesn't matter. It means the road you sold me got smaller while mine didn't."

"That's fair."

"It's not enough." He scratched at his jaw. Sun had bleached his beard in patches over the last weeks. "If I keep going south, I'm not backing a job anymore. I'm backing a woman I never met and a hole in the ground. Mira doesn't get to lose for that."

Vel looked at the station sign instead of Dren's face.

"I've still got a healer in Roth waiting on the second half of a bill," he said. "That's the road I should be on."

Grief tightened under her ribs, not the old break there, but the other thing behind it.

"You've been good company," she said. The sentence felt thin as paper.

"I've been paid company." His mouth twitched once. "Some of it was paid. The last bit wasn't." He shifted on the wall, tested the left hand, let it fail and hang. "I don't think you're wrong about the Scour. I think you're short on the number of things that can go wrong before you get there."

"Probably."

"Yeah." He picked at a loose thread on his pack strap. "And the kid still doesn't know why he's walking south. I can see that from here. I'd rather not be around when he works it out."

Kem came out blinking into the raw light. His face was thinner than at the start, the softness of Vault life stripped off by walking and heat. Since the fracture he had gotten quieter. Not Hoarded-quiet. More like somebody who had finally realized words cost breath and breath was work.

"Dren."

"Yeah." Dren looked at him. "Heading home."

Kem took it in. He took in the pack, the arm, the way Dren had timed the leaving to the flashes down south. "Now?"

"Now's when north makes sense." Dren nodded toward the relay mast. "I wait an hour and whoever's working that road starts wondering what came out of this wreck."

Kem glanced south, then back at the bad arm. "Your hand."

Dren flexed it once. Two fingers lagged. "Still there."

"I could look at it. I mean properly. Not..." Kem lifted his own hand and dropped it again. "Not the way I did before."

"Don't," Dren said it gently. "You've got eighteen and a bad plan. Spend them on the bad plan."

"My arm isn't your problem," Dren said. "Mira is. Mine too. That's why I'm going north with what I've got left."

Kem looked as if he might argue anyway. Then he didn't. "All right."

"There's a first." Dren stood, hitched the pack higher with his good shoulder, and winced when the movement pulled across the scar. "For what it's worth, you spend different now."

Kem's mouth tightened. "I would hope so."

"In the Spent Towns you threw levels around like a drunk lord at a festival. Now you stop and count before you do something stupid." Dren looked between him and Vel. "Keep that. Both of you."

Kem put out his hand. Dren looked at it, then took it. One firm shake. No performance in it.

"Tell Mira..." Kem started, then stopped.

"Yeah," Dren said.

He turned to Vel last. Another flash answered from farther south, quick and flat in the growing light.

"Don't let them pin you to the road," he said. "That's all."

Then he went north through the broken yard, cutting away from the direct line before a watcher could put three bodies together with one ruined station. His boots made a dry scraping sound over old loading gravel. Vel watched him until the ground folded and took him. He never looked back.

After that the waystation felt wrong. Two packs where there had been three. The empty place Dren left behind had weight. So did the south.

Vel did not let them stay long enough to feel either one properly.

"Pack," she said.

Kem did. No argument. Bedroll. Canteens. Journal. The little salvage that made up their remaining life. By the time the sun was fully over the flats they were moving through the old service lane east of the road, using broken pump walls and dead orchard lines for cover.

Two sets of footsteps made the morning sound wrong.

Vel kept expecting Dren's heavier tread on her right and getting scrub, wind, cracked masonry. The service lane had once fed farm units and transfer sheds. Hoarded planners had laid it with the same bad confidence they laid everything: straight enough to look efficient, shallow enough to fail as soon as maintenance stopped. Plates of old road rose at angles out of the dirt. Rebar showed through in rusted seams. She mapped the breaks as she walked, step left here, skirt the sink there, old habit running ahead of thought.

They passed a farmstead where someone was still trying. An old woman pushed a hand plow through ground that broke in thin plates under the blade and gave back a dry ceramic sound. She never looked up. A cracked board by her well read UNIT OUTPUT REVIEW SUSPENDED PENDING APPEAL DISPOSITION. The appeal box had been pried off and stolen.

Kem slowed beside the sign long enough to read it. "They labeled the well a unit."

"They label anything they plan to count."

He looked like he might say more. Light hit her eye from the south instead. One quick hard flare. Then a second.

"Down," Vel said.

Kem moved before asking why. Good. Better than good. He dropped with her off the lane and behind what had been the far wall of a storage shed, both of them sliding the last foot on gravel and old mortar dust. Vel pulled him farther until the broken wall took them below sightline.

"Relay?" he whispered.

"Yes."

Another flash. Then, after a stretch long enough to be intentional, a third from farther east.

Kem turned his head toward her. "What does that mean."

"It means somebody saw movement and doesn't want to waste a horse guessing."

She risked a glance over the wall. Dust lifted off the direct road in two separate lines now. One kept south. One broke east.

"Stay down," she said.

The rider came off the road at an angle that told her he had done this before. No hurry. No theatrics. Just a measured sweep across the side lanes and field cuts, letting the horse pick the best ground while he looked where a tired person would try to hide. Vel flattened farther into the hollow. Beside her Kem had gone still enough to pass for somebody who had learned fear properly.

The horse passed thirty yards out. Close enough for the leather creak to carry. Close enough for Vel to see the rider's coat was travel-dulled red at the collar and cuffs, the color hidden from distance and obvious once you knew to look. Reserve, then, or somebody equipped by them. The man did not search like a Margins collector. He searched like a person who expected prey to be intelligent and therefore disappointing.

He circled once around the old farmstead. The woman with the plow kept pushing her blade through the field as if armed riders quartering past her beans were weather and not worth the neck turn. The rider paused by her well, looked at the broken appeal notice, drank from his own canteen instead, and moved on.

Vel waited until the hoofbeats had gone far enough to become road sound again. Then she waited longer. Her back was damp against the broken stone. Flies found the sweat at her hairline. Beside her Kem's breathing stayed shallow and controlled. Good. He was learning that panic made noise.

"We're not moving yet," she said.

"How long."

"Until the second rider clears or until they decide the first one saw nothing worth the trouble."

Kem stared up at the cracked underside of the wall cap. Sun bled through one split seam in a blade of white.

"So this is the road now," he said.

"This is the road now."

He let that sit. The heat in the hollow rose fast. The smell was hot stone, mouse droppings, old plaster, and the faint iron stink of rust cooking in the sun.

"How long were you going to wait," Kem asked, "before you told me that from the farmhouse we weren't taking the basin road at all."

"I told you we were looping east."

"You told me we were taking cover." He turned his head toward her. His face was close in the dimness, dust already pasted to the sweat at his temple. "This isn't cover, Vel. This is a different journey."

"It became a different journey when Sable said commander, two stationed, maybe four. That changes the line."

"It changes the line. Fine. It also changes what I need to know."

Vel kept her eyes on the gap in the wall. "I've been telling you what matters."

"No." His whisper sharpened, then checked itself. "You've been telling me the piece that keeps me walking. That's different."

She could have answered that. She could have said that keeping him walking had in fact been what mattered. The heat, the rider, the narrow strip of shade they were sharing made the small answer look cheap even before she put words on it.

Outside, a second set of hoofbeats crossed the service lane and kept going east. This rider did not come as near. Vel counted the steps by sound, felt them in the wall more than heard them by the end, and kept counting until there were none.

Kem waited through the counting. Then he said, quieter than before, "No more steps. Not until you tell me why."

"I told you the mechanism. The cascade, the sites, what zero does-"

"Not the mechanism. You." He dragged in a breath and let it out slow. "Why you, specifically. Why you've been walking toward zero for eight years. Why you carry that journal like it's the only object in the world. Why Thessa looked at you like you'd killed someone. Why Dren can look at us and see a shape you still won't say out loud."

The Ledger hummed at the edge of Vel's awareness. Fourteen. Enough to do something foolish if the riders came back. Not enough to waste on lying.

She looked through the crack in the wall at the old woman still working her field. The plow blade lifted. Dropped. Lifted again.

"I could answer the route part first," Vel said.

"I know you could."

"And it'd be true."

"I know that too."

Kem wiped sweat from his upper lip with the back of his hand. Dust streaked there and stayed. "Tell me the other one."

Vel let the silence run until it stopped being a tactic. The riders had moved on. The road had not. South was still being watched by people who knew what they were doing. Dren was already gone because of it. There was no cleaner hour coming later.

"All right," she said.

She shrugged her pack off, worked the inner flap loose, and took out Maren's journal. The leather was warm from her back. When she set it in her lap the heat of it stayed in her palms.

"Her name was Maren."

Kem said nothing.

"We spent together eleven years. Deep routes, Margins work, settlements nobody with sense or money volunteered for. Healing. Repairs. Pulling people out of structures that should've been condemned before I was born." Vel kept her voice low out of habit now, though the riders were gone. "She was better at the people part. She'd take a fever with the inside of her wrist, tell the patient not to lie to her, and half the room would decide to trust her before I had my tools out. I always had to earn it."

A fly buzzed around Kem's ear. He ignored it.

"We split the jobs. She took the uglier healing because she had the hands for it. I did structural work, field repairs, the boring things that keep roofs up and pumps running. She kept the route journal." Vel opened it to the middle without really seeing the page. "Her maps were bad. Useful, but bad. She'd mark a well half a mile wrong and note which mule bit, which house still had clean bandages, who lied about their cough."

Kem's mouth moved once at that. Not a smile exactly. The beginning of one that knew better.

"You loved her," he said.

"Yes."

That answer was easier than the next one. Vel kept going before she could stop herself into smaller pieces. The old plaster taste came back at the root of her tongue so fast she had to swallow before the next sentence would hold.

"She hit zero first. Eight years ago. It wasn't planned." Vel looked down at the open page. Maren's hand slanted left when she was tired. This page did. "We were south of here in a settlement built over old mine galleries. Row houses, two rooms each, all patched floor joists and borrowed walls. We'd already spent too much that week. Fever, bridge work, one idiot who thought he could move a kiln alone. Maren was at three. I was at sixty-two."

Kem stayed still. The rhythm of his breathing shifted. Less anger in it now. More attention.

"The floor went first," Vel said. "Not the dramatic kind. Just a crack under your boots, one plate, then the next, like somebody breaking crockery inside the walls. Dust came up hot and chalk-bitter. The front room dropped knee-deep and kept going. There was a kid under the lintel and another one still inside. Maren spent one stabilizing the back wall so the father could drag himself clear. Spent another on the boy when the beam came down on his shoulder. I had both hands on the crossbrace and splinters already in my palms. She went in for the girl."

Vel could still see it if she let herself, so she did not have to invent anything cleaner. Dust so thick it turned spit to paste. The sound timber made one breath before it stopped being timber. Maren's hair tied back in the red strip she used when she needed both hands. The page edge dug a crease into Vel's thumb.

"I told her not to." Vel shut the journal halfway, then opened it again because shutting it felt worse. "That wasn't principle. I just did the count faster. One level left, roof already moving, no clean way out. She went anyway."

Outside the wall the plow scraped. Scraped again.

"She got the girl halfway to the door. The ceiling came after. Maren spent the last of it getting the weight off the child's chest." Vel had to wet her mouth before the rest would come. The dust was back in it anyway, old plaster and blood. "The threshold opened right there in the room. Not metaphorical. Not a feeling. A place. Cold air hit my face hard enough to raise the sweat on my arms. It smelled like wet stone. The light was clean and white and wrong for that room. I could see stone steps, a terrace wall, a woman turning with a basket because of the noise." Her hand tightened on the page edge until the paper bent. "Maren looked back at me. Then it closed and she was gone."

Kem drew breath to say something, stopped, and tried again. "You saw through."

"Yes."

"And you knew. Right then."

"I knew it wasn't death." Vel looked at him. "I didn't know what it was. I knew it wasn't death."

Heat crawled under her shirt. There was mortar grit in the seam of her trousers digging into the back of her knee. None of it touched the older thing.

"That was eight years ago," Kem said.

"Yes."

"You've been trying to repeat it since."

"Not the collapse." Vel looked away toward the crack in the wall. The old woman had finally stopped plowing. She stood with one hand on the handles, staring nowhere Vel could see. "But yes. I've been trying to find a way to make the crossing happen on purpose ever since."

He shifted one shoulder against the stone. "The journal. The routes. Every settlement where somebody vanished."

"All of it."

"Vel."

She turned back.

"Say the rest."

There it was. No room left to split the answer finer.

"I was at sixty-two when Maren crossed. I spent thirty-one levels over the next eight years working out what mattered and what didn't. Other sites. Other stories. Old Margins talk that the Hoarded treated like peasant superstition right up until they started stationing soldiers on it. Two years after Maren crossed, an old woman in the deep south told me the only useful thing anybody ever had. She'd seen three vanishings. She said the threshold held wider when the drop was bigger and when the spending around it piled up fast enough to push somebody through."

Kem nodded once. "Cascade."

"Yes."

He looked at her hands on the journal. "Someone with levels."

Vel's thumb moved along the journal spine. "The cascade needed someone who could—" She stopped. Kem was watching her, not angry, just waiting for whatever came next. "I went looking for somebody to use. Somebody with enough levels that the drop would hold the threshold open."

He didn't move but something behind his face shifted.

"I heard about a Hoarded lord's son who'd walked out with two hundred and eighty-six and a habit of burning whatever was in front of him." She kept her eyes on him. "I went north. I found you. I saw a road south."

Kem's eyes stayed on hers, which was harder than if he'd looked away.

"At the start," he said.

"At the start."

He rubbed a chip of mortar between finger and thumb until it powdered. "You keep saying that like it helps."

"It's true."

"It's not the question." He wiped the dust on his knee. "I know you used me first. I learned that on the road collapse. I learned it again when Thessa told me you were asking after defectors years before I existed to you. What I don't know is how long you kept doing it after you stopped being able to pretend I was just a number."

Vel let that hit full weight. Nothing outside the wall moved except the loose hinge on the appeal notice banging gently in the wind.

"A while," she said.

Kem laughed once without humor. Very quiet. "Useful."

"You wanted the real answer."

"I did."

"So listen to it the first time." Vel settled the journal flatter on her thighs. "You stopped being a number fast. Faster than I had any use for. You were soft and stupid and proud in all the wrong places, and then you kept doing things I couldn't fit back into the plan. Back with those scouts you spent yourself stupid and I wanted to shake you. You stitched Dren's arm with your hands shaking and I didn't know what to do with that. You burned ninety-eight in the Margins and every level of it made the Scour worse for me and still I couldn't tell you to stop. Not there. Not with people standing in front of you already half used up by the system I was asking you to survive."

Kem looked down. "So every decent thing I did was you watching the route get longer."

"Sometimes, yes." The word scraped going out. "Sometimes I watched you save somebody and thought about what it cost me. That is true. It stayed true after I hated it."

The admission sat between them. No clever shape to it. No excuse big enough to go over the top.

Outside, a horse called somewhere far enough away to sound ordinary.

"I've been asking for weeks," Kem said. "After Thessa. After the mechanism. Since the farmhouse, really. Every time you gave me one more useful piece and stopped right where it started to matter."

"Asking isn't the same as stopping."

He looked up sharply.

Vel met it. "You kept walking. I could keep the answer broken up. That's not noble. That's just what I did."

He breathed out through his nose. "At least that sounds like you."

"I didn't have a better version."

"No." He touched the journal cover once, not taking it, just putting a finger on the leather like he had to confirm it was an object and not a shrine. "Tell me the part with the Margins. Dren already did. I want yours."

Vel watched his hand withdraw.

"I'm doing the same thing," she said.

Kem said nothing.

"I spent my first level at seventeen because a girl was drowning and the Hoarded standing on the bank wouldn't—" She looked at Kem. His face had gone still. Her thumb pressed hard into the journal spine. "That's the version that makes me sound like I've always been the person who spends for strangers. Maren was. She'd walk into a place and spend because someone was hurt in front of her." Vel looked at the crack in the wall. "I went looking for someone to spend. That's different."

Wind moved through the broken wall. Dust settled on the journal page.

"I walked into the Margins and saw what the Ledger really looks like when it's a bill instead of a philosophy. Fallow at four. Labor compounds. Kids already counting what parts of themselves were worth debt. And I still had this private thing underneath it. This stupid huge personal thing."

Kem's voice was barely above breath. "You kept asking me not to waste levels because you needed them for her."

"Yes."

"You let me spend in the Margins because you couldn't say no in front of them."

"Yes."

"And you were angry when I did."

"Yes."

He rubbed a hand over his face and held it there a second. "That's filthy."

"Yes."

The old wordless rhythm of the field carried on outside. Blade in dirt. Lift. Drop.

Vel turned a page in the journal and found one of Maren's cramped later notes crowded into the margin of a water table sketch. The letters were nearly on top of each other, written in the dark or with numb fingers or both.

"She'd walk into a place and spend because someone was standing there hurt," Vel said. "I went looking for someone to spend." She shut the journal this time. "I don't know how to make those the same thing. I kept trying anyway."

Kem stared at the cover in her lap. "Dren thinks that's what the system does. Uses people. Counts what's left after."

"Dren's right."

"Are you sorry."

Vel could have said yes and meant part of it. She could have said no and meant part of that too. The problem with truth was that it kept coming in ugly pieces.

"I'm not sorry I found you," she said. "I'm not sorry I kept you alive long enough to get here. I'm not going to lie and say I wish I'd left you in the snow with your signet and your two hundred and eighty-six." The next part took longer. "I am sorry for every careful word I used to make it sound like that wasn't what I was doing."

Kem took that in with no expression at first. Then something in his face changed, not softening, not forgiveness. Just the registration of a thing finally said without padding.

"What if she's not there?" he asked.

Vel had rehearsed better answers than the true one. She let them go.

"Then I'll be at zero in a world without the Ledger," she said. "And that's still better than this one."

He watched her a long moment. Enough time for a rider to come back. Enough time for the heat in the hollow to settle into the bones.

"All right," he said at last.

"All right what."

"All right I'll keep walking." He shifted, braced a hand on the wall, but did not stand yet. "Not because it's fine. It isn't. You used me. Same road, same number, you'd do it again."

Vel thought of Maren in the collapsing room. Of Dren going north on one good arm. Of Kem in the Margins with his hands bloody from field medicine and his levels vanishing into strangers.

"Yes," she said.

"Good." He nodded once, almost to himself. "Then no more pieces. From here to the Scour I want the whole route. Distances, bad ground, what you think the Reserve has waiting, where you think the commander will want us. All of it before we spend another level or walk into another ditch."

Vel looked at him, then at the journal.

"All right," she said.

"I'm not forgiving you."

"I know."

"Good."

A fresh flare hit the relay mast to the south. Kem's head turned toward it before Vel's did. That too had changed.

"We should move when it stops," he said.

"Yes."

"You're sure they're gone."

"No."

He stood anyway.

This time, when they left cover, he didn't walk ahead out of anger or lag behind out of thought. He stayed beside her and made her talk.

They cut west first, because east was what the riders had just checked. A dry drainage run let them move bent over for nearly half an hour before it opened onto broken service ground again. Vel kept the journal out between them as they walked, one hand on the bottom edge so the wind would not take the pages.

"From the farmhouse," Kem said, keeping his voice low out of habit now, "if we'd gone straight."

"Two hard days on foot to the basin rim. Maybe a little less if the road held and nobody cared who saw us."

"And instead."

"Instead we burned the same two days staying off the obvious approach. Yesterday we made maybe six miles south in total. The rest was east, hiding, and waiting for riders to get bored somewhere else." Vel tapped a page with one finger. "This station." Then another. "Dead orchard line. Then the canal where we hid. The direct road is here. Basin here."

Kem leaned closer. "So we're east of the approach."

"Yes. About half a day. That helps if the commander expects desperate people to come straight at her. It hurts if she's been on the ground five years and already closed the side lanes."

"Which one do you think."

"I think five years is long enough to distrust easy answers."

He made a face. "Useful."

"You asked for all of it."

"I did." He looked back at the page. "Water."

"One communal well ahead. Maybe. If it hasn't been fouled or watched. After that a dead settlement west of the basin. Maren marked a cistern there. Could still hold. Could hold a body. Route notes were optimistic."

"I noticed."

Vel glanced at him. "You can read her hand now."

"I can read your hand pretending not to be hers." His mouth did a small unpleasant thing. "That came with time."

They took the communal well at a crawl. Not the direct path. Vel circled wide through a stand of dead trellis posts and watched the lip from fifty yards out before letting him go near it. Fresh hoof marks ringed the stones. The bucket rope had been cut and tossed aside. On the well house wall somebody had written FIELD ACCESS RESTRICTED BY ORDER OF SITE COMMAND in red paint that had not weathered yet.

Kem read it and went very still.

"Recent," he said.

"Yes."

He touched the cut rope with his boot. "They're pushing perimeter out from the basin."

"Yes."

"Because of us?"

"Because of the threshold. Because of whoever came before. Because they don't guard a hole in the ground out here for sport." Vel looked along the service line, checking the empty openings between walls. "Maybe because of us too."

Kem crouched, rewound the frayed end of the rope once around his hand without seeming to notice he was doing it. "Take me through the basin."

Vel did.

They moved from cover to cover while she talked. Not fast. Nothing about the day allowed fast. The land had opened and failed and been opened again by mining until it carried old intentions badly. Broken field walls. Transfer sheds without roofs. A stretch of trackbed leading nowhere. One administrative board sunk crooked in a drift, still bearing a list of RESOURCE YIELD DEFICITS in meticulous ink protected by a rusted glass pane. Hoarded care always outlasted Hoarded usefulness.

"The basin's wide," Vel said. "Too wide to rush. Wind-scoured. Layered rock. Very little cover once you break rim. The old gallery line comes in from the west under the upper shelves. Maren used it once for weather, not for soldiers. If it still goes through, it gives us a descent without being silhouetted the whole time."

"If it still goes through."

"Yes."

"And if it doesn't."

"Then we pick a shelf and pray the commander likes doctrine more than improvisation."

Kem snorted once. "You really do think highly of her."

"I think five years on one piece of ground turns a person into part of it."

He took that in, then nodded toward the journal. "What about the permanent station."

"Maren's notes were old even when I started using them. I know there was a camp. I don't know where it sits now." Vel turned a page while walking and immediately regretted it when the wind tried to fold the paper back on itself. She caught it before it tore. "Sable's warning gave me bodies, not layout. Commander. Two stationed. Maybe four if the new pair got there first. That means observation on the rim, water under control, and at least one line of retreat they trust more than the others."

Kem was quiet for a stretch. Then: "You didn't tell me the farmhouse was still two days out."

Vel kept her eyes on the ground ahead. "I didn't think it mattered once we left it."

"It mattered to me."

"I know."

"Did you know then."

"Probably."

He let that ugliness breathe a few steps. "I hate how often your honesty gets worse when it arrives late."

"That's fair."

They found shade at noon under the shell of a loading shed with one wall still standing and the floor sunk into an old mine void. Vel crouched on a beam that complained under her weight and laid the journal open across her knees. Kem stood until the heat beat the principle out of him and then sat opposite her with his back to a steel upright warm enough to sting through cloth.

The last pages were dense with notes. Maren's smaller slanted script crowded the margins around later additions in Vel's flatter hand. Distances marked, revised, crossed out. Small warnings. One crude map of the basin west face. Another of the gallery line as Maren had known it before the Hoarded started paying soldiers to care.

Kem pointed. "This mark."

"Collapsed storehouse. Good sightline north. Bad sightline south because the rim breaks there."

"Useful to us or to them."

"Depends who gets there first."

"This one."

"Dry wash. No cover except in the bottom. If we go that way, we're seen crossing the lip."

"Then why write it."

"Maren wrote everything. Some of it to use. Some of it so we'd know why not."

Kem traced the old gallery line without touching the page. "If the commander knows desperate people won't want the open rim, she'll watch the gallery."

"Yes."

"And if she knows that we know that."

"Then she might leave it thin and expect us to outsmart ourselves."

Kem looked up. "That is a hateful woman to meet on fourteen and eighteen."

"Yes."

He stared at the map a moment longer. Then, quieter: "Was Maren practical."

"Annoyingly."

"That's not an answer."

"She was practical the way some people are honest. As a habit. She'd put food by before weather changed. She'd count rope before a bridge. She knew exactly how much tincture was left in the box without opening the box." Vel looked down at the cramped margin note by the cistern mark. "She'd also give away the last clean bandage if somebody bled in front of her and then act surprised when I swore."

Kem rested his forearms on his knees. "That sounds expensive."

"It was."

"And you loved her anyway."

"Yes."

"That's at least consistent."

Vel almost smiled. Almost was all it got.

They waited through the worst of the afternoon under the shed because the road belonged to people with more patience than they had shade. When the sun started to tilt and the heat shifted from hammering down to pressing sideways, they moved again.

The dead settlement west of the basin showed itself first as upright lines against the glare. Then as houses without roofs. Then as a street grid the Hoarded had once bothered to straighten. Shaping-built walls were still standing in places where every timber member had gone. Windows opened onto rooms full of light and no life. At the entrance a notice board leaned crooked under a small drift of sand.

PRODUCTIVITY TIER ASSESSMENT  
ALL UNITS REPORT TO SECTOR ADMINISTRATION FOR ANNUAL OUTPUT REVIEW

Someone had written underneath it in a different, angrier hand: *They left. We're still here. The notice is still here too.*

Kem stopped long enough to read it twice.

"The tone is consistent," he said.

"With the Hoarded or with the dead."

"Both."

They used the settlement carefully. Too much geometry. Too many sightlines cut by straight streets and missing roofs. Vel kept them in the back runs where kitchens had once opened onto alleys, stepping around fallen cookstoves and the white powder of old plaster. One courtyard held the cistern Maren had marked. It was half full, silted, and alive with insects. Good enough. Kem lowered a cup on a length of scavenged cord while Vel watched the alley mouth. He handed her the first fill without comment. Their fingers touched on the metal and moved on.

That might have been the whole exchange if not for the sound that came next.

Not hoofbeats. Not close. A metal clack from the north street and then a voice, too far to make out words but near enough to collapse the air in her chest.

Vel caught Kem by the sleeve and pulled him backward into the cistern yard. He came without asking. They flattened behind the low retaining wall while sound moved through the empty houses: boot on stone, then another, then a scrape like somebody shifting a rifle sling or a tool against masonry.

Kem's lips were at her ear. "Reserve?"

"Probably."

"One?"

"At least one."

The steps stopped. Vel counted three breaths. Four. A dry laugh came from the street, cut off fast. Then the steps moved on again, crossing the next intersection instead of turning into their yard.

Kem did not look afraid in any theatrical way. He just looked very awake.

"That settles the water question," he murmured.

"Yes."

"And the perimeter."

"Yes."

They stayed down until the steps had gone fully and the next sound from the street was only wind pushing dust through a broken doorway.

When they rose, Kem did not wait for her to say move. He put the cup away, shouldered his pack, and followed her deeper into the settlement where walls still made shadows long enough to hide in.

By the time the sun dropped red against the far haze, they had found a room with half a roof and one doorframe that gave onto an alley instead of a street. Vel chose it because there was only one obvious angle of approach and because the wall opposite the opening still held the faded remains of a Hoarded safety notice, which meant nobody had bothered to strip the place for firewood in years. The notice read FALL-HAZARD LIABILITY TRANSFER ACCEPTED BY OCCUPANT SIGNATURE BELOW. The signature line was blank.

Kem set his pack down and stared at it.

"They really did have paperwork for the roof killing you."

"They had paperwork for everything."

He sat on the floor with his back to the wall and shut his eyes for a second. When he opened them, they went to the journal in her lap again.

"You were going to tell me at the Scour," he said. Not accusing this time. Just assembling the timetable.

"Yes."

"Because if I walked out then, the route was over."

"Yes."

He breathed out. "That's vile."

"Yes."

"And efficient."

"Yes."

He huffed a short laugh that had no amusement in it. "You are having an exceptionally honest day."

Vel leaned her head back against the wall. The stone held the day's heat and gave a little of it back. "Don't count on a second one."

Silence settled, but not the bad silence from before. This one had edges and shape. A thing two people could sit inside without pretending it was anything else.

After a while Kem said, "Tell me one thing that has nothing to do with thresholds."

Vel turned her head toward him. "What."

"About her. Maren. Something useless."

Vel looked at the journal. Outside the doorframe the alley had gone the color of bruised copper.

"She cheated at cards," she said.

Kem blinked. "That's not what I expected."

"She was terrible at hiding it. Thought the point of cheating was letting the other person know you respected them enough to make the game interesting."

"That's not cheating. That's a cry for help."

"It's why nobody played with her twice unless they liked her."

"Did you."

"I liked her before the cards."

"That was prudent."

"No."

The smile went away, but it left the room changed.

Night came hard once it started. Somewhere beyond the west wall a loose sheet of metal knocked itself slowly against stone. Wind worked the empty settlement like it was trying to remember the people. Vel kept the journal closed after that. There was no more route knowledge in it that mattered tonight. Only Maren's hand and hers sharing old paper like they still shared anything.

Kem slid down farther until he was almost lying on the floor. "If the commander has pushed patrols this far, does that mean she knows we're coming."

Vel considered before answering. "It means she knows somebody always comes, eventually. Maybe she knows about us. Maybe she just knows what the Scour is worth and doesn't believe in quiet weeks."

He folded an arm over his eyes. "I hate how often your best answer is maybe."

"Mine too."

A pause.

"Dren was right," Kem said.

"About what."

"That other doors would open if somebody could afford them." He lowered his arm and looked at the half roof above them. "Mira. The healer. The whole awful arithmetic of it." He turned his head on the rolled pack he'd made into a pillow. "He goes north with one bad arm and one hundred and twenty and that's the sensible journey. We go south because neither of us can leave a thing alone."

Vel listened to the wind move through the empty street.

"Yes," she said.

He let that answer stand. Then: "I still would have spent in the Margins."

"I know."

"I'm saying it because I don't want memory cleaning it up later."

"I'd have done it anyway." He shut the eye again. "Even if you'd told me clean. Maybe especially then. I don't know what that says about me."

"That you're not Hoarded enough to watch a child drown and call it prudence."

"That sounded almost kind."

"Don't get used to it."

"Too late."

They let the night thicken around that. No fire. No talking once full dark settled. Once, far to the south, a brief pale flash climbed the rim line and vanished. Signal plate or lantern shutter or moon on metal. Vel could not tell. She did not like not being able to tell.

Fourteen hummed at the edge of her awareness. Eighteen hummed at his. Thirty-two lay between them and a permanent garrison at the Scour. Dren was somewhere north with a ruined arm, one hundred and twenty levels, and an eight-year-old girl waiting for a healer to make her legs hold. Vel kept seeing his face when he'd said *I've been picking her up off the kitchen floor since spring.* She kept seeing Maren in the falling room. She kept seeing Kem in the hollow under the wall, finally asking the question with no place left to set it down unfinished.

Sometime after midnight Kem spoke into the dark without opening his eyes.

"No more surprises," he said.

Vel listened to the wind for a beat before answering.

"I'll do what I can," she said.

It wasn't a promise. He knew that. So did she. But in the room with the blank liability form on the wall and the journal between them, it was the straightest line she had left.
