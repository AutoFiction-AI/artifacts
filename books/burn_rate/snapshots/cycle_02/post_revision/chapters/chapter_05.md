# Chapter 5

It had been two days since the outriders. Vel had put those two days to work. She took them off the main south track before sunset and kept them off it: through thigh-deep water that made Kem hiss through his teeth, over a scree shoulder where even goats would think twice, under deadfall so tangled he had to crawl on one elbow to keep from cracking the rib the hillside had given him. The first night they slept under an uprooted cedar with wet dirt against their backs and no heat at all. At dawn she sent him back half a mile to brush away the clean lordling tread he'd left in mud, then made him cross the stream barefoot because boots held prints in the leather. By midday the second day both of them smelled like cold water, leaf rot, and the iron note of his reopened palm.

Vel hadn't spent a point. She still had twenty-eight on the Ledger, enough if the Hoarded needed time to sort themselves out, enough if the next team still had to search instead of following a clean trail south. Kem hadn't spent either. That should have reassured her more than it did. He'd obeyed. He'd kept his hands off the deep end. But every so often the grin from the ruined clearing came back over his face before shame drove it off again, and she could not tell which one would win if he got scared the wrong way.

The cost had come from other places. His left heel was rubbed raw through the secondhand boot he'd traded into after abandoning the corridor pair. His cracked rib had stiffened from two cold nights and was punishing every breath he didn't bargain around. He'd torn the split in his palm open three times on stone and thorn because his body still trusted walls more than ground. Vel's shoulders felt flayed where both packs had ridden the first day, and the grouse she'd snared that morning was the last real food between them and whatever the next three days offered. If they kept moving through another dark stretch without sleep or something hot in their stomachs, he'd start making stupid choices just to make the body stop hurting.

So when the stream bent under hornbeam and cut a narrow shelf of dry ground above its own bank, she stopped and made herself look hard before she let wanting become decision. The place gave her water, deadfall, and a strip of stones on both approaches where tracks would break up. Enough root and brush hid a mean low blaze if she kept it short. The bank below narrowed into a throat of rock. Anyone splashing up it would make noise long before they reached camp. The ridge above was too steep for horses and too exposed for men who'd been trained to think mud was an administrative inconvenience. It was barely safe enough.

It wasn't safe. It was safe enough to risk because Kem's stride had shortened to the careful, held-together walk of a man one bad step from locking up entirely.

"Here," she said. "Camp."

Kem let his pack slide down one shoulder at a time. Halfway through, the rib caught him. He froze with his teeth bared, waited out the white spike of it, then lowered the load so carefully it looked ceremonial.

"Sit," she said. Then, before he could take that as permission, "No. Don't. If you lock up now, you'll stay locked."

"I'm not sure I have a vote."

"You don't. First wipe our approach. Then shelter. Then heat. Then food. You're doing all of it."

She handed him a willow switch and pointed down the shelf. "Start at the bend and work back. Use the stones when you can. If you kick loose dirt, smooth it."

He looked at the bank, then at her. "This feels obsessive."

"It keeps us from getting found."

"It's a stream shelf. No sane man would want it."

"No sane man sent outriders after us either. Go."

He went. Bad temper made him fast for twelve steps. Pain took over after that. He moved down to the bend with the switch dragging in the gravel, stopped twice to press the heel of his hand into his side, then kept going because she'd ordered it and because the order was easier to take than pity. By the time he came back, sweat had silvered the line above his lip and the reopened cut in his palm had striped the willow red in two places.

"Happy?" he asked.

"No."

"Comforting."

"Drop the branch. You're setting shelter."

He let out a breath through his nose, glanced at her as if checking whether this particular insult was negotiable, then crouched. The motion cost him enough that annoyance gave way to the tired compliance she'd started trusting more than apology. Standing again was worse. That was good. The body taught faster than speech and lied less.

She handed him the rolled canvas, cord, and two forked stakes she'd cut that morning.

"Between those roots," she said. "Back edge low. Opening away from the stream."

He looked at the canvas, then at the ground she meant him to claim. "This is a home."

"It's an argument with weather. Start."

He did, and he did it badly.

The first line sagged because he'd tied the knot as if elegance mattered. The second pulled free because he drove the stake into soft bank instead of gravel. When he bent to reset it, the rib made him stop halfway and breathe with his eyes shut. Vel watched without moving. If she stepped in now, he'd learn the wrong thing again. She'd already taught him enough wrong lessons just by being the person who knew how to fix what he broke.

He got the ridge line up on the third try. The back edge still listed.

"Too high," she said.

"If I lower it more, I won't fit."

"Then you'll sleep curled. Lower it."

The rope went still in his hands. Three days ago he would've argued. Now the protest came out as one hard tug on the knot, then he pulled the whole thing lower. That counted as improvement.

By the time the lean-to was more or less standing, sweat had pasted his shirt to the groove of his spine despite the cooling air. His hands shook when he worked the last knot tight. He hid the tremor by reaching for another length of cord he did not need.

"Enough," Vel said.

"It doesn't look stable."

"It looks like it'll still be there when you wake up. That's stable enough."

He stepped back. One corner drooped. The opening was narrow enough that he'd have to crawl in sideways if the rib was still bad by night. It would probably keep dew off the blankets. It would also offend every instinct he'd inherited from generations of people who thought a servant should absorb all visible mistakes before breakfast.

He saw her reading that on him and made an unhappy sound.

"I know," he said. "I know what face I'm making."

"Do you."

"It's the one you're trying to beat out of me."

"I'm not beating anything out of you. I'm keeping us alive."

"By making me tie ropes until I understand class relations?"

Vel bent, picked up the fallen stake, and slapped it lightly against his shoulder. "By making you learn what your hands are for."

He took that and looked away toward the stream. Below them the water ran shallow over stones, clear enough that the last light kept catching silver off its skin. His own hands flexed once at his sides. It wasn't just pain. She saw recognition in it, the itch.

"There," Vel said quietly. "That's the part I care about."

He kept looking at the stream. "What part."

"The one where you're deciding whether a level would be easier."

His thumb found the split in his palm and pressed at the edge of it. "It would be."

"Yeah."

"And this is punishment."

"This is calibration."

He laughed once without humor. "Those are not far apart."

She did not answer. Silence had become one of the few tools that made him fill the space honestly.

It worked.

"I keep thinking about the hillside," he said. "Not the scouts. The pulse. The moment before it went wrong. It was in my hands and it felt..." He stopped, angry already at the direction of his own sentence. "It felt clean. That's disgusting. I know it is."

Vel said nothing.

"I saw what I did after." His gaze moved to the split in his palm as if the memory were written there. "The woman with the arm. The tracker on the ground. You yelling at me. I know all of that. I was sick when I looked back at it and I was still..." He swallowed. "Still wanting it again."

That landed harder because he had not shaped it to land. He just let it sit between them, ugly and alive.

"That's why you're watching my hands," he said.

"Yes."

He nodded once. There was no argument in it, only the flatness of being correctly read.

"All right," he said. "Keep watching."

Vel took the canvas bag off her shoulder and set it by the flat rock. It smelled of waxed cloth, damp tinder, and old smoke. Kem's attention moved there at once.

He tried for a smile and lost it halfway through. "Heat next."

"Heat next."

"I know how to do this in a room."

"You know how to point at a grate while someone else carries the bad part."

"That is unkindly accurate."

"Use it."

The wrap held flint, striker, char cloth, and the tinder she'd kept dry under the cooking pot. He picked them up like evidence against his upbringing. She built the first nest where he could see, using dried grass, shredded birch bark from a deadfall, and twigs no thicker than his little finger. Her own hands wanted to take the kit back and finish it before he bled on everything. She made them stay at her sides.

"Char cloth on the edge," she said. "Strike down. When it catches, fold it in and breathe small. You're warming your fingers, not blowing a trumpet."

"That was almost encouraging."

"It wasn't."

The first strike threw nothing because the angle was wrong. On the second, steel skipped and shaved the blister on his thumb instead of the flint. Blood welled bright against skin made for paper, porcelain, signet wax, not this. He sucked air through his teeth. She made herself watch the mistake land. If she took the striker now, he'd remember rescue instead of angle.

"Shit."

For half a beat his whole body leaned toward the easier answer. A Flame Charm would have cost him almost nothing at two-forty-five. It was less than the skin he'd already lost. The option moved through him like remembered music. So did her stare. He reset the cloth with his good fingers and tried again.

A spark flashed and died.

"Closer," she said.

He missed again. Then again. He swore at the striker, at the flint, at the whole stupid subject of combustion. On the next pass the cloth took. The corner glowed ember-red. His breath caught. For a beat he looked almost offended that the flint had finally agreed with him, and that nearly pulled a laugh out of her.

"Now the nest," she said. "Careful."

By the time he got onto his haunches the rib had him breathing through his nose in thin controlled pulls. He tucked the char into the grass and blew too hard. The glow flared and vanished.

"It was there."

"It was."

"I had it."

"You did."

His shoulders dropped. For a second she thought he'd ask whether half a level was a smaller embarrassment than learning. Instead he built another nest. It was looser this time and better for it. He did not look at her while he worked. His next breath came smaller. Smoke thickened between his hands, then a thread of flame found the bark and climbed. Relief moved through her before she could stop it. Fire meant food, less shaking, a better chance he'd sleep instead of lying awake with the rib. She disliked how personal that relief felt.

Kem made a soft astonished sound, almost private. It wasn't triumph. It sounded more like disbelief that the world would answer him slowly and still answer at all.

He fed it kindling too fast, nearly choking it, corrected, and got a usable bed of coals going with her voice at his shoulder and nowhere else.

"Water," Vel said. "Then the bird."

His head came up. "No."

"Yes."

"I have just now achieved one successful tiny domestic miracle."

"You'll waste it if you stop."

His stare landed on the canvas bag where the grouse waited wrapped in dock leaves. "I don't think that's how miracles work."

"Good thing this isn't one."

She sent him to fill the pot first. That bought his hands time to steady and gave the coals time to settle. While he knelt at the stream, dipping and lifting with the awkwardness of a man raised among basins that arrived full, Vel checked the bank again. There were no bent fern stems above the shelf, no fresh dung, and no stone rolled recently enough to show a clean underside. The sky over the ridge was paling toward evening. If searchers were close, they'd choose easier ground than this. If they were smart, they'd wait for dawn and look for smoke.

Which meant the risk wasn't gone. It was only delayed.

When he came back with the pot balanced in both hands, he set it too near the coals and hissed as steam dampened his burn. She nudged it with her boot into the right position. His glance at her was brief, automatic, already asking whether that counted as help he should not have taken.

"It counts as me not wanting dirt in the water," she said.

"Comforting distinction."

The grouse hung from his fingers gray-feathered and cold, its neck at the wrong angle from where she'd wrung it that morning. He held it farther from himself than was practical, as if modesty might survive another thirty seconds. Watching him hesitate over it took longer than killing it had.

"What's first?" he asked.

"Pluck it."

A long time passed.

Feathers came away in clumps or single stubborn barbs. He pulled too lightly, then too hard, stripping skin along the breast until wet pink showed through. Down pasted itself to the blood on his split palm. Every few minutes he straightened halfway, pressed one hand to the rib, waited out the grinding pain, and bent back down. Every pause pulled at the part of her that wanted food now and less mess later. She let that part go hungry.

She let him do it badly. There was no other way to teach honest work except to let it stay ugly long enough to become real.

"Now open it."

"I don't know where."

She touched the blade-tip to the line below the breastbone. "Short cut. Keep shallow. If you burst the guts, you're eating the mistake too."

He looked at the bird, at the knife, at her. "That feels pedagogically loaded."

"It is."

He punctured the intestines anyway.

The stink hit and stripped the joke out of him. His shoulders locked. One swallow worked through his throat. Vel smelled it too, rank and immediate, and had to drag her own dinner-hunger back under control. Then the knife went back in. Guided by the line she'd shown him and his own refusal to quit now that quitting would look like exactly what he feared, he opened the cavity, hooked his fingers inside, and drew the wet rope of the bird's insides onto the rock beside him.

"Everyone does this?" he asked, not looking up.

"Everyone who eats. Or somebody does it for them."

That landed. He went still over the bird, not from the rib this time. When he started again, he rinsed the cavity with both hands, slower than before.

"Somebody did it for me for twenty-six years," he said. "Every bird, every fish, every plate lifted away while it was still warm enough that I never had to think about what happened before."

"Yeah."

He rinsed the cavity in stream water, jaw tight. A green hazel switch became the spit. The branch sagged when he set it over the coals. He adjusted it wrong. Fat began to run at once. Residual feathers crisped. Within minutes one side was blackened and the top stayed offensively raw.

"Turn it," Vel said.

He did. The other side darkened faster than the first. Reaching farther across the heat pinched the rib hard enough to make him stop and brace one hand on his thigh. On the next try he lifted too fast, the spit rolled, and the bird dropped straight into the embers.

"God. Damn it."

He snatched for it and planted the heel of his hand in a coal. This time the swear came out loud enough to shake a bird from the brush.

Vel knocked the grouse back onto the stick with another branch before he could make the burn worse. A fresh blister was already swelling beside the older ones, clear fluid beneath soot and blood. Enough. Burned hands stopped teaching and started making people stupid.

"It's ruined," he said.

"It's dinner."

"But look at it."

The bird listed over the glow, black on two sides, ash in the skin folds, one pale strip still insultingly uncooked.

"I'm looking."

"Nobody would serve this."

"I'm not asking anybody."

She moved his hands into the right places, showed him how high to prop the spit and how slow to turn. She'd given him as much failure as hunger could afford. Under that much supervision the third side finally cooked through. It was done enough, anyway. She cut it down onto the flat rock between them and salted the worst of the shame with the last pinch from her packet.

By the time they ate, full dark had settled through the hornbeam branches. The stream ran behind them. The little fire in front of them had dropped to a red bed with three thin tongues of light. No voices moved in the trees.

The bird tasted of ash, smoke, and punishment. Vel cut away the worst of the blackened skin with her knife and ate what was left. Across from her, Kem chewed without complaint on meat his blistered hands had made badly and honestly. They drank hot stream water that tasted of iron and leaves and called it enough.

For a while the only sounds were chewing, the water, and the small settling noises of wood giving itself up to heat.

"In the Vault," Kem said at last, "meals arrived as if plates grew them. Bread if someone had decided bread matched the rest of the arrangement. Wine already breathing in the glass. Garnish placed by a person I never saw and therefore did not have to imagine."

"You've imagined one now."

He looked down at the carcass between them. "Apparently by dropping it into ash and then burning my hand to rescue it."

"Still counts."

A small breath escaped through his nose. The line of his shoulders loosened by a finger's width and stayed there.

Vel sat with her knees up and the knife across them. Firelight caught on the new burns striping his fingers, on the half-dried blood, on the thin grim concentration he hadn't managed to relax out of himself even while eating. He'd spent the whole day hurting in ways he could have bought his way out of and, for once, had not.

"Can I ask you something?" he said.

"You can ask."

"Why today."

She looked at him.

"Not why you started," he said quickly. "I know you've already decided that's not mine. I mean why this. The shelter, the bird, the whole lesson. You could've kept walking and insulted me tomorrow."

"I considered it."

"I gathered that."

She stripped a curl of bark from the stick in her hand. "Because tomorrow doesn't help if your hands reach before your head does."

He let out a breath that turned into something like a laugh and failed. "So we're back there."

"We never left."

He nodded. The honesty of that seemed to ease him more than a softer answer could have.

"I thought if I didn't spend for two days it'd stop," he said. "The wanting."

"Did it?"

"No." He pulled at a shred of meat with his teeth, swallowed, and stared at the bones in his hand. "It changed shape. It's less the blast now. More the thought that I could fix everything wrong about my body in half a second if you'd let me. The rib. The hands. The walking. I keep thinking about how small that cost would be compared to what I already lost."

"That's how it starts feeling cheap."

"Isn't it cheap for me?"

Vel looked at him until he met her eyes.

"No," she said. "It only feels cheap because you've got enough left to fool yourself with."

He absorbed that without flinching. Maybe he was too tired to flinch. Maybe he knew it was right.

"My father would've called that excellent moral instruction," he said. "Delivered with inadequate bedside manner."

"I wasn't hired for manners."

"No. You were hired for contempt, apparently."

"That part's free."

A breath of laughter escaped him this time and stayed. The sound was small, brief, and real. Then the quiet came back.

He turned one of the bones in his fingers. "I was ashamed after the fight."

"I know."

"But it wasn't only shame." He rubbed his thumb along the snapped end of the bone until it clicked. "I don't want you thinking I've solved anything because I bled on a bird. I was sick about what happened and I was still..." He looked out into the dark beyond the shelf. "Still half in love with it."

Vel did not look away. If she softened now, he'd think she was sparing him. If she pressed wrong, he'd close.

"That's the honest part," she said.

"It isn't a good part."

"No. Still honest."

The bone clicked again between his fingers. "Does that ever stop."

She thought about the first time a level answered under her skin. About the way power offered itself as clean arithmetic while consequence came later, muddier, harder to count.

"No," she said. "You just get better at hearing what comes after it."

He nodded slowly. "That seems worse than what I was hoping for."

"It is worse than what you were hoping for."

Another strip of grouse disappeared. He ate because the body wanted food even while the mind kept circling uglier things. Vel respected that more than speech.

They sat until the meat was mostly gone and the water in the pot had cooled enough to stop steaming. Night tightened around the bank. Somewhere across the stream something small pushed through brush and kept moving. It wasn't a boot. It was too light.

Kem wiped his fingers on the grass and spoke so quietly she almost missed the start of it.

"I keep meaning to ask about your first time."

She let the question sit. He did not hurry to fill it.

"Not the route," he said. "I know you won't — that's fine. The spending. When you started." He picked at a strip of bark beside him. "You don't talk about it the way people do when they hate something. But you don't sound like — I can't work out which direction you come at it from."

He'd asked before and she'd shut him down. He knew better now than to ask like he was owed it.

Vel dragged a strip of bark through the dirt with her thumbnail. The little fire had dropped low enough that the light coming off it turned every ridge of his knuckles ocher and made the cuts on his palms look deeper than they were.

"Avermouth," she said. Then, because the word didn't mean anything alone: "I was seventeen. My mother sold cloth at the canal market."

She hadn't planned to say that much. But he was sitting there with his burned hands and his honestly cooked bird, and the asking had been careful enough.

"A girl went off the towpath rail into the water. Eight, maybe nine. Rotten wood — everybody knew it was rotten, everybody complained, nobody fixed it. She went through and the current took her under the bridge."

"In spring?"

"Snowmelt still coming down. She came up once and that was it."

Vel watched the bark split under her thumb.

"Forty people standing there. Hoarded coats. Spent traders. You could see them doing the math. Three levels, maybe four, for a pull. Wet clothes. One man with a House seal on his coat close enough the splash hit his boots." She snapped the bark. "Nobody moved."

"Were you scared?"

She looked at him. That wasn't the question she'd been ready for.

"I don't remember scared," she said. "I was seventeen and full and angry. I went in." She let the stick drop. "Missed her on the first grab. Water was faster than I thought. Spun us both under the bridge. I spent one level on a grip — didn't know the name for it, just knew I needed a hand to stick. Got one hand on the piling and one in the back of her dress and held on till two men came in far enough to drag us out."

"You were seventeen," he said, quieter.

"She cried the whole time they wrapped her up. Good sign."

"And you."

"Two ninety-nine. Dripping canal water over my mother's stock. Nobody thanked me. One woman asked if I'd ruined the cloth."

Kem's jaw worked once in the low light.

"What did your mother say?"

Vel hadn't thought about that in years. "She said, 'Get out of the street before you fall over.' Cleaned my hands when we got home. Asked if the girl was breathing when I let go. I said yes. She said good."

He made a sound under his breath. "Just good?"

"That was the whole thing."

The fire shifted. A tongue of flame climbed and failed.

"Two ninety-nine after the canal," Vel said. "Then two ninety-eight. Then the next one. Forty-one years of that."

Kem sat with the number.

"The Hoarded would call it waste," he said at last.

"They would."

"My father would've said it cleaner than that. Back when he could still finish the sentence all the way to the end."

"Tell me about him."

Ash striped the backs of his hands. He turned them over between his knees.

"He was loud," Kem said. "Not — it wasn't noise. He was just there. In every room. He argued about everything. Trade routes, seating, whether Breve was an idiot or just dedicated to the appearance of it. He'd correct your arithmetic at dinner and then make you defend the answer." He picked at the soot on his knuckle. "That was the part that was alive."

"When did it change?"

"It wasn't one day." He looked at the fire. "Pieces went missing. Small ones first. He stopped finishing jokes. He'd ask something and forget he'd asked before anyone could answer. Someone would say his name and there'd be this — " He waved vaguely beside his head. "He'd come back from it. Give you one sentence that sounded fine if you didn't know he'd been gone."

"The Hoarded liked that."

"They needed it to be something. Uncle Aldris called it balance. The physician — I can't remember if it was deepening discipline or refined preservation. One of those. He said it while my father was holding a cup he'd forgotten to drink from." Kem gave a short ugly sound that wasn't quite a laugh. "Ten minutes with the cup. And there was a word for it."

"And your mother?"

He started to answer and stopped. His throat jumped once, as if the words had gone down wrong.

"She never used the clean names," he said. "She just — adjusted. Moved a plate before his hand had to search for it. Repeated things. If she straightened his sleeve after he spilled it looked like household order, not — " He broke off. "If anyone had said what was happening out loud, they'd have had to say what all of it was. So nobody did."

Vel listened to the stream and his breathing.

"I kept thinking it would stop where it was," he said. "Not get better. Just stop. Let him stay at the level of damage I'd already — " He rubbed the bridge of his nose hard. "That's a disgusting way to put it."

"It's an honest one."

"Yeah." He let his hand drop. "Last Seventhday before I left. He sat through half the Rite staring at the centerpiece. The cup tilted and my mother straightened it without looking at me. Uncle Aldris smiled."

Vel shifted the stick in the coals. "How many years of that?"

"Fifteen. Give or take."

"And you stayed."

"Yeah." The word came out flat. "I couldn't do one more. I didn't have a theory about it. I just couldn't sit there and smile with the rest of them."

"He let you go?"

Kem's hands went still between his knees. "My father wasn't — he didn't see it. My mother said my name once. Just once. I didn't turn around."

For a while neither of them spoke. Kem rubbed ash off one knuckle with his thumb and only smeared it wider.

"Can he still talk?" Vel asked.

"Some days. Fewer now. The words come out right but the meaning's somewhere else." He stared into the coals. "His Ledger's still full."

The words sat there.

Vel nudged a stick deeper into the coals and waited until the tip of it glowed.

"Eat," she said.

"I am."

"More."

So he did. The grouse was cold now, the fat turned white along the charred skin. When the last of it was gone, he stacked the bones in a neat little line and wiped his hands on the grass.

"That was the worst meal I've ever had," he said.

"Yeah."

"I made it."

"You did."

The line of bones held his attention for a while. Smoke had dried into his hair. Ash streaked one cheek. Under the dirt and soreness and fatigue, something in him had gone quieter.

"Thank you," he said. "For making me do it. All of it."

"Tomorrow you'll do it again."

He looked over. "All of it?"

"If we need it. And you're taking the heavy pack."

He took a careful breath and glanced toward his side before looking back at her. "How heavy."

"Thirty-five."

Silence held for a beat.

"You've been carrying thirty-five pounds through this country with twenty-eight levels left."

"Yes."

His eyes moved to her bedroll, her pack, the scarred hands in her lap, as if he were trying to assemble the story she'd told and the body sitting across from him into the same person.

"All right," he said. "I'll take it."

Vel banked the coals and laid the heavier pack beside his blanket so he couldn't miss it in the morning. His went close to the fire. Hers went between him and the trail.

The highlands stayed quiet.

From her blanket she could hear him settle by stages, each small adjustment negotiated around the rib. The stream kept running in the dark. Twenty-eight hummed low and steady at the back of her mind. In the morning he'd build the fire himself, shoulder the wrong pack, and hurt through it without buying the easy answer.

For tonight, that was enough.
