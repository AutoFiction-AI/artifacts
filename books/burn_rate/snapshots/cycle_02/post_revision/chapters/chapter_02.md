# Chapter 2

The morning Kem Aldraste left the Vault, the Conservatory Rite began, as it always did, with the presentation of objects.

This week the Aldraste household had supplied the table: a cracked ceramic bowl that a single Mending Charm could make whole, a bundle of cut flowers already browning at their edges, an unlit oil lamp with a full reservoir, and a small mechanical clock whose mainspring had wound down three hours ago. The objects sat on a stone pedestal at the center of the Conservatory hall, four mundane invitations to act under excellent lighting so every guest could appreciate them not being addressed.

Kem Aldraste sat in the third row of the Aldraste section and watched a petal fall from the bouquet.

Around him, sixty-three Hoarded lords and ladies held the same practiced stillness they brought to the Conservatory every week. The hall was built for it. High ceilings stretched sound thin. Stone walls absorbed noise. Benches cut from the same quarry ensured no seat creaked differently from another. Even the air felt managed, still and temperate, never warm enough to invite fidgeting and never cool enough to prompt a shiver. The whole place had been built for inaction.

The Rite had no officiant. No one spoke. That was the point. For two hours every week, the great families of the Vault sat near objects that could be improved, repaired, revived, or corrected with a single ability use and let them go on failing under the lamps. The cracked bowl showed its white break. Oil waited in the lamp's belly. The flowers sagged lower. The clock kept its hands at the same three positions. Nobody moved.

Two weeks ago the Ventrathi family had contributed a wounded songbird. It had chirped weakly for the first ninety minutes and then gone quiet in a way that was distinct from sleep. Afterward the Ventrathi matriarch received low-voiced praise over supper, the kind usually reserved for an ambitious centerpiece or a difficult wine. A healing charm for something that small would have cost a single level, one three-hundredth of the capacity every person in this room carried, and sixty-odd people had sat in cushioned silence while it died.

Kem had attended the Conservatory Rite every week for twenty-six years. Missing sessions had happened perhaps a dozen times: illness twice, a diplomatic reception once, and once when he was nine and hid in the kitchens until a servant found him and carried him back. That servant lost a level for the exertion. She had been at two hundred and ninety-eight, doing acceptable work. After that she was at two hundred and ninety-seven, which was not a meaningful difference in any functional sense but mattered enormously in the Vault's social accounting, where the gap between three hundred and anything less than three hundred was the gap between person and instrument.

Kem's mother sat in the row ahead, at the end nearest the aisle. Lady Aldraste's posture was precise without being rigid, a calibration she had maintained for decades, her shoulders in a line that communicated authority rather than effort. Dark hair was pinned with small silver clasps in the formal Hoarded style. Her robe, the color of good iron, had been pressed that morning by servants who did not have the option of being wrinkled. She was watching the pedestal, or appeared to be. Since his teenage years, Kem had understood that his mother watched everything: the other families' composure, the politics of who fidgeted and who did not, the slow rearrangement of social capital that happened in this room under cover of stillness. She missed very little and mentioned less.

There was also the thing she did with Lord Aldraste's collar. Every week, sometime in the first half hour, her hand went out without looking and adjusted the fabric at his neck, a fold gone wrong, a clasp that had shifted, something small and particular. She did it with the unhurried precision of a person who had been tending this man's appearance for thirty years, maintaining the surface of something whose interior she could not repair. Kem did not know whether the other families noticed. Kem noticed every time.

His father sat at the head of the Aldraste section. Lord Aldraste's posture was perfect, then beyond perfect, the kind of composure that had outlasted the person who cultivated it and now ran on memory alone. Hands rested on his thighs in a symmetry that looked arranged because it had been. His robes were immaculate, the collar recently adjusted by his wife, his face set in an expression that had once been dignity and was now simply the shape his face held when no one directed it.

Open eyes stared at nothing. Vacancy stared back through them. Kem had watched his father's attention thin for years, like cloth left too long in the sun, until what remained was structure without thread. Lord Aldraste at sixty-one still carried the same three hundred levels he had been born with, every point of potential intact, the full reservoir never touched for any purpose, and the man carrying that reservoir was dissolving by increments so small most of the family had stopped registering the change. The old measured precision was gone. Speech came slower now, with the stop-and-start of a mechanism shedding teeth. Pauses opened mid-phrase now, and what came after them did not always connect to what came before. Servants guided him to meals. His valet dressed him and arranged his limbs in the morning. In the Conservatory he sat with the flawless composure of a man who had spent six decades perfecting the posture and would probably maintain it for the remaining years of his body's function, regardless of whether the mind behind it continued to attend.

The Hoarded called this Stillness. They lowered their voices around the word. Three nights earlier an aunt had praised Lord Aldraste's composure over poached pears while his valet, standing behind the chair, quietly put his right hand back on the armrest.

Kem watched the clock on the pedestal not tick.

For a long time now, he had been watching his father disappear. It had not always been like this. There were years, and Kem could not have named the exact point when they ended, when Lord Aldraste had been present, sharp even. He had run the House's affairs, negotiated alliances, spoken with a precision the other families respected. Rare laughter had surfaced at things that were actually funny. Once, when Kem was twelve, his father had set a hand on his shoulder and said something about patience. The sentence was gone. The weight of the hand remained.

That person was gone. Kem searched his father's face for some sign of interior life, a reaction to the falling petal, a flicker toward the unlit lamp, anything that suggested machinery still ran behind the blank composure, and found nothing. The Ledger read three hundred. The man read empty.

The second petal fell.

Kem stood up.

His legs moved before the rest of him caught up. Then the reason hit. If he sat back down, today would become next week and next year, and one day his mother would be straightening his collar while there was nobody left behind his eyes either. Kem stayed standing.

His mother said his name.

"Kem."

In her voice, his name became an acknowledgment, spare and exact. It stayed steady with the same composure that held everything else in place, and under it sat something she would never show this room. She did not stand. She did not reach for him. Her eyes, entirely present unlike her husband's, fixed on him, and she said his name once and let the Conservatory's silence close around it.

Kem walked out.

Footsteps on the stone floor became the loudest sound the Conservatory had heard in weeks. Each step was a small detonation of decorum: heel striking stone, robe whispering, air shifting. He walked past the Drassic section, the Ventrathi section, and the minor families in the back rows who were already, he understood, rehearsing how they would describe this to the people who had missed it. Past them all, Kem kept going.

The Conservatory doors were shut. Large, ornamental panels carried carvings of the founding of the Vault, a history of deliberate inaction rendered in hardwood by artisans who had been paid in marks and spent levels they would never recover to produce a monument to people who spent nothing. The doors were designed to be opened from the outside by servants, because the Hoarded did not open their own doors.

At twenty-six, Kem had never opened a door for himself. Brass handles were polished weekly. The mechanism, in principle, was simple: pull or push, depending on hinge direction, and wood moved. Twenty-six years in the Vault meant every door had been opened for him, closed behind him, held at the appropriate angle for the appropriate duration by people whose job it was to manage doors so that men like Kem never had to think about them.

His hand closed on the handle. Something deeper answered first: a reflex he had not known he possessed, an instinct that reached for the three hundred levels sitting untouched at the edge of his awareness, the background hum of the Ledger, the number always there and always full.

A Projection Shield deployed in the corridor with a sound like the air splitting. Force erupted outward from Kem's extended hand, rippling, crackling, so far in excess of the task that the excess became its own statement. He had not intended one, but the room heard it anyway. The Shield hit the Conservatory doors and blew both off their hinges. One panel slammed sideways into the corridor wall and cratered the plaster. The other went tumbling end over end down the hallway and came to rest against a decorative column twenty meters away with a sound that suggested the column had opinions about the impact.

Stone dust drifted in the sudden stillness.

His arm buzzed from fingertips to shoulder, not pain exactly but a clean internal emptiness, as if something had been siphoned through the bones. His heart was pounding. He had expected ability use, if it ever happened, to feel controlled and ceremonial. Instead it felt like sneezing with a siege engine.

Kem stared at the wreckage. Both brass handles remained attached. One had bent.

The Ledger had always been background, a quiet awareness of capacity sitting full and ready at the edge of his mind. Now he checked it.

Two hundred and ninety-seven.

Opening a door had cost three levels when a brass handle and a human arm could have managed it for free.

Behind him the Conservatory stayed silent, but the silence had changed. Discipline had gone out of it. Shock had moved in. Sixty-three people had just watched an ability deployed in a room where abilities had not been used in living memory. Their attention pressed between his shoulder blades. Without looking back, Kem stepped through the ruined doorway and went into the Vault's corridors.

---

The corridors were built for people who did not hurry. Wide stretches of polished stone ran ahead. Ability-sustained lamps lined the walls, each one another yearly level taken from a servant. Portraits of Hoarded lords hung in gilt frames, painted by Spent Town artists whose names Kem had never learned. The carpet runner was thick enough to erase footsteps entirely. Gold thread in the border caught the light. Kem had walked these halls all his life without asking what it cost to keep them shining. Now every surface looked paid for.

Already his legs complained. The longest walk he normally took inside the Vault was the route from his chambers to the Conservatory, perhaps eighty meters, with a pause halfway in the gallery where a bench invited rest. He had already gone farther than that. The burn in his calves had progressed from complaint to argument. A blister was starting on his right heel. The shoes were handmade, commissioned from a Spent Town cobbler who had spent two levels on the fitting, perfect for marble floors and wrong for everything else.

Near the eastern residential wing, Kem took a wrong turn. Then he took another. Several hundred rooms spread across the Vault's wings and levels. The corridors all looked essentially identical: polished stone, blue carpet, portraits, lamps. Twenty-six years of living here had not taught him the way out. Servants had always walked ahead. He had always followed. By the second wrong turn his right heel was wet inside the shoe.

At the next corner the corridor changed.

Stone went a shade darker. Walls thickened slightly. Two heavy unmarked doors sat flush with the wall in a way that suggested reinforcement. Portraits disappeared. So did the carpet runner. Lamps burned at the same brightness as everywhere else, but the light seemed to illuminate less. There was a smell here under the usual lamp oil, sharp and worked, like metal heated and cooled again. Kem had known this wing existed the way he knew the kitchens existed, as a fact without detail, a section of the Vault he had been redirected away from so smoothly as a child that he had not realized the redirecting was deliberate until years later. No one discussed it. Stillness got euphemisms. This part of the Vault got omission.

The silence behind those doors felt different from the Vault's usual quiet. The rest of the Vault held the silence of nonaction, a held breath, a negative space. This stretch held density. It suggested containment rather than emptiness. People were doing something back there and being quiet about it.

Kem kept moving. Curiosity tugged, but not enough. Stopping here would turn the exit into one more thing to think around. Right now the useful act was the ugly one: keep going.

A wrong turn taken out of frustration dropped him into the gate hall, a wide ceremonial space he half remembered from childhood. The ceiling was vaulted. This was the Vault's main entrance, though it was rarely used because the Hoarded rarely left. Two guards stood near the gate in understated Vault uniform, neither armed. Hoarded guards observed and reported. They did not intervene physically, because physical intervention was action and action was spending. Actual enforcement, when it was needed, came from somewhere else in the Vault. Kem had never been told where.

Both guards watched him approach. The older one, a man with the expression of someone who had trained himself to see without reacting, held his position and said nothing. The younger guard, a woman perhaps thirty, had a crease between her eyebrows that suggested she had not expected today to contain anything worth creasing over. Her mouth opened, then shut.

"I'm leaving," Kem said. The words came out flat and declarative, the grammar of a man who had practiced formal speech his whole life without ever having to say anything that mattered.

"My lord." The younger guard's eyes flicked to the older guard, who did not look back. "The gate is — I'd need to send word to Lady Aldraste before —"

"Open it, please."

"Protocol requires notification of the household before any —"

The Projection Shield came again. Smaller this time. Some part of his mind had remembered the last deployment and attempted calibration. It was still too much, still a rippling wall of force crackling with surplus energy. The latch mechanism shattered. The gate groaned and swung outward on its hinges, not violently but firmly, and late-spring air flooded into the hall with a complexity of smell and temperature that hit Kem like a change in altitude. He smelled soil and growing things, and beyond them a breadth of air so open his chest tried to brace against it.

Two hundred and ninety-five.

By the gate, recessed into the wall on the left, stood a row of ceremonial pedestals, one for each major House. Every pedestal held a House signet, a heavy ring of worked metal bearing the family crest. The signets stayed here because the families did. Nobody wore them. Servants polished them weekly. On formal occasions people admired them. Otherwise they sat alone.

The Aldraste signet was silver-dark, heavy, the crest a closed circle. Kem picked it up. Warm metal settled into his palm, heavier than it looked. It fit exactly on his finger because it had been measured for him in infancy and stored here waiting for a day nobody expected to arrive.

Leaving it on the stand would have made the exit look deliberate. His hand was already closing around the ring before the thought finished. He slid the signet on and kept moving.

Kem walked through the gate.

---

The world was louder than the Vault.

He stopped just beyond the threshold because the sky kept going. Vault ceilings ended somewhere. Even the grandest hall resolved into stone and beam. This blue kept going. It went past the gate towers, past the pine crowns, past any edge Kem could find, and the amount of space in it made his stomach drop the way it did when a stair ended sooner than expected.

Sound hit him all at once, layered and unmanaged and alive. Birds produced several kinds of noise at once, none of them curated or dampened by architecture. Wind moved through full-grown pines instead of the Vault's manicured garden specimens; they creaked and shed things and made a rushing sound when the breeze moved through their crowns. Insects worked the air. Somewhere water ran. Under all of it lay the ambient texture of a landscape existing without anyone's oversight.

Outside the Vault, the ground pitched and dipped. A path descended from the gate toward a tree line several hundred meters away, and the path was dirt, packed dirt with loose stones and exposed roots and ruts where rain had channeled. Kem's shoes, designed for polished stone by a cobbler who had reasonably assumed they would never leave a building, touched the dirt and immediately began to fail.

Kem walked.

Downhill proved itself to be a different kind of labor from flat ground, and neither set of muscles had been meaningfully developed by twenty-six years of sitting in ceremonial silence. The blister on his heel announced itself with each step. A stone worked under the ball of his right foot through the thin sole. Sun landed on the back of his neck with a directness he had not prepared for and began, with the impersonal patience of natural processes, to burn him.

Once his right foot slid on loose dirt and he windmilled hard enough to wrench his shoulder. He caught himself badly, one knee hitting the path. Dust streaked his robe. There was no discreet way to recover from nearly falling when nobody was present to pretend not to notice it. He stood, brushed dirt off with limited success, and kept going because climbing back toward the gate felt impossible.

Forty minutes, or something close to it, brought him to the tree line. By then his legs were trembling. Sweat darkened the back of his shirt. Shade under the pines counted as improvement. Needles cushioned the ground, giving slightly under his weight and smelling of something alive and decomposing. A small brown animal darted across the path ahead of him. Startlement sent him sideways into a tree trunk. Bark met his shoulder hard and proved itself unsmooth.

Breathing hard, he rubbed bark grit off his shoulder and counted what he'd brought out of the Vault.

He had robes designed for sitting and shoes designed for marble. He had no food, no water, no pack, no map, and no money, assuming money was even physical. He had the House signet and two hundred and ninety-five levels of raw ability he did not know how to use without wrecking the nearest door.

He could read, speak several languages, and discourse at length on governance, all of which left him no closer to water. He could also sit motionless for two hours while flowers died.

Hunger arrived.

Vault meals had given him appetite before, the polite anticipation of courses arriving under silver covers. Hunger came from farther down: the hollow pull below his sternum, the shakiness in his hands that only became legible when his stomach growled loud enough to startle a bird off a low branch. In the last hour he had walked farther than he normally walked in a week. Whatever reserves his body carried had been burned through. Now it was presenting him with a demand he had no manual process for answering.

He listened for water and could no longer tell where it had been. Pine cones lay burst open in the needles. A thorned bush nearby held dusty blue berries that might have been edible or might have killed him before dusk. Kem crouched, considered them, and straightened again. Dying three hours out of the Vault over a speculative berry felt embarrassingly on brand.

Theory, at least, he had. Sustenance Charms were a basic utility ability, one of the first things children in the Spent Towns learned to calibrate, or so his tutors had told him. The principle was straightforward: draw on the Ledger to generate food and water from ambient energy, scaled to need. A single person's meal might cost one or two levels. The method was efficient, practical, and common outside the Vault. It was something any person at level one hundred and fifty did daily without thinking about it.

Kem had never used a Sustenance Charm. In fact Kem had never used any ability before this morning. He knew the charm the way he knew the historical causes of the Spent Town migration, as arranged information in his mind, disconnected from the physical reality it described.

So he activated it.

The intention was modest. He wanted something to eat, bread maybe, a portion sized for one man sitting on a log in a pine forest.

What arrived was bread, eight loaves of it, golden-crusted and steaming, laid out on the ground around the log in a rough semicircle. Then came a roast, enormous and glistening, radiating heat and the smell of rendered fat, the size of something that should have been wheeled to a banquet table. Fruit followed, apples and pears and things he did not recognize, piled in mounds. Wheels of cheese landed beside them. A cask appeared that smelled alcoholic. Smaller dishes appeared in a widening ring: pastries in stacked rows, cured meats on wooden boards that materialized along with the meats, pickled vegetables in ceramic jars, bowls of stew steaming in the cool pine-filtered air.

The food kept coming. Kem stared. Kem had no idea how to make it stop.

A final surge produced a three-tiered cake, elaborately iced and studded with candied fruit, which materialized on a tree stump roughly two meters from the log. The stump was not level. The cake began to slide. Kem lunged and caught the bottom tier with both hands. Warm icing smeared his fingers, his sleeves, and the signet ring.

Holding the cake, he recognized the menu. There were apricot pastries from winter receptions, the rosemary glaze Cook used when Council guests came, and pears poached to the exact bronze color his mother preferred. The charm had reached for the only meals he knew and laid one of House Aldraste's modest lunches on the forest floor.

Again he checked the Ledger.

Two hundred and eighty-eight.

Lunch had cost seven levels.

On the flattest piece of ground he could find, Kem set the cake down. The ground was not flat enough. It settled at an angle. Ants discovered the icing almost immediately and began a purposeful advance.

Bread in hand, Kem sat on the log and ate. The bread was excellent. Its interior was soft. The crust shattered properly. The flavor was exactly what he was used to because it had been generated from his palate's only reference point. While he chewed, a bird landed on the roast and began eating with a directness he found briefly instructive. Another bird arrived. Then something larger came out of the trees, a fox, rust-colored and cautious, which approached the roast with its head low and its eyes on Kem and then committed to the meat with the single-mindedness of an animal that had decided to deal with consequences later.

He considered taking some of the food with him. Bread made sense. Cheese did too. Water might have, if he could find any in the excess. The practical problem was immediate and humiliating: he had no bag, no wrapping cloth, and no hand that stayed clean for long. He tried tucking a loaf under one arm. It flattened against the robe and shed crumbs down his side. He put it back. The fox watched without blinking.

Kem ate bread, watched the fox eat roast, and counted.

Twelve levels were gone in the first hour: three for the Conservatory door, two for the gate, seven for lunch. The path ahead showed him nothing he knew how to read: no smoke, no roofline, no well. His heel had bled through the sock, and his calves had started to shake each time he stood still too long.

The fox looked up from the roast, muzzle dark and wet. It regarded him for a moment, then went back to eating.

A fly found the roast. Then a second one did. Kem stood. Calves complained. The blister had soaked through his sock, and wet fabric slid against raw skin with each step. He left the banquet to the ants, the birds, and anything else with a better claim on seven levels' worth of lunch. South was the direction the path offered, so south he went.

The pine forest thinned. Trees gave way to scrubby grassland. Shade gave way to full sun. Heat hit him clean and merciless the moment the last pines fell behind. His neck was already pink. His face felt tight. Out here the air tasted drier, full of dust and pollen, nothing like the Vault's managed atmosphere. At the edge of awareness the Ledger kept humming, quieter now by twelve points. The sensation was hard to name, a fullness slightly less full, like a room with a few books pulled from the shelf. Kem barely noticed it.

South stayed south. His body did what it could, which was less than he would have liked and more than he had any right to expect from a body that had spent twenty-six years doing nothing. Feet bled. Shoulders ached from the unfamiliar rhythm of swinging his arms. Sun caught the signet and flashed. Kem turned the crest inward against his palm and kept walking until the metal bit. Every few steps the thought of stopping came up. Each time he saw the Conservatory bench, his father's open eyes, and chose the road instead.

Behind him, from the pines, birds had started screaming at one another over seven levels' worth of lunch. Sugar and roast fat rode the heat after him. Kem did not turn around. He kept walking until the road took the smell away.
