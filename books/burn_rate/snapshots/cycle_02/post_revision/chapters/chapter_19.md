# Chapter 19

Footsteps struck stone behind Vel before Kem had air enough to finish trying to laugh.

She kept one hand locked around his sleeve and turned toward the sound. Three people came fast from the nearest bend: an older woman in a green-stained apron, a boy with a sloshing bucket, and behind them a woman carrying a rolled survey under one arm with a mason's hammer hooked through her belt.

Vel knew the hammer first. Maren had always carried tools as if they were part of the argument. Then the rest of her arrived. There was more gray at the temples now, a notch missing from one ear, new lines at the mouth. The hard line between her brows was the same. It was the look she wore when something hit her sideways and she had not decided what to do with it yet.

For one bad second Vel forgot Kem's weight entirely. He sagged against her, and the cracked rib under her left breast drove a knife through her breath.

The older woman reached them first. "Don't drop him on my road."

Maren stopped two paces away.

Dust moved between them. Somewhere farther down the street, somebody shouted for rope. Kem made a ragged sound low in his throat and tried to stand without using either of them.

Maren looked at Vel's face, the dirt dried across her shirt, the hand pressed to her side. Then she looked at Kem and the clean blankness in both their expressions, the after-look of people who had just come out of the hum for good.

"Vel," she said.

The name came out rough, like she had not used it in a while and had not expected the first use to be this one.

Vel said, "Yeah."

That was what she had. Maren was alive, older, with stone dust on one boot and a council roll under her arm and both feet planted in a world that existed. Eight years of road, and Vel's mouth could only manage that.

Kem's knees buckled.

Maren moved before the older woman could swear again. She got under Kem's other arm with the same efficient turn of the body Vel remembered from riverbanks, collapsed camps, and one fight behind a tannery in Roth that had ended with both of them bleeding and laughing because neither of them had died. Her forearm pressed warm and solid against Vel's wrist. She was real enough to lean on.

"Intake house," the older woman said. "Boy, stop staring and bring the water. Both of them, now."

Vel said, "He spent the last two getting us through."

"I can see that he did something dramatic," the woman said. "He still has to lie down."

Maren's shoulder took more of Kem's weight. "Can you walk?"

Vel almost said yes out of habit. She took one step and the answer changed under her. "If nobody makes me run."

"Nobody's asking that much," Maren said.

They moved uphill together. The street under Vel's boots was packed earth laid between walls of hand-cut stone. Tool marks caught the late light. A woman at a doorway flattened herself aside with an armful of split willow. Two children stopped carrying a basket between them long enough to stare, then kept going when the older woman snapped her fingers at them for standing in the path.

So this was Sera. The name flickered somewhere old and road-worn, but the pain under Vel's ribs was louder than memory.

Kem tried to lift his head. "Did we."

"We did," Vel said.

"Good," he muttered, and nearly went down again.

The intake house was two rooms of pale stone with a long bench under one wall and shelves of folded blankets on the other. Bundles of drying herbs hung from the rafters. A chipped blue bowl sat on the table beside a stack of clean bandages and an obsidian blade laid on cloth. The room held no shimmer of healing and no latent hum in the air. It was only a room built to hold hurt people until hurt stopped moving.

Sera pointed with her chin. "Bed. The one by the window. If either of you vomits on the blankets, you wash them yourselves."

Maren helped Vel get Kem onto the mattress. He hit it hard enough to grunt and then went slack in the alarming way zeroed people seemed to do, as if sleep and collapse were using the same door.

Vel stayed bent over him a moment longer than she needed to. When she straightened, black drifted in at the edge of her sight.

Sera caught it. "You too."

"I'm standing."

"You are briefly standing. Lie down before you argue yourself interesting."

Vel did not move.

Maren had set the survey roll on the table. Her hand stayed on it as if she had forgotten she was holding it. She looked worse for surprise than Vel did for pain, which ought to have been satisfying if Vel had room for anything that clean.

"You found it," Maren said.

Vel looked at the obsidian blade, the blankets, the herbs in the rafters, the wall somebody had built stone by stone without a single easy trick in it. Then she looked back at Maren. "Yeah."

Maren's mouth tightened once. It could have been anger. It could have been relief trying not to show itself in public. "Of course you did."

Sera cut across whatever would have come next. "If you two want to do this properly, do it after they've both stopped swaying."

Vel let herself be pushed to the second bed because resisting would have meant falling. Maren did not touch her again after that. She stood at the foot of the bed while Sera made Vel drink water, then said, "I was in council. I left in the middle of Pol being impossible, so he'll still be impossible when I get back to it."

Vel's laugh caught on the bad rib and turned into a hiss.

Maren glanced at her side. Her eyes stayed there a beat too long. "When you wake up, come down to the lower settlement."

Vel wanted to ask a dozen things and had no clean hold on any of them. She settled for, "All right."

Maren nodded once and took the survey roll with her.

Vel woke to a room gone quieter and darker.

For one blank instant she reached for the hum and found only the wrong loose silence under her skin. Then the rib announced itself, and the ache in her shoulder, and the memory came back in order: the threshold, the light, Kem's face going empty at zero, Maren standing two paces away with a hammer on her belt.

Sera was at the table grinding dried leaves with the heel of her hand. "You lasted longer than the last voluntary one. He cried in his sleep and then tried to climb out the window."

"How long?"

"Long enough." Sera tipped the ground herbs into a cup and added hot water from a kettle hanging over a small brazier. "Your friend is still out. You were not meant to be up before him, but you look like the sort of person who hears 'not meant to' as an invitation."

Vel swung her feet to the floor. The room tilted, corrected, and held. "Where's Maren."

"Lower settlement. House by the well with too many herb pots in the window. She came back twice to check whether you'd woken, which I'm sure you'll both find a sensible thing to behave normally about."

Vel took the cup Sera held out. The steam smelled bitter and green. "You say that to everybody."

"No. Some people make normal faces." Sera looked toward Kem's bed, where he lay on his back with one hand curled over the blanket like he had been reaching for something in sleep and not found it. "Go on, then. Before I remember I'm supposed to be in charge here."

Outside, late light had turned the upper stones the color of old bread. Vel walked carefully, one hand against her ribs when the slope steepened. Nobody stopped her. People made space when they had to and otherwise stayed in the business of finishing the day. A woman hauled two buckets from a cistern by rope and counterweight. At an open-sided infirmary, an apprentice washed red from a basin while an older man sharpened obsidian on a flat stone. She heard tool on tool, water on clay, and wind in leaves. The world kept arriving at her without the hum under it.

The lower settlement curved around a well and a cluster of cookfires. Maren's house sat a little off the ring, pale stone with a narrow garden pressed against the wall. Herbs crowded clay pots under the window. Somebody had argued them into rows and then let them go a little wild anyway. Vel could not tell whether the order or the disorder was more familiar.

The door stood open.

Inside, the room was warmer than the air outside and smelled of tea, damp wool, and stone that had kept a day's heat. A worktable took the center of it, scarred with knife marks and darkened where cups had been set down carelessly for years. Three rolled plans lay across one end under a river stone, beside a length of measuring cord and a little jar of nails. Shelves ran along the far wall holding bowls, seed packets, folded cloth, and a row of labeled clay jars in Maren's hand. A banked hearth sat to the left. Two chairs faced each other across its cold space as if people used them often enough to wear a habit into the floor.

Maren stood at the table with both hands around a cup. She had taken off the hammer. It hung from a peg near the door beside a work coat dusted white from stone.

She turned at the sound of Vel's step.

This close, the changes were worse and better than distance had allowed. The skin at her throat had loosened a little. There was a thin scar under her chin Vel had never seen before. Her forearms were stronger. Her face had not kept the road on it; it had taken on the slower wear of weather, labor, and years in one place. The old quickness was still there, but it had settled deeper. Nothing about her looked preserved. Everything looked used.

Vel stopped just inside the threshold. She had crossed half the world to get here and now had no idea what counted as far enough into the room.

Maren set the cup down.

Neither of them moved.

Wind touched the open door. Somewhere at the back of the house water ticked once into a basin. Vel saw the second cup already on the table, cooled enough that a skin had formed on the tea. She saw the extra blanket folded over the back of one chair. She saw a basket of onions under the window and a pair of mud-dried work boots by the hearth. Eight years had happened in objects before either of them said a word.

"You can close the door," Maren said.

Vel shut it. The latch clicked too loud.

"Sit down if you're staying upright," Maren said after a moment. "I don't want you hitting the floor and making me solve that first."

Vel lowered herself into the chair nearest the table. The rib punished her for pretending the movement did not matter. She kept her face as still as she could.

Maren pushed the waiting cup across to her and went back to the other chair. A whole table width sat between them. It was more than enough room for caution and not enough for distance.

Vel put her fingers around the cup. It was still warm.

Maren said, "I used to do this stupid walk to intake every time somebody came through who might know your name."

Vel looked up.

Maren's eyes stayed on the table, on the measuring cord under the river stone. "I did it for five years. Then I got tired of coming home with the same answer."

Vel's first instinct was to say she had come as fast as she could. The sentence reached her mouth and died there. Eight years did not get smaller because the road had been difficult.

"I know," she said, and knew as soon as she said it that the line was useless.

Maren gave a short, airless laugh. "No. You don't."

That landed and stayed there.

Vel drank tea because it gave her hands something to do. It had mint in it and something bitter underneath that kept pulling her back into her body.

Maren watched her over the cup's rim for a second and then said, "You look worse than I hoped and better than I expected."

"I can work with that."

"I wasn't offering work yet."

"No," Vel said. "I know."

Maren leaned back. The chair answered with a small wood complaint. "Do you."

Vel looked at the shelves instead of at her, at the labeled jars, the folded cloth, the nails, and the plans waiting under stone weights. All the ordinary things only existed because Maren had been here long enough to need them twice. "You built a life."

"Yes."

The word had no apology in it. Vel was glad of that and hurt by it in the same instant.

Another silence opened. This one held.

Maren broke it without looking away. "I'm glad you're alive."

Vel let out the breath she had been pinning behind her teeth. "All right."

"I'm not ready for the rest of it," Maren said. "Not yet. I left council in the middle of an argument and Pol's going to send somebody down with a map any minute because he thinks weather negotiates with stubbornness."

"All right," Vel said again.

Maren's mouth twitched once. It did not become a smile. "You still answer the smaller question when the bigger one is waiting right behind it."

"It's the one I can do sitting down."

That almost got a real laugh. It missed by very little.

A knock sounded once against the frame and then went away before either of them spoke. Some committee runner checking, deciding better of it. The house settled again.

Maren said, quieter now, "Did you come here to stay, or did you come here to prove you could reach the door."

Vel looked at the cup in her hands. Steam barely rose from it anymore. "I came here because you were here."

Maren's fingers tightened around her own cup. No answer came, because none was easy enough for this early.

Vel did not press. She drank the tea. Maren sat across from her with both feet planted on the floor she had worn into habit. The room held them in the same square of light until the light shifted and the upper path began carrying other people's footsteps instead.

When Kem came back to himself, the first thing he noticed was the quiet.

It was not silence. Outside the window, a bird called, wind moved through leaves, and somebody said water and got an answer back. It was ordinary settlement noise. People were still awake, still moving through the last work of the day.

The quiet was inside him.

He lay on stone. The cloth under his back did not hide the grain of it. Above him, blocks fitted together without the seamlessness of Shaping. Every joint was visible. Every surface was scored by tools. He counted chisel marks in one block while his mind kept reaching for what was missing.

What was missing was the Ledger.

He reached for it before he knew he was doing it, the same stupid reflex as a tongue finding the gap where a tooth used to be. Nothing answered him. There was no hum, no tally, no number running at the edge of thought. For twenty-six years it had been there, first at 300, too high to feel like danger, then descending until every spend had a shape. He had felt it at 245 after the outriders, at 148 before the river, at 50 leaving the Margins, at 31 where Vel had started, at 12 in the tunnel, and at 2 with the threshold failing in front of him.

Now there was only the reach and the miss.

It felt less like losing a limb than losing a noise. His mind kept listening for it anyway. The listening became its own pressure.

He pushed himself upright. The room was small but not mean. A wooden bed frame held the straw mattress. A table stood under the single window with a clay cup on it. The walls were hand-quarried stone, each block a slightly different shade. Nothing in the room tried to hide the work that had made it.

He drank the water. It tasted like water.

A woman filled the doorway, maybe sixty, with short gray hair and old burn scars from wrist to elbow. Her apron was stained dark green. A basket of dried herbs hung from one hand, sharp and peppery.

"You're the second one today," she said. "The woman woke about four hours before you did. Got to her feet faster, too."

"Vel."

"That her name? She didn't offer it. Just stood up and walked out of here like the room was slowing her down." The woman set the basket on the table and looked him over with practiced calm, weighing him for tears or vomiting or both. "I'm Sera. Intake house. Eat the bread. Your body is going to feel wrong for a while. Some people get dizzy. Some people cry. One man threw up for six hours."

The name hit a buried place all at once. He heard Fallow in the Margins, flat as weather, naming the people the Scour had taken and not returned. Sera and Hoster had gone in front of him and been talked about afterward as if the earth had eaten them.

"Sera from the Scour?" he said.

Her eyes sharpened. "If you met Fallow, yes. Is he still alive."

"He was when we left."

"Good." She pushed the loaf toward him. "Eat. We can all enjoy the cosmological revelation after you stop looking like you're about to slide out of your own skin."

The vanished were not vanished. They were old women with herb-stained aprons telling him to chew properly. Kem stared at the bread for a moment before picking it up.

"I'm not going to cry," he said.

"That's what the throwing-up man said. Then he cried and ruined my floor."

The bread was dense and dark, crust cracked, heat still trapped in the middle. He ate it at the window while looking over rooftops dropping into a valley. Stone and timber buildings stood with gardens between them. The rooflines were uneven without looking improvised, each building carrying the choices of the person who had made it. The place had not been built to prove anything. It had been built because people expected to stay.

"How do I get to the council hall?"

"Main path. Ten minutes. You won't miss it." Sera tucked a cloth over the basket. "If they're still on the cistern, don't step between Pol and the map unless you want your first day here to involve being shouted at by three practical people at once."

Outside, his legs felt wrong in a new way. Each step came a fraction off-center, as if the Ledger had been a weight hung somewhere inside him and the rest of his body had not yet learned to carry itself without it.

The main path was packed earth worn smooth by feet. People knelt in gardens, working actual soil that wanted rotation and rest and manure and time. A man carrying root vegetables nodded once and kept going. Two children ran past without slowing for the newcomer. An old woman on a bench watched him with patient curiosity and waited to see what he would do next.

At an open-sided building halfway down the path, he slowed.

Three people were bent over a table. A young apprentice held a sliver of dark volcanic glass while an older woman guided her wrist. On the table, a man sat with one leg out and a split in his calf shining wet in the light.

The older woman looked up and saw Kem watching. "If you're going to stand there, come hold his ankle."

He did. The man's skin was hot under his hands. Blood slid over Kem's fingers while the apprentice worked the obsidian along the torn flesh and the older woman kept talking her through it.

"Shallower. Let the edge do the work. Good. Now wash it."

The apprentice poured from a clay jug. The man on the table flinched so hard his other heel banged the wood.

"Sorry," the apprentice said.

"Don't apologize," the older woman said. "You're helping him. He gets to flinch."

The man looked at Kem. "First day?"

"Yeah."

He jerked his chin toward the gash in his leg. "Temporary scaffold at the cistern. Ladder split under me." He took a breath through his teeth and let it out. "It hurts more than the abilities did. Better, though. At least I know where it is."

By the time the apprentice tied off the bandage, Kem's palms were streaked red. The older woman jerked her chin toward the door. "Wash at the cistern before you go up to council. Oriel hates blood on paper."

The council hall was bigger than the buildings around it and no grander than it had to be, two stories of stone with a lintel carved into open hands. Inside, the room smelled of damp clay, lamp oil, and the mud people had tracked straight in from work. A long table took the middle. Maps were weighted with river stones instead of seals. A clay cutaway of the upper hillside sat at one end, scored with terraces and channels, its basin dark from real water someone had poured through it to prove a point. Beside the model lay a snapped ladder rung and a bent bucket hoop.

He had reached the place in the middle of an argument and apparently in the middle of evidence.

A broad-shouldered man with a broken nose jabbed two fingers into the clay hillside. "You move the cistern mouth west and every bucket crosses the unfinished edge. Jalen already went through one ladder. I am not handing the rains a second try."

A lean woman with one sleeve rolled to the elbow shoved the bent hoop toward him. "Jalen went through a ladder because you've kept the temporary scaffold up twelve days longer than temporary means. If the cistern stays east, the lower houses haul double all dry season."

A woman with close-cropped hair and ink on her fingers said, "And if intake keeps bedding six new people a week who can't carry a bucket on day one, everybody gets to keep being right while somebody else hauls it."

Kem looked from the wet track in the clay model to the split rung on the table and understood that the argument was not about lines on a plan. It was about where bodies would slip, bleed, and spend hours carrying water because there was no easy trick left in the world to do it for them.

The ink-fingered woman noticed him first. Her eyes dropped to his washed but still stained hands. "The infirmary drafted you already. Efficient."

"I was standing in the wrong place."

"That'll keep happening." She leaned back. "I'm Oriel. That's Pol, that's Ede, and Cassery's pretending to be asleep."

The old man at the end of the table opened one eye. A wax tablet rested on his chest with a stylus tucked into the cord around his wrist. "I'm resting."

Oriel looked back at Kem. "Voluntary?"

The question was not courtesy. It sorted him. It asked whether he had come by choice or been thrown through, whether he was stable or shattered, whether he was the kind of new arrival who might work soon or the kind who might wake up screaming because the missing hum felt too much like dying.

"Yes," he said.

"Good. Then maybe you'll stop flinching every time you look inward by tomorrow."

Pol asked, "Can he dig?"

Kem blinked. "I just walked in."

"And the east terrace needs hands before the rains, so I'm asking." Pol looked him over as if trying to see through the clean shirt to muscle, old injury, willingness, whatever mattered most in a place that had to build everything with arms and tools. "What were you at?"

"Three hundred."

The number landed badly. Not with awe. With irritation, like he had admitted to never carrying his own weight. Pol looked at his own hands on the table. The joints were thick, the fingers bent wrong, the nails rimmed with old clay.

"Put him on the lower crew."

"He doesn't go on a crew today," Oriel said. "He can barely walk straight."

Pol spread both hands over the clay hill, smearing one wet channel. "Then when? I've been on that slope since I came through. Intake keeps sending me people who've never held a shovel."

Ede said, "Because intake keeps sending you people who crossed in the morning and stop hearing the hum by afternoon. Give them a day before you ask them to carry stone."

"A day doesn't build a retaining wall."

"Neither does a man with his leg open because you kept him on bad wood."

Pol stared at the broken rung for a beat. The fight in him changed shape without leaving. "If the east terrace slips, the lower houses lose half their onions."

Ede pointed at the clay basin. "If the overflow cuts the wrong way, the lower houses lose the well path."

Cassery, eyes closed again, said, "The charming part of this conversation is how everybody's right and still impossible."

Nobody laughed.

Kem stood there while they weighed water against terraces, training against urgency, one broken ladder against a whole season of food. Nobody asked his house. Nobody asked who his father had been. They were already arguing about when his hands might join the rest of the settlement's hands. The speed of it shocked him. The lack of reverence did not hurt nearly as much as it should have.

Oriel reached for a roll tied with red cord and held it out. "Your friend Vel went to the lower settlement. She was looking for Maren."

"I know."

"Maren is on the southern district committee now. Infrastructure and intake." Oriel shook the roll once. Charcoal dust loosened from one end and marked her thumb. "Take this to her if you're going anyway. She walked out before we settled whether the overflow cut runs south of the well path or through Pol's beloved terrace, and if I have to listen to this again without at least trying to send the map after her, I will die here in front of you."

Cassery said, "Don't threaten people with paperwork on their first day."

Oriel ignored him. "West path. Twenty minutes. Give her the roll. Tell her Pol still thinks the east terrace can wait."

Pol said, "Because it can."

Ede said, "It cannot."

Kem took the map.

The west path dropped between terraced plots and low stone walls that broke the wind. At a communal oven built into the hillside, a woman pulled loaves while a boy chalked fresh names onto a board beside her. Two people carrying water yokes made him flatten himself against the wall so they could pass. Nobody slowed down to explain the place to him.

He found the lower settlement in the late light, thirty or so buildings following the slope around a well. A narrow garden ran beside one pale stone house, herbs in clay pots lined with a care that made the rows look argued with and then settled.

The door stood open.

Inside, the room looked worked in from every side. Plans and measuring cord covered the middle of the table. A work coat hung on the peg by the door. A second cup sat half-finished beside Maren's hand, and Vel's cup had gone empty near the far edge. The chairs faced each other across a distance that had not quite turned back into anything easy.

Vel was on her feet anyway, one hand braced on the chair back. Maren sat at the table with Oriel's absence already written around her in rolls of paper, loose notes, and a patience that had plainly been used hard.

Kem stopped in the doorway with the sketch under his arm.

Neither woman looked finished with the silence between them.

Vel turned first. Relief crossed her face, then folded back under caution. There were three people in the room now, too many for one feeling to take over. "You made it."

"Yeah."

Maren looked at the roll he was carrying. "Oriel couldn't wait ten minutes."

"She said if I was coming down here I could carry my own excuse."

Maren let out a breath through her nose that almost became a laugh. She held out a hand and Kem gave her the sketch.

"You're the one who crossed with her," she said.

"I am."

"Started at three hundred."

Kem nodded.

"And used all of it."

"By the end."

Maren's thumb ran once along the cord tied around the roll. "Did she tell you what she needed you for before or after."

Vel said, "Maren."

"No. He's in my house at zero. He gets to answer."

Kem looked at Vel. She did not interrupt again. "After," he said. "Not at the start. At eighteen."

Maren closed her eyes once. When they opened, they stayed on Vel. "Of course after."

Vel took that without flinching. Or maybe the flinch was only small enough that Kem could miss it from anywhere but this room.

Maren set the new roll down on top of the others and said, "I had intake come tell me every time somebody crossed who might know your name. They did it for five years. Three years ago I told them to stop. I got tired of walking up there to collect strangers."

Vel's hand tightened on the chair. "All right."

"No. That's not an answer either." Maren dragged the back of one wrist across her mouth and looked away toward the window, toward the lower settlement, toward anywhere that was not eight years standing up in her kitchen. "Why now, Vel."

"Because I got here now."

"That's a road answer."

"It's the one I've got."

Maren set her cup down gently enough not to slam it, hard enough to spill. Tea ran over her fingers and spread across the table toward the maps. "You always did that. I'd ask for the thing under the thing and you'd hand me directions."

Vel drew one shallow breath against her ribs. No answer came.

"Sit down if you're staying," Maren said. "I'm done talking to you like you're about to vanish again."

Vel lowered herself into the chair opposite. The movement cost her. She hid most of it, not all.

A table width now separated them. Nobody reached across it.

Maren wiped her wet fingers on her trouser leg and said, quieter, "I'm glad you're alive. I'm not ready for the rest of it, so don't push."

"All right," Vel said.

Maren nodded once toward the door without looking at Kem. "Guest room's two houses over, west side. Somebody will have set it up."

He backed out before either woman changed her mind.

The lower settlement kept moving while daylight thinned. A woman drew water hand over hand from the well. Two men under an awning shaped timber with mallets and chisels, talking about grain storage as they worked. Nobody turned the newcomer into an event.

At a cookfire, a young man stirring a pot of grain and onion said, "Hungry?"

"I ate at intake."

"That bread? That's not food. That's orientation." The man filled a clay bowl and set it on the wall beside him. "Take it if you want."

Kem kept walking because he still did not know how to refuse kindness without making an insult of it. Nobody called after him. When he glanced back from the next turning, the bowl was still waiting on the wall.

The guest room was small and stone-walled, with a single west window, a bed frame, a chair, and a clay basin for washing. Somebody had already put a blanket on the bed, rough-woven and heavy enough to matter.

From the window he could see the settlement finishing its day. A man crossed the lane with split firewood against one shoulder. Children born here ran between the gardens. Smoke lifted from cookfires. Down the slope, the infirmary apprentice was rinsing a basin from red to pink to clear.

At 300 his hands had been soft. Lord Aldraste's hands had been soft too, laid just so on formal knees while the eyes went vague and the mouth forgot half a sentence at the Conservatory Rite. In the collapsed mine at 25, Kem's own hands had shaken hard enough that he had to brace them on rubble after the family crawled out. One child had put both palms on her mother's face, checking. For a single ugly breath, crouched there scraped raw and half-spent, he had understood the appeal of a life that never asked that much of flesh. Then the child laughed because her mother was real, and the thought had gone.

The bread sat in his stomach. The blanket waited on the bed. Down the slope, the infirmary held a man with a stitched calf and a basin gone pink from washing it clean. Water came up from the well by arm and rope. Nothing in any of it hummed.

He reached into his pocket and took out the House signet.

The metal was dark. The Aldraste crest cut into the face had gone slightly smooth along one edge from weeks in his pocket. He had worn it through the Spent Towns until a tavern keeper's face closed at the sight. In the Margins, a child had turned it over in her dirty hands and said it sounded boring.

Kem set it on the windowsill.

In the lowering light it was only a ring now, dark metal cooling on stone. Nobody in the lane below would know the crest. Nobody would change expression at the sight of it.

Outside, the air had cooled. He walked to the edge of the lower settlement where the ground dropped away and the view opened across the valley.

Up the slope, the intake house roof showed pale between the trees. Three people worked around it. One carried a ladder. Another dragged a cut thornbush across the path Kem had used that afternoon, laying branch over branch until the line of the trail broke. Near the lower switchback, somebody lit a watchfire while there was still daylight enough to see by. They worked without shouting or hurry. By the time the ladder came back down, nothing on the slope marked where he had crossed.

He stayed there until the watchfire took over from daylight.

Sable Orm stood at the edge of the Scour basin and let the wind push loose hair across her mouth. Her hands were chapped. The boots were wrong for this terrain and always had been. At 229, the number still hummed at the back of her mind. The sound made her jaw tighten. Eight weeks ago she would have called that reaction unthinkable.

Behind her, the Red Reserve commander approached. The two agents who could still walk waited by their camp, one holding the arm a deflected ability had broken, the other sitting on a rock with the blank stare of somebody reconsidering his profession. The basin floor lay empty and wind-scoured, layered rock exposed in ochre and gray. The point where the threshold had opened looked like any other air in the world.

"Senior Auditor." The commander's voice stayed flat and professional. "I need to know what goes in the report."

Sable looked at the place where the door had been. Fifteen minutes ago, maybe twenty, light had poured through a seam in the world. She had seen buildings on the other side, and people, and a sky. For one instant, standing close enough to feel the draft from wherever it led, the number in her head had wavered. It had not dropped. It had not changed. It had only wavered, like a signal reaching the edge of its range, and she had tasted what its absence would feel like.

She had burned seven levels to hold that door open for a man she had been sent to retrieve. She could have gone through. In the instant the threshold held, while the light was still pouring, she could have stepped forward and left the Ledger and the Council and every structure that had organized her life. She had not. She did not know if what kept her on this side was courage or cowardice. Perhaps it was neither. Perhaps the lie needed a keeper on this side, because the lie was the door's only defense left.

"Senior Auditor."

She turned. The commander was around fifty, lean, scarred along the jaw with a scar she had chosen to keep. She had spent five years at this post, watching people vanish and filing reports that called it termination.

"The targets..." Sable began, then stopped at the taste of the word. She thought of the council chamber and the reports she used to write without thinking. She started over. "The subjects reached level zero during the confrontation. I observed the termination event. Standard level-zero collapse, neurological shutdown consistent with total depletion."

The commander watched her.

"No threshold event was observed. No anomalous energy signature. The subjects were eliminated through sustained depletion in combat conditions." Sable kept her voice even. The old machinery was still there, the reflex to classify, to file, to press the impossible flat enough to fit a form. "Level-zero termination. No threshold event observed."

Wind came up between them carrying dust and the smell of sun-baked rock.

The commander had seen the threshold open. She had watched two people walk through it. She had watched Sable burn seven levels to keep the door steady. The lie was transparent enough that maintaining it required more than belief. It required agreement.

"Level-zero termination," the commander said.

"That is correct."
