# Chapter 3

She found him by the smell.

Two days north of the settlement where she'd healed the farmer, late-spring air went sweet and rotten at once. Vel stepped off the road into a meadow churned flat by one man's panic. A Sustenance Charm had gone off there with banquet logic: loaves split open by fox teeth, fruit liquefying in the grass, a metal basin of stew sagging as the conjured shape gave up. Enough food remained to feed a caravan, if the caravan liked flies.

That alone told her most of what she needed. Kem Aldraste had left the Vault three days ago at three hundred. The gate story had outrun him. She put the Projection Shield at three, maybe four, and the feast at eight if he'd overshot badly, ten if he'd been spectacular about it. When she added the smaller comforts that always tempted people with high numbers, the total made her stop in the road.

He was at two hundred and eighty-six, give or take.

At twenty she had still been rich enough to miss a spend and keep moving. At thirty-one the Ledger never shut up, and fourteen levels in three days for food, warmth, and whatever else had offended him made her jaw lock.

Four hundred meters farther on, she found him sitting on a fallen log with his boots off and one foot in his lap, studying a blister with scholarly concern. His road boots were soft Vault leather, good for polished floors and not much else. One sole had split. As Vel watched, a pale Glow rose over the raw skin and cost him a level just to inspect his own foot.

She stayed in the tree line long enough to take him in. He was tall, mid-twenties, with sunburn starting at the nose and ears where three days outdoors had already made an argument with his skin. Cream-and-gray Vault silks were snagged and dirty now, but expensive even ruined. His hands had no calluses and no scars. On the right hand, bright in the afternoon light, sat the Aldraste signet.

Vel stepped out of the trees.

Surprise crossed his face, then caution, then relief so quick he almost hid it.

"Hello," he said, letting the Glow vanish. "I'm trying to work out whether this road turns south again or whether I've managed to misread an entire set of very obvious signs."

"It goes south."

"Good." His eyes dropped to her boots, the road pack, the knife at her belt, and settled on competence with naked hunger. "Are the Spent Towns close?"

"A week."

That landed hard enough that he glanced at his feet. "On these?"

"No."

A breath went out of him. "Right."

Vel came closer. Up close, the damage looked worse. Blisters split into each other, raw skin under raw skin, and he had walked on them anyway because there had been nobody to tell him not to.

"How many levels now?" she asked.

The answer came after that brief inward stillness people had when they checked the Ledger. "Two hundred and eighty-six."

There was not even shame in the number, only surprise at himself.

"I know," he added, too quickly. "That's more than it should be."

"That's fourteen."

"Yes."

"In three days."

After another small pause, he said, "Yes."

She looked at him until he filled it, because of course he did.

"I've been treating inconvenience as if it were danger," he said. The words came formal at first, then less so. "Anything uncomfortable, I spent on. Cold. Darkness. Hard ground. I seem to have assumed the world was making errors for me personally."

That was the first intelligent thing he'd said.

"What about now?" Vel asked. "What would you spend on now?"

He glanced at his own ruined feet. "Mending, probably."

"Wrong."

"I thought that might be the answer."

He shifted, wincing when skin pulled. "There was also a stream incident I would prefer not to discuss."

"Leaves?"

His face changed from offense to resignation. "Yes."

Vel sat on the other end of the log because her legs had started making demands. The road lay empty on both sides. Somewhere behind them the meadow kept rotting in the sun.

"You know what a level costs?" she asked.

"In theory."

"That's not what I asked."

Silence held for a moment. Wind moved through the trees and did nothing to cool the air.

"No," he said at last. "I know it's permanent. I know it doesn't come back. I know all the words. At two hundred and eighty-six it still feels like a principle instead of a wound."

He was honest again, and Vel hated how much easier honesty made him to deal with.

"At this height," she said, "you can still pretend the count is scenery. You won't be able to later."

A cart rolled past them going north, vegetable crates rattling in the bed. The driver looked once, saw a road woman and a ruined aristocrat, and kept his eyes on the horse. Nobody slowed for other people's arithmetic.

"What are you doing out here?" Vel asked.

That made him still. One hand went to the ring before he seemed to notice he was doing it.

"My father is level three hundred," he said. "He's been three hundred for sixty-one years. He sits in the Conservatory every week, perfectly preserved, and there is less of him in the room each year. Nobody says it. Nobody names it. They call it discipline and composure and a triumph of principle." His mouth tightened. "It's rot with good manners. I got tired of watching it happen."

"Stillness."

"You know the word."

"Everyone outside the Vault knows the word. Most just don't pity people who bought it on purpose."

A flinch might have been easier. Instead he nodded once, as if the insult cleared the air.

"I don't need pity," he said. "I just couldn't stay."

"And now?"

"I want zero."

He said it plainly, not like a speech or even like hope, more like an address copied onto a scrap of paper.

Vel looked at him again: the split boots, the ruined feet, the ring, the face still pink from weather he had not learned yet. Everything the Hoarded preserved badly sat in front of her in one expensive body.

The number had weight because it changed the road, not because it was large. With that much left in him, the journey south stopped being only hers. It became possible in a new, uglier way, and she hated the relief that brought.

---

"I'm heading to zero," Vel said. "South. I know the route."

He turned fast enough to pull at the blistered skin and had to hide the pain afterward. "You know what it is?"

"I know more than you do."

That was not the same as answering. He knew it and said nothing for a beat.

"What are you offering?" he asked.

"A guide. Training. Fewer stupid spends. We walk together."

"What do you get?"

"Your levels." No point dressing that up. "I'm at thirty-one. South gets harder. I need someone who can spend when the road stops being negotiable."

His expression sharpened. Hoarded manners came back over it like a gate lowering into place. "That's unexpectedly direct."

"You asked."

"I did." He looked at her with more care now. "And what, precisely, would you control in this arrangement?"

"The route. The pace. The stops. When you spend. What you spend on."

"Everything, then."

"Enough."

A short laugh came out of him, tired and disbelieving. "You realize that from a numerical standpoint this is absurd."

"You used a Glow to look at your foot."

"Fair."

"You can't start a fire. You can't tell clean water from dirty. You can't walk six more miles in those boots. Your number doesn't matter if you spend it solving every irritation you meet."

A change crossed his face at that, not surrender exactly, but the first sign that he knew she was describing facts rather than humiliations.

"And what about zero?" he asked. "If I walk with you, what am I walking toward?"

Vel let the pause sit. She did not like saying even this much, but if she gave him nothing he would either refuse or keep asking until the conversation went sour.

"There's something there," she said. "A real place. It isn't death or Stillness."

"You've seen it."

"Yes."

"Then why not say more?"

"Because you don't need more yet."

"I think I do. If I'm — look, I'd be spending my levels on your road. I want to know what I'm walking into."

"South," Vel said. "It's hard. It's expensive. It's better with a guide."

"That is not what I meant."

"I know."

He stared at her. Under the aristocratic phrasing there was a younger, angrier man trying to climb out.

"What aren't you telling me?" he asked.

Vel answered the smaller question, the one she could survive answering. "Enough that you should assume I'm not done talking."

That made him blink.

"That is a remarkable sentence."

"It's the sentence you're getting."

He could have walked then. She gave him the space to do it. Instead he looked down at his feet, the boots, the road stretching south through trees he still couldn't name.

"I need a condition," he said.

Vel waited.

"If you're holding something back from me, tell me that much. You don't have to say what." He stopped, rubbed the heel of his hand along his knee. "I just — I need to know when I'm not seeing the whole thing. That's what I'm asking for."

"I can do that," Vel said. "There are pieces I'm not giving you now."

His jaw worked. "I don't like that."

"No."

"And you still expect me to agree."

"Yes."

He sat with that. So did she. The bargain changed shape in the silence and both of them knew it.

Finally he said, "If I refuse, I spend down alone."

"Fast."

"If I accept, I put my remaining life under the judgment of a stranger with thirty-one levels and a very poor bedside manner."

"Under somebody's judgment. Mine's better."

That startled a laugh out of him, real and brief and gone at once.

"I haven't accepted yet."

"Then don't waste time announcing it."

His mouth flattened. "You are not especially warm."

"I've got thirty-one. Shop somewhere else."

Another silence followed, shorter than the last.

"Fine," he said. "I accept the guide, the route, the pace, and your authority over ability use. I reserve the right to complain."

"Quietly."

"I can't promise that."

"You'll learn."

"I also don't trust you."

"Good."

The answer came so fast it almost made him smile again.

"I'd like to, eventually," he said. "That's inconvenient, isn't it."

Vel stood. "First lesson. Those boots are dead."

His gaze dropped. "I had noticed."

"Throw them away."

"What do I wear instead?"

"There's a trading post six miles south."

"With what money?"

Vel looked at the ring.

His hand closed over it on instinct.

"No," he said.

"It's metal."

"It's my House seal."

"It's boots."

He stared at her. His thumb turned the ring once, slowly. Then the hand came away from it and went to the front of his ruined outer robe instead.

"This clasp is silver," he said slowly. He worked the fastening at his throat loose, then the matched weights at his cuffs, each piece heavy enough to matter. "So are these. And the belt fittings." He held them in his palm and stared as if he had never once considered that clothing could be taken apart. "They were on me when I left. I assume traders enjoy hating the Hoarded as much as anyone else does."

Vel tipped one piece in her fingers. It had real weight. It was Vault work, ugly but valuable.

"Boots, socks, a cheap pack, dried food," she said. "If the trader hates your House, maybe less."

"That seems fair." He turned one piece over in his fingers. "These were just — what you wear to a Rite. I didn't think of them as money."

"Get used to the joke. It doesn't stop being true."

He bent to take the pieces he could strip without taking his clothes apart. When he straightened, the road made him hiss through his teeth.

"How far did you say?"

"Six miles."

"Barefoot."

"Yes."

"That's punitive."

"No. That's south."

He looked at her, trying to decide whether this was cruelty or information. With Vel it was usually both.

"All right," he said. "If I complain, does that count as my reserved right being exercised?"

"Depends how much."

He shoved the silver into a pocket, left the ruined boots by the log, and put his feet on the road.

Pain hit at once. It showed in the set of his shoulders more than his face. Pride had at least that much discipline left in it.

"Don't spend," Vel said.

"I wasn't going to."

"You were thinking about it."

"Yes."

"Don't."

They started south.

His pace was bad from the beginning. He was too careful to be efficient and too stubborn to stop. Gravel worked into split skin and made him breathe in small measured pulls. He kept going anyway. Vel noted that and refused to make it kindness.

The road ran through mixed forest thick with spring growth. Warm air carried mud, green bark, rot from the feast behind them. By full dark they would have boots, a pack on his shoulders, maybe enough dried meat to get through the next stretch. By dawn the road would tip upward into the highlands and he would learn new parts of his body the hard way.

Beside her, Kem limped south with the Aldraste ring still on his hand and the cost of his education rubbing raw under every step.

The number stayed with her: two hundred and eighty-six. It was too high to waste and high enough to alter what the end of this road might ask of both of them. She could not afford his ignorance and could not fully cure it either.

He was going to hate her later. That much was certain.

Later could keep its place.

For now he was walking south when he could have turned back, and she was walking beside him when she could have left him to wear himself down alone. That was enough movement for one day.

"Vel?"

"What."

"Is every practical fact in the world delivered in that tone, or is it just me."

"Just you."

He kept talking as they walked, and she let him. The road went south. The sun dropped. Neither of them looked back.
