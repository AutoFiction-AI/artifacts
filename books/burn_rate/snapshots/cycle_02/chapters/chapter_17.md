# Chapter 17

The wind hit first. Not a gust but a condition. A constant gritty pressure came off the Scour like breath from a furnace, carrying powdered rock and the flat mineral smell of laid-bare geology. Kem shielded his eyes with one hand, looked south, and felt the world drop out from under itself.

The Scour was a wound. Not a valley or a canyon. A wound, as if something had taken a bite from the earth and left the inside showing. The erosion basin stretched maybe two kilometers across, carved down through layered rock striped ochre, pale gray, and a deep oxidized red that looked like old blood in the summer sun. The walls fell away in terraces and shelves. Wind had cut them into overhangs, columns, and narrow fins of harder stone left standing after centuries of grinding. No vegetation. No shade. No cover. Sun bounced off the exposed rock so the heat came from everywhere at once.

Vel stood beside him at the ridgeline with one hand on her pack where the route journal sat. Her face did something Kem had learned to read over six weeks. Not emotion exactly. The registration of a cost she had not budgeted for. She looked at the basin floor the way she looked at everything: measuring.

"There it is."

Somewhere down there, eight years ago, Maren had reached zero. The threshold had opened, shown a flash of somewhere else, and closed. Vel had watched. She had been watching ever since, from sixty-two levels, from thirty-one, from twenty, from fourteen, burning herself down across eight years to follow a woman through a door that might never open twice.

Fourteen levels. That was what Vel had left. Kem had eighteen. Thirty-two between them. He had spent more than that on a single defensive perimeter during the agent fight south of the Spent Towns. In the basin below, the number no longer felt large enough to be stupid.

"I count four," Vel said. She was looking through a gap in the ridgeline rock toward the basin's navigable descent, a switchback path cut into the wall and the only ordinary way down. Two figures stood at the top of it. Two more waited on the floor near a cluster of small stone structures.

Not the untrained level-three-hundred agents who had accidentally detonated a grain silo and given themselves migraines. These were Red Reserve. The people who knew what abilities were for and had been permitted to use them.

"Two at the descent. Two on the floor. The descent pair are fresh. They're standing wrong. Too stiff. Too visible. They've been posted recently. The floor pair are permanent. Look at the camp."

Kem looked. The structures near the basin floor were not field shelters. They were low stone buildings with canvas-and-leather roofing, built for years of use. A fire pit with a carbon-black ring. A gear rack. Clothing on a line, bleached by sun and wind. Someone had been living here a long time. The polish on the path between the structures came from boots, not weather.

"Sable said permanent garrison," Kem said. "I was hoping she was being dramatic."

"Sable doesn't do dramatic."

"No. She does bureaucratic. Which is worse."

Vel almost made a sound that in another person would have been a laugh. "I need to map their positions properly. That's two levels."

Two levels out of fourteen. A seventh of everything she had left, spent on looking. But squinting from a ridgeline was not a plan.

"Do it."

Vel closed her eyes and spent. The air around her shifted with the subtle pressure change that came before a storm. When she opened them again, her gaze moved with inhuman precision, sweeping the basin in hard clean arcs.

"The commander is near the threshold point. Woman. Compact build. She's been patrolling the same strip for years. I can see the wear in the rock. Loops back and forth. Same route. Different times of day. Five years of that. The other permanent agent runs a circuit between the camp and the east wall. He's covering the tunnel exit. The descent pair are young. One keeps touching her gear."

The sharpness left her eyes. Vel sat down hard on the ridgeline rock. "Twelve."

Kem let the number settle. Twelve and eighteen. Thirty.

"I should mention," he said, "that at my current level I could perform the Conservatory Rite without anyone noticing. I'm finally discreet enough for a Hoarded ceremony. Took losing two hundred and eighty-two levels, but the form is improving."

Vel's mouth moved. The foundation of a smile without the architecture.

A voice rose from the basin floor. Not shouted. Carried.

"I can see you on the ridgeline. I've been watching you for thirty minutes."

Vel went still. Kem's hand moved toward nothing, an old reflex from when reaching meant options. He had twelve options left. Thirteen if he counted not using any of them.

"I know what you're here for," the voice called. "I'd like to talk before we do this the other way."

"She's alone down there," Vel said. The Perception was fading but not gone. "The circuit agent is at the eastern wall. The descent pair haven't moved."

Kem stood. "I'll go."

"If she—"

"Then you'll have twelve levels and no cascade partner and this will have been a very long walk for nothing." He kept his voice flat. Six weeks of approaching zero had burned the drama out of him. "But she announced herself. She asked to talk. That's a person trying not to spend."

"I hate procedure."

"No you don't. You hate other people's procedure."

He took the switchback descent. Tight turns. Worn stone. Thousands of patrols. His boots were held together by friction and optimism, and the soles had worn so thin he could feel the heat of each step.

The commander met him where the path leveled onto the basin floor. She was shorter than the voice suggested, compact and weathered, somewhere in her forties. Brown skin darkened further by five years of Scour sun. Calloused hands. Field clothes patched so many times the patches had patches. Hair cut short for wind, not fashion. She stood the way Vel stood, with her weight settled and her feet already where they needed to be.

At the edge of the camp, beside the gear rack, a row of stones sat on a flat shelf of rock. Seven of them. Different layers, different colors. Arranged with the care of a person who had been alone with rocks for five years and had started paying attention to them.

"Commander Osen," she said. "Red Reserve. Scour garrison."

"Kem Aldraste."

"I know. Your retrieval order has been sitting in my dispatch pouch for six weeks." She nodded toward the switchback. "Those two up there are for you. The Council sent them three weeks ago. They're competent enough."

"Just competent enough?"

"Nobody sends their best people to sit in a hole in the ground. The best people are back at the Vault not spending anything." She said it without bitterness. "I volunteered for this posting and renewed twice. People don't do that for promotion."

"Why do they do it?"

Osen looked past him at the featureless stretch of basin floor behind her. The threshold point, or what Kem assumed was the threshold point, because it looked like every other square meter of rock.

"Because I wanted to know if it was real." She turned back. "It's real. I've watched three people hit zero and go somewhere else. One breath they're standing there. Next breath they're not here." Her eyes stayed on him. "My orders are to prevent that from happening again."

The dispatch pouch at Osen's hip was worn white at the fold. Her patrol had polished a route into the stone. The seven rocks by the gear rack were lined from dark to pale with patient care. Kem thought of Sable and disliked how quickly the thought arrived.

"You came here to talk," Kem said. "Not about my retrieval."

"Your retrieval is a footnote. If you want to go north, the two agents at the descent will escort you with minimal incident. That's the easy version." Osen crossed her arms. "But you're not here for that, and the woman on the ridge isn't either."

"No."

"Then listen carefully, because I am trying to stop you before anybody starts spending." Her arms dropped again. "The threshold has degraded."

Wind pushed dust between them. Kem felt the map shift under his feet.

"Three years ago the Council sent a specialist to damp the site. Natural erosion had already started changing the basin. The dampening accelerated it. The readings have been wrong ever since if you're still using eight-year-old numbers."

"How wrong?"

"Wrong enough that two people going to zero here is no longer a crossing I'd bet a level on." Osen spoke like a field report. Spare. Exact. "At best you get a flicker. At worst you spend yourselves empty and the site does nothing."

Kem's mouth went dry. Above him, on the ridgeline, Vel would be listening. Even with her Perception fading, the basin carried sound, and Vel had spent forty years listening for the piece of information that would kill a plan.

Eight years of calculations. The cascade mechanism. Proximity. Descent rate. All of it built on a threshold that had been altered under her feet.

"What would make it hold?" Kem asked.

Osen gave him a long look. "More energy improves any mechanism. That is not the same thing as me briefing you through my perimeter."

"So there is a fix."

"There are theoretical compensations in a report I was never supposed to read. I'm not handing you a cleaner attempt. I'm giving you updated arithmetic so you stop walking toward bad odds and make me crack bones about it." She glanced toward the ridge. "If the woman up there believes two people can still do what happened here eight years ago, she keeps coming. If she knows the site was crippled on purpose, maybe she doesn't."

"The Council did that to keep people out."

"The Council did it so they could keep saying there was nothing here worth finding." No change in her voice. "I'm telling you because ignorance helps no one on this side of the basin. Not you. Not me."

Kem looked past her again at the bare rock. No marker. No altar. Just a strip of basin floor worn faintly smooth where Osen turned at the end of each patrol.

"And if we keep coming?"

"Then I do my job."

He believed her. That was the problem. She was not a liar trying to scare him off. She was a competent person telling him exactly how little chance they had left.

Kem turned and climbed back up the switchback. The basin wall threw heat at him like a second sun. By the time he reached the ridge, sweat had soaked his shirt and dust had turned to paste on the back of his neck.

Vel was still where he had left her, journal open on her knees. She had heard enough. Her face had gone blank in the specific way it did when feeling had been pushed off the surface so the work could continue underneath it.

"Two won't hold," she said.

"No."

"Did she give you anything useful?"

"She said — the Council sent someone. Three years ago. Damped the site on purpose." He sat down beside her. The rock burned through his trousers. "Your numbers are wrong. They changed it under you."

Vel looked down at the open page. Maren's handwriting crowded the margins with wind notes, rock notes, a small x at the cascade site, and the word *here* in a hand that had been alive eight years ago and was not here now.

"Eight years." She was quiet for a while. "I spent eight years walking toward a door they — eight years."

"Vel."

"The tunnel," she said. "The tunnel still gets us onto the basin floor east of the camp. Eight hundred meters to the threshold instead of dying on the switchback."

"And if two isn't enough?"

She shut the journal. Her hand stayed on the cover for a second. "Then we're standing there at twenty levels and it doesn't open. I don't know. Maybe it holds, maybe it — I can't tell you what the numbers are anymore."

"That's a terrible plan."

"Yeah."

Kem looked south. Four agents. Thirty levels between them, and that was before the tunnel took its share. "When do we move?"

"Two hours. Light behind us at the exit. In their eyes, not ours. Maybe that buys confusion. Maybe it buys nothing."

---

Three hours behind them, Sable Orm stopped on a northern ridge and looked down into the basin.

The Scour lay open below her in bands of exposed rock and hard light. She could see the garrison camp. She could see the permanent patrol lines worn into the basin floor. Farther east, two figures moved below the skyline toward an old mine cut in the wall.

Sable had been tracking for nine days. Two Perception spends had brought her here. Two hundred and thirty-six levels remained, enough for almost any official purpose and, if the report was right, enough to brace a damaged threshold.

During the third week of pursuit she had opened files no one thought to hide from an auditor. Threshold assessment. Dampening operation. Specialist report. The key line sat in her memory with clerical precision: *Two-contributor cascade insufficient at current degradation parameters. Three-contributor minimum recommended for stable threshold event.*

Vel and Kem were two. Sable was three hours away.

For nine days she had filed her movement under observation, intelligence gathering, confirmation of a retrieval target's final disposition. Below her, two figures cut for the old mine entrance and the patrol lines began to bend toward them. Observation stayed on the ridge.

She started down.

---

They moved laterally along the ridgeline as the shadows stretched, staying below the lip where rock met sky. Wind shoved grit into every seam of clothing. Kem's eyes watered until the tears cut clean tracks through the dust on his face. Up here the rock was sharper, less polished, with lichens and dead grass in the cracks. Thirty meters below, nothing lived.

The tunnel entrance was a horizontal scar in the wall, two meters high and three wide, its edges too clean for nature. Miners had cut it decades ago, extracting something the Hoarded found profitable enough to spend on and unimportant enough not to visit. The usual arrangement. Someone else's labor turned into someone else's preservation.

The ceiling sagged where old Reinforcement had long since faded. Rubble narrowed the passage to barely a meter. Cool air moved out of the dark carrying wet stone and old dust.

Vel knelt at the entrance with the journal open. "Maren mapped the interior. Main shaft for two hundred meters, then down and east. Three partial collapses and one full blockage. That was eight years ago."

"So it's worse."

"Probably."

"Good. I was worried this might be straightforward."

They stepped inside. The dark was immediate and total. Not night. Underground dark. Kem lit his palm. Warm light opened over rough walls, broken stone, and a ceiling that seemed offended by the responsibility of holding itself up.

Sixteen.

The first hundred meters forced them low. Then lower. The passage pinched into a crushed crawl where a slab the size of a wagon leaned across the tunnel on cracked bedding lines. Every shift of Kem's weight made stone answer stone.

Vel flattened beside the gap and looked once. "If that settles while we're under it, we're done."

"That's very clarifying."

"It's your spend."

Three levels for a structural Reinforcement. He had once spent more than that warming bathwater he had no intention of entering. Kem put both hands on the slab. Cool stone. Damp at the seam. When he spent, the subtraction came in three clean absences. The Ledger's hum thinned around them.

Thirteen.

"Go."

Vel slid under first. No hesitation. She trusted the Reinforcement and, worse, she trusted him to hold it. Kem followed with the light bobbing ahead of his face and rock scraping both shoulders. The slab groaned above his spine. When he cleared it, he dropped the hold. Stone settled hard behind them. Dust burst through the gap. The opening narrowed to a crack.

No way back.

The tunnel dipped. Water had been working the rock for years. The floor ahead had split into a dark slot crossed only by a spine of intact stone along the left wall. Kem tested it with one foot. The ledge sheared under him. His stomach dropped. Vel caught a fistful of his shirt and hauled him back before the rest of the edge went with it. The falling rock hit somewhere below after a long enough pause to feel personal.

"Don't do that," she said.

"I'll add it to the list."

Vel spent on Navigation. No light. No sound. Just a hard little tightening in the air and a change in her body. She put his hand on the wall and told him where to place each foot. Left edge. Crack. High step. Don't trust the flat stone. Her voice stayed flat and exact while more grit pattered into the dark below.

Ten.

The only continuation was a constriction where ceiling and floor had shifted toward each other until the tunnel seemed to close into stone. Vel looked at it, swore once under her breath, and spent again.

Evasion. Two more levels.

She went through like the gap had briefly remembered it was supposed to be a passage. Kem had to force his way after her, chest and back grinding on opposite walls. There was not room to panic. Panic required room. His father's life rose up stupidly in his head: broad halls, wide doors, a body preserved from every pressure except the one that had finally hollowed him out.

The tunnel widened just enough to breathe. Vel was waiting, one hand braced on the wall. At eight levels her hands had started to shake. Not fear. Ledger signal loss. The hum that had been with her for forty years was fading toward silence.

Heat replaced mineral cold. Wind touched the tunnel mouth ahead. Kem set a Ward at the exit, the lightest defensive shell he had left, enough to buy three seconds if someone was waiting outside.

Twelve.

That was everything he owned in the world. Between them they had twenty. Enough for a mistake and a defense, maybe. Not enough for invention.

They stepped out into late-afternoon sun. The basin floor hit like a fist made of heat and light. Red-gray rock, wind-scoured smooth, hot enough to feel through worn soles. Shallow erosion channels cut the floor into troughs and waist-high shelves of harder stone.

The threshold point lay somewhere eight hundred meters west, another patch of red-gray stone blanched by sun.

The garrison camp sat to the south-southwest. Stone buildings. Fire pit. Gear rack. Clothesline. The circuiting agent was between the camp and the eastern wall, closer than Kem wanted and moving on the line Vel had predicted.

The descent agents were still hidden behind the curve of the basin wall.

Commander Osen stood at the far end near the threshold point, waiting.

"Eight hundred meters," Vel said. Breathing hard now. Tunnel, heat, spending, all of it compounding in a body that had been counting for forty years.

"Open ground."

"Not quite." Vel pointed with two fingers. A shelf of wind-cut stone jutted from the floor twenty meters ahead. "That buys us ten seconds of thinking."

The circuit agent turned. Saw them. Shouted.

"Move," Vel said.

They ran.

It was not much of a run. Eight and twelve did not produce speed so much as refusal. Kem's boots slipped on polished stone. Heat struck up through his soles. The alarm bounced off the basin walls. By the time they hit the shelf of rock, the two descent agents were already appearing at the base of the switchback and fanning wide.

The stone gave them cover to the ribs and nothing else.

Across the basin, Osen's voice carried with the same terrible clarity as before.

"You can't make it work," she called. "It's not enough anymore."
