# Chapter 5

Two days since the outriders and Vel hadn't spent a point. Twenty-eight on the Ledger, enough if the Hoarded took their time, enough if Kem stopped reaching for levels every time the world asked for plain effort first.

By late afternoon his breathing had changed behind her. Yesterday's open-mouthed huffing had narrowed into careful pulls, each one worked around the cracked rib he'd carried out of the fight. The secondhand trader boots he'd gotten after abandoning the ruined corridor pair kept the stones off his blistered soles, but the left heel was rubbing him raw all over again. When the stream bent under a stand of hornbeam and gave her water, cover, and a strip of dry ground above the bank, she stopped.

"Here. Camp."

Kem let his pack slide down one shoulder at a time. Halfway through, the rib caught him. He froze with his teeth bared, waited out the white spike of it, then lowered the load to the ground so carefully it looked ceremonial.

"Sit," she said. Then, before he could, "No. Don't. If you lock up now, you'll stay locked."

"I'm not sure I have a vote."

"You don't. Fire. Food. Shelter. You're doing all of it."

His face went through disbelief, annoyance, and the tired acceptance she'd started trusting more than any promise. Getting from standing to a crouch took visible planning. So did standing back up. Good. The body would teach him faster than talk.

Vel dropped the fire kit onto the flat rock by the stream. The wrap held waxed cloth, striker, flint, char cloth, and a bundle of dry tinder she'd kept tucked under the pot. He looked down at it as if the arrangement of those objects had been designed to embarrass him personally.

"I've seen fire," he said.

"You've seen Flame Charms."

"I've seen fireplaces."

"Someone else's."

His mouth tightened. He picked up the striker and the flint and turned them in his hands as if an explanation might be printed on the metal.

She built the first tinder nest where he could watch, using dried grass, birch bark peeled from a deadfall, and twigs no thicker than knitting needles.

"Char cloth here," she said, setting it on the flint. "Strike down. When it catches, tuck it into the nest and breathe like you're warming cold fingers."

"That sounds simple."

"It isn't."

The first strike made nothing because the angle was wrong. On the second, the steel skipped and shaved the blister on his thumb instead of the flint. Blood welled at once, bright against the pale skin of hands used to paper and porcelain, not steel, and he sucked air through his teeth.

"Shit."

For a second his whole body leaned toward the easier answer. A Flame Charm would cost almost nothing at his current height. Maybe less than a level. The option hummed in him. So did the refusal. He reset the char cloth with his good fingers and tried again.

A spark flashed and died.

"Closer," she said.

Another miss. Another. Then the cloth took, ember orange at one corner. Surprise crossed his face so naked she almost laughed.

"Now the nest. Careful."

By the time he got onto his haunches, the rib had him breathing through his nose in thin controlled pulls. He transferred the cloth with both hands, blew too hard, and watched the ember flare and vanish.

"It was right there."

"It was."

"I had it."

"You did."

His shoulders dropped. For a beat she thought he might ask for permission to spend the level and get it over with.

Instead he built another nest. It was looser this time and better. A spark caught. His next breath came smaller. Smoke thickened between his hands, then a thread of flame found the bark and climbed. Kem made a small, startled sound under it, and she almost laughed.

She fed the new fire kindling until it could hold itself upright.

"Water first," Vel said. "Then there's a grouse in my pack. Wrapped in dock leaves. You're cleaning it."

The look he gave her was the same one he'd given the fire kit, only more betrayed.

---

The grouse hung from his hand, gray-feathered and cold, its neck at the wrong angle from where she'd wrung it that morning. He stared at it. The bird stared nowhere.

"What's first?"

"Pluck it."

A long time passed.

Feathers came away in clumps or single stubborn barbs. He pulled too lightly, then too hard, stripping skin along the breast until pink flesh showed through. Blood, down, and dust pasted themselves across his raw palms. Every few minutes he straightened halfway, pressed one hand to the bad rib, waited, and bent back to it.

She let him do it badly.

"Now gut it."

"I don't know where the cut goes."

She showed him where to start. The blade had to stay short and the belly cut shallow, and he wasn't supposed to puncture the intestines.

He punctured the intestines.

The stink hit and his whole face tightened. One swallow worked through his throat. Then the knife went back in. Guided by her hand and his own unwillingness to quit, he opened the cavity, hooked fingers inside, and pulled the wet length of the bird's insides free onto the rock beside him.

"Everyone does this?" he asked.

"Everyone who eats. Or somebody does it for them."

Something shifted across his face.

"Somebody did it for me for twenty-six years," he said. "Every bird, every fish, every clean plate after."

"Yeah."

Stream water rinsed the cavity. A green hazel switch became the spit. The branch sagged when he set it over the fire. He adjusted it wrong. Fat ran. Residual feathers curled black. Within minutes one side was charred and the top stayed raw.

"Turn it."

He did. The other side blackened. Reaching farther across the flame pinched the rib hard enough to make him stop and brace his free hand on his thigh. On the next try he lifted too fast, the spit rolled, and the bird dropped straight into the coals.

"God. Damn it."

He snatched for it and planted the heel of his hand in an ember. This time the swear came out with enough force to startle a bird from the brush.

Vel knocked the grouse back onto the stick with another branch before he could make the burn worse. Already a new blister was rising beside the older ones, clear fluid under soot and blood.

"It's ruined," he said.

"It's dinner."

"But look at it."

The grouse listed over the flames, black on two sides, ash in the skin folds, one pale strip still insultingly uncooked.

"I'm looking at it."

"Nobody would serve this."

"I'm not asking anybody."

She moved his hands into the right places, showed him how high to prop the spit and how slow to turn it. Under that kind of supervision the third side finally cooked through. Done enough, anyway. She cut it down onto the flat rock between them.

---

The bird tasted of ash, smoke, and punishment. Vel cut away the worst of the blackened skin with her knife and ate what was left. Across the fire, Kem chewed without complaint on meat his blistered hands had made badly and honestly.

Dark came down through the hornbeam branches a little at a time. The stream ran behind them, the fire burned in front, and no voices moved in the trees.

"In the Vault, meals arrived finished," Kem said after a while. "Plate. Garnish. Hot bread if someone thought hot bread went with it. I knew there were kitchens. I never followed that knowledge far enough to reach the bird."

"You've reached it now."

He looked at the crooked carcass between them. "Apparently by setting it on fire three different wrong ways."

"Still counts."

His mouth twitched. Then it flattened again.

"Can I ask you something?"

"You can ask."

"Why did you start?"

He meant something older than zero. He'd asked that question already and she'd shut him down.

"That's not route business."

"No."

"Then it's not your business."

"I know."

No push after it. No argument. He picked a scrap of meat free from one small bone and ate it anyway.

Vel dragged a strip of bark through the dirt with her thumbnail. Firelight kept catching on the pale ridges of his knuckles, the new burns, the half-dried blood. He'd spent the day hurting in ways he could have bought his way out of and, for once, hadn't.

"I was seventeen," she said.

The words slipped out because she was too tired to catch them.

"There was a canal in Avermouth. South of the Vault, before the Spent Towns start proper. Market day. End of it. People packing up. My mother had been selling cloth since dawn and I was carrying what hadn't moved."

She watched the bark curl under her thumb.

"A girl went through the rail on the towpath. Eight maybe. Nine. Rotten wood, bad footing, straight into the canal where it narrows under the bridge. Cold water. Early spring. Snowmelt still running down."

"How many people saw it?"

"Forty. Maybe more. Half Hoarded. One of them wearing a House seal on his coat, standing close enough to get splashed."

Kem went very still.

"Nobody moved. The Hoarded stayed dry. The Spent stayed dry with them. You could see the arithmetic go across every face. Three levels, four maybe, for a Strength pull. A wet coat. A ruined pair of gloves. Nobody wanted to be the first idiot in the water."

"And you were."

"Apparently."

She rubbed her thumb over the bark until it split.

"I went over after her. Seventeen, full at three hundred, too stupid to understand what that current could do. It took us both under the bridge. I spent one on a grip working, didn't know the proper name for it then, and got one hand around a piling and the other in the back of her dress. Stayed there until two men on the bank came in far enough to drag us out."

"One level."

"One. First one I ever lost. I came up with canal water in my nose, both palms torn open on the wood, and that seal-coat still dry. The girl's mother was screaming. Nobody thanked me. I was standing there at two ninety-nine."

The fire gave a soft crack.

"I came out of it at two ninety-nine," Vel said. "Next time something needed doing, I went to two ninety-eight. That's been forty years."

Kem rolled that around in silence. Night pressed closer. Across the stream something small moved through the brush and kept moving.

"The Hoarded would call that waste," he said.

"They would."

"My father would've said it cleaner than that. Fifteen years ago, when he could still finish a sentence."

"Tell me about him."

Ash striped the backs of his hands. He turned them over between his knees as if he'd found an answer there and needed a second look.

"My father's Ledger is three hundred," he said. "Always has been. Sixty-one years and not a point gone."

"I know what the Hoarded admire."

"It's more specific than admiration." His gaze drifted to the fire. "My place at the Conservatory Rite was beside him. Every Seventhday, for twenty-six years. Same chair. Same room full of untouched things. Same families proving what good stewards they were by not reaching for any of it. Him on one side of me. Three hundred on the Ledger. Less and less of him in the chair."

"Sounds like hell."

"It was normal." He took a short breath, careful around the rib. "At first he was loud. Argumentative. He'd fight with my mother over trade routes, over Council seats, over which families were circling which alliances. Then the sentences started coming shorter. They came out hollow, as if the words were drying out before they reached his mouth. He'd look at a point on the wall and stay there."

"The Hoarded liked that."

"They celebrated it. Uncle Aldris called it balance. The physician called it deepening discipline. My mother knew better. She had to. But if you say what it is out loud, then you have to say what all of it is. The rite. The houses. The whole preserved machine."

"So nobody says it."

"No. You sit beside your husband every Seventhday and watch more of him go missing and call that virtue." The branch in his fingers snapped before he seemed to notice. "Then your son stands up in the middle of the room and walks out, and his mother says his name once and lets him go. Maybe because stopping him would take action. Maybe because she wanted somebody to do one real thing in that room before it finished eating him. I don't know."

The broken bit of wood landed in the fire. Resin hissed. Light jumped up his face and dropped again.

"They'll say it was philosophy," he said. "Cleaner that way. What happened is my father is disappearing in public and everybody around him keeps calling it a gift. I sat there with them for fifteen years. I couldn't go back in next Seventhday and do it again."

For a while neither of them spoke. Kem rubbed ash off one knuckle with his thumb and only smeared it wider.

"Can he still talk?" Vel asked.

"Some days. Fewer now. The grammar's still there when it happens. The meaning isn't. It's like listening to somebody read from a page he can't see."

"And his Ledger?"

"Three hundred. Perfect."

The word sat there.

Vel nudged a stick deeper into the coals and waited until the end of it caught.

"Eat," she said.

So he did. The grouse was cold now, the fat turned white along the charred skin. When the last of it was gone, he stacked the bones in a small orderly pile and wiped his hands on the grass.

"That was the worst meal I've ever had," he said.

"Yeah."

"I made it."

"You did."

The line of bones held his attention for a while. Smoke had dried into his hair. Ash streaked one cheek. Under the dirt and soreness and fatigue, something in him had gone quieter.

"Thank you," he said. "For making me do it. All of it."

"Tomorrow you'll do it again."

He looked over. "All of it?"

"Fire. Food. And you're taking the heavy pack."

He took a careful breath and glanced toward his side before looking back at her.

"How heavy?"

"Thirty-five."

Silence.

"You've been carrying thirty-five pounds through this country with twenty-eight levels left."

"Yes."

His eyes moved to her bedroll, her pack, the scarred hands in her lap, as if he were trying to arrange the separate facts into one person he could recognize.

"All right," he said. "I'll take it."

Vel banked the coals and laid the heavier pack beside his bedroll so he couldn't miss it in the morning. His went close to the fire. Hers went between him and the trail.

The highlands stayed quiet.

From her blanket she could hear him settle by stages, each small adjustment negotiated around the rib. The stream kept running in the dark. Twenty-eight hummed low and steady at the back of her mind. In the morning he'd build the fire himself, shoulder the wrong pack, and hurt through it without buying the easy answer.

For tonight, that was enough.
