# Chapter 7

The road improved before the town appeared. For six days Kem had been walking on dirt tracks, game trails, whatever path Vel chose that kept them below ridgelines and out of sight. Now the ground under his boots was packed and graded, not anything the Vault would recognize as a proper road, but something a Shaper had laid down and people kept up. The surface was compressed and even, rainwater channels cut along both sides. Someone maintained it. The road existed because people kept spending on it.

Vel didn't comment on it. She adjusted her pack and kept walking, her pace the same efficient tempo she held whether the ground was flat or climbing, and Kem had stopped trying to match it and settled into his own stride, which was longer but less economical. His blisters had hardened into calluses over the last four days. That counted as progress.

The settlement came into view as they crested a long rise: a cluster of buildings in a shallow valley, sixty or seventy structures, with fields spreading out on three sides and a line of low hills to the south. Early summer light sat heavy on the rooftops, and the heat had a different quality here than in the forested highlands, drier and closer, the kind of warmth that pressed against exposed skin and stayed. It was the largest thing Kem had seen since the Vault, and it looked nothing like the Vault. The buildings were stone and timber, shaped but not uniform. Some walls curved where a Shaping ability had been applied freehand, others squared off with hand-laid block. Repairs showed as visible seams between ability-work and manual labor, and nobody had bothered to make the seams invisible. The Vault's architecture existed to deny that maintenance was necessary. This place wore its maintenance like working clothes.

What struck him was the sound.

The Vault was quiet. That was the first principle of Hoarded living: stillness, silence, the absence of exertion. Sound meant effort, and effort meant spending, and spending was failure. Kem had grown up in a place where raised voices were a scandal and footsteps were padded by design.

This settlement hummed.

A low, warm vibration ran under everything: Fertility Charms, he realized, active in the fields to the east. Not one but dozens, the combined output of farmers spending levels daily to make soil yield more than it would on its own. The sound was like standing near a hive, a thrumming he felt in his back teeth before he heard it with his ears. Layered over that came the crack and grind of Shaping work, someone reshaping stone on the settlement's far side, a distant rhythm of ability striking material. And closer, another pulse he couldn't identify at first. Rhythmic, gentle, warm. It rose and fell like breathing.

"What's that?" he asked.

Vel had stopped to let him look. She followed his gaze toward a low building on the settlement's southern edge, its door propped open with a stone. "Healing ward. They've got a healer who takes patients. That pulse is a Restoration Charm running."

"Running. As in, continuously?"

"She keeps it going for an hour each morning. Costs her a level every few weeks, depending on how many people she's working on."

A level every few weeks. If the healer was at, say, 180, she'd been spending steadily for years, maybe decades. Her entire adult life directed at making other people's bodies work properly. In the Vault, that woman would be considered reckless. They'd call her depleted. The kind of person polite families mentioned as a cautionary example: *she had such potential, but she spent it all.* In the Conservatory Rite, they would have seated her near the door, if they'd admitted her at all.

From where Kem stood, listening to the warm pulse of her ability carrying over the morning air, she sounded like the most useful person in the valley.

They walked in. The settlement's main road was wider than the approach, lined with buildings that served as both homes and businesses: a tanner's workshop with hides stretched on a frame outside, a grain store with its doors open and the smell of dry seed carrying out, a place that sold tools and rope and had been expanded three times in different decades, each addition visible in the stonework. People were out, working, moving with the unhurried competence of people doing familiar jobs, calling to each other across the road, carrying loads. A man with a crate on his shoulder nodded at Vel as he passed. She nodded back. Two women sat on a bench outside a doorway, shelling peas into a bowl between them, talking in voices too low for Kem to catch. One of them looked up at him, registered his height and his pallor, and went back to her work.

Nobody nodded at Kem. They looked, though. Briefly, the way people looked at a stranger who clearly didn't belong here, taking in the height, the pale indoor skin that was only now starting to redden across his nose and forearms, the clothes that hadn't been mended because they'd never been worn long enough to need it. He could feel himself being assessed and sorted: outsider, soft, sheltered. The last one was accurate enough that it landed.

A woman was repairing a section of wall on a building to their left. She had her hand flat against the stone and the material was moving under her fingers, bulging outward to fill a crack, settling back to flush. Her face was concentrated, her free arm braced against the wall for balance, and when she finished she stepped back and shook her hand out like the joints ached. A man on a ladder above her called down a criticism Kem couldn't catch, and she laughed and told him to do it himself if he was going to complain about her pointing.

She moved to the next section and started again. Each repair cost a sliver of level, not much at once but enough to add up. Every time she pressed her hand to the stone she was permanently diminished by some small measure. She was choosing to do it because the wall needed fixing.

Kem looked at his own hands. His Ledger pulsed at the edge of his awareness: 225. He'd bled levels on trivial things all week: a Flame Charm two nights ago because Vel's flint was damp and he hadn't wanted to wait, a Light Charm in a cave they'd sheltered in, a minor Strength boost on a steep climb that his legs could have managed if he'd been willing to hurt for it. Twenty levels gone on problems that matched what this woman was solving with her hands and a sore wrist.

"We need supplies," Vel said. She'd been watching him watch the settlement, but she didn't say anything about it. "Food for the next stretch south, and I need to ask about route conditions."

She led them to a tavern, broad and low, with a clay-tile roof and a yard where someone had set up benches under a canvas shade. A few chickens scratched in the dirt near the entrance. Inside was dim and cool, smelling of grain beer, cooked onions, and the sour bite of old ale worked into the wood over decades. Half a dozen patrons sat at tables, men and women in working clothes, eating midday meals or drinking slowly. They looked up when Vel and Kem entered. The looks settled on Kem in the same measuring way they had outside, held for a beat, and moved on. One man went back to his food. A woman at the bar turned fully to study him, then turned away.

The tavern keeper came from a back room, a crate of clay bottles braced against her hip. She was perhaps fifty, heavy-set, with her hair pulled back under a cloth and forearms thick from years spent lifting, pouring, and hauling stock. A leather brace wrapped her left knee, the kind of thing you wore for a joint that had been complaining for years and you'd stopped trying to fix. She set the crate on the bar with a practiced thud and looked at them.

"Passing through?" she said to Vel.

"Couple of days. We need dried food for six days' travel south. Whatever you've got."

"I can put that together." The keeper's eyes moved to Kem and took him in once. "Him too?"

"Him too."

She started pulling items from below the bar: wrapped packages of dried meat, hard bread, a pouch of grain that smelled nutty. Her hands were quick and practiced, no abilities, the motions of someone who'd stocked this bar a thousand times. Kem stood back while Vel negotiated quantities and price, and he let his eyes move around the room. The tavern was plain but swept clean. Decorative grooves had been shaped into the stone walls near the ceiling, a small touch of craft that had cost a fraction of a level and was worth it to whoever lived here. The tables were scarred from use. A cat slept on a chair near the window, its tail hanging off the edge.

The keeper set the food packages on the bar and named a price. Vel counted marks from a leather pouch, Kem's marks, technically, converted from the portable valuables he'd taken from the Vault. Coins and small trade goods that amounted to more money than most people in this room earned in a season. Vel paid without comment, and the keeper sorted the coins into a box beneath the counter.

Then her eyes dropped to Kem's right hand, resting on the bar.

His House signet sat on his finger, silver, heavy, carved with the Aldraste crest, the still flame, the mark of preservation. He'd taken it from the ceremonial stand at the Vault gate ten days ago, not because he'd decided to bring it but because leaving it would have meant he'd thought the whole thing through. He'd been wearing it since. It was his father's crest and his grandfather's. In the Vault, it had been the kind of thing you wore without ever looking at.

The keeper's face changed.

It wasn't anger. It was a practiced withdrawal. The professional courtesy she'd been showing Vel drained out of her expression and what replaced it was flat and careful. She looked at the signet, then at Kem's face, then at the signet again, and her mouth became a line. The crease between her eyebrows deepened. She was a woman who had seen that crest before, or crests like it, and had concluded something about its bearers long ago, and nothing about Kem's sunburned face or his awkward posture in her tavern was going to reopen the question.

She finished stacking the food packages. She pushed them across the bar toward Vel, not Kem. "Anything else?" she said, and the temperature of her voice had dropped to a place where *anything else* meant *please leave*.

"No," Vel said. "That's fine."

Kem opened his mouth. He didn't know what he was going to say. An explanation, an apology, some version of *I'm not what you think,* which was absurd because she was looking at the physical proof of exactly what he was. He closed his mouth. The keeper picked up her empty crate and went back through the doorway without looking at him again.

He stood there with his hand on the bar and the signet on his finger and the silence the keeper had left in her wake. The man at the nearest table glanced over, saw the ring, and looked away, finished with him already.

Kem looked down at the signet. The silver flame was still crisp, still bright. The keeper had looked at it the way people looked at rot in a grain sack. He covered it with his other hand.

Vel was shouldering her pack. She'd seen the whole thing. She didn't turn toward him or offer commentary. She gave him room to stand in it.

"I didn't know," he said, once they were outside.

Vel squinted against the midday sun. "You didn't know what?"

He started to answer and stopped. The keeper's face came back to him, the neat way it had closed. The ring had done all the talking.

"Any of it," he said. "What any of this looked like from out here."

"Yeah." She adjusted a strap on her pack. "Come on. I need to find someone about the southern routes."

---

They found Dren at the market yard.

Vel had stopped at the settlement's well and asked a man drawing water about a trader who knew the country south of the Spent Towns. The man pointed them west. "Dren. He's loading up over there. Supposed to head east tomorrow, I think."

"We need him heading south."

The man shrugged. "Good luck. He goes where the money is."

The market yard was a packed-dirt clearing on the settlement's western edge where carts and pack animals and stacked goods created a working chaos Kem found difficult to parse. People argued over quantities, loaded crates, inspected produce. A boy chased a loose goat between two carts and crashed into a woman carrying a bolt of cloth, who swore at him with the rote exhaustion of someone who'd chased that same goat herself last week and would probably chase it again tomorrow. A man was using a minor Shaping ability to repair a cracked cart wheel, the wood swelling and sealing under his palm, a job that would've taken a carpenter half a day and here took thirty seconds at the cost of a fraction of a level. The spending was constant and casual, part of the working rhythm, and nobody around treated it as anything other than getting things done.

Dren was loading his cart near the yard's far fence. He was shorter than Kem had assumed, stocky and road-darkened, with hands that looked like they'd been tanned alongside the leather goods stacked behind him. A scar ran along the outside of his left forearm, old and faded, the kind of thing you got from a rope that slipped or a crate edge that caught you wrong. He worked with the efficiency of a man who'd done this job for twenty years, each item placed where it balanced the load, each tie cinched with a knot that looked permanent. His boots were battered, resoled at least once, the leather cracking at the ankles. He wore a belt crowded with gear: a knife, a water flask, a coil of cord, a pouch Kem couldn't identify.

Vel walked up to him directly. "You Dren? I'm told you know the routes south."

He looked up from a strap he was tightening. His eyes went over Vel first, quick and practical: the knife, the road dust, the fact that she came straight to his cart instead of looking for someone else to ask. Then he looked at Kem longer, at the signet, at the expensive cloth gone dirty at the cuffs, at the half-step of hesitation before he settled where he was standing.

"I know them," Dren said. He went back to his strap. "Southern routes aren't popular. Spent Towns thin out after two days. Then it's rough hill country and then you're in the Margins, and nobody goes to the Margins who doesn't have to."

"We're going on purpose," Vel said.

"Figured." He tested the strap with a sharp tug. "You and the lord."

"He's not —"

"He's Hoarded. I can tell by the way he's standing. He's standing in my market yard like he's waiting for someone to bring him a chair." Dren looked at Kem. "No offense."

"I'm not waiting for a chair," Kem said.

"Sure. What are you paying?"

Vel named the sum. Kem watched Dren's face as the number landed, a flicker around the eyes, the mental arithmetic of a man who already knew where the money would go before he had it. The amount was generous. Better than an eastern trading run. Better than two.

Dren straightened up from his cart. He was doing calculations behind his eyes, and Kem recognized the process because he'd watched Vel do it: the same weighing, the same tallying. But where Vel counted levels, Dren counted marks, sale prices, and days away from home.

"South to where?" Dren asked.

"Past the settlements' southern edge. Through the hill country to the rougher terrain beyond. We need someone who knows the water sources and the safe camps."

"I know the water. Terrain I can manage. What's in it is the hard part. Bandits. Desperate folks drifting north from the Margins. People who'll take your pack while you sleep and feel bad about it later." He squinted at Kem. "He know how to handle himself?"

"He's learning," Vel said.

"How fast?"

"Fast enough."

Dren didn't look convinced. He rubbed his jaw with the back of his wrist, leaving a streak of road dust on his cheek that he didn't bother with. "How long are we talking?"

"Five, six days to the edge. Then you're done and you head back."

"I was heading east tomorrow. Buyers in Callowfield, reliable contract. That's money I can count on. You're asking me to dump a sure job for a one-off into country I don't especially like."

"I'm asking you to take good money for a week's work. You know the route."

"I know it. That's why I don't like it." He leaned against his cart and crossed his arms. His forearms were thick from years of loading cargo, and the old scar caught the light. "Look. My girl's sick. Mira." He said it to the wheel rut by his boot instead of to either of them. "She's eight, and her legs — there's a healer up north who can treat it, but she costs more than I've got. Every run I take is part of getting there." He looked at the space between himself and Vel. "What you're offering gets me closer than Callowfield would. But Callowfield doesn't go south."

"It's six days," Vel said. "We're not looking for trouble."

"You're paying me double an eastern run to go south fast. I've been hauling cargo twenty years." He looked at Kem. "He's wearing a Hoarded signet in Spent Towns. That's not six days I want to sign up for."

A silence opened. The market yard moved around them. Carts loaded, people argued, the boy chased his goat back the other way. Dren waited in the middle of it with the patience of someone who'd spent years in negotiations and knew the first person to fill a silence usually paid for it.

"There are people looking for him," Vel said. "They're behind us. If we move smart, they stay behind us."

Dren absorbed this. His expression didn't change. "Hoarded retrieval?"

"Close enough."

"Great." He scratched the back of his neck. The gesture pulled his collar aside and showed more road-darkened skin, a life lived outdoors. "So if they catch up, I'm the bastard standing next to him."

"We've handled them before," Vel said.

"How many levels did 'handling them' cost?"

Vel didn't answer immediately, and that was its own kind of answer. Dren caught it.

"Right." He uncrossed his arms. "Your price, six days, south route. I want half up front and the other half before I turn back. And I need the morning to offload what I can from the cart and store the rest."

"We leave tomorrow morning," Vel said.

"Tomorrow morning means I'm selling at a loss. Anything I can't move by tonight sits in someone's shed and I'll be lucky to get two-thirds of it back. That cost comes out of your side."

Vel looked at Kem. Kem looked at the pouch on her belt. His Vault money, converted into the currency of a world where people worked for what they had.

"Agreed," Kem said.

"Agreed," Dren said. He stepped away from the cart and took a fuller measure of Kem, like he was deciding whether the load was worth the road. "You ever carried a pack for more than a day before this?"

"Ten days now."

"How's it feel?"

"My shoulders hurt."

Dren's mouth twitched once, the closest he'd come to approval. "Tomorrow morning. I'll be here at first light." He turned back to his cart, then stopped. "And put that ring somewhere it can't catch the light. I don't need the southern settlements deciding I'm running escort for a Hoarded inspection."

He started untying the ropes on his cargo, already doing the math on what to sell and what to store. Vel turned and headed for the edge of the market yard. Kem followed, adjusting his pack where the straps bit into the muscle at the base of his neck. The ache ran from there into the shoulder joint, the kind Vel had told him would eventually become background noise. It hadn't yet.

They found a spot outside the settlement to camp for the night, in a grove of trees where the ground was soft with leaf litter and the air smelled of warm earth and dry grass. Vel built a fire the way she always did, flint and dry tinder, no abilities, each motion practiced. The settlement sat below them in the fading light, its shapes softening as evening came on. The Fertility Charm hum had quieted as the farmers stopped for the day, but the healing ward's pulse still ran, faint and steady, carrying over the distance.

Kem sat on a rock near the fire and turned the signet on his finger. The metal was warm from his skin and from the long day's sun. He thought about taking it off, putting it in his pocket. Then he pictured the keeper pushing the food toward Vel and left it where it was.

He left it on.

"Vel."

She looked up from feeding a stick into the fire. The flames caught the lines around her eyes, the gray in her hair, the old scars on her knuckles.

"How much have you spent since you found me? Total."

She considered. "Three. Outrider fight. Nothing since."

"So you're still at twenty-eight."

"Yeah."

Kem checked his own Ledger. The number sat at the edge of his awareness and came into focus when he reached for it: 225. He'd been 300 eleven days ago. Seventy-five levels gone. Vel had spent three in the same stretch, and all of them with people trying to kill them.

"I used a Flame Charm two nights ago," he said. "Because the flint was damp."

"I know."

"That was nearly a level."

"Close."

"That was a patch of wall for her."

Vel poked the fire. A log shifted and dropped a spray of sparks.

"Yeah," she said.

He waited. She fed another stick to the fire. Nothing else came.

The healing ward's pulse faded as the evening deepened. Somewhere in the settlement a door thudded shut. A dog barked once. Insects took the grass around them.

When the dark finished moving in under the trees, Kem kept his hands in his lap. He did not call a Light Charm. He sat there until the ring went dull on his finger.
