# Chapter 18

Nearly three hours after the commander told them the threshold might fail, Vel and Kem had two hundred meters left and nowhere decent to hide.

The sun had slid west. Long ridge shadows striped the basin floor. Heat still leaked out of the layered rock, but the hard white glare from before was gone. The commander had spent those hours doing the same thing over and over: cutting every clean line west and forcing them sideways, keeping any level they burned too far from the threshold to matter. Vel had answered with patience, bad footing, and blood instead of spending.

She crouched behind a rib of sandstone that covered half a shoulder if a person believed in miracles. Her left ankle hurt from a bad landing. Her mouth tasted like old dust. Kem lay three meters off her left knee, breathing through his mouth, chin already bloodied from one earlier fall, still at twelve. She still had eight. Four Reserve agents held the basin. Two garrison fighters ahead. The blink-step specialist circling south. The commander at the point itself, patient as drought.

High on the north scree, gravel hissed down in a narrow, steady ribbon. Too regular for wind. The commander looked that direction once and looked away. She kept every agent on the basin floor. Nobody could break from the line to investigate the ridge and still keep Vel and Kem off the point.

"You hear me say spend, you spend," Vel said.

Kem wiped blood off his mouth with the back of his hand. "And if you don't?"

"You use your body."

He nodded once. "I can — yeah. Okay."

"Only plan we've got."

She touched the rock, then went. Kem moved with her.

The first Combat Pulse cracked the stone where his head had been. He had already thrown himself sideways. Ugly move. Late by a breath. Alive. Six weeks ago he would have taken it full in the face.

Vel spent one level on perception.

The basin snapped into hard focus. Northeast garrison behind a low shelf. East garrison building a suppression lane. Blink-step specialist closing from the south. Commander holding back, saving her own hand.

"Left."

Kem cut left without asking.

The suppression lane thickened a half second too slow and took only Vel's calf instead of her whole stride. It felt like running through wet sand. She tore free. A force beam scorched past. Hot stone spat chips across her shoulders. One punched into the flesh under her shoulder blade and stayed there.

The specialist came in fast, arriving first as a gap in the basin and then as a body on her left, shoulder already turned into the punch.

Vel got her forearm up. The hit ran through bone and tendon. Her wrist gave with a wet pop. Pain flashed white, then narrowed. She let the blow turn her instead of trying to stop it. The agent overcommitted, skidded, and slammed shoulder-first into the rock.

"Keep moving," she said.

Another Pulse shattered the shelf behind them. Dust jumped. Through it Vel saw the specialist loading a second blink and his partner closing to receive it.

She spent two levels together, one to catch the blink, one to kick its exit point backward and dirty.

The specialist vanished and reappeared twenty meters the wrong way, straight into his partner. The collision burst bright and concussive. Both men went down in the dust.

Five left.

Losing levels this low felt local, like a part of the body going out and staying out.

Kem was up again, running bent and quick, using the body he had built mile by mile. He had learned to read a shoulder before an ability. He had learned to fall without dying. That was worth more than anything the Vault had ever taught him.

High on the north scree, the ribbon of falling stone started again, lower now.

The commander chose that moment to stop waiting. Vel felt the charge through her teeth before she saw the light gather in the woman's hands, a killing line tuned for this basin and this distance.

Vel was off-angle. Too far east. Wrist blown, ankle half numb, five levels left and no clean geometry.

Kem saw it. He stepped between them and spent five.

The shield he threw came up as a wall, four meters wide and canted just enough to catch the beam and throw the worst of it away.

The impact tore rock out of the basin. Vel hit the ground on her bad shoulder. The buried chip drove deeper. Dust filled her mouth. When she looked up, Kem was on both knees, palms split open from bracing, down from twelve to seven.

"Up," Vel said.

He made a noise that might have been a laugh. "I'm — shit. Give me a second."

---

He came up loose with pain, hard to carry cleanly, all bad angles and sudden dead weight while Vel's own body was close to the end of its good behavior. She got under his arm with her good side and dragged him moving.

The garrison pair closed. These were basin workers, not rich amateurs with too many levels and no sense. Less flourish. Better placement. One caught Kem's ankle in a binding and dropped him face-first into the rock. His chin split wider. The other cracked the ground between them, opening a narrow crevice meant to keep their levels from touching in the same place.

Vel spent one level on misdirection and let the next binding catch an afterimage. Empty stone took the hit.

Four left.

"Kem, move."

He tore the binding loose with both hands. Skin came off under his nails. He got up anyway, one side of him holding itself too carefully.

The commander called from the threshold. "I've watched three people try to reach this point. You can still walk away."

Vel kept running.

Kem answered as he answered now: by doing the thing in front of him.

The blink-step specialist came again from the south, loaded for a kill. Kem waited until the blink took hold. Then he spent two levels and pinched the whole ability stack dark.

The specialist stumbled out of transit at ordinary speed, ordinary strength, suddenly just a man on bad footing. His punch clipped Kem's shoulder hard enough to hurt and nowhere near hard enough to end him.

Kem hit him back. Once. Twice. The man's head cracked on layered stone and stayed down.

Five.

The ribbon of gravel on the north wall had become a figure now, tiny but human, coming down the ugly route on purpose. The commander saw it too. She still did not turn an agent to deal with it. If she thinned the line for even a few seconds, Vel would have the point.

Another garrison binding started for Kem. Vel spent one level and drove the caster's own frequency back through it. The line snapped sideways into his ribs. He folded where he stood, both hands clamped to his side.

Three left.

The lane opened.

"Now."

Kem caught her forearm and spent three levels on motion.

The basin disappeared under them. Stone blurred. Wind tore tears out of Vel's eyes. One heartbeat, then another, and the threshold point was under their feet.

Kem let go. He had two. She had three. Behind them the wounded agents were coming hard. In front of them the commander was close enough for Vel to see the threadbare places in her windscarf and the disbelief under her anger.

---

Vel spent the first of her last three levels into the ground.

The stone answered with a deep vibration, old and hungry.

She spent the second to hold it open. Air thinned over the unmarked point. The shape of the world started to slip.

The commander lunged, building a containment field on instinct, trying to catch Vel above zero and freeze the whole attempt there.

Vel spent the last level before the field closed.

She had lived fifty-eight years under the Ledger and spent forty-one of them on purpose. Both counts ended here.

Zero.

Silence hit.

The threshold tore open.

It held badly. Ragged edges. Fraying light. Through it Vel saw hand-cut stone, packed-earth streets, people moving under a sky that had never carried the Ledger's hum.

She stepped through.

The crossing took one stride. On the other side the air smelled of dust and herbs. Silence lay over the whole place, settled into the stone and the air.

She turned.

Kem was still in the basin, on one knee at the edge of the tear, blood on his chin and hands. The opening was already shrinking. Two people had not been enough.

He had two levels left. The commander slowed beside him, not attacking now, just watching the door fail.

"Stay where you are," she said. "It's closing and you don't have enough."

Kem looked at Vel through the narrowing gap with the flat concentration he got when the math was bad and still had to be done.

The north-scree noise hit the basin floor at last as a rush of loose stone and hard breathing.

Then seven fresh levels slammed into the cascade.

Sable Orm came off the scree with both hands up, gravel still running around her boots. Dust covered the front of her field coat. Blood shone at one palm where the rock had taken skin. She looked like a woman who had skidded three hours down bad rock and meant to spend more before the night was over.

She spent.

The first two levels steadied the tear. The next ones widened it. By the seventh the frame was holding, ugly but real.

The commander turned to her. "Auditor."

"I am —" Sable stopped. "Do not say anything right now."

She did not look at the commander again. She looked through the opening at hand-cut stone, packed earth, and people moving under that silent sky.

"Go," Vel said.

Kem spent his last two levels.

There was no flare to it. Just the final shove of energy into a failing mechanism and then the clean blankness that took his face when the Ledger left him.

He crossed.

He hit the packed earth stumbling. Vel caught him with her good arm. He hissed around the bad rib and almost went down anyway.

"Hey," Vel said.

He tried to laugh. The rib turned it into a hiss. "We — yeah."

Behind him, Sable stood on the basin floor at 229, hands lowered, wind dragging at her ruined hair. She stayed where she was while the agents regrouped. The commander looked from Sable to the place where the tear had been and said nothing.

The threshold folded in on itself. The Scour vanished. Silence stayed.

Somewhere in the stone streets behind Vel, a voice called out. Then another, closer. Footsteps started toward them.

Vel kept one hand on Kem's arm and looked toward the nearest turn in the street.

She waited for Maren to come around it.
