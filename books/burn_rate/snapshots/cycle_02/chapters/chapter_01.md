# Chapter 1

The farmer's shin was broken in two places. Bone showed through the split skin above his left ankle. Infection had already climbed from the wound, a band of heat and redness running up the calf, the flesh shiny with fluid that smelled sweet and wrong. Three days, his wife had said. A wall collapse in the grain shed. Six hours under timber and stone before anyone got him out, and the settlement had nothing better than boiled rags and willow-bark paste.

Vel knelt beside the cot on the packed-earth floor and set one hand above the break, one below. Hael made a noise through his teeth but held still. Forty, maybe. Hands built by soil and lumber, nails cracked dark with ground-in dirt.

"Still," she said. "Don't move the leg."

The ability came when she reached for it. The Ledger shifted. Behind her sternum, something changed weight. Thirty-three became thirty-two.

Heat moved from her palms into the ruined leg. Damage resolved itself under her hands: two slant fractures through the tibia, splinters loose in the swollen tissue, infection gone deeper than she'd judged. It had reached the marrow. It had reached the exposed bone.

"Shit," she said.

Hael's wife jerked her head up from the window. "What?"

Maret had been wringing the same cloth since Vel arrived. Her knuckles stayed white around it. Missed meals had pared her down. The dress she wore had been let out once and taken in again, the seams visible where somebody had redone the stitches by hand. That morning she had offered Vel bread anyway, and it had been the best thing in the house.

"It's deeper than it looked. I'm pushing again."

"Does that cost you more?"

Vel put both hands flat against the bone and didn't answer.

Thirty-two became thirty-one.

The infection resisted with the blind stubbornness of anything that had been eating flesh for three days. Vel drove through it. Bacteria died in hot waves. Swelling dropped under her hands. Redness drained back down the calf. Fracture lines knit. Splinters pulled inward and fused. By the time she let go, Hael's breathing had gone from measured pain to startled relief.

Her hands shook when she sat back. That tremor always came after ability use, the body's way of registering permanent loss. She wiped her palms on her trousers and looked at the leg.

The leg was functional now, scarred but usable. He'd limp for a week and ache longer than that, but he would walk. He would work. He would haul water from the well and brace his own roof the next time it tried to kill him. The settlement needed that more than it needed almost anything.

"That's it?" Hael said.

"That's it. Stay off it today. Tomorrow you can move around. Nothing heavy for a week."

He touched the scar, pressed too hard, winced, and pressed again. Gratitude came over his face slowly, as if pain had to clear room for it. Vel wanted out of the house before it finished landing.

Maret crossed to the cot and laid a hand on her husband's forehead, checking for fever. The trembling hadn't left her yet. When she caught Vel looking, she tucked the hand behind her back.

"What do we owe you?"

"Whatever you can manage."

Maret went to the tin over the stove. With the care of someone who knew the number before she opened it, she counted out seven marks. The marks made a thin sound. It was thin money. Then she brought down a jar of preserved cherries, cloudy glass, fruit still a hard dark red.

"The cherries are good," Maret said. "I put them up last autumn, before the —" Her mouth closed. "They're good."

It had all been before the fever, before eleven people died and the Hoarded sent no one, before the grain contract got cut down to match the settlement's new usefulness. Everybody in the room knew the sentence she had stopped inside.

Vel took the marks and the jar. "Thank you."

Her pack waited by the door, canvas worn soft, straps mended with cord and old leather. Vel checked the main pocket from habit and stepped out into late spring sun.

Turned earth and hedgerow bloom rode the air. Twenty houses sat around a well with a crumbling stone lip and a winch held together by rope and a bent nail. South of the houses, fields ran toward an untrimmed tree line. Wheat and barley had been planted in alternating strips, but whole patches had failed where the ground had gone mean or the workers had gone missing.

The grain shed stood half-collapsed on the edge of the road. Hael's wall had folded inward. Roof beams split along the grain. Nobody here had the levels to Shape it back together. Nobody had the spare labor to rebuild it by hand. Their grain sat in a lean-to behind the meeting hall instead, open to damp and rats. Vel had seen settlements die in increments like that.

Kids played by the well with a length of rope, tossing it back and forth after the game had already gone stale. One stared. One waved. Vel raised a hand back. Both had the same narrow shoulders Maret did. Farther down the road, a woman reset a fence post with a flat stone because the settlement's only hammer had broken and nobody had marks for another.

The Hoarded had once kept this place upright with Fertility Charms and a grain contract mean enough to be useful. Rust-fever took eleven people in six weeks. The next contract came back thinner and brought no extra labor or timber. No one from the Vault came to look at the caved-in shed or the wet grain slumping under canvas.

Vel sat on the low stone wall by the well and ate one of the cherries. Tart. Brandy, maybe, or something trying its best to be brandy. Maret had put up cherries before the fever and traded the jar for a leg. Syrup stuck to Vel's thumb.

Thirty-one levels remained.

The number lived behind her sternum with the steadiness of an old injury. At three hundred she had barely noticed it. At two hundred it had been background. At one hundred she had started counting on purpose. Below fifty the count kept time with her pulse.

Forty-one years of spending had brought her here. Healing jobs. Fights. Shelter work. Flood rescues. Fires. Bones. People dragged out of water or rubble because nobody else would spend on them. The hard part still lay ahead.

Vel had meant to use one level here. Bone-set, infection clear, tissue repair, back on the road. At thirty-three, one level could still be filed under margin. At thirty-one, it sat in the account like a missing tooth.

Her hands were still shaking. They would stop in an hour.

---

The settlement gave her a room at the back of the meeting hall, really a storeroom with the sacks shoved out and a cot dragged in. Burlap dust hung in the mattress. The window never closed all the way. Olla had said the north trader might come through before dark, so Vel had taken the room instead of starting for the next village with one level missing from her plan.

Supplies spread across the floorboards: water skin, dried beans, knife, needle kit, two rolls of bandage. Vel knew the pack by weight already. The other count mattered more.

That left her with thirty-one levels.

Each night for eight years she had run through what those levels could still buy. Healing came first. Three good applications, maybe four, before the ability degraded into something fit only for shallow cuts and fevers caught early. A few defensive tools remained on the combat side: Redirect, Perception, a short Conceal if she needed half a minute to disappear. Utility had held up better. Endurance still covered the long marches. Nightsight and Weatherskin remained. She could still travel. Carelessness would strip the rest off her.

Thirty-one narrowed everything. A kit with the expensive tools left in it and no room for mistakes.

Eight years ago she had watched a door open at zero. Since then every private calculation had bent toward the Scour. Its location was clear enough. Route notes sat in her pack. What waited there was clear too. Zero was passage.

The math remained ugly.

One person reaching zero could trigger the threshold. That much she had seen. The crossing itself had been brief and ugly, a flicker more than an opening. To hold it steady, the descent needed more force than one exhausted body could produce. Two people spending down together. A cascade. Enough draw to keep the door open long enough for a clean crossing. Her side of the equation was already thin. The other side needed height.

What she needed was a partner. Someone starting far above her. Someone willing to burn everything.

That was the impossible part.

Years had gone into careful questions in settlements, in Spent Towns, at trader fires, at the edges of the Margins where old explorers still remembered names. Any Hoarded defectors? Any lordlings missing off a north road? Anyone at two-fifty or higher who had walked out and stayed out? The answer had always been no or something close enough to no to kill a week. The Hoarded did not leave. They rotted in place and called it discipline.

Time had narrowed while she searched. Every job took levels. Every emergency took levels. Every stop where she was the only person within a day's walk who could do the hard thing took levels. At thirty-three she had still had room to absorb the world. At thirty-one, the world was beginning to eat the plan faster than she could protect it. Below twenty, her side of the cascade would fail.

Now mattered more than a better plan.

If Sorren brought nothing tonight, she would leave at first light for the trading post two days north and keep listening there. That had been the whole point of waiting. North road traders were useless for most things and good for one: they heard trouble early. Somebody always heard a courier ride too hard, a house carriage overturn, a noble go missing, an estate hire more guards than it should.

Vel packed half the kit back into the bag and sat for a while on the cot's edge. She had thirty-one levels, eight years behind her, one shrinking margin, and a southbound route she still trusted more than she trusted luck.

By the time the light in the window had gone gold, Sorren's cart had come into the yard.

---

Vel stayed for supper because that was what she had been waiting for.

The meal was boiled grain with root vegetables and enough rosemary to disguise how thin it all was. Four settlement adults sat with her at the meeting-hall table. Sorren took the fifth place and set his spoon down like a man already tired of holding it.

He looked the part of a route trader. He was compact and fifty-something, with one shoulder drawn a little high from decades of leading a loaded cart with the same arm. Dust sat in the lines of his neck. His mule looked no fresher than he did.

Road talk opened the meal: crop failures, mud at the eastern fork, which settlements still had lamp oil. Olla handled most of it. White hair, palsied hands, memory sharp enough to hold thirty years of field rotations without writing any of it down. She worked traders with the patience of somebody who had been doing useful gossip extraction since before Sorren grew his first beard.

"The eastern road still passable?" Olla asked.

"Passable enough," Sorren said. "Mud past the fork. They had rain."

"And Cairn Hill?"

"Standing. Better than most."

Vel ate a few bites, then put her spoon down. "Anything odd farther north?"

Sorren looked at her over the bowl. "Odd how?"

"Hoarded traffic. Couriers. House caravans. Anything running harder than it should."

That bought her half a glance from Olla. Vel ignored it. Too many years of asking careful questions had burned the shyness out of her.

Sorren tapped his spoon once against the rim. "Actually, yes." He looked around the table, deciding if the story was worth the room. "Cairn Hill was full of it at midday. Vault courier came through blowing a horse to foam. Said a Hoarded lord had walked out."

Nobody spoke for a beat.

"Walked out," Olla said. "Out of the Vault?"

"That's the report."

"What does that mean?"

"Means he used an ability at the gate. First one in his life, if you believe the courier. Blew stone loose in the gate hall and walked through the dust." Sorren took another bite, chewed, swallowed. "House Aldraste. The son."

"The preservation house," Olla said. "Three hundred for six generations."

"Six that they'll admit to."

"How long ago?" Vel asked.

Sorren's eyes came back to her. The question had landed too fast. Too interested. He heard it.

"Today," he said. "Morning, maybe. Noon by the time it hit Cairn Hill. Courier changed horses in Breck's yard and kept going."

The room stayed quiet in a different way now. Less disbelief. More rearrangement.

"And after?" Vel asked. "Which road?"

Sorren lifted one shoulder. "Depends who was lying. Courier said north. A carter swore he heard east. Breck thinks the house man didn't know and picked a direction that sounded decisive." The spoon tapped again. "What everyone agrees on is that the boy burned levels doing it. Ten, twelve in the first stretch. Maybe more. And every fool in Cairn Hill started calculating what a Hoarded runaway might be worth."

"Worth to who?" Olla asked.

"To the Hoarded if you hand him back. To the rest of us if you hide him just to spite them. Breck sold feed to two riders before I left, and neither looked like farmers." Sorren scraped the bowl clean. "Fresh news turns a road ugly fast."

Vel said nothing.

Olla leaned back in her chair. "If it's true."

"If it's true," Sorren agreed. "Could still be courier drama. House Aldraste trying to make a smaller scandal look like a large one so the small facts disappear inside it."

"What small facts?"

"Maybe he didn't walk out. Maybe somebody carried him. Maybe he got hurt. Maybe he got farther than they want people thinking." Sorren shrugged. "All I know is the courier was riding like he'd been promised his own hide back if he was quick."

That beat the usual smoke. There was a courier behind it, a house name, and riders already moving.

"Why the interest?" Sorren asked Vel.

"Not often you hear about a Hoarded man leaving on purpose."

"Not often you hear about one doing anything on purpose." He tapped the spoon one last time and set it down for good. "If he is out there, he won't last clean. Boy's never used an ability before today. He'll waste levels, panic, and every house rider north of here will smell it."

The conversation slid back toward lamp oil and wheel grease. Vel let it go. Her meal sat finished in front of her and she didn't remember eating half of it.

The Aldraste boy had what she needed. He also had half the north road looking for him already.

Walking out made him a lead. Rich, frightened, possibly injured, maybe already heading the wrong direction. If Sorren had heard by supper, house riders had heard sooner. By morning every trader between here and the Vault would be carrying a version of the story, and every version would pull the trail a different way.

After eight years of asking, she had a house name, a same-day sighting, and Breck's yard in Cairn Hill.

Vel thanked Olla for the meal and the rosemary, then thanked Sorren for the road talk. Outside, evening had gone cool. The mule worked its jaw in the yard. Somebody at the well hauled the last bucket of the day.

Back in the storeroom, she pulled the journal from her pack.

The cover was soft leather with worn edges and a spine restitched with thread that did not match. The pages were thick enough for weather and crowded with two hands. One was small and precise, brown with age. The other was looser and quicker, layered in later over the first. They held distances, water sources, settlement counts, and warnings about ground that looked stable until it wasn't. On one southern route the older note said *passable in dry season.* The later hand had answered beneath it: *barely, and the creek crossing will eat a day if you're loaded.*

Vel turned pages carefully. The journal made her careful in a way almost nothing else did.

The southern routes took the most space, page after page of paths toward the Margins and the Scour. The notes were old now. Settlements moved. Roads washed out. People died and took their landmarks with them. The bones of the route still held.

Her thumb stopped near the back where the two handwritings crowded each other hardest, over a corrected distance, a note about shelter, and another about wind off the basin. The margin stayed under her thumb a moment longer than it needed to, and then she shut the book.

North would come first. South could wait.

If the rumor was true, the boy had left the Vault that morning. Fresh enough that he might still be near the Vault roads. Fresh enough that the search around him would still be widening. Searchers were part of the cost now. So was the possibility that she reached him and found nothing useful at all, only a panicked aristocrat bleeding levels into ditches because he had finally learned his hands could do things.

He might refuse her. He might already be half caught. He might never have wanted zero in the first place.

Vel wrapped the journal back in oilcloth and slid it flat against the inside of the pack. Then she tightened the straps and set the pack by the cot. At first light she would go north, listen at every stop, and find the place where rumor turned back into tracks.

Dark filled the storeroom. The window became a blue square. Sorren's mule chewed in the yard with steady animal indifference. A door shut somewhere. Steps crossed the packed earth. The well pulley creaked once, then stopped.

Thirty-one sat behind her sternum, exact as ever. The journal rested within arm's reach. Beyond it, south, lay the route she had been studying for eight years. Beyond that, if she was right, the door. The flash of it and the absence it had left came back for a second, and she shut that thought down before it could widen.

Vel lay down on the cot without undressing. The last calculation before sleep was a subtraction problem with bad numbers in it: thirty-one minus the road north, minus whatever it cost to catch up to House Aldraste's son, minus whatever it took to make him understand what she was asking. She kept one hand looped through the pack strap until sometime in the dark her grip let go.
