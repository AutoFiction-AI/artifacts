# Chapter 15

The Margins smelled like exposure. Hot stone, alkali, old water pulled too far underground. Six weeks ago Sable would have called it dirt and moved on. Now the heat lay on her scalp, the backs of her hands, the strip of skin above her collar where the Vault's formal neckline had given way, three weeks earlier, to an unlaced opening she no longer noticed.

Two hundred and thirty-eight sat at the edge of her awareness. She checked it the way she used to check her schedule, automatically, while doing something else. The schedule had never shifted. The number did, and every drop carried the same contradiction: less of what she had been, more of whatever she was becoming.

The settlement was eight structures around a collapsed well. Her route had passed east of it along a former Hoarded supply road, now just packed earth with old cart grooves baked hard in the sun. The detour happened because a man sat outside the largest house sorting grain one kernel at a time, and the rhythm of his hands was wrong.

Sable knew that rhythm. She had seen it in Council chambers, in administrative offices, in Lord Aldraste reaching for a glass and arriving there late.

The man's fingers moved, stopped, moved again. Three kernels. A pause. Two more. A longer pause.

Sable sat on the low stone wall across from him. "You're Hoarded."

He looked up. The eyes found her a beat after the sound of her voice. Once they did, the person behind them was still sharp.

"Was," he said. "Fifteen years."

"What level did you leave at?"

"Three hundred." He went back to the grain. "I know what you're looking at."

His name was Rennic. Forty years in the House of Accounts. Never important enough to make policy, important enough to prepare the papers that let other people pretend they had made it alone. The standard birth Orientation had gone on his record before he was old enough to understand what a record was. After that, his Ledger stayed at three hundred for fifty-two years. It held there until the day he walked out.

He told her this while sorting edible from spoiled. Once he could have done it by sight while talking about something else. Now each kernel needed inspection.

"I left because of this," he said, lifting his left hand. A fine tremor lived in the fingers, more imprecision than shake. "One morning I reached for a pen and nothing happened. Half a minute, maybe. Then the hand remembered itself. My supervisor said I needed focus discipline."

The word discipline came slower than the rest of the sentence, as if it had to cross thicker ground to reach his mouth.

"I believed him for two months," Rennic said. "Then it happened in a briefing. Whole room waiting, pen in my hand, and I just sat there while the arm hung useless and the title did nothing." He looked at the hand. "Listen to me. I still sound like I'm writing it up."

Something tightened in Sable's chest. She knew that maneuver. Make the terror sound procedural and maybe it would stay sortable.

"How long has it been happening?"

"Since before I left. That's why I left." He set down one grain, then another, then had to decide again which pile the third belonged in. "Everyone says it clears outside. Spend a little, wake the dead parts up. I've spent plenty. I'm at a hundred and twelve. Fifteen years of using what I've got. It doesn't come back."

He flattened both hands on the table, holding them still by force. "Whatever preservation does, it gets finished before anybody admits it started."

Sable stayed for water and the rest of the account. The well had failed because the original Shaping contract specified the cheapest reinforcement anyone thought they could defend in a ledger review. Three families remained in the settlement. Everyone else had gone south in stages, following the logic of a place built to consume people faster than it could keep them.

The cup he handed her had been cracked and resin-patched. In the Vault, damaged things were stored. Here somebody had needed this cup again tomorrow.

"You're here for the Aldraste boy," Rennic said.

"I was."

His attention flickered and returned. "You've spent. I can see that much. You move like somebody who's had to use her own weight."

The cup stayed in her hands another moment. Councilor Erenn's pen. Lord Aldraste's delayed smile at the last reception. The quiet administrative wing where senior officials were transferred when their work became inconsistent. Sable had filed those memos herself. She had typed reduced capacity due to distinguished service and never once read it as a diagnosis.

"Thank you for the water," she said.

"You're not going back for him."

No answer came. Her pack went over one shoulder, field-made now, assembled from Margins canvas and cord after she abandoned the Vault equipment none of them knew how to live with. Four days earlier she had cut her walking staff from a dead tree and skinned the bark with a knife bought for three marks and two levels spent on a Warming Charm for a family whose fire had died in the night. She still kept the expenditure in her head by category and date. The authorization field was empty. She had spent it anyway.

Rennic was still sorting when she left the settlement. Three kernels. Pause. One. Pause. The patience of damaged machinery inside living flesh.

###

The farmhouse had lost its eastern wall.

From fifty meters out, Sable counted what the structure could still do. Three walls standing. Roof bowed where the center beam had cracked. Enough shade to shelter people who understood the odds and stayed anyway. Hoarded construction again, fast and cheap, with the real cost pushed forward onto whoever had to keep breathing inside it after the contract closed.

Inside, the first thing she registered was separation. Two packs against the north wall, not touching. Warm coals in the fire ring. A man she didn't know on an upturned crate near the gap in the wall, left arm wrapped in fresh bandaging, good hand resting on the nearest pack as if he'd already decided what left with him if the place came down.

He looked at her and didn't reach for a weapon. "She's here," he called toward the back room.

Vel Ashwick came through first. Sable had studied the file for weeks: healer, unauthorized user, forty years in the field, wanted in three jurisdictions. The paper had listed violations and approximate levels. It had not caught the way Vel stood as if every stillness in her had already been costed and approved.

Kem Aldraste followed her.

The Council portrait tried to rise in Sable's memory and she pushed it down. The man in front of her was sunburned, leaner than the painting, bruised along one forearm, hands rough with work that had plainly been learned late. His eyes landed on her with none of the dazed aristocratic softness the portrait preserved.

"Auditor Orm," Kem said. The title came out first from habit. The rest of him arrived a second later. "I'm not going back."

"I know."

They stood there with the heat and the dust and the open wall between them. Plaster let go overhead and dropped to the floor in a soft powdery clatter. Nobody bothered reacting.

Sable found the old entry point by instinct. "The current situation," she began. The sentence collapsed before it could pretend to become a briefing. She was in a half-broken farmhouse, not a review chamber. "I'm not here to bring you back."

Vel's answer came clean and immediate. "Then why are you here?"

Several responses presented themselves and none survived inspection. "I can give you a provisional explanation," Sable said. "I can't make it hold."

The man on the crate made a sound through his nose. Almost a laugh.

"Can I sit down?" Sable asked.

"You're asking permission to sit on dirt in a building nobody owns," Vel said.

The floor met her knees the clumsy way it always did. Six weeks ago she would have treated that as humiliation. Now it was logistics. Vel pulled a waterskin from one of the packs without looking. Kem drank first and passed it across. The exchange was practiced and entirely stripped of tenderness.

Across from her, Kem folded down with the automatic movement of someone who no longer expected furniture. "How many have you spent?"

"Sixty-two."

"How's it feel?"

"The first use produced more proprioceptive feedback than I was prepared for. After that I kept talking like this." Sable heard herself and stopped. Dren covered his mouth. Vel waited.

"It feels like being in my body," Sable said. "Ridiculous thing to discover at forty-seven, but that's the nearest version."

"It's not ridiculous," Kem said.

Silence settled hard enough for her to read its shape. Twenty-seven years of meeting rooms had trained her to notice distance as data. Vel and Kem were three meters apart, and the gap had the texture of people who still knew each other's habits and no longer trusted what those habits meant.

"You're not speaking to each other," Sable said.

"We handle what needs handling," Vel said.

"That's not the same thing."

The sentence was so nakedly intrusive that Sable almost apologized on instinct. Dren spared her the effort.

"She can't help it," he said. "Give her a minute."

Kem watched her directly, with the steadiness he'd picked up somewhere outside the Vault. "You knew my father."

"I worked with Lord Aldraste for twenty years."

"Have you seen what's happening to him?"

The approved answer assembled in her mouth and stayed there unused. She remembered Lord Aldraste at the last reception, his wife's hand hovering near his elbow, not guiding, prepared to catch. She remembered the four-second delay before he answered a simple greeting.

"Yes," she said.

Kem waited. The old Kem would have filled the gap. This one left it open.

"The Stillness," Sable said. No qualifier. No euphemism.

"You've seen it in others?"

"At least four Council members. Councilor Erenn's pen stops halfway through his signature. He waits three seconds and finishes like nothing happened. Councilor Dais asks the same question twice in the same meeting because the first one has already gone missing by the time the answer reaches her." Sable heard the cataloging in her own voice and let it run. "Your father too. You've got the closer look at that than I do."

Kem rubbed his thumb over the heel of one hand, rough skin against rough skin. "I had twenty-six years of meals and councils and carriage rides with him. Around year fifteen I started noticing the gaps. He'd answer in his own voice and still not be all the way there."

"And the man I met today," Sable said. "Rennic. House of Accounts clerk. Left the Vault fifteen years ago at three hundred. He's at a hundred and twelve now and his hands still stop on him. Sorting grain takes an hour because every few motions the signal disappears."

Vel lowered herself to the wall beside the doorway, close enough to hear everything, far enough to keep the distance she was keeping.

"So it follows people out?" Kem said.

"Yes."

"And the Hoarded know?"

Sable thought of the reassignment memos and the phrasing she'd used to bury them. "Yes."

Kem's face flattened into the old Hoarded blankness. On him it looked borrowed. "They praise my father's discipline. They make speeches. He loses half a sentence and they call it virtue."

"Shit," Dren said quietly.

The retrieval order sat inside Sable like an object she had swallowed whole. "I can't bring you back," she said. "Protective observation protocols meant confinement, monitored access, restricted contact. You'd get a room, a schedule, and your father declining across a table from you until you learned how to call that duty."

Nothing in the room moved for a moment.

"I watched Rennic sort grain for an hour," Sable said. "If I take you back, that's what I'm delivering. Cleaner walls, maybe. The same ending."

Kem nodded once and left it there.

###

Vel said, "What happened to your team?"

"My last report to Lady Aldraste went out while I still had them with me," Sable said. "After that, I sent them back. Three weeks ago."

Her back tried to straighten into Vault posture. She let it and kept talking. "By then three agents had taken involuntary expenditure badly enough that field competence became its own crisis. One fused himself to a boulder with a Binding ability. We had to chip him free. He spent nine levels on the extraction and cried for two hours, mostly because nine was more than he'd spent in the rest of his life combined."

Dren winced in professional sympathy.

"The other two adapted," Sable said. "They were getting useful. Too useful. They had started spending on work outside the assignment because doing things kept solving problems. Another two weeks and I would have brought home five people who had learned action was better than preservation."

"So you sent them back," Vel said.

"Yes. Then I filed a mission-complete report stating the target had died by natural depletion." Sable looked at the forming callus in her right palm. "That report has been sitting in the archive for just under three weeks. I've been following you alone since the day I filed it."

Dren let out a breath. "All right. That's bad."

"There are two things you need before dark," Sable said. "First, the Council has now authorized the Red Reserve."

Vel's expression tightened around the eyes.

"Four trained combat spenders. Two were already attached to the permanent Scour posting. Two more were authorized when the retrieval was judged a failure. The new pair will be in the two-fifty to two-seventy range if the roster I saw was current. They are not like the team I brought."

Kem looked at Vel directly for the first time. "How bad?"

"The ones she brought panicked and overspent," Vel said. "These won't. Trained two-fifties know how to make you burn levels faster than you can afford." She looked at Dren's arm, then away.

"The second thing is older," Sable said. "The Red Reserve has kept a classified garrison at the Scour for years. Two operatives on permanent station. One commander in place for five."

That landed harder than the new deployment. Vel's attention sharpened. Dren pushed upright on the crate.

"Five years," Vel said.

"At minimum. Retrieval access wasn't intelligence clearance, so I don't have the whole structure. I have enough. Dedicated budget line. Dedicated reporting cycle. Standing post." Sable looked from one of them to the next. "Kem didn't create this response. The Hoarded were already guarding the threshold."

A beat passed. Kem kept looking at the dirt between them. His thumb worked the heel of one hand. "They've had soldiers there for five years. My father's — his hands stop and they give speeches about his discipline, and the whole time they had people at the threshold because they knew — " He stopped. Tried again, quieter. "They knew what was down there."

Nobody corrected him.

Dren stood. "How many bodies at the Scour when we arrive?"

"Commander and two stationed. Maybe four if the new pair reaches them first. I don't know where the order caught them."

That gave Vel something concrete enough to use. "Then we assume four."

Dren nodded once. His mouth tightened; he was already pricing the losses.

Sable rose and took up the staff. Her knees objected. "I'm going south."

Kem looked up. Vel didn't.

"Alone," Sable said. "I'm not joining you. I need to see the threshold. The garrison." She gripped the staff. "I need to stand there myself."

"It's real," Vel said.

"I believe you. I still need my own eyes on it." The bark pressed into the callus in her palm. "Be careful of the commander. Five years at one post. She knows that ground."

Now Vel looked at her. "We'll assume she does."

Kem's thank-you came after a pause long enough to cost him something. "All right. Thank you."

The answer that rose first belonged to the old office. Sable let it die there. "Don't wait for me."

She stepped out through the missing wall into the heat. The farmhouse stayed behind her with its cracked beam, its uneasy alliance, its warning now fully spoken. South lay flat and baked to a pale glare. The staff struck packed earth. Two hundred and thirty-eight rode at the edge of her awareness, quiet for now, waiting for the next demand.

Three hours to the Scour if the road held.

Maybe the threshold. Maybe rock and wind and nothing else. Either one would be easier to deal with than another mile of not knowing.
