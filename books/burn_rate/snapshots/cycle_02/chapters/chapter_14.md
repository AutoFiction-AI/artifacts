# Chapter 14

"Path forks," Vel said. "South-southeast. Water first."

The lower route dropped into a settlement built around a well and an eastward lean. Kem saw the lean before he saw the people: three houses listing toward a split in the ground, one already down in a heap of stone and joists. Dren went to the bucket and started hauling with one hand, the bad forearm braced under the rope. The pulley shrieked. Nobody else said anything.

That was how they traveled now. Vel gave directions. Kem answered if an answer was required. Everything she had taught him still lived in his body after trust went bad, which was almost funny if he let himself think about it that way. Thirty-one hummed at the back of his skull, the number she had carried when they met, his now.

Smoke drifted from cookfires on the west side of the settlement. On the east, a fissure ran wide enough to hide its bottom in shadow. Children kept well clear of it, which meant the place had been living with the thing long enough to learn its edges. An old man stacked salvaged boards by a retaining wall. Two women argued about where to move grain before dark.

Then the help call came, high and steady, stripped of panic by repetition. Both of them turned toward the same two-story house at the eastern lip, its upper floor already slanting toward the crack.

Vel set down her pack. Kem did the same. Dren started after them, looked at his arm, and stopped with a muttered curse. He found a low wall instead and said, "I'll keep them back."

A stone-dust man from the crowd was saying, to anyone and no one, "East wall shifted. My sister's in there. Two kids. Floor's gone soft."

No discussion. Vel went to the west side and laid her palm on the stone. The stillness of her body changed in a way Kem knew too well, attention narrowing until she seemed built around it. One level left her there. He could not see the spend, but he saw the answer land in the set of her shoulders.

"Northwest corner's taking weight," she said. The old operational register had survived the fracture intact. "Southeast is gone. Beam runs north to south and it's cracked midway. Reinforce that. Clear the east door. Woman inside, maybe two children."

The familiarity of it hit before the resentment could. "How much beam?"

"Two meters. Both sides."

Stone grated under Kem's boots as he crossed to the east wall. The doorway was choked with a dropped lintel and loose blocks. Through a slit between them he could make out dark, dust, a shape huddled low to the floor.

"We're coming in," he said.

At two hundred, Reinforcement had been easy enough to mistake for entitlement. At thirty-one it fought him. He pressed both hands to the wall, found the cracked timber by feel and vibration, and spent.

Two levels. The timber did not heal. It clenched. The house answered with a lower groan, still failing, but slower. Twenty-nine.

"Holding," he said.

"Door."

Lifting the fallen stone cost two more. The blocks came up badly, scraping and bumping and catching on one another like they resented being moved. Dust burst out through the opening and coated his tongue. Twenty-seven.

Kem ducked inside. One room, half divided by a storage partition that had already collapsed on one side. Breath rasped somewhere beyond it. Dust made the air chewable.

"Light," Vel called from outside.

She had moved to the northwest corner and was bracing the wall with both hands, human weight added to whatever calculation she had made. Her jaw was locked. A tremor ran through her forearms that had not been there an hour ago.

He spent a level for light. Weak white glow pooled across the room. A year ago he could have lit a hall. The smallness of this hurt more than the cost. Twenty-six.

The woman was against the far wall with a child on each side. Dust had turned the wet tracks on her face to mud. "Can you walk?"

"Floor dropped," she said. "I can't get them over it."

A gap split the boards between her and the door. One joist hung broken and twisted into the dark below.

Down on his knees, Kem put one hand on the intact board and one on the broken joist. Another groan rolled through the house. Dust shook loose from above.

"Ceiling's moving," Vel said. "Minutes."

One more level. The joist locked hard enough to take weight. The hanging boards settled. Twenty-five.

"Come now," he said. "Take the small one first. Keep hold of the other."

The mother obeyed without thanking him. Good. There was no room in the building for gratitude. She lifted the younger child to her hip, caught the older girl's hand, and crossed with the careful, flat-footed pace of someone walking a river stone in spring runoff. The boards held. Kem kept his palm against them until all three were through. By the time he stumbled back into daylight, a section of second-floor wall had smashed into the place they had just left.

Sun hit him full in the face. Across the yard Vel let go of the corner and stepped back breathing hard, dust in her hair, eyes on the house instead of on him. Six feet of air between them. A crowd rushed through it.

The brother reached his sister first. Somebody shoved a cup of water at her. Somebody else started hauling loose stone away from the base of the wall, because the next problem had already arrived.

Then the younger child planted both hands on her mother's face and started checking. Cheeks. Mouth. Ears. Hair. Then back to cheeks again, pressing dusty skin as if she could keep the woman in place by touch alone. The mother shut her eyes and let her do it.

A quarryman with dust in the seams of his neck stopped in front of Kem and Vel. "Thank you. Both of you." His gaze moved from one to the other, took in the distance and the way neither of them crossed it. "You traveling together?"

Silence did the answering.

"Right," the man said. "None of my business. West side's solid if you're staying the night. Could use hands on the retaining wall in the morning too."

He moved on before either of them replied.

Warm rubble made a passable seat. Kem lowered himself onto it and found a cut across his palm he had not noticed inside. He wrapped it with one of Vel's plain field bandages and did a sloppy job. She watched for half a second with the old corrective reflex on her face, then took up her pack and turned away.

Twenty-five rang thin in him. The number was no longer background. It was a wire pulled tight behind his eyes. Dust sat on his tongue. Stone grit had gotten into one boot. Every bruise from the last half hour had started speaking at once.

For one breath every hurt place in him pulled the same direction: preservation. Sit still. Stay whole. Never crawl into a house that wanted down. Never learn the exact price of a hand, a lung, a cut palm, a level. The Conservatory rose in him in scraps: cool stone, untouched air, his father's face emptied out into calm.

That calm had looked false to him there. Sitting in rubble with dust pasted to the back of his throat, it looked almost merciful.

Then he looked up.

The smaller child was still holding her mother's face between both hands, pressing hard enough to leave pale finger marks in the dust. The older girl had wedged herself against the woman's side and would not let go.

The temptation broke there. The understanding stayed. He could still see why someone would choose cool stone and untouched air. Looking at the girls, it stopped sounding like an answer.

He sat with that until the settlement went back to its ordinary business of not collapsing fast enough to count as disaster.

---

By evening they were on the west side above the crack, camp laid out in the same cold geometry as the last two nights: Vel on one side, Dren in the middle, Kem on the other. Fire. Pot. Bedrolls. No wasted motion and no wasted words.

Dren sat against his pack cleaning his knife one-handed. When he flexed afterward, two fingers lagged before closing. "Still useful," he said. "Mostly."

That was all he gave them. Fear for Mira stayed where he always kept it, under the practical layer.

A settlement child drifted in at dusk and started investigating Kem's pack with the fearless entitlement of the half-feral. Eight, maybe. Bare feet tough enough for rubble. Thin wrists. She turned over his spare belt, his cloth roll, his waterskin, and found the signet where it had slipped loose near the bandages.

"What's this?"

Gold flashed warm in her palm. In a settlement like this it looked obscene.

"Family ring," Kem said. "From north. Means I come from people who think keeping everything untouched counts as a life."

The girl slid it onto her thumb, watched it spin, and shrugged. "Sounds boring."

A laugh escaped him before he could decide whether he still knew how. "Yeah," he said. "It was."

She dropped the ring beside his pack and wandered off toward the cookfire when the stew smell got more interesting than aristocratic symbols.

Heat from her hand lingered in the gold when he picked it up. Dust sat in the grooves of the crest. The ring had been made for polished rooms and careful storage; here it looked rich and stupid in his palm.

He put it away.

Across the fire, Vel had the route journal open on her knees. He had already noticed the two handwritings days ago. Tonight the neater one stopped halfway down a distance column. After that the notes were Vel's, tighter and harder pressed, crowded into the margins as the road bent south.

She traced tomorrow's line with one finger. Her hand paused once, almost imperceptibly, over the last line in the other hand. Then she kept going.

North still existed. The quarryman had offered them solid ground, maybe work in the morning. Twenty-five levels spent sparingly could buy Kem years. He could turn around, disappear into some Spent town, learn to mend roofs or unload carts, spend one point a year and call it wisdom.

Instead he watched Vel mark a route through broken ground with sixteen levels left and a hand that had not entirely stopped shaking since the house wall. He tried, for a second, to picture himself taking the north road at dawn. The picture would not hold. It kept breaking on the house, the joist under his palm, the girl's hands on her mother's face.

Kem got up and crossed to the fire.

"Where does her writing stop?"

Vel looked at him for the first time since the rescue. She understood the question. After a beat she touched the page. "Two, maybe three days south. Depending on the ground."

He looked at the mark, then at the lines after it in Vel's tighter hand.

"And tomorrow?"

"Bad footing till noon. Better after the ridge."

A nod did for agreement. He handed the journal back, went to his bedroll, tightened the straps on his pack, and set it within reach of his hand for the dark.

Dren was already asleep, or doing a fair imitation. Somewhere lower in the settlement a child sang to herself while pots were scraped clean. Firewood snapped. Vel turned one page, then another.

Kem lay down without taking the signet back out. Anger was still there. So was the unanswered damage between them. Morning would not repair either one.

His pack waited beside him, buckled for south.

They would walk south.
