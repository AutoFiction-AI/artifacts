# Chapter 6

Five days after Kem Aldraste walked out of the Vault, and the morning after the outrider recovery detail failed to bring him back, Sable Orm stood in the Council chamber.

The chamber had seventeen chairs, and Sable knew the occupant of each by the sound it made sitting down. Councilor Breve's knees cracked. Councilor Hain breathed through his mouth when he thought no one was listening. Lady Aldraste settled into her chair without a sound. Around this table, that counted as rank.

Sable had spent twenty-three years in this room, nearly all of them behind the recording desk and none of them under summons. To her, the chamber was a ledger with furniture: every column aligned, every entry supposed to end where it began.

"The Aldraste situation," Councilor Breve said.

Sable waited. Across two decades of Council service, she had learned that the first sentence of an address was rarely the one that mattered.

"Young Lord Aldraste has been outside the walls for approximately five days. He has spent an estimated twelve levels in that time. The outrider recovery detail authorized under standing protocol made contact yesterday. It did not recover him."

Around the table, pages stopped moving. Twelve levels. In the Vault, the number was obscene. Sable's nearest colleague had spent four across an entire career and had been spoken to about it afterward as if survival itself required moderation.

"The House signet was taken from the ceremonial stand," Lady Aldraste said. Her voice carried the calm of a woman who had decided exactly how much of herself the room was going to get. "My son did not plan this. That is the charitable interpretation, and I will be offering no other."

Sable recognized the structure at once. Lady Aldraste was entering a breach into Council business, not a son into family trouble.

"The question before the Council," Breve said, "is the next method of retrieval."

The first team had gone out under ordinary authority. Whatever came next would carry the Council's name more openly than anyone at the table wanted.

"There has been discussion regarding the deployment of specialized resources."

Specialized resources. Sable had encountered references to them in Council records three times in twenty-three years. Each reference had been redacted before filing. The hand changed each time. The secrecy had a procedure.

Her mind went at once to the sealed wing on the eastern corridor. Twenty-three years of passing those doors had taught her that some silences were manufactured more carefully than others.

"The Red Reserve," Lady Aldraste said.

Breve looked directly at Sable. "For the benefit of those present, the Red Reserve is a Council-maintained unit of Hoarded personnel authorized to train in the use of abilities. For security purposes."

They were authorized to train. They were permitted to spend. People had been drilling behind the eastern doors all along.

"The Reserve is not a viable option for this operation," Hain said. He said it too quickly.

Lady Aldraste answered in the cadence of someone reading from a prepared document. "Deploying trained spenders for a retrieval would constitute a public acknowledgment that the philosophy of preservation cannot address all institutional concerns. The existence of the Reserve is predicated on its non-existence. Using it visibly destroys the premise."

"Agreed," Breve said. "The Reserve is not deployable for this purpose."

"Auditor Orm," he said.

Sable's stomach performed a maneuver she did not have an administrative term for.

"The Council would like to discuss your availability for a field assignment."

"I am available for any assignment the Council requires." The correct response came out before the incorrect one finished forming.

"The retrieval of Kem Aldraste requires a lead operative with institutional credibility, administrative competence, and discretion. You have been recommended by multiple Council members." Breve did not look at Lady Aldraste when he said it.

"Councilor Breve. I have not left the capital in forty-seven years." Sable delivered the line as relevant data, not protest. "I have never operated field equipment. I have never navigated terrain. My longest recorded distance on foot in a single day was approximately two kilometers during the record-room relocation of Year Eighteen."

"Which you managed with distinction," Breve said.

"The logistics of document transport across three buildings are not comparable to pursuit."

"You are precisely what we need." Lady Aldraste met Sable's eyes as if confirming a selection already entered into the file. "Someone who represents what we are. Not what we have hidden."

Nobody contradicted her. Hain cleared his throat and stopped there. One edge of Lady Aldraste's thumbnail was torn. In twenty-three years, Sable had never seen that.

"I will need a team," Sable said.

"Of course. We have prepared a list of suitable operatives."

"Field-trained?"

Breve looked down at his papers. Hain adjusted a cuff. No one said yes.

"I see," Sable said.

Breve slid a folder across the table. The cover page read *Council-Authorized Retrieval: Aldraste, Kem / Lead Operative Acceptance Packet*. Beneath it sat route warrants, equipment inventories, secrecy acknowledgments, district courtesy notices, and a sheaf of blank Form 7-Cs clipped in groups of five.

"You will sign before departure," Breve said.

Sable opened to the liability page. "Any unauthorized expenditure, unlogged route deviation, equipment loss, barter commitment, or local procurement undertaken in the field will be assigned to the team lead for review."

"That is standard language," Hain said.

"For what standard."

"Field work."

Sable looked at him. "By your own account, the Council is not deploying field personnel."

No one answered. Lady Aldraste adjusted one page in front of her, a motion so small it could only have been irritation.

Sable turned the next page. "No cash allotment."

"Unnecessary," Breve said. "This is not a trade excursion."

"Food, ferries, local guidance, emergency transport, replacement supplies, animal hire, and passage fees all exist outside trade excursions."

"The districts understand Council priority."

Sable had drafted enough interdistrict correspondence to know that districts understood delay, procedure, and the profitable edges of both.

"And the expenditure forms?" she asked.

"Total expenditure is not to exceed the pre-authorized limit of two levels per operative per day," Breve said. "Ability use must be pre-authorized by the team lead and documented."

"Documented when."

"At the time of use."

She let the page rest against the folder. "In the field."

"Yes."

There were moments when the Council's devotion to doctrine rose high enough that it stopped resembling belief and became architecture instead. A person could stand inside it and still not understand how it had been built.

"I would like a clarification entered into the file," Sable said. "If immediate danger requires expenditure before documentation can be produced, is the field team expected to preserve paperwork or bodily integrity."

Hain answered first. "The protocol contains an emergency provision."

"Reducing the written explanation from two hundred words to one hundred," Sable said, still reading. "Yes. I have located the mercy."

For the first time that morning, Breve looked almost embarrassed. It improved him slightly.

"Sign the packet, Auditor Orm," Lady Aldraste said. "Retrieve my son. Keep the Council deniable. You may categorize the rest as you please."

Sable signed because refusal would only have delayed the same signature by the length of a more crowded argument.

The Council's list of suitable operatives contained eight names. By midday it had become five citizens in Sable's office and three written refusals: one joint condition, one inventory conflict, one exemption request citing seven separate protocols. All five remaining operatives were level 300. That narrowed nothing.

Tarrell arrived with three notebooks and a personal seal and announced, before sitting down, that he had been told this was a documentation assignment. Kess had volunteered and looked pleased with herself in the strained, overbright way of someone who had mistaken apprehension for zeal. Lenn came with a field manual memorized and a pack cinched high enough to flay him by noon. Marris kept checking her Ledger as though the 300 might turn out to be a clerical error. Chael, a records porter, brought a compass and the only shoulders in the room that looked as though they had lifted anything heavier than paper.

"I was told this was a documentation assignment," Tarrell said again.

"It is a field retrieval," Sable said.

"Then my classification headers are wrong."

The crate from Council improved nobody. It held six ration packs, six bedrolls tagged with serial numbers, six Communication charms in lead-lined cases, a medical kit, a hand-drawn map, and a four-page protocol that repeated the two-level daily expenditure cap three times and omitted practical money entirely. The medical instructions referenced an Appendix D that did not exist. No weapons had been issued.

Sable read the Form 7-C requirements aloud once so the room could absorb the insult in full. Two hundred words before any authorized ability use. One hundred in direct and immediate physical threat, still prior to deployment.

"Prior to," Lenn repeated.

"Yes," Sable said.

One trip down to the service corridor outside Archive Three sorted most of what the packet had mislabeled. Tarrell hit a wall with his notebooks before the second landing. Kess bled through a heel wrap. Lenn knew the names of load-bearing knots and not the feel of them. Marris kept her balance and lost her breath. Chael carried his own pack, two spare bedrolls, and everybody else's dignity without complaint.

Sable reassigned the weight accordingly. Chael took the extra rations and the medical kit. Tarrell was permitted one notebook. Marris got the field log because it was the only object she could manage without fumbling. Kess lost the parasol. Lenn retied every pack in the room, including Sable's, because competence made of theory was still competence of a kind.

When Sable asked who could start a cooking fire without lamp oil, only Chael answered.

"Records furnace," he said. "I used to help in winter."

"That is now a qualification."

By dusk she had a team that could carry itself badly, a stack of pre-labeled emergency forms, and less faith in the Council's definitions than she had that morning. She wrote the narrowest instructions she could devise across the top page of each packet: if you are bleeding, spend first and write later; if you are not bleeding, find me before you do anything permanent; if I am unconscious, Chael authorizes.

They left at dawn.

The gate was where Kem Aldraste had walked out five days earlier. The ceremonial stand where House signets were displayed still held an empty square of flattened velvet in the Aldraste position.

"Senior Auditor." The gate warden held the departure ledger open. "How many in the party?"

"Six. Council-authorized retrieval operation."

"Estimated return date?"

"Three to four weeks."

He wrote it down with immaculate handwriting. Nothing in his manner suggested that six level-300 Hoarded citizens walking out to pursue a defector across open country was unusual enough to warrant a different pen.

From its case, Sable took the Wayfinder charm, a small metal disc cool against her skin, the tracking glyph cut into one face. The cost was two levels.

Two levels. Her mother had died at 296, ashamed of the four she had spent across an entire life. Sable had spent none.

Kem's file supplied the face: formal, composed, twenty-six years of stillness and then the gate.

She activated the glyph.

Her knees went first.

Stone struck through her skirt as she caught the archway. Direction flooded in, south-southeast, distance rendered as strain rather than miles, the clean pull of a living line running through her body toward someone moving away. The knowledge had nothing to do with sight or hearing. It entered lower and stranger, as if some buried instrument inside her had been struck hard enough to wake.

300 became 298.

The threshold stone hit the back of her legs when she sat.

"Senior Auditor?" Lenn asked.

"A moment."

Breathing came too fast. The Wayfinder did not care. It kept pulling at the base of her skull. Against the pace her present team had any honest chance of holding, the target felt four or five days ahead. Against a single road-hardened body, less. The charm did not bother separating distance from incompetence.

298. Her mind kept returning to the number and dropping against it, the way a foot drops when it expects one more stair and finds only air.

She made herself stand.

"The target is south-southeast," Sable said. Her voice held. "Still moving. We proceed south."

They walked through the gate. Warm air struck her face carrying dust, animal smell, and sunlight already stored in stone. The sky was too large. Nothing overhead contained it. The road under her boots gave slightly where packed earth had been worn by wheels and weather, and each irregularity announced itself all the way up her legs. Behind her rose the walls that had contained her entire life. She did not look back.

By the first hour, the road had begun revising the Council's assumptions. Kess had bled through her wrap again. Tarrell surrendered his notebook to Chael after discovering he could not carry paper and a pack at the same time. Marris could not keep the field log while walking. Lenn abandoned the pace he had planned in his head. Chael, carrying half the room, was the only one who never complained.

The Council map showed a crossing two kilometers on: a district-maintained cut with a plank bridge and, in the margin, the notation *cooperation expected*. Sable had seen the phrase on correspondence for years. On paper it meant somebody else would do something inconvenient later and invoice the correct office.

On the road it meant a queue of grain carts, three sweating laborers knee-deep in a runoff channel, and half a retaining wall lying open in the water.

The bridge had not collapsed. It had been removed. Two long planks rested on the far bank while the laborers reset the stone lip that held the crossing in place. One of them was using a low Shaping ability in short, practiced bursts, smoothing fractured edges so the new stones would seat cleanly. Each burst cost something. The worker did not treat that fact like a sermon. He treated it like lifting.

A woman standing on a wagon tongue held a ledger board and shouted names as if this were a market queue rather than a blocked road. When she saw six clean-coated Hoarded citizens approaching with Council packs and untouched faces, her mouth flattened into something that was not surprise.

"Crossing's one cart at a time once the plank goes back," she said. "If you're on foot, you wait behind the grain."

Sable presented the district courtesy notice. "Council-authorized retrieval operation. Passage is required."

The woman read the first line, skipped the stamp, and handed it back. "Fine. Required. The wall's still broken."

"The notice states cooperation is expected."

"It is. We're cooperating with the people whose grain goes rotten if this road stays shut."

"This operation has priority standing," Sable said.

"With who?"

Sable almost said *the Council* and realized half a second before speaking that the answer would change nothing here unless it arrived attached to labor, marks, or food.

"If the district clerk needs to log the warrant, where is the office?" she asked.

"East spur. Two miles." The woman nodded toward a side road. "If you want it written up and billed later, go there. If you want across now, you wait or you help."

Tarrell stared at the channel. "We were issued no local payment instruments."

"That sounds terrible," the woman said, not sounding terrible at all.

Behind them, a mule stamped and shook flies from its ears. Ahead, the Shaper pressed a wrist to the reset stone and spent again. The line moved by inches. The Wayfinder kept tugging south.

Sable calculated. The east spur would cost time they did not have. Waiting behind three grain carts would also cost time they did not have. Activating a Communication charm to report delay would cost a level, require a justification, and reach an office that had omitted cash from the kit and called a dismantled crossing manageable.

Her preparation had accounted for signatures, schedules, and the possibility of immediate bodily harm. It had not accounted for the road's refusal to care about any of those.

"Chael," she said. "Lenn. Can you move stone?"

Chael looked at the wall. "Yes."

Lenn looked at it as though the field manual might still produce a hidden appendix if he stared hard enough. "I believe so."

"Good. We will help."

The woman on the wagon tongue studied Sable for one beat, then jerked her chin toward the channel. "Get in, then. If the next cart sits till noon I want it to be because the work's bad, not because you lot stood there thinking about it."

The water in the cut was only calf deep, but it ran fast where the retaining stones had come loose. Chael climbed down first and planted himself wide. Lenn followed with the rigid bravery of a man who would rather have received a diagram. Sable handed Marris the field log, stripped off her gloves, and went in after them because ranking six people from dry ground while they carried stone for her seemed likely to end the wrong part of her life.

The first rock nearly took her balance with it. Its weight was not the abstract kind recorded on manifests. It dragged, scraped, shifted, and taught her more in one wet second than the Council's packet had managed in four pages.

Kess and Tarrell stayed on the bank until the woman with the ledger board pointed at the loose end of a plank and said, "If you've got arms, use them." Kess did. Tarrell did after a visible conversation with himself. Marris took names and crossings because it was the only task no one had to explain twice.

For forty minutes the retrieval team became unpaid district labor. The Shaper seated stone. Chael and Lenn hauled. Sable learned which muscles objected to current and which objected to being asked anything at all. Kess slipped once and barked her shin hard enough to sit down in the mud. Tarrell dropped his end of the plank when a wagon wheel jolted sideways, then apologized to the plank.

The woman on the wagon tongue ran the line with ruthless fairness. One grain cart, then another, then the team on foot, then the third cart. No one cut because of a seal. No one bowed because of 300. A child riding sacks of meal watched Sable stagger up the far bank with her hem soaked to the knee and asked his father, not quietly, whether priests were supposed to do roadwork.

Sable would have corrected the category if she had not been busy getting air back.

When they finally crossed, the woman held out the courtesy notice again. A strip of mud had dried across the Council stamp.

"Take it to the clerk if you care about repayment," she said. "Or don't. We were fixing the road either way."

Sable took the paper. "What is the nearest settlement south?"

"Depends what you call a settlement." The woman pointed with the edge of her board. "You keep on this road another three hours and you'll hit Low Bridge and whatever passes for an inn there. If you're looking for a young man in clothes too good for his judgment, he came through yesterday with a woman who looked like she could sleep in a ditch and wake up winning the argument."

The Wayfinder pulled south-southeast. The line held.

"Did he ask for supplies?"

"He paid for bread and walked out before it cooled."

Kess said, very quietly, "He paid."

The woman heard her anyway. "That surprises you."

"We were issued paperwork," Tarrell said.

"Then I hope it eats."

No one answered that. There was too much of it to answer.

They walked on because the target was still moving and the day had already spent more of itself than Sable had authorized. By the time the light began thinning, Kess's heel had opened again, Lenn's shoulders were raw where the straps rubbed, and Tarrell had started coughing after swallowing runoff. Low Bridge remained ahead. Sable stopped the team short of it because getting there by dark would have required a pace Kess could not keep and Lenn should not.

The camp they found was a hard patch beyond the road where wagon ruts widened and an old fire ring suggested other failures of planning. Sable ordered boots off. Lenn cleaned what he could under instruction. Chael coaxed a fire out of bark scrap and stubbornness on the third attempt. Marris portioned rations. South kept humming inside Sable's bones.

Only then did the blank Form 7-Cs reassert themselves.

Sable spread one on her knee while the others ate and stared at the printed headings.

DATE OF EXPENDITURE.

LOCATION OF EXPENDITURE.

AUTHORIZING OFFICER.

NATURE OF THREAT.

JUSTIFICATION OF PERMANENT LEVEL LOSS.

MINIMUM 200 WORDS.

She wrote the easy parts first: the date, the gate departure, her name, the expenditure amount of two levels. She stopped at *Nature of Threat*.

What had the threat been, precisely. They had risked losing the line before the pursuit began. They had risked sending six untrained citizens into open country with no more guidance than a hand-drawn route and doctrine. Without the Wayfinder they would have left the gate already slower and stupider than the person they were trying to catch.

She wrote: *Target mobility combined with absence of trained tracking capacity within assigned party.*

It was true. It was also nothing like the truth of the charm entering her body and staying there.

Tarrell leaned toward the page before she could stop him. "If you require additional language, the packet's doctrinal appendix suggests several approved phrasing structures."

Sable looked up.

"You memorized the appendix."

"Only the first page," he said. "The second appeared repetitive."

That was the nearest thing to humor the road had produced since morning. It passed around the fire in exhausted forms. Kess made a sound that might have been a laugh if her heel hurt less. Even Marris lifted her head.

"Recite it," Sable said.

Tarrell blinked. "Recite."

"Yes."

He did, because Hoarded training had turned obedience into a reflex long before any of them had begun questioning the uses of it.

"When evaluating justified expenditure, the reviewing authority shall consider proportionality, institutional necessity, availability of lower-cost alternatives, and maintenance of doctrinal confidence among observing parties."

"There," Sable said, and wrote down the words that could survive audit.

She completed the report because she had signed for the irregularities and because the Council would notice missing paper before it noticed missing people. At the bottom, in the box marked *LESSONS FOR FUTURE DEPLOYMENT*, she wrote: *Initial operating assumptions require revision.*

Then she set the form aside and opened the map again.

Kem had left the Vault five days earlier. A woman at the crossing had seen him in Low Bridge yesterday. Those were the first numbers all day that meant anything. The target was one day ahead at the last confirmed stop and still moving south with a companion who knew how to keep him moving. The rest of the gap was not country. The rest of the gap was Sable's team: Kess's torn heel, Lenn's shoulders, Tarrell's breath, the lost time at the bridge, the whole polished stupidity of sending six preserved people into the road and calling it pursuit.

South of Low Bridge the line opened into farms, wagons, and side tracks. Witness memory would thin there. If she put the team into Low Bridge by midday, they were still one day behind a person. If she missed it, they were back to chasing what people said they had seen.

She folded the half-dry report, leaned across the fire, and woke Chael first because he would stand up the fastest.

"We leave before light," she said.

Chael pushed himself onto one elbow. "How bad?"

"Kem Aldraste passed Low Bridge yesterday. Low Bridge by midday or we lose the line."
