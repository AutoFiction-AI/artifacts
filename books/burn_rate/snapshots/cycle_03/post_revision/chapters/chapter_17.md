# Chapter 17

The wind hit first. It was not a gust but a condition, a constant gritty pressure coming off the Scour like breath from a furnace. It carried powdered rock and the flat mineral smell of laid-bare geology. Kem shielded his eyes with one hand, looked south, and felt the world drop out from under itself.

The Scour looked like a wound. It did not look like a valley or a canyon. It looked as if something had taken a bite from the earth and left the inside showing. The erosion basin stretched maybe two kilometers across, carved down through layered ground striped ochre, pale gray, and a deep oxidized red that looked like old blood in the summer sun. The walls fell away in terraces and shelves. Wind had cut them into overhangs, columns, and narrow sandstone fins left standing after centuries of grinding. There was no vegetation, no shade, and no cover. Sun bounced off the exposed rock so the heat came from everywhere at once.

Vel stood beside him at the ridgeline with one hand on her pack where the route journal sat. Her face did something Kem had learned to read over six weeks. It was not emotion exactly. It was the registration of a cost she had not budgeted for. She looked at the basin floor the way she looked at everything: measuring.

"There it is."

Somewhere down there Maren had reached zero. Vel went very still beside him.

Vel had fourteen levels left. Kem had eighteen. Thirty-two sat between them. He had spent more than that on a single defensive perimeter during the agent fight south of the Spent Towns. In the basin below, the number no longer felt large enough to be stupid. Somewhere north, Dren had done the sane thing and gone back to his life, and Kem hated how sensible that now looked.

"I count four," Vel said. She was looking through a gap in the ridgeline toward the basin's navigable descent, a switchback path cut into the wall and the only ordinary way down. Two figures stood at the top of it. Two more waited on the floor near a cluster of small garrison buildings.

These were not the untrained level-three-hundred agents who had accidentally detonated a grain silo and given themselves migraines. These were Red Reserve. These were the people who knew what abilities were for and had been permitted to use them.

"Two at the descent. Two on the floor. The descent pair are fresh. They're standing wrong. Too stiff. Too visible. They've been posted recently. The floor pair are permanent. Look at the camp."

Kem looked. The structures near the basin floor were not field shelters. They were low buildings of fitted block with canvas-and-leather roofing, built for years of use. A fire pit sat between them in a carbon-black ring. A gear rack leaned under an awning. Clothing hung on a line, bleached by sun and wind to a tired colorless gray. Someone had been living here long enough for boots to polish a path between doorways.

"Sable said permanent garrison," Kem said. "I was hoping she was being dramatic."

"Sable doesn't do dramatic."

"No. She does bureaucratic. Which is worse."

Vel's mouth moved as if a smile had started and then refused to build the rest of itself. "I need to map their positions properly. That's two levels."

That cost two levels out of fourteen, a seventh of everything she had left spent on looking. But squinting from a ridgeline was not a plan.

"Do it."

Vel closed her eyes and spent. The air around her shifted with the subtle pressure change that came before a storm. When she opened them again, her gaze moved with inhuman precision, sweeping the basin in hard clean arcs.

"The commander is near the threshold point. Woman. Compact build. She's been patrolling the same strip for years. I can see the wear in the ground. Same route, different hours. The other permanent agent runs a circuit between the camp and the east wall. He's covering the tunnel exit. The descent pair are young. One keeps touching her gear."

The sharpness left her eyes. Vel sat down hard on the ridgeline. "Twelve."

Kem let the number settle. Twelve and eighteen made thirty.

"I should mention," he said, "that at my current level I could perform the Conservatory Rite without anyone noticing. I'm finally discreet enough for a Hoarded ceremony. It took losing two hundred and eighty-two levels, but the form is improving."

Vel looked at him. This time the smile made it halfway. "Good. Maybe your father would be proud from a medically safe distance."

A voice rose from the basin floor. It was not shouted. The basin simply carried it.

"I can see you on the ridgeline. I've been watching you for thirty minutes."

Vel went still. Kem's hand moved toward nothing, an old reflex from when reaching meant options. He had twelve options left. Thirteen if he counted not using any of them.

"I know what you're here for," the voice called. "I'd like to talk before we do this the other way."

"She's alone down there," Vel said. The Perception was fading but not gone. "The circuit agent is at the eastern wall. The descent pair haven't moved."

Kem stood. "I'll go."

"If she-"

"Then you'll have twelve levels and no cascade partner and this will have been a very long walk for nothing." He kept his voice flat. Six weeks of approaching zero had burned the drama out of him. "But she announced herself. She asked to talk. That's a person trying not to spend."

"I hate procedure."

"No you don't. You hate other people's procedure."

He took the switchback descent. The turns were tight, and the footing had been worn smooth by thousands of patrols. His boots were held together by friction and optimism, and the soles had worn so thin he could feel the heat of each step through them. Dust slid away under his weight in little sighs. Every meter down made the basin walls rise higher around him until the ridgeline and Vel both became part of the sky.

The commander met him where the path leveled onto the basin floor. She was shorter than the voice suggested, compact and weathered, somewhere in her forties. Brown skin had gone darker under five years of Scour sun. Her hands were calloused. Her field clothes had been patched so many times the patches had their own repairs. She stood the way Vel stood, with her weight settled and her feet already where they needed to be.

At the edge of the camp, beside the gear rack, seven rock samples sat on a flat ledge. Kem saw a dark piece of basalt, a curl of pale shale, a block of iron-red sandstone, and four other scraps from the basin walls. They had been lined up by color and grain with the care of a person who had been alone with geology for a long time and had started arranging the company.

"Commander Osen," she said. "Red Reserve. Scour garrison."

"Kem Aldraste."

"I know. Your retrieval order has been sitting in my dispatch pouch for six weeks." She nodded toward the switchback. "Those two up there are for you. The Council sent them three weeks ago. They're competent enough."

"Just competent enough?"

"Nobody sends their best people to sit in a hole in the ground. The best people are back at the Vault not spending anything." She said it without bitterness. "I volunteered for this posting and renewed twice. People don't do that for promotion."

"Why do they do it?"

Osen looked past him at the featureless stretch of basin floor behind her. The threshold point, or what Kem assumed was the threshold point, looked like every other patch of stripped ground until he noticed the wear of her patrol strip and the way she never quite turned her back on it.

"The brief makes it sound easy," she said. "One patrol route. One camp. One classified nuisance in a place nobody wants to live. Then you see somebody hit zero and leave. After that you either file for transfer or you stay. Going home after that feels like lying."

"You saw it yourself."

"Three times." Her eyes came back to him. "So listen before you decide how much of yourself to spend."

"You came here to talk," he said. "Not about my retrieval."

"Your retrieval can wait. This can't." She tipped her chin toward the ridge. "The threshold has degraded."

Wind pushed dust between them. Kem felt the map shift under his feet.

"Three years ago the Council sent a specialist to damp the site. Natural erosion had already started changing the basin. The dampening finished the job. If the woman on the ridge is still using eight-year-old numbers, the two of you may buy a flicker and nothing else."

Kem's mouth went dry. Above him, on the ridgeline, Vel would be listening. Even with her Perception fading, the basin carried sound, and Vel had spent forty years listening for the piece of information that would kill a plan.

Vel's whole cascade calculation had been built on a threshold altered under her feet.

"What makes it hold?" Kem asked.

"More margin. More total spend. The report isn't generous about your odds." Osen spoke like a field memo stripped to essentials. "Two contributors are no longer reliable."

"And you're telling me this because?"

"Because ignorance isn't mercy." She pointed west, then north, then east without taking her eyes off him. "That shelf, the wash, the bright crust in the middle, the old mine exit. I know how long each route leaves a body in the open. If you keep coming, I keep you off the point until dark. People hear garrison and imagine a charge. Mostly this post is angles."

Kem followed the line of her hand. The basin stopped looking wide and started looking arranged.

"So that's it. You wait us out."

"Mostly." She rubbed dust off one thumb against the side seam of her trousers. "The young ones think this is a retrieval. It's erosion management with paperwork."

Kem thought of Sable and disliked how quickly the comparison arrived.

"That's bleak."

"It's accurate." She nodded toward the ridge. "Take the new arithmetic back to Vel. If she still wants it after that, at least she'll be choosing the real basin."

The threshold was here. That was the worst part. It had been turned into a posting and a report category. Someone had made a working life out of standing next to it.

"And if we keep coming?" he asked.

"Then I do my job."

He believed her. That was the problem. She was not a liar trying to scare him off. She was a competent person telling him exactly how little chance they had left and exactly how she planned to spend the rest of the daylight ruining what remained.

Kem turned and climbed back up the switchback. The basin wall threw heat at him like a second sun. By the time he reached the ridge, sweat had soaked his shirt and dust had turned to paste on the back of his neck.

Vel was still where he had left her, journal open on her knees. She had heard enough. Her face had gone blank in the specific way it did when feeling had been pushed off the surface so the work could continue underneath it.

"The site's still there," she said before he sat down.

"Yes."

"But the basin isn't the basin Maren mapped."

"No."

She looked down at the open page. The page held wind notes, rock notes, a small x at the cascade site, and the word *here*. Vel put two fingers on the page and then lifted them again.

"The x still lines up with Osen's patrol strip," she said. "The threshold point hasn't moved. The approach has."

"She told me how she holds it." Kem sat beside her. The ridge burned through his trousers. "She doesn't rush people. She keeps them off the point until dark. She knows every clean line across the basin and she breaks them one by one."

Vel shut her eyes for a moment. "That makes sense."

"You sound offended by it."

"I am. I spent eight years respecting the wrong problem."

Kem looked south again. Four Reserve agents. Thirty levels between him and Vel, and that was before the tunnel took its share. The basin floor did not look impossible. It looked administratively impossible, which was somehow worse.

"What do we do?" he asked.

Vel reopened the journal and set it flat on the ridge between them. The wind tried to lift the pages. She held them down with both hands.

"We don't give her the switchback. We don't give her the center line. We use the tunnel, come out east, and force her to solve our position in motion instead of watching us descend in order." She tapped the page with one fingernail. "Maren used the old mine because the eastern wall had better footing and less open exposure. If Osen's permanent circuit still covers that exit, then the gap isn't geography. It's timing."

"And if the threshold doesn't hold?"

Vel stared at the journal long enough that he thought she might not answer.

"Then I learn that from the threshold," she said. "Not from a commander who has spent five years standing next to it."

That was not hope. It was worse and truer than hope. It was Vel refusing to let somebody else be the final witness to her life.

"When do we move?"

"Two hours. Light behind us at the exit. In their eyes, not ours. Maybe that buys confusion. Maybe it buys nothing."

They spent the waiting time turning themselves into what could run.

The packs had made sense on a road. They made less sense now. Vel stripped hers down first with the blank efficiency of a person cutting dead weight off a cart axle. She set aside the blanket, the cook pan, the spare shirt, and the little packet of dried root Dren had insisted they buy in the last settlement because the road south carried no mercy and less food. She tucked the journal back into the pack, thought better of it, and pulled it free again. That stayed.

Kem copied her. His extra shirt came out. The cheap plate he had been carrying since the Spent Towns came out. The half bar of soap he had believed, with Hoarded optimism, would matter by the end of this journey came out. He touched the signet in his pocket by accident and left it there because taking it out would have required explaining to himself why he still had it.

They wedged the discarded gear under an overhang above the tunnel line and weighted it with broken shale so the wind would not scatter it across the ridge. Kem had the absurd feeling they were packing for two different futures. In one, they came back for the gear at dusk, bruised and furious and still alive in the old world. In the other, the ridge kept their things because people who crossed did not return for soap.

Below them, the garrison pattern went on.

The permanent circuit agent walked his eastern line with the patience of a clock. One of the retrieval pair at the switchback kept rolling her shoulders as if reminding herself she had joints to trust. Osen never strayed more than a certain distance from the threshold strip. Even when she bent to drink, she did it facing west.

Vel watched the rotations until the Perception spend had become mere memory and she was back to ordinary sight. Ordinary sight was enough. She counted steps. She tracked where the circuit agent slowed at a wash cut and where he sped up over bedrock. She muttered times under her breath and wrote two more marks in the margin of Maren's page.

"She leaves the east wall thin for forty breaths at a time," Vel said eventually.

"That doesn't sound generous."

"It isn't. It's just human." She snapped the journal shut. "That's what we've got."

The sun lowered. Heat changed character but did not lessen. Instead of hammering straight down it came sideways off the basin walls, baking one half of Kem's face and leaving the other dry with wind. He drank from his canteen and the water tasted like hot metal. Vel drank less. He noticed and said nothing because there was nothing to say that wasn't stupid.

"Do you think Osen's lying about the threshold?" he asked after a while.

"No."

"That was quick."

"Liars sell hope. She sold weather."

Kem looked at her.

"That was almost quotable," he said.

"Shut up."

The answer settled the last uncertainty in him. Osen had not offered a cleaner route, a hidden angle, a desperate maybe. She had laid out exactly how they would fail. That was why Vel believed her.

When the light had gone amber enough to knife sideways through the basin, Vel stood and rolled one shoulder. "Time."

---

Three hours behind them, Sable Orm stopped on a northern ridge and read the basin twice before trusting the first impression.

The Scour lay open below her in bands of exposed ground and hard light. She could see the garrison camp. She could see the permanent patrol lines worn into the basin floor. Farther east, two figures were moving below the skyline toward an old mine cut in the wall.

Sable had been tracking for nine days. Two Perception spends had brought her here. Two hundred and thirty-six levels remained, enough for almost any official purpose and not nearly enough to make this feel official.

At the farmhouse she had not yet opened the threshold files. She had the Council authorization, the permanent garrison brief, and enough certainty in her own procedure to mistake those for the urgent facts. The dampening assessment came later, during the third week of pursuit, after the farmhouse and after she had already sent her remaining agents back north. Pulling it from the last live relay office north of the basin had cost her nearly two days, enough to burn the road lead she should have kept from the farmhouse. An auditor's seal had opened cabinets no one thought needed hiding from an auditor.

The specialist report had not offered a clean answer. It had only stripped certainty from the old one. The site was unstable. The original crossing could not be treated as repeatable. Contributor load, energy margin, and failure behavior had all changed. The recommendation at the end was blunt: a stable opening now needed at least three contributors, and even that bought probability rather than promise.

Below her, the two figures cut for the mine entrance and the patrol pattern bent toward them. Sable watched the change happen and felt the lie in her own paperwork catch up with her. For nine days she had filed her movement under observation, intelligence gathering, and confirmation of a retrieval target's final disposition. Observation had been honest only while distance still counted as neutrality.

She opened her satchel. It held blank report forms, a retrieval warrant, spare pencils, seal wax, a second shirt, and other official weight with no downhill use. She kept the water, the bandages, the copied notes, and the knife she had learned to carry badly. The rest she wedged beneath an outcrop and weighted with a slab of shale.

The fastest descent was the north scree. It was also the stupid descent. It slid underfoot, skinned hands, and advertised every step with hissing gravel. It was the only route that cut time instead of preserving decorum.

She started down.

---

They moved laterally along the ridgeline, staying below the lip where rock met sky. Wind shoved grit into every seam of clothing. Kem's eyes watered until the tears cut clean tracks through the dust on his face. Up here the ground was sharper and less worn, with lichens and dead grass in the cracks. Thirty meters below, nothing lived.

The tunnel entrance was a horizontal scar in the wall, two meters high and three wide, its edges too clean for nature. Miners had cut it decades ago, extracting something the Hoarded had found profitable enough to spend on and unimportant enough to visit themselves. It was the usual arrangement. A rusted length of rail still jutted out of the dust near the entrance, half buried and too bent to serve any purpose now except accusation.

Cool air moved out of the dark carrying wet mineral rot and old dust. The ceiling sagged where ancient Reinforcement had long since faded. Rubble narrowed the passage to barely a meter. Drill marks still showed in places, neat lines of human labor preserved in rock long after the laborers' names had been spent out of any record that mattered.

Vel knelt at the entrance with the journal open. The pages rattled under her hands.

"Maren mapped the interior," she said. "Main shaft for two hundred meters, then down and east. Three partial collapses and one full blockage. That was eight years ago."

"So it's worse."

"Probably."

"Good. I was worried this might be straightforward."

She gave him a look and tucked the journal under her shirt, inside the remaining strap of her pack. Then they stepped inside.

The dark was immediate and total. It did not feel like night. Night at least admitted the existence of sky. This was underground dark, close and old and absolute. Kem lit his palm. Warm light opened over rough walls, broken debris, and a ceiling that seemed offended by the responsibility of holding itself up.

Sixteen.

The first stretch forced them low. Then lower. The miners had cut the main shaft straight, but time had not respected their intentions. One side had slumped inward where water had eaten a seam in the wall. Splintered timber braces stuck out of the debris like snapped ribs. Kem had to turn his shoulders to pass a section where collapsed marl had left the tunnel shaped like a throat.

The floor tilted unexpectedly under loose grit. Vel put one hand back without looking, and Kem took it long enough to keep himself from sliding face-first into the wall.

"This was a mine?" he whispered.

"It was. The Hoarded weren't interested in worker comfort."

"They continue to maintain brand consistency."

Her grip tightened once and released.

The passage pinched into a crushed crawl where a slab the size of a wagon leaned across the tunnel on cracked bedding lines. Every shift of Kem's weight made rock answer rock. Fine dust trickled down his sleeve and caught in the sweat on his forearms.

Vel flattened beside the gap and looked once. "If that settles while we're under it, we're done."

"That's very clarifying."

"It's your spend."

Three levels for structural Reinforcement. He had once spent more than that warming bathwater he had no intention of entering. Kem put both hands on the slab. It felt cool, damp at the seam, with the kind of patient weight that made human plans seem insulting. When he spent, the subtraction came in three clean absences. The Ledger's hum thinned around them.

Thirteen.

"Go."

Vel slid under first. She did not hesitate. She trusted the Reinforcement and, worse, she trusted him to hold it. Kem followed with the light bobbing ahead of his face and gravel grinding into both shoulders. The slab groaned above his spine. He had to let the breath out of his lungs to clear the tightest part. When he finally pulled himself through, his knees hit the far side hard enough to sting.

He dropped the hold.

The slab settled behind them with a heavy abrupt thud. Dust burst through the gap. The opening narrowed to a crack no wider than his wrist.

There was no way back through that crack.

For a few seconds they just listened to the tunnel reaccept its own weight.

Then Vel moved again.

The shaft dipped. Water had been working the rock for years. The floor ahead had split into a dark slot crossed only by a spine of intact bedrock along the left wall. Kem tested it with one foot. The ledge sheared under him at once. His stomach dropped. Vel caught a fistful of his shirt and hauled him back before the rest of the edge went with it. The falling debris hit somewhere below after a long enough pause to feel personal.

"Don't do that," she said.

"I'll add it to the list."

She spent on Navigation. There was no flare and no visible change, just a hard little tightening in the air and a change in her body, as if a second skeleton had briefly aligned itself inside her own. She took his wrist and put his hand on the wall.

"Left edge. Crack. High step. Don't trust the pale patch. It sounds solid and isn't."

Her voice stayed flat and exact while she fed him the route one placement at a time. He kept his palm against the wall and felt the tunnel sweat against his skin. More grit pattered into the darkness below. Somewhere water dripped and echoed like slow counting.

Ten.

The shaft forked twenty meters later. The right branch had collapsed into a choke of timber and rubble. The left slanted down under a low arch of rough-cut ceiling. Vel touched the left wall once, nodded to herself, and kept moving. Maren's map lived now in whatever part of Vel's head had once been reserved for safer long-term plans.

The only continuation after that was a constriction where ceiling and floor had shifted toward each other until the tunnel seemed to close into itself. Vel looked at it, swore once under her breath, and spent again. Evasion cost two more levels.

She went through like the gap had briefly remembered it was supposed to be a passage. Kem had to force his way after her, chest and back scraping opposite walls. Rock dust filled his mouth. The light in his palm smeared against the wall and made the space feel even smaller. There was not room to panic. Panic required room. His father's life rose up stupidly in his head: broad halls, wide doors, a body preserved from every pressure except the one that had finally hollowed him out.

When he shoved himself free on the far side, he lay on his back for one breath longer than he should have and stared at the ceiling two handspans above his nose.

"If this whole plan works," he said, breathing hard, "I do intend to complain about it at length."

"You can do that while running."

The tunnel widened just enough to stand bent instead of doubled. Vel was waiting with one hand braced on the wall. At eight levels her hands had started to shake. It was not fear. It was Ledger signal loss. The hum that had been with her for forty years was fading toward silence, and the body that had always lived inside that hum was being asked to improvise.

Heat replaced mineral cold. Wind touched the tunnel mouth ahead. Kem set a Ward at the exit, the lightest defensive shell he had left, enough to buy three seconds if someone was waiting outside and close enough to trigger on the first body through.

Twelve.

That was everything he owned in the world. Between them they had twenty. Enough for a mistake and a defense, maybe. Not enough for invention.

Vel adjusted the journal under her shirt and looked at him once. The look was not sentimental. It was inventory.

"If they catch us in the mouth," she said, "we turn around and die in a tunnel."

"Strong preference for the open air, then."

"Yes."

They stepped out into late-afternoon sun.

The basin floor hit like a fist made of heat and light. Red-gray bedrock, wind-scoured smooth, shimmered hard enough to hurt the eyes. Shallow erosion channels cut the floor into troughs and waist-high shelves of harder material. The threshold point lay somewhere eight hundred meters west, another patch of stripped ground blanched by sun and distance.

The garrison camp sat to the south-southwest. Two low buildings, a fire pit, a gear rack, and the line of washed-out clothes made an ugly little village out of duty. The circuit agent was between the camp and the eastern wall, closer than Kem wanted and moving on the line Vel had predicted.

The descent pair were still hidden behind the curve of the basin wall.

Commander Osen stood near the threshold strip as if she had known the exact minute they would appear.

"Eight hundred meters," Vel said. Her breathing had roughened now. Tunnel, heat, and spending had all started collecting in the same body.

"Open ground."

"Not quite." She pointed with two fingers. A shelf of wind-cut sandstone jutted from the floor twenty meters ahead. "That buys ten seconds."

The circuit agent turned. He saw them and shouted.

"Move," Vel said.

They ran.

The first stretch skimmed downhill across polished bedrock that wanted ankles more than footing. Kem heard the Ward at the tunnel mouth go off behind them with a flat hard crack and knew the spend had bought exactly what he had paid for. Dust burst from the entrance. Somebody swore. Vel hit the shelf first and dropped behind it with one knee down. Kem came in a heartbeat later, lungs burning.

"How many?" he asked.

"All of them if she has any sense."

"She does."

Osen did not charge. That was the worst part. She pointed once toward the north side of the basin and once toward the south. The permanent circuit agent cut away from the camp. One of the descent pair appeared at the top of the switchback and started down at a run. The other did not bother with the path. She vanished once and reappeared twenty paces lower, a short blink-step that made Kem's stomach flip.

"That seems unfair," he said.

"South line just got shorter," Vel said.

Osen herself stayed where she was, moving only enough to remain between them and the threshold.

The pattern snapped into place inside Kem. Osen had already described it, but seeing it happen made it uglier. She was not trying to meet them. She was cutting every straight line west and leaving only the longer, meaner routes across the basin's bad footing.

"She told you the truth," Vel said.

"She really did."

Osen's voice carried over the heat. "Keep them off the center. Don't force a spend unless you've got a body for it."

The order made Kem want to laugh and throw up together. They had become a scheduling problem.

Vel peered around the shelf and made her choice before the agents finished moving.

"North wash," she said. "Now."

She broke from cover first, not toward the threshold but northwest along an erosion trough that only pretended to be protection. Kem followed. Heat came up through his soles. The basin floor wore a bright mineral crust over softer grit, and twice it cracked under him with a nasty pottery sound that promised deeper collapse if he trusted it.

The trough bent around a low rise of pale shale. For five seconds they were hidden from Osen. On the sixth, the ground leveled and threw them back into open sight.

"Down," Vel snapped.

Kem dropped instinctively. A precise burst of shaped gravel hissed over where his head had been and shredded itself against the far lip of the trough. It was not enough to kill him. It was enough to teach him where the line was.

"Still unfair," he said, spitting dust.

"Get up."

The trough ended at a low step in the basin floor, not high enough to look dangerous until Kem tried to clear it at speed. Vel made it. Kem planted wrong. Grit rolled under his boot. His legs went out from under him and his chin smashed the edge hard enough to blank his vision.

He tasted copper immediately.

By the time he shoved himself upright, blood was already running down his throat and dripping off his jaw. Vel had doubled back three steps and was hauling him by the collar toward the next scrap of cover, a rib of sandstone no wider than a grave marker and not much more useful.

"Teeth?" she asked.

He tongued them fast, found all of them, and nearly laughed from relief. "I still have an unpleasant number."

"Good. Move."

From behind the rib, Kem saw the line settle wider. The permanent agent had taken the north angle. The blink-step specialist was arcing south in short efficient bursts that never looked flashy enough to be impressive and were therefore worse. The second descent agent held middle distance. Osen remained at the threshold strip, patient as drought.

She was not spending much. She did not need to. She had geography, training, and time, and any one of those would have been enough to ruin a person.

"How far to the wash?" Kem asked.

"Thirty meters and ugly."

"My favorite kind."

Vel looked once toward the threshold. It sat west of them in brutal plain sight, close enough that his body wanted to lie about it. Osen had somehow made eight hundred meters look shorter and farther at the same time.

"On me," Vel said.

They ran again.

This stretch dropped through a fan of loose gravel into a dry wash cut deeper than the first. The basin floor here had broken into layered steps of marl and basalt, each one hot enough to sting through worn soles. Kem heard the south agent blink-step again, heard the air pinch and release, and did not look because looking would have cost him footing.

The wash gave them fifteen good seconds.

Then it kinked west, opened, and offered a clean diagonal to the threshold that made every exhausted part of Kem want to believe in miracles. He saw Vel see it too. She leaned that way.

Osen had been waiting for exactly that.

The commander touched the ground with one hand.

The bright crust ahead of them cracked and sloughed away in a long sliding fan, a precise ugly failure that took the only clean diagonal west with it. Loose debris poured down the slope in a sound like dry applause. Vel stopped so hard her bad side dipped. Kem skidded into her shoulder.

"North," she said at once.

"That is farther from the point."

"Everything is farther from the point. Move."

They scrambled up the wash's northern lip. The climb was only chest high, but the marl broke under Kem's hands and sent him sliding back half a body length before he found purchase on a seam of harder rock. Vel got up first and turned to yank him the last bit. By the time he landed beside her, blood from his chin had dried tacky down his throat and the inside of his mouth tasted like a copper coin.

Osen's next order came over the basin, calm enough to make it feel personal.

"North and south. Make them tell you where they want the point."

Vel heard it too. He could tell by the flatness that came over her face. This was not a commander trying to end a fight fast. This was a commander trying to make the next several hours behave like policy.

The route north took them over a low shelf split by erosion channels. Vel judged the distance, chose speed over safety, and jumped the last drop instead of climbing it. She landed wrong. Her left foot folded under her on the slope. She caught herself on both hands, hissed once through her teeth, and stood anyway.

"What happened?" Kem asked.

"Ankle. It still works."

It worked with a hitch now. Her next step told the truth even if she didn't.

The threshold remained off to their left, white with heat and distance. The agents kept widening around them. The tunnel mouth behind them was gone as an option. The straight line west was gone too. Osen had taken the basin Kem first saw from the ridge and turned it into something administrative: not a battle, not a chase, but a long deliberate subtraction.

Vel wiped blood and dust off her hands onto her trousers, set her weight on the bad ankle as if daring it to argue, and looked north for the next piece of cover.

"Keep moving," she said.
