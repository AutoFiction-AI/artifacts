# Chapter 2

By the second afternoon outside the Vault, the smell of seven levels' worth of lunch was still following Kem Aldraste south.

It came over the rise in turns, sweet first and then rotten: split pears, cooling fat, wet sugar, the thick fly-buzz of food nobody had meant to leave in a meadow. Each time the breeze shifted, it told him exactly how far he had managed to get since feeding half the local animal life. Not far. Four hundred meters, perhaps. His right heel had broken open that morning. His left sole had split at the bend. The sun had got into the back of his neck and stayed there.

He kept the Aldraste signet turned inward so the crest stopped flashing in the light. Every few steps the ring bit his finger, as if the House wanted reminding that it had come with him. He let it bite. The road ahead ran through low grass and heat haze without improving at any visible point.

The road did not care who his father was. It did not care that he had been taught governance, precedent, and four dead languages. It cared about feet, water, shade, and whether a man understood the difference between wanting bread and accidentally provisioning a feast.

Two days earlier he had still believed leaving was the hard part.

---

In the Conservatory, the weekly objects had been a cracked bowl, browning flowers, an oil lamp, and a clock left unwound under excellent lighting. The great houses sat around them in formal silence while the objects failed in public. That was the Rite. One use from any person in the room could have repaired, lit, revived, or corrected the whole arrangement. Nobody moved.

His father had sat at the head of the Aldraste section with the full three hundred intact and his mind almost entirely gone. Lord Aldraste's body remained exact. The face still carried dignity. The posture still held. But the person inside had thinned year by year into pauses, vacant eyes, and the careful assistance of servants who pretended not to assist. The Hoarded called that Stillness and lowered their voices around the word. Kem's mother, precise as ever, had reached over midway through the Rite and corrected Lord Aldraste's collar without looking at him. A second flower petal had dropped from the arrangement. Kem had stood.

His mother said his name once.

That was all. No command. No plea. Just "Kem," spoken with the same control she brought to everything else, and enough feeling under it to make staying impossible.

He walked out. At the Conservatory doors he discovered, all at once and in the most absurd available way, that twenty-six years of never opening anything for himself had not prevented the Ledger from waiting. He reached for the handle. A Projection Shield answered. The doors went off their hinges hard enough to crater plaster. Three levels disappeared with the impact. Kem stared at the wreckage like a man who had sneezed and found a wall missing.

The Vault after that turned strange. Corridors he had crossed all his life without seeing them suddenly looked paid for down to the lamp oil. He took wrong turns because servants had always known where to go on his behalf. One of those turns carried him past a darker stretch of corridor the household never named: thicker walls, sealed doors, no carpet, silence with work in it rather than emptiness. He kept moving because curiosity was not more urgent than leaving.

At the gate hall two guards tried protocol. Kem said he was leaving. One of them began explaining notification requirements. He broke the gate latch with another Shield before she finished the sentence. Two more levels. Air hit him from outside, damp and cold and much too large. By the ceremonial pedestals he took the Aldraste signet because leaving it behind would have made the whole departure look considered. It was not considered. It was only late.

The road down from the Vault started teaching immediately. Dirt shifted. Roots caught at his shoes. By the time he reached open country his calves had begun to shake and his right heel had started to blister. He had brought robes for sitting, shoes for marble, and nothing else useful: no bag, no water skin, no map, no money he knew how to handle, and two hundred and ninety-five levels he did not trust.

Hunger arrived before competence did. Kem knew the theory of a Sustenance Charm. He knew it the way he knew migration histories and trade law, as clean information with no dirt on it. In a meadow beyond the pines, with his stomach pulling hard and the sun on the back of his neck, he asked the Ledger for bread.

The Ledger, having no experience of moderation through him, supplied House Aldraste.

Loaves hit the grass first. Then a roast. Then fruit, cheese, pastries, pickled vegetables, bowls of stew, and at the end a three-tiered cake that nearly slid off the stump where it appeared. He caught it badly and stood there with icing on his sleeves, looking down at one of the family's respectable guest lunches spread across weeds and dirt. The menu was unmistakable. The charm had reached into his memory for the only meals he knew and fed the meadow accordingly.

Seven levels.

He ate bread while birds and then a fox joined him. He tried to think of a practical way to carry any of it and failed. He left the feast to insects, animals, and the heat, and walked south with twelve levels gone in his first hours outside.

---

The arithmetic looked worse by nightfall.

He did not make another ability attempt. After the door, the gate, and the banquet, using a charm for anything smaller than a siege felt like asking a drunk giant to thread a needle. When dusk came he kept what remained of the bread under his robe, followed running water by sound, and found a stream narrow enough to step over and cold enough to hurt his teeth. He drank too fast, made himself cough, washed icing off the signet, and sat on the bank until the dark completed itself around him.

Sleeping outside turned out to be less like sleeping and more like lying on hard ground while the world refused to stay theoretical. Every sound demanded classification. Insects worried at his ears. Something heavy moved once in the brush and kept moving. Cold came up through the soil and settled in his hips. He dozed in short collapses and woke each time certain he had heard the Conservatory clock start ticking.

Morning did not improve him. His back had stiffened. The right heel had burst inside the boot. His left sole began to split before the sun fully cleared the horizon. He ate stale bread and walked because the alternative was turning north, and north led to the Vault, the Rite, his father's open eyes, and the life waiting for him if he returned to it.

By noon he had learned another road fact: injury made distance dishonest. Four hundred meters with good feet was a correction. Four hundred meters with wet fabric grinding against raw skin was an occupation. He kept stopping without meaning to stop. First to retie a lace that did not need retightening. Then to shift the signet because the metal had rubbed his finger raw. Then because black spots swam briefly at the edge of his sight when he stood too quickly.

Three times he considered spending.

The thoughts arrived dressed as practicality. A Mending Charm for the torn sole. A minor Restoration for the heel. Perhaps a careful pull for water. Each time he imagined the same result: a repaired boot costing a wall, a healed blister costing a horse, a cup of water arriving as a flooded ditch. He had enough levels left to do catastrophic things in comfort. He did not yet know how to do small ones.

So he sat when his body made him sit. He stood when the pain eased enough to permit it. He kept south because south was the direction available, and because motion, however stupid, was still different from the family art of remaining where one had been placed.

The wind shifted again. Rot and sugar found him.

Kem pressed the heel of his palm against his eyes until light broke into color. When he dropped it, the road remained. Low grass moved. Heat stood over the open ground. His foot throbbed in time with his pulse.

Two hundred and eighty-eight still hummed at the edge of him, ridiculous and enormous and useless in all the ways that mattered. Enough to buy doors off hinges, enough to feed forty people by mistake, enough, perhaps, to keep him alive if he ever learned how not to spend like a disaster. The number no longer felt like status. Out here it felt like something he had to drag south until it turned into use.

He made it to a fallen log by the roadside before the heel gave him terms again. Getting the boot off took longer than it should have. He hissed when the sock pulled loose. For a while he sat with his foot in his lap and watched the road as if watching hard enough might teach him how to use it.
