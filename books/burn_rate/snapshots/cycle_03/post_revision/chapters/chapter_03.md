# Chapter 3

She found him by the smell.

Two days north of the settlement where she'd healed the farmer, late-spring air went sweet and rotten at once. Vel stepped off the road into a meadow churned flat by one man's panic. A Sustenance Charm had gone off there with banquet logic: loaves split open by fox teeth, fruit liquefying in the grass, a metal basin of stew sagging as the conjured shape gave up. Enough food remained to feed a caravan, if the caravan liked flies.

The waste annoyed her less than the arithmetic behind it. The road could chew through a fool by way of weather, bad boots, streams that looked shallow until they weren't, and the small constant humiliations of having a body in open country. That part wasn't new. The new part sat farther south, beyond the towns and the ridge lines and the slow country where most people turned sensible and went home. The last pages of the route journal didn't care much about weather. They cared about reserve. They cared about what a person had left when the road stopped being road and started asking for something uglier.

Standing in the stink of spoiled fruit, Vel realized the idiot had brought enough.

She hated the relief before it had even finished arriving.

Four hundred meters farther on, she found him sitting on a fallen log with one boot off and his foot in his lap, staring at a blistered heel with the focused misery of a man trying to negotiate with his own skin. His road boots were soft Vault leather, good for polished floors and ceremonial corridors and not much else. One sole had split at the bend. As Vel watched, a pale Glow rose over the raw heel and cost him a level just to tell him what pain had already explained.

He dropped the light a moment later and swore under his breath without conviction.

Vel stayed in the tree line long enough to take him in. He was tall, maybe mid-twenties, with sunburn starting at the nose and ears where three days outdoors had already made an argument with his face. His cream-and-gray Vault clothes were snagged and dirt-marked now, but expensive even ruined. No calluses showed anywhere. Bright in the afternoon light, the Aldraste signet flashed on his right ring finger.

This was Kem Aldraste, heir to one of the richest dead houses in the world, sitting in the weeds with his skin coming off.

Vel stepped out of the trees.

He looked up fast. Surprise hit first, then caution, then a burst of naked relief he nearly got under control in time.

"Hello," he said. "Are you real, or have I finally overdone the heat?"

"Real."

He let out a breath. "Good. I was worried the hallucinations would start with someone less practical looking."

That was at least more useful than a greeting. Vel came closer. Up close, the damage looked worse. Blisters had burst and bled into each other. Dirt sat in the creases of his feet. He had walked on them anyway because nobody had told him not to and because the road never asked whether a person deserved the lesson.

"You spending on that?" she asked.

His eyes flicked to the heel in his lap. "I was trying not to lose the skin entirely."

"By looking at it."

"Yes." He winced. "No. All right. That sounded bad even before you said it back to me."

Vel crouched in front of him. "How many now?"

That brief inward stillness crossed his face while he checked the Ledger. "Two hundred and eighty-six."

Two more gone since whatever point the gate story had last reached her. One of them had vanished in front of her for the privilege of examining a blister. He still sounded more startled by the number than ashamed of it.

"You know that's terrible," he said quickly. "I do know that. I'm not pretending I don't."

"You know it's bad," Vel said. "Not the same thing."

He looked at her as if he wanted to argue and had already discovered he didn't have the footing for it.

"Are the Spent Towns close?" he asked instead.

"A week."

He stared.

"A week on a normal pair of feet," Vel said. "On yours, longer."

"Right." He looked down at the torn skin. "Yes. That seems fair."

The word fair, in that voice, almost made her leave him there.

"What else?" she asked.

"What else what?"

"What else have you spent on since this?" She jerked her chin back toward the rotting meadow. "I know about the food."

Color came up under the sunburn. "A little warmth last night."

"How little?"

"One level."

"For spring wind?"

"It was colder than I anticipated."

"What else?"

He rubbed a thumb against his knee. "I used a Glow this morning because I thought something was following me. It was a goat."

"Good use."

"I know you're being cruel. I just don't have a defense prepared." He paused. "And there was a stream."

"What about it?"

"I misjudged the bank. My clothes were wet. I considered walking damp an indignity of a kind I had not been trained for."

Vel waited.

He shut his eyes once. "I dried them with a Charm."

"Anything else."

"There are probably two or three smaller embarrassments that are not coming to mind because the larger ones are standing on them."

That was closer to honest than most people got while they were still above fifty.

Vel sat on the other end of the log because her knees had started making demands. The road lay empty in both directions. Heat pressed down through the trees. Behind them the feast kept rotting in the grass.

"You know what a level costs?" she asked.

He gave her a tired look. "If I say yes, you'll ask whether I know it in my body or just in theory."

"Do you?"

"In theory." He rubbed at his mouth, then corrected himself. "No. That's too easy. I know it's permanent. I know it doesn't come back. I know all the family language about discipline and sacred restraint and the moral terror of waste. But at two hundred and eighty-six it still feels like a number I could misplace and recover later. My body hasn't understood permanence yet. It still thinks this is all reversible."

Vel stayed on the log, jaw locking once against the ache in her knee. A minute ago she'd been close enough to walking back to the road. Honest made that harder than begging did.

"It'll learn," she said.

"I had gathered."

He shifted to pull the boot back on, and she caught his wrist before he could force torn leather over the raw heel. His skin was warm and softer than road skin had any right to be.

"No."

He glanced from her grip to his foot. "Do you mean no boot or no movement or no in general?"

"I mean stop doing that."

"Then I need a more detailed plan."

Vel let go. "You can walk six miles if I wrap those feet and if you stop acting like pain is a paperwork error."

"Six miles to what?"

"Trading post."

"And after that?"

"South."

He took that in. "You're going south?"

"Yes."

"To zero?"

She watched his face sharpen around the guess. He had the look of a man assembling a thought from scraps he didn't know he had.

"You know what it is?" he said.

"Enough."

"Enough to go there on purpose?"

"Yes."

He looked away first. His thumb found the signet as if it had moved there by itself.

"What are you doing out here?" Vel asked.

The formal answer arrived on his face before the true one did. She watched him feel it happen.

"My father is level three hundred," he said. "He has been level three hundred for sixty-one years. He sits in the Conservatory every week in a perfect body and there is less of him there each time. Everyone calls it composure. They say he has achieved peace. They say-" He cut himself off, pressed his lips together, started again smaller. "I sat beside him two days ago and realized we were all using pretty words for rot. That's the useful version. The longer one just gets angrier."

"Stillness," Vel said.

He nodded. "Yes. That."

"Everyone outside the Vault knows the word."

"I thought they might." He gave a short, humorless breath. "We pretend it isn't visible. That's a habit, not a fact."

Vel looked at him, at the ruined feet and the soft skin and the ring he could not stop turning. The Hoarded had preserved him badly. That much was already obvious. The less obvious thing was that he'd walked away anyway.

"And now?" she asked.

He answered without theatrics. "I want zero."

Vel's stomach pulled tight. By dark she could get him to the trading post, trade the coat if she had to, and keep him off those feet long enough for the bandages to matter. The stretch beyond the towns still wanted a spender with reserve to burn at the far end. Thirty-one wouldn't do it. Two hundred and eighty-six might.

She stood.

"I'm going to wrap your feet," she said. "Then we're walking to the trading post."

He blinked. "You have skipped over several negotiations I was expecting."

"We can talk while you bleed slower."

"That sentence would worry me more if I had alternatives."

"You do. Stay here and lose the rest by inches."

He opened his mouth, shut it, then held out his foot with bad grace. "Fine. But if you're about to cut anything, I'd like advance notice. I've had a day full of unwelcome discoveries."

Vel set her pack down, pulled out the small roll of clean cloth she kept for jobs that paid badly and bled anyway, and a needle wrapped in oilskin. She tipped water over his heel first. Mud ran over her skin. He hissed through his teeth and tried not to pull back.

"You don't get to spend on this," she said.

"I was not planning to."

She lifted a brow.

"I was planning to think about planning to," he said. "Is that also forbidden?"

"Yes."

The needle went in. Fluid welled and ran. Kem made a sound like a laugh being strangled. His grip locked on the log hard enough that the knuckles blanched.

"If you're trying to teach me anything," he said tightly, "you should know this is an unusual educational framework."

"I'm teaching you that pain isn't an emergency."

"It feels eager to disagree."

She drained the worst of the blisters, pressed the skin flat, and bound the heel. The other foot took longer. By the end his face had gone pale under the burn and sweat stood along his hairline.

"Can you stand?" she asked.

"Can and want are not aligned."

"Stand."

He did, badly. His knees shook once and corrected. He took a testing step and flinched hard enough to bare his teeth.

"Six miles," he said.

"If you stop talking about it."

"I talk when I'm miserable."

"Then today you'll talk a lot."

He looked at her, searching for the joke under the sentence and not finding one. "You really are like this."

"Put the boots on loose. We buy new ones there."

He bent to obey, slower this time.

"With what money," he said, "exactly?"

Vel looked at the signet.

He covered it at once. "No."

"Didn't even make an offer."

"You looked at it in a way I found extremely specific."

"Boots first. Sentiment after."

"This is not sentiment."

"Then it's stupid."

He laughed once in disbelief, then winced because even that seemed to pull somewhere lower in his body. "You have known me ten minutes."

"Long enough for a sample."

They started south.

Kem lasted less than a quarter mile before the road taught him the difference between wrapped feet and healed feet. He limped without trying to hide it. Vel cut his pace down until it stopped resembling normal travel and became a measured insult to distance. That was still faster than he'd have managed alone.

"Do people actually walk like this for weeks," he asked after a while, "or am I seeing a special degraded version?"

"People with working feet walk faster."

"That wasn't what I meant."

"It's the answer you need."

He breathed out through his nose. "You do that on purpose."

"Yes."

The road curved around a stand of scrub pine and dropped toward a creek bed lined with alder and stone. Kem stared at the water as if it were a legal trap.

"Can I ask a practical question without being mocked for upbringing?"

"Probably not."

"Encouraging." He pointed. "How do you tell whether that's safe?"

Vel crouched at the bank. "You smell it. Look for flow. Dead things. Upstream camp. Algae. Scum. Animal sign. Rot." She dipped a knuckle, lifted a bead of water, then tasted it. "This is fine."

He watched with concentrated offense. "You can tell all that from one look?"

"Mostly from twenty years of not wanting to shit myself to death."

"Useful. Horrifying, but useful."

He knelt with care and reached for the surface.

"Not there," Vel said. "Current runs cleaner a little in. Make a bowl."

He did. Water slopped over his cuff and he muttered something Hoarded and brittle under his breath.

"What was that?"

"A liturgical complaint from my grandmother. It seemed appropriate."

"Road doesn't care about your grandmother."

"The road has made that point repeatedly."

He drank, then sat back on his heels and looked at the creek, the bank, the trees leaning over it as if the world had committed a private offense by containing this much unarranged detail.

"How far have you come alone?" Vel asked.

"Three days."

"No. How far?"

He thought about it. "I don't know."

"That's worse."

"I didn't expect mileage to matter more than direction."

"Direction tells you where to die. Distance tells you whether it happens before dark."

Kem went quiet and stepped into the creek with his mouth set hard. The stones tipped under his weight because he placed each foot like a man asking permission. On the second one his balance went bad, arms windmilling, and pale light gathered under the signet before Vel said, "No," sharply enough that the Glow died before it fully formed.

Kem froze on the middle stone.

"I was going to catch myself," he said.

"You were going to spend because you leaned wrong."

"I was going to fall."

"Into knee-deep water."

"On cut feet."

"Then fall better."

He opened his mouth, closed it, and opened it again. "You're serious."

"Completely."

He got to the far bank the ugly way, soaked from one shin down and furious in a thin, bewildered fashion that would have been funny if it weren't attached to three hundred levels of catastrophe.

"If I die because I honored that instruction," he said, "I intend to haunt you in very specific language."

"You'll have to walk back from zero first."

That stopped him long enough that she got five quiet steps before he caught up.

"You have seen it," he said. "The place?"

"Yes."

"And you're still talking like that?"

"Like what?"

"Like it is a road marker and not the end of every known category."

Vel kept walking. The pack rubbed at one shoulder. Heat gathered between her shoulder blades. Ahead, the road cut through open grass and the trading post's roofline began to show above a stand of cottonwoods.

"What are you offering," Kem said, "beyond boots and cruelty?"

"A guide. Training. Fewer stupid spends. I get you to the south country alive."

"And what do you get?"

There was no point dressing that answer up. "Your reserve."

He took two more limping steps before the words caught up with him. "Meaning?"

"Meaning I'm at thirty-one and you're not. Meaning the road will keep trying to peel little pieces off you before the part that matters gets a turn."

He looked at her, sharp now despite the pain. "The part that matters."

"Yes."

"And you won't tell me what that is."

"Not yet."

He made a frustrated sound. "Then how am I meant to judge this?"

Vel kept her eyes on the roofline ahead. "Judge the part you have. I know the route. I know what kind of mistakes leave a person with a big number and nothing useful left of it. If you keep spending on cold toes and bad footing, your count won't arrive in one piece."

He stared at her. "So you don't just need levels. You need me manageable."

"I need you listening."

"That sounds worse."

"It's the same road."

"Then why spend the last hour teaching me how to drink from a creek?"

"Because if you die of infection six miles from soap the far end won't care why."

His mouth tightened. When he spoke again, the anger had gone out of it and left math behind.

"You want me intact."

"Useful."

"That is not a comforting correction."

Vel said nothing.

He walked in silence for a few paces. Pain made his limp worse when he stopped thinking about it. The trading post came clearer: weather-silvered boards, a watering trough, two tether posts, a wagon axle leaned against the wall waiting for repair. Someone had planted onions in a patch of dust behind it because road people would plant anything if it gave them one more reason not to move.

"If I go with you," he said, "the arrangement is that you decide when I spend."

"Yes."

"And when I stop."

"Yes."

"And where. And what counts as worth it."

"Yes."

He let out a tired laugh that had no pleasure in it. "You could at least pretend to be subtle."

"We're almost at the post. I'm not wasting breath pretending."

"So does being manipulated, apparently."

Vel stopped. The movement made him stop too because he was not coordinated enough yet to limp and think around corners.

"If you think that's what this is," she said, "go on alone. Spend to dry your cuffs. Spend to light your camp. Spend because the ground is hard and the owls sound judgmental. Keep enough for whatever speech you plan to make when you hit zero by yourself."

He flinched, not at the words but at the picture in them. That was good. Pictures held longer than principles.

"I didn't say no," he said.

"Then decide faster."

The post door opened. A woman in a stained apron came out carrying a sack of feed to the trough. She glanced at them once, took in Vel, took in Kem, took in the silk, and looked away in the efficient road manner that meant she had seen stranger trouble and had no intention of inviting new.

Kem lowered his voice. "I need one condition."

Vel waited.

"If you're leaving something out, say that you're leaving it out. Don't answer around it so neatly I only notice later."

"Fine," she said. "If I won't answer, I'll tell you I won't answer."

"That's not trust."

"No."

"It's not enough either."

"It's what exists."

He rubbed one eye, then dropped it. "I hate that you're the best option in sight."

"That's road for you."

"Is there anything you don't turn into a lesson?"

"Sleep, sometimes."

He almost smiled despite himself. Then the moment passed and he nodded once, short and irritated. "Fine. I accept the guide, the route, your authority over ability use, and the insulting educational framework."

"Good."

"I reserve the right to complain."

"You already proved that."

"I also don't trust you."

"That part's healthy."

He exhaled. "I was hoping, very briefly, that you might say something normal."

"We're buying boots."

Kem made a face and limped after her to the porch.

The woman at the trough had gone back inside by the time they reached the porch. Up close the trading post smelled of lamp oil, grain dust, leather, and old coffee. A narrow bell hung over the door and gave one halfhearted knock when Kem pushed it open.

Shelves lined the walls in cramped order: nails, soap, salt, thread, lamp chimneys, dried beans, cheap knives, boot grease, feed biscuits, bandage rolls. Everything a little more expensive than it should have been and a little less clean. The woman behind the counter was maybe fifty, broad across the shoulders, hair twisted into a knot so tight it pulled the skin at her temples.

Her gaze went to Vel first.

"Need boots," Vel said.

"For him," the woman said.

"No," Kem said. "For a passing dog with similar feet."

The woman's mouth twitched despite herself. "Then it's him."

Vel leaned on the counter. "Room for bandaging. Hard sole. Something that survives weather and bad judgment."

"That's a big category." The woman's eyes flicked down to Kem's feet, then back up. "How bad?"

"Bad enough that I don't want him trying them on more than once."

Kem looked between them. "I'm here. We can all proceed as if I have hearing."

"Can you pay?" the woman asked him, not unkindly.

He glanced at the shelves as if marks might appear there through force of etiquette. "That depends what form of payment the establishment accepts."

Vel looked at the signet again.

He covered it at once. "No."

The woman saw the motion. Her face flattened. "I don't take House pieces."

Kem stared. "You recognized it from that distance?"

"Everybody recognizes trouble." She wiped her hands on the apron. "I've got no use for Hoarded metal and less use for the questions that come after. You have coin, use coin. You have goods, use goods. Ring stays where it is or goes somewhere else."

For the first time since Vel had met him, shame moved across Kem's face clean enough to read.

"I don't have coin," he said.

"Then what do you have?"

He looked down at himself. The answer sat on him in cream-and-gray layers.

Vel said, "Coat."

"This coat cost more than the building."

"Then you'll get boots and a shirt out of it."

"This is extortion by weather."

"This is trade," the woman said. "Weather is separate."

Kem shut his eyes for a second. When he opened them, the formal mask had come back on in thin, strained patches.

"What would the coat purchase," he asked, "assuming the current market values are governed by daylight robbery?"

"Boots, one plain shirt, one pack if the seams hold, bandage roll, soap scrap, flint." The woman looked him over again. "Maybe not the shirt if the embroidery's mostly thread and not metal."

"It isn't embroidered."

"Then maybe."

He pinched the bridge of his nose. "I need the coat at night."

"You need feet more," Vel said.

"That is not mutually exclusive."

"Road says it is."

His jaw worked. He stripped the coat off with visible reluctance and laid it on the counter like a body being surrendered for burial. Under it he wore a fine linen shirt already damp at the spine, sleeves rolled badly, collar gone gray with sweat. He looked younger without the outer layer, less like a House son and more like a tall man making expensive mistakes.

The woman turned the coat, checked seams, lining, buttons, then gave one short nod.

"Good cloth," she said. "Stupid cut."

"That was my impression of most of the Vault, yes," Kem muttered.

"Shirt too," Vel said.

He looked at her in genuine alarm. "I am wearing the shirt."

"You have one in the trade."

"You are assuming both success and decency from this room."

"Decency's not for sale here," the woman said, disappearing into the back. "Boots might be."

When she returned she carried three pairs, a rolled shirt, and a pack that had been mended twice along one strap. She dropped them on the counter one by one.

"Sit," Vel said.

Kem sat on an upturned feed crate with the air of a man accepting public execution. Vel unwrapped the bandages while the woman handed over the first pair. Too narrow. The second rubbed the heel. The third had enough room for cloth and swelling. Kem set his teeth through the test and did not ask for Glow. That counted for something.

"Take those," Vel said.

"They're hideous."

"They're boots."

"I see the family resemblance."

The woman snorted. "Pack too. Not because the coat covers it. Because I'd rather not see you back here carrying blankets in your arms."

"That almost sounds kind."

"It's preventative." She shoved the rolled shirt at him. "Change in the storage room if modesty survived the road."

It had, apparently. He came back in the plain shirt a minute later looking offended by the fabric. The cut was coarse and hung badly on him. It also fit a person who meant to move in it, which seemed more relevant.

Vel took the discarded coat, folded it, and watched the woman weigh it with the rest of the trade. Kem stared at the counter where the ring sat alone now, stripped of everything else with a House smell on it.

"You'll want these too," the woman said, adding two pairs of thick socks after a moment. "Call it professional mercy."

"You do have decency," Kem said.

"I didn't say it was free."

She took the coat and old boots, passed over the boots, socks, shirt, pack, flint, soap scrap, a stub of candle, and the bandage roll, then looked at Kem's ruined feet once more.

"You walk in those tonight, you'll bleed through by moonrise," she said. "Slow him down or leave him."

"Planning to slow him," Vel said.

"Good."

Kem settled the new boots on the floor beside him and reached for the pack. The repaired strap rasped over his new shirt when he lifted it to test the weight.

"That's unpleasant," he said.

"You're carrying your own things now," Vel said.

"I had inferred the symbolism."

"It's not symbolism. It's load."

They refilled water at the trough outside. The sun had dropped low enough to throw long shadows from the tether posts across the yard. Kem moved with the caution of a man trying to keep his skin attached. The woman came to the doorway while Vel tightened the pack on his shoulders.

"If anybody asks," she said, "I sold to a road fixer and some fool from the north. I don't know houses. I don't know names."

Vel nodded once.

Kem looked at her. "Do they ask often?"

"Often enough." The woman's gaze touched the signet and moved off it. "Cover that if you know what's good for you."

He looked down at the ring as if he'd forgotten what was on him. For the first time since Vel found him, he twisted the crest inward against his skin.

Good. One less stupid question to answer at the next post.

They left the post at dusk and walked until the sky bled orange along the ridge line ahead. Vel got them off the road into a stand of scrub oak and low grass where last year's fire had left a ring of blackened stones ready for reuse. Kem set the pack down with a noise that was almost obscene in its relief.

"We're stopping," he said.

"We're stopped."

"Good." He sat on the ground too fast, flinched at what the jolt did to his feet, and let his head tip back toward the darkening sky. "I understand, abstractly and physically now, why people complain about walking."

Vel handed him the flint and steel.

He opened one eye. "What am I doing with this?"

"Making fire."

"No. I can tell from shape what it is. What I mean is, specifically, what delusion do you have in mind?"

"Kindling's there." She pointed to the scrub they'd gathered on the way in. "Tinder under the rock. Start learning."

He looked from the flint to the wood and back again. "You could do this in half the time."

"Yes."

"And instead we're staging a moral demonstration."

"Instead I'm not teaching you that your body is decoration."

He sat with that, annoyed enough to be quiet. Then he knelt by the fire ring and tried.

The first strike sent sparks into the dirt. The second jumped wide. The third clipped his knuckle and he swore, more feeling in it this time.

"Angle," Vel said.

"That is not a complete instruction."

"It's the one you earned."

He tried again. One strike sparked. The next missed. The third threw another spray of light and raised a thread of smoke. His whole face changed when the tinder finally caught, not triumph exactly, but startled satisfaction, a child-level surprise that effort could turn into result without numbers passing through it first.

"There," he said, breathless with annoyance at himself for being pleased. "That seems insultingly primitive."

"Still works."

"Yes. I see the argument."

They fed the flame carefully until it held. Vel set a small pot with creek water near the edge to warm, more for foot cleaning than drink. Kem leaned back, braced on his arms, shoulders trembling from the accumulated strain.

"You really mean it?" he said after a while. "No ability use unless you say so?"

"Yes."

"Even if something would be easier?"

"Especially then."

He nodded once, eyes on the fire. "All right."

That came too easily. Vel distrusted it on principle.

"Take the boots off," she said.

His head snapped toward her. "That sentence has become a threat."

"Take them off before they dry to the bandages."

He obeyed more slowly. Blood had spotted through at both heels, just as the woman predicted. It wasn't catastrophic. It wasn't good either. Vel cleaned and rewound the feet while the water steamed. He breathed through it and let the pain have the room it needed. That, more than the roadside obedience, made her look at him again.

"Why are you doing this?" he asked when she tied the last strip of cloth. "Not the grand answer. Not zero. This part. Me?"

Vel rinsed off in the pot and dumped the pink water into the dirt.

"Because thirty-one doesn't buy me enough mistakes," she said.

"That's not an answer."

"It's the one you need tonight."

"No," he said, then corrected himself with a tired shake of the head. "No, that's not fair. It is an answer. It's just not the one I was asking after."

Vel sat across the fire from him. The dark was coming on for real now, edges softening, tree trunks reducing to columns and breaks in the dark. Crickets started up in the grass.

"You said you'd tell me when you were leaving things out," he said.

"I am leaving things out."

He accepted that with a visible effort. "Thank you for at least not pretending otherwise."

For a while they ate in silence: hard bread from the post, dried apples, a heel of cheese Vel had been carrying since the farmer's settlement. Kem chewed like every bite required private argument. When he finished, he looked at the fire again with the same suspicious wonder he'd given it when it caught.

"Did you know," he said, "before you saw me? About me, specifically?"

"Only the story."

"House son walks out. Wastes levels. Heads south."

"Enough."

He worried the crust to crumbs. "You came north because of that?"

Vel said nothing.

"That's a yes by road standards, I think."

"It's a silence."

"You're very committed to classification."

"Keeps things tidy."

"Nothing about this feels tidy."

That was true enough that she let it sit.

The fire settled lower. Beyond it the dark held everything the world didn't need them to see yet. Kem rolled his shoulders once, then looked down at the ring turned inward against his skin.

"At the post," he said, "she looked at that and saw trouble. I spent twenty-six years being told people would see dignity."

"People in the Vault might."

"Yes. Well." He worried the ring with his thumb. "I suppose that counts as one more educational framework."

Vel watched the motion. "Why keep it?"

He didn't answer at once. That by itself told her it wasn't vanity, or not only vanity. Vanity answered faster.

"I took it because leaving it felt staged," he said at last. "If I walked out and arranged the symbolism of it nicely on a table first, then I'd still have been doing House work. I wanted to leave badly, I think. Untidily." He looked up. "And then, once I had it, taking it off started feeling like agreeing that they got to define what it meant."

That was more complicated than the road usually allowed people to be. Vel found herself tired enough to hear it anyway.

"It won't buy you much south," she said.

"No. I noticed."

"Might buy you trouble."

"That too."

He twisted it again until the crest faced inward once more. "Then I'll learn to carry trouble more practically."

Vel lay back on her rolled blanket with the route journal under her forearm. The leather had gone soft at the edges over the years. Kem noticed the motion.

"Is that the route?" he asked.

"Part of it."

"Yours."

"Mine now."

"That is not the same answer."

"No."

He watched the worn leather a moment. "The someone you're leaving out."

"Yes."

"What did she leave you besides directions?"

Vel looked past the fire. "The only notes I've got on where the road stops behaving clean. Where fear gets expensive and a bad spend keeps costing."

Kem was quiet for a beat. "And you trust that."

"I trust the pages."

"And her."

"Enough to still be carrying them."

"Why?"

"Because I owe her."

The word sat between them.

"For what?"

Vel kept her voice flat. "For leaving them with me."

He looked from the journal to her face. "So that's the real arrangement. I follow you south, and whatever she left in that book gets a say in what happens to me."

Vel did not correct him.

He studied her a moment longer, not angry exactly, but newly careful. "Right," he said. "That's worse."

He pulled his blanket up and turned onto one side with the awkward care of a man learning that every position had a cost. A little later he rolled back again because the first arrangement hurt too much. Then he rolled again because the second did. Vel listened to the rustle and the hissed breaths and said nothing. Pain taught better when it wasn't being watched.

The fire burned down. Night widened around them. Somewhere to the west an owl called once and waited, as if expecting a reply from a world with more manners.

Kem's voice came out of the dark after a long time.

"Are you asleep?"

"No."

"Good. I have one more question and then I'll shut up because I can feel your patience from here and it has edges."

Vel waited.

"If the count matters at the far end," he said, "what matters on the way besides feet?"

That was a better question than most.

"Judgment," she said. "Pace. Weather. Infection. Other people. Knowing which bad thing is worth paying to avoid and which one just wants to scare you into spending."

"And if I can't tell?"

"Then you ask."

"And if you're wrong?"

Vel turned her head and looked at him across the coals. "Then we pay for it."

He held her gaze a second, then nodded in the dark. "All right."

His breathing went longer and slower after that. Not sleep right away, but the road version of surrender. Vel waited until it tipped fully over, then drew the route journal into her lap and opened it by the last of the coals.

The earlier pages held mileage, wells, turns in the ridge, the names of settlements that no longer existed or had changed shape since the ink first dried. The later pages tightened. The handwriting there pressed harder, crowded closer, as if the writer had begun to understand that neatness was a luxury the south wouldn't reimburse. Weather notes thinned out. Supply notes disappeared. Distance stopped mattering. Reserve mattered. Timing mattered. The threshold pages had always looked wrong to Vel, less like travel instructions than an argument with a machine.

She traced the scored paper where the older hand had dug in too hard.

The threshold notes all drove at the same ugly point: whatever reached the far end had to arrive with reserve still in one piece and a head steady enough to choose where it went. She had enough for the road if nothing spectacular happened. She did not have enough to make the far end answer.

Two hundred and eighty-six.

The relief sat sour in her stomach.

Kem shifted in his sleep, one fist closing around the blanket edge, the other around nothing at all.

If he learned fast, if he stopped spending on fear every time fear changed clothes, if nobody from the Vault caught them first, he might still have enough when they reached the threshold.

Tomorrow meant slow miles, dry camp, and killing the Glow before it formed again if she had to.

Vel closed the journal and lay down without moving off it.

By dawn he would still be at two hundred and eighty-six. If he wasn't, the road had already started eating what she came north to keep.
