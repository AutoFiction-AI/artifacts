# Chapter 14

"Path forks," Vel said. "South-southeast. Water first."

The lower road dropped into a settlement built around a crack that had not finished deciding what it wanted. Kem saw the seam first: a split running north-south through the middle, three houses on the east lip leaning toward it, the west side clustered around a well and a retaining wall of quarried blocks that looked older than the trust placed in them. One roof had already gone in. Somebody had stacked the salvage beside it in neat unhappy rows.

Dren squinted at the well. "Good."

That was all. Not good ground. Not good rest. Water. His bad forearm was wrapped tight against his chest and he kept looking at the bucket as if it might crawl off before he reached it.

Vel headed straight for the well. Dren veered with her. Kem followed because anywhere else would have read as performance.

At the curb, a woman with a shaved scalp and flour on her forearms kept winding the bucket rope as if newcomers were no more surprising than weather. Dren reached for the handle.

"You pull, you fill it for the next person too," she said.

"Fine."

"And if your people are planning to quarrel, do it west of the crack. East side's touchy today."

Dren looked at Vel. Vel looked at the bucket. Kem, against his own judgment, said, "We're not planning to quarrel."

"Then you're ahead of most people." Her attention caught on his voice, the careful old structure still clinging to it.

Dren snorted once. It cost him.

He started hauling one-armed, the bad forearm braced under the rope. The pulley shrieked. Water came up slow and black and cold enough that Kem's mouth hurt before it touched him. Vel drank first, quick and efficient. Dren drank next. Kem washed grit from his lips, then kept scrubbing at his wrists after the useful part was done.

That was how they traveled now. Vel gave directions. Dren handled the world if it needed handling. Kem answered when silence would have drawn more attention than speech. Everything Vel had taught him still lived in his body after trust went bad, which was almost funny if he let himself think about it that way. Thirty-one hummed at the back of his skull, the number she had carried when they met, his now. He could not decide whether that made the whole thing intimate or obscene.

The flour-armed woman jerked her chin toward the west rise. "If you're staying till dark, camp up there. East lip's moving."

"South road?" Vel asked.

"Depends what you mean by road." The woman let the rope run and caught it again. "Track south out of here still exists. Ground under it's got opinions. Quarry cut on the west side's ugly but holds. Mostly."

"Mostly," Dren repeated.

"You want certainty, try the Vault."

Kem let that one land and kept his face out of it.

A quarryman came up carrying a brace of salvaged planks across one shoulder. Stone grit lived in the seams of his neck. "If you're camping, west side only. East side's for people who already know which houses are lying."

"Three bedrolls," Dren said. "One fire."

The quarryman looked from Dren to Vel to Kem and paused in the open space between the last two. "One fire's ambitious."

No one answered.

He shifted the planks. "Your problem. Keep it uphill from the grain and don't put your packs where kids can find them."

That should have been the end of it. It almost was. Then somewhere east a voice started calling for help, not loud and not wild, just steady with the rhythm of someone who had been saying the same words for long enough to wear the panic down to function.

Both Vel and Kem turned toward it at once.

The two-story house at the eastern lip had gone past leaning and into decision. Its upper floor slanted toward the crack. A man in a stone-colored shirt kept circling it without getting close enough to matter. A child was crying somewhere inside in the flat repetitive way children cried when the part of them that expected adults to fix things had started to fail.

Vel set down her pack. Kem did the same. Dren took three steps after them, looked at his arm, swore, and changed direction toward the crowd instead.

"I'll keep them back," he said.

The stone-shirted man was saying, to anyone and no one, "East wall shifted. My sister's in there. Two kids. Floor's gone soft."

No discussion. Vel went to the west side and laid her palm on the stone. The stillness of her body changed in a way Kem knew too well, attention narrowing until she seemed built around it. He could not feel the spend leave her. What he saw was the intake of breath afterward, small and annoyed, like a tax she had expected and still resented.

Dren called from behind the crowd, "How much?"

"One," Vel said without looking up.

There it was. Seventeen left, then. Kem hated that part of himself was relieved to hear her say it out loud.

"Northwest corner's taking weight," she said. The old operational register had survived the fracture intact. "Southeast is gone. Beam runs north to south and it's cracked midway. Reinforce that. Clear the east door. Woman inside, maybe two children."

The familiarity of it hit before the resentment could. "How much beam?"

"Two meters. Both sides."

The brother started for the door. Dren caught him by the back of the shirt with his good hand and hauled him short.

"No," Dren said.

"That's my sister."

"And that's a house trying to eat anybody sentimental enough to go first. Wait."

The man strained once against him, then sagged. Dren let go only enough to keep him from falling.

Stone grated under Kem's boots as he crossed to the east wall. The doorway was choked with a dropped lintel and loose blocks. Through a slit between them he could make out dark interior, suspended powder, a shape low to the floor.

"We're coming in," he said.

At two hundred, Reinforcement had been easy enough to mistake for entitlement. At thirty-one it fought him. He pressed both hands to the wall, found the cracked timber by feel and vibration, and spent.

Two levels. The timber did not heal. It clenched. The house answered with a lower groan, still failing, but slower. Twenty-nine.

"Holding," he said.

"Door," Vel said.

Lifting the fallen stone cost two more. The blocks came up badly, scraping and bumping and catching on one another like they resented being moved. A burst of lime and grit came through the opening and coated his tongue. Twenty-seven.

Behind him somebody in the crowd said, not quietly enough, "You hear that? They don't even have to look at each other."

Another voice answered, "Maybe they did too much of that already."

Kem would have laughed if he had been less busy.

He ducked inside. One room, half divided by a storage partition that had already collapsed on one side. Breath rasped somewhere beyond it. The air was thick with old plaster and fresh stone powder, enough to chew.

"Light," Vel called from outside.

She had moved to the northwest corner and was bracing the wall with both hands, human weight added to whatever calculation she had made. Her jaw was locked. A tremor ran through her forearms that had not been there at the well.

He spent a level for light. Weak white glow pooled across the room. A year ago he could have lit a hall. The smallness of this hurt more than the cost. Twenty-six.

The woman was against the far wall with a child on each side. Pale tracks ran through the grime on her face. "Can you walk?"

"Floor dropped," she said. "I can't get them over it."

A gap split the boards between her and the door. One joist hung broken and twisted into the dark below.

Down on his knees, Kem put one hand on the intact board and one on the broken joist. Another groan rolled through the house. Powder sifted from above onto his shoulders and hair.

"Ceiling's moving," Vel said. "Minutes."

One more level. The joist locked hard enough to take weight. The hanging boards settled. Twenty-five.

"Come now," he said. "Take the small one first. Keep hold of the other."

The mother obeyed without thanking him. Good. There was no room in the building for gratitude. She lifted the younger child to her hip, caught the older girl's hand, and crossed with the careful, flat-footed pace of someone walking a river stone in spring runoff. The boards held. Kem kept his palm against them until all three were through. By the time he stumbled back into daylight, a section of the second-floor wall had smashed into the place they had just left.

Sun hit him full in the face. Across the yard Vel let go of the corner and stepped back breathing hard, grit in her hair, eyes on the house instead of on him. Six feet of air stood between them. A crowd rushed through it.

The brother reached his sister first. Somebody shoved a cup of water at her. Somebody else started hauling loose stone away from the base of the wall because the next problem had already arrived.

Then the younger child planted both hands on her mother's face and started checking. Cheeks. Mouth. Ears. Hair. Then back to cheeks again, pressing skin as if she could keep the woman in place by touch alone. The mother shut her eyes and let her do it.

Kem had just enough breath left to think sit down before the quarryman with the planks stepped into his path.

"Good," the man said. "Now come look at the west wall."

Kem stared at him.

"We just dragged three people out of your house."

"That was her house," the quarryman said, jerking his chin toward the sister. "The wall's mine, and if it goes the well follows. I said thank you in my head."

"He did not," the flour-armed woman called.

The quarryman ignored her. His gaze moved from Vel to Kem, took in the distance and the way neither crossed it. "You traveling together?"

Silence did the answering.

"Right," the quarryman said. "Whatever you've got going on, keep having it for another hour. The wall doesn't care."

That should not have been funny. It was. Kem felt the laugh hit his ribs and fail there.

The west retaining wall ran above the well and below six houses built on optimism and reused stone. Grain sacks, crockery, two bedsteads, and half a cooper's shop had already been dragged back from the lip. Chickens worked calmly through the debris as if collapse were just another season.

Vel crouched at the lowest course and went still. When she rose, some of the color had gone out of her face.

"One more," she said. "Sixteen now."

There. Exact. Public. No guesswork.

The rescued brother looked lost enough to resent arithmetic. "Can you hold it?"

"Till dawn if you stop making it work so hard." Vel pointed along the wall. "Strip the top course by the third house. Move the grain farther west. Nobody on the upper path after sunset. If anything starts shedding, wake me before you wake your priest."

"We don't have a priest," the flour-armed woman said.

"Good," Vel said. "One less delay."

That should have ended their part in it. It didn't. Orders started moving and the settlement made room for them inside the work before any of them had agreed to stay. Kem got put on stone. Dren got rope. Vel got every question with weight in it. Refusing would have taken more energy than lifting.

Kem lasted three loads before his shoulders started to shake. The cut reopened under the fourth block. Dren worked the bad arm like he hated it into obedience. Across the yard, the younger child kept climbing onto the cot to check her mother's face every time the woman coughed, as if features might come loose between breaths.

Twenty-five rang thin behind Kem's eyes. Sweat dried cold under the grit on his back. Stone had found its way into one boot. Every bruise from the last hour started speaking at once.

For one breath every hurt place in him pulled the same direction: preservation. Sit still. Stay whole. Never crawl into another house that wanted down. Never learn the exact price of split skin, a lung, a level. The Conservatory rose in him in scraps: cool stone, untouched air, filtered quiet, his father's face emptied out into calm.

That calm had looked false to him there. Standing on a wall that could still take the well and the grain and half a night's sleep with it, it looked almost merciful.

Then the smaller child flattened both palms to her mother's cheeks again, counting what was still there by touch. Kem bent and picked up the next block.

By dusk the top course was down where Vel wanted it, the grain was stacked farther west, and the upper path had been cleared. The quarryman walked the line, nodded once, and declared that if anyone died overnight it would at least happen on ground that had been looked at properly.

"That's almost poetry from him," the flour-armed woman said.

"Shut up, Sena."

So she had a name. It did not make her any softer.

Sena came over with a strip of clean cloth and held it out to Kem. "Your cut."

"I have bandages."

"Bad ones, from the look of it."

He glanced toward Vel before he could stop himself. She had been talking to the brother in low clipped sentences, probably about where not to sleep. Her head turned a fraction, old reflex catching the movement. For half a second the corrective look came over her face, the one that wanted to clean the cut, wrap it properly, and tell him where he had been stupid. Then she let it die.

Sena saw that too. Of course she did. "All right," she said. "You're not married. You're just exhausting."

Kem took the cloth.

"Thank you."

"If you were married I wouldn't have thanked you either," she said, and went to shout at a boy who had climbed where Vel had forbidden climbing.

By the time the light finished going, the settlement had sorted the three of them into a category of its own: useful, inconvenient, and worth watching.

---

By full dark they were on the west rise above the crack because there was nowhere sensible else to sleep. The fire was small because the wood was poor and because nobody in the settlement believed in burning more than a pot required. Dren laid his bedroll between theirs without comment. Nobody argued with the geometry.

Sena brought up a pot of barley and greens thickened with something that might once have met meat. "Eat this before the wall changes its mind."

Dren took the bowl first. "You talk kindly."

"I save my manners for funerals."

She handed bowls to Vel and Kem without bothering to ask whether they wanted them. A moment later she came back with a heel of bread and looked from one bedroll to the next.

"If you're all leaving at dawn," she said, "take the west cut. East path's done until we pull two sheds down. If you're not leaving at dawn, tell me before first light so I don't waste water on travelers."

"We'll tell you," Vel said.

Sena's eyes shifted to Kem, waiting for a contradiction. None came.

"Fine." She started away, then stopped. "And if either of you plans to run north in the dark, do it quietly. The dogs bark at everything and we're tired."

Dren choked on his stew. Vel kept her face flat. Kem looked down into the bowl until the heat in his ears passed for campfire.

That, apparently, was the settlement's official opinion on their private business.

Dren sat against his pack after eating and cleaned his knife one-handed. When he flexed afterward, two fingers lagged before closing.

He worked them open and shut until they answered a little better.

"Still useful," he said. "Mostly."

Below the rise, the settlement moved in smaller noises now. Pot lids. A baby wailing once and being settled. Somebody hammering after dark because panic did not honor sleep. From farther east came the occasional small clatter of loosened stone.

A child drifted in from the lower fires and started investigating Kem's pack with the fearless entitlement of the half-feral. Eight, maybe. Bare feet tough enough for rubble. Thin wrists. She turned over his spare belt, his cloth roll, his waterskin, and finally found the signet where it had slipped loose near the bandages.

"What's this?"

Silver-dark metal flashed warm in her grip. In a settlement like this it looked less noble than ridiculous, a little polished piece of some other species' habits.

"House signet," Kem said.

"What's that mean?"

He opened his mouth on the old explanation and hated it before it formed. "It means," he said, starting again, "that I was raised somewhere doors opened before I touched them."

The girl looked at the ring, then at him, then at the settlement spread below them in patched roofs and unstable stone. "That's lazy."

"Yeah," he said. "It was."

She slid the ring onto her thumb, watched it sink to the knuckle, and held it up to the fire. "Silver, though."

From below, Sena's voice carried without the rest of her. "Only if you melt the house off it."

"Can I melt the house off it?" the girl asked.

"No," Kem said.

"Then why are you carrying it?"

That was the question. Not what it meant, not where it came from, not whether he had been stupid enough to believe in it. Why he still had it after all the chances to throw it into water, scrub, roadside pits, a collapsing settlement's bottomless crack.

He did not have a good answer, which was how he knew any true answer would sound bad.

"I left wearing it," he said at last. "Then I kept leaving."

The girl considered that with more seriousness than the sentence had earned. "That's not much of a reason."

"No."

"Can I keep it anyway?"

"Also no."

She made a face, dropped the ring into his lap, and reached instead for the buckle on his pack.

"No," Kem said again.

"You don't keep anything fun."

"There is nothing fun in that pack."

"Then why keep it?"

Dren laughed openly at that. Even Vel's mouth shifted, not into a smile exactly, but away from total refusal.

The girl caught the movement. "See? She thinks I'm right."

Vel said, "Eat something and go bother people who aren't armed."

"He's armed."

"Badly," Dren said.

The girl looked at Kem's knife, which he had set beside the bedroll, then at the bandaged cut and road-raw face above it. "Yeah," she said, and wandered off toward the larger fire when some more interesting argument broke out there about who had stolen whose spoon.

Warmth lingered in the silver-dark metal when he picked the ring up. Grit sat in the worn grooves of the crest. The thing had been made for polished rooms, careful storage, servants who knew where to place the tray. Here it looked rich and stupid and small enough to lose.

He put it away. Not deep in the pack, not back on his body. Between.

Sena came up one last time with a waterskin and a kind of administrative exhaustion that reminded Kem, unwillingly, of the Vault. Not in manners. In certainty.

"We've got room in the shed if the three of you decide you're sick of proving something with bedroll distance," she said.

"We're fine," Vel said.

Sena looked at the exact spacing of the three bedrolls. "Sure."

She handed Vel the skin. "If the east side starts shedding, I want warning before the dogs make it everyone's problem."

"You'll get it."

"And if you wake the wall with whatever this is" - she jerked her chin between Vel and Kem - "I'm charging you for the panic."

When she was gone Dren said, "I've been on roads with worse innkeepers."

"No, you haven't," Vel said.

"Fair."

The silence after that did not feel peaceful, just occupied.

Across the fire, Vel had Maren's route journal open to tomorrow's stretch. She had added to it since the wall: a small correction beside the next distance mark, a note where the east path had failed, a darker angle drawn toward the quarry cut. The journal kept absorbing disaster like it had room for infinite bad roads.

Dren squinted over the rim of his bowl. "What're you at now?"

Vel did not look up from the page. "Sixteen."

That answered more than the number. She had heard him earlier, or guessed the question, or simply decided the settlement did not get to be the only witness to her arithmetic.

Kem watched her follow tomorrow's route down the page. She paused once halfway through the distance column, where certainty seemed to thin out, then kept going.

North existed, not grandly and not as some pure escape, just as a road back out of all this. The quarryman had offered solid ground and maybe work in the morning. Twenty-five levels spent sparingly could buy Kem years if years were all he wanted. He could turn around, disappear into some Spent town, learn to mend roofs or unload carts, and keep himself alive a long time.

But the joist under his wrist came back first, then Dren hauling grain one-handed because he had already attached a future to a girl waiting north, then Vel saying one and then one more and then sixteen because he had stopped letting numbers stay implied, then the child on the cot below checking her mother's face every time the woman coughed.

He got up and crossed to the fire.

"What changes after the quarry cut?"

Vel looked at him for the first time since the wall. She understood the question and also that it was not the one he had almost asked.

"Bad footing till noon," she said. "Better after the ridge. Less cover. More distance between water."

"How much more?"

"Enough that we fill everything here."

"And if the ridge is gone too?"

"Then we cut west and lose half a day."

He nodded once. "What are the other surprises?"

Her face shut for a second on the word. Then reopened, not to the truth he might have wanted, but to the smaller thing he had actually asked. "Loose shale in the cut. Old blasting pits. One washed bridge footing that isn't a bridge anymore. That's what I know."

It was not apology. It was not confession. It was usable.

He pointed at the journal. "How far before that stops being route and starts being guesswork?"

After a beat Vel touched the page. "Two, maybe three days south. Depending on the ground."

He looked at the mark, then at her. "Then you tell me when it does."

Her face shut for a second. "All right."

"And the sixteens and seventeens."

"What about them?"

"I don't want to guess anymore." He heard how flat he sounded and kept going anyway. "If it changes and it matters to me, you say it."

Dren stopped pretending not to listen.

Vel kept the place in the journal. Firelight tightened the tendons at her wrist. "All right," she said.

It was so plain it almost missed him.

"And the road," he said. "If we're moving tomorrow, I want the road before we're on it."

"All right."

Nothing repaired in the saying. The damage was still there, whole and waiting. But the next day's terms had shape now. Not trust. Terms.

He reached toward the journal without having planned to. Vel looked at him. For a moment he thought she would refuse, not out of secrecy but out of habit so old it would take more than one night to break. Instead she rose, came around the fire, and set the book down on the ground between their bedrolls.

"Don't lose it," she said.

"I wasn't planning to."

"People plan badly."

It was the longest thing she had said to him since the house. It was also, in its bent and meager form, almost ordinary.

She went back to her side of the fire and lay down with her boots still on.

Dren watched the journal where it lay between them. "That's cozy," he said.

"Go to sleep," Vel said.

"Trying."

Below them, a dog barked once and was answered by another. Somebody coughed behind one of the west-side houses. The wall held. The crack kept its own counsel in the dark.

Kem sat with the journal a long minute before he opened it. The mileage held clean for two, maybe three days. After that the margins thickened with contingencies, quarry cuts, washed crossings, routes that had once been lines and were now negotiations.

He closed the book and kept it beside his pack.

The pack stayed buckled because unbuckling it would have meant claiming something either way. Staying. Leaving. Morning. He was not ready to call any of those done.

But he left the journal where he could reach it, and when the east side gave one small settling clatter in the dark, all three of them woke at once.
