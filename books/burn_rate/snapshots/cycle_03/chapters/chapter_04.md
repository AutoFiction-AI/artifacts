# Chapter 4

Vel woke him before dawn by shoving the heel of her hand into his shoulder and saying, "Don't sit up fast."

Kem sat up fast anyway. Cold air hit the sweat dried into his shirt. For one confused second he thought they were under attack already. Then he followed her gaze to the dead fire.

At the edge of the ash bed, where they'd buried the coals under dirt before sleeping, a boot print pressed clean through the gray crust into damp black underneath. It belonged to neither of them.

Kem crawled closer on stiff elbows. The tread was narrow through the heel and dense at the ball, made for grip on wet ground, not the soft corridor leather he'd worn out of the Vault and not the secondhand road boots rubbing his heels raw now. He knew that pattern anyway. Hoarded enforcement kept their field boots on racks outside the retrieval offices with little waxed tags tied to the laces, each pair catalogued, maintained, and almost never dirtied.

"How long ago?" he asked.

"Night." Vel was already pulling the cook pot into her pack. "No dew settled in it."

"So they stood over us while we slept."

"Or one of them did." She kicked loose dirt over the print, not because hiding it mattered now but because she did not leave useful things lying around. "Move."

Kem got to his feet too quickly. Yesterday's climb had gone into his calves and stayed there overnight. The muscles answered his urgency with a bright, contemptuous cramp. He bent, breathed through it, and reached for the pack.

Vel caught his wrist before his hand closed on the strap. "Check the perimeter first."

"For what?"

"For what else they learned."

There were three more traces once she made him look properly. A branch bent toward the bedroll depression where he'd slept. Mud marked a stone where someone had stepped from the game trail into their camp margin and back out again. A second print, partial this time, sat near the place where he'd pissed after dark. All of it had clean edges and deliberate placement, the kind of neatness the Hoarded loved so much they mistook it for virtue.

"They didn't take anything," Kem said.

"They didn't come to steal."

She made him scatter the bedroll dents with a broken bough, kick soil over the piss patch, and carry the ashes downhill to dump them in running water. It felt absurdly domestic given the circumstances, like tidying a room while men with writs waited outside the door. Vel did not care how it felt. By the time a gray seam opened in the eastern sky, camp had become a patch of cold ground and flattened leaves, and they were climbing.

The ridge trail rose south through old highland forest, too narrow for carts, wide enough for deer and bad decisions. Vel had chosen it yesterday because the stone shelves under the mud kept a trail from printing deep. This morning it meant exposure. Rock spared them from leaving much sign, but it also kept them on one line with the people following.

Kem's new boots were honest boots, which was already an improvement over anything he'd ever owned. They were trader-bought road leather with thick soles and ugly stitching, overcharged because the man at the roadside stall had looked at Kem's clothes, the signet on his hand, and the educated shape of his uselessness, then named a number that included contempt. The boots could survive ground like this. Kem was less certain about the feet inside them.

Vel moved twenty paces ahead, head tilted sometimes toward the backtrail, sometimes toward the trees downslope. She had spoken maybe twelve words in the last hour. Every few minutes she changed something small about their route, stepping from trail to rock, from rock to root flare, from root flare to the dry center of a fallen trunk, and Kem followed with the concentration of a man attempting a ritual in a language he did not understand.

When he slipped, she did not turn around. She only said, "If you have to catch yourself, catch yourself quiet."

"I'm trying."

"Try lower."

He did not know what that meant until the next slip, when he went down on one knee, felt the instinct to curse rise up, and swallowed it before it left his mouth. The silence afterward had a weight to it. The forest did not forgive noise just because it was human.

The trail kept climbing. Sweat started under his pack straps and found every rubbed patch of skin from yesterday. He did not check his Ledger. He knew the number still: 286. It lived at the edge of awareness like a second pulse, present without asking. Looking at it would not help his footing.

Vel stopped so abruptly he nearly walked into her.

She crouched beside a patch of mud where runoff crossed the trail in a shallow slick. Four prints sat in it clearly. Two were theirs. Two were not theirs. One of the new tracks had cut down hard through the heel, driving a V into the mud where the wearer had planted to turn. Another sat lightly, almost polite, but too narrow and too deliberate for any local trader hauling salt or cloth.

Vel touched one print with two fingers. "They're off the road now."

Kem crouched beside her. "You can tell that from this?"

"I can tell from this that somebody came up behind us, saw us leave the trail for rock twice, and adjusted." She pointed at a broken fern frond downslope. "I can tell from that that at least one of them is staying left to keep our line if we cut toward the ravine. And I can tell from the birds being wrong that they're close."

Kem listened. Wind moved through the high branches. Insects worked somewhere under the leaf rot. The ordinary chatter of birds had thinned to a few isolated calls that seemed less like conversation than warning after warning failing to spread.

"How close?"

"Close enough that the forest has already noticed before you did."

She stood. Kem did too, slower this time.

"Could still be local enforcement," he said, because the alternative was worse and because saying things aloud sometimes made them less true.

"Local enforcement doesn't spend on trackers for one man unless he's carrying tax silver."

"How do you know they spent?"

Vel looked back at the mud, then at the trees, and then at him. "Because we broke line on bare rock three times this morning and they're still holding our bearing. Somebody already burned a level on Perception, or something close enough that it doesn't matter what name you put on it."

The thought landed all at once. Somewhere behind them, in a room with lacquered tables and precise handwriting and men who believed the world could be solved by filing it properly, someone had authorized the first spend. Not for defense, not in emergency, and not because the Scour had opened or a village had gone under. They had authorized it for him.

"My mother," Kem said before he meant to.

Vel did not answer that. She only scanned uphill, down, and across the fold of the land ahead of them.

"We don't outrun a tracker in timber this thick," she said. "Not with you leaving half your education on every branch."

"I could use Concealment."

"You could waste ten levels making yourself dim around the edges." She pointed downslope. "Move."

The way down was not a path so much as a decision to stop respecting vertical arrangements. Vel cut through brush, across a slanted shelf of stone, and into a drainage gully slick with last week's rain and this morning's leaf slime. Kem lasted six steps before his right foot shot out from under him and dumped him hard on his back. He slid three meters, banged his elbow against buried rock, and opened the half-healed cut in his palm all over again grabbing for something to stop on.

Vel looked down at him. "Quiet."

Kem bit the inside of his cheek, tasted blood, and nodded.

She offered no hand. He got up with mud on his spine and pine needles inside his shirt. They crossed the gully, climbed the far side through young saplings that slapped wet leaves across his face, and came to a narrow basin where a deadfall lay rotting between two standing trees. Moss had claimed the upper curve of the trunk. The earth behind it dipped just enough to hide bodies without stealing the uphill view.

Vel took one look and chose it.

"Pack off," she said. "Stay low. If I say move, you move before the sentence finishes."

Kem slid the pack from his shoulders and crouched behind the log. It smelled of wet wood, fungus, and the sweet rot of things returning to dirt. Vel settled to his left, one knee down, one foot planted, gaze fixed uphill through a screen of fern and young ash.

"How many?" he asked.

"More than one."

"That's not a number."

"It's enough of one."

She closed one eye, sighting along gaps in the brush. Kem forced himself to do the same. At first he saw only forest: trunks rising, roots twisting, filtered morning light moving in narrow bands. Then the forest reorganized. He caught a shoulder where there should have been bark, gray cloth between saplings, the wrong geometry of straight lines in a place built entirely from curves.

Voices followed, low but not low enough.

"...not saying I object," one of them muttered. "I'm saying the paperwork says minimum visible injury and no facial damage if feasible, and I'd like somebody to define feasible before he starts throwing house-level tantrums."

Another voice answered, crisp and young. "The directive says intact and ambulatory. It doesn't say comfortable."

"That isn't better."

The third voice came from farther back and flatter than the others. "Trail holds two. One favors the left side now. Heavy one slipped in the drainage."

Kem felt heat climb his neck. Vel's mouth did not move, but one finger tapped once against the dead wood between them, telling him to stay still.

The flat voice spoke again. "They're ahead. Thirty meters at most."

Leaves shifted. Gray coats moved through the trees.

There were seven bodies in all. Six wore Hoarded field livery in the gray of minor-house enforcement, coat hems muddy now, insignia stitched small at collar and cuff. The seventh stayed ten paces back in plain field cloth the color of old bark, no crest visible, eyes on the ground more than the people around him. He was the tracker, lean enough to disappear until he looked straight at you.

The lead agent, a dark-haired woman around Kem's age, lifted one hand when she saw them, palm out in the formal sign of Hoarded authority. Kem had grown up with that gesture opening corridors, ending arguments, emptying rooms.

"Kem Aldraste," she said. "By directive of the Hoarded Council, under recovery authority granted to the enforcement branch, you are ordered to submit to escort and return to the Vault in substantially-"

"Move," Vel said.

Kem moved.

***

Vel went right and downhill, low enough that for a second Kem thought she had dropped rather than run. Then his eyes caught up. She was already across half the distance, one hand brushing trunks for balance, the other free.

The nearest agent barely got his stance set. Vel reached him before his weight did. Two fingers touched the soft place below his ear. Nothing dramatic happened. There was no flash and no blast. The man's knees unlocked as if some internal pin had been pulled, and he went down into the ferns with a confused grunt and no further usefulness to anybody.

One level had bought one agent.

The woman beside him reacted cleanly and went to shield first, which was good training. A translucent disc snapped into being between her and Vel, chest-high, textbook size, exactly the sort of thing Kem had watched in demonstration courts with polite admiration and no understanding. Vel did not bother testing it. She stepped outside the line, came in from the shield side the woman had left open because training exercises liked symmetry more than survival, and laid two fingers against the side of her neck.

The shield vanished before it hit the ground because it had never been on the ground at all. The woman followed it down.

Two levels had taken two agents.

The lead agent fired her Combat Pulse a fraction later than she should have, because for a fraction too long she had been watching her people disappear instead of acting. The pulse crossed empty air. Vel had read the movement in the woman's shoulders before the light gathered. Two short steps left, pivot through, heel of the hand to the sternum with almost insulting efficiency. The agent folded at the waist, then at the knees, then in a loose collapse all the way to the soil, eyes wide with the offended shock of a body no longer obeying orders.

Three levels had taken three agents, and Kem had not finished the breath he took when the formal hand went up.

The surviving line faltered. That mattered more than the bodies.

One gray-coated man broke first. He turned and shoved back through the brush so hard branches snapped after him. Kem did not blame him. Another agent half turned as if to follow, saw the tracker still in position, saw the lead woman on the ground, and stayed from pure bureaucratic momentum. If the order had not been cancelled, then perhaps reality had not changed enough to justify leaving.

It had changed plenty.

The tracker moved wide left, staying outside Vel's immediate reach, attention cutting between Kem, the trees, and the spaces a retreat might take. He was not here to win a fight. He was here to keep the line attached to its target until someone else won it for him.

The remaining gray-coated man looked at Kem and made the decision that training, rank, and paperwork all encouraged. He went for the politically valuable body.

Energy gathered around his hand.

Kem was still behind the deadfall with his pack half under one knee, blood on his reopened palm, and the absurd thought that perhaps he was about to be arrested by people splashed with mud in a forest basin that smelled like mushrooms. Then the man's Combat Pulse launched, and absurdity made room for terror.

The pulse came fast enough that he did not think a full sentence before his body answered it. Power rose through him the way vomiting rose, involuntary and total. It came up from the Ledger's hidden reservoir, slammed through his gut, flooded his chest, and broke out through both arms before intention could do more than shout after it.

He had meant to do what Vel had done. He had meant to keep it narrow. He had meant, stupidly, to be good at something by instinct.

What left him was a wall.

His own pulse swallowed the incoming one without effort Kem could feel. The gray-coated man disappeared inside the expanding force, then reappeared six meters back as a body no longer in command of its own direction. He hit a tree trunk hard enough to crack something that made a wet second sound after the wood struck. The woman beside him caught the lateral edge of the blast and cartwheeled into the brush.

The wave did not stop there. It hit the hillside at an angle above the basin, and the earth let go.

Soil sheared loose with a sound deeper than thunder and less honest. Roots tore. Stones broke free. A whole skin of slope started moving at once, sliding downhill in a broad, filthy sheet. One of the agents Vel had dropped rolled toward the new edge, and Vel lunged, caught the back of his coat, and hauled him behind the deadfall an instant before mud and shattered rock crossed the place where his head had been.

Kem stared because staring was easier than helping.

Trees tilted. One came over in a burst of leaves and splinters, smashed across the trail they'd climbed that morning, and split open down the middle. Dust punched into the air. Stone hit stone and kept hitting. The ground shook through the soles of Kem's trader-bought road boots until he felt the vibration in his teeth.

Then the slide spent itself against the fold of the basin, and the forest went quiet in the ugly, suspended way a room went quiet after somebody screamed.

Kem looked down at his hands.

They were shaking. Fear was part of that. Something worse was the rest.

Projection Shield in the Vault had felt like incompetence wearing light. The Sustenance Charm had felt like embarrassment made edible. This was neither. This felt like a sealed chamber in him had burst open and exposed moving machinery underneath. His arms buzzed. His chest was tight. Every finger ached with the appetite to do it again, but better this time, and maybe bigger.

His Ledger had changed. He felt the loss before he checked it, the way a tongue found a new gap between teeth.

Two hundred and seventy-one.

Fifteen levels for one Combat Pulse. He had reached for one and spent fifteen as easily as a panicked man emptied a bucket.

"Kem," Vel said.

He looked up.

She was on her feet again, mud to the knee from yanking the downed agent clear. The tracker had gotten behind a boulder near the far side of the basin. The woman Kem had thrown into the brush was pushing herself up on one arm with blood in her hair and the other arm hanging wrong from the shoulder down. The lead agent was still out. One man was running somewhere unseen. Two bodies lay where Vel had put them. The fight was not over. It had simply been interrupted by geology.

"If you're about to get clever," Vel said, "don't."

The injured woman dragged herself to one knee. Light gathered in her good hand, weak but real. The tracker leaned out from behind the boulder with both palms up, shaping something slower, narrower, and probably much more sensible than anything Kem was about to do.

Kem reached for Speed before he had even decided to disobey her.

The world thinned and lengthened. Leaves slowed in the air. The woman ahead of him turned her head through syrup. The tracker's hands were still coming together. Kem meant to cross the distance and stop them before either technique finished.

He crossed it.

He did not stop.

The tree appeared in front of him with the awful dignity of something that had always been exactly where it was and had no intention of apologizing. Kem hit it chest-first hard enough to drive all air out of him at once. Bark exploded against his face. Pain lit his left side white. A slab of bark broke loose above him and bounced off his skull.

Momentum refused to end just because impact had happened. It threw him sideways, spun him into the ground, and sent him sliding through loam and crushed fern until a boulder took over the work of stopping him. He came to rest with his cheek in wet dirt, mouth full of iron and rot, and a bell tolling somewhere behind his eyes.

Eight levels, he thought dimly. Speed had cost eight levels and delivered him directly into a tree.

For a few seconds he could not inhale enough to know whether the damage was serious. Then breath came back in sharp pieces and brought the rest of him with it. His left ribs clicked in a way ribs should not. His shoulder burned. Dirt stuck to his tongue where he had bitten it.

Above him, Vel shouted something that might have been his name or might have been an insult.

Kem rolled onto one elbow and pushed up.

The basin tilted once and settled. His Ledger hummed at 263 now. The number still did not feel low. It felt stripped of theory. Those twenty-three vanished levels were no longer abstract capacity. They were slope, impact, broken wood, pain.

The tracker was adjusting course toward him with irritating intelligence, using trees and rock, never offering the straight line Kem wanted. The injured woman had made it halfway upright and was gathering another weak pulse one-handed. The lead agent was still down. Vel was closing on the tracker from the other side, but the basin was too torn apart now for her to reach either of them before one of them fired.

Kem's mind grabbed for the next familiar tool: a shield, small this time, personal, human-sized, a thing meant to protect a body instead of redesigning a landscape.

He threw the shape out in front of him.

The shape did not listen.

Light burst from the ground at his feet and raced outward in a clear dome that kept widening after any reasonable person would have called it sufficient. It hit five meters, then ten, then fifteen. Brush flattened under the pressure wave. The tracker lost his footing and went down hard against a root mass. The injured woman was swept backward into fallen limbs with a cry that cut off when the branches caught her. Vel cursed and flung herself behind a boulder as the edge of the dome stripped bark from saplings and drove a dead branch across her forearm hard enough to score skin.

Air shoved out from Kem in all directions. Leaves tore free overhead. The dome shone for three impossible seconds, enclosing half the basin in clean borrowed geometry, and then collapsed because Kem had no idea how to feed it without feeding it everything.

The forest after it looked handled by a god with poor judgment.

Ten levels had produced a shield large enough for a village square.

Kem's breath came ragged. The cracked rib on his left side had stopped discussing the matter and moved on to direct complaint. Tingling ran through both hands again, fiercer now. It did not feel like strength. It felt like the body's appetite learning the shape of permission.

His Ledger read 253.

The tracker was face-down in a tangle of fern and root, but he was moving. Kem saw one hand grope toward the cord at the man's belt. It was not a weapon. It was a whistle, iron or bone, something made to carry through trees. The injured woman, pinned under branches now, had enough use of her good arm left to raise it one more time. Weak light shook between her fingers.

Vel was still twenty paces out.

Kem did not try for a named technique. He shoved at the nearest thing inside him and gave it direction.

Force and light tore from his right hand, rough and unshaped. He aimed for the space between the woman and the tracker. He missed by three meters, which was still close enough to ruin everything there.

Earth blew upward. Stone, dirt, and roots punched into daylight. The blast ripped the whistle cord free before the tracker could get it to his mouth and flung both him and the woman in opposite directions. The woman landed in a folded heap beyond the branches and stopped moving. The tracker struck the ground shoulder-first, rolled twice, and ended flat with both hands spread in front of him as if surrender and self-preservation had become the same posture.

Silence came back in scraps.

Eight more levels were gone. Kem's Ledger read 245.

Forty-one levels were gone, and the fight had lasted less time than breakfast ever had in the Vault.

The basin looked as if weather had been weaponized. Half the hillside above it was now a raw brown scar bleeding loose soil. A crater smoked damply where his last blast had struck. Trees leaned or lay broken. Dust moved through the slanting light and turned it gold.

Vel stepped out from behind the boulder.

A narrow cut marked her forearm where the shield had driven the branch across it. Mud darkened one knee. She looked first at the agents, then at the slide, then at the crater, and only then at Kem.

Her face went still in the way faces did when anger had moved past heat into counting.

Kem was grinning.

***

He knew he should not have been.

His tongue was bleeding. Dirt had pasted itself into his hair. The reopened cut in his palm stung with every pulse. Something in his left side had cracked enough that breathing deep felt like being introduced to a knife. Forty-one levels had vanished in four minutes. Shame was already on its way, sensible and deserved.

The grin kept happening anyway.

His body had never done this before, not well, not badly, not at all. He had spent twenty-six years preserving a soft expensive arrangement of skin and muscle for sitting, standing, bathing, and walking from chamber to chamber on polished floors. In the last five minutes that preserved body had hit a tree hard enough to see white, bled into the dirt, thrown force through both hands, and stayed standing. Pain was everywhere. So was a filthy, rising joy that made pain feel less like warning than proof.

Vel moved before he did. She went straight to the tracker, kicked the whistle clear of his hand, checked his throat for a pulse, then crossed to the woman in the branches and shoved deadwood off her until her chest could rise properly. She worked without ceremony and without softness. The point was not mercy. The point was not leaving problems half solved.

"Pack on," she said. "Now."

Kem bent for it and nearly dropped again when the rib caught.

Vel looked over once, took in the way he had frozen around the pain, and changed plans without discussing the fact. She yanked open an enforcement field pouch, found a roll of binding cloth, and came back.

"Arms up."

"We're moving now, you said."

"We are. Arms up anyway."

He obeyed because the alternative was arguing while half folded. Vel wrapped the cloth hard around his ribs, twice, then a third time for spite or stability. He hissed when the compression locked the cracked place down.

"There," she said. "Now it'll hurt different."

"That's not a recommendation."

"I didn't sell it as one."

She shoved his pack into his hands and went back to the bodies. Kem got the straps over his shoulders one careful side at a time. The binding did help, which annoyed him because it validated her tone.

Vel stripped the field pouch of anything useful in seconds: more bandage cloth, a water flask, a packet of dried meat, two small waxed papers, and a leather document case from the lead agent's belt. She also took a second whistle from another coat and tossed both into the crater one after the other, where mud swallowed them with a sound too small for how much worse they could have made the day.

"If one of those had gone off before you blasted the ground open," she said, "we'd have had company before noon."

"More of them?"

"Not from the Vault. Nearest relay post, most likely. Minor house, whoever got saddled with retrieval duty in this stretch. Doesn't matter. We aren't on the ridge anymore."

That was new enough to cut through the afterglow. Kem looked past her at the ruined slope, the broken trail, the basin now marked so loudly a blind man could probably find it.

"You were going to keep us on the ridge?"

"It was the fastest line south." She opened the document case with her thumb against the clasp. "Now it's an advertisement."

The papers inside were thick and cream-colored despite the mud on the outside, sealed in waxed sleeves against weather. Hoarded script marched down the first page in the precise, bloodless hand bureaucracy favored when it wanted cruelty to look objective.

Vel scanned fast. Kem stepped closer despite himself.

The first line held his name. The second held his house. The third, smaller and somehow worse, held the condition of return.

SUBJECT TO BE RECOVERED AMBULATORY, RECOGNIZABLE, AND FIT FOR ADMINISTRATIVE PRESENTATION.

Kem laughed once before he could stop himself. It came out thin and disgusted.

"Fit for presentation," he said.

"You grew up with these people."

"I know. It still sounds insane when they write it down."

Vel turned the page.

"Thirty levels authorized for the team," she said. "Separate allotment for specialist tracking already approved and partially used. Contingency clause if contact goes bad."

"What kind of contingency?"

She angled the page so he could read the relevant paragraph himself.

IN EVENT OF ACTIVE RESISTANCE, RETRIEVAL LEAD MAY REQUEST SUPPLEMENTARY PERSONNEL AND FIELD ALLOTMENT FROM NEAREST COUNCIL-ALIGNED RELAY WITHOUT RETURN TO PRIMARY AUTHORIZING BODY.

The paragraph held no emotion and no alarm. It was just a line item for escalation.

"So if they don't report back," Kem said, "someone else comes south."

"Or if the runner gets to somebody who can read." Vel folded the pages once and slid them into her own pack. "Either way, this isn't one embarrassed family trying to fetch you before gossip spreads. It's institutional now."

Vel was already moving again, checking the basin's downhill edge, the broken trail, the quickest line to water.

"We drop into the creek bed east of here," she said. "We stay in it until the stone gives way or dark does. No ridge. No open ground if I can help it. If I tell you to step where I stepped, do it exactly. If I tell you to freeze, you stop breathing ambitious thoughts."

Kem followed her to the edge of the basin. "And if they catch us before that?"

She turned back. Her expression had changed, still flat but aimed at him now instead of the pursuers.

"Feet first," she said. "Hands. Whatever doesn't cost you levels." She checked the downhill treeline. "You don't reach unless I'm down or someone's about to put a hole in you and running won't fix it."

"I didn't know how big it was going to—"

"I know." She was already moving, scanning the slope below them. "You aimed for one and spent fifteen. That's not something you fix by trying harder next time."

He looked past her at the scar where the hillside had let go.

"I was trying to do what you did."

Vel pulled the strap tighter across her shoulder and started down. "I've spent forty years learning what one level does." She didn't look back. "You've been at it half a day and your hands are still shaking. Keep them off the Ledger."

It was true. His hands were shaking.

Kem could still feel the appetite in them, a live metallic itch under the skin. He could feel it in the way the sight of the ruined basin did not make him want to look away. Part of him was horrified. Part of him wanted one more chance at getting it right.

"I don't know how to turn that off yet," he said. That sentence came out more honestly than most things he had said since leaving the Vault.

Vel didn't stop walking. "Then you don't use it."

Kem followed her through the torn undergrowth toward the east side of the basin with the care of a man carrying his own structural weakness inside a wrapped shirt. Every step found a new objection in his body: left rib, left shoulder, cut palm, bruised knee, tongue, the fresh skin the pack straps had worn raw across his back. The trader boots were full of grit now, and the right heel had started rubbing again in the place yesterday had opened.

At the basin floor, Vel paused just long enough to kick loose dirt over the deepest edge of the crater, not to hide it, which was impossible, but to collapse the sharp outline into a messier wound. Then she led him into a stand of alder so dense their packs scraped bark on both sides. Fifty meters farther on they found the creek: narrow, cold, fast over stone.

Vel stepped into the current without hesitation. Water climbed over her boots and flashed silver around her calves.

"In," she said.

Kem obeyed. The creek was snowmelt-cold. Pain shot through his rubbed heels and up his shins. The shock of it made the whole rest of him feel briefly cleaner and worse at the same time.

They turned south inside the watercourse, walking from stone to stone where possible, through the current where not. The sound of it covered what noise their bodies still insisted on making. Above the creek banks the forest carried on in its damaged silence.

After a while Kem realized he was smiling again.

The grin did not come because the day had improved, because the Council wanted him back in presentable condition, or because forty-one levels gone in one morning was survivable math. It came from someplace lower and less articulate. His blood was up. His body hurt everywhere and felt awake in every hurt place. He had broken things. He had been broken against things. The world had hit him back and discovered he was there.

He should have hated that. A decent version of himself probably would have.

Instead he kept seeing the moment before the first pulse left him, the impossible rush after, the way the forest had changed shape around his mistakes. Shame moved beside the memory, heavy as wet wool. So did a mean, bright delight that refused to apologize.

Vel glanced back once, saw his face, and looked away again as if she had confirmed a fear.

The creek bent south. Mud took the place of stone for a stretch, sucking at his boots. Branches knitted overhead and cut the light down to green shade. Behind them, somewhere above the banks, lay the wrecked basin, the broken trail, the six agents, the tracker, the papers, the proof. Ahead of them lay more trees, less certainty, and whatever kind of education Vel intended to extract from him before the next team arrived.

Kem adjusted the pack higher against his shoulders. The binding around his ribs pulled tight. The Ledger rubbed at him from the inside with every step. It sat at 245.

Vel's three levels had dropped people clean. His forty-one levels had torn a hillside loose. The comparison was absurd, humiliating, and completely correct.

The grin came back anyway.

He kept walking south.
