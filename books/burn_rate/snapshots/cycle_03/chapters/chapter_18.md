# Chapter 18

The shelf bought them one breath.

Kem hit it first, both hands out, boots skidding on the polished stone. Vel came in after him hard enough to jar the old crack in her left ribs. The rock covered them from the waist down and almost nowhere that mattered above it.

Across the basin, Commander Osen did not raise her voice.

"You can't make it work," she called. "It's not enough anymore."

Vel kept her head low and looked anyway. The floor of the Scour spread west in red-gray shelves and wind-cut troughs, eight hundred meters of bad stone and no mercy in the layout. The threshold point was still a pale, unmarked patch near Osen's boots. Two descent agents were already fanning wide from the switchback. The circuiting specialist was cutting south to turn the shelf into a pocket. Farther east, two basin garrison fighters were moving for angles. Nobody was rushing. That was the part that made it hard.

Osen knew exactly how much time sat in eight and twelve.

The first Combat Pulse hit the top lip of the shelf and took a slab of sandstone off with it. Chips sprayed down the back of Vel's neck. Kem folded tighter against the rock on instinct.

"Was that supposed to be a warning?" he asked.

"No."

Another Pulse shaved the left side. Osen was not trying to dig them out by force. She was cutting the cover into smaller pieces and letting the basin do the rest.

Vel tracked the angles in her head and did the arithmetic she had been doing since the tunnel mouth. Twenty levels between them had looked workable in the dark, in the journal, in every stupid year she had built herself around the old equation. It had to land almost all at once and almost on top of the point. That had always been the rule. Now the threshold was damped, starved, made reluctant by deliberate hands. Eight and twelve would still make a crossing if they arrived clean and close enough. Spread across eight hundred meters, it became expensive dying.

Kem wiped one hand on his shirt and left a red streak there from barked skin. "Tell me what we're doing."

"Not spending."

He looked out toward the point as if maybe there was a different basin available on the other side of this one. "That does sound like us."

"You hear me say spend, you spend. If I don't, you use your body."

"And if my body objects."

"It can object later."

He nodded. There was a little too much breath in him already, a little too much dust on his tongue, but he nodded.

The descent agents split farther apart. One went high along the north-side shelves, low and quick. The other settled into a crouch at the foot of the switchback where he could catch a westward run and still cover a retreat. Osen stayed by the threshold point with the patience of a person who had built a whole posting out of saying no.

"She needs us burning levels out here," Vel said. "That's all this is."

"I hate how simple that sounds."

"Move on three. Not west. North trough first."

He looked where she meant and saw it a moment later: a shallow erosion channel twelve meters off, hardly deep enough to hide a leg, but it bent out of sight behind a harder rib of stone. It was not safe. It was simply next.

Osen spoke again, still calm enough to make the words travel clean. "Walk back east and you keep what you've got."

Vel said, "One."

The east garrison fighter shifted his feet to cut the trough.

"Two."

Kem bent his knees.

On three they went.

Kem pushed off a half breath too early because the next Pulse had already started to build light in the corner of his eye. He came up high instead of low. The shot cracked the shelf where his head had been, and he had to throw himself sideways to keep the rest of himself attached. The move was ugly and a breath late, but it kept him alive. Six weeks earlier he would have taken it clean in the face and called the result surprising.

Vel hit the floor running. Stone heat drove up through her soles. Another shot tore a strip of ground open behind her heel. Kem landed out of that throw with no grace at all, stumbled, and smacked his chin on the basin floor hard enough that she heard tooth on stone over the alarm and the gun-crack echo.

"Still there?" she said.

"I hate this place."

That meant he was alive.

The north trough was narrower than it had looked from cover. Vel dropped into it and found herself running bent over in a cut in the stone no deeper than a cooking trench. Kem came after her with blood already sliding down the front of his chin. The descent agent on the north shelf changed angle and forced them lower. Osen had the basin mapped in bodies now: one to close north, one to close west, the specialist still running south to threaten any southward break, garrison fire from east, herself at the point. There were no gaps, only places where a body might fit if it did not mind damage.

Vel chose damage.

The trough ended in a sloped spill of gravel. She took it fast anyway, boots skidding. Her left foot hit loose stone, rolled, and drove a bright hot wire up through her ankle.

She did not fall. She almost did. The ankle went soft and mean under her for two steps and then came back enough to be used badly.

Kem caught up beside her at the rib of harder stone and hit it shoulder-first.

"You all right?" he asked, looking at her ankle because he had gotten annoyingly better at reading bodies.

"No."

"Good. I was worried it was only me."

The line of gravel high on the north scree started up then, a narrow hiss that came down in a steady ribbon. Vel looked without lifting more than one eye above the rock. Wind did not make a ribbon like that. Osen looked too. Just once. Then she turned back to the basin and kept every agent where they were.

Nobody could spare a body for the ridge and still keep Vel off the threshold.

The garrison fighter east of them tried a suppression lane next, not a kill-shot but a sheet of pressure set low across the route ahead. Vel saw it coming in the shift of his shoulders and changed direction before it thickened, cutting deeper into the north curve where the footing went from polished to sharp. Kem followed and lost another patch of skin from one palm catching himself.

He said, not quite asking, "Is that her."

"Could be rock."

"That is not comforting."

"Wasn't meant to be."

They got thirty more meters west before the descent agent high on the shelf cracked the stone in front of them and forced them down into a kneeling scramble. The return lane from the east clipped the back of Vel's calf with a thick drag of force that did not cut flesh so much as tell muscle it belonged to someone else. She yanked the leg free before the effect could finish taking hold.

Kem saw the limp the instant it started and looked like he wanted to say something helpful. He had known her too long now to try.

The basin gave them another rib of cover, worse than the first. Vel went to one knee behind it, breathing through dust and the small ugly grinding in her ankle.

Osen was still visible beyond the heat-waver and late light, one hand resting at her side, not hurried, not wasting anything. Vel had spent enough years around people with levels to know the kind of vanity that came with big numbers. They liked to demonstrate. They liked to let power arrive in sentences. Osen did not have that disease. She was using superior resources the way a careful quartermaster used better stores: without romance, because she expected to need them later.

"You ever think," Kem said between breaths, "that there should be a procedure for this."

"There is."

"I meant one not written by the people shooting at us."

"No."

The specialist made the south curve then, not close enough to strike, just visible enough that their cover stopped being cover and turned into a trap. Vel saw the geometry settle into place. Osen was not trying to kill them on the next move. She was making the next move obvious.

South meant the specialist. North meant shelf fire. West meant Osen's line. East meant back the way they had come.

She said, "We go west anyway. Short run. Then cut north."

Kem took one look at Osen's position and said, "I assume you don't mean because it's wise."

"No."

"Good. I'd hate for my education to be complete at this point."

The west run bought them a different kind of trouble. The ground flattened, which let them move. It also gave Osen the clean line she had been building toward. Light gathered in the commander's hands with a flat, disciplined brightness, not theatrical, not rushed. Vel saw it and knew at once that this was not for warning. It was for correction.

"Down," she said.

She dropped before Kem could answer. He followed a beat later and still not fast enough. The beam took the space over them and peeled a black line out of the stone. Heat slapped the back of Vel's neck. Kem's sleeve caught the edge of it and smoked.

They rolled into the north cut harder than planned. Kem's shoulder hit first. Vel's ankle folded again. The pain came up white and immediate, then settled into something she could carry as long as she did not ask the foot for honesty.

They were farther west. It was not enough to matter. It was enough to keep doing.

By the time the first rush of movement ended, the alarm had become part of the basin the way wind was part of it, a continuous mechanical grief rattling off the walls. Dust had pasted itself to the sweat under Vel's shirt. Blood from Kem's chin had dried once and started again.

That was the first ten minutes.

The next hour taught them the rest of Osen's method.

She never let the line break clean. When Vel and Kem used stone, the east garrison fighter cut the next stone. When they hugged north, the descent agent on the shelves drove them lower with ugly, exact shots that did not try to kill so much as place feet. When they tested south, the specialist blinked just far enough into view to make the route a lie. Every westward gain cost them twice in sideways distance, and sideways distance was what killed the old arithmetic. Vel could almost admire the shape of it when she hated her enough.

They learned which rocks rang before they split. They learned which layers of sandstone would hold a hand and which would slough off in sheets. They learned that one of the garrison fighters liked low bindings at shin height and the other preferred force pulses aimed where a running body wanted its next step. They learned that Osen did not call many orders aloud because she did not need to. The Reserve were that well trained, or that well scared of getting it wrong in front of her.

Vel kept every level.

That part took work. At eight, Perception was useful enough to hurt. Every time a lane started to close or a shot built in a half-hidden hand, her body reached for the old answer before her mind could stop it. Spend one, see more, survive easier. Spend two, dirty the geometry, make the basin stupid instead of lethal. Spend now, feel better now.

She would not spend here. She would not spend eight hundred meters out, or six hundred, or four. The threshold had been weakened on purpose. Osen had told them that in the tunnel with the satisfaction of a person reciting maintenance history. Whatever had once answered eagerly to a two-person descent no longer did. Vel needed the point awake under her feet. She needed the release happening where the ground itself could take it. Anything she fed the basin early just taught the basin she was willing to pay.

So she paid in the old currencies instead.

She paid skin off her right palm hauling herself up a ledge that broke under Kem's weight anyway. She paid the cracked ribs in her left side every time she twisted too hard to keep him moving. She paid the ankle over and over until the joint stopped giving sharp reports and settled into a thick, hot wrongness.

Kem paid with his face first. The second time Osen cut a lane under them, he lost footing on a strip of stone polished smooth by old floodwater and went down chin-first again. This time the split opened for real. Blood ran over his throat and into his collar.

Vel hauled him up by the back of the shirt.

"You still with me?"

"I was about to ask you the same thing."

"Then ask after we cross."

"That is an irritatingly optimistic sentence."

Later he paid with breath. A descent agent drove a force line into a narrow cleft just as Vel shoved him through it, and the rebound hit him full in the ribs. Not broken, Vel thought, because he did not make the right noise for broken. Bruised hard enough that every third breath turned cautious after that.

He noticed her noticing and said, "If you say you're fine, I'm allowed to be fine."

"You aren't."

"I don't enjoy that this is your affectionate register."

That almost got a laugh out of her. It did not, but it came close enough to be useful.

Once, with the sun still high and the ridge edges hard in the light, they got within five clean strides of a better west line. Vel saw it happen a second before the others did: a run of broken shelves that stepped downward toward the threshold side, enough cover between them that a fast body might stay half-hidden all the way to the last third of the basin.

She pointed.

Kem nodded.

Then Osen moved for the first time with anything resembling urgency.

She did not attack them. She put a clean, terrible beam into the second shelf in the sequence and collapsed the whole set in a coughing red slide. Stone went where their feet were about to go. Dust blew up. The line vanished under rubble before either of them could commit to it.

Kem stared at the ruin and said, "This feels targeted."

"It is."

"I don't mean generally."

Vel looked west, at Osen already still again. "Yes."

They spent the next stretch north because the south had become impossible. The specialist had learned their pace by then. He started appearing not where they were but where pain would make them want to be. Vel did not give him the clean kill he wanted. She made him work for the miss. That meant ugly routes, sharp climbs, using the body for leverage instead of speed. Kem kept up with a stubbornness that had lost its shine and become something heavier.

They crawled once, flat to the stone under a shelf barely high enough to take their ribs. Vel's shoulder blade scraped the rock chip lodged under it and sent a fresh current of nausea through her. Kem's split chin dripped on the stone between them.

"If I survive this," he said very quietly, because the shelf carried sound, "I'm going to have opinions about your definition of route fixing."

"Keep them for later."

"I have been keeping them for later for weeks."

"Keep harder."

The gravel ribbon on the north scree came again, lower than before.

Vel saw it while flat on her side under the shelf: a narrow run of dislodged stone moving in a controlled path, stopping, starting, correcting. Not a fall. A person making a bad descent on purpose and refusing to die of it. Osen saw it too. Her head turned a degree, no more.

Still she kept the basin floor staffed. That told Vel two things. First, the commander judged the immediate crossing threat as worse than whatever was on the ridge. Second, she was right.

The bad part was that Vel could not use the knowledge yet.

By midday the heat coming out of the rock had turned from assault to punishment. It no longer struck. It sat on the basin and made every breath feel second-used. Kem's shirt was dark down the spine. Vel's hands had started slipping on stone unless she wiped them first.

They found a deeper fold in the ground at one point and got nearly a minute there before the east garrison fighter started shaving the lip off it shot by shot. Vel used the time to look, not rest. West still too open. South closed by the specialist. North possible if they accepted exposure from the shelf gun. She chose north.

Kem tipped his head back against the stone and closed his eyes for one second.

"Don't sleep," she said.

"That was not sleeping."

"Looked aspirational."

He opened one eye. "How far."

She measured against the threshold point, Osen, the old fault line running under the basin, the journal marks in her head, and the sheer amount of hell still visible between here and there.

"Six hundred."

He made a face. "That is extremely rude."

"I know."

"Do you want the useful lie or the useless truth."

"Neither. I want your legs under you."

He got them under him.

The north route cost them most of the next hour and bought them actual progress for the first time. The descent agent high on the shelves had good placement but worse footing the farther he chased them along that side. Vel forced two changes of angle that made him expose himself to his own people. Osen corrected, of course. She always corrected. But correction took time, and time let bodies move.

They were not fast. Nothing about eight and twelve produced speed any longer. It produced refusal. It produced the choice to keep loading weight onto one bad ankle and one bruised side because lying down would be simpler in a way Vel could not permit herself to examine.

At one narrow break in the shelves she had to shove Kem up by the back of the thigh and then climb after him one-handed because the other wrist was already threatening treachery from how often she had caught herself on it. The stone edge bit into the meat of her palm. Kem rolled and caught her forearm before she slid back.

For a second they were both hanging on strain and leverage and nothing graceful.

"This," Kem said through his teeth, "is not my preferred form of trust exercise."

"Climb."

"That was not me refusing."

He got her up. They broke west again. A Pulse from the east hit the shelf behind them and sent chips into Vel's neck and ear. A fragment cut the shell of her ear and made the whole left side of her head feel wet. She kept moving.

The shelf route narrowed into a run of leaning slabs. They crossed it half sideways. The specialist appeared ahead of them in a blink, not close enough to strike but close enough to freeze the next choice. Vel feinted south. He bit on it and committed another blink to cut her off.

She did not spend to catch it. She did not have the level. She just changed her mind faster than he expected and went low north while he was still arriving south.

It bought them maybe forty meters.

Forty mattered.

Later, when the light started to flatten and the western ridge threw longer shadows across the basin floor, Osen spoke again.

"I've watched three people try to reach this point," she called. "One thought speed would solve it. One thought power would. One thought the site remembered mercy. It does not."

Kem, panting into a strip of shade that could not really be called cover, said, "Is she trying to comfort us."

"No."

"She's very consistent."

Vel did not answer because the line had lodged in her anyway. Three people had tried. She had known there were others, abstractly, in the way a person knew any secret worth killing for had already been tested on someone poorer. Hearing the number inside the basin made it smaller and fouler. It was not many. It was just enough.

It changed nothing tactical. It still made her angrier.

The next move went bad fast.

They had used a run of eroded columns to get farther west than Osen liked. Vel saw it in the first real adjustment the commander made all afternoon. She sent the specialist hard south to north instead of north to south, reversing the pressure. The east garrison fighter set a suppression sheet not in front of them but behind, to prevent retreat. The north descent agent stopped trying to herd and simply fired for impact.

Vel said, "Down."

There was nowhere good to go down to. Kem chose a slit between two stones and got there first. Vel followed. A Pulse hit the lip above them and burst the seam open. Rock came in.

Kem twisted toward her without thinking. The falling slab missed her head and took his shoulder instead, driving him flat and forcing a sound out of him he would not have made on purpose.

Vel shoved the stone off before it settled. The slab was not heavy by quarry standards. It was heavy enough when you were doing it with a ruined ankle and a rib cage already sour from old damage.

Kem rolled over holding the shoulder close.

"Dislocated?" she asked.

He breathed once, twice, swallowed. "No. I don't think. It still belongs to me."

"Then get up."

"You're very encouraging."

"I know."

He got up, loose with pain and bad angles after that. That was when Vel started hauling him more often instead of assuming he would simply follow. She used the good side for it. The bad side already had enough complaints filed.

They crossed the next stretch almost hip to hip, not from tenderness but because separate bodies were easier to isolate. The garrison fighters understood that as soon as Vel did. A low binding cracked under Kem's ankle and dropped him to one knee. He tore at it with both hands before it fully closed. Skin peeled under his nails. The east fighter reloaded for a second attempt.

Vel looked at the distance still left, looked at the numbers she was hoarding, and said, "No."

Not to the binding. To the impulse.

She yanked Kem up by the elbow instead, and together they flung themselves sideways into a cut in the floor before the second binding landed. The line snapped shut on empty stone.

That evasion cost her the ankle again. She almost blacked out for a second from how quickly the pain climbed. Kem saw her mouth tighten and said nothing. That was how she knew he was actually worried.

The north scree gave another hiss of gravel, lower still.

By the time the sun had started its west slide in earnest, they had lost any sense of clean forward movement. Progress came in diagonals, in stolen shelves, in moments where Osen's perfect pattern was only good instead of perfect. Vel measured the basin now less by meters than by what remained between tactical shifts: the next rib, the next trough, the next bend where the specialist would have to show his hand, the next patch of shadow, the next place the ground might remember that it had once opened.

Kem had stopped asking how far except when he really needed the answer. That was growth, or exhaustion, or both.

At one bad pause behind a split column of sandstone, he put his head back against the rock and laughed once without humor when the sound of it turned into a cough.

"What's funny."

"I had an image. From the Vault."

"Bad time."

"Yes. That seemed true as I was having it. There was a man in our east court whose entire position existed because he knew how to arrange views. Not roads, not supply, not anything with a body in it. Views. He once spent six weeks on the question of whether a particular cypress made the conservatory feel too available."

Vel watched the specialist's line through a split in the stone.

"And."

"And I am thinking of him now while hiding behind a rock the size of a dead goat. I feel this says something unpleasant about how minds work."

"It does."

"Do you know what it is."

"No."

"Good. I would have hated for you to become interpretive at the end."

That time she did laugh, once and short, because the alternative was some worse use of air.

Then Osen took the column apart from the top down and the moment ended.

They reached the rib of sandstone with a little under two hundred meters left and nowhere decent to hide.

It was not a single run that got them there. It was the accumulation of bad choices that had been slightly better than the alternatives. By then Vel's left ankle hurt with every heartbeat. Dust had turned the inside of her mouth into old cloth. Kem lay three meters off her left knee, breathing through his mouth, chin bloodied from the earlier falls, shoulder held a fraction too carefully. Both of them were still carrying the same levels they had started with because there had been no honest place to spend.

The sun had slid far enough west that ridge shadows striped the basin floor. Heat still leaked out of the stone, but the white noon glare was gone. Osen had spent those hours doing the same thing over and over: cutting every clean line west and forcing them sideways, keeping any level they burned too far from the threshold to matter. Vel had answered with patience, bad footing, and blood instead of spending.

High on the north scree, gravel hissed down again in a narrow, steady ribbon. It was low enough now to be hopeful.

Osen looked that direction once and looked away. She kept every agent on the basin floor. If she thinned the line for even a few seconds, Vel would have the point.

Vel spat dust, found there was not enough spit for it, and said, "You hear me say spend, you spend."

Kem wiped blood off his mouth with the back of his hand. "And if you don't."

"You use your body."

He nodded once. "I can. I mean, I can for another minute or so."

"Only plan we've got."

She touched the rock, then went. Kem moved with her.

The first Combat Pulse cracked the stone where his head had been. He had already thrown himself sideways. The move was ugly and late, but it left him breathing. Six weeks ago he would have taken it full in the face.

Vel spent one level on Perception.

The basin snapped into hard focus. Northeast garrison behind a low shelf. East garrison building the suppression lane she had been feeling for half the afternoon. The specialist closing from the south with another blink loaded in the back of his posture. Osen holding back at the threshold, saving her own hand for the point.

"Left," Vel said.

Kem cut left without asking.

The suppression lane thickened a half second too slow and took only Vel's calf instead of her whole stride. It felt like running through wet sand while someone else owned the tendons. She tore free before it set. A force beam scorched past. Hot stone spat chips across her shoulders. One punched into the flesh under her shoulder blade and stayed there.

The specialist came in fast, arriving first as a gap in the basin and then as a body on her left with his shoulder already turned into the strike.

Vel got her forearm up. The hit ran through bone and tendon. Her wrist gave with a wet pop. Pain flashed white, then narrowed to something sharp enough to use. She let the blow turn her instead of trying to stop it. The agent overcommitted, skidded, and slammed shoulder-first into the rock.

"Keep moving," she said.

Another Pulse shattered the shelf behind them. Dust jumped. Through it Vel saw the specialist loading a second blink and his partner closing to receive it.

She spent two levels together, one to catch the blink and one to kick its exit point backward and dirty.

The specialist vanished and reappeared twenty meters wrong, straight into his partner. The collision burst bright and concussive. Both men went down in the dust.

Five.

Losing levels this low felt local. It was like a small useful organ shutting off and not promising return.

Kem was up again, running bent and quick with the body he had built mile by mile. He had learned to read a shoulder before an ability. He had learned to fall without dying. He had learned that a person could do a surprising amount with ordinary muscle if the alternative was ledger arithmetic performed by someone richer.

The commander chose that moment to stop waiting.

Vel felt the charge through her teeth before she saw the light gather in Osen's hands, a killing line tuned for this basin and this distance. The shot was not for Kem. It was for the route ahead of Vel, the exact strip of stone her body had to trust next.

She was off-angle and too far east. Her wrist was blown, her ankle half numb, and five levels did not buy clean geometry from there.

Kem saw it.

He stepped between them and spent five.

The shield he threw came up as a wall four meters wide and canted just enough to catch the beam and throw the worst of it away.

The impact tore rock out of the basin. Vel hit the ground on her bad shoulder. The buried chip drove deeper. Dust filled her mouth. When she looked up, Kem was on both knees, palms split open from bracing, down from twelve to seven.

"Up," Vel said.

He made a noise that might have been a laugh. "That was — shit. Was that five? I just spent five on one wall." He pressed his palms flat on the stone and stared at them. "Okay. I'm coming."

He came up loose with pain, hard to carry cleanly, all bad angles and sudden dead weight while Vel's own body was close to the end of its good behavior. She got under his arm with her good side and dragged him moving.

The garrison pair closed. These were basin workers, not rich amateurs with too many levels and no sense. There was less flourish in them and better placement. One caught Kem's ankle in a binding and dropped him face-first into the rock. His chin split wider. The other cracked the ground between them, opening a narrow crevice meant to keep their levels from touching in the same place.

Vel spent one level on misdirection and let the next binding catch an afterimage. Empty stone took the hit.

Four.

"Kem, move."

He tore the binding loose with both hands. Skin came off under his nails. He got up anyway, one side of him holding itself too carefully.

The commander called from the threshold, "I've watched three people try to reach this point. You can still walk away."

Vel kept running.

"Walk back to what," Kem said, not turning. Then he was moving too.

The specialist came again from the south, loaded for a kill. Kem waited until the blink took hold. Then he spent two levels and pinched the whole ability stack dark.

The specialist stumbled out of transit at ordinary speed and ordinary strength, suddenly just a man on bad footing. His punch clipped Kem's shoulder hard enough to hurt and nowhere near hard enough to end him.

Kem hit him back once, then twice. The man's head cracked on layered stone and stayed down.

Five.

High on the north wall, the gravel ribbon had become a figure now, tiny but human, coming down the ugly route on purpose. Vel saw the body slide, catch, keep coming. Osen saw it too. She still did not turn an agent to deal with it. If she thinned the line for even a few seconds, Vel would have the point.

Another garrison binding started for Kem. Vel spent one level and drove the caster's own frequency back through it. The line snapped sideways into his ribs. He folded where he stood, both hands clamped to his side.

Three.

The lane opened.

"Now," Vel said.

Kem caught her forearm and spent three levels on motion.

The basin disappeared under them. Stone blurred. Wind tore tears out of Vel's eyes. One heartbeat, then another, and the threshold point was under their feet.

Kem let go. He had two. She had three. Behind them the wounded agents were coming hard. In front of them Osen was close enough for Vel to see the threadbare places in her windscarf and the disbelief she was refusing to let become expression.

Vel did not waste the moment speaking.

She spent the first of her last three levels into the ground.

She did not send it at Osen or forward. She sent it down.

The buried seam under the threshold point answered with a deep vibration that ran up through the soles of her boots and into her bad ankle. That first spend did not open anything. It woke the site. The old cut in the world had to recognize a descent before it would take one.

She spent the second to hold a mouth in it.

Air thinned over the unmarked stone. The shape of the world started to slip, not yet torn but worrying loose where the seam ran weakest. That second spend was not for crossing either. It only kept the split from sealing the instant it formed.

Osen lunged then, building a containment field on instinct and training, trying to catch Vel above zero and freeze the whole attempt there.

Vel spent the last level before the field closed.

The first two levels had primed the place and held it open. The last one was the fall itself. Thresholds answered to nearby expenditure, yes, but they answered hardest to a number becoming none. Without zero there was force and light and waste. With zero there was a descent.

She had lived fifty-eight years under the Ledger and spent forty-one of them on purpose. Both counts ended here.

Zero.

The hum did not simply stop. It tore out of her.

Her jaw went loose. Pressure she had carried for so long it had stopped reading as pressure vanished from the roots of her teeth and the backs of her eyes. Her stomach dropped as if the ground had missed a stair. Her knees nearly followed. A cold flash ran over her scalp and down the inside of both arms. For one sick second she thought she had gone deaf because the constant internal tone was gone and her body still reached for it.

Then the rest of the world came back in pieces.

Wind still scraped the basin. Someone shouted. Loose stone hissed down the north scree. But the Ledger's background presence, the faint counting pressure that had sat under every hour since she was a child, was absent so completely it left shape behind, the way a pulled tooth left the tongue searching a hole.

Perception went with it.

For decades the world had never ended at her skin. Even when she was not actively using the ability, even when she was tired or sleeping or half drunk on bad road liquor, some part of her had always known where weight sat nearby, where people moved, where levels collected bright or thin in the dark. The basin had never needed to be seen all the way to be inhabited. Osen's field had been a pressure in the air before it was light. Kem had been a count to the left before he was a body. The wounded garrison fighter had remained present at the edge of awareness even while she ran the other direction.

That whole layer of the world dropped away.

Now there was only what her ordinary senses could manage. She had dust in her mouth. Pain throbbed in her wrist. The bad ankle threatened collapse. Osen's containment field existed only where light marked it and nowhere beyond that. Kem was visible only when she looked straight at him. The north wall stayed empty until the falling figure showed itself against it. Everything outside sight became guesswork again.

The loss struck a second time as nausea. Vel gagged and swallowed dust.

The threshold tore open.

It held badly, with ragged edges and fraying light, a mouth forced in bad cloth. Through it Vel saw hand-cut stone, packed-earth streets, people moving under a sky that had never carried the Ledger's hum. The sight came at her without the old overlay, with no counting pressure on the bodies beyond and no sense of levels because there were none to sense. There were only people and built stone and evening light.

She stepped through before the body could think too long about what had changed.

The crossing took one stride and all of it.

On the other side the air smelled of dust, herbs, and a cookfire somewhere beyond the first bend in the street. The silence was not true silence. Voices moved in it. Wind moved in it. A cart wheel knocked once on stone. But no hidden tally sat under any of it, and there was no second layer. The absence was so large it felt physical, like standing after years with a weight strapped to the spine and finding posture suddenly ungoverned.

Vel turned at once.

Kem was still in the basin, one knee at the edge of the tear, blood on his chin and hands. The opening was already shrinking around him.

Two people had not been enough.

Now the failure was visible. The mouth had force, but it did not have duration. Her zero had opened it. Kem's remaining two levels could finish only one job. He could spend them into the tear and keep it alive for a moment longer, but then he would be standing at zero in the basin while the frame died in front of him. Or he could spend them on himself and try to force the crossing, and the mouth would close while his body was still in it. The damped threshold wanted a third contributor, not another desperate guess at the old math.

Osen was not standing idle beside him.

The containment field she had thrown for Vel had changed shape with the failure. One band of it cinched the tear's edge tight, trying to choke width out of it. Another had hooked around Kem's calves and dragged him half a pace off the lip. Each time he leaned toward the opening, the band hauled him short again.

"Stay where you are," Osen said. "It's closing and you do not have enough."

She had one hand locked on the shrinking ring around the tear and the other feeding that clamp at Kem's legs. She was not sparing him. She was choosing the only interference that did not become self-damage. Any hard offensive spend from that position would go straight into the active threshold and feed the cascade she was trying to starve. A killing Pulse at the mouth would not seal it. It would widen it. So she pinned him short of the point and waited for the door to die.

Kem looked through the narrowing gap at Vel. "Two doesn't — it's not enough for both, is it." The flat concentration he got when the math was bad had already settled over his face.

The north-scree noise hit the basin floor at last as a rush of loose stone and hard breathing.

Osen half turned. Vel could see the exact second she understood who had made that descent.

Then seven fresh spends struck the cascade in rapid succession.

Sable Orm came off the scree with both hands up, gravel still running around her boots. Dust covered the front of her field coat. Blood shone at one palm where the rock had taken skin. She looked like a woman who had skidded three hours down bad stone and meant to spend more before night if that was the price of getting the answer in front of her.

Osen snapped the clamp at Kem harder and started to redirect the outer ring toward Sable's wrists.

She stopped halfway through the motion.

The threshold shuddered when her field shifted. Vel saw the tear flare wider for one ugly blink. Osen saw it too. That was the constraint made visible. If she abandoned the choke entirely for a clean attack, she would feed the opening with her own force and risk giving them the very breach she had spent years preventing. So she held the failing geometry with one hand and tried to spoil Sable from the edge of it instead.

Sable spent.

She was nowhere near zero. That was why the seven levels could help without taking her through. Thresholds answered to nearby expenditure, not only to terminal descent. Her body stayed anchored where it was because she was still miles above none. The energy went into the mouth instead of into a crossing.

The first two spends steadied the tear. Vel saw the shaking in the frame lessen and the ragged edge stop sawing inward.

The next ones widened it.

By the seventh the mouth was holding, ugly but real, no longer collapsing faster than the people around it could think.

Osen turned fully to her then, furious now because fury cost nothing.

"Auditor."

"I am," Sable said, and stopped there, breath still wrong from the descent. She looked once at the commander's hands on the containment lattice, once at Kem caught short of the lip, then through the opening at the stone street behind Vel. "Do not say anything right now."

She did not look back at Osen. She looked through the tear instead, at hand-cut stone, packed earth, and people moving under that quiet sky. Whatever question had dragged her out of the Vault and all the way south was no longer a question. The answer was standing there with blood on its face.

With Sable's seven levels in the frame, the mechanics changed.

Now the threshold was being held open by outside spend. Kem's last two no longer had to brace the mouth and make the crossing in the same instant. They only had to take him to zero while the mouth remained stable enough to accept a body.

Vel said, "Go."

Kem looked once at Sable, once at Osen's hands in the field, then at Vel.

He spent his last two levels.

There was no flare to it. Vel could not feel the Ledger leave him the way she would have an hour earlier. That was gone. She only saw the change in his face: the reflexive listening that had lived under his expression since she met him cut blank in an instant, as if someone had shut a window inside him.

The clamp at his calves failed when he did.

Whether Osen lost the leverage or the threshold rejected the geometry once his count hit none, Vel could not tell. Kem lurched forward. The mouth took him clean.

He crossed.

He hit the packed earth stumbling. Vel caught him with her good arm. He hissed around the bad rib and almost went down anyway. His hands were still trying to brace against a force that no longer existed.

"Hey," Vel said.

He looked at her, looked past her at the stone street, the built walls, the evening light, and then down at his own empty hands.

"It's so quiet," he said, not to her exactly.

Behind him, Sable stood on the basin floor still far above zero, hands lowered now, wind dragging at her ruined hair. She stayed where she was while the surviving agents regrouped. Osen looked from Sable to the place where the tear had been and said nothing. There was no useful sentence available to her that did not amount to witness.

The threshold folded in on itself. The Scour vanished. Silence stayed.

Vel felt the aftershock of its staying all the way through her body. No hum returned. No hidden pressure settled back into the bones. The world remained stubbornly ordinary in the frightening new way.

Somewhere in the stone streets behind her, a voice called out. Then another, closer. Footsteps started toward them.

Kem turned his head at the sound like a man relearning what distance meant.

Vel kept one hand on his arm and looked toward the nearest turn in the street.

For the first time in forty years, she had to wait for the corner to produce the body before knowing who was coming.

She waited for Maren to come around it.
