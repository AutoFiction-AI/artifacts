# Chapter 6

The Council chamber had seventeen chairs, and Sable Orm knew the occupant of each by the sound they made sitting down. Councilor Breve's knees cracked. Councilor Hain breathed through his mouth when he thought no one was listening. Lady Aldraste settled into her chair without a sound. Around this table, that counted as rank.

Sable had spent twenty-three years in this room. She had organized its agendas, drafted its minutes, revised its protocols, and, on four occasions she did not discuss, corrected the arithmetic of Council members who outranked her. To her, the chamber was a ledger with furniture: every column aligned, every entry predictable.

What she did not know was why she had been summoned to stand before it rather than sit behind the recording desk.

"The Aldraste situation," Councilor Breve began.

Sable waited. Across two decades of administrative service, she had learned that the first sentence of a Council address was rarely the one that mattered.

"Young Lord Aldraste has been outside the walls for approximately five days." Breve checked his notes. The gesture itself was an indictment. "He has spent an estimated twelve levels. In five days. The perimeter enforcement detail authorized under standing recovery protocol made contact yesterday. It did not recover him."

Around the table, hands stopped over paper. Twelve levels. In the Vault, the number was obscene. Sable's nearest colleague had spent four across an entire career, two on a Perception ability during an emergency audit and two on a Light charm during a power failure that threatened the record vaults. She had been spoken to about it.

"The House signet was taken from the ceremonial stand," Lady Aldraste said. Her voice carried the calm of a woman who had decided exactly how much of herself this room was going to get. "My son did not plan this. That is the charitable interpretation, and I will be offering no other."

Sable recognized the structure at once. Lady Aldraste was entering a breach into Council business, not a son into family trouble.

"The question before the Council," Breve said, "is the next method of retrieval."

The enforcement team had gone out under standing recovery authority. This new pursuit would bear the Council's name.

"There has been discussion regarding the deployment of specialized resources."

Specialized resources. Sable had encountered references to them in Council records three times across her career. Each reference had been redacted before filing. The hand changed each time. The secrecy had a procedure.

Her mind went to the sealed wing on the Vault's eastern corridor. Twenty-three years of passing those doors had taught her that some silences were manufactured more carefully than others.

"The Red Reserve," Lady Aldraste said.

"For the benefit of those present," Breve said, looking directly at Sable now, "the Red Reserve is a Council-maintained unit of Hoarded personnel authorized to train in the use of abilities. For security purposes."

They were authorized to train. They were permitted to spend. People had been drilling behind the eastern doors all along.

"The Reserve is not a viable option for this operation," Councilor Hain said. He said it too quickly.

Lady Aldraste answered in the cadence of someone reading from a prepared document. "Deploying trained spenders for a retrieval operation would constitute a public acknowledgment that the philosophy of preservation cannot address all institutional concerns. The existence of the Reserve is predicated on its non-existence. Using it visibly destroys the premise."

"Agreed," Breve said. "The Reserve is not deployable for this purpose."

"Auditor Orm," Breve said.

Sable's stomach performed a maneuver she did not have an administrative category for.

"The Council would like to discuss your availability for a field assignment."

"I am available for any assignment the Council requires." The correct response came out before the incorrect one finished forming.

"The retrieval of Kem Aldraste requires a lead operative with institutional credibility, administrative competence, and discretion. You have been recommended by multiple Council members." Breve did not look at Lady Aldraste when he said it.

"Councilor Breve. I have not left the capital in forty-seven years." Sable delivered the line as an inventory of relevant data, not a protest. "I have never operated field equipment. I have never navigated terrain. My longest recorded distance on foot in a single day was approximately two kilometers during the record-room relocation of Year Eighteen."

"Which you managed with distinction," Breve said.

"The logistics of document transport across three buildings are not comparable to field pursuit."

"You are precisely what we need." Lady Aldraste met Sable's eyes as if confirming a selection already entered in the record. "Someone who represents what we are. Not what we have hidden."

Nobody contradicted her. Hain cleared his throat and stopped there. Lady Aldraste's hands lay flat on the table, fingers aligned. One thumbnail had a torn edge. In twenty-three years, Sable had never seen that.

"I will need a team," Sable said.

"Of course. We have prepared a list of suitable operatives."

"Field-trained?"

Breve looked down at his papers. Hain adjusted a cuff. No one said yes.

"I see," Sable said.

Breve slid a folder across the table. The cover page read *Council-Authorized Retrieval: Aldraste, Kem / Lead Operative Acceptance Packet*. Beneath it sat custody receipts, route warrants, equipment inventories, secrecy acknowledgments, and a sheaf of blank expenditure forms clipped in groups of five.

"You will sign before departure," Breve said.

Sable opened to the liability page. "Any unauthorized expenditure, unlogged route deviation, equipment loss, barter commitment, or local procurement undertaken in the field will be assigned to the team lead for review."

"That is standard language," Hain said.

"For what standard."

"Field work."

Sable looked at him. "By your own account, the Council is not deploying field personnel."

No one answered that. Lady Aldraste adjusted a single page in front of her, a motion so small it could only have been irritation.

"The district courtesy notices will be honored," Breve said. "The Council seal remains persuasive."

Sable turned the next page. "No cash allotment."

"Unnecessary," Breve said. "This is not a trade excursion."

"Food, ferries, local guidance, emergency transport, replacement supplies, animal hire, and passage fees all exist outside trade excursions."

"The districts understand Council priority."

Sable had drafted enough interdistrict correspondence to know that districts understood delay, procedure, and the profitable edges of both.

"And the expenditure forms?" she asked.

"Total expenditure is not to exceed the pre-authorized operational limit of two levels per operative per day," Breve said, reciting from memory. "Ability use must be pre-authorized by the team lead and documented."

"Documented when."

"At the time of use."

She let the paper rest against the folder. "In the field."

"Yes."

There were moments when the Council's devotion to doctrine rose so high it ceased to resemble belief and became architecture instead. A person could stand inside it and still not understand how it had been built.

"I would like a clarification entered into the file," Sable said.

Breve's face took on the patient attention of a man being burdened with procedure by one of his own.

"If immediate danger requires expenditure before documentation can be produced, is the field team expected to preserve paperwork or bodily integrity."

Hain answered first. "The protocol contains an emergency provision."

"Reducing the written explanation from two hundred words to one hundred," Sable said, still reading. "Yes. I have located the mercy."

For the first time that morning, Breve looked almost embarrassed. It improved him slightly.

"Sign the packet, Auditor Orm," Lady Aldraste said. "Retrieve my son. Keep the Council deniable. You may categorize the rest as you please."

Sable signed because refusal would only have delayed the same signature by the length of a more crowded argument.

The Council's list of suitable operatives contained eight names. Three declined immediately, one citing a joint condition, one citing a commitment to an ongoing inventory project, and one submitting a written objection that referenced seven separate protocols governing voluntary assignment exemptions. The remaining five reported to Sable's office at the designated hour.

All five were level 300. That narrowed nothing.

Tarrell arrived with three notebooks and a personal seal and announced, before sitting down, that he had been told this was a documentation assignment. Kess had volunteered and looked pleased with herself in the strained, overbright way of someone who had mistaken apprehension for zeal. Lenn came with a field manual memorized and a pack cinched high enough to cut his shoulders raw by noon. Marris kept checking her Ledger as though the 300 might turn out to be a clerical error. Chael, a records porter, brought a compass and the only shoulders in the room that looked as though they had lifted anything heavier than paper.

"I was told this was a documentation assignment," Tarrell said again, perhaps believing repetition might alter classification.

"It is a field retrieval," Sable said.

"Then my classification headers are wrong." Tarrell turned the top notebook over in his hands. "I indexed three tiers of source annotation for documentation-class work. None of that transfers to retrieval."

Tarrell looked down at the notebooks with an expression of quiet betrayal.

Kess studied the route map on Sable's desk. "Will we be walking the entire distance?"

"The target is traveling south on foot. We will need to do the same."

"Right. That should be fine, I've been walking the courtyards every morning." Kess folded her hands on the map. "For how long?"

"The estimate is twelve to fifteen days," Sable said. "That estimate was drafted inside this building, so I advise caution."

Kess placed a parasol on the desk with the care of someone setting down a small hope.

The crate from the Council sat beside the map. Sable had reviewed the manifest the night before. She had not opened it. That omission corrected itself immediately.

Inside were six ration packs sealed in waxed paper, six bedrolls tagged with serial numbers, six Communication charms in lead-lined cases, a medical kit, a hand-drawn map, and a four-page document titled *Standard Field Protocol for Council-Authorized Operations*.

Sable opened the protocol and read aloud. "Total expenditure is not to exceed the pre-authorized operational limit of two levels per operative per day."

No one spoke.

She continued. "All ability use must be pre-authorized by the team leader through completion of Form 7-C: Justification of Level Expenditure. The form requires a written explanation of no fewer than two hundred words per instance of ability deployment."

"What if the situation requires immediate action?" Lenn asked. His voice stayed level by effort.

Sable turned the page. "The emergency provision reduces the written justification to one hundred words in circumstances of direct and immediate physical threat. The form must still be completed prior to deployment."

"Prior to."

"Yes."

No one laughed, which made it worse.

The rest of the kit improved nothing. The medical instructions referenced an Appendix D that did not exist. The map called a river ford twelve kilometers south "manageable" and described the crossing pace as standard administrative. No weapons had been issued. Several district courtesy notices had been tucked under the map, each bearing a Council stamp and a sentence promising reimbursement on later review. None contained a mark, a voucher, or anything that would become food before an office opened.

Chael picked up the suturing needle and held it in the center of his palm, studying it as though it might explain itself.

"Has anyone here stitched a wound?" Sable asked.

Silence, then Lenn: "I have read about the procedure."

"That is now a qualification," Sable said. "You will carry the medical kit."

She did not stop there. If the Council intended to send six level-300 citizens into the world under the fiction that a stamped packet constituted preparedness, then the fiction could at least be tested before dawn.

Sable took the team down to the service corridor outside Archive Three, where the floors were stone rather than polished wood and the stairs turned tight enough to punish error. She had each operative load a pack, climb two flights, descend, cross the corridor, and repeat the circuit until the straps settled into whatever truth they were going to tell.

Tarrell failed first. His notebooks shifted, the pack pulled sideways, and he introduced his shoulder to a wall with enough force to rattle the framed fire regulations.

"That was poorly distributed," he said, winded.

"Yes," Sable said. "Remove two notebooks."

"I need one for route notes, one for contact records, and one for reflective annotations."

"You need one back."

Tarrell surrendered two notebooks like a man parting from relatives.

Kess made it through three circuits before the brightness in her face began separating from confidence. Lenn completed six with grim satisfaction and then stood at attention while the skin above his collarbones reddened into damage. Marris moved carefully and kept balance, which turned out to be useful, but her breathing went shallow after the second flight. Chael carried his own pack plus a bedroll under one arm and looked faintly embarrassed to be observed doing it.

Sable altered assignments as the corridor instructed her. Chael took the spare bedrolls and extra rations because he could actually bear the weight. Tarrell was permitted one notebook. Marris got the field log because her hands remained steady when everyone else's had started finding interesting new tasks. Kess was instructed to leave the parasol and wrap her heels before dawn. Lenn had to retie every pack in the room, including Sable's. He did so with a competence made entirely of theory, which was still superior to the Council's original packing.

By the second hour, the fiction of a field team had developed enough shape to reveal its holes. Nobody knew how to judge water by weather. Nobody knew how long a heel wrap lasted under real road conditions. Nobody knew whether the Communication charms had range limitations because the briefing sheet identified only "operationally suitable distance." Nobody knew how to set a watch rotation, and when Sable asked who could start a cooking fire without lamp oil, everyone except Chael looked offended by the question.

"Records furnace," Chael said, when she looked at him. "I used to help in winter."

"That is also now a qualification."

The stores clerk arrived at midday with the custody cards Sable had signed for but not yet seen. Each lead-lined charm case had to be logged to a specific carrier. Each ration pack had a serial number. Each bedroll had a return condition checklist. The clerk, who smelled faintly of cabinet dust and ink, read the liability language aloud as if recitation improved it.

"The lead operative assumes accounting responsibility for any deviation from route schedule, expected district courtesy, or expenditure documentation."

"Expected district courtesy is not an object," Sable said.

"It is a logistical assumption."

"That does not improve it."

The clerk waited. His entire profession was waiting until language became compliance.

Sable signed the remaining lines.

When he left, the room contained six charm cases, six bedrolls, six ration packs, one map, one medical kit, one compass, one notebook, and the exact quantity of institutional faith that had fit in the crate.

Tarrell watched her stack the custody cards. "If the forms are mandatory, should we begin pre-completing them."

Sable considered that for three full seconds and disliked that consideration had been required.

"Yes," she said. "We will prepare what can be prepared."

They spent the afternoon producing advance absurdities. Sable filled out names, dates, locations, and probable categories of emergency on blank Form 7-Cs until the word probable ceased to resemble a useful concept. She created a packet for each operative and labeled the top sheet with the narrowest instructions she could devise: if you are bleeding, spend first and write later; if you are not bleeding, find me before you do anything permanent; if I am unconscious, Chael authorizes; if Chael is unconscious, the problem has exceeded paperwork.

Tarrell copied the headings in smaller script. Marris numbered the sheets. Lenn suggested organizing injuries by severity and possible terrain source. Kess asked whether blister rupture counted as direct physical threat. Sable informed her that the answer depended on how attached she was to walking.

By evening, Sable had also produced a route schedule dense enough to reassure the Council and loose enough to survive first contact with weather. The Council's "manageable" first halt at twelve kilometers was crossed out. She marked six instead. She added a second possible halt at four if the team proved worse than expected and a third at eight if the team proved surprisingly competent. She expected to use only one of those numbers and distrust it.

Tarrell watched her alter the map. "The protocol does not authorize retroactive justification."

"Then it will be late," Sable said. "If someone needs to spend to prevent injury, they spend first and report to me after. I signed for the irregularities. That is now my error, not yours."

Nobody argued after that.

"We depart at dawn," Sable said. "Bring only what you can carry on your back. If you cannot carry it, you do not bring it."

Kess's eyes shifted to the parasol.

"You do not bring it," Sable said.

Dawn arrived the way it arrived in the Vault, precisely, light entering through windows engineered to illuminate without warming. Sable had slept two hours. The remaining six hours had gone to rereading the field manual, repacking by her revised assignments, and writing three versions of the opening mission log before accepting that the road would not begin more obediently because she had described it well.

The changes were visible at the main gate. Tarrell had one notebook. Kess's heels were wrapped in linen. Marris wore a single pack after Lenn rethreaded the straps for her. Chael carried the medical kit and two extra bedrolls. Lenn had lowered his own pack to a position that suggested recent instruction. It still sat too high.

The gate was where Kem Aldraste had walked out five days earlier. The ceremonial stand where House signets were displayed still held an empty square of flattened velvet in the Aldraste position.

"Senior Auditor." The gate warden had held the post for thirty years without incident, because incidents did not happen at the Vault's gate. "I am required to log your departure. How many in the party?"

"Six. Council-authorized retrieval operation. The reference number is in the briefing file."

"Estimated return date?"

"Three to four weeks."

He wrote it down with immaculate handwriting. Nothing in his manner suggested that six level-300 Hoarded citizens walking out to pursue a defector across open country was anything unusual. The Vault maintained its composure.

From its case, Sable took the Wayfinder charm, a small metal disc cool against her palm, the tracking glyph cut into one face. The cost was two levels.

Two levels. Her mother had died at 296, ashamed of the four she had spent across an entire life. Sable had spent none.

Kem's file supplied the face: formal, composed, twenty-six years of stillness and then the gate.

She activated the glyph.

Her knees went first.

Stone bit her palm as she caught the archway. Direction flooded in, south-southeast, distance, pace, the clean pull of a living line running through her body toward someone moving away. The knowledge had nothing to do with sight or hearing. It entered lower, stranger, as if some buried instrument inside her had been struck hard enough to wake.

300 became 298.

The threshold stone hit the back of her legs when she sat.

"Senior Auditor?" Lenn asked.

"A moment."

Breathing came too fast. The Wayfinder did not care. It kept running at the base of her skull, pulling south-southeast. At foot pace, the target was roughly nine days ahead and still moving. She could not dismiss it without spending again.

298. Her mind kept returning to the number and dropping against it, the way a foot drops when it expects one more stair and finds only air.

She made herself stand.

"The target is south-southeast," Sable said. Her voice held. "Approximately nine days at our pace, assuming our pace proves measurable."

No one said anything. Five faces watched her with the politeness of people who had just seen something happen for which the Vault had given them no approved vocabulary.

"We proceed south."

They walked through the gate. Sable Orm, forty-seven years old and level 298, stepped over the Vault's threshold and into the world.

Warm air struck her face carrying dust, animal smell, and sunlight already stored in stone. The sky was too large. Nothing overhead contained it. The road under her boots gave slightly where packed earth had been worn by wheels and weather, and each irregularity announced itself up through the bones of her feet. Behind her rose the walls that had contained her entire life. She did not look back.

By the first hour, the revised plan was already revising itself. Kess had bled through one heel wrap. Tarrell surrendered the notebook to Chael after ten minutes of pretending he could carry it and his pack at once. Marris could not keep the field log while walking, so the first halt came early and beside a drainage ditch instead of at any Council-approved landmark. Lenn abandoned the pace he had planned in his head. Chael, carrying half the room, was the one who never complained.

Marris opened the log on her knee. Sable dictated the entry while the Wayfinder kept humming south in her bones: first halt advanced, pace reduced, injuries minor, pursuit continuing.

It was the last sentence that remained accurate for several hours.

The Council map showed a crossing two kilometers farther on: a district-maintained cut with a plank bridge and, in the margin, the notation *cooperation expected*. Sable had seen the phrase on correspondence for years. It meant somebody else would do something inconvenient later and invoice the right office. On paper, it was a perfectly serviceable arrangement.

On the road, it meant a queue of grain carts, three sweating laborers knee-deep in a carved runoff channel, and half a retaining wall lying broken in the water.

The bridge had not collapsed. It had been removed. Two long planks rested on the far bank while the laborers reset the stone lip that held the crossing in place. One of them was using a low Shaping ability in short, practiced bursts, smoothing fractured edges so the new stones would seat cleanly. Each burst cost something. The worker did not treat that fact like a sermon. He treated it like lifting.

The line of waiting carts had been arranged by practical desperation. A woman standing on a wagon tongue held a ledger board and shouted names as if this were a market queue rather than a blocked road. When she saw six clean-coated Hoarded citizens approaching with Council packs and untouched faces, her mouth flattened into something that was not surprise.

"Crossing's one cart at a time once the plank goes back," she said. "If you're on foot, you wait behind the grain."

Sable presented the district courtesy notice. "Council-authorized retrieval operation. Passage is required."

The woman read the first line, skipped the stamp, and handed it back. "Fine. Required. The wall's still broken."

"The notice states cooperation is expected."

"It is. We're cooperating with the people whose grain goes rotten if this road stays shut."

"This operation has priority standing," Sable said.

"With who."

Sable almost said *the Council* and discovered, half a second before speaking, that the answer would change nothing here unless it arrived attached to men, marks, or labor.

"If the district clerk needs to log the warrant, where is the office?" she asked.

"East spur. Two miles." The woman nodded without warmth toward a side road. "If you want it written up and billed later, go there. If you want across now, you wait or you help."

Tarrell stared at the runoff channel. "We were issued no local payment instruments."

"That sounds terrible," the woman said, not sounding terrible at all.

Behind them, a mule stamped and shook flies from its ears. Ahead, the Shaper pressed a palm to the reset stone and spent again. The line moved by inches. The Wayfinder kept tugging south.

Sable calculated. The east spur would cost time they did not have. Waiting behind three grain carts would also cost time they did not have. Activating a Communication charm to report the delay would cost a level, require a justification, and reach an office that had omitted cash from the kit and called a dismantled crossing manageable. Another Wayfinder use would cost two more and tell her only that Kem continued to move away while she stood in a queue.

Her preparation had accounted for weights, signatures, schedules, and the possibility of immediate bodily harm. It had not accounted for the road's refusal to care about any of those.

"Chael," she said. "Lenn. Can you move stone."

Chael looked at the wall. "Yes."

Lenn looked at the wall the way he looked at the field manual when searching for a page that did not exist. "I believe so."

"Good. We will help."

The woman on the wagon tongue studied Sable for a moment, then jerked her chin toward the channel. "Get in, then. If the next cart sits till noon I want it to be because the work's bad, not because you lot stood there thinking about it."

The water in the cut was only calf deep, but it ran fast where the retaining stones had come loose. Chael climbed down first, planted himself wide, and started taking rock from the laborers on the bank. Lenn followed with the rigid bravery of a man who would rather have received a diagram. Sable handed Marris the field log, stripped off her gloves, and went in after them because ranking six people from dry ground while they carried stone for her seemed likely to end the wrong part of her life.

The first rock nearly took her balance with it. Its weight was not the abstract kind recorded on manifests. It dragged, scraped, shifted against her palms, and taught her more in one wet second than the Council's route packet had managed in four pages.

Kess and Tarrell stayed on the bank at first. Then the woman with the ledger board pointed at the loose end of a plank and said, "If you've got hands, use them." Kess did. Tarrell did after a visible conversation with himself. Marris took names and crossings because it was the only job no one had to explain twice.

For forty minutes the retrieval team became unpaid district labor. The Shaper seated stone. Chael and Lenn hauled. Sable learned which muscles objected to water current and which objected to being asked anything at all. Kess slipped once and barked her shin hard enough to sit down in the mud. Tarrell dropped his end of the plank when a wagon wheel jolted sideways, then apologized to the plank.

The woman on the wagon tongue ran the line with ruthless fairness. One grain cart, then another, then the team on foot, then the third cart. No one cut because of a seal. No one bowed because of 300. A child riding sacks of meal watched Sable stagger up the far bank with her hem soaked to the knee and asked his father, not quietly, whether priests were supposed to do roadwork.

Sable would have corrected the category if she had not been busy getting air back.

When they finally crossed, the woman held out the courtesy notice again. A strip of mud had dried across the Council stamp.

"Take it to the clerk if you care about repayment," she said. "Or don't. We were fixing the road either way."

Sable took the paper. "What is the nearest settlement south."

"Depends what you call a settlement." The woman pointed with the edge of her board. "You keep on this road another three hours you'll hit Low Bridge and whatever passes for an inn there. If you're looking for a young man in clothes too good for his judgment, he came through yesterday with a woman who looked like she could sleep in a ditch and wake up winning the argument."

The Wayfinder pulled south-southeast. The target was still moving.

"Did he ask for supplies."

"He paid for bread and walked out before it cooled."

Kess said, very quietly, "He paid."

The woman heard her anyway. "That surprises you."

"We were issued paperwork," Tarrell said.

"Then I hope it eats."

No one answered that. There was too much of it to answer.

They walked on because the target was still moving and the day had already consumed more of itself than Sable had authorized.

By then the road had changed shape in her mind. It was no longer the clean southern line she had marked in red over breakfast. It was a machine for converting administrative confidence into sweat, delay, and raw skin. Kess limped. Lenn's shoulders had opened where the straps rubbed. Tarrell's single notebook had taken splash damage and curled at the edges. Sable's skirt dried stiff with channel mud as she walked.

At the next halt, she revised the schedule again. Low Bridge, if the woman had judged correctly, was still farther than the Council packet's remaining daylight assumptions. Reaching it before dark would require a pace that Kess could not keep and Lenn should not. The first approved overnight point on the map lay another four kilometers beyond that and had been described as a sheltered administrative layby, which now sounded less like information than an accusation.

"We're stopping before the layby," she said.

Tarrell pushed damp hair out of his face. "That is an unapproved overnight adjustment."

"Yes."

"Should I note the reason as road obstruction."

"Note the reason as the road existing."

Marris wrote it down exactly that way before Sable could improve it.

The camp they found was not a camp so much as a patch of scrub beyond the road where wagon ruts widened into hard ground and an old fire ring suggested other failures of planning. Chael gathered fallen wood. Lenn unpacked the medical kit with reverence and discovered that most of the bandages had been packed beneath the metal instruments instead of above them. Kess removed her boots and hissed through her teeth at the state of her heels. Tarrell stood in the middle of the clearing with his notebook and looked as though someone had assigned him weather personally.

Sable took stock because stock was what she knew how to take. One ration pack had split at the wax seam during the channel crossing. One bedroll was damp. Kess's heels were abraded but still usable. Kess's shin was swelling. Lenn had two raw grooves above the collarbone and one blister at the base of his thumb from stone handling. Chael had a scrape across the wrist and no interest in discussing it. Tarrell had developed a cough after swallowing runoff and kept insisting it was minor, which made it almost certainly real.

"Boots off," Sable said.

Tarrell stared at her.

"All of you. I am not discovering tomorrow morning that tonight's avoidable damage has become tomorrow's classification problem."

That got the boots off.

The sight of five level-300 feet after one day on the road would have improved a number of sermons if sermons had possessed the necessary honesty. Kess's heel skin had peeled open in two wet crescents. Marris had rubbed one little toe bloody because she had not trimmed the nail square. Lenn's socks were damp from the channel and had already begun abrading the back of both ankles. Tarrell, whose shoes had clearly been selected by a man who believed in floors, had blisters rising under the ball of each foot like accusations.

Lenn cleaned what he could under Sable's direction. He held the forceps the way he held the field manual, with too much respect and not enough ease, but his hands remained steady. Chael got a fire going on the third attempt using dry grass, bark scrap, and language that suggested the records furnace had not been an entirely silent education. Marris portioned rations. Tarrell recorded inventory losses until Sable took the notebook away from him and told him to swallow food before he swallowed more paper.

By the time the light started going, the camp had acquired the minimum qualities of continuance. Bedrolls were unrolled. The fire held. Feet were wrapped, water boiled, and a watch rotation improvised. South still hummed inside Sable's bones.

Only then did the packet reassert itself.

The blank Form 7-Cs were in the outer compartment of her pack, still dry. Sable spread one on her knee while the others ate and stared at the printed headings.

DATE OF EXPENDITURE.

LOCATION OF EXPENDITURE.

AUTHORIZING OFFICER.

NATURE OF THREAT.

JUSTIFICATION OF PERMANENT LEVEL LOSS.

MINIMUM 200 WORDS.

Tarrell leaned over before she could stop him. "Should I prepare a clean copy."

"No."

"Your handwriting is legible under normal office conditions," he said carefully. "The current surface is less ideal."

"That remains my burden."

He sat back, chastened not by the words but by the fact that she had said them while looking at a campfire.

Sable wrote the easy parts first: the date, the gate departure, her name under authorizing officer, and the expenditure amount of two levels. She paused at *Nature of Threat*.

What had the threat been, precisely? They had risked failing to locate him. They had risked failing to begin at all. They had risked sending six untrained citizens into open country with no more guidance than a hand-drawn route and doctrine. None of it sounded dangerous enough for the form, and yet without the Wayfinder they would have left the gate already slower and stupider than the person they were trying to catch.

She wrote: *Target mobility combined with absence of trained tracking capacity within assigned party.*

It was true. It was also nothing like the truth of the charm hitting her body, the way direction had entered her like a blade of cold metal and stayed there.

Around the fire, the others had become quieter. Kess was holding her wrapped heel above the ground with both hands. Lenn kept checking the bandage roll as if more material might appear by scrutiny. Chael ate in the calm, efficient manner of a man whose body had already accepted the day as fact. Marris had the field log open beside her bowl and was copying the afternoon delay into a cleaner hand than exhaustion deserved.

Sable resumed writing.

*Immediate deployment was necessary to establish pursuit vector before further loss of distance. Delay pending full documentation would have increased separation between retrieval team and target by an operationally unacceptable margin. No lower-cost alternative was available at point of departure. The assigned team lacked practical field knowledge, visual tracking experience, and familiarity with nonurban navigation conditions.*

She stopped at one hundred and three words.

The form wanted nearly twice that. The form presumed that a permanent injury to the self could become more reasonable if described at greater length.

Tarrell, from the far side of the fire, said, "If you require additional language, the packet's doctrinal appendix suggests several approved phrasing structures."

Sable looked up.

"You memorized the appendix."

"Only the first page," he said. "The second page appeared repetitive."

That was the nearest thing to humor the road had produced since morning. It passed through the group in exhausted forms. Kess made a sound that might have been a laugh if her feet hurt less. Even Marris lifted her head.

"Recite it," Sable said.

Tarrell blinked. "Recite."

"Yes."

He did, because Hoarded training had turned obedience into a reflex long before any of them began questioning the uses of it.

"When evaluating justified expenditure, the reviewing authority shall consider proportionality, institutional necessity, availability of lower-cost alternatives, and maintenance of doctrinal confidence among observing parties."

"There," Sable said, and wrote down the words that could survive an audit.

She completed the report because she had signed for the irregularities and because the Council would notice missing paper before it noticed missing people. Then she took out the route schedule, crossed off Low Bridge, crossed off the sheltered administrative layby, and rewrote tomorrow from the ground up.

Chael watched her from across the fire. "You going to sleep."

"Eventually."

"You should rotate the packs again first. Kess won't make another day with that heel if she keeps the same weight."

Sable looked at the packs. He was right. Of course he was right. The road had spent the day appointing authorities she had not anticipated.

"Thank you," she said.

Chael shrugged once. "It's a pack."

They redistributed in silence. A little weight off Kess. Some off Tarrell, who had tried to carry embarrassment and notebook and had capacity for only two. More onto Chael, who accepted it without drama. Sable took additional rations onto her own back because she had led them into a queue with nothing to trade but labor and that fact felt measurable.

When the others finally lay down, she remained by the fire with the finished Form 7-C drying beside her knee.

At the bottom was a final box: *LESSONS FOR FUTURE DEPLOYMENT.*

Sable held the pencil over the line for a long time.

The Council had issued six charm cases and no practical money. It had provided an emergency provision that still required writing before survival. It had called a dismantled crossing manageable and assumed the road would pause for a seal. She had believed more of that packet than she should have, and the cost had come due in mud, lost time, torn skin, and the look on Kess's face when the heel wrap came off.

She wrote: *Initial operating assumptions require revision.*

She folded the form, put it back in the case with the Wayfinder, and listened to the southward pull continuing through her body while the fire settled and the team slept badly around her.
