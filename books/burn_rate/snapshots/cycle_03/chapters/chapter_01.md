# Chapter 1

The farmer's shin was broken in two places. Bone showed through the split skin above his left ankle. Infection had already climbed from the wound, a band of heat and redness running up the calf, the flesh shiny with fluid that smelled sweet and wrong. Three days, his wife had said. A wall collapse in the grain shed. Six hours under timber and stone before anyone got him out, and the settlement had nothing better than boiled rags and willow-bark paste.

Vel knelt beside the cot on the packed-earth floor and set one hand above the break, one below. Hael made a noise through his teeth but held still. Forty, maybe. Hands built by soil and lumber, nails cracked dark with ground-in dirt.

"Still," she said. "Don't move the leg."

The ability came when she reached for it. The Ledger shifted. Behind her sternum, something changed weight. Thirty-three became thirty-two.

Heat moved from her palms into the ruined leg. Damage resolved itself under her hands: two slant fractures through the tibia, splinters loose in the swollen tissue, infection gone deeper than she'd judged. It had reached the marrow. It had reached the exposed bone.

"Shit," she said.

Hael's wife jerked her head up from the window. "What?"

Maret had been wringing the same cloth since Vel arrived. Her knuckles stayed white around it. Missed meals had pared her down. The dress she wore had been let out once and taken in again, the seams visible where somebody had redone the stitches by hand. That morning she had offered Vel bread anyway, and it had been the best thing in the house.

"It's deeper than it looked. I'm pushing again."

"Does that cost you more?"

Vel put both hands flat against the bone and didn't answer.

Thirty-two became thirty-one.

The infection resisted with the blind stubbornness of anything that had been eating flesh for three days. Vel drove through it. Bacteria died in hot waves. Swelling dropped under her hands. Redness drained back down the calf. Fracture lines knit. Splinters pulled inward and fused. By the time she let go, Hael's breathing had gone from measured pain to startled relief.

Her hands shook when she sat back. That tremor always came after ability use, the body's way of registering permanent loss. She wiped her palms on her trousers and looked at the leg.

The leg was functional now, scarred but usable. He'd limp for a week and ache longer than that, but he would walk. He would work. He would haul water from the well and brace his own roof the next time it tried to kill him. The settlement needed that more than it needed almost anything.

"That's it?" Hael said.

"That's it. Stay off it today. Tomorrow you can move around. Nothing heavy for a week."

He touched the scar, pressed too hard, winced, and pressed again. Gratitude came over his face slowly, as if pain had to clear room for it. Vel wanted out of the house before it finished landing.

Maret crossed to the cot and laid a hand on her husband's forehead, checking for fever. The trembling hadn't left her yet. When she caught Vel looking, she tucked the hand behind her back.

"What do we owe you?"

"Whatever you can manage."

Maret went to the tin over the stove. With the care of someone who knew the number before she opened it, she counted out seven marks. The marks made a thin sound. It was thin money. Then she brought down a jar of preserved cherries, cloudy glass, fruit still a hard dark red.

"The cherries are good," Maret said. "I put them up last autumn, before the —" Her mouth closed. "They're good."

It had all been before the fever, before eleven people died and the Hoarded sent no one, before the grain contract got cut down to match the settlement's new usefulness. Everybody in the room knew the sentence she had stopped inside.

Vel took the marks and the jar. "Thank you."

Her pack waited by the door, canvas worn soft, straps mended with cord and old leather. Vel checked the main pocket from habit and stepped out into late spring sun.

Turned earth and hedgerow bloom rode the air. Twenty houses sat around a well with a crumbling stone lip and a winch held together by rope and a bent nail. South of the houses, fields ran toward an untrimmed tree line. Wheat and barley had been planted in alternating strips, but whole patches had failed where the ground had gone mean or the workers had gone missing.

The grain shed stood half-collapsed on the edge of the road. Hael's wall had folded inward. Roof beams split along the grain. Nobody here had the levels to Shape it back together. Nobody had the spare labor to rebuild it by hand. Their grain sat in a lean-to behind the meeting hall instead, open to damp and rats. Vel had seen settlements die in increments like that.

Kids played by the well with a length of rope, tossing it back and forth after the game had already gone stale. One stared. One waved. Vel raised a hand back. Both had the same narrow shoulders Maret did. Farther down the road, a woman reset a fence post with a flat stone because the settlement's only hammer had broken and nobody had marks for another.

The Hoarded had once kept this place upright with Fertility Charms and a grain contract mean enough to be useful. Rust-fever took eleven people in six weeks. The next contract came back thinner and brought no extra labor or timber. No one from the Vault came to look at the caved-in shed or the wet grain slumping under canvas.

Vel sat on the low stone wall by the well and ate one of the cherries. It was tart, brandy maybe, or something trying its best to be brandy. Maret had put up cherries before the fever and traded the jar for a leg. Syrup stuck to Vel's thumb.

Thirty-one levels remained.

The number lived behind her sternum with the steadiness of an old injury. At three hundred she had barely noticed it. At two hundred it had been background. At one hundred she had started counting on purpose. Below fifty the count kept time with her pulse.

Forty-one years of spending had brought her here through healing jobs, fights, shelter work, flood rescues, fires, bones, and people dragged out of water or rubble because nobody else would spend on them. The hard part still lay ahead, south and eight years late.

Vel had meant to use one level here, set the bone, clear the infection, repair the tissue, and get back on the road. At thirty-three, one level could still be filed under margin. At thirty-one, it sat in the account like a missing tooth.

Her hands were still shaking. They would stop in an hour.

---

The settlement gave her a room at the back of the meeting hall, really a storeroom with the sacks shoved out and a cot dragged in. Burlap dust hung in the mattress. The window never closed all the way. Olla had said the north trader might come through before dark, and this place sat on the south grain loop out of Cairn Hill. If news broke near the Vault in the morning, it hit Breck's yard by noon and kept moving with whichever trader bought feed there. Vel had taken the room instead of starting for the next village with one level missing from her plan because this was one of the first poor places south to hear rich people panic.

Supplies spread across the floorboards: water skin, dried beans, knife, needle kit, two rolls of bandage. Vel knew the pack by weight already. The other count mattered more.

She was down to thirty-one now. Breck's yard, the road beyond it, and anything that went bad on the way would have to come out of that.

Each night for eight years she had run through what those levels could still buy. Healing came first. Three good applications, maybe four, before the ability degraded into something fit only for shallow cuts and fevers caught early. A few defensive tools remained on the combat side: Redirect, Perception, a short Conceal if she needed half a minute to disappear. Utility had held up better. Endurance still covered the long marches. Nightsight and Weatherskin remained. She could still travel. Carelessness would strip the rest off her.

What remained narrowed everything. It left her with a kit that still held the expensive tools and no room for mistakes.

Eight years ago she had watched a door open at zero and Maren go through it. Since then every private calculation had bent toward the Scour. Its location was clear enough. Route notes sat in her pack. What waited there was clear too. Zero was passage.

The math remained ugly.

One person reaching zero could trigger the threshold. That much she had seen. The crossing itself had been brief and ugly, a flicker more than an opening. To hold it steady, the descent needed more force than one exhausted body could produce: two people spending down together, a cascade, enough draw to keep the door open long enough for a clean crossing. Her side of the equation was already thin. The other side needed height.

What she needed was a partner who started far above her and was willing to burn everything.

That was the impossible part.

Years had gone into careful questions in settlements, in Spent Towns, at trader fires, at the edges of the Margins where old explorers still remembered names. Any Hoarded defectors? Any lordlings missing off a north road? Anyone at two-fifty or higher who had walked out and stayed out? The answer had always been no or something close enough to no to kill a week. The Hoarded did not leave. They rotted in place and called it discipline.

Time had narrowed while she searched. Every job took levels. Every emergency took levels. Every stop where she was the only person within a day's walk who could do the hard thing took levels. At thirty-three she had still had room to absorb the world. At thirty-one, the world was beginning to eat the plan faster than she could protect it. Below twenty, her side of the cascade would fail.

Now mattered more than a better plan.

If Sorren brought nothing tonight, she would leave at first light for the trading post two days north and keep listening there. But Cairn Hill was better than listening blind. Breck's yard sat where the Vault road, the east ferry road, and the south grain road still touched each other before separating into lies. Couriers changed horses there. House servants got drunk there. Stable boys watched mud dry on wheels and learned more than their employers meant them to. A last confirmed sighting in Breck's yard was worth weeks of secondhand stories from trader fires farther out.

Vel had just cinched the pack closed when Olla knocked on the open frame. She held herself in the doorway with both hands because the palsy got worse when weather turned.

"Rain before dark," Olla said. "We've got three grain sacks taking damp. If you've still got enough of yourself left to lift one, I could use the arm."

Vel stood. "I've got the arm."

Behind the hall, the lean-to smelled of wet straw, old mice, and the slow spoil of grain that should have been under a proper roof. Canvas hung low on one side where the rope had stretched out. Water already sat in the dip.

Olla handed Vel a bent iron hinge somebody had turned into a pry bar. "Get those boards under the back row first. If the bottoms go, we may as well hand the whole harvest to the rats."

They worked without speed. Vel levered sacks high enough for Olla to shove broken fence rails under them, then shifted the next. Grain weight dragged at her shoulders. Her hands had mostly stopped shaking, but not all the way. Every time she braced and lifted, she could feel the place where the two lost levels had gone. It wasn't pain. It wasn't weakness exactly. It was subtraction.

Olla watched her over one of the sacks. "You're staying just for Sorren, then."

"For the road he brings."

"Same thing tonight." Olla pushed a rail into place with the toe of her boot. "Breck loads him last on market days. Sorren buys lamp oil, salt, and whatever gossip he can flip south for free. If a courier came through Cairn Hill in a hurry, Sorren'll have heard before the horse cooled."

"Breck's yard still the first change point out of the Vault road?"

"Only real one before the east fork. Water, spare tack, enough oats to rob a house servant without apology. If somebody blows a horse and keeps going, that's where they do it."

Vel set down another sack. "And if somebody wants to ask after a missing rich boy."

Olla glanced at her, sharp despite the tremor in her hands. "That too."

Maret came around the corner carrying two warped planks against her hip. Her children trailed after her with armfuls of kindling and the solemn concentration of kids doing grown work because there was nobody else to do it. Maret lowered the planks beside the lean-to and looked at the sacks, then at Vel.

"He came on that leg?" she asked.

"Not far," Vel said.

"Far enough." Maret wiped her wrist across her forehead. "If Sorren's got nails on the cart, tell Olla before he sees me. She'll get a fairer price."

"I always get a fairer price," Olla said.

"You get a louder argument."

That was close enough to a joke that one of the children smiled before remembering not to. Vel slid the planks under the last row. The ground back there had already gone soft. Another rain like tonight's and half the sacks would start turning from the bottom up.

When the work was done, Olla stood with both hands on her hips and looked at the lean-to as if order alone might keep it standing.

"That buys us a night," she said.

"Maybe."

"Confidence like that is why people pay for your company."

Vel left the pry bar by the wall. "People pay because I don't talk much."

Olla snorted. "That too."

They walked back around the hall together. The south road showed a long stripe of late light between hedges. Vel looked at it once out of habit. The plan in her head still ran south. The day did not.

Olla slowed at the pump and dipped one hand in the cold water, letting the tremor shake itself out. "If Sorren has heard something real, don't waste time asking what people think it means. Ask who saw what, where the horse changed, and which yard boy got stepped on. Meaning goes bad first."

Vel nodded.

"And if it's only rumor?"

"Then you lost half a day and a room with bad bedding."

"I've lost more for less."

By the time the light in the window had gone gold, Sorren's cart had come into the yard.

---

Vel stayed for supper because that was what she had been waiting for.

The meal was boiled grain with root vegetables and enough rosemary to disguise how thin it all was. Four settlement adults sat with her at the meeting-hall table. Sorren took the fifth place and set his spoon down like a man already tired of holding it.

He looked the part of a route trader. He was compact and fifty-something, with one shoulder drawn a little high from decades of leading a loaded cart with the same arm. Dust sat in the lines of his neck. His mule looked no fresher than he did.

Road talk opened the meal: crop failures, mud at the eastern fork, which settlements still had lamp oil. Olla handled most of it, walking him back up the road in useful order by asking what Breck charged for horse water, whether the east ferry still took carts after dark, and which Cairn Hill smith was drunk enough to sell nails straight from the back room.

"The eastern road still passable?" Olla asked.

"Passable enough," Sorren said. "Mud past the fork. They had rain."

"And Breck?"

"Charging triple for oats and pretending surprise anybody objected."

"He usually saves that for funerals and harvest."

Sorren gave a dry little breath through his nose. "Vault courier hit the yard at midday blowing a horse to foam. Whole place stopped moving long enough to listen."

Vel put her spoon down. "What did he say?"

Sorren looked at her over the bowl. He heard the speed in the question and filed it away. "Said a Hoarded lord had walked out."

Nobody spoke for a beat.

"Walked out," Olla said. "Out of the Vault?"

"That's the report."

"What does that mean?"

"Means he used an ability at the gate. First one in his life, if you believe the courier. Blew stone loose in the gate hall and walked through the dust." Sorren took another bite, chewed, swallowed. "House Aldraste. The son."

"The preservation house," Olla said. "Three hundred for six generations."

"Six that they'll admit to."

"How long ago?" Vel asked.

Sorren's eyes came back to her again. The question had come too fast, with too much interest in it. He heard that too.

"Today," he said. "Morning, maybe. Noon by the time it hit Cairn Hill. The courier changed horses in Breck's yard and kept going before the foam had run off the saddle leather."

The room stayed quiet in a different way now, with less disbelief in it and more rearrangement.

"Last clean sighting?" Vel asked.

"Breck's yard," Sorren said. "After that it turns into mouths."

"And after?" Vel asked. "Which road?"

Sorren lifted one shoulder. "Depends who was lying. Courier shouted north. A carter swore he heard east. One of Breck's boys says two house riders came after and asked whether anybody soft-handed had gone toward the ferry road. Breck says the house man didn't know and picked directions that sounded expensive." The spoon tapped once against the rim. "What everyone agrees on is that the boy burned levels doing it. Ten, twelve in the first stretch. Maybe more. And every fool in Cairn Hill started calculating what a Hoarded runaway might be worth."

"Worth to who?" Olla asked.

"To the Hoarded if you hand him back. To the rest of us if you hide him just to spite them. Breck sold feed to two riders before I left, and another man was already asking how much reward a house name was worth if delivered with both hands tied." Sorren scraped the bowl clean. "Fresh news turns a road ugly fast."

Vel said nothing.

Olla leaned back in her chair. "If it's true."

"If it's true," Sorren agreed. "Could still be courier drama. House Aldraste trying to make a smaller scandal look like a larger one so the small facts disappear inside it."

"What small facts?"

"Maybe he didn't walk out. Maybe somebody carried him. Maybe he got hurt. Maybe he got farther than they want people thinking. Maybe he got ten miles and they're calling it two so nobody asks why he had that much road." Sorren shrugged. "All I know is the courier was riding like he'd been promised his own hide back if he was quick."

That beat the usual smoke. There was a courier behind it, a house name, a last verified place, and riders already moving.

"Why the interest?" Sorren asked Vel.

"Not often you hear about a Hoarded man leaving on purpose."

"Not often you hear about one doing anything on purpose." He tapped the spoon one last time and set it down for good. "If he is out there, he won't last clean. Boy's never used an ability before today. He'll waste levels, panic, and every house rider north of here will smell it."

The conversation slid back toward lamp oil and wheel grease. Vel let it go. Her meal sat finished in front of her and she didn't remember eating half of it.

The Aldraste boy had what she needed. He also had half the north road looking for him already.

Breck's yard was still the best move because it was the last place where the story had bodies attached to it: Breck, the yard boys, the courier's blown horse, the men who bought feed in a hurry. By the time the rumor reached the next settlement it would be all direction and no mud.

If Sorren had heard by supper, house riders had heard sooner. By morning every trader between here and the Vault would be carrying a version of the story, and every version would pull the trail a different way.

Vel thanked Olla for the meal and the rosemary. She thanked Sorren for the road talk and followed him outside before he could climb onto the cart.

Evening had gone cool. The yard sat blue with last light. Sorren's mule stood hipshot between the shafts, chewing with the kind of patient contempt animals reserved for human trouble.

Sorren set his bowl on the cart rail and started checking the rear lashings. "You've got another question."

"Same one smaller."

"That usually costs extra."

Vel took one of Maret's marks from her pocket and set it on the rail. The coin looked mean in the dusk.

Sorren glanced at it. "Road's gone up today."

"Then say what it buys."

He took the mark and thumbed it once against his knuckle before pocketing it. "Courier came in on a chestnut with white stone dust ground into the knees and a cut under the girth. Vault road dust, not quarry dust. He wanted a fresh horse and the Aldraste posting rider. Didn't get all the way down before he started shouting. Breck's youngest boy caught the bridle and heard the family name plain."

"The boy heard north?"

"The boy heard the courier shout north. An hour later two riders out of the same house came asking whether anyone had seen a soft-handed young man take the east ferry road. Breck swears they were fishing, not tracking. Could be true. Breck lies for money, but he lies carefully."

"The yard boy. What's his name?"

"Jemin."

"Will he remember?"

"He remembers every horse that bites him and every man who tips badly. If the runaway came through there on foot, Jemin will remember the boots, the mud, whether he was limping, whether he bought food, whether he looked like he knew one road from another. Breck will remember who paid. Jemin will remember what happened."

Vel kept her face still. "How many riders were moving when you left?"

"Two on house money for certain. Another three men talking bounty over their feed. By dawn there'll be more. That's your trouble. Breck's yard is the right place because the trail still touches the ground there. It's also the wrong place because everybody knows that."

"Any sign the runaway was hurt?"

"Only sign is the level burn." Sorren tightened the last lashing. "If he'd never spent before today and dropped ten or twelve in the first stretch, he'll be shaking by now. Maybe sick. Maybe half blind with panic. Maybe smarter than all of them and already gone where a horse can't follow. But if you want anything truer than rumor, you want Breck's yard before the third retelling."

Vel looked past him toward the north road darkening between the fields.

Sorren swung himself onto the cart step and gathered the reins. "Go early if you're going. By noon it won't be a yard anymore."

Back in the storeroom, six marks sat in her palm and one had gone into road talk. She set the rest beside the cot and pulled the journal from her pack.

The cover was soft leather with worn edges and a spine restitched with thread that did not match. The pages were thick enough for weather and crowded with two hands. One was small and precise, brown with age. The other was looser and quicker, layered in later over the first. They held distances, water sources, settlement counts, and warnings about ground that looked stable until it wasn't. On one southern route the older note said *passable in dry season.* The later hand had answered beneath it: *barely, and the creek crossing will eat a day if you're loaded.*

Vel turned pages carefully. The journal made her careful in a way almost nothing else did.

The southern routes took the most space, page after page of paths toward the Margins and the Scour. The notes were old now. Settlements moved. Roads washed out. People died and took their landmarks with them. The bones of the route still held.

Near the back, the quicker hand cut across a mileage correction in the margin: *not five miles. seven. Vel guesses short when she's hungry.* The words leaned downhill as if written on a bent knee. A brown thumb smear crossed the edge of the note where the page had once been turned with greasy hands.

Vel could see the night it came from without trying. Wet wool steamed by a fire that wouldn't draw right. Maren sat cross-legged with the journal propped against one thigh, hair half out of its tie, stealing the pencil back every time Vel tried to amend the distance in her own favor. Vel remembered burnt beans and damp leather, and she remembered Maren laughing with food still in her mouth and nearly choking on it.

Farther back, where the route narrowed toward the basin and the notes stopped being about weather and started being about the ground itself, Maren's hand crowded the page harder with reminders about water under the west shelf, wind off the basin bad enough to kill a fire, and shelter cut into black stone on the south side. One line ran half sideways across the margin because she'd written it in a hurry: *keep the journal dry this time.*

Maren had shoved the book into Vel's hands at the edge of the Scour with grit on her lips and blood worked into one knuckle. Vel remembered that more clearly than the sky. The door had opened white and wrong, dust lifting toward it instead of falling. Maren had turned once in that light, close enough for Vel to see the crack in her lower lip, and then the opening took her and the world sealed like nothing had ever been there.

North would come first. South had waited eight years already. But north had its own cost. Breck's yard by dawn or the track dissolved into bought stories, and bought stories always favored the people with better horses and more money to spend on other people's memories.

The runaway might refuse her. He might already be half caught. He might never have wanted zero in the first place. Worse, he might want it for some reason stupid enough to make him reckless with the only thing that made him useful. If the Hoarded reached him first, he went back into a clean room and never came out. If bounty men reached him first, he became a tied pair of hands sold north for house coin. If nobody reached him first, he could still burn himself down wrong and make the whole chance useless.

Vel wrapped the journal back in oilcloth and repacked for speed. She put the spare shirt at the bottom, bandages high, and the knife where her hand could find it without looking. She poured the cherries into a rag and left the empty jar by the wall. Glass on the north road was a fool's burden.

Before she cinched the straps, she opened the journal one more time and used a blank strip near the back for north-road notes she hated writing there: *Breck's yard. Jemin. Chestnut with white stone dust. House riders asking east. Start there.* Her hand looked blunt beside Maren's quicker slant. She blew on the ink, checked the old mileage table, and ran the north math one more time.

Eleven miles by the cart road to Cairn Hill. A little under ten if she cut the fallow strip east of the settlement, took the mill track through the alder stand, and forded the creek below the broken footbridge. The shorter line bought her hours and kept her out of sight if house men had already started asking after lone travelers. It also meant wet boots before sunrise, no shelter if the weather broke hard, and a bad ankle turn if she put a foot wrong in the creek stones. Wet feet were better than a dead lead.

Her thumb stayed on the basin page longer than it needed to. Maren was all over that sheet in ways nobody else would have seen: the hard downstroke on numbers when she was cold, the shelter note squeezed into the top margin because she never left enough room for the important thing until it arrived, the grease crescent from dried fish eaten sitting on their packs because the ground had been all thorn. Vel let the page go and shut the cover.

She lay down fully dressed but did not let herself go under. When the room thinned from black to iron gray, she got up, shouldered the pack, and crossed the hall.

Olla had fallen asleep in a chair by the cold stove with a shawl over her knees and yesterday's account slate in her lap. Vel slid two marks under the cup by Olla's elbow for the room and the meal, then took the heel of bread set out under a cloth on the table. Whether that had been left for morning or for her, she didn't check.

Outside, dew slicked the well rope. Vel filled the skin, cinched the cap, and turned north before the sky had decided on color. The fields smelled of wet dirt and bruised barley. The hum behind her sternum held at thirty-one, exact as ever. The journal rode flat against her back. Somewhere ahead, between Breck's yard and whatever road the rumor had spoiled next, a Hoarded boy was either escaping or being shaped into a story about escape. Vel put the settlement behind her and kept walking before daylight could make staying look reasonable.
