# Chapter 10

The morning after Vel told Kem about the threshold, she handed him Maren's journal because her ribs would not let her climb the shale cut with one arm wrapped across her left side and the other guarding the book. That alone changed the road.

He caught it against his palm with a startled grab that almost made Dren laugh. The cover was dark with years of thumb oil and rain, the corners repaired twice, once badly and once by someone patient enough to make the bad repair hold. Kem looked down at it as if she had passed him something alive.

"Don't bend the spine," Vel said. She was already turning sideways to take the slope, boots testing the loose stone before she trusted it. "And if you lose it, I'll spend the last twenty-two breaking your neck."

Dren snorted and shifted his pack higher with his good arm. "There. That's affection."

Kem opened to the page she had marked at dawn. Maren's handwriting ran slanted and impatient along the top margin. Vel's newer notes sat underneath in smaller blockier script, water marks and trader names and dates squeezed around the older writing without crossing it out.

"These circles?" he asked.

"Clean wells."

"The double slash."

"Washed-out road."

He looked again. "The underline twice."

Vel braced one hand against the shale when the left side of her chest bit hard enough to blur the light for a second. "Ground good enough to sleep on."

He nodded once and kept reading as he came after her, one hand on the rock, careful now with the journal. Four days earlier he would have kept asking until he had either pulled the whole logic loose or annoyed her into silence. That morning he only asked for what he needed to keep them moving. She answered. That was new too.

At the bottom of the cut, the path split around a dead poplar that had dropped half its bark and was still doing the work of a signpost out of spite.

Kem glanced from the tree to the page. "Left here."

"Yeah."

"Why mark a dead tree if trees die?"

"Because roads are lazier than trees," Dren said.

Vel took the journal back long enough to turn one page and push it at Kem again. "Because the wash keeps the ground bad on the right fork, and anything bad enough long enough gets written down."

He held the book while they crossed the wash. When the bank crumbled under Dren's boot and Dren swore and caught himself late, Kem passed the journal to Vel without looking up, reached out, and steadied the man's pack strap with his free hand. There was no ability flare and no instinct to solve it with levels. There was only weight and balance and a clumsy correction that worked well enough.

Vel noticed because she noticed everything that cost and everything that didn't.

By noon Dren's arm had seeped through the bandage again. They stopped in the shade of a split basalt outcrop where a trickle ran down black stone into a basin green with miner's moss. Vel crouched to peel the cloth away, and the smell that came off the wound was meat, old sweat, and the sweet edge of healing gone half wrong.

"That face isn't reassuring," Dren said.

"It's not for you."

Kem set his pack down and was already kneeling when she jerked her chin at the side pocket. "Blue roll. Tin of salve. Not that one. The cracked lid."

He found both without rummaging the pack into chaos. That was new as well. Before the threshold talk, she would have reached for the supplies herself rather than let him learn where anything lived. Now she could hear her own voice giving directions before she had decided whether she liked what it meant.

Dren watched that too. "You're telling him where you keep things. I might cry."

"Use the other arm if you do."

Kem handed her the salve. His hands had changed over the last weeks. They were still Hoarded hands in the bones, long-fingered and too fine, but sun and rope and stitching had worked the softness out of the palms. Yesterday he had put a needle through Dren's skin with those hands while trying not to throw up. Today he held the basin steady and did not look away.

"Does it need redoing?" he asked.

"No. It needs cleaning and luck."

"Good. I'm still short on technique."

"You noticed."

He made a face that would have been offended if pain and embarrassment had not gotten there first. Dren laughed, then regretted it because laughter shook the wounded arm and turned the laugh into a hiss.

Vel cleaned the stitches. Kem poured water when she told him to. Dren bit down on a strip of cloth and kept the rest of his language inside his teeth. When the worst of it was over, Vel packed fresh salve along the torn edges and wrapped the arm again.

Kem looked at the bandage, then at the journal where it rested by Vel's boot. "You marked the river twice on today's page."

Vel tied the knot with one hard pull. "Because there are two ways to get to it."

"A bridge and a ford."

"If the bridge is still there."

He waited. When she did not leave the answer buried, he looked up.

"Flood took it a few years back if the road talk is current," she said. "There might still be pilings. Traders sometimes cut east for the ford when the water drops."

Dren flexed his fingers experimentally and grunted at the result. "And if the water doesn't drop?"

"Then we stop pretending maps are promises."

Kem took that in without reaching for the next five questions. Vel found that more unsettling than the questions had been.

The road held that altered shape for four days. She told them where the next water sat before Dren asked. She pushed the journal toward Kem when she wanted Maren's older route marks checked against the ground in front of them. Once, when the south track split across a sheep meadow and the signpost had been sawn off for firewood, she only said, "Third line from the bottom. Read it," and let him choose. He chose the right branch. She said nothing after because there was nothing to correct.

On the second night she woke to the fire burned low and found Kem sitting first watch with the journal balanced on one knee and the ridge line in front of him matched to the page. He had not moved past the marked spread. He had not gone digging for whatever else the book kept. When he heard her breathing change, he held it out without turning.

"The notch on the west ridge," he said quietly. "Is that the one Maren says looks like a missing tooth."

Vel pushed herself up on one elbow. Her ribs complained immediately. "Yeah."

"Then we're farther south than I thought."

"You are."

He handed the journal back. "I didn't read ahead."

"I know."

The answer left them both still for a second. Then he said, "You should sleep another hour. Nothing's moving."

She should have argued on principle. Instead she lay back down and let him keep the dark a little longer.

By the fourth morning he had stopped treating every silence like a locked door. Vel had stopped using every practical answer like a concession. It was not peace. It was not safety. It was only a road rhythm that had not existed before the fire.

That morning she scratched a new line in Maren's journal while the camp ash still held a little heat.

"We're cutting west," she said.

Dren looked up from the strip of dried rabbit he was working between his teeth. "West is away from the river."

"Only for half a day."

"Why?"

"I need current route talk."

Kem watched her hand move across the margin. "From who?"

Vel capped the pencil. "Someone who used to hear the south before it went thin."

That was not much of an answer. It was more than she would have given a week ago. Kem let it sit. She noticed that too.

Four days after the grain-silo fight, the settlement looked the same.

From the ridge she could still read the old layout: stone-and-timber houses around the communal well, gardens on the south edge where the light held longest, the storehouse by the eastern road. Twelve years had changed details, not shape; the storehouse had grown a second story, and a fence cut across what used to be pasture, but the place itself had not moved an inch. Nothing here had been allowed to sag, split, or go to seed.

"How far?" Dren asked.

"Half an hour."

He shifted his pack and winced when the movement tugged his arm. "She knows we're coming?"

"No."

Kem said nothing. He had been watching Vel since sunrise, since she had redrawn the route in Maren's journal and hauled them off the road they had been riding for three weeks. Usually he would have spent the walk down prying at it. Today he kept the question in.

The switchback path was all gravel and runoff scars. Vel's feet remembered the bad places before the rest of her did: the second turn where the stone liked to roll, the patch of bare rock lower down that could throw a careless ankle, the place near the bottom where the hillside still smelled faintly of old goat shit whenever the sun got on it. Heat settled early in the valley. Below them the gardens gave off bean leaf, damp soil, and the bruised green smell of herb stems snapped by quick hands.

At the northern edge she saw the cart.

It sat outside the storehouse, clean-sided and too fine for local work. The hardware had the machined neatness that only the Hoarded thought looked trustworthy. A settlement trader would have oiled the boards dark, patched the joinery twice, and tied something useful under the seat. This one looked as if a policy had approved every screw.

Vel lifted a hand. Kem stopped. Dren had already narrowed his eyes at the cart.

"Hoarded," he said.

"Yeah."

"Collector or messenger?"

"Representative, I'd guess."

They cut wide through the south gardens, keeping bean frames and a row of early peas between themselves and the well. The settlement was too small for stealth to last. First sight still mattered.

Thessa stood by the well with a woman in Hoarded trade linen, the kind cut narrow through the sleeves so fabric would not bunch under account cuffs. A leather case was tucked under the woman's arm. She was saying, in the dry patient tone of someone who had filed whole villages into columns, that the bean shortfall could be logged as seasonal variance pending corrective compliance.

Then the words thinned.

Her mouth kept moving, but the sound ran out halfway through the sentence, and her gaze slid past Thessa's shoulder to fix on the air beside the well post. For three beats she stood there without blinking or shifting, lips parted, waiting on a thought that would not come back.

It was Stillness, three weeks south of the Vault, with quota language still in the woman's throat.

The lapse lasted only seconds. The representative blinked, found her place, and resumed the sentence as if weather had crossed between one word and the next. She made a note with the stylus from her case, and her hand had not moved during the blank gap.

Thessa's face gave nothing away. She had seen it before; Vel could tell from how little surprise there was in her.

Cold moved through Vel's hands, not fear but recognition. The Hoarded liked to speak of discipline as if it were a moral triumph. Here it was instead: a woman in good linen while the system ate pieces out of her between one quota and the next.

Kem had gone rigid beside Vel. His breathing changed.

"That's what my father does," he said quietly. "When he goes away in the middle of a sentence."

"I know."

"He looks exactly like that."

The representative closed her case, said she would return next month for updated figures, and headed back toward the cart with the careful steps of somebody carrying liquid close to the lip.

Thessa watched her go. Then she looked toward the bean frames and saw Vel.

Recognition hit first, and then the settlement version of Thessa slid over it: open face, easy hands, a voice made to calm neighbors and send traders away feeling seen. She said something quick to a woman passing with a bucket and came toward them.

The warmth held until she was close enough for Vel to see her eyes.

"Vel," she said. Then, after a beat that took in all three of them, "Fine. Inside. Put the packs down before one of you falls over."

"Thessa."

"Don't start in the lane." She had already turned toward the stone house at the northwest corner. It was the same house, the same narrow front step, the same patched shutter Vel had once promised to mend and never had. "Bring the wounded one. I've got water hot, or close enough. Bread too, if nobody insults it."

"You're very welcoming."

"I've had twelve years to practice." She kept walking. "Come on."

Vel followed. Kem came after her in silence. Dren's boots scraped gravel with the even drag that meant he was listening hard and saying nothing because the saying could wait.

---

Thessa's house smelled of dried thyme, old stone, and baking crust. Under the front window sat a table with four chairs, and shelves of labeled jars lined one wall above a cooking alcove blackened by long use. A broom hung by the door with the bristles up so they would dry straight. Nothing in the room was decorative unless you counted order.

Thessa moved through it without asking what anyone wanted. She set the kettle back on the fire, pulled cups from the shelf, and cut bread in thick unembarrassed slices. The knife went down beside the board, not thrown but firm. By the time she turned around, all three of them were waiting on her without meaning to.

Kem sat when she pointed. Dren took the wall by the door. Vel stayed standing until Thessa came at her with a hard look and the kettle in both hands.

"Sit down."

"I'm fine."

"You're limping on the left and carrying your ribs like they owe you money. Sit down."

Vel sat.

Thessa poured tea, set the cups out, shoved the bread to the middle of the table, and then saw the dark stain creeping through Dren's sleeve.

"No," she said. "Absolutely not. Come here."

Dren glanced at Vel, then pushed off the wall with a grimace and came to the table. "You're sweeter than you sound."

"And you're leaking on my floor." Thessa reached for his wrist. "Sit."

He sat because she made it sound less like a request than a structural fact.

She unwound the outer bandage far enough to inspect the stitches. Kem tried not to stare, failed, and stared anyway. Thessa's thumb pressed near the wound, just shy of cruel and well past gentle.

"Who did this?" she asked.

"I did," Kem said.

Thessa looked at him over Dren's arm. "You."

"Yes."

"On purpose."

"That seemed to be the idea."

Some unwilling amusement tugged at the corner of her mouth and vanished before it became anything generous. "It's ugly."

Dren said, "Arm's still on."

"That is the strongest argument for it." She peeled the bandage back a little more, inspected the angry red around the stitches, and reached for a squat jar from the shelf. "He'll keep it if you all stop pretending a road heals faster than a bed."

Vel took her tea. The cup was warm enough to sting her palm. For a few breaths the room held only the crackle from the hearth, the sound of Thessa rewrapping Dren's arm, and a child shouting somewhere outside about a runaway chicken.

Then Thessa tied the cloth off, released Dren, and finally sat across from Vel.

"You vanish for twelve years," she said, "and this is what walks through my door."

Dren lifted the freshly wrapped arm a fraction. "Door's still standing. That's a start."

Thessa ignored him and kept her eyes on Vel. "You're heading south."

"I need routes."

"Of course you do." Thessa took a sip of tea, set the cup down, and rose again for the map shelf before she had fully swallowed. "The old bridge is gone, or gone enough. Flood finished the center span three years back. Traders use a ford east of it if the water is low enough to be trusted, which it usually isn't. If the river's up, you'll find the bridge first and curse me from there."

"What about farther in?"

"Eat first." She nudged the bread toward Vel. "Mara baked that at dawn and she'll haunt me if I let it go stale on company."

"I'm not hungry."

"Then hold a piece in your hand and make me feel useful."

Vel took the bread because arguing with hospitality was a waste of ribs.

Thessa spread a cloth map over the table. Vel pulled Maren's journal from her pack by habit, opened it to the river pages, and set it half-turned toward Kem so he could line the older notes against Thessa's newer marks.

Thessa's eyes flicked down to the motion. Something small tightened around her mouth.

"You let him lean over that now," she said.

Vel frowned at the map. "He can read."

"That wasn't what I meant."

Kem kept his gaze on the page. That at least was tact, or the shape of it.

Thessa pressed the heel of her hand to the western edge of the cloth map. "After the river it gets thin. There are field camps, more Hoarded contracts, and fewer settlements willing to say they saw anyone. There is a dry creek bed south of the bridge that still carries storm water in spring. If you hit it, follow it only until noon. After that it twists you east and wastes half a day."

Vel tapped a penciled mark in Maren's margin. "The ridge path still open."

"Sometimes." Thessa reached over and marked the cloth with a charcoal stub. "Depends whether the wash ate the switchback this year."

Kem followed the exchange with one finger on the journal page. He did not ask for permission each time he moved the book. Vel noticed that too. So did Thessa.

"And south of that?" Dren asked.

"South of that I hear things and choose not to go find out if they are true."

"Why not?"

Thessa looked up at Vel.

"Because I live here now," she said. "Because I mend roofs and weed gardens and haul sacks and mean to keep enough of my number to stand up when winter gets ugly. Because I got tired of opening the door to people I loved and counting how much of them was still left. Pick whichever answer annoys you least."

Vel's jaw tightened. "I'm not asking you to come."

"No." Thessa's mouth twitched, not close to a smile. "You never ask plain. You show up with road dust on your cuffs and your plans already packed and pretend that isn't a form of asking."

Kem looked between them. Dren tore bread one-handed and watched the front window as if it might become useful.

Thessa's gaze shifted back to Kem and stayed there longer this time. She took in the pallor still clinging to him under the sunburn, the calluses on his palms, the way he sat like a man who had spent twenty-six years indoors and was only recently learning that chairs were not obligations.

"You're from the Vault."

"I was," Kem said. "I'm not, exactly. It's complicated."

"It usually is." She studied him another second. "What level?"

Vel answered first. "Not relevant."

"It's relevant to me."

Kem glanced at Vel once, then back to Thessa. "One-seventy-eight. That was this morning."

Thessa went still. Her own cup stopped halfway to her mouth. Vel could see her making the comparison whether she meant to or not. The gap between them was a hundred levels, more than a person like Thessa could spend in a lifetime of bad decisions and decent reasons.

"Oh," she said.

Then, flatter: "You went looking again."

"Thessa."

She set the cup down with care that looked one slip away from throwing it. "Don't put my name in your mouth like that fixes anything."

"I came for route information."

"And a map. And a meal you didn't plan to stay for. And whatever else you dragged in with you." She pressed her thumb against the table grain once, twice. "Fine. You want route information. South from here turns worse after the river. There are more Hoarded camps and more local men willing to sell your footsteps for winter salt. Does that help."

Vel reached for the journal again to mark the dry creek. Thessa's eyes followed her hand.

This time she really looked at the book.

She stared at the bent spine, the gut-thread repair at the corner, the dark smear where rain had once dried into the leather. Recognition crossed her face so fast it was almost ugly.

"You're carrying her pages."

Vel's fingers rested on the open spread.

"Maren's journal." Thessa touched the air above it and did not touch it. "Hallson's trader did that repair. Couldn't knot a cord to save his life, so she made him do it twice while you laughed at him." Her voice pulled tight. "You still have it."

Vel said nothing.

"You still have it," Thessa said again, softer and therefore worse. "You walked out of here twelve years ago. You heard from Barrens eight years ago that she crossed. Barrens. And you're still dragging her handwriting up and down the south road like the paper's going to answer back if you wear it thin enough."

"Thessa."

"No. Let me get this wrong if I need to." Her eyes stayed on the journal. "I found out from Barrens. Not from you. You were a ten-minute walk away and you sent the news through three other mouths because that was easier than knocking on my door and standing there while I heard it."

Vel took a breath too deep and pain hooked under the left ribs hard enough to make her hand close around the edge of the table. The room smelled of thyme and hot bread. Maren's notes lay open under Thessa's stare, cramped handwriting running up against fresh pencil marks and bread crumbs.

"I know whose journal it is," Vel said.

Thessa looked up at last. "Do you."

It was not really a question. It was an accusation with the grammar half stripped off it.

Kem's attention moved between them. "Vel said Maren found the threshold."

Thessa turned to him so fast the chair legs scraped stone. "Five years ago she sat where you are. Same table. Same stupid good bread. She didn't start with Maren. She started with numbers."

Kem went still.

Thessa shoved on before anyone else could. "She asked if any Hoarded had run south. I said probably, people run from everything. She said no, not names, what numbers. What their Ledgers looked like. Who still had enough on them to keep spending after the river. Whether anybody above two-fifty had vanished angry enough to burn through the road. That is what she wanted from me."

Vel's voice went flat. "Enough."

"No." Thessa's warmth dropped clean out of her. "No, not enough. He gets to know you didn't just stumble into him out there. You were asking the arithmetic before you had his face."

Kem's hand tightened around the cup. "Before me."

Vel did not look at him. "Thessa is angry."

"I am," Thessa said. "I'm angry that you took my routes and her pages and the news I should have had from your mouth. I'm angry that you went south, came back lower every time, and expected the people who loved you to clap because somebody else's child kept breathing. I'm angry that five years ago you sat in my kitchen with that book open and asked me which Hoarded runaways still had enough left in them to matter."

"Stop talking around it," Kem said.

The words were quiet. That made the room tighter, not looser.

Vel looked at him then.

He had the same expression he wore when he was fitting one confusing fact against another and refusing to let either one slide out of place just because the picture would be kinder without it.

"Did you know," he asked, "that you were looking for someone like me."

The question was stripped down enough to hurt.

Vel wanted the smaller one. She wanted bridge, ford, dry creek, anything with ground under it.

"I knew I couldn't do the south alone."

Kem did not let her have the smaller answer. "That's not the same thing."

Thessa laughed once, raw and short. "No. It isn't."

Vel's grip tightened on the table. The wood edge dug into her palm. She could feel the old map marks under her fingers, the fresh charcoal from Thessa, the bent warmth of the cup she had barely touched.

"At first," she said, and hated how much air the two words needed, "yes."

No one moved.

Kem's eyes stayed on her face. "Because of the number."

"Yes."

He swallowed once. The movement in his throat looked painful. "And now."

Vel could feel every bad answer lined up behind her teeth. I don't know. It changed. You changed it. None of them would land clean, and anything clean here would be a lie anyway.

"Now you're here," she said.

He stared at her a second longer, as if waiting for the part she had not managed to force into the open. When it did not come, he set the cup down so carefully the ceramic made almost no sound.

"Fine," he said. "Then don't ask me for another level blind."

The words landed harder than if he had shouted. Dren looked away from the window at that, finally, and then back again because the front of the house had started to matter more than the inside.

Thessa wiped the heel of her hand under one eye, angry at the wetness there. "Later, when the road gets quiet, ask her what she'd have done if you'd walked out of that place at seventy-eight instead of one-seventy-eight. Ask that and watch her mouth."

Vel turned on her. "Don't."

"No. You don't get to choose the tidy version now."

Dren's head came up toward the front window. "We've got company."

No one moved for a beat.

"The Hoarded woman," he said. "Coming here now."

Thessa was on her feet before the sentence finished. She crossed to the window, looked once, and came back with the last of the softness gone out of her face.

"She does quota review once a month," she said. "Settlement reports. Traveler notes."

Vel stood. "Traveler notes."

Thessa looked at the bread, the cups, the map, the journal, and then at the front door as if she could already see the shape of what the representative would write.

"Three years ago they offered grain priority," she said. "Timber allotments. A healer twice a season. Medicine when the children got lung-fever. All I had to do was keep track of who passed through and where they were headed."

"You took it."

"Yes." There was no softness in it. "I took it. Winter stopped feeling like a knife at the door for a while. Roofs got fixed. People ate. I took it."

No one argued with her.

Through the window, the representative was already crossing the open ground toward the house. The document case was under her arm. In her free hand she carried a short relay scroll, the kind that could wake a station thirty miles away if the stylus touched it right.

Vel asked, "Does she know it's us?"

"She saw you by the well. Whether she understood it fast enough, I don't know." Thessa scrubbed a hand over her face. "Her lapses are worse now. Last month she lost nearly a minute in my kitchen while I was lying to her about barley totals. She stood there with her eyes gone and then asked me for the count again. I've been making bargains with a woman who's disappearing a piece at a time."

Her hand fell away from her mouth. "And the relay is already live. If she finds you in my front room, the bargain is dead. If I drag you out to her, the bargain is still dead and I get to help them do the writing. Grain goes. Timber goes. Maybe the healer too. So don't stand there acting like handing you over is the smart clean choice for me. It isn't cleaner. It's just meaner."

Kem rose. "Why help us then?"

Thessa looked at him with something close to pity and nothing close to comfort. "Because she saw you. Because the relay's warm. Because I'm done feeding road names to a woman who forgets her own mouth mid-sentence. Because if I have to lose the deal anyway, I'd rather lose it lying."

Dren was already reaching for his pack. "Back way?"

Thessa nodded at once. "Kitchen door. Out the lane, then west between my place and Garran's shed. Orchard after that. The irrigation ditch runs south about a mile before it empties into a dry creek. Stay under the bank if they scan."

She caught Vel's eye then, and the look had none of the public settlement warmth left in it.

"I can buy you a little time," she said. "Not much. If she asks, I had northbound grain men in for water and sent them on. If she asks twice, the lie gets worse. So move while I'm still good at it."

Vel folded the journal shut and slid it into her pack. Thessa watched the motion with a face that had gone flat from holding too much at once.

"Thessa," Vel said.

"Don't thank me."

That should have been the end of it. Instead Thessa's mouth tightened and she added, not looking away from Kem, "Ask her the seventy-eight question when you're three days south and too tired to pretend you forgot it."

Kem said nothing.

Vel had no answer that would not cost more time than they had. She went.

---

They left through the kitchen, past drying herbs and the chipped washbasin, into the narrow lane behind the houses. Sun hammered the clay. Before Vel hit the back gate, she heard the front latch lift and Thessa's voice swing bright and practiced through the house.

"Back already? I was just writing the barley count. No, nobody interesting. Three grain men from the north road, thirsty as cattle and twice as stupid."

The lie was warm, easy, and entirely irreversible.

Vel spent one level on a tight Perception sweep.

The world snapped sharper and hit her behind the eyes. The representative had reached the front room. The relay scroll was lit in her hand. Thessa was standing between the woman and the table, smiling with all her teeth and talking about northbound wagons that did not exist. Two settlement enforcers were already coming in fast from the east road, drawn by procedure, nerves, or the relay. Fifteen minutes, maybe less, before the search narrowed to directions and guesses.

Twenty-one.

They cut through the orchard. The trees had been charmed hard years ago and then left alone, so the branches sprawled low and ugly, fruit still green, irrigation channels sunk deep enough to hide a crouched body. Dren dropped into the ditch first. Kem followed, stooping to keep below the lip. Vel came last, boots sliding on clay split by old water lines.

"How long?" Dren asked.

"Quarter hour before they're pointed south. Maybe less if she stops forgetting the room she's in."

Kem kept moving. He did not look at Vel when he spoke. "I'll cover us."

"I know."

"Overlay first. Shield if they get close. Blur if they track." He said it to the ditch wall, to the dry roots sticking through it, to anything but her. "Eighteen if I keep it tight."

Dren said, "Then keep it tight."

Kem lifted both hands. The concealment settled over them in a fitted shell, light bending off the ditch lip, sound thinning, their movement smearing into heat shimmer and dust.

Seven levels.

It was not the wild excess he had spent at the start of the road. It was shaped work now, held close, calibrated to bodies and ditch walls and the distance they actually needed.

Vel dropped a second concealment over their tracks, one level only, enough to fog the prints in the clay and blur the broken edge where they had climbed in.

Twenty.

They moved south in a bent half-run. Dren favored the left arm harder now, keeping it pinned close to his ribs. Every bad step showed in his jaw. The ditch curved away from the orchard toward a dry creek.

Behind them, ability use prickled through the Perception residue still fading in Vel's skull. A scan passed over the orchard. Over the lane. Over the ditch. It went north first.

Thessa had bought them that.

Vel did not turn to look back. She could still hear the bright shape of the lie in her head.

Forty minutes south, Kem's concealment wavered.

"It's thinning," he said.

"How long?" Dren asked.

"Not long."

He shifted without waiting for Vel this time. A shield snapped around them so close the air pressed at Vel's skin. Five levels. Then the tracking blur, six more, washed over their movement and turned their outlines watery.

That made eighteen.

When he lowered his hands, his fingers ticked once against his palms. He closed them and kept them closed.

They came out of the ditch into a dry creek bed and kept going. Open ground spread ahead in scrub and brittle grass, with low hills lifting toward the river. No pursuit sat visibly behind them. That did not mean much. Visibility had never been the same thing as safety.

Kem glanced inward at his Ledger. "One-sixty."

Vel nodded. The number landed between them and stayed there.

Dren had the cloth map in his good hand, folded down to the charcoal marks Thessa had made at the table. When the trail split around a wash of broken stone, he took the left branch after one quick look.

Kem went with him at once.

Usually he would have checked with Vel, or asked for the journal, or looked back long enough to make it clear the road was still being chosen together. This time he stayed beside Dren and matched himself to the trader's pace.

Thessa's kitchen kept riding in Vel's head: bread torn open on the plate, Maren's journal spread wide, Barrens in Thessa's mouth, Kem saying because of the number. Thessa had lied for them anyway. Thessa had asked the meaner question and sent it south in their packs with the bread smell and the map charcoal and the image of that Hoarded woman standing blank in the middle of a sentence.

Ahead, Dren said, "Water by nightfall if the map's right."

Kem answered him without turning. "Good."

When the trail narrowed to single file, Dren kept the lead, Kem kept the middle, and Vel took the back without anyone naming the change out loud. The gap between Kem and Vel was only a few paces. It held anyway.

The hills lifted toward the river. Heat shook above the ground. Vel adjusted her pack over the sore line of her ribs and followed them south.
