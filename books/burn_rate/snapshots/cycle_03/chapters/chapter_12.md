# Chapter 12

Vel didn't sleep the first night south of the river. She sat with her back to a tree and her knees drawn up, ribs ticking every time she drifted, while Kem took second watch and failed to pretend he wasn't watching her back. The question from the crossing was still there with the rest of the camp gear. Was she teaching him how to decide what mattered, or teaching him into a shape she needed. He hadn't asked it again after dark. He didn't need to.

The river itself kept moving a few yards away, flat and black in the dark, as if it hadn't taken two hours out of three people who couldn't spare them. Kem's palms were rope-burned from the crossing. He kept flexing his hands when he thought nobody was looking, feeling out the rawness. Dren's boots had dried stiff with silt and his left sleeve kept brushing his side because the arm under it still wasn't right. Vel could feel every bend and twist in her cracked ribs when she shifted against the trunk. None of it was dramatic. It was just the body collecting interest.

What had changed on the bank was quieter than any of that. Kem had taken the smaller answer she gave him at the river and left the larger question alive. That made the silence sharper. If he'd pressed, she could have cut him off clean. Instead he watched what she told him not to spend on, watched what she spent anyway, and filed it somewhere behind his face for later.

At dawn Dren rolled his blanket with one hand and his teeth, swore once when the bad arm pulled, and stood. Kem doused the ash, checked the knots on the packs, and said nothing. Vel said nothing back. They walked into gray morning under trees already thinning at the edges.

By the second day south the forest had given way to scrub and then to open ground, and the soil was the color of chalk. It was exhausted chalk, the specific pale of ground that had been asked for everything it could give and then asked again. Vel had seen depleted farmland before, in settlements where Fertility Charms had been overworked for a generation. This was different in scale. The depletion went to the horizon, south and east and west, the fields marked by wooden stakes driven into ground too tired to hold them upright. Half the stakes leaned. Nobody had straightened them. Nobody had the levels to care about stakes.

Two days after the river, the land had changed in stages, first the forest thinning, then the soil lightening, then the structures getting worse. Now there was no pretending they were anywhere but the Margins.

The heat didn't help. Early summer in the south was dry and heavy, the kind that pulled moisture from skin and pack straps and road alike. Dren's canteen was half-empty by midday and he hadn't mentioned it. Kem had stopped reaching for Sustenance Charms weeks ago, one of the economies Vel had hammered into him, but she could see his hand drift toward his belt pouch and stop, drift and stop, the old reflex surfacing and being caught. He drank water and kept walking. That much she gave him.

The first settlement appeared around noon: a cluster of low buildings arranged around a central well, the structures showing the seams of cheap Shaping work. The walls met at angles that weren't quite right. Rooflines sagged where the original builder had run out of levels mid-construction. The Shaping itself had been competent. The specifications hadn't cared about the people living under them.

A woman sat outside the nearest building on a stool, shelling dried beans into a wooden bowl. She looked up when they approached. Her eyes went to Kem first, to his height, his hands, the boots that were battered now but still identifiably Vault-made. The beans kept clicking into the bowl while she measured him.

"Water?" Vel asked.

"Well's there." The woman nodded toward the cistern. "Thirty-two people drawing from it, so take what you need and move on."

"We're passing through," Vel said.

"That's what they all say."

Vel filled their canteens. Seven people were visible in the settlement. Nobody crossed the square without carrying something worth the steps. A man stopped at the well and hauled up one bucket, then stood with both hands on the rope a moment before he tried for the second. A girl dragged a sack of meal by its tied mouth instead of hoisting it. Vel recognized the pace because she'd worn it herself for years, before Kem, before she'd had someone else's levels to budget alongside her own.

The notice board was by the well. It was proper construction, Hoarded administrative hardwood with iron fittings, the kind of thing a district overseer would have had installed. The notices on it were layered deep, older ones buried under newer ones, the whole thing a geological record of bureaucratic attention paid to a place that had never received any other kind.

The top notice was current. Hoarded administrative stock, cream-colored paper:

NOTICE OF PRODUCTIVITY RECLASSIFICATION: MARGINS DISTRICT 7, SECTOR 14-B

Pursuant to Directive 1172 (amended), all residents of the above-designated sector are hereby classified under the revised Productivity Tier system:

TIER ONE (Levels 40+): Full daily output expected. 0.3 levels-per-annum standard rate. Interest on existing ability-debt accrues per schedule (Appendix C-7, Table 3, subsection ii).

TIER TWO (Levels 15–39): Reduced output. 0.15 levels-per-annum. Supplementary manual labor allocation may be assigned. Interest rate adjustment per Formula 9-B (revised).

TIER THREE (Levels 5–14): Maintenance category. Residents classified as Renewable Expenditure Units and may be reassigned to Development Opportunity programs at District Overseer discretion.

TIER FOUR (Levels 1–4): Non-productive. Account closure processing per Standard Protocol 6.

APPEALS: Reclassification appeals may be submitted to the Bureau of Expenditure Oversight, Northern Administrative Center, during standard business hours (first and third Wedgeday of each month, 10:00–14:00). Filing fee: 2 levels.

Vel read it twice. Once for the language, once for what it would do to a life.

"Renewable Expenditure Units," Kem said. He was reading over her shoulder. His voice had that careful, too-even quality it took on when something from the world outside the Vault didn't fit any framework he'd brought from home. "That means people."

"Renewable because they have children," Vel said. "Children are born at three hundred."

Kem's hand closed on the edge of the board. "And the filing fee. Two levels. If you're Tier Four, you've got one to four levels left."

"Half of everything you have," Vel said.

He stared at the notice: Appendix C, Table 3, subsection ii. His thumb dug into the board's edge until the wood gave a dry little squeal.

Dren hadn't stopped at the board. He was standing thirty feet down the road, watching south. His bad arm hung slightly forward, the way he carried it now. Kem's stitches had held, but the flesh underneath had healed tight and wrong, and Dren protected the arm like a thing he couldn't afford to break a second time.

"We moving?" he said.

Vel pulled the notice from the board. It tore along a nail hole. Underneath was an older one, its ink fading to brown:

DEVELOPMENT OPPORTUNITY PROGRAM: COHORT 47
Duration: 5-year rotating assignment (renewable)
Daily ability expenditure requirement: Per Tier classification
Compensation: Room, board, and 2% annual reduction in outstanding ability-debt balance

There was no reason given. Vel folded both notices and put them in her pack anyway.

***

They passed two more settlements by mid-afternoon. Each showed the same marks: cheap Shaping, depleted soil, careful people. The notice boards varied in age but not in content: Productivity Tiers, Expenditure classifications, debt schedules referencing appendices nobody here had ever seen. In one settlement, someone had written across the bottom of a notice in charcoal: *formula doesn't balance; asked district office; they said resubmit next quarter*. The handwriting was shaky. Tier Three, Vel guessed. Or Four.

In the third settlement, they found the labor operation.

It was visible from a quarter mile: a compound surrounded by a wall of Shaped stone, about eight feet high, the Shaping layered thick enough that the stone had that fused, seamless look of serious ability investment. Inside, the tops of low buildings were visible, and workers moved between them. They came and went through a single gate that a woman in her fifties controlled.

The enforcer sat on a chair by the gate with a ledger book in her lap and a clay cup on a crate beside her. When workers passed through, she checked names. When they came back in, she checked again.

"Debt work," Dren said. He'd seen it before. "Hoarded contract. Workers spend their daily quota, she marks it, end of the season the district office processes the reduction. In theory."

"In theory," Vel said.

"In theory."

A child sat against the compound's outer wall, in its shade. Maybe eight years old, small for the age, wrists no thicker than broom twigs. His face was flushed, not from the sun but from fever, the glassy heat of infection. An older girl sat beside him, twelve or thirteen, one palm on his forehead and the other braced in the dust. She did not look away when they came up.

Kem saw the child at the same time Vel did. His stride changed, a slight hitch, a redirection, his body deciding before his mind had caught up.

"Don't," Vel said, quietly enough that she wasn't sure whether she'd said it to him or to herself.

***

Kem knelt beside the child. The girl pulled her brother closer.

"He's got a fever," Kem said.

"I know he's got a fever." The girl looked at the way Kem had gone straight to his knees in the dust, as if a man with his voice and boots wasn't supposed to do that. "He's had it three days."

"Three days is too long. Has anyone healed him?"

"Has anyone what? Healed him?" She said it the way you'd say *has anyone flown him to the moon*. "With what?"

Kem looked at Vel. She knew what he was asking. She also knew what the answer cost, not to the boy but to the cascade, the mechanism, the plan she'd spent eight years building. She didn't say yes. She didn't say no.

He put his hands on the child's forehead and closed his eyes. The Curative ability came as a clean pulse of warmth that Vel could feel from six feet away, like a door opening into a heated room. Kem's calibration had improved. This was targeted and precise, the fever and infection only. Vel felt eight go. A month ago he would have burned twenty or forty on the same job. The boy's face lost its feverish tightness and relaxed into something that looked like ordinary sleep.

The girl stared at Kem. She touched her brother's forehead, then his cheek. Her hand was shaking. "How many was that?"

"Eight," Kem said. He stood up, and Vel caught the usual change, slight and wrong at the same time, as the Ledger settled at 140.

"Thank you," the girl said. It didn't sound simple. Gratitude was in it, but so was the fury of having needed a stranger's charity in the first place.

The loss hit her a beat later. She'd built the basin around Kem reaching the Scour with about 90 levels, enough for a sustained descent to stabilize the threshold alongside her own. At 140, he was already ten shy of where she'd wanted him by this point. She could adjust. She'd adjusted before.

Then Kem walked toward the compound gate.

"What is this?" he asked the enforcer.

The enforcer looked up from her ledger. "Labor compound. Contract work under District authority."

"And the wall?"

"Compound perimeter. Workers stay on-site during assigned shift. Per regulation."

"How long's a shift?"

"Sunrise to sunset. Per regulation."

"And if someone needs to leave? If their kid's sick?"

The enforcer's face didn't change. She was practiced at this. "Shift completion is required before exit authorization. Per regulation."

Kem looked at the wall. Vel saw his hand flex at his side. The reflex came before every ability use now.

"Kem," she said.

He didn't look at her. He put both hands flat against the Shaped stone and pushed.

The Disruption came as a sustained wave, not a single blast but modulated pressure, weeks of Vel's teaching turning into something close to control. The Shaping cracked along its seams first. The fused layers split with a sound like ice breaking on a river. Then fifteen feet of wall, the gate, and the mechanism holding it together slumped into rubble and pale dust. The enforcer fell off her chair. Workers on the other side stood blinking in the sudden daylight where the wall had been.

The shove ripped twenty off him in one go. Vel felt the remaining 120 pull tight across the next three days. The slack she'd built into that route was gone.

"You can leave," Kem told the workers. Some were already moving. Others stood where they were, staring at the gap as if it might rebuild itself. One woman picked up a tool she'd been using and set it down again, carefully, in the exact spot where it had been. Then she walked through the opening without looking back.

The enforcer got up from the dust, ledger book clutched to her chest. "You've destroyed Hoarded-contracted infrastructure. There'll be — the district office will —"

"Send the invoice to House Aldraste," Kem said.

Vel wanted to laugh. The sound died behind her teeth.

The backup arrived within the hour. Three enforcers from a larger compound to the south. Two men and a woman, all somewhere in the fifty-to-seventy range by their movement, all built like people who'd spent careers on enforcement rather than labor. They assessed the rubble, the dispersing workers, and Kem sitting on a chunk of fallen wall drinking water, and drew the correct conclusions.

The first one opened with a Combat Pulse aimed at Kem's chest. Kem threw up a shield that caught it. The detonation punched the air and blew dust in a circle around him. Four levels, maybe five. The second enforcer was already flanking left, and Kem caught it with a Perception flash that cost him two more, his head snapping toward the movement before the man had committed. The third enforcer hung back, gauging.

Then all three pressed at once. Kem's defensive barrier was rough but solid, a sustained field that held while the enforcers hammered it with everything they had. The air around the barrier rippled with spent energy, nine levels going in just to keep it standing.

He dropped the barrier and hit the first enforcer with a targeted Force Strike before the man could reset. The hit cost three and put the enforcer down hard. The second caught a Strike to the shoulder. Three more, and the man's arm went slack. The third enforcer looked at her two colleagues on the ground and at Kem, who was breathing hard and bleeding from a gash on his forearm where a glancing pulse had opened the skin, and she turned and ran.

By then the fight had burned thirty-five off him. He was at 85.

Kem sat down in the rubble. He tore a strip from the hem of his shirt and wrapped his forearm, the blood soaking through immediately, a dark spreading stain. Two months ago he would have healed the cut with an ability and not thought about the cost. Now he tied the cloth tight with his teeth and flexed the hand to check the tendons. It was field triage, the kind Vel had taught him in the first week, kneeling over a scraped knee he'd wanted to burn three levels on.

A worker came back through the gap in the wall. An old man, sixty or seventy, stooped and slow. He walked to the enforcer's chair, which was lying on its side in the dust, and he righted it. Then he sat down in it. He didn't do anything else. He just sat there and looked out at the road where the enforcers had gone.

Nobody stopped him. Dust kept sliding off the broken wall. The old man sat in Danner's chair and watched the road.

Vel stood at the edge of the rubble and felt the bottom drop out. The Scour needed Kem at 90. He was at 85. Five levels gone past the edge, and Kem was wrapping his arm with torn cloth and wearing the expression of someone who wasn't finished.

She needed to say it plain. She did not need to explain the mechanism. She only needed to tell him to stop.

She opened her mouth.

And closed it. Workers kept coming through the gap. The girl stayed by her brother with one palm on his cooling forehead. Behind her, a man waited with a split roof brace across both shoulders. Another woman stood with her thumb plugged into a bucket seam to slow the leak. If Vel said it now, all of them would hear it.

Vel bit the inside of her cheek until she tasted blood.

Word ran faster than dust settled. By the time Kem had the bandage knotted, people were standing at the edge of the rubble in a loose line, not crowding, not begging, just waiting with the patient faces of people used to hearing no. Vel looked at them and heard her own voice before she'd decided to use it.

"One at a time," she said. "Anything that keeps water moving or a roof up goes first."

A woman in a split straw hat stepped forward with a hoe handle in one hand. "East cut's gone," she said. "If the water reaches it like this, it spills into the gravel bed and dies there. Channel feeds four settlements when it works."

Vel knew the cut. She'd seen the line of it from the road, the old contractor ditch carrying runoff east toward a chain of wells. If the break held, the water they'd just freed could actually travel. If it didn't, the whole thing turned into a wet joke.

Kem looked at her. This time he waited.

"Fifteen if you build ugly," Vel said. "More if the foundation's rotten all the way through."

"Ugly's fine," the woman said at once.

"Show me," Kem said.

The east cut had caved where old Shaping met a later patch job. Water was already nosing down the reopened ditch, brown and impatient. Kem climbed into the break, boots sliding in powder-dry clay, and put both hands against the collapsed face.

"Don't chase pretty lines," Vel said. "Pin it. Give it somewhere to bear."

"Right."

He spent. The Shaping dragged through him in pulses that made the tendons in his neck stand out. Earth compacted under his palms with a deep grinding sound. Old stone shifted. One section slumped anyway. Kem swore, reset his feet, and shoved harder. The cloth on his forearm darkened where the wound had opened again. Water hit the rebuilt wall in a hard brown sheet, wavered, then bent where he'd forced it to go.

The woman went into the ditch without taking off her shoes. Water climbed over her ankles and around her calves. She slapped the new wall once with her palm, testing it like a live thing. Then she put both hands over her mouth.

The repair ate fifteen. When the wall held, he was at 70.

Vel could have stopped it there. Instead she said, "Next."

A girl led her mother forward by the wrist. The older woman's eyes were milk-clouded, the pupils almost gone under the white. "She hasn't seen my face clean in four years," the girl said. "She knows me by the scarf and by my voice."

Kem looked at Vel again. "Ocular?"

"Fine work," Vel said. "Easy to make worse."

The older woman gave a dry little laugh. "Worse is I miss the piss pot half the time and sew my own thumb into the hem. If he ruins it, I'll hate him accurately."

Dren snorted behind them. Even Kem smiled, brief and startled.

"I've never done it," Kem said.

"Then don't lie with your hands," Vel said. "Take the film. Leave the rest."

He knelt in front of the woman and set two fingers lightly at her temples. This spend came thin and bright, not the broad warmth of fever-healing but something narrow as wire being threaded through wet tissue. Kem flinched with her when she sucked air through her teeth. The white cloud loosened across one pupil, then the other, milky strands burning away to gray.

When he let go he had a line of blood under one nostril. Vel did the subtraction before the red reached his lip: six more, leaving him at 64.

The woman blinked hard and looked straight at the girl. "Blue," she said, voice gone rough. "Your head rag's blue. I forgot it was blue."

The girl laughed once like she hadn't meant to and caught her mother by both shoulders.

"It's blurry," Kem said. "I can't get deeper than that today."

"Blurry's still a face," the woman said. "That's plenty."

After that the line kept moving. Dren handled it without being asked. "You, later. You, now. If it isn't falling or rotting today, it waits." He said it flat and nobody argued with him. When a man with a broken finger came forward splinted in sticks, Dren held the hand still while Kem spent three levels knitting the joint back into place. When the leaning wall had to be braced, Dren shouldered half a beam with his good arm and hissed through his teeth when the bad one knocked his ribs. A child's infected tooth took two levels, and she cried more from the warm wrongness of the ability than from pain. A man who'd spent four years limping on a hip healed crooked took four more, and Dren gave Kem a shoulder to brace against when the man nearly fell in surprise trying his full weight. By full dark another fourteen levels were gone. Kem was at 50.

Vel didn't spend on a sweep. She climbed the rise above the channel and took the terrain the slow way with her eyes and the habits that had kept her alive this long: road bend, washout, three low roofs west, dead cedar stand, south track splitting around a salt-white ridge.

Dren had been quiet all day. He'd watched Kem heal the child and said nothing. He'd watched the wall come down and said nothing. When Kem was rebuilding the irrigation channel, Dren had walked into the ditch and started carrying rubble out of the way with his good arm, the bad one tucked against his body. He didn't ask. He just worked.

Now he sat on a stone at the edge of the repaired channel, watching the water run. His face had the look of a man who'd started holding his daughter next to the people around him and couldn't stop. He was at 130. The people around him were at 4, at 8, at 15. A month ago 130 would have felt like wealth. Here it looked like distance, the space between his daughter and the people in this settlement, measured by whether anyone with levels was willing to spend them.

***

They found Fallow an hour before dark, at the edge of the furthest settlement.

Fallow's structure was smaller than the others and better maintained. The roof patch sat square. The swept floor visible through the open door had been brushed right up to the threshold. A wooden chair with one leg shorter than the others had a folded cloth wedged under it, and the cloth had been refolded so many times the middle had worn thin. Outside, a garden held three rows of ash-beans in the chalky soil. Each plant was staked with a twig and tied with strips of rag, the rows dead straight. Half the plants were failing. All the ties were fresh.

Fallow was sitting in the chair when they arrived. Vel didn't need a Perception ability to read the four in them. Fallow turned their head by degrees. Their hands stayed on their knees. Nothing on their body moved without a reason.

Fallow was about sixty, though the body read older. Field sun had cut deep lines into the skin. The hands were mostly tendon and callus, the fingers permanently curved from tools held so long the shape had stuck. Fallow wore a shirt mended in seven visible places, each repair in a different-colored thread: brown, white, blue, red, green, yellow, brown again.

"Sit," Fallow said. There was one chair and they didn't gesture at it. "Ground or wall. Chair's mine."

They sat. Dren leaned in the doorframe.

"Dren said you know the routes south," Vel said.

"I know the routes everywhere around here. I've walked most of them at one point or another." Fallow's eyes moved from Vel to Kem and stayed there long enough to count the boots, the pack straps, the bleeding sleeve. "You're the one who broke Danner's wall."

"Danner?"

"The enforcer. She's been running that compound six years. Longest operation in this district." Fallow said it without judgment. The wall was a fact. Its absence was another. "She'll file a reconstruction request with the district office. Takes three weeks to process. Four more for a Shaper to come down. Your people get maybe seven weeks before it's back up."

Kem had spent twenty levels and bought seven weeks. Vel let the number sit where it landed.

"What were you before this?" Kem asked. He was leaning forward, the way he did when he was genuinely trying to understand something and not performing interest.

"They sent me down here while the Ledger still needed three digits to write me down," Fallow said. "They called it a Development Opportunity. Thick paper. Red seal. Thirty years of development. Very developed. Now I'm four."

Kem sat with that. His hands hung loose between his knees.

"My mother grows the same thing," Dren said. He was looking at the garden. "In her yard back home. Those're ash-beans, right? Alkaline soil?"

Fallow glanced at him. For a second some older alertness came back into their face. "They don't produce much."

"Hers either. She says they're stubborn enough to be worth the effort."

"Yeah. They're worth it." Fallow turned back to Vel. "You want the south routes."

Vel had the route already, inked in Maren's hand. What she needed was everything that had happened there since. "I need the south route," she said. "And I need to know whether zero still takes people there."

"Three days south. The land drops into a basin, big, all rock and wind, nothing growing. Old mining territory. Hoarded ran Shaping crews down there sixty, seventy years back. Stripped it and left." Fallow rubbed a thumb along one knuckle. "And yes. It still takes them."

Kem looked from Fallow to Vel. "Takes them how?"

Fallow was quiet for a moment. Not for effect. Just spending the breath carefully. "Not often. Long gaps, then it happens fast. I watched two go."

"Gone," Kem said.

"If I knew where, I'd charge for the map. Sera was first. I was hauling stone on the rim and saw her pick her way down. She stopped once with a hand on her thigh, breathing like every step cost her. I looked away to shift the load. When I looked back the rock was empty."

"And the second?"

"Hoster was the other. Eight years ago, maybe. Windy enough to crack your lips. He reached the floor, stood there swaying, and a white slit showed up in the air ahead of him. Thin as a knife cut. Then it was only stone again."

It had been eight years. Vel kept her face still and felt the old flash come back behind her eyes.

"Nobody talks about it," Fallow said. "What's the point? People at zero disappear. It's one more thing that happens to people nobody protects. Hoarded don't come down here to investigate. Margins people don't have the levels to care about mysteries." They looked out at the garden. "Sera and Hoster are just names now."

Vel reached into her pack and pulled out Maren's route journal. The leather had gone soft from years of handling, the corners dark with sweat and dirt. She opened to the southern pages.

Maren had mapped this stretch in detail: water sources, drop-offs, settlement names that might already be gone. On one page she had drawn the basin: an oval, hash marks for the descent, a small x on the floor. Beside it, in careful script: *here*.

"Route journal?" Fallow asked.

"Yeah."

"That handwriting's not yours."

"No."

Fallow watched Vel handle the pages. They didn't push. They turned to Kem instead. "You're the one at what, fifty now? After today?"

"Fifty," Kem said.

"And you're still heading south with your sleeve bleeding through."

"Yeah."

Fallow's mouth moved a little. Not quite a smile. "Most people around here don't walk themselves toward nothing. Nothing comes and gets them."

Vel closed the journal. The leather settled against the folded notices from the board when she put it back in her pack.

The basin was three days south. She could feel the distance in her body, in the dry pull under her ribs, in the narrowing room between what she needed and what was left.

***

They left Fallow's settlement in the failing light. Vel wanted hours. Three days to the Scour, and every hour mattered now that the route she'd built in her head no longer fit the body beside her. They walked in the formation they'd developed over weeks: Vel reading the terrain ahead, Kem carrying the heavier pack because his body could take it, Dren at the rear watching behind.

The road was empty. A footbridge over a dry creek bed was about to give, so Vel reinforced it with a Structural ability. The bridge took one of hers. The wood stiffened under their weight. Eighteen.

They walked in silence for a while. The pale soil cooled under their feet as the light faded. Somewhere to the west, a settlement's cooking fires put a thin haze against the sky.

Dren caught up and walked beside Vel. This was unusual. He kept rear guard and didn't seek company unless he had logistics to report.

"My agreement with you was to the edge of the Margins," he said. "We're past that."

"Yeah."

"Payment was for guide work. Routes, getting you through the Spent Towns and pointed south. That's done."

"Yeah, it's done."

He was quiet for another fifty paces. Vel could hear his breathing, steady, a walker's breath, with a hitch on the left side where his bad arm moved against his ribs.

"I'm not leaving yet," he said.

Vel looked at him. His face was set in the dying light, committed the way a man's face gets after he's turned the same choice over too many times already.

"I'm not asking for more payment," he said. "I'm just — I'm staying on."

"Okay."

"Don't ask me why."

"I won't."

They walked. Behind them Kem was a dark shape against the road. Fifty levels. The number sat in Vel like a cracked tooth.

A minute later he came up beside her. The cloth on his forearm had dried stiff. "Fifty's bad for whatever you've got in your head, isn't it."

"Yeah."

"You don't have to make it look polite."

"I wasn't."

He walked three more paces before speaking again. "If something ahead needs spending, I'm not asking permission first."

"All right."

He glanced at her, not satisfied. "No. I mean it. You tell me what the road costs. Distance. Water. What I burn if I use something. You don't do the deciding in your head and hand me the clean version after."

That landed close enough to bone that Vel felt it in her teeth. "Fine," she said. "I give you the number. You spend or you don't."

"Good."

"Don't mistake that for trust."

"I don't."

He dropped back again, as if that settled what could be settled.

The notices in her pack rasped against the journal buckle every time she moved. Once the corner of the Productivity Tier notice caught in the leather and tugged at her shoulder until she jerked the strap straight.

She had not told him to stop. She had done worse. She had started giving him the numbers.

Three days south the basin waited. Maren had marked it. Fallow had named two others, Sera and Hoster. When the flash came back now, it brought white stone, hard wind, and the dark wet line creeping through Kem's sleeve.

She walked south, and every mile pressed harder.
