# Chapter 14

Melie's hand found the hem of Jin's shirt in her sleep and stayed there. He sat with his back against the west wall of the main hall, her body curled against his thigh, her breathing damp on his forearm, the stone's cold coming through his shirt. Around them, in the dimness before the oil lamps were relit, the compound was still a room of people who had given up pretending to rest — knees drawn up, blankets twisted, the particular stillness of bodies that had stopped trying to sleep and were instead waiting for morning to give them permission to move.

The previous night's rupture had dragged the sleeping mats into new borders. The Kondos were pulled tight around their sleeping mats in a knot that excluded Arash, who slept or did not sleep near the south wall with his back to his father. Bett Dao sat upright against the far column with her legs stretched out in front of her, Hala's hand on her knee, though both of them were awake. The Okoyes were a cluster around baby Ada. Suna Mera had her three children arranged close to the kitchen doorway. The Leos kept to their corner, Vera's arm around Pik.

Jin watched them all. He had become a watcher over these eighteen days, and watching was the only thing he was good at here. Good at watching, bad at everything else — at talking, at declaring, at holding his own story together in front of the whole hall after they had heard it crack.

Melie stirred. Her fingers tightened in his shirt. She did this in her sleep now, as though she needed proof he was still there. He didn't move.

When the custodians' bell sounded — one strike, flat and dull — no one seemed surprised. It was too early for the naming. It didn't matter. Fasa appeared in the doorway from the covered walkway, her hair wrapped in cloth, her face carrying the exhaustion she usually kept out of her public bearing. She didn't announce anything. She stood and waited for the room to organize itself into the semicircle.

It took longer than usual. People got up heavily. Venn Kondo stood, and for the first time since Jin had been at the shrine, the man did not summon his family into formation. The Kondos gathered around him by habit, but the shape was looser than before. Tila touched Venn's back — a small, steadying gesture that Jin noticed because Venn usually planted himself hard enough to steady other people.

Jin lifted Melie. She woke partially, her face pressed into his neck, her breath sour with sleep. He carried her to the edge of the semicircle and sat on the stone floor with her in his lap. She was six and heavy, and the weight of her against his chest was the most grounding thing left in his life.

Fasa stood in the open space between the families and the custodians' bench. Olet sat behind her, his face as unreadable as it had been on the first day. The three assistant custodians flanked him. Beyond them, through the hall's open wall, the valley lay under gray light, the brown flood still holding the roads below.

"The naming will proceed," Fasa said. Her voice was quieter than usual. "After last night, I know some of you would rather it didn't. The naming does not ask what you would rather. It asks what is true."

She paused. The pause was long enough to feel deliberate — the liturgist's pause, Jin thought, the one she used before she called a family's name.

But it was Gitta Tesh who spoke.

"I would like to speak."

The voice was small. It came from the Tesh section of the semicircle, where Miro stood with his hands at his sides and Enn sat on the floor with Kiri pressed against her. Gitta was on a low stool someone had brought for her days ago, the stool that stayed in the naming circle because her knees made the stone floor impossible. She sat very straight. Her hands were in her lap, one folded over the other. They were not shaking.

Jin noticed that. After the previous night, when her hands had trembled so visibly that Suna had watched them from across the room, the stillness was worse.

Fasa looked at Gitta for a long moment. Something passed between them. Fasa's mouth set; Gitta did not look away. What was coming had already gathered too much weight to stop. Fasa stepped to the side.

"Speak," she said.

The silence that followed was the silence Jin would remember later, when everything else about the day had blurred at the edges. He would remember the pressure before it: forty-eight people holding their breath, the flood below, an oil lamp guttering near the east wall, Melie shifting in his lap with her fingers still wrapped in his shirt.

Gitta did not stand. She spoke from the stool, her eyes directed at a point on the floor a few feet in front of her. She spoke slowly, and each word arrived with the same weight as the one before it, so that the sentences had no peaks and no valleys, just the level surface of a woman who had been rehearsing this for sixteen years and now no longer needed to rehearse.

"My name is Gitta Tesh. My son is Miro. His wife is Enn. Their daughter is Kiri." She paused. "And Fen."

Fen was standing behind Miro. Jin could see him — tall, dark-haired, still as something carved. His face was open, unbraced, untouched yet by what was about to hit it.

"I have called Fen my grandson," Gitta said. "I still do. But he is not Miro's son, and he is not the child of any relative of ours."

The room did not react at first. The words were out in the air like something that had been dropped, and no one had decided yet whether to pick them up.

Miro turned to look at his mother. Jin searched for the right word and found none. Miro's face emptied. He stared at her as if the room had shifted under a fact he had always stood on without looking down.

"Sixteen years ago," Gitta said, "I traveled this road. Not this road exactly. A road south of here, in the foothills. It was during the trouble then — the registration sweeps, the papers. You remember." This last part was addressed to no one and to everyone. Some of the older adults — Deshi, Mava, Peyam — shifted slightly. They remembered.

"I was walking to my sister's village. Alone. Miro was at home with Enn. They had been married a year and had no children yet." She paused. Her hands stayed flat in her lap. "I found them on the road. A man. A woman. A girl, maybe eight or nine. They had been dead for — I do not know how long. Days, maybe. Maybe less. The heat was bad. There were flies."

Jin felt Melie's hand tighten on his shirt. She was awake, watching Gitta with the unfocused attention of a child who knows something serious is happening but cannot locate its source. Jin put his hand over hers.

"The infant was alive." Gitta's voice did not change. "He was lying beside the woman's body. He was sunburned and dehydrated and he had stopped crying — he was past crying. He was just making a sound. A small sound, like a hinge." She stopped. Her mouth worked once, quickly, and then she continued. "I picked him up. I carried him to my sister's village. I told my sister he was the child of a woman I knew from the market, a woman who had died in the sweeps and had no other family. My sister helped me feed him. Goat milk at first, then rice water."

She looked up. For the first time since she had begun speaking, she looked directly at someone. She looked at Fen.

"When he was strong enough, I brought him home to Miro and Enn. I told them the same story — a woman from the market, no family, she had asked me to take him. They believed me because I do not lie." Here her mouth pulled tight, the muscles in her cheeks bunching once around the ugliness of what she had just said. "I do not lie, and so they believed the lie."

"I named him Fen. That is not the name his mother gave him. I do not know what name his mother gave him. I do not know his mother's name, or his father's name, or the name of the girl who died beside them. I did not search their belongings. I did not look for papers. I took the child and I left them there, on the road, because the child was alive and they were not, and I could carry one and not four."

The hall broke into small sounds — Nessa Okoye's sharp intake of breath, the scrape of someone's shoe on stone, baby Ada beginning to fuss and being shushed quickly by Bram. Over all of it sat Fen's silence. He made no move, no sound. The skin at his throat worked once, then held.

Jin watched Fen's face. He couldn't stop. The boy's mouth hung slightly open. His eyes stayed on Gitta as though every wall in the room had suddenly shown its seams. Fury hadn't reached him. Grief hadn't either. He was still in the first drop, feet feeling for ground that was gone.

Miro stood up.

That movement broke something in the room. Miro stood before his mind caught up, a broad forty-five-year-old man with thick workman's forearms, his trust in the visible world still plain on his face. His hands went to his sides, his mouth opened, and then he sat down again, hard, as though his legs had quit. Enn's hand went over her mouth. She stared at Gitta as if smoke were already in her lungs.

Kiri, eight years old, still pressed against her mother, looked from face to face with the fierce, hunting concentration of a child who knows the adults have broken something and needs to identify the pieces.

"Why," Miro said. The word came out flat. It was the only word his body could produce.

"I have told you why," Gitta said. "He was alive. They were dead."

"Why didn't you —" Miro's voice cracked. He swallowed. Started again. "Why didn't you tell us. Why didn't you tell me. He's — I've — for sixteen years —"

"Yes."

"He's my son." Miro's voice was raw, and Jin recognized the sound of it — the sound of a man trying to hold onto something that had already changed shape. "He's my son. I raised him. I'm his father."

"You are his father," Gitta said. "That is also true."

Enn made a sound. It was not a word. Her hand was still over her mouth, and the sound came through her fingers — a low keen that she cut off by pressing her palm harder against her lips. Her other arm pulled Kiri closer, as though proximity could protect the child from what was happening. Kiri's face was white.

Jin looked at Fen.

The boy had not moved. He stood in the same position — behind where his father had been standing, now behind where his father sat — with his arms at his sides and his face gone very still. The difference mattered: blankness retreated behind a wall, but this kind of stillness stayed present for every particle of what was happening and still could not find a single movement equal to it.

Jin knew that face. Three months ago he had worn it in his kitchen with Reva's note in his hand and Melie asking him to come look at the horse she had drawn. He had walked to the table and told her it was a good horse because there had been nothing else his body knew how to do.

Fen was falling now. Jin could see it. Nobody else seemed to — they were watching Gitta, or watching Miro, or looking at their own hands because the spectacle of a family coming apart was too naked to witness directly. But Jin watched Fen because he knew what that stillness cost, and he knew the boy could not hold it much longer.

"I am not asking forgiveness," Gitta said.

Fasa, who had been standing to the side with her hands clasped in front of her, lifted her head.

"I stood up because this hall is where hidden things are said," Gitta continued. "I took a living child from a dead family. I lied to my son and his wife. I gave the child a name that is not the name his parents chose for him. I do not know his real name, and there is no one alive who does. I have carried this for sixteen years. I did not come here to be pardoned." She opened her hands — a gesture that included the naming circle, the hall, the custodians, the assembled families. "I came here to say it aloud."

The room held. Jin could feel it holding — the collective suspension of all forty-eight people as they decided, one by one, what to do with what they had heard. Some of them were looking at Fen. Most were looking at Gitta. Venn Kondo was staring at the floor. Bett Dao's face was turned toward the open wall, toward the valley and the flood. Suna Mera had her arms around Asha and her eyes were wet. Her mouth stayed set hard. Jin knew that face from his own mornings at the cistern: recognition had reached her before pity.

Fen moved.

He moved like someone underwater — deliberate, slow, each motion costing something. He stepped backward, away from Miro, away from the semicircle. His foot caught the edge of a sleeping mat and he didn't stumble, didn't look down, just adjusted and kept moving. He crossed the hall toward the open wall. He did not run. He walked through the gap in the stone columns and stepped into the gray morning light and kept walking, down the slope toward the terraced garden, and the room watched him go, and no one followed because no one knew what to follow him toward.

Miro's face was in his hands. Enn had pulled Kiri into her lap, and Kiri was crying with the borrowed grief of the room, loud and helpless because the adults around her had come apart.

Gitta sat on her stool with her hands open in her lap, fingers still slightly curled as though the weight were there. Her shoulders had not dropped. Shock held them up.

Fasa looked at Olet. Olet's face stayed flat. He let an extra beat pass before he spoke.

"The naming has heard Gitta Tesh," Olet said. "The naming does not close until the naming is complete."

"The naming can wait," Fasa said, and her voice held a sharpness Jin had not heard from her before. She looked at the room. "We will pause."

---

The hours after the confession did not blur. Tea still had to be boiled. Children still had to piss and wash and ask questions nobody could answer.

Jin carried Melie to the cistern and splashed water over her face and his own. The water was cold enough to sting. Across the yard, Miro crossed the upper terrace with a tin cup in his hand and called Fen's name toward the garden. The name went out bare across the stone. No answer came back.

Enn stood in the hall opening with Kiri braced against her leg. Gitta was on her stool a few feet behind them, both hands around her prayer beads. When she started to rise, Kiri saw and clutched a fistful of Enn's skirt. Enn shifted without seeming to, just enough to keep the child on the far side of her body. Gitta sat again.

Melie sat on the stone edge of the cistern and swung her legs.

"Papa. Is that boy going to be OK?"

Jin looked at her grimy face, the crease sleep had left on one cheek.

"I don't know," he said.

"His grandma was crying."

"She wasn't crying."

"Inside crying, then."

His hand stopped on the cistern's edge. The stone was wet and rough under his palm, gritty with mineral deposits. Melie was watching him with the blunt certainty he was learning to dread. She knew things. She was six, and she knew things about him he had spent three months believing he was hiding.

"Let's get breakfast," he said.

In the kitchen overhang, Hala Dao was making porridge. She moved with deliberate, slamming force, each action a little harder than it needed to be — the wooden spoon striking the pot's rim, the lid replaced with a crack, the water jug set down with a thump. Hala's way of handling the morning was to cook at it until it gave up. Her daughter Raya sat on the step peeling a tangerine in one long ribbon, staring at the pith in her lap instead of anybody's face.

Bram Okoye came through with Ada on his hip. His glasses were smeared and there was dried milk on his shoulder.

"Seen Fen?" he asked.

Jin shook his head.

"Miro's gone down the terraces three times already," Bram said. "Enn won't leave the hall."

All morning the Tesh corner kept rearranging itself around the missing boy. Miro filled a bowl and carried it to Fen's mat before remembering the mat was empty. Later he took tea to the hall opening, stood there with the steam in his face, and came back with the cup still full. Enn spoke only to Kiri. When porridge ran down the child's chin, Gitta reached automatically with the corner of a cloth. Kiri jerked away hard enough to knock her spoon against the bowl. Enn wiped her clean instead. Gitta folded the cloth and laid it on her own knee.

They sat under the kitchen's stone overhang and ate. The morning was humid and gray, and the flood filled the valley with its usual low rush. The rain was still holding off. The clouds sat heavy over the mountain, waiting.

Near midday, with Melie asleep in the crook of his arm, Jin went to the prayer courtyard for air. From there he could see the lower wall beyond the last herb terrace. Fen was sitting against it with his knees drawn up, staring past the flooded switchback as if the valley might give him back the morning before Gitta spoke. Miro stood over him with a bowl in one hand and both shoulders bent forward, stripped of every easy father's posture. A little way up the path, Enn had come down after him and stopped.

"Fen," Miro said. The boy's shoulders locked at the name. "Come inside. Just eat something."

Fen did not turn.

Miro crouched and set the bowl on the wall beside him. "You haven't had anything."

Still nothing. The flood kept up its low rush below them.

Miro put out a hand toward the back of Fen's neck, the old steering touch of a father guiding a child through doorways and crowded rooms. Fen pulled away before the hand landed, quick and clean. That made it worse. Miro's fingers hung in the air for a second, then closed into his palm.

"I'm still your father," Miro said.

Fen's voice came out rough and flat. "I know."

Enn took one more step and stopped again. "Kiri's scared," she said. "She keeps asking where you went."

Fen dragged both hands over his face. When he spoke again, he was looking at the bowl instead of either of them. "That's her name for me."

No one answered at first. Jin felt the sentence travel all the way up the terraces.

"It's yours too," Miro said.

Fen shook his head once. "I don't know what anything is right now."

Enn made a sound like she meant to go to him. She did not move. Miro looked back at her, then at the boy, then down at the cooling bowl between them.

"All right," he said finally, and there was no authority left in it. "We'll leave you a while."

He said Fen's name once more before he turned away, softly this time, almost to himself. Fen did not answer. Miro picked up the untouched bowl and went back up the terraces with Enn behind him, neither of them close enough to touch the other.

Evening came slowly. The rain started in the late afternoon — heavy, pounding, the kind that turned the courtyard into a shallow lake and made the flood below surge audibly, a sound like a living thing clearing its throat. The families drew inward. The lamps were lit. Fasa did not call another naming.

Jin sat with Melie in the main hall. She was drawing on a scrap of paper Leni Okoye had given her, pressing hard with a stub of pencil, making and remaking the same four-legged shape until the paper went soft under the eraser.

The hall was full and quiet. People talked in low voices. The Teshes occupied their corner like a family under quarantine. Miro and Enn sat shoulder to shoulder with Kiri between them. Gitta kept her stool three feet away. Each time Kiri slumped with sleep, Enn or Miro steadied her first. Once Gitta lifted a hand as if to tuck the blanket higher around the child. Miro had already done it.

He had spent the afternoon carrying things across that gap — a dry shirt for Fen when the rain started, a cup of tea he left cooling at the doorway, a towel folded and unfolded in his lap until the cloth held the shape of his fists. None of it crossed. Enn answered Gitta only when she had to, and never turned her whole body toward her. The distance was three feet. It ran the length of the road.

Gitta sat with the beads in her lap and did not move. Her face gave away nothing. She kept both hands on the beads and her shoulders square, withholding even the smallest reach toward comfort.

Fen did not come back for hours.

When he appeared in the doorway in the small hours, the rain was still heavy. His clothes were soaked through and his hair was plastered to his forehead, and he stood there dripping on the stone threshold, and the room went quiet the way it had gone quiet that morning, in a wave: conversation dying, faces turning, bodies going still.

Fen looked at the room. His eyes were swollen. His jaw was set in the strained, painful way of somebody holding his face together by force. His shirt clung to his chest. He was shivering, though whether from cold or from the day's damage was impossible to say.

He did not look at Miro or Enn. He looked at Gitta.

She looked back at him. She did not stand. Her hands stayed clasped over the beads in her lap. She did not reach for him or speak to him or cry or open her arms or make any of the gestures the moment seemed to demand.

Fen crossed the hall. His wet shoes left dark prints on the stone. He reached Gitta's stool. He stood there for a moment that seemed to pull the whole room tight around it, and then he sat down.

He sat on the floor beside her stool, so close that the space between them was smaller than his hand. He did not lean against her or reach for her. He sat with his head bowed, water running from his hair to the stone between his feet, and his breathing was ragged and audible. Gitta did not move.

Miro made a sound so small Jin almost missed it. Enn's hand closed around Kiri's ankle and stayed there. Kiri watched from between her parents' knees with the raw, sleepless face of a child learning that even names could come loose. At the doorway, Fasa stood watching without moving. She stayed there until the hall had gone still around them, then turned away toward the custodians' quarters. The hall kept its distance. No one crossed to them, and no one said Fen's name.

Gitta had carried her lie for sixteen years. Jin had carried his for three months. Watching Fen sit in the spill of lamplight beside the woman who had taken him from beside the dead family on the road and called him hers, Jin felt the smaller lie under his breastbone cramp hard enough to steal a breath.

Across the hall, Gitta kept both hands on the beads in her lap. Fen sat with his head lowered, rainwater still falling from his cuffs. Miro and Enn watched from three feet away and did not move. Melie's fingers bunched tighter in Jin's shirt. The rain battered the open wall. No one in the hall closed the space between them.
