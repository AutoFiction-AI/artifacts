# Chapter 5

Venn counted before the bell. Not because the number changed, but because order lived in the saying of it and the saying had to happen somewhere before the custodians began theirs. Twelve Kondos on the hill. Twelve sleeping mats. Twelve bowls at breakfast. Twelve bodies he meant to take down the switchback the moment the road showed stone again. Arash sat beside him on the wall, long legs stretched out, jaw set toward the valley as if he could stare the flood backward. There were nineteen days until the wedding. The gauging stone at the third bend was still half lost under brown water. Below it the seven-road convergence had gone flat and wrong, nothing but current where routes should have split cleanly. Every morning Venn made the same calculation: if the rain stopped today, if the lower mud held, if the crews in the lowlands had not given the road up for some other crisis. Every morning it came out the same. Not yet.

He had slept badly because Mava had given him Iru in pieces after supper. Not the whole story. Just the parts sharp enough to cut on contact. The word. The old valley dialect from Ishe. The child saying it in sleep with her hand at her ear. Venn had been twelve when Iru came back up this hill on a door ripped off somebody's shed. He remembered the wet pine boards flexing under her weight, remembered a strip of yellow cloth stuck to her shin with blood and rainwater, remembered one broken wheel from the cart still turning uselessly in the ditch when the men climbed down to fetch her. He did not need Suna Mera's little girl to make that death uncanny. Iru had been uncanny enough dead.

Arash rubbed his palms on his knees and kept looking at the valley.

"You think Fasa's going to make a room of it?" he asked.

Venn did not look at him. "She rang the bell before breakfast. She didn't do that for weather."

Arash's mouth tightened. He was Venn's son in the jaw and the shoulders and nowhere else clear enough to trust.

"Mava shouldn't have to speak if she doesn't want to," Arash said.

"If Fasa wanted quiet, she'd have come to our doorway. She wants witnesses."

Arash gave a short breath through his nose. Not agreement. Not refusal either. That in-between had become his habit over the past few days, and Venn disliked it more than open argument. Open argument could be answered. The half-space between one thing and another just spread.

The bell rang from the custodians' walkway. It sent a single iron note through stone.

"Everyone," Venn said.

They came at once. Tila came with the cloth wrap of needles and thread still in her hand. Habel came with Ori tucked close against her hip, the girl watching the Mera side even now, as if Asha might step out from behind a doorway and solve this by being ordinary again. Mava crossed more slowly than the others, with Arash steady at her elbow. Ger arrived behind Habel, then Rem and Osh, then the younger ones, subdued for once by the bell and the room it promised. There were twelve. All of them were here.

The naming hall was already full. Rain light washed in from the open side, gray and cold. Lamps burned along the inner wall. Bram Okoye leaned so far forward both palms were planted on his knees, ready for the room to give him an excuse. Jin Parra sat against the far wall with Melie in his lap, his face turned inward. Gitta Tesh's thumbs moved in their private circle. Hala Dao's shoulder was angled in front of Bett without seeming to be. The Leo family held themselves close together at the end of a bench. Suna Mera stood near Deshi with Asha on her hip and Kael pressed against her side, her body already arranged around refusal. Fasa stood at the front with the valley at her back. Olet sat to one side, hands on his knees, like stone that had chosen the shape of a man.

Venn put the Kondos on the right, near the inner wall, in the arrangement that said what he needed it to say. Tila stood beside him and Arash held the place at his shoulder. Mava sat because standing too long made her knees buckle, with Habel and Ori close behind her. Ger took the far end. Rem and Osh drew the younger ones into the line. No gaps. No openings. If the hall wanted to look at the Kondos, it would look at them as a unit.

Fasa began before he had fully settled.

"The naming continues," she said. "Four days ago each family spoke itself aloud. Since then this house has watched you eat, sleep, quarrel, and wake. Some things do not stay where you first set them."

Venn felt the back of his neck tighten.

"The Mera family and the Kondo family," Fasa said. "Come forward. Stand where the room can see you together."

The silence hit hard enough that even the Okoye baby stopped fussing.

Suna answered first. "No."

She held Asha against her chest and did not look at Venn. "We're not doing that. What happened between Asha and Mava stays with us. I'm not standing my daughter up for the hall."

Venn should have welcomed it. The refusal came from the other side. The Mera woman was doing his work for him.

But she had refused without even turning to him. She kept her eyes on Fasa, cutting the Kondos out of it entirely, and that slight stung worse than a direct quarrel. It made his family sound incidental, a thing to be managed between shrine women and a mother with a frightened child.

"My son marries in nineteen days," Venn said. He gave the words to Fasa, not to Suna. "The road is still under floodwater, and my family is waiting on it to clear. I won't have our name tied to another family's because a child made a sound in her sleep. We came here to pass through, not to be bound to strangers."

Fasa let the sentence die on the stone.

"Mava," she said.

Mava's hand rose to her left ear and began its slow circles. Ori saw it and caught Habel's wrist with both hands.

Venn shook his head once.

Fasa did not look at him. "Speak for yourself, Mava Kondo. Tell the room what Suna Mera asked you yesterday."

Mava lowered her hand to her lap. "The child used a word."

"Say it."

Mava swallowed. "Nushi."

The word sat in the middle of the hall. Venn heard rain against the open stone, the soft gutter of lamp oil, somebody dragging a breath through a narrow throat. He had not heard that word aloud in decades. Hearing it now cracked open the old picture against his will: the lifted door, the muddy hem, Mava making a sound Venn had never heard from a grown woman and never forgot.

"Whose word is it?" Fasa asked.

"My sister's." Mava's voice was thin but steady. "Iru's. Old valley speech from Ishe, where she married. It means something like the other half of my face. No one here speaks it. No one here should know it. Iru has been dead forty years."

Fasa turned to Suna. "Do you know the valley speech of Ishe?"

"No."

"Has anyone in your family traveled there?"

"No."

Fasa looked past Suna. "Deshi. Peyam."

Old Peyam opened his eyes, beads cupped in both hands, and gave one deliberate shake of his head.

Deshi said nothing. She fixed on Mava so completely the rest of the hall might as well have emptied.

"Then say what is in front of you," Fasa said. "A child has spoken a dead woman's word with no road to it that anyone in this room can name. Stand together and answer to that."

Suna's arms tightened around Asha. "I already answered."

Fasa kept her eyes on her. "I heard the refusal. The thing itself is still here."

Venn rose before he knew he had decided to.

"This is grief," he said. The hall gave his voice back to him. "My aunt's grief. A word she's carried in her mouth for forty years. A four-year-old saying something strange does not make a bond between families. It does not rewrite who we are. I won't have my family hauled into a story because a ritual wants more than the facts can bear."

His words died in the benches. Bram leaned farther forward, as if this might finally become an argument he could win. Jin stared at the floor between his feet. Cade Leo set a hand on Pik's shoulder and kept it there. Even the children held still.

Then Deshi stood.

Venn had filed her days ago: the sharp old woman from the Mera side, measuring rice by bite and children by noise. He knew the type because his own family had made women like that and depended on them. She should have stayed behind Suna, hard and traditional and on her own side of the room. If anyone in the Mera line should have distrusted this, it should have been her.

She crossed the hall instead.

Her sandals made almost no sound. She stopped in front of Mava's bench and looked down at her.

"Your sister," Deshi said. "What was her name?"

"Iru."

"And Nushi was hers for you?"

"Yes."

Deshi turned her head toward Asha for one beat, then back to Mava. "My granddaughter didn't get that from me."

She sat beside Mava on the Kondo bench.

The room changed shape with that one movement. Venn felt it happen the way he felt a roof beam shift in bad weather, not visible at first, only wrong in the body. If Deshi Mera could plant herself among the Kondos before witnesses, then the line between one family and another had already gone soft in everyone's mind.

Suna made a small shocked sound. "Deshi."

"Be quiet," Deshi said.

She took Mava's hands in both of hers and settled around them with an old, practiced certainty.

"Tell me about Iru."

Mava looked down at the hands around hers. Her throat worked once, twice. Then the sound came out of her ragged and low, and Ori buried her face in Habel's skirt.

"She was four years younger," Mava said. "Pretty. Spoiled. Always laughing at me for worrying. She married into Ishe and kept talking about the valley as if she'd invented it. She was coming home to see me when the cart went over right below the shrine turn. They brought her back on a door because the wheels had shattered."

Venn could see the door again. He had helped wash the mud off Iru's heel because no one had thought to move the child from the room. The skin there had been cold and waxy and torn at the ankle where the wood had bitten. He had thrown up afterward behind the cistern and told no one. Mava did not know he remembered that much. Nobody did.

Deshi did not release her hands.

"Twenty-nine," Mava said. "That's how old she was. Twenty-nine."

Venn stayed on his feet because sitting would have looked like surrender and stepping forward would have put his hands on a woman who did not belong to him. Tila's fingers touched the inside of his wrist, light as thread tension. He did not know whether she was steadying him or warning him. He did not move.

Fasa did not call the room back to order. She stayed where she was and let the silence stretch around the bench where the two old women sat. Bench wood creaked under shifting weight, but every face stayed on Mava's open mouth. Olet sat at the edge of the hall with his hands on his knees, outside the pull of it. The hall had tilted and would not tilt back.

She wept without trying to stop. She did not weep loudly. The tears kept dropping off her jaw onto the front of her blouse while Deshi sat close and held on. Habel moved to Mava's other side and pressed a palm between her shoulder blades. Arash stayed behind the bench with his hands in his pockets and did not once glance at Venn. The arrangement Venn had built at the start of the hour came apart around them and stayed apart.

He sat down because his knees did it first.

Across the hall Suna spoke into the quiet. "She said it asleep. I'd never heard it from her awake. She was dreaming, and her hand was on her ear, and she said Nushi like she knew who she was calling."

Nessa pulled the baby tighter. Bram's mouth stayed open a moment before he shut it. Venn felt damp air on the sweat cooling under his shirt.

Gitta's thumbs were still circling each other. Fen Tesh watched the bench without blinking, shoulders drawn tight enough that even his next breath seemed delayed.

Bram cleared his throat. "Then let's be exact about what we're saying. Either the child made a sound that happened to resemble a word, or we're being asked to admit that something passed into her that no one can account for. Those are not the same claim."

For one breath Venn was grateful to him. A claim could be argued. A premise could be broken apart. Bram liked order almost as much as Venn did, only his kind lived in language instead of people.

"Belief is your own work," Fasa said. "This room is for naming what it has seen."

"What I've seen is an old woman crying and a child touching her ear."

Venn seized on the sentence even before Bram finished it. Yes. Keep it there. Keep it on what any pair of eyes could swear to. Tears, gesture, accident. Nothing that traveled. Nothing that joined.

Nessa's hand landed on Bram's forearm. He nodded once and shut his mouth on the rest, corrected by the touch before the room could do it for him.

"I'm not dismissing it," he said. "I'm asking what you expect us to do with it."

"Not turn away," Fasa said.

Venn stood again. The hall felt narrower than it had an hour earlier. Wet wool, lamp smoke, human breath, the smell of old people crying. He could feel the pressure of every face turning not toward Mava now but toward him, waiting to see whether the head of the Kondos would call the thing by a name he could never pull back.

"Turn away from what?" he said. "From my aunt grieving her sister? From a child not knowing what comes out of her own mouth? Iru is ours. Her death is ours. The road took her from our family, not from this room."

Deshi lifted her face to him. Her eyes were dry and steady.

"A dead woman's word is in my granddaughter's mouth," she said. "What should I do with that? Pretend I did not hear it? Pretend your aunt is not crying into my hands because no one living has said that word to her in forty years?" She looked at him the way she might have looked at a pot she knew had been left too long on the fire. "You are the man who likes order. Tell me the orderly response."

Heat ran under his collar. He bit the inside of his cheek hard enough to taste iron. The old machinery in him kicked for purchase anyway: routes, dowries, sleeping assignments, debt owed at a wedding, who carried which parcel, who answered at checkpoints if armed boys decided to make an afternoon of it. He knew how to solve those things. He knew how to make a family move through the world in one shape. This was not one of those things, and the uselessness of that fact landed in him like a slap.

"The Kondo family will speak on this privately," he said at last.

Arash looked away. The movement was small, but Venn saw it. Bram shifted and did not pick up the offer. Fasa remained still.

"Private speech made this room necessary," she said. "What hides in families comes here to be spoken where others can hear it."

Tila broke in before he could answer. "Enough. He's heard it. Mava's shaking. Let the old woman breathe."

Fasa's gaze moved to Olet. He sat as he had sat all morning, hands on knees, unreadable. After a moment she nodded.

"We stop here for today," she said.

Still no one scattered. Peyam rose from the Mera side with his beads looped over one wrist and crossed the hall to the bench. Habel made room. He sat beside Mava without a word, another old body bracing the space around her. Bett's eyes never left Mava. Hala leaned in and whispered something to her sister that Venn could not catch. Cade Leo turned fully toward Wynn for the first time all morning and said something low into his shoulder. Jin was the last of the untouched families to go; he paused in the doorway with Melie on his hip and looked back once before the rain took him out of sight.

When the hall had thinned, Mava spoke again.

"She was coming to visit me," she said. Her voice was rubbed raw. "Iru had jars of quince preserve wrapped in cloth under the seat because she knew I liked it and never made it right. She rubbed my ear when I was a child and when I was old enough to be ashamed of crying. She'd say Nushi and laugh at my face and I would stop. I still do it because of her." She pressed her fingertips to her ear and let them fall. "I just stopped doing it where people could see."

Deshi nodded once. "Then the word did not stay buried with her."

Habel kept her hand on Mava's back. Peyam bowed his head over his beads. Fasa let the sentence stand where it had fallen. Across the hall Bram pressed his lips together, argument trapped there. The sentence went into the stone with everything else that mattered in the room and stayed there.

Tila touched Venn's sleeve. "Come outside."

They stood under the kitchen overhang with rain dripping from the edge of the roof hard enough to splash their sandals. The banked fire smelled of wet ash. Beyond the courtyard wall the flood kept up its blunt roar.

For a while Tila said nothing. She let the rain fill the space and waited, knowing a question too soon would only lock him harder.

"The wedding is in nineteen days," Venn said.

"I know."

"The road is still gone."

"I know."

"And now this."

He lifted a hand toward the hall and let it drop. The word, the tears, Deshi crossing the room, Arash standing with his hands in his pockets while the Kondos came apart in public. None of it fit cleanly inside one sentence.

Inside the hall Mava's voice carried out in scraps, low and steady now, telling Deshi things he had never heard her tell him. He could feel the story leaving the family already, passing through the open doorway into common use.

Tila looked toward the sound. "Mava hasn't cried for Iru since the funeral."

Rain struck stone in quick hard beats. Venn could still see Deshi taking the floor from everyone else, the whole room holding still when he tried to drag it back under him.

"She's crying now," he said.

"Because somebody finally asked for the whole thing," Tila said. "Not the custodian. Deshi."

Venn turned to her. "And what do you think happens next? We stand them together once and then what? Fasa asks again. Olet listens. The room decides it has learned something about us that belongs in every mouth on the hill. By the time the road opens, the Kondos are not twelve people traveling to a wedding. We're a question."

Tila did not flinch. "No one said anything about breaking the family apart."

"They don't have to say it yet." He kept his voice low only because the walls were close. "On this hill they call it naming. Down there it's checkpoints and men with ledgers and the wrong answer at the wrong table. I need twelve bodies walking one road when the flood drops. I need one story, not seven families chewing on ours."

She watched him for a long moment. Rainwater dripped off the roof between them in a steady line.

"You're talking like Deshi came to steal Mava," she said.

"She crossed the hall and took my aunt's hands in front of everybody."

"She held them."

"In front of everybody."

Tila's mouth tightened, not with anger but with the weariness that came over her when he insisted on a distinction after the room had already moved past it.

"If you make a public fight out of a private comfort," she said, "you will lose both."

He looked away to the flood because looking at Tila meant admitting she had seen the room more clearly than he had. Beyond the courtyard, water shoved itself through the trees in one broad brown body. Somewhere under it were the switchbacks, the third bend, the convergence, the road that should have been carrying his family toward the wedding instead of trapping them in shrine law and other people's dead.

The noon meal bell sounded from the kitchen. Nobody hurried to it. That told him more than any argument could have.

Families came out of the hall in slow knots, speaking low. Bram and Nessa moved together for once, his hand at the baby's back while she talked into his shoulder. Hala Dao crossed to the cooking fire with Bett just behind her, both women already reaching for work because work kept the body from showing too much. The Leos kept close enough to touch. Suna emerged last among the Meras with Asha on her hip and Deshi not with her, which meant the old woman had chosen to remain behind with Mava after her own family rose to go. That detail lodged under Venn's ribs.

He went to the water jars and washed his hands longer than necessary. When he straightened, Deshi had appeared at the kitchen fire with one of the shrine bowls in her hands. She said something to the assistant at the pot, took a ladle of broth without waiting to be served, and crossed the courtyard as if the path were hers. Suna saw it and stopped dead. Deshi did not look at her.

Venn stepped into her line.

"The Kondo family will see to our own," he said.

Deshi stopped because his body forced the stop, not because his words had weight with her. The bowl steamed between her hands. Pepper and onion cut through the smell of rain.

"Then see to her after she drinks this," Deshi said. "Crying empties a body."

"You have said enough for one day."

At that she looked at him directly. There was no fear in it, and no ritual solemnity either. Just the flat attention of an old woman refusing a foolish obstacle in her kitchen.

"I did not cross that hall to claim your aunt," she said. "I crossed because no one else moved fast enough. Step aside."

Before he could answer, Tila took the bowl from Deshi's hands.

"Thank you," Tila said.

It was done so quickly he had nothing to oppose without making a spectacle of himself in the yard. Deshi gave one curt nod, as if a task had been passed from one competent pair of hands to another, and went back toward the Mera side. Suna was still standing where she had stopped. Deshi brushed past her without a word. Asha, riding on Suna's hip, twisted to watch Tila carry the bowl into the hall.

That small turning of the child's body felt worse than the bowl.

Venn called the Kondos to eat on the right side of the courtyard, away from the Meras, and they obeyed because habit still held where conviction had frayed. Ger brought the younger ones. Rem and Osh took the bowls. Arash fetched water without being asked. Habel stayed near Mava while she drank. Ori did not sit. She stood against Habel's leg and watched the doorway where Asha had vanished behind Suna's shoulder.

No one spoke much over the meal. The rain kept up. Spoons struck bowls. Somewhere on the far side of the overhang Bram started an argument too quietly to travel, and Nessa cut it off before it could rise. Bett and Hala worked through their food in practical silence. Fasa moved from family to family with the face she wore when she believed waiting was a kind of labor. Olet did not come out at all.

When Mava finished the broth, she set the bowl down and said, almost to herself, "She used to put too much pepper in everything. Iru. Said food should make you notice your own mouth."

Habel's head turned sharply toward her. Ori pressed closer.

Tila said, "Drink the rest while it's hot."

Venn looked across the courtyard. Deshi was not watching them. That made it worse. If she had been looking, he could have answered the look. Instead she was peeling a boiled yam for Kael with the same dry concentration she might have used any other day, as if carrying broth to Mava were already folded into the ordinary business of noon.

After the meal he gathered the Kondos into the sleeping quarters before the room could thin and reform around separate errands. The partition cloth dropped behind the last of them with a damp slap. Outside, the courtyard voices blurred together. Inside, the air smelled of wool, stored clothes, wet sandals, and the medicinal oil Habel rubbed on Mava's knees at night. Mats were stacked against the wall to make space. The younger ones hovered near Rem and Osh, close enough to listen and old enough to know listening was dangerous.

Venn stood where the entrance let him see all of them.

"What happened in that hall does not alter who we are," he said. "When anyone here is asked about today, you keep to what can be said cleanly. Asha Mera used a word. Mava knew it. The rest is grief and a room making too much of it. We do not help the shrine build more from that than it has."

No one answered at first. Rain ticked against the roof tiles. Through the partition somebody laughed once in the Leo room and then stopped, as if they too had remembered where they were.

Habel was the first to speak. "Ori heard it too."

"Heard what?"

"Mava in the hall. Asha last night in the courtyard when Suna brought her over." Habel's hand stayed on Ori's shoulder. "She asked me if Aunt Iru found the wrong house."

The words landed in the cramped room and stayed there. One of the younger boys sucked in a breath. Tila shut her eyes for a beat.

"What did you tell her?" Venn asked.

"Nothing yet. I was waiting to know what to say."

"You tell her dead people stay dead," he said.

Ori looked at him with the naked concentration children had when they were trying to decide whether an adult had answered the right question. She did not cry. That steadiness unsettled him more than tears would have.

Mava spoke from her mat. Her voice had scraped itself raw in the hall and not recovered. "Dead people stay dead, yes. Their words don't always."

He turned to her. "A word does not make kin."

"No." She looked back at him without heat. "But hearing it in that child's mouth did not feel like nothing either."

"Feeling is not the measure."

"It is when the feeling is grief," Tila said quietly.

Venn ignored her because if he answered Tila first the room would hear where the stronger resistance sat.

"We are not here to be measured by grief," he said to Mava. "We are here to get off this hill in one piece and continue to the wedding."

Arash, who had been leaning against the rear wall with his arms folded, said, "Nobody's stopping us from leaving when the road opens."

Venn turned too fast. "That is exactly what I am trying to prevent."

"By telling Mava what she can hear?"

"By keeping forty-three people from deciding our family can be talked into pieces."

Arash pushed off the wall. "Standing next to the Meras in one hall isn't the same as breaking the family apart."

"Not in one hall," Venn said. "In seven more conversations after. In the road crew's questions. In checkpoint lines. In whatever story the custodians think they have earned the right to tell about us before we are gone."

Arash's face sharpened, not with open defiance but with the effort not to show it. "You think some soldier in the lowlands is going to ask whether a four-year-old said an old woman's word in her sleep?"

"I think men with guns make trouble out of whatever lies nearest to hand," Venn said. "And I think a family travels better when it does not hand strangers extra material."

Rem shifted with one of the younger girls pressed to his side. Osh stared at the floor. Ger looked from Venn to Arash and back again, already measuring where the force in the room would land. Venn hated that look. He knew it because he had worn it in his own father's house.

Mava pulled the blanket higher over her knees.

"You keep saying ours as if that settles it," she said.

"It does settle who Iru belonged to."

"Iru did not belong." She coughed once into the heel of her hand, then went on. "She was my sister. She married where she pleased. She died on the road. Now a child says her word, and you want to cork it because you are afraid of what other people might do with it."

The nakedness of the sentence made the younger ones look down.

"Yes," Venn said. There was no point pretending otherwise. "I am afraid of what other people might do with it. That is my job."

Silence after that. He heard the truth of the admission even as he made it and disliked the sound of it in the room. Not because it was false, but because it gave away more than he intended.

Tila sat on the edge of the rolled bedding and folded the empty broth cloth in her lap.

"Protection isn't the same thing as shutting every door," she said.

"Sometimes it is."

"Not this one."

He looked at her. Tila almost never crossed him in front of the younger ones. When she did, it was because she had already decided the cost of silence was worse.

"You saw the room," he said. "You think they will leave this alone?"

"No," she said. "I think they saw an old woman say her dead sister's word and another old woman go sit with her. I think they will keep talking because that is what people do when they are trapped together. You cannot stop that by telling Mava to call it coincidence."

At the edge of the room Ori finally spoke, so softly he almost missed it.

"Was Aunt Iru scared?"

Habel bent toward her, but Mava answered first.

"Yes," she said. "I think she was."

"Did it hurt?"

Mava closed her eyes. Venn felt the whole room waiting on the answer, even the youngest ones with no clean grasp of what had been asked.

"Yes," Mava said again. "The cart broke under her. The hill cut her up. It hurt."

There was no point in softening it. Venn was almost grateful for that. At least pain, once named, stayed in the body where it belonged.

Ori pressed her face into Habel's hip.

Venn said, more harshly than he intended, "Enough."

The partition cloth lifted before anyone could obey. Deshi stood in the doorway with Peyam just behind her, beads looped over one wrist, both of them framed by the gray light from the hall.

For a second the room held perfectly still.

Deshi looked from Venn to Mava to the children crowded around Habel and did not bother pretending she had arrived at a convenient moment.

"I brought the shawl she left on the bench," she said.

The blue wool was folded over her arm. Mava's, not hers. Venn had not even noticed it was missing.

"Leave it there," he said.

Deshi ignored him and crossed to Mava's mat. Peyam stayed by the doorway like a witness who had no intention of speaking unless asked.

"You should have it before the damp gets in your shoulders," Deshi said, laying the shawl over Mava's knees.

Mava touched the edge of it. "Thank you."

Deshi straightened with a hand at her own back. Age showed in the movement for the first time all day. Then she looked at Venn.

"Peyam wants to sit with her after evening prayer if she allows it," she said. "If she wants quiet, we'll leave her quiet. If she wants to talk, she'll talk."

"We've had enough visitors."

"That is not your choice alone."

It was the calmness of the sentence that struck hardest. No shout. No sanctimony. Just flat refusal to let him make the room smaller than it now was.

Mava said, before he could answer, "He can come."

Peyam bowed his head once in acceptance, as if this had been the entire matter.

Deshi looked at Venn again. "I did not cross that hall to steal anybody. I crossed because she broke open in front of me. There is no law worth much that asks a person to step back from that."

Then she turned and went out. Peyam followed her. The partition fell closed behind them.

No one spoke for several seconds. Rain thudded on the roof. Outside, somebody called for a missing cup.

Venn had the absurd urge to go after Deshi and drag the argument into the courtyard where it could at least belong to one afternoon instead of seeping into the whole hill. But the children had heard enough, and rage performed in front of children was its own kind of surrender. He knew that from his father too.

"Rest," he said at last to Mava. "All of you who can, rest."

It was not an ending. It was only the least broken order he could still give.

The room loosened without returning to normal. Rem took the younger ones to the far mat with a game of knucklebones and too-bright gentleness. Osh ducked out to fetch dry wood for the evening fire. Ger sat near the doorway and stared through the partition cloth as if trying to see the shape of the conversations happening beyond it. Habel eased Ori down beside Mava and stroked the girl's hair until her breathing slowed. Arash remained standing.

Venn waited until the younger ones had drifted far enough into their own noises that the room would not remember every word.

"You undermined me in there," he said.

Arash did not pretend to misunderstand. "I stood with Mava."

"You stood with your hands in your pockets while the room looked at our family as if it had come half unstitched."

"Because it had."

The answer landed so cleanly Venn almost missed its force.

"Watch yourself," he said.

Arash's jaw flexed. "I am."

"Then act like a man with a road ahead of him."

"I do."

"Do you?" Venn stepped closer, keeping his voice low enough that it would not spill to the children. "There is a house waiting on the wedding road. There are people there taking this delay as misfortune, not insult. Every day the road stays closed, we spend more of that good will. Every hour this hill teaches people to talk about us as if we belong to their stories, we spend more. You think that does not matter because you do not carry it."

Arash did not step back. "I carry plenty."

"Not enough."

For a second Venn thought the boy would finally raise his voice. Instead Arash spoke even more quietly.

"You keep saying wedding as if the word itself clears mud," he said. "It doesn't. And shouting over Mava won't either."

"I was not shouting at Mava."

"The room couldn't tell the difference."

That one got through. Venn felt it in his chest, a physical blow from the child he had taught to stand straight, to keep count, to answer for the family when called.

"What do you believe happened?" he asked.

Arash rubbed a hand over his mouth. "I believe Asha said a word she shouldn't know. I believe Mava heard it. I believe Deshi heard enough to move. That's already more trouble than I know what to do with."

"That is not an answer."

"It's the only honest one I've got."

Honest. The word made Venn want to strike something. As if honesty were simple. As if keeping twelve people moving toward a future required less truth than standing in a hall and naming whatever broke your heart.

He looked away first. That, too, was a small defeat, but the room was full of them now and one more scarcely changed the shape of the day.

By late afternoon the rain eased to a fine steady mist. The courtyard darkened early under the weather. Supper was thinner than noon, the grain stretched with more water than anyone admitted. Venn ate it sitting beside Tila while the children leaned heavy with exhaustion and the adults kept their voices down out of habit, not peace. Deshi and Peyam did come after evening prayer. They sat with Mava near the back wall of the hall, and nobody stopped them. Suna kept Asha to the far side and did not once bring the child near, but once, when the little girl twisted to look toward Mava, Deshi's face changed in a way Venn could not read. Not tenderness exactly. Recognition, maybe, and alarm riding under it.

The hour after supper should have belonged to the winding down of a normal day: washing bowls, settling mats, the final checking of latches though there was nothing to lock. Instead the whole compound carried the tautness of a house after somebody had slapped somebody else at a feast. No one wanted another scene. No one believed the scene was over.

Venn went back to the courtyard wall when the lamps were lit. The mist silvered in the dark beyond the eaves. Down below, the flood sounded wider at night. He could no longer see the gauging stone, only the broad, wrong sheen where the road ought to be.

After a while Arash came and stood beside him. Not close enough to touch. Not far enough to call it avoidance.

For several breaths they listened to the water.

"Nineteen days," Venn said.

Arash did not pretend not to know what he meant. "I heard you the first time."

"Did you."

"Yes."

Venn kept his eyes on the valley. "When the road opens, we move that morning. No delays. No extra visits. No more of this room making a lesson out of us."

Arash was quiet long enough that Venn turned to look at him.

"If the road opens, we move," Arash said.

"When."

Arash did not correct him. That small mercy irritated Venn almost as much as contradiction would have.

"When," Arash said.

From inside the hall came the low braid of voices that had haunted the whole day: Mava rough and thinning with fatigue, Deshi sharp and dry, Peyam's deeper murmur only now and then. They were not arguing. They were doing something worse in Venn's mind. They were getting familiar.

"You hear that?" Venn said.

"Yes."

"That is exactly what I mean."

Arash leaned his forearms on the wall. "You mean people talking."

"I mean my aunt's grief becoming common property."

"Maybe grief doesn't stay private just because you order it to."

Venn let the sentence sit between them. There were answers to it, dozens of them. None felt solid enough to use. He had spent the day learning how quickly a room could make his best language sound like stubbornness dressed in family duty.

"When I was twelve," he said, surprising himself, "I helped wash Iru's foot."

Arash turned. In the lamp spill his face lost some of its hardness and showed its age again, which was to say not enough.

Venn went on because once he had started, stopping would have made the admission theatrical, and he hated theater.

"No one told me to leave the room. They were too busy. Mud was caked up under her heel. I remember thinking if I got the mud off, she'd look less dead." He swallowed. "That was forty years ago and I can still feel the skin."

Arash said nothing. Venn was grateful for that too. Sympathy from his son would have been harder to bear than silence.

"So do not tell me I am speaking from distance," Venn said. "I know exactly what road that was. I know what it took from Mava. I know what it took from the family. I will not stand here and hand it over because a child repeated a word."

Arash looked back toward the hall. "Nobody can take Iru from us."

"You say that because you have not spent your life learning how easily people take what is not theirs and call it a new order."

The political trouble in the lowlands had made that lesson plain in a hundred ways: men reassigned to roads they never chose, shops shut for lacking the right paper, boys held overnight because a name was misspelled somewhere upstream of a checkpoint. Venn did not have to explain the rest. Arash had seen enough of the same world to follow the line.

Still, his son said, "This isn't a checkpoint."

"No. It is worse. A checkpoint only wants an answer. This place wants confession."

Arash gave the smallest shake of his head. "Maybe it wants witness."

Venn almost laughed at that, but there was no humor in him.

"Witness is what people call it when they approve of what is being done," he said.

Neither of them spoke after that. The flood kept roaring. In the hall, Mava's voice dropped low enough to lose the words, but not the fact of being heard. Somewhere a child coughed in sleep. Tila crossed the courtyard once with folded blankets in her arms and did not interrupt them.

At last Arash said, "I'm going in."

Venn nodded without turning. Arash waited half a second, maybe for something more, then went.

Venn stayed at the wall until the lamps in the hall burned lower. He counted without looking, the old habit moving in him on its own: Tila in the sleeping room, Mava under her blanket, Habel and Ori beside her, Ger, Arash, Rem, Osh, the younger ones settled in a row. There were still twelve Kondos under one roof. He could reach the number cleanly every time.

From inside, Mava's voice carried once more through the thin partition, and another family's old woman answered as if she had every right. By tomorrow the whole hill would still be turning it over in low voices, the same way they worried floodwater and road crews and any other trouble that looked built to last.
