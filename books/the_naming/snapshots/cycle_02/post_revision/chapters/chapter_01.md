# Chapter 1

The rain found the seams in Suna's shawl before they were halfway up the switchback. Kael walked ahead, his sandals slapping the stone steps with a determination she recognized as bravery; he was eight and trying to look steady for his sisters. Behind her, Deshi climbed with one hand on Peyam's elbow, breathing hard between gusts, and Suna could hear the older woman's complaints building: the mud, the steepness, the weight of the bags they had dragged from the truck before leaving it where the water covered the wheels.

Asha rode on Suna's hip. She was four years old and too heavy for this climb, but she had stopped walking twenty minutes ago, and there was no fight Suna could win against that kind of exhaustion. The canvas shoulder bag dug across Suna's ribs on the opposite side. She kept one palm over it whenever the path narrowed, because the little money left, the children's school papers, and Sev's folded note were inside with Asha's spare dress. She told herself she was protecting the money. The note was what her hand checked for.

On any other journey she and Sev would have traded the children between them without speaking. His hands would have come back for Asha when Suna's left arm went numb, and she would have taken one of the sacks from him in return. Her body still made room for the handoff. It never came. She shifted Asha higher, hitched the bag strap back onto her shoulder, and kept climbing.

Asha's fingers found the wet collar of Suna's blouse and rubbed the fabric between thumb and forefinger. She touched everything. She touched buttons, bark, wooden spoons, and the hems of strangers' clothes when Suna was not fast enough to pull her back. Now she touched the rain, palm open, catching what she could and licking it from her skin.

"Rimi," Suna said. "Stay with Kael."

"I'm with Kael."

"Where I can see you."

Rimi moved one step closer to her brother. She was twelve, angular, already carrying her father's long face, and Suna would have been grateful for the obedience if it had not meant the girl was too frightened to argue.

The shrine came through the rain at the last bend: stone walls the color of wet iron, a covered walkway jutting from the entrance, and behind it the larger compound on the hilltop. Bodies already crowded the overhang. Bags leaned against the wall. A child complained in a bright, relentless voice. Other families had reached shelter first.

Peyam said something to Deshi that the wind took. Deshi answered loudly enough for everyone near them to hear. "Of course there are others. Where else would they go?"

There was nowhere else. Below them, where the seven roads should have met, there was only water. The gauging stone at the third bend had disappeared. Brown, silt-heavy floodwater pushed through the valley with enough force to erase its own edges. Suna could see the roof of a truck at the waterline, a side mirror catching the last dirty light, and a road sign half under. The flood slid into the trees on both sides and vanished around the far bend. Its sound climbed the hill under the rain, steady and pressurized, another weather laid beneath the first.

"Mama," Asha said, and pressed her wet palm to Suna's cheek.

"I know," Suna said. "We're here."

A lean man stood at the mouth of the walkway, rain darkening his shoulders. He did not wave them in. He did not step forward to take a bag. He simply waited until they crossed under the roof and the rain stopped striking their faces.

"How many in the family?" he asked.

Suna set Asha down. Her back flared with relief. "Six," she said, and the old reflex that still counted Sev made the correction scrape her throat. "Family name Mera."

"Given names."

"Suna. Rimi. Kael. Asha. Deshi. Peyam."

His eyes moved across them as she spoke, landing on each face in turn. "Your destination before the road closed."

Suna felt the square of folded paper through the wet canvas under her hand. "Gelden. My husband went ahead. We're meeting him." The lie came out flat. She had said it too many times to hear the seam in it, but her body still knew where it split; the skin between her shoulders went tight every time.

"I am Olet. Head custodian of this shrine. You will have a place to sleep and food. There are rules. You will hear them when everyone has arrived."

"How many are here?"

"You make seven families."

Seven families. Suna looked past him into the hall. The hall had a stone floor, stone walls, and above the main span a narrow upper gallery under a ceiling smoked dark by old lamps. It was large enough for the bodies and too small for the distance they would want between them. On the far side, a wide arch opened over the valley, and the flood filled it like a second wall. Tree crowns marked the buried roads. The sound came through the arch and settled into the hall. It would remain when the rain eased, and it would remain after dark. It was the sound of being kept.

Oil lamps already burned along the walls. On the gallery, an angular woman with rough hands moved from bracket to bracket, tending them with quick economy. She gave Suna one short, measuring look from above and kept moving. Two younger custodians stacked mats against the west wall and took wet bundles from the newest arrivals without speaking.

Suna counted anyway, but she counted composition instead of total because total kept sliding. She marked the children first, then the elders, then the men who looked ready to start trouble once the room got tighter. She marked the women who already had a grip on the practical center of things. She marked who was sick, who was frightened, and who was pretending not to be either. Counting people was easier than counting the fact that Sev was not among them.

Olet kept the threshold moving. The same three questions went to each family, and the answers gave Suna something firm to fasten onto.

The broad man at the south wall answered "Kondo" with clipped impatience and kept speaking while Olet wrote. He was Venn Kondo, and he said the name as if everybody in the hall ought to have heard it before. His wife, Tila, was saving an embroidered wedding cloth from the rain by draping it over her forearms instead of packing it away where the border work might crease. A younger man named Arash hauled a second sack in from the walkway and kept one hand ready at the elbow of the elderly aunt, Mava, who sat on a bundle rubbing her left ear with her palm in slow circles. Another woman, Habel, snapped two boys out of a puddle with one look. A narrow-faced girl called Ori kept turning to stare back down the switchback at the drowned road. The wedding cloth, the old aunt's circling hand, and Venn's certainty fixed the family in Suna's mind before Olet finished writing their destination.

Near the entrance, the thin young father said "Parra" so quietly Olet made him repeat it. The second time he said it louder, but not by much. Jin Parra lowered a sleeping girl from his back while Olet wrote. Her name was Melie. He had one bag, which he set between his feet, and he curved his body around the child until the rest of the hall had to go around him.

The man in glasses came next with a baby strapped to his chest. He said "Okoye" in the voice of someone who expected names to matter because they could be checked. Bram Okoye tried to ask how long the road would stay underwater before Olet had reached the destination question. Olet took the name, not the question. Bram's wife, Nessa, pulled a ten-year-old boy, Tomas, through the doorway before he could stop to stare at the flood. The teenage girl, Leni, took in the room in one fast sweep and arranged her face into boredom so deliberate it was almost insolent. The baby on Bram's chest was Ada, and she had the damp, furious dignity of somebody too young to understand why the whole world smelled wrong.

The Dao family clogged the walkway because the woman answering for them would not let the children string out on the steps. "Dao," she said, and then, when Olet asked for names, she gave "Hala" before naming the rest. She kept three children moving with a hand between shoulder blades and her body angled so that the rain could not split them. Beside her, Bett Dao stood once beneath the arch and listened to the flood as if it had spoken directly to her. She had a guarded face and the compact, weathered hands of somebody who could fix almost anything given the right tool. An older man with the same jawline shepherded the smaller children in under the roof while a long-limbed girl drifted a pace ahead and then back again. Hala kept talking. Bett kept listening. Suna remembered both.

After them came the Leo family with the concentrated quiet of people used to being noticed for the wrong reasons. Cade Leo answered the questions himself, watchful even while he stood still. His partner Wynn stayed close enough that their shoulders touched. Cade's mother, Vera, carried a canvas bag like it contained something breakable or valuable or both, and the boy beside them, Pik, kept so near the adults that his sleeve never stopped brushing one of them.

The last family took the longest because the oldest woman would not be hurried. Olet wrote "Tesh" while the son answered for them in a respectful, anxious voice. The old woman was Gitta Tesh: small, white-haired, exact, and so deliberate with her feet that each step looked chosen twice. Her son Miro stayed beside her without crowding. His wife Enn carried two bundles and said nothing at all. A tall teenage boy named Fen hovered behind them with both hands half raised as if he expected Gitta to slip even when she plainly would not. The girl behind him, Kiri, kept turning to look down the switchback at the drowned road.

"Suna." Deshi had found a dry strip of floor and was easing herself down with one hand braced against the stone. Peyam bent beside her, careful with his knees, and drew his prayer beads from his pocket as soon as he sat.

"Rimi, Kael, come here," Suna said. "Asha, don't."

She was too late. Asha had crossed the wet stones and laid her hand on the Kondo wedding cloth, fingers spreading into the heavy embroidery.

Tila Kondo looked up first. Her face was tired past annoyance. "Easy, little one."

"That's not ours," Suna said, catching Asha's wrist.

"It's all right," Tila said, but she lifted the cloth higher, out of reach. Venn glanced over, judged the incident too small to deserve his voice, and went back to counting children while Habel stripped a soaked shirt off one of the boys and wrung it hard into the puddle.

By the time Gitta crossed under the roof, the hall held forty-three stranded travelers, their wet bags, their children, their impatience, and the steady rush from the valley below.

Olet waited until the path stayed empty. Then he walked to the center of the hall and spoke.

He did not raise his voice. He spoke into the noise and the noise dropped around him.

"This is a transit shrine. It sits at the meeting of seven roads and has served travelers longer than records count. You are welcome here. You will be fed from our stores. You will be given places to sleep. In return, you will follow the shrine's schedule. Meals are communal. Labor is shared. There is a daily gathering. You will hear more tomorrow."

"If the road's under till morning, that's one kind of problem," Bram Okoye said. "If it's longer, people need to plan differently. Which is it?"

Olet turned to him. "The road opens when the water drops. The water drops when the rain stops. The rain will stop when it stops."

From the south side came a hard breath that almost passed for a laugh. Venn Kondo had folded his arms across his chest and was staring at Olet with the patience already gone out of him.

"Sleeping quarters," Olet said.

He had a system. Suna saw that at once. He did not ask what anyone wanted. He pointed. Families moved.

"The Kondo family. The large room on the south side."

Venn moved first. At the doorway he stopped and turned back. "My family is twelve. My aunt is seventy-three. We don't put our elders in corners and tell children to make do around them. If this is the Kondo room, I want to hear what provision you're making for us."

"It is the largest room."

"I heard 'largest.' I asked whether the shrine had another arrangement for a family our size."

"It is the largest room available, and it has been assigned to the largest family."

Venn held there a moment longer. Tila touched the middle of his back. He went in. Arash took Mava's elbow and helped her over the threshold while Habel drove the wettest children in ahead of her. Ori was the last to turn away from the flood.

"The Okoye family. The northeast corner of the main hall."

Bram made a small turn of both hands, and Suna saw a private argument gathering itself. Nessa looked at the corner and then at the baby. The space lay open on two sides and had no door and no wall worth the name. Even so, Bram took Tomas by the shoulder and led them there. Leni trailed after them with her same practiced indifference, now tighter around the mouth. Ada had begun to cry in short, offended bursts.

"The Mera family. The room beside the kitchen."

"Beside the kitchen?" Deshi said. "So our blankets take smoke all night and by morning everything we own smells like old onions and ash. That's the room?"

"It is what is available."

One of the younger custodians bent for Suna's shoulder bag. "We'll carry our own," she said, quickly enough that Deshi looked at her. The bag held the money, the papers, and Sev's note. She could not have said why that mattered to a stranger more than to Deshi or Peyam, but it did.

Deshi looked at Suna as if the answer had come from her. Suna picked up their bags.

"The Parra family. The alcove by the east wall."

Jin stood, shifted Melie onto his hip without waking her, and went there without a word. The alcove was barely deep enough for two mats, but he set his bag behind the girl as a backstop and sat with one knee up, closing the open side with his body.

"The Tesh family. The west annex."

Gitta rose. Miro offered his arm. She took it and moved off at her same measured pace while Enn gathered the smaller bundles and Fen took the heaviest one before anyone asked him to. Kiri followed, looking over her shoulder at the doorway until the annex swallowed them.

"The Dao family. The room past the cistern."

Hala counted heads under her breath, touched Bett's elbow, and steered the children down the passage. Bett gave the flood one last look before she followed. The older man closed the rear of the group, keeping the smallest child close enough that the girl's shoulder knocked his knee every other step.

"The Leo family. The alcove near the prayer courtyard."

Cade did not move immediately. "Is there a curtain there?"

"No."

Cade glanced at Wynn, at Vera, at Pik standing close against his leg. Then he nodded once and led them toward the courtyard anyway.

"These are your spaces," Olet said. "Arrange them as you wish. The kitchen area is communal. Water is from the cistern. The prayer courtyard is open to all. The custodians' quarters are not. If you need something, ask."

"If the only radio is in your quarters, then the news only comes through you," Bram said from the northeast corner. "That matters when people are deciding what they're walking into."

"There is a radio. It is in the custodians' quarters. We will share what news comes."

"Can we hear it ourselves, then, so we're deciding from the same facts?"

Olet let the question finish. "The custodians will share what news comes."

Bram's hands turned again. Leni watched the exchange with bright, merciless attention. When she caught Suna looking, she dropped her gaze and began untying a bundle with movements too sharp to be careless.

The kitchen room was small but dry. Cold stone met Suna's feet. A slot of a window opened onto the kitchen overhang, and ash smell and old smoke were already coming through it.

"Six of us," Deshi said from the doorway. "In this. You'd call a pantry crowded before you'd call this a room."

"We'll manage." Suna laid out mats from the stack in the hall and counted as she worked so that her hands did not start shaking: three for the children, one for Deshi, one for Peyam, and one for herself. The room was four meters by three, perhaps. With her arms spread she could nearly touch both walls.

"The large family has a room three times this size. They can turn around in there without stepping on somebody's hands."

"They're twelve."

"We're six. That's not a sleeping arrangement. That's storage."

Suna opened the food bag and counted what mattered. They had two bags of rice, dried lentils, salt, and the tin of oil that had leaked in the truck and turned the inside of one sack rancid. If the custodians fed them, the ruined oil would not matter. If they did not, it would matter fast. She slid the smaller canvas bag, the one with the note inside, under the rolled blanket at the head of her mat before she did anything else.

"Peyam needs space for his knees," Deshi said. "He's not a folded blanket you can shove on a shelf. You know that."

"I know."

Suna set Peyam's mat along the far wall, where he could stretch without anyone stepping over him. She put Deshi beside him, because the older woman would need the wall to lever herself up in the morning. She placed the children's mats in a line between the elders and the door. If Sev had still been there, habit would have put him nearest the entrance. Habit had not carried the sacks up the switchback or answered Olet or kept the lie standing for three months. She took the mat by the door herself.

Deshi saw where she put it. "Your husband could have used his strong back for something more useful than going ahead, if going ahead is what he did."

The words struck low and clean. Suna kept smoothing the edge of Kael's mat even after it lay flat. "He isn't here," she said.

"No," Deshi said. "He isn't."

Suna went back into the hall for another blanket, a water bucket, and one of the clay bowls stacked by the kitchen wall. The shrine had already begun rearranging everybody around itself.

Inside the Kondo room, Venn was still arguing, though the argument had shrunk to the domestic scale of where people would sleep. Tila had hung the wedding cloth over the driest part of the wall so the border could drip without touching the floor. Habel stripped wet shirts off the youngest boys and wrung them into a basin. Arash unknotted sacks with his mouth set tight, glancing every few moments toward Mava, who had been settled on a folded blanket and had not stopped rubbing her left ear. Ori stood in the doorway until Venn told her to move twice.

At the Okoye corner, Bram had rigged a line between a wall peg and a pillar so their damp clothes could hang higher than the baby could kick them. Nessa had Ada against her shoulder now and was patting her back in a rhythm that matched the rain. Tomas crouched at the open edge trying to see the flood without leaning far enough to earn a rebuke. Leni sat cross-legged on a rolled mat, not helping, though Suna suspected she had already finished one more task than anybody had noticed.

Jin Parra had made his alcove into a wall around Melie. He had spread one blanket under her and another over her, though his own shoulders were still wet. When one of the assistants offered him a second mat, he nodded once without looking up. The gratitude on his face was so quickly hidden it barely counted as expression.

The west annex was quieter. Enn Tesh stacked their bundles by size and kept the food farthest from the damp wall. Fen had already chosen the place where Gitta would step down least awkwardly in the night. Miro tested the floor with his heel before setting each mat. Kiri hovered at the threshold until Gitta told her, in a voice too soft for Suna to catch, to come inside. Kiri obeyed at once.

Past the cistern, Hala Dao had the look of a woman who could have taken command of a sinking boat. She was hanging wet shawls near the passage where the air moved best while the children stood in a line she had made out of pure authority. Bett crouched by an open sack, checking food tins for water seep. She held each lid to the light before setting it down. The older man had lowered himself onto the threshold and was rubbing his calves through his trousers with an absent, pained concentration.

Near the prayer courtyard, Cade Leo had solved the curtain problem without Olet's help. Vera held one end of a length of cord while Wynn knotted the other to a hook in the wall. They had turned two shawls and a rain sheet into a hanging screen that gave the alcove privacy without shutting out air. Pik sat on the rolled bedding inside the little enclosure and watched every hand that moved above him. When the screen held, he let his shoulders drop.

By the time Suna returned to the kitchen room, the compound already sounded like it had been full for weeks. Children cried as if they had always cried there. Wet clothes dripped as if they had always dripped from those pegs. Nobody would have believed that, an hour earlier, the hall had belonged only to stone, lamps, and custodians.

Deshi took one look at the extra blanket and decided not to ask where Suna had found it. That was as close to approval as the older woman ever came. Suna set the bucket by the door, tucked the clay bowl into the food bag, and listened to the room tighten around the shape of six bodies and one lie. The kitchen smell deepened as fires were lit outside. The flood pressed at the window slot with its endless voice. There was no date on the end of any of it.

By nightfall the rain still had not let go. It came in long runs, hammering the roof until the lamps thinned, then easing just enough to remind them what quiet was before starting again. The shrine answered with groans from its beams and corridors.

Suna fed her children from their own supplies: rice cooked in the communal kitchen, lentils, and a scrap of pickle that Deshi cut into slivers and placed on each mound of rice with medicinal care. The kitchen was the most exposed part of the compound, a stone counter and fire pit under a deep overhang that blocked the worst of the rain but none of the wind. Water blew in sideways whenever the gusts changed. Smoke flattened under the roof and then twisted out toward the valley.

Hala Dao had taken command of one side of the counter without announcing it. She did not need to. Her hands moved with the authority of somebody who had cooked for too many people for too many years to waste a motion on asking permission. When Suna came in with her pot and rice sack, Hala shifted a chopping board, made room near the fire, and asked only, "How many?"

"Six."

Hala measured the answer with one glance. "You'll want the hotter spot, then. This side catches less wind."

It was the kind of kindness that did not make a scene of itself. Suna took it. "Thank you."

That was most of their talk. They made room for each other with glances and brief touches of the hand. Hala split an onion with the heel of her palm guiding the knife, and Suna felt a jolt of recognition so sharp it made her look up. Her own mother had cut onions that way, heel of the hand against the blade spine as if the knife were another kitchen tool that answered to pressure and not caution. Hala saw the look and lifted one shoulder.

"Bad knife," she said.

"Still works."

"Everything still works until it doesn't."

Bett came in and out of the kitchen without ever seeming to join it. She set down a dented tin, checked the sky, moved a pot farther from the edge where the rain blew in, and left again. Once she crouched to retie a child's sandal with quick, competent fingers and stood before the child had finished crying. Suna understood why Hala spoke for the family and why Bett did not need to.

Beyond the overhang, the compound arranged itself around food. Bram stood under the eaves arguing with one of the younger custodians in a voice he was trying to keep civil. The argument looked familiar even before Suna heard the words. He wanted to know whether the radio had said anything more about the roads or the checkpoints in the lowlands. The young custodian only repeated that Olet would share any news. Bram's sentences kept lengthening as the other man's did not. Nessa stood nearby with Ada on her shoulder and did not interrupt, though her face said she wished the answer were different. Tomas had found Kael and was showing him a beetle stranded in a bucket lid. Leni leaned against a post pretending not to watch anyone while watching everybody.

At the far end of the overhang, Cade and Wynn fed Pik from the same bowl, passing it between them so the boy never had to step into the press of strangers. Vera ate last and kept turning her head each time somebody came too near the hanging shawl that screened their alcove. Inside the Kondo room, voices rose and dropped as sleeping order became an argument large enough for Venn to take personally. The wet wedding cloth hung high on the wall now, dark and heavy as a banner.

Suna stirred the rice and tried not to imagine Sev in some dry room with only his own hunger to manage. The thought arrived anyway, oily and unwanted. She turned the flame down too late and smelled the first scorch at the bottom of the pot.

"Lift it," Hala said at once.

Suna lifted it. The worst of the burn stayed at the bottom. "I had it."

Hala nodded as if that was true enough to let stand. "You were listening somewhere else."

Suna almost said, No, I was counting. Instead she tipped lentils into the second pot and watched them disappear into the boil.

When the food was ready, Deshi came to inspect it with the insulted gravity of a queen examining tribute. "Too much water," she said.

"Then it will stretch."

"Everything stretches when the room is small."

Peyam took his bowl without complaint and went into the prayer courtyard, open to the sky, where he stood barefoot on the wet stone and prayed before he ate. Suna could see him from the kitchen overhang: thin back straight, beads hanging from one hand, rain running down his face. He had not asked whether prayer was allowed. He had found the courtyard, taken off his sandals, and begun. When he finished, he lifted his face into the rain for one quiet breath, then put his sandals back on and came inside to his food gone lukewarm.

They ate in pieces because there was no room and no agreed custom yet for doing otherwise. Kael and Tomas sat on their haunches near the wall and shoveled rice with identical seriousness, though they had not spoken enough to count as friends. Rimi ate standing up because Asha had taken her place on the upturned bucket. Deshi clicked her tongue at that but let it happen. Melie Parra, awake now, stood in Jin's alcove with both hands on the edge of her bowl while he blew on each bite before passing it to her. In the Dao room, Hala had settled three children around one pot and was eating only after they had slowed. Bett was nowhere Suna could see. Perhaps she ate later. Perhaps she forgot.

When Suna carried the washed pot back from the cistern, Nessa Okoye was there rinsing Ada's cloth. The baby had fallen asleep against her chest, mouth open, one hand caught in her mother's neckline.

"Your little one touches everything," Nessa said quietly, not as criticism.

"She does."

Nessa wrung the cloth and glanced toward the kitchen room. "Six of you in there?"

"Six."

"That's hard."

Suna heard the other question waiting behind it and answered the version that had not been spoken. "My husband went ahead to Gelden."

Nessa's face changed by less than a breath. "Then I hope he found somewhere dry."

"So do I," Suna said.

It was not the hardest lie she had told. That made it worse.

After dinner, if you could call it that, Suna washed the children's faces and hands at the cistern. Kael submitted. Rimi turned her back to the room and did it herself, guarding the small privacy she had begun claiming six months ago. Asha made the washing into another investigation. She rubbed Suna's wet wrist. She pressed her palm to the cool stone rim. She tasted the water.

"Don't drink that, Asha."

Suna was wringing out the cloth when Asha reached past her and touched the face of a boy waiting his turn.

He belonged to the Kondo wedding family, nine or ten perhaps, round-cheeked and still rubbing at the mark his wet collar had left on his neck. He blinked in surprise and then stood still for it, as if he could not decide whether he had been insulted or chosen.

"Sorry," Suna said, catching Asha's wrist. "She does that."

"It's fine," the boy said. He touched his own cheek once, curious now, and went back toward the Kondo room where Habel was calling for somebody named Ori and getting no answer.

Asha watched him leave with her hand still open, fingers curled around nothing.

The shrine darkened by layers. The lamps brightened. The rain went black. Somebody laughed once in the hall and then stopped, as if laughter itself had surprised the room. Olet made one final round through the sleeping spaces. The angular woman who had tended the lamps followed him at a distance carrying more oil. Neither of them checked whether anybody was comfortable. They checked only whether the shrine was running as they expected it to run.

Their room was lamplight, stone, and six people packed tighter than sleep allowed. Peyam fell asleep almost as soon as he lay down. His beads clicked once and then stopped. Deshi turned her back to the room, though Suna could hear from her breathing that she was still awake.

Suna settled the children in order. Kael lay on his back with his knees up, as he had done since he was three. Rimi turned to the wall and breathed too evenly. Asha came last. Even with her eyes closed, her hands kept moving over the mat, the blanket seam, and the weave of the cotton. She found Suna's sleeve and worried the hem between thumb and forefinger until her breathing lengthened. The fingers slowed, paused, then searched once more before finally going still.

Suna lay on her back and did not sleep.

The compound was louder now that everyone was trying to be quiet. Venn Kondo talked low and hard in his room, and every now and then Tila answered in a voice too soft for the words to carry. Ada Okoye cried because the air, the stone, the people, and all of it smelled wrong. Jin murmured to Melie in the east alcove. Gitta coughed once in the annex and then the Tesh corner went still again. Bare feet moved through the corridor. Somewhere a pot lid shifted and settled.

Under all of it, the flood filled the silences from below with patient weight.

"Mama." Kael was nearly asleep, his voice gone small. "When are we going to Papa?"

"When the road opens."

"When does the road open?"

"When the water goes down."

"When does the water go down?"

"Sleep, Kael."

He did. Children could do that. They could take an answer with holes in it and drift anyway. Rimi stayed awake beside him. Suna could tell from the held steadiness of her breathing. At twelve, Rimi was already rehearsing calm for the people around her. She had her father's long face and, Suna feared, his talent for silence when silence meant nothing good.

Suna pressed her hand to the floor beside the mat. The stone was cold and clean and indifferent. She kept her palm there until the cold climbed into her wrist.

In the morning, or in two days, or whenever the rain stopped and the roads came back out of the flood, she would take the children to Gelden. She would go to her husband's family and explain. That was the plan. It had been the plan since he left, not since he went ahead. He had left.

The fact sat in her body like swallowed metal. He had not gone to prepare the house or find work. He had packed a bag while the children were at school and Deshi was at the market and Peyam was praying. He had taken his good shoes and the money from the tin above the stove. He had left a note that said *I'm sorry, I can't do this.* Suna had read it once, folded it down to a square no bigger than a stamp, put it in her pocket, and gone to collect the children from school. She had bought half the onions she meant to buy because her hand could not work simple numbers in the market. She had burned the first pot of rice that evening. She had still set the table for six before she remembered there were only five of them home.

That night she had told Deshi that her son had gone ahead to Gelden to prepare. Deshi had looked at her, unreadable, and said, "Without telling me?"

"It was sudden," Suna had answered. "He wanted to get ahead of the rains."

Three months had passed under that word. For three months Kael had asked when they were going to Papa and Suna had said soon. For three months Rimi had watched her with that old, evaluating face. For three months Deshi's questions had worn at the walls of the lie without ever knocking them down. Suna had kept a marriage standing by habit and grammar. She had used the present tense like scaffolding. She had pointed the children toward a man who was nowhere at the end of the road.

Now the road itself was gone. The shrine held thirty-seven strangers around her, plus her own five. Olet had given them a room the size of an animal stall and spoken with the finality of a man who had watched too many families come apart indoors. At the far end of this journey, whenever the flood released it, no husband would be waiting. There would only be his family's house and their faces when they understood what he had done. There would be the pause before the first question. There would be Deshi hearing the truth out loud at last.

Panic never came as a wave. It came as a list: what she owed, what she could not pay, rice for maybe four days if she stretched it, Kael's sandals thinning at the sole, Rimi's silence getting longer, Asha on other people's things and other people's faces, Peyam's knees, Deshi's questions, the lie about the husband, the money running out, the road underwater, and the note in the bag. She still carried it in the inner pocket with the children's things, and sometimes she touched the folded square the way Peyam touched his first bead, not from hope but to confirm the damage was still there and still shaped the world around it.

She rolled carefully onto one elbow and reached under the blanket at the head of her mat until her fingers found the canvas bag. The room smelled of damp cloth, old smoke, and the sour edge of too many sleeping bodies. Her fingertips located the note by its hard little corners through the cloth. She did not take it out. She knew the pressure of the fold. She knew the cramped slope of his handwriting. She knew that if she unfolded it again the words would not improve.

Somewhere in the compound, a woman started humming. The melody was low and repetitive, turning back on itself until it settled into the air. It was for another child in another room. Even so, Suna listened. Beneath the panic and beneath the lie worn smooth by use, another feeling rose and embarrassed her with its force. It was not safety. Nothing that generous was on offer here. It was only relief, and even that came with shame attached. There were walls. There was a roof. There were other adults awake in the dark, listening for their own children. For one night, at least, she was held inside the same fear as everyone else and did not have to invent it by herself.

Asha's hand found hers. Small, warm, damp from sleep, the child's fingers closed around two of Suna's and rested there, reading even this.

The rain hammered the shrine. The flood kept talking in the valley. Suna closed her eyes and did not sleep, and outside the roads stayed gone.
