# Chapter 2

Fasa was wiping the lamp ledges again when the hall began to lighten, weak brass daylight pushing through the open side after a night of rain. She moved from ledge to ledge with a cloth, though the stone where the oil lamps would sit had been wiped yesterday and the day before that and every day for twenty years. The work was not cleaning. It was attention. Prayer required it. Diagnosis required it. So did any encounter that meant to get at the truth instead of merely passing through the motions of asking.

She set the cloth down and crouched over the first lamp. The wick was trimmed. The oil sat just below half. Her hands knew the sequence, which left her mind free for what it had been doing since yesterday's arrivals: sorting shapes.

Seven families. Forty-three people in Olet's ledger. They had come up the switchback through rain, carrying bundles wrapped in feed sacks, sleeping children, tin pots, one birdcage without a bird. Fasa had watched from the hall while Olet and the assistants received them below. She never went down to greet arrivals. Receiving belonged to Olet. Naming belonged to her.

What she had, before anyone spoke in the hall, were impressions only. The Kondos moved like a practiced column and took up space without apology. Suna Mera kept three children and two elders in motion with one pair of hands. Jin Parra carried his daughter as if the compound might try to keep her. Bram Okoye watched the walls, the exits, the custodians. Gitta Tesh answered registration questions before Olet had finished asking them. Among the Daos, Hala did the speaking, Bett none of it, and a man Hala called Kal kept two boys tucked close to his knees. The Leos formed a small square around their son and held it even while Cade struggled with the form. That was enough to begin with.

She straightened and set the last lamp in place. The floor had been swept. The benches made a loose half-circle around the worn patch of stone where she would stand. Beyond the open wall, the valley was still brown water and drowned road. The flood kept up its low, steady rush beneath the morning noises of the shrine. People stopped hearing it after an hour. Their bodies did not.

She had been wrong before. She had taken reserve for deceit, grief for calculation, composure for innocence. Twenty years of namings had not given her certainty. They had given her the discipline to keep looking after first impressions curdled or softened or proved false.

Olet's step sounded behind her, weight falling harder on the right foot as always.

"Are they ready?" she asked.

"They are here," he said. "I will ring the bell when you wish."

"What do you make of them?"

"Seven families stopped by water." His pauses never felt like hesitation. "The rest is your work."

He waited in the doorway. He could wait longer than anyone Fasa knew. The consistency of it had unsettled her once. Now it felt like weather.

The bell, when it rang, would not sound like invitation. Rice was already steaming in the kitchen. Children could smell it. Adults could smell it too. Fasa believed in the naming with the full stubborn weight of her twenty years, and belief did not make the bargain kinder. The road brought people here. The shrine put a question between them and breakfast and made them answer it in front of strangers.

She did not think the leverage was the heart of the rite. The heart was witness: the way a family arranged its own story once hunger, shame, pride, and other people's eyes were all in the room together. That was where the gaps showed. That was where language stiffened or thinned. That was where she learned what to return to later.

"Ring it," she said.

---

Olet struck the bell three times. The iron note carried through the kitchen overhang, past the cistern, into the sleeping rooms where mats were still warm from bodies. The sound cut the morning in two.

They entered with the wary order of people stepping into a clinic or a courtroom.

The Kondos came first, all twelve of them, Venn leading and Tila counting under her breath as they crossed the threshold. Mava lowered herself to the bench nearest the open wall and set her palm circling against her left ear at once. Arash lingered half a stride out of line, not enough for anyone to call it defiance, enough for Fasa to mark it.

The Meras followed close behind. Suna carried Asha on one hip and kept the other two children moving with a touch to the shoulder, a nudge at the back. Deshi entered with her chin up and Peyam with his prayer cap in both hands, turning it once before he sat.

The Okoyes came in a tight knot: Bram ahead, Nessa with baby Ada pressed high against her chest, Tomas looking from adult to adult, Leni already choosing the place nearest the door.

Jin Parra brought Melie in by the hand and did not look at anyone long enough to invite a greeting.

The Teshes were quiet enough that Kiri's sandal scraping stone sounded loud. Miro guided his family to the far bench. Enn kept hold of her braid. Fen stood until Gitta chose her seat, then sat only when she did.

The Daos filled more space than Fasa had expected at first glance. Hala entered with Raya and Sem. Kal came behind with his wife and their two boys, both children thin-legged and solemn. Bett was last, Nori at her side, the space between their bodies so exact it looked measured.

The Leos came last of all. Cade held Pik's hand. Wynn carried the canvas bag with the day's necessities. Vera followed them with her mouth already set against the room.

Forty-three family members in the hall, five custodians against the walls and doorway, rain beginning again outside. The lamps stayed dark; daylight was harsher than flame and better for seeing what people failed to hide.

Fasa stepped onto the worn patch of stone at the center. The room settled around her in small noises: Ada's wet breathing against Nessa's shoulder, a shoe scraping, the first hard taps of rain on the overhang, the flood beneath it all.

"My name is Fasa," she said. "I keep the naming at this shrine. Olet oversees the shrine's daily life. This part is mine."

The word mine was never ownership. It was burden.

"If you stay here longer than a night, you name yourselves. Your family name. Your number. The relations you claim. The road you came by and the road you mean to take when the water gives it back. One voice may speak for a family, or several may. I will ask when I need more. Say only what you will stand behind tomorrow."

Nobody shifted at the last sentence. The stillness in the room changed anyway.

"Who will begin?"

Venn Kondo was on his feet before the question had fully left her mouth. He planted himself as if the hall had been built to receive exactly this posture.

"The Kondo family stands twelve," he said. "Venn Kondo speaking. My wife Tila. My son Arash. My sister Habel and her daughter Ori. My aunt Mava. My brother Pol's house with mine. We are bound for Tessar. My son's wedding waits there."

At the word wedding, Arash's mouth flattened. His gaze stayed on the open wall and the rain beyond it.

"All traveling together for the marriage?" Fasa asked.

"All of us," Venn said.

He sat with the measured control of a man paying a tax he disliked and expected to be done with.

Suna Mera stood next. Asha reached for her skirt, and Deshi peeled the child's fingers loose without looking down.

"Suna Mera. Six. My mother-in-law Deshi. Her brother Peyam. My daughter Rimi. My son Kael. My youngest, Asha. We're headed south, to my sister's town."

"And your husband?"

Suna's hands locked together so tightly the cords stood out under the skin. Rimi's head came up. Kael stopped swinging his foot.

"He's ahead of us," Suna said. "We're meeting him there."

Deshi did not turn toward her. The old woman's stillness had the flat force of a slap.

"Thank you," Fasa said.

Bram Okoye rose before she called the next name. He stood as if the room contained students who might yet be persuaded.

"Bram Okoye. Five. My wife Nessa. My daughter Leni. My son Tomas. The baby is Ada."

"Where have you come from?"

"The capital." He drew breath through his nose. "There are checkpoint tables on the eastern roads now. Household declarations. Papers. Categories. We took the mountain road instead."

Nessa tightened her hold on Ada. Leni bent over her bitten thumbnail and worried it until the skin reddened.

"You taught?" Fasa asked.

"I did."

The past tense landed harder than the rest of his answer.

Jin Parra did not stand. He sat forward with both hands on his knees and spoke as if every extra word cost him something material.

"Jin Parra. Two. Me and my daughter, Melie."

Fasa waited.

"We are going north," he said after a moment. "To her mother's family."

"And her mother?"

His jaw seized hard enough that she heard the click when it let go.

"She died."

Three adults looked toward the child on the floor before they caught themselves. Melie kept braiding a bit of frayed string she had found between the stones.

"When?" Fasa asked.

"Three months ago."

"Thank you."

He nodded once, not gratitude, only acknowledgment that the required wound had been exposed and could be covered again.

Miro Tesh rose with one hand braced on the bench behind him.

"Miro Tesh. Five. My wife Enn. My daughter Kiri. Fen. My mother, Gitta. We were on our way to a burial in the valley before the road washed out."

Enn had wound her braid around her fist by the time he finished.

"And Fen?" Fasa asked.

Miro's glance went to his mother.

"He was raised under my roof."

Gitta lifted her face a fraction.

"Fen is my son's son," she said.

The sentence arrived clean. No drag, no excess, no tremor in it anywhere.

"Thank you," Fasa said.

Hala Dao stood with the abruptness of someone stepping between a blow and the body meant to take it. Kal shifted as if to stand beside her, but a single look from Hala kept him seated.

"Dao family. Nine. Me. My children Raya and Sem. My brother Kal, his wife, and their two boys. My sister Bett. Nori, Bett's girl. We're headed for the coast."

"And your husband?" Fasa asked.

"I'm not married."

Fasa turned to Bett. "And you?"

Bett met her eyes and kept her mouth shut.

Nori pressed both palms between her knees. One of Kal's boys whispered something to his mother and got hushed at once.

"Bett isn't married either," Hala said.

The words cost her. Fasa could hear it.

"Thank you," she said.

Cade Leo stood last. Wynn's thumb touched the inside of his wrist for a second and disappeared.

"Cade Leo. Four. Wynn is my partner. Pik is our son. Vera is my mother." He swallowed. "We left because the new forms want a family shaped one way, and we aren't shaped that way."

The word partner hit the room and stayed there. Deshi's mouth sharpened. Vera looked straight back at her until the expression went blank again.

"Pik starts school in the autumn," Wynn said. "We need to be settled before then."

"And the boy?" Fasa asked. "By birth, or adoption?"

Cade's answer came rougher than the rest. "He's ours. That's the true part."

Vera put her hand on Pik's head, not soothing him, staking ground.

"All right," Fasa said. "The first naming is heard."

The room shifted as soon as she said it. Before the bell, each family had kept its arrangement inside its own sleeping space. Now the arrangements sat in open air where forty-two other people could measure them.

Kael had heard his mother place an absent man ahead of them on the road and say it without blinking. Arash had heard his father bind him to a wedding before strangers. Bram had brought the state's language into the shrine's hall and made the likeness plain enough to sting. Jin had forced death past his teeth with his daughter three arm lengths away. Hala had spent her own breath covering Bett's refusal. Cade had offered the room a word for his family and watched the room weigh it. And Gitta had laid down a lie so smoothly it almost vanished into the stone.

This was what Fasa trusted in the naming. Not mercy. Not comfort. Pressure. Put people under witness, make them say who they were while food smoked from the kitchen and rain sealed the road and strangers listened hard enough to remember. The truth did not leap out whole. First came the strain around it: the child who looked up too fast, the partner whose hand flashed out and back, the old woman whose answer slid into place with no scrape at all.

Olet rang the bell once from the doorway to release them to the morning meal. Children moved first, hungry and relieved. Adults followed more slowly, sorting themselves back into units, each family reassembling around the version of itself it had just spoken aloud.

Jin was the last to leave. He held out his hand without speaking. Melie took it and came with him, the string braid dangling from her fist. He did not look back.

Fasa stayed where she was. Rain blurred the valley. The flood below remained brown, patient, and entirely unimpressed by what had been said in its hearing.

The next naming would not ask for more names. It would ask, meal by meal and morning by morning, whether the names already spoken could bear weight. Gossip would press against them. Children's attachments, children's refusals, ordinary hours in close quarters would press until some claims held and others split along the grain she had glimpsed this morning.

She folded the cloth. She pinched out the one lamp she had lit. Then she went toward the kitchen, where the smell of rice had thickened and forty-three people were about to take their first meal under the force of what they had said.
