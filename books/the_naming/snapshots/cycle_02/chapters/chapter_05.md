# Chapter 5

Venn counted before the bell. The night's new trouble had beaten him to the custodians. Twelve Kondos in the courtyard, twelve names under his breath, twelve bodies he meant to take off this hill the moment the road showed stone again. Arash sat beside him on the wall, long legs stretched out, jaw set toward the valley. There were eleven days until the wedding. Brown floodwater still ate across the switchbacks below, flattening the place where seven roads should have met cleanly. Every morning Venn made the same calculation, and every morning it came out the same: not yet.

The bell rang from the custodians' walkway. It sent a single iron note through stone.

"Everyone," Venn said.

They came at once. Tila came with the cloth wrap of needles and thread in her hand. Habel came with Ori tucked close against her hip, the girl still watching for Asha Mera whenever the families crossed paths. Mava crossed more slowly than the others, with Arash steady at her elbow. After supper the night before, Mava had told him the story in pieces, stopping twice to press her mouth flat before the sound got out. Her sister was Iru. She had been dead for forty years. She was twenty-nine when the cart went off the shrine road and dropped into the ravine. Nushi, the old valley word Iru had used only for Mava, still lived now only when Mava bent over Ori's fever or grief and forgot herself. Then Suna Mera's child had said it in her sleep. Venn had called it coincidence because the other answer would put his family inside someone else's claim.

Ger arrived behind Habel, then Rem and Osh, then the younger ones, subdued for once by the bell and the room it promised. There were twelve. All of them were here.

The naming hall was already full. Rain light washed in from the open side, gray and cold. Lamps burned along the inner wall. Bram Okoye leaned so far forward both palms were planted on his knees, ready for the room to give him an excuse. Jin Parra sat against the far wall with Melie in his lap, his face turned inward. Gitta Tesh's thumbs moved in their private circle. Hala Dao's shoulder was angled in front of Bett without seeming to be. The Leo family held themselves close together at the end of a bench. Fasa stood at the front with the valley at her back. Olet sat to one side, hands on his knees, like stone that had chosen the shape of a man.

Venn put the Kondos on the right, near the inner wall, in the arrangement that said what he needed it to say. Tila stood beside him and Arash held the place at his shoulder. Mava sat because standing too long made her knees buckle, with Habel and Ori close behind her. The others took the places they knew without asking.

Fasa began before he had fully settled.

"The naming continues," she said. "Four days ago each family spoke itself aloud. Since then this house has watched you eat, sleep, quarrel, and wake. Some things do not stay where you first set them."

Venn felt the back of his neck tighten.

"The Mera family and the Kondo family," Fasa said. "Come forward. Stand where the room can see you together."

The silence hit hard enough that even the Okoye baby stopped fussing.

Suna answered first. "No."

She held Asha against her chest and did not look at Venn. "We're not doing that. What happened between Asha and Mava stays with us. I'm not standing my daughter up for the hall."

Venn should have welcomed it. The refusal came from the other side. The Mera woman was doing his work for him.

But she had refused without even turning to him. She kept her eyes on Fasa, cutting the Kondos out of it entirely, and that slight stung worse than a direct quarrel.

"My son marries in eleven days," Venn said. He gave the words to Fasa, not to Suna. "The road is still under floodwater, and my family is waiting on it to clear. I won't have our name tied to another family's because a child made a sound in her sleep. We came here to pass through, not to be bound to strangers."

Fasa let the sentence die on the stone.

"Mava," she said.

Mava's hand rose to her left ear and began its slow circles. Ori saw it and caught Habel's wrist with both hands.

Venn shook his head once.

Fasa did not look at him. "Speak for yourself, Mava Kondo. Tell the room what Suna Mera asked you yesterday."

Mava lowered her hand to her lap. "The child used a word."

"Say it."

Mava swallowed. "Nushi."

The word sat in the middle of the hall. Venn heard rain against the open stone, the soft gutter of lamp oil, somebody dragging a breath through a narrow throat.

"Whose word is it?" Fasa asked.

"My sister's." Mava's voice was thin but steady. "Iru's. Old valley speech from Ishe, where she married. It means something like the other half of my face. No one here speaks it. No one here should know it. Iru has been dead forty years."

Fasa turned to Suna. "Do you know the valley speech of Ishe?"

"No."

"Has anyone in your family traveled there?"

"No."

Fasa looked past Suna. "Deshi. Peyam."

Old Peyam opened his eyes, beads cupped in both hands, and gave one deliberate shake of his head.

Deshi said nothing. She fixed on Mava so completely the rest of the hall might as well have emptied.

"Then say what is in front of you," Fasa said. "A child has spoken a dead woman's word with no road to it that anyone in this room can name. Stand together and answer to that."

Suna's arms tightened around Asha. "I already answered."

Fasa kept her eyes on her. "I heard the refusal. The thing itself is still here."

Venn rose before he knew he had decided to.

"This is grief," he said. The hall gave his voice back to him. "My aunt's grief. A word she's carried in her mouth for forty years. A four-year-old saying something strange does not make a bond between families. It does not rewrite who we are. I won't have my family hauled into a story because a ritual wants more than the facts can bear."

No one answered him. Bram leaned farther forward. Jin stared at the floor between his feet. Cade Leo put a hand over Pik's shoulder and left it there. Even the children were quiet.

Then Deshi stood.

Venn had filed her days ago: the sharp old woman from the Mera side, measuring rice by bite and children by noise. He knew the type because his own family had made women like that and depended on them. She should have stayed behind Suna, hard and traditional and on her own side of the room.

She crossed the hall instead.

Her sandals made almost no sound. She stopped in front of Mava's bench and looked down at her.

"Your sister," Deshi said. "What was her name?"

"Iru."

"And Nushi was hers for you?"

"Yes."

Deshi turned her head toward Asha for one beat, then back to Mava. "My granddaughter didn't get that from me."

She sat beside Mava on the Kondo bench.

Suna made a small shocked sound. "Deshi."

"Be quiet," Deshi said.

She took Mava's hands in both of hers and settled around them with an old, practiced certainty.

"Tell me about Iru."

Mava looked down at the hands around hers. Her throat worked once, twice. Then the sound came out of her ragged and low, and Ori buried her face in Habel's skirt.

"She was four years younger," Mava said. "Pretty. Spoiled. Always laughing at me for worrying. She married into Ishe and kept talking about the valley as if she'd invented it. She was coming home to see me when the cart went over right below the shrine turn. They brought her back on a door because the wheels had shattered."

Deshi did not release her hands.

"Twenty-nine," Mava said. "That's how old she was. Twenty-nine."

Venn stayed on his feet because sitting would have looked like surrender and stepping forward would have put his hands on a woman who did not belong to him. Tila's fingers touched the inside of his wrist, light as thread tension.

Fasa did not call the room back to order. She stayed where she was and let the silence stretch around the bench where the two old women sat. No one rose. No one looked to Olet. The hall had tilted toward Mava's open mouth and would not tilt back.

She wept without trying to stop. She did not weep loudly. The tears kept dropping off her jaw onto the front of her blouse while Deshi sat close and held on. Habel moved to Mava's other side and pressed a palm between her shoulder blades. Arash stayed behind the bench with his hands in his pockets and did not once glance at Venn. The arrangement Venn had built at the start of the hour came apart around them and stayed apart.

He sat down because his knees did it first.

Across the hall Suna spoke into the quiet. "She said it asleep. I'd never heard it from her awake. She was dreaming, and her hand was on her ear, and she said Nushi like she knew who she was calling."

Nessa pulled the baby tighter. Bram's mouth stayed open a moment before he shut it. Venn felt damp air on the sweat cooling under his shirt.

Gitta's thumbs were still circling each other. Fen Tesh watched the bench without blinking, shoulders drawn tight enough that even his next breath seemed delayed.

Bram cleared his throat. "Then let's be exact about what we're saying. Either the child made a sound that happened to resemble a word, or we're being asked to admit that something passed into her that no one can account for. Those are not the same claim."

"Belief is your own work," Fasa said. "This room is for naming what it has seen."

"What I've seen is an old woman crying and a child touching her ear."

Nessa's hand landed on Bram's forearm. He nodded once and shut his mouth on the rest, corrected by the touch before the room could do it for him.

"I'm not dismissing it," he said. "I'm asking what you expect us to do with it."

"Not turn away," Fasa said.

Venn stood again.

"Turn away from what?" he said. "From my aunt grieving her sister? From a child not knowing what comes out of her own mouth? Iru is ours. Her death is ours. The road took her from our family, not from this room."

Deshi lifted her face to him. Her eyes were dry and steady.

"A dead woman's word is in my granddaughter's mouth," she said. "What should I do with that? Pretend I did not hear it? Pretend your aunt is not crying into my hands because no one living has said that word to her in forty years?" She looked at him the way she might have looked at a pot she knew had been left too long on the fire. "You are the man who likes order. Tell me the orderly response."

He had answers for dowries, property lines, sleeping assignments, routes, debts, and the right shape of a wedding visit. He opened his mouth on that habit and found nothing there.

"The Kondo family will speak on this privately," he said at last.

Arash looked away. Bram shifted but did not pick up the offer. Fasa remained still.

"Private speech made this room necessary," she said. "What hides in families comes here to be spoken where others can hear it."

Tila broke in before he could answer. "Enough. He's heard it. Mava's shaking. Let the old woman breathe."

Fasa's gaze moved to Olet. He sat as he had sat all morning, hands on knees, unreadable. After a moment she nodded.

"We stop here for today," she said.

Still no one scattered. Peyam rose from the Mera side with his beads looped over one wrist and crossed the hall to the bench. Habel made room. He sat beside Mava without a word, another old body bracing the space around her. Bett's eyes never left Mava. Hala leaned in and whispered something to her sister that Venn could not catch. Jin was the last of the untouched families to go; he paused in the doorway with Melie on his hip and looked back once before the rain took him out of sight.

When the hall had thinned, Mava spoke again.

"She was coming to visit me," she said. Her voice was rubbed raw. "Iru had jars of quince preserve wrapped in cloth under the seat because she knew I liked it and never made it right. She rubbed my ear when I was a child and when I was old enough to be ashamed of crying. She'd say Nushi and laugh at my face and I would stop. I still do it because of her." She pressed her fingertips to her ear and let them fall. "I just stopped doing it where people could see."

Deshi nodded once. "Then the word did not stay buried with her."

No one contradicted her. Habel kept her hand on Mava's back. Peyam bowed his head over his beads. Even Fasa said nothing.

Tila touched Venn's sleeve. "Come outside."

They stood under the kitchen overhang with rain dripping from the edge of the roof hard enough to splash their sandals. The banked fire smelled of wet ash. Beyond the courtyard wall the flood kept up its blunt roar.

For a while Tila said nothing. She let the rain fill the space and waited, knowing a question too soon would only lock him harder.

"The wedding is in eleven days," Venn said.

"I know."

"The road is still gone."

"I know."

"And now this word."

The sentence stopped there. He had nothing that fit after it.

Inside the hall Mava's voice carried out in scraps, low and steady now, telling Deshi things he had never heard her tell him. He could feel the story leaving the hall already.

Tila looked toward the doorway. "Mava hasn't cried for Iru since the funeral."

Rain struck stone in quick hard beats. Venn could still see Deshi taking the floor from everyone else, Arash standing behind the bench and not coming to his side, the whole room holding still when he tried to drag it back under him.

"She's crying now," he said.

"Because somebody finally asked for the whole thing," Tila said. "Not the custodian. Deshi."

He did not answer. Under the overhang he counted without looking, the old habit moving in him on its own: Tila at his side, Mava inside, Habel and Ori with her, Ger, Arash, Rem, Osh, the younger ones in the sleeping room. There were twelve Kondos under one roof.

The count landed and did nothing.
