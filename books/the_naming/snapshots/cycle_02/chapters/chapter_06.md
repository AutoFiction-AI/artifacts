# Chapter 6

The next afternoon, the children had taken over the lower terrace.

Jin watched from the kitchen overhang where he'd been scrubbing pots for the past hour — volunteer work, the kind nobody argued about, because it kept him useful and silent and away from the naming hall where the adults were still sorting through whatever had happened yesterday between Mava Kondo and Suna Mera's mother-in-law. He didn't want to know. He didn't want to have an opinion about it. He wanted to scrub the black crust off the rice pot and watch his daughter.

Melie was in the middle of the group, which was new. For the first four days she'd circled the edges, watching the Kondo children and the Mera boy with the cautious hunger Jin recognized because he'd felt it at her age — wanting to be included but not knowing how to step in. She'd stood near Rimi Mera once, close enough that the twelve-year-old had to notice her, and Rimi had handed her a smooth pebble without looking down, an offhand kindness that Melie had carried in her pocket for two days afterward, turning it over and over until the warmth of her hand was indistinguishable from the warmth of the stone.

Now she sat cross-legged next to Nori Dao, the two of them arranging sticks in a pattern that seemed to involve rules Jin couldn't follow. Nori was a year older and a head taller and kept rearranging Melie's sticks without asking, and Melie — his Melie, who had spent three months going quiet at the table, who had stopped singing in the bath, who had developed the habit of checking the front door whenever she heard footsteps — didn't object. She just waited for Nori to finish correcting her placement and then added her next stick with careful concentration. The patience was new. Or not new. Recovered. She'd been a patient child before Reva left.

Jin's hands stopped in the water. He made himself keep scrubbing. The pot was copper, dented at the rim, and the burnt rice came away in black flakes that dissolved between his fingers. He pressed his thumb against the metal hard enough to whiten the nail. The water was cold from the cistern, cold enough that his knuckles ached, and he pressed harder.

Reva had walked out on a Tuesday. He remembered the day because she'd left her lunch in the fridge — the container she packed every morning, the one with the cracked blue lid — and he'd come home from the site and seen it sitting there on the shelf and known before he checked the closet, before he found the note, before he counted what was missing. She'd left her lunch. She hadn't planned on going that morning. Or she had planned it and then still packed her lunch out of habit and then left it there and walked out anyway, and he didn't know which version was worse — the impulsive abandonment or the deliberate one that was so rehearsed she could go through the motions of staying even while she was leaving.

He'd told Melie that her mother had died. He'd told her that evening, quickly, before anyone else could tell her something different, because Reva's sister knew and her sister's husband knew and Jin could already see the machinery of explanation gearing up around him, the concerned faces and the careful words and the particular head-tilt of pity that precedes unsolicited advice. He'd needed to control the story. A dead mother was clean. A dead mother couldn't come back. A dead mother wasn't a woman who looked at her daughter and her husband and their kitchen with the cracked blue container still in the fridge and decided she wanted something else.

The shame of it lived in where other people's eyes went first: the sink, the unmade bed, Melie's shoes by the door. Men whose wives died got casseroles, soft voices, neighbors who checked in and brought extra food on weekends. Men whose wives left got the quick sideways look, the change of subject, the pause before sympathy turned into appraisal. He'd watched it happen in the first week after Reva walked out, before he'd settled on the lie, when his brother-in-law stood in the kitchen doorway and let his gaze move over the room before it landed on Jin. Maybe they were right to look. A woman had looked at this life and gone.

He pulled the pot from the basin and set it upside down on the stone ledge to dry. His hands were red and his knuckles were swelling slightly from the cold. Below him, on the terrace, Melie laughed.

The sound went through him like a needle. She was laughing at something Nori had done — knocked over the entire stick arrangement, maybe, or said something in the blunt way that Nori had, a seven-year-old's declarative certainty about the world that seemed to delight Melie in a way that adult gentleness never did. Jin watched his daughter's face: open, unguarded, the gap where her front teeth used to be visible because she was laughing with her whole mouth. She hadn't laughed like that since before Reva left. Since before the note, the empty closet, the lie.

She was thriving here. In this stone compound surrounded by strangers who asked too many questions and custodians who rang a bell for every meal and prayer and an old woman who wanted to know the maiden name of his dead wife. She was thriving because there were other children and because no one here knew the specific shape of what was missing from her life, and the story Jin had told at the naming — my wife passed, we're traveling to her family — sat over the truth like a lid, and the lid let Melie move through this temporary world without the particular cruelty of being the girl whose mother chose to leave.

She was the girl whose mother chose to leave. He couldn't even think the words without his mouth bracing for the catch before the consonant. He pressed his teeth together and waited for it to pass. It always passed. It had been doing this since he was twelve, long before Reva, long before any of this, and it always passed.

He stacked the clean pots on the ledge, dried his hands on the cloth hanging from the overhang post, and stood watching his daughter play until the light shifted and the shadows of the compound walls reached the lower terrace and the children began to scatter toward their families.

---

Bett Dao was at the cistern when Jin brought Melie down to wash her hair before the evening meal.

She was filling a clay jug, one hand braced on the stone basin's lip, and she straightened when she heard them on the steps. Her sleeves were rolled to her elbows in precise folds, the kind of careful cuffing Jin associated with people who'd worked with their hands for years — tidiness as reflex, not vanity. She wore a thin brass ring on her right hand, tarnished nearly black, that she turned with her thumb while she waited for the jug to fill. She nodded at him — the same flat acknowledgment she gave everyone, not unfriendly but not an opening — and went back to her water.

"Papa, it's cold," Melie said, touching the surface with one finger and pulling it back.

"I know. It's always cold." He'd washed her hair here twice since they'd arrived, both times in the late afternoon when the stone basin caught the last warmth from the sun. The water came from a spring somewhere beneath the shrine, cold even in monsoon heat, and Melie complained about it every time with the specific outrage of a child who has been warned about something unpleasant and then discovers that the warning did not make it less unpleasant.

"Nori says her aunt washes her hair with soap flowers," Melie said, looking at Bett's back. "She says they make your hair smell like the garden."

"We don't have soap flowers."

"Maybe Bett has some."

"We're not going to bother her. Come here."

Jin cupped water in both hands and brought it to Melie's head. She ducked and squealed and he caught her by the shoulder, firm but careful, the grip of a parent whose child was always about to dart away from the one thing that needed doing. "Hold still. I'll be fast."

The water ran through her dark hair and down the back of her neck. She shivered and he felt the shiver travel up through his hand into his wrist. Her hair was getting long — past her shoulders now, the longest it had ever been. Reva had always kept it trimmed, sitting Melie on a kitchen stool with a towel around her shoulders and cutting with the good scissors, the ones with the orange handles that were still in the drawer at home. Jin didn't know how to cut hair evenly. Melie didn't seem to mind the length, or if she minded she hadn't said, and he hadn't asked because asking might surface the question of why her mother wasn't here to do it.

He worked the soap through her hair — a brown block the custodians provided, harsh and unscented, the kind that stripped everything — his thumbs moving along her scalp in the pattern he'd learned from watching Reva do this. His hands remembered the small circles. The gentleness required at the temples where the skin was thin. His hands had learned this from a woman who was alive and somewhere else, living a life that didn't include the small warm skull under his fingers, and his hands did it anyway because muscle memory doesn't care about betrayal.

"Papa."

"Hmm."

"Is Nori's mum here?"

His fingers went still in her soapy hair. "I'm not sure about their family," he said. He didn't know the Daos' arrangement beyond the obvious — Bett and her older sister Hala, Sem and Raya usually with Hala, Nori orbiting Bett so naturally that Jin had stopped trying to sort out how the household had been arranged before they all got stranded here. He hadn't asked, because asking invited questions in return, and questions were the one thing he couldn't afford.

Bett was still at the basin, her jug full now, but she hadn't moved to leave. She was listening — not obviously, her body angled away, her free hand worrying a thread loose from her sleeve cuff — but listening in the way Jin recognized because he did it himself: face turned from the conversation, attention fixed on every word.

"I am," Bett said, without turning around. Her voice was even, unperformative. "Nori's mine. Hala's my sister."

"Oh." Melie's face was upside down, looking up at Jin through the fall of her wet hair. "Then Hala's the aunt with the soap flowers?"

"Yeah," Bett said. "That's Hala."

Jin reached for the tin cup by the basin and rinsed Melie's hair, pouring with one hand and shielding her eyes with the other, and Melie endured it with the stoic misery of a child who has learned that protest doesn't shorten the process.

Bett had turned and was watching them. Not watching — something less intrusive. She held the jug on her hip, elbow locked beneath the weight, and her face, which Jin had only ever seen from across the hall as a guarded surface, was doing something he hadn't expected. She was looking at Melie's wet hair and Jin's careful hands with an expression that wasn't pity and wasn't judgment. It looked like memory. Like she was watching something she had done once, for someone, in another kitchen, and the watching cost her something she was choosing to pay.

Then she turned and climbed the steps with the jug on her hip, and the moment closed behind her.

Jin wrung Melie's hair between his palms and twisted it into the rough shape of a braid. He didn't know how to braid properly. Another thing Reva had done. Another skill he'd never learned because he'd assumed she would always be there to do it, and the assumption had felt so solid, so much like ground, that he'd never noticed he was standing on something that could move.

"Come on," he said. "Dinner soon."

Melie took his hand. Her fingers were cold and small and damp, and she swung his arm as they climbed the steps, and Jin held on.

---

The evening meal was set out on the stone ledge that served as the communal table — flatbreads stacked under a cloth, a lentil pot still bubbling over the last of the coals, rice with fried shallots, and three small bowls of condiments the Dao women had prepared from their own stores. Jin knew the Daos had been on kitchen rotation because Hala had been there all afternoon, her broad back bent over the cutting board, humming something under her breath while she worked, filling the kitchen with sound the way other people filled it with talk. Bett had been grinding spices in the stone mortar when Jin had passed through with Melie on the way to the cistern, her wrist turning in steady circles while the stone gave back a wet rasp under each pass, her face absent and focused at the same time, as if the mortar were holding a thought steady for her.

The children ate together, as they had since the third or fourth day — a cluster of bodies on the lower step, plates balanced on knees, the older ones helping the younger ones without being told. Rimi Mera tore flatbread for Asha, who was sitting in Rimi's lap, four years old and unbothered by the fact that she was using a stranger's twelve-year-old as furniture. Kael held his own plate with the defensive grip of an eight-year-old who has been told he is old enough to manage. The Kondo children ate in their own tight group — Venn's influence, visible even at mealtimes in the way they kept their elbows close and their voices down — but Ori, the youngest at nine, kept glancing over at the other children, then back to her own plate, then over again, as if the space between the groups might bite.

Melie sat between Nori and Sem Dao. Sem was eleven, quiet, with the same compact build as his aunt Bett and the same economy of movement — he did things once and correctly, without wasted effort, as though he'd been taught that resources were finite and motion was a resource. He'd started saving a spot for Melie at meals without being asked, a small territorial gesture that Melie accepted without comment, the way children accept kindness they haven't been taught to question.

Jin ate standing, leaning against the kitchen overhang's wall, tearing bread and scooping lentils without tasting either. He'd taken some of the fried shallot rice and a smear of the brown-gold paste from one of the small bowls — rich, sweet, with a depth he vaguely identified as nut-based. He ate mechanically. The food was good. The Daos could cook. He chewed and swallowed and watched his daughter.

Melie was eating with both hands on her bread, tearing and dipping with the focused intensity of a child who takes food seriously. She dipped her torn bread into the brown-gold paste, took a large bite, chewed, dipped again. Nori was telling her something about a game they would play tomorrow, and Melie nodded while she chewed, her attention split between the conversation and the food with the particular greed of a six-year-old who hasn't fully learned to pretend she isn't hungry.

She paused. Her hand stopped halfway to her mouth. She looked at the bread in her fingers as if she'd never seen bread before.

"Melie?" Jin set his plate on the ledge.

She scratched her neck. Then her forearm. Both movements quick and distracted, the way you scratch at a mosquito bite without deciding to. She took another bite. She chewed. She scratched her neck again, harder.

"What's wrong?" He was already moving toward her, not running, but the five meters between the kitchen wall and the lower step felt like a distance he couldn't cross fast enough, each stride landing on stone that seemed to stretch.

"Itchy," Melie said. She scratched her forearm and Jin saw the skin — raised, blotchy, a red rash spreading from her wrist to the inside of her elbow in the time it took him to reach her.

He knelt beside her. "Show me."

She held up both arms. The rash was on both now, blooming outward from the insides of her elbows, welts rising under the skin like something pushing up from beneath. Her face was changing — the skin around her mouth puffing, her lower lip thickening, a flush climbing her cheeks that had nothing to do with the evening heat. She scratched at her throat with both hands.

"I feel funny," she said. Her voice was wrong. Thicker, squeezed, a six-year-old's voice forced through a narrowing passage.

Jin grabbed her plate. Bread, lentils, the brown-gold paste smeared across the edge. He looked at what she'd eaten and didn't know what was wrong and the not-knowing opened a hole in his chest that felt exactly like falling because he was her father and he was supposed to know. "What did you eat? Melie, what did you eat just now?"

"The bread. And the—" She coughed. A small cough, nothing, except beneath it was a wheeze Jin had never heard from her, a high thin scraping sound like air pulled through wet cloth. She coughed again. Her eyes were watering.

"Melie." He pulled her into his arms. She was light — she'd always been small for six, the kind of lightness that made strangers guess she was younger — and her body was hot against his chest, hotter than the evening warranted, and the rash was spreading down her neck now, red welts rising on skin he'd been washing with his own hands twenty minutes ago. "Breathe. Just breathe for me."

She tried. The wheeze was louder. Her fingers went to her throat, scratching, pulling at the skin as if she could widen the passage by hand.

"Something's wrong with her." He said it to the courtyard, to everyone, to anyone. Adults were turning. Suna had already stood from where she'd been eating with Deshi and the children. Hala was coming out of the kitchen, wiping her hands on her apron, her face shifting from confusion to something faster than confusion. "Something's wrong — she ate something, I don't know what—"

"Let me see." Bett was beside him. She'd been sitting four meters away with Nori, eating from her own plate, and now she was here and he hadn't seen her cross the space. Her hand went to Melie's face — not gently, efficiently. Chin up. Lips checked. Two fingers under the angle of Melie's jaw where the swelling was worst.

"Her throat's closing," Bett said. Her voice dropped, and the words still cut clean through the courtyard. "What did she eat?"

"Bread, lentils, the—"

Bett picked up Melie's plate. She touched the smear of paste with her finger and brought it to her nose. "Cashew," she said.

"She's eaten cashews before. She eats them all the time, she's never—"

"Not this." Bett set the plate down. She was moving fast now, her body stripped of everything that wasn't functional, her hands already reaching behind her as if she expected something to be placed in them. She looked at Melie's swelling face and something in her locked into place — not coldness, the opposite. "Hala. The kit. Go."

Hala ran. Not walked, not hurried — ran, her feet slapping the stone, back into the kitchen area where the Daos kept their things, and the sound of containers being shoved aside carried across the courtyard.

"What's happening to her?" Jin's voice broke on the second word. Melie was wheezing against his chest, each breath a whistle through a gap that was narrowing by the second, and her fingers were clawing at her throat hard enough to leave red marks on skin that was already welted, and her eyes were swollen to slits and he could feel her ribs heaving against his forearms, each inhalation pulling her ribcage wide as her body fought for air that wasn't coming fast enough.

"Anaphylaxis." Bett said the word like it had been worn smooth from use. "Tree-nut allergy. Cashew. It's our family's allergy. Sem has it. My son had it."

The words didn't arrange themselves into sense. His daughter was choking in his arms and this woman he'd spoken to three times in a week was saying something about families and allergies and a son, and Melie was making a sound now — a high, thin, aspirated whistle on each inhale, air forced through a space the width of a straw — and the sound was the most terrifying thing Jin had ever heard because it was the sound of something that could stop.

"Hold her still." Bett took Melie from his arms. She didn't ask. One arm under Melie's back, the other supporting her head to keep the airway as straight as possible, she lifted her in a single clean motion and set her on the stone step, propped against her own bent knee. "Melie, I know it's scary. Don't scratch. Look at me."

Melie looked at her. Her eyes were wet and swollen and terrified, her face a stranger's face, puffed and distorted, and her hands were still going for her throat. Bett caught both small wrists in one hand and held them against Melie's chest. "You're going to breathe. It's tight but you're going to breathe. Look at me."

Hala came back with a tin box — battered, scratched, a dent in the lid that had been hammered mostly flat. Bett opened it one-handed, her other hand still holding Melie's wrists. Inside: a small glass vial and a syringe wrapped in white cloth. The cloth was clean and carefully ironed, the creases sharp, and this was the detail that would stay with Jin afterward — the ironed cloth, the exact folds, the way the battered tin had been kept as if order itself could hold a throat open.

Bett drew the dose with the same economy she brought to everything else. She glanced once at the vial and filled the syringe without pausing. The dosage seemed to live in her fingers, not in her head.

"Epinephrine," she said to Jin. She didn't look at him. Her eyes were on Melie's face, reading the swelling, measuring the progression with an attention that was clinical and also something more than clinical — something personal and hard-earned. "It'll open her airway. Her heart's going to race. It'll scare her but it's the drug working."

"Do it." He didn't know what epinephrine was. He didn't care what it was. Melie was wheezing and her lips were going grey and her fingernails were digging into Bett's palm where Bett held her wrists and Jin would have let this woman pour fire into his daughter's body if it meant the whistling would stop.

Bett pushed Melie's shirt up and exposed her outer thigh — the skin there still clear of rash, pale and goosebumped in the cooling air. She drove the needle in. No flinch, no tentative pause. She pressed the plunger with the steady force of practice, and then she held the needle in place and counted — silently, her lips moving, her thumb keeping the muscle steady while the drug entered.

She withdrew the needle and set the syringe on the stone. "Now we wait."

The courtyard was quiet. Forty-odd people, some standing and some still sitting with plates in their laps, watching. Suna had her hand on Asha's head, holding her daughter against her hip. Hala stood behind Bett with her arms crossed over her chest, her face tight and furious with worry. The Kondo children had stopped eating. Old Peyam had put down his pipe. Nori Dao was standing exactly where Melie had been sitting a minute ago, holding the plate Melie had been eating from, her face white and absolutely still. She knew what this was. She had grown up knowing.

Melie breathed. The whistle was still there. Bett's palm was flat between Melie's shoulder blades, feeling each breath — checking the vibration, the depth, the resistance, the way you'd feel a pipe for blockage, your hand reading what your ears couldn't measure. Twenty seconds. Thirty. Jin counted with her, or tried to, but the numbers kept slipping because his hands were shaking and his jaw was locked and the sound of that whistle was still there, still narrowing, still the width of a straw—

And then it changed. Loosened. Deepened. The sound of a passage widening by millimeters, the wheeze dropping half a tone, and Bett's hand went still on Melie's back.

"There," Bett said.

Melie took a breath that sounded like breathing. Then another. The swelling around her eyes was still bad — her face still looked distorted, unfamiliar — but the color was coming back into her lips and her breathing was slowing from panicked shallow gasps to something Jin's body recognized before his mind did: she was getting air. She was going to keep getting air.

"Papa," Melie said. Her voice was hoarse and her eyes were still nearly shut. "My heart's going really fast."

"I know. That's the medicine. It's supposed to do that." He was repeating what Bett had told him. He was reaching for Melie, pulling her from Bett's knee into his own arms, and she came — hot and shaking and alive, her heart hammering against his chest through both their shirts, her breathing ragged but open, open, the air going in and coming out. He pressed his face into her damp hair and smelled the harsh soap from the cistern and the faint residual sweetness of the cashew paste and his own sweat, and he held her, and his hands were shaking so badly that he could feel them vibrating against her back like something mechanical.

"She can't eat tree nuts," Bett said. She was still sitting on the step, her hands on her thighs, the syringe on the stone beside her. The ironed cloth had come open and the glass vial caught the last daylight, a small bright point. Her voice was steady but her jaw was set in a way that Jin recognized as effort — steadiness as a choice, not a condition. "Cashews, pistachios, walnuts. Almonds are sometimes safe but I wouldn't test it here. Not without a clinic."

"She's never—" Jin's jaw locked. The consonant caught and held, the old hesitation, and for a moment he couldn't make the word come out. He pressed his mouth against Melie's hair and waited for his throat to release. "She's never been allergic. To anything. She eats everything."

"She does now," Bett said.

Its flatness startled him. It wasn't cruel. It wasn't bewildered. It wasn't wrapped in any of the language the compound had been generating for a week about manifestations and connections and what the naming revealed about families. It was just the fact, set down on the stone beside the syringe and the vial and the ironed cloth: your daughter has this thing now. It is real. It will not go away because it shouldn't be there.

Hala knelt beside Bett and put a hand on her sister's shoulder. Bett didn't lean into the touch. She didn't acknowledge it except by not pulling away. She sat on the stone step with Hala's hand on her shoulder and looked at Melie breathing in Jin's arms, and Jin saw her gaze go to the spot on Melie's thigh where the needle had entered — a small red dot, already closing, unremarkable. Whatever Bett saw in that dot lived in a place Jin didn't have access to. He understood, without knowing the details, that she had done this before for someone who was not here anymore.

"I've got one more dose," Bett said. She nodded at the tin. "After that we'd need to get to a clinic, and the road's not open."

"What if it happens again?"

"Don't let her eat tree nuts."

"I don't know what has tree nuts in it." His voice was rising and he couldn't stop it — the flood, the loss of volume control, the specific humiliation of a man unable to protect his child from a bowl of paste at dinner. "I've never had to know. She ate cashews at home, I gave her cashews, I put them in her — she's eaten them her whole—"

"So come to me before she eats." Bett stood. She brushed the stone dust from her knees — one brisk pass of each hand, no fuss. The syringe went back into the tin, the vial beside it, the cloth folded over both with the same careful creases. She closed the lid and tucked the tin under her arm. "I've been managing this allergy for eleven years. Sem's had it since he was born. I can tell you what's safe and what's not."

Jin stared at her. He was holding Melie against his chest and his daughter's heart was still hammering and this woman was offering to manage what he couldn't manage, to know what he didn't know, and the offer was made without softness, without the gentle tone people used when they felt sorry for you. It was made the way you'd hand someone a tool: here, you need this, take it.

"Why does she have your family's allergy?" He didn't mean it as accusation. He meant it as the question it was — the thing he could not hold inside his body, because his daughter had just had her throat close from a condition she didn't have yesterday, a condition that belonged to strangers, and a woman he barely knew had saved her with a needle and a counted silence and hands that knew exactly where to push the plunger.

Bett looked at him. The guarded surface was back — the face she showed the compound, economical, closed. But the muscles around her eyes were tight in a way that had nothing to do with composure and everything to do with something she was choosing not to say.

"I don't know why," she said. "I just know what it looks like."

She climbed the steps. Hala followed, her hand briefly touching the back of Bett's arm as they went — a sisterly gesture, practiced and unremarkable, the habit of two women who had been looking after each other for a long time. Nori was still holding Melie's plate. Bett took it from her daughter's hands as she passed — gently, the way you take something with an edge from a child — and set it face-down on the ledge where no one else would eat from it.

The courtyard emptied in stages. The crisis was already becoming the thing people would talk about in low voices after the children slept — did you see it, how fast, the Dao woman's kit, how did she know. Suna caught Jin's eye as she passed with Asha on her hip and Rimi trailing behind. She didn't speak. Her gaze moved from Melie to the food ledge to Asha's face and back again, already recalculating — her children, the food, what else might change without warning, whether the world she thought she understood had shifted again beneath all of them.

Jin sat on the step with Melie in his arms. Her heart was still going too fast. He could feel it through her ribs, a hummingbird tremor against his chest, and he kept hearing Bett's voice — it's the drug working, it'll scare her but it's working — and repeating it to himself as if the repetition could reach whatever part of him was still falling through the hole that had opened when he heard that first wheeze. Her breathing was steady now. Regular. The swelling was going down, slowly, the puffiness around her eyes receding enough that she looked like herself again — the gap teeth, the dark hair he'd just washed, the chin that was Reva's chin. He saw it every time. Every single time.

"Papa," she said. Her voice was scratchy and small. "My throat still feels weird."

"It'll get better. The medicine's still working."

"How did she know what to do?"

"Because someone in her family has the same thing you have."

"But I don't have it. I ate cashews at Auntie's house. I ate them loads of times."

"I know."

"So why did my throat do that?"

He didn't have an answer. He had a lie about her dead mother and the copper pot he'd scrubbed that afternoon and the memory of her laughing on the terrace twenty minutes before her throat closed, and he had the present fact of her body in his arms — hot, trembling, breathing — and a debt he owed to a woman who'd known what was happening because her family carried the same thing, her child, her nephew, her blood, and now his daughter's blood too, impossibly, undeniably, a connection written in the body that no story he could construct would explain away.

"I don't know," he said.

Melie pressed her face into his shirt and didn't ask again.

The custodians' bell rang for evening prayers. The compound moved around them. Somewhere above, Bett's voice carried briefly — saying something to Nori, ordinary words, a mother's evening instructions — and then the compound settled into its nightly routine of mats and lamps and the sound of rain beginning again on stone.

Later, when Jin carried Melie to their sleeping space and laid her down and sat beside her with his back against the wall, he found the tin box on the floor next to their mat. Bett had left it without telling him. No note, no instructions, no explanation. Just the dented tin with the ironed cloth inside and the single remaining dose of the drug that would keep his daughter breathing if this new, impossible thing in her body decided to close her throat again.

He sat with the tin in his hands and listened to Melie breathe and understood something with the particular clarity that comes only when terror has passed and left the ground rearranged. He could not keep her safe alone. He had never been able to keep her safe alone. The lie he'd built — the dead wife, the self-sufficient father, the man who needed nothing from anyone — was exactly as strong as Melie's certainty that she could eat cashews. Both had been true this morning. Neither was true tonight.

Around him the compound breathed. Rain on stone, mats shifting, a baby crying somewhere — Ada, the Okoye infant, her small voice carrying through the thin walls and then stopping as someone picked her up. The flood's steady rush from the valley below, unchanged, indifferent. Melie's breathing beside him, even and open.

The old catch fired on nothing — no word, no thought he was trying to form, just the body's habit of seizing before speech. He pressed his thumbs against the dented lid of the tin and held it in his lap and listened to the rain keep hitting the courtyard stone and did not sleep for a long time.
