# Chapter 4

By the fifth morning the shrine was waking by rote: Asha's mat damp with sweat, the cooking fire already lit somewhere beyond the partition, a hush that meant the custodians' bell had not yet rung. Suna lay still for a moment and took stock. Asha breathing against her side, the small furnace heat of a child in deep sleep. Kael in the corner of the mat, curled tight, one ankle hooked over the other. Rimi asleep with her forearm across her eyes — twelve and already perfecting the art of blocking things out. Deshi's mat empty, which meant she was up, which meant criticism of the morning meal was already being prepared alongside the rice.

Peyam was on his mat by the far wall, still asleep or pretending, his thin chest rising under the worn cotton of his shirt. His devotional beads were wound around his left wrist even in sleep. He was seventy-two and had not missed morning prayers since arriving at the shrine — up before the bell every day, crossing the courtyard to his corner of the prayer yard while the stones were still damp with the night's rain. He didn't ask Suna's permission or tell her his schedule. He simply went. Of all the Mera family members she was managing alone, Peyam was the only one who required nothing from her.

She eased Asha off her shoulder and stood. The mat needed hanging again. She rolled it, tucked it under her arm, and crossed to the courtyard. The air outside the sleeping quarters was cooler but not cold — monsoon humidity, the stones still holding the night's damp. The sound of the flood was there as always, a low massed rush from the valley below that she had stopped noticing by the second day and now only heard again when she listened for it. Like living next to a river, except the river was the road, and the road was gone. She hung the mat over the low courtyard wall and pressed it flat. The stone beneath her hands was already warming.

Breakfast was communal now, the families rotating through kitchen duty on the custodians' schedule. This morning was the Kondos' turn, and when Suna reached the cooking area under the stone overhang, Tila Kondo was already portioning rice into bowls while two of the younger Kondo cousins carried them to the main hall. The smoke from the cooking fire drifted low under the overhang, thick and sweet from monsoon-damp wood, the kind that got into clothes and hair and stayed. By the fifth day everyone at the shrine smelled of it — smoke and damp cotton and the mineral flatness of cistern water.

Deshi was there too, seated on the bench near the wall with the blue shawl she'd brought from home pulled around her shoulders, watching the Kondos work with the alert stillness of a woman conducting an inspection she hadn't been asked to perform. The shawl was frayed at both edges, pilling along the fold where she clutched it closed, and she had refused Suna's offer to wash it because it would lose what was left of its shape. It was the only thing Deshi had kept close through the whole journey — not sentiment, Suna thought, only the stubbornness of a woman who would not be separated from what was hers.

"The lentils are thin," Deshi said as Suna passed.

"They're fine."

"They're thin. More water than dal. You could read a letter through them."

"Then don't eat them."

Deshi made a sound balanced between agreement and objection — the sound of a woman who had won the exchange by provoking a response. Suna let it go and went to collect her children for breakfast.

The main hall was already filling. Forty-three people eating in shifts of twenty and twenty-three, the arrangement worked out by the custodians on the second day and adhered to since without further argument. The Mera family ate in the first shift with the Kondos and the Leos. Suna gathered Kael and Rimi — Asha was still half-asleep on Rimi's hip, her head tipped against her sister's shoulder, mouth slack — and found their spot on the long bench. The stone floor of the main hall was cool underfoot, not yet reached by the morning sun. The open side of the hall faced the valley, and the light that came through it was flat and gray, filtered through the cloud cover that hadn't fully broken in five days.

Suna had told the children their father had gone ahead. He was waiting for them at the end of the road, at their destination, and they would meet him when the water went down and the route opened again. Rimi had stopped asking about it on the third day. Kael had never asked. Asha was too young for the question. The lie turned breakfast mechanical. Her mouth kept drying out, so she had to wash every few bites down with water, and every time one of the children looked up she fixed her attention on the rice until they looked away.

Across the hall, the Kondo family occupied their usual half of the second bench, their twelve members arranged with the unconscious precision of a family that had been traveling together and knew its own geometry. Venn sat at the end, upright, eating with the deliberate pace of a man who considered meals a public act. Beside him, at the far end of the bench where the draft from the valley came up cool and steady, Mava Kondo sat with a bowl of dal in her lap, her left hand rising periodically to work at her ear, palm pressed to the cartilage in a slow looping motion as habitual and unconscious as breathing. Suna had noticed the habit on the first day and marked it the way she marked most things about other families: noted, sorted, set aside.

Asha ate slowly, picking rice grains out of her bowl with her fingers and dropping half of them on Rimi's thigh. She was four and had no interest in efficiency. Her attention moved across the hall, watching faces, watching hands, her gaze traveling from person to person with a four-year-old's undiscriminating appetite for the visible world. She touched everything — the bench, the bowl, Rimi's ear, the stone wall behind them — her hands in constant exploratory motion, absorbing textures like a second kind of sight.

She reached up and rubbed her left ear.

It was the precision that caught Suna, not the fact of it. Children touched their ears constantly — Asha especially, who touched everything. But this was not a casual scratch. Her palm settled over the cartilage and turned in slow, deliberate circles, fingers loose and slightly spread. The motion belonged to Mava. Suna looked across the hall at the old woman, still rubbing her own ear on the far bench, and then back at Asha, who had already stopped and gone to picking at her rice again, her attention elsewhere.

Suna took another bite and kept her face still. Across the hall Mava's hand made another slow circle. Asha had already gone back to her rice. Kael kicked the bench leg again, and Suna told him to stop.

The play group formed after breakfast, as it had for the past two days — the shrine's younger children sorting themselves by affinity rather than family. Kael went immediately to the Kondo boys, who had invented a game involving pebbles and a crack in the courtyard stone that seemed to function as both goal and boundary. Rimi drifted toward the older girls — Raya Dao, a few of the Kondo cousins — hovering at the edge of their circle, careful not to press in and not to stand alone either. Asha was with the younger ones: Nori Dao, Pik Leo, Ori Kondo. They had claimed the corner of the main hall near the open side where the light came in and the stone floor was warm with reflected sun, and they moved among each other with the uncomplicated physicality of children who had not yet learned to maintain the distances their parents kept.

Suna was sorting laundry — hers and Deshi's and the children's, the limited clothes they'd arrived with, washed and rewashed in the cistern until the fabric was thinning at the seams. She could see Asha from where she sat against the wall, the bright head moving among the other children, and she registered each position the way she registered everything about her children: automatically, continuously, the way you track weather or the level of water in a pot. Asha by the wall. Asha near the open side, holding Nori's hand. Asha standing next to Ori, reaching for something in Ori's pocket. Asha near Mava.

Mava was sitting in her usual spot — the section of bench near the open side where the draft came up from the valley. She had a small cloth bag in her lap, and she was sorting dried lentils with her fingers, picking out the small pale stones that hid among the grains and setting them aside in a neat row on the bench. She did this every morning after breakfast. It was her contribution to the kitchen — a task that required no standing, no lifting, no argument with Venn about whether she was overexerting herself — and she performed it with the patient sureness of someone who had been sorting lentils for longer than Suna had been alive, her fingers reading the difference between grain and stone by weight and texture alone. The stones she removed were small and smooth, washed to the same color as the lentils by decades of monsoon water, and she found them without looking.

Asha was standing near her, watching the old woman's fingers move through the grain. Mava gave her the mild automatic smile of a woman who had been around children her whole life and neither resented nor sought out their attention. Asha pointed at her and said something Suna couldn't hear across the hall.

Then she said it again, louder, turning toward Suna. "Nushi."

"What?" Suna looked up from the damp shirt in her hands.

"Nushi." Asha pointed at Mava again, then turned back to the old woman and the lentils. It was not a question. It was identification — the flat confidence of a child naming what she saw, the way she said bird or cup or Mama.

The word landed oddly. Suna repeated it once under her breath, testing its shape, and it stayed strange in her mouth. Across the hall Mava kept sorting lentils, head bent, fingers moving through grain and stone. Suna twisted water from the shirt until it ran down her wrist and went back to the laundry.

The afternoon was the dead time at the shrine. Rain started again after the midday meal — heavy, steady, the kind that lasted hours and drove everyone inside, turning the courtyard into a shallow pool of runoff that crept slowly toward the terraced garden. The main hall settled into the cramped, restless quiet of forty-three people with nowhere to go. The space was large enough for all of them, but only just — bodies shifting on benches and mats, conversations murmured in corners, the ambient hum of enforced proximity. Baby Ada Okoye was fussing. Someone was snoring. Moths had found the first lit oil lamp and were circling it in the gray afternoon light, their bodies tapping against the glass with a sound like small fingernails.

Kael was playing quietly with the pebble game he'd learned from the Kondo boys. Rimi had borrowed a book from Leni Okoye and was reading against the far wall, knees drawn up. Deshi was asleep on her mat, her blue shawl tucked under her chin, mouth open, snoring with the easy disregard of a woman who had stopped caring what she sounded like in her sleep decades ago.

Asha had worn herself out in the play group and climbed into Suna's lap after the meal, her eyelids dropping, her body performing that heavy, gradual collapse that small children did when sleep took them between one word and the next. She was asleep in minutes, her weight tipped against Suna's chest, one hand curled around a fold of Suna's shirt. Her breathing deepened and steadied.

Suna held her and watched the rain. The light in the main hall was gray and even. Across the hall, other families moved in their own slow orbits — Hala Dao wiping down the cooking pots with a rag, Jin Parra sitting near the wall with Melie asleep across his knees, one hand resting on her back in the absent protective gesture of a man who slept lightly. Cade Leo and his partner Wynn sat together with Pik between them, Wynn's head on Cade's shoulder, the three of them arranged in a quiet private triangle that excluded the room. Mava was on her bench, eyes closed, hands in her lap. Not rubbing. Still.

Asha shifted against Suna's chest. Her breathing stayed deep and even, the breath of real sleep, not the light doze that could be broken by a voice or a closing door. Her left hand, the one that had been gripping Suna's shirt, loosened and rose.

She brought it to her left ear and began to rub, palm laid over the cartilage, the movement slow and even.

Suna watched the hand move. The motion was precise and unhurried, the same tempo and pressure she had seen at breakfast, the same habit she had watched Mava Kondo repeat for five days. But Asha was asleep. Her eyes were closed, her body slack, her breathing unchanged. There was no one across the hall to watch, no gesture to mirror. Her hand moved against her ear with the certainty of a thing the body knew how to do on its own, without instruction or audience.

Suna sat very still. The muscles in her arms locked around Asha's warm weight. Rain hit the courtyard stones outside. The moths tapped the lamp glass. Asha's hand kept its slow circuit. Only when the hand stopped did Suna let herself move, and by then the thought was already there: I need to talk to the old woman.

---

She found Mava after the rain eased, sitting alone on the courtyard steps. The stones were wet and dark, and the sky had gone to that thick white pause between rain spells, clouds rearranging themselves before the next fall. The air was cooler and smelled of stone and wet earth and the green bite of monsoon growth pushing up faster than anything could eat it. The flood sound was louder after rain, swollen with fresh runoff, a continuous low roar from the valley that made ordinary voices feel temporary.

Mava was watching the view. From the courtyard steps you could see down the slope to where the switchback road disappeared into brown water — the point where the seven roads converged, all of them drowned. The water was the color of milked tea, opaque, slow-moving at this distance, its surface pocked with rain. A bird Suna couldn't name was working the updraft above the waterline, banking and tilting with the concentrated effort of something hunting.

Suna sat down next to her. She didn't know Mava. Five days of shared confinement had made them neighbors but not acquaintances — the proximity of shared meals and sleeping quarters and the main hall's enforced closeness, without the private conversation that would have turned proximity into familiarity. Mava was a Kondo, one of Venn's twelve, and the Kondo family kept its own close. Suna had heard Mava laugh at a card game two nights ago — a dry, low sound that carried across the hall with more force than the old woman's small frame suggested — and she had seen her sorting lentils and rubbing her ear and walking slowly to the latrine and back, and that was the full extent of what she knew about a woman who had lived seventy-three years and carried a grief Suna was about to find.

"Can I ask you something?"

Mava looked at her. Up close, her face was deeply lined, the skin around her eyes creased in a way that suggested decades of squinting against bright light — a life lived outdoors, or at altitude, or both. Her eyes themselves were sharp and dark and unhurried, the eyes of someone who had long ago stopped feeling obligated to respond quickly.

"You can ask."

"Your ear." Suna touched her own left ear. "The way you rub it. You do it all the time."

Mava's hand rose, almost involuntarily, to her ear. She caught herself and lowered it. A small embarrassed sound, almost a laugh. "Old habit. My sister used to say I'd wear a hole through it."

"Asha does it too."

The faint amusement faded. Mava's face stayed open but the quality of her attention shifted — she was listening now, not just hearing.

"My daughter. She's four. She started doing it this morning — your exact habit, left hand and all. I thought she was copying you. Kids do that. They steal gestures." Suna paused. She was aware that what she was about to say would sound unreasonable, and she could not make it sound less so, so she said it directly. "She did it in her sleep. Just now, in the main hall. She was completely asleep, and her hand came up and did it anyway."

Mava did not speak. She looked at Suna for several seconds with an expression Suna could not categorize. There was no disbelief in it, no alarm either, only the look of a person hearing a sound she had been straining to hear for a very long time.

"There's something else," Suna said. "She used a word I don't know. She pointed at you and said it. Nushi."

Mava made a sound. It was not a word. It was a small involuntary thing, the sound of air leaving the chest when the chest is struck — not hard, not violently, but in the precise place where it cannot brace. She put her hand flat on the stone step and pressed down, as if she needed to confirm that the stone was solid, that she was sitting on something that would hold.

"Say it again."

"Nushi."

Mava closed her eyes. She sat like that for several seconds, her face turned slightly toward the valley, the wet air moving past them both. When she opened her eyes they had a bright, hard shine that was not tears — the gloss of pressure held at the surface.

"My sister called me that." Her voice was low and steady, each word chosen from a very small and very precise set. "Nushi. It's from our home dialect — an old valley word from the village we grew up in, past the Anke pass. Iru used it only for me. Nobody speaks that dialect anymore. I don't think there's anyone left alive who does, except me. And I barely speak it. I just remember it."

"Your sister."

"Iru." Mava said the name the way she might set down something fragile — with both hands, with her full attention. "My younger sister. She started calling me that when we were small, and then it stuck. Even when we were grown women. Even when I told her it made me sound like a cat." The corner of her mouth lifted for a second, more memory than smile — the ghost of an old joke that had outlived the person who'd made it. "She died on this road. Forty years ago. She was coming down from the pass in a cart with a traveling group — porters, another family, I don't remember who they were — and the road gave way in the monsoon. Three people went down with it. Iru was one of them. She was twenty-nine."

She said it simply. The words had the worn smoothness of a thing told many times — not to others, Suna understood, but to herself. The private recitation of a loss that had not dimmed but had been shaped by forty years of repetition into something she could carry.

"I haven't heard anyone say that word since she died," Mava said. "It isn't a word you'd find in a book. It was just what she called me, in a language that was already dying when we were girls."

Suna did not speak for a moment. She was aware of the stone step under her, the damp seeping through the fabric of her skirt where she sat, the flood sound filling the valley below with its steady undifferentiated roar. She was aware that she had come here hoping to be told something ordinary — that "nushi" was a Kondo family nickname, a grandmother's word, something the children shared among themselves — and that she was not going to be told anything ordinary.

"Your daughter's never heard me use that word," Mava said. It was not a question. "Not here. If it comes out at all now, it comes out with Ori when she's sick or grieving, and I don't even hear myself do it."

"No," Suna said. "She hasn't."

They sat together on the wet steps. The bird was still working the updraft above the waterline. From inside the main hall came the muffled sound of children's voices — Kael's shout, the babbling run of the younger play group, a baby crying and being hushed — the ordinary noise of forty-three people crammed into a space that had been built for travelers passing through, not for travelers stuck.

Mava rubbed her ear. She caught herself doing it and this time did not stop. Her palm worked the ear in its old slow circle while she watched the valley as if the landscape had another shape underneath it.

"The road," she said. "Iru died right below us. Somewhere down there, under the water. I didn't know that when we arrived — Venn planned the route, but he never knew what stretch of road this was to me. Habel did. She was fifteen when it happened, old enough to remember the search and the way my mother sounded when they brought the body up." She paused. "I recognized the road on the second day. I've been watching the water cover the place where she fell, and I haven't said anything about it to anyone, because what would I say? It was forty years ago. The cart went off the road and she went down with it, and they found her body two days later in the river below. She was twenty-nine years old and she called me Nushi and nobody has said that word to me since."

Suna thought: twenty-nine, the same age as Jin Parra. She did not know why she thought this. It was the kind of useless, exact connection her mind produced when it had no useful response to offer — filing details into categories because the alternative was admitting that the space in front of her held no category at all.

"I don't understand how Asha could know that word," Suna said.

"No." Mava's voice was quiet. "I don't either."

Suna felt the skin tighten along her arms. If Mava had laughed, or shrugged, or named some ordinary source for the word, Suna could have filed it and gone back inside. Instead there was only the old woman beside her, the water below, and the fact itself sitting between them with nowhere to be put.

"Can I —" Suna started, and stopped. She had been about to ask if she could bring Asha to Mava, to sit them together, and she realized she did not know what she expected to happen if she did. What she expected the old woman and the child to do in each other's presence that would make this resolve into sense.

"I'd like to see her do it," Mava said quietly. "The ear. I've done that my whole life. My mother did it too. Iru did too."

"Iru did it?"

"All of us. On my mother's side. Left ear. I never noticed until Iru pointed it out — she thought it was funny. She'd do it and then point at me doing it and laugh." Mava's hand was on her ear again, and she let it stay this time. "It's just something our bodies did."

Our bodies. Not our family, not we. Our bodies — as if the gesture belonged to the flesh itself, passed along through some channel that ran beneath choice or knowledge, carried in muscle and tendon and the blind habit of a hand finding the same place on the same ear, over and over, through generations that didn't know they were repeating anything.

Suna sat with her a while longer. They did not talk much more. Mava asked after Asha — how she was sleeping, whether she was eating well, whether the damp in the sleeping quarters bothered her — the practical questions of a woman who had raised children and grandchildren and great-nieces and understood that the body was the first thing you checked, regardless of what else was happening. Suna answered. The rain started again, softly, more mist than fall, and they watched it settle on the courtyard stones and on the brown surface of the flood below.

When Suna went back inside, the main hall was dim and warm with breath and lamp-oil, the evening meal being prepared in the kitchen outside, its smoke drifting in through the open side. She found her children: Kael at his pebble game, Rimi reading, Asha on their shared mat, awake now, arranging a strip of cloth she'd found somewhere into shapes that followed a logic only she understood.

"Asha."

"Mama."

"Who's Nushi?"

Asha looked at her with the transparent certainty of a child who did not understand why an adult would ask something so obvious. She pointed through the doorway, toward the courtyard, in the direction where Mava had been sitting and might still be sitting.

"Nushi," she said.

Suna picked her up and held her, pressing her face into Asha's hair, which smelled of smoke and sweat and the warm-grain scent of a child who had been sleeping on a cotton mat all afternoon. Asha allowed it for a moment, then squirmed, and Suna let her go. She watched her daughter return to the mat and the strip of cloth, small fingers working it into a new arrangement.

That night Suna slept badly. Deshi snored on her right side, one hand tucked under her cheek. Peyam murmured through the tail end of his prayers, the beads clicking softly against each other in the dark. Rain fell and stopped and fell again, and the flood below the shrine filled the night with its low, constant sound — water over stone, water over earth, water over the place where the roads converged and drowned.

Asha slept deeply, curled against Suna's side, her face pressed into Suna's ribs. At some point in the middle of the night — the rain had paused and the only sound was the flood and the breathing of her family and the forty other lives separated from them by nothing but thin partitions of stone — Asha's left hand rose to her ear.

"Nushi," she said, not waking.

In the dark her hand found the ear again and moved there with the same patient turn.

Suna lay still and watched the hand move in the dark. The gesture no longer looked borrowed. It looked settled, at home in the body making it. By morning she would have to let somebody else see it — Mava again, maybe more than Mava. She could not keep this one inside the narrow partition of her own watching and still call it ordinary. The water below them sounded like the road giving way under Iru's feet. She held her daughter against her ribs and did not sleep for a long time.
