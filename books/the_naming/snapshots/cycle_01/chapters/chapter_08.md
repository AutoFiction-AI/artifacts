# Chapter 8

Suna had been watching Tomas Okoye for two days before she understood what she was watching.

It started the morning after the families agreed to keep sitting for the naming. Not agreed — submitted. Bram Okoye had argued his case with a teacher's precision and it hadn't mattered, because the custodians held the food stores and the Kondos had twelve mouths and the calculation was simple and humiliating: you eat, therefore you comply. The compound's mood since then had been the mood of people performing cooperation that fooled no one, and against that backdrop Suna noticed Tomas because noticing things was how she managed, and managing was how she stayed upright.

He was ten, slim, with his mother's round face but his father's long fingers — the kind of hands a boy didn't grow into until fifteen or sixteen, so that now they looked borrowed, oversized, as if he'd been assembled from parts that hadn't consulted each other. He played with the younger children more than most ten-year-olds would tolerate, carrying baby Ada on his hip when Nessa needed both hands, showing Kael how to stack pebbles without using the flat ones as a base. His patience with smaller children looked to Suna like the competence of a boy conscripted early into helping — not resentful patience, but resigned patience, the kind that came from being the older sibling of a baby who still woke three times a night.

The hesitation appeared at the morning meal. Or the morning before. She couldn't pin it, which bothered her, because Suna tracked moments the way other women tracked weather. She tracked what Asha ate and how much Kael drank and whether Rimi had slept on her left side again, which meant her shoulder would ache by noon and she'd be sullen until someone acknowledged it. She tracked the cistern's water level and the number of flatbreads remaining from the previous evening and which families had contributed labor to the kitchen that day and which had found reasons not to. She tracked other people's children too, not from sentiment but from the same instinct that made her count the rice portions before every meal — more people to account for meant more variables, and Suna handled variables by counting them.

So she couldn't say exactly when Tomas started catching on his consonants.

He was asking Kael to pass the water cup, and the word stuck. Not a stutter — Suna had grown up with a cousin who stammered on vowels, and she knew the sound of it, the way a stutterer's whole face participated in the effort. This was different. Tomas's jaw locked briefly before the hard consonant, the muscle along his cheek tightening for less than a second, and then the word released with a slight elongation of the first syllable. *C-cup.* A catch and release. A small mechanical failure in a mouth that had been working fine for ten years.

She knew the mechanism. She had been listening to it for ten days from the mouth of Jin Parra.

Jin's hesitation was subtle enough that most people seemed not to register it, or else they folded it into the general impression of a man who spoke as little as possible. Suna had noticed on the second day. She noticed speech the way she noticed how a person held a knife or whether they washed their hands before touching the communal bread — not from judgment, but from the restless inventory that ran beneath her waking life like water under paving stones. Jin caught on certain consonants, the hard ones, the plosives, the sounds that required the jaw to close and open with force. C, k, p, t. His jaw would lock for a fraction of a second, the muscle along his cheek visible beneath the skin, and then the word would emerge slightly stretched, as if he'd had to push it through a space that had narrowed without warning.

Tomas was doing the same thing. The same consonants. The same small muscular event along the jaw.

Suna set down the flatbread she'd been pulling apart for Asha and watched. Tomas was talking to Melie now — Jin's daughter, six, who had attached herself to the older boy with the uncritical loyalty of a child who recognized someone kind. He was explaining a rule of the pebble game, and the word *put* stuck in his mouth the same way. The catch. The elongation. The jaw unlocking like a hinge that had briefly rusted.

Across the morning circle, Nessa Okoye sat with Ada in her lap, bouncing the baby absently on one knee. She was watching her son. Nessa's face had no defense — never had, as far as Suna could tell. Fear, hope, confusion, tenderness — everything moved across her features without permission, which made her easy to read and hard to look at, because what Suna saw in her face right now was not general worry. It was recognition. Specific, consonant-by-consonant recognition.

Their eyes met. Suna looked away first, but not before she'd registered the question in Nessa's expression: *You see it too?*

She saw it. Four families now. The Meras and the Kondos, through Asha's gesture and Mava's dead sister's word. The Parras and the Daos, through Melie's impossible allergy. And now the Okoyes — the family of the man who had stood in the naming hall and argued, correctly and uselessly, that the ritual was coercion — connected to the Parras through a ten-year-old boy whose jaw had learned to lock on consonants he'd never had trouble with before.

Five families. Five out of seven.

Suna finished pulling apart the bread and handed the pieces to Asha, who received them with both hands and ate with her mouth open, watching her mother with the unreadable calm of a four-year-old who did not know that her own body had already become evidence.

---

That afternoon the rain came in hard and the children compressed into the main hall. Twenty-one of them, ranging from Ada's one year to Fen Tesh's sixteen, occupying the stone floor in a shifting mass of noise and negotiation while the adults arranged themselves along the walls and tried not to contribute to the volume. Deshi had positioned herself near the cold cooking fire — an act of territorial habit that no longer drew comment from anyone except Rimi, who rolled her eyes with the practiced contempt of a twelve-year-old who had decided that adults were a failing species.

Suna stationed herself where she could see the children's clump without sitting in it. The older ones — Leni, Fen, Raya Dao — maintained their adolescent separateness near the walls, performing the studied indifference of teenagers trapped with younger children they considered beneath their dignity. The younger ones pooled at the center in a shifting economy of games and grievances. Tomas was in the middle, as usual, mediating a dispute between Kael and Sem Dao over a set of smooth stones that had become, through the alchemy of confinement, more valuable than anything the adults could offer.

She watched Tomas talk. She counted the hesitations. Three in ten minutes, all on hard consonants. *T-take it back. I already p-put it there. C-come on, Sem.* The children around him didn't seem to notice, or processed it as unremarkable the way children processed most things — a man limped, a woman hummed, a boy started catching on his k's. The world was full of such things if you were eight or six.

Jin Parra was sitting against the far wall with his knees drawn up, Melie leaning against his side, her thumb in her mouth. He was watching the children with the specific vigilance of a man whose daughter's body had already done something impossible — whose daughter's throat had swelled shut from a food she'd eaten her whole life, claiming an allergy that belonged to another family. His jaw worked slightly as he watched, the unconscious motion of a habit he'd carried since adolescence.

The same motion Tomas was learning.

Suna looked at Jin's jaw, then at Tomas's jaw, then at Nessa. Nessa was nursing Ada in the corner with a cloth draped over her shoulder, her free hand resting on her thigh. Her fingers were opening and closing in a slow rhythm that had nothing to do with the baby and everything to do with the boy across the room whose mouth was becoming someone else's.

---

The next morning was the first without rain since the fourth day. The clouds didn't clear — they thinned, turning the light from gray to a diffuse white that made the courtyard stones steam as the moisture lifted. The children spilled outside with the urgency of animals released from a hold, and even Deshi stood at the edge of the covered walkway and lifted her face to the warm air with an expression that, on anyone else, would have been called relief.

Suna was wringing laundry at the cistern. The work was necessary and constant — forty-three people produced a volume of soiled fabric that had become the compound's most tangible communal problem, a problem she addressed with the same methodical attention she brought to everything else because the alternative was to address nothing, to let the vertigo underneath the method have its way. She wrung a shirt of Kael's until her knuckles whitened against the wet fabric, draped it over the stone lip, picked up the next piece.

Kiri Tesh was sitting on the courtyard steps about ten meters away, her legs stretched in front of her, sorting seed pods she'd collected from the edge of the terraced garden. She was a wiry child, all elbows and sharp collarbones, with a habit of biting the inside of her cheek when she concentrated that made her look older than eight. She had her father Miro's dark hair, but she carried herself with a physical self-possession that belonged to neither parent — Miro was cautious in movement, Enn nervous, and Kiri was neither. She arranged her seed pods on the stone step with the focused determination of a scientist organizing specimens, dividing them by size into precise rows.

She was humming.

Suna didn't register the melody at first. It entered as background, the hum of a child busy with a task, part of the compound's ambient soundtrack where someone was always making sound — coughing, arguing, praying, scraping pots. Then the melody turned at a specific interval. A minor third dropping to a major second in a pattern that was not common, not universal, not the kind of thing a child would pick up from exposure to ordinary songs.

Suna stopped wringing. Her hands stayed in the wet fabric but her arms went still. Water dripped off her elbows and darkened the stone at her feet.

The melody was her mother's lullaby.

Not a similar melody. Not a tune with the same general shape. The specific song — the one with the descending line that reversed itself in the third phrase, the one that looped back to its own beginning without ever resolving, the one her mother had sung in the old dialect that nobody in this region spoke because it came from a mountain valley four hundred kilometers south where the roads were bad and the villages had been emptying for thirty years. The song was built on intervals that didn't follow the scale most people learned. Suna's mother used to say the tune was older than the words, that the melody had existed before anyone thought to put a child to sleep with it.

Her mother had died when Suna was nineteen. The lullaby had died with her — not deliberately, not as an act of refusal, but because the song belonged to a voice and a set of arms and a particular darkness that was her mother's bedroom in her mother's house, and it couldn't be reproduced in any other setting without becoming wrong. Suna had not sung it for her own children. She hadn't sung it here. She was certain of this because she hadn't thought of the song in months, possibly years, and the quality of hearing it now — from the mouth of an eight-year-old Tesh girl who had grown up in a different region, a different family, a different everything — was the quality of meeting a dead person on the street. Not shock. Not horror. The flat impossibility of a presence where an absence should be.

Kiri hummed through the third phrase, hit the reversal, and continued. Her pitch was good. Not perfect — she was eight — but the intervals were right. The rhythm was right. The held note before the loop, the place where her mother used to pause and let the breath carry the silence before the tune began again, was right.

Suna's hands released the fabric. She stood there with wet arms, water running down her wrists, watching an eight-year-old girl hum her dead mother's song while sorting seed pods on the steps.

She should go over. She should ask Kiri where she'd heard the melody. She should approach the way she'd approached Mava two weeks ago — carefully, looking for the ordinary explanation before admitting the extraordinary. Someone in the compound might know the song. She might have hummed it in her sleep. The melody might be more common than she thought, a variant that had traveled farther than she knew.

She didn't move.

She stood at the cistern and listened, and the melody looped twice more before Kiri lost interest and started talking to one of the younger Kondo children who'd wandered over, and the song stopped as easily as it had started, as if it had been nothing — a thing Kiri's mouth had done while her hands were busy, a fragment of sound with no history and no weight.

Six families. The Teshes, now. Through a lullaby that belonged to a dead woman from a place none of them had ever been.

Suna picked up the next piece of laundry. Her hands were trembling in a way that had nothing to do with cold water and everything to do with the specific frequency of the third phrase, which was still in her ears like a tone that continued after the source had stopped. She wrung the fabric until the tremor was absorbed into the work, and when Deshi called from across the courtyard to ask whether she planned to wash everything or just the things that mattered, she answered with a steadiness that cost her something she could feel but couldn't name.

"All of it," she said. "I'm doing all of it."

Deshi looked at her for a beat longer than necessary, and Suna understood that her mother-in-law's eye missed very little, and that the effort of seeming fine was itself a kind of signal to anyone watching closely enough.

---

Later that afternoon she went looking for Enn Tesh. She found her in the sleeping quarters, folding blankets with a sharpness that suggested she was working something out through the medium of linen. Enn's face was lean and mobile, the face of a woman perpetually bracing for the next problem, and she wore worry so openly that it had become a kind of social feature — people checked Enn's expression the way they checked the sky.

"Your daughter," Suna said. "Kiri. Does she hum a lot?"

Enn looked up, a blanket corner pinched between her fingers. "She hums constantly. Since she was three. It drives Miro mad."

"The tune she was humming this morning, on the courtyard steps. Do you know it?"

"I didn't hear her this morning." Enn folded the corner with unnecessary precision, matching edge to edge. "Which song?"

Suna almost hummed it. The notes were in her throat, ready, and she almost let them out, and then she stopped because something in her refused to produce the melody here, for this purpose. To hum it would be to confirm it — to make it real in a way that hearing it from Kiri's mouth had not quite accomplished, because hearing could be doubted but producing could not.

"A slow one," she said. "It doesn't end — it loops back."

Enn shrugged. "She picks things up. From the radio, from other children. She's got an ear."

"The radio doesn't play this song," Suna said, and the flatness of the statement — its certainty, its weight — made Enn stop folding and look at her with the sharpened attention that everyone in the compound was beginning to pay when a sentence carried more than its surface could hold.

"What song is it?" Enn asked.

"I don't know," Suna said. The lie was small and practical. She wasn't ready to say *my dead mother's lullaby* because saying it would make it part of the pattern, and the pattern was already larger than she could manage while also managing Asha's gestures and Kael's restless sleep and Rimi's sharpening silence and Deshi's observation and the fact that her husband was not ahead of them on the road but three months gone and probably never coming back.

She carried the lie out of the sleeping quarters and into the gray-white afternoon, and she added it to the ledger of things she couldn't yet afford to say.

---

That evening, after the children were settled and the compound had reached the uneasy quiet that passed for nighttime peace, Suna found Nessa at the edge of the prayer courtyard.

Nessa was standing with Ada asleep against her chest, the baby's weight pulling her slightly forward. She had wrapped Ada in a length of faded blue-gray cloth — her own shawl, repurposed, the fringe knotted where it had begun to unravel — and in the oil-lamp light she looked like something half-formed, not quite separate from the stone behind her. She had a way of holding Ada that wasn't cradling so much as armoring — the baby positioned between herself and whatever was coming, as if a sleeping one-year-old could absorb impact.

Suna had never spent time alone with Nessa. Their interactions had been practical — the choreography of women who cooked in the same kitchen and washed at the same cistern and negotiated sleeping arrangements for children who increasingly refused to sleep where they were told. They weren't unfriendly. They simply hadn't needed each other yet.

"Can I sit with you?" Suna asked.

Nessa shifted sideways on the low courtyard wall. "Please."

Suna sat. The stone was still warm from the afternoon's thin sun, and the warmth seeped through her skirt into her thighs. The courtyard smelled of the wet herbs from the terraced garden, a green mineral scent that the rain drew out of the soil and that clung to everything for hours after the water stopped.

They sat in silence for a while. Ada made small sounds in her sleep — the grunting, liquid noises of a baby whose breathing was a performance even unconscious. Moths circled the nearest oil lamp, their shadows huge and flickering against the courtyard stones.

"Tomas," Suna said.

Nessa's hand moved to Ada's back. "You've noticed."

"I've noticed."

"Since two days ago. Maybe three." Nessa stopped. She looked at the stones between her feet, and when she spoke again her voice was lower, compressed by something heavier than volume. "He's never had any kind of speech issue. Nothing. He talked early, talked clearly — his teachers used to say he was the most articulate boy in his year. Bram used to joke he'd talk for a living, same as his father." She paused. "Same stammer included, now, I guess."

"It's the same pattern," Suna said.

"It's exactly the same. The same sounds. The same thing with his jaw." Nessa's voice had dropped further, as if the volume itself were dangerous — as if speaking softly enough might keep the fact from solidifying. "I've been watching Jin for days. I don't know why — because I noticed the hesitation, because it seemed unusual, and I watch people the way you watch people. I see you watching."

"And now Tomas does it. Not a general stammer. Not his own version."

"Jin's version. The same consonants. The same—" She touched her own jaw, a small circling gesture. "The same lock."

"Have you told Bram?"

"He's heard it. He's got an explanation for it — Tomas is imitating Jin unconsciously, the way kids pick up accents. Children are sponges, that's what he says. But he's heard it, and when Tomas does it at the table, Bram stops eating and looks at his plate like the food's going to tell him something." She shook her head. "Bram's got four explanations. Imitation, suggestion, coincidence, and confirmation bias. He cycles through them. He's on confirmation bias this week."

"Does he believe any of them?"

"He believes all of them until ten at night, and then he lies next to me and holds my hand and doesn't believe any of them." She looked at Suna. "Don't tell him I said that."

"There's another one," Suna said. "Another manifestation. That's why I came to you."

Nessa's hand stilled on Ada's back. "Who?"

"Kiri Tesh. She was humming a song this morning on the courtyard steps. A lullaby."

"Lots of children hum."

"My mother's lullaby. A song from my mother's village, in a dialect nobody up here speaks. My mother's been dead nineteen years. I haven't sung it since."

The silence that followed had weight. Not the companionable silence of earlier — this one pressed. Nessa pulled Ada fractionally closer, and the baby grunted in protest, and Nessa loosened her hold but didn't change the position of her arms.

"How can you be sure it's the same song?" she asked. "Maybe it's something close. A common tune."

"It's not common. The intervals are wrong — I mean they're right, but they're wrong for the normal scale. The melody doesn't resolve. It loops back instead of ending. My mother used to say that was the point — a lullaby shouldn't stop, because sleep should feel like going around again, not arriving somewhere. Nobody writes songs like that. It was hers."

"Could you have hummed it without knowing? In your sleep, or while you were working?"

Suna sat with the question. Outside the courtyard, the flood's sound carried up from the valley, that constant rush that had become so woven into the compound's atmosphere that it registered only during strange silences, and then with the force of something that had been listening the whole time.

"I don't think so," she said. "But I can't be certain. Which is almost worse than knowing."

"It's six families now," Nessa said quietly. "If you're counting."

"I'm counting. Asha and the Kondos. Melie and the Daos. Tomas and Jin. Kiri and the Meras. That's the Teshes too. Everyone except the Leos."

"The Leos are quiet. Cade hardly talks to anyone who isn't Wynn or Vera."

"Have you noticed anything with Pik?"

Nessa shook her head. Then she said, "Give it time," and the bitterness in her voice wasn't dramatic bitterness, wasn't the performed bitterness of a complaint — it was the flat bitterness of a woman stating a probability she didn't want to be right about.

"Nessa."

"What?"

Suna chose her words the way she chose her steps on wet stone — testing the surface before committing weight. "When I watched you this morning — watching Tomas — you looked like you already knew. Not surprised. Like you'd been waiting."

Nessa's face did what it always did, which was everything. The oil lamp caught it all — the tightening around her eyes, the way her mouth pressed shut and then opened again as if the pressure of keeping it closed was worse than what would come out.

"I've been waiting since Melie's throat swelled shut," she said. "Since the allergy. Because when I watched Bett lean over her — knowing exactly what to do, knowing it was the Dao allergy before anyone said it — I thought: it's going to find us too. It's going to reach one of mine."

"And it has."

"And it has." She adjusted Ada's weight, resettling the baby higher against her chest. Ada's face was mashed against Nessa's collarbone, one fist curled against the faded cloth. "But this isn't — what's happening to Tomas isn't the first time something's been taken from me."

Suna waited. She could feel the shape of what was coming the way she felt the air change before rain — not the fact itself, but the displacement it produced, the pressure of something about to take up room.

"We had another child," Nessa said. "Before Tomas. A boy. His name was Efe."

The name entered the air between them and stayed.

"He died at three months. A fever. It came fast — by morning he was hot, by afternoon he wouldn't nurse, and by evening his breathing had changed. Bram drove for two hours to find a clinic that was open, and it didn't matter. By the time they got a line in, he was—" She broke off. Not dramatically. The sentence simply ran into something it couldn't pass. She breathed. Started again from a different place. "We buried him in the yard behind our house. Bram dug the hole himself because he said he wouldn't let someone else do it. The hole was very small. That's what I remember most. How small the hole was."

Suna didn't touch her. The impulse was there — hand on knee, hand on arm, the practical touch she used with her children when words weren't enough — but something in Nessa's stillness told her that touch right now would crack the structure that was holding the words in their sequence, and Nessa needed the sequence to finish.

"We don't talk about him," Nessa said. "We — Bram and I decided, after. That we wouldn't carry it forward. That Tomas and Leni would grow up without the weight of a dead brother they'd never met. We thought that was the right thing." Her voice was steady and her eyes were wet and the two facts weren't in contradiction — they were the specific product of a grief that had been managed for so long it had developed its own architecture, its own load-bearing walls. "I don't know anymore if it was the right thing."

"Is that why you've been waiting?" Suna asked. "Because if the children are manifesting other families' losses—"

"What's the loss?" Nessa finished. "What's the gap? Melie's allergy connects to the Daos, and the Daos lost a son. Asha connects to the Kondos, and Mava lost a sister. So when Tomas starts speaking like Jin — what's missing? Whose absence?" She looked at Suna with the directness of someone who had decided to be fully present rather than protected. "Ours. Maybe. Or maybe Jin's — his wife."

"His wife died," Suna said, and as she said it she heard the echo of her own constructed story — *my husband went ahead* — and recognized, not for the first time, how a lie took on the texture of fact when nobody pushed against it.

"His wife died," Nessa repeated, and her tone carried something that might have been doubt, or might have been the particular attention of a woman who had learned to listen past the surface of declarations.

The compound sounds filtered through the stillness around them — someone coughing in the sleeping quarters, the creak of the kitchen overhang's timber frame, the low murmur of the custodians' radio bleeding through their closed door, an indistinct voice reporting news from somewhere that wasn't here.

"I don't know what's happening," Suna said. "I don't know if it's the shrine, or the custodians, or something the children are doing to each other without knowing it, or something that would have happened anywhere if you put this many families in a small enough space for long enough. I don't have an explanation."

"Bram cycles through his four explanations like a man shuffling cards. He deals himself a new hand every morning." The faintest edge of humor entered Nessa's voice — not amusement but the reflex of a woman who had survived difficulty partly by describing it. "I don't have four explanations. I've got zero. I've just got my son's jaw doing something it shouldn't be able to do."

Suna reached across the space between them and put her hand on Nessa's knee. The gesture was practical, not sentimental — the economy of touch she used with her children when language was beside the point. Nessa's hand came down and covered hers, and they stayed like that for a while, two women on a warm stone wall with a sleeping baby between them and the flood's sound rising from the valley below like a fact that wouldn't be argued with.

"Efe would've been eleven this year," Nessa said. "Almost the same age as Tomas. They would've fought constantly — brothers do. Bram would've had to mediate, because I cave in about three seconds. Leni would've bossed them both." She was smiling now, a small smile that had nothing behind it but loss and the particular tenderness of imagining a life that hadn't happened. "Instead, Tomas is a patient older brother to a baby sister, and he's the kindest boy I know, and now he talks like a stranger he met ten days ago."

"Do you think we should tell the custodians?" Suna asked.

"About Tomas? They'll find out. They see everything."

"About Efe."

The name again. Suna felt its weight differently the second time — not surprise but confirmation, the way a word gained solidity through repetition.

Nessa was quiet for several breaths. "About the gap, you mean. About what might be pulling Tomas toward Jin's pattern."

"Yes."

"I don't know. I don't know what they'd do with it." She turned Suna's question back. "Would you tell them about the lullaby?"

Suna thought about Fasa's steady gaze during the namings, the way the custodian held silence like a vessel and waited for it to fill. She thought about Olet's flat calm, which was either faith or indifference, and which unsettled her precisely because she couldn't tell which.

"Not yet," she said. "Not until I understand what they want from us."

"They want the map," Nessa said. "Fasa keeps saying it — the true kinship map. As if we're hiding it instead of trying to figure out what the hell is happening to our kids."

Nessa stood, lifting Ada higher against her chest, the baby's weight resettling into the familiar position. "I should go in. Bram's probably lying in the dark composing his fifth explanation."

Suna let the joke pass. She stayed on the wall after Nessa went inside, listening to the flood, feeling the stone cool beneath her as the last of the day's heat bled away. She counted. Asha's borrowed gesture: the Mera-Kondo connection. Melie's impossible allergy: the Parra-Dao connection. Tomas's new hesitation: the Okoye-Parra connection. Kiri's lullaby: the Tesh-Mera connection.

Four connections. Six families. The Leos were the only ones untouched, and Nessa had been right — give it time.

The melody came back to her, uninvited, the way her mother's voice sometimes surfaced between waking and sleep — not as memory but as presence, as if the song existed independently of anyone who had ever sung it and had simply been waiting for a mouth to carry it forward. Kiri's mouth. An eight-year-old in a different family from a different region who sorted seed pods by size and bit the inside of her cheek when she concentrated and hummed songs she had no reason to know.

Suna pressed her lips together and didn't hum.

She sat with the silence and the flood and the cooling stone, and she held the new weight of Nessa's dead son alongside the older weight of her own absent husband and the growing weight of her daughter's borrowed gestures, and she catalogued each weight separately because that was what she did, and because the alternative was to feel them all at once, which was a vertigo she couldn't afford. Not yet. Not here. Not with three children sleeping in the next room and a mother-in-law whose sharp eye missed nothing and a naming ceremony she would have to sit through again tomorrow and a lullaby that had crossed nineteen years and four hundred kilometers to arrive in the throat of a stranger's child.

The oil lamps in the main hall threw soft light across the courtyard. Inside, someone coughed. Ada cried once — sharp, sudden, a single note — and then stopped. The flood went on.
