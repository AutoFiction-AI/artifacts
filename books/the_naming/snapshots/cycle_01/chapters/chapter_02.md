# Chapter 2

The morning light through the main hall's open side had the color of rinsed brass, weak and already blurred by the humidity that preceded rain. Fasa moved through the space with a cloth, wiping the stone ledges where the oil lamps would sit, though they had been wiped yesterday and the day before that and every day for twenty years. The preparation was not cleaning. It was attention — the same sustained looking that preceded prayer, that preceded diagnosis, that preceded any act of genuine encounter. She had learned, in twenty years, that the preparation mattered more than the ceremony itself. The ceremony was speech. The preparation was seeing.

She set the cloth on the ledge and crouched to check the oil level in the first lamp. The wick was trimmed, the reservoir half full. She moved to the second. The routine of her hands freed her mind to do what it had been doing since yesterday's arrivals: reading.

Seven families. Forty-three people, if she counted correctly from Olet's registration ledger. They had come up the switchback path through yesterday's rain, arriving in clusters and singles over the course of the afternoon — some carrying luggage on their heads, one family hauling a cart they'd abandoned at the second turn, a young man climbing the last stretch with his daughter on his back and a bag in his free hand. She had watched from this hall, standing where she stood now, while Olet and the three assistant custodians received them below. She had not gone down. She never went down. The receiving was Olet's charge. The naming was hers.

What she had, before the naming began, was a set of observations. They were not conclusions. They were shapes that might sharpen into meaning or dissolve into the ordinary strangeness of strangers thrust together.

The Kondo family: twelve people, the largest group, arriving in a column with the patriarch at its front and the youngest children sheltered in the middle, moving with the disciplined coordination of a family that traveled together often. The patriarch — Venn — had spoken to Olet at registration with the diction of a man who considered his name sufficient introduction. His wife, Tila, had counted sleeping mats and then asked about the kitchen's water supply, twice, the repetition of a woman who needed to know her household could function before she could rest. The elderly aunt, Mava, had a slow left-hand gesture that Fasa had watched three times before recognizing it as habitual rather than palsy — a circling of her palm against her left ear, steady and self-soothing and as natural to her as breathing. She had settled into the corner nearest the open wall and sat looking at the rain with the patience of someone who had looked at a great many things in seventy years and had made her peace with waiting.

The Mera family: a woman of thirty-eight, her mother-in-law, an elderly uncle, three children. No husband. The woman — Suna — had a practiced competence about her, the efficiency of someone managing on insufficient resources. She had organized her children's sleeping area with the economy of practice, the kind of arrangement that spoke not of a household that ran smoothly but of one that ran on her hands alone. The mother-in-law, Deshi, had watched her with an expression Fasa could not yet read: judgment or concern or that corrosive blend of both that family produces when the person you depend on is also the person you blame.

A man alone with a daughter. Jin, the registration said. The child was six. They had taken the smallest sleeping space without being asked, as if practiced at taking up as little room as possible. The man carried the child's bag and his own in the same hand, a habit that suggested the other hand needed to be free for the child at all times. His speech, in the few words Fasa had heard him exchange with Olet, had a slight catch — a jaw-lock before certain consonants that she had first thought was the clenching of cold or exhaustion and then recognized as a permanent feature of his voice.

The Teshes: a man in his forties named Miro, thick-handed, his weight carried low, the build of someone who had worked outdoors most of his life. His wife Enn, dark-haired, a nervous habit of pulling her braid over her left shoulder and then pushing it back. Their daughter Kiri, eight, already asleep before the evening meal was served. A teenage boy, Fen, sixteen, tall, dark-haired, who had helped unload the Teshes' luggage without being asked and then sat on the edge of the group and read a book that was too waterlogged to read. And the grandmother — Gitta. Small and thin and careful in her movements. She had answered Olet's registration questions with a composure Fasa recognized. It was the composure of someone who had been asked these questions before and had her answers in order — not rehearsed, but prepared. The difference was subtle and might be nothing. Some people were simply organized.

The Okoyes: a family that moved with the urgency of people fleeing something rather than traveling toward something. The father, Bram, was tall and thin, with glasses and the posture of a teacher — slightly forward, engaged, hands shaping the air as he spoke. His wife, Nessa, carried the baby against her chest and kept her other hand on the ten-year-old boy's shoulder even when the boy was sitting still and needed no steadying. The eldest, a girl of fifteen named Leni, had said nothing to any adult since arrival. She had bitten her nails through the evening meal and watched every family in the hall with the unfiltered attention of someone who had not yet learned to disguise her looking.

The Daos: two sisters and their children. The older sister, Hala, had spoken for both of them — sturdy, protective, a woman who positioned her body between the room and the people she was responsible for. The younger, Bett, had not spoken at all since arriving. Not to Olet, not to the other families, not, as far as Fasa could tell, even to her own daughter — a girl of seven named Nori who held Hala's hand rather than Bett's and seemed to understand the arrangement as permanent rather than circumstantial. Bett was the only adult in the compound who had not exchanged a single social word since arrival. Her silence was not shyness. Fasa knew shyness, and it looked down, looked away, looked for cover. Bett's silence looked straight ahead.

The Leos: two men, a child, and an older woman. The younger of the two men, Cade, had started three sentences during registration and abandoned each one before finding the phrasing he needed. His partner, Wynn, had steadied him with a hand on his arm — a touch that was brief and habitual and spoke of years of knowing when the hand was needed. The child was seven, a boy named Pik, and Cade had not used the word adopted with Olet. He had said our son. The older woman was Cade's mother, Vera, and she had watched her son struggle with the registration form with the expression of a woman who had decided, years ago, that she would fight anyone who made her son explain himself and was assessing whether this was going to be one of those times.

Fasa straightened and set the last lamp on its ledge. She stepped back to survey the hall. The stone floor had been swept. The low benches were arranged in a loose semicircle facing the spot where she would stand. Behind her, the valley view: brown floodwater covering the roads below, and beyond the water the green smear of submerged fields and the suggestion of trees. The sound of the flood was the shrine's constant breath — a low roar that the ear adjusted to within an hour but that the body never quite forgot. She could feel it in the soles of her feet, transmitted through the stone.

She had been wrong before. She had read families as closed who turned out to be merely shy, read silences as concealment when they were only grief, seen patterns that resolved into coincidence or wishful thinking. The naming revealed, but it revealed on its own schedule, and twenty years had taught her to be patient with its pace and her own errors. What the years had given her was not certainty but the discipline to wait for it, and the willingness to look at strangers and believe — genuinely, with the full weight of her practice and her faith — that what they declared was never the whole of what they were.

She heard Olet's step behind her, unhurried, identifiable by the particular weight he placed on his right foot.

"Are they ready?" she asked.

"They are here," Olet said. It was his way — he answered what was true rather than what was asked. "I will ring the bell when you wish."

"Give me a moment."

He waited. He was good at waiting. In twenty years she had never seen him impatient, and the consistency of his calm had shifted, over those years, from reassuring to unsettling to simply the weather of his character. He stood in the doorway with his lean frame and his face that revealed nothing, and she had long since stopped trying to read him the way she read the families. Olet was not a text. He was a wall — not opaque but unneeding of interpretation.

"What do you think of them?" she asked.

"I think they are seven families stranded by a flood." He paused, and the pause was not hesitation but the natural rhythm of a man who treated silence as punctuation. "What they are beyond that is the naming's question, not mine."

She nodded. The bell, when it came, would mean obligation. She was about to ask forty-three strangers to stand in this stone hall and say who they were. Most would find it a small discomfort — a toll paid for shelter, unreasonable but endurable. Some would find it invasive. One or two might resist, though resistance in a building where the custodians controlled the food stores was a luxury only the very principled or the very well-provisioned could sustain.

She did not think of the naming as coercive. She thought of it as a question the road itself asked, a question the shrine merely gave form and voice and the discipline of witness. Twenty years of watching strangers arrive and declare and depart had taught her that the declaration was never the whole truth — but the shape of the untruth, the way a family chose to arrange itself for display, told her more than honesty would have. The gaps were the naming's real material. The silences, the pauses, the words chosen and the words avoided. What a family did not say was the door the naming would eventually open.

"Ring the bell," she said.

---

Olet rang it three times. The iron note carried across the compound, through the kitchen overhang where someone was boiling water for tea, past the cistern, into the sleeping quarters where mats were still unrolled from the night. The sound cut the morning into before and after. It was the sound of being asked.

They came in clusters, the way families always did — the large groups first, the small ones drifting in behind, the solitary figures last. The Kondos arrived and took up the most space, which was not rudeness but the physics of twelve bodies accustomed to arranging themselves as a unit. Venn led. His wife Tila counted heads as they entered — Fasa saw her lips move, tallying — and steered the younger children toward the bench nearest the open wall, where the light was best and the air moved. Mava settled into her seat with the measured descent of a woman whose joints made every change in altitude a negotiation, and her left hand went immediately to her ear, circling. Arash, the eldest son, came through the doorway last among the Kondos. He was built like his father but leaner, less settled in his body, and he positioned himself at the edge of the family cluster — not quite apart, not quite among them, a young man maintaining a distance so slight that only someone watching for it would notice.

The Meras followed: Suna first with Asha on her hip, then the older children, Rimi walking with her eyes on the floor and Kael scanning the room as if looking for something he expected to find. Behind them came Deshi, whose posture entered the room before she did — spine straight, chin elevated, the carriage of a woman who considered herself the standard against which arrangements were measured. Peyam came last, a thin old man in a faded prayer cap embroidered with a pattern so worn that only the thread's shadow remained. He found a spot near the wall and folded himself into it with the efficient grace of someone who had been sitting on stone floors for seven decades and knew exactly how to distribute his weight.

The Okoyes came together: Bram slightly in front, Nessa behind with baby Ada held close to her chest, Tomas walking between them, and Leni trailing. The girl sat on the bench nearest the door, as close to an exit as the hall's geometry allowed, and crossed her arms in a posture that Fasa had seen in a hundred teenagers — the posture of someone who wanted everyone to know she was here under protest.

Jin Parra came in carrying Melie, set her down near the Mera children, and took a seat at the end of the bench without looking at anyone. He sat with his hands on his knees and his shoulders slightly hunched, the posture of someone who had already calculated the minimum amount of space he was entitled to and was occupying exactly that much.

The Teshes entered quietly — Miro first, then Enn with Kiri between them. Enn's hand was on Kiri's shoulder, steadying or steering or simply maintaining contact, and she kept adjusting her braid with her free hand, pulling it forward and then pushing it back. Fen followed, and Fasa noticed that the boy had found a different book — a thin paperback he slid into his back pocket as he entered, the kind of instinctive concealment of a private pleasure that teenagers performed without thinking. At the back, Gitta moved with the slow precision of someone for whom each step was a considered act. She chose a seat on the far bench, arranged herself, and folded her hands in her lap. Her thumbs rested against each other. She did not look around the room. She did not need to.

The Daos arrived as two smaller bodies within the family body: Hala with Raya and Sem, then Bett with Nori. Hala guided them to a bench near the Okoyes with the efficiency of a woman accustomed to positioning people. Raya sat with her mother's face but a sharper chin, her legs drawn up, her sandal strap twisted and apparently bothering her — she kept reaching down to adjust it without committing to actually fixing it. Sem sat beside his sister and immediately began picking at a callus on his right palm, the focused absorption of a boy who had found something more interesting than the room. Bett sat at the end of the bench. Nori sat beside her with three inches of space between the girl's knee and Bett's thigh — close but not touching, a gap that seemed too precise to be accidental.

The Leos came last. Cade walked in with Pik's hand in his, and Wynn walked beside them carrying a canvas bag that sagged with a parent's preparedness — the things Pik might need if the ceremony took longer than a seven-year-old's patience could hold. Vera, Cade's mother, entered behind them, compact and gray-haired, and sat on the bench beside Wynn with her arms crossed over her chest and her gaze already moving across the room, cataloguing everyone with the vigilance of a woman who had learned that the world did not always accommodate her son and had decided to be ready for it.

Forty-three people in a stone hall. Five custodians — Olet at the doorway, the three assistants along the far wall, Fasa at the center. The sound of breathing, of children shifting, of rain beginning outside, of the flood's constant exhalation from the valley below. The oil lamps were not yet lit — the morning was bright enough that their light would be wasted, and Fasa had learned to read faces better in daylight than in the amber generosity of flame.

She stood where the floor was worn smooth by feet. Twenty years of namings had polished the stone to a dull sheen, and she felt the coolness of it through her sandals. She let the silence work for a moment. Silence in a room this full was not an absence — it was the sound of the flood and the rain and the baby breathing and someone's foot scraping against stone and a child whispering and the whisper being hushed.

"My name is Fasa," she said. "I am the custodian of the naming at this shrine. Olet, whom you met when you arrived, oversees the shrine's daily life. The naming is my charge."

She let the word charge sit. It was a word she chose deliberately — not duty, not role, not responsibility, but charge. Something given and something carried.

"The naming is a condition of shelter here. That is not my choice, and it is not recent — it is the shrine's law, older than I am and older than Olet and older than the road that brought you. Every group that stays more than one night is asked to declare itself. Your family name. Your number. Each member's name and their relation to the speaker. Where you have come from and where you are going. This is what the shrine asks in exchange for its roof."

She watched their faces. Venn Kondo had the expression of a man who found the process unnecessary but not worth contesting — he would comply because compliance cost him nothing he valued. Bram Okoye was frowning, a frown she recognized — a mind already assembling an objection it had not yet decided to voice. Suna Mera had shifted Asha to her other hip. Jin Parra had not moved. Gitta Tesh was watching Fasa with an attention so mild it was nearly invisible.

"Each family speaks for itself. One person may speak for the whole family, or each member may speak in turn. I will ask questions when I have them. The naming is not an interrogation." She paused. She believed this, mostly. "It is a declaration. You tell me who you are. That is all."

The rain hit the stone overhang outside with a sound like dry rice poured from a jar.

"Who will speak first?"

Venn Kondo stood. He had been waiting for the question — his body had been preparing for the act of standing since she began speaking, weight shifting forward, hands on his thighs, the gathering of a man who considered being first not an act of volunteering but a confirmation of rank. He was broad-shouldered, thick in the neck, graying at the temples, and he stood with the particular authority of someone who expected the room to organize itself around him.

"We are the Kondo family," he said. "Twelve. I am Venn Kondo." He turned and gestured with an open hand, precise, introducing his people the way a man introduces his holdings. "My wife, Tila." Tila inclined her head — a small, practical acknowledgment. "My son, Arash." Arash, from the edge of the group, lifted one hand briefly and let it fall. "My sister Habel and her daughter Ori." Habel drew Ori against her side, and the nine-year-old leaned into her mother's ribs without looking up. "My aunt Mava." The old woman in the corner made no gesture. Her left hand continued its slow orbit around her ear. "My brother Pol's sons, their wives, and their children." He gestured toward the remaining members — younger men and women, children between four and eleven who looked up when indicated and then lost interest. "We are traveling to Arash's wedding. The bride's family is in Tessar. When the road opens, we will continue."

"Twelve," Fasa said. "All Kondos? By blood or by marriage?"

"By blood or by marriage. Yes."

"Thank you." She noted the declaration: the most complete she would hear that morning. Every member accounted for, every relation specified, the purpose of travel stated, the family name offered as both fact and credential. Venn sat down with the controlled descent of a man who had paid a toll and expected his receipt. "Who is next?"

Suna Mera stood. She set Asha on the bench beside Deshi, and the four-year-old immediately reached for her grandmother's sleeve. Deshi permitted this without looking down. Suna straightened and clasped her hands in front of her — strong hands, work-roughened, held together as if to keep them from doing something more useful than speaking.

"Suna Mera. There are six of us." She drew a breath. "My mother-in-law, Deshi." Deshi's chin rose a fraction — an acknowledgment that was also an evaluation. "Her brother, Peyam." The old man in the prayer cap touched his forehead in a gesture that compressed greeting, acknowledgment, and a kind of habitual blessing into a single motion of the hand. "My daughter Rimi. She's twelve." Rimi continued staring at the floor. "My son Kael, eight." The boy was watching his mother with a concentration that Fasa recognized — the alertness of a child listening for a wrong note, who had learned that sometimes the adults' words did not match the adults' faces. "And Asha. She's four."

"Six," Fasa said. "Traveling where?"

"South. To my sister's town."

"And your husband?"

The pause was barely a breath. Fasa had been watching for it, and she almost missed it — a fractional tightening of the clasped hands, a stillness in the shoulders that was gone before it could be named.

"He went ahead. He's waiting for us there."

Fasa held the moment. She did not challenge. She noted. A woman traveling south to her sister's town with her husband's mother and her husband's uncle but without her husband, who had gone ahead. The arrangement was not impossible — it might mean nothing more than different schedules, different obligations, the ordinary logistics of moving a family across a country in crisis. But the pause was there, and the pause had a weight to it, and the weight might be embarrassment or might be something else.

"Thank you," she said. She turned to the room.

Bram Okoye stood before she could invite the next family. He stood the way he must have stood in front of thirty students — spine straight, weight forward, chin level, a man who had spent years making himself legible from the back row.

"Bram Okoye. My wife, Nessa." Nessa's arms tightened fractionally around Ada. "Our children. Leni, fifteen. Tomas, ten. Ada — she's one. We are five."

"Where are you coming from?"

"The capital." His jaw worked for a moment before the rest came. "There are checkpoints on the eastern roads. Identity verification, household declarations. We decided to take the mountain route instead."

"Which is now underwater," Fasa said.

"Which is now underwater." He met her eyes. The repetition was not agreement — it was the acknowledgment of a shared fact between two people who might draw very different conclusions from it.

"You're a teacher?"

"I was." He did not elaborate. Nessa looked at the floor. The correction — was, not am — carried the weight of something lost recently enough to still draw blood when touched.

Fasa understood what Bram had not said and did not need to: the checkpoints and the identity verification and the household declarations that had driven his family to the mountain road were part of the same political machinery that had diverted the road crews and left the valley flooded. A government that wanted to know who everyone was, that demanded families declare themselves in categories the state designed. The naming asked a version of the same question. She was aware of this parallel. She had been aware of it for months, and the awareness made her conduct the naming with more care, not less — but she could not demand that the families feel the difference between the shrine's question and the state's. She could only ask, and listen, and note.

"Thank you," she said.

Jin Parra did not stand. He spoke from where he sat, his hands on his knees, his voice flat and pitched to carry the minimum distance.

"Jin Parra. My daughter, Melie. She's six."

Fasa waited. Jin did not continue. The hall waited with her — the quality of the silence changed, became expectant, became the silence of thirty-seven adults and their accumulated curiosity pressing gently on one quiet man.

"Two of you," she said.

"Two."

"Where are you going?"

"North. To her mother's family."

"Her mother isn't with you?"

The jaw-lock. She could see it clearly this time: a brief seizure of the muscles that controlled his jaw, a held breath that resolved into speech one beat later than natural. The hesitation was part of his voice, she thought, the way some people's voices carried a rasp or a lisp — a feature, not a flaw. But the hesitation lengthened around this question, and the lengthening was not the feature. It was the question.

"She died."

"I'm sorry," Fasa said. "When?"

"Three months ago."

"And you're bringing Melie to her mother's people."

"Yes."

He offered nothing more. Fasa could feel the room's attention pressing on him — the sympathy, the curiosity, the faint discomfort of watching a man declare the shape of his life in three sentences and then close the door. Melie, who had moved from the bench to sit on the floor with Asha Mera, was not listening. She was braiding a piece of string she had found in a crack between the flagstones, holding it up to the light from the open wall and examining it with the total seriousness of a child who had found the one task in the room worth doing.

The declaration was the minimum possible. Name, number, destination, the single fact of the wife's death offered as a wall rather than a window. No narrative, no detail, no emotional register beyond flat statement. Fasa had heard grief declarations before — some were floods, some were angry, some collapsed into weeping that the room absorbed in collective discomfort. Jin's was a closed hand. She could not see what it held, and she did not know whether what she could not see was more grief or something else entirely.

"Thank you," she said.

Miro Tesh stood. He rose with the unhurried competence of a man who had spent his life standing up and sitting down in rooms where people waited — a field, a market, a job site. His hands hung at his sides, thick-fingered, the nails trimmed short and clean, the hands of someone who worked outdoors and was careful about it.

"The Tesh family. I'm Miro. My wife, Enn." Enn pulled her braid over her shoulder and held it there, as if the braid were a handle she needed to anchor herself. "Our daughter Kiri — she's eight. My son Fen." He paused, the ordinary pause of a man moving through a list and arriving at a relationship that required a different grammatical construction. "Sixteen. And my mother, Gitta."

"Five," Fasa said. "Where are you traveling?"

"My wife's cousin passed. We're going to the funeral. Were going." He corrected himself with a grimace that was both grief-adjacent and practical — the grimace of a man acknowledging that the dead cousin was still dead and the road was still flooded and neither fact was going to improve by being discussed in this hall. "In the valley."

"I'm sorry for your loss." The formality was directed at Enn, who dipped her head. "And Fen — he is your son?"

"He's my mother's grandson. She raised him."

Fasa turned to Gitta. The old woman sat as she had sat since entering — hands folded, thumbs still, back straight, her face arranged in an expression of such mild patience that it was nearly impossible to read as anything other than courtesy. She was small and white-haired and still with the economy of someone who conserved energy not from frailty but from long practice in knowing exactly how much each gesture cost.

"He is your grandson?" Fasa asked.

"He is my grandson." Gitta's voice was low and clear and carried no inflection that could be interpreted as anything but a simple declaration of a simple fact. She did not elaborate. She did not pause. She did not offer the small softening gestures — a glance at Fen, a nod, a smile — that grandmothers typically offered when speaking of their grandchildren in public. The sentence sat in the air with the solidity of something that had been true for so long it no longer needed proving.

It was the smoothest declaration Fasa had heard that morning. Every other family had produced a moment of friction — Venn's formality, Suna's fractional pause, Bram's corrected tense, Jin's closed hand. Gitta Tesh had no friction. Her declaration was a surface without visible seam, a door so flush with its frame that the eye passed over it. He is my grandson. Four words, evenly weighted, perfectly still.

Fasa noted this and moved on. She would return to it later, in the privacy of her own thinking. For now, she filed it alongside the other shapes: another observation, another thing that might be nothing, another surface whose smoothness might indicate composure or might indicate practice.

"Thank you," she said.

Hala Dao stood. She rose with her shoulders squared and her body angled slightly in front of the bench where Bett and the children sat, the unconscious geometry of a woman who had been positioning herself between her people and the room for so many years that the stance had become her resting posture.

"The Daos," she said. "I'm Hala. My children — Raya, thirteen, and Sem, eleven." Sem stopped picking at his callus and looked up at his mother with an expression that was half attention and half the particular interest of a boy who enjoyed hearing his own name said aloud by someone he admired. "My sister Bett." Hala gestured. Bett, at the end of the bench, did not acknowledge the gesture — did not nod, did not shift, did not perform any of the small social signals by which a person confirms that she has been named and accepts the naming. She sat with her arms at her sides and her face set. "And Nori. Bett's daughter. Seven."

Nori, in the gap between Bett's thigh and the end of the bench, looked at the floor.

"Five," Fasa said. "Where are you going?"

"The coast. Visiting family." Hala's voice had the flattened tone of someone who considered this none of Fasa's business but had decided that the price of objecting was higher than the price of answering.

"And your husbands?"

"I'm not married." The sentence came without apology, without explanation, without the defensive sharpness that some women brought to the statement. It was fact, the same way twelve was fact for the Kondos.

Fasa looked at Bett. "And you?"

Bett met her eyes. Her face in the morning light was a surface without movement — guarded, set, the controlled musculature of someone who had decided precisely how much of herself was available and was not going to revisit the decision. She did not speak. The silence lasted two breaths, three. The rain filled it. The flood beneath the rain filled it.

"Bett isn't married either," Hala said. The words were protective — not answering for Bett but covering for her, the way an older sister covers when the younger one won't play the game.

Fasa held Bett's gaze for a moment longer. Bett did not look away. She did not offer the small social concession of a nod or a shrug or even the thinning of her lips that would have signaled irritation. She simply sat inside her refusal and let Fasa's attention pass over her the way stone lets rain pass over it — without absorbing, without responding, without being changed.

"Thank you," Fasa said.

Cade Leo stood last. He stood slowly, and Wynn's hand went to his wrist and then dropped — a gesture of support withdrawn because the support might be visible and the visibility might become a thing that needed explaining in front of forty strangers. Pik, between them, looked up at Cade and then went back to rolling a chip of stone between his fingers, the smooth repetitive motion of a child whose hands always needed occupation.

"We're the Leos," Cade said. His voice was steady, but the steadiness was assembled — Fasa could hear the effort holding it level, the way you could hear the effort in a bridge that was carrying more weight than it had been built for. "I'm Cade. This is my partner, Wynn." The word partner landed in the room and lay there. Fasa noted who looked up and who did not. Venn Kondo's expression did not change. Deshi Mera's did, briefly — a flicker of assessment that might have been disapproval or might have been nothing more than the registering of new information. "Our son, Pik. He's seven. And my mother, Vera."

Vera sat with her arms crossed over her chest and her jaw set in an expression that did not dare anyone to speak — it simply communicated, with the economy of a woman who had lived sixty-one years and spent a meaningful number of them defending her son's choices, that she was present and she was watching and she had opinions she was not yet sharing.

"Four," Fasa said. "Where are you traveling?"

"We're relocating." Cade glanced at Bram and back — recognizing the parallel, acknowledging it, filing it. "The new registration laws require household declarations. Categories." He stopped. The word categories sat in the air like something he had picked up and found heavier than expected. "We're going somewhere the forms are simpler."

Wynn spoke. He had a quiet voice, the kind that made people lean forward to hear and in leaning forward gave him their attention without his having to demand it. "Pik starts school in the autumn. We want to be settled before then." The practical detail — school, autumn — was a floor laid over the more complicated architecture of what Cade had been trying to say. Wynn had built the floor with the ease of someone who had done it before.

"And Pik is your son?" Fasa asked. "By birth, or —"

"He's our son." Cade's voice found its footing on this sentence. It was the same grammatical structure as Gitta's — he is my grandson, he is our son — but where Gitta's had been seamless, smooth as poured water, Cade's vibrated with the effort of compressing a complicated truth into three words. The effort was visible. It was also, Fasa thought, the effort of a man who meant exactly what he said and needed the room to hear it.

Vera uncrossed her arms and placed one hand on Pik's head. The boy did not look up, but his hand stilled on the stone chip. The gesture was territorial and tender in equal measure — a grandmother's hand claiming a grandchild for anyone who might be confused about the matter.

Fasa looked at the Leos and understood. They were a family whose shape did not match the categories the naming assumed. The naming asked for blood and marriage, for lineage and relation, for the architecture of kinship as the tradition understood it. The Leos had built their family outside that architecture, and the tools of declaration available to them forced their truth into forms it did not fit. Cade's hesitation was not concealment. It was the difficulty of answering a question whose grammar did not contain his answer.

"Thank you," she said. "All of you. The first naming is recorded."

She stood at the center and looked at the hall. Forty-three faces in morning light, the rain strengthening outside, the flood's voice rising from below. The children had already begun the slow gravitational drift that children always began — Melie Parra was showing Asha Mera her string braid, leaning close, the two girls' heads almost touching. Sem Dao was watching Fen Tesh with the frank assessment of a younger boy who had identified an older boy worth studying. Pik Leo had found a second stone chip and was clicking the two together in a quiet, private rhythm. Kiri Tesh had fallen asleep against Enn's arm.

The adults sat in their declarations. The words they had spoken hung in the hall like the smoke from the morning cooking fire — thinning, dispersing, but present, and Fasa could taste them. She had what she always had after a first naming: a map drawn in absence, its borders defined not by what the families had said but by what they had not said, and by the particular way each family had arranged its silence.

Jin Parra had given the minimum. His declaration had the shape of a man holding a door shut with both hands, and behind the door was something he could not afford to let into a room with his daughter in it. The dead wife might be true. Death at three months would still be raw, and rawness could explain the flatness, the refusal of detail, the jaw-lock lengthening around the questions he did not want. But flatness could also be the surface of a lie too important to perform — the face a man wore when the performance itself would crack.

Bett Dao had not spoken. In twenty years, Fasa had watched many people decline to speak at the naming. The shy covered their silence with gestures. The angry covered theirs with posture. The guilty could not keep their hands still. Bett's silence was none of these. It was a decision made long ago and maintained without effort, a border so established it had become terrain.

The Leos had spoken carefully — not falsely but insufficiently, forced by the naming's grammar into a version of their family that left out whatever made it real. Their discomfort was not the discomfort of people hiding something. It was the discomfort of people who had been asked to stand in a doorway built for a different body.

And Gitta Tesh had spoken perfectly. Her four words — he is my grandson — had arrived with the evenness of a heartbeat, no pause before or after, no emotion to read, no seam to find. The declaration was a surface so smooth that the eye moved past it to the rougher, more interesting textures on either side. Which, Fasa thought, might be exactly the point.

She did not know this yet. She suspected it the way the naming had taught her to suspect — not as accusation, not as judgment, but as attention held steady and pointed in one direction. The naming would return. It always did. The first declaration was a foundation, and what each family had built on that foundation this morning would either hold or begin, in the days and namings to come, to show its cracks.

The bell rang once — Olet at the doorway, signaling the end of the naming and the beginning of the morning meal. The families rose. The children went first, pulled by hunger and by the relief of movement after sitting still. The adults followed, sorting themselves back into their family units with the practiced choreography of people who had been keeping track, all through the ceremony, of exactly where their people were.

Jin Parra was the last to leave. He collected Melie from the cluster of children with a single word and a hand held out, and the girl took it and walked beside him toward the sleeping quarters without protest, swinging his hand once, the string braid dangling from her other fist. He did not look back.

Fasa stayed. The hall was empty now except for the lamps and the benches and the sound of the rain and, beneath the rain, the flood. She stood where she had stood and looked through the open wall at the brown water covering the roads — patient, opaque, indifferent to the declarations that had been made in its hearing. Seven families had told her who they were. Seven families had offered their names and their numbers and their reasons for being on the road, and the offering had cost most of them nothing more than a few minutes of mild discomfort.

The next naming would cost more. It always did. The first was foundation. The second was the first weight placed upon it. Fasa did not know yet where the cracks would appear, but she knew they would appear, because they always did, because no family's story was as solid as the stone it was spoken on, because the naming asked and waited and asked again, and eventually the asking found the place where the story could not hold.

She folded her cloth. She blew out the one lamp she had lit. She walked toward the kitchen, where the families were assembling for the morning meal, and the sound of the flood followed her, steady and patient, the voice of water that had nowhere else to be.
