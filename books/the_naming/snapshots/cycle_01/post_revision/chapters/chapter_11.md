# Chapter 11

The thing that kept her awake wasn't the rain or the mosquitoes or the stone under the mat, though all three were doing their part. It was Tomas. He'd developed a new sound in his sleep — a small catch before each exhale, a micro-stutter that had nothing to do with dreaming. It sounded like Jin Parra's voice. It sounded like the hesitation Jin carried at the front of certain words, that brief lock of the jaw she'd watched him fight through at every naming, except now it lived in her ten-year-old brother's breathing and nobody was talking about it.

Leni lay on her back and listened to it. She counted the catches. Fourteen in the last minute, or maybe twelve — she kept losing count because Ada would grunt and shift against her mother's chest and Leni's tally would reset. Her mother slept curled around Ada on the mat nearest the wall, her breathing slow and deliberate even in unconsciousness, like she was rationing air. Her father was on the far side, the bulk of his back to the room, his glasses folded on the stone by his head.

She sat up. The sleeping quarters were divided by cloth partitions that did nothing for sound and even less for smell — two weeks of bodies and cooking smoke had settled into the fabric like stain. Through the nearest partition she could hear one of the Kondo children coughing, a dry persistent sound that someone would tsk about in the morning. Beyond that, the steady backdrop of the flood — the valley water grinding against its own banks, a noise she'd stopped noticing for days at a time and then suddenly heard again, like a refrigerator cycling on.

She didn't decide to get up. She just did. Her feet met the stone — cool, almost cold, the kind of cold that traveled up through the soles and settled in the knees. She picked her way between mats, stepping over the edge of the Mera family's section where Kael had flung his arm across the gap between families. The main hall was a dark room of breathing. Oil lamps turned low at the exits cast the kind of light that made corners deep and faces unreadable. She counted shapes as she moved — Gitta Tesh was a small curve on her mat near the wall, unnervingly still for a seventy-year-old. Fen slept two mats over from his grandmother, long enough that his feet extended past the mat onto bare stone. He'd kicked his cover off.

She stepped into the covered walkway between the sleeping quarters and the prayer courtyard. It was open on one side, and the rain came in at an angle, misting her left arm and shoulder. She pulled her sleeve down over her hand. The air smelled green and brown at the same time — vegetation growing so fast it smelled alive, mud churning somewhere below. She could see the courtyard ahead, the stone paving slick and dark, and she nearly turned back because the courtyard was usually empty at this hour and empty things at night had a specific weight she didn't want to carry alone.

But it wasn't empty.

Two figures sat on the low wall at the courtyard's far edge, where the paving met the stone lip that overlooked the terraced garden and, beyond that, nothing — the valley, the flood, the dark. They sat with the deliberate closeness of people who had chosen each other and seen no reason to pretend otherwise.

Leni stopped walking. She stood in the shadow of the covered walkway, her wet shoulder against the stone pillar, and she looked.

She recognized Arash Kondo by the set of his shoulders, which were his father's shoulders made leaner, less squared, as if someone had taken Venn's frame and relaxed the hinges. He sat with his elbows on his knees, his head turned toward the other figure, and something about the angle of his neck — tilted, open, listening — was so unlike anything she'd seen in the Kondo family that it took her a moment to be sure it was him. The Kondos held their heads a certain way. Level. Declarative. Arash's head was tilted like a question.

The other figure was Bett Dao. Her hair was down. Leni had never seen it down — during the day Bett wore it pulled back and fastened with a strip of cloth, tight against her head, practical as everything about her. Now it fell past her shoulders, damp from the rain or maybe just the humidity that clung to everything, and it softened her. Her face seemed looser somehow. Less held. She sat with one knee drawn up, her arms wrapped around her shin, and she was talking.

Leni couldn't hear the words. The rain was a curtain of small sounds between them, and Bett's voice was low, almost below the pitch that carried. But she could hear the rhythm — a low steady current of speech, unhurried, without the stops and starts that marked Bett's daytime voice, which was all short sentences and ended conversations. This was different. This was someone talking the way you talked when you'd decided to tell the truth and had enough time to tell it properly.

Arash said something. Bett laughed outright, sudden and surprised, as if the funny thing had caught her off guard. Arash's hand moved. Leni watched it travel from his own knee to Bett's back, then slip under the wet hem of Bett's shirt at her waist, flat against her skin. Bett went still. She did not recoil. She turned, caught his wrist, and pressed her mouth to the inside of it, right where the pulse sat close to the skin.

Leni bit the side of her thumb. She knew what she was looking at, or she was starting to know it, the way you came to know something your body had already understood before your thoughts caught up. Arash Kondo had a wedding to get to. He was traveling to his wedding. The whole Kondo family — all twelve of them — was heading to the same destination, and the destination was a woman Arash was supposed to marry, and here he was in the rain at midnight with Bett Dao's skin under his hand and her mouth on his wrist as if that changed nothing and everything at once.

She thought about Venn. Venn at the naming, his voice heavy with the authority of a man who spoke for twelve people and expected the number to grow. Venn whose face could go from controlled to dangerous in the time it took someone to imply his family wasn't exactly what he'd declared it to be. She thought about what Venn would do if he walked out here and saw his son's hand under this woman's shirt.

Rain thinned for a second, enough for Leni to hear Bett say, "Don't touch me like that if you're still going to marry her."

Arash left his hand where it was. "When the road opens, I can't marry her. Not after this."

"Then don't."

"You know it's not that simple."

"It is for me."

He kissed her then, and it wasn't quick or tentative. His free hand went into her wet hair. Bett grabbed the front of his shirt and pulled him harder against her, and Leni had to look down at the paving for a second because the scene felt too private and too impossible at once. When she looked back, Arash's forehead was resting against Bett's. Their hands were locked together between them on the wall, rain running over both of them as if the weather had no interest in being discreet.

Leni stepped back. She moved slowly, keeping her weight on the outside edges of her feet the way she did when she was trying not to wake Ada, and she retreated down the covered walkway until the courtyard was a rectangle of wet stone framed by the pillars and the two figures on the wall were just shapes.

She didn't know what to do with what she'd seen. The scene lodged in her head like a splinter and kept working itself deeper: Bett's mouth on Arash's wrist, the flat way she'd said marry her, the way he'd kissed her instead of denying anything. These were two people who had gone past accident. Inside the compound, inside the forced routines and the naming and the shrinking food stores and the rain that never stopped, these two people had found each other, and the finding was a problem because Arash's wedding was on the other side of the flood and Bett was not the bride.

She kept walking.

* * *

The outer corridor wrapped around the compound's eastern side, where the stone overhang was narrower and the rain reached further in. Leni hugged the wall and moved by feel, her hands trailing the stone — rough grain, patches of damp moss, the occasional groove where the mortar had crumbled between blocks. The compound was small enough that she could walk its full perimeter in five minutes, but at night, without the daytime traffic of forty-three people and their noise, it felt larger. The silences between the rain had depth.

She heard Peyam before she saw him.

Prayer reached her first. The murmur was rhythmic without being melodic, nothing like the shrine's morning prayers that Fasa led in a voice that curved and landed and rested before curving again. This was different. The words were different. They came in something older and more private than the common language or the shrine's ritual vocabulary — a language with the texture of childhood, kept alive only in prayer, only in the sentences directed at a god who understood the original terms.

He was in the small alcove at the eastern edge of the compound, the nook between the terraced garden wall and the kitchen storage. Rain came down on him steadily, his thin shirt dark and clinging to the bones of his back. He knelt on a piece of cloth he'd brought from his mat, a woven square with a frayed edge that Leni recognized as part of a larger garment — a shawl, maybe, or a scarf from wherever the Meras had come from — repurposed into a prayer cloth. His hands were pressed together in a shape the shrine's prayers never used. His thumbs tucked inward. His wrists stayed low. The whole posture had a different architecture. He was addressing a different god, or the same god by a different name, or something else entirely that Leni didn't have the framework to identify. It didn't matter. The shrine asked everyone to pray the same way, and Peyam had smiled and complied during the day, his old face agreeably blank, and then he had waited for the night to find his own words and his own direction and his own posture for addressing whatever he actually believed in.

Leni watched from the corridor. The rain hit his shoulders and ran down his arms, and he didn't seem to notice or didn't care. His eyes were closed. His lips moved at a pace that suggested memorized text, something recited so many times it had become automatic, but the quality of his voice — the care in it, the almost-tenderness — contradicted the automaticity. He meant every syllable. He was seventy-two and kneeling in the rain in the middle of the night, alone, talking to something he trusted enough to get wet for.

She felt like an intruder. The privacy of it was enough. She was fifteen and she didn't know how to kneel in the rain for anything. She didn't have a language reserved for one purpose. She didn't have a prayer cloth that used to be someone's shawl. Watching Peyam, she felt the specific smallness of being young enough that your deepest convictions could still change by next week. He had carried these words across however many years and however many roads, and the words were still whole, still worth kneeling in the wet for, and she wasn't sure she had a single sentence she'd fight to keep.

She moved on.

The sleeping quarters backed onto the main hall through a series of cloth-draped doorways, and Leni passed through two of them, navigating by the geography of breath and body heat that she'd memorized over two weeks of confinement. The Dao section was quiet — Hala's steady breathing, the children in a row, Nori's small shape curled under a blanket with one bare foot sticking out. The Tesh section: Miro snoring softly, Enn on her side, Kiri's tangled hair on the pillow, Gitta's terrifying stillness, Fen on his back with his mouth slightly open. The Mera section: Deshi asleep with her jaw set even in unconsciousness, Kael sprawled, Rimi curled tight, Asha's small body against Suna's. There was no sound from either of them, but Suna's hand rested on Asha's head, fingers spread across the crown, protective even in sleep.

Then she came to the Parra section, which was barely a section at all — two mats, a travel bag, and a lamp.

The lamp was on. Turned low, but on.

Jin sat on his mat with his back against the stone wall. Melie was asleep beside him, her head near his hip, one hand flung out palm-up as if she'd been reaching for something in a dream. Jin wasn't reading or working or doing anything she could name. He was sitting. His face was visible in the lamplight, and Leni stopped in the shadow of the partition to look at it, because Jin's face during the day was a closed door — polite, minimal, the face of a man who had decided that expression was too expensive. But now, with no audience he knew about, the door was open.

He looked wrecked, with the bruised, emptied face of someone who had been carrying something too heavy for too long and had set it down for a moment just to see what it had done to him. The shadows under his eyes were deep enough to look permanent. His mouth was slightly open, his jaw slack in a way she'd never seen during the day, when the jaw was always working against the hesitation, fighting for each word. Now it hung loose. His body was slumped. His hand rested on Melie's arm, his thumb moving in a slow, unconscious stroke — back and forth, back and forth — as if the motion was keeping time to something only his body could hear.

This was the man the naming had broken open that afternoon. Fasa's questions, delivered with that gentle implacable rhythm — when did she die, what was the illness, where is she buried, is there family on her side — and Jin's answers crumbling under the weight of specifics he'd never needed to manufacture because nobody had asked before. Dates that didn't add up. Details that contradicted. The moment when Jin's face went from strained to empty and he said, "I'm not going to do this," and walked out of the naming circle with forty-two people watching and Melie calling after him. Leni had watched from the edge of the room, biting her thumbnail until it bled, and she'd thought: he's going to break. She'd been wrong. He hadn't broken. He'd just walked out. Which was maybe worse, because a man who broke could be helped, and a man who walked out had decided he was past help.

He was here, in the lamplight, watching his daughter sleep, his thumb moving on her arm. He was wearing the same shirt he'd had on when he walked out of the naming. He hadn't changed it. He hadn't eaten, either — she'd noticed his untouched bowl at the evening meal, pushed to the edge of the low table and quietly collected by Suna, who had done it without comment in a way that made Leni understand Suna had decided to look after Jin whether or not he'd asked for it.

Melie shifted in her sleep. Her hand closed into a fist, then opened again. Jin's thumb stopped moving. He looked down at her — and Leni saw the thing she hadn't been able to see during the day, through the polite wall and the minimal answers and the laborer's hunched shoulders. Jin loved this kid in a way that was physical and terrified and completely unequal to the situation. He loved her the way you loved something that could be taken from you, and the awareness of that possibility was the thing that kept the lamp on, the vigil going, the hand on the arm. He was keeping watch the way you kept watch over something you didn't trust the dark to protect.

Leni's eyes stung. She pressed the heel of her hand against her left eye and held it there until the sting faded, then wiped her nose with her sleeve. She thought about touching his shoulder, the way her mother would have, or saying something — but there was nothing to say that wouldn't make it worse, and she didn't want to be the reason his face closed again. She moved on.

* * *

She was back in the covered walkway, heading for her family's section, when another sound found her under the rain and the flood's constant grind, or maybe beyond them. It came from the valley floor or from the hills on the far side, carried across the water by some trick of distance and night air that the daytime never allowed. A series of low concussions. Irregular. Spaced too far apart to be thunder, too rhythmic to be natural collapse. They came in clusters — three, then silence, then two, then a longer silence — and each one arrived with a slight delay after a flash of light so distant it was barely visible, just a throb on the horizon like heat lightning but the wrong color, more orange than white.

Leni stood in the walkway and watched the horizon. Her hands were at her sides and she realized she'd stopped breathing.

The sounds came again. Four this time. They were faint and far off, but deep enough to register in the body before the ears could fully process them. They were the kind of sounds that adults would have opinions about. Her father would say "ordnance" or "military vehicles" or something precise and analytical, and her mother would hold Ada tighter, and Venn would make some pronouncement about the road, and the custodians would say nothing because the custodians never acknowledged the crisis in the lowlands as having anything to do with what happened at the shrine.

Leni didn't have an adult's vocabulary for this, and she didn't want one. She had the raw fact: something was happening out there, in the dark, in the country they were supposed to be traveling through. Something that produced sounds and light and that she was powerless to do anything about. The shrine sat on a hilltop and the road was flooded and the radio gave fragments and the fragments were bad and nobody was coming to help them. She'd known all of this for two weeks, the way you knew the floor was solid — as background, as a given that didn't require attention. But standing here, barefoot on wet stone, watching the distant sky pulse with a light that shouldn't have been there, the knowledge came off the walls and pressed against her skin.

She was scared. The word was simple and she let herself have it without dressing it up. She was scared.

Another set of concussions reached her, further away this time, or maybe the same distance but from a different direction. The rain intensified — a brief squall that drove horizontally through the open walkway and soaked her sleeve and the side of her face. She tasted stone dust in the water. She wiped her mouth with her wrist and stood there until the squall eased and the sounds didn't return, and then she went inside.

The sleeping quarters were warmer than outside, body-heated, the air thick with breath and the residual smell of the evening's cooking fire. She navigated by memory — past the Kondo section, where someone was grinding their teeth in a rhythmic scraping that she'd learned to tune out during the first week — past the Mera section, to the Okoye mats.

Tomas was still making the sound. The small catch before each exhale. Jin's hesitation, living in her brother's sleep. She looked at him — his face slack and young, his mouth slightly open — and she thought about how unfair it was that a ten-year-old could develop someone else's stutter without being asked, without being told, without understanding what it meant or where it came from. Tomas didn't know about Jin's wife. Tomas didn't know about any of it. He was ten, he played with the other kids, he'd been teaching Kael how to make a slingshot from rubber bands and a forked stick he'd found behind the kitchen. He just breathed differently now.

Her mother was awake. Leni saw it in the set of her body — Nessa lay facing the wall, Ada tucked against her chest, but her breathing was too even, too controlled. It was the breathing of someone pretending to sleep. And then Leni saw the other thing: her father's hand, extended across the narrow gap between their mats, resting lightly on her mother's hip. And her mother's hand was over it, her fingers laced between his, both of them holding on in the dark with the careful pressure of people who didn't want to wake the children.

Leni looked at their joined hands and felt a small, quiet shift in her chest. She recognized the gesture for what it was. They were afraid. Her parents, who had argued with the custodians and organized meal schedules and carried baby Ada and analyzed the political crisis with the measured vocabulary of educated adults — they were lying in the dark holding hands because they were afraid, and they thought no one could see them.

Leni lay down on her mat. The stone was warm where Tomas's body heated it beside her, cooler at the edges where the mat didn't quite cover the floor. She pulled her journal from under the folded cloth she used as a pillow and found the pen tucked in the spine. The light was almost nothing — the nearest oil lamp was four meters away, turned so low it barely threw a shadow — but she'd written in worse conditions. She wrote in the margins sometimes, and sideways, and in letters so small she'd have to squint to read them later. The point was never legibility. The point was getting the words out of her head and onto something that wasn't her.

She didn't think about what to write. That was the rule she'd made for herself early on. If she thought too hard, the sentences started sounding like school — neat, balanced, fake. The journal was where she was allowed to be stupid and messy and unfair and scared.

So she kept the pen moving. She wrote Jin in the lamp glow, his thumb on Melie's arm as if staying awake could keep her safe. She wrote Bett laughing in the rain, Arash saying he couldn't marry a woman waiting for him down the flooded road, and Peyam kneeling in a shirt so wet it clung to every bone of his back. She wrote the orange flashes on the horizon and the way the sound had reached her chest before it reached her ears. Then she wrote about her parents' hands.

She stopped once, her pen resting on the page. The ink was bleeding slightly in the humidity — a soft halo around each letter, as if the words were already dissolving into the paper. She could hear Tomas's breath — catch, exhale, catch, exhale — and beyond that the rain, and beyond that the flood, and beyond that the amount of dark between the shrine and any place she'd ever called home.

The page turned ugly fast. She crowded the margins with names, gestures, and bits she did not want to lose: Melie's fist opening in sleep, Peyam's prayer cloth darkening on the stone, Tomas breathing with Jin's catch in it, her father's fingers threaded through her mother's in the gap between the mats. A few times she started a bigger thought and crossed it out so hard the paper roughened under the pen. Every time the writing tried to become a lesson, she hated it.

After a while the page looked less like a diary and more like proof that the night had actually happened. That was enough.

She capped the pen and slid the journal back under her pillow. She lay on her side facing Tomas. She watched his chest rise and catch and fall. After a while she reached over and put her hand on his arm, the way Jin's hand had rested on Melie's, and she held it there until her own breathing slowed enough to let sleep in.

Outside, rain kept ticking against stone and running from the eaves in ropes. No more distant concussions reached her, at least not before her thoughts started slipping. Somewhere across the hall a lamp wick gave a tiny hiss. Tomas's breath kept catching under her hand, then easing, then catching again. The valley stayed dark beyond the walls. If Arash and Bett were still in the courtyard, if Peyam was still on his cloth, if Jin still had his lamp lit, she couldn't know. The flood ground below the hill until the sound blurred and sleep finally got in.
