# Chapter 1

At 4:12 a.m., the apartment was dark except for the microwave clock and the strip of hallway light under Ravi's door. Anika was already awake. She had been jolting up all night to the radiator's bone-knock, palms slick on the blanket until her chest stopped bucking. Six months ago, predawn had meant the brass diya on the kitchen shelf, two breaths while Maya cupped a match around the wick, and one careful spoon of sugar in tea because school mornings should start sweet even when everything else was late. Now predawn meant compression shorts, laces double-knotted, and a training shirt hanging from the bedroom doorknob like a warning.

She lay still and tried to make her body heavier than the blanket. Her calves twitched anyway. The first bus to the rec center left before the sky lightened; miss it and they stood under the pharmacy sign in diesel wind, bleeding minutes they did not have. Ravi repeated that every morning in the same tone he used for utility due dates and train transfers. He had not said Maya's name in this kitchen once.

The floorboard outside her room gave a soft, familiar pop. Then another sound, quieter: ceramic against counter, then the long scrape of a metal spoon in a pot. He was standing in Maya's spot by the stove. Anika knew it before she saw him. Every morning since the funeral he stood exactly where Maya used to stand when she packed two lunches at once, talking to herself in a low running list: yogurt cup, napkin, extra spoon, bus card, do not forget oranges.

Anika sat up too fast, and black static slid over her eyes for a second. She waited it out, then pulled on her socks and the shirt that still smelled like last night's detergent and old rubber from the court bag. In the mirror over her dresser she looked nine, not eleven. The collarbones were too sharp. Her braid from yesterday had kinked into a loose rope. She did not fix it.

Ravi was at the sink when she walked in, one hand braced against the counter, the other rubbing the outside of his left ankle in quick circles. He did that before every shift and every training block, as if the old injury could be negotiated with. The kitchen smelled like instant coffee and curry leaves burned in yesterday's tempering pan. The steel tumbler with dented sides, Maya's tumbler, was on the drying rack. He had turned it upside down so water could drain out. He had not put it away.

"Banana," he said without turning. "Half now, half after warm-up. Salt water in the blue bottle. We leave in six minutes."

Anika opened the fruit bowl and stared at the bruised bananas. "I didn't sleep."

"I know."

"Then maybe we skip this morning." She peeled half the banana but did not eat it. "I can do homework before school."

He set his mug down. "You can do homework tonight. Shoes."

She kept her voice flat because if it rose it might crack. "We used to light the lamp before school."

For a moment he did not move. The spoon in the pot ticked once as steam pushed it. When he spoke, he kept his eyes on the stove.

"We're not doing lamps at four in the morning. We're doing work."

"It takes thirty seconds."

"Niks."

She hated that nickname from his mouth when it sounded like a command. "You don't even look at her photo anymore."

Now he turned. There were crescents of purple under his eyes, and stubble he missed along his jawline. His voice was low enough that she had to lean in to catch it.

"I'm looking at you," he said. "That is what I've got."

The words landed harder than if he had shouted. He reached for the folded paper on the table, the one with columns and times and abbreviations she was learning to decode: ghosting, multiball, lunges, serve targets. Next to Monday through Sunday he had drawn boxes for initials.

"You think this is about squash," he said. "It isn't. This is how we keep you moving. If you stop, that room in your head gets bigger. You've felt it."

He did not say grief. Your mother stuck behind his teeth. What he could not say was that he was terrified of losing the only person left in this apartment.

Anika bit into the banana. It was too soft at the center, and she almost gagged. She forced it down with one swallow of salt water that tasted metallic from the old bottle cap. Her throat burned.

"Shoes," Ravi said again, quieter.

She tied them. That was the turn. It was not agreement, exactly; it was surrender of one small morning thing.

They took the stairs because the building elevator had been dead since January, a handwritten OUT OF SERVICE sign curling at the tape corners. On the second-floor landing Mrs. Kline's tabby, Jasper, watched through the railing with a torn blue ribbon still looped around his neck from Christmas. Outside, late-winter air punched the sweat out of Anika's skin before they reached the bus stop.

Ravi checked his watch, then the bus app, then the street. He spoke in short units while they waited, but this morning she made him earn each one.

"Today's full block. No cuts."

"On two hours of sleep?" Anika kept her eyes on the wet asphalt. "That's how people puke before six."

"People who hide behind that line puke," he said. "You're not one of them."

She huffed once. "Fine. Three ladders, but if my chest locks up we switch to straight drives."

"No switches," Ravi said. "If your chest spikes, you call it before you freeze, you take forty seconds, and you restart the rep. That's the deal."

She dragged her thumbnail along the bottle label. "Forty real seconds? Not your fake coach seconds?"

His mouth twitched, not quite a smile. "Timer's on the floor where you can see it. Say your reset."

She stared at the wet streak of headlights on pavement. "Long exhale, then feet."

"Again."

"Long exhale, then feet. Then move."

"Good."

The Q66 hissed open. They rode in the back because the front heater blasted too hot. Anika rested her forehead against cold window glass and counted storefront shutters and dark laundromat signs. At a red light she saw her reflection ghosted over a pharmacy awning. Her face flickered with the reflection of the bus's route map, lines and arrows crossing her cheeks like somebody else planned her day for her.

The rec center looked closed from outside, a block of concrete with one buzzing security light over the side entrance. Ravi had keys from his volunteer coaching badge. Inside, the hallway smelled like bleach and old sweat. Their footsteps echoed. Somewhere in the building a pipe knocked three times in a row.

On Court 2, the HVAC started and stopped in irregular blasts, a metallic rattle that sounded like someone shaking a toolbox above the ceiling. The wall paint was cracked in one high corner where years of hot balls had chipped it. Anika loved this court and hated it in equal measure. Here, nothing stayed hidden for long: bad footwork, bad timing, bad lies to yourself.

Ravi set his phone on the floor by the tin and started the timer. Its cracked screen threw pale blue light on his knuckles.

"Warm-up. Eight minutes."

Anika jogged lines from sidewall to sidewall, from the T to the back corner, and from front-right nick to front-left nick. Her shoes made squeaks that overlapped with the HVAC rattle until she could not tell where one sound ended and the next began. Sweat started at the base of her neck a few minutes in. Her lungs opened a little as the pace settled, and by the clap at eight her shirt was already damp.

"Ghosting ladder one. Twelve corners each sequence."

She bent, hands on thighs, waiting for the dizziness to settle.

"Ready," she said.

He nodded at the floor markers. "Go."

She drove to the front-right drop and recovered to the T. She cut to the front-left drop and recovered again. She reached the mid-right volley, recovered, and then dug the back-left before resetting. She tracked the order by the numbers in her head, running one through twelve and then reversing it before she repeated the whole sequence. On the second sequence her right quad started to tremble near the knee. On the third, the back of her throat tasted sour.

"Lower hips," Ravi said.

She dropped lower. Her shoes skidded on a sweat patch and she caught herself on the wall with her palm. Plaster dust stuck to her skin.

"You keep reaching from the waist," he said. "Move first or you're going to jam your knee."

"I'm moving. The floor's slick."

"Then call it slick and reset. Don't turn panic into technique."

She shot him a look and pushed off hard.

"Faster than that."

She wanted to tell him that faster than that was where the panic started. She went anyway. Recovery step, lunge, push off, recover. Her braid smacked the back of her neck in a wet, rhythmic slap.

At rep nine she heard a high electronic chirp from the hallway and froze for half a beat. It was only the front desk scanner, somebody swiping in early for the weight room, but the sound was wrong and too familiar. It was the hospital monitor at 3:11 a.m., then 3:12, then flat line replaced by the soft rush of oxygen and a nurse saying her name like she was both a child and witness.

She missed the next corner completely.

"Reset," Ravi said.

She did not move.

"Anika. Reset."

Her chest pulled tight, each inhale short and useless. She shook her head once like she could clear it physically. The court wall blurred. On the whiteboard outside, somebody had written COMMUNITY HOURS in red marker, and for a second the word COMMUNITY looked like CHEMOTHERAPY because letters moved when she got like this.

Ravi stepped closer but stopped two feet away, as if an invisible line on the floor marked the limit of what he could do.

"Long exhale," he said.

She tried. It came out as a cough.

"Again."

"I can't."

"You can. Slow it."

She dragged air in through her nose, held two counts, pushed it out through clenched teeth. Again. Again. The fourth one caught, and the fifth one landed. Her heartbeat was still loud but no longer random.

Ravi's voice shifted back to drill tone, almost brisk. "You lost one rep. You don't lose two. Start at eight."

"Can we do drives instead?"

"No."

"Please."

"No cuts. Start at eight."

There it was, the wall between them. She wanted him to say her name the way Maya used to say it when panic hit at dentist appointments or crowded festivals, palm warm at the back of her neck, voice soft and stupidly confident: We've got this, just breathe with me. Ravi gave her numbers, not comfort. Ravi gave her structure shaped like mercy and called it enough.

She started at eight.

By the end of ladder one, her shirt was fully soaked and sticking to her spine. He gave her forty seconds of rest, not a second more.

"Ladder two," he said. "Add split step before every front-court lunge."

Anika stared at him. "That's extra reps."

"That's efficiency."

"It's more."

"Yes."

The answer was clean, almost polite. It made her angrier than if he had snapped.

Ladder two burned different. The split steps loaded her calves with each transition until both legs felt wired and unstable. On the first back-corner recovery she felt a sharp stitch under her left rib and bit the inside of her cheek hard enough to taste blood. She kept moving.

"Head up," Ravi said.

"I'm trying."

"Do it, don't narrate it."

She almost laughed at that, because narrating was all she had when things went sideways. She counted floor tiles instead. Twelve along the short line. Nine from service box to sidewall. She made the court a map and ran it like a person trying not to think.

Halfway through the second sequence the memory came anyway, not as an image this time but as smell. Antiseptic wipes and heated plastic tubing. The hospital room door that never stayed fully closed. Maya's skin after radiation, dry and warm and oddly sweet with medicated lotion. Anika's foot caught late, and her knee thumped the floor.

Ravi flinched. Just once. A small forward jerk he killed before it became a step.

"Up," he said.

Anika pushed herself to standing. Her palm left a damp print on the court. "That hurt."

"Where?"

"Left knee. I slammed it on the turn."

"Sharp or blunt?"

"Blunt now, but it spikes when I plant."

Ravi's jaw jumped once. "Then keep moving and tell me the second it goes sharp. I'm not guessing today."

She swallowed whatever answer was coming. He did not ask about the memory; the smell got filed under noise and left there. He tracked only mechanics, as if pain had categories and only some of them counted.

They finished ladder two three minutes behind his target time. Ravi did not comment on the delay. He wrote numbers in a small notebook with a pen that had a bus-depot logo on it, likely stolen from the dispatch desk, the ink smudged where his hand rested too hard.

"Water," he said. "One minute. Then ladder three."

Anika drank too fast. Salt water hit her empty stomach and sat there like a hot stone.

"I need longer," she said.

"You need consistency."

"I'm going to puke."

"Then puke and reset."

She looked at him, waiting for the softening that did not come.

"I'm serious," he said. "Your body can do more than your panic says it can."

"How do you know?"

He paused, and when he spoke his voice was rougher, less practiced. "Because I watched your mother do chemo on four hours of sleep and still pack your lunch. Because I watched her stand up when she couldn't feel her hands. That's how I know."

The words left no room to hide. They were the closest he had come to saying fear out loud. His eyes slid away from hers as soon as he finished, back to the timer, back to the drill sheet.

"Ladder three," he said.

She nodded because her throat had closed again.

She cleared the opening sequence clean.

Rep six of the next one pinched her vision at the edges, and before the final turn the hot stone in her stomach had climbed into her throat.

She barely made it to the side door before she vomited into the janitor sink in the corridor. What came up was banana, salt water, and acid, with strings of spit clinging to the rust stain by the drain. Her abs cramped so hard she had to brace one hand against the cinderblock wall. A fluorescent tube overhead buzzed and flickered. In the next room somebody dropped a weight plate with a clang that shocked through her ribs.

Ravi stood in the doorway and handed her a paper towel roll without comment. He did not touch her. He did not look disgusted either. He only stood there, rigid, waiting.

She rinsed her mouth at the cold tap until the acid taste dulled. Her face in the scratched mirror looked feverish and furious.

"We're done," she said, testing him.

"We're done when you choose to stop, not when your stomach makes the call."

"I just threw up in a janitor sink."

"And you're still standing. Good. Now we train the part after empty."

She wanted to say something cruel. The sentence crowded her teeth and stayed there. She spat into the sink one last time, wiped her mouth, and walked back onto Court 2 before he could tell her to.

That was the moment she noticed it: her chest was still raw from crying that almost happened, but the grief image had slid out of focus. The hospital room was gone. The scanner chirp was gone. In their place she cataloged pure bodily data: calf cramp on the left side, blister hot spot under right arch, and salt crust drying at the corner of her lips. If she kept the effort high enough, there was no space left for the other thing.

Ravi tossed a ball lightly from hand to hand, watching her stance.

"Finisher," he said. "Suicides. Five sets."

She squared up on the service line. "Five full?"

"Five full."

"You said three yesterday."

"Yesterday you did not finish ghosting."

"I almost passed out."

"Today you're still standing. Go."

The first sprint felt clean enough to fake control. On the next one, she turned late at the back wall. Soon her lungs felt like tearing cloth; she clipped the T with her shoe, stumbled, caught herself, and kept going until there was no Anika making choices, only movement stitched from command and refusal.

When she crossed the final line, she dropped to hands and knees. Sweat dripped from her nose and pooled on the lacquered floor. Her heart was a fist punching inside her sternum. She waited for the grief wave to crash in while she was vulnerable.

It did not come.

That absence scared her almost as much as the grief itself.

Ravi knelt beside her then, not touching, but close enough that she could smell coffee on his breath.

"Look at me," he said.

She did.

"You did the full block."

She nodded once.

"You wanted proof you could stay in it. That's proof."

Her voice was shredded. "It shut off."

He frowned. "What shut off?"

She searched for a word he would accept. "Noise."

He exhaled through his nose, slow. "Good. Use that."

Use that. It sounded like a tool in the bag with tape and extra grips. Maybe it was.

They cooled down with slow ghosting, no timer. Her legs wobbled on the stairs to the locker room. In the shower, hot water needled her skin until pink turned to red. She pressed her forehead to tile and felt the pulse in her gums. Her stomach was hollow and angry. She ate the second half of the banana anyway while standing under the hand dryer because there was no time.

On the bus to school, Ravi typed on his phone with two thumbs, probably dispatch messages for the morning shift. He did not look up when he spoke.

"You can sleep twenty minutes when we get home tonight. Not before homework."

Anika leaned against the window. "I wasn't planning to sleep in class."

"Plan harder."

"You make everything sound like a drill."

He pocketed the phone. "Because drills still reach you."

The line should have started a fight, but she was too tired to throw it back. At her stop, he handed her lunch container and a folded index card.

"Schedule for the week," he said. "Read at lunch. We sign tonight."

She unfolded it on the sidewalk. Black marker scored the card so hard the letters raised on the back: 4:30 court most weekdays, one recovery-and-footwork day in the middle, match play Saturday, film and mobility Sunday. Bottom line: NO MISSED MORNINGS WITHOUT MEDICAL REASON.

He tapped the card once. "Structure is love, Niks. Remember that."

He said it like a promise and a warning in the same breath. She pinched the card until the paper edge dented her skin.

Then he was gone toward the bus stop, shoulders squared against the wind, lunch he forgot still on the kitchen counter at home.

School started at 8:05, and by second period Anika could feel the dawn block hardening inside her legs. The classroom heat was turned too high, dry and stale, and the fluorescent hum stacked until every voice sounded wrapped in cloth. She kept her pencil moving through math warm-up and verb drills so no one saw the tremor in her hand.

Halfway through social studies, her chin dropped once toward her notebook. Ms. Alvarez called her name, and the room went quiet in the way middle-school rooms did when somebody got caught.

"Anika, stay after the bell."

When the class emptied, Ms. Alvarez closed the door and lowered her voice. "This is the third time this week you've almost fallen asleep in my room."

"I'm fine," Anika said. "I just got up early."

"Your hands are shaking." Ms. Alvarez nodded toward the pencil marks jagged across the worksheet. "If this keeps up, I have to loop in the counselor. I already emailed home once."

Anika's stomach dropped harder than it had during suicides. "Please don't do that. I can fix this this week."

"I already did," Ms. Alvarez said, not unkindly. "Thursday after school. You, me, and the counselor."

"Can we do Friday?" Anika asked too fast. "Thursday I have training, then a bus transfer, and if I miss the slot I get home after dark."

"This isn't a slot problem," Ms. Alvarez said. "This is you nodding off in class. Thursday."

By the time Anika got to lunch, tomato soup had skinned over in the cup, and her phone was vibrating under the table. Ravi: `Got teacher email. Don't miss bus. 3:18 Court 2.`

Across from her, Zoe stabbed at a tangle of cafeteria spaghetti and watched her over the steam.

"Was that your dad again?" Zoe asked.

"Yeah. Schedule check."

Zoe pushed her tray forward. "We're supposed to finish the migration poster today. You bailed yesterday."

"I can't stay," Anika said. "I've got court."

"You've got court every day." Zoe's laugh came out flat. "That's not busy. That's your dad running your pulse from his phone."

Anika's spoon clicked against the cup. "Drop it, Zoe."

"No. One question." Zoe leaned in. "If you told him no for one day, what happens?"

Anika looked down at the soup. "I said I can't."

Zoe held her stare for one more second, then stood, tray rattling. "Cool. Then don't ask me to keep covering for you with teachers."

She walked off before Anika could answer, chipped black polish flashing against the plastic tray edge.

At 3:24, Anika reached Court 2 with her backpack still on and her school ID lanyard slapping against her sternum. Ravi already had the timer running and the ball tin open.

"You're six minutes late," he said.

"Ms. Alvarez held me." She dropped her bag and pulled off her hoodie. "She emailed you."

"I know." He did not ask why; his jaw was already set. "Six-corner ghosting. Forty seconds on, twenty off. Six rounds."

The first two rounds burned the leftovers of morning fatigue out of her hips. By round four, the blister under her right arch split open and soaked her sock warm. She clipped the front wall, caught herself, and tasted copper.

"I need water," she said.

"After the round."

"I'm bleeding through the sock."

He handed her tape from his bag without stopping the timer. "Wrap it on the break. Keep moving."

She finished the round, taped the arch so tight it throbbed, and went straight into drive-volley lock. She restarted once on backhand volleys. There were no speeches and no collapse this time, only the mechanical grind of thirty more minutes until her shoulder shook and the ball finally started dying low off the front wall.

On the bus home, Ravi's phone buzzed twice with the school district number. He read the screen and swore under his breath.

"What?" Anika asked.

"Counselor wants both of us Thursday," he said. "They're calling it a fatigue concern."

She watched the rain bead on the window and smear into long gray lines. "You mad at me?"

"I'm mad at timing," he said. "If school starts documenting this, they can flag attendance, and every tournament day becomes a fight. We don't have room for another fight."

By the time she reached home, evening had flattened into that gray-blue hour when the apartment windows reflected the room instead of the street. The kitchen light was on. The smell of mustard seed and onion hung in the air, and a pot of rice sat covered on the stove. Ravi was at the table with the same folded schedule from the morning, now rewritten on clean lined paper in thick black strokes, as if harder pen pressure could keep the day from splitting open.

He had added checkboxes for morning pulse, post-session soreness, homework completion, and class alertness, and printed `EAT` in capital letters across the lunch slot. In the lower right corner he had drawn two signature lines: `Anika Iyer` and `Ravi Iyer.`

When she sat, he slid a steel tumbler of water toward her without meeting her eyes. The gesture was quick, almost tender, then gone.

"Eat first," he said.

They ate in near silence. Rice, dal, one spoon of pickle too sharp on Anika's scraped throat. Ravi asked about homework, the counselor email, and the alarm in the same clipped tone he used courtside, but his knee kept bouncing under the table hard enough to rattle the chair.

After dishes, he taped the schedule to the fridge with two magnets: one from the Queens Oncology fundraiser 5K Maya ran before she got sick, and one shaped like a cartoon bus from his depot holiday party. The paper hung slightly crooked and started to curl at the corners. He pressed it flat with both thumbs, then straightened it again.

"Read it out," he said.

Anika read each line aloud: times, blocks, school, mobility work, Saturday match play at 7:00 a.m., Sunday film review after laundry, no missed mornings without medical reason, counselor meeting Thursday, and no detours after last bell. By the last line, her own voice sounded like it belonged to someone older and meaner.

"Any changes?" he asked.

"Yeah," she said, before she could swallow it. "This is costing me people. Zoe told me I'm disappearing. My teacher thinks I'm too tired to stay in class. My foot bled through my sock and you still ran the timer. I need one morning to sleep, and I need one morning where we light the lamp and say Amma's name like she existed."

Ravi's face went still in a way that was worse than shouting. For three heartbeats he said nothing. Then he took the pen, crossed out `THU 4:30 A.M. COURT`, and wrote `THU 5:15 A.M. LIGHT + MOBILITY` in smaller block letters.

"That's the change," he said. "One morning. Not a collapse."

He set the pen down, looked at her directly this time, and tapped the line he had just added.

"But tomorrow and Friday stay. If school is watching, we show them structure, not chaos."

He held out the pen again. "Sign."

Anika signed her name, each letter careful because her hand was shaking. Ravi signed below hers in fast slanted script. He capped the pen and placed it on top of the fridge where they used to keep temple envelopes and spare birthday candles.

For a second they stood shoulder to shoulder looking at the paper, both breathing like they had just finished a rally neither one wanted to lose.

"4:12 alarm," Ravi said. "I'll wake you if you miss it."

"I won't miss it," Anika said.

She believed herself.

When she went to her room, she could feel tomorrow already waiting in her calves, taped to the fridge in black marker.
