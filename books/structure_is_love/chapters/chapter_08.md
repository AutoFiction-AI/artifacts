# Chapter 8

The Midtown club smelled like eucalyptus cleaner and old money before the sun was fully up. At six-thirty, exactly when she'd known they'd be here, the lobby lights were already bright enough to flatten everyone into the same pale color. She had slept in splinters after Zoe's party, frosting sugar still ghosting her tongue when the 4:40 alarm dragged them out. Jackets dripped by the entrance from a thin spring rain, and the front desk girl kept sliding a hand towel over the marble in small anxious circles, as if the puddles could ruin the place's reputation.

Anika signed in with the pen chained to the clipboard and looked up at the bracket board. Her name sat on line twelve in dry-erase block letters: **ANIKA IYER - QUEENS REC**. Next to most of the other names, somebody had written academy initials in tiny smug pen strokes. Her first-round opponent had a last name Anika could not pronounce fast. Her second-round opponent was still TBD.

Ravi stood so close to her shoulder that she could feel the damp from his jacket through her warm-up hoodie.

"You know the objective," he said.

He always said objective like there was only one noun in the world worth respecting.

"Top eight qualifies for regional pool," she said. "Two wins minimum. Maybe three if seeding goes weird."

"Not maybe. We don't need maybe. Two clean wins and then we evaluate."

Jonah Feld came through the side door from the practice courts, unwinding fresh tape around his right wrist while balancing a paper cup of black coffee on his clipboard. Someone had stuck a glitter unicorn sticker on the back of that clipboard long ago, and he had never peeled it off. He nodded once at Anika, then glanced at Ravi.

"You're early for once," Jonah said. "Good. Courts are fast this morning. Ball's staying low."

Ravi gave him the flat nod he used when he wanted to show gratitude without conceding anything.

"She'll adjust."

Jonah looked at Anika's legs, not her face. Coaches did that when they wanted truth.

"How's the shin?"

The question pressed straight into the sore spot she had been hiding. Anika tightened her jaw.

"Fine," she said. "Warm-up twinge."

Jonah's mouth turned to the side. He had a habit of chewing the inside of his cheek when he did not buy what he heard.

"Twinge means data, not drama," he said. "If it spikes, tell somebody before you do anything heroic."

Ravi cut in before she could answer.

"We'll manage it."

Jonah held Ravi's stare for a second too long, then tipped his coffee cup in a tight, reluctant salute and moved toward another player. Anika watched him go and wished she had said something different, or at least slower.

She changed in the locker room with girls from academies whose duffels had matching logos and separate shoe compartments. Their voices ricocheted off tile and mirrors until her head felt hollow.

She sat on the bench under the hand dryer and laced her shoes. Right loop, left loop, pull, tuck. Maya's old digital watch blinked in thin gray bars. A month ago she had reset it every time it drifted. Now she let it wander. Queens Oncology Billing Services still sat behind everything she looked at, faint gray letters ghosting under homework and cereal bowls.

She bounced on the balls of her feet near Court 5. The first landing pinched. The next sent a sharp nick up the inside of her right shin. Then a brighter line flashed toward her knee and dulled, as if her leg had decided to lie for her.

Ravi leaned in through the warm-up crowd noise.

"What's the number?" he asked.

They had turned pain into numbered increments because numbers felt coachable.

"Three," she lied.

"Good. Keep it there."

By the end of her first match, the pain had climbed from warning to threat.

The opener ran quick on paper, 11-5, 11-7, 11-6, but each lunge to the front right corner sent a bolt through the lower third of her tibia so hot it made her teeth click. Her opponent, a compact girl named Rina, kept apologizing whenever rallies ended on lets.

"Sorry, my bad," Rina said after one collision near the T.

At eleven years old, Anika did not know anybody else who apologized during a sanctioned tournament point. She liked Rina for it and hated her for making liking possible in the middle of panic.

After the handshake, Anika walked toward the chairs in a straight line until she was out of sight of the glass and then bent over with both hands on her knees. Her right shin buzzed with the same metallic rebound she heard in court walls after a hard drive. She pressed her thumb into the spot through the fabric and felt a precise ridge of soreness, not muscle, not skin, something deeper and less negotiable.

Ravi handed her a bottle with electrolyte powder already mixed.

"Good pace," he said. "You gave away too many loose points in the middle. We'll tighten that."

"My right—"

"Hydrate first. Talk second."

She drank because she had learned that arguing took oxygen she needed later.

The second round was against Sloane Becker from Hudson Elite. Between points she tapped her racket frame once against her thigh before stepping into the service box.

Sloane did not hit harder than Anika. She hit smarter. High rails when Anika wanted pace. Slow floaters when Anika wanted structure. Straight drives that kissed the wall and died. By the middle of the second game, Anika could feel Sloane probing for movement, especially to the front right.

Pain surged hot enough to fuzz the edges of her vision.

She did not say it.

At 7-7 in game three, Anika chased a boast that died too close to the tin. Her right foot planted. Something in her shin flared so sharply she saw white spots in front of the ball. She still reached it, flicked the pickup, and watched Sloane bury a winner past her shoulder.

Anika turned away and walked to the back wall, breathing through her mouth. Not because she wanted drama. Because the inside of her leg was pulsing with each heartbeat and she could not swallow around it.

Ravi stood behind the glass with both hands on the rail. He caught her eye and gave the signal he used when she wanted to come off tempo: one fist pumping down, two quick jabs toward the front corner. Push. Shorten. End it now.

She hated him for the signal because it worked.

She went short twice and stole the game 11-9. She took the fourth 11-8 on a rally where she could not feel her toes by the end.

When she came out, Jonah was waiting with a folded towel and that same unreadable coach face.

"You're limping," he said.

"It's just stiffness," Ravi answered for her.

Jonah ignored Ravi.

"Anika. You don't owe anybody hero points. You hear me?"

She looked at the floor instead of either adult. A dark wet footprint from somebody's shoe spread on the tile like an ink blot.

"I hear you," she said.

Ravi checked the bracket app on his phone and swore under his breath.

"Qualifying match got moved up. Court 2 in a few minutes."

No time to let her breath settle, let alone the bone grinding at the inside of her shin.

Her qualifying opponent was Marisol Vega, seeded fourth, from a private Manhattan club where the courts had temperature control and towel service. Marisol had broad shoulders and hummed under her breath during warm-up, not pretty but steady, like a metronome she carried in her throat.

The first game lasted thirteen minutes. Marisol fed height and pace changes until Anika's lungs went ragged. 9-9. 10-10. 11-10 Anika on a backhand nick. 11-all after a stroke call that made half the crowd groan. 12-11 Marisol with a soft drop that died before Anika could get under it. 13-11 Marisol when Anika's drive clipped high on the tin because she was half a step late.

Anika came to the towel with her face blank and both calves trembling.

"You're still in this," Ravi said. "Her backhand is leaking when she's stretched. Keep pressure there and finish in front."

"I can't load my right leg on the lunge," Anika said.

Ravi's nostrils flared. "Then don't lunge stupidly. Early prep. More split-step. We didn't come here to survive one game."

The line between coach voice and father voice had disappeared so long ago that she sometimes forgot it had ever existed.

Game two: Anika 11-6. She found pace. She hid pain. She watched Marisol's shoulders tighten when rallies got physical.

Game three: 11-9 Marisol. Anika had three balls she normally killed, and each time her right shin warned her a fraction early, enough to make her hesitate, enough to turn winners into neutral balls.

Between games, Anika sat on the low bench with the towel over her head and pressed the rough cotton into her eyes until sparks popped behind her lids. She synced her breathing to the squeal of shoes on the next court, in through her teeth, out slow, pretending the rhythm belonged to her.

Ravi crouched in front of her, elbows on knees.

"Look at me."

She looked.

"One game. That's all. One game and we qualify. After that we can ice, image, whatever you want. But one game now. Understand?"

The promise hung between them, formal and brittle, each word waiting for a loophole.

"If I stop, we lose the slot," she said.

"If you stop, somebody else takes it," he said. "You know what the pool means. Coaching support, travel subsidy, visibility. This isn't just a medal."

Thirty-eight thousand four hundred dollars sat behind his words. Anika pinched the towel seam until the rough loops bit her palm.

The words from the hardship office came back with fluorescent hum around them: **We can't process another fee waiver without proof of placement trajectory.**

Anika stood before the ref called two-minute warning.

The referee on Court 2 was a college kid in rectangular neon-orange glasses who called scores in a voice that cracked at the edges.

"Final game," he said. "Love-all. Play."

Marisol took an early 4-1 lead. Anika clawed to 4-4 with two lucky clips and one brutal rally that left her seeing stars at the edges of the court.

At 5-5, Marisol drove tight to the backhand corner. Anika pivoted, sprinted, planted, and pushed off right.

A white-hot stab ripped through her shin, sudden and precise, metal deep in bone.

Her knee buckled. She caught herself with the racket, popped the ball up, recovered by instinct, and somehow won the point when Marisol guessed short and the ball sailed long.

She did not celebrate. She walked to the service box and set her face in place.

From behind the glass Ravi gave the signal again: fist down, fist down, jab forward. Keep going.

The next five points narrowed into a tunnel. Anika took 6-5 with a forehand drop that clipped the nick. Marisol leveled at 6-all when she pinned Anika in the back right and forced a weak boast. Marisol went up 7-6 after a long rally where each breath scraped Anika's throat raw. Anika pulled it back to 7-all on a stroke decision that made Ravi slap the rail once, hard. She edged ahead at 8-7 after a desperate counterdrop and a scrape that left a gray floor streak on her left knee. Marisol took the next point, then the lead at 9-8, and Anika pulled it to 9-all.

At 9-all, Marisol served hard to the body. Anika jammed a backhand return and drifted too far left. Marisol saw the open lane and killed the ball to the front right.

Anika moved before thought. She took a right step, loaded, and fired.

The pain hit so hard her vision narrowed to a ring, black at the edges, court-white in the middle. She got the ball by a fingertip of racket and sent a weak lob high enough to buy one more second. Marisol took it early and drove crosscourt. Anika stumbled, reached, dug, and felt a tearing vibration up the tibia with the impact.

The rally kept stretching, each contact rattling up her arm until her grip felt flayed.

The crowd behind the glass had gone quiet in that mean way crowds do when they smell blood and discipline together.

Marisol finally floated a ball too short in the middle. Anika stepped in and hit a forehand volley drop so soft the ball seemed embarrassed to touch the floor.

She reached 10-9 and earned match ball.

Ravi did not clap. He pressed both palms to the glass and mouthed one word that she could read even through reflections.

**Finish.**

On match ball, Marisol served deep. The rally moved side to side, then front to back, then side again. Anika could not push properly off right anymore, so she started cheating half a step early, gambling on patterns instead of reacting. On the fourteenth shot, Marisol went for a front-left winner. The ball sat up just enough.

Anika lunged with her left leg instead, dragged the right like dead weight, and knifed a backhand counterdrop that died inches above the tin.

Marisol arrived late, scraped, and sent the ball into the metal.

The referee's voice cracked on the final score.

"Eleven-nine. Match to Iyer."

Sound came back all at once: claps, sneaker squeaks, somebody's ringtone, Ravi shouting her name through the glass. Anika turned toward center court for the handshake. Marisol met her with a grim smile and a damp palm.

"Good fight," Marisol said, still breathing hard. "You're scary."

"You too," Anika said.

She meant it. Marisol touched the red charm bracelet once, like checking it was still there, then walked off toward her coach without looking back.

Anika took three steps toward the door.

On the fourth, her right leg gave out completely.

There was no graceful collapse, no dramatic faint. Her shin folded under load and dropped her sideways. Her shoulder hit first, then hip, then the side of her face. The racket clattered and spun away. Pain came not as a stab this time but as a deep grinding shock that made her gag immediately.

She rolled onto her back and made a noise she had never heard herself make, low and animal and humiliated.

People were already inside the court. The referee knelt near her shoes, saying something about space. Marisol hovered by the door with both hands over her mouth. Ravi dropped to his knees by Anika's head and reached for her calf.

"Cramp," he said fast. "It's a cramp. We need electrolyte and stretch."

"Don't touch it," Anika gasped. The words barely came out around the nausea.

A woman in navy tournament medical scrubs slid in between Ravi and Anika with the confidence of somebody who had done this in worse rooms. Her badge said **Denise Delgado, ATC**. A tiny enamel pin of an otter in goggles was clipped above her name, and cinnamon gum made the air sharp when she spoke.

"Mr. Iyer, give me space and keep that leg still," Denise said. "Anika, eyes on me. Wiggle your toes."

Anika did. Her right foot twitched.

"Good. Where's it worst?"

"Inside right shin. Lower. Nine. Maybe ten."

Denise nodded once and palpated gently along the tibia. At one point Anika flinched and swore under her breath.

"Pinpoint tenderness along the distal tibia," Denise said. "Sideline call: match is over and she's non-weight-bearing right now."

"She can stand," Ravi said. "She's been playing through this for weeks. It spikes, then settles."

Denise looked up at him with no drama and no deference.

"That's exactly why she doesn't take another test step."

She signaled to another medic for an air splint and a transport chair. A boy with trembling hands rolled the chair in and bumped into the wall. The crowd near Court 2 had gone from excited to awkward in under a minute, parents staring at phones they were not reading.

Anika tried to sit up. The motion sent a hot wave through her shin and into her stomach. She vomited neon orange electrolyte onto the court floor.

Ravi jerked back. His face changed, not softer, just stripped.

"Anika. Hey. Hey, breathe."

She wanted to answer, but her body had moved past language.

Denise and the second medic splinted the leg, buckled her into the transport chair, and pushed her toward the medical room behind the lobby. Every crack in the hallway tile sent a fresh jolt up bone. She pressed the heel of her hand against her mouth and fixed on the fluorescent buzz and the sway of hallway tiles to keep from floating out of her body.

In the medical room, Denise cut Anika's sock at the seam and checked swelling. The scissors rasped through damp fabric, and every tiny tug felt aimed at the bright center of pain.

"We're calling for urgent imaging," she said. "Could be a stress reaction, could be a fracture line. Either way, she's done competing today."

Urgent imaging meant forms, timestamps, and adults asking when this started. Anika could already hear herself deciding which truth would cost less.

"She qualified," Ravi said, as if the sentence could bargain.

"She qualified and she's done. Both things can be true."

He started pacing the length of the room in short loops, hand over mouth. Each turn made the chair arm creak; each creak made Anika brace for the next pulse up bone.

"We'll take her to urgent care ourselves," he said. "No ambulance."

Anika did not miss the money math under that. Another ambulance bill would land in the same month as everything else.

"Fine," Denise said. "Then she stays in that chair all the way to your car. No test steps."

She handed Ravi a one-page transfer note with tournament logo at the top and checkbox language at the bottom. Ravi scanned it, jaw flexing.

"You wrote suspected fracture."

"Because I suspect a fracture."

"Do you know what this does to seeding?"

Anika stared at the checkbox and felt nausea rise again. Her leg had failed on live court, and seeding was still the first emergency in the room.

Denise held his gaze. "I know what one more push-through can do to a crack like this. Pick your loss."

The subway was impossible with the chair and splint, so Ravi called a rideshare with surge pricing and did not complain about the fare. In the back seat, Anika kept her right leg straight across the seat and watched raindrops race each other down the window. Her hoodie stuck damp to her neck. The city slid past in broken frames: steam from a vent, a man carrying tulips under a trash bag, a deli awning with one letter burned out.

Ravi called somebody from the front seat. Jonah, maybe. Anika could hear only his side.

"She got through. She qualified. Then she went down hard... No, not muscle... I know what you said... Don't start, Jonah... I'm not asking for blame right now."

He hung up and immediately opened the tournament app again, thumb moving too fast over the screen.

"Dad," Anika said.

"Yeah."

"Please stop checking."

He looked at her in the rearview mirror. For a second he seemed surprised she still existed inside this logistics storm.

"Okay," he said, and put the phone face down on his thigh.

The urgent orthopedic clinic sat on the second floor above a pharmacy that sold discount compression sleeves and greeting cards.

At intake, the receptionist asked in a flat sequence without looking up.

"Patient name, date of birth, insurance, and what happened."

"Squash tournament," Ravi said. "She'd been grinding through shin pain, then she folded on court."

He sounded like he had practiced the sentence in the elevator.

They waited forty-two minutes. Pain settled into a mean steady throb that made time stretchy. Anika watched condensation bead and slide down the water cooler and felt phantom starts in her calves, the muscle memory of drills with nowhere to go.

When a medical assistant called her name, she mispronounced Iyer, apologized, and moved them straight to imaging.

In the exam room, an X-ray tech named Hari Patel positioned Anika's leg with quick competent hands.

"Can you point exactly where it hurts most?" he asked.

She pointed.

He took three views and then one more with a marker at the tender spot. Each repositioning dragged a thin, jagged line of pain through her shin.

They were back in the exam room for less than ten minutes before Dr. Sonal Mehta came in carrying a tablet and a paper chart thick with copied forms. She wore bright teal clogs scuffed near the toe, and her stethoscope tubing was patched near the earpiece with a strip of silver duct tape.

She introduced herself directly to Anika first.

"I'm Dr. Mehta. This is a rough way to end a tournament day."

She reviewed the X-rays in silence for a full minute, then turned the tablet so both Anika and Ravi could see.

"See this cortical line along the right distal tibia?" she said, tracing with her finger. "That's a stress fracture, not just shin splints. If impact keeps loading this spot, the crack can propagate into a full break."

Ravi leaned forward.

"Could this still read as a stress reaction? We've got regionals in six weeks. She can bike, keep conditioning, maybe tape and track pain—"

Dr. Mehta held up a hand, calm but hard.

"No impact activity for six weeks minimum. No running, no jumping, no match play, no ghosting, no treadmill incline work. None of it."

"Six weeks is the whole lead-in," Ravi said. "If she shuts down completely, she loses—"

"If you keep loading this bone, she doesn't lose six weeks," Dr. Mehta said. "She loses months, and maybe surgery."

She shifted her attention back to Anika.

"Can you tell me how long you've had focal pain here?"

Anika looked at Ravi by reflex. Dr. Mehta waited.

"About a month," Anika said. "Worse in damp weather. Worse on front-court lunges."

Ravi pressed both palms flat on his knees.

"You said three out of ten this morning."

"I said what worked," Anika said before she could stop herself.

Silence filled the room, buzzing under the fluorescent lights.

Dr. Mehta did not pretend she had not heard.

"Kids under pressure underreport pain all the time," she said. "That's why we use objective restrictions, not vibes or optimism."

She tapped her tablet and pulled up the return-to-play form with bold checkboxes.

"I'm writing the order now. You'll leave with a printed copy and a portal copy: no impact for six weeks, protected weight-bearing with boot and crutches for now, and sports follow-up in ten to fourteen days. If your tournament program asks for documentation, we send it directly."

She turned the tablet toward Ravi again. A signature box glowed at the bottom of the page.

"Sign here so there's no confusion," she said. "Your signature confirms you received the order and that it controls training at home and at the club until sports medicine clears her."

Ravi's face tightened.

"Send it to me. I'll decide what goes to the program."

"You don't get to choose that part," Dr. Mehta said. "In sanctioned junior events, this injury note is mandatory reporting. It goes to guardian and event medical records."

For the first time all day, someone told Ravi no in a voice that did not shake.

He stared at the floor tile pattern, little gray squares repeating into a grid that had no weak point.

"Fine," he said. The word sounded bitten.

Dr. Mehta examined Anika's leg again, this time explaining each step before touching. She checked pulses, sensation, compartment softness, knee range, ankle range. She fitted a black walking boot and then made Anika try crutches in the hallway.

"Left foot steps, crutches together, right leg floats," she said. "No hero hopping."

Anika nodded and moved three careful lengths of the hall. The boot was heavy and made her right leg feel borrowed.

When they returned to the room, Dr. Mehta handed over the paperwork.

At the top, in all caps: **RIGHT TIBIAL STRESS FRACTURE**.

Under restrictions: **NO IMPACT ACTIVITY X 6 WEEKS. NON-NEGOTIABLE.**

At the bottom was her signature, thick and slanted, and a direct clinic line.

Ravi took the pages as if they were a bill collector's final notice.

At discharge, the nurse stapled a packet with pain-management instructions, bone-loading education, and a follow-up appointment slip. Cartoon frogs marched across her scrub cap. As she wrapped the spare ace bandage around the packet, she leaned toward Anika.

"You can be upset and mad at the same time," she said quietly. "You don't have to call pain motivation just so adults back off."

In the pharmacy downstairs, Ravi bought calcium chewables, ibuprofen, and crutch pads, then stood in line for ten minutes staring at the debit terminal while it processed. The total came to $86.14. He flinched harder at the number than he had at the diagnosis.

Outside, rain had turned from mist to cold needles. Ravi folded the clinic packet under his jacket and flagged another car. He helped Anika in, careful now, too careful, his hands hovering before each touch as if permission had become a language he did not know.

Traffic on Queensboro Bridge stalled. Brake lights burned red into the wet darkening afternoon. Anika rested her forehead against the window and watched the river shear into gray panels below.

Ravi spoke without looking at her.

"You should've told me it was worse."

"You would've sat me," she said.

"Maybe."

"Not maybe. You would've sat me and we would've lost the slot."

"You don't know that."

"I know how you talk when there's a choice."

He swallowed and rubbed his left ankle with his right hand, an old habit when his own joint flared in damp weather.

"You think I pushed you because of rankings," he said.

"I think you watched me drag my right leg at nine-all and still gave me the keep-going signal," she said. "You told me to finish when I couldn't plant."

The sentence hung between them, raw and too accurate.

Ravi's hand came off his ankle and hovered toward her boot before curling back into a fist.

"I gave the signal," he said. "Yeah. We were one game from the slot and you said you could still move."

He shook his head once, hard, trying to throw the last rally out the window.

"I should've stopped it when you grabbed the wall at seven-all. I didn't."

His jaw jumped. "But don't talk as if I was the only one chasing that spot."

Anika dragged her nails along the seat seam until a loose thread snapped.

"I was eleven. You were the adult in the glass."

Traffic lurched and threw both of them forward. Ravi caught the door handle with white knuckles and kept his eyes on the wet road.

"We've got Dr. Mehta's order now," he said. "If we break it, they report us and you lose more than six weeks. That's where we're at."

Anika kept her forehead against the window until cold started to burn.

"I heard the order," she said. "I didn't hear you."

She dug her thumbnail into the webbing of her left hand until the skin went hot.

Anika closed her eyes. Behind her eyelids she could still see Court 2, white lines crisscrossed and glaring, a space built for running with prohibition stamped across it.

By the time they reached Jackson Heights, evening had settled fully. The apartment stairwell smelled of frying onions from the downstairs unit and bleach from somebody's half-finished mopping job. Ravi carried the crutches and clinic packet. Anika took the stairs one step at a time with the rail and boot and all the patience she usually spent elsewhere.

Inside, the kitchen was exactly as they had left it that morning: steel tumbler in sink, turmeric stain near the stove, the grocery flyer Priya had mailed them folded under a chili-pepper-shaped magnet.

Ravi set the paperwork on the table and stood there with both hands braced on the chair back.

"Eat first," he said. "Then meds with food."

Anika dropped into the kitchen chair and pulled Maya's notebook from her bag. The cover had gone soft at the corners from months of sweat and bus rides. She turned to a fresh page.

Date. Tournament name. Opponents. Scores.

Then she wrote: **Qualified. Collapsed. Right tibial stress fracture. Six weeks no impact.**

Her handwriting got smaller on the next line.

**Debt does not care if bone heals.**

She scratched it out hard enough to tear the paper.

Ravi moved around the kitchen opening cabinets too loudly, pulling rice from one shelf, lentils from another, then putting both back. He ended up heating leftover lemon rice from last night and overcooking it by five minutes. The mustard seeds popped in the pan and one jumped onto his wrist. He cursed, then laughed once at himself without humor.

When plates were on the table, he slid the clinic packet toward her.

"We need this where we can see it," he said.

He took a magnet from the fridge, then another when the first one slipped under the packet's weight. The order sat center height now, impossible to miss. Bold letters on white paper under fluorescent kitchen light:

**NO IMPACT ACTIVITY X 6 WEEKS**

**RUNNING / JUMPING / SQUASH PROHIBITED**

Near the header, a blue clinic stamp read: **RIGHT TIBIA, STRESS FRACTURE.**

At the bottom, Dr. Mehta's signature cut diagonally across the page, a barrier line.

Ravi pulled the legal-pad interval sheet off the side of the fridge, read the Monday block once, then folded it twice and shoved it into the junk drawer under the takeout menus.

He opened a text thread with Jonah and Priya, took a photo of the order, and typed out loud as his thumbs moved.

"Confirmed right tibial stress fracture. Six weeks no impact. No court work until medical clearance."

He added another line, paused, then backspaced the whole sentence until the cursor blinked on empty space.

"What did you delete?" Anika asked.

He hit send and turned the screen toward Anika for a second before locking it.

"Details for later," he said. "Tonight they get the medical facts."

Anika stared at it until the letters blurred.

Without drills waiting in the morning, there would be hours to fill that had no court lines and no rally counts. Hours where grief could walk in without paying membership dues.

Ravi sat down across from her. He picked at his food, did not eat much, and kept glancing at the paper on the fridge as if it might rewrite itself if he watched long enough.

"I told you to finish when you were already hurt," he said finally. "I made that call."

Anika touched the edge of the notebook page where she had torn it and felt the rough fibers catch her fingertip.

"Then tell Priya that part too."

He rubbed the cracked edge of his phone case with his thumbnail, not meeting her eyes.

"I'm not handing over every mistake by text for people to screenshot," he said. "We'll talk in person."

He stood, reopened the junk drawer, and pulled the folded interval sheet back out.

The watch on her wrist beeped the hour. Before she could pull away, Ravi caught her wrist, thumbed the side buttons, and reset the alarm to 4:40. He held her arm toward the microwave clock until both green displays matched.

The numbers blinked once, then held. She kept her arm where he left it, skin stinging under the strap.
