# Chapter 6

The subway lurched into Lexington and threw Anika half a step into her father's shoulder. He caught her with the side of his hand, eyes still on the stack of papers balanced on his forearm. The top sheet had a gray header and a bar code and the kind of tiny print adults pretended they understood the first time through.

The folder that had sat half-packed under his depot lanyard the night before was now arranged in strict layers: pay stubs clipped together, rent ledger, utility statements, Anika's school verification letter, tournament invitation, and a section he called "medical support docs" in a voice that tried to make it sound like any other category. On the train, every stop shook one corner loose. He kept pressing the pages flat with his palm, like he was calming a fever.

Anika stood with one hand on the sticky pole and watched his thumbnail drag a dark line through photocopier toner. She could smell damp denim and the sour metal breath from the tunnel. Her right shin gave her the thin hot warning it always gave now when she stood too long and kept still, but she did not shift weight. If she shifted, he would notice.

At 59th Street they climbed out and crossed into air that bit through her sweatshirt. Midtown was bright in the way that felt expensive: clean glass, brushed steel, door handles polished so hard the city looked fake in them. The private club sat half a block from a bakery that sold nine-dollar croissants the size of her face. Ravi glanced at the chalkboard menu, then away.

Inside the club lobby, everything was quiet enough that her sneaker squeak sounded like she had done it on purpose. A woman in a navy blazer gave them visitor tags and pointed toward administration. The carpet swallowed their footsteps. Anika kept hearing Queens rec-center echoes in her head anyway, that rubber smack and HVAC rattle that never stopped. Here, even the air-conditioning sounded educated.

The administrative office was behind a frosted-glass door stenciled with JUNIOR DEVELOPMENT PROGRAM. Ravi opened the folder before they sat down, checking contents again even though he had already checked on the train, on the sidewalk, and once in the elevator. His left ankle stiffened when he pivoted, a small hitch she had known since kindergarten, from his old playing injury he never admitted hurt.

A woman came out from the back carrying two paper cups and a stapler in her teeth. She was maybe in her thirties, with a mustard cardigan that had one cuff darkened by fountain-pen ink. Her black hair was twisted into a clip shaped like a tiny shark. She set the cups down, removed the stapler, and gave them a quick smile that looked practiced but not cold.

"Ravi and Anika Iyer? I'm Nora Patel. Sorry about the wait. Scanner died on me again." She tapped her monitor with two fingers. A sticky note on the side read CALL MAA DENTIST in block letters, and each time the screen lagged she rubbed the knuckle of her ring finger like it hurt.

Ravi stood too fast. "No problem. We have everything. I think."

"Good. Let's get you through intake." She led them to a cubicle with low walls and one plastic fern. "This appointment is required for all hardship requests. Since she's a minor, you sign, she co-signs athlete acknowledgment, and we need household income, debt obligations, and supporting statements." She said it in one breath, then looked at Anika. "You can ask questions whenever. Kids should know what we're filing in their name."

Ravi set the folder down between them before Anika could answer.

Nora clicked through tabs and slid a packet across the desk. HARDHIP FEE WAIVER ADDENDUM A. The first page had boxes for tournament level, travel burden, and guardian employment status. The second page was all numbers. Monthly net income. Housing cost. Utilities. Transportation. Existing debt over $500. Medical debt. Delinquency status. Collections risk.

Anika stared at the columns and had the same feeling she got when Jonah set up ghosting drills with too many cones and called it simple. There were too many lines to fail at.

Nora asked for IDs first, then pay stubs. Ravi had them ready, paper-clipped to a yellow sticky note marked FEB/MAR. She typed while he spoke.

The keys in Nora's keyboard came in bursts, a dry plastic rattle that reminded Anika of balls skidding off side-wall nick and dying.

"Bus depot dispatch, full-time," he said. "Net around thirty-nine hundred. Overtime jumps around. Some app-driving nights."

Anika kept her eyes on the form so she would not look at his face. When he said app driving nights, she pictured his phone glowing at 1:10 a.m. through the bathroom door.

"How much does the app driving add, roughly?"

"Six hundred on a bad month, maybe a thousand if I burn my weekends."

Nora entered it. "Any direct support from a co-guardian?"

Ravi's jaw moved once before he answered. "No. My wife passed last year."

Anika watched the muscle in his cheek jump and knew he had practiced saying that sentence like a line from a script he hated.

"I'm sorry." Nora's voice lowered and became less procedural for exactly two seconds. Then she clicked forward. "I still need every debt over five hundred."

The condolence hit and vanished so fast Anika almost wondered if she had imagined it.

"Standard household stuff," Ravi said. "Utilities, nothing unusual."

"Mark utilities and move on," Ravi said. "We're not here to put on a poverty show."

Nora did not look up. "I'm not asking for a show. Thin paperwork gets denied, and then your daughter pays full fees."

He slid over a white envelope and two folded notices. Nora flattened them. "City Housing Utility Arrears, eighteen-sixty. Status: past due." She entered it without comment and reached for the next stack. "Medical debt statements, please."

Anika felt her right shin pulse twice, sharp and hot, the way it did at the start of a hard drill. Her body never waited for permission to react.

Ravi hesitated, then pulled the gray packet from the bottom of the folder. The corner caught on the table and a page slipped free. It spun once and landed face-up by Anika's wrist.

QUEENS ONCOLOGY BILLING SERVICES. ACCOUNT HOLDER: RAVI IYER. PATIENT: MAYA IYER. CURRENT BALANCE: $38,400.00. PAST DUE PORTION: $6,400.00. DAYS DELINQUENT: 93. NEXT ACTION: COLLECTIONS REVIEW.

For one second she read only MAYA IYER and saw her mother's hand on a hospital rail, skin waxy under fluorescent light. Then the numbers snapped into focus and stayed there.

Under the total there were line items in smaller print, each one a month from the end of Maya's life turned into a price tag. Inpatient oncology admission: $14,200. Infusion services: $9,640. Emergency fever management: $8,950. Pharmacy adjuncts: $2,110. She recognized the words and not the numbers, then recognized the numbers and felt her throat close.

Nora did not pretend she had not seen Anika reading. She spoke directly to her, careful and matter-of-fact. "These are gross balances after insurance adjustments, not one-time due today. We document them to justify fee relief."

Ravi reached for the loose page. "She doesn't need line-by-line detail."

"She just saw it, and she's co-signing this application," Nora said. Not sharp. Just true.

"She's eleven."

"Then she gets numbers, not rumors," Nora said.

The scanner coughed and flashed red. Nora stood, smacked the side panel with the heel of her hand, and sat back down. "Sorry. This machine needs prayer and violence." She gave Anika a quick apology smile, then returned to the form. "Current payment plan is eight hundred a month, right?"

"Eight hundred," Ravi said. "We're on a plan."

Nora glanced at the statement. "Late three cycles, ninety-three days delinquent, no external transfer yet. Is that accurate?"

"Yeah."

"I need more than 'yeah' for the file," Nora said. "If collections hits before Friday, transport support freezes. Tell me exactly where this stands."

"No transfer yet," Ravi said. "They call, I pay before cutoff."

Nora marked a box and circled one line on the addendum. "For hardship approval, I need a brief explanation of delinquency and expected correction timeline. Two to four sentences is fine."

Ravi took the pen and wrote fast, pressing so hard the nib scratched. Anika leaned enough to read sideways.

Household income reduced after spouse death. Overtime used to maintain payment plan but irregular due to child tournament travel and school schedule. Request temporary waiver and payment-plan stabilization period.

He did not write that he had sold Maya's gold chain. He did not write that she had heard him take app-driving calls at 1:10 a.m. in the bathroom with the fan on so she would not hear him taking strangers from Midtown bars to LaGuardia.

Nora collected the pages and pulled another form from her drawer. "Athlete acknowledgment. Anika, this says you understand fee-waiver status can change and you'll report financial updates. Just sign here and initial this line."

The pen was heavy and warm from Ravi's hand. She wrote A N I K A, each letter separate, then looked at the sentence above her signature again.

I certify that this application reflects current household financial hardship, including debts and delinquent obligations.

She pressed her initials into the margin hard enough to dent the next page.

Nora stamped everything RECEIVED with a square red stamp and stacked the documents into a fresh folder. "You'll get a decision in five business days. If approved, tournament registration fees are reduced and equipment subsidy stays active for this quarter." She glanced at Ravi. "You may also qualify for emergency transport support for Manhattan trips, but I need one more thing for that."

"What?"

"Proof that oncology account has not entered external collections yet. A current portal screenshot is enough." She handed him a checklist card. "Send it before Friday and I can attach it to this file."

Ravi took the card like it weighed more than paper. "We'll send it."

Nora looked at Anika again, this time less like an administrator and more like a person who had seen too many families learn numbers in public. "You did your part today," she said quietly. "That's not nothing."

Anika nodded because if she opened her mouth, she was not sure which voice would come out.

They walked back through the polished lobby. In the revolving door, Ravi's folder slipped and one envelope flashed red where the stamp hit it in block capitals: FINAL NOTICE. He shoved it down before the door finished turning.

Outside, traffic shoved sound against the glass towers and made a blunt roar. Ravi steered her toward the subway entrance and did not ask if she was hungry. She followed half a step behind, watching his shoulder blades through his jacket. His posture had changed from appointment posture to transit posture: head down, objective clear, no detours.

At the corner, she said, "How much is left?"

"We handled the form." He did not slow.

"I saw the number."

"Those statements always look worse than they seem."

"It's thirty-eight thousand."

He hit the walk button with his thumb hard enough to make it click twice. "It's not a cash-now number."

"Then what is it?"

Ravi exhaled through his nose, eyes still on traffic. "It's a payment plan. It's adult paperwork. You don't need to carry it."

She waited until they were on the platform because the train noise gave her cover. "How do we pay it?"

"Same way we have been. Work. Planning." He kept his voice low and fast. "Don't spin out."

"What if it goes to collections?"

He turned then, finally looking straight at her. His face had gone flat in the way it did before a match when she asked for reassurance and got instructions.

"It won't if I keep up," he said. "And I will."

The train screamed into the station. He stepped in first and moved to the door area where there were fewer seats and fewer conversations. She stood beside him, knuckles white on the pole.

Across from them, a kid in a prep-school blazer slept on his mother's shoulder, mouth open, earbuds leaking tinny drums. When the train slammed into a switch, his teeth clipped his lip. He jerked awake with a small shocked cry, and his mother pressed a napkin to the blood, whispering into his hair until he settled.

Anika stopped counting stops and watched her own reflection jump in the window glass.

Then the numbers pushed back in anyway. One fee waiver might shave two hundred off a tournament. Transit support might save another eighty. Thirty-eight thousand four hundred still sat there like a wall, so she broke it into things her body understood: sets, weekends, drills she could turn into money if she kept winning.

At forty dollars a set, she would need nine hundred and sixty sets. At two hundred a weekend, almost four years.

Ravi's phone lit in his hand. UNKNOWN 866. He killed the call before the second ring and shoved the phone deeper into his jacket pocket. Anika swallowed the question she was about to ask and pressed the light button on Maya's watch instead. 1:18 p.m. Numbers glowed green, indifferent.

At Queensboro Plaza, Ravi finally spoke again. "You have school reading tonight and mobility work. Don't let this become a thing in your head."

It already was a thing in her head. It had shape and color and interest rates.

By the time they got home, the apartment smelled like burnt mustard seed from the pan Ravi forgot to lower before the train. He scraped the blackened bits into the sink and started over without comment. The kitchen window was open a crack and spring rain pushed mist onto the sill.

Anika put her bag down by the table where Maya used to sort grocery receipts into neat stacks: produce, transit, medicine, things that could wait. The spot was empty now except for today's forms and Ravi's keys.

He made tea with too much ginger and set one mug in front of her. "Drink. Then homework."

She sat but did not touch it. "What does collections review mean?"

Ravi kept his back to her, stirring dal with short angry circles. "It means they take another look."

"Is that when they call all day?"

"Sometimes. Today was five calls before lunch, and Friday's eight-hundred payment is the cutoff before referral fees."

"Who called?"

He turned the stove down and faced her. "Anika."

His voice was warning, not answer. "If I answer, you don't turn this into a training target. Promise me."

"I promise. Who called?"

He braced both palms on the counter. "Yes. Meridian Recovery. They leave the same script every time: 'This is an attempt to collect a debt, and any information obtained will be used for that purpose.'"

The words made her stomach drop harder than the dollar amount had.

"How many calls?"

"Five today. Two yesterday." He rubbed his forehead with two fingers. "If I miss Friday, they kick it to external collections."

"How do you not miss it?"

"I move other pieces." His laugh came out thin and mean. "I pushed ConEd to next week and took the Saturday overnight dispatch shift. That's how."

"Eight hundred every month is a lot." The words came out flatter than she meant. "And you still have rent and everything."

"Yes," he said. "I know how numbers work."

She flinched at the edge in his tone. He saw it, hit the counter with the heel of his hand, and said, "Fuck. I'm doing it again."
The word cracked across the tile. Her shoulders jumped up toward her ears.

"Look," he said, softer but still tight. "I'm not mad at you. I'm mad at this whole circus. I should've redacted that page before today."

"I can help," she said.

"By being eleven?" It came out too fast, then he closed his eyes like he wanted to pull the sentence back. "No. By doing your school and training and sleeping. That's your lane."

"Winning helps, right?"

Ravi set the spoon down. "Winning helps with program stuff. Maybe. But it isn't a debt strategy."

"It can be."

"No." He met her eyes, voice back in command register. "Don't build fairy tales out of pressure. You play because you play. I pay bills because I pay bills."

She heard the lie in the split between those two sentences. He had built his own fairy tale months ago and called it discipline.

He plated rice and dal and pushed the plate toward her. "Eat while it's hot."

She kept her hands in her lap. Steam curled off the rice and faded. When she finally lifted the fork, she took two bites, then set it down.

Ravi watched her leave most of the plate untouched, jaw ticking, and scraped his own half-finished dinner into the sink, eyes on the drain.

His phone buzzed across the counter. Unknown 866 number. The preview line read FINAL NOTICE: ACCOUNT SUBJECT TO COLLECTION TRANSFER. He turned the screen face down and let it ring out.

Anika stared at the darkened phone until the vibration stopped. The apartment went so quiet she could hear rain hitting the fire escape.

After dishes, Ravi took a work call in the hallway, voice low. She caught fragments through the thin wall.

"I can take Saturday shift... yes, overnight... no, leave my Monday dispatch as is... I need the differential."

When he came back in, he was already in old sweatpants, rubbing his left ankle like it had locked up. "Mobility band work," he said. "Ten minutes. I'll time it."

She did the routine on the living-room rug while he watched the clock, then checked off a box on the whiteboard near the fridge. He had drawn the week's plan in black marker: school, training, recovery, commute blocks. Every box closed, every arrow straight.

At nine-thirty he told her lights out and went back to the kitchen table with his laptop and the folder. She brushed her teeth, changed, and shut her bedroom door until only a strip of hallway light cut under it.

She waited.

Ravi's chair scraped, paused, then scraped again. Paper shuffled. The laptop notification pinged. She pictured him on the billing portal, typing passwords, clicking payment-plan options, deciding what not to pay this week so he could pay what scared him most.

Anika sat at her desk and pulled Maya's half-used notebook from under her math binder. The cover still had a faded sticker from a lab-supply conference and a coffee ring that had gone the color of old tea. She opened to the section she kept hidden behind taped-in worksheets.

The existing page had today's facts in her tight block letters.

In the morning I went to school.

In the afternoon we had the waiver appointment.

In the evening I did mobility work.

My shin pain score was 4/10 while standing and 2/10 after band work.

From the kitchen came Ravi's debt-collector voice, low and careful. "No, don't move it to external before Friday. I said Friday." A cabinet door slammed hard enough to rattle the glasses. She closed the notebook on her fingers until the cardboard edge bit skin.

When she opened it again, she turned to a fresh page and wrote the date. Then she drew a line down the center and titled the columns PUBLIC PLAN and PRIVATE PLAN.

Under PUBLIC PLAN she copied tomorrow's schedule from the whiteboard exactly, so if Ravi saw the page he would see compliance.

Under PRIVATE PLAN she wrote what she had already decided on the train.
Tuesday and Thursday: add forty minutes of footwork after assigned drills. Do not mention it.

She wrote:
Wednesday: run stairs in the building after homework, twelve flights six times.

A hot wire of pain jumped down her right shin. She pressed her thumb into the tender ridge until the pulse eased, then struck out the stairs line and replaced it.
Wednesday: hallway ghosting and split-step holds after homework, door locked, towel at threshold to kill ball noise.

Saturday: thirty minutes of solo ghosting before Jonah arrives.
Daily: cut breakfast sugar, skip school snacks, save stipend money in an envelope.

She paused and listened. Ravi coughed in the kitchen. Chair scrape. Silence.

She added one more line.

Target: win enough this year to keep the oncology account out of collections.

The sentence sat in the center of the page like a dare. She did not cross it out.

Her watch gave a single beep at 10:00 p.m. She covered it with her palm so the sound would not carry.

From the hallway, Ravi knocked lightly and opened the door before she answered. "Lights out," he said. He saw the notebook and assumed homework. "You done with reading?"

"Almost." She slid the notebook half an inch under her textbook.

He nodded, eyes on her face like he was checking for fever. "Big day. Sleep matters."

"Okay."

He started to close the door, then stopped. "About earlier. I snapped. I'm sorry."

She looked at him and said the line that would end the conversation fastest. "It's fine."

"It's not your job to fix adult messes," he said.

She kept her expression neutral, the one she used when teachers asked if everything was all right at home. "I know."

He stood there another second, wanting something he could not phrase, then shut the door.

Anika waited until his footsteps moved away and the kitchen faucet slammed on, then off. She pulled the notebook back out.

Instead of adding another full row, she tore a narrow strip from the edge of scrap paper and wrote tomorrow's extra work in abbreviations only she used: HG 40, SSH 12x6, G30. She folded it three times and tucked it behind Maya's watch face where the cracked strap met the casing, a hiding place Ravi would never check because he never touched that watch.

Then she slid under the blanket, shin throbbing with a soft electrical pulse, and listened to the apartment settle around her: pipes knocking, rain tapping the sill, Ravi shifting in the kitchen before everything went still.

She reached under her pillow for Maya's watch, clicked into alarm mode, and set a second wake-up thirty-eight minutes earlier than the whiteboard schedule for tomorrow.

Before she buckled the watch back on, she pressed the folded strip flatter under the cracked strap until the edge carved a raw crescent into the skin at her wrist.

When she finally slept, her calves kept twitching like they were still climbing stairs, and her hand stayed wrapped around the watch face while her wrist throbbed in the same hard rhythm as her shin.
