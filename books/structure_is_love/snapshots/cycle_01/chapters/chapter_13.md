# Chapter 13

The first rest day began with mustard seeds popping in hot oil and the tiny metallic rattle of the kitchen window in its loose frame. Anika woke before the alarm anyway, because her body had learned to wake at five thirty and stay alert for commands, but the clock on Maya's old watch read 6:42 and no one had banged on her door. Humid light pushed through the curtain in bands. Somewhere in the building somebody was blasting old Hindi songs through a phone speaker, and somebody else was cursing at a jammed laundry cart in the hall.

She lay still and counted backward from sixty the way Tess had taught her when her shin flared at night. The right leg felt tender in the damp, not knife-sharp, just that deep bruise ache running along the inside of the bone. It was honest pain instead of panic. On the fridge last night, in blue marker, the Saturday square had said REST in Ravi's printed block letters. She had touched the word with the pad of her thumb before bed as if it could smear off.

In the kitchen Ravi was standing in his depot T-shirt and old track pants, stirring onions with his left hand and rolling his right ankle in tiny circles against the tile. The ankle circles were his morning tell. He only did them when sleep had gone bad. A stack of folded grocery bags sat by his elbow in perfect white triangles, ready for the drawer.

"You're up late," he said without looking at her. His voice was neutral, not clipped. "Sit. I'll plate." 

Anika sat. The wooden chair rocked once on the uneven leg, then settled. The kitchen smelled like curry leaves and overboiled tea and the bleach they used on Fridays to scrub the sink ring that never came all the way out.

"Did you message Zoe back?" Ravi asked. "You said she wanted library today."

That made Anika look up. "Yeah. Noon." 

"Good." He slid a plate toward her, two thin dosas and potato masala with too much turmeric. "Eat before it gets rubbery."

He poured chai into her steel cup, then his. No lectures about protein timing. No reminder to ice the shin. He asked if her math packet was finished. He asked if she needed money for glue sticks. He told her a bus had broken down near Woodside and he would have to cover an extra shift Monday unless Malik picked it up.

Ordinary, she told herself. This is ordinary.

She chewed slowly and let the grease coat her tongue. Last month she would have been calculating the calories while she ate, running the numbers beside rep counts in the margin of her notebook. Today the notebook stayed on her shelf. She had promised herself that. Rest day meant no court, no secret ghosting in her room, no compensating run on the stairs. She would go to the library with Zoe, return her borrowed graphic novel, maybe walk to the corner store and buy the tamarind candy Maya used to hide in the freezer behind peas.

Ravi drank half his tea in one pull, set the cup down, and wiped the rim with his thumb. His phone buzzed face-down near the salt tin. He did not reach for it.

"You sleep okay?" he asked.

Anika nodded. "Mostly."

"Nightmares?"

"No."

He nodded back, as if he had checked a box.

The phone buzzed again, then once more, a short-long-short pattern from a thread he had forgotten to mute. Anika watched his jaw tighten. He flipped the phone over in one movement, scanned the screen, and turned it face-down again.

She saw only three words before the glass went dark.

`Outside by 8:30?`

He stood. "Finish fast. You need to change." 

The words dropped into the kitchen like a glass hitting tile.

Anika put her fork down. "Change for what?"

Ravi opened the overhead cabinet and took down the small canister of new balls he saved for match prep. "Sparring. One hour. Maybe seventy-five minutes if pace is good."

She did not move. "Today is rest day." 

"This is not training volume. This is a live read. You need left-wall pressure reps and we got a slot."

He said we got, not I booked. The back of her neck went hot.

"You said Saturday is off," she said. "You wrote it."

"I wrote it before this chance opened. Listen to me. Nia from Staten Island is in Queens for cousins. Thirteen, top bracket, lefty, ugly tempo. You need this look."

He was already at the sink rinsing her water bottle, his hands moving fast, procedural. He set two electrolyte packets on the counter as if they had always belonged there.

Anika stared at them. She knew that silver brand. He only bought it for tournament days because each packet cost almost four dollars.

"You scheduled someone," she said.

"I confirmed someone."

"Without asking me."

"It's one hour."

"It's my rest day."

Ravi exhaled hard through his nose. "Don't start legal language at breakfast."

She stood so quickly the chair leg scraped and jumped. "It's not legal language. It's what we agreed." 

He pointed at her shin with the spoon handle. "And your leg looks good this week. Damp day, yes, but gait is clean. If we wait, the next lefty exposure is regionals. You want to walk in blind?"

The old script came in on rails: fear, schedule, opportunity, waste. Her skin knew it before her brain did. A hum started low in her ribs.

"No," she said.

His head snapped up. "No what?"

"No sparring today." 

"Anika."

"No."

She heard the thinness in her own voice and hated it. She swallowed and felt the tea sit heavy in her throat.

Ravi set the spoon down with deliberate care. "Go put on your shoes. We're leaving in twelve minutes."

"I'm not going." 

He looked at her a long second, then smiled with only one side of his mouth, the smile he used with dispatch drivers when they asked for impossible coverage. "You're tired. I get it. This is exactly why we maintain routine. You'll feel better once we start moving."

"I'm not tired. I said no."

"Don't confuse discomfort with injury." 

"I didn't say injury. I said no."

He was still calm, but the calm had edges. He reached for the calendar on the fridge, the one with color blocks for PT, school, and training windows, and tapped the Saturday square.

"Rest means no impact load," he said. "It does not mean lying in bed all day pretending life paused for us."

"You know that's not what I meant." 

"Then stop acting like I ambushed you. I'm adjusting to real conditions. That's coaching and that's parenting."

The hum in her ribs became a throb in her jaw. She could hear the downstairs neighbor's pressure cooker whistle through the floor, three sharp bursts, and then the kitchen was all heart noise and refrigerator motor.

"You hid it," she said. "You waited until I finished eating and then told me to change." 

"Because if I told you last night you would have spun yourself sick about it."

"You don't get to decide that for me."

"I've always had to decide that for you." He spread both hands, palms up, a gesture like offering evidence to a jury that did not care. "Since your mother got sick. Since before that, honestly. You were eight years old and forgetting homework in the freezer. You need structure or everything falls apart."

Anika felt the words hit one by one. Eight. Homework. Freezer. Structure. Everything falls apart. She had a clear memory of the purple folder with fractions tucked behind frozen okra, and Maya laughing so hard she had to sit down on the step stool. Ravi had laughed too, once. Back then he could still laugh and not treat it like leakage.

"I'm not eight," she said.

"Then prove it. Get your bag."

She stared at him. He stared back. Neither moved.

When Ravi spoke again his voice dropped, quieter and rougher. "We don't have the luxury of throwing away opportunities, Niks. We're already late on oncology payment this month. If you keep slipping ranking windows, this whole pathway closes and then what? You think the debt gets kinder because you needed a day off?"

He was not shouting. The softness made it worse.

"Don't do that," she said.

"Do what? Tell the truth?"

"Don't put the debt on me."

"It's on all of us." He took one step toward her. "You think I like driving strangers at one in the morning so ConEd doesn't shut us off? You think I like choosing between your tournament fee and groceries?"

The shame came up like bile. The number from the paperwork flashed in her mind in clean black print: 38,400. She could see the line item for infusion chair rental, absurd and precise.

Ravi saw the flinch and pressed.

"Your mother knew exactly what things cost," he said. "She sat in billing with me when her hair was falling out and she still asked about your court time first. She did that because she believed this mattered. She believed you were built for more than this small, scared version of yourself."

The kitchen narrowed. The window fan clicked on and off as the extension cord failed to sit right in the socket. Anika's right shin pulsed with her heartbeat.

"Don't use her," she said.

He kept going, eyes wet now, voice turning jagged. "I held her when she couldn't breathe and she told me, 'Keep her moving.' She did not say, 'Let her quit the first week it gets uncomfortable.'"

The sentence landed hard enough that Anika had to grip the chair back.

Maya's letter from the locker lived in the top drawer of Anika's desk, folded twice, paper going soft at the creases. In it Maya had written about ordinary life in a voice so plain it hurt: school projects, stupid jokes, sleep, friends, the right to be eleven when possible. Wider than winning. Not because winning was evil, but because a life had to fit around it or it would crush the person inside.

Ravi did not know that wording. He knew enough to bend the rest.

"No," Anika said.

He blinked. "What?"

"No." 

"Anika, enough."

"No."

He slapped his palm on the counter, not full force, but hard enough to rattle the spoons in their steel cup. "Stop this performance." 

She flinched, then straightened. "I'm not performing."

"You're turning one adjustment into a crisis because Priya filled your head with negotiation bullshit."

"Don't talk about her like that."

"Then stop acting like a tenant with terms and conditions. You're my daughter." 

"Then be my dad," she said, and her voice came out steadier than she felt. "Not my scheduler."

That made him go still. For two beats she thought he might apologize. His face softened at the mouth and hardened again.

"Get your shoes," he said.

Anika looked at him, at the damp hair at his temples, at the old ankle rolling unconsciously against tile, at the dish towel over his shoulder with bleach stains from years ago. She thought, If I keep explaining, I will end up on court. If I bargain, I lose.

"No," she said once more.

Then she turned and walked to her room.

Ravi called after her. "You walk away now, and you're proving every concern I had was right."

She did not answer.

In her room the air was hotter and still. The fan squeaked on every third turn. She pulled the small black duffel from under the bed and unzipped it all the way so the sound of the zipper teeth was one clean rip, no hesitation.

What counts as overnight when you are eleven and angry enough to shake?

Two T-shirts, school polo, underwear, socks, toothbrush in a zip pouch, deodorant, phone charger with frayed tape at the neck, shin sleeve, PT band, one pair of shorts. She took her algebra packet, then put it back, then shoved it in anyway. She packed Maya's watch charger even though the watch battery lasted four days and she would probably be back before that. She took the notebook. She took the letter and slid it inside the notebook cover where it would not fold again.

Ravi appeared in the doorway with her racquet in his hand, as if he had already won.

"Put that down," she said.

"Take your court shoes and we can still make the slot." 

"I'm leaving." 

"For where?"

"Priya's."

He laughed once, short and bitter. "You called her already?"

Anika held up her phone. On screen Priya's name showed 8:11 a.m., call duration 00:43.

Ravi's eyes flicked to it and away. "You're escalating this on purpose."

"You did that at breakfast." 

"I'm your parent. I made a decision."

"You made a secret." 

His grip tightened on the racquet handle until his knuckles paled. "Don't do this to me."

The sentence was raw, stripped of coaching language, and it almost pulled her back. Almost.

She zipped the duffel and slung it cross-body. The strap dug into the side of her neck.

"Move," she said.

"If you leave this apartment over one session, don't expect this to disappear by dinner."

"I know." 

"You think Priya can house you long term? In that one-bedroom with her files and no space?"

"I said tonight." 

"And tomorrow?"

"We'll talk tomorrow. Not now." 

He leaned on the doorframe, ankle stiff now, no circles. "You're making a child decision."

"Maybe," she said. "I'm still not going."

He did not move. She stepped toward him until she could smell soap and sweat and yesterday's bus diesel still clinging to his shirt. His jaw jumped once.

He stepped aside.

She walked past.

In the hallway she heard him say her name, not loud, not command voice, just name. She kept walking.

The stairwell smelled like damp concrete and fried onions from the second-floor family who cooked early. The elevator had been broken for three weeks, a handwritten sign taped over the button with a cartoon frown face somebody had drawn in blue pen. By the second landing her shin throbbed and she hated that her body noticed. She tightened her grip on the rail and kept descending.

On the street the August heat wrapped around her in one wet slap. She stood under the awning and opened the rideshare app. Priya had already sent her card and a note: `Grey Camry. Driver name: Salma. Stay by the deli door.`

Three missed calls from Ravi blinked at the top of the screen.

She typed one line and hit send before she could edit it into something softer.

`I am safe. I am going to Priya's.`

She stared at the uncontracted words and did not fix them. She wanted them hard.

The Camry pulled up in seven minutes, hazard lights blinking like a nervous heartbeat. The driver was a woman in her fifties with mint-green nails and a Knicks visor clipped to the dashboard, and she had a tiny plastic Ganesha glued beside the meter with one missing arm.

"Anika?" she asked.

"Yeah." 

"Seat belt, please." She glanced in the mirror, took in the duffel and the face and whatever else was obvious, then added, "Traffic on Hoyt is garbage. We go 31st Avenue, okay?"

"Okay."

Salma turned the radio down from a call-in show about rent hikes and muttered, not to Anika exactly, "Every landlord in this city thinks we print money in the trunk."

It was such a specific adult complaint that Anika almost laughed. She did not.

They moved through Jackson Heights toward Astoria in short bursts, red lights every block. Men were unloading crates of mangoes outside Patel Brothers. A kid in a soccer jersey chased a pigeon across the crosswalk and got yanked back by his sister by the hood. At the underpass a bus hissed to a stop and the side panel ad for a private academy flashed white shoes, white courts, white teeth.

Ravi called again. Then again.

On the fourth call she answered and held the phone near her chest.

"What," she said.

"Where are you?"

"In a car." 

"Tell the driver to turn around."

"No."

"Anika, listen to me. We said things hot. Fine. Come back and we reset." 

"Reset to what?"

He inhaled, audible, trying to find stable ground. "To us. To family. You don't leave home because of an argument."

"It wasn't an argument." 

"Then what was it?"

"You broke the deal and used Mom as a weapon." 

Silence, then, "I did not weaponize your mother." 

"You did." 

"I was reminding you what matters." 

She looked out the window at a pawn shop sign blinking CASH TODAY over a rack of cracked guitars. "I've got to go," she said.

"Don't hang up on me."

She hung up.

Her hands shook after, not dramatic shaking, just a fine vibration in the fingers like after too many ghosting sets. She pressed her palms against her thighs until they warmed.

Priya's building in Astoria had narrow brick steps and a broken intercom where the 4 button stuck if you pressed too hard. Priya opened the door before Anika could ring. She was in black joggers and a faded legal-aid clinic T-shirt, one earring in and one still in her hand, hair clipped back with a binder clip because she had lost the real claw clip somewhere between work and home. She had two phones as always, one wedged between ear and shoulder with a client on hold, one face-down on the console table buzzing every thirty seconds.

She looked at Anika's bag, then at Anika's face. "Come in. Shoes off if they're wet." 

No interrogation. No courtroom voice.

Anika stepped inside. The apartment smelled like detergent and cumin and the lemon cleaner Priya used when she was anxious. Ceiling fan, couch that converted to a bed, narrow galley kitchen, stacked file boxes labeled `Housing`, `Family`, `Immigration`, handwritten in fat black marker. Maya's photo was on top of the bookshelf, not formal, just a printout from a picnic with her mouth open mid-laugh and sunglasses crooked.

Priya lifted a finger to Anika and spoke into the phone on her shoulder. "Ms. Barros, I'm here. I have your papers. Do not sign anything tonight. I'll call you at nine in the morning and we do this together. Yes, I mean it. Drink water. Bye." She ended the call and finally put both phones on the table.

"Okay," she said. "You safe right now?"

"Yeah." 

"Did he touch you?"

"No."

"Did he block the door?"

"He stood there. Then he moved."

Priya nodded once. "Good. We're going to keep this specific. You can stay here tonight. You can stay tomorrow night if needed. We decide next step when your body settles down." 

Anika dropped the duffel by the couch and sank onto the cushion. The springs complained.

"I don't want to blow everything up," she said.

Priya snorted softly and walked to the fridge. "That part is done already. He called me at 8:14 and 8:19 and then sent three paragraphs about opportunity windows." She pulled out a plastic container of rice and another of egg curry. "Do you want this hot or room temp?"

"I already ate." 

"You argued for an hour after that. Eat anyway."

Anika watched her scoop rice into a bowl. Priya always scraped the sides clean with the spoon before microwaving, as if leaving food stuck to plastic was a moral failure.

"He'll say I overreacted," Anika said.

"He can say what he wants. You said no. He did not accept no. That is the data."

"He booked a spar with someone I don't even know." 

"I know." Priya handed her the bowl. "He told me she was some lefty phenom and the slot cost him sixty bucks he can't get back."

Anika stared at the steam rising from the rice. Sixty dollars was ConEd plus half a week of MetroCards. She felt guilt climb her throat, fast and familiar.

Priya saw it and clicked her tongue. "No. Don't eat that cost for him. He spent money on a session after agreeing to a rest day. That's his decision, not your debt." 

Anika dug the spoon in and burned her tongue because she did not wait. Egg curry, too much chili, Priya's style. The pain was bright and useful.

Priya leaned against the counter and folded her arms. "Do you want me to text him now or after you shower?"

"Now."

Priya picked up the family phone, the one with the cracked blue case, and typed without hesitation. She read aloud as she wrote, calm and plain.

"Anika is with me at my apartment tonight. She is safe. Do not come over unannounced. We will speak tomorrow at 7 p.m. with ground rules."

She hit send.

Public fact, just like that. Not a threat, not a plea.

Anika's watch vibrated with an incoming message before Priya had set her phone down.

Dad: `Ground rules now? This is my child, Priya.`

Dad: `Anika answer your phone.`

Dad: `You don't get to abduct my daughter.`

Priya read over Anika's shoulder and rolled her eyes. "I swear he should have billed me for this drama if he's going to use legal words wrong."

Even then, Anika noticed, Priya's left foot tapped twice against the tile. That was her worry tell. She joked when she was trying not to panic.

"Do I answer?" Anika asked.

"One message. Boundaries only. No argument."

Anika typed: `I'm safe. I'm staying here tonight. We talk tomorrow with Priya there.`

She erased `I'm` and wrote `I am`, then changed it back to `I'm`. She sent it.

Ravi did not answer right away. When he did, it was one sentence.

`You're making strangers part of family business.`

Priya saw that too and said, "I raised his wife's sister in this city while he was doing double shifts. I'm not a stranger." 

She set water to boil, then remembered she already had the kettle full from this morning and turned the flame down. "Shower first," she told Anika. "Then we sort practical things. School clothes. Toothbrush. Any meds left at home."

"I packed most of it."

"Good. That's your brain working under stress."

Anika took the shower and let the water run hot until the tiny bathroom mirror fogged. The argument replayed anyway. `She told me keep her moving.` Did Maya say that? Maybe she did, once, during chemo when stairs were hard and everyone spoke in instructions because fear was easier in logistics. Maya could have said it and still meant something wider. That was what made it dangerous, how a true sentence could be used to force a false life.

When she came out in clean shorts, Priya had set up the couch bed with an old blue sheet printed with faded planets. She had also pulled a small toiletry bag from a drawer and lined up travel shampoo, a sealed toothbrush, and a tiny tube of toothpaste in military order.

"I steal these from conferences," Priya said, seeing Anika look. "One day this habit will save civilization."

Anika sat on the edge of the couch and toed off her socks. The right shin ached deep, weather ache plus stair ache plus adrenaline dump. She pressed two fingers along the bone until she found the tender strip and stopped there.

Priya noticed. "Pain scale?"

"Three." 

"Real three or athlete three?"

Anika thought. "Five." 

"Thank you. Ice pack in freezer door." 

Priya's work phone buzzed. She glanced at the screen and swore once, softly. "Client emergency. I need fifteen minutes on the balcony. You okay alone?"

"Yeah."

Priya paused in the doorway to the balcony, then turned back. "One more thing. You did not break your family by setting a boundary. You revealed where it was already cracked."

Then she stepped outside and closed the door most of the way, voice low as she took the call.

Anika held the ice pack against her shin and listened to the fan, the balcony traffic, the murmur of Priya saying words like notice period and landlord harassment and emergency filing. Grown-up war language, different battlefield.

Her phone lit again. This time it was a voicemail from Ravi, not a text.

She put it on speaker at low volume and listened.

"Niks," he said, and she could hear city noise behind him, maybe he had gone outside to the stoop where reception was better. "I am... I am angry, yes. But I don't want this to become a circus. Come home tonight. We'll skip the session. Fine. We can reset tomorrow. I don't sleep when you're not here. Call me back."

He sounded tired enough to be honest and controlling enough to keep one hand on the wheel. Both at once, as always.

Anika did not call back.

She opened her notebook instead. The pages from chapter six onward were dense with small writing: times, reps, pain numbers, debt math, reminders to ask Tess about shoe inserts, reminders to smile at teachers so they would stop asking if she was okay. Today she wrote only one line.

`Rest day held.`

She stared at the words. They looked thin for what they cost.

Priya came back in, rubbing her forehead. "I ordered groceries to your dad's place last week by accident," she said, as if reporting weather. "Three pounds of onions and no tomatoes. He texted me like I had committed fraud." 

Anika laughed once, involuntary. It hurt her ribs and felt good anyway.

Priya smiled, brief. "There. Human noise. Keep that." 

The apartment settled toward evening. They ate leftover rice and pickle from the jar, watched half an episode of a dumb baking show where everybody cried over sponge texture, and ignored the first two calls from Ravi. On the third, Priya answered on speaker and said, "We're not doing this live. Tomorrow seven. If you keep calling every ten minutes, I mute you until morning."

Ravi's voice came through tight and fast. "You're enjoying this too much."

"No," Priya said. "I'm tired and my back hurts and I'd rather watch bad television. See you tomorrow." She ended the call.

Night came with thunder that never turned into rain, only pressure. Anika lay on the couch bed in Priya's too-small living room and listened to footsteps from upstairs, pipes knocking, a motorcycle revving and revving three blocks away. She was not in her own room with the fan squeak and the glow sticker planets on the ceiling that she had been embarrassed to remove. She was not on court. She was not in the middle of drills where every second had a purpose assigned by somebody else.

The emptiness should have felt like relief. It felt like standing at the top of a stairwell in the dark with one hand not quite finding the rail.

She took Maya's letter out from inside the notebook cover and unfolded it with careful fingers. She did not read the whole thing. She touched the part where the pen pressure had cut deeper into the paper, the places where Maya had paused and started again.

From the bedroom Priya called softly, "You need anything?"

"No," Anika said.

A beat, then Priya again: "I put your school clothes on the chair. Alarm is set for six thirty."

"Okay."

"Goodnight, kid." 

"Night."

Anika put the letter back, then reached for her watch and set tomorrow's alarm herself. The digital buttons were worn smooth from Maya's thumb and now hers. 6:30. School from Astoria. Bus or train depending on how bad the transfer was. A public schedule, different from a private command.

Her phone buzzed one last time. No text this round, just a calendar notification Ravi had not deleted.

`8:30 AM - Sparring (Nia, Court 3)`

Anika opened the event, pressed delete, and confirmed.

She turned the phone face-down on Priya's coffee table. Outside, thunder moved farther east without breaking. She listened until the building quieted and her own breathing found a steady tempo.

Tomorrow there would be mediation, rules, fallout, money, logistics, all the adult words that could turn into traps if you did not hold the center. Tonight there was a thin mattress, a packed duffel by the couch, and one boundary that had survived contact.

She let herself sleep with the bag where she could touch it.
