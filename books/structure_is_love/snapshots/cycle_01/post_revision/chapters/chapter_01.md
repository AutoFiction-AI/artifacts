# Chapter 1

At 4:12 a.m., the apartment is dark except for the microwave clock and the strip of hallway light under Ravi's door. Anika is already awake. She has been awake since 2:53, then 3:17, then 3:44, each time counting the radiator knocks until her chest stopped jolting. Six months ago, predawn meant the brass diya on the kitchen shelf, two breaths while Maya cupped a match around the wick, and one careful spoon of sugar in tea because school mornings should start sweet even when everything else was late. Now predawn means compression shorts, laces double-knotted, and a training shirt hanging from the bedroom doorknob like a warning.

She lies still and tries to make her body heavier than the blanket. Her calves twitch anyway. The first bus to the rec center leaves at 4:31 if they make the light on Roosevelt. Ravi has said this three mornings in a row, the same tone he uses for utility due dates and train transfers. He has not said Maya's name in this kitchen once.

The floorboard outside her room gives a soft, familiar pop. Then another sound, quieter: ceramic against counter, then the long scrape of a metal spoon in a pot. He is standing in Maya's spot by the stove. Anika knows it before she sees him. Every morning since the funeral he stands exactly where Maya used to stand when she packed two lunches at once, talking to herself in a low running list: yogurt cup, napkin, extra spoon, bus card, don't forget oranges.

Anika sits up too fast, and black static slides over her eyes for a second. She waits it out, then pulls on her socks and the shirt that still smells like last night's detergent and old rubber from the court bag. In the mirror over her dresser she looks nine, not eleven. The collarbones are too sharp. Her braid from yesterday has kinked into a loose rope. She does not fix it.

Ravi is at the sink when she walks in, one hand braced against the counter, the other rubbing the outside of his left ankle in quick circles. He does that before every shift and every training block, as if the old injury can be negotiated with. The kitchen smells like instant coffee and curry leaves burned in yesterday's tempering pan. The steel tumbler with dented sides, Maya's tumbler, is on the drying rack. He has turned it upside down so water can drain out. He has not put it away.

"Banana," he says without turning. "Half now, half after warm-up. Salt water in the blue bottle. We leave in six minutes."

Anika opens the fruit bowl and stares at the bruised bananas. "I didn't sleep."

"I know."

"Then maybe we skip this morning." She peels half the banana but does not eat it. "I can do homework before school."

He sets his mug down. "You can do homework tonight. Shoes."

She keeps her voice flat because if it rises it might crack. "We used to light the lamp before school."

For a moment he does not move. The spoon in the pot ticks once as steam pushes it. When he speaks, he keeps his eyes on the stove.

"We're not doing lamps at four in the morning. We're doing work."

"It takes thirty seconds."

"Niks."

She hates that nickname from his mouth when it sounds like a command. "You don't even look at her photo anymore."

Now he turns. There are crescents of purple under his eyes, and stubble he missed along his jawline. His voice is low enough that she has to lean in to catch it.

"I'm looking at you," he says. "That is what I've got."

The words land harder than if he had shouted. He reaches for the folded paper on the table, the one with columns and times and abbreviations she is learning to decode: ghosting, multiball, lunges, serve targets. Next to Monday through Sunday he has drawn boxes for initials.

"You think this is about squash," he says. "It isn't. This is how we keep you moving. If you stop, that room in your head gets bigger. You've felt it."

He does not say grief. Your mother sticks behind his teeth. What he cannot say is that he is terrified of losing the only person left in this apartment.

Anika bites into the banana. It is too soft at the center, and she almost gags. She forces it down with one swallow of salt water that tastes metallic from the old bottle cap. Her throat burns.

"Shoes," Ravi says again, quieter.

She ties them. That is the turn. Not agreement, exactly. Surrender of one small morning thing.

They take the stairs because the building elevator has been dead since January, a handwritten OUT OF SERVICE sign curling at the tape corners. On the second-floor landing Mrs. Kline's tabby, Jasper, watches through the railing with a torn blue ribbon still looped around his neck from Christmas. Outside, late-winter air punches the sweat out of Anika's skin before they reach the bus stop.

Ravi checks his watch, then the bus app, then the street. He speaks in short units while they wait.

"Today is full block. No cuts."

Anika nods.

"Three ghosting ladders."

She nods again.

"If your chest spikes, control exhale first."

"I know."

"Say it."

She stares at the wet streak of headlights on pavement. "Long exhale, then feet."

"Good."

The Q66 hisses open. They ride in the back because the front heater blasts too hot. Anika rests her forehead against cold window glass and counts storefront shutters and dark laundromat signs. At a red light she sees her reflection ghosted over a pharmacy awning. Her face flickers with the reflection of the bus's route map, lines and arrows crossing her cheeks like somebody else planned her day for her.

The rec center looks closed from outside, a block of concrete with one buzzing security light over the side entrance. Ravi has keys from his volunteer coaching badge. Inside, the hallway smells like bleach and old sweat. Their footsteps echo. Somewhere in the building a pipe knocks three times in a row.

On Court 2, the HVAC starts and stops in irregular blasts, a metallic rattle that sounds like someone shaking a toolbox above the ceiling. The wall paint is cracked in one high corner where years of hot balls have chipped it. Anika loves this court and hates it in equal measure. Here, nothing is hidden for long. Bad footwork, bad timing, bad lies to yourself.

Ravi sets his phone on the floor by the tin and starts the timer. Its cracked screen throws pale blue light on his knuckles.

"Warm-up. Eight minutes."

Anika jogs lines. Sidewall to sidewall. T to back corner. Front right nick, front left nick. Her shoes make squeaks that overlap with the HVAC rattle until she cannot tell where one sound ends and the next begins. On minute three, sweat starts at the base of her neck. On minute five, her lungs open a little. On minute eight, he claps once.

"Ghosting ladder one. Twelve corners each sequence."

She bends, hands on thighs, waiting for the dizziness to settle.

"Ready," she says.

He nods at the floor markers. "Go."

She drives to the front-right drop and recovers to the T. She cuts to the front-left drop and recovers again. She reaches the mid-right volley, recovers, and then digs the back-left before resetting. She tracks the order by the numbers in her head, running one through twelve and then reversing it before she repeats the whole sequence. On the second sequence her right quad starts to tremble near the knee. On the third, the back of her throat tastes sour.

"Lower hips," Ravi says.

She drops lower. Her shoes skid on a sweat patch and she catches herself on the wall with her palm. Plaster dust sticks to her skin.

"Do not reach," he says. "Move first."

"I'm moving."

"Faster than that."

She wants to tell him that faster than that is where the panic starts. She goes anyway. Recovery step, lunge, push off, recover. Her braid smacks the back of her neck in a wet, rhythmic slap.

At rep nine she hears a high electronic chirp from the hallway and freezes for half a beat. It is only the front desk scanner, somebody swiping in early for the weight room, but the sound is wrong and too familiar. Hospital monitor at 3:11 a.m., then 3:12, then flat line replaced by the soft rush of oxygen and a nurse saying her name like she is both a child and witness.

She misses the next corner completely.

"Reset," Ravi says.

She does not move.

"Anika. Reset."

Her chest pulls tight, each inhale short and useless. She shakes her head once like she can clear it physically. The court wall blurs. On the whiteboard outside, somebody has written COMMUNITY HOURS in red marker, and for a second the word COMMUNITY looks like CHEMOTHERAPY because letters move when she gets like this.

Ravi steps closer but stops two feet away, as if an invisible line on the floor marks the limit of what he can do.

"Long exhale," he says.

She tries. It comes out as a cough.

"Again."

"I can't."

"You can. Slow it."

She drags air in through her nose, holds two counts, pushes it out through clenched teeth. Again. Again. The fourth one catches, and the fifth one lands. Her heartbeat is still loud but no longer random.

Ravi's voice shifts back to drill tone, almost brisk. "You lost one rep. You don't lose two. Start at eight."

"Can we do drives instead?"

"No."

"Please."

"No cuts. Start at eight."

There it is, the wall between them. She wants him to say her name the way Maya used to say it when panic hit at dentist appointments or crowded festivals, palm warm at the back of her neck, voice soft and stupidly confident: We've got this, just breathe with me. Ravi gives her numbers, not comfort. Ravi gives her structure shaped like mercy and calls it enough.

She starts at eight.

By the end of ladder one, her shirt is fully soaked and sticking to her spine. He gives her forty seconds of rest, not a second more.

"Ladder two," he says. "Add split step before every front-court lunge."

Anika stares at him. "That's extra reps."

"That's efficiency."

"It's more."

"Yes."

The answer is clean, almost polite. It makes her angrier than if he had snapped.

Ladder two burns different. The split steps load her calves with each transition until both legs feel wired and unstable. On the first back-corner recovery she feels a sharp stitch under her left rib and bites the inside of her cheek hard enough to taste blood. She keeps moving.

"Head up," Ravi says.

"I'm trying."

"Do it, don't narrate it."

She almost laughs at that, because narrating is all she has when things go sideways. She counts floor tiles instead. Twelve along the short line. Nine from service box to sidewall. She makes the court a map and runs it like a person trying not to think.

Halfway through the second sequence the memory comes anyway, not as an image this time but as smell. Antiseptic wipes and heated plastic tubing. The hospital room door that never stayed fully closed. Maya's skin after radiation, dry and warm and oddly sweet with medicated lotion. Anika's foot catches late, and her knee thumps the floor.

Ravi flinches. Just once. A small forward jerk he kills before it becomes a step.

"Up," he says.

Anika pushes herself to standing. Her palm leaves a damp print on the court. "That hurt."

"Where?"

"Left knee. I slammed it on the turn."

"Sharp or blunt?"

"Blunt now, but it spikes when I plant."

Ravi's jaw jumps once. "Then keep moving and tell me the second it goes sharp. I'm not guessing today."

She swallows whatever answer was coming. He does not ask about the memory; the smell gets filed under noise and left there. He tracks only mechanics, as if pain has categories and only some of them count.

They finish ladder two three minutes behind his target time. Ravi does not comment on the delay. He writes numbers in a small notebook with a pen that has a bus-depot logo on it, likely stolen from the dispatch desk, the ink smudged where his hand rests too hard.

"Water," he says. "One minute. Then ladder three."

Anika drinks too fast. Salt water hits her empty stomach and sits there like a hot stone.

"I need longer," she says.

"You need consistency."

"I'm going to puke."

"Then puke and reset."

She looks at him, waiting for the softening that does not come.

"I'm serious," he says. "Your body can do more than your panic says it can."

"How do you know?"

He pauses, and when he speaks his voice is rougher, less practiced. "Because I watched your mother do chemo on four hours of sleep and still pack your lunch. Because I watched her stand up when she couldn't feel her hands. That's how I know."

The words leave no room to hide. They are the closest he has come to saying fear out loud. His eyes slide away from hers as soon as he finishes, back to the timer, back to the drill sheet.

"Ladder three," he says.

She nods because her throat has closed again.

In the first sequence, she makes all the corners.

In the second sequence, her vision pulses at the edges by rep six.

In the third sequence, the hot stone in her stomach climbs into her throat.

She barely makes it to the side door before she vomits into the janitor sink in the corridor. Banana, salt water, acid, strings of spit clinging to the rust stain by the drain. Her abs cramp so hard she has to brace one hand against the cinderblock wall. A fluorescent tube overhead buzzes and flickers. In the next room somebody drops a weight plate with a clang that shocks through her ribs.

Ravi stands in the doorway and hands her a paper towel roll without comment. Not touching her. Not looking disgusted either. Just present, rigid, waiting.

She rinses her mouth at the cold tap until the acid taste dulls. Her face in the scratched mirror looks feverish and furious.

"We're done," she says, testing him.

"We're done when you choose to stop, not when your stomach makes the call."

"I just threw up in a janitor sink."

"And you're still standing. Good. Now we train the part after empty."

She wants to say something cruel. She wants to say You already lost one person in this family, do you really want to break the second one yourself? The sentence crowds her teeth and stays there. She spits into the sink one last time, wipes her mouth, and walks back onto Court 2 before he can tell her to.

That is the moment she notices it: her chest is still raw from crying that almost happened, but the grief image has slid out of focus. The hospital room is gone. The scanner chirp is gone. In their place is pure bodily data. Calf cramp left side. Blister hot spot under right arch. Salt crust drying at the corner of her lips. If she keeps the effort high enough, there is no space left for the other thing.

Ravi tosses a ball lightly from hand to hand, watching her stance.

"Finisher," he says. "Suicides. Five sets."

She squares up on the service line. "Five full?"

"Five full."

"You said three yesterday."

"Yesterday you did not finish ghosting."

"I almost passed out."

"Today you're still standing. Go."

During set one, she is quick. During set two, she turns slower at the back wall. By set three, her lungs feel like they are tearing. In set four, she clips the T with her shoe, stumbles, catches herself, and keeps going. By set five, there is no longer an Anika making choices. There is only movement stitched from command and refusal.

When she crosses the final line, she drops to hands and knees. Sweat drips from her nose and pools on the lacquered floor. Her heart is a fist punching inside her sternum. She waits for the grief wave to crash in while she is vulnerable.

It does not come.

That absence scares her almost as much as the grief itself.

Ravi kneels beside her then, not touching, but close enough that she can smell coffee on his breath.

"Look at me," he says.

She does.

"You did the full block."

She nods once.

"You wanted proof you could stay in it. That's proof."

Her voice is shredded. "It shut off."

He frowns. "What shut off?"

She searches for a word he will accept. "Noise."

He exhales through his nose, slow. "Good. Use that."

Use that. Like it is a tool in the bag with tape and extra grips. Maybe it is.

They cool down with slow ghosting, no timer. Her legs wobble on the stairs to the locker room. In the shower, hot water needles her skin until pink turns to red. She presses her forehead to tile and feels the pulse in her gums. Her stomach is hollow and angry. She eats the second half of the banana anyway while standing under the hand dryer because there is no time.

On the bus to school, Ravi types on his phone with two thumbs, probably dispatch messages for the morning shift. He does not look up when he speaks.

"You can sleep twenty minutes when we get home tonight. Not before homework."

Anika leans against the window. "I wasn't planning to sleep in class."

"Plan harder."

"You make everything sound like an assignment."

He pockets the phone. "Because you listen to assignments."

The line should start a fight, but she is too tired to throw it back. At her stop, he hands her lunch container and a folded index card.

"Schedule for the week," he says. "Read at lunch. We sign tonight."

She unfolds it on the sidewalk. Block letters in black marker bite through the index card: MON 4:30 A.M. COURT. TUE 4:30 A.M. COURT. WED ACTIVE RECOVERY / FOOTWORK. THU 4:30 A.M. COURT. FRI 4:30 A.M. COURT. SAT MATCH PLAY. SUN FILM + MOBILITY. Bottom line: NO MISSED MORNINGS WITHOUT MEDICAL REASON.

He taps the card once. "Structure is love, Niks. Remember that."

He says it like a promise and a warning in the same breath. She pinches the card until the paper edge dents her skin.

Then he is gone toward the bus stop, shoulders squared against the wind, lunch he forgot still on the kitchen counter at home.

School starts at 8:05, and by second period Anika can feel the dawn block hardening inside her legs. The classroom heat is turned too high, dry and stale, and the fluorescent hum stacks until every voice sounds wrapped in cloth. She keeps her pencil moving through math warm-up and verb drills so no one sees the tremor in her hand.

By lunch, she has only enough appetite for three spoons of tomato soup. Her phone buzzes under the cafeteria table. Ravi: `Hydrate. 3:18 Court 2. Second session: six-corner ghosting, drive-volley lock. Reply.`

She sends: `I can do this every day.`

His answer lands before she locks the screen: `Then prove it. No late bus excuses.`

Across the aisle, Zoe lifts her yellow taped pencil in a silent question. Anika taps her phone and gives one tight shake of her head. Zoe looks down at her tray, and the distance settles without either of them naming it.

At 3:24, Anika reaches Court 2 with her backpack still on and her school ID lanyard slapping against her sternum. Ravi already has the timer running and the ball tin open.

"You're four minutes late," he says.

"The teacher held us for homework reminders."

"The clock doesn't care why."

He points to the service box tape marks. "Six-corner ghosting. Forty seconds on, twenty off. Eight rounds."

The first three rounds feel like rust getting scraped off her joints. Round four drives a stitch under her ribs. By round five, the blister under her right arch opens inside her sock and smears hot wetness across the insole.

"I need water," she says after she clips the front wall and catches herself with one hand.

"You get water after a clean round."

"I'm dizzy."

"Then control your breathing and hit the marks. We don't train quitting."

She runs round six with copper in her mouth and sweat dripping off her chin in thick drops. She earns the water in round seven, takes two swallows, and coughs one back up. During round eight, she misses the back-left corner, pivots, and dry-heaves into the side trash can without stopping the timer.

Ravi hands her a roll of athletic tape. "Wrap the foot. We're not done."

"It's bleeding."

"Then wrap it tight."

She tapes the arch until the pressure burns, then steps back on court.

"Drive-volley lock drill," Ravi says. "Twenty forehand drives, twenty backhand volleys. You miss two in a set and you restart."

By the thirteenth forehand, her shoulder starts to shake. She overhits two backhand volleys in a row, and both balls thud high and dead.

"Restart," he says.

"My hand is cramping."

"Then hold the racket like it matters."

She restarts. The skin at the base of her thumb splits where the grip has worn thin, and the handle turns slick. On rep thirty-eight, her wrist buckles and she sends the ball into the tin.

Ravi feeds the next ball anyway. "Again. Finish what you started."

She does. By the final backhand her fingers are numb, and her shirt is pasted cold against her spine. She does not feel strong. She feels empty enough to keep moving.

By the time she reaches home, evening has flattened into that gray-blue hour when the apartment windows reflect the room instead of the street. The kitchen light is on. The smell of mustard seed and onion hangs in the air, and a pot of rice sits covered on the stove. Ravi is at the table with the same folded schedule from the morning, now rewritten on clean lined paper in thick black strokes, as if harder pen pressure could keep the day from splitting open.

He has added checkboxes for morning pulse, post-session soreness, and homework completion, and printed `EAT` in capital letters across the lunch slot. In the lower right corner he has drawn two signature lines: `Anika Iyer` and `Ravi Iyer.`

When she sits, he slides a steel tumbler of water toward her without meeting her eyes. The gesture is quick, almost tender, then gone.

"Eat first," he says.

They eat in near silence. Rice, dal, one spoon of pickle too sharp on Anika's scraped throat. Ravi asks about homework, socks, and the alarm in the same clipped tone he used courtside, but his knee keeps bouncing under the table hard enough to rattle the chair. She answers yes, yes, yes because any other word would crack something open.

After dishes, he tapes the schedule to the fridge with two magnets: one from the Queens Oncology fundraiser 5K Maya ran before she got sick, and one shaped like a cartoon bus from his depot holiday party. The paper hangs slightly crooked and starts to curl at one corner. He presses it flat with both thumbs, then straightens it again.

"Read it out," he says.

Anika reads each line aloud: times, blocks, school, mobility work, Saturday match play at 7:00 a.m., Sunday film review after laundry, no missed mornings without medical reason. By the last line, her own voice sounds like it belongs to someone older and meaner.

"Any changes?" he asks.

Yes rises first, sharp and almost reckless. Then comes the ask for one morning to sleep, one morning to light the lamp and say Maya's name without a stopwatch running. Zoe lowering her eyes over lunch flashes behind it, a warning about what this schedule is already costing.

Instead she says, "No changes."

Ravi hands her a pen. It is one of Maya's old lab pens, blue ink, hospital logo worn off from years of thumb friction. "Then sign."

Anika signs her name, each letter careful because her hand is shaking. Ravi signs below hers in fast slanted script. He caps the pen and places it on top of the fridge where they used to keep temple envelopes and spare birthday candles.

For a second they stand shoulder to shoulder looking at the paper, both breathing like they just finished a rally neither one wanted to lose.

"4:12 alarm," Ravi says. "I'll wake you if you miss it."

"I won't miss it," Anika says.

She believes herself.

When she goes to her room, she can feel tomorrow already waiting in her calves.
