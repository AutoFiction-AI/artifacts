# Chapter 17

A week after the lemon-rice Saturday she had written down in her notebook, Anika stepped out of the Lexington Avenue station with socks already damp from a leak in somebody else's umbrella and scorched-metal brake dust clinging to the inside of her jacket. Midtown wind pressed cold through the fabric anyway. The private club sat half a block down, all glass and brushed steel, with a doorman who held the door as if everybody coming in had earned that kind of welcome. Anika hated that she noticed it. She hated that she wanted it.

Inside, the air hit her like a refrigerator. The lobby floor had the same pale shine as the hospital corridor outside Maya's oncology wing, and she felt the old jolt before she could stop it. She looked down at her wrist. Maya's digital watch blinked 8:13 in gray block numbers. The strap had cracked at the third hole months ago, and Anika had reinforced the back with clear tape that had gone cloudy at the edges. She rubbed it once with her thumb, then clipped her bag shut.

Ravi was already at the registration table, talking to a volunteer in a tournament polo two sizes too big for him. Ravi kept shifting his weight off his left ankle, the way he always did when it stiffened in cold weather. He had worn his good navy shirt, the one he ironed with a dish towel because the iron plate stuck. The collar was still a little crooked. He turned when she came in, and he started to say something sharp and practical, then checked himself and lifted his chin toward the courts instead.

"You ate?" he asked.

"Half a bagel on the train. Banana too." She opened her bag to show the peel folded inside a sandwich bag because he still asked for evidence when he got anxious.

He gave one fast nod. "Warm-up court is three. Jonah's inside. You have twenty-two minutes. We go through length first, then front-court holds."

It would have been a command six months ago, maybe even six weeks ago. This morning it sounded like a request dressed as logistics. Anika heard the difference and did not look at him while she answered.

"I want five minutes alone before I hit." She said it like she was reading from a list. "No talking. Just breathing and movement."

Ravi's mouth tightened. For a second she thought he would push. Then he said, "Okay. Five. Then work." He glanced at her right leg before he could stop himself. "If anything in your shin lights up, you tell me right away."

"I know the agreement." Her voice came out flatter than she meant. "I know."

At court three, Jonah Feld stood by the back wall with his ball bucket and a travel mug that said WORLD'S OKAYEST UNCLE in chipped white letters. He had replaced his usual wrist tape with bright green kinesiology strips that zigzagged up his forearm like somebody had drawn lightning on him with a marker.

"Morning," he said. "You look like you slept zero hours and one minute."

"I got four." She started lunges without waiting. "Maybe four and a half."

"Luxury." He rolled a ball underhand to her forehand. "Today you don't chase every miracle pickup. If the rally is dead, let it die. We need clean points, not movie points."

Ravi stayed outside the glass while she did her first five minutes alone. He did not wave. He just stood with his hands in his jacket pockets, rocking once on that stiff ankle, reading the draw sheet taped to the wall even though he already knew it by heart.

The draw sheet read: Semifinal, Court One, Anika Iyer versus Cora Bell.

Cora walked in three minutes later with her academy duffel, immaculate as always, knee sleeve black against her left calf. The zipper on her bag had a tiny silver charm shaped like a dachshund. Cora touched it with her index finger before she set the bag down, a quick superstitious tap that looked private enough to be embarrassing if anyone teased her for it. She looked at Anika through the glass and gave one short nod.

"You good?" Cora asked when they crossed at the T.

"Yeah," Anika said.

"Cool. Let's not make this ugly." Cora's public voice was polished, almost bored, but her cheeks were pink with adrenaline and she kept smoothing one flyaway hair behind her ear every few seconds.

The club had filled by nine. Parents in long coats, academy kids in matching warmups, two men with sponsor lanyards and shoes that had never seen rain. A printed banner hung near court one: NORTH ATLANTIC ELITE PATHWAY SHOWCASE. Anika had memorized what came next if she won today: year-round academy slot, travel support, coaching subsidy, a room at summer camp that people in Queens talked about like it was an address in another country. She had not asked how much the package was worth. She had done the math anyway, over and over, with the numbers she already knew.

The oncology balance sat at thirty-eight thousand four hundred. The utility arrears sat at one thousand eight hundred sixty. The fifteenth of every month felt like a countdown clock. If she reached the final and signed, Ravi could drop one overtime shift, maybe two. Maybe his eyes would stop looking like he had been awake for years.

The referee called two minutes. Anika bounced on the balls of her feet and listened to the court: the squeak of Cora's shoes, the distant metallic rattle from the old HVAC vents even in this polished place, the hollow thud of another match three courts over. She pressed Maya's watchband into the heel of her palm, found the white service-box line with her eyes, and matched her breath to the vent rattle until panic thinned.

First game started fast. Cora served tight to the wall, heavy and precise, trying to pin Anika deep and force lazy boasts. Anika answered with length that kissed the side wall and died near the back nick. They traded points in stretches that felt like held breath: thirty-two shots, twenty-eight, forty-one, both of them moving so hard their shirts darkened at the spine. At 6-6 Cora sent a crosscourt that sat up half an inch too high, and Anika stepped forward and carved a backhand volley drop so soft the ball looked surprised to be on the floor.

Ravi did not clap big. He pressed his knuckles once to his mouth and sat down.

At 9-8, Cora pushed a brutal rally, dragging Anika corner to corner until Anika felt her lungs go metallic. She heard Jonah in her head anyway: if the rally is dead, let it die. Cora guessed wrong on a hold, committed early to the backhand corner, and Anika flicked the ball short to the opposite front with her wrist almost at full extension for a clean winner. Then she struck a forehand drive that Cora scraped up too late. 11-8.

Anika walked to the back glass between games and put both palms against it, cool and smooth, letting the sting in her skin pin her in the present. She watched her own reflection breathe. She looked eleven in the glass, not like the hard silhouette she carried around in her head.

Ravi leaned in when she sat.

"Good width," he said. "Her middle is opening. Keep your first step forward when she floats. Don't gift her the T."

"My timing's there," Anika said. Her heart was hammering so hard she could hear blood in her ears. "My right calf is twitchy but okay."

He did not pretend not to hear that. "Zero to ten?"

"One. Maybe two. Calf, not shin."

"Track it. Tell me if it moves."

Second game was the best squash Anika had ever played in front of people who mattered. Cora raised her level and stopped missing easy balls. They built points like arguments, each shot forcing the next concession. Cora changed pace with ugly little float balls that dropped dead in the back corners, then ripped flat drives when Anika edged too far forward. Anika answered by taking the ball early, stepping in and cutting angles so sharp the crowd made that involuntary noise people made when they were trying not to cheer for one kid over another.

At 10-9, game ball Anika, Cora saved with a backhand nick that rattled the tin and rolled out to a murmur. At 10-10, Cora got her own game ball after a fifty-shot rally that left both of them bent over their rackets. Anika stared at the service box, then looked up and saw sponsor lanyards in the front row, faces blank as clipboards.

She served body line, got a weak return, and punished the loose ball to the back. 11-10. Next rally Cora attacked first and Anika absorbed, absorbed, absorbed, then lifted a desperate lob that kissed the side wall and stayed in. Cora sprinted, reached, clipped the ball high. Stroke, Anika. 12-10.

Ravi stood this time, both hands on the rail, and forgot to hide what was on his face. Pride came through him so clean it was almost frightening. Not because it was new. Because she had gone so long without seeing it outside of results sheets.

Cora reset fast in game three. She shortened points, hunted Anika's backhand volley, and started testing the front corners with perfect weight. Anika got half a step late twice and paid for it. At 3-3 she lunged deep to the forehand and felt a small live wire flick against the inside edge of her right shin. It vanished in two strides. She told herself calf. She told herself court sting. She did not look at Ravi.

Cora took the game 11-6 with clinical patience. At 8-5 she held a drive for just a beat and sent it wrong-footing across. At 10-6 she served into the body and punished the loose return. There was no drama and no fist pump. She just turned and walked to the back, smoothing that flyaway hair behind her ear again, face blank the way academy kids learned to keep it.

Between games, Anika sat and tightened her right shoe even though it was already tight.

Ravi crouched so his face lined up with hers. "Talk."

"It's there," she said, eyes on the knot. "Right shin. Not sharp all the time. Flickers when I push off deep lunges."

"Number."

"Three sitting. Four on hard push."

He did not answer immediately. He looked toward the officials' table where the tournament physio sat with an iPad and an orange water bottle covered in peeling stickers. Tess Keating was not supposed to be here today; she had texted earlier that another clinician would staff the event. Ravi looked back at Anika.

"Can you keep your mechanics clean?"

"Yeah." She swallowed. "I can."

"If it jumps, we stop and call medical," he said.

She nodded, and then she hated that she had nodded because nodding made it real.

Game four started with rain drilling against the high windows and crowd noise turning thick. Anika won the first two points on length. Cora answered with a hold-and-flick that left Anika sliding. 2-1. 2-2. At 3-2 Anika attacked short and Cora scrambled, lifted, recovered. The rally stretched. Thirty shots. Thirty-five. Anika moved into the back right corner for a ball dropping tight to the wall and pushed off hard to recover the T.

Pain hit this time like a screwdriver driven straight into bone.

Her right leg buckled half an inch. She still reached the middle. She still won the rally because Cora hit the tin on the next ball. The point counted. The pain did not leave.

She walked to receive serve and every step sent a bright pulse up her shin. It was not muscle pain and it was not calf pain. Bone pain had its own personality. It was clean and mean and specific.

Cora served. Anika tried to plant and drive. Her foot landed a fraction off and the shock traveled up the same line, shin to knee to hip. She hit short. Cora punished. 3-3.

On the next rally Anika opened with decent length, then got dragged front right. She lunged, felt the edge of the world go white for a second, and bailed out with a weak boast. Cora killed the ball in two shots. 4-3 Cora.

Ravi stood up so fast his chair scraped loud enough for half the front row to turn.

Anika bounced once at the service box and did math because math felt safer than fear.

If she withdrew now, the offer packet died. She had heard enough hallway talk to know that. The sponsor wanted finalists, not stories. No sponsor meant no academy subsidy. No subsidy meant monthly dues at clubs they could not afford. It meant Ravi back on the late-night app-driving loop, drinking gas-station coffee and pretending he was not swerving with fatigue. It meant the fifteenth again and the oncology portal open on his cracked phone screen, minimum payment blinking red.

She saw the numbers in sequence: thirty-eight thousand four hundred, one thousand eight hundred sixty, and bus salary at three thousand nine hundred if no shifts were missed. Overtime was variable. Everything was variable except what was owed.

Cora served again, deep to backhand. Anika got there late and framed the return high, and the ball went down. 5-3.

The referee called "Hand out" and looked at her too long.

Ravi pressed both palms to the glass and mouthed, `Talk.`

Anika toweled off and went to the back wall. Her throat felt full of tin. She could keep playing. She could probably steal this game if she shortened points and accepted ugly squash. She could also hear Tess from months ago, clinical and maddeningly calm: when bone pain returns at impact, you don't negotiate with it.

Maya's letter from Locker 214 had one line she had read so often the paper had gone soft at the fold. I don't want your life to become a punishment you're good at.

She looked at Ravi, then at the referee. "Medical timeout," she said, and her voice came out thin.

The court door opened. The tournament physio jogged in with a badge that read DR. ELLA MORALES. She had a crescent of turmeric stain near the cuff of her white fleece, like she had eaten between matches and wiped her hand on the wrong sleeve. Her black curls were twisted into a pencil-held bun that kept trying to collapse.

"Tell me where and when," Morales said, kneeling.

"Right tibia," Anika said. "Medial side. Pain on push-off and impact. Started as flickers game three. Sharp now."

"Scale."

"Three at rest. Seven when I lunge hard."

Morales pressed along the shin with two fingers, precise and merciless. Anika hissed at one spot halfway down.

"There?"

"Yeah."

"Hop test." Morales steadied her elbow. "Single-leg, right side. One hop."

Anika tried. The first hop landed and she sucked air through her teeth so hard she tasted metal. She could not bring herself to do a second.

Morales glanced toward the officials' table, then back to Anika. Her tone stayed level. "You have prior stress fracture history on this side, correct?"

"Yes. Spring." Anika hated how childlike the single syllable sounded.

"Based on focal tenderness and impact pain, continuing puts you at high risk of worsening a bony stress injury. I advise retirement now."

Ravi had stepped through the door and now stood one pace behind Morales, hands open at his sides as if he had forgotten what to do with them.

"Can she finish this game if she shortens movement?" he asked, too quickly.

Morales did not match his speed. She slowed down the way people in authority did when they knew someone wanted to rush them past caution.

"No," she said. "This is not about fitness discomfort. This is localized tibial pain with load intolerance in an athlete with prior fracture history. If she continues, she could turn a manageable setback into a complete fracture and a much longer shutdown."

Ravi's jaw jumped. "How long if it's a flare?"

"We image first. Could be days, could be weeks, could be worse if you gamble right now."

Cora stood near the short line, racket hanging loose in her hand, trying not to stare and failing. Her face had lost its polished boredom. She rubbed the dachshund charm once with her thumb and looked away.

Anika sat on the bench with a towel around her shoulders and watched the sponsor table through the glass. One of the lanyard men was already typing on his phone. Beside him, a woman in a camel coat and bright red sneakers spoke into a headset mic, then shook rain off her sleeve with irritated little flicks. Anika recognized her from the pre-event briefing packet Ravi had shown her: Lindsay Cho, pathway liaison.

Lindsay walked to the court door as officials conferred. She was probably thirty, maybe younger, with eyeliner sharp enough to cut paper and two tiny silver safety pins in one ear. When she smiled, it looked trained. When she stopped smiling, a crease stayed between her eyebrows as if the expression never fully left.

"Ravi, can I borrow you for a second?" she asked from the threshold. "Just outside?"

Ravi looked at Anika first.

"Go," Anika said. She wrapped both hands around her water bottle because her fingers had started shaking.

Through the glass she could not hear them, but she could read enough in posture. Lindsay spoke with her palms up, professional concern face on. Ravi kept glancing back toward Anika and then down at the floor. Lindsay leaned in closer, voice lower, then tapped the sponsor badge against her chest as if to remind him what she represented.

Morales stayed by Anika's bench. She opened a small notepad and wrote with a fountain pen that scratched softly in the pauses.

"You did the right thing calling this," she said.

Anika laughed once, ugly and brief. "I didn't call it yet."

"You asked for medical. That is calling it in practice." Morales capped the pen. "You are eleven. You should not have to carry this alone."

"I'm not carrying it alone." Anika looked through the glass at Ravi and Lindsay. "He's carrying it too."

"Then make sure he's carrying your health and not only your result."

Lindsay and Ravi came back inside. Ravi's face had gone colorless except for two high spots in his cheeks. Lindsay kept the professional smile on but her jaw was set.

"Okay," Lindsay said, palms out. "We can post tactical pending imaging. That keeps tomorrow's review live."

"No," Morales said. "I'm charting medical retirement. Focal tibial pain, prior fracture history, load intolerance."

Lindsay turned to Ravi. "I'm not asking her to keep playing. I'm asking for wording that buys twenty-four hours. If the board reads medical semifinal retirement, this cycle auto-freezes."

Anika heard auto-freezes and wanted to throw her bottle at the glass.

Ravi spoke before she could.

"Auto-freezes means what, exactly?" he asked.

"For this round, the offer pauses and usually dies," Lindsay said. "There are later windows. Appeals sometimes work."

"Sometimes," Ravi repeated.

"Yes. Sometimes." Lindsay's voice thinned. "I'm trying to keep one door unlocked."

"Do not relabel a fracture risk as strategy," Morales said.

Ravi looked at Anika. She had seen that look before when overdue notices came in and he pretended he had not opened them. Calculating, terrified, angry at the math itself.

"Usually dies means withdrawn?" he asked.

"For this cycle, yes," Lindsay said.

Anika felt heat rise behind her eyes. She did not cry. She counted the ridges on the bottle cap instead.

She counted the ridges on the cap: one, two, three, four, five.

"Dad," she said.

He turned to her immediately.

"If I go back in, I might break it." Her voice shook anyway. "Not maybe sore. Break it."

Lindsay opened her mouth. Morales cut in first.

"That is correct based on current signs," Morales said.

"And if I break it, I'm out months." Anika kept talking because if she stopped she would lose nerve. "Then no showcase, no season, maybe suspension if I hide symptoms again."

Ravi's fingers flexed at his sides. He took one breath, then another, like he was learning how.

"What do you want me to do?" he asked her.

The question hit harder than the pain. He had asked her preference before, about food, bus routes, even practice music. He had never asked it like this, with real authority attached.

"I want to retire," she said. "Medical. Not fake tactical." She swallowed. "I want my leg to work in December."

Ravi nodded once. "Okay."

Lindsay's smile disappeared for good. "Then I need your confirmation now for the report."

He turned toward her, and when he spoke his public voice was still there, but it had changed shape. It was no longer command voice. It was boundary voice.

"Door closes, it closes," he said. "We're done today. She's retiring on medical advice." He glanced at Anika. "Her decision."

The referee called both players to center for formal retirement procedure. Cora came forward, face sweaty and open in a way Anika had never seen from her.

"I hate winning like this," Cora said quietly, not for cameras.

"Then don't act happy," Anika said, and she tried to smile so Cora would know it was not a knife.

Cora gave a tiny snort. "I'm not that good an actor." She squeezed Anika's shoulder once, awkward and brief. "Take care of it. For real."

The referee stepped between them with the clipboard and asked, "Retirement confirmed?" Anika said yes out loud, then signed beside Morales's note while her hand shook hard enough to drag the pen line uphill. Cora's palm was damp when they shook; her grip tightened once and let go.

When the announcement came over the house mic, it cracked on Anika's name and then settled: "Bell defeats Iyer by medical retirement, fourth game, five-three." A volunteer at the sponsor table uncapped a thick black marker and drew one hard line through ANIKA IYER on the printed "Tomorrow Showcase" sheet taped to a stanchion.

Applause came in scattered patches from people who were not sure what emotion was appropriate. On the board beside court one, the score froze where she had left it: Bell d. Iyer ret., 8-11, 10-12, 11-6, 5-3.

Anika sat while Morales filled out the incident sheet. Ravi stood behind her chair and kept one hand on the backrest, not touching her, just there. Lindsay paced three steps away with her headset pressed tight.

"Yes, medical retirement," Lindsay said into the mic. "Documented by event physician. Put us in next-cycle review for now." She did not look back.

Ten minutes later, a local livestream guy with perfect hair and a phone gimbal drifted over for post-match comments. He held the mic toward Ravi because Anika was a minor and looked like she might bite someone.

"Mr. Iyer, rough end to a huge semifinal," he said in his bright practiced tone. "Was stopping precaution, or strategy for tomorrow's showcase?"

Lindsay, three feet away, met Ravi's eyes and gave the smallest possible shake of her head, as if begging him to keep it soft.

Ravi did not look at her. He looked straight at the camera.

"Medical," Ravi said.

"But with pathway stakes this high, some families push one more game," the interviewer said.

Ravi cut him off. "Not my kid."

He looked straight at the camera. "Old fracture site lit up. Staff said stop. She stopped. She's eleven. No match and no package are worth her leg."

The interviewer blinked, thrown off script.

"Does this affect your family's academy plans?"

"Probably, yes," Ravi said. "We'll deal with money after imaging."

He gave no spin, no tactical story, and no pretense that this was chess.

The clip was online before they left the building. Anika knew because two kids from another draw glanced at her phone and then at her with a mix of pity and respect she could not decode.

By noon the semifinal court had been reset for the next match. People moved on fast in rooms like this. Sponsors shook hands. Coaches argued line calls. Someone laughed too loudly near the coffee station. Anika sat in the physio corner with an ice wrap around her shin that smelled faintly of bleach and rubber and stared at the sponsor packet in Ravi's hands.

He had folded it in quarters without meaning to, crisp creases across the logo. She knew what was inside because she had watched him read it at the kitchen table when he thought she was asleep: development roster slot, academy tuition assistance, travel stipend language, committee review clauses written in polite corporate sentences. It had looked like rescue printed on heavy paper.

Now it looked like paper.

Ravi's phone buzzed with an email while she watched. He opened it, read three lines, and closed it again. The subject line stayed visible on the lock screen for a second: `PATHWAY OFFER STATUS UPDATE.` The first sentence said the current-cycle offer was withdrawn after medical retirement and could be reconsidered only in a later review window.

Ravi crouched in front of her. "Morales wants imaging this afternoon if we can get in," he said. "If not, tomorrow morning first slot."

"Can we afford urgent care today?" The question came out before she could censor it.

He exhaled through his nose. "We can afford not to wait." Then he added, because he had promised fewer evasions, "I'll move money from the utility payment if I need to."

"That puts us back in warning window."

"I know."

"How much did the sponsor package cover?"

He hesitated. "Enough to change things." He rubbed at his forehead. "Not enough to erase debt, but enough to breathe."

Anika looked past him at the court where Cora now warmed down with long elastic-band stretches, face set back to neutral. Cora's coach handed her a paper cup, and Cora took it with both hands as if it were hot tea, not electrolyte powder. She looked human when nobody watched her. Everybody did.

"I'm sorry," Anika said.

Ravi's head came up sharp. "Do not apologize for this."

She flinched at the full form and he softened immediately.

"Don't apologize," he said again, quieter. "I need your records folder and your insurance card before we leave. I also have to dump tonight's overtime and call the depot in the next fifteen minutes." His voice went rough anyway. "That's what today costs first."

Anika studied him the way she studied opponents in the first five points, looking for the bluff. She did not find one.

"Lindsay said the door closes," she said.

"Maybe this door closes." Ravi lifted one shoulder. "There are other doors, and some of them are ugly and slow and involve buses at five in the morning. We know that route."

"You mean public courts and no academy."

"I mean whatever keeps you playing and still lets you walk when you're twenty." He gave a short humorless laugh. "My own ankle is a weather report now. I would like you to skip that inheritance."

She almost smiled.

Morales came back with discharge paperwork and a photocopied list of imaging centers that accepted same-day sports referrals. "I called two places," she said. "Queens Ortho can see her at 3:40 if you can make it. Bring prior records if you have them."

"Thank you," Ravi said.

Morales handed the clipboard to Anika first, not Ravi. "You sign here acknowledging recommendation: no further match play today and no impact activity until imaging review."

Anika took the pen. Her signature looked shaky and too round, like a younger kid's handwriting. She hated that too, and then she let herself hate it for exactly two seconds before she set the pen down. This was the kind of anger that could eat a whole week if she fed it.

On the way out they passed Lindsay near the lobby staircase. She had taken off the headset and now held it looped around two fingers, spinning it in a slow circle. The red sneakers had a scuff on the toe that she kept rubbing with her thumb like she might erase it if she worked hard enough.

"Anika," she said. Her tone had lost the corporate gloss. "For what it's worth, your first two games were exceptional."

"Thanks," Anika said.

Lindsay looked at Ravi. "I pushed hard in there. It's my job."

"You tried to relabel an injury," Ravi said.

"I tried to keep one review window open," Lindsay said. She tapped the headset against her wrist. "If this turns into a story about sponsor pressure on an injured kid, that window closes too."

"Are you warning us or threatening us?" Anika asked.

Lindsay held her gaze. "I'm telling you how this system punishes people who don't play along."

"Then let it punish us," Ravi said. "Her file stays medical."

Outside, the rain had turned thin and needly. Ravi opened their one intact umbrella and angled it so most of the dry space covered her right side without making a show of it. They walked to the subway entrance in silence. At the corner he slowed because his left ankle had seized, and he rolled it once against the curb before they crossed. Anika pretended not to notice, then noticed anyway.

On the downtown platform, he checked the train app and cursed under his breath when the E line delay ticked from six minutes to fourteen.

"Fuck," he said, then looked at her. "Sorry."

"It's fine." She shifted weight off her shin. "I was gonna say it too."

They stood shoulder to shoulder while buskers played off-key violin two cars down. A man in a suit ate chips from a vending-machine tube and licked orange dust off his thumb. A little girl in a sparkly dance jacket practiced toe points against a tiled pillar while her mother read a legal pad full of handwritten notes. Normal city life kept moving around them, indifferent and loud.

Ravi broke the silence first. "When you called medical, I was angry for about ten seconds," he said. "Not at you. At... everything. The timing. The money. All of it."

Anika kept her eyes on the tracks. "I know."

"Then I watched you try to hop." He shook his head. "I felt sick. I kept thinking about that spring qualifier, how I kept signaling you to keep pushing when you were already cooked."

She glanced up. "You stopped me today."

"You stopped first," he said. "That matters."

The train came roaring in, wind shoving cold through the tunnel. They found two seats near the door. Anika propped her leg on her bag. Ravi took out his phone and opened the billing app by reflex, then locked it again without looking. He rubbed his thumb over the cracked edge of the case, thinking.

"I need to call Priya," he said.

Anika looked over. "Why?"

"Because she'll hear this from someone else in ten minutes and call yelling. Better she hears it from me now." He gave a tired half-smile. "Also because she told me if I ever pressure you through injury again, she will personally drag me into the East River."

Anika laughed, startled out of herself. "She would too."

"Yes," Ravi said. "She absolutely would."

He made the call when they hit tunnel signal near Queens Plaza. Priya answered on the second ring, voice already sharp.

"What happened? I just saw a clip."

Ravi kept it simple. "Pain flare in old tibia site. Medical advised retirement. Anika called timeout. We're heading to imaging this afternoon."

There was a pause. Then Priya said, "Put her on."

Anika took the phone.

"Hi," she said.

"You okay, kid? Real answer."

"Scared. Mad. Also hungry." 

Priya huffed a laugh. "Good. Hunger means your body still trusts you. I left lemon rice in your fridge last night. Eat that after imaging."

"You made lemon rice?"

"I made too much and your father can't pace himself around pickle, so yes." Priya's voice softened. "I'm proud of you for stopping."

Anika swallowed. "Thanks."

"And if anyone tells you this ruins your life, send them to me. I've got two phones and enough battery for violence." 

Anika laughed again, real this time, and handed the phone back.

When they came above ground in Queens, the afternoon had gone the color of dirty aluminum. By the time they reached the apartment, fried onions from downstairs and damp wool from coats hung to dry on door hooks clung to the hallway. Home hit first, not rescue and not failure.

Ravi unlocked the door and stood aside so she could go first.

"Ice twenty minutes, then we leave for imaging in an hour," he said automatically. Then he caught himself. "Unless you want food first."

"Food first," she said.

"Okay. Food first." He set his keys in the bowl Maya had once painted with tiny blue fish and never glazed correctly. The rim still scratched your fingers if you moved too fast.

In the kitchen he heated Priya's lemon rice while Anika sat at the table with her leg on a second chair and the training-and-grief notebook open to a blank page. She stared at the line where she usually wrote reps and times. Her hand hovered.

Finally she wrote: `Semifinal retirement. 8-11, 10-12, 11-6, 5-3. Pain at medial right tibia. Called medical myself.` She paused, then added another line. `Dad backed it in public.`

She looked up. Ravi was at the stove, stirring with one hand and holding his phone with the other as he typed a message. She could see his reflection in the microwave door: shoulders still too tight, mouth set, but not performing certainty anymore.

"Who are you texting?" she asked.

"Jonah and Tess," he said. "And the sponsor coordinator. I wrote: `Anika retired on medical advice. We are proceeding with imaging and following protocol. Please remove her from tomorrow's draw.`"

He turned, met her eyes, and added, "I also wrote: `Any future conversation has to include her, not go around her.`"

Anika blinked. "You sent that?"

"I did."

The microwave beeped. He set a plate in front of her, steam carrying lemon and mustard seed and curry leaf through the room. The scent dragged last Saturday into the kitchen: no plan, no bracket, just a version of family that did not require anyone to bleed for it.

They ate in near silence at first. Halfway through, Ravi put down his spoon.

"I need to say this clean," he said. "If money gets worse because of today, that is on me and the adults. Not on you."

Anika looked at him, waiting for the catch.

There was no catch.

"Okay," she said.

He nodded once, as if she had signed a contract.

After lunch she iced her shin and watched rain collect in the fire escape gutter. Notifications kept lighting her phone: `you okay?` from Zoe, three missed messages from kids she barely knew, one clipped text from Cora that read `Heal right. See you on court when it's real.` No heart emoji, no extra words. It sounded exactly like Cora.

At 2:41, Ravi zipped her imaging folder with old scans and referral forms. At 2:43, he set the sponsor packet on top of the recycling bin, looked at it for a long second, then folded it once more and put it in the desk drawer instead.

"Why not trash it?" Anika asked.

He shrugged. "Because losing something and pretending it never mattered are not the same thing."

She considered that. It sounded like something Priya would say, except less polished and more tired.

When they left for Queens Ortho, he handed her the apartment keys before he locked the door.

"Hold these," he said.

It was a small thing. It felt like a larger one.

On the train to the clinic, Anika touched the taped underside of Maya's watch and watched the minute change from 3:17 to 3:18. Her leg hurt. Their money was still a mess. The sponsor path had narrowed to almost nothing. None of that had been fixed by making the right choice.

But when the doors opened at their stop, Ravi waited for her pace instead of pulling ahead, and she did not have to pretend not to limp for either of them.

For now, there were scans, paperwork, and the slower route back to a court that would still be there when her leg was ready.
