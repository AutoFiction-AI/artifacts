# Chapter 4

By Friday evening, when Anika double-knotted her shoes, the old HVAC above Court Two had started its metal cough, three hard rattles and one long whine, like somebody dragging a chair across tin. The sound bounced inside the public rec center and settled in her teeth. She checked Maya's watch out of habit, then corrected herself because it was not a habit yet, not really. She had started wearing it only this week, and the strap still sat wrong on her wrist bone unless she pushed it up with her thumb.

6:43 p.m.

Ravi stood by the cinder-block wall with a clipboard he did not need. He had printed a match sheet from work and clipped it to the board even though the ladder organizer had his own forms. He liked paper that could be held. He liked columns. He liked an object that did not answer back. A smear of bus-depot grease stained the side of his left thumb, and he kept rubbing it with his right index finger as if he could erase it before anyone noticed.

"You warm up through the middle," he said. "Don't chase early winners. Make her hit one more ball every rally. She wants you rushing. Don't give it."

Anika nodded once.

"Run it back."

"Middle first. One more ball."

"And your serve routine."

"Four bounces, breath out, eyes up."

He looked at the watch on her wrist, then at her face, and he did not mention Maya. He never mentioned Maya before matches. It was one of his rules that was not written anywhere.

Across the glass, the ladder board listed names in dry-erase marker. ANIKA IYER sat under RUNG 7. Tonight she played for RUNG 6. Above her name was LEAH SETHI, fourteen, Forest Hills, current holder. Anika knew Leah from two junior events where they had not played each other. Leah had the academy posture that looked taught, shoulders down and neck long, even when she bent to tie a shoe.

Leah arrived with a navy duffel and a little brother trailing her, maybe six, still in a school uniform hoodie, carrying a lunchbox covered in cartoon sharks. Before she even looked at the court, she crouched and told him, "Don't trade the clementine this time. You hear me? That's mine for after." The boy saluted with two fingers and sat on the bleachers, swinging his legs hard enough to knock his heels against the metal seat.

Leah's warmup jacket was zipped to her chin despite the heat. A frayed friendship bracelet wrapped twice around her racket throat; she unwound it and rewound it before every stretch, the same exact motion, as if she were tightening a screw. When she finally glanced at Anika through the glass, her mouth tipped sideways in a smile that was almost friendly and not at all friendly.

"You're the kid," Leah said when they met at the door for the coin toss. "Rung seven kid."

"I'm Anika," Anika said.

"Yeah. I know. Everybody knows." Leah bounced on her heels once. "You hit forever. That's what people keep saying."

The marker, Mr. Salazar, flipped the coin with a thumbnail scarred white across the knuckle. He was retired NYPD and volunteered twice a week, and he always smelled faintly of fennel seeds and mint. "Call it."

"Tails," Anika said.

Leah won the toss anyway and chose to receive.

As they knocked up, Leah fed high, soft balls to the back left and watched Anika run them down. They were not hard enough to score, just hard enough to make her move extra steps before the match started. Anika sent the ball back to length and kept her face blank. She could feel Ravi at the side wall, not visible from this angle but present as pressure, like another line painted on the floor that she had to stay inside.

The first game opened with a twenty-six-shot rally. Leah went early to the front right, then feathered a counter-drop that died crooked because of the warped floorboard near the tin. The ball took an ugly second bounce. Anika still got there and lifted it high. Leah sighed theatrically, like she had been assigned tedious homework, and reset from the T.

That was the pattern. Leah made a show of patience whenever she made Anika run. She rolled her neck. She checked her nails between points. She asked for the ball to be wiped when there was no sweat on it yet. She let Anika serve, then held up one finger to the marker as if to say wait, wait, I'm still not ready. The tiny delays worked because they were legal and because they made everyone notice time.

At 4-4, Leah caught Anika's eye and said, quiet enough that only the two of them could hear, "You look twelve."

"I'm eleven," Anika said before she could stop herself.

"Wow." Leah whistled once. "Then this is your bedtime match."

Anika served hard to the backhand and took the next rally in eighteen shots by refusing the front court until Leah blinked first. She did not look at Leah after the winner. At the service box she tapped the ball into the same worn patch: one, two, three, four. On four she emptied her lungs and lifted her eyes to the front wall before she struck. The routine made a fence around her.

She took the first game 11-8.

Between games Ravi leaned in from the door and passed her the bottle without asking. "Good. Keep length. Don't talk."

"She keeps slowing it," Anika said.

"Then you speed your feet, not your shot choice."

"I know."

His jaw flexed once. "Then do it."

The second game started fast and turned sour in the middle. Leah stepped across Anika's line twice and asked for lets that were never lets. Mr. Salazar gave one. Ravi muttered something sharp under his breath that ended in "ridiculous," and then he went still when Mr. Salazar glanced toward him.

At 7-6, Leah clipped a ball that sprayed chalk dust from the side wall. She looked at Anika while she twirled her racket handle and said, "My coach said if you can keep the little grief girl moving, she cracks by game four."

The words landed before Anika had language for them. The phrase grief girl sounded like a style of play, like a grip change.

She stared at Leah's mouth and missed the score call.

"Seven-all," Mr. Salazar repeated.

"You can hear fine, right?" Leah asked. "I heard your mom was sick a long time before. People get used to hospital sounds."

Something under Anika's sternum tightened so hard she thought she might hiccup. She did not answer. She served short by accident, got punished straight down the wall, and did not chase because she knew she would not reach it.

At 10-10, they played the longest rally of the night so far, thirty-nine shots by Mr. Salazar's count, and Anika felt her forearm begin to burn from the elbow down. Leah won it with a low counter-drop that scraped the tin and rolled dead. Leah did not celebrate. She just lifted one shoulder and walked backward to receive, as if to say this outcome was administrative, already filed.

Leah took the game 12-10.

Ravi met Anika at the door, voice low and clipped. "You gave her your ears. Stop doing that."

"She talked." Anika's throat felt lined with chalk.

"Everybody talks. Your job is to be deaf."

"You can't just be deaf."

"You can if you want to win."

She drank and swallowed twice because the first swallow came back up with acid.

The third game opened with Leah serving body-lob and then pinning Anika in the backhand corner until her calves trembled. The ball on Court One hit glass with a report like a hand slapping tile. Somebody in the bleachers laughed too loud. A little kid dropped a metal bottle and it rolled the length of the aisle, clanging against each stair edge. Sound piled on sound.

At 3-5, Anika over-hit three balls in four rallies. She knew it while the ball was still leaving her strings. Her timing had gone half a beat early, the same way it went in class when she tried to write notes and listen at the same time.

Leah noticed and widened the gap to 8-4, then 9-5, then 10-6.

Between rallies she said nothing now. She did not need to. She just held the T with her chin high and forced Anika to run around her. At game ball, Leah asked Mr. Salazar to repeat the score even though she had clearly heard it.

"Ten-six," he said.

"Thanks." Leah smiled at Anika. "Just checking she can count when she's tired."

Leah closed it 11-6 on a serve that nicked the side wall and died in the back.

The match stood at two games to one against Anika.

Ravi crouched by the court door for the first time, bringing his face level with hers. Sweat darkened the collar of his jacket. "Listen to me. She's trying to make this social. Don't let her. Pin her deep left, deep left, then step in only if she floats one."

Anika nodded.

"Run the pattern."

"Deep left twice before I go short."

"And if she talks?"

"I call the score and hit."

He tapped the clipboard with two fingers, the same quick tap he used when checking bus arrivals. "Good. Breathe out on bounce three and stay on your toes between rallies."

He said it like there were only two buckets and everything in the world fit in one of them: execution and noise.

By the fourth game, sweat had soaked through Anika's shirt spine. Her fingers were pruning inside the grip. She started towing each breath in by force. The ball looked normal until it did not. Sometimes it came off Leah's racket with a faint blur around it, as if the fluorescent lights were slightly out of phase.

Leah kept up the body games. On every point she won, she walked straight past Anika's shoulder, too close, not touching but close enough for heat. On every point she lost, she tied and retied her ponytail without need. She asked for the ball changed at 5-5. Mr. Salazar refused. She laughed and said, "Worth a try," then stared at Anika while she spun the old ball in her palm.

At 7-7, the electronic scoreboard on the outer wall chirped to confirm another court's result. One short beep came first, then another, then a sustained alarm because somebody had entered the score wrong and the machine did not like mismatches.

The pitch hit Anika behind the eyes.

For half a second the court glass turned into hospital glass. The side door became the ICU sliding door with the rubber seal that sighed open too slowly. The chirp became the monitor in Maya's room after midnight, first regular, then quick, then angry. She saw the green line stair-step and flatten and jump again. She saw a nurse with a ponytail ask, "Can you step outside for a minute?" while Ravi argued about one more minute, one more minute, please. She smelled chlorhexidine and warmed plastic tubing. She remembered the paper gown against her own forearm when she leaned over the bed and Maya's fingers were too dry to hold on.

"Anika." Mr. Salazar's voice cut through. "Play."

The ball was already in motion. Leah had served and come forward. Anika reacted late, clipped the frame, and sent the shot high enough to survive. The rally stretched to eight shots, then twelve, then seventeen.

Her chest tried to lock. She forced a breath into the bottom of it and kept moving. Leah went short to the front left. Anika reached it and drove straight. Leah volleyed crosscourt. Anika's feet slid on sweat and recovered. The ball came back to the middle. She refused the cheap drop, hit length, hit length again, and waited until Leah overreached on a boastful volley. The opening appeared for half a blink. Anika punched the ball low into the backhand nick.

She hit a winner.

The score moved to 8-7.

She did not fist-pump. She did not look at Ravi. She walked to the service box and did the routine with hands that were no longer steady.

On bounce two, her hand twitched and nearly lost the ball. She caught it, reset, and forced four clean bounces before she let her breath go and brought her eyes up to serve.

At 9-8, Leah finally broke the silence. "Did that noise mess you up?" she asked, smiling as if they were sharing a joke. "You looked gone for a second."

Anika bounced the ball and said, "Score?"

"Nine-eight," Mr. Salazar said.

"Thanks," Anika said, not to Leah.

She served tight to the back left, took the next rally with patient length, then guessed right on Leah's crosscourt and cut it off early for 10-8.

Leah saved one game ball with a perfect drop that died on the second bounce. On the next rally Anika forced a boast and caught the volley into open space for 11-9.

The match was level at two games all.

Ravi's voice came through the open top panel of the door before she reached him. "One game. One. You're fitter than her tonight."

"I'm dizzy," Anika said.

He put the bottle in her hand. "You're dizzy because your heart rate is high. That's not an injury. Breathe low. One game."

She wanted to ask for one minute alone in the corner by the vending machine where no one could look at her. She wanted to press her forehead to cool metal and stop hearing the scoreboard beep replaying under everything else. Instead she drank because he was watching the bottle level and she could feel him counting her swallows.

The fifth game became ugly in the way long matches become ugly. Good technique bent around survival. Leah's shoulders stayed pretty, but her movement slowed by one fraction on deep corners. Anika's legs felt full of wet sand, and her right quad twitched every time she pushed off. The rallies got longer because neither could put the ball away clean.

The score crawled from 2-2 to 3-3 to 5-5.

At 6-6, Leah hit a hard body shot that clipped Anika's hip and dropped. "Sorry," Leah said automatically, then added, "You okay, kid?"

"I'm fine," Anika said.

She was not fine. The edges of the court had started to pulse with her heartbeat.

At 8-7 Anika, Ravi called from outside, "Middle!" in a tone that sounded like command and prayer braided together. Mr. Salazar looked at him and lifted a warning finger. Ravi shut his mouth but not his eyes.

Leah took the next two points with brutal patience, forcing Anika front-right and then lifting over her head again and again until her lungs stung. 9-8 Leah.

On game point at 10-9, Leah wiped her face with her wrist and said, not cruel this time, just factual, "You're tough. I'll give you that."

It was the first honest thing she had offered all match. It made Anika angrier than the taunts.

They traded to 10-10. Then 11-11.

At 11-11, the rally began with a weak serve from Anika that should have been punished. Leah stepped in and chose a drop too fine, clipping tin by a millimeter. They got a let and replayed the point.

Anika bent forward with her hands on her knees, inhaled once, twice, and lifted her head before Mr. Salazar could call time.

She served deep. Leah countered deep. They moved each other corner to corner without risk for twenty shots, then thirty. Anika felt something in her mind detach from panic and become pure counting.

One more ball.

One more ball.

One more ball.

At shot thirty-six, Leah's length landed short by half a foot. Anika stepped across and drove to deep backhand. Leah scraped it up for shot thirty-seven. Anika volleyed to the opposite corner for shot thirty-eight. Leah got there late and sent up a defensive lob that hung in the center too long.

Anika could have hit a winner to the front. She saw it and refused it. She played safe length once more, deep and heavy, to pin Leah behind her.

Shot thirty-nine came back high.

Leah tried a boast from a bad body position. The ball sat up in the middle. Anika stepped in and killed it straight. The ball cracked low, kissed the sidewall nick, and died.

Anika led 12-11.

She had match ball.

Leah delayed for the first time without pretending. She walked to the back wall, touched the bracelet on her racket throat, and exhaled through her teeth. When she turned, she looked older than fourteen and also exactly fourteen, all edges and effort.

On the first bounce, the scoreboard chirp flashed through her skull again. She palmed the ball, started over, and counted one, two, three, four until the sound in her head matched rubber on wood. She breathed out between her teeth, kept her eyes up, and served.

She served hard to the body. Leah got a racket on it but popped it loose. The rally went short, only eight shots, and each one carried the weight of fifty. Leah attacked first with a short ball that sat high. Anika lunged, dropped, recovered, and watched Leah scramble forward. Leah reached the ball and lifted it too crosscourt. Anika was already there. She punched a firm straight drive past Leah's shoulder.

Leah stretched, missed by inches, and slapped the floor once with her palm in frustration as the ball bounced dead.

She won it 13-11.

Three games to two.

Anika had a split second of silence after match ball, the kind that arrives before the room decides how to react. Then the bleachers clapped. Somebody whistled. The little brother with the shark lunchbox yelled, "Come on, Leah," too late and too loudly and then looked embarrassed.

Leah stood, breathed, and offered her hand through the sweat. "Good match," she said. No smile now.

"Good match," Anika said.

Leah added, quieter, "You didn't crack."

Anika did not know what answer belonged there. She squeezed once and let go.

She turned toward the door where Ravi was already stepping in, face lit with a pride so bright it almost looked like relief. "That is how you finish," he said, loud enough for the bleachers. "That is discipline."

The floor tipped under her feet.

She tried to swallow and got only saliva and acid. The smell of rubber and old sweat sharpened into something metallic. Her stomach folded hard, once, and she clamped her jaw shut because people were watching and she had just won and winners did not throw up on glass courts like babies.

The second heave hit harder than the first.

She made it three steps toward the side door before her body took the decision away. She dropped to one knee by the trash can and vomited in a hot, violent stream that splashed the rim, then the concrete, then her own left shoe. The force left strings of spit hanging from her lip. She coughed and retched again until there was nothing left but bitter foam and a burn in her throat that felt flayed.

The gym went quiet in pockets. A few people looked away out of politeness. A few did not.

Ravi crouched beside her immediately, one hand on her back between the shoulder blades, rubbing circles that were almost tender and almost impatient. "Breathe through your nose," he said. "Slow. Slow."

Mr. Salazar appeared with paper towels and set them beside the can. "Kid might need the medic station."

"She's okay," Ravi said before Anika could speak. "She overreached and still closed. That's all."

Mr. Salazar did not move. "She told you she was dizzy before game five. That's not nothing."

He thumbed his radio and spoke toward the desk without taking his eyes off Anika. "Front desk, Court Two. Minor athlete, post-match emesis with reported dizziness. Bring first-aid kit and incident log."

Anika spat into the can and wiped her mouth with the back of her wrist. Her hand shook so hard she had to use both hands to twist the water bottle open.

Leah hovered two steps away, towel around her neck, uncertain whether to stay. Her little brother had stopped swinging his legs. He watched Anika with the alert, scared stare children get when an adult body does something sudden and ugly.

"You want my electrolyte tabs?" Leah asked. "I've got lemon ones."

Before Anika answered, Ravi said, "We have what we need. Thank you."

Leah nodded once, then looked at Anika anyway. "You should still take one. Those help."

Anika managed, "I'm okay," though the words came out scraped.

Leah stayed put. She swallowed, looked at Mr. Salazar, and said, "I also ran my mouth in game two. About her mom and hospital sounds. Put that in the report too."

Mr. Salazar's jaw shifted. "I heard enough that I should've shut it down. That's on me."

The man in the park-district jacket came off the bleachers and stopped clapping. "If that's in play, it goes in writing."

Ravi took the paper towels and wiped the side of Anika's shoe as if cleaning mud after practice. "Look at me," he said.

She lifted her head.

"You won a five-game attritional match against an older player who tried to get in your head. You kept your length when your legs were cooked. You did exactly what we trained."

Her stomach cramped again. She pressed her fist below her ribs until it passed.

"Do you hear me?" he asked.

"Yeah."

"Tell me what held."

She swallowed. "I kept it deep and finished."

"And?"

"I still got back to the T."

"Exactly." He stood and addressed nobody and everybody at once, voice carrying toward the bleachers and the organizer's table. "This is what separates kids who have talent from kids who have spine. She played through dizziness and still executed."

The front-desk manager, Ms. Okafor, arrived with a red first-aid tote and a yellow carbon incident pad. She set a folding chair beside Anika and pointed to it. "Sit, please."

Ravi turned toward her. "We're doing a short cool-down and heading home."

"No, you're not," Ms. Okafor said, calm as a locked door. "She's a minor with dizziness plus emesis. Policy is twenty minutes monitored rest, fluids, orientation check, and guardian signature before release. If you leave early, I document unsafe departure and notify the ladder committee."

Ravi's mouth thinned. "She won the match."

Mr. Salazar answered before Ms. Okafor did. "Winning doesn't clear symptoms."

Anika sat on the folding chair with sour taste coating her tongue while Ms. Okafor clipped a pulse ox to her finger, counted respirations, and asked her name, date, and what court she was on. Her insides felt hollowed out, scraped clean with a spoon. She wanted someone to ask if she was scared when the beep hit. She wanted someone to say the words hospital monitor out loud, to prove the flash had been real and not weakness. Nobody asked that exact thing, but nobody pretended nothing had happened either.

Ms. Okafor handed her a paper cup. "Small sips. If that comes back up, tell me right away."

They did not do Ravi's ten-minute cool-down walk. They sat under the EXIT sign while two new players took Court Two and thudded balls against the front wall where she had just knelt. Mr. Salazar timed the interval on his phone and wrote two entries in the incident log: post-match vomiting with dizziness, and opponent conduct warning for comments about family bereavement and hospital noise.

At minute twenty, Ms. Okafor had Anika stand, walk six steps, turn, and walk back. "You're steady enough to go," she said, then looked at Ravi until he met her eyes. "No extra training tonight. If she vomits again, gets a headache, or seems confused, you take her to urgent care."

Ravi signed the yellow carbon form hard enough to tear the paper and folded the copy into his clipboard.

By the time they reached the bus stop, the usual bus had already come and gone.

Ravi stared at the route board, then said, quieter, "I know you felt bad out there."

She waited.

"But this is competitive sport," he continued. "No one gives points for feeling bad. They only remember the result."

There it was, the rule beneath all the other rules. He said it in his bus-depot voice, matter-of-fact, same tone he used for route changes and delay reports. It was not cruel. It was not kind. It was simply true in the way he believed truth worked: hard surfaces, no cushions.

She glanced down at Maya's watch. A smear of vomit had dried near the buckle. She rubbed it off with her thumb until the metal shone again.

"I remember," she said.

Ravi nodded, satisfied, and checked his clipboard though there was nothing left to check except the folded warning.

When the next bus finally hissed to the curb, the night had gone sharp with late-winter cold. Steam rose from a street grate and drifted around their ankles. Ravi texted someone from the ladder committee the final score: 11-8, 10-12, 6-11, 11-9, 13-11. He typed quickly, then showed Anika the screen before hitting send, as if numbers could pin the match in place and keep it from changing shape.

She watched the message go. Her body still felt raw and emptied, but the digits looked clean, objective, almost pretty.

On the ride home she sat by the window and counted each stop between the rec center and their building, pressing her thumbnail into the groove of Maya's watchstrap on every count so she would not drift back to the ICU beep. At seventy-fourth Street a woman boarded carrying grocery bags that smelled like cilantro and wet cardboard. At eighty-second, a man in a restaurant apron fell asleep standing up, forehead against the pole. It was a normal night on a normal bus with normal bodies.

Ravi stared straight ahead and said, without looking at her, "You move to rung six tomorrow morning."

"Okay."

"We start at five-thirty. Recovery jog, then ghosting. Short session."

Anika rested her head against the cold glass. "Okay."

The bus lurched, and acid rose in her throat again. She swallowed it back and kept counting stops.

When they climbed the apartment stairs, Ravi's old left ankle stiffened on the second landing, same as always. He caught the rail and hid the wince a beat too late. At their door he fumbled the key once, then got it on the second try.

Inside, the apartment smelled faintly of curry leaf and detergent. The kitchen light had been left on over the sink. Ravi set his clipboard on the table, set her racket beside it, and said, "Shower. Then half a banana, salt, and sleep."

She went to the bathroom, peeled off her shirt, and saw where the body shot had already purpled her hip. She touched the bruise and flinched.

In the mirror her face looked older and smaller at the same time. There was dried spit in the corner of her mouth she had missed. She cleaned it with warm water and stood there another minute listening to the pipes click inside the wall.

Winning had not made the monitor sound disappear.

Winning had turned it into training data.

She brushed her teeth, spit until the taste was almost gone, and came back to the kitchen in clean pajamas. Ravi had cut half a banana into exact coins and sprinkled salt like medicine. He slid the plate across without comment.

She ate. The salt stung her cracked lip. He watched the clock over her shoulder and wrote tomorrow's schedule on a torn envelope in block letters.

5:30 RUN + MOBILITY
6:00 GHOSTING (20)
6:30 BREAKFAST

He tore a strip of tape with his teeth and fixed the envelope by the fridge light switch, where she would see it at dawn.

Anika looked at the list and then at her own left hand, which still shook a little when she held it in the light. She tucked the hand under the table so he would not see.

"Good night," Ravi said.

"Night."

In bed she lay on her side and listened to the building settle: radiator hiss, distant TV laughter through the wall, pipes knocking twice. Every so often one of those sounds landed on the same frequency as the scoreboard chirp and her stomach clenched in answer.

She put two fingers on Maya's watch where it rested on the crate beside her mattress, not wearing it now, only touching the cool plastic edge. She counted fifty breaths the way she counted rally shots.

At breath thirty-seven she finally slept.
