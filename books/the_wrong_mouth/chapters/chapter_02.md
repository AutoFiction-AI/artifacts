# Chapter 2

Maren woke to a voice cataloguing the structural deficiencies of his ceiling.

*The primary beam has a lateral crack approximately two hand-spans in length. I would estimate six months before failure under current load. The thatch needs replacement along the southern face — there is daylight visible through the weave, which suggests water ingress during rain. There is also a bird's nest above the door frame. Swallow, I believe, though my ornithological training was limited.*

"Shut up," Maren said to his ceiling.

*Additionally, you sleep with your mouth open. I did not need to know that about you.*

The morning light came through the gaps in the thatch in thin bars, full of dust. Late summer light — the kind that promised heat by midday and kept its promise. Maren lay on his straw mattress and tried to piece together the walk home. The ditch. His vomit in the ditch. The Garsen family watching from the farmhouse door while he staggered upright and wiped his mouth and told them everything was fine, just the spiritual exertion, very normal, nothing to worry about. The long walk back to Fenhold with his hands shaking and his ceramic bowl tucked under his arm and a dead man explaining, in the patient tone of a quartermaster discovering weevils in the flour, exactly how many years of System data Maren had been living without.

Twenty-eight years. His entire life. Every notification, every quest prompt, every threat marker and skill update and ambient data stream that the rest of the world swam through from birth like fish in water — all of it, gone. Not dimmed or degraded but absent, as if someone had built a house and forgotten to install the windows.

He sat up. His body ached in places that made no sense — shoulders, the base of his skull, the tendons along both forearms. It was the deep, dragged soreness of hours spent hauling stone, though the heaviest thing he'd carried yesterday was a bowl that weighed less than a loaf of bread.

*The muscular soreness is consistent with fragment integration. My combat training is embedding into your motor pathways. You will find you have reflexes you did not possess yesterday. I would recommend a period of controlled physical activity to—*

"I'm going to wash my face," Maren said. "You don't need to narrate that."

*I am not narrating. I am providing operational context. The distinction is—*

"It's the same thing."

*It is not the same thing.*

Maren swung his legs off the mattress. The packed-dirt floor was cool under his bare feet — the clay walls held the night's coolness the way a cellar held preserves. His dwelling was one room. A sleeping pallet, a table he'd built himself seven years ago that listed slightly to the left and had never stopped listing, a shelf where the bowl sat next to the candles and the dried herb bundles he used as props. Everything in this room was a stage set. The ritual ash in its jar — ordinary fireplace ash, scooped from Derry's hearth. The binding cord on its hook — purchased from a rope vendor in the market and soaked in juniper water because it made the house smell like a practitioner's home. Eleven years of this. Eleven years of building a room that told a specific lie to anyone who stepped inside it.

He found the water bucket by the door. Still half full. He splashed his face, rubbed his eyes, pushed his wet fingers through his hair. The water smelled faintly of iron from the well.

*There is a System notification in your ambient field. Shall I read it?*

Maren stood there with water dripping from his jaw. Twenty-eight years of silence where the System should have been, and now there was a voice offering to fill it like a clerk reading appointments to a man who'd been asleep his whole life.

"No."

*It is a regional contamination advisory. Level two. The Garsen farmstead is flagged for residual quest activity — which, given that you performed a sham ritual over the body yesterday and the actual clearing was accomplished entirely by my arrival, is a detail that should concern you more than—*

"I said no."

Water ran off Maren's jaw and tapped the bucket rim four times.

*Your skill tree is remarkable.*

Maren pulled on his shirt — the same one from yesterday, stiff with dried sweat along the collar. He owned two. The other was drying on the line outside and probably stiff with yesterday's dried sweat too. "If this is another—"

*I am reading your System profile. Or rather, the part of it the System is willing to show cleanly. You are registered as a sin-eater. Class designation: active. Level seven. Specialization: consumption. These are the public entries visible to any System user who looks at you. The deeper record beneath them does not resolve properly. No progression data. No consumption log. No fragment integration history. No capacity scores. The public entries are tidy enough. Beneath them, whole sections stop. Headers remain. The supporting structure is gone.*

Maren stopped with one arm through a sleeve. The light caught the dust floating between his body and the far wall, motes turning slow in the morning warmth.

"How do you know what my profile looks like to other people?"

*I can see both layers. The System presents a public face and maintains an architecture behind it. Most people see only the face. My order trained us to read deeper. Veil-sigil holders were — are —* The correction came back tight, each word dragged into place. *We were trained in architectural reading. Interface diagnostics. Your public profile says sin-eater. Under it, the record misaligns. Sections refuse to render. It does not resemble any healthy record I was taught to read.*

An image pushed into Maren's mind — not his own, but from a place behind his own thoughts. A silver sigil on dark armor, the shape of an eye half-veiled. The weight of it against a chest that wasn't his. The cool metal through a linen undertunic, the way it shifted when you moved your sword arm. The pride and the responsibility and the specific, quiet satisfaction of wearing a symbol that meant you were trusted with things most people didn't know existed.

Maren shook it loose. The memory faded like heat off a stone, slow and residual.

*There is something else. Your consciousness does not interact with the System in the manner I would expect from documented System-blindness. The Order kept records of the condition — rare, perhaps one in a hundred thousand births. In those cases, the profiles present as degraded. Partial rendering. Reduced functionality. Entries blur or fail when queried. Yours does not present as damaged. The public layer resolves cleanly enough, then the deeper pattern thins and shifts when examined. That combination is —*

He stopped. The sentence hit a wall and dropped.

"That's awfully specific," Maren said.

*It is an observation.*

"From less than a day in my skull."

*The integration process is disorienting. My thoughts are not always well-ordered. I will rephrase: your profile presents an anomaly that my order would have considered significant. That is all.*

Maren dragged the shirt down over his ribs. Aldric left him with dust in the light and the creak of the shelf.

He pulled the shirt the rest of the way on and tucked it into his trousers. The bowl was on the shelf where he'd left it last night. Ordinary ceramic, brown-glazed, a chip on the rim from five years ago when he'd dropped it packing up after the Halversen ritual. He'd carried it for eleven years and it had never done anything except hold water and ash and the occasional handful of dried rosemary. He left it where it was.

He ate cold porridge from last night's pot, standing at his listing table. The porridge had set into something you could probably use to fill masonry cracks. He ate it anyway, scraping the pot with a wooden spoon, because food was food and he'd spent eleven years in a village that paid him just enough to eat poorly.

*Do you always eat standing up?*

"When the chair's got a broken leg, yeah."

*The chair has been broken for how long?*

"Couple of years."

*And you have not repaired it because—*

"Because I don't know how to fix chairs. I fake sin-eating. Different skill set."

*Clearly.*

The morning outside was warm and bright and full of small village sounds — a rooster that didn't know when to stop, someone hammering something wooden, the distant complaint of livestock. Maren stood in his doorway and let the sun hit his face. It was the kind of morning where everything looked like it always had — the packed-dirt path to the square, the leaning fence around Tam's goat yard, the scorch marks on the stone wall of the grain store where a hero patrol had tested some ability months ago and nobody had cleaned it up because nobody ever cleaned those things up. They just accumulated, a record of careless power that the village stepped around the way you stepped around horse shit.

He started walking.

*Threat assessment for this area: negligible. No active hostiles within standard scan range. Ambient contamination levels: low overall, concentrated near the eastern woodline and the Garsen property. Regional hero activity: one patrol, ranger-class, moving northeast along the main road at approximately four miles' distance. They are not heading toward this village.*

"I didn't ask."

*You did not need to. Morning briefing was standard procedure in my order. Situational awareness for the operating environment. It was — this was something I did every day.*

Maren let the rooster and the hammering fill the gap. He didn't push it.

Fenhold's square was just a widening of the main path where three tracks met — a well, a bench, old Poul's horse trough, and enough open dirt for a dozen people to stand around in without bumping elbows. It was already occupied by the low commerce of late summer. Grenna was shaking a rug over her fence, beating it with a wooden paddle in steady, punishing strokes. Poul was leading his carthorse toward the trough, the animal's hooves raising clouds of fine dust. Two children ran between the houses, changing direction at nothing, stopping at nothing, reaching for things Maren couldn't see.

He'd never been able to see it. Twenty-eight years. And he'd never stopped watching the people who could.

Grenna paused mid-swing to glance at something above the roofline. Her eyes tracked left, held, then returned to the rug. A notification. Something in the System's constant ambient chatter had caught her attention for half a second before she dismissed it. Poul's horse balked at the trough and Poul's gaze flicked to a spot about three feet to the left of the animal's head — a threat marker, maybe, or a quest flag, though threat markers near a village water trough weren't usually worth the flinch. The children's path made no sense unless you understood they were following invisible waypoints, their trajectories determined by prompts only they could see.

Maren had learned this language the way a deaf man learns to read lips. Not the System itself, but the shadows it cast on other people's faces. The flinches and glances and micro-pauses. Where people looked when a notification appeared. How long they looked. Whether they smiled or frowned or dismissed it. He'd built a career on reading those shadows — matching his responses to what a blind man should do, should notice, should miss.

"Morning, Maren." Grenna's voice carried the particular half-pitch-up that people used with him. Warmer than normal, slightly louder. The voice you'd use with a child or someone recovering from an illness. "Watch the path by the bench, love — Poul's been spilling feed again."

There was no spilled feed. Maren knew this because Poul hadn't come from the feed store and because Grenna's eyes, when she said it, were focused on a point near the ground about two feet left of the bench — a System marker of some kind that her brain had translated, automatically, into a physical hazard she could warn a blind man about. She'd been doing this for years. The whole village had. A quiet, uncoordinated conspiracy of small translations. Every puddle that was really a quest residue. Every "broken step" that was really a scorch mark. Every "careful, the sun's in your eyes" that really meant a notification was flashing in the space between Maren and whoever he was talking to.

*She is lying to protect you,* Aldric said. *The marker is a minor resource node. Copper-grade. Barely worth collecting. She has translated it into a physical obstacle because she assumes you cannot process System information and she does not want you to appear confused in public.*

"I know," Maren muttered, quiet enough that Grenna wouldn't catch it. He smiled at her, stepped carefully around the nothing by the bench. "Thanks, Grenna."

*You are remarkably skilled at this.*

He kept walking. He wasn't sure whether that was meant as admiration or diagnosis.

*Both,* Aldric said.

"Stay out of my thoughts."

*I cannot. Your thoughts and my awareness share the same — the architecture is not partitioned. I hear what surfaces in your mind the way you hear what I say. It is not a choice I would make either, believe me.*

The walk to the well was a gauntlet of translations. Tam shouted from his goat yard about a "soft patch" in the road that was actually a low-grade contamination residue Maren could smell but couldn't see — that dry, faintly sour tang with a metal edge, the one he'd always assumed was normal, the background stink of living near a dungeon mouth. Hella waved him away from her doorstep with something about spilled wash-water, which was actually a scorch mark that had warped the packed earth into a shallow depression. Each interaction required Maren to play two roles simultaneously: the blind man who relied on their kindness, and the fraud who knew the kindness was based on a misunderstanding he'd cultivated for over a decade.

*Skill assessment,* Aldric said, unprompted. *I have been reviewing the details of your registered capabilities. Your listed skills include: Consumption, rank three. Contamination sensing, rank two. Ritual binding, rank one. Fragment dissolution, rank one. These are — I should note — the minimum ratings consistent with an active sin-eater designation. They appear to be automatically assigned defaults rather than earned progressions. You have gained no experience in any sin-eating skill in eleven years of ostensible practice.*

"Are you going to read my entire file to me?"

*I already have. That was the entire file. Four skills, minimum rank, no progression. Behind the entries, the architectural data remains as I described. Thin. Incomplete. Your profile has the depth of a door painted on plaster. It suggests access without offering it.*

Maren reached the well. He lowered the bucket, the hemp rope rough and familiar in his hands. The splash echoed up the shaft, cool and distant.

"Useful comparison," he said. "Thanks."

*The comparison is accurate, not useful. There is a difference, though I concede the distinction may not matter to you.*

He hauled the bucket up and drank from it, cold water against his dry mouth, the iron tang of the well on his tongue. Around him the village was doing what it always did — Grenna beating the rug, the children chasing invisible objectives, Poul's horse finally drinking from the trough now that whatever marker had spooked it had either been dismissed or moved. The hum was there too, as it always was, low and constant near the eastern woodline where the contamination ran densest. A sound Maren had lived with his whole life without understanding what it was. The System's background noise, the operating frequency of the infrastructure that organized everyone's existence except his.

He'd always heard it. He just hadn't known what he was hearing.

---

The Wollen house was on the west side of the village, set back from the path behind a vegetable garden that was doing better than the garden deserved. Maren had visited three months ago to clear the contamination after the old woman died in her bed. He'd performed his usual ritual — the bowl, the ash, the murmured approximation of words that sounded appropriately solemn — and collected twelve coppers and left the contamination exactly where it was. This morning one of Britt's children had come to fetch him because the cat was back to crouching by the rear alcove and screaming at nothing, and because Maren had told Britt to send for him if the clearing ever felt unsettled before harvest. Follow-up visits made him sound thorough. Sometimes they earned him a few extra coppers.

The daughter, Britt, met him at the door. She was forty and had the raw-knuckled hands of someone who washed things for a living — laundry, dishes, floors, the body of her dead mother. She'd hired Maren because the contamination had started making the cat behave oddly and she'd heard, correctly, that residual quest energy could affect animals before it affected people.

"Maren." She smiled the smile of someone who wanted to believe good news. "Come in, come in. I just need to know if it's settled this time."

He stepped inside. The house was clean, sparse, smelled like soap and boiled cabbage and, underneath, the dry-sour taint of old contamination, settled into the place so long Britt probably registered it as weather.

*Residual contamination, grade three,* Aldric said. *Concentrated at the shrine in the rear alcove but with lateral spread into the sleeping area and the food storage. The occupants — I count four individuals registered at this address — have been sleeping in low-grade contamination for approximately ninety days. Prolonged exposure at this level correlates with fatigue, joint pain, mood disturbance, and in young children—*

"Everything looks clean," Maren told Britt. "The clearing took well. She's at rest."

Britt's face did something that Maren had seen a hundred times and had never learned to watch without a specific, localized shame in his chest. Relief. Not the small relief of a resolved inconvenience but the deep, structural relief of someone who had been frightened for her mother's soul and had just been told by a professional that the fear was over. Her eyes went wet. She blinked it back.

"Thank you," she said. "Really. Maren, thank you."

She pressed three coppers into his hand. He took them.

*The contamination in this house has been building for three months,* Aldric said. *It will continue to build. The cat she mentioned is likely already symptomatic. In six to eight months the youngest occupant — there is a child registered here, age four — will begin showing early signs of exposure. Fatigue. Night terrors. You are taking money from this woman for work you did not do, and her family will suffer the consequences of your failure in specific, measurable ways.*

Maren closed his fingers around the coppers. They were warm from Britt's hand.

"Thank you, Britt. You take care."

He walked home. The afternoon heat was building — the air thick and still, the dust on the path warm through his boot soles. The drier, sourer stink of old contamination thickened near the Garsen edge of the village, with a sharper ozone edge drifting off the eastern woodline, and the hum was louder there too, a low vibration that sat in his teeth. He'd walked this path a thousand times and he'd always noticed the smell and the hum and he'd always let them slide past because they were just the way things were, the background texture of a world he couldn't fully perceive.

He sat on the bench outside his door and put the three coppers on the wood beside him. They sat there, small and warm and damning.

*I will refrain from further comment on that transaction,* Aldric said. *My opinion is, I believe, adequately established.*

"Yeah."

Maren worried a splinter loose from the bench. The wood had gone hot under his palm. A fly landed on his arm and scrubbed its legs together while the three coppers sat between them like a second set of eyes. Morning had been bickering. This was worse.

"How long were you with the Order?" he asked. Not because he wanted to know. Because if he kept staring at Britt's coppers he'd hear her thanking him again.

*Fourteen years. I was commissioned at twenty-one. Standard path — field postings, dungeon surveys, System-anomaly investigation. I reached senior investigator at twenty-eight.* The fly lifted from Maren's arm and settled on the bench slat. *I was thirty-five when I died.*

"How?"

*A dungeon collapsed. Survey mission. Routine — a minor anomaly in the sub-architecture, the kind of entry we catalogued by the dozen. The collapse was instantaneous. No structural warning. No System alert.*

"Aren't there supposed to be warnings?"

*The System monitors structural integrity in active dungeon environments. A collapse without advance alert is either a catastrophic sensor failure or—* The wall again. He hit it mid-thought and the thought stopped. *The investigation classified it as structural failure. The inquiry lasted two days.*

"Two days."

*Two days. For a senior investigator. My order submitted six requests for extended review. All denied. The case was filed and the file was sealed and my name was added to the memorial wall in the eastern chapel and that was — that was the end of Aldric Venn.*

The full name landed in the space between them. Maren hadn't known it until now. Aldric Venn. A real name, the kind of name that had been spoken by real people in real rooms — shouted across training yards, murmured in evening prayers, written in reports and sealed in files. A name with weight.

"I'm sorry," Maren said. Then, because the sincerity startled him and he didn't know what to do with it: "That's shit. I mean — two days? That's really shit."

*Your capacity for eulogy is noted.*

"Did you have — was there anyone? Family, or—"

Maren bent the splinter until it snapped off in his thumbnail. The dog by the eastern woodline barked twice. Aldric didn't answer until the second bark died.

*I had my order,* Aldric said. The formality was still there, but it dragged in places now, threadbare at the elbows. *I had duties. I had a barracks room with a window that faced east, and every morning the light came through at an angle that struck the far wall at the hour of first prayers. I maintained my own sword because I did not trust anyone else with the edge. I had — there was a woman in the commissary who remembered how I took my tea. Every morning. Without being asked. I never learned her name.*

Maren's hands stopped on the bench.

*I have thought about that more in the last day than seems reasonable. Her name. I did not think to ask it in fourteen years, and now I cannot stop — she had short hair. Brown. She poured the tea left-handed. That is what I have. That is everything I have of her. I had morning prayers. I had evening reports. I had the weight of my own body and the feeling of cold water on my face after training and the way bread tasted when you had earned it through physical work, and I did not appreciate any of it, I did not understand that these things were — that they could be taken, that everything could be—*

The last word snagged and vanished. Maren's own throat tightened around nothing. When Aldric tried again, the voice came back scraped raw, too stripped to hide behind sarcasm or protocol.

Maren sat on his bench in the late summer heat with three coins beside him and no idea what to say. The dead paladin wasn't a client. There was no angle here, no version of events that would make this better, no deflection smooth enough to redirect grief this specific.

"I don't know what to — I'm not good at this part," Maren said. His voice sounded wrong to him. Too plain. Not enough.

*That is the first honest thing you have said in my presence.*

"Yeah, well."

The fly had come back. It landed on one of the coppers and Maren watched it navigate the surface of the coin, its legs picking across the stamped face of whoever was important enough to be on a copper. The dog near the eastern woodline was barking again — it had been barking all day, probably at the contamination that sat in the trees like a smell the animal couldn't get away from.

*I am not asking for your sympathy,* Aldric said. The words came back squared off again, each one set down carefully. *I am stating facts. I had a life. It was taken. I am now — here. And I cannot leave. I have tried. The architecture does not permit separation. I am embedded in your consciousness the way — the metaphor escapes me. I am here. That is the operational reality.*

"Okay."

*Okay is not a response to what I have just told you.*

"It's the only one I've got."

Maren turned one of Britt's coppers under his thumb. The stamped face had worn nearly smooth.

*There is a quest notification for the eastern farmsteads,* Aldric said eventually. His voice had resettled into its usual register — formal, clipped, the voice of a man filing a report to an order that no longer existed. *Harvest season. The contamination will need clearing before the grain comes in.*

"Yeah."

*You cannot do it. You do not know how.*

"I know."

*The people in this village have trusted you for eleven years. They are sleeping in contamination you were paid to remove. The Wollen family is—*

"I know."

He didn't say it angrily. The words came out flat, worn thin from use before he'd even said them.

The fly stepped off the copper and lifted into the heat. Aldric didn't follow it with another word.

The sun had dropped low enough to warm Maren's boots through the leather. The three coppers sat on the bench between his hand and his thigh.

"Tomorrow," Maren said. "You tell me how to clear the eastern farms."

*You are agreeing to act?*

"I'm agreeing to listen. Don't make it bigger than it is."

Heat soaked through his boots before Aldric answered.

*Very well.*
