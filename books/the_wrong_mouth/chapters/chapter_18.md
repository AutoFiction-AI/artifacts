# Chapter 18

The hum was quieter on this side of the valley.

Not gone — the hum was never gone, not since Fenhold, not since the first morning he could remember hearing it — but stretched thin, like water across too much stone. Three days past the Woundmouth and Maren's body still hadn't forgiven him. His knees ground when he bent them. The muscles along his ribs ached in a deep, wrung-out way, as if someone had reached inside his trunk and twisted everything a quarter turn to the left. But his hands were steady. That was new.

The road — what passed for a road this deep, where dungeon territory gave way to farmland and the path widened from packed dirt to actual ruts — had scorch marks in the earth. Old ones, layered dark over darker, from heroes who'd passed through seasons ago. Nobody cleaned them. Nobody ever cleaned them. Early autumn had crept in while he wasn't paying attention, and the light hit those burn marks differently now, cooler, less shimmer and more of a pale, flat quality that made the road look tired.

*The contamination index has dropped significantly since your... intervention,* Aldric said. He paused on "intervention," and Maren braced out of habit for the insult that never came. *The residual levels in this stretch are within normal seasonal parameters.*

"So it worked."

*I believe I just said that.*

"You said 'contamination index' and 'seasonal parameters.' I said 'it worked.'"

A pause. Then, quieter: *Yes. It worked.*

Maren looked down the road. Easier than answering that.

The early-autumn air sat in the back of his throat with a crispness that wasn't unpleasant — just present, like a taste he couldn't quite place. The fields on either side of the road had been harvested recently. Cut stubble scratched at his ankles where the road narrowed, and the smell was turned earth and drying grain and, underneath it, the faint sweet-ozone note of contamination residue that had been sitting too long in the soil.

*There's quest-light pooling in the drainage channel to your right,* Aldric said. *Several weeks of accumulation. No active quest attached — just residue that hasn't been cleared.*

Maren hadn't asked. He stepped around the ditch anyway.

The road crested a low hill and the valley opened up — farmland, a scatter of buildings in the middle distance, the dark mouth of a dungeon visible on the far ridgeline like a bruise against the tree line. Quest-light pooled around it, brighter than the residual traces in the ditches, and even from this distance the hum thickened. Someone would need to clear it eventually.

Maybe him.

The thought still had a quality of absurdity to it. Like wearing shoes that fit but belonged to someone else.

*Your stride has improved,* Aldric noted. *Less compensatory tilting. The muscular damage from the consumption appears to be resolving.*

"How long before the rest goes?"

*Unknown. The Woundmouth has no precedent in my records.*

Maren kept walking until he found a place to sit.

Maren sat on a flat stone at the roadside to eat. The bread was stale — two days old, bought from a farming woman who'd sold it to him without looking at his face, which was either generosity or indifference, and Maren had been grateful for either. His jaw worked at the crust. Crumbs fell on his shirt and he brushed them into the dirt.

Derry would have had better bread. Derry always had better bread — baked by Lenna, or bought fresh because he couldn't pass a baker's stall without stopping and couldn't pass a cow without offering an opinion on its quality. It was a stupid little impulse, and it had never stopped being funny.

The thought arrived and sat there, like the ache in his ribs. Not sharp anymore. Not even unpleasant, exactly. Just present.

During the three days since the Woundmouth, he'd kept worrying at the thought, testing where it changed from sore to sharp. Derry had told Caul their direction. Not their location, not their camp, not anything that would have gotten Maren killed immediately — just the direction. Toward the Crook. Enough for Caul to narrow the search. Enough for people to die.

But also: Derry had a daughter learning to count, a wife with first-year tomatoes in the ground, a cow that kept pushing through the fence. Around campfires and on long roads, he'd kept bringing them up — the counting, the tomatoes, the damned cow. He named those ordinary pieces over and over, maybe because repetition helped keep the world from tipping. And then he'd done the math Maren couldn't argue with. His daughter needed a father more than Maren needed a friend.

The math was right. Maren could see it clearly — he'd always been good at seeing what other people were feeling, it was the foundation skill of his entire fraudulent career — and the math was right.

He still couldn't forgive it. Maybe later. Maybe not.

He finished the bread, brushed his hands on his trousers, and stood up. His knees complained. The road went on.

* * *

Thessa was sitting on a low wall at the edge of a settlement Maren hadn't been able to see from the road. The village was small — a handful of stone buildings clustered around a well, a livestock pen, the low walls that marked property in this part of the country. She was eating an apple. Her stained hands made the fruit look very white.

"You're alive," she said. Not a question. An assessment.

"Apparently."

She gave him the usual inspection, moving from face to hands to stance to feet, looking for failure.

"Your feet are wrong."

"My feet."

"You're favoring your left side. The Woundmouth consumption pulled everything right — the asymmetry's contamination-patterned. Correct it or you'll be limping inside a week."

"Thanks."

"It wasn't a compliment." She took another bite of her apple. The crunch was very loud in the morning quiet. "Barren Rill's east. Mid-grade spill. Start behind the house; it's already in the well line. Their sin-eater died in spring. Two children in the house. Dead creek out back. You can clear it."

Everything from Thessa arrived already stripped to function: report first, implication built in, no room left for discussion.

"That's the first one, then," Maren said.

She finished the apple and tossed the core over the wall into someone's garden. "It's the first paying job. There are three more sites between here and Silt. I'll strip the two nearest. You take Barren Rill and the one past it." She stood up, wiping her hands on her trousers. The apple juice left a streak on the dark fabric. "Ten days. Meet me in Silt if your insides stay where they belong."

*She's not the most encouraging teacher I've encountered,* Aldric said. *The Order of the Veil had a man who once told me my prayer posture made him question whether I had a spine. He was gentler.*

Maren didn't answer him. He watched Thessa hook the bag higher on her shoulder.

"So that's it," he said.

"What were you expecting? A ceremony?" She slung the bag over her shoulder. "You're a sin-eater. There's contamination. Go eat it."

She set off down the lane without looking back.

*That was an acknowledgment,* Aldric said.

"That was work."

*From her, yes.*

Maren listened to her boots fade over stone. Barren Rill. One site past it. Silt in ten days.

"Barren Rill first," he said.

*East, then north,* Aldric said. *I'll keep you off the wrong road.*

He started walking.

* * *

Barren Rill was exactly what it sounded like — a thin settlement strung along a creek bed that had mostly dried to mud, fed by runoff from the hills where a dungeon mouth sat, old and leaking quest-light into the groundwater. Behind the farmstead's main house, the contamination sat in the hardpack as a faint shimmer, raw quest-light without any of the neat labels Aldric could pull from it.

The farmer's name was Reth. Stocky man, weather-beaten, with the cautious courtesy of someone who'd been told a sin-eater was coming and wasn't sure whether to be grateful or afraid. He had calloused hands and a habit of rubbing the back of his neck when he talked, as if the words had to be physically pushed out. Two children — a boy of maybe seven who stood behind his father's leg, and a girl younger than that, hiding behind her brother's shoulder and peering at Maren with the frank evaluation of someone who hadn't yet learned to pretend she wasn't staring.

"It's been three seasons," Reth said. He stood with his arms folded, the posture of a man protecting something without knowing how. "Our Dalla — she was our sin-eater, my wife's aunt — she passed in the spring and nobody came after. We sent word. Twice."

"I'm here now," Maren said.

*The contamination profile is mid-grade, consistent with approximately nine months of accumulation,* Aldric reported. *Concentrated behind the house, with secondary spread into the well-water table and the eastern field. No fragment signatures — the contamination is inert, not hosted. A standard clearing.*

"Standard clearing," Maren repeated aloud, for Reth's benefit.

"What's that mean?"

"Means it'll take a few hours. I'll need the area behind the house. Somewhere to sit."

Reth studied him. Maren knew the sequence by now: Is this person real? Can he do what he says he can? Is he worth the fee? He'd always passed that test through performance. The confident posture, the right words, the practiced gravity of a man doing spiritual work. Now he didn't bother. He just stood there with his worn pack and his stained ceramic bowl visible through the open flap, his grinding knees and his unwashed hair, and let the man see what he was getting.

"You look young for this," Reth said.

"I am."

Something in that — the simplicity of it, maybe, the lack of the expected reassurance — made Reth uncross his arms. "Right. Well. There's a bench out back. I'll show you."

The bench was a plank across two stumps, set against the rear wall of the farmhouse where the stone held the day's warmth. The contamination pool started six feet in front of it and spread across the hardpack toward the creek bed. This close, it had its own smell — the sweet-ozone note of quest residue, older and more sour than fresh contamination, with a metallic undertone that sat on the tongue. Nine months of accumulation. Three seasons of slow poison leaching into the water, into the fields, into the soil where the children played.

Maren set the bowl on the bench beside him and sat down. The ceramic was cool against his thigh through the fabric. An ordinary bowl — bought in the Fenhold market when he was seventeen, chosen for its weight and how it sat in his hands, how the glaze caught light in a way that looked ceremonial to people who didn't know better. No System properties. No resonance. Just fired clay with a chip on the rim that he could feel with his thumb. He'd performed the fake ritual with this bowl hundreds of times. He'd performed the real one with it — the separation, with Vess. The supervised attempts at the Crook with Thessa barking corrections. The Woundmouth, which had been something else entirely, something that defied the word "ritual" the way a flood defied the word "rain."

This would be the first time it was just work.

*Shall I walk you through the contamination boundaries?* Aldric said.

"Go ahead."

*The primary pool extends approximately two meters from your position to seven meters at its widest, tapering east toward the creek. Moderate depth — the contamination has penetrated roughly half a meter into the soil. The composition is agricultural: grain residue, livestock-adjacent contamination byproducts.* A pause. *Nothing exotic.*

"Nothing exotic," Maren repeated. The comedy of a dead paladin delivering contamination flavor profiles as though reading a wine list was not lost on him. He picked up the bowl.

*Maren.*

"What?"

A beat. *The family is watching from the kitchen window.*

He'd known. He could hear them — Reth's weight shifting on the floorboards, the children's breathing, the younger girl whispering something her brother shushed. Every family watched like this: faces at the window, breath held, that tight hungry stillness that meant clear it, take it, make the yard safe enough for children again.

All those years, he'd taken their money and left. The contamination had stayed. The residue had seeped. Children had grown up breathing it. Back in Silt, Thessa had laid all of that out in her dry working voice, one fact after another, and the anger in it had been worse for how little she decorated it.

He held the bowl in both hands and closed his eyes.

Closing his eyes wasn't about sight. The yard and the shimmer were plain enough. What he'd never had was the useful layer, the part that told everyone else what any of it meant. Still, it marked a handover: bread, road, and Derry on one side, the ground opening under his hands on the other.

Thessa had taught him the real approach sequence in three sentences. *Open the channel. Name the contamination. Let it enter.* She'd delivered it with the impatience of someone explaining how to scrub a pot. The rest he'd learned badly: how loose his chest had to go, how lightly to hold the bowl, when to breathe. He'd vomited in the grass, started over, and listened to Thessa tell him to stop forcing the pull and let the contamination come on its own.

He opened the channel.

The contamination answered.

It rose through the ground in cold waves. His feet first, then his hands on the bowl, then the skin of his forearms where his sleeves were rolled to the elbow. Nine months of accumulated quest residue, the slow poison of spiritual waste left to fester, entering the one kind of person who could take it in and break it down.

It hurt. Not the scouring agony of the Woundmouth — not the sensation of having the architecture of the world forced through his chest. A working hurt. The ache of sustained effort, the burn of muscles doing what they were built for after years of disuse, the particular discomfort of holding himself open when every instinct said to close.

*Contamination entering the consumption field,* Aldric said, low in Maren's head, the words settling instead of striking. *Dissolution rate is good. Better than your last practice attempt. The residue is breaking down cleanly.*

"How much?" Maren's voice came out thick. The contamination sat in his mouth like a taste — grain and old iron and something animal underneath, the ghost of dead quests that had seeped into this farmer's soil and sat there waiting.

*Approximately a third of the pool has cleared. The water table is already starting to recede.* Aldric was quiet for a moment. *Their sin-eater's layering is still visible in the residue. She kept this contained for years.*

He meant Dalla.

"Yeah," Maren said. "She did."

The consumption settled into a rhythm. Maren sat on the plank bench with the bowl in his hands and the autumn sun on the back of his neck and the contamination moving through him like a slow tide, and he broke it down. Layer by layer, the accumulated months of neglect dissolving through his body and out the other side into something the ground could absorb. The process had no drama to it. No spectacle, no interface fireworks, no light show that the family at the window could watch and be reassured by. Just a man sitting very still with a ceramic bowl, breathing slowly, while the bad thing in the ground got smaller.

At the halfway mark, the girl — the younger one — came out the back door and sat on the steps. Her brother hissed at her from inside but she didn't move. She had dirt on her knees and was holding a piece of bread with a bite taken out of it.

"Is it working?" she asked.

*She's approximately four,* Aldric said. *Brown dress, let down at the hem twice. She has what appears to be jam on her chin.*

"Yeah," Maren said. "It's working."

"Can you see it going away?"

"The shimmer, some," Maren said. And then, because the question deserved honesty and honesty was the only thing he had left that was worth anything: "Not the useful part. He tells me when it's done."

*A severe simplification,* Aldric said.

The girl considered this for a while, chewing her bread. "That's funny," she said, not laughing, and went back inside.

*She's right,* Aldric said. *It is.*

The last of the contamination broke apart just as the sun moved behind the ridgeline and the air turned properly cold — the kind of cold that meant the season had genuinely shifted, that summer's long hold had loosened and wouldn't tighten again until spring. Maren sat with the empty bowl in his hands and the clean ground in front of him and the water moving back toward the well with nothing riding in it, and he was tired. Not the system-shocking, bone-deep collapse of the Woundmouth. Just tired. The way a body gets tired after a day of physical work — muscles used, energy spent, the simple animal accounting of effort and cost.

Reth came out when it was done. He stood at the edge of where the shimmer had been — where the packed dirt was just packed dirt now, holding nothing worse than the memory of a dry summer — and his face did something complicated that Maren couldn't read from this distance but could hear in the way his breathing changed.

"My aunt used to do this," Reth said. "Dalla. I watched her when I was small. She'd sit for hours, just like that."

"Yeah."

"She never said much about it after. Just went and washed her hands and ate something."

"That sounds about right." Maren stood up. His knees registered their objection. He let them.

"What do I owe you?"

The question every family had asked since he first started carrying the bowl. He'd always had a number ready — rehearsed, itemized, calculated his first week on the job for maximum plausibility. A practiced fee for a practiced fraud. The numbers came to him automatically and he let them pass.

"Whatever you can manage," Maren said. "I'm not particular."

Reth gave him six coppers and a meal. The meal was better than the money — stew with actual meat in it and bread that hadn't gone stale, eaten at the farmer's table while the children watched and the girl asked him three more questions about whether the shimmer looked different up close, whether the bowl mattered, whether the water would taste strange now. A little, yes, and it shouldn't. She seemed satisfied by the consistency of his answers if nothing else. The boy didn't speak but put an extra piece of bread on Maren's plate when his father wasn't looking, which was its own kind of sentence.

*The contamination field has fully dissolved,* Aldric said as Maren cleaned the bowl at the well outside. The water was cold against his hands, clean-smelling for the first time in nine months, tasting of nothing but iron and stone when it splashed his wrist. *No residual trace. The groundwater readings are returning to pre-accumulation levels. The eastern field should be safe for planting by spring.*

"Good."

*The next contamination site on Thessa's list is eight miles north. A crossroads settlement. Minor pooling, no fragment signatures.* A pause. *I'll walk you through the route when you're ready.*

"I'm ready."

He dried his hands on his trousers and put the bowl in his pack. The ceramic clinked against the water skin, the stale bread he'd saved from morning, the six coppers — the small collection of objects that constituted his entire professional inventory. The girl was in the doorway, waving hard enough to flap her sleeve. He lifted a hand back before she could decide he was being rude.

The road north was quiet. Early-autumn dark came on faster than summer dark, and the light had already gone gold at the edges. Stones in the wall threw their own shadows. Each rut in the road stayed visible until the dark swallowed it. The hum threaded through the air, constant as ground under his boots. It was part of the architecture of a world built on top of something older than itself, and it needed ordinary, unglamorous, underpaid people to keep the seams from splitting open.

*Eight miles,* Aldric said. *There will be a cairn where the road divides. Take the branch that leaves the sheep wall at your left shoulder.*

"So the marsh side."

*No. The other one. I am trying to preserve what little confidence Thessa has misplaced in you.*

Maren's mouth twitched sideways. It wasn't quite a smile. It was near enough.

He kept north with the bowl tapping against his back. Behind him, Barren Rill settled into evening: clear well, clean yard, children loud enough to hear from the road. Ahead, the hum thickened somewhere past the cairn. For eleven years the bowl had gone from yard to yard and left the rot in the ground. Something had answered him once, in a dead farmer's house. At the fork, he took the branch Aldric meant and kept walking.
