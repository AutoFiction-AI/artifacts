He's performing a sin-eating ritual over a dead farmer when something answers — a problem, because he's been faking these for eleven years and he is System-blind. Everyone else sees the interface. He sees nothing. What hits him is everything at once — the hero's combat training, the hero's memories, and the hero himself: a paladin, horrified to find out where he's ended up. The paladin's first act is to try to make him kneel and pray. His first act is to throw up in a ditch. The paladin starts grudgingly reading him the interface he's never seen — skill descriptions, threat levels, quest details — delivered like a man reading a grocery list at a state funeral. He's been blind his whole life and now he has the worst possible tour guide. The System notices him for the first time and sends a bounty far too large for one eaten quest, and heroes are coming. He eats the next quest blind, gets the order wrong, and gets an assassin who is livid about sharing a skull with a paladin. The botched consumption scrambles something — when the two fragments collide, what comes out has no System classification, and for the first time the paladin has nothing sarcastic to say about it. What he wants, once the running stops, is to stop being a fraud. He's faked everything for eleven years and the first real thing he's ever done is killing people. He has to know why he was born blind, what the System fears, and why the paladin has gone quiet about what he sees when he looks at the System's bones. In a world where quest-light pools outside dungeon mouths and heroes leave scorch marks on roads no one cleans, he is the first thing in a long time to make the System flinch, and he can't even see it happen — but the paladin can, and the paladin is afraid.
