# Chapter 10

The road past Harrow turned to packed earth and stayed that way. No scorch marks out here — the heroes went the other direction, toward the dungeon mouths, and they left their damage there. Maren's feet had been swollen since yesterday and the cooler morning air was the first mercy in a week, settling into the backs of his hands like water. Late summer letting go. He could feel the season turning in the way the shadows fell longer and earlier, the grass along the road going from green to something brittle and pale at the tips.

Derry hadn't said much since they'd run from Harrow. Not nothing — he'd offered Maren some dried meat at dawn, asked if the road felt right, made a half-observation about the clouds that he didn't finish. But the chatter was gone. The Derry who filled every silence with stories about Pell's counting lessons and Lenna's tomato plants was walking beside Maren now like a man saving his words for something he hadn't decided to say yet.

*Two figures on the ridge. North side. Half a mile back.*

Vess, calibrating threats. She'd been doing it since Harrow — quiet updates delivered with the flat precision of someone checking instruments.

*Hero-class?* Maren thought.

*Farmers. Hay rakes. Not a concern.*

Aldric hadn't spoken all morning. When the paladin went quiet, it meant he was either seeing something in the System's architecture that he didn't know how to name or composing a lecture so thorough it required structural planning. Both demanded silence, and both usually ended with Maren feeling worse about himself.

They came around a bend where the road widened into a clearing — someone had cut back the brush on both sides, maybe for a seasonal market that nobody used anymore, the stumps sawed low and gone grey — and the man was sitting at the clearing's far edge. Sitting, not standing. His hands on his knees, empty.

Maren stopped.

Derry almost walked into him, then looked where Maren was looking, and his hand went to Maren's elbow. Not to guide him. To hold on.

The man wore ranger gear that had seen real weather — leather darkened at the stress points, stitching re-done along one shoulder where something had torn through it and been repaired without vanity. His pack sat against a stump, buckled shut. A short sword rested on the ground beside his right boot, positioned where he could reach it but clearly not where he was reaching for it. His hair was cropped close and his face had the colour of someone who slept outside more often than in. He favoured his right knee — Maren could see it in how he sat, the leg extended slightly, not quite willing to fold all the way.

He had the look of a man who used to smile more. That was what Maren noticed first, before the gear or the weapon or the deliberate stillness: the architecture of a face that had been built for expressions it didn't make anymore.

*Hero-ranger. Active class. High level. The bounty is open on his interface.* Vess, each word placed like a stone. *He can see what you are. He's been here at least an hour.*

*And he has not drawn his weapon,* Aldric said. The paladin's voice was careful, which was different from his usual formality. Careful like a man stepping around something fragile on the floor. *That is not a good sign.*

Maren understood why immediately. A ranger who attacked was a problem you could solve — by running, by fighting, by being lucky. A ranger who sat down and waited for you to walk to him wanted something that couldn't be solved with any of those.

"There's two ways this goes," the man said. His voice carried the clearing without effort — trained to project over distance, the habit so deep it operated even in conversation. "One of them involves me trailing you for another three days, and honestly my knee isn't up for it this week. So I'd rather the other way."

Derry's hand tightened on Maren's elbow.

"What's the other way?" Maren said. His own voice came out steadier than the inside of him warranted, the performance booting up on its own — charm and deflection and the right tone, mechanical as breathing.

"We talk." The man shifted on the stump, resettling his weight off the bad knee. "I'm Caul. I've been behind you since Harrow. Ahead of you now, which I realize looks staged, but the road only goes one way from here and I walk faster than you do."

*He is telling the truth about his name. Caul Henning. Registered with the Wayward Reach ranger company. Twelve years active service. His party roster shows —* Aldric stopped. A silence that felt different from his usual pauses — not deliberate, not tactical. Arrested.

*What?*

*His party roster shows two members deceased. Recent. Three months ago.*

"You can sit," Caul said. "I've got water. Your friend looks like he's about to fall over."

Derry did look bad. The colour had left his face and his shirt was damp at the collar despite the cool morning, the sweat of a man whose body had decided to be afraid before his mind caught up. But he was looking at Maren, not at Caul, and Maren could read that look with eleven years of professional accuracy: *Do we run?*

*Running would be inadvisable,* Vess said. *He has sight lines on both exits from this clearing. Brush cover to the south but the terrain drops into a gully thirty metres in. He chose this spot.*

"Yeah," Maren said to Derry, though it wasn't really an answer to the question Derry was asking. He let go of the tension in his shoulders — a conscious decision to rearrange his body into something that looked like ease — and walked to the middle of the clearing and sat on the ground. Not because he wanted to sit. Because standing in front of a seated man with a sword on the ground made the power too obvious, and Maren's whole professional life was about rearranging the power in a room until he could find a way to work it.

Derry sat too, but farther away. Not beside Maren, the way he would have a week ago. About four feet to Maren's left, knees drawn up, arms wrapped around them. The distance was small and Maren measured it the way he measured everything — automatically, with the part of himself that catalogued what people were telling him with their bodies before they'd decided to tell him anything with their mouths.

"So," Maren said.

"So." Caul looked at him. His eyes were the light grey of old iron and they held Maren's face with a patience that wasn't threatening — worse than threatening. The patience of a man who had already decided what this conversation was and was waiting for Maren to arrive at the same understanding. "You're the unclassified. The bounty."

"I've been told."

"Your profile had a sin-eater registration before the classification scrambled. Did you know that? It's still there underneath. Eleven years of service logged."

Something in Maren's chest went very still. Not a tightening — a cessation, the way a room goes quiet when everyone hears the same sound at once.

"I know who I am," Maren said. "I don't know what you want."

"I want to tell you something." Caul recapped a water skin he'd been holding and set it beside his boot. The gesture was unhurried, almost domestic — a man settling in for a conversation over a table, not confronting a fugitive in a clearing. "After that, you can go. For a while." He looked at Derry, then back at Maren. "Your friend should hear it too."

Derry said nothing. His arms were locked around his knees and he was staring at the dirt between his feet with the focused attention of a man trying to disappear into the ground.

"Three months ago," Caul said, "my party cleared a dungeon near the Ashfold border. Standard contract. Minor threat creatures, containment perimeter, System-flagged quest. We'd done a hundred of these. Probably two hundred. You stop counting after the first year."

He spoke the way someone reads aloud from a document they've memorized — each sentence placed, cadenced, delivered with the even rhythm of a field report. But the rhythm was too even. Too practiced. And Maren recognized it immediately because he recognized performance, because performance was his entire professional skill, and this man had rehearsed what he was about to say. Not to be convincing. To survive saying it.

"The area was supposed to be clean. The report said so — class-certified sin-eater, registered, verified. System records showed the contamination cleared eighteen months before we got there. Haley checked it herself. She always checked. She was — " The practised cadence held, barely. "She was careful about that."

*Haley Keth,* Aldric said, very quietly, reading from whatever the System kept on file for the dead. *Healer-class. Twenty-six at time of death. Renn Ostara. Shield-bearer. Twenty-nine.*

Caul's hands stayed on his knees. "The dungeon was routine. Threat level minimal. But the contamination wasn't gone. It had settled into the substrate — below what a standard scan reads, in the deep residue that a sin-eater is supposed to pull. It was old contamination. Layers of it. Years' worth, built up because someone had been marking the area clean and walking away."

The clearing was quiet. Somewhere behind them a bird was repeating a three-note call, over and over, and Maren wanted it to stop because the sound was too ordinary for what was happening in his chest.

"Haley went down first. The contamination spiked when the quest activated — the old residue interacted with the fresh System energy and it all came up at once. Like pulling a tarp off a floor that's been rotting underneath." Caul paused. The practised rhythm broke and what was under it showed briefly — a man trying very hard to hold a shape that grief kept pulling out of true. "Renn tried to get her out. The spike caught him too."

Maren's hands were in his lap. He was looking at them. They were still, which surprised him, because the rest of him felt like something had come loose inside his ribs and was swinging free, knocking against the walls of him with each breath.

*He is describing contamination persistence from incomplete sin-eating,* Aldric said. The paladin didn't sound like the paladin — not lecturing, not moral, not correcting. Something Maren hadn't heard from him before. Something that sounded like a man reading a report he'd already known the contents of. *A genuine sin-eater clears the contamination from the substrate. A fraudulent one only removes the surface. What he is describing is exactly —*

*I know.* Maren thought it hard enough that the thought had edges. *I know what he's describing.*

Caul was watching him. The ranger's face hadn't changed — still the settled, practised expression, the field-report composure — but his eyes were doing something that wasn't professional. They were looking at Maren the way you look at a word you're trying to read in bad light.

"The sin-eater who certified that area was real," Caul said. "Registered. Verified. Had the class designation, the proper ritual sequence on file. Everything checked. And the contamination was still there, because the rituals were empty. The motions without the work." He paused. Let the silence sit. He was good at that — the silence of a tracker who'd spent days waiting for movement, comfortable with stillness as a tool. "Do you understand what I'm telling you?"

"Yeah," Maren said. "I understand."

"There are more of you than people think. Fake sin-eaters. Some of you are System-blind, some just found out that doing the ritual wrong pays the same as doing it right." Caul's fingers pressed into his kneecaps, the knuckles whitening briefly. "The System doesn't audit the work. It checks the certification, the registration. It doesn't check whether the contamination actually cleared, because the System assumes — " He stopped. Drew breath through his nose. When he continued, the rehearsed cadence was back, but thinner, more audibly a structure he was leaning on to stay upright. "The System assumes sin-eaters do their jobs."

Derry made a small sound. Not a word — a tight exhaled noise, the kind of sound he made when he was trying to process something too large for speech. Maren didn't look at him. He couldn't look at Derry right now, because if he looked he would see Derry's face doing the thing it was doing, which was understanding exactly what Caul was describing and knowing exactly who it applied to, and that understanding would be written in every line of Derry's open, honest, terrible face, and Maren would not be able to keep looking at Caul's grey eyes with anything resembling composure.

"Haley liked birds," Caul said. The shift was sudden — a crack in the practiced frame that he didn't try to repair. "She kept a journal. Drew them. She wasn't any good at it — the proportions were always wrong. Heads too big, wings too short. Renn used to tell her they looked like angry potatoes with beaks." Something moved in his face that was not a smile. "He was better at drawing than she was but he'd never have told her that. He just — he let her show him and he'd say they were good."

*Don't say anything,* Vess said. The assassin's voice carried its usual operational flatness, but there was a precision in it that Maren had learned to recognise as something more than professional — a quality of attention that only appeared when Vess was seeing something she considered real. *He isn't asking for a response. He's showing you what he lost. Let him.*

Caul reached into the front of his ranger's jacket and took out a folded piece of paper — creased and re-creased so many times the folds were nearly worn through, the paper gone soft at the edges. He didn't unfold it. He held it in his hand for a moment, then put it back inside the jacket.

"I've been tracking since they died. Not your bounty specifically — I picked that up in Harrow. Your profile matched reports I'd been collecting. I've been tracking the problem. The certification gaps. Areas where the contamination records don't match the service history." He smoothed the front of his jacket where the paper sat. "There are a lot of gaps."

"I'm not — " Maren started, and the lie was right there, fully formed, ready to deploy: *I'm not what you think, I don't know what you're talking about, you've got the wrong man.* Eleven years of practice had made the lie as natural as swallowing. He could feel it in his mouth, shaped and familiar, comfortable as an old shoe.

He didn't say it. Not because honesty won — honesty had never won a fight inside Maren, had never even shown up to one with real conviction. But the lie would have required him to look at Caul's face while he said it, and Caul's face was the face of a man carrying a piece of paper with a dead woman's bad bird drawings on it, and some performances just couldn't survive the audience they were playing to.

"You're not what?" Caul said. Patient. Waiting the way a stone waits.

"I don't — I'm not going to — " The self-correction kicked in, the compulsive mid-sentence revision, and this time it wasn't strategic. This time it was the honest confusion of a man who had three or four things in his mouth and couldn't find one that was safe to say. "I don't know what to tell you," he said. "That's — yeah. I don't know what to tell you."

It was the truest thing he'd said in thirteen days. The confession sat in his chest like a river stone and the lie sat next to it and neither could move because both were exactly the same weight.

Aldric said nothing. The silence from the paladin was enormous, and Maren understood it completely: this was what Aldric had wanted Maren to hear since the first day. The cost. The specific, named, human cost of eleven years of fraud, delivered not by a dead paladin's moral lectures but by a living man holding a dead woman's drawings. Aldric didn't need to speak because the whole conversation was his argument, made by someone who had standing to make it, in a clearing on a road that Maren couldn't walk fast enough to leave behind him.

"I'm not asking you to tell me anything," Caul said. "I'm telling you. So you know what's out there. What's been left behind in the villages where sin-eaters collected the fee and didn't do the work." He looked at the sky — checking the light, or checking something in himself that needed sky to find. "You're headed for the Crook."

Maren didn't confirm or deny. His mouth was dry and his hands were very still in his lap.

"I know you are, because there's nowhere else from here unless you're planning to walk into dungeon territory and die. Which I don't think you're doing." Caul stood up from the stump. The right knee gave him trouble — Maren could see the hitch, the way he braced one hand against the stump and pushed through the stiffness, the joint complaining in a way he'd clearly learned to manage but not to hide. "I'm going to give you a day. Not because you've earned it. Because the Crook is where sin-eaters answer for this kind of thing, and I'd rather they handle it than me doing something on a road that I'd have to write up afterward." The rehearsed cadence cracked again, just slightly, and the voice underneath it was tired in a way that went deeper than a bad knee and a long walk. "I want this done right. Haley would've — she would've wanted it done right."

"A day," Maren said.

"Starting now. I'll be a day behind you. If you're at the Crook when I arrive, we do this properly. If you're not — " He picked up his short sword and sheathed it in a motion so practised it looked involuntary, the hand finding the scabbard the way a tongue finds a missing tooth. "Then we do it differently."

He shouldered his pack. The buckles clinked against each other — a small, ordinary sound. He looked at Derry for the first time with something that wasn't tactical assessment. A moment of what might have been recognition — one ordinary man looking at another ordinary man and seeing the specific misery of being here because of someone else's decisions.

Then he walked past them, back the direction they'd come from. His footsteps on the packed earth were even and unhurried. The bad knee didn't slow him — just changed the rhythm, a slight hitch every other step, an asymmetry in the sound that Maren listened to until it faded into the distance and the bird started singing again and the clearing was empty except for the two of them and the three voices in Maren's skull, all of them quiet.

---

Maren sat where he was. The ground was cool through his trousers and a root was pressing into his left thigh — a specific, insistent discomfort that he leaned into because it gave him something to organise his attention around that wasn't the conversation that had just happened.

*Haley Keth,* Aldric said eventually. *She completed her healer certification at twenty-two. Three years of study at the Ashfold academy. She specialised in contamination triage — the medical side. How to stabilise someone exposed to quest residue.* A pause. *She should not have needed to use that training on herself. The area was supposed to be clean.*

*I know.*

*You do know. You have known for eleven years what your work leaves behind.*

*Yeah. I know that too.*

Aldric went silent. Not the pointed quiet of disapproval — Maren knew that version, could feel its edges. This was heavier. The silence of someone sitting with something too large for speech, which was exactly what the paladin had been accusing Maren of avoiding for weeks, and it turned out Aldric wasn't any better at it than Maren was. Just more willing to stay in the room with it.

Maren closed his eyes. The red behind his eyelids was warm from the morning sun and it looked like nothing and like the inside of a body and he kept them shut because opening them meant the clearing was still there and Derry was still in it, four feet to his left, quiet.

"Maren." Derry's voice. Small. The voice of a man who hadn't spoken in a long time and wasn't confident the words would come out in order. "That was — he was talking about — "

"Yeah."

"He's saying that the — that what we — that villages where someone — "

"Yeah."

Derry was quiet. Maren heard him swallow. Heard his hands move against the fabric of his trousers, a dry scraping sound, restless.

"His friends died because someone did what we did," Derry said. Not a question. A sentence he was testing the weight of, holding it out in front of himself to see if it would break something.

Maren opened his eyes. The clearing looked the same — the grey stumps, the brushed-back scrub, the road curving away in both directions, the bird still going at its three-note song as though the morning hadn't changed. The root was still pressing into his thigh.

"Not us specifically," he said. "He said the sin-eater was certified. Class-registered. We were never — I didn't have real credentials, I just had the bowl and the — " He heard himself doing it. The hedging, the careful distinction between their specific fraud and the general category of fraud, the precise parsing of guilt that let him find the gap where his particular culpability didn't quite fill the shape. He heard the performance and he hated it and he couldn't stop, because stopping meant saying the flat simple version, which was: *We did this. Maybe not to his friends. But to someone's friends, in some village, on some day, the contamination we left behind did this or will do this and the only reason we don't know whose friends is that nobody's come to sit on a stump and tell us.*

"But someone like us," Derry said.

"Someone like me."

"Like us, Maren. I fed you the cues. I told you what the System was showing so you could fake the responses. I was there. Every ritual. Every village."

*He is correct,* Vess said. No judgment. An assessment of fact.

Maren looked at Derry. The four feet between them had become architectural — not a gap but a structure, something Derry had built and placed himself on the other side of. His arms were still locked around his knees. His red-brown hair was dirty from travel and pressed flat on one side where he'd slept on it wrong. His face was doing the thing Maren had been afraid of: not anger — Derry didn't have the wiring for the kind of anger this warranted — but understanding. The slow, thorough, irreversible kind. A man looking at his oldest friend and reassembling every shared memory in light of a clearing and a ranger and two names that belonged to dead people who trusted the System to keep them safe.

"How many villages," Derry said. "In eleven years. How many places where the contamination is still — "

"I don't know." True. Maren had never counted. Counting would have required him to think about the villages as a collective, an accumulated weight, and the entire architecture of the fraud had depended on each village being an isolated event. Twelve coppers, a ritual, a bowl, a performance, a nod from the bereaved family. Move on. "I never counted."

"A lot," Derry said. Not asking.

"Yeah."

Derry nodded. He looked at the dirt between his feet again. Then he said, in the voice he used when he was retreating to the only safe ground he had: "Pell's going to be three next month. Lenna's been planning something — she told me before we left. She's going to make a cake with honey from the Grenning apiary. Pell's never had honey cake before." He paused. "She's counting everything now. Stairs, stones, peas in a pod. Lenna says she won't stop. She'll count anything you put in front of her."

Maren said nothing. He understood what Derry was doing — he'd watched it for years, the retreat to Pell and Lenna when the world became too large, the family used as a sea wall against things that couldn't be faced directly. But this time the retreat didn't sound like comfort. This time when Derry talked about Pell it sounded like measurement. Like he was placing his daughter's small ordinary life on one side of a scale and watching something on the other side rise to meet it.

"All those years," Derry said, quieter now, "the contamination in those villages. In our villages. In Fenhold." His voice was running but the connective words had gotten smaller, the sentences losing their cushioning. "People were just living in it, Maren. And we were — we were right there, doing the ritual, and they were watching us and thinking — " He stopped. "Pell was living in it."

The three words landed like a dropped weight.

*He is right,* Aldric said. Not to punish. Just to confirm. The gentleness was worse than the moral lectures had ever been.

Derry turned around. He was still kneeling by his patch of dirt and his eyes were red but he wasn't crying, which was harder to look at than crying would have been. Crying had a simplicity to it. What Derry's face was doing was more complicated — a reassessment, the features of a man who was good and knew he was good and was discovering the specific dimensions of a thing he'd done that wasn't good, and measuring it against his daughter's face.

"Did you know?" he said. "All those years. Did you know the contamination was staying?"

Maren opened his mouth. The truth was right there, simple and complete: *Yes. I didn't know the details or the mechanism or that it could kill people. But I knew the ritual was empty. I knew nothing was being cleared. I didn't stop because stopping meant losing the income and the identity and the one thing I could do, which was pretend to be something I wasn't, and I was good at that and I was afraid of what I'd be without it.* The truth was right there and it was the most dangerous thing in the clearing — not because Derry would hurt him but because Derry would understand him, completely and precisely, and then Derry would forgive nothing, and Maren would deserve both the understanding and the absence of forgiveness.

"I didn't think about it," Maren said. "Not the way you're — I didn't think about it staying. I thought about the ritual and the fee and the next village. I don't know, Derry. I don't know what I thought."

Half a truth and half a lie. Derry knew it. He'd spent eleven years watching Maren build sentences out of exactly this material — true pieces arranged in a shape that wasn't quite true — and he'd learned the architecture even if he'd never built one himself. His face did a small, specific thing: a tightening around the eyes that wasn't anger or grief but was the expression of a man revising his understanding of someone he loved.

He stood up. He turned toward the road without waiting for Maren, without offering a hand, without the half-second pause where he'd usually check Maren's direction and match his pace. He just started walking.

Maren got up. His left leg was stiff from the root and he shook it out. The ceramic bowl in his pack shifted when he shouldered it — he felt it through the canvas, the smooth familiar weight of it against his spine. The same bowl. The same one he'd carried into houses where people were grieving and used to perform a ceremony that did nothing while they stood in the doorway with the particular relief of people who believed the contamination was being lifted from the room where their husband or mother or child had died.

He followed Derry.

They walked. The road was empty and the packed earth was dry and the shadows had that longer, thinner quality that late summer takes on when autumn starts winning. The hum was louder out here — farther from Harrow's System-dense interference, the background frequency of ambient System activity settled into Maren's hearing like tinnitus, the operational noise of classification running beneath the world's surface. He'd heard it his whole life. Aldric had given him the name for it in the first week: the System's maintenance hum, the sound of the wall that held.

Derry walked ahead. Not far. Maybe ten feet. But he didn't fall back. In all their years walking between villages — carrying the fraud like a shared weight between them, splitting it the way they split the fee — Derry had always walked beside Maren. Matching pace. Filling silence. Making the con look like a friendship, or the friendship look like a con, or — and this was the thing Maren had never let himself look at directly — simply being his friend, genuinely, while also being his accomplice, and somehow holding both in the same open, readable, disastrously honest face without either one destroying the other.

Now Derry walked ten feet ahead and the distance was the realest thing Maren had measured in weeks.

*The ranger was not incorrect about the certification framework,* Aldric said after a long silence. The paladin had settled back into his formal register, but there was something under it — not righteousness, not correction. A fatigue that Maren hadn't heard before. The tiredness of a man who had been right about something for a long time and found no satisfaction in the confirmation. *The System does not verify outcomes. It tracks credentials and logs completed rituals. A registered sin-eater who performs the ritual sequence is recorded as having performed the ritual. The contamination itself is never independently assessed.*

*I know,* Maren thought.

*You have known for eleven years.*

*I said I know.*

The paladin went quiet. Not the sharp silence of a point being made. The heavy silence of a man sitting in a room with a thing that was too large and too real and that couldn't be argued away or reorganised through institutional language or official procedure, because the thing was just dead people in a dungeon that should have been safe.

Vess said nothing either, and her silence was its own kind — professional, contained, the silence of someone who had no operational recommendation for the situation and therefore saw no purpose in speech. But Maren had been carrying her long enough to hear the texture of her quiet, and it wasn't empty. It was the silence of a woman who knew what it felt like to carry a thing you'd done that couldn't be undone. Her contract. Her missing sister's name. The gaps in her that nobody was going to fill.

They walked through most of the morning. The road climbed gradually and the air cooled as they gained elevation. At one point they passed a farmstead set back from the road — stone walls, a thin line of smoke from the chimney, a dog that watched them from the gate without barking. Someone had been burning field stubble; the smell of it reached them on the breeze, sharp and ashy and ordinary. Harvest ending. The world doing its work.

Derry stopped at a stream crossing where the water ran fast and clear over flat brown stones. He knelt and cupped water to his mouth. He drank for a long time, then sat back on his heels and stayed there, looking at the water, his wet hands resting on his thighs.

Maren stopped a few feet behind him.

"My daughter's going to be three," Derry said to the stream. Not to Maren — to the water, or to the stones, or to whatever part of the world would listen without having an opinion. "Next month. Lenna's going to make the cake and Pell's going to — she's going to count the candles." He almost laughed. "Three candles. She'll count them six times."

"Derry."

"I keep thinking about — " His voice was running now, the way it did when he was processing something too big for a single sentence, but the usual qualifications and codas were stripped away. Just the bare sentence, trying to carry what it needed to carry. "I keep thinking about the contamination in those villages. In our villages. All those years, Maren. Every time we did the ritual and took the money and walked away and the — and it was still there. In the ground. In the houses where they were sleeping."

Maren stood behind him and looked at the back of Derry's head — the dirty red-brown hair, the sunburned neck, the collar of his shirt gone dark with sweat that had nothing to do with the temperature — and he thought about what it would cost to say: *I'm sorry.* Two words. He'd said them a thousand times to a thousand people about a thousand things he wasn't sorry about, smooth and easy, the apology as professional tool. He couldn't say them to Derry. Not because they wouldn't be true but because Derry would know they were true, and the genuine version of those words carried a weight that Maren wasn't sure he could hand to another person without both of them going under.

Derry stood up. He wiped his hands on his trousers. He started walking.

The ten feet between them held for the rest of the day — steady, precise, the distance of a man who hadn't left yet but had started to measure the cost of staying. They climbed into the cooling hills where the road narrowed and the trees grew closer together and the dungeon territory began its long approach, and the hum grew louder, and the shadows stretched, and neither of them said Pell's name again.
