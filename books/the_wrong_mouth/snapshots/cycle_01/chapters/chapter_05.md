# Chapter 5

Aldric hadn't spoken since the night before.

Maren had walked for hours with that silence inside him — not the ordinary absence of noise but the specific pressure of someone choosing not to speak. He knew the shape of it. He'd heard it from priests who caught him mid-fraud, from Derry's wife the night they left Fenhold, from villagers deciding whether to pay him for a ritual they suspected was fake. It was the loudest kind of quiet he knew: the kind that meant someone had seen something they hadn't decided how to carry yet.

The road ran between two fields that had gone fallow long enough for the weeds to turn woody — knee-high tangles of dry yellow scrub that rattled when the wind shifted. Late-summer sun pressed against the back of his neck. The pack chafed the same raw spot on his right shoulder it had been working on since Fenhold. Derry walked a half-step behind, the way he'd been walking since they left the hollow where the botched consumption had scrambled everything sideways, and neither of them had spoken since the creek crossing.

Vess was not quiet.

She'd been inside his skull for less than a day and she was already doing something Maren could only describe as *watching* — a constant low-level alertness that sat behind his eyes like the leading edge of a headache. She scanned the road ahead, the treeline to the east, the ditch running alongside the packed dirt, the angle of shadows on the ground. She didn't narrate the way Aldric narrated the interface. She just did it, and Maren felt it as a prickling presence behind his eyes — uninvited attention, constant, thorough.

*Two hundred yards. South. Two, armed, approaching from the sheep fold.*

She delivered this the way you'd tell someone their soup was getting cold.

"What?" Maren said, out loud, and his voice came out wrong — hoarse, scraped thin from disuse.

Derry's head turned. "Hm?"

*They've been paralleling the road from the fold. They're closing now.* A pause, almost clinical. *They look like they've been waiting.*

"Someone's coming," Maren said.

Derry's hand went to his belt — not for a weapon, because Derry didn't carry weapons, but for the camp knife, the short utility blade he used for cutting rope and splitting kindling and prying lids off jars. His fingers touched the handle and stayed there. The act of touching it was as far as his courage went.

"How do you—" Derry started.

*Aldric.* Vess's voice, sharp. Not angry — demanding. Like she'd knocked on a door she expected to find open.

The paladin's voice came back like a latch lifting. Flat, controlled, every word set down with the precision of a man filing a report for an audience that no longer existed.

*Two hero-class combatants. System profiles active. They have been tracking the bounty marker. The younger one is reading your classification field and seeing nothing. He is confused. The woman is not confused.*

"How many?" Maren asked. The question was aimed at both of them — the dead paladin and the dead assassin — and the absurdity of asking two ghosts for a tactical count while standing on a road in the afternoon sun was something he would have found funny thirty seconds from now if thirty seconds from now weren't about to be a different kind of experience entirely.

*Two,* Vess said.

*Two,* Aldric confirmed. Then, with something that might have been reluctance: *The bounty is flagged for hostile engagement. They are authorized to kill.*

They stepped out from behind the sheep pen's collapsed wall at maybe sixty yards. The late-afternoon sun was behind Maren, which meant they were walking into it, squinting, and for one stupid second he thought: I could wave. I could wave and keep going and they'd think I was some farmer heading home.

The woman was the closer threat. He knew this without knowing how he knew it — the knowledge arriving in his muscles before his mind could name it. She was forty, maybe older, with weather-darkened skin and bracers that had been repaired more than once, the leather patched over with strips of mismatched hide that said years of field work and no budget for new gear. A short sword on her hip, its pommel wrapped in a strip of faded blue cloth — wind-frayed, knotted with the tight, automatic knot of something retied every morning. Someone's color. Someone she carried with her.

Her partner was younger. Mid-twenties. He held a mace in one hand and kept adjusting his grip, shifting his fingers the way someone does when they've practiced a thing in a training yard but haven't yet done it against a body that bleeds. He had a port-wine birthmark on his left jaw that ran from his ear to his chin, and a pack that sat wrong on his shoulders — too new, too stiff, the buckles still bright.

"That's the mark," the woman said. Her voice carried easily. Calm, professional, the tone of someone who had done a version of this before.

The young one squinted at Maren. "His profile's empty. There's nothing — I mean literally nothing. I've never seen that."

"I know."

*The younger one's threat level is moderate. The woman is high. She has a class-enhanced blade — System augmentation on the edge. Her stance indicates—*

Aldric's assessment dissolved into irrelevance because Maren's body was already moving.

His right hand found the camp knife. It was in Derry's pack, slung over Maren's right shoulder — the short blade, ash handle, edge decent but meant for rope and kindling — and his fingers closed on it the way a hand closes on a railing when the stair gives out. Not decision. Not thought. The body knowing where the weapon was before the mind finished registering the word weapon.

He pulled the knife free. The pack shifted on his shoulder. Behind him, Derry made a sound — a small choked syllable that might have been "Maren" if it had gotten far enough to form.

The woman drew her sword and the hum changed. Not louder — denser. Something in the System's local field activated around the blade, a thickening that Maren could hear before he could place it — a drone, insect-close, pressing against the inner ear. The air around the edge rippled with a faint heat-shimmer that was not heat. Aldric could read it. Maren could only hear the drone.

She came forward. Not running — walking with a specific practiced economy, each step covering exactly the distance she wanted, her blade in a guard position that her System probably overlaid with optimal angles and threat trajectories and all the other combat mathematics Maren would never see. He could see only the woman: her weight on her toes, the set of her shoulders, the way her eyes stayed on his center mass.

He ran toward her.

He didn't choose to run. His legs did it — Aldric's training, the paladin's doctrine of meeting an engagement rather than absorbing one. But the body running this doctrine was Maren's, six inches shorter and sixty pounds lighter than the body the training had been built for, and the stride length was wrong. His feet hit the packed dirt at intervals that didn't match the pattern his muscles wanted, and the misalignment made him stagger on the second step.

*Lower,* Vess said. *You're too tall for this stance.*

That didn't make sense — he was short, he'd always been short — but his knees bent and his center of gravity dropped and the running worked. Not a paladin's advance. Something closer to the ground, faster, wrong-looking. He could feel the competing instructions in his own legs — Aldric's long training stride fighting against Vess's crouch — and the result was a lurching, ugly sprint that covered the distance in less time than it should have because neither set of instincts would give.

The woman swung.

She was good. The blade came in flat, a lateral cut aimed at his ribs, and the System enhancement on the edge made the air whine with a sound like a finger drawn across wet glass. Maren's left arm came up — forearm turned out, palm flat, the guard position for a shield he didn't have and had never held. Aldric's body memory. Fourteen years of training with a steel disc on his left arm, deployed onto bare flesh and thin cotton.

The blade caught him below the elbow. Not deep — the System enhancement was for piercing, and the flat angle converted the cut to a scraping drag across the outer forearm — but the pain expanded from the point of contact in a single wave that went up his arm, into his shoulder, along his jaw. His arm went cold below the cut. Blood ran down his forearm and into his palm and between his fingers and it was hot — the only hot thing on his suddenly cold arm — and the specific warmth of his own blood pooling in the creases of his palm was the most physically real thing he had felt in six days.

His right hand came up.

This was Vess. Not the motion but the intention underneath it — the way his hand found the gap between the woman's sword-arm and her neck, the geometry of a body opening itself in the follow-through of a swing. She was committed to the arc. Her sword was out wide, pulling her shoulder with it, and the left side of her neck was exposed in the space between her jawline and the collar of her jerkin. The camp knife was in his fist and his fist was moving and he was inside her guard, closer than a sword could reach.

He stabbed upward into the soft triangle below her jaw.

The knife went in. He felt the whole thing through the handle — the brief resistance of skin, then the give of something underneath that was not muscle and not fat, then something cartilaginous that the blade pushed through with a small wet pop. The handle twisted in his grip as the knife seated itself, and the twist wasn't his — Vess, turning the blade a quarter rotation to widen the channel, the automatic, practiced motion of someone who knew how bodies came apart the way a carpenter knew how joints fit together.

The woman grabbed his right wrist.

Her fingers dug into the tendons above his hand — each ridge of her grip distinct against his forearm, each finger a separate pressure point. She was strong. She was dying and she was stronger than he was and her hand on his wrist had nothing to do with combat and everything to do with the body's refusal to accept what was happening to it.

*Pull it. Side-draw. Don't lift.*

He pulled the knife sideways. Something opened in the woman's throat — not the great arterial spray of a traveling minstrel's war ballad, just a steady dark flow that ran down her collar and soaked the neck of her jerkin and dripped from the bottom edge of the leather onto the road. The blue cloth from her sword pommel was visible in the dust where the sword had fallen. She'd dropped it when her hand went to his wrist.

Her grip loosened. Not gradually — all at once, like a rope going slack when you cut it. She sat down. Not fell — sat, in the road, her legs folding under her with a strange neatness. Her mouth moved. No sound came out. Her eyes were on his face and they were present — fully present, alert, processing — and then they weren't. The change was invisible. One moment someone was looking at him and the next moment something that used to be someone was looking at nothing, and the instant between was so thin he couldn't have pointed at it if his life depended on knowing where it was.

She died sitting up in the dust with her hand still raised, reaching for the wrist of the man who'd killed her.

The young one was already running. He'd dropped his mace somewhere behind him — Maren heard it clang on the packed dirt — and his too-new pack bounced against his spine as he ran, the bright buckles catching the sun in small flashes. He ran the way people run who have never had to run seriously: all elbows and knees, no economy, his breathing already ragged at twenty yards.

Maren's legs tensed. Vess. The professional's reflex against leaving a witness — the instinct to pursue, to close the angle, to finish the geometry she'd started.

*No.* Aldric. The first word he'd spoken with force since the fragment collision.

Maren's legs stopped.

He stood on the road. The air tasted like dust and copper. His left arm hung at his side and the blood from the cut had run down to his fingertips and was dripping — each drop hitting the dirt with a small sound he couldn't stop hearing, couldn't stop counting. The camp knife was in his right fist and his fingers were locked around the ash handle — physically locked, the tendons contracted into a cramp that his conscious effort couldn't reach. He tried to open his hand. The hand stayed shut. He tried again, harder, and his forearm muscles stood out against the skin and the fingers didn't move and the knife stayed where it was.

Derry was behind the sheep pen. Maren could hear his breathing — fast, shallow, pressed flat against the stone as if less space meant less danger.

The woman sat in front of him. The dust had settled in a haze around her that the low sun turned amber. A fly had already found the blood on her collar. The blue cloth lay in the dirt beside the sword. The knot was still tight — a simple knot, the kind you tie on things you expect to keep.

He looked at his right hand. At the knife he couldn't drop. At the dark slick on the blade and between his fingers and in the lines of his palm. He thought, with a clarity that felt borrowed from someone more capable of thinking: Those are not my hands.

---

He threw up behind the sheep pen wall, twenty feet from the body.

The vomiting was mechanical — his stomach clenching, the thin bile of a body that hadn't eaten enough to produce anything substantial coming up in two hitches, and then nothing. Just the clenching, the dry heaves, his diaphragm spasming against an empty cavity. He braced his right hand against the stone — the knife had finally dropped when the vomiting started, his fingers releasing all at once the way the woman's had — and the wall was warm from the sun and gritty against his palm and he focused on that. The texture of the stone. The warmth. The specific grain of it under his fingertips.

Derry was sitting with his back against the wall's far end, his hands between his knees. He'd been there during the fight. Behind the wall. He hadn't run and he hadn't helped and neither of those facts were the kind of thing either of them would mention.

"— should move, I think we should — the other one ran, right, he'll go back to wherever they came from, and there'll be more, won't there? So we should probably — I mean not right now, you're — you need a minute. But soon. We should move soon. I've got the cloth strips, the ones Lenna cut for — she cut them from that old shirt of mine, the one with the—"

He stopped. He'd talked himself into a domestic detail that belonged to a life that was now very far away, and the words dried up.

Maren spat and straightened. His left arm had started bleeding through the sleeve again, a slow dark spread that the drying blood at the edges made look worse than it was. Six inches, outer forearm, shallow enough that the edges were already trying to close. He'd need to cut the sleeve off to clean it. He'd need to pick up the knife.

He didn't pick up the knife.

"Let me—" Derry was already up, already rummaging in the pack for the cloth strips. His hands were shaking. Not as badly as Maren's, but shaking, and he was talking while he rummaged, the words spilling out continuous and unremarkable: about whether there was a creek nearby, about whether this was the right roll of cloth or the other one, about how Lenna always said you should boil water before washing a wound but they didn't have a fire so maybe just—

He wound the cloth strip around Maren's forearm with the same care he used for binding fence posts. Tight, even, methodical. His hands steadied as they worked, the way hands do when they have a task that's smaller than the thing they're frightened of. He tied the knot and patted it once, lightly.

"There," he said. "That's — it's not deep. You're okay. Are you okay? That's a stupid question. I mean the arm."

*A clean kill,* Aldric said.

The voice was quiet. Almost gentle. Maren recognized the register from the memories he carried — the paladin's tone for addressing young soldiers after their first blood. Calm. Affirming. The specific trained kindness of a man who had watched people die for fourteen years and had developed, over those years, a language for making it bearable.

*She felt no extended pain. The strike was precise and the angle correct. You — your body — performed with discipline under—*

"Shut up."

Derry's hands stopped on the bandage.

"Not you." Maren's voice was rough. Scraped. "Him."

Derry nodded. He went back to smoothing the edges of the cloth strip, pressing them flat against Maren's arm with careful fingers.

*I understand that the shock of—*

"You don't understand shit." The words came out harder than he intended — blunt, stripped of the charm and deflection he usually wrapped around things that mattered. The performance had dropped and the voice underneath was just anger and fear and the raw misery of a man whose body had done something unspeakable while he watched from inside it. "Don't — don't put your words on it. Don't call it — a clean kill? That's the framing you're going with? A clean kill?"

Aldric was silent.

"Because it wasn't clean. Nothing about that was — I had a camp knife. A camp knife, Aldric. The thing Derry cuts rope with. And I stuck it in a woman's throat and she held onto my arm while she died. She grabbed me. She *held on.* And you want to tell me that was clean?"

*I was attempting to provide—*

"I know what you were attempting." He was half-sitting against the wall, his back against the warm stone, his left arm cradled against his chest and the blood seeping through the bandage already. "You were attempting to make it okay. To — to put it in whatever box you've got. 'Clean kill, no suffering, proper engagement.' You've done this before. You've got a whole system for it, a whole way of talking about killing people that makes it fit inside your — your doctrine, or your framework, or whatever the word is for the thing that lets you sleep after you've—"

He stopped. Not because he'd finished. Because his throat had closed up and the next word was stuck somewhere behind his sternum and it wasn't coming.

The blue cloth was visible from here. Lying in the dust beside the dropped sword. The knot, tight and habitual. Someone's color, carried into a fight that ended with the carrier sitting dead on a road she'd probably walked a dozen times before.

*I have no framework for this.* Aldric's voice had changed. The trained gentleness was gone. What was left was something rawer, something without the architecture of protocol underneath it. *I had a framework. I am attempting to use it because it is the only thing I have. Because the alternative is describing what it felt like to watch your body kill that woman with a precision that was partly mine and partly Vess's, and I do not have—*

He stopped. The sentence didn't end. It just stopped, which was — from Aldric, whose sentences always closed like doors — its own kind of alarm.

Vess spoke into the space his silence left.

*They were a scouting pair. Standard bounty protocol for unknown targets — send two to confirm, then dispatch a full party. Based on their approach vector, the staging town they came from is less than a day east. When the younger one reports, someone will come looking.*

She said this the way someone reads a weather forecast.

"How long," Maren said. His throat had loosened enough for two words.

*Tomorrow. Maybe tonight, if he ran the whole way.*

Derry was standing with the remaining cloth strips in his hands, watching Maren's face with an expression that Maren recognized because he'd spent eleven years reading faces for a living. It was the look of a man who had just seen something he was going to spend a long time not looking at directly — not horror, not fear, something worse than both. It was the look of a person realizing that someone they've known for years is capable of things they aren't, and the distance between who they thought their friend was and what their friend can actually do is a gap that no amount of normal conversation will close.

"We should move," Derry said. "Before — yeah. We should move."

They moved.

Maren left the knife on the ground by the sheep pen wall. He didn't look at it. Derry picked it up — Maren heard the scrape of blade on dirt, heard Derry wipe it on something, heard the blade go back into the pack — and neither of them mentioned that it was Derry who picked it up or that the blade had been cleaned or that the thing that needed cleaning off it was a person.

They walked. The sun moved across the sky and the shadows stretched east and the road went the way roads go — the same dust, the same ruts, the same dry weeds rattling in the ditch. Maren's left arm bled through the bandage once and Derry retied it without speaking. The hum from the nearest dungeon mouth was a distant pressure on the inner ear, rising and falling with the terrain. Quest-light pooled in the ditch to their right — faint and pale in the late-day sun, the thin shimmer that looked like heat haze to Maren and looked like data to everyone else alive.

His hands shook. He held them against his thighs as he walked, pressing his palms flat against the fabric of his trousers, and the pressure helped for a few seconds and then it didn't and the shaking came back. It wasn't fear. The fear had burned off somewhere during the first mile. The shaking was the body's record of what had happened — written in the small muscles of his hands and the tendons of his forearms, a physical testimony that his mind could try to outpace but his hands couldn't.

Aldric said nothing. His silence was different now — not the held-breath quiet of the night before, not the silence of a man who'd seen something he couldn't describe. This was the silence of someone who had tried to help and been told, accurately, that his help was the wrong shape for the wound.

Vess said nothing because Vess had already said everything she considered relevant.

Derry talked. About the road — how the ruts were deeper here, which meant carts came through, which probably meant a market town ahead, which was good because they needed water and his waterskin was nearly empty and did Maren remember the well in Fenhold, the one by the south wall, with the rope that always jammed? Pell used to hang on the crank handle and try to pull it up herself. She was too small to pull the rope but she'd hang on with both hands and her feet would leave the ground and she'd laugh—

He stopped. He'd talked himself to the memory and the memory was his daughter and his daughter was behind them, getting farther away with every step, and the talking had carried him to exactly the place the talking was supposed to carry him away from.

"Sorry," Derry said.

"Don't be sorry."

"I just — she keeps — I keep thinking about her. Is that stupid? We're out here and you just — and I'm thinking about whether she can count to twenty yet. She was stuck on fourteen. She'd go thirteen, fourteen, fourteen, fourteen."

"Not stupid," Maren said.

The light failed slowly. Derry found a spot off the road where a fallen oak made a windbreak, its trunk mossy and soft enough on top that sitting against it didn't hurt. They sat. Derry across from him, his pack between his knees. Neither of them built a fire. The evening air was still warm — late summer, the kind of night that wouldn't cool off until the small hours — and the light drained from the sky in stages, the blue going grey going colorless, and the quest-light in the ditches along the road brightened as the dark came on. Pale. Cold. The System's residue, pooling in every low place, patient as groundwater.

Maren sat with his back against the fallen trunk and his hands pressed flat against his thighs and listened to the hum. It was louder here than it had been on the road — they were closer to a dungeon mouth, maybe, or the residue in the ditches was denser than usual. The sound sat just below the range of normal hearing, a bone-deep vibration that Aldric and Vess and every other System-sighted person in the world filtered out without noticing because the interface told them what the hum meant and meaning was enough to make it disappear. Maren had heard it his whole life. Twenty-eight years of that low, persistent sound, and he'd never known what it was until Aldric arrived and told him it was the world running.

He had killed a woman. He didn't know her name. She had blue cloth on her sword — someone's color, a person she carried into the field the way a soldier carries a letter. She had bracers patched with mismatched leather. She was approximately forty and she had been good at what she did and she had died sitting in the road with her hand reaching for the wrist of the man who'd killed her.

He had killed her with a camp knife and he could still feel the resistance of the blade going in — the skin, the give, the quarter-turn that Vess's muscle memory had executed through his tendons. He could feel it in his palm the way you feel a splinter after you've pulled it out. The ghost of the entry. The body's memory of the thing the mind was trying to put down.

Eleven years. Eleven years of fake rituals, practiced gestures, borrowed grief, the careful architecture of a lie maintained through charm and observation and Derry's whispered System readings from the back of the crowd. Eleven years of nothing real — no genuine connection to the work he pretended to do, no authentic act, nothing true all the way down. And then something answered. And then the System noticed. And then his body — operated by dead people whose skills lived in his muscles like tenants he hadn't invited — had put a blade into a woman's throat on a road outside a sheep pen while he watched from inside, unable to stop it and unable to look away.

The first real thing he had ever done.

He pressed his shaking hands flat against his legs and held them there and watched the quest-light brighten in the ditches — pale, cold, permanent — and did not sleep.
