# Chapter 9

They smelled Harrow before they saw it. Ozone and char, layered thick enough to close Maren's throat while the town was still just a smudge between hills. Underneath, something sweeter — hot stone baking in late summer, and the particular warmth that quest-light left on surfaces after it had pooled long enough. The road had been getting worse for two hours. Scorch marks overlapped on the packed dirt until the whole path looked like the floor of a forge, burn patterns crossing at angles that made no sense until you understood that heroes didn't clean up after themselves. Some of the marks were old, pale gray, scuffed by cart wheels. Others were darker. One was still warm when Maren's boot caught its edge, and he moved his foot off it the way you'd step off a hot coal.

*Thirty-seven,* Aldric said. *Thirty-eight. Thirty-nine — that one is fresh, less than a day old. Someone deployed a class-three fire projection in the middle of a public road.*

"How do you know it's class three?" Maren muttered, barely moving his lips. He'd gotten better at that — the ventriloquism of talking to his own skull. A week of practice, and he could almost pass for a man muttering to himself, which was at least socially acceptable if not dignified.

*The scorch radius. The char depth. The residual heat signature is still readable on the interface. Do you want the full damage assessment or can I trust you to walk around it?*

"I can see a burn mark on the ground. I've been seeing burn marks."

*You have been seeing scorch marks the way a child sees a hearth fire. I am reading you System-verified damage signatures with class attribution, decay rates, and—*

*Four,* Vess said.

Aldric stopped. Maren waited.

*She does that on purpose,* Aldric said.

*Four heroes ahead. Two at the gate, one on the wall, one buying something from a stall just inside. The gate pair are low-tier — two, maybe three. Wall is tier four. The one at the stall—* She paused. *Interesting.*

"Interesting how? Interesting meaning they won't bother us, or interesting meaning we turn around right now?"

*Interesting meaning I can't get a clean read at this distance and I don't like not knowing.*

Derry was already sweating. He'd been sweating since they'd spotted the first road marker for Harrow, a charred wooden post with something carved into it that Derry could read and Maren couldn't. Whatever it said had drained the color from Derry's face and replaced it with a thin, damp sheen.

"Big place," Derry said, which was unnecessary. He wiped his forehead with his sleeve and the sleeve came away dark. "Lot of people, though. That's good, right? More people means it's harder to spot one — harder to pick one person out of—"

"Derry."

"Yeah."

"You're fine."

"I know I'm fine. I'm saying it's good. The crowd thing. That's — yeah."

Derry stopped talking for about fifteen steps, which under these conditions was something like a personal best.

Harrow sat in a shallow bowl between two hills, and the hum hit Maren before he could see the town proper. He'd always heard it — the low vibration that System-heavy areas made, a wasps' nest buried in a wall. In Fenhold it had been background, the kind of sound you stopped noticing the way you stopped noticing your own breathing. Here it was a pressure. It sat behind his sternum and stayed. The air felt denser, like walking into a room where too many people were talking. His ears popped twice on the slope down.

*The interface density is remarkable.* Aldric's tone shifted — less irritation, more the careful attention of a man cataloguing something he'd only read about in reports. *Quest notifications are layered three deep in some areas. I can barely isolate individual prompts. There — that board ahead, the tall frame by the market square. Do you see it?*

A wooden frame taller than he was stood nailed to a post at the edge of the market square. Things were pinned to it. Papers, maybe. Leather strips. Some kind of thin fabric that moved in the breeze. To him it was a bulletin board outside a village hall, just bigger and busier.

*That is a quest board. There are forty-one active quest postings. Sixteen standard dungeon clearances. Eight contamination reports — the residue levels listed on some of these are, frankly, appalling. Nine bounties. Four escort requests. And four that I would classify as — * He paused. *Recreational.*

"Recreational."

*Someone is offering three gold pieces to recover a cat from a low-tier dungeon mouth. The cat is named Brindlefur. There is an illustration. It appears to be hand-drawn. The cat looks — Maren, the cat appears to be wearing a small hat in the illustration, and I cannot determine whether that is artistic license or an actual hat.*

Vess said nothing, which was its own kind of commentary.

"Three gold for a cat," Maren said.

*The bounty on your head is four hundred times that amount,* Aldric said. *In case you needed perspective.*

They passed through the gate without challenge. The two heroes there barely looked at them — one was eating something that dripped grease onto her gauntlet, and the other was arguing with a cart driver about road-damage fees. The argument was loud and specific, involving scorched wheel rims and a dispute over which hero's fire ability had done the actual damage. Maren kept his eyes forward, his pace unremarkable, his mouth shut.

Aldric did not keep his mouth shut.

*Gear shop. Gear shop. Another gear shop — that one specializes in elemental resistances, judging by the signage. Tavern. The sign says "The Cinder" and there are scorch marks on the sign itself, which I suspect is intentional. Tavern. That building has no sign and I have no idea what it is, but there is a significant amount of quest-light pooled against its foundation and someone has drawn a rude picture on the wall in what appears to be—*

The quest-light was the only part Maren could verify independently. It gathered in the gutters like pale, lukewarm water, faintly luminous even in full daylight. In Fenhold it collected in ditches and hollow stumps, barely enough to notice. Here it ran in actual rivulets between cobblestones, and where it pooled against walls the stone had taken on a glow like bone held against a candle flame. Maren stepped over a stream of it that ran across the road from a cracked drain and felt the warmth through his boot.

A man walked past them and raised one hand, casually, the way someone might wave to a friend. Something happened — a sharp, localized heat on the left side of his face, a crack like a branch splitting, and then a scorch mark appeared on the wall beside him, black and smoking. The man hadn't broken stride. He was adjusting the strap on his shoulder bag with the hand that had just put a burn mark on a building.

"What," Maren said, very quietly.

*Class-two fire projection. Casual discharge. He appears to be — ah. He moved up one rank on the damage board. He was testing a new calibration.* Aldric's tone carried the specific disbelief of a man who had once served an order that regarded System abilities as solemn responsibilities. *He scorched a wall. For a leaderboard position.*

*Damage rankings,* Vess said. *Overhead. Every building has one. It is a local hero ranking system based on verified damage output. Updated in real-time.*

Maren looked up and saw nothing but rafters and awning poles and the pale sky between them. The rankings were invisible to him, like everything else. He was standing in a town built entirely around information he couldn't access, navigating by scorch marks and smell and the commentary of dead people.

*The man who just scorched the wall is ranked sixty-three,* Aldric said. *He is now sixty-two. He appears to be — yes, he is checking. He has stopped walking to verify his new position. He looks pleased.* A pause. *A woman across the street appears displeased. I believe she was sixty-two. There is a — she is gesturing at him. He is gesturing back. This may escalate.*

The woman fired something at a wall further down the street. Another scorch mark. The heat reached him from twenty feet away. A child walking between them didn't flinch. Nobody flinched. This was weather to them.

Maren walked under the invisible leaderboards and the visible scorch marks and tried not to think about the four hundred cats' worth of bounty floating over his own head.

---

The market square was worse. Heroes were everywhere — not fighting, not questing, just existing in the specific way that people with System-enhanced abilities existed in their natural habitat. Two women were comparing weapon stats loudly enough that Maren could hear numbers he couldn't verify. A man in scaled armor was doing something to the air around his hands that made it shimmer and pop, drawing a small crowd of onlookers who apparently found this entertaining. A knot of younger heroes — teenagers, maybe, with the shiny-faced confidence of people who'd just gotten their first class assignments — clustered around the quest board, arguing about which dungeon had the best loot-to-risk ratio.

*She'd kill you in two moves,* Vess said.

Maren flinched before he realized she wasn't talking about anyone nearby. She was looking through his eyes at a woman leaning against a wall across the square, cleaning under her fingernails with a short knife.

*The woman with the knife. Two moves. Maybe one and a half, depending on your reaction time, which is poor.*

"Thanks."

*The man behind her — four moves. The boy at the fruit stall, the one in the green coat — he looks young but his stance is trained. Three, if he doesn't hesitate. Five, if he does. The woman with the ale — she's not a threat, she's had too much. The tall one at the corner—*

"Can you stop counting?"

*No.*

"Is there anyone in this town who couldn't kill me?"

*The cat on the quest board, presumably. Though I lack information about the hat.*

Derry had fallen behind again. Maren turned and found him three stalls back, nodding vigorously at a vendor who was showing him something wrapped in leather. Derry's smile was so wide it had to hurt. His shirt was soaked through at the collar and the dark stain was working its way down between his shoulder blades.

"What are you doing?"

"Just looking!" Derry's voice cracked upward, too loud, too cheerful. "This is — what did you say this was? A grip thing?"

The vendor — a broad woman with forearms like rolled rope — looked at Derry with the flat patience of someone who'd dealt with confused farmers before. "Grip wrap. For polearm users. Absorbs impact on the shaft."

"Right, right, grip wrap! My cousin uses a — he's got a thing, a pole, a spear thing, and he always says the grip is the most important—"

"You don't have a cousin with a spear," Maren said.

Derry's smile didn't falter so much as solidify, like plaster setting wrong. "I might. You don't know all my cousins."

"I know all your cousins. There are two. One raises goats and the other one can't lift his right arm above his shoulder."

The vendor looked between them with the expression of someone deciding whether to be amused or annoyed. Maren put a hand on Derry's arm and steered him away from the stall. Derry resisted for a moment, turned back, waved at the vendor, said "Sorry, we might come back, it's really nice stuff, the grip thing," and then let himself be moved.

"You have to stop doing that," Maren said, low.

"Doing what?"

"Buying things. Talking to people. Smiling like that."

"Like what?"

"Like a man who's about to be arrested and wants everyone to think he's having a nice day."

Derry wiped his face with both hands. The sweat came right back, beading along his hairline within seconds. "I'm trying. I am. I just — every time someone looks at me, my mouth opens, and it starts — I told that woman back there about Pell's counting. With the beads. The little counting frame with the beads. She didn't ask. She was selling rope. I told a rope vendor about my daughter's arithmetic."

*His cortisol levels must be extraordinary,* Aldric observed. *The man is producing enough sweat to—*

"Not helpful," Maren said, to Aldric and to no one visible, which earned him a glance from a passing hero that he pretended not to notice.

They needed food that would keep, and they needed to know which road past Harrow went toward the Crook without cutting through active dungeon territory. They needed to accomplish both without anyone looking at Maren long enough to see what wasn't there.

*The dried-goods vendor three stalls east,* Aldric said. *But the man sitting beside him — armored, large, on a crate — has a passive scan ability. I can see the active aura. He is reading the classification of everyone who passes within ten yards.*

"So we go around."

*The next nearest food vendor is across the square. Past the quest board. Past the two taverns. Past the table.*

Maren looked. The table was, in fact, on fire. A woman in scalemail sat next to it, laughing, while a man in a leather coat poured ale over the flames. The ale did nothing useful. A third person — a boy, maybe sixteen — was trying to smother the fire with his jacket and the jacket was also now on fire.

"Do they know," Maren said, "that the table is on fire?"

*They appear to consider it recreational.*

"And the jacket?"

*Also recreational, I must assume, given that no one is alarmed. The boy is — he is laughing now. The jacket is ruined. I want you to know that in fourteen years of service I never once set furniture on fire without tactical justification.*

Derry had stopped beside a stall selling polishing compound and was asking the vendor about the proper way to maintain leather boots, which was a topic he did actually know something about, and the vendor was answering with the weary tolerance of a man who'd had this exact conversation six hundred times. Derry's hands were in his pockets, but Maren could see the tremor in his forearms.

Maren reached the food vendor on the far side of the square — a thin man with sun-bleached hair and a stall that smelled like salt and old smoke. His provisions looked road-worthy: dried meat, hard bread, something in waxed paper that might have been cheese or might have been soap. Maren was reaching for his money when the vendor's eyes shifted.

Not to Maren's face. To a point slightly above and to the left of Maren's head — the place where, Aldric had explained weeks ago, System-sighted people saw the classification data that accompanied every person like a name pinned to a shirt.

The vendor looked at the spot. Looked at it again. His expression did something subtle and wrong — not suspicion, but the face a person makes when they reach for a step that isn't there.

"Class?" the vendor asked. Conversational. The way someone in any other town might ask *where are you from.*

"Sorry?"

"Your class. Just — you passing through? Most of my regulars are ranged-damage types, they like the jerky. Melee goes for the bread. What's your build?"

The hum in his chest intensified, though that might have been his heart. "Support class," he said, and he said it the way he'd said a thousand things over eleven years of fraud — with the easy, tired confidence of a man stating something too boring to lie about. "We're just passing through. Need road food."

"Support should still display, though." The vendor squinted at the empty space. "Everyone displays. Even noncombat roles. Healers, buffers, scouts — they all show a classification."

"Gear interference. It's been — look, it's a whole thing with my interface. It's a known issue."

The lie was smooth enough. Eleven years. But the vendor's hand was slow reaching for the jerky, and his eyes kept drifting back to the void where Maren's classification should have been, the way a person's eyes will return to a gap in a fence where a board should be — not alarmed, but bothered in a low, persistent way.

*He is not satisfied,* Aldric said. The sarcasm was gone. *He is going to—*

"Hey, Korr?" The vendor turned his head toward the armored man on the crate three stalls over. "Can you run a—"

Maren put coins on the counter. Too many — enough to buy the vendor's entire stock of jerky twice over. "Just the bread and the jerky. Keep the change. We're in a rush."

The vendor looked at the coins. Looked at the void. Looked at the coins again. Greed and curiosity fought a brief, visible war across his face, and greed won, but not by enough to stop his mouth.

"I've never seen a blank read," he said, handing over the food. "Not once. Not even broken gear gives you a blank. You'd get an error code, at least. Some kind of—"

Maren took the food and walked away. Behind him, the vendor was still talking — not to Maren anymore, but to the woman at the next stall, and Maren heard the word *blank* and then *weird* and then something he couldn't catch because Derry was beside him now, breathing hard, clutching a small jar of boot polish he'd apparently purchased.

"You bought boot polish," Maren said.

"She was very convincing."

"You're wearing sandals."

Derry looked down at his feet. He was, in fact, wearing sandals. He'd been wearing sandals for the entire trip. He looked at the boot polish in his hand with the expression of a man who has done something he cannot explain and knows it.

"We're leaving," Maren said. "Now. Before—"

*The woman at the stall,* Vess said. *The one the vendor spoke to. She has turned her head. She is looking at you.*

"Before that."

---

Maren didn't turn around. The road north forked past a tavern with a green-painted sign — he could see that much — and the left fork should take them toward dungeon territory and eventually the Crook. He angled toward it.

*She has stood up,* Vess said. *The woman. Blue cloak. She was seated at the stall where the man with the passive scan sits. She looked at you when you passed and now she is walking.*

"Could be nothing."

*She is walking at pursuit pace. Not running. Matching your speed at fifteen yards. She signaled with her off hand — military shorthand, two fingers, lateral sweep. That is a flanking directive.*

The market noise continued around them. The burning table. The leaderboard. The boy with the smoldering jacket, who had given up on his jacket and was now watching it burn on the cobblestones with his arms crossed. None of it had changed, but the air around Maren had turned thin, and the hum in his chest had become a specific, directional pressure, like a finger pressing on his sternum.

*Two more are moving from the tavern entrance on your right,* Vess said. *They were seated. They are not seated now. The one in front is tier four, minimum. He moves like someone who has done this before.*

Derry hadn't heard any of it — the fragments were inside Maren's skull — but he'd seen something change in Maren's face, or his pace, or the way his shoulders had come up, because Derry's own face drained to the color of old cheese.

"What?" Derry whispered. "What is it? What's—"

"Don't look behind us."

Derry immediately looked behind them.

"I said—"

"Sorry, sorry, I know, I just — there's a woman, she's sort of — she's walking fast—"

*She is not the primary concern,* Vess said. *The man from the tavern is. He has a scan active. A real one, not the passive ambient type. He is actively querying you. And he is getting—*

*Nothing,* Aldric said. His composure had cracked and the words came faster than his usual measured cadence. *He is getting nothing. The bounty posting describes a physical type that matches approximately forty percent of the male population in the region, which is why physical identification alone was never sufficient. But the System anomaly — the unclassified entity designation — is unique. She did not identify you by your face. She identified you by the absence. By the void where your classification should be. Your blankness is — in a town where every person, every animal, every piece of furniture with a System tag is visible and readable — your blankness is the loudest thing in this square.*

A voice behind them, not shouting but pitched to carry across a market without sounding like a command: "Hey. Support class. With the — stop walking, please."

*Please,* Vess noted. *That is professional. That is someone who has been trained to de-escalate before escalating.*

Maren did not stop. He walked faster. The distinction between walking fast and running was closing rapidly, and Derry was beside him, breathing in those awful wet hitches, the boot polish still in his hand.

"Oh god," Derry said. "Oh god, oh shit, Maren—"

"Walk."

"I'm walking. I'm walking, but they're — she's—"

*Run,* Vess said.

The word was flat, professional, a field assessment delivered without emphasis, and that was what made Maren's legs move. Vess didn't waste syllables. If she said run, the window for walking had closed.

They ran. Maren's pack bounced against his spine. Derry's sandals slapped the cobblestones — too loud, too flat, the wrong footwear for flight and the wrong man for it too. Behind them the voice called out again, louder, and other voices picked it up — not a mob, not yet, but the specific rising attention of a crowd that had noticed something interesting happening and was turning to watch.

*Left at the fork,* Aldric said, his formality abandoned entirely, directions barked like a man reading a map while the house burned. *Left, then straight, the road opens past the—*

*Right,* Vess said.

*I said left.*

*You said left because the road goes north. I said right because there are three heroes between us and your left fork and two of them have already turned.*

Maren went right. The turn took him between a shuttered gear shop and a squat building that stank of animal fat and tallow. Quest-light pooled in the alley's gutter, warm against his ankles when he splashed through it, and the scorch marks on the alley walls were so dense they'd merged into a continuous dark stain, years of hero traffic burned into the stone like a record of carelessness.

Derry was behind him. Still running. Still making that sound — not panting but something closer to the noise a man makes when he's trying very hard not to scream and the effort is coming out through his breathing instead.

*Alley opens onto a side street in twelve yards,* Vess said. *The side street runs downhill toward a secondary gate — a breach, actually, a wall section damaged by a high-tier ability that was never properly repaired. There is a gap wide enough for two.*

"How do you know that from in here?"

*I can see the structural data on the interface. The damage report is still filed. No repair ticket was ever issued. This town does not maintain its own walls.* A pause. *That is remarkably poor infrastructure management.*

They burst out of the alley onto the side street and Maren forced himself to slow. His lungs burned. His legs wanted to keep running but the side street had people on it — a woman with a handcart, two children playing some kind of game with System-visible markers Maren couldn't see, a man loading sacks onto a mule.

"Walk," Maren said. "Normal. Like we belong here."

Derry was doubled over beside him, hands on knees, making bellows sounds. The boot polish had fallen somewhere in the alley.

"I can't — I don't remember what normal—"

"Walk like you're going to check on the cow. The cow got out again. You're annoyed but not surprised."

Derry made a noise that was either a laugh or the first stage of something worse, but he straightened up and walked. The walk was terrible — stiff-armed, too rigid, a man wearing the idea of casual movement like an ill-fitting coat — but it carried him forward through the gap in the wall and onto the dirt road beyond.

The hum lessened. The quest-light thinned to occasional dull patches in the roadside ditches. The scorch marks became older, paler, spaced apart enough that the packed dirt between them looked like actual road again.

Behind them, Harrow went on — voices, the clatter of the hero economy, a distant crack that was either a System ability or another table meeting its end — but no one came through the gap. Not yet.

They walked. The road bent north into scrub hills where the soil was darker and the faint sweetish smell of old contamination hung in the motionless air. Late summer heat pressed down. The dust stuck to Derry's soaked shirt and turned it a muddy brown.

*They saw your face,* Aldric said, after a silence long enough to feel deliberate. He'd recovered his composure, or enough of it that the syntax was back — full sentences, subordinate clauses, the architecture of a man filing a report to an order that no longer existed. *Multiple heroes. In a town with a quest board, a bounty roster, and real-time interface updates. Your physical description combined with the unclassified entity anomaly is now a confirmed sighting. Not a rumor. Not a second-hand report. A verified reading by at least three System-equipped observers in a populated staging area.*

Maren didn't answer. He walked. The jerky and bread were in his pack, at least. His money was considerably lighter.

*Whatever approach relied on anonymity is finished,* Aldric said. *Moving quietly through populated areas is no longer viable. They will distribute the sighting through the standard bounty network. The next town, the next patrol, the next hero with a passive scan — they will know what to look for. Not a face. A void. Your absence is now your identifying characteristic.*

"I heard you."

*I am not certain you understand the—*

"I understand."

The paladin went quiet. Maren could feel him in there — the hovering disapproval, the half-composed lecture on tactical awareness — but for once Aldric chose not to deliver it, and the restraint was worse than the lecture would have been.

Derry was walking beside him with the rigid, mechanical gait of a man whose legs hadn't caught up with the fact that they were no longer sprinting. His hands shook. Not the subtle tremor from the market but a proper shake, the kind that made his fingers useless, and he pressed them flat against his thighs to make them stop and they didn't stop.

"Maren," Derry said.

"Yeah."

"I talked to a woman about cloaks."

"I know."

"I don't wear cloaks. I've never — I told her about Pell. About the counting beads. She was selling cloaks and I told her my daughter was learning to count. And then I bought boot polish." He looked down at his empty hands. "I was wearing sandals."

"Yeah."

"My hands won't stop."

Maren looked at Derry's hands. The shake was bad — fingers spread, the tendons visible, the kind of tremor you got when the body's terror outran the mind's ability to manage it. Maren's own hands were steady, and he didn't know what that meant. Maybe the killing two weeks ago had burned something out, some connection between fear and its physical expression. Or maybe his fear had just moved deeper, past the hands and the sweat and the shaking, down into the flat dry recognition that things were worse now and would keep getting worse and the road ahead led into dungeon territory because the road behind had his face on it.

His absence on it. The shape of nothing where a person should be.

*The Crook is four days north,* Vess said. *Five if the roads deteriorate past dungeon-approach standards.*

*If we reach the Crook,* Aldric said.

Vess did not answer. She didn't need to. The silence acknowledged what the words had laid out — that reaching the Crook was no longer the expected outcome but a thing that would need to be managed, negotiated, survived toward, with Maren's empty profile moving ahead of him like a signal fire.

The sun was high. The hum sat in Maren's chest, low and constant. Behind them, somewhere in Harrow, a woman in a blue cloak was filing a report that would make the next town harder and the town after that harder still, and the bounty that floated invisibly over Maren's head — four hundred cats, twelve hundred grip wraps, a number so large it bent the System's attention toward him like light through a lens — had just become specific.

Not a rumor anymore. A confirmed sighting. A void that walked and bought jerky and ran badly through alleys. A man-shaped gap in the System's architecture, seen and recorded in a town that existed to see and record exactly this kind of thing.

Maren walked north. The scorch marks on the road grew older and fewer, until there were none at all, and the road was just dirt, and the dirt was just the world, and the world didn't care what the System thought he was or wasn't.

But the people in it did. That was the problem. That had always been the problem.
