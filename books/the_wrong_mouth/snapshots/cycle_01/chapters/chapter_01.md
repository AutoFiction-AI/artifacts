# Chapter 1

The farmer had been dead two days by the time they sent for Maren, and the house smelled like it.

Not the body. Orla Dunnock had packed her husband in salt and dried lavender the way her mother had taught her, and Haff Dunnock lay on the kitchen table looking more like cured meat than a man who'd collapsed in his own barley field. The smell was the other thing. The sweet-ozone reek that crept in through the walls after someone died near dungeon territory, the contamination smell that meant residue was pooling somewhere close, settling into the dirt and the well water and the grain stores. Maren had smelled it in a dozen farmhouses across the Fenhold flats. It was his cue to arrive, look grave, and collect his fee.

He set the ceramic bowl on the table beside Haff's left hand. Close to the body but not touching, near enough that the family could see it mattered. The bowl was eleven years old, purchased from a market stall in Fenhold for four coppers when Maren was seventeen and had just decided that pretending to eat sins was easier than actually working. It had no System properties. It had no properties at all, besides being watertight and small enough to carry in a satchel without clinking against the candle holder. He'd chipped the rim twice and glued it back both times with pine resin, not because the bowl mattered but because the family needed to see a sin-eater who cared for his tools. The glue had darkened with age and looked, if you squinted, almost like a deliberate marking.

"Orla," he said. "I'm going to need quiet for a bit."

She nodded. She was standing in the doorway with her arms crossed over her chest, not from coldness but from the way people held themselves when they didn't know what to do with their hands. Her son Tam — fourteen, maybe, with his father's heavy jaw and his mother's set mouth — stood behind her, trying to look like he wasn't watching. He was watching.

Maren lit the candle. Beeswax, not tallow — he'd learned early that the wax mattered to people. Tallow smelled like the rendering they did every autumn, and nobody wanted rendering-smell mixed with their father's last rites. The beeswax cost him two coppers out of his twelve-copper fee, but the investment paid for itself in trust. The flame held steady in the still air, throwing the room into gold and harder shadow, making Haff's salt-packed face look almost dignified.

The hum was there, as it always was. Low, constant, like something large and electrical sitting just past the edge of hearing. Maren had heard it his whole life. He'd asked Derry once what the hum sounded like to someone who could see the System, and Derry had said, "What hum?" That told Maren everything he needed to know about being the only person in the world who noticed the background noise.

He placed his hands on the rim of the bowl. Pressed down until his knuckles whitened. This was the first posture — supplication, the sin-eater opening himself to receive. He'd copied it from a woodcut in a book he'd stolen from a traveling merchant when he was sixteen, and he still wasn't sure whether the woodcut was accurate or artistic license. It didn't matter. Orla and Tam had never seen a real sin-eater work. Neither had anyone else in Fenhold.

Maren closed his eyes and began the murmuring. Low, rhythmic, a not-quite-language that sounded devotional if you didn't listen too closely. He'd invented it. The first few years he'd been terrified someone would recognize that the words were nonsense — fragments of a lullaby his mother had sung, mixed with numbers and the names of rivers he'd crossed as a boy. But nobody had ever stopped him. Nobody had ever said, *that's not how the ritual goes.* Because nobody in Fenhold knew how the ritual went. Sin-eating was like plumbing: everyone agreed it needed doing, nobody wanted to watch it happen, and the only qualification the job really required was showing up.

He murmured. He pressed his hands against the bowl. He let his face do the work — the slight downward pull of the mouth, the furrowed brow, the quality of concentration that told Orla and her son something difficult and holy was happening in the space between the bowl and the body.

He watched them through his lashes. This was the part that mattered.

Orla's shoulders dropped an inch. Her breathing slowed. Tam shifted his weight from his left foot to his right, which meant he was relaxing into the performance rather than studying it. Maren catalogued the changes the way a carpenter reads grain — automatically, with professional interest, noting where the pressure sat and where it didn't. In another year, Tam would be the man of this house. He'd be the one to send for the sin-eater when a neighbor dropped. If Tam believed today, he'd believe then, and that was another twelve coppers every time death came to the Dunnock family's corner of the flats.

The contamination smell was stronger near the body. Sweet and sharp, like overripe fruit left in the sun with a metallic undertone that clung to the tongue. Maren had been tasting it for eleven years, in every farmhouse and field shed and dirt-floor chapel where he'd knelt beside the dead and pretended to eat what killed them. The taste never changed. Nothing ever happened when he performed the ritual. He'd stopped expecting it to, the way a man who knocks on the same locked door every morning eventually stops expecting anyone to open it but keeps knocking because the knocking itself has become the job.

He shifted to the second posture — reception. Hands lifted from the bowl, palms up, fingers spread. The stolen book had shown the sin-eater's hands in this position with lines of light flowing into them, which Maren had always assumed was embellishment. Quest-light pooled outside dungeon mouths like pale standing water, and sometimes a faint shimmer hung in the air after someone died — a residue, like heat-haze with color — but nothing had ever flowed into his hands. His hands were just hands.

He held the posture. Counted to forty in his head. Forty was the number he'd settled on years ago — long enough to look effortful, short enough that the family didn't start fidgeting. At thirty-two, he usually began the closing murmur, the rise in pitch that signaled the ritual was ending, that the sins had been eaten and the contamination cleared and the dead could rest.

He got to fourteen.

Something answered.

Not from the body. Not from outside the farmhouse. From a place Maren didn't have a word for — behind his sternum, or underneath his thoughts, or in the space where the hum had always lived. A pressure, sudden and enormous, like a fist closing around something he hadn't known was there. His hands locked. His fingers went rigid, still spread in the reception posture but no longer under his control. The air in his lungs stopped moving.

Then the paladin hit him.

Not from outside — there was no impact, no blow. It was an implosion. Everything Aldric had been — fourteen years of combat drills, the specific way to shift weight before a sword stroke, the prayers he'd said at dawn for a decade, the face of a woman he'd loved and never told, the smell of stone corridors in the Order's chapter house, the grief and the grief and the grief of a man who had died serving something he believed in — all of it crashed into Maren's skull like someone had kicked open a door to a room that had always been sealed and now everything was falling on him at once.

His spine arched. His hands snapped closed into fists he hadn't made. Something in his shoulders knew how to throw a punch and was trying to, right there at the kitchen table, Haff Dunnock's body three inches from his elbow. His right arm cocked back. His weight dropped to the balls of his feet in a fighting stance that belonged to a man he'd never met.

Orla said something. The words didn't reach him.

His legs moved wrong — not his legs, someone else's muscle memory trying to inhabit the same limbs, a body that knew how to pivot and advance and drop its center of gravity for a shield-brace Maren had never learned and could not stop performing. His knee hit the table leg. The candle toppled. Beeswax spattered across Haff's salt-crusted shirt, and the thought that surfaced was absurd and entirely his: *that cost me two coppers.*

Then the voice.

It came from inside him — not through his ears, not arriving from any direction. It occupied the same space as his own thoughts but was clearly, violently not his. A voice like a man reading from a scroll. Formal, clipped, absolutely certain of its own authority.

*Kneel.*

Maren's knees tried to buckle. Not from weakness — from obedience. The paladin's body had known how to kneel in prayer the way Maren's body knew how to smile at a mark, and the kneeling was trying to happen through his legs without his consent.

*Kneel and pray. Now. We are in the presence of the dead and you will show respect.*

He braced against the table. His fingernails scraped salt from the wood. Orla was backing toward the doorway, pulling Tam with her, and even through the chaos of his own muscles betraying him, Maren saw her face — not fear exactly, but the alarm of something that was supposed to be safe becoming unpredictable. She'd trusted him. Twelve coppers of trust, beeswax-scented and professional, and the sin-eater she'd hired was thrashing against her kitchen table with his eyes wet and his arms doing things arms shouldn't do.

*Kneel. I will not ask again.*

"Get — off —" The words came out choked and wrong because his throat was doing something new, trying to shape words in a register too low for his vocal cords, the paladin's baritone fighting his tenor for the same mouth.

The memories kept coming. A sword pommel in cold rain. The weight of plate armor on shoulders that had carried it so long the body didn't know how to stand without it. A young man, maybe twenty, taking his first oath in a chapel where quest-light pooled in stone fonts like colored water — and the oaths meant something, every word, and the man saying them believed with the specific ferocity of someone who had never yet been disappointed by the thing he served.

Maren's eyes were wet and he didn't know whose grief it was.

He stumbled backward. His hip caught the chair. He went down, one hand on the floor, the bowl still on the table — he'd placed it with a con man's instinct for keeping props out of the violence, pushed back against the wall, away from the thrashing — and his stomach finally revolted.

He made it outside. Barely. The farmhouse door was four steps from the table and he covered them in three, hand on the frame, the late-summer sun hitting his face like an accusation. The ditch beside the path was dry, weedy, humming with something small and persistent in the grass. He fell to his knees — not prayer, just gravity — and threw up.

It was not a dignified thing. It was loud and convulsive and it tasted like the contamination, ozone and rot, as though his body was trying to expel whatever had entered through the only method it understood. His hands pressed into the dirt. Small stones bit into his palms. His stomach clenched again and he spat bile into the dead grass, strings of it hanging from his lip while his back heaved and the muscles along his ribs spasmed in patterns that weren't his — the aftershock of combat training forcing itself into a body that had never been drilled.

The hum was louder now, much louder, and beneath it the paladin's voice, still insisting, still certain:

*This is a desecration. You have defiled the ritual. You must — you must account for this.*

"I'm throwing up," Maren said into the weeds. "In a ditch. Give me a minute."

*You were not performing the ritual correctly. You were not performing it at all. You were — what were you doing?*

"My job." He spat. The taste didn't go away. "What are you?"

*I am Aldric of the Order of the Veil. I am a knight-paladin of the Fourth Seal. And I appear to be dead. And I appear to be inside — what is your classification?*

"My what?"

*Your System classification. Your class. Your level. Your — you are not a sin-eater. The ritual was wrong. Every element of it was wrong. What are you?*

Maren pressed his forehead into the dirt. It was warm from the sun and gritty against his skin. Behind him, through the farmhouse door, Orla was talking fast and low to Tam, the way people talked when they were deciding whether to run or whether to bar the door.

"I'm a fraud," he said, because it was easier than lying right now and because the truth had a kind of weight to it when you were on your knees in a ditch with bile on your chin. "I've been faking this for eleven years. Nothing ever answered. You — you shouldn't be here."

The silence that followed was not the silence of someone thinking. It was the silence of someone reassessing every assumption he'd made in the last thirty seconds and finding none of them intact.

*You are not a sin-eater.*

"No."

*You have been performing false rituals over the dead for eleven years.*

"Yes."

*In villages. Over people who trusted you.*

"Yes."

*And the contamination — every ritual you have performed — it was never cleared. It is still there. In every village, every field, every —*

"I know."

Another silence. Then, in a voice that had lost its formal clipping and become something rawer, something closer to a man than a title:

*What kind of person does this?*

Maren spat again. His stomach was done but his hands were shaking, a tremor that started in his wrists and ran up to his elbows, and he couldn't tell whether it was the vomiting or the paladin's outrage or his own muscles trying to sort out which movement patterns actually belonged to him.

"The kind who's lying in a ditch," he said. "That's what I've got for you right now."

He didn't go back into the farmhouse. Orla brought the bowl out — held it at arm's length, like something that might bite — and set it on the path beside the ditch without speaking. She didn't ask for the twelve coppers. She didn't ask anything. She went back inside and the bar slid into place, and that was fair enough.

Maren sat on the edge of the ditch with the bowl in his lap, turning it in his hands. The chip on the rim where he'd dropped it three years ago in Kenning was smooth under his thumb. The pine resin had darkened with handling. Eleven years of fake rituals, and the bowl was the most honest thing about the whole operation — it had never pretended to be anything other than a piece of fired clay.

The sun was lower. He'd been in the ditch longer than he'd thought. Shadows stretched from the farmhouse across the stubbled barley field, and the hum had settled from its earlier spike into something thick and constant, like a headache that had decided where to live.

*Your pulse is elevated. Your hydration is insufficient. You have lost approximately —*

"Stop."

*I am providing a medical assessment.*

"I don't want a medical assessment."

*You have vomited extensively. You are sitting in a ditch. You are, by your own admission, a fraud who has allowed contamination to accumulate in populated areas for over a decade. The least you can accept is a medical assessment.*

The voice had settled. It was still inside him, still occupying that impossible space behind his thoughts, but it had gone from raw and panicked to cold, the way a man who'd been shouting might suddenly drop to a quiet, clipped tone because shouting hadn't worked. Maren knew the shift. He'd used it himself, on marks who needed a different register of pressure.

"All right," he said. "Give me the medical assessment."

*Your System profile reads as follows —*

"I don't have a System profile."

*Incorrect. You have a profile. It is unusual. It reads as — it reads as empty. Not absent. Empty. There is a classification field and it contains nothing. There is a level field and it contains nothing. There are skill allocations and they —*

"They contain nothing. Yeah." He rubbed the grit from his forehead. "I'm System-blind. I've been blind my whole life. I can't see the interface. I can't see the overlays or the skill descriptions or the threat levels. None of it. I've never seen any of it."

The quality of silence changed. Maren had been reading silences professionally for a decade — the silence of a widow deciding whether to pay, the silence of a village elder deciding whether to believe, the silence of Derry when Maren told him the take was less than it actually was — and this one carried something he hadn't heard from the paladin before. Not outrage. Not disgust. Something more disoriented, like a man reading a map who'd just discovered it was upside down.

*You are System-blind.*

"That's what I said."

*And you have been performing the sin-eating ritual — you have been performing it for eleven years — without seeing the interface.*

"Without seeing anything. Yeah."

*Without seeing the contamination patterns, the residue concentrations, the — how? How have you been doing this?*

"I watch people." He rubbed his eyes. His fingers smelled like dirt and stomach acid. "I watch what they do when the System prompts them. When someone's threat indicator changes, they flinch — it's subtle, but they all do it. When a quest notification comes through, people's eyes track something I can't see. I learned to read the room instead of the screen. And Derry — he's my partner, my — he feeds me the details I can't get. The numbers, the levels, whatever I need to sound like I know what I'm talking about."

*You have been reading a room for eleven years instead of doing the actual work.*

"Yeah, well. Nobody complained."

*Nobody complained because nobody knew the contamination was not being cleared. They trusted you. They —*

"I know what they did."

His voice came out harder than he'd intended. His hands had stopped shaking, which was something, but his shoulders were knotted and there was a new kind of tension in his back — the paladin's posture trying to assert itself, a spine that wanted to be straighter than Maren had ever held his own.

The air smelled like contamination and beeswax smoke and the specific vegetable rot of a ditch in late summer. A fat black fly was working the inside of the bowl. Maren flicked it away.

*I am going to read you your current interface status. In full.*

"I don't —"

*You need to hear this. You have needed to hear this for twenty-eight years, and I will not sit inside the skull of an uninformed man. I find the prospect morally untenable.*

Maren almost laughed. Morally untenable. The dead paladin in his head found the arrangement morally untenable. The arrangement where Maren was sitting in a ditch covered in his own sick, having just lost the only steady work he'd ever had, Orla Dunnock's door barred against him and his reputation in this corner of the flats probably finished — *that* was the part the paladin found untenable.

"Fine," he said. "Read me the interface."

*System designation: Unregistered. No class assigned. No level assigned. Skill allocations: none. Active quest involvement: none. Passive quest contamination exposure: significant — I will return to that item. Local threat assessment: ambient. There are three active quests within a two-mile radius of your current position. The nearest is a contamination-clear request, priority low, assigned to a registered sin-eater operating in the Fenhold flats.*

The paladin paused.

*That would be you. Except you are not registered. And not a sin-eater. And the contamination has never been cleared. The accumulation log shows eleven years of uncollected residue in the surrounding agricultural district. Estimated affected area encompasses the entire Fenhold flats. Contamination density in several zones exceeds safe habitation thresholds.*

The voice did something it hadn't done before. It slowed, and the slowing was not emphasis but something involuntary — the cadence of a man who was reading and processing the data at the same time and not liking what the processing returned.

*There are children living in this.*

Maren said nothing. The fly came back to the bowl.

*Continuing. Nearby entity profiles. Orla Dunnock: farmer, level four, no combat classification, threat assessment negligible. Tam Dunnock: unclassed, juvenile, pre-assignment. The deceased —*

A longer pause.

*Haff Dunnock, farmer, level six, non-combat. Cause of death listed by the System as environmental exposure, subcategory: contamination-related organ failure. Long-term.*

Maren's hands stopped moving on the bowl.

*The farmer died of contamination exposure. Accumulated. Over years. In the agricultural district where you have been performing false rituals for —*

"I heard you."

*Then you understand that his death —*

"I said I heard you."

He stared at the chip in the rim. The pine resin. The fired clay underneath, grey-brown, ordinary. He'd bought it the same week he'd done his first fake ritual, over the body of a woman named Grella whose family had given him milk and bread afterward because they thought he'd done something holy. He could still taste that bread, the heavy grain of it, the warm crust.

Silence. Then the paladin continued, and the formal cadence had reconstituted itself, stiff and precise, the way a man who has nearly broken composes himself by retreating to the language of reports.

*Equipment loadout. One ceramic bowl, no System classification, no System value. One candle holder, iron, no System classification, no System value. One leather satchel, no System classification, no System value. Total combat readiness: nonexistent. Total threat profile to local entities: negligible.*

Another pause, shorter.

*Total threat profile to the System: I am unable to evaluate. Your ambient signature — the impression the System reads when it encounters your presence — is unlike anything in my reference frame. I had access to Order of the Veil records for fourteen years, comprehensive records, and nothing in those archives describes what your profile looks like from this vantage. You are not merely unregistered. You are something the System does not have a category for. And it has a category for everything.*

A bird called from somewhere behind the farmhouse. The sound was two short notes rising, and after them a silence that felt too large.

"So what does that mean?" Maren said. "For me. Practically."

*Practically. It means you are the most confusing thing I have encountered in fourteen years of service, and I once encountered a dungeon that had organized its threat creatures alphabetically.* A beat. *It means your System profile is a gap. A hole. And holes in the System are not natural and they are not ignored. It means I am dead and I am inside a gap, and I — I have no idea what happens next.*

The formal cadence wobbled. Just for a moment, under the military syntax and the moral certainty, the voice of a man who had been dead for however long he'd been dead and was registering what that meant — not just dead, but dead and gone from everything he'd been, the Order and the chapel and the dawn prayers and the woman whose face Maren had caught in the flood of memories, gone from all of it and put instead inside the skull of a man who faked miracles for twelve coppers, with no body and no way to kneel and no way out.

Then the cadence came back. Stiff. Deliberate.

*Continuing the interface reading. Your current stamina allocation: none. Your mana reserve: none. Resistance profiles: none. Speed modifier: base human, unenhanced. Perception modifier: base human, unenhanced, with the noted System-blindness anomaly. Strength modifier —*

"Base human. Yeah. I get it."

*I am being thorough.*

"You're reading me a list of all the ways I'm nothing."

*I am reading you the System interface. These are the contents. If they happen to constitute a comprehensive inventory of your inadequacies, that is a property of accurate data, not editorial commentary on my part.*

Maren pressed his thumb into the chip on the bowl. The resin was warm from his hands.

"You know what," he said. "Keep going."

*Your current position relative to the nearest dungeon mouth: one point three miles, bearing northeast. Contamination concentration along that vector: heavy, distributed across agricultural parcels, concentrating in low-lying terrain and water collection points. Number of occupied farmsteads within the contamination zone: fourteen. Estimated population of those farmsteads: sixty to seventy people, including approximately twenty children under the age of —*

"I said keep going. I didn't say kill me with it."

*I am reading the interface.*

"You're reading it like a man who's figured out it's the only weapon he's got."

Silence. When the voice came back, something in it had shifted — still cold, still formal, but the edge had changed from prosecutorial to something else, something Maren couldn't read yet because he'd only known this voice for an hour and hadn't built the map.

*That is not entirely inaccurate.*

Maren leaned back against the weedy bank of the ditch. The sky was enormous above him, late-summer blue going to copper at the edges where the sun was deciding whether to set. His body was mostly his again. The combat reflexes had quieted. His spine still wanted to be straighter than he'd ever held it, and there was a residual stiffness in his shoulders that felt like someone else's military posture trying to settle into civilian use. But his hands were his. His legs were his. His stomach was empty and sore and entirely his own.

*One additional item.*

"Go ahead."

*I can see the contamination. Not the data — the actual contamination, the residue itself. From this vantage, from inside you, looking through whatever this arrangement is, it reads differently than it did when I was alive. It is not merely data overlays. It has physical weight. Texture. Something close to color but not precisely color. The barley field there is saturated. The soil under your hands is saturated. The air around the farmhouse is — the contamination you were hired to clear, in this field and every other field you have visited for eleven years, is not a number on a screen. It is a physical presence in the ground where people grow their food and draw their water and raise their children.*

"Yeah," Maren said. "I know."

But he didn't, really. He'd always known, in the abstract, that the contamination was there. He could smell it. He could see the faint shimmer at the edges of his vision when the light was low. He'd just never had someone describe it to him with the measured fury of a man who had spent his life believing that every job existed because the world needed it done.

He sat there for a while. The bird called again. The fly found the bowl again. The paladin was quiet, but it wasn't the quiet of absence — it was the quiet of a man who was choosing, for the moment, not to speak, because he was afraid that the next thing he said would break something that couldn't be fixed.

Maren held the bowl. The ceramic was blood-warm from his hands. Eleven years of fake rituals, and the first real thing that had ever happened during one was a dead man reading him, in specific and itemized detail, the exact dimensions of the damage.

He flicked the fly away.

"I'm Maren," he said. "Since we're — since you're going to be in here. I'm Maren. Twenty-eight. From Fenhold, originally. I've been faking the sin-eating since I was seventeen. I can't see the System. I can't see anything the System does. And I don't know what you are or why you're here, but I'm not going to pray. I should tell you that now."

*I gathered.*

"And I'm going to need the bowl. Whatever happens next — I don't know what happens next — but I'll need the bowl."

*The bowl has no System properties whatsoever.*

"I know. I still need it."

Silence. Not hostile. There was nothing warm available between them, and wouldn't be — that was already clear — but the temperature had dropped from active fury to something closer to the resigned irritation of two people stuck in the same room during a storm, each offended by the other's presence but unable to leave. The paladin was inside Maren's skull the way a sound lived in a bell. Whatever had put him there wasn't offering a way back out.

*Then keep the bowl,* the paladin said. *And tomorrow you will explain to me in full what you have been doing in these villages. Every village. Every ritual. Every family who paid you for a service you did not perform. I want a complete accounting.*

"Tomorrow."

*Tomorrow. I have — I have read enough for today.*

Something in the repetition. In the way *I have read enough* landed — not as moral exhaustion but as something plainer, closer to the bone. The paladin had been reading the interface the way a man performs triage, cataloguing the damage because the cataloguing was the only tool available, and now the tool was spent. He was dead. He was in a ditch. His Order was somewhere behind him and his body was gone and his prayers would go to no one because he had no knees to kneel on and no hands to fold, and the mouth that could have spoken them belonged to someone who had just admitted to eleven years of spiritual fraud.

Maren didn't say anything to this. Sincerity wasn't a skill he'd practiced. He knew what it looked like — he'd watched it in other people for decades, the way their faces opened and their voices dropped and their hands went still — but performing it and feeling it were different operations, and he had only ever been trained in the first.

The sun set behind the barley field. The contamination smell didn't fade — it never faded — but the light changed the way it sat in the air, the faint shimmer of residue going from invisible in direct sun to something you could almost see at the edges, like heat-haze with a tint, pooling in the low places between the furrows.

Maren sat in the ditch with a dead paladin in his head and a chipped ceramic bowl in his lap and the taste of bile still on his teeth, and the thought that came was not large or dramatic or worthy of the moment. It was small, and practical, and it was his:

He was going to need water. And he was going to need to figure out what to tell Derry. And he was going to need to not be in this ditch when morning came, because Orla Dunnock would open that door eventually, and when she did, the look on her face would be worse than anything the paladin had read to him from the interface.

The hum went on. It always had.
