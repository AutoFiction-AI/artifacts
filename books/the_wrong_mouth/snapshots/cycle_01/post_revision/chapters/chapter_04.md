# Chapter 4

The road south from Fenhold was packed earth going to powder in the late-summer heat, and Maren's boots left prints that filled in behind him like the ground couldn't be bothered to remember he'd passed. Two days of walking. Two days of Aldric's commentary — quest markers, threat assessments, ambient contamination readings, hero patrol routes — delivered in the cadence of a man filing reports to an order that no longer existed. Two days of Derry's nervous chatter about anything that wasn't the bounty, wasn't the voice in Maren's head, wasn't the fact that they'd abandoned everything and the abandoning hadn't made them safe.

*Quest notification. Range: approximately three hundred meters, northeast. Level eight, expired. Residual contamination minimal.*

"Thanks," Maren said.

Derry glanced back from two steps ahead — the distance he'd been keeping since Fenhold, not leading so much as placing space between them without committing to it. "What?"

"Nothing."

Derry's shirt was dark between the shoulder blades. He'd been adjusting and readjusting the straps of his pack every half mile, a habit that had nothing to do with the fit and everything to do with needing his hands occupied. He was carrying bread from the last village they'd skirted — he'd traded a copper for a loaf and a copper for directions south, and both transactions had taken him three times as long as necessary because he'd gotten the baker talking about her oven and the directions man talking about road conditions and Derry filled silences like water running downhill, automatic and effortless.

"You keep talking to yourself," Derry said. He wiped his face with his sleeve. "People notice that."

"There's no people."

"There will be. Settlement somewhere past this ridge — I saw chimney smoke this morning."

*Correct. Settlement bearing west-southwest, approximately two miles. Population estimate based on ambient System activity: forty to fifty individuals. Agricultural. No hero presence detected.*

The information scrolled through Maren's skull in Aldric's voice. Three days of this and Maren still couldn't decide if the constant narration was a gift or a punishment. Both, probably. The paladin translated the entire visible architecture of a world Maren had lived in for twenty-eight years without seeing, and he did it with the slight, persistent distaste of a man reading ingredients off a package he wouldn't eat from.

They walked. The sun sat heavy on the road and the dust had a specific quality out here, finer than Fenhold's, more like chalk than dirt. It got into Maren's throat and stayed. Each stone came through the thin soles of his boots — he'd been meaning to get them resoled for two seasons, and now that was the kind of problem that belonged to a life he'd walked away from.

Derry started talking about whether they should cut around the settlement or push through it, whether the detour added an hour or two, whether there'd be a well. He talked as he always did when he was scared: too much and about nothing, each sentence hitched to the next with "and" and "but" until the original thought disappeared under qualifications. His hands kept going to the pack straps. Pull. Adjust. Release. Pull.

Maren let him talk. It was familiar.

Then the hum changed.

He'd been hearing it his whole life — that low-frequency drone at the edge of perception, louder near dungeon mouths and contamination zones, the sound of the System doing whatever the System did. Most people filtered it out unconsciously. Maren couldn't. His brain had nothing to filter it against. It was always there, under everything else, like the sound of his own blood.

But now it thickened. Got denser, one note fattening into a chord. Something ahead of them, past the next rise where the road dipped between two low hills.

"Stop," Maren said.

Derry stopped. "What?"

*Hold on.* Aldric's voice shifted — less narration, more attention. The difference between reading aloud and actually looking at something. *Quest marker. Abandoned. Level twelve, deactivated approximately three weeks ago. The contamination was not cleared after deactivation. It has been pooling.*

The smell hit next. Ozone-sweet and rotten underneath, the specific reek of quest residue left to ferment in summer heat. Fresh contamination smelled almost clean, chemical. This had been sitting for weeks, baking in the warmth, and it had turned — that sour undertone like fruit going to vinegar, the smell of something biological beginning to break down.

"Yeah, I can smell it," Derry said. He'd gone still. His hands were locked on the pack straps and not moving. "That's contamination. From a quest?"

"Abandoned one." Maren walked to the top of the rise and looked down.

The hollow was maybe thirty paces across, a natural depression where two low ridges met. Quest-light pooled in it like rainwater — Maren could see the shimmer, that pale glow that was all the System's brilliance amounted to from his side of the blindness. Without the data Aldric could read overlaid on it, it was just light caught in a dip in the ground. Pretty, almost, if you didn't know what you were looking at. The contamination sat above the pool like humidity, thick enough that the grass at the edges had yellowed and gone brittle. No insects. Even the flies had pulled back.

*Contamination density is increasing at approximately four percent per day. The topography is funneling the drift westward — toward the settlement. At current rate, it reaches the settlement boundary in six to eight days. If it contacts their water source first, which is likely given the drainage pattern, the timeline may be as little as four.*

*In children especially,* Aldric added, and his voice had changed register, moved from System narration into something that sounded almost like the prayer cadence Maren had heard in those first terrible hours, *the effects of sustained exposure at this density are — significant.*

"We should go around," Derry said. He'd come up behind Maren and was staring at the hollow with the expression of someone who understood exactly what he was looking at and wanted no part of it. "There's got to be a way around. We add an hour, maybe two, and we just—"

"Four days until it hits their water."

"And someone will clear it. A real sin-eater, someone who actually — Maren. Come on."

Derry was right. They had a bounty on their heads — four hundred gold, visible to every hero in three regions. Every hour they stood still was an hour for someone to close the distance. The smart play was to walk wide, add the detour, keep moving. Let whoever was supposed to clear the contamination clear it.

But nobody was going to clear it. The quest had been abandoned three weeks ago and no sin-eater had come, and Maren knew as surely as he knew the weight of his own ceramic bowl in his pack, the specific heft of eleven years of fraud, exactly why. There weren't enough sin-eaters. There never were. The work piled up faster than the workers could manage, and the System kept generating quests and the quests kept generating contamination and the contamination kept pooling in hollows like this one, drifting toward settlements where farmers drew water from streams and children played in the fields and no one thought about what was seeping through the soil because someone was supposed to have cleaned it up.

Someone like him. Who hadn't.

"Maren." Derry's voice was careful now. "We've got a bounty. Every hero in range can see it. We don't have time to—"

"I know."

*You cannot be serious.* Aldric's voice had gone clipped, which meant he'd already assessed the situation and was marshaling objections in formal order. *You have never performed a genuine consumption. You do not know the ritual sequence. You do not understand the containment protocols. The failure modes of a Level twelve consumption attempted without training or supervision—*

"What happens if nobody clears it?"

Silence from Aldric. Then, quietly: *You know what happens.*

"Tell me how to do it."

*I am a paladin. I studied sin-eating theory within the Order. I did not practice the discipline. The difference between theoretical knowledge and practical execution in this specific—*

"So it's theoretical." Maren was already walking down the slope. His boots slid in the dry grass and he caught himself with one hand against the warm earth. "Walk me through the theory."

---

He set up at the edge of the pool as he'd set up a thousand times before. Bowl on flat ground, candle to the left, himself kneeling with his palms on his thighs. The postures of his old life. He could do these in his sleep, had done them in his sleep once — Derry shaking him awake at a farmer's bedside and Maren's arms already moving through the opening gestures before his eyes were open.

But the intention was different, and the difference sat in his chest like a fist, and his chest didn't want to open.

*The containment pass must come first. You establish the boundary of the consumption zone before attempting any clearing action. Think of it as — you are creating a vessel around the existing pool. Not with the bowl. With your awareness. Your attention. The real ritual requires genuine openness to the contamination. You allow it to contact your consciousness without your consciousness rejecting the contact. This is the component that cannot be faked.*

"Great. How do I do that?"

*I — that is precisely the gap between theory and practice. The texts describe it as a form of acceptance. You are not consuming the contamination yet. You are acknowledging it. Allowing it proximity.*

Maren looked at the pool. The shimmer was right there, an arm's length away. Up close, the contamination had physical weight. The glow itself was faint and directionless; the real force was pressure on the skin, like standing too close to an oven. His forearms broke into gooseflesh. The sweet-rotten smell was thick enough to coat his tongue.

His body knew what contamination meant the same way it knew what cliff edges and snapping dogs meant. By staying away.

"Maren." Derry stood at the top of the ridge, both hands wrapped around his pack straps, his knuckles pale. "I don't — look, this doesn't seem like a good idea, and I know you don't want to hear that, but I don't — do you actually know what you're doing? Because if you don't, maybe we could find someone, or — I mean, we could leave a message at the settlement, tell them there's contamination, let them send for—"

"Send for who, Derry?"

Derry's mouth worked for a moment. Then he looked away.

Maren knelt. The ground at the pool's edge was damp and warm, heat radiating up through his knees, and the dampness soaked through his trousers immediately. He placed his hands on his thighs and tried to do the thing he'd been pretending to do for eleven years.

Opening. That was Aldric's word. Opening yourself to the contamination. Letting it in.

For eleven years, every ritual Maren had performed was about closing. Closing the performance. Sealing the lie. Keeping the boundary between what he appeared to be and what he was. Every gesture, every murmured word, every careful angle of the bowl was designed to keep the contamination out while looking like it was being let in. His whole career was a locked door dressed up as an open one.

Now he had to actually unlock it, and every instinct he had pulled the other way.

He breathed. The contamination smell flooded his sinuses — ozone and rot and that chemical sweetness, like something organic being dissolved. His eyes watered. His throat clenched and he swallowed bile and kept breathing because Aldric had said openness and openness meant not pulling back when every nerve said pull back.

The first contact was heat. It came from inside, as if the contamination had found its way through his skin and was warming him from the blood outward. His fingers tingled against his thighs. The hum rose in pitch, or his hearing sharpened, or both.

*Good. That is — yes, that is initial contact. Now the containment pass. You must establish the boundary before—*

But Maren's body was already moving.

The old choreography — the broad sweeping arm motion he'd used in a thousand fake rituals, the one he always did first because it was dramatic and the paying families liked drama — pulled at his muscles like a reflex. Before Aldric finished speaking, Maren was halfway through the clearing pass, his right arm tracing the arc that had never cleared anything, that had always been pure theater.

Except now the contamination was inside him. And the clearing pass, done before containment, opened a channel that wasn't bounded by anything.

*No — Maren, stop. That is the clearing motion. You have not established containment. You are doing it in the wrong—*

The contamination surged.

The pool of quest-light flared — bright enough that Maren could see it without any help, raw light now, the pale yellow-white of a wound going septic. The hum spiked to a whine that drilled through his back teeth and settled behind his eyes. And the contamination that had been sitting in its hollow pushed outward through the uncontained channel his botched ritual had opened, hitting him like swallowing a river.

It went through him. Straight through. And it brought everything that was embedded in it.

She hit like a blade between his eyes.

Aldric had arrived as weight and grief, a presence settling into Maren's skull with the heft of a man sitting down in a room he didn't want to be in. This came in edges. Fast, angular, precise even in violence. Something landing in his consciousness like a knife in a doorframe — point first, quivering, still carrying the force of the throw.

Maren's hands spasmed. The bowl tipped sideways and rolled into the warm mud. The candle went out. He was on all fours with his fingers digging into the wet earth while something new rearranged itself behind his eyes.

*What—* Aldric started.

*Where am I.*

A demand, flat and immediate. And in the half-second before Maren's body seized — his back arching, his fingers clawing furrows in the mud — the new voice was already cataloguing. Location unknown. Position compromised. No weapons. Body not hers. Someone else's hands in the dirt, someone else's lungs pulling someone else's air, and another presence already here, reading as — holy order, formal classification, a paladin —

*You have got to be—*

The collision happened.

The fragment's arrival had already happened. The collision was what came next, when two opposed System classifications occupied the same unclassifiable space. Maren felt it like a bone breaking — the wrongness first, the deep structural protest of something being forced past its design tolerance. His vision whited out. His jaw locked. Something inside his skull crackled like ice splitting in a glass, and his body moved without his direction — his hands lifting from the mud and tracing patterns he'd never learned, his head turning to track something that only the fragments could see, his spine straightening and then curving as if pulled by two different forces at once.

The quest-light around them flared to a brightness that hurt. The hum climbed past hearing into something felt, a vibration in his teeth and his sternum and the bones of his wrists. Even the ground seemed to shift — the warm damp earth going cold under his knees and then hot again, as if the land itself couldn't decide what it was near.

Then it stopped.

He was face-down at the edge of the hollow, covered in mud and his own sweat. The candle was out. The bowl was somewhere behind him. And inside his skull, where there had been two voices — his own thoughts and Aldric's careful narration — there were now three, and the three didn't fit.

*— the hell are you,* the new voice said, directed at Aldric. No warmth. No curiosity. The tone of someone clearing a room.

*I am — I was — Aldric, Knight-Servitor of the Order of the—*

*A paladin. In this host.* The word "host" landed with a contempt so specific it had texture. *And the host is — what's wrong with this body? Why can't I see the interface? Why can't I—*

She stopped. The silence lasted two full breaths.

*I'm dead.*

Maren pushed himself up to his knees. His arms shook. He could taste copper and ozone and the sweet rot of contamination that had passed through him and left residue on everything — his tongue, his teeth, the lining of his throat.

*I was on a contract.* The voice had gone flat — not calm but forcibly stabilized, like a table propped level on an uneven floor. *Torren Blackhall. Third bell. East gallery. Shift change with a blind corner at the stair. I was three days from finishing it. I was—*

She stopped again. Recalibrated. Maren could feel her doing it — a mind faster than his reorganizing its own architecture around information that changed everything.

*How long.*

"What?" Maren's voice came out wrecked, scraped raw from the bile and the ozone.

*How long have I been dead.*

"I don't — I don't know. The quest's been abandoned three weeks, but you could've been in the contamination longer than—"

*I didn't ask about the quest. How long since I died.*

*The contamination layers suggest the fragment has been embedded for—* Aldric began, falling back into his report cadence.

*I wasn't asking you.*

"I don't know," Maren said. He sat back on his heels. His hands had gone still, which surprised him, because nothing else had. "I'm sorry. I really don't know."

Derry was shouting something from the ridge. Maren could hear him but couldn't process the words, because two voices had started talking in his skull and his own thoughts were trying to find room between them.

*The host is System-blind,* Aldric said, addressing the new presence with the careful diction of a man explaining a fire to someone standing in it. *He cannot see the interface. I have been serving as—*

*System-blind.* A pause — not hesitation, calculation. *And you're reading the interface for him. A paladin, playing guide dog for a System-blind — what is he? What's his class?*

*That is — somewhat complicated.*

*Uncomplicate it.*

*He is a sin-eater. Fraudulently. For eleven years. He has no genuine training. What you just experienced was his second real consumption. The first was me. He does not know what he is doing. The System has placed a bounty on him because his classification has—*

*A fraud.* The voice — Vess, the name surfaced from somewhere in the fragment, sharp-edged and exact — went flat in a way that made Aldric's formality sound like warmth. *I was consumed by a fraud. In a botched ritual. In a ditch.*

"It's a hollow," Maren said, because his mouth still worked faster than his judgment.

*The distinction doesn't help your case.*

Aldric made a sound that might have been agreement. The first thing the paladin and the assassin had agreed on.

*So I'm dead.* Each word deliberate, placed like a stone on a scale. *I'm a fragment. In a System-blind fraud. With a paladin. My contract is open. Blackhall is still alive. My guild doesn't know what happened to me—*

She hit a gap. Maren felt it — a hitch in the current of her thoughts, like a foot stepping where a stair should be and finding air. She'd reached for something and it wasn't there.

*My handler. I should — the name was—*

The gap again. Wider this time, and Maren could feel the edges of it — not blurred, not uncertain, but cleanly cut, as if something had been removed with precision rather than lost through decay.

*There's a piece missing.* There was no panic in it. Just the clinical notation of a professional discovering one of her tools was broken. *I can feel the relationship. I know the protocols, the drop locations, the code sequences. But the name is gone. That shouldn't—*

*Fragment degradation.* Aldric's voice had gone quiet — genuinely quiet, not his usual loaded silence. *Embedded consciousnesses lose coherence over time without proper consumption. Your memory structure has — gaps. This is consistent with prolonged embedding in uncleared contamination.*

*I know what degradation means.* She said it once, precise and already finished with the subject. *What's the tactical situation.*

---

The tactical situation was this: two fragments from opposed System classifications — Order of the Veil, holy alignment; Nightguild, shadow alignment — were sharing a skull that the System couldn't write on, and whatever had happened during the collision had changed something fundamental about the space they occupied.

Maren sat on the slope above the hollow with his back against a scrub pine, breathing through his mouth because the contamination smell was still thick in his nose. The bark dug into his spine through his shirt. His trousers were caked with warm mud to the knees. Derry crouched three feet away, opening and closing his hands on the pack straps, his face the color of old whey.

"So there's two of them now," Derry said.

"Yeah."

"In your head."

"Yeah."

"And one's a paladin and one's an — assassin? She's an assassin?"

"Was."

Derry absorbed this. His hands went to the pack straps, released, went back. "And they don't get along?"

Inside Maren's skull, Vess and Aldric sat together like a knife and a hymnal shoved into one drawer — technically possible, fundamentally wrong. Vess was conducting a systematic inventory of her situation with the efficiency of someone packing for an operation: what she retained, what was missing, what capabilities were functional. She did it without self-pity, which was worse than self-pity would have been. The gaps were damage. She documented them and moved to the next item.

Aldric was providing a strategic briefing that Vess hadn't asked for and wasn't acknowledging.

*— the bounty is four hundred gold, highest priority bracket for hero intervention. Current heading south-southwest, nearest hero staging point is Harrow, approximately four days on foot—*

*I can read the threat data myself. What I'm asking is why you let him attempt a consumption he wasn't qualified to perform.*

*I did not "let" him. He does not take orders. He does not take orders from anyone. This is the central difficulty.*

*Then the central difficulty is that I'm stuck in a skull with an incompetent host and a paladin who can't maintain operational discipline over his own—*

"They're talking about me," Maren said to Derry.

"Bad?"

"Depends on your definition."

Derry looked at the hollow, at the quest-light still pooling in it. "Did it — the ritual. Did it work?"

Maren looked at the pool. The shimmer was different now — dimmer in patches, brighter in others, the contamination disturbed but not dissolved, like mud stirred in water. The smell had shifted too, more acrid, the stink contamination took on when it had been disrupted without being properly processed.

"No," Maren said.

*It did not,* Aldric said. His voice had gone thin and formal. *You disrupted the pool without establishing proper containment. It is moving now. Not cleared.*

*And I embedded instead of dissolving,* Vess said. *That is the other result.*

"Can it still hit the settlement?" Maren asked.

*Yes,* Aldric said. *Possibly faster. The west lip is failing.*

He kept going because he always kept going. *During the collision the System initiated a reclassification. It could not—*

He stopped.

"What couldn't it do?" Derry asked.

Maren waited.

Aldric tried again. *It could not assign a designation to what resulted. When I look at you now, from inside, I see—*

The rest vanished. Maren waited for the correction, the report cadence, the clean second attempt. It did not come.

*Do not ask me to describe it. I cannot continue commentary.*

"Aldric."

Nothing.

*Good,* Vess said. *Then listen. That bank gives way, the runoff takes the low field and then the well. Move dirt or leave.*

Maren slid down the slope before he had finished deciding. The bowl lay half-buried where he'd knocked it over. He dug it out of the mud, wiped it once on his sleeve, and drove the rim into the soft earth at the west edge of the hollow. Ceramic scraped stone. He levered up a slab of wet dirt and dumped it against the sagging lip where pale seepage had already started to creep.

Derry stared at him. "What are you doing?"

"Buying them time."

"With a bowl."

"It's what I've got."

For one second Derry stayed where he was. Then he swore, slid down after him, and dropped to his knees on the cleaner side of the bank.

"Not like that," he said. "You'll have it slough off. Here. Pull from the roots. Pack it hard."

Maren did. So did Derry. The work turned stupid and physical fast — bowl in, pry up mud, dump, tamp with both hands, again. Grass tore. Stones rolled under Maren's knees. The contamination warmed the muck nearest the glow until it felt almost body-hot, then sent needles of cold up his wrists when he shoved too deep. Ozone sat on the back of his tongue. Every breath tasted spoiled.

*Two feet left,* Vess said. *The ground drops there.*

"Two feet left," Maren said.

Derry shot him a look and shifted anyway. They built the bank ugly and low: mud, torn grass, loose rock, the kind of emergency wall that only made sense if the other option was nothing. By the time Maren's shoulders started shaking, the west lip stood a handspan higher than it had.

Thin threads of luminous seepage still wormed through.

Vess took a beat to judge it. *If it holds, maybe until morning. If the ground slumps in the dark, less.*

Maren braced his hands on his thighs and looked toward the chimney smoke beyond the ridge. "We warn them."

Derry laughed once, sharp and tired. "No."

"We can't leave it like this."

"I know exactly what it looks like." Mud streaked Derry's forearms to the elbow. He wiped sweat off his mouth with the back of one wrist and only made a bigger smear. "I heard the part about the well. I heard the part about children. I've got one too, remember? I'm not walking into that settlement with four hundred gold hanging over your head because you picked one bad hole you couldn't pass."

Maren opened his mouth.

Derry kept going, words tripping over one another now that he'd started. "No, listen. I left Pell once already. I left Lenna. I did that. I'm here. Fine. But I'm not doing this part too. We go in there looking like this, smelling like this, and either they panic or they hold us long enough for somebody with a sword and an interface to notice. Then what? Then I die in a place that doesn't even know my name."

Maren looked back at the hollow. The bank they'd built was already sweating light through the seams.

*He's right,* Vess said. *You go in covered in opened contamination and they stop hearing after the first sentence. This buys them something. Not enough. Something.*

Barely enough. A wall of mud and roots. Until morning if the ground was merciful.

He wanted to say there had to be something else. A better ritual. Better hands. Better life, maybe. The words didn't come. Aldric stayed silent.

Half a mile south, a creek cut under the road through reeds and flat grey stone. Maren knelt in the shallows and scrubbed contamination mud off his fingers, his wrists, the outside of the bowl. The water took the dirt and left the smell. Derry washed downstream without looking at him. When they climbed the far bank and found the road again, Derry let Maren go first and kept a half-step of distance that was no longer accidental.

Nobody spoke after the creek crossing.
