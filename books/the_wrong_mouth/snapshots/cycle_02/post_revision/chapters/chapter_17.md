# Chapter 17

Three days past the wreckage of the Crook, the roads gave out entirely.

Not all at once. The deterioration had been gradual, ruts softening into packed earth, packed earth dissolving into root-cracked mud, until what remained was just a suggestion of direction between rockfall and scrub. The autumn air had an edge to it now. Maren's breath fogged in the mornings, though by midday the sun still warmed the stone. Quest-light didn't pool here the way it had near Silt or Harrow. It seeped. Thin runnels of pale luminescence bled through cracks in the rock and trickled downhill like water after rain, except it moved too slowly and sometimes in the wrong direction, uphill, sideways along a cliff face, following paths that had nothing to do with gravity.

The hum was constant now. Not the background drone he'd grown up filtering. This was pressure behind his teeth, a vibration in the bones of his face that made his sinuses ache. It had started as a nuisance two days ago and thickened until he couldn't ignore it, until he was walking through it the way a person walks through heavy wind.

*Left at the split rock. Then follow the bleeding.*

Aldric's guidance had simplified. In the early days of the journey, his directions came formatted like military dispatches: compass bearing, estimated distance, landmark descriptions that assumed Maren cared about northeast versus north-northeast. Now the paladin pointed and named. Left. Down. That gap. The change was partly economy, partly something else. Aldric was speaking less, and when he spoke, his voice carried a quality Maren couldn't name. Not quite fear. Closer to the sound a man makes when he's standing on a high ledge and being very careful about where he looks.

"You alright in there?" Maren said, out loud. There was no one to hear him. The last person he'd seen was three days gone, a contamination surveyor heading the other direction, walking fast, who hadn't even nodded.

*I am observing.*

"That's not what I asked."

Silence for a dozen steps. The rock path narrowed between two shoulders of dark stone, and Maren's pack scraped the wall on his left. Quest-light ran in a slow stripe at ankle height, warm against his boot when he grazed it.

*The interface here is — I can see the structure more clearly. The architecture. It's becoming more legible the closer we get, but the legibility is not—* A pause. Aldric did not pause. Or he hadn't, for the whole length of their shared existence, through running commentary and moral objection and the kind of constant narration that made Maren want to scream on good days. When the paladin stopped mid-sentence, something had changed the shape of his thinking. *The edges are physical here. I do not mean merely visible. I mean physical. The containment has thickness.*

"So when you say physical, you mean what?"

*The way a load-bearing wall is physical. You cannot see what it holds unless it fails, but the wall itself has mass, joins, stress points. And this place—* Another pause. *The wall is thin here. There are places where the containment material has been stressed beyond its design tolerance and the joining compound has degraded. Fractures is close, but not exact.*

"You're describing a building."

*I am describing what I see. The metaphor is imprecise but not inappropriate. The System is constructed. It was built. Whatever built it intended it to hold.*

Harrow had looked like infrastructure. Maren remembered that now: the quest boards and gear shops and scorch marks layered on every road, the whole town built around a System that organized everything. He'd thought of it as bureaucracy at the time, the filing system of heroism, but Aldric's description from weeks ago had changed the shape of it. It was a wall, and all the infrastructure, the classes, the levels, the quests, the bounties, was its public face, the side people could see and navigate by, while the actual structure went deep and held something on the other side.

He stepped over a narrow channel where quest-light ran like a creek, pale and faintly warm against his shin through his trouser leg. He could see it. He'd always been able to see quest-light, the raw glow without the data overlay everyone else perceived. But here the glow was different. Sick-looking. The light stuttered and pulsed with no discernible rhythm, like a heartbeat that couldn't find its pace.

The skull was quiet around Aldric's voice. Maren noticed the silence the way he noticed a missing tooth, the tongue kept going to the gap. Vess would have had something to say about this terrain. An assessment. *Defensible here. Kill zone there. If anyone's waiting, you'd want elevation.* The flat, practical narration of a woman who saw the world as a series of tactical problems, and who, underneath the tactical language, noticed the color of the light and the smell of the rock and the exact weight of the autumn air but would never have said so. He missed her. Not the way he missed Derry, which was the specific ache of losing a person who knew him from before. This was the miss of losing a perspective. The world was less precisely observed with only two of them in here.

He had expected the silence to feel simpler after the separation. One less dead person in his head. One less set of opinions. One less knife laid crosswise through every decision. Instead the quiet kept producing ghosts. At blind corners he still waited for Vess to mark the angles. When he sat down to chew jerky, his jaw braced for the flat little comments she used to make about the way he held a blade or a spoon or his own shoulders. Night was worse. He'd wake half a heartbeat before dawn because nobody had muttered that the left side of camp was exposed. The absence did not come clean. It came in habits his body had not yet agreed to give back.

The terrain tilted downward. The path, the idea of a path, led between two rock faces that leaned toward each other, not quite meeting, leaving a stripe of grey autumn sky above. Quest-light bled from both walls in slow runs that pooled at the bottom of the gap and drained somewhere ahead, downhill, toward whatever the Woundmouth actually was.

Maren's bowl clinked against his hip where he'd tied it. The ceramic was cold through his shirt. He'd been carrying it outside his pack since the separation, since the bowl had done real work for the first time, dissolving the boundary between Vess and himself and sending her into the formless quiet of dissipation. Every few minutes the weight of it bumped against him and the bump was a reminder that he carried tools now, not props. Different man holding it.

The last stretch of ground had a softness to it that had nothing to do with mud. The rock under his boots was firm, but the pressure in it changed from step to step, as if the hill were breathing under a crust. Once his heel came down near a seam of quest-light and the stone answered with a warm shiver that ran up his shin and into his groin, not pain exactly, just a bodily warning that made his stomach pull tight. The smell had changed too. Less surface rot, more the mineral stink that came when a well bucket scraped too deep and brought up water from somewhere old.

*Stop.*

Maren stopped. His boots were ankle-deep in pooled quest-light, warm and thick, and the hum jumped, a physical pressure against his eardrums, like altitude change or the moment before a thunderclap.

*Look down.*

He looked down, though looking meant nothing beyond the raw glow and the dark stained rock. The quest-light ran ahead of him into a depression in the ground that widened and deepened, and he felt it before he understood what he was seeing. The ground opened into a wound. Cave mouth and cliff edge were too clean for it. The rock itself looked torn, the edges ragged and discolored, quest-light pouring from the opening like blood from a cut that had been left too long. The contamination smell was overwhelming: the familiar ozone-sweet with rot underneath, like old wood split open to show it had gone bad all the way through.

The Woundmouth.

It was smaller than he'd imagined. He'd expected spectacle, a cavern, a vast opening, the kind of thing that earned its name through drama. Instead it was maybe eight feet across, an irregular tear in the stone with quest-light hemorrhaging from every edge, pooling in the depression around it in a warm, pulsing lake that reached Maren's calves. The hum came straight out of the opening itself, a vibration that resonated in his ribs and made his vision swim at the edges.

What made it wrong was not size. It was obedience. The whole cut in the land fed this one place. Every thread of quest-light in sight bent toward the tear. The stone nearest the lip had a smoothed, worked feel under his boots, as if the wound had been licked at for years by something patient. Even the cold air seemed to drag that way. When he breathed out, the exhale wanted to settle into the hole instead of rise.

*This is where the architecture is thinnest. This is where the wall—* Aldric stopped. Started again with the careful precision of a man choosing each word because the wrong one might make the situation real. *The containment here has failed to the degree that what it holds is nearly visible to me through the interface. I can see things that should not be visible from this side.*

"Describe them."

*No.*

"Aldric."

*Not yet. You will see for yourself, I think. When you consume. If—* He caught the word. Corrected it. *When you consume. The ritual. You need to set up.*

The *if* had been there, though, sitting in the paladin's voice, and the correction was its own kind of honesty.

---

Maren knelt at the edge of the Woundmouth.

The quest-light was warm against his legs, warmer than any pool he'd worked before, body temperature or close to it, as though the light itself were alive and fevered. He untied his bowl from his hip. The ceramic was white and cracked, and when he set it at the lip of the wound the light found the inside glaze and lit it from within before the overflow started. Quest-light ran into it immediately, filled it, spilled over the rim in a slow overflow. The bowl was too small for what was being offered.

He had learned the real sequence. Thessa had taught him at the Crook, grudgingly, with corrections that landed like slaps: the proper order of gestures, the breathing, the way to open himself to contamination rather than performing openness. The difference between the fake version and the real one was the difference between miming the act of eating and actually putting food in your mouth. One was theatre. The other was consumption, taking the thing inside yourself, letting it change you, doing the work of dissolution that the System could not do on its own.

His hands were steady. That surprised him. They hadn't been steady for the practice consumption at the creek, hadn't been steady at the Crook when Thessa stood over him correcting his form in front of the other sin-eaters, hadn't been steady during the combat consumption when the valley was collapsing around them. But now, kneeling at the edge of something that could kill him, they were calm. He didn't trust it. Calm before a consumption usually meant his body hadn't figured out how bad it was going to get yet.

Thessa had drilled him on the difference between fear and warning. Fear made your stomach jump and your thoughts go noisy. Warning was simpler. Warning was a throat that started to swell, fingers that went numb, a pulse that lost count of itself. Stop for those, she'd said. Not because the ritual failed. Because it was working and your body was the part paying. If the bowl cracked farther, let it crack. If the light backed up instead of feeding through, pull out your hands before it cooked the tendons. He had nodded at the Crook like a chastened student. Now the instructions came back in her dry, unimpressed voice, each one more useful than comfort would have been.

*Maren.*

"Yeah."

*I want you to know that I chose correctly.*

"That's a hell of a thing to say right before I—"

*I chose to reach for you because you were the only kind of host who could do this. Convenience had nothing to do with it, nor proximity. You were necessary. I want you to know that while you can still hear me say it with composure.* A beat. *I may not have composure shortly.*

"You're really selling the optimism here."

*Begin.*

He began.

The first gesture was the opening: hands flat against the stone beside the wound, palms down, letting the contamination read his skin. Thessa called it the greeting. You showed the contamination what you were, and it showed you what it was, and from that mutual recognition the work could start. In the fake version, he'd always mimed this part with his eyes closed and a solemn expression, holding the posture long enough for the family to feel the ritual's weight. In the real version, his eyes were open and there was nothing solemn about it. The contamination crawled up through his palms like heat from a stove, then kept going into his wrists and up his forearms, a tingling pressure that pushed into his chest and settled behind his sternum. The ozone smell filled his mouth and his sinuses.

The second gesture was the opening of the channel. It used a different mouth, the interior one, the passage Thessa described as the space between what you are and what the world is full of. He'd struggled with this at the creek, struggling to distinguish metaphor from instruction, until Vess had said, bluntly, *Stop trying to understand it. Just do what your body already knows how to do.* And she'd been right. His body knew. Eleven years of faking had built the postures into his muscles even though the intent behind them had been hollow, and when he let the postures carry him without resistance, the channel opened like a door swinging on hinges that had been waiting.

The contamination poured in.

Residue pools and abandoned quests had taught him trickles and streams. This was the accumulated contamination of something that had been bleeding since before anyone alive could remember, structural contamination, woven into the rock and the light and the hum, as old as the wound itself. It hit him like cold water from a height. His lungs seized. His vision went white.

And then, for the first time in twenty-eight years of blindness, he saw the System.

Aldric had never been able to give him this. The paladin's narration, the formal vocabulary, the careful moral framing all dropped away, and Maren saw the interface with his own eyes. The overlay every other person in the world had been born into, sitting on reality the way ink sits on paper, had appeared.

There was light everywhere. It was layered over reality like a second skin. Every rock was annotated, every crack in the stone carried data, classifications and numbers and categories scrolling in columns he couldn't read because he'd never learned the visual grammar. The taxonomy of everything. Every insect near the Woundmouth had a classification hovering beside it. The rock itself was labeled. The quest-light pooling around his knees carried strings of data he could see but not parse, like looking at text in a language you don't speak. The world, organized. The world, named.

It was beautiful. He hadn't expected that. The light was intricate and precise, a vast careful system of annotation that turned the raw mess of reality into something navigable, something that could be understood at a glance by anyone who'd grown up reading it. He understood, suddenly and viscerally, why Aldric had sounded like a man reading a grocery list at a state funeral, because that was what the interface was. A list. An enormously detailed, constantly updating list of what everything was, what it could do, and how dangerous it was. People lived inside this list. They were born into it and they died inside it and they never thought to wonder what it was attached to.

He saw his own classification: UNCLASSIFIED ENTITY. The words hung in the air in front of him, bordered in pulsing red, wrong against the organized architecture of everything around them. A warning to everything that could read it. And below it, the bounty figure, a number so large it looked like a printing error.

Near the lip of the Woundmouth the annotations thickened into practical clutter. Contamination gradients stacked over the pooled light. Hazard bands nested inside hazard bands. A route marker kept trying to draw a safe approach along the left side of the depression and then collapsing into unreadable static where the stone had already torn too far. He saw, in one sick clear instant, how a trained hero would have come at this place: where to plant boots, where the interface would tell them to stop, where the numbers would start climbing too fast to ignore. The System had rules for even this. Or it had tried to.

He knew bits of the grammar without knowing how he knew them. Red meant stop. Gold meant reward or bounty or the kind of attention that got men killed. Grey washed over spent things. He'd learned that over eleven years of watching other people's faces tilt toward prompt-light, over Derry's muttered translations, over the small pauses in village talk when the air in front of somebody's nose mattered more than the person standing beside them. Seeing it all at once was like finding out a language he'd heard through walls his whole life had a written form after all.

*Maren. Don't stop. Keep going. You have to go past it.*

Aldric's voice was distant, not because the paladin had moved but because Maren was moving, past the interface, through the interface, into the machinery behind the labels. The light thinned. The classifications stretched, distorted, the clean annotations fraying at their edges as if the writing had run out of surface to adhere to. The data degraded. He was going somewhere the System hadn't been designed to let anyone go, and the System's architecture was responding by failing in his presence the way it always had, not violently, not with alarm, but with the quiet structural inability to account for what he was.

Behind the interface was the wall.

He could feel it before he could see it, a pressure, a density, a thing with mass and age that his body registered the way his body registered gravity. The wall was not a metaphor. Aldric had described it in architectural terms weeks ago, sitting in camp with the smoke going sideways, and Maren had understood it intellectually then. He understood it in his bones now. The containment was constructed from something that wasn't rock or light or data but was all three compressed into a single barrier, and it extended in every direction, up, down, sideways, deeper than his spatial perception could follow. It had been built with enormous care and maintained through constant repair, patched and repatched in layers he could feel the way you feel the layers of paint on an old wall when you run your hand over it.

And it was failing.

The failure was patient, the way a dam goes bad: one crack at a time, the cracks slowly connecting, the seepage getting worse by degrees that any single observer would call manageable but that the structure itself knew was terminal. The Woundmouth was one crack. The worst one, maybe, or just the one he'd reached first, but through it he could feel the others, dozens, scattered across the containment's surface, hairline fractures in a structure that was older than any recorded history.

The old repairs were still in it. He could feel that too. Tight, careful seams where someone had once taken a leak into themselves and forced it shut long enough for the next season to happen. Rougher patches laid over those, hurried work done by people who were probably half dead by the end of it. Sin-eaters. Real ones. Not a title, not a village performance, not Maren in a clean shirt making solemn hands over a bed. Bodies fed into the wall as maintenance and sent back out shaking.

He was at the crack. From this side, the Woundmouth was a fissure in the wall that went deeper than the physical wound in the rock above, and through it he finally saw what the containment had been built against.

Black grass grew out of empty air. It bent under a wind he could not feel, then flashed hard and translucent, blade after blade turning to glass and back to grass again. Beyond it, a river hung upright like a sheet nailed to nothing, pouring sideways instead of down. Pale fish moved through that water and then were not fish anymore, just a scatter of leaves still swimming in the same pattern.

The sight hit him like a fist under the sternum. His nose started bleeding at once, hot over his lip. The channel he'd opened convulsed wider without his permission, and the hum in his skull spiked until his molars rang.

It held no malice and wanted nothing from him. He knew that with the same ugly certainty he knew when a bridge was about to give under a wagon. It was just a world too large for labels, too indifferent to stay inside them, pressing against the barrier because that was where it had always been.

And at the fissure he felt two ways the work could go. If he pushed outward, the crack would unzip. It wanted to. The pressure on the far side leaned into him, offering something horribly simple: one hard shove and the writing would fail here. No classifications. No bounty. No neat red warning in the air telling the world what he was.

If he pulled inward instead, opened the channel wider and took the bleed through himself, the edges of the fracture would draw tight around the wound. That was the real job under the bowl and the ritual and all the years he'd spent faking it. Sin-eaters sealed leaks. They took the wall's damage into themselves and forced it closed.

Thessa and every other real sin-eater had been patching what they could reach. Maren could reach deeper because the System couldn't write on him, couldn't make him part of the structure while he worked. The same wrongness that made him a crack was the thing letting him seal one.

He ground his palms harder into the stone, tipped the bowl into the outflow, and chose the direction that hurt. Inward. Through him. Seal it.

The understanding was clear and cold and it arrived with a cost, because understanding was not free here. The consumption had been running while he processed, and his body had continued doing the work of dissolution without his conscious direction, and the work was too much. The contamination from the Woundmouth was exponentially denser than anything he'd consumed before. It poured through the channel he'd opened and his capacity was not sufficient. He'd been kneeling in the quest-light for how long. Minutes. Longer. His sense of time had dissolved along with the contamination.

His hands had locked against the stone, fingers white, the tendons standing in rigid cords. The ceramic bowl beside him was overflowing, quest-light running over his knees and soaking his trousers to the skin. The hum had become a sound he could feel in his teeth, in his jaw joints, in the plates of his skull. His heartbeat was wrong, too fast, then too slow, then skipping beats entirely, his chest doing something that didn't feel sustainable. His breath came in hitches. Something in his throat was closing, not from panic but from the contamination, the muscles of his esophagus clenching against the volume of what was filling him.

*Maren. Stay with me. Maren. Stay.*

He couldn't answer. His mouth was full of the taste of contamination, ozone and copper and something older, something that tasted the way deep groundwater tastes when it's been in the rock for centuries, mineral and cold and impossibly heavy. His vision strobed between the raw world and the interface overlay, flipping back and forth like a lamp with a failing filament. He could see the data, the labels, the classifications, and then he couldn't, and then he could, and his brain was fighting both because it had spent twenty-eight years learning to see without the overlay and now the overlay was being forced through channels that had never carried it.

*Your heart is arrhythmic. I don't — I am not a healer, I don't know the—* Aldric's voice cracked into something Maren had never heard from him, stripped of the paladin's formality and report cadence. It was the voice of someone watching a person die and having no hands to stop it. *Keep breathing. Just keep breathing. I can — I can try to — I don't know what I can do. But I am here and I am not leaving and you will keep breathing.*

The paladin's formality was gone, torn off by the situation the way weather tears a sign from a post. The subordinate clauses were gone too, along with the careful attribution and the reports filed to an order that no longer existed. What remained was a dead man refusing to let another man die, holding on through nothing but the sheer stubbornness of someone who had already lost everything he valued and would not lose this too.

Maren could feel Aldric's presence, not as a voice but as a pressure, a weight of attention bearing down on him from inside his own skull. The paladin was doing something. Aldric had stopped praying weeks ago, quietly, without announcement, the way a man stops writing letters to an address that never replies. What he was doing now was simpler and harder than prayer: he was paying attention. He was watching Maren's heartbeat and his breathing and his consciousness with the focus of a man who believed that witnessing was itself an act that could hold a thing together.

Maren's left hand spasmed off the stone. The contamination in his chest surged. His vision went white again and then dark and then settled into something that was neither, a grey, pressurized awareness of his own body failing, his consciousness flickering at the edges like a candle in wind.

*You are not done. You are not finished. Stay.*

---

Maren couldn't feel his legs.

He was on his side. When had he fallen? He lay in the pooled quest-light, the warm sick glow soaking through his clothes and hair, the side of his face pressed against stone that smelled like ozone and age. The bowl had tipped. It lay near his right hand, on its side, empty, the quest-light that had filled it absorbed or drained back toward the wound. The hum was quieter, reduced enough to notice, as if the wound had exhaled and the pressure behind it had eased a little.

*You sealed part of it. The fracture is too deep for one pass, but the edges are narrower. You cauterized the worst of the seepage. The flow has slowed.*

"Shut up." His voice was a croak. His throat was raw, contamination-burned, and every word scraped going out. "Give me a minute."

*You don't—*

"Give me a fucking minute, Aldric."

The paladin went quiet in a new way. The frightened silence of earlier was gone, and so was the observant silence of the approach. This one was obedience from someone who recognized that the request was not negotiable.

Maren lay there. His whole body shook with a fine tremor that made his teeth click against each other. He could taste blood, copper and salt, and when he spat sideways onto the stone, it was pink. He'd bitten the inside of his cheek at some point during the consumption, or maybe the contamination had burned something on its way through. His chest ached with a deep, bruised soreness, the feeling of muscles that had been clenched for too long without oxygen. His heartbeat was present again, not regular, not calm, but present, one after another, which was the minimum standard.

He watched the quest-light seep along the ground near his face. It moved differently now, slower, less fevered, the stuttering pulse smoothed into something closer to a steady flow. The Woundmouth was still there. You didn't close a wound this old with one consumption. But the hemorrhaging had slowed. The edges of the tear looked less ragged, the quest-light bleeding from them thinner, weaker.

He'd sealed a crack. Part of one. With his face in the dirt and blood in his mouth and his heart stuttering like a broken clock.

*The interface has faded. I expected that. You are still System-blind. The residue has not faded. Your pulse is irregular, your throat is swelling, and if you attempt another structural sealing before this clears, I believe it will kill you.*

"Good about the first part." Swallowing hurt. He regretted it immediately. "The rest can go to hell."

He got onto his hands and knees because lying there had started to feel too much like dying. The movement set the whole gully swaying. His stomach clenched hard enough to fold him over. He got the bowl under his mouth just in time for the first heave, a spill of sour spit streaked pink and threaded with pale light. The second hurt worse. Nothing solid, just bile and hot metal and a stretch of coughing that ended with him drooling into the bowl because his throat had decided swallowing was optional now.

When he could breathe again, he dragged his canteen free, tried a mouthful of water, and coughed half of it back through his nose. The burn behind his sternum flared so hard his fingers cramped around the strap.

*Not again,* Aldric said. *Not today.*

Maren wiped his mouth with the back of his wrist and sat back on his heels. Quest-light ran off his sleeves in thin strings. "Wasn't planning on it."

*At the fracture you tested the outward seam before you pulled inward. I felt it.*

"Yeah."

*You could have opened it. Why did you not?*

Maren hooked two fingers downhill. Quest-light was already escaping the depression in thinner runnels, slipping between rocks, searching for somewhere lower to go. "Because it runs that way. Fields are that way. Cisterns. Stock ponds. Some kid with a stick. I open it and all this starts moving."

Aldric said nothing for long enough that Maren could hear the new, slower drip of quest-light back into the bowl.

Then: *I can see other fractures now. The pattern carries. Southwest, three days if you can still walk straight, there is another near a settlement. The name is blurred, but the clearance flag has been hanging for six months with no response.*

"How bad?"

*Deep enough that I would not trust anyone else to reach the center of it. After that there are smaller faults along the same drainage. After that I lose them in the distance.*

Maren looked down at the bowl in his hand. Pink spit and pale light swirled together in the cracked glaze and settled into separate layers.

"How many altogether?"

*Too many.*

Maren looked back at the Woundmouth. Too many. The wound behind him, the one three days ahead, the ones after that. Too many for one body, one bowl, one throat that already felt sanded raw from the inside.

He tipped the mess out onto bare stone, rinsed the bowl with the last finger of water he could spare, and hooked it back to his hip. The leather strip slipped twice before his hands obeyed him. On the first try to stand, his knees folded. On the second, the world narrowed to a bright ring and nearly dropped him again. The third got him vertical.

*Maren—*

"I heard you." His voice still sounded ruined. "No more today. I can walk."

He turned southwest because the quest-light was already running that way. It slid out of the Woundmouth in thin, patient threads and disappeared between the rocks toward whatever waited downhill. Three days, Aldric had said. Six months with no answer. Maren took the first step with his heart still misfiring in his chest and followed the bleed.
