# Chapter 11

The road past Harrow got worse in a way that felt deliberate, as if the land itself was shedding the pretense of being traveled. Ruts deepened and multiplied. Scorch marks on the remaining flat stones grew older and more layered, the kind that came from years of hero-class abilities fired in the same direction without anyone repairing the surface underneath. The ditches were choked with quest-light residue — not the faint shimmer Maren was used to from Fenhold but thicker deposits, waist-deep in places, the pale glow moving sluggishly like something between water and fog.

Derry walked ahead of him. Twenty feet now. Since Caul, they hadn't gone shoulder to shoulder once; yesterday Derry had kept a steady ten feet between them all day, the silence still shell-shocked, still trying to decide what to do with Caul's accusation. Now the distance had doubled. The silence had gone tight as fence wire. Derry had pulled it that way one careful stretch at a time, with the kind of attention to the road that suggested he was picking through something far meaner than ruts.

Pell hadn't come up since the night before Caul found them. That was the real tell. Derry talked about his daughter the way most people breathed — because it was necessary, because it happened without effort, because stopping would require a conscious decision that something had fundamentally changed. The absence of Pell's name in conversation sat between them like a third traveler, taking up more room than either of them acknowledged.

*The road drops about forty feet in the next half-mile,* Aldric said. *There is a creek bed at the bottom. The contamination density increases near the water.*

"Worse how?"

*Pooling. Quest residue collected in a natural basin. Roughly six feet by four, moderate depth. Active contamination, not residual — something was abandoned here recently enough that the residue has not fully settled.*

Vess, who had been quiet most of the morning: *Small enough to practice on.*

Maren's foot caught on a root and he stumbled. Derry glanced back, then looked away.

"I wasn't thinking about—"

*Yes, you were,* Vess said.

She was right. Since Caul's quiet, measured explanation of how Haley and Renn had walked into a dungeon that should have been clean, Maren had been running the numbers. Not the kind of numbers Aldric could read off the interface — the other kind, the human arithmetic. Eleven years. Roughly monthly rituals. Call it a hundred and thirty villages, farmsteads, crossroads where someone had died or a quest had expired and the contamination needed clearing. A hundred and thirty times he'd knelt with his ceramic bowl and his murmured nonsense and his careful, practiced sincerity, and the contamination had stayed exactly where it was.

How many people that had killed, he didn't know. Thessa might. Thessa probably kept count.

The creek bed came into view through a stand of scrubby sycamores — the water barely a trickle this late in the season, running over dark stones that were darker near the banks where the quest-light had stained them. The pool was exactly where Aldric had said it would be: a shallow depression in the creek bed's widest point, the residue sitting in it like something poured, that faint pale luminescence Maren could see without the System's overlay. All it looked like was light. It looked warm, almost inviting, if you didn't know what it was.

The smell hit him next. Ozone and something sweeter underneath, the specific contamination odor that most people filtered through their interface as data — threat levels, clearance requirements, class-appropriate warnings. To Maren it was just a smell. It had always been just a smell.

"I'm going to try something," he said.

Derry had stopped at the tree line and stayed there.

"Maren—"

"I know."

"You don't know what I was going to say."

"You were going to say it's dangerous, or I don't know what I'm doing, or both, and you'd be right about all of it." Maren set his pack down on a flat stone near the bank and took out the ceramic bowl, chipped at the rim. "I'm going to do it anyway."

Derry's mouth worked. Whatever he wanted to say got chewed up by the distance between them, and Maren was already kneeling, already arranging the bowl on the flat stone the way he'd arranged it a hundred and thirty times before, the familiar choreography of the fake ritual settling into his body like a language he'd spoken too long.

*That is not how you begin,* Aldric said.

"I know that too."

*The containment posture — Thessa would have started with the containment posture. Hands open, fingers spread, body low. The bowl serves as a focal point for directing the consumption, not as a receptacle.*

Maren remembered Thessa's test. She'd asked him to demonstrate a containment pass, then a residual clear, and he'd failed both so completely that she'd actually laughed — not cruelly, just the exhausted surprise of a professional watching someone demonstrate total ignorance of her field.

But he'd watched her hands while she laughed. The way she held them — loose, not rigid, the fingers not spread in the ceremonial way Aldric was describing but separated naturally, the way you'd hold your hands if you were about to pick up something fragile and you weren't sure how much it weighed.

*Lower,* Vess said. *Whatever you're doing with your back, stop. You look like you're praying. You're not praying.*

"Thanks."

*You're trying to eat something. Get lower. Your center of gravity needs to be beneath the contamination, not above it.*

This was the strange coalition. Aldric brought theory — the Order of the Veil had studied sin-eating mechanics from the bank, with measurements and nomenclature. Vess brought the body knowledge of someone who'd spent years learning how to move through spaces that fought back. And Thessa's remembered impatience filled the gaps with practical instruction stripped of both theory and grace.

Maren got lower. Mud and small stones pressed through his trousers at the pool's edge, cold and gritty. The bowl went in front of him on the flat stone where the quest-light was thickest. The light moved around the bowl's chipped rim like water parting, then closing.

The hum was louder here. A low-frequency pressure behind his ears that he'd heard his whole life without anyone else acknowledging it. In Fenhold it had been background noise. Here, this close to the pooled residue, it had a quality he'd never noticed — not steady but pulsing, like something with a rhythm older than a heartbeat.

His hands opened. His fingers spread. Not the ceremonial spread but the fragile-object spread, the Thessa version.

And then he had to do the part he'd never done.

---

The fake ritual was performance. That was all it had ever been. At sixteen, he'd learned the postures from watching a real sin-eater work a field outside Fenhold — an old woman whose name he'd never learned, bent over a pool not much larger than this one, her hands in the light for what seemed like an hour while the farmers waited behind her. The shapes had been easy enough to memorize.

The real thing required something underneath the shapes. Thessa hadn't named it because she didn't think it needed naming — it was obvious, to her, the way her own breathing was obvious. You opened yourself to the contamination. You let it in. Not metaphorically. The contamination was a physical presence with weight and texture and temperature, and the act of consumption was the act of taking that presence into your body and dissolving it there.

That meant letting the thing in.

*The Order's theory,* Aldric said, and Maren could hear him choosing words carefully, the old stiffness in his speech pulled tight over something underneath, *describes the initial phase as a voluntary permeability. The sin-eater's consciousness must become receptive to the contamination's signature without resisting it. Resistance prevents dissolution.*

"So stop resisting."

*Yes.*

"Helpful."

*I have never claimed to be a practitioner. I am providing the theory.*

Vess: *Ignore the theory. Think about when you get hit — your body tenses. Absorbs impact. This is the opposite. You need to not tense.*

That was closer. Not perfect — nothing about this was going to be perfect — but closer. Maren looked at the pool. The quest-light sat in it, faintly warm, that ozone-sweet smell thickening in his throat. After so many fake consumptions, his body knew the choreography by reflex: lean forward, murmur the incantation he'd cobbled together from half-remembered fragments of the old woman's words, place his hands on either side of the bowl, breathe in, breathe out, wait. In the fake version, nothing happened during the wait. Maren held the pose until it felt like long enough, then stood up and told the family the contamination was cleared.

Now he leaned forward, and instead of murmuring the fake incantation he said nothing. Both hands went into the quest-light.

It was warmer than he'd expected. Not hot — warm the way skin was warm, body temperature, alive-feeling. The texture was wrong for light. It resisted his fingers with the gentle friction of something between water and loose powder, a substance that hadn't quite decided on a state. The hum shifted pitch when he touched it, dropping lower, and the smell surged — ozone cracking in the back of his throat like biting tin.

*Contamination levels are responding,* Aldric said. His voice had shifted, the professional reserve thinning into something more attentive. *The interface registers your interaction as — it is difficult to translate. The System sees you touching the residue but cannot classify the interaction. The classification attempt is cycling. It is trying to assign you a role in the process and failing.*

"That's because I don't have one."

*Precisely. That absence is — I am observing something. Continue.*

Maren tried not to tense. Vess's instruction came back to him — the opposite of absorbing impact. Getting hit, your muscles locked up before the blow landed, your body smarter than you were, preparing for damage before the conscious mind even registered the threat. The opposite was letting the blow arrive without preparation. Taking the damage open, without the reflex that protected you from it.

So he let the quest-light in.

The first thing that happened was his stomach turned over. It wasn't nausea yet — just the precursor, the body's early warning that something was entering that didn't belong. The warmth from the pool crawled up his forearms and across his wrists, not on his skin but under it, a sensation like something threading through the muscle fibers of his arms. The ozone taste intensified until he could feel it in his sinuses, behind his eyes, a chemical sharpness that made his vision blur at the edges.

*The contamination signature is — hold. Something is —*

Aldric stopped.

The silence was immediate and total. Not the trailing-off of someone interrupted or distracted — the specific silence of a man who had seen something and could not continue speaking because the thing he'd seen had taken the words out of his mouth.

Maren knew this silence. The same quality as the creek where the fragments had collided and Aldric had gone somewhere inside himself that Maren couldn't reach. But this was different. Aldric was not retreating. His consciousness felt denser inside Maren's skull, heavier, like a weight shifting to one side — leaning toward whatever the consumption was showing him through the System's architecture, paying attention with an intensity Maren could feel pressing against the inside of his forehead.

*Keep going,* Vess said, and her voice was careful in a way Maren had never heard from her. She'd noticed the silence too.

Maren kept going.

The contamination was inside him now. Only a thread of it, maybe less, the leading edge of something larger, but enough to register. It moved through his chest like a cold wire, settling against his ribs, and the wrongness of it was profound, a foreign presence in territory that his body recognized as occupied. His lungs pushed back. His actual breathing hitched, his diaphragm spasming as if he'd swallowed water wrong, and for three horrible seconds he couldn't get air in.

Then something dissolved. Not all of it — a fraction, a wisp of the thread. A sensation like something breaking down in his chest, the cold wire losing coherence, fraying into strands, and then a warmth that was different from the pool's warmth, that came from inside rather than outside. The contamination — some of it, a little of it — had been consumed.

His body rejected the rest.

It wasn't nausea. It was worse than nausea. Every muscle from his throat to his pelvis seized simultaneously, a full-body clench that drove him forward onto his hands, the bowl rocking on the flat stone beside him, creek water splashing up between his fingers. His vision whited out for a count of two — not darkness, whiteness, as if the nerve channels for sight had been flooded with something too bright — and then it came back in pieces, the creek bed reassembling itself in fragments: mud, stone, the quest-light still pooling, his own hands flat in the shallow water with the fingers locked open, the tendons standing out like ropes.

Vomit tore out of him. Creek water and the hard bread from that morning and something else, something that tasted like copper and ozone, that burned his throat coming up and left a film on his teeth. The retching came in waves, three of them, each pulling from deeper in his gut, and by the third there was nothing left to bring up and his body was just convulsing around emptiness, his back arching and his jaw locked open over the water.

When the retching stopped, the hum was quieter. Not by much — maybe a quarter of its previous volume — but the difference was obvious, the way you'd notice if someone turned down music you'd been listening to for hours. The pool had shrunk. The quest-light at its edges had receded, the pale shimmer pulling back from the banks by a handspan on each side, and the contamination smell had thinned from chemical sharpness to something stale and dissipating, like the ghost of the odor that had been there.

Something real had happened. Not enough, not well, but real, and for the second time in his life nobody had died.

*Maren,* Vess said. Then, after a pause that was long for her: *You need to roll onto your side.*

---

Rolling onto his side wasn't happening. His arms wouldn't unlock, and his knees had found some arrangement with the creek-bed mud that was keeping him upright, and changing the arrangement seemed like the kind of decision that could go wrong in ways he was too wrecked to calculate.

The tremors started in his hands and moved inward. Not shaking — tremors, a different thing, a deep-muscle vibration that seemed to start in the bone, as if whatever anchored his muscles had been knocked loose and the muscles were trying to find their hold again. His fingers were still locked open, spread in Thessa's fragile-object position, and the tendons across the backs of his hands jumped and rippled in patterns that didn't correspond to any signal he was sending.

"That went great," he said. His voice was wrecked — thin and ragged, the sound of someone who'd been screaming, though he didn't remember screaming.

*You screamed,* Vess confirmed. *About midway through. Derry is standing approximately thirty feet away and hasn't moved since it started.*

Maren turned his head — slowly, because the creek bed was doing something unreliable with its distances, the stones shifting their relative positions in ways that made his inner ear rebel. Derry was at the tree line, exactly where he'd been when this started, his arms crossed over his chest. Maybe it was concern. Maybe it was him holding himself together. The distance between those two readings was the distance Derry had traveled from Maren in the past two days.

"I'm fine," Maren said. It was the most obviously false thing he'd said since the last time he'd told a family their contamination was cleared.

*Roll onto your side.* Vess again, and the patience in her voice still didn't sound like Vess. *If you pass out facedown over the creek, you'll inhale water. It's about four inches deep. That's enough.*

Eventually Maren rolled onto his side. The mud was cold and smelled like iron and crushed leaves and the fading contamination. There, with his cheek against the wet ground, he let the tremors work through him, the deep-muscle vibration loosening its hold on his ribs and his forearms and the long muscles along his spine in slow, stuttering increments.

*You're shivering,* Vess said. *That's your body trying to regulate temperature after the consumption. It'll pass in a few minutes. Keep your knees drawn up — conserves heat and keeps your airway open if you vomit again.*

"How do you know this?"

A pause. Then: *I was trained to understand what bodies do when they've been pushed past capacity. The specifics are different. Recovery protocol's similar.*

Some observation about the gap between assassination training and sin-eating recovery tried to form, but his throat was too raw and the thought came apart before it reached his mouth.

Aldric was still silent.

The quiet from that part of his skull had a weight to it. Not absence but a dense, watchful presence — someone who had seen a thing and was still turning it over, examining it from angles, testing it against what he already knew. Maren could feel Aldric's attention directed inward, toward something Maren couldn't see and Aldric wouldn't describe. Not yet. Maybe not at all.

"Aldric."

Nothing.

"What did you see?"

Nothing.

*He won't answer,* Vess said. There was no irritation in it. She was reporting a fact. *Whatever he saw during the consumption, he's still looking at it. Or he's afraid of what it means.*

"Is that a problem?"

*Depends on what he saw.*

Maren pressed his cheek harder into the mud. The cold was organizing — giving his senses something specific and local to hold onto instead of the diffuse wrongness the consumption had left behind, a residual unsteadiness in the center of his chest where the contamination had partially dissolved and partially been expelled. The place where the cold wire had frayed felt tender, raw, like the inside of a healing burn.

"How much did I clear?"

*Maybe a fifth of the pool. Hard to estimate without System data.* A beat. *Not bad for someone who's literally never done this before.*

"A fifth."

*Of a very small pool. A training pool. Not the kind of contamination that collects outside active dungeon mouths.*

"Right."

*But not nothing.*

In the mud, he let that sit. That wasn't nothing. A fifth of a small pool, cleared through a combination of remembered instruction and borrowed instinct and whatever counted as sincerity in a man who'd spent his adult life perfecting the art of looking sincere, and the physical cost came clear as he inventoried it: hands still trembling, chest tender where the contamination had threaded through and been rejected, throat raw from screaming and vomiting, muscles in his back and sides aching with the deep soreness of recent seizure, a persistent wobble in his vision when he moved his eyes too fast. The rest of the day was going to be a bad walk. Maybe longer.

But a fifth of the pool was gone. The part of the creek bed where the quest-light had receded looked different — the stones paler, closer to their natural color, the water running over them without the sluggish quality the contamination gave everything it touched.

*Drink water,* Vess said. *Slowly. If it comes back up, wait and try again.*

His water skin was in his pack, on the flat stone where he'd left it, six feet away. Getting up to reach it seemed like the kind of ambitious project that required planning and maybe a committee.

Derry brought it to him.

He appeared at the edge of Maren's vision, crossing the distance from the tree line. At Maren's head he crouched and set the water skin on the ground without quite touching him, his hand steady, his face — from this angle, sideways, through mud-smeared vision — closed in a way Maren recognized from years of reading faces professionally.

"Drink it slow, not — Pell had a fever last spring and she kept trying to gulp water and it came right back up, and I kept saying just sip it, just a little, but—" He stopped. His mouth closed over whatever came next, and he stood and went back to the tree line.

Maren drank. Slowly, the way Vess had said. The water tasted like leather and the copper residue that still coated the inside of his mouth, and his throat closed against it once before accepting. It stayed down.

"I can do this," he said. Not to Derry. Not entirely to Vess, though she was the one who answered.

*You can do this badly. Which is better than not doing it at all, and worse than doing it well. The gap between where you are and where a real sin-eater is could kill you.*

"Yeah."

*I'm not saying don't. I'm saying know what you're risking.*

Time passed in the creek bed. The tremors eased. The mud dried on his cheek in a thin crust that cracked when he finally sat up, pulling himself upright against the flat stone where his pack sat. His ceramic bowl was still there, the quest-light residue drying on its surface in a faint, flaking film. The bowl came up in his shaking hands. It was still an ordinary bowl, but it had carried a whole career of prop work, of ritual theater, of kneeling over bodies and contamination pools and speaking his cobbled-together nonsense with the practiced conviction of a man whose entire livelihood depended on looking like he meant it.

For the first time, he'd meant it. His body was wrecked and a fifth of a small pool was cleared and Aldric had seen something that had stopped his mouth and Derry was standing at the tree line deciding whether to keep walking with a man who'd just screamed and vomited and shook in a creek bed for an hour. And the contamination smell that had been thick and chemical when Maren knelt down was thinner now. The air above the pool smelled more like mud and water and the first edge of autumn in the leaves overhead.

The bowl went back into its cloth, his fingers fumbling the edges, the fine tremor still visible in his hands. Too much time went into tucking it back into the pack because the alternative was looking at Derry, who had turned his face down the road toward the Crook — toward Caul's offered judgment, toward whatever version of this story ended with Derry going home to his daughter.

*When he's ready to talk,* Vess said — meaning Aldric, and something in her voice was different, a tone Maren hadn't heard from her, less flat, more careful, the carefulness of someone handling information that might break — *you should listen.*

"Why?"

She didn't answer. The silence was hers this time — not refusal but insufficiency. She didn't know enough to say.

Maren stood up. His legs held, barely, the muscles in his thighs protesting through a sustained wobble that he managed by leaning on the flat stone and then on his pack and then on nothing, standing free for a moment, swaying, before his balance decided to cooperate.

"Let's go," he said.

Derry was already walking. Twenty feet ahead, the distance maintained, the silence still pulled tight between them.

Inside Maren's skull, the paladin held his own silence — the dense, attentive quiet of a man who had seen something he was not yet ready to name. It wasn't because the evidence was uncertain. It was because naming it would change everything, and Aldric was still deciding whether Maren was ready for a world that looked different from the one they'd been walking through.

The road got worse. The hum got louder. And the ceramic bowl, wrapped in its cloth and pressed against Maren's spine, had a faint warmth to it that hadn't been there before — residual heat from the consumption, or the contamination it had channeled, or something else that Maren didn't have a name for yet.

Walking hurt. His right side took the worst of it where the seizure had pulled something, and his hands still trembled when he let them hang. Still, he kept moving.
