# Chapter 1

The farmer had been dead two days by the time they sent for Maren, and the house smelled like it.

Not the body. Orla Garsen had packed her husband in salt and dried lavender, following her mother's method, and Haff Garsen lay on the kitchen table looking more like cured meat than the farmer who'd collapsed in his own barley field. The smell was the other thing. The sweet-ozone reek that crept in through the walls after someone died near dungeon territory, the contamination smell that meant residue was pooling somewhere close, settling into the dirt and the well water and the grain stores. Maren had smelled it in a dozen farmhouses across the Fenhold flats. It was his cue to arrive, look grave, and collect his fee.

He set the ceramic bowl on the table beside Haff's left hand. Close to the body but not touching, near enough that the family could see it mattered. The bowl was eleven years old, purchased from a market stall in Fenhold for four coppers when Maren was seventeen and had just decided that pretending to eat sins was easier than actually working. It had no System properties. It had no properties at all, besides being watertight and small enough to carry in a satchel without clinking against the candle holder. He'd chipped the rim twice and glued it back both times with pine resin, not because the bowl mattered but because the family needed to see a sin-eater who cared for his tools. The glue had darkened with age and looked, if you squinted, almost like a deliberate marking.

"Orla," he said. "I'm going to need quiet for a bit."

She nodded. She was standing in the doorway with her arms crossed over her chest, not from coldness but because her elbows were clamped so tight they pinned her hands in place. Her son Tam Garsen, maybe fourteen, with his father's heavy jaw and his mother's set mouth, stood behind her trying to look like he was not watching. He was watching.

Maren lit the candle. Beeswax, not tallow. He'd learned early that the wax mattered to people. Tallow smelled like the rendering they did every autumn, and nobody wanted rendering-smell mixed with their father's last rites. The beeswax cost him two coppers out of his twelve-copper fee, but the investment paid for itself in trust. The flame held steady in the still air, throwing the room into gold and harder shadow, making Haff's salt-packed face look almost dignified.

The hum was there, as it always was. Low, constant, like something large and electrical sitting just past the edge of hearing. Maren had heard it his whole life. He'd asked Derry once what the hum sounded like to someone who could see the System, and Derry had said, "What hum?" That told Maren everything he needed to know about being the only person in the world who noticed the background noise.

He placed his hands on the rim of the bowl. Pressed down until his knuckles whitened. This was the first posture, supplication, the sin-eater opening himself to receive. He'd copied it from a woodcut in a book he'd stolen from a traveling merchant when he was sixteen, and he still was not sure whether the woodcut was accurate or artistic license. It did not matter. Orla and Tam had never seen a real sin-eater work. Neither had anyone else in Fenhold.

Maren closed his eyes and began the murmuring. Low, rhythmic, a not-quite-language that sounded devotional if you did not listen too closely. He'd invented it. The first few years he'd been terrified someone would recognize that the words were nonsense, fragments of a lullaby his mother had sung, mixed with numbers and the names of rivers he'd crossed as a boy. But nobody had ever stopped him. Nobody had ever said, *that's not how the ritual goes.* Because nobody in Fenhold knew how the ritual went. Sin-eating was like plumbing: everyone agreed it needed doing, nobody wanted to watch it happen, and the only qualification the job really required was showing up.

He murmured. He pressed his hands against the bowl. He let his face do the work, the slight downward pull of the mouth, the furrowed brow, the quality of concentration that told Orla and her son something difficult and holy was happening in the space between the bowl and the body.

He watched them through his lashes. This was the part that mattered.

Orla's shoulders dropped an inch. Her breathing slowed. Tam shifted his weight from his left foot to his right, relaxing into the performance instead of bracing against it. Maren catalogued every small surrender on instinct, with professional interest, noting where the room loosened and where it still held. In another year, Tam would be the man of this house. He'd be the one to send for the sin-eater when a neighbor dropped. If Tam believed today, he'd believe then, and that was another twelve coppers every time death came to the Garsen family's corner of the flats.

The contamination smell was stronger near the body. Sweet and sharp, like overripe fruit left in the sun with a metallic undertone that clung to the tongue. Maren had been tasting it for eleven years, in every farmhouse and field shed and dirt-floor chapel where he'd knelt beside the dead and pretended to eat what killed them. The taste never changed. Nothing ever happened when he performed the ritual. He'd stopped expecting it to. He did the motions, got nothing, and came back the next time someone died because the work was still there waiting for him. Today the smell had an extra edge. Not stronger. Gathered. The room had spent two days holding its breath and now, the instant he raised his hands, it let everything out through him.

He shifted to the second posture, reception. Hands lifted from the bowl, palms up, fingers spread. The stolen book had shown the sin-eater's hands in this position with lines of light flowing into them, which Maren had always assumed was embellishment. Quest-light pooled outside dungeon mouths like pale standing water, and sometimes a faint shimmer hung in the air after someone died, a residue like heat-haze with color, but it had always stayed where it was. This time the shimmer over Haff's body tightened. Even with his useless eyes he could mark it, pale grease in the air, drawing itself thin toward the space between his hands. The hum rose out of the floorboards and into his teeth. The bowl buzzed under his fingertips.

He held the posture. Counted to forty in his head. Forty was the number he'd settled on years ago, long enough to look effortful, short enough that the family did not start fidgeting. At thirty-two, he usually began the closing murmur, the rise in pitch that signaled the ritual was ending, that the sins had been eaten and the contamination cleared and the dead could rest. By twelve the room had narrowed to the bowl, the corpse, and the pressure building between them. He should have broken posture and stepped back. Instead he made the one mistake he'd never made in eleven years: for half a second he stopped selling the ritual and actually reached into it. Not out of faith. Out of the stupid reflex to steady himself against whatever had just leaned toward him.

He got to fourteen.

Something reached back.

Not from the body. Not from outside the farmhouse. From a place Maren did not have a word for, behind his sternum, or underneath his thoughts, or in the space where the hum had always lived. A pressure, sudden and enormous, like a fist closing around something he had not known was there. His hands locked. His fingers went rigid, still spread in the reception posture but no longer under his control. The air in his lungs stopped moving.

Then the paladin hit him.

Not from outside. There was no impact, no blow. It was an implosion. Everything Aldric had been, fourteen years of combat drills, the specific way to shift weight before a sword stroke, the prayers he'd said at dawn for a decade, the face of a woman he'd loved and never told, the smell of stone corridors in the Order's chapter house, the grief and the grief and the grief of a man who had died serving something he believed in, all of it crashed into Maren's skull like someone had kicked open a door to a room that had always been sealed and now everything was falling on him at once.

His spine arched. His hands snapped closed into fists he had not made. Something in his shoulders knew how to throw a punch and was trying to, right there at the kitchen table, Haff Garsen's body three inches from his elbow. His right arm cocked back. His weight dropped to the balls of his feet in a fighting stance that belonged to a man he'd never met.

Orla said something. The words did not reach him.

His legs moved wrong, not his legs, someone else's muscle memory trying to inhabit the same limbs, a body that knew how to pivot and advance and drop its center of gravity for a shield-brace Maren had never learned and could not stop performing. His knee hit the table leg. The candle toppled. Beeswax spattered across Haff's salt-crusted shirt, and the thought that surfaced was absurd and entirely his: that cost me two coppers.

Then the voice.

It came from inside him, not through his ears, not arriving from any direction. It occupied the same space as his own thoughts but was clearly, violently not his. The voice was formal and clipped, each word set down with the weight of recitation and command.

*Kneel.*

Maren's knees tried to buckle. Not from weakness. From obedience. The impulse landed in his legs with the same ugly certainty his own mouth used when it found the right smile for a mark before he'd decided to wear it.

*Kneel and pray. Now. We are in the presence of the dead and you will show respect.*

He braced against the table. His fingernails scraped salt from the wood. Orla was backing toward the doorway, pulling Tam with her, and even through the chaos of his own muscles betraying him, Maren caught her face, not fear exactly, but the alarm of something that was supposed to be safe becoming unpredictable. She'd trusted him. Twelve coppers of trust, beeswax-scented and professional, and the sin-eater she'd hired was thrashing against her kitchen table with his eyes wet and his arms doing things arms should not do.

*Kneel. I will not ask again.*

"Get — off —" The words came out choked and wrong because his throat was doing something new, trying to shape words in a register too low for his vocal cords, the paladin's baritone fighting his tenor for the same mouth.

The memories kept coming. A sword pommel in cold rain. The weight of plate armor on shoulders that had carried it so long the body did not know how to stand without it. A young man, maybe twenty, taking his first oath in a chapel where quest-light pooled in stone fonts like colored water, and the oaths meant something, every word, and the man saying them believed with the specific ferocity of someone who had never yet been disappointed by the thing he served.

Maren's eyes were wet and he did not know whose grief it was.

He stumbled backward. His hip caught the chair. He went down, one hand on the floor, the bowl still on the table. He'd placed it with a con man's instinct for keeping props out of the violence, pushed back against the wall, away from the thrashing. His stomach finally revolted.

He made it outside. Barely. The farmhouse door was four steps from the table and he covered them in three, hand on the frame, the late-summer sun hitting his face like an accusation. The ditch beside the path was dry, weedy, humming with something small and persistent in the grass. He fell to his knees, not prayer, just gravity, and threw up.

It was not a dignified thing. It was loud and convulsive and it tasted like the contamination, ozone and rot, as though his body was trying to expel whatever had entered through the only method it understood. His hands pressed into the dirt. Small stones bit into his palms. His stomach clenched again and he spat bile into the dead grass, strings of it hanging from his lip while his back heaved and the muscles along his ribs spasmed in patterns that were not his, the aftershock of combat training forcing itself into a body that had never been drilled.

The hum was louder now, much louder, and beneath it the paladin's voice, still furious, still certain.

*This is a desecration. You opened the rite and then — the entire sequence was — I cannot determine what you did.*

"I'm throwing up," Maren said into the weeds. "In a ditch. Give me a minute."

*You were not performing the ritual correctly. You were not performing it at all. You were — what were you doing? The opening was real but nothing after it was.*

"My job." He spat. The taste did not go away. "What are you?"

*I am Aldric of the Order of the Veil. Knight-paladin of the Fourth Seal. I appear to be — I am dead. And I appear to be inside — what is your classification?*

"My what?"

*Your System classification. Your class. Your — you are not a sin-eater. The rite was wrong from the first word but the opening was real. How?*

Maren pressed his forehead into the dirt. It was warm from the sun and gritty against his skin. Behind him, through the farmhouse door, Orla was talking fast and low to Tam. He could hear the quick planning in it, whether to run from him or drop the bar.

"I'm a fraud," he said, because it was easier than lying right now and because the truth had a kind of weight to it when you were on your knees in a ditch with bile on your chin. "I've been faking this for eleven years. Nothing ever — you shouldn't be here."

Aldric did not answer at once. The hum chewed at the back of Maren's teeth. Orla's voice kept threading through the farmhouse door, too fast to make out. When the paladin spoke again, he started from a different place.

*Fraudulent rites do not admit contact,* Aldric said at last. *That corpse — the residue had coherence, but that alone — you made an opening. Somehow.*

"I lifted my hands because that's what the picture in the book did."

*At some point you stopped pretending and actually reached.*

Maren remembered the hum in his teeth. The pressure in the room. That stupid half-second of leaning because something had leaned first.

"I leaned," he said.

*Yes.*

"That counts?"

*Improperly. Briefly. Apparently enough. I took the contact and then — I should have dissolved. I did not.*

Maren lifted his head an inch. "And every other village?"

*If you were faking the opening, the contamination just — stayed. Where it was. This time you gave it a path.*

He shut his eyes again.

*You have been staging this over villages for eleven years,* Aldric said.

"Yes."

*Then every place you touched is still carrying what you left there.*

"I know."

The next words came rougher, some of the parade-ground snap gone out of them.

*What kind of person does this?*

Maren spat again. His stomach was done but his hands were shaking, a tremor that started in his wrists and ran up to his elbows, and he still could not tell whether it was the vomiting or the paladin's outrage or his own muscles trying to sort out which movement patterns actually belonged to him.

"The kind who's in a ditch," he said.

The farmhouse door opened a handspan.

Orla Garsen stood there with the bowl in both hands, arms locked straight as if she were returning a sprung trap to the woods. Tam hovered behind her shoulder, pale around the mouth. Maren pushed himself upright on one knee and wiped his mouth with the back of his wrist.

"Looks bad," he said. "It's fine. Took more out of me than usual, that's all."

Neither of them moved.

"Spiritual exertion," he added, because the lie arrived on old rails. "Ugly, but normal. Nothing for you to worry over."

Orla came close enough to set the bowl on the packed path beside him. Nothing more.

"You forgot this," she said.

"Right."

He picked it up. The ceramic was warm from the house.

"Keep the kitchen shut till sunset," he said. "Then do what you need to do."

Orla gave one short nod that did not mean belief. She drew Tam back toward the door. They watched him from the farmhouse doorway while he got his feet under him and tucked the bowl beneath his arm. Then the door shut. A second later the bar slid into place.

Fair enough.

***

Fenhold was a long enough walk that a man could almost empty himself on it. Maren tried. By the time the Garsen farmhouse had dropped behind the barley and the path widened into a rutted road, his stomach had stopped turning over, but his hands still shook around the bowl.

Late-summer light had gone copper at the edges. Stubble scratched at his boots where the path narrowed. Every few steps some scrap of Aldric's posture tried to reassemble him from the spine outward, shoulders back, chin level, stride longer than his own. Maren kept having to ruin it on purpose.

*Your System profile reads as follows,* Aldric said.

"I was wondering when you'd start that."

*Unregistered designation. Classification field present, unfilled. Level field present, unfilled. Skill allocations: none. Active quest involvement: none.*

"I told you. There's nothing there."

*Incorrect. There is a structure there. It is empty. That is — that is different. I do not know what to make of it yet.*

Maren hitched the bowl higher against his ribs.

"I'm System-blind," he said. "Been blind my whole life. I can't see the interface. Never have."

Silence.

*You have lived twenty-eight years without access to the interface.*

"That'd be the one."

*How have you been navigating anything?*

"I watch people. When something changes for them, they — eyes move. Shoulders jump. They stare at empty air and act like it told them what day it is. You learn to read that."

*And your partner?*

"Derry reads me the parts I need. Numbers. Names. Anything that'll keep me from sounding stupid."

*So you have been guessing. From their faces.*

"It paid for candles."

The road dipped. The contamination smell thickened in the low ground and thinned again when he climbed out of it. The hum had not gone down. It sat under his skull now, steady and intimate.

*Passive contamination exposure: significant,* Aldric said. *There are three contamination-clear requests within two miles. The nearest is attached to the Garsen holding and assigned to a registered sin-eater operating the Fenhold flats.*

Maren barked a laugh that scraped his throat. "That's awkward."

*Do not mistake me for amused.*

The road bent. The farmhouse dropped behind a stand of willow and came back again in broken pieces between the branches.

*Accumulation record across this district extends eleven years,* Aldric said. *Fourteen occupied farmsteads inside the current spread.*

Maren kept walking.

*Nearby deceased entity. Haff Garsen. Farmer. Level six. Cause of death: environmental exposure. Subcategory: contamination-related organ failure. Long-term.*

The road kept moving under him. That was rude of it. He had the feeling the whole world should have stopped for a second and let that sentence sit.

"No."

*Yes.*

"He was turning his own field last week."

*The interface attributes the failure to prolonged contamination exposure.*

Maren stopped hard enough that the bowl knocked against his ribs. The pines along the roadside went soft for a second and then came back into shape.

He pictured Haff on the table with salt in the seams of his shirt and lavender stuffed at his throat. Then Grella, eleven years earlier, and the bread her family had given him after he'd done the same lie over her cooling body. He started walking again because standing still with that in him was worse.

"There are more?" he asked.

*Fourteen farmsteads in the nearest spread. Estimated population sixty-eight. Approximately twenty children.*

He kept his eyes on the track. "Stop giving me the numbers."

*You need to hear them.*

"I don't need twenty children as a number right now."

Silence. Then, more quietly:

*No. Perhaps not.*

That changed something, small but real. Not warmth. Not pardon. Just the first clean fact about the man in his skull that was not accusation.

They walked in it for a while.

When Aldric spoke again, the cadence had tightened in a different direction, less outraged now, more like a field report filed by someone who had accepted that the damage was already done and would need counting anyway.

*One practical detail. From inside this arrangement I can perceive the residue itself, not merely the interface notation. Where it pools. Where it thins. Looking through you changes the read.*

"What does that do for me?"

*If you insist on walking into contaminated ground again, I can tell you where it is worst. I can also tell you when a well is spoiled before you drink from it.*

Maren glanced at the ditch beside the road and thought about all the times he had knelt in mud and ponded runoff because it was there and he was thirsty.

"Useful," he said.

*Useful, if you plan on surviving.*

He had not made a plan that far.

The road rose. Fenhold's outer fields showed themselves in strips through the trees, dark earth, late barley, a scarecrow listing sideways in the evening wind.

"What am I to the System, then?" he asked after a while. "Practically."

*At present you are a profile with the entries missing,* Aldric said. *A thing the System has made space for and failed to fill. I have seen damaged interfaces, cursed interfaces, corrupted records. I have not seen this.*

"That sounds bad."

*It sounds unfinished,* Aldric said. *I am not accustomed to profiles I cannot evaluate.*

Maren almost laughed at that, but the laugh would have come out too close to a choke.

"I'm Maren," he said instead. "Since this seems to be sticking."

A beat passed.

*Aldric.*

"I got that part."

*I am in your head. Stop being polite.*

"I'm not going to pray," Maren said.

*I had gathered.*

"Good."

They walked another stretch in the smell of cut barley and old contamination. Somewhere out in the darkening field a nightbird started up.

*Tomorrow,* Aldric said, *you will give me the villages. Every one. I need to know the spread.*

Maren shifted the bowl under his arm.

"Tomorrow I'm getting water and finding Derry and trying not to throw up again."

*Then the villages,* Aldric said.

By the time the first Fenhold roofs showed through the dusk, Aldric had gone quiet again. Not gone. Just holding himself back, as if speech cost more now.

Maren crossed the village with his head down. A woman carrying wash took one look at his shirtfront and the bowl under his arm and stepped aside without speaking. He let her.

His room over the shed smelled like dust, old straw, and the damp wool of the blanket he'd never once managed to air out properly. He set the bowl on the floor by the bed, found the water crock by touch, drank until his stomach threatened mutiny, and dropped onto the mattress without taking off his boots.

In the dark behind his eyes, Aldric spoke once more.

*Tomorrow.*

Maren put an arm over his face. "Yeah."

The hum followed him down. It always had.
