# Chapter 12

The official room turned out to be Municipal Arbitration Hall, which always ran too hot for the kind of business it did. The lobby radiators clanged behind grilles painted the color of old teeth, and the heat came up through the tile in damp waves that turned thrift-store blazers shiny under the arms. Everyone waiting carried paper or plastic or both. In Cordera, the people with money kept their disasters in private portals and sealed conference rooms. The people without it took a number, sweated under civic lighting, and watched their trouble crawl toward them in red digits over a service desk.

Mira stood by Courtroom C with her CommonFrame badge clipped where it belonged and felt the heat gathering under her bra band. The filtered air in the hall carried floor wax, old coffee, and, through the revolving doors each time they opened, the city's regular mix of salt, fryer grease, and machine coolant. A clerk behind a plexi window kept calling names in a voice practiced enough to strip the names clean of any family attached to them.

Across from the courtroom, a woman in a church-blue cardigan kept taking off her reading glasses and polishing them with the hem of her sleeve as if the problem might be on the lenses. One hinge had been repaired with copper wire twisted so neatly it looked like jewelry until you saw the green stain on the acetate. She had a bakery visor folded flat in her tote, the plastic brim split and repaired with transparent tape. When she leaned toward the desk to ask a question, Mira saw the flour ground into the seam of her thumbnail.

"Excuse me," the woman said to the clerk. "Will this be done before three? My grandson gets out at three-fifteen. The school starts charging after four."

The clerk did not look up. "All matters proceed in docket order, Ms. Paredes."

Yolanda Paredes nodded as if she had been given something useful. She sat down again and pressed her lips together over bad dentures that clicked once before settling. Mira knew the file under her arm better than the woman did. It described a bargain labor body, a B-line janitorial lease, and six overnight shifts a month cleaning offices in the civic core because Yolanda's own body could not handle stair towers anymore. It listed old pelvic hardware, one bad lung, and a documented standing limit of eleven minutes before spasm. She rented bodies because rent did not care what her hip could do in the morning. CommonFrame was seeking twelve thousand eight hundred forty dollars in equipment loss, contamination control, and unauthorized modification surcharges, plus a thirty-six-month suspension from future bargain leases. That was the neat way of saying: no more work route, no way to keep her landlord sweet, no way to get upstairs in buildings willing to pay cash for overnight labor.

The frame in question had split a seam in the right forearm on shift, then frozen in a freight elevator and started saying, in a voice the security footage did not match to Yolanda, Please do not put me back in the water. The next day it convulsed hard enough to smash two wall sconces in a law office lobby and throw a bucket cart into polished stone. Nobody had died. CommonFrame was still billing the event like a small riot.

Mira had opened the file three days ago and known before she reached the second page what the room was for. Someone had scrubbed the early anomaly flags out of the intake record. The yellow notice bar she had seen on the first version was gone, and the maintenance note showing the frame had reported phantom smoke odor and involuntary speech before it ever reached any outside hands had gone with it. In its place sat a clean little story about unauthorized tissue intervention leading to user-induced cognitive destabilization. She could see Celia's touch in the polish, legal's timing in the rewrite, and the poor woman on the bench being turned into a cautionary pamphlet.

Celia arrived without hurrying. She never hurried in public. Her silvering hair was still neat despite the mist outside, and she had one of her expensive pens tucked in the inside pocket of a coat that probably cost more than two months at Brine Court. Her hands worried at each other once before settling, the arthritic knuckles making a brief hard knot under the skin.

"You're early," she said.

"The docket said eleven-thirty."

"It says many things." Celia glanced at Yolanda, at the crowded benches, at the clerk. "There's still time to keep this within the file."

Mira almost laughed. It would have come out ugly. "The file is the problem."

"The file is what exists in this room." Celia's voice stayed low and polished, but the heat had taken a little of the lacquer off it. "I told you yesterday what can happen after this, and I wasn't threatening you. I was trying to describe gravity."

Mira looked through the courtroom window at the empty witness chair. "Gravity mostly seems to happen to the people without offices."

"Do not be theatrical."

"I'm at work. I don't know how."

Celia let that sit. For a second Mira saw the older worker inside her, the one who had probably once stood in hot municipal buildings with somebody else's wages in her mouth like a stone. Then it was gone. "If you confine yourself to what this claim can support, Ms. Paredes may leave with a payment schedule instead of an enforcement referral. If you decide to improvise, there will be consequences that don't stop at you."

There it was again, the offer from yesterday translated into threat because threat was the truer dialect. It put salary and license on the table, along with the little raft of respectability Mira had spent years building out of ugly work and clean shoes. Her job meant the landlord still answered her emails. It meant Gabe's prescriptions could sit on an auto-refill for a few more months. It meant she could walk into CommonFrame in the morning without being searched.

It also meant Yolanda Paredes was about to be billed for a defect that had been printed into her shift.

The courtroom door opened. A bailiff with a pink birthmark climbing out of his collarbone called the next matter and held the door with two fingers, careful of the paper cup of mint tea balanced in his other hand. "Paredes versus CommonFrame Holdings, claims subrogation review."

Yolanda stood too fast and had to catch herself on the bench. Mira saw the hitch in her left hip, the quick private wince she tried not to show strangers. Celia moved first, as if the room belonged to her. Mira followed because, for another few minutes, it still technically did.

Courtroom C was small enough that shame had nowhere to sit but inside the body. The table surfaces were laminated to look like wood. The city seal behind the arbiter's chair had been polished so often the gull in the emblem looked rubbed thin. Arbiter Selene Pike was already in place, a woman in her sixties with a strip of green athletic tape wrapped around the middle finger she used to drag the microphone closer. On the bench beside her sat a tin of sugar-free peppermints, its lid dented in the shape of a thumb.

CommonFrame's counsel stood when Mira came in. Elias Braddock was lean, carefully bearded, and wearing a navy tie with tiny silver anchors printed all over it so he could pretend to belong to a port city without ever smelling like the docks. He smiled at Mira with the kind of courtesy designed to make refusal look unprofessional.

At the back, one row behind the public seats, Yaret Sanz sat with a slim black folio on his knee. He had crossed one ankle over the other and looked as if he had been arranged there by an architect trying to suggest calm. His eyes met Mira's once, then moved away. That was worse than a stare.

Yolanda lowered herself into the respondent chair and put both hands flat on the table as if she were waiting for blood pressure. Mira sat at CommonFrame's side beside Celia, who set her pen down exactly parallel to the edge of her pad. The clerk swore everyone in. The words were old, municipal, and useless. Truth was not what rooms like this were built to recognize. They were built to assign cost.

Braddock began in a tone soft enough that the microphone had to do some work. "This is a straightforward claim arising from prohibited third-party tissue intervention in a licensed bargain labor frame, followed by a predictable cognitive instability event causing material damage, loss of serviceability, and sanitation response costs."

Mira had written enough claims notes to know that straightforward and predictable were blades sharpened on repetition. Braddock clicked his screen and the itemization came up on the monitor facing the bench:

Loss of frame serviceability: $7,200.

Biohazard remediation and transport: $1,460.

Unauthorized modification surcharge: $2,800.

Commercial property damage reserve: $1,380.

Administrative recovery fee: $1,000.

Total pending liability: $12,840.

Next line down: Lease privilege review recommended.

Yolanda made the smallest sound in the back of her throat. It was not a protest, just the body's little leak when it recognizes a number it cannot survive.

Braddock kept going. "CommonFrame's records show no cognitive irregularity of this frame prior to off-book intervention by an unlicensed tissue operator. The respondent acknowledges seeking such intervention. We therefore request affirmation of liability and temporary suspension of future lease access pending full compliance review."

Temporary was only thirty-six months written as a throat clear.

Arbiter Pike looked over the rim of her glasses. "Ms. Paredes, do you dispute the factual basis of the claim?"

Yolanda swallowed. "I dispute all of it."

"Then tell me what you dispute."

Yolanda's fingers curled over the table edge. "It was already wrong when I had it. The arm opened while I was mopping. Not all the way, but enough I could see wet under it. And it kept smelling smoke. Burned hair, like that. There wasn't any fire."

Braddock lifted one hand. "To be clear, odor complaint alone is not evidence of manufacturing defect."

Yolanda turned toward him, then back to the arbiter because poor people in such rooms learn fast where eye contact counts. "It talked."

Braddock's face did not change. "Meaning what, precisely?"

"Meaning it said things. Not me. Somebody else." She tugged the cardigan closed over her chest. "In the elevator it said, Please don't put me back in the water. I don't talk like that. I don't know what water."

Braddock let a beat pass, the generous beat of a man letting someone sound foolish at full length. "And after this alleged speech event, you did not return the frame through licensed service."

"Licensed service told me six days." Yolanda's voice roughened. "I had two shifts before rent day."

"So you chose to bring company property to an illegal repair site."

"I chose to keep working."

He inclined his head as if she had made his point for him. "How much did you pay the unlicensed operator?"

Yolanda looked down. "Eighty. Cash, folded in his hand before he even touched the arm."

"Cash?"

"Yeah."

"And did this operator provide any written release, contamination waiver, or tissue chain documentation?"

"No. He wrapped the seam and told me not to lift with it till morning."

"Did you understand, when you signed your lease agreement, that unauthorized modification voided coverage?"

Yolanda hesitated long enough for the answer to become dangerous. "I signed what they put in front of me. Rent day doesn't wait for the fine print."

"That was not my question."

Mira watched the woman shrink sentence by sentence. It was not dramatic, and that was what made it familiar. The shoulders came in first. Then the words got shorter. Then shame did the last part, and the room helped by pretending shame was evidence. Braddock never raised his voice. He did not need to. He had a chart, a contract, and a woman who had missed wages to be told that survival counted as consent.

Arbiter Pike asked Yolanda about her work schedule, her service call, the clinic. The questions were cleaner than Braddock's and did almost the same damage. Mira heard herself in the cadence. Claims had trained her to strip a life down until it fit a box. Ms. Paredes, when precisely did you first observe forearm seam compromise. Ms. Paredes, were you at any point informed that odor experiences may arise from cross-environmental contamination. Ms. Paredes, please answer yes or no where possible.

Yes or no where possible was another way of saying leave your life outside.

Yolanda said, "I called because it was saying things. The girl on the line told me to power down and return it after my lease block. I said I couldn't, I had two buildings left. She said that was my choice." Her eyes moved to the monitor and then away. "I know what all that money means. You don't have to read it slow."

For the first time, something twitched in Celia's jaw.

Braddock clicked to the maintenance log. The timestamps were clean, the notation was clean, and the whole thing was too clean. Mira had spent enough years in archives to know when a record had been ironed. The original first-call note had come in at 02:14, flagged by an overnight service rep who hated her job enough to type honestly: reports involuntary vocalization, smoke odor without source, probable mnemonic bleed. Now the entry read 11:32, after the visit to the back-room clinic, and the note had become respondent reports post-modification instability.

Someone had not only changed the story. Someone had changed the clock.

Mira heard the blood in her ears, not as a pulse but as a pipe sound, water forced through somewhere too narrow. She could see the exact terminal where the alteration had likely happened. She could see the compliance dropdown. She could see how easy it had been.

Arbiter Pike held up her taped finger before Braddock could speak. "Five minutes. I want the service-call chain loaded before we proceed."

The hall felt even hotter after the courtroom, as if the building had been storing other people's fear in the walls all morning and was ready to give some back. Mira stood beside a dead ficus in a brass planter while a clerk hustled past with a stack of files hugged to her chest. Through the narrow window in the courtroom door she could see Yolanda still sitting at the table, one hand over her mouth, the other resting on the bakery tote as if she meant to keep it from being taken too.

Celia came up beside her and caught the fabric at Mira's elbow between two fingers. She did not squeeze hard; harder would have been simpler.

"If you speak past the log," she said, "you're done. They'll take the license before lunch."

Mira looked through the wire glass at Yolanda. "They're already taking hers."

Celia let go. "And they'll keep taking after they're done with you."

When they went back in, Braddock turned toward her at last. "CommonFrame would like to call Ms. Alves as records custodian and claims adjuster familiar with the service history."

Celia's pen moved a fraction of an inch toward Mira's hand. It did not touch her, but it came close enough to feel like instruction.

Mira stood when her name was called. The witness chair was still warm from the last body in it. When she sat, the vinyl stuck to the back of her thighs through her skirt. The clerk asked if she would swear to tell the truth. Mira said yes. Her own voice sounded dry, filed down, useful.

Braddock smiled his anchor-tie smile. "Ms. Alves, in the ordinary course of your employment, did you review the Paredes claim file?"

"Yes."

"And based on the reviewed materials, did the frame present to CommonFrame with evidence consistent with unauthorized tissue intervention?"

Mira looked at the monitor again, at the clean timestamps and the neat lie.

"Not the first time I saw it," she said.

A hush tightened across the room. It was not dramatic; it was the ordinary kind that came when everyone decided at once that the script had slipped.

Braddock did not look at Celia. He was too disciplined for that. "I'm sorry?"

"The first version of the file didn't say that." Mira kept her hands flat in her lap so nobody could see what wanted to happen to them. "It showed a service complaint before any outside repair. Odor event, involuntary speech, forearm seam compromise, possible mnemonic bleed. Those notes are gone from the version you entered today."

Braddock's courtesy hardened by a degree. "Ms. Alves, if you have concerns about internal notation revision, those are not the subject of this hearing."

"They are if you're using the revision to bill her."

"Answer the question asked."

Arbiter Pike leaned forward, tape flashing green around her finger. "Ms. Alves, do you have a foundation for what you are asserting?"

Mira could feel Celia beside her like a heat source. She knew exactly what happened next if she kept going, and she knew it in bills, in lost access, in Gabe asking how bad it was and trying to make a joke too early, and in Brine Court Housing's auto-email on the first of the month arriving to a salary that no longer existed.

Yolanda was looking at her with the stripped face people wore at clinic doors and collection counters: not hope, not exactly, because hope cost too much by then. Just attention so total it felt like debt.

Mira said, "Yes."

Braddock cut in. "On what basis?"

"On the basis that I handled repeated bargain-line anomalies with the same pre-repair pattern and that this file originally matched them." Mira heard her own training taking over, the precise narrowing she used when everything inside her wanted to break shape. "B-line aggregate labor frames, lots four-C through four-H. Phantom odor. Unsourced speech. Residual image intrusion. Seam stress in tissue that tested within ordinary wear tolerance. The events presented before off-book intervention, not after it."

Braddock laughed once without sound. "You're offering speculative pattern language from unrelated files."

"No. I'm offering company knowledge."

"Ms. Alves," Celia said, finally speaking. Her voice was immaculate again, which meant it had cost her something. "Stay within the Paredes record."

Mira turned just enough to look at her. "This is the Paredes record. It's just not the polished one."

Arbiter Pike's eyes moved between them. "Ms. Alves, if you are alleging evidence alteration, say so plainly."

So Mira did, because there was a point at which you either laid the blade on the table or kept pretending it was silverware.

"CommonFrame altered the service log and removed early anomaly flags to make this look like contamination from illegal repair," she said. "The frame was already unstable when Ms. Paredes leased it. The carryover was in production."

The word carryover landed badly. It was too soft and too familiar, and she saw that at once.

"No. Not carryover. Overlap. The bargain line is seeded with retained mnemonic material. We keep calling it contamination on the customer side because that's cheaper than admitting it's manufacturing."

Braddock was on his feet. "I object to this entire line as unsupported, irrelevant, and plainly retaliatory. This witness is under separate internal review."

"For what?" Arbiter Pike asked.

Celia answered before he could. "Improper file access and suspected contact with unlicensed repair actors."

There it was. Given to the room cleanly, as if she were handing over a coat.

Mira heard herself say, "Not suspected."

Celia's head turned.

Mira kept going. If she stopped now the room would rush back over her and set. "I've been sending people to off-book repair because your licensed service windows bury poor renters in downtime, penalties, and replacement fees. I've also performed intake on those repairs. I know the difference between a dirty stitch and a seeded memory event. This wasn't caused by an eighty-dollar patch in a laundry back room."

Yolanda inhaled so sharply it clicked in her dentures.

Braddock said, "So the witness admits participation in illegal tissue handling."

"I admit participation in keeping people from getting ruined for defects you printed into them." Mira looked at the arbiter, not at him. "CommonFrame's bargain line uses aggregate lattice and salvage recomposition. Returned shells with high residual load are not always pulped. They're sorted, reseeded, and sent back out. I saw the anomaly rates. I saw the service histories. I saw shells in Warehouse Nine tagged by residual richness instead of defect type."

The last part changed the room. Braddock's face stayed composed, but the composition now required labor. Beside him, Celia's pen stopped halfway to the pad, then lowered with deliberate precision. The line of her mouth tightened as she recalculated what leverage remained now that Warehouse Nine had been said aloud.

Arbiter Pike said, very carefully, "Did you just reference an off-record facility?"

"Yes."

"Were you authorized to access it?"

"No."

"Do you have documents supporting what you have said?"

Mira thought of the wafer in her bag, warm from being carried close to her body all morning. She thought of Lin in Brine Court, hidden in a life stitched from everybody else's leftovers and their own fierce will, and of how much she was not saying, even now.

"I've copied data and contemporaneous notes," she said. "Not with me at this table."

Braddock spread his hands. "So we are to suspend a valid claim on the basis of admitted theft, unlawful repair activity, and a disgruntled employee's conspiracy theory."

"I'm not disgruntled," Mira said. "I'm late."

That almost made Yolanda laugh. It made Arbiter Pike's mouth twitch, then flatten.

"Ms. Alves," Pike said, "did the original Paredes service complaint contain a reference to involuntary vocalization before any unlicensed intervention?"

"Yes. Before anybody off-book laid a hand on the frame."

"Did it contain a reference to odor anomaly?"

"Yes. Smoke odor without source, same entry block."

"Did you personally view those entries?"

"I did."

"Can you state when?"

"Three days ago, before litigation prep locked the file."

Braddock said, "Again, unsupported."

Pike ignored him. "And in your professional assessment, would an exterior forearm patch produce involuntary speech in a frame absent preexisting cognitive instability?"

This was the question that mattered because it let the room pretend it only wanted technical truth. Mira gave it the kind that would hurt.

"No," she said. "Not unless the patch tech somehow sewed an entire second nervous event into the tissue, which didn't happen here. An arm seam patch can introduce infection, poor adhesion, ugly scarring, electrical mismatch if the work is garbage. It doesn't manufacture a woman begging not to be put back in the water. What it can do is give the company something cheap to point at after the fact."

Yaret stood without being asked. He did it so smoothly the chair scarcely moved.

"Arbiter," he said, "the Municipal Tissue Safety Bureau requests leave to note the witness's admissions for enforcement purposes."

His voice was formal enough to iron itself. Mira hated that she almost found it soothing.

Pike said, "You may note what you have heard. You may not convert this hearing into a seizure event."

"That is not my intention."

No, Mira thought. His intentions were always prettier than his results.

Braddock was already regrouping. "Even if one indulged this witness, the respondent still knowingly voided her coverage by seeking illicit intervention. Contract terms are clear."

Yolanda found her voice again, ragged but present. "Contract terms don't clean a thirty-two-story building at two in the morning."

Braddock turned to her with professional sadness. "Ms. Paredes, your circumstances don't alter the agreement."

"No," Mira said. "But your production fraud does."

Celia shut her eyes once. When she opened them, she looked tired enough to be honest but chose otherwise. "Mira. Stop."

It was the first time all morning she had used Mira instead of Ms. Alves.

Mira felt something in her chest go very cold despite the heat. There it was, the last invitation out. She could shut up now, lose a little dignity, and keep the paycheck. People did it every day. She had done it every day. That was the whole machine.

She looked at Yolanda's copper-wired glasses, at the bakery visor in the tote, at the hand flattened over a number that would eat a life. Then she looked back at the arbiter.

"CommonFrame knows the anomaly rate isn't accidental," she said. "They know because the rate is useful. Poor donors sign scan rights in debt paperwork. Returned bargain shells are recomposed instead of destroyed. Residual-rich stock gets pushed back into circulation. When the overlap breaks surface, you call it customer damage, illegal repair contamination, cognitive instability, anything that invoices clean. That's what this hearing is. It's not a neutral review. It's cost recovery for deliberate manufacture."

Nobody applauded. Nobody gasped. This was not that kind of room. The clerk kept typing. Somewhere out in the hall a printer started up. The only visible sign that anything had changed was Yolanda lifting her head, slowly, as if it had been resting under somebody else's hand.

Arbiter Pike removed her glasses and polished them with a square of cloth pulled from her sleeve. She took enough time doing it that the silence became procedural instead of dramatic. "The court is not prepared to adjudicate allegations of production fraud on oral testimony alone," she said. "However, it is also not prepared to assign liability on a record whose chain and content are now under direct dispute by a sworn company employee with relevant technical experience."

Braddock said, "Arbiter, this is an abuse invitation."

"Counsel," Pike said, without looking at him, "you will survive a continuance."

She set the glasses back on. "Pending referral to special review, the Paredes liability claim is stayed. No interim charges will issue to the respondent. Lease suspension is likewise stayed until the record is examined." She looked at the clerk. "Preserve the transcript and service file access history. Full chain."

Yolanda closed her eyes. Her mouth opened a little on the exhale, and Mira could see the exact effort it took not to mistake delay for salvation. It was not a win. It was the bill held above her face for a few more days. In rooms like this, even time cost.

Braddock sat down with the careful economy of a man already planning the next room. Celia did not sit. She was looking at Mira with a flat, exhausted fury that had no space for surprise because surprise required innocence and they had both spent that years ago.

Yaret stepped forward only when the clerk said adjourned. He waited until the microphone went dark and the arbiter had risen. Then he opened the black folio and removed two forms plus a thin polymer card.

"Ms. Alves," he said, as if they were meeting in a lobby and not at the edge of her wage. "Based on your admissions today, your tissue-handling license and claims access credentials are suspended effective immediately pending formal investigation. You are ordered not to enter licensed repair premises, alter or remove biological stock, or access protected service records."

He held out the polymer card that marked her temporary suspension. The lettering looked cheap for something that could close so much.

"In addition," he went on, "the Bureau is issuing a civil penalty for unlicensed repair participation in the amount of five thousand dollars. Payment instructions are enclosed."

Five thousand dollars landed harder than the big number on Yolanda's monitor because this one had her name on it. Five thousand dollars was rent and Gabe's medication and the grocery money Jo sometimes pretended was change. It was every stupid decent reason Celia had known how to weaponize.

Mira took the papers because making him repeat himself would not make them lighter.

Braddock began gathering his case tablet. "CommonFrame will also, of course, be terminating Ms. Alves for cause."

For cause was another blade.

Celia finally spoke, and now the polish cracked enough to show the old hunger underneath. "Your payroll ends today," she said. "Your health coverage runs to the end of the month if legal approves it. Security will escort you for collection of personal items at the annex under supervision. You will surrender your badge now."

Mira unclipped it. The skin beneath the clip had sweated a pale half-moon into her blouse. She put the badge on the table. It made a thinner sound than she expected.

Yolanda half rose from her chair. "Ma'am," she said to Mira. "I didn't ask you to lose your job."

Mira turned. Yolanda's hand was still on the tote with the broken visor inside it. She looked sick with gratitude, which was one of the worst looks a person could be forced to wear in public.

"I know," Mira said.

That was all there was room for. A guard had already appeared at the door, this one broad-shouldered and embarrassed, with acne scars silvering one cheek under the courtroom lights. He did not touch her. He just lifted an arm toward the hall in that careful way institutions used when they wanted force to look optional.

Mira picked up her bag. The wafer was inside, along with her keys, a half packet of painkillers, and the lunch she had not eaten. The room felt hotter now, though maybe it was only that everyone in it had permission to look at her differently. She was no longer a colleague or an adjuster or anything respectable in office terms. She was a contaminant in office shoes.

Celia capped her pen with a hard little click. "You've made this much harder than it needed to be."

Mira met her eyes. "For who?"

Celia did not answer. That was answer enough.

The guard walked her out through the hall where the next set of poor people waited with folders on their knees. Some glanced up, saw the papers in her hand, the dead badge, Sanz behind her, and looked back down fast. They knew an ejection when they saw one. Bureaucracy had choreography. The clerk at the window did not pause her work. Overhead, the red numbers rolled to the next case.

At the security desk a woman with chipped bronze nail polish returned Mira's pocketknife after logging it out of the morning tray, then hesitated and kept the badge on her side of the glass. "Company property," she said, apologetic because apology was free.

Outside, the civic-core steps shone with old rain. The afternoon had turned bright in that mean autumn way that made every puddle look metallic. Ferry horns moved somewhere beyond the buildings. A tram bell clanged on the avenue. The heat of the hall left her all at once, replaced by wind off the water pushing through her blouse where the badge clip had been.

The forms in her hand were already softening at the corners from sweat. They named a suspension, a fine, and termination pending final processing, and each word did its own small separate harm. She could picture the payroll deposit that would not land on Friday. She could picture Gabe saying he was fine too quickly. She could picture Brine Court Housing's subject line in her inbox and the way the auto-generated courtesy always sounded most insulting right before eviction.

Behind the glass doors, Yaret was already turning back toward the interior as if she had never required more than a signature. Celia was nowhere visible, and that suited Mira. She did not want to see whether Celia had stayed to work or to watch.

She folded the forms once, then separated the Bureau fine from the rest and worked it into thirds until the number made a white seam in the paper. That went into her coat pocket. The rest went in her bag with the lunch and the wafer and the painkillers, all of it the same weight now. Then she went down the steps before the guard inside decided that even standing there counted as trespass.
