# Chapter 9

Blue Hour after close smelled of boiled lint, cautery gel, and the sweet old leak from the steam line Jo kept meaning to bully the landlord into fixing. Mira had wiped the wetbench twice and still there was pink under one fingernail from a courier's borrowed ear. Tavi had the anomaly wafer under the reader lamp and was lining stripped screws along the lip of the table from smallest to largest, one of his worse habits when he was scared enough to look busy.

Lin sat on an upturned detergent bucket with the intake cards stacked on their knee, reading every label even after they already knew what the bottle or bin was for, as if printed words were another kind of handhold. Jo's old green cardigan swallowed their wrists. A thread had come loose at the cuff and Lin kept winding it around one finger without noticing.

The wafer was not much to look at. A translucent sliver the size of two postage stamps, archive-sealed, one corner chipped when Mira had pried it loose in the records room with her thumbnail and a prayer she had not believed in. She had taped it inside the lining of her bag for two weeks and still checked every morning to make sure it was there. Tonight Tavi had fitted an adapter from a retired wash console to a clinic reader that still thought it was processing skin-tax invoices.

"If this shorts the board, that's on you," Mira said.

Tavi did not look up. "You're the one who stole corporate memory candy."

"You told me to steal proof."

"I told you to steal proof you could use without burning my only decent reader." He gave the cable a small twist and the lamp stopped flickering. "There. If it catches fire now, it'll at least be elegant."

Mira leaned in despite herself. When he moved, the bench gave back the smell of soap and solder and the clean salt of his skin under a long shift's sweat. She hated that her body still knew the difference. Chapter seven had not made him easier to look at. It had only stripped away the distance that used to do some of the work for her.

The screen took its time. An old CommonFrame handshake icon blinked, went gray, blinked again. Beneath it, columns of code began to crawl up the display.

Mira knew claims strings the way other people knew neighborhood shortcuts. Intake source, damage class, solvent charge, denature certification, renter liability. She could read most of what passed on the screen before Tavi touched anything. What she could not read was why the disposal lines kept repeating a second code field after denature weight.

WH9

RSR

YLD

Another set of serials rose under it. Bargain models. Cheap line. Some flagged as carry instability, some as joint failure, some with no damage class at all. The second field sat next to all of them.

"That's not pulp," she said.

Tavi's mouth flattened. "No."

"Then what is it."

He picked up one of the screws, turned it once between finger and thumb, and set it back in line. "Yield."

Mira looked at him. "Yield of what."

"Whatever doesn't wash clean." He tapped the screen. "Look at the fractions. If this was destruction, they'd log weight and solvent use. This is retention. How much they can strip before the shell gets sent to slurry."

Lin lifted their head. "Strip what."

Tavi glanced at them and dropped the technician dodge before it fully formed. "Memory trash. Reflex carry. Sensory loops. Whole leftover pieces if they're rich enough."

"Rich," Lin repeated, testing the word as if it had a bad taste. "Rich like money."

"Rich like concentrated." Tavi rubbed the heel of his hand over his mouth. "Though, yes, also money."

More lines filled in. Mira saw route dates. Pickup windows from claims, from resort return docks, from hospital leasing partners. One code marker sat in the source column over and over again: D-POOL. She knew that one without help. Debt restructure pool. The clause she had spent years dressing up for clients who had missed three payments and signed away scan salvage in exchange for a smaller collection call.

She heard herself ask the question in the same voice she used with screaming renters. Flat enough to keep the room standing. "Why is a debt pool in disposal routing."

Tavi let out a breath through his nose. "Because debt contracts buy you cheap pattern stock."

"You said aggregate lattice," Mira said. "You said shortcut. You didn't say this."

"I said what I knew."

"You knew enough to leave out the part where they harvest people who can't pay."

"Mira."

She had not raised her voice. The fact that he used her name anyway almost made it worse.

Tavi pushed back from the bench, then forward again, as if there was nowhere in the room he could put his body that would not look like retreat. "I built wash tolerances," he said. "I built carry thresholds so the bargain line wouldn't collapse every third run. I knew they were stretching donor residue. I knew they reused dirty leisure stock because rich clients pay extra to feel like nothing touched them and the company figures out how to sell the touch twice. I didn't know they were sorting debt pools at the back end. Not like this. Not with this much structure."

"Structure," Mira said.

"You want the blunt version? Fine. It's a recipe." He pointed at the screen harder than he needed to. "This says somebody isn't failing to clean the bodies. This says they're grading the leftovers and feeding them forward on purpose."

Lin had gone very still. Stress did that to them sometimes. Not a machine stillness. A braced one, as if they were listening to a floor above their head. Their finger kept worrying the loose thread at the cardigan cuff until the skin at the knuckle went pale.

"Nine," they said softly. "I know that number."

Mira turned. "From where."

Lin shut their eyes. "Not know. It's in me. Cold air. A fan with something hitting it every turn. Lemon powder. Somebody saying hold the blue tags because Mirabel docks for mistakes." They opened their eyes again and looked at the screen, not at either of them. "It was in teeth. One of the pieces in teeth."

Tavi swore under his breath, then louder when Mira shot him a look. "Fuck. Warehouse Nine."

He shoved aside the screw line and pulled a canal map onto the second monitor. The building sat farther down the industrial water than CommonFrame claims, past the public salvage slips and the decommissioned municipal plant with the broken gull deterrent wires. Mira had passed the block on the tram and never looked twice because the city was full of dead buildings that kept paying taxes.

"It's listed as a mothballed denature site," Tavi said. "Used to be public before CommonFrame bought the easement. They keep it off the glossy maps because nothing good happens in facilities with the word disposal on the front. On paper it only handles overflow pulp and contaminated returns."

"On paper," Mira said.

"Right."

He zoomed in on the loading side. Service roads. Canal gate. A small staff entrance. Mira felt the shape of the place settle in her head like a claims diagram, rooms implied by utilities, by truck lanes, by what kind of mess had to flow where.

"Can we get in," she asked.

Tavi scratched at the burn scar on his forearm, another habit he only had when he was trying not to pace. "Outer fence, probably. Inside, maybe. The disposal crews are contractors. Bad pay, bad masks, everybody thinking they only work with meat. Security is mostly drones because nobody expects anyone to break into a building everybody thinks is a dump."

"Do you have an old badge."

"Mine is too old and too linked to me. If my face shows up on an interior camera, somebody from vats might still know it." He looked at her. "Yours might work on the first door. They haven't fired you yet. They just want you nervous."

Mira stared at the map. "Then I go tonight."

"No," Tavi said immediately. "You go before dawn. Shift change. Less traffic, more blind spots."

"Fine."

"Also no to you going alone."

She almost laughed at that. "You just explained why you can't come."

"I can't. That doesn't mean alone is smart."

Lin stood up so abruptly the detergent bucket rocked. "I'm going."

Mira already had the refusal ready. It came up and stopped against the sight of Lin's face, which had gone pale in a way their bargain skin did badly, all the color dropping without the softness of human capillaries to disguise it.

"No," she said anyway. "Absolutely not."

"You're not going into a place that made me and calling that protection."

"I'm not calling it anything. I'm keeping you out of a warehouse full of body slurry and whoever's getting paid to sort it."

"Then keep me out after you know what it is." Lin took one step closer. Their voice did not rise, which made Mira listen harder. "Do not make me wait at home while you go count people I might be made from."

The deliberate full form hit the room like a snapped ruler. Chosen. Lin did that when they wanted every word to stand upright on its own legs.

Tavi looked away. Mira wished he would not witness this. She wished, with the dull uselessness of wishing for things already spent, that none of them had become people who needed to have arguments like this in a laundromat after midnight.

"You might not be made from anything in there," she said.

"Then I will come back and be wrong in person."

Mira bit the inside of her cheek until the copper started. That was the trouble with Lin. They could sound so careful and still put the knife exactly where she had least room to move around it.

Tavi reached under the bench and pulled out two folded contractor ponchos, industrial orange gone dull with old use. "If you're both ignoring me, at least ignore me competently."

Mira took one. "You don't get to phrase it like permission."

"I don't think this is a permission situation." He handed the second to Lin, then dug in a drawer for two hair caps and a packet of cheap respirators. "Dock side gets a smoke break around four-fifteen. Wait till the inbound truck unlatches, then move with the blind corner on the left. If you hear the crane bell twice, the wash sluice is opening. Stay away from the floor drains when that happens because they'll pull a shoe clean off your foot."

"That's a weirdly specific thing to know," Lin said.

"I knew somebody who lost a shoe."

"Only a shoe," Mira said.

"Mostly a shoe."

That got half a breath of a laugh out of Lin. Tavi looked almost grateful for it. Then he turned the wafer screen back to Mira and froze the routing table.

"Take a photo of this in your head if not with your eyes," he said. "WH9. RSR. YLD. D-pool. If you find a terminal, look for seed lots, not damage archive. Damage archive will show you symptoms. Seed will show intent."

Mira had no interest in thanking him yet. What she said instead was, "If we don't come back by first light, burn the bench log and wake Jo."

Tavi's expression tightened, but he nodded. "You say that like I need coaching."

"You need reminding."

He accepted that too.

By the time Mira tucked the respirator into her pocket and slid the wafer back inside her bag lining, Warehouse Nine had stopped being theory. It had a route code, a shift change, and a place on the canal where CommonFrame said nothing lived except waste.

* * *

The walk down-canal took them through the part of Cordera that always smelled as if the city had turned its pockets out and left the worst things at the curb. Wet rope. Hot metal. Kelp gone black in the grates. A vent from the rendering works coughed steam across the pavement and left Mira's ponytail damp under the hair cap. Lin stayed close without crowding her, their borrowed boots making a slightly different sound on the concrete, a hollower tap from soles not yet worn into them.

Warehouse Nine rose at the edge of the water in blocky dark sections, municipal bones under a CommonFrame skin. The original lettering had been sanded off the brick, but the ghost of SANITARY RECLAIM still sat under the newer metal plate that said simply W9 ANNEX. Decommissioned buildings were not supposed to have lit dock clocks. This one had three.

They waited behind a stack of cracked polymer bins while a truck backed into the loading bay. The vehicle carried no company logo, only a contractor plate and a mud-streaked sticker that said CLEAN HANDS, CLEAN CITY. When the rear doors opened, cold denature vapor rolled out and then the sweeter underneath smell followed it, protein gone warm again in the middle where the coolant had failed.

A woman at the dock scanner took the driver's sheet without looking up from the little phone propped beside her monitor. A child on the screen, missing front teeth, was trying to tape silver paper to a cardboard moon. The woman had lilac eyebrows under her cap and a strip of strawberry-print tape wrapped around the knuckle she kept cracking between scans.

"You left this one sweating in queue again," she said to the driver. "If Mirabel sees surface thaw, she bills a second rinse and I'm not eating that."

"I made up the time on the bridge," the driver said.

"Your bridge problems are your bridge problems."

She stamped the manifest, never taking her eyes off the child gluing craters onto the moon. The dock door cycled open wider. Mira touched Lin's wrist once and moved.

Inside, the air changed weight. It was thicker, humid under the cold, as if every vent in the building had agreed to share one wet lung. The respirator helped with the bleach and did nothing for the rest. Mira could taste old fat, saline, ammonia, and something sweet enough to feel indecent on the tongue. The floor under her boots had a permanent tack to it. Overhead fans pushed the stink around without lessening it.

Nothing about the main sort hall looked secret in the way expensive crimes were supposed to look secret. It looked cheap. Temporary partitions. An office made of frosted panels. A whiteboard with shift initials and lunch breaks. Cases of nitrile gloves stacked next to a vending machine that had sold out of everything except salted peanuts and mint gum. The horror was not hidden behind gold doors. It had a time clock and a microwave.

Lines moved through the center of the hall in parallel. On the nearest conveyor lay stripped bargain shells on denature cradles, some whole, some not, all with their ports uncapped and tagged. Hair had been netted down or shaved. Mouths were wedged open with rinse spacers. A foot twitched when the belt jolted, not from life but from bad balancing at the hip. Somebody had written RETURN SPOILAGE on one tag in thick marker and drawn a smiley face beside it.

Lin stopped for half a second. Mira felt the stop before she saw it.

"Keep moving," she murmured.

"I'm moving."

They moved along the shadow of stacked pallets toward the catwalk stairs. Mira read the signs as they passed and felt the shape of the building alter inside her head.

She had expected categories like claims used. Joint failure. Surface contamination. Unauthorized modification. Trauma. All the ways a company could pretend a body's trouble started after purchase. Instead the placards over each lane read:

G0 CLEAN

G1 AMBIENT

G2 CONTRACT

G3 LEISURE

G4 HOLD

Under each code sat threshold numbers. Retention index. Sensory density. Narrative persistence.

Narrative persistence.

Mira stopped so abruptly Lin nearly walked into her.

On the wall beside the lane board hung a laminated pay notice:

RECLASSIFICATION MUST MATCH YIELD BAND OR PAYROLL WILL REJECT BONUS.

Below that, in smaller type:

CONTRACT MATERIAL INCLUDES D-POOL AND RECOVERY-CONSENT SOURCES.

No mention of defect. No mention of safety. The board cared only about how much somebody was still in the shell when it came off the line.

At the G2 lane, a worker with one sleeve rolled above an eczema patch ran a handheld over a headless torso and clicked his tongue. "You tagged this ambient."

The woman with the strawberry tape came over without putting down her scanner. Up close her apron was dotted with tiny sequined cherries, the kind sold cheap in market stalls for school performances. "Because the first pass said ambient."

"Second pass says contract and leisure carry."

"Then bump it." She glanced at the open torso port, bored as a cashier. "D-pool on the base, premium skin residue on top. That pays blue, not gray."

"Mirabel's going to say it's mixed."

"Mirabel can say whatever she wants after she does my station for an hour."

She slapped a new tag onto the shell and sent it down the blue-marked lane.

Mira felt something inside her go cold enough to harden. D-pool on the base. Premium skin residue on top. It was not even hidden language if you knew where the bodies and the debt contracts touched. CommonFrame took scan salvage from people too poor to fight the fine print, took unwiped pleasure residue from clients rich enough to assume their weekends evaporated on command, and paid warehouse workers a bonus to sort the good leftovers from the plain ones.

Lin's hand closed on her sleeve. Not clutching. Warning.

Mira followed their gaze.

At the far lane, under G4 HOLD, stood wire rolling racks filled with shells wrapped in clear polymer for further processing. Not pulped. Not destroyed. Stored. Some had ports sealed with yellow caps. Some had whole faces still attached, damp hair plastered to temples, eyes taped shut. Three carried the same pale rib scar, curving under the left side in a cheap neat line Mira knew too well from the nights Lin slept on their back. One of the wrapped shells had the same ear notch, or near enough that Mira's own skin prickled under her coat.

Lin did not speak. Their mouth had gone slightly open under the respirator, which made the mask pulse with each breath.

Mira stepped between them and the rack until she blocked the view. "Don't freeze here."

Lin swallowed. "They kept the mistakes."

"Because somebody can sell a mistake twice if they log it right."

It came out crueler than she intended. Lin nodded anyway, eyes still on her, not looking past. Good. Mira could work with that. She could not work with Lin unraveling in the middle of a warehouse that categorized personhood by bonus band.

They reached the catwalk stairs. A bell rang once somewhere overhead, then the squeal of an old pulley. Workers did not hurry. One bent to initial a clipboard. Another pulled off a glove to scratch the side of his nose and then put the same wet glove back on. Every ordinary movement in the room made the place worse. If the workers had looked monstrous, Mira could have filed them under danger and moved on. Instead they looked like everybody she saw at tram stops before dawn. Tired. Underpaid. Protective of their breaks. Willing to grade whatever came down the line because the rent did not care where the bonus came from.

At the top of the stairs sat the office in frosted panels. A paper sign taped to the door read:

DO NOT LEAVE COFFEE IN SINK

Below it someone had added in marker:

THIS MEANS YOU, RAY

Mira tried her badge. For a second nothing happened. Then the lock clicked with the small humiliated sound of a system obeying someone it meant to punish later.

Inside, the office was warmer and somehow fouler, old coffee left to turn on a hot plate, printer toner, damp paper, the bodily stink caught in the vent filter and recirculated until it became part of the furniture. A plastic fern leaned gray with dust in one corner. An employee raffle flyer for half-price flu shots hung beside a compliance poster reminding staff to log all narrative-active incidents before meal break.

Lin shut the door softly behind them and stared through the frosted panel at the racks below. Mira went to the nearest terminal.

The screen woke on a warehouse dashboard. Throughput. Weight. Yield bands. Seed hold counts. She did not know this interface, but she knew CommonFrame's logic. Every department renamed the same cruelty and called it workflow.

She plugged in the wafer adapter. The port fought her once, then took.

Menus opened. Internal routing. Hold lots. Blend tables. She clicked past two pages of clearance prompts and hit the first file that used the word seed instead of residue. The page loaded in neat gray columns.

LM BARGAIN AGGREGATE SERIES

BASE SOURCE: D-POOL RECOVERY CONSENT

ENRICHMENT: LEISURE CARRY, WASH WAIVER AUTHORIZED

TARGET RETENTION: .23-.31

RECOMPOSITION ROUTE: W9 / HOLD / RECIRCULATE

Mira's hand tightened on the mouse until the cheap plastic creaked. There it was, without shame, without even the defensive spin of a legal brief. Not failure. Authorization. Waiver. Recirculate.

She opened the next lot.

Hospital rental spillover.

Debt pool.

Resort return.

Wash waiver.

Recirculate.

The ugly elegance of it kept unfolding. Poor people's scans for the base because contracts could be written to call theft consent. Rich people's unwiped weekends for enrichment because even their discarded pleasure held premium value if you smeared it thin across enough bargain stock. The overlap was not a leak in the wall. It was how the wall had been built.

Below the office window, a pallet jack beeped three times. Lin flinched at the sound and looked down again.

"Mira," they said, very quiet.

She turned.

On the floor beneath the G4 racks, workers were rolling out another cage. This one held heads in denature cradles, faces upward, ports cleaned for reuse. The features were not identical, but they belonged to the same cheap family, the same jaw economy, the same bargain symmetry. A few wore traces of the lives that had used them. Glitter caught in one eyebrow. A bruise shadowed along another throat. Somebody had left tiny silver stickers on a pair of nails that no rinse had fully taken off.

Lin stepped to the glass as if pulled by the sternum.

Mira saw the danger then, not as an alarm or a guard but as recognition, the way it could stop a person more completely than fear. To Lin, those wrapped shells were not evidence. They were almost-relatives. Near-selves. Bodies that might have lent them a scar, a gait, a smell lodged at the back of the teeth. Warehouse Nine did not need chains if it could make a person wonder whether the room below was their family archive.

"Stay with me," Mira said.

Lin did not answer. Their hand had come up to their rib, fingers pressed over the scar through Jo's cardigan.

Nothing on the screen called it defect. Everything called it yield.

* * *

Mira jammed the wafer deeper into the port and hit export.

The terminal thought about it. An authorization bar crawled across the screen and then stalled at twenty-two percent.

ACCESS LIMITED

REQUEST SUPERVISOR KEY

"Of course," she muttered.

She did not have a supervisor key. What she had was a claims badge, an archive theft, and enough understanding of bad systems to know that the people who built them hated extra steps. She backed out, opened the print queue instead of the export menu, and selected summary tables, routing manifests, and whatever blend sheets the terminal would let her touch before it noticed her hand did not belong there.

For one beautiful second it worked.

Then the office printer behind her screamed awake.

Dot-matrix hammers began punching paper with a noise from a cheaper century. Mira spun around just in time to see the first sheet jerk into view, green-and-white striped margins, the heading SEED ROUTE SUMMARY rattling across the top.

"Shit."

Below the window, heads lifted. Workers looked up. The strawberry-tape woman pulled one side of her respirator loose.

"Who's printing up there?" somebody shouted.

The second page started. The terminal flashed amber.

Lin jerked back from the glass as if waking in the wrong room. Mira tore the first sheet free, grabbed the next two as they spat out, and yanked the wafer from the port so hard she bent the adapter pins.

A bell rang twice overhead.

The wash sluice.

Tavi's warning hit her half a second before the floor machinery answered. Somewhere below, valves opened with a deep mechanical cough. Water rushed through pipes inside the walls. The whole building seemed to inhale.

"Move," Mira said.

Lin was looking down again.

The double bell had done something to them. On the floor below, the G4 rack had been rolled closer to the rinse trench, wrapped bodies stacked shoulder to shoulder under clear polymer, faces fogged by condensation. One of the shells had its eyes open. Tape had slipped. Cheap irises stared at nothing, but from where Lin stood the line of bodies looked less like inventory than a queue waiting to be called by the right name.

"Lin."

"I know that bell."

"You can know it outside."

"That scar is mine."

"Maybe. Probably. It doesn't matter right now."

Lin turned, and the hurt on their face was quick and naked enough to cut her for saying it. Mira grabbed their hand anyway.

Below them the office door handle jerked once from the outside and missed the latch. Somebody swore. The terminal went from amber to red.

Mira pulled Lin into the hall. The catwalk was narrow, wet underfoot from condensation blown sideways by the vents. At the far end, a security drone came off its wall cradle with an insect whine and swung its camera toward them.

It did not look menacing. That was the trick. A white flat body the size of a serving tray, red light at the center, plastic shell wiped clean. The kind of machine municipalities bought by the dozen because it looked like procedure instead of force.

"Unauthorized access detected," it said in a voice almost polite. "Remain in place for site review."

"No," Mira said, and they ran.

The drone followed, not fast enough to catch them in open ground but fast enough to keep the alarm live. Red strobes began to pulse over the hall. Below, workers moved with more irritation than panic, dragging lanes to a stop, cursing whoever had made paperwork for them at this hour.

Mira hit the stair rail too hard and her palm slid on old disinfectant. She kept her grip. Lin stumbled behind her, shoulder thudding once against the wall. At the bottom of the stairs the wash sluice opened into the trench with a roaring gush of pink-gray water and solvent foam. The stink rose fresh and hot. Something soft struck Mira's boot, then rolled away in the current. She did not look down long enough to identify whether it had been part of a hand or a badly folded wipe.

"Left," she said. "Stay left."

They cut behind the halted G2 lane. Workers were shouting now.

"You from compliance?"

"Hey."

"Hey, you can't be in here."

The strawberry-tape woman had come around her station with the scanner still in one hand. Up close Mira saw the tired crease of concealer under her eyes and the moon-glue smudge on her wrist from the school project video she'd been watching at the dock. Human enough to have a child making paper craters before dawn. Human enough to slap a blue tag on a body because it paid better.

"That print's not for floor staff," the woman snapped. "Give it here."

Mira kept moving. "Then tell your boss to stop leaving the queue open."

It was a stupid line, but it bought half a second because the woman looked offended before she looked alarmed.

Lin stopped dead again at the edge of the G4 rack.

The wrapped shells were closer here. Mira could see the cheap seams at the shoulders, the color mismatch where reused skin had been blended and printed over, the repeated rib scar shining under condensation film. One shell had a market wristband still stuck under the plastic. Another carried the faint purple shadow of somebody's church stamp on the heel. Lives clung at the edges even after the building had washed, sorted, wrapped, and reclassified them. Nothing in the warehouse had finished the job it pretended to do.

Lin lifted a hand toward the nearest face as if they could make contact through the polymer.

"They look like they're waiting," they said.

Mira got both hands on Lin's shoulders and turned them bodily toward the exit lane. The orange contractor poncho crackled under her grip. "They're not waiting. They're tagged. That's different, and if you stand here they'll tag you too."

Lin stared at her for one stunned second, then nodded. The nod was tiny and furious and enough.

The drone dipped lower. "Remain in place for site review."

"Fuck your review," Lin said, and for one wild instant looked pleased with themself for choosing the word.

Mira almost laughed. The sound came out as breath.

They ran between stacked bins toward the loading dock. A worker grabbed for Mira's sleeve and caught only the edge of the poncho, tearing it straight down the side. Cold air hit her arm. Another alarm joined the first, not a siren exactly but a pulsing tone designed to annoy people into obedience. The dock door was half closed. Mira ducked under it and felt the metal skim her back. Lin dropped to hands and knees and came through after her, palms slapping wet concrete.

Outside, canal air hit like a slap of rotten pennies and rain. Better than inside. Better by miles.

They did not stop at the first stack of bins or the second. Mira dragged them along the seawall edge until the warehouse lights were partly blocked by rusted freight containers and the alarm had flattened into distance. Only then did she bend over with her hands on her thighs and pull the respirator down.

The first breath without it brought the warehouse back anyway. Bleach. fat. hot ink. She spat into the canal and watched the spit string itself thin in the wind.

Lin braced both hands on the wall and retched dryly twice. Nothing came up. They had not eaten since midnight. After a moment they pressed the heel of one hand against their rib and looked at Mira with eyes made enormous by shock and anger and the effort of staying assembled.

"I'm sorry," Mira said.

Lin blinked. "For what part."

"The part where I said it didn't matter."

They nodded once. "It mattered."

"I know."

She took the crumpled print sheets from inside her jacket. The paper was damp with sweat and mist, green stripes blurring at the edges, but the text had held. Three pages only. A fourth had torn in the printer when she ripped the stack free. Not enough for a tribunal. Not enough for the whole city. Enough for her.

She crouched under the container's shadow and flattened the pages on her knee.

SEED ROUTE SUMMARY - W9 HOLD

LM SERIES / BARGAIN AGGREGATE

BASE SOURCE: D-POOL RECOVERY CONSENT / NQ CLUSTER / PORT AUTH 44

ENRICHMENT INPUT: LEISURE CARRY, PREMIUM WEEKEND / WASH WAIVER 3A

SECONDARY INPUT: AMBIENT LABOR REFLEX / HOSPITAL OVERFLOW

TARGET RETENTION BAND: .23-.31

RECIRCULATION STATUS: APPROVED

Below that sat partial lot numbers, shipment dates, blend percentages. No executive signature. No top-level map of how many lines or how many years. Just enough bureaucratic plainness to make denial filthy.

Mira turned to the next page. More of the same. Different source clusters. Different premium routes. One line flagged narrative-active hold with an internal note:

DO NOT DENATURE UNTIL REBLEND DECISION.

Not an accident. Not a sanitation lapse. A decision point.

Rain stippled the paper. Mira shielded it with her body.

"They weren't sorting for damage," Lin said. Their voice had steadied into that careful register they used when the feeling underneath was too large to set loose all at once. "They were sorting for what stayed human enough to sell."

Mira looked back at the warehouse. One high window glowed over the canal, yellow through wire glass, as ordinary as a night office anywhere in the city. Somewhere inside, somebody was reprinting the pages she had taken and cursing the extra paperwork.

She had wanted, some stubborn stupid part of her, to find negligence. Corner cutting. Bad wash cycles. Sloppy salvage. Something ugly and common enough that a company could be dragged into admitting it. Negligence had a shape she knew how to argue with. It took claims forms. It took blame. It left room for the lie that if enough pressure got applied, the machine would correct toward fairness because fairness had been the plan all along.

This was cleaner than negligence. Meaner. A production method. A city built into price bands and then fed back into itself: poor debtors for structure, rich clients for texture, everybody else for filler, all of it sorted by workers who needed the bonus and filed under words dull enough to fit on a payroll notice.

Lin crouched beside her and touched the corner of the first page with one fingertip, gentle as if it might bruise. "I thought maybe I was a leak," they said. "Something that happened because they were lazy."

"No."

It was all Mira trusted herself to say at first.

Then, because Lin deserved more than a clean denial, she forced the rest of it out. "You were built. Deliberately. So were the others. Whatever pieces of people they used, they used them on purpose."

Lin looked sick again, but not surprised. Surprise had been back in the office, when the racks came into view and recognition hit like a door opening in the wrong part of the mind. This was worse and steadier.

"Do we have enough?" they asked.

Mira folded the pages along the damp creases and slid them back inside her coat. The paper was already softening with weather. She would need plastic sleeves, copies, somewhere dry, somewhere safer than Brine Court and safer than the clinic. The wafer in her bag held more codes than before, but not the whole archive. The printout proved method without scale. Intent without authorship. Damning, and still not enough to bring down a building by itself.

"We have enough to stop calling it contamination," she said. "We don't have enough yet to prove who signed off, how far it goes, or how many people are in it."

Lin watched her tuck the papers away. The canal wind pushed wet hair across their forehead. They did not brush it back.

"Then we go again?" they asked.

Mira thought of the office printer hammering alive, the repeated scar under plastic, the bonus notice on the wall, the child on the dock worker's phone gluing silver paper to a cardboard moon while bodies came off the truck behind her. She thought of Tavi saying seed, not damage. She thought of every renter she had ever told that an anomaly surcharge was unfortunate but standard.

"Not there like this," she said.

That answer did not satisfy either of them. It was the truest one she had.

When they finally started back toward the city, Mira kept one hand over the papers inside her coat as if the wind might steal them. They were only fragments, three wet pages torn from a printer in a room that stank of coffee and rot, but they had shifted the weight of the whole system in her head. Not failure. Not sloppiness. Design.

The map was still incomplete. The corner she held was full of people.
