# Chapter 1

By three on Friday the CommonFrame Claims Annex had stopped smelling like filtered air and started smelling like Cordera after money had worn another body for a while. Salt came in first from the canal loading door, then fryer grease from the market below the tram. Then came the sharper things the building tried and failed to erase: solvent, coolant, stale perfume, sweet tissue rot. Mira stood at table four with her hair tied back so tight it pinched at the mole near her left temple, gloves snapped over her callused fingertips, and worked through the return queue fast enough to keep the overhead board from flashing red.

Friday mattered because the city liked fresh flesh for the weekend. By six, labor frames had to be washed, patched, relabeled, and sent upstairs for whatever the next shift of paying strangers wanted from them. Dock crews returned broad-shouldered lift units with cracked metacarpals and tendon strain from pretending one body could do the work of three. Hotel clients sent back companion shells smelling of cardamom gin, latex-safe lubricant, and somebody else's expensive mouthwash. Adrenaline packages came in with gravel in the knees and seawall rash on the palms because no one who paid to be twenty-four and unbreakable ever stayed indoors. The Annex sorted what could be billed, what could be denied, what had to be pulped, and what could still be rented before dark if somebody like Mira moved quickly and did not start asking theological questions about the merchandise.

She was good at it. She could usually tell what kind of lie a renter planned to tell before she opened the bag.

A warehouse shift body from North Quay came back with both shoulders hot and swollen, skin crazed with old adhesive. The return note said stair slip. Mira found the rectangular ghost marks of an unauthorized brace under the deltoid and tagged it labor overuse, shared fault, partial fee reduction because the renter had been too poor to stop before something tore. A leisure body with a broken front tooth and yacht wax in the hair claimed accidental fall. Mira found the heel-web bruising from a restraint harness and the burst capillaries under both eyelids from autoerotic stupidity, then attached the right waiver code and moved on. Another came back carrying three hours of nightclub bass in the temporal ports and a split at the corner of the mouth where somebody had wanted a rougher version of themselves than their own face allowed. She flushed the residue packet, noted that the lessee had paid for deluxe discretion, and sent the shell toward wash with a damage surcharge that would never hurt the person who'd caused it.

The porter from intake rolled over another sealed return sling and nudged it against her table with the soft impatience of a man who had been on his feet since dawn. His orange rain hood was still wet from the canal mist, and he had a kingfisher tattoo under one ear that had gone green at the edges with age. The label hanging from the sling read `LM-127B / bargain aggregate / express turnover / private proxy lease`. Somebody upstairs had drawn a fast blue circle around `express`.

"Last one from harbor pickup," he said. "Then you've got six waiting by cold storage and one puking memory in bay two."

"Nothing in bay two pukes memory," Mira said, eyes still on the tablet. "It reclassifies liability."

The porter snorted through his nose, pleased enough to count as a laugh, and moved on.

Mira broke the seal on the sling. Cold preservative breath pushed out, chemical and faintly sweet. The body inside was an adult bargain frame from the cheap line that tried to look like nothing in particular and therefore looked familiar to everyone. It was medium height, with soft shoulders and rental-adjustable hips. The nape port was still capped with a paper privacy seal from a rush lease, damp at one corner where somebody had sweated through it. One iris, clouded under shutdown glaze, was a shade lighter than the other. There was a notch in the left ear that the intake sticker did not mention.

She peeled back the absorbent wrap from the chest and stopped.

The scar sat under the left rib line, pale and healed, a narrow curved seam the color of old milk. It was not a fresh tear or a rental cut. It was healed tissue. The kind of thing a printed body should not have unless it had lived long enough to scar or the print map had been built with one on purpose. Bargain bodies did come with cosmetic leftovers sometimes, freckles from bad calibration, asymmetrical nails, hairlines a little too human to be elegant. They did not come with surgical history that no one had billed.

The groin told a different story and a more ordinary one, if ordinary was the word for what the city did with rented flesh when it thought liability had been prepaid. The interface skin was rubbed raw in two places. Dried lubricant had gone tacky along the inner thigh seam. There were thumb-shaped bruises high on both legs, one newer, one faded enough to be yellowing at the edges. The mouth was split slightly inside the lower lip. Nothing here would surprise the Annex. It was only the combination that made Mira keep looking.

She pulled the print schema onto the wall screen and overlaid it on the body. Factory map: smooth rib line, unnotched ear, standard iris pair, no custom marks, no surgical modifications, print timestamp eleven days earlier. The unit was eleven days old. It was fresh stock. The scar on the table was older than that by weeks at the bare minimum, maybe months. Mira touched the seam with a gloved finger, feeling the slight ridge under the skin. The body was cool and slack under her hand. Healed tissue should not have been waiting in something printed eleven days ago for anonymous use.

She swabbed the temple ports and fed the strip into the residue reader.

Most bargain-line bleed came back dirty but legible. It might arrive as a flash of elevator panic, a slap of club light, hunger, motion sickness, or somebody else's borrowed orgasm arriving late and stupid into the wash cycle. The reader translated residue into a shallow sensory packet, not enough to give back a life, just enough to sort sanitation failure from lawsuit risk. Mira expected noise. She got water.

It was not canal water or shower tile. It was green-blue chlorinated water under afternoon sun, bright enough to hurt. The camera angle sat low and small at child's height, looking across a municipal pool lane where a boy in orange trunks had gone under and not come back up on time. A whistle shrieked once. Adults on hot concrete started yelling. The packet cut out on the shape of the boy's heel vanishing below the chopped glitter of the surface.

Mira stared at the screen until the reader timed out and cleared itself.

Deep childhood residue almost never survived a legitimate wash on premium stock. On bargain stock it sometimes survived as static, a smell, a phrase, one stubborn image that should have died in the rinse. But this unit had a damp paper privacy seal still on the neck, meaning rush handling, minimal bench exposure. The proxy lease would tell her what kind of body use it had been sold for. The scar would tell her whether the factory map was lying. The drowning boy told her the problem was no longer going to fit neatly inside a damage fee.

She ran the packet again.

The same pool returned, and the same orange trunks flashed under the surface. This time the clip held one second longer, enough for a child's left hand to slap wet tile in panic. It was a small hand, with blue chipped polish on the thumbnail. The boy going under was not the hand's owner. Whoever had seen this had been close enough to taste chlorine and fear, and young enough that the tile edge towered high at chest level. The packet cut, leaving a smear of sunlight in Mira's eyes that was only the scanner's afterimage but stayed anyway.

She looked back at the body on the table. It had one lighter iris, a notched ear, a healed scar, and raw sex wear. It was eleven days old on paper and carrying something decades older in the ports.

A bad wash could leave behind arousal. It could leave panic. It could leave somebody's one humiliating song lyric. It did not explain healed scar tissue in fresh stock. It did not explain a child's pool terror surviving a dampened proxy lease. It did not explain why the bruises on the thighs looked older than the return timestamp, as if the body had arrived pre-handled.

The overhead board chimed and flashed her lane number in yellow. It was a throughput warning. She had spent too long on one cheap frame.

Mira checked the intake tag again. Proxy lease through concierge intermediary. No renter name on the surface label, only account shielding and a privacy rider thick enough to kick anything sensitive up one floor if she let it. She almost did. Friday afternoon was not the hour for principles. Friday afternoon was for getting bodies back into the building's appetite before the overtime freeze made everyone's mistakes more expensive.

She should have classified the visible damage, attached the rider, and sent LM-127B to wash or pulp depending on what the deeper scan said. Instead she slid the body to the side rail, marked the file `verification pending`, and felt the board shave a little more money off her week.

Around her the Annex kept moving. A woman at counter three screamed because the body she'd returned missing a fingertip had better not be charged as custom damage when the fingertip had never belonged to her in the first place. Somebody in cold storage laughed so hard they coughed. The tram went past outside, a metal moan through the wall. Mira stripped off one glove, flexed the ache out of her fingers, and opened the lease file.

* * *

Proxy work always carried a tone. It was not wealth exactly. Wealth could be loud, apologetic, horny, frightened, stupid, charming, or some ugly combination that thought money made contradictions disappear. Proxy work sounded cleaner. It sounded like somebody had been hired to move the shame from one desk to another without letting it touch skin.

LM-127B's record came up under a shielded leisure account with three layers of indirection and a surcharge big enough to cover Mira's rent for two months if CommonFrame had ever believed in paying the people who handled its ugliness. The unit had been leased for nine hours, pickup at a private dock room, return through harbor intake. Add-ons: emergency damage indemnity, genital remap, memory dampening premium. That last line made her sit straighter.

Memory dampening was for clients who wanted their weekends to stay compartmentalized. People bought it for affairs, violence, grief tourism, and for corporate idiots who spent all week in arbitration language and did not want a borrowed body carrying their panic back into the wife or husband asleep beside the original. Dampening did not always work. Nothing in the Annex always worked. But people paid extra because failure was supposed to arrive as blur, not as a child at poolside watching a boy drown.

Mira opened a contact request. The system asked twice whether she intended to query a shielded account on a bargain-line unit. She checked yes twice and entered the official reason: undocumented physical modification with nonconforming residue profile. That alone would put a small amber mark on her activity log. She could almost feel Celia's expensive pen hovering over it somewhere in the building.

The proxy responded on the third ring.

The assistant appeared from the back seat of a hired car, one shoe off and tucked under her opposite thigh while she rubbed at the bunion bulging through a black stocking. She was in her thirties maybe, hair still pinned from a workday that had started respectable and turned punishing, lipstick worn away at the center from too much coffee. A garment bag hung beside her. On the seat behind it sat a paper carton from a pediatric pharmacy with cartoon whales printed on the side, half hidden under files. She glanced at Mira's badge, then at some message outside frame, and arranged her face into professional impatience.

"You have two minutes," she said. "If this is billing, send it to the account address."

"Mira Alves, CommonFrame Claims. I'm calling because the returned unit has damage outside standard rider assumptions and a print mismatch that may affect liability."

"It won't," the assistant said. "Bill whatever the rider covers and close it."

"I can't close it until I reconcile the mismatch."

The assistant exhaled through her nose. Traffic light and canal glare striped the side of her face as the car moved. "What mismatch."

Mira kept her voice in the register the company liked from people at her salary point: exact, calm, a little apologetic, as if ugly facts were regrettable weather. "The unit carries a healed subcostal scar not present on the production map. There's also residual mnemonic retention inconsistent with the service package. I need to know whether your principal reported prior modification, surgical intervention, or an off-book remap."

"No."

"No to which part."

"All of it." The assistant shifted the phone, then winced as the movement pulled something in her foot. "The shell was picked for discretion. That's the whole point. We're not filing a supplemental statement."

That little we did it. Mira saw the word turn a body into a paper towel and a night's work into a housekeeping issue. She kept her own face still.

"The dampening rider suggests your principal had specific privacy concerns," she said. "The residue packet isn't shallow enough to fit normal bleed. If there was a panic event, near-drowning, or childhood recall trigger attached to water exposure, I need that noted."

For the first time the assistant looked fully at her.

"A childhood what."

"Recall trigger. The packet contains a juvenile aquatic incident."

"Jesus Christ." It came out half disgust, half fatigue. The assistant pressed thumb and forefinger to her eyes, careful not to smear what was left of her makeup. "He paid extra for dampening because the last time a shell carried anything vivid, his husband saw the playback summary and I had to spend two days making brunch excuses. There wasn't a statement then and there won't be one now. Do you understand me?"

That gave Mira two pieces of information and made both of them worse. First, the principal was important enough to think CommonFrame sold not bodies but deniability. Second, he had a history of memory bleed strong enough to buy against it, which meant LM-127B should have come back cleaner than most bargain stock, not dirtier.

"Did your principal disclose any old thoracic procedures to intake?" Mira asked.

"No, because he didn't have thoracic procedures through your shell."

"The scar predates the lease."

"Then the scar predates our problem."

"Not if the factory map is false."

The assistant barked one surprised laugh, ugly with exhaustion. "Do you people hear yourselves? It's a discount body. He used it because nobody tracks burner shells the way they track bespoke stock. That's why we paid for the rider. That's why I'm taking this call instead of him. Charge the account. Destroy the unit. I've got a child waiting at urgent care and a principal who thinks discretion can be outsourced, so if you need me to say the body is disposable, I'm saying it now."

There it was: not just disdain but need and hurry, somebody else's child with a pharmacy carton and cartoon whales, and somebody else's man who could spend more on discretion than Mira earned in weeks and still make this woman do the apologizing. The city stacked its humiliations efficiently.

"I need that stated on record," Mira said.

"You need what you can defend if someone above you asks why you made this call on a Friday. Put this on your record: no statement. No complaint. No interest in recovery. Bill the rider and pulp the shell."

The line went dead before Mira could answer.

For a second she saw her own reflection in the black screen, thin under the Annex light, dark curls escaping at the temples, mouth already setting into the line Gabe called her rent face. Then the internal messenger chimed.

`Why did you open a live query on shielded proxy leisure stock?`

Celia did not waste words even in text.

Mira typed back: `Undocumented scar. Deep residue. Needed claimant position.`

The dots appeared, vanished, reappeared.

`Then you have it. Close the exposure. My office in ten.`

Mira read the message twice, as if a third reading might make the wording gentler or less specific. It did neither. She could have ignored it for six minutes and maybe bought herself time to bury the file cleanly. Instead she pulled LM-127B's print history one more time. The unit was eleven days past fabrication. Its batch origin was partially masked by internal code and marked as aggregate line. Salvage component percentage was redacted unless she had factory clearance, which she did not.

She clicked into the residue packet metadata. The system had classified the drowning image as nonbillable mnemonic contamination, low litigation risk, auto-clear on disposal. In other words: if she let the body go down the chute, the image would go with it and no one above claims would ever need to admit it had existed.

At the next table, a coworker she barely knew was eating cold noodles one-handed over an open torso report, picking sesame seeds off the print gel with the absent concentration of the overworked. Nobody in the Annex looked over. Nobody cared what one bargain model had done to Mira's afternoon. That was the company's favorite fact about the room. Everything that happened here looked, by design, like volume.

She closed the file, stripped off her gloves, and went to Celia's office with the chlorinated afterimage still hanging at the edge of her sight.

* * *

Celia Rusk's office had one glass wall facing the claims floor and blinds that were almost always half-closed, a compromise between surveillance and the performance of privacy. She kept the place too tidy to be natural, with one cup for pens, one stack of hearing packets, and one framed city permit from before CommonFrame had gotten large enough to stop hanging permits where workers could see them. Celia herself sat behind the desk in an ash-colored suit that looked expensive until you noticed the shine starting at the cuffs. Her silvering hair was cut clean at the jaw. Her hands, already knuckling with arthritis, occupied themselves with a black fountain pen that probably cost more than Mira's shoes.

"Sit down," Celia said.

Mira sat.

On the screen between them glowed LM-127B's file, thin and ugly with annotations. Celia had already read everything. Of course she had.

"You burned eleven minutes on a budget shell with a no-statement proxy," Celia said. Her voice in person was the same as in writing: controlled enough to sound kind if you were tired or naive. "At four-forty on a Friday."

"The map doesn't match the body."

"I saw that."

"The residue's wrong too."

"Mira."

Celia said her name the way managers said names when they wanted to remind you the company had loaned them your first name for disciplinary use.

"The hold shelf is full," Celia went on. "Cold storage is at weekend threshold. If you escalate every bargain anomaly into an existential inquiry, you will turn throughput into hearings by Monday."

"I'm not escalating every anomaly."

"No. Only the ones that interest you."

Mira bit the inside of her cheek hard enough to taste blood. It was a childish habit and she hated that Celia had likely noticed it months ago. "The unit's eleven days old with a healed scar. It came back from a dampened lease holding a child's drowning memory. That isn't normal wash failure."

Celia twirled the pen once between stiff fingers. "There is no category available to you that will improve those facts."

"There should be."

"There isn't." Celia leaned back, watching her with an expression so nearly sympathetic it annoyed Mira more than open contempt would have. "We're claims. We classify exposure after the money has already made its choice. If the factory printed bad stock, that is factory. If a shielded client carried something embarrassing through dampening, that is premium services. If you keep a bargain shell on ice because it unsettles you, that becomes my problem and then yours."

The fountain pen clicked softly against the desk. Outside the glass wall, bodies kept arriving under white sling plastic. The claims floor moved around them like a tide around a piling.

"Do you want me to code it contamination and pass a fee to the account," Mira asked, "or code it manufacturing defect and let factory bounce it back as unauthorized modification."

"I want you to reduce risk." Celia's tone sharpened by half a degree. "The proxy has declined recovery. There is no claimant statement. There is no active renter challenge. Clear the shelf. If you need language, use chain discrepancy, wait the minimum, then dispose."

Chain discrepancy was the safest dirty category in the building. It bought seventy-two hours if you were careful and nobody looked twice. It also flagged the unit as dead inventory awaiting paperwork correction, which meant by Monday some night-shift idiot or automated sweep could still send it to pulp if Mira did nothing else. Celia had given her a procedural out and an instruction to stop thinking. That was more generosity than most managers offered. It landed like a warning anyway.

"You checked the residue packet yourself?" Mira asked.

Celia's eyes flicked to the screen, then back. "Do not make me answer questions you already know I should not entertain."

There it was, very small. It was not agreement or confession. It was only the fact that Celia's caution had edges.

"Clear the shelf by end of shift," Celia said. "And log your time honestly. If audit asks why you queried a shielded proxy, I will tell them you got conscientious and then sensible."

Mira stood. "Right."

"Mira."

She paused at the door.

"Conscientious and sensible," Celia repeated. "In that order if possible."

Back on the floor, the overhead board had gone from yellow to a pulsing orange that made everyone work faster and think worse. Mira pulled LM-127B from the side rail and took the body through the staff door into hold processing, where the air dropped another five degrees and the sounds of the Annex flattened into distant machinery and tray wheels. The hold shelf ran along one wall: tagged bodies under thin plastic, waiting for paperwork, family disputes, court orders, or whatever other form of delayed violence the city could invent. Celia wanted it clear because a full shelf meant attention. Mira understood that. She also understood that attention had already found this unit whether anyone admitted it or not.

She laid LM-127B on the secondary scan cradle and peeled back the neck seal completely. The port at the nape stared up like a small round wound with tidy engineering around it. She checked the thighs again under cooler light. The bruises were real, layered in time. One was nearly fresh, and one was older. She checked the mouth, the ear notch, the rib scar. Under the magnifier the scar tissue showed mature collagen alignment, not cosmetic surface texturing. It had not been printed to look healed. It was healed.

Mira had seen cheap stock do ugly things. She had seen bulk lines seeded with freckle maps from the wrong demographic template, cartilage memory that made a returned hand keep curling around tools nobody had leased with it, and phantom cramps in uterine builds that had never menstruated because some donor pattern had not washed clean. CommonFrame called those issues nuisance carryover unless a lawyer called them something worse. But healed tissue in eleven-day-old stock was history, not nuisance.

She keyed in a deep residue review. The machine asked for disposal confirmation before proceeding because it was built on the assumption that nobody wanted to know more about a doomed body than billing required. She confirmed. Then, before the scan started, she opened the maintenance tab, slid a blank calibration wafer from the drawer under the cradle, and palmed it into the side port where the machine wrote temporary cache during service checks. Training staff used wafers like that to teach new adjusters how to grade bleed. Taking one was not theft in any way the company respected. Using it here was.

The scan arm descended with a mosquito whine.

Light moved under LM-127B's skin in a slow grid. The wall monitor filled with tissue density maps, port logs, wash abrasions, rental remap signatures. Mira watched the highlights bloom around the rib scar. There was internal reinforcement there too, not enough to alter function, just enough to prove the body had been grown around the seam rather than cut after printing. It had not been modified. It had been authored.

Her hands stayed very still on the console.

The mnemonic packet opened deeper this time, and it was not just the pool.

Heat pressed on bare shoulders. Rough municipal concrete chewed shallow crescents into wet toes. A child breathed too fast. The chemical bite of chlorine and sun-baked sunscreen sat at the back of the tongue. The boy in orange trunks laughed because he was still above water right up until he wasn't. Adults shifted from annoyance to panic in one ugly second. Someone dropped a ring of keys. Somebody yelled a name that the packet lost in static. Then the perspective lurched forward, a child's own body unable to decide whether to run or jump, and the certainty came huge and immediate that this was how a day split open and stayed split.

Mira yanked her hands back from the console as if the surface had gone hot. The packet cut. The room returned in pieces: coolant hum, tray wheels somewhere beyond the door, her own breath rasping loud in the cold.

That was not post-lease arousal haze. That was not a client's blurry fear of deep water carried accidentally across a luxury weekend. The memory had structure, age, and perspective. It belonged to a life with before and after in it. The body under the scanner had come off a bargain aggregate line eleven days ago and was carrying an event old enough to have scar tissue's emotional twin.

Her first thought was practical because that was how she had been trained by poverty and office work both: if this got kicked up, who would own the paperwork. Factory would bury it. Premium services would deny it. Claims would document the visible damage and let the rest go down the chute because no fee schedule accounted for history.

Her second thought came meaner and more honest. CommonFrame sold blank stock to people who wanted not to be themselves for a while. What if the bargain line was only blank at the price point where workers like Mira had to call it blank? What if cheap bodies came loaded because loaded was cheaper than clean?

The scan completed with a cheerful tone that made her want to hit the machine.

`Temporary cache available,` the screen informed her. `Clear for disposal.`

She copied the packet to the calibration wafer and watched the progress bar crawl. Six percent. Twenty-one. Fifty-eight. She listened for footsteps the whole time and heard only the distant metal cough of the pulp chute cycling somewhere below. When the copy finished she slid the wafer free, wiped it once on her sleeve out of useless instinct, and tucked it into the folded hem inside her left cuff where the plastic sat flat against her skin.

Now came the part that counted.

The classification screen offered its usual menu in tidy gray boxes:

`renter damage`

`unauthorized modification`

`manufacturing variance`

`mnemonic contamination`

`biohazard disposal`

`administrative hold`

Anything honest would move the body toward attention she could not control. Anything efficient would kill the file. Mira looked through the narrow hold-room window at the shelf and thought about all the other units she had signed away in her years here, every one a solved problem as long as the paperwork balanced. She thought about the assistant in the car saying burner shell as if using a cheap body made the night's damage morally compostable. She thought about the little hand slapping wet tile in terror while a boy went under. She thought about the scar the factory had pretended not to print.

Mira selected `administrative hold`.

The system opened a smaller box asking for reason. She entered: `chain discrepancy between production schema and physical unit; disposal suspended pending verification`. It was true enough to survive first glance. It was not true enough to explain why her pulse had shifted into the hard exact rhythm it took when she knew she was about to cost herself money.

Expected duration, the system asked.

`72 hours`.

Then the screen asked for an authorizing supervisor.

Mira hesitated, then entered Celia's general floor code for provisional review, the one used when managers gave verbal instruction and did not want to come downstairs to sign for it themselves. The system accepted it. That was the ugliest part. The building was full of doors you could open if you already understood how much everyone wanted not to notice.

She printed the hold tag, looped it around the body's wrist, and watched the shelf light at station H-9 turn amber.

The change was small and quiet, and it felt irreversible enough.

LM-127B slid into the hold berth with the other delayed bodies, but it did not look like them to Mira now. It was not a spoiled lease or a fee event. It was something with a scar that had taken time, with sex written fresh across it and a drowning child preserved somewhere under the wash. A body printed to be empty had come back carrying too much life.

Outside the hold room the Claims Annex kept feeding the weekend. Someone laughed. Someone cried at a counter. A printer spat out invoices fast as breath. Mira stood with one hand over the hidden wafer in her cuff and understood, not fully and not yet in words she could defend, that this was bigger than one bad unit and one rich man's disposable night. You did not get healed history in fresh stock by accident. You did not get childhood terror in a dampened burner unless somebody, somewhere upstream, had decided the bargain line could be built out of whatever the city could not afford to protect.

She logged the hold, closed the screen, and went back out to the floor before she could talk herself into being sensible.
