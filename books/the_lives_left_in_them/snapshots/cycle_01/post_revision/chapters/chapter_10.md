# Chapter 10

The tram shrieked below Brine Court before it came around the bend, and the sound ran through the apartment's thin floorboards into Mira's teeth. She had slept a little after dawn, or at least she had shut her eyes long enough to wake with the same pictures waiting behind them: hooks, drain channels, marker codes on salvage bins, a clerk at Warehouse Nine sorting whole bodies by how much other life they still carried.

The room smelled like wet concrete and detergent from the hall. Warehouse bleach sat under it anyway, stubborn as a stain.

Gabe was in the kitchen, working a pan one-handed. Yesterday's rice hissed in oil with an egg broken through it and the last of a scallion chopped fine. He cooked with his hips held crooked, keeping pressure off the bad part of his back without ever making a performance of it. The white edge of his lumbar brace showed where his T-shirt had ridden up. On the table beside him lay a free tide sheet from the dock office, folded into quarters, softened at the creases from being handled too much. He still circled the high-water times in pencil even in weeks when nobody called him for legal work.

He glanced over when she came in. "You look like a government hallway."

Mira took two plates from the dish rack. "Good morning to you too."

"No, I mean it with love. Damp, fluorescent, something terrible happened there."

"You should be lying down."

"You should stop using my medical file as conversation." He scooped rice onto the plates and nodded toward the kettle. "Tea's hot. Lin went out with Jo an hour ago. They took the good umbrella and left us the one that leaks right over the handle, which I think counts as personality development."

There was a mug upside down by the sink, drying with careful precision. Lin had lined it exactly with the crack in the draining board, as if neatness could pay rent. Mira poured tea and sat.

Gabe dropped into the chair across from her with the small, involuntary breath he always tried to swallow before it became a sound. Then he grinned, because she had noticed. "See? Graceful. Gazelle stuff."

"You're thirty-one."

"And still young enough to be insulted by that tone."

The rice was too salty and exactly what she needed. He had thrown in one packet of soy too many and compensated with lemon from the heel of a fruit someone downstairs had handed him the day before. Gabe collected those little neighborhood economies the way other people collected favors, never enough to feel like he owed anyone, just enough to keep the week patched.

She ate faster than she meant to. He watched, not unkindly.

"Bad yesterday?" he asked.

Mira kept her eyes on the plate. "Work."

"That word is doing a lot."

"It usually does."

He tore open a pepper sauce packet with his teeth and lined the empty foil beside his plate in a neat silver row. He always did that. Even as a kid he had stacked wrappers, ticket stubs, bottle caps, anything that looked like it had a count built into it. "All right," he said. "Then here's my medical recommendation. Seawall. Wind's right. Food carts'll be open after the rain. You can sit there and pretend not to tell me things."

She looked up. "Since when do you prescribe fresh air?"

"Since my patient looks haunted and annoying." He shoveled in a bite and shrugged. "Also I need to walk before tomorrow. If I lock up in this chair all day, I'll move like a landlord."

That was closer to truth. The weather had turned in the night, a first early-autumn front rolling hard off the water, and changes in pressure worked their way into Gabe's spine like gossip into a building. Mira had heard him up twice in the dark, once in the bathroom, once just standing at the kitchen window waiting for the pain pills to become more than theory.

She should have gone to Blue Hour. She should have been with Tavi and Jo, should have been building an evidence chain from the partial manifest on the wafer before courage cooled into caution. Instead she thought of the bins at Warehouse Nine, of residual richness written in black marker on a body with one hand half pulped away, and the thought of another room full of damaged flesh made something in her close.

"Fine," she said.

Gabe pointed his fork at her. "You didn't have to sound so moved."

"I save my warmth for clients."

"Then I'm billing you."

They left an hour later with the leaking umbrella, though the rain had mostly finished. Cordera's air came salted and oily off the harbor, carrying fryer grease from the market, coolant from the tram line, a sweet rot from the canal that nobody admitted noticing anymore. The street below Brine Court shone with old rain. Delivery scooters hissed through puddles. A gull worked at a burst trash bag with the concentration of a surgeon.

Gabe wore his old navy dock jacket even though one zipper tooth was gone and the left cuff had gone shiny with age. He had patched the inside pocket with yellow sail tape. He kept his tide sheet folded in there like an official document. As they walked downhill he dragged his left foot only a little, disguising the hitch by setting his pace to the slope, but Mira knew the arithmetic. Each block cost him something. The city charged interest on every staircase.

He talked through the market in his public voice, the easy one that made strangers hand back an extra lime or save him the last cheap coffee without feeling hustled. He asked a fishmonger whether the storm had scared the mackerel into honesty. He told a flower seller her carnations looked like they had survived a knife fight. She laughed so hard she had to take the cigarette out of her mouth.

At the bottom of Canal Street the seawall opened up all at once: concrete steps slick with spray, gulls shouldering one another off the rail, ferry horns carrying low over the water. The sky had not settled on a single job. To the east it was a clean pale blue. Over the harbor the clouds still hung in bruised folds, dark underneath, bright at the edges where the sun worried at them.

The food carts had come back out fast. In Cordera weather, people started selling hot things the minute the rain dropped from impossible to rude.

Gabe steered them toward a fry cart with a striped awning and a handwritten sign for smelts, cassava cakes, and pepper broth. The woman running it wore a green visor even under cloud cover and had one thumbnail grown long and lacquered copper, useful for peeling foil lids and slapping rude hands away from the counter. She saw Gabe and rolled her eyes in recognition before he had even reached her.

"If you ask me for extra vinegar again," she said, "I'm charging by the splash."

"Sita, wound me if you want, but don't ration me."

"Hear him." She tipped her head toward Mira. "He says that every week like I'm the cruel state."

"You wish you had their consistency."

Sita snorted, set two paper trays down, and, after pretending not to, gave Gabe the little cup of pickled peppers she normally sold separate. He slipped the coins over with a care that told Mira he had counted them before leaving home.

They took their food to the lower steps where the concrete still held last night's rain in shallow dents. Gabe lowered himself carefully, one hand on the rail, jaw gone flat for three seconds with the effort. Then he sat, exhaled through his nose, and immediately ruined the dignity of the moment by shaking half the peppers over his fish.

"You going to eat that," he asked, looking at her untouched broth, "or are you just taking it to court?"

Mira lifted the paper cup. It was hotter than she expected. Pepper and fish stock and cilantro steam climbed into her face. Her body answered before the rest of her had time to stay suspicious.

For a while they let the seawall do the talking. Waves slapped the lower pilings. Gulls screamed over a dropped strip of batter. Behind them a pair of children in school sweats tried to kick a flattened bottle cap into the storm drain while their grandmother shouted corrections in Spanish from under a clear plastic hood. Out on the freight lanes, cranes along North Quay stood like thin black handwriting against the brighter patch of sky.

Gabe ate with quick efficiency and then slowed, settling into the weather. This was one of the few places where he stopped trying to look useful. He watched the water the way some people watched television, fully given over to it, reading mood and timing out of a surface that never kept one shape long.

"High water's late," he said after a while, tapping the folded tide sheet through his jacket pocket. "Storm nudged it."

"You still read those on your days off?"

"Tide notices, union boards, gig boards, menus, eviction threats. I'm a broad-minded man."

She smiled despite herself.

He caught it. "There she is."

"Don't get excited."

"Too late. I saw evidence."

The word snagged on her and turned into Warehouse Nine again, into marker on bins, half a serial visible through pulp, and Lin going very still beside her in that wet cold place because the body on the cart had shared the shape of one of Lin's remembered scars. Mira set the broth down before she spilled it.

Gabe looked out at the water when he asked the next thing, which was how she knew he meant it. "That bad?"

Mira could lie to clerks, supervisors, investigators. Gabe had a way of making lies feel not immoral exactly, just cheap. "Worse than I thought," she said.

He took that in with no theatrical change of face. "Worse how?"

She watched a harbor tug push against the side of a container barge, all blunt force and correction. "Not a wash problem. Not sloppiness. They're doing it on purpose."

Gabe's chewing stopped. "The overlaps."

She nodded.

"On purpose."

"Yeah."

Wind came hard across the water. It flattened his jacket against his chest and lifted a strand of her hair loose from its tie. Above them the ferry gave one deep horn blast that seemed to move the air in her ribs.

Gabe rubbed the heel of his hand over his mouth. "Jesus."

Mira had expected outrage, or disgust, or at least the clean relief of someone outside the thing saying yes, that's monstrous, now go break it. What she got instead was Gabe staring toward North Quay with the squint he used on loading rates and weather, as if the information had entered him already looking for practical consequences.

His phone buzzed. He pulled it out, glanced at the screen, and let it buzz itself dead.

"Who was it?" she asked.

"North Quay Recovery. Or one of its nephews."

"About the old frame charge?"

"What else do men like that talk about? They don't call to ask after my spirit."

The debt had been hanging over him so long it had become a piece of furniture in the apartment, ugly and easy to bruise yourself on. Seven thousand eight hundred dollars because a heavy-lift rental had come back damaged after a salvage shift, a number large enough to be abstract until the moments it wasn't: the legal jobs he turned down, the collection threats, the way his shoulders changed when an unknown number lit the screen.

He slid the phone away and reached for more fish. His hand shook once before he got it steady.

Mira heard herself say it before she had decided whether she meant to. "I could put this somewhere. What I found. Enough people see it, enough of the right people, maybe the whole fucking line burns."

Gabe let out one quiet breath that was almost a laugh and had no humor in it. "There it is."

"What?"

"The face. I knew it was that face."

"I'm serious."

"I know." He looked at her then, not teasing, not soft. "Mir. If CommonFrame burns this week, what do you think happens Friday morning?"

"To them?"

"No. To me."

She opened her mouth and shut it again.

He rested his forearms on his knees. The wind pressed his hair flat, showing the little scar inside his right temple where he'd once gone into a bollard as a teenager and claimed it made him look strategic. "In this body?" he said. "I can stand maybe forty minutes if I lie about the pain, maybe an hour if the floor's kind. I can carry groceries up the stairs one bag at a time. I can wash dishes leaning on my left hip and then hate myself for the next two hours. The dock doesn't pay for any of that."

Mira said nothing.

"The dock app pays for lift class, cold tolerance, fast recovery, shoulder range. It pays for knees that don't blow out on wet metal. It pays for a spine that isn't mine." He kept his voice level, practical, almost bored, which made each word land harder. "If you blow up the cheap end of this market overnight, North Quay doesn't pause to admire the ethics. It fills those berths with people who can afford cleaner frames and leaves the rest of us onshore. If I sign on in my own body, I get maybe scanner work, gate checks, some miserable seat in a booth if somebody's cousin dropped dead. That's if Recovery doesn't garnish it before I touch it. If I go in through a gray broker with a heavy-lift frame already booked, I get cash. Three hundred on a bad shift. More if the load's ugly and the weather's worse. That's rent, groceries, pills, the electric staying on, the landlord staying theoretical for one more week."

Mira stared at the cranes across the water until they blurred and sharpened again. Warehouse Nine had given her something clean in one direction, at least: a factory full of proof, an evil with bins and paperwork. She had walked out of it wanting a single line from crime to exposure to consequence. She had not, until that moment, placed her brother's body on the other side of the line.

"I'm not saying leave them alone," Gabe said.

"It sounds a little like that."

"No." He shook his head. "I'm saying don't talk like the city's gonna pause and respectfully invent a better option because you showed everybody the blood under the floor."

His voice roughened on the last few words. Pain did that to him, scraped the polish off and left the grain.

Mira looked at him properly. The brace was digging into his ribs even sitting down. At the neck of his T-shirt there was a patch of adhesive shadow behind one ear where the transfer contacts had sat on his last shift, the skin still dull and pink. He had come to the seawall in the body he was born in, the one that hurt, the one the city priced low, and she had almost used him as a pure witness against the system that kept him fed.

"How often?" she asked.

"What."

"How often are you renting now?"

He gave her a sideways look. "That's the first question?"

"It's the useful one."

There was the smallest flicker of approval. Mira could still speak sibling, even from inside a bad realization.

"Two, three times a week if the board's decent. Four if rent's got teeth." He picked a pepper from the paper tray and held it by the stem without eating it. "And before you do the numbers, no, it doesn't pay clean. Broker takes a cut. Pod time takes a cut. If I bring the frame back with any scrape they can photograph under bright light, somebody takes another cut. Then Recovery circles whatever wage work still touched my name. That's why I stay gray when I can."

"And when you can't?"

"Then I smile nice and fill out forms and watch money I never held get taken by people who say it like weather."

He popped the pepper into his mouth and chewed, eyes watering immediately. "Shit."

Mira almost laughed. It hurt in her throat. "You did that to yourself."

"I contain multitudes and poor judgment."

The joke hung between them, thin but real. Then he put the empty paper tray aside and said, in a voice cleaner than the rest of the conversation had been, "I don't need CommonFrame innocent. I need somewhere to clock in on Thursday."

That sat in her harder than if he had shouted.

Behind them someone dropped a glass bottle. It broke in a bright ugly burst against the upper promenade, and half the gulls took flight at once. Their wings flashed white against the harbor and turned gray as soon as they crossed into cloud.

Mira rubbed her thumb against the seam of the broth cup until the paper softened. "I hate that this is the choice."

"It isn't a choice yet." Gabe leaned back on his hands and grimaced at the pull in his spine. "That's sort of the problem, isn't it. You thought you had one job. Turns out you've got two."

"Expose them and keep people alive."

"There you go. Very elegant. You should put it on a brochure."

"Fuck off."

"See, that was warmer."

They stayed until the broth went lukewarm and the wind worked through her jacket. On the way back up the steps Gabe paused once, one hand on the rail, not speaking until the pain wave passed. Mira waited beside him without looking directly at him. That was one of the old agreements between them, older than either of their adult disasters: witness without making a stage.

At the top, he said, too casually, "I need to stop at Harbor Shift before we head home."

She looked over.

"Need to lock tomorrow's frame before surge hits. Storm week always gets stupid."

"You're working tomorrow?"

"That was the 'walk before tomorrow' part of the invitation, yes."

"You left that out."

"You were busy being haunted."

He started toward the tram underpass, and after a beat she followed.

***

Harbor Shift occupied the ground floor of an old ticket office under the elevated tracks, three blocks in from the seawall. The sign out front still showed the ghost of whatever ferry service had used the building before biotech found better margins in flesh. Now the windows were pasted over with labor-body ads: heavy-lift, cold-room, ladder-flex, night-face hospitality, all smiling models with impossible shoulders and teeth too even to trust. In the corner of every poster sat the small square of the CommonFrame mark, discreet as guilt.

Inside, the air smelled of disinfectant, wet coats, and burned coffee from a machine older than either of them. A line had formed between chrome stanchions bolted to the floor, as if a little order could make the room less degrading. Everyone in it stood in the bodies they had been born in or lived long enough to become ordinary in: damaged knees, bad skin, tired mouths, wrists wrapped for repetitive strain, one man with oxygen tubing tucked under his collar, a woman in hotel housekeeping shoes rubbing the base of her thumb like she was trying to grind pain back out through the joint. Near the wall a teenager in school trousers held a courier helmet against his chest and kept mouthing arithmetic to himself, pricing the shift before he'd even been assigned one.

Mira had seen every kind of body returned to claims. She had handled rent damage after bachelor weekends, sewer jobs, offshore sex packages, stunt tourism, vanity bruising, cheap assaults. She knew in theory that the bargain lines serviced labor as much as pleasure. Theory had never looked this much like a Tuesday queue at the pharmacy.

Gabe's pace slowed. It was not shame exactly. It was preparation. He put on the expression she had watched him use with landlords, triage nurses, cops at transit checks, any counter where being easy to like might lower the fee by one small humiliating amount.

"You don't have to wait," he said.

"I'm here."

"Could be boring."

"I survived claims."

"Fair."

At the counter a clerk worked through reservations with the focus of somebody trying very hard not to take the room home. She had a sunflower pin crocheted onto her cardigan and a strip of silver tape wrapped around the bridge of her glasses. Every few minutes she turned her wedding ring backward, stone to palm, before calling the next person up. When the man with the oxygen tubing left, walking slower than when he'd come in, Gabe stepped forward.

"Afternoon, Nessa."

She glanced up, recognized him, and gave him the tired half smile of a woman who remembered everybody's worst paperwork. "Afternoon, Gabe. You here to confirm North Quay salvage?"

"If nobody richer stole it."

"Not yet."

Her fingers moved over the terminal. The screen light turned her taped glasses pale blue. Mira watched the clerk's face perform small arithmetic changes: routine, annoyance, sympathy, the professional blank that replaced both.

"Deposit bumped," Nessa said.

Gabe's charm held for one second and then tightened at the corners. "By how much."

"One twenty."

"It was sixty yesterday."

"Yesterday the compliance patch hadn't finished syncing." She winced as soon as she heard herself, ashamed of the language and still unable to avoid it. "I'm sorry. North Quay flagged the old damage claim against your work profile. Any heavy-lift reservation cross-linked to that account pulls higher risk now."

"Higher risk to who."

Nessa gave him the look of someone who had no power and enough self-respect not to pretend otherwise. "You know the answer."

Gabe set both palms on the counter. He had cleaned up his language automatically. Access did that. "Can you move me to a Class Two and keep the berth?"

"Berth requires Three. Fifty-kilo minimum on manifest."

"Can you split the deposit."

"Half today, half before induction."

"And if I show with half tomorrow?"

"They can reassign."

Behind them the line shifted. Nobody made a fuss. Everyone in the room knew the etiquette of other people's precarity. Do not look interested. Do not look relieved it isn't your turn yet. Do not look at all if you can help it.

Gabe pulled out his wallet. The leather had gone thin at the fold and been reinforced with clear packing tape. He counted once, stopped, counted again. Mira knew before he did that he was short. She could see it in the way he flattened the bills on the counter, as if straightness might increase value.

"I've got ninety-two," he said.

Nessa swallowed. "I can hold the slot for fifteen minutes."

Gabe huffed one breath through his nose. "Generous empire."

Nessa's mouth twitched. "Don't flirt with me when I'm extorting you. It cheapens the craft."

That got a sound out of him, not quite a laugh. He rubbed his thumb over the edge of a bill until it curled. Mira reached into her coat pocket before either of them made it into pride.

"Here," she said.

He looked over, immediate refusal already there.

"Don't," he said quietly.

"You need twenty-eight."

"Mir."

"You need twenty-eight."

He held her eyes for a beat too long, anger and gratitude mixing into the expression that had haunted both of them since they were children and there had not been enough money for the thing that was plainly needed. Then he looked away.

"I'll pay you back tomorrow," he said.

"Sure."

Nessa took the bills without comment. Her nail polish had chipped down to half moons at the cuticles. She printed the confirmation slip on a machine that squealed and jammed once before surrendering a strip of thermal paper.

"Induction at five forty," she said. "Pod bank C. Bring your own contact gel if you've got it. House stock's watered."

"Always nice to hear that sentence about a medical process," Gabe said.

"Welcome to the future."

He folded the slip and tucked it into the yellow-taped pocket of his jacket with the tide sheet. For a second, watching the two pieces of paper disappear together, Mira thought that the whole city was in miniature there, weather and borrowed flesh, schedules and debt, survival filed beside survival until the pages wore thin.

As they turned from the counter the woman in housekeeping shoes stepped up. She was older than Mira had first thought, with a lipstick line gone feathery at the edges and a lunch sack printed with cartoon pears. "I need stair-flex if you still have one," she said before Nessa could ask. "West End tower. Twenty-six floors and the service lift's out again."

"I've got one until six," Nessa said. "After that it goes premium hospitality."

The woman closed her eyes briefly, doing whatever fast ugly math that required. "Six gets me three rooms short."

"I'm sorry."

"Yeah," the woman said, not mean, not forgiving either. "Everybody is."

Mira moved aside for the courier kid, the man with the oxygen, the next body in line, the next need. Around the room the posters kept smiling their smooth impossible smiles: better shoulders, better hands, better night skin, better work. None of the people beneath them looked as if they believed the ads. They believed the shift. That was different.

Outside, the late light had started to thin. Water dripped from the underside of the tracks in a slow, regular tick. A tram thundered overhead, shaking rust dust down from the beams.

Gabe lit a cigarette and then seemed to remember who he was standing next to. "Don't."

"I wasn't going to say anything."

"Your eyebrows were."

He took one drag anyway, shallow, more ritual than intake, and crushed it under his heel after two breaths. The gesture made her sadder than the cigarette would have. He was trying to be temporary even in his bad habits, as if anything that took up space in his body had to justify the rent.

They walked half a block before he said, "Don't do that thing in your head where this turns me into your argument."

Mira looked at him. "I wasn't."

"Bullshit."

There was no heat in it. That made it harder to dodge.

"I was thinking," she said.

"Yes. Exactly. In bullet points." He adjusted the front of his jacket where the brace caught under the zipper. "I'm not your clean example, Mir. Not for them, not against them. I'm just a guy who needs a spine he doesn't own for about ten hours tomorrow."

The sentence landed so plain and so ugly there was nothing to improve in it.

They stopped at the crosswalk while a freight tram clanged through. On the far side of the street, down by the bus shelter, three workers in borrowed dock bodies waited with their hoods up against the spray. Even standing still they were different from the bodies underneath them: broader through the chest, harder in the knees, built for lift and repetition and cold. One of them was eating soup out of a deli container with hands too thick and clean to be born hands. When he laughed at something the smallest lag rippled through the face, that tiny half beat Mira knew from returns, the evidence of somebody occupying matter made for speed rather than ease.

She thought of Lin. She thought of the bargain shells at Warehouse Nine, sorted not as people, not as corpses, not even as damaged goods, but as useful residual density for future stock. She thought of the line inside Harbor Shift, of the housekeeping worker bargaining with the clock, of the courier boy pricing his body before he got to use it, of Gabe quietly taking watered contact gel because he could not afford standards.

The city had built an economy out of distance from pain. The rich bought cleaner, prettier distances. The poor bought whatever distance they could get and called it a workday.

"I know what they're doing," she said, mostly to herself.

"Yeah."

"I know what they deserve."

"So do I. That still doesn't tell me what I do Friday."

"I don't know what happens after."

Gabe looked at her then, really looked, and his face lost the last of the swagger it wore in public. What remained was younger and older at once: her little brother, the man who had learned to joke because jokes kept the room from smelling too strongly of failure.

"Well," he said, softer now. "Figure that part too."

There was no comfort in it. That made it useful.

They bought soup on the way uphill because the wind had turned sharper and because neither of them wanted to go home yet and say what the afternoon had become. The vendor ladling from the steel vat wore a denim apron covered in enamel pins from dead ferry lines and hummed old merengue under his breath between customers. Gabe, returning to form by inches, asked him whether the soup was medicinal. The man said, "Depends what you did," and gave them extra bread because he approved of the question.

By the time they reached Brine Court, the windows over the stairwell had gone mirror-dark. The tram screamed around the bend again, the same sound, the same metal complaint. Upstairs, their apartment held the warmth of shut rooms and the faint wet-fabric smell Lin always brought back from Blue Hour now, detergent threaded through city air.

Gabe toed off his shoes at the door and stood there one second too long, eyes closed. Then he took the confirmation slip from his pocket, set it on the table like a bill, and went to the bathroom to peel off the brace. Mira heard the muffled curse when the straps dragged over raw skin.

She unpacked the soup. Lin had left a note by the stove in careful block letters: JO STOLE ME FOR DINNER. DO NOT WAIT. Someone had added, in Jo's thicker handwriting beneath it: FEED YOURSELVES ANYWAY.

Mira almost smiled. Then Gabe came back out in his undershirt, moving slowly, and the smile went nowhere.

The brace had left red trenches around his waist. Above one hip the skin was mottled yellow and old plum where pressure had pooled from yesterday's shift. He saw her looking and reached automatically for his shirt.

"Don't," she said.

He stopped.

She did not touch the bruises. She wanted to and did not. There were kinds of care he could take and kinds he could not bear. Instead she slid the bowl of soup toward him. "Eat before the pills."

"Yes, ma'am."

He sat. The joke was dutiful this time, not lively. He ate half the bowl and most of the bread and fell asleep on top of the blanket before finishing the second pain pill, one arm thrown over his face against the kitchen light. Mira took the pill bottle from the mattress before it rolled off and put a folded towel under his knees the way the physical therapist had once shown him before the insurance visits ran out.

Then she stood at the sink with the Harbor Shift receipt in one hand and the soup spoon in the other, looking out at the dark window that gave her back only the room.

Warehouse Nine had not become less monstrous because Gabe needed a rental spine. Lin had not become less real because a housekeeping worker needed stair-flex by six. The bodies in those bins were still stolen from the poor twice, once in life through contracts nobody rich ever had to sign, and again after, chopped into useful traces and sold back as access. None of that changed. What changed was her faith that proof alone would be enough.

If she blew the factory open without anything ready behind the blast, the first bodies crushed by the collapse would not belong to executives. They would belong to people in taped wallets and housekeeping shoes and oxygen tubing and old dock jackets with tide sheets folded in the pocket. The city would call that unfortunate. The city called all its appetite unfortunate when somebody poorer had to carry it.

Below the building the tram came through once more, metal shrieking over the curve. From the hill, faint but clear, the ferry horn answered from the dark water.

Mira set the receipt on the counter and flattened it with her palm until the thin paper went damp. She still wanted CommonFrame exposed. She wanted Warehouse Nine dragged into daylight and Lin named in a language the law could not grind back down into inventory. She wanted the whole elegant lie ruined. But wanting ruin was the simple part. Exposure without somewhere for the fallout to land would be one more way the city fed on the poor. Keeping people alive through what came after was another job entirely, and now she knew the proof had to go to Blue Hour before it touched daylight.
