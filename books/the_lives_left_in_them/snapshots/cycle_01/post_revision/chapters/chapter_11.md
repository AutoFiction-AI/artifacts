# Chapter 11

Mira found Tavi at the wetbench in Blue Hour's back room, with the late-night dryers thumping out front hard enough to make the laundromat sound, from the street, like one patient heart with too much laundry on it. The steam had thinned enough to show lint pasted to the pipes and the water marks climbing the cinderblock in old brown rungs. He had a pair of borrowed wrists opened under the lamp and was working grit out of a tendon channel with a dental pick; somebody from the shrimp sheds had dropped the frame before close. The room still carried fryer grease under the detergent, iodine under the steam, and that sweet tired tissue smell cheap stock got after a double shift in salt air.

He did not look up when Mira came in. “If that’s another ankle for tonight, tell them I’m dead and Jo already sold the corpse for parts.”

“It’s me.”

That made him glance over. He saw her face and put the pick down at once. “Jesus. What happened?”

Mira shut the door behind her and turned the lock. The click sounded louder than the dryers. “I need you to listen before you start fixing it.”

“That bad.”

“Worse.”

He reached for the paper cup by the sink, took half a swallow of coffee gone cold, and then peeled the sleeve off it in a slow spiral the way he did when he needed his hands occupied. The skin over the burn scar on his forearm looked waxy in the bench light. “All right. Talk.”

She had meant to do it clean. She had meant to lay out Warehouse Nine the way she laid out a damage claim: sequence, evidence, cost. Instead the words came out with the smell still on them. She described the loading bay slick with old mash and coolant, the bins tagged for residual richness instead of defect type, and the shells lined up under strip lights with administrative stickers on their heels. CommonFrame used the same little yellow bars for salvage credits, only these had numbers that matched debtor riders and expired lease contracts. The room held half-pulped faces that had been spared the grinder because someone farther up the canal had decided there was still enough life left in them to stretch across a cheaper product line. She told him that the sorter notes kept repeating recomposition yield, donor blend stability, wash variance acceptable in value tier. She told him about the rack of bargain models waiting for seed, clean on the outside and empty in the legal sense only.

Tavi did not interrupt. Once, when she said donor blend, his mouth opened and closed again. When she got to the part about debtor scans hidden in consent riders, he rubbed his thumb across his flattened nose hard enough to pink the skin.

When she finished, the dryers kept thumping. Somewhere out front a coin changer coughed and fell quiet.

Tavi said, “Fuck.”

“That’s all you’ve got?”

“No.” He dropped the coffee sleeve on the bench. Damp paper curled against the metal tray. “That’s the part I can say before I start saying things I can’t take back.”

Mira leaned against the sink because she had been standing on warehouse concrete since yesterday in some private part of herself and had not realized it until now. “I thought you already knew enough to be sick. You didn’t know this.”

“I knew the wash cheat was deliberate. I knew they were stretching residue because the margins on bargain stock were impossible otherwise. I didn’t know they were sorting live richness at the disposal end like meat grades.” He looked at the opened wrists under the lamp, not at her. “Lin saw it too.”

“Yes.”

He shut his eyes for a beat. “Fuck.”

Mira took the anomaly wafer copy out of her coat pocket and set it on the bench beside the ruined coffee sleeve. It looked pathetic there, a square of stolen proof no bigger than a communion wafer, all the rot of the company compressed into something you could lose in the couch cushions. “We can’t sit on it anymore.”

He gave a short laugh with no humor in it. “That sentence can mean six bad things.”

“It means we stop pretending this is a claims problem or a repair problem or one warehouse full of ugly paperwork. We tell people.”

Now he looked at her. The looseness had gone out of him. He straightened from the bench so fast the stool wheels clicked backward. “No.”

The answer landed hard enough that for a second she thought she had misheard him under the dryers. “No what.”

“No, not like that.”

“Like what. Words in public?”

“Like panic in public.” He pointed at the wafer without touching it. “You put that raw into the city and by breakfast the Bureau freezes every bargain line in circulation on safety grounds. They’ll call it temporary, like a clamp that’s only supposed to stay on for a minute, and then they’ll leave it there till the tissue dies. They start doing seizure checks in rooming houses and break bays and sex hotels. Every renter south of the canal who uses a cheap body because their own can’t climb stairs or lift freight or take a man twice their size gets told the stock is contaminated and their lease is void pending review. Pending review means no work, no cash, and a bill for unauthorized cognitive damage if they complain wrong.”

Mira said, “They’re printing people out of stolen scans.”

“I know what they’re doing.”

“Do you.”

“Yes.” The word cracked out sharper than he usually let himself sound. He dragged a hand over his face, then planted both palms on the bench and forced his voice back down. “Yes, I do. That’s why I’m telling you not to swing at them blind. You don’t cut into a body without somewhere for the fluid to go.”

He started moving as he talked, not pacing exactly, just setting a clamp beside the sealant gun and rolling a length of synth gauze tight enough to dent the cardboard core. He always needed a task under his hands. “You know Marisol from the hotel row, the one with the left knee that catches when she’s scared? You know the twins Jo sent us who split one dock frame between them because one shift each is the difference between rent and sleeping in their van? Your brother. Half the fucking quay. They’re on bargain stock because premium wash is for people who can afford to treat a body like luggage. If CommonFrame gets cornered too fast, they don’t save those people. They cut them loose. They send out a service suspension and a sorry for the inconvenience and a notice from debt resolution with cleaner fonts than the funeral home uses. We’d be standing here noble as saints while everybody we’ve been patching gets dropped back into the bodies they were trying to get out of for twelve hours at a time.”

The worst part was that he was not sounding like Celia. He was sounding like Gabe on the seawall, talking about what rent demanded from a damaged spine. Mira hated him a little for that. She hated the city for making everybody with working eyes arrive at the same rotten arithmetic.

“So what,” she said. “We protect access to the machine that’s grinding them.”

Tavi laughed again, angry now. “No. We cut the machine where it bleeds and not where it kicks.”

“That’s neat.”

“It’s true.” He stabbed a finger toward the back room wall, as if all the neighborhood bodies on the other side of it were listening. “You want the leak? Fine. Leak the debt riders. Leak the donor consent language buried in the contracts. Leak the disposal variance and the fake damage charges that keep people paying for contamination they didn’t cause. Get labor lawyers. Get one reporter who can read a yield sheet without turning it into a ghost story for rich people over brunch. Get the city looking at fraud first, because fraud makes paper move. If the first thing they hear is personhood, they’ll turn Lin into a freak exhibit and the rest of the living ones into defective inventory with better press.”

Mira folded her arms because otherwise she was going to reach for him and either shake him or hold on, and she did not trust either impulse. “You’re asking me to keep Lin hidden.”

“I’m asking you to keep Lin alive.”

“In a room.”

“For a little longer, maybe. Until we know how many there are and where. Until we’ve got maintenance keys copied and parts routes mapped and somewhere safer than your kitchen to put anybody who wakes up. You blow this open before that, CommonFrame starts pulping evidence and the Bureau helps them call it compliance.”

She could smell his skin under the steam, solder and soap and the stale clean cotton of the T-shirt he had slept in. The memory of his mouth last week came up mean and inconvenient. “And your part in it.”

He took the hit without blinking. “You think that’s what this is.”

“I think you’ve got reasons to prefer a careful story.”

“Of course I’ve got reasons.” He spread his hands. Grease lived in the lines of his knuckles no matter how hard he washed. “I helped build one of the shortcuts. I’ll wear that when it’s time. But if you think I’m hiding because I’m scared for my own neck, you’re not listening. They can hang me from the Claims Annex sign if they want. I’m talking about the woman cleaning legal offices in a rental pelvis because her own got cracked at nineteen and Medicaid gave her six months of pills and a pamphlet. I’m talking about kids doing freight in borrowed backs because their real ones won’t hold a crate. I’m talking about Lin and anyone like Lin getting swept into a warehouse with a nicer label on the door.”

He was breathing hard now, not loud, but enough to lift the scarred hollow above his collarbone. Mira noticed he had lined three scalpels up beside the sink by length, perfectly parallel, without knowing he had done it.

She said, “So your endgame is what. A better scandal.”

“My endgame is enough pressure to crack the contracts open without letting them crush the people underneath first.” His voice dropped. “Mira, if you say this wrong, the city will hear unsafe bargain stock. They won’t hear stolen lives. They’ll hear nuisance. They’ll hear liability. Rich people love an ethics talk right up until it threatens same-day service.”

The dryers stopped. The room lost its patient mechanical pulse and felt abruptly smaller, only the drip from the bench sink and the tram beginning its climb somewhere outside. She could hear the front of the laundromat now: a cart wheel bumping tile, Jo’s low voice to somebody headed home, the rattle of the metal grate.

Mira picked up the wafer and closed her hand around it. The corners bit her palm. She had come wanting reinforcement, maybe even absolution. She had imagined Tavi swearing, kissing her temple, saying yes, now, burn it down. Instead he had given her a plan full of sequence and casualties, another adult answer shaped like a delay.

“You should’ve been there,” she said. “You should’ve seen how they tagged them.”

Tavi’s face changed then, some hardness giving way into something worse because it was tenderness and grief at once. “I know. I should’ve been there instead of you.”

That almost undid her. She put the wafer back in her coat before it could. “I’m not promising anything.”

“I know.”

“And if you start acting like this is only a messaging problem, I’m gone.”

“It isn’t.” He scrubbed both hands over his face and came away looking older, bench-tired rather than flippant for once. “It’s a triage problem. That’s different.”

She unlocked the door. The front room was dark except for the soda machine glow and the strip of orange streetlight under the grate. Tavi did not try to stop her. When she stepped out into the damp detergent air of the laundromat, what she carried with her was not agreement, only the knowledge that the first person she had chosen to tell wanted a knife where she wanted a siren.

Brine Court’s hallway smelled like wet plaster, cumin, and the old radiator that hissed even when the heat was off. Somebody on the second floor had burned garlic. Somebody on the fourth was fighting soft enough to make the words worse. Mira let herself into the apartment and found Gabe asleep on the couch with the television muttering weather at low volume, one hand tucked under his shirt where the brace usually rubbed him raw. His actual brace hung over the kitchen chairback like a shed shell.

Lin sat at the table in one of Gabe’s old yellow rain shirts, sleeves rolled up six careful times. The free advertiser from the bodega lay in front of them, apartment listings cut into narrow columns and stacked by neighborhood. They had made three piles: studios, one-bedrooms, impossible fantasies with harbor views. Beside the piles sat a chipped mug full of pencil shavings and the little scissors Jo used on loose threads.

Mira stopped in the kitchen doorway.

Lin looked up at once. Their lighter iris caught the weak stove light and held it. “You’re late.”

“I know.”

“You ate?”

“At Jo’s.” It was a lie too small to matter. Lin knew it anyway by the look they gave her, but they let it pass. Mira hung her coat on the chair, careful not to knock Gabe’s brace down. “What’s this.”

Lin glanced at the piles as if seeing them from outside for the first time. “Addresses.”

“I can see that.”

“I wanted to know what people ask for a room with a door that closes.” They touched the top clipping with one finger. “This one says proof of income, government identification, and first month plus deposit. This one says no pets and no subletting and no exceptions. This one says professionals only, which I think means poor people with clean shoes.”

Despite herself, Mira smiled. It hurt. “That’s exactly what it means.”

Lin set the scissors down blade-closed. “Did Tavi agree with you.”

Mira pulled out the chair opposite them and sat. The vinyl was split under one thigh; foam pushed up through the crack. She could hear Gabe snore once and turn over in the other room. “Not exactly.”

Lin went still in the particular way they did when stress hit hard, thumb pressed against the mug until the pencil shavings inside stopped whispering against the ceramic. “Tell me.”

So Mira told them. She did not repeat every word from Blue Hour, but she told enough: Tavi’s fear of a blunt disclosure, the service suspensions, the seizure orders, and the way a safety review could become a starvation tool in less than a day. She even said the part she had most resisted hearing: that if the city moved fast, poor renters and living overlaps might take the first blow while executives found counsel and delay.

Lin listened with their hands folded around the mug of pencil shavings. The room filled with tram noise, then the faint rattle of dishes from the apartment below. When Mira finished, Lin looked down at the apartment clippings instead of at her.

“And you think he’s right,” they said.

“I think he’s right about what happens when institutions get frightened in public.”

“That is not what I asked.”

Mira rubbed a thumb over the table’s peeling laminate. Someone had once tried to paint it white. The old wood bled through at the corners. “I think parts of it are right.”

Lin nodded once. “So the plan is fraud first.”

“The plan might be contracts, debt riders, donor theft, disposal records. Enough to force scrutiny without giving them an excuse to disappear everybody into a safety process.”

“Everybody meaning me.”

“Lin.”

“No, say it correctly.” Their voice stayed quiet, which made Mira listen harder. “Everybody meaning me, and anybody else like me, if there is anybody else like me.”

Mira looked at the rain shirt cuffs around their wrists, rolled so evenly they almost looked measured. Lin had started doing that with sleeves, towels, and any paper they touched because order gave them one way not to dissolve into borrowed fragments. On the fridge behind them the rent notice had slipped half out from under the magnet, Brine Court Housing in block red letters above the number they already knew by heart. Twenty-four hundred dollars sat on the page with all the weight of a threat. It was only paper, but nothing about it felt abstract.

“I’m trying to keep you alive,” Mira said.

Lin’s mouth twitched, not quite a smile. “You and Tavi keep saying that like it solves the other part.”

“The other part is easier to fight if you’re breathing.”

“Breathing where.”

Mira opened her mouth and shut it again.

Lin reached for one of the clippings and flattened it. “This one is for a room above a butcher on Pell Street. The window faces a wall. It says the toilet is in the hall and the hot water works most mornings. I read it three times because it sounds terrible and also because somebody is allowed to live there under their own name.” They looked up. “You want me to stay grateful for a better hiding place.”

“That’s not fair.”

“No.” Lin’s finger stayed on the clipping. “Warehouse Nine was not fair. Waking up on your bench with somebody else’s stairwell in my head was not fair. Being kind about it does not make it a different shape.”

The television in the other room changed from weather to an ad. A cheerful voice promised instant approval on consumer credit. Gabe cursed in his sleep and rolled again.

Mira kept her own voice low. “If we do this wrong, they can take you as evidence. As property. As contaminated stock. I’m not being dramatic. I’ve read the statutes.”

“I know.” Lin’s eyes flicked to the brace on the chair, then to the rent notice, then back to Mira. “You and Gabe and Tavi are all counting bodies against money. I understand that. I’m not stupid.”

“I know you’re not.”

“Then don’t talk around me like I’m a package you’re deciding how to ship.”

The words landed clean and deep because they were true. Mira had spent the whole evening laying other people’s strategies over Lin’s life like tracing paper: hide them, time them, use them later, protect them by deciding for them. She had called it caution because caution sounded less ugly than control.

Lin went on, still calm. “If you leak a warehouse and a set of contracts, CommonFrame says there were irregular practices in a value-tier disposal stream. They blame three managers. They improve the wash. They offer credits to renters who can prove distress. They keep owning whatever wakes up in the cheap line because nobody made them admit we exist.”

“That’s not all that would happen.”

“No.” Their voice roughened a little now, not much. “It is only the part that happens to me.”

Mira looked at the clipped apartments, at the impossible harbor-view stack Lin had built with embarrassing honesty, the one-bedrooms with tile floors and legal locks and monthly rents that might as well have been lunar distances. “Recognition doesn’t fix any of that overnight either.”

“I know.” Lin touched the side of their neck where the serial port sat hidden under hair that had grown just enough to soften it. “Recognition gets me on the form at all. It gets the next one on the form. It gets names where there are only numbers now.”

Mira thought of the warehouse tags, yellow bars on plastic heels. She thought of the sticky concrete and the way Lin had gone silent there, too silent even for fear. “Names are not enough if they still come for you.”

Lin’s laugh was brief and astonished. “Mira. They already come for me. The difference is that right now they can say they are collecting property.”

From the couch Gabe muttered, “Turn the damn boat around,” to whatever dream had him, then fell quiet again.

Lin lowered their voice further. “When I woke up, you asked me what I wanted to be called. You didn’t ask because it was sentimental. You asked because being called something mattered immediately. It still matters. Survival without being named is only a slower conveyor.”

There it was without rhetoric or tactical varnish: a life that could not stay borrowed forever without becoming another form of disposal.

Mira looked at the apartment clipping under Lin’s hand. The Pell Street room had a hall toilet, hot water most mornings, and a window that faced a wall. Under any normal measure it was miserable. Tonight it looked almost luxurious because somebody could rent it by saying I’m here and forcing the city, however reluctantly, to answer back.

“If the moment comes,” Lin said, “I’m not staying in the kitchen while you explain me in safer words.”

Mira heard Tavi in that too, not in tone but in certainty. Everybody had an endgame now. Everybody could make a case from pain that was real. She felt suddenly, absurdly, like the least prepared person in her own conspiracy.

“I don’t want safer words,” she said.

“Then don’t choose them.”

Lin gathered the clippings into one pile and squared the edges on the table. The motion was domestic, fussy, and devastating. Mira watched their long careful fingers align rooms they could not legally rent and understood that the argument had shifted underneath her while she was busy deciding what to reveal. It was no longer only about proof. It was about whose future counted as too dangerous to insist on.

The meeting request from Celia arrived at 9:12 the next morning with the subject line off-site reconciliation, which was the kind of language CommonFrame used when it wanted something both deniable and important. The message contained an address in South Basin and nothing else.

Mira stared at it in the break room while a vending machine clattered out stale peanuts for somebody in returns. Through the Annex glass she could see the canal traffic moving thick and gray below, tugboats and rental ferries and one barge carrying vat substrate under a tarp that would not fool anyone who had ever smelled it. She thought of deleting the message. Instead she memorized the address and went.

South Basin was three tram stops and a financial class away from Brine Court. The lobby smelled like lemon polish and sealed air, with no mildew and no fryer grease wandering in from the street. A desk clerk in a slate jacket nodded her toward the lift without asking for a name, which told Mira the instructions had been left in advance. Unit 4C opened on the first knock.

Celia Rusk had taken off her shoes and was standing in stocking feet on cream carpet that probably cost more than a month at Blue Hour. Her blouse was as immaculate as ever, pale gray silk, collar exact. The incongruity was in the socks: thick brown wool, the kind older women bought for pain and never meant anyone to see. An electric kettle clicked on the counter behind her.

“Come in,” Celia said. “You look cold.”

“It’s September.”

“In Cordera. That still counts.” Celia stepped back. “Close the door.”

Mira did, then hated how quietly it sealed. The apartment was not large, but every surface in it advertised money the way Brine Court advertised lack. It had dry walls, a couch without exposed springs, windows thick enough to keep the gulls out, and a galley kitchen with four matching plates and a bowl of clementines that had not been bought one at a time. A folded throw lay at the end of the sofa, and a bathroom visible down the hall held actual white towels. Mira’s whole body reacted before her principles could catch up. The heat settled on her skin. There was no mold smell anywhere. With a flash of shame so sharp it felt almost like desire, she realized Gabe could sleep here without the damp climbing into his back.

Celia watched her notice it all. “Tea?”

“No.”

“I’m making some anyway.” She moved to the kettle with her usual measured efficiency, but when she tore the sugar packet she had to use both hands and brace one thumb against the counter. It was arthritis, unmistakable now that Mira saw it outside the office polish, outside the expensive pens and the little flexes between meetings. The pain sat in the room unvarnished. “Sit, if you like.”

Mira stayed standing.

“All right.” Celia poured water over the tea bag and let it darken. “This unit belongs to CommonFrame. We keep a few for visiting auditors, board members, people too important to commute with everybody else. It could be reassigned very easily.”

Mira did not pretend not to understand. “To me.”

“To you.”

“Why.”

Celia lifted the mug carefully, both hands around the heat. “Because I’d prefer not to lose one of the only competent adjusters in the building.”

“That line might work on somebody else.”

“It might.” Celia met her eyes over the rim. “You’re not somebody else, which is why I asked you here and not into conference room B.”

She crossed to the dining counter and slid a slim folder toward Mira. Inside was a one-page internal transfer memo already half-completed, blank only where Mira’s signature would go. It named her senior anomaly review specialist, effective immediately, with a salary adjustment to fourteen hundred and fifty per week before deductions, a temporary housing allotment for six months, family occupancy permitted for one additional resident, and an administrative hold on the current disciplinary referral pending internal reassignment. The whole thing was written in the clipped merciful language institutions used when they wanted obedience to feel like rescue.

Mira kept reading because stopping would have meant admitting how hard the numbers were hitting. Fourteen fifty a week before deductions, family occupancy, and six months of dry walls shoved Brine Court’s red notice back into her head alongside Gabe’s brace and the landlord’s wet cough on the phone every first of the month. She could feel the apartment around her like a hand at the base of her neck.

“There’s also room,” Celia said, “for a retention bonus if the transition is smooth.”

Mira looked up. “How generous.”

“How practical.”

“In exchange for what.”

“For staying employable and letting some matters remain inside a system capable of correcting them.”

Mira laughed once. “Correcting.”

“Yes.” Celia did not flinch from it. “Not perfectly or cleanly, but more usefully than a public detonation that hands Tissue Safety a pretext to seize half the city.”

It was Tavi’s logic wearing silk and corporate housing, and Mira hated how immediately she recognized the overlap.

“You know, then,” she said.

Celia set the tea down. “I know enough.”

“That’s a management phrase.”

“Fine.” A flicker of impatience cracked the polish. “The aggregate line was never a wash failure. It was a margin decision. The donor language in the debt riders should’ve been stripped years ago. Disposal variance at Warehouse Nine was supposed to stay a bookkeeping problem, not become visible to anyone who could read a body. Is that plain enough for you.”

Mira went very still, not from shock but because movement would have spilled something. Celia knew, not by rumor or suspicion or anomaly rates and bad instincts, but well enough to name the lie correctly.

“How long,” Mira said.

“Long enough that the question no longer interests me.”

“It interests me.”

“Of course it does. You’re still young enough to think chronology is the same thing as remedy.”

Mira shut the folder. “You knew and you left it running.”

“I knew pieces. I learned more. I stayed because staying let me keep some of the ugliest proposals off the table.” Celia’s mouth tightened. “Don’t make the mistake of believing refusal is purity. Refusal mostly changes who gets eaten first.”

The line was ugly enough to feel true in her mouth. That was what made Celia dangerous. She could say the rotten thing plainly and make it sound like adulthood.

Mira gestured at the apartment. “So this is what staying gets me.”

“This is what it can get you: warm walls, a salary you can actually plan against, distance from the Bureau, and a chance to move into internal review where you can do some good instead of some theater.” Celia’s voice softened, not sweeter but more direct, worker to worker under the executive skin. “I know what Brine Court costs when it stops pretending to be affordable. I know what two missed pay cycles do to a family. I know exactly how righteous people sound when they haven’t had to choose between telling the truth and keeping the bathroom mold out of their brother’s lungs.”

That landed because it was not abstract. Mira saw, with furious clarity, the shape of the temptation Celia had built: not status but dry towels, rent paid on time, Gabe able to walk from bed to kitchen without damp biting his spine, and Mira herself not flinching every time the tram card balance dipped below ten dollars. An apartment door that shut was enough to make the humiliation of wanting it immediate and total.

She said, “And in return I help you call people product more elegantly.”

“In return you keep your access and use it.” Celia folded and unfolded her aching fingers around the mug. “You think public revelation will produce recognition because you’re still thinking like an honest employee in a dishonest company. It will produce containment. Hear me carefully. If you force this into daylight before anyone with power has prepared the ground, the first official category attached to your hidden people will be hazardous material.”

“My hidden people.”

“You know what I mean.”

“I know exactly what you mean.”

Celia looked away toward the sealed window. From four floors up the canal was a gray ribbon between new glass and old brick, all of Cordera pretending money had ever cleaned anything instead of moving it out of sight. “I’m not offering absolution,” she said. “I’m offering survivability.”

The word sat between them. It was almost kind. It was also a bid.

Mira put her hand on the transfer memo again. The paper was heavy, expensive company stock, enough that even silence had nicer texture on it. For one obscene second she imagined taking it home and setting it on the table in front of Gabe, which would mean no more notices and no more bargaining with laundromat cash and late fees and whether they could stretch her salary through another month. She imagined telling Tavi they would do it slower, smarter, from inside. She imagined telling Lin to wait in a room with better heat.

The future Celia was selling did not feel impossible. That was the most dangerous part. It felt humiliatingly, seductively usable.

Mira slid the memo back across the counter. “You didn’t bring me here because I’m competent.”

“No?”

“You brought me here because you think I’ve seen enough to become expensive.”

Celia’s mouth curved, almost approving. “That too.”

“And because whatever you know, you know it’s real.”

This time Celia did not answer at all. Her silence was more precise than agreement.

Mira looked at the folder, the clementines, the wool socks on cream carpet, the whole upholstered case for surrender. “If I sign this, my silence becomes part of payroll.”

“Most useful silences do.”

“Maybe.” Mira stepped back from the counter. “Mine doesn’t.”

Celia exhaled through her nose, not dramatic, just disappointed the way a manager might be disappointed by a smart employee who had chosen sentiment over process. “You are making an error.”

“Probably.”

“Inspector Sanz is less patient than I’ve been.”

“I know.”

“There are consequences to preferring spectacle over leverage.”

Mira thought of Tavi at the bench, arguing for a scalpel. She thought of Lin sorting impossible apartments into neat piles and insisting on a name that could stand on a form. Spectacle was not the choice in front of her. The choice was which damage she was willing to schedule and in whose body.

“This isn’t leverage either,” she said. “It’s a buyout.”

For the first time that morning Celia’s face lost all managerial softness. What remained underneath was older, harsher, and far more working class than the silk blouse. “Everything is a buyout when you have less money than the thing across from you.”

Mira believed that too. She believed it enough that refusing hurt.

She opened the apartment door. Cooler hallway air slid in around her ankles. “Then this one’s still no.”

Celia did not rise to follow her. “If you change your mind, do it before the month turns. South Basin units are not held forever.”

Mira almost laughed at that, the pure corporate pettiness of the line. Instead she stepped into the hall and pulled the door shut on the warm dry room.

The elevator mirrors showed her a woman who looked more temptable than she wanted to admit. On the street the city hit her all at once again: salt, tram brake sparks, fryer oil from a corner cart, the canal’s chemical breath. She stood there a second with her hands in her coat pockets until feeling came back into them.

She had refused the apartment, the salary, and the soft administrative version of disappearing. None of it put anything back together. Tavi still wanted a leak thin enough to slide past the law before it noticed. Lin wanted names, daylight, and a future that could survive paperwork. Celia wanted her bought and useful. Mira started uphill toward Brine Court knowing only that she had turned down one endgame and lost the fiction that the others could still be made into the same one. Her CommonFrame badge tapped once against her keys inside her pocket as she climbed, an ugly little reminder that whatever came next would still have to move through official rooms.
