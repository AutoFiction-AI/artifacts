# Chapter 13

The front glass sweated under Jo's forearm as she changed the hours with a red laundry marker fat as a thumb so nobody could pretend not to read it. It was the next morning, still before dawn.

PUBLIC WASH 6-10  
DROP-OFF ONLY 10-4  
PICKUP 4-8

The blank middle of the day was not blank at all. It was the clinic, hidden in plain sight behind steam and the rattle of old dryers, and now that Mira had no salary to leave for and no legal cover left to lose, it might as well have been her real address.

The marker had bled in the condensation, giving every letter a small wound. Jo stood on the milk crate by the door with the cap in her teeth and looked down at the sidewalk traffic with the satisfaction of someone who had just changed a border.

"If anybody asks," she said around the cap, "dryer three is eating belts, dryer six is eating quarters, and my staff is tragically incompetent."

Gabe, hunched on the folding stool by the counter with his brace under a thermal shirt and a clipboard on his knee, lifted two fingers. "I object to one-third of that."

"Object seated," Jo said. "You're on the stool because your back still thinks it owns the schedule."

Mira locked the front till and slipped the key into the pocket that already held the fine notice from the Bureau. It was five thousand dollars on heavy municipal paper. She had folded it twice, then once more, until the number lived in a white seam against her thigh. The paper had the same dead, confident feel as the hearing room. It still surprised her that a city could throw a price onto your life with one clean stamp and expect you to walk home carrying it.

The first knock at the back came before Jo climbed off the crate.

By eight-thirty the laundromat smelled like hot detergent, fryer oil from the empanada cart outside, and the metallic sweetness of opened tissue sealant. Jo kept the front half noisy and respectable. She ran three empty dryers with damp towels inside so the back-room wetbench could hum and click under cover. She took real laundry from two old men who liked her enough not to notice anything and not enough to ask. Lin worked the aisle between washers in a brown delivery cap pulled low and a cardigan that hid the serial port at the nape. Anybody glancing in from the street would have seen a shy helper sorting tagged shirts. Anybody who stopped longer would have seen the line of people pretending to wait for wash.

Mira had spent years training her body to move fastest under fluorescent contempt. The hearing had not taken that out of her. It had only changed who was standing on the other side of the table.

The woman on the repair chair introduced herself as Leonie Pardo in a voice that made the name sound borrowed. She was maybe twenty-eight, with lemon bleach dry on the cuffs of her janitor pants and a plastic strawberry barrette clipped into hair so short it had nothing to hold. She worked one cough drop from cheek to cheek, training her mouth into a new habit.

"It happens when the spin kicks up," Leonie said. She kept looking toward the dryers even though the door to the back room was shut. "The sound gets in my elbows. Then my hands start this thing."

She lifted them. They pinched and released in the air, thumb to forefinger, thumb to forefinger, in neat little bites. It was not random twitching or a seizure. It was a practiced motion.

"You sew?" Mira asked.

"No. I tear things, mostly." Leonie gave a quick embarrassed smile. "Office trash, bad mop heads, my own pockets. I don't sew."

"How long?"

"Three days. Since Saturday night." Leonie swallowed the cough drop, winced, then went on. "And I keep hearing somebody say blue line first. Not in my head like a thought. More like my mouth already knows it and I'm late catching up."

Mira wrote it on the paper intake slip. `blue line first`. She wrote `pinch motion bilateral, triggered by spin cycle`. She wrote `client denies sewing history`. Her handwriting had gone hard and narrow since the hearing, the letters packed shoulder to shoulder, every inch of paper feeling bought with blood.

Tavi leaned over from the wetbench with a probe light clipped to his ear. He had already changed into the apron he used for sealant work, the one with old burns silvered into the chest. "Let me see the left forearm."

Leonie rolled the sleeve. The bargain frame underneath was hotel-clean on the surface and failing in all the places money hid. The seam at the inner wrist had gone pink and damp. Cheap skin, overused flexion, too many nights scrubbing somebody else's sink with leverage the model had not been printed to repeat this often. There was also a faint line of puckered scar tissue over the radial tendon that did not belong on any factory-fresh wrist.

"That scar yours?" Mira asked.

Leonie frowned at it, affronted by the idea that her own arm could surprise her. "No."

"You ever rent for the docks? Freight? Rigging?"

"I clean legal offices in Civic West." She glanced toward the front room. "You need me to say yes to something? Because I don't."

"No." Mira touched the scar with a swab and Leonie's fingers immediately made the pinching motion again, faster now, stitching invisible cloth in the steam. "I need you to keep saying exactly what happened."

From the front, Jo called, "Gabe, if that man with the laundry bag asks whether I can rush his sheets, tell him yes if he has cash and no if he has a poem."

"What if he has both?"

"Then charge him twice."

The room laughed, including Leonie, and Mira felt the laugh move through the wall like borrowed weather. That was how Blue Hour stayed open now. It was not secrecy or even caution. It stayed open because Jo had turned panic into shift management and because the neighborhood had started protecting the desk with the automatic ferocity people gave a working boiler in winter.

Mira cleaned the wrist, checked the seam depth, then moved to Leonie's neck to test response. The skin there went colder under her glove. It was a residual surge. The dryers thudded into a faster cycle and Leonie's whole body tensed, not with fear but with a readiness that had not begun in her.

"Blue line first," Leonie said softly. "Orange page is a trap."

Tavi's head came up.

Mira wrote that down too.

"You heard that exact phrase before?" he asked.

Leonie blinked. "I heard it just now."

"No," Tavi said. "I mean in the carryover. Has it repeated?"

"When I was cleaning the arbitrators' bathrooms yesterday. A faucet rattled and there it was again. I nearly stitched the hem of a judge's robe to the sink towel." She laughed once, then put a hand over her mouth, embarrassed by the sound, the reflex of somebody who had spent too long in offices where even laughter felt billable. "Sorry."

"Don't be sorry," Mira said. "Just stay still."

She slid the scanner over the wrist and watched the cheap display sketch stress brightening under the skin. She hated relying on the scanner now. It logged more than it needed to, and every device in the room had started to look like something that could testify for the wrong side if seized. Tavi still needed it to work fast. They all knew that. They also all knew he was going to burn half his system one day before he let the Bureau take it whole. The thought came to Mira as a bodily certainty, not a prediction, and she pushed it away.

"Twenty for the seam and the wash," she said. "If we have to do a memory flush past local residue, that's extra. Cash only."

Leonie looked at the floor. "I've got thirteen, a transit card with maybe two rides, and meal vouchers from the court building because they keep ordering too much lunch and pretending it's dignity."

Mira felt the fine notice against her leg again. It was thirteen dollars. She could calculate what that meant faster than mercy. It meant two bags of broth if Gabe's pain spiked and he couldn't cook. It meant part of the light bill Jo pretended not to mention. It meant one quarter of the painkillers Tavi bought in singles because buying a bottle drew attention. It also meant Leonie had already come here after deciding against some other necessity.

"Ten and the vouchers," Mira said.

Leonie exhaled so hard the cough drop smell came back out, medicinal cherry over bleach. "I can do that."

"I know you can."

Tavi gloved up and took the arm with the reverent impatience he got around damaged tissue. "You're going to feel pressure, then warmth, then me swearing because these bargain cuffs were printed by sadists with a quarterly bonus."

"That part I believe," Leonie said.

He flashed her a grin and bent over the seam. Public Tavi sounded loose enough to slide out of any room. His hands never lied. He stabilized the wrist, opened the inflamed seam with a microblade, and exposed the inner lattice in a fan of wet pearl. The old scar sat threaded through it like a private signature. Somebody else's healing laid into factory stock. Mira watched Leonie's face as Tavi irrigated. There was always the body in front of her and the absent body pressing through it. Blue Hour had stopped being strange when it got too common to afford wonder.

Lin came in with a stack of paper slips and a pencil sharpened down to insult. "Three more waiting. One can stand. One says he can't hear brass instruments without vomiting. One is only here because his girlfriend thinks the hand isn't his."

"The hand isn't his?" Tavi asked.

Lin held out the slip. "He says it signs his ex-wife's name whenever he gets drunk."

"He waits," Mira said.

Lin nodded. Their face stayed careful in public, but in the soft wet heat of the back room a small, mean delight crossed it. "I told him drinking first and then visiting us was disrespectful to the science."

Tavi barked a laugh. "See? Natural clinician."

Lin's mouth twitched at the praise, then settled. Under pressure they sometimes went very still, like stillness was a rail they could grab with both hands. Mira had learned to watch for that. Stillness meant the fragments were crowded.

"What else?" she asked.

Lin checked the slips. "A grandmother rental from Market South. Teenager hand reflexes, mostly percussion. She brought fritters in a paper bag because she thinks that changes the price."

"It changes Jo's mood," Mira said.

"Nothing changes Jo's mood," Gabe called from the counter. "It rotates on fixed machinery."

Jo opened the back door with her hip, set down a mug of coffee by Mira's elbow, and took Leonie's vouchers without counting them. "Front room says two people saw you in the Hall yesterday," she told Mira. "One thinks you're brave, one thinks you're stupid, and both need help with their borrowed tendons. Congratulations on your new celebrity."

Mira kept her eyes on Leonie's arm. "Did either one bring money?"

"One brought cash. The other brought canned peaches and a prayer card."

"Cash first."

"See?" Jo said to nobody and everybody. "Institutional training. They could not beat it out of her with a whole hearing."

The words landed softer than the joke asked. Mira knew Jo meant to keep the room moving, but the hearing still sat on her skin like chemical residue. She had testified. They had fired her before the microphones cooled. She had walked out of Arbitration Hall with a Bureau inspector serving her the fine on the steps while the poor renter she had saved cried from relief and shame at the same time. There was no applause and no clean turn of history. There was just the city using paper to remind her what counted as legal speech.

Leonie watched her with the quick side-eye of people who had spent too much of life in offices. "You really said it there?"

"Enough of it."

"And now you're here."

"Now I'm here."

Leonie considered that, then nodded, filing Mira's answer where she'd file a correct intake response.

By noon the back room had filled to its workable limit. Steam hung in the fabric stacked along the walls. Tavi was tying off Leonie's stitch line when Lin brought in the man with the faithless hand and the girlfriend who looked angrier than he did.

The man smelled of anise liquor, fryer grease, and fresh blood. A bar napkin had been wrapped around his right hand so long it had pasted itself to the split knuckle underneath. His name, once Mira got it out of him, was Omar Becerra. The girlfriend planted herself by the wetbench and said hers was Yasmin Soto and she was only here because his hand had embarrassed them both.

"It keeps signing some woman's name," Yasmin said. "Not mine. Not his. He was closing tabs at the bar and wrote it on three receipts like he meant to marry the paper."

Omar glared at the floor. "It's my ex-wife's name."

"Then why'd you punch the dryer?" Tavi asked.

"Because the dryer laughed at me."

Tavi looked at Mira. "He's drunk."

"Obviously," Yasmin said. "He's also bleeding on Jo's towels."

From the front room, Jo called, "If he bleeds on my towels, he's paying linen rates."

Omar made the mistake of trying to flex the hand. The napkin darkened. Mira caught his wrist before he could hide it and peeled the paper free with saline. The split sat across the second knuckle, shallow but ugly, skin curled back like wet label stock. His fingers twitched under her hold, not pain alone. A writing cramp. The tendons at the base of the thumb kept loading and releasing around an absent pen.

"How much have you had?" she asked.

"Three shots and one argument."

"No memory flush," Tavi said at once. "Not with ethanol onboard. I'll clean the knuckle, maybe tape it. That's it."

Yasmin threw both hands up. "He came because you fix overlap."

"We do," Mira said. "We don't do guesswork with a soaked brain and call it medicine."

Omar muttered, "Told you."

Lin had already found a pencil. They set a detergent invoice on the stool beside him. "Write your name anyway."

"Why?"

"Because if it's nonsense, nonsense will keep the same shape."

That made Omar look at them for the first time. He sat because pain sat him. Mira steadied the invoice against his knee. He took the pencil in his right hand and printed `O`. Then `m`. Then the fingers changed their mind. The wrist softened. The thumb slid higher on the shaft. A looping capital `E` flowed onto the paper in a hand too graceful to belong to the rest of him, followed by three quick letters and the beginning of a surname before Omar swore and flung the pencil.

Yasmin did not say I told you. She did not need to. The look on her face had done that four times already somewhere else.

Tavi picked the pencil off the floor. "That could still be disinhibition. Booze turns grooves loud. Doesn't mean donor trace yet."

"It means something," Yasmin said.

"It means his hand remembers a script he doesn't," Lin said. "That isn't nothing."

Mira folded the invoice over the half-written name before Omar could stare himself sick at it. "Have you ever rented service frames? Banquet, hostess, anything that lives with a pen in the fingers?"

"Barback. Wedding cleanup. One holiday season in a hotel shell." He swallowed. "I print. Always have. My ex used to laugh because my block letters looked like a ransom note."

That mattered. It wasn't proof. It was a route.

"Here's the triage," Mira said. "Tavi closes the knuckle and splints the hand. I log the script event and the trigger. You come back sober tomorrow or the day after and do the test again. If it repeats clean, it graduates from drunk trick to evidence."

Yasmin looked like she wanted faster justice than the room could sell. Omar looked relieved and ashamed in equal measure.

"And if I don't come back?" he asked.

"Then the hand keeps writing without you," Mira said. "Your choice."

He let out one hard breath through his nose. "Tape it."

Tavi irrigated the split knuckle, swore when Omar jerked, and wrapped two fingers together with white splint tape. Mira wrote on the intake slip while the hand still trembled on Omar's thigh:

`OMAR BECERRA`

`right-hand motor script episode under ethanol; partial unfamiliar cursive signature`

`partner witness: repeats during drinking / no deep wash performed`

`return sober for replication before donor trace assigned`

She underlined the last line once. That was the care outcome. It wasn't a cure or certainty. It was a boundary that might keep the evidence from turning to sludge.

After Yasmin paid in six damp bills and a roll of quarters, the queue kept moving: the freight temp whose knees locked at gull cries, the busboy whose bargain jaw bit down on somebody else's wedding vows, the grandmother rental from Market South whose fingers drummed teenage rhythms on tabletops when she got bored. Lin kept the queue from curdling. Gabe handled payment, gossip, and the back buzzer, flattering people just enough that they forgot he was also watching for inspectors. Jo ran the laundromat like a border crossing.

Work replaced clock time. Mira trusted work more.

When she finally straightened, her lower back a strip of hot ache and her gloves dusted with dried sealant, Leonie was shrugging into her coat. The pinching motion in her hands had gone.

"If it comes back?" Mira asked.

"I write it down." Leonie touched the folded intake slip Mira had given her. "On paper."

"Good."

Leonie hesitated. "That thing about the orange page. You want me to remember more of it?"

"If it comes, yes."

"Feels ugly, remembering somebody else's trap."

Mira could still feel the raised seam of the scar through the swab in her glove. "It probably was somebody else's trap. That's why I want it."

Leonie nodded once and went out through the detergent aisle, shoulders set. Lin was already at the back door before she reached it, holding the latch with the practiced invisibility of a helper. At the front glass, Jo flipped the sign to CLOSED for one hour though voices still pressed from the waiting line and laundry kept spinning. The hours had been rearranged and the faces had shifted. It was the same work with less cover and more witnesses. Blue Hour looked less like a side job every day and more like a place the neighborhood would notice if it disappeared.

Mira pulled Leonie's paper slip back from the completed stack and copied two phrases onto a cleaner page.

`blue line first`

`orange page is a trap`

The letters looked less like notes than the beginning of something that would have to survive them.

* * *

Rain came in sideways that night and freckled the front window with canal grit. Jo sent the last real laundry out with a free scolding and locked the door. Gabe dragged the folding table closer to the back-room light, his left foot catching twice on the mat when he got tired enough to stop hiding the drag. Lin brought over the day's intake slips in clipped stacks. Tavi stripped his gloves and flexed his long fingers one by one, trying to unknot the hours out of them.

The room had the wrung-out intimacy of after-service kitchens. Damp heat clung to everything, the coffee had gone bitter on the burner, and everybody was past hungry and not yet stopped.

Mira spread the slips across the table. Leonie's phrases sat beside three other fragments from the past ten days, all written by different hands because Blue Hour had always jotted what mattered wherever paper existed: a receipt roll, the back of a detergent invoice, Jo's account notebook, even a food carton lid once, until the grease made the ink surrender.

`blue line first`

`don't sign the orange page`

`Stitch can fix a sling with no light`

`parking lot saints stealing fries`

Lin touched the last one with a fingernail. "That one is mine." They said it without claiming ownership exactly. More like they were pointing to a pressure bruise. "Or part of mine."

Mira lifted her head from the slips. Lin had taken off the delivery cap. Their hair at the temples was damp and flattened, and the notch in the left ear showed white under the light. "When did you get it?"

"This afternoon when the dryers changed cycle." Lin closed their eyes for a second, not dramatically, just to hold the thread. "I could smell old fryer grease and wet rope. Somebody was laughing with a mouth full of fries. Not rich-people weekend laughing. Tired laughing. The kind that spends itself on the way out."

Gabe leaned over the table. "Say the line again."

"Parking lot saints stealing fries."

"No, the other one."

Lin frowned in concentration. "Stitch can fix a sling with no light."

Gabe opened his mouth like he had a joke coming, then caught on something harder. He hooked two fingers under the edge of his brace and hauled himself a little straighter with a hiss.

"Jesus," he said. "No. I know that. Stitch wasn't a phrase. Stitch was a person."

Tavi turned from the sink, where he had been rinsing residue traps. "Dockside?"

"North Quay." Gabe tapped the `orange page` note. "Pool Six, I think. Recovery debt pool. After a bad injury they move you into one of those restructure programs where they front you therapy in exchange for owning the next ten years of your body in installments." He gave Mira a crooked look. "You know. Civic compassion."

Mira knew. She knew enough to hate the neatness of it.

"Who was Stitch?" she asked.

"Woman named Marisol Velez. Or maybe Velas. Everybody just called her Stitch because she'd sit on a pallet with a sail needle and repair strap tears better than company stock. Little stuff, side money, favors. She'd sew pockets shut too if she thought they hung sloppy." Gabe's face shifted as he pulled the memory closer. "Pool Six was all dock rehab contracts for a while. Crushed hands, busted backs, people who still needed to work and couldn't afford to heal like civilians."

Tavi came over, drying his fingers on his apron. "North Quay Recovery used orange addendum sheets for scan retention. Or they did when I was at CommonFrame. They called it gait library and motor behavior modeling if they were being polite."

"Polite to who?" Jo asked.

"The investor class."

Mira looked from the notes to Gabe. The North Quay letterhead had already started its old work on her body, the same sour little brace behind the ribs that every official page seemed able to trigger now. "How do you know it was Pool Six and not one woman saying one thing once?"

He reached into his jacket pocket and took out a folded, softened paper that had lived too long against his body. "Because they tried to slide me the same shit after my back went. Different pool, same color. I kept it because I wanted something to burn on purpose later."

He unfolded the page carefully. The sheet carried North Quay Recovery letterhead and offer language, with debt restructure options presented like mercy and organized like a trap. At the bottom, in a block of smaller print, Mira saw it: `supplemental kinesthetic archive authorization`. Scan retention was buried inside rehabilitation terms. It was a body turned into collateral through font size. The fine notice in her pocket seemed to answer it, one ugly paper recognizing another.

Tavi whistled without humor. "There it is."

"That's your letter," Mira said.

"Yeah. And I said no because I was sober enough that week to read the whole thing and mean about paper as a hobby. Pool Six went around before me. Dock cases. They were desperate." Gabe tapped `Stitch`. "If fragments from Lin and from today's clients are pulling her up, then they reused that archive hard."

Lin kept their eyes on the table. "How many bodies can one person end up in?"

Tavi answered because he had the technical ugliness in him. "Depends how rich the residue was, how much they cut it, what line they were seeding. More than one. Maybe a lot more than one."

Mira's grip tightened on the pen. She had spent the hearing thinking in single bodies because one body was hard enough to make the city look at. A lot more than one turned the whole thing industrial again.

Jo sucked her teeth. "That answer can go straight to hell."

Mira made a new sheet and wrote in block print this time.

`MARISOL VELEZ ?`

`dock nickname: Stitch`

`North Quay Recovery / Pool Six`

`phrases: blue line first / orange page trap / parking lot saints`

`motor carryover: pinch-stitch motion`

The list steadied her. It did not comfort her. It steadied her. Ink, names, route, witness. Things that could survive a roomful of liars if she kept them exact.

"How do we find her without going through anything digital?" Lin asked.

"We don't touch formal databases," Mira said automatically.

"No shit," Gabe muttered. "Sorry. I mean, obviously."

Jo was already thinking ahead. That was one of her oldest talents, to move from horror to logistics without pretending the horror had improved. "Velez, maybe Velas. Marisol. Dock rehab. I know a Nena Velez from pickup laundry. Or Vela. Something with an e and a grudge. Short woman, mean eyeliner, always returns my clothespins in clumps like she's paying ransom. North Quay side, Pelican Courts maybe. Pelican Arms if I'm wrong."

"Courts," Gabe said. "Arms is retirees, card sharks, and one ferret man. Dock spillover lands in Courts."

"Beautiful," Jo said. "Between the two of you we've narrowed it down to one damp bird."

Mira wrote `Nena ? / Pelican Courts ? / Pelican Arms ?`. "Tomorrow."

Tavi put a finger on the orange page. "If she's alive, don't go in saying scan reuse. That sounds like a scam because that's exactly what it'll sound like. Go in saying repair desk, memory carryover, neighborhood referral."

"And if she's dead?" Lin asked.

The room took that in without answering right away.

Mira heard the rain ping against the dryer vent. She heard Gabe's brace settle when he shifted. She heard Lin breathe once through their nose, deliberate and shallow, anchoring.

"Then we write down what the family says and we keep looking," she said.

Lin's eyes went back to the notes. "If she's alive," they said, quieter now, "does she know we're carrying pieces of her around?"

No one answered. Gabe kept worrying a thumb under the edge of his brace. Tavi stared at the orange-page note, jaw hard, wanting to scrape the ink off with a blade. Jo's jaw set hard enough to move the muscle in her cheek.

Jo pushed away from the table. "You can be devastated after you eat something. I've got arroz in the back and it's not getting younger."

Gabe snorted. "Food by threat. That's care in this building."

"You're welcome."

While Jo banged pans in the utility sink kitchenette and Tavi hunted clean spoons that did not exist, Mira kept staring at the paper. Gabe's old letter lay under Leonie's intake slip, her municipal fine notice under that, all three corners curling in the steam. `supplemental kinesthetic archive authorization` sat beside `orange page is a trap`, the clean clause and the warning it had dragged out of somebody's body years later.

When Jo set a chipped bowl by her elbow, Mira did not thank her. Jo would have hated that. She just took the spoon and kept reading Gabe's letter until the orange page print blurred.

* * *

Pelican Courts sat three blocks inland from North Quay where the good air gave up. The building had once been painted sea green and now wore the color in little stubborn islands around the windows. A freight drone shell had been bolted over the entrance to make a rain awning. The lobby smelled like boiled cabbage, bleach, and the medicinal rubber of cheap braces drying on radiators. The bleach hit Mira with the same false-clean sting as Arbitration Hall. Her mouth went metallic before she even saw the stairs.

The elevator door had been chained shut with two `OUT OF ORDER` notices gone gray at the tape edges. The mailboxes beside it had been relabeled so many times the plastic windows looked cataracted. No clean `Velez`. One slot said `N. Velas` in faded marker. Another had only `3F` and the torn belly of an old grocery sticker where the name should have been. Jo's memory had given them a building and a fight about bleach, not a door.

Gabe came because it was dock business and because Mira had not asked him not to. He walked with the careful economy of somebody bargaining with his own spine each step. By the first landing the brace had started talking under his shirt in tiny plastic clicks, and guilt kept time with it in the back of her teeth. He squinted at the mailboxes. "Excellent. Mystery stairs. My back has been begging for enrichment."

The second-floor `Velas` turned out to be a man in a mesh undershirt and ice tape from ankle to calf who opened the door three inches, listened to the name Marisol, and told them his sister had moved out last winter and if they were creditors they could drown scenic. Across the hall, a woman with a door chain and a cigarette tucked behind one ear looked them over like she was pricing scrap.

"If you mean Nena with the eyeliner, she's on three now," the woman said. "End unit. And if you're selling Jesus or payment plans, practice somewhere else."

By the time they reached the third floor Gabe's mouth had gone thin, but he only said, "If this staircase grows another flight, I'm suing God personally."

Mira matched her pace to his without comment. He would've hated pity and hated being outrun more.

"Get in line," Mira said.

He grinned without showing teeth. "There she is."

Nena Velez opened the door with one hand still in a yellow cleaning glove and a toothbrush silvered with wig dye in the other. She was shorter than Jo had suggested, compact and braced against the world at all points, in an inside-out supermarket apron over leggings printed with tiny moons. One eyelid carried fresh black liner. The other was bare. She looked from Gabe to Mira to the hallway behind them, plainly expecting a camera crew to step out of the stairwell.

"If this is collections, you can die tired," she said.

"It's not collections," Mira said.

"Then it's church, press, or legal aid and I'm booked solid on all three."

She started to shut the door. Gabe put his palm up, not pushing, just occupying the air.

"Pool Six," he said. "North Quay. We're asking about Marisol. People called her Stitch."

"A lot of bastards know dock words," Nena said.

Mira pulled the notebook from her coat pocket and stalled for half a breath. The cover had gone soft from coat heat. After the hearing, showing handwritten proof to a stranger felt too much like offering up one more part of herself for public handling. Then she opened it anyway. "Then don't take ours on faith. These have come out of other people's bodies." She kept her finger on the lines as she read. "`Stitch can fix a sling with no light.` `Parking lot saints stealing fries.` `Blue line first.`"

From deeper in the apartment a chair leg scraped hard across the floor.

"Who said blue line?" a woman called.

The toothbrush stopped in Nena's hand.

Grief changed her face without softening it. Mira had seen that before in waiting rooms and hearing rooms and hallways outside burn units. It was never just sadness. It was exhaustion with a knife hidden inside.

"Who are you?" Nena asked.

"Gabe Alves. I worked the docks before my back quit negotiating. This is Mira. We run repair out of Blue Hour."

"The laundry?"

"Yeah."

Nena lowered the toothbrush until dye flecked the sink. Recognition tightened one corner of her mouth and stayed there. "You were in the Hall yesterday."

Mira nodded once.

The words left the old hearing taste at the back of her throat: coffee burnt to tar, toner heat, the Bureau's clean paper sliding across the steps into her hand. The fine notice was back at Blue Hour, but her body still checked for it.

Nena looked disgusted on Mira's behalf and annoyed at herself for feeling it. "Fine. Come in before Mrs. Aparo across the way builds a theory."

The apartment was smaller than Brine Court and cleaner in the desperate way of places where order was a defense. Towels dried over chair backs. The couch had been covered with a flat sheet patched at the corners. On the windowsill, three cracked mugs held clipped herbs trying their best in weak light. Every horizontal surface carried evidence of work: grocery circulars folded to coupons, a half-mended backpack, medication blister packs stacked in a bowl.

Marisol Velez sat at the kitchen table in a cardigan the color of old bruises, a steel walker parked beside her with a pink-thread patch sewn onto its hanging pouch. She was younger than Mira expected, not much older than Gabe, with dock-broad shoulders gone narrower from months or years of not using them the same way. Her hair had grown back after some old shave or surgery scar in an uneven black wave she had clipped on one side with a child's green barrette. A sewing kit lay open by her teacup. She was hand-stitching the pocket of a house dress shut with tiny furious movements.

Stitch, Mira thought, and felt the word lock in place.

Marisol lifted her chin, needle still between her fingers. "If they're selling funeral plans, I already survived too many things to make it worth it."

"They're not," Nena said. "Laundry people. Repair people."

"Those are different trades."

"Not in this city."

Gabe gave a small salute toward the sewing kit. "People used to call you Stitch at the quay."

Marisol's mouth bent. "People used to call me other things too. Most of them were true."

Nena hung the cleaning glove on the sink tap and leaned against the counter, not relaxing. "Say what you're here to say."

Marisol pointed at the notebook. "Say them again."

Mira did. This time she added the details she had not wanted to waste in a hallway. "One client pinched the air like she was hand-stitching canvas. Another had an old radial scar no factory wrist should've had."

Marisol's fingers answered before the rest of her body did, thumb to forefinger in quick neat bites. Then she looked down at her own hand, the betrayal plain on her face.

"Blue line first was sling work," she said. "If you started on orange, the edge curled and cut your thumb. Parking lot saints was Lot B after second shift. Pepe's truck, if he hadn't sold out. Stolen fries if he had." Her other hand went to the inside of her wrist. "The scar was a sail hook on a rain job. Port clinic gave me six stitches and an invoice."

Mira took out her notebook, then stopped with it closed on the table. "We run a black-market repair desk for bargain-body carryover. Memory fragments, motor habits, scars that don't match the renter. We've been tracing patterns by hand because anything digital can get seized. Your habits and phrases came up in fragments from more than one body."

Nena's face hardened instantly. "No."

"I'm not asking her to sign anything."

"That's what everybody says before the paper comes out."

Marisol lifted a hand without looking at her sister. "Nen."

Nena pressed her lips together but did not apologize.

Mira kept her own voice flat and useful, customer-facing and processable. "I'm going to say something ugly and I want to say it fast so you can throw us out if you need to. We think CommonFrame reused a stored motor and memory archive from your rehab contract in multiple bargain-line bodies. We think some of what we've seen in clients and in one hidden overlap person traces back to you."

Silence moved across the apartment like a spill.

From the hall came a child running, then being called back in somebody else's language. A tram rattled somewhere downhill.

Nena let out one small incredulous laugh. "That is a new scam, I'll give them that."

"It's not a scam," Gabe said. "North Quay orange addendum. Scan retention buried in therapy terms. They offered me one after my injury."

Marisol set the needle down very carefully. "Orange page."

Mira opened the notebook then, not to show it yet, just to be honest about her hands. "You remember it?"

Marisol's eyes stayed on the tabletop. "They told me it was for gait mirroring. They said if I let them keep the archive, they could tune the rehab frame faster and keep my therapy slots from getting cut. This was after the sling snap. After they told me I could either learn to walk different or learn to stay home." She touched the base of her spine with two knuckles like she was checking a weather injury. "I thought it meant for me. My body. My retraining. Not this." She gestured at the room, at the city outside it, at whatever number of strangers had been moving with her hands in them.

"Not a product line," Gabe said.

"Exactly."

Nena was staring now, not at Mira but at Marisol, as some older dread finally found a face she couldn't wave off. "They kept asking for updates after your therapy ended," she said slowly. "Those check-in mails. I thought it was billing."

"Some of it was," Marisol said. "Everything is billing."

Mira took out the page Gabe had shown them last night and slid it across the table. "Is this the same form family?"

Marisol barely glanced at the page before her jaw clenched and she nodded so sharply it was almost a flinch. "That color. That language. 'Supplemental kinesthetic archive authorization.' They love words that sound like they're helping themselves sound innocent."

Nena swore and pushed off the counter hard enough to rattle the spoon jar. "I kept her paperwork. Most of it."

She crossed to the stove, crouched, and tugged open the oven drawer. Instead of pans it held files, utility bills, and rubber-banded packets wrapped in grocery bags against damp. She came up with a stack of contracts fat from being saved too long and dropped them on the table with the fury of someone who knew paperwork weighed more than metal in the right hands.

"Roof leaks," she said when she caught Mira looking. "The oven is the driest thing we own."

The top packet turned out to be debt notices. The next was an appeal for extra therapy sessions, then three months of payment plans, then a pharmacy printout with mold stippling the edges. Marisol kept sorting, exact even with the slowness. Gabe separated two pages that had glued themselves together at a corner. Mira lined up dates. Only then did the rehab contract emerge from the middle of the stack, the orange addendum water-warped and half-stuck to a notice about missed installments.

Mira leaned in. The page laid out North Quay Recovery therapy continuation terms and scan retention, with third-party modeling rights nested inside an innocuous paragraph about motion-training efficiencies and derivative therapeutic applications. A cross-reference code in tiny print kicked the ugliest part to another clause three pages back. Nothing in the language said `bargain bodies`. Nothing in it said `your laugh might end up in a stranger who never met you`. Nothing in it had the courage to describe what it was buying.

"Can I copy this?" Mira asked.

Nena folded her arms. "No pictures."

"No pictures," Mira agreed immediately. "By hand."

That seemed to matter. Maybe it mattered because cameras stole cleanly and handwriting admitted a witness.

Marisol pushed the page toward her. "Copy the whole clause. Not just the pretty infection on top."

Mira uncapped her pen. The kitchen light buzzed while rain tapped at the rusted sill. She copied every line, then the cross-reference code, then the date, then the contract number, then the names of the two North Quay representatives at the bottom. Her wrist cramped halfway through and she kept going. This was the opposite of testimony in the Hall. There was no microphone and no oath except the one implied by getting it exactly right.

When she finished, the copied clause and Gabe's page sat side by side like family in bad blood. They shared enough poisoned language to belong to each other, but the bridge from `archive authorization` to `your hands in strangers` still sat under legal fog.

"This proves they kept something," Nena said. "It doesn't prove where it went."

"Not by itself," Mira said. She thought of Leonie's intake slip in her pocket, of Lin's fragment notebook on the Blue Hour table, of Tavi's habit of translating corporate filth into working language. "By itself it's fog. With the fragments and Gabe's paper, it's a route."

"Then don't write her down as certainty yet," Nena said.

"I won't," Mira said.

She capped the pen and lifted her head. "Have you seen carryover yourself? Not just billing contact."

Marisol thought. "Once on the tram, a girl in a freight rental kept patting her own pocket closed. Over and over. Same way I used to do before shifts because I was always losing the locker key. I nearly said something. Then I thought, no, that's what tired looks like on everybody." Her laugh was dry and embarrassed and mean. "Guess I was still giving companies the benefit of the doubt. Stupid habit."

"That's not stupidity," Mira said.

"It gets stupid when you survive this long."

Nena sat down finally, all the force gone out of it at once. Without the posture she looked younger and much more tired. "Is this because of the hearing?" she asked Mira. "People have been talking."

"The hearing got me fired. This is because the fragments keep repeating."

"Fragments," Nena said, tasting the word like bad medicine.

Lin's question from the night before came back to Mira: does she know we're carrying pieces of her around? There was no version of the answer that did not sound like theft because it was theft.

"There are people," Mira said carefully, "who have habits from you. Phrases. Maybe a gull joke. One person with a scar pattern near the wrist that matches old repair you carried. We think CommonFrame seeded multiple bargain bodies from archived donors whose contracts hid what the archive would really be used for."

Marisol rubbed a thumb over the pink-thread patch on her walker's pouch, feeling the tiny neat cross-stitches. "How many people?"

"We don't know yet."

"Then you came before you knew."

"Yes."

Marisol nodded once. Maybe it was approval, or only recognition that this, at least, was honest. "Good. If you'd waited for certainty you'd have come when the lawyers were ready instead of when the truth was."

Nena rubbed her eyes with thumb and forefinger, careful of the one painted lid. "What do you need from us?"

"Permission to write you down," Mira said. "Real name. Nickname. Contract origin. Anything you'd want matched if we see more."

"And what do we get?"

The question was not hostile. It was an adult question.

Mira answered it the only way she could. "Not justice fast enough. Not money, probably. A record that won't belong to them. A chance, later, to prove this didn't happen by accident."

Gabe added, "And if we find more, you find out before the city does."

Nena waited on Marisol's face.

Marisol picked up the needle again, considered the thread, then put it back down without using it. "Write it right," she said. "Use Velez with a z. And put Pool Six next to it. I don't want those bastards cleaning up the route they used to get to me."

Mira wrote:

`MARISOL VELEZ ("STITCH")`

`North Quay Recovery / Pool Six`

`orange addendum signed under therapy continuation`

`probable living donor trace; clause copied for cross-check`

She did not label Marisol a victim. The word was too clean and too finished. Marisol sat three feet from her, alive, pissed off, mending a pocket shut because she hated how fabric betrayed people.

"One more thing," Marisol said.

"Okay."

"If some hidden person has my hands in them, don't come back here asking whether I want them returned. That's not how anything works." She stopped, annoyed at the need to find language. "Tell them I know what it feels like to get used like a spare part. That's all. They'll know the rest."

Mira wrote that down too, though not as a quote. She wrote `message: donor recognizes exploitation; no return logic`.

On the walk back, Gabe was quiet until they hit the canal wind. Then he said, "North Quay still owns chunks of me on paper."

Mira caught the hitch in his next step and turned toward him.

He kept his eyes on the sidewalk. "I knew that already. It just feels different watching the clause hatch."

She did not offer comfort because he hated it when it came wrapped. She said, "We'll put your letter in the record."

That got a breath of a laugh out of him. "See? Romance."

"Shut up and keep moving."

But when he stumbled on the curb she took his elbow before he had to ask, and he let her.

* * *

They started the registry after midnight because there was no cleaner hour available.

Jo came out of the front office with three composition books, her old laundry accounts ledger, and a roll of clear packing tape. "If this turns into your usual pile of damp scraps, I will haunt every one of you while alive," she said.

The ledger was bigger than Mira expected, clothbound and swollen at the spine from years of detergent humidity. Jo had used it for pickup balances once. Half the pages were blank. On the filled pages old names and shirt counts marched in pencil columns from a time when laundry had been the whole business and not the camouflage for an illegal repair desk trying to document stolen selves.

Tavi gave it one glance and made a face. "You know I can build you an offline index, right? Local only, no net, mirrored on two drives. Searchable. Cross-linked."

"And if the Bureau walks in?" Jo asked.

"Then I eat the drives."

"You don't have the jaw for that."

Gabe sank onto the stool and eased his back against the wall. "Paper has one advantage over Tav's heroics."

"Only one?" Tavi said.

"If they seize a drive, they say you altered it. If they seize a book full of six kinds of handwriting, sweat, grease, and Jo's old detergent math, they have to work harder."

Mira opened the ledger to the first blank spread. The paper was thick enough to resist cheap ink. It smelled faintly of starch and mildew and the old clean labor of people's clothes. She ran her palm over it once.

"Handwritten is slower," Lin said. They had changed into an oversized T-shirt and sat cross-legged on the folding chair, hair still damp from the sink. "But slower might help. If we write it, we have to decide what counts."

That was the real argument. Tavi's fingers stopped drumming the crate. Jo held the capped pen above the margin without writing. Gabe shifted on the stool and did not crack a joke.

Tavi pulled up a crate and straddled it backward, forearms folded on the chair back. "What counts is everything we can verify. Scar patterns, repeat phrases, contract origins, donor traces, repair dates, aliases. If this becomes sentiment, it dies in court."

"If it becomes pure evidence, it dies as us first," Lin said.

Their voice was not louder than his. It did not have to be. Under stress Lin often got cleaner, not rougher, and it made everyone else sound defensive by comparison.

Jo pointed at the page with a capped pen. "Both of you are right and that's disgusting, so let's organize."

Mira drew columns.

`overlap / reported name or alias`

`body details / scars / model notes`

`recurring fragments`

`probable donor trace`

`source of trace`

`witnesses`

`status`

With the columns in place, Leonie's phrase no longer floated loose from her wrist scar or Marisol's clause. Mira could move from body to fragment to witness without trusting steam-blurred memory to keep the route intact.

Jo assigned jobs with the efficiency of a field commander and a laundromat owner, which in Cordera were adjacent trades.

"Mira writes master entries because she's got the claims hand. Tavi copies any technical notes that need translating into normal language. Gabe does pool names, dock references, and who owes what to which vampires. Lin gets a separate notebook for fragments we can't place yet, because if I mix certainty and mystery on one page I'll start drinking before breakfast."

"What do you do?" Gabe asked.

"I keep you from becoming a cult."

No one argued.

They began with Marisol Velez because living names had weight and because Mira had promised Nena she would not turn one damp contract into certainty faster than the evidence deserved.

Mira copied the Pelican Courts notes onto a scratch page first and left the `status` line blank. Tavi spread Gabe's North Quay letter beside the clause Mira had copied by hand from Marisol's orange sheet. They were from different years and different debt cohorts, but the rot bloomed in the same places. `supplemental kinesthetic archive authorization` sat on both pages like a weed nobody had dared call by name.

Tavi tapped the cross-reference code Mira had copied from the addendum, then the matching one buried in Gabe's letter. "There. That's the handoff. North Quay keeps the archive under therapy language, then licenses modeled output downstream under derivative applications. They'd never write `bargain body` because that sounds like what it is."

Gabe leaned closer, reading slower than pain let him. "Pool Six got continuity-of-care bait. Mine says wage recovery incentives. Same trap, different bait."

Mira laid Leonie's intake slip beside both forms.

`blue line first`

`orange page is a trap`

Then she added Lin's note.

`parking lot saints stealing fries`

Three fragment lines. One stitch motion. One radial wrist scar. One living woman who had named all of it before they'd shown her the contract. Mira sat with the pages until the pattern stopped being hope and became method.

"That's enough," she said. "Not for court. For the book."

She wrote `living donor` in the status column. Under it she wrote `no meaningful consent for reuse`.

Tavi added a note beside `orange addendum`: `scan retention language buried under therapeutic modeling clause`. He crossed out `kinesthetic` once, annoyed at his own spelling, then rewrote it smaller. Gabe added `Pool Six / North Quay / dock rehab debt cohort`. Jo initialed the bottom because she had witnessed Nena's name from laundry records and because she believed signatures were a neighborhood technology long before the city claimed them.

Lin watched all of it, then said, "Put the pink-thread patch."

Mira's pen stalled over the witness column. "What?"

"On the walker pouch. It's hers. If one of us sees that kind of repair again, it matters."

Tavi started to object on evidentiary grounds, then stopped. "Actually, yeah. Distinguishing habits."

Mira added `personal marker: pink-thread patchwork / sews pockets shut`.

"Good," Lin said.

They took Omar Becerra next because his intake slip kept snagging on the difference between evidence and humiliation. Mira laid out the folded detergent invoice with the half-hidden cursive, Yasmin's witness note, and the treatment line Tavi had scrawled under the tape order. The room gathered around it more quietly than they had around Marisol's clause. This one was smaller, messier. Exactly the kind of thing bad systems liked to eat first.

Tavi tapped the folded edge. "Put the full name in. If the same signature turns up in another body six weeks from now, initials won't help."

Lin shook their head. "Not yet. That woman didn't come to us. Omar did."

"It's not about consent. It's about matching traces."

"It is about consent," Lin said. Their voice stayed calm, which made Tavi sound harsher than he meant to. "If we write a stranger's whole name into the master book because a drunk man's hand took a bad turn, we're doing a cleaner version of what they do."

Gabe rubbed his mouth. "They're both right, which is getting old."

Jo looked at Mira. "Make the ugly call, claims girl."

Mira read the slip again. Partial unfamiliar cursive signature. Partner witness. Wedding-hotel rental history. Return sober. The exact name sat under her palm like something live and unwanted. If it repeated in another body, it mattered. If it never appeared again, then all they had done was drag one uninvolved woman a little farther into the city's appetite.

"We record the event, not the woman," she said.

Tavi leaned back on the crate. "That's weaker."

"It's cleaner," Lin said.

"It's defensible," Mira said. "And if it repeats sober or in another client, we upgrade."

She turned to a fresh line and wrote:

`OMAR BECERRA`

`body note: right hand splinted after self-inflicted knuckle split`

`recurring fragment: reported former-spouse signature during intoxication; exact name withheld pending corroboration`

`source of trace: partner witness + partial cursive sample / prior wedding-hotel rental history`

`status: unresolved overlap signal / return sober for replication`

She sanded the wet ink with the side of her thumb and looked up. No one liked the compromise, which was how she knew it was probably the right one.

After Omar, they moved through Leonie Pardo, the freight temp with gull-locked knees, the busboy with the wedding jaw, and the grandmother rental from Market South whose fingers drummed teenage rhythms on tabletops when she got bored. Each entry forced a choice. Was this a fragment or a symptom? Did the scar belong to the renter or the donor or somebody between? Could a phrase count if it had only been heard once? The work was slower than repair and stranger. There was no cauterizing and no quick visible fix. There was only pattern and witness and the discipline not to pretend certainty where there was only recurrence.

At one point Tavi reached automatically for the terminal on the side shelf, then stopped with his hand in the air. Mira watched him look at the dark screen with the stunned hostility people saved for a dead relative in a borrowed suit.

"You should still back some of it up," he said, not looking away from it. "Not everything. Maybe cross-references only. If the book burns, we lose months."

Jo was sharpening pencils with a utility blade. "If the book burns, we lose months whether or not you hide codes in a machine. This way at least five people can memorize how it works."

Gabe tipped his head toward the ledger. "And this can travel."

That landed. Nobody filled the pause. Gabe's mouth flattened. Tavi's hand stayed on the dead terminal. Jo shaved another pencil curl with the utility blade, too carefully. Mira did not have to name the image to feel it arrive: doors kicked in, equipment seized, screens lifted by gloved hands that called themselves procedure.

Mira drew a second symbol at the bottom of each page, a box for duplication later. Not yet. Later, when they had enough names worth copying.

Lin had been writing in the fragment notebook in their careful public hand, but when Mira glanced over she saw the letters loosening page by page into something more natural, more lived in. Borrowed social phrasing giving way to choice. They had made a page for unplaced details:

`fried dough sugar on knuckles`

`church hymn / panic`

`green barrette`

`parking lot saints`

`left wrist old scar, radial`

The list gave Mira a physical chill that had nothing to do with the late-autumn damp. So many people reduced to transferable pieces because the city had built an economy that treated the poor as raw material twice, once alive and once archived.

"We need one for donors and one for overlaps," Lin said. "Separate but linked."

"Why?" Tavi asked.

"Because they're not the same wound."

He nodded immediately. "Yeah. You're right."

So Mira split the next section. `DONOR TRACE`. `OVERLAP PERSON / BODY`. She kept them on different pages with shared references, no return logic, and no property language. She could feel the old claims training in her hand trying to collapse everything into the most billable category. She fought it line by line.

Gabe passed over his North Quay letter for copying. "Put mine in the donor-risk section, not the confirmed section."

Mira let the pen hover over the blank line while Gabe held himself still against the wall.

"Do it," he said. "If I disappear into rehab debt next month, I want the book ahead of me."

She wrote:

`GABE ALVES`

`North Quay Recovery solicitation declined`

`not donor-confirmed`

`risk category: attempted archive capture after spinal injury`

His face tightened in an odd cramped way; relief had reached the room with humiliation on its heels.

"You all right?" Tavi asked.

"No," Gabe said. "Which is why this is excellent documentation."

Jo slid a bowl of salted peanuts toward him without comment. He took a handful and ate them like medicine.

The room kept going. Steam clicked in the pipes. Rain thinned and then returned. On the front glass, the red marker hours glowed backward from the streetlight outside, a schedule for one business and a code for another.

Near two in the morning Lin said, "I want my own page."

No one moved for a second.

Mira felt the request in her ribs. Lin had been the secret under everything, proof and person and liability and friend. She had spent half her own mind trying not to reduce them to the category that would keep them safest. Safety had never been clean.

"Okay," she said.

Lin came to the table and pushed the ledger gently until it sat square before them. "Not the serial first."

"What first?" Mira asked.

Lin thought about it. "The rib scar. The ear. The smell I remember before I remember a room. Fryer oil and detergent. Then the serial if you need it."

Mira wrote what Lin asked, in that order.

`LIN`

`visible markers: pale rib scar no file origin / left ear notch / serial port at nape`

`sensory anchor: fryer oil + detergent`

`status: living overlap person, hidden`

Tavi added a technical line about bargain aggregate origin, then crossed out two words that sounded too much like inventory. Jo wrote `protected by Blue Hour` in the witness column and underlined it once. Gabe, after a long moment, wrote `household knows` with a hand rougher than Mira had ever seen from him.

Lin held still over the page, wary, waiting to see whether the words would stay put. "It looks more real like that."

"It's more real like that," Mira said.

That was the turn. She felt it in her chest like a latch catching. Marisol's name sat on one page, Lin's on the next, Gabe's in the donor-risk section below Jo's old detergent columns. Pool Six led to Pelican Courts. The pink-thread patch linked a walker to a stranger's borrowed motion. A leak file could be disputed. This thing would have to be carried out, copied, and believed by hands that already knew what each line cost.

Jo closed the composition books and stacked them under the ledger. "Master book stays here tonight," she said. "Tomorrow we start duplicate notes in case the city gets energetic."

"I'll take first copy duty," Tavi said.

"You'll do it in longhand like God intended for criminals," Jo said.

"Cruel woman."

"Effective woman."

Mira shut the ledger and felt its weight settle into both palms. Its cloth cover was damp from room steam, its corners softened by old detergent years, and there was fresh ink inside, not yet dry in places. It was ugly, ordinary, and suddenly the most important thing in Blue Hour that was not a breathing person.

The back buzzer sounded.

All five of them turned toward the door, then toward the shelf where the scanner sat dark, then back to the ledger in Mira's hands.

When the buzzer sounded again, she set the ledger beside the wetbench where the monitor used to be, opened it to a fresh page, and reached for the pen before she reached for any tool.
