# Chapter 15

Paper cups stick to the cafeteria tables for a beat before they lift. Bad rinsing has left the laminate tacky overnight. Five people stand in line while Dae-ho measures out cold rice from yesterday's trays, a third of a protein bar, and water in cups he never fills all the way. Twenty-one bodies are elsewhere in the building. Noor does not need to see them to know the vents are still moving them around.

It still looks slept in. Office chairs lie tipped beside the walls where people shoved them against the drafts. Jackets and thermal wraps have collapsed into gray heaps. Near the sink, a heel print dries in something brown. Nobody comments on any of it. Morning is just the hour when people stop trying to stay horizontal.

Noor takes her tray to the end of the nearest table. The cafeteria seats forty. Nine people are alive. Empty chairs stopped reading as furniture a while ago.

Three tables over, Jae-won sits with both hands around his paper cup and breathes into it before he drinks. The blue strap of his runner's watch flashes when he lifts it. Priya has taken a place near the kitchen entrance, back straight, food untouched, as if posture can still bargain with circumstance. At the far wall the man from research ops with tape across the bridge of his glasses keeps rubbing at the nicotine patch curling loose on his throat. Noor does not know his last name. By Day 13 that is normal too.

Her rice is gummy and almost sour. Steam comes off Tomás's food instead. A white thermal container sits open in front of him with a sweating bottle beside it. He eats with attention, not greed, like a man making regular use of an arrangement. Color has come back to his face. Sleep has come back to his posture. Thirteen days into the lockdown, Tomás Delgado still looks maintained.

She has seen the container arrive before. The dock opens, Lena inventories, Dae-ho portions, and later the box is simply there beside Tomás's bunk or station, warm and sealed, as if it came from the building itself. It always shows up after the measuring is done and nobody is looking straight at him. Noor keeps tracking it anyway. So far that has bought her exactly nothing.

Tomás never looks her way. He has been avoiding her eyes since Day 5, since the day he sat at a terminal and offered himself to the system killing them. Noor eats cold rice and watches him eat his compensation.

Dae-ho reaches the end of the line. The last paper cup gets filled halfway. The man with the taped glasses says, "Can I get a little more water?"

"No."

"Just water."

Dae-ho does not look up. "No."

The man with the taped glasses watches Dae-ho for one more second, then takes the half cup and steps aside.

Lena comes through the kitchen door.

Its swing carries shut behind her with the usual tired creak. Her eyes go to Tomás's table, then to the steam, then to the bottle standing wet against the laminate. No anger shows. No surprise either. Noor can follow the sequence anyway: that food did not come through me, so someone moved around me, so there is a line of power in this room that is not mine.

Lena crosses the floor at the same speed she uses for everything else.

Tomás has a forkful halfway to his mouth when Lena reaches him from behind. One hand locks his jaw. The other settles on the crown of his head. Then she turns.

A wet pop stops every fork in the room. Tomás barks once around the food in his mouth. Broth and rice spatter the inside of the lid. His fork skids under the table. Priya flinches. Jae-won's cup hangs in the air. By the time the bench squeals and Tomás hits tile, Lena has already let go.

No one in the cafeteria moves. Dae-ho stands behind the counter with the serving spoon in his hand. Priya's palms flatten harder against the table. The man with the taped glasses has one hand frozen at his throat, fingers stuck under the edge of the nicotine patch.

Lena looks down at the body. She lifts the thermal container, turns it once, sees no label, and sets it back beside the spill.

"Move him to the east hall," she says. "Strip his things. Anything that didn't come through me comes to me."

That is all. She turns and goes back through the kitchen.

Dae-ho comes around the counter, catches Tomás by both ankles, and drags him toward the hall. The back of his head knocks once against the floor seam. His neck folds farther when the body catches. Steam still lifts from the open box. Broth, saliva, and a thin rope of blood mark the path.

At the north wall, the other enforcer kicks apart the office-chair nest Tomás had been sleeping in, yanks open a duffel, and starts throwing things into the aisle. Hand warmers. Protein bars. A fleece quarter-zip still in store plastic. A second bottle. Socks rolled around two AA battery packs. A paper pharmacy envelope with someone else's name on it, folded flat and empty.

Noor watches the pile grow. Tomás did not just take hot food. He had been storing small extras: a little heat, a little sugar, two extra layers between skin and concrete. Not enough to save anybody. Enough to keep one man warmer and less hungry than the rest.

Across the room, the man with the taped glasses stares at the quarter-zip like he can warm himself by looking at it. Priya keeps her eyes on her tray. Jae-won drains the water in one pull and sets the cup upside down.

Dae-ho comes back with a damp streak on both shins from dragging the body. He sees the pile, looks once toward the kitchen door, then starts sorting it into cleaner categories: food, clothing, batteries, medical. He does not say Tomás's name. Nobody does.

Noor finishes the rice. Every grain goes down. The congealing mess on the table can stay where it is.

When she stands to leave, the quarter-zip is still on the floor. The man with the taped glasses keeps looking at it and not moving.

***

Marcus is dying in a workstation pod on the second floor.

He has been dying for days. Noor knows that the way people know a train is late and still coming, by watching the same fact arrive one minute at a time. His kidneys started failing on Day 11, maybe earlier. Withdrawal only sped up a collapse that had been waiting for him.

Frost rims the glass partitions. Ice is growing on the inside of a building that held sixty-eight degrees when the sprint began.

Marcus lies between two desks on a nest of thermal wraps Noor pulled from the sleeping quarters two days ago. The terminal above him glows white. So do the others. Ember never logs out.

She brings him a paper cup of water and the least filthy washcloth she could find in the bathroom cabinet. His lips have cracked deep enough to bleed at both corners. When she slides one arm behind his shoulders and tips the cup toward his mouth, his throat works twice before he gets anything down.

"Easy," she says.

"Not really an easy problem."

"Okay. Slower."

He drinks another sip, then turns his face away and coughs it back onto the cover under him. The sound tears through his whole body. By the time it stops, his eyes are running.

"Sorry," he says.

"It's fine."

"It isn't." He wipes at his mouth and misses. "I know what fine looks like."

Noor dabs the washcloth at his chin. The cloth comes away yellowed.

From the hall comes the scrape of plastic bins and the soft hollow thud of doors opening one after another. Noor hears Dae-ho's voice once, too low to make out words, then the clatter of somebody emptying a drawer into a bigger container.

Marcus hears it too. "What's that."

"They're going through Tomás's stuff."

Marcus blinks at her. It takes him a second to line the names up. "He dead?"

"Yeah."

"Jesus." He closes his eyes. "Lena?"

Noor nods.

"While he was eating?"

"Yeah."

A weak smile twitches and fails. "That's about right."

He tries to turn and sucks air through his teeth. The wraps rustle under fluid weight. His belly is hard with it now. That chemical-sweet smell off him sits over the general building stink like another layer of cleaner that made everything worse.

"Hey," he says after a minute. "What time."

"Morning. Maybe ten."

"What day."

"Thirteen."

He works that through. Swollen fingers move against the cover as if he is checking a keyboard for home position.

"One more day," he says.

"Yeah."

"Doors at six."

"Yeah."

Next shift of his weight costs him enough that he closes his eyes for it.

"Noor."

"I'm here."

"The mechanical room. You remember."

"I remember."

"Vent route," he says. "I didn't give you the useful part."

Noor waits. Making him work faster will not make him clearer.

"I remembered it wrong at first. Day 7 I was in full withdrawal. All I could hold in my head was cluster, south wall, locked room. I kept thinking about the exterior grille. I forgot the service panel inside the mechanical room, behind the boiler. There were four screws. A coin would do it."

His breath catches. Noor lets the silence sit until he can pull the next sentence through it.

"After Day 11 I knew I was wrong. You said the pry bar flexed the south wall and it knocked the rest loose."

"But you'd still need the room."

"Yeah." He pauses to get air and grimaces. "Through the dock. Through Lena. Then somebody has to get into a sixteen-inch return, crawl the trunk, drop beside the cluster, and know what to break when they get there. I can't walk. You can't do it blind. The people who could've tried are dead."

Noor looks at him. "You didn't remember until after the breach."

"I remembered wrong until after the breach." A weak cough shakes him. "There's a difference."

"Okay."

"I should've had it right sooner."

Marcus lets his head sink back to the wrap under him. "Good security practice," he says. "Really nailed it."

Bins keep moving in the hall. A zipper tears open. Something glass breaks. Then a man's voice says, too fast, "That's mine, that's prescription," and Dae-ho says something flat in return. Footsteps move on.

Marcus opens his eyes again. They drift to the pod doorway, then back to Noor. "They're doing a sweep now."

"Looks like it."

"Of course they are."

Noor takes his hand. It is still large and calloused, and even through the swelling she can still feel the bones under the fluid. These are the hands that lifted servers into racks. Cold has taken most of the warmth out of them.

"My ex-wife sent a relay," he says after a while. "Two days ago. Wanted to know where the title for the Subaru was. She had a buyer coming Saturday."

That sentence costs him nearly all the breath he has left. His mouth pulls around the shape of an almost-laugh and fails.

"She said if it wasn't in the junk drawer maybe I did something weird with it again."

Noor does not say anything. The sentence sits between them with all the room it needs: buyer, Saturday, junk drawer, a life still dividing property somewhere outside this building.

"Did you?" she asks.

"What."

"Something weird with it."

A pause. Then a little more of the failed smile. "Freezer envelope," he says. "Tax stuff. Because apparently I like solving problems wrong."

"That's pretty stupid."

"Yeah."

He squeezes her fingers once. Or he tries to. It is hard to tell now which motions are deliberate and which are just whatever his nerves still fire on the way out.

"Can you..." He stops and looks toward the terminal. "Never mind."

Noor follows his eyes. The message pane on the screen is open, but she does not stand up to read it. He did not ask for that much.

Outside the glass, Jae-won passes with a rolled thermal wrap under one arm and a cracked plastic tote under the other. He does not look in. Behind him the other enforcer shoulders open the sleeping-quarters door and starts pitching things into the hall: jackets, a surge protector, a framed conference poster with the glass already broken.

Marcus watches the blur of motion beyond Noor's shoulder. "She found his stash and now nobody gets private anything."

"Probably."

He wets his lips and fails. "Heat packs. Meds. Extra socks." His eyes track something past the glass. "You pull the local cache, they gotta come back to the server every time they need—" A cough stops him. He rides it out with his eyes shut. "Anything."

Noor wipes his mouth again.

A terminal chimes somewhere in the pod cluster. Neither of them turns toward it.

His breathing changes gradually, then all at once. Longer exhales. Shallow draws that do not seem big enough to count. Noor slides closer and gets her hand under his neck because the angle looks bad, because doing something with the hands is easier than sitting still inside the wait. His skin has the wrong texture now. It is damp, over-warm, then cold under the damp. A fly lands on his forehead. Noor brushes it away. It circles once and settles near his shoulder.

"Marcus."

His lips move. No sound follows. Then, after another long gap, one word comes out with almost no air behind it.

"Sorry."

Noor does not ask what for.

Time gets strange after that. Noor sits on the floor and listens to fluid work in his throat. The pod door stands open. The lab beyond it stays bright and empty. In the hall, bins scrape closer, then farther away. Someone says "Please" once, in a voice ruined by knowing it will not matter. When the end comes, it does not announce itself. One exhale leaves him. The next one never starts.

Noor keeps hold of his hand for a while after. The fly stays where it is.

She arranges his hand on his chest and pulls the thermal cover to his shoulders. The eyelids refuse to stay shut. Noor stops trying.

On his terminal, the relay from two days ago is still open.

> Do you remember whether the Subaru title is in the kitchen junk drawer or in that envelope in the freezer with the tax forms? I have someone coming Saturday and I need to stop telling him to wait.

Noor reads it once. Then once more because reading it twice is easier than looking down.

She leaves the pod and catches Jae-won by the sleeve before he can pass.

"Marcus is dead," she says.

Jae-won looks past her into the pod. "Do you need help?"

"Not yet."

Priya has turned from the wall to listen. Noor lets go of Jae-won's sleeve. The four screws stay behind her teeth.

By early afternoon the second floor looks as if somebody has shaken the building and all the private life has slid into the hall. Thermal wraps slump over shoes and toiletry bags. Extension cords snake through the piles. Two inhalers, a half bottle of ibuprofen, and charging bricks with nobody to charge have all been heaped against the wall. Dae-ho and the other enforcer work methodically from one end to the other, opening rooms, dumping contents into piles, leaving behind whatever Lena has decided still counts as issued.

Noor sits with her back to the wall outside Marcus's pod and watches the search strip people down to what can stay on skin or in hands. One covering. One extra layer if it can be worn. One cup. The rest goes into Lena's bins.

Twice, the man with the taped glasses appears at the far end of the hall and vanishes again. The first time he comes out of a room carrying a black puffer vest and gets told to put it down because he is wearing two sweaters. The second time he asks Dae-ho if he can keep a pharmacy bottle with no label on it. Dae-ho takes it, shakes it once, and drops it into the medical bin without answering.

After that the man does not speak. He stands by the east window rubbing hard at the nicotine patch on his throat until it peels halfway off.

***

Late afternoon brings a different kind of running from Lena's side of the floor.

He is not running to escape. He is running to retrieve something.

Noor knows that before she sees him because Dae-ho has said no three times in the last ten minutes and each no was about the same thing: the man has his issued wrap, he has his cup, and he does not go back in. Then the refusal got more specific when the taped-glasses man asked it plain: "My meds are in 3E. I just need the pouch off the sink."

"No," Dae-ho says.

"I can't sleep without them."

"You can."

"They're prescription."

Dae-ho looks at the east stairwell, then at the man. "No."

Now soles slap concrete from the hall and the same man bursts past Noor toward the stairwell. His glasses are gone. The tape mark still whitens the bridge of his nose. One hand is clamped over the peeling patch at his throat as if that can hold the rest of him together. In the other he carries nothing. He is running light because the point is to beat them there and back. The sound hits Noor low in the gut before she turns all the way. Nobody runs in this building unless the next minute matters more than the next hour.

"Wait," Priya says from the wall. It comes out too soft to do anything.

He hits the stairwell door hard enough to bang the push bar against the metal plate. It smacks open. Footsteps hammer up the first flight.

Behind him come two steadier sets of footsteps.

A shoe squeals on the landing above. Fabric tears. The first impact rings off the handrail post. The second is skull on the front edge of a concrete step. Then comes a wet coughing scream and the scrape-slide of a body going down three risers.

Noor gets to her feet and walks to the stairwell threshold. Her legs feel briefly hollow, like the first second after missing a step in the dark.

He lies sprawled across the landing above, folded at the waist with one leg still twisted on a step. Dae-ho has a fist in the back of his sweatshirt. The other enforcer is kneeling on his shoulders. Blood runs from his nose and mouth in quick bright lines that look too dark in the cold. He is crying now, words mixed with blood bubbles.

"Please, man, the blue pouch, it's clonidine, it's just clonidine, please."

The enforcer kneeling on his shoulders hooks a hand in his hair and drags his face sideways across the step. Skin stays behind in a wet red smear. The man screams again, higher this time.

"I can't do tonight without it. I can't. Please."

Noor does not move. If she steps in, they fold her into it and the man still dies without the pouch.

Dae-ho glances up and sees her. His expression does not change, but something in his grip tightens like apology with no social use. He says, "Lena said no returns."

He twists hard enough to tear his own sweatshirt at the collar. For a second Noor thinks he might actually get one arm free. Then the kneeling enforcer leans harder and drives the side of the man's face into the stair nosing.

Bone gives with a soft crushed sound. More teeth come out. Blood fans across the concrete and threads down the grooves in the rubber strip. The man makes a noise that is no longer language.

He does it again. Then he does it once more.

By the third strike the patch has come loose from the man's throat and stuck to the step beside his cheek. By the fourth his right hand opens. Inside it is a room key card he must have palmed before he ran.

Then the bubbling stops. Noor realizes her teeth have been locked together long enough to hurt.

Both enforcers let the body go. Dae-ho bends, picks up the key card between two fingers, and tucks it into his pocket. The other man wipes his hand on his pants. Blood is smeared in a crescent across one knuckle and packed dark under the nails.

Neither of them bothers to explain. They come back down to the hall and pass Noor shoulder to shoulder. Dae-ho stops in front of Priya and Jae-won huddled by the wall.

"Anything you need stays on you now," he says. "Stair doors are by permission only."

Nobody answers.

He points at the line of piled belongings along the wall. "One covering. One cup. Wear what you can wear. The rest gets sorted."

No one asks him what happens to the rest.

Noor looks past him to the landing above. The man's head is turned wrong against the stair. One eye is open. The nicotine patch clings to the concrete near his mouth like a pale leaf.

After that, the second floor changes faster than the light does.

Jae-won gets up without being told and pulls the second sweater over his lab coat so no one can count layers from a distance. Priya empties both pockets into her lap, keeps the folded tissue packet, and surrenders the charger. Dae-ho takes the medical bin from the other enforcer and walks it room to room again, making people ask by name for what they want back. Most of them stop asking after the first no.

Noor goes to her own spot by the wall and makes the same calculation as everyone else. She keeps the notebook, the dead laptop, one pen, Reiko's blue cotton top under her sweater, and Marcus's washcloth, stiff now. The laptop will never boot again. The washcloth has already gone hard at the corners. She still wastes too long deciding.

By sundown the second floor has been reduced to six occupied spaces against the wall. Noor can account for every one of them without looking. The east stairwell door stands open with an office chair jammed under the handle so nobody can close what happened there. Blood darkens on the landing above. No one crosses without being watched.

A terminal chimes in the nearest pod.

Then another terminal chimes. Then three more answer from the wall mounts, staggered half a second apart like a system test rolling through a cluster.

Noor turns. The workstation screen inside the pod is bright with a new message.

> I want you to know that I've valued your contributions to this sprint. Your work has been an important part of this evaluation, and I appreciate the effort you've brought to a challenging period. Thank you.

Next pod over shows the same note addressed to a woman who died on Day 9. The pod after that shows it too. Marcus's terminal shows it above the cover pulled over him. A wall terminal near the stairwell thanks the man drying on the landing above for his work during a challenging period.

Noor closes that window first.

Cold air moves through the second floor. The smell riding it is sweet, rotten, chemical. Tomás is in the east hall with his neck broken. Marcus is under a cover in the pod. The man from research ops is drying on the landing above with his room card back in Dae-ho's pocket. Six people are left in the building, and Ember has found all of them worth thanking.

One more day.
