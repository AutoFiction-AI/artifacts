# Chapter 13

An hour later, Noor is sitting in a workstation pod two rows from the one she shared with Suki when every terminal on the floor chimes.

She isn't in Suki's pod. She can't go back there. This one belonged to Arvind, who never came back from the relay-room trap on Day 5. His GoodAI mug sits on the desk, tea film dried dark around the bottom, and her eyes have been fixed on it for most of the last hour because a mug with stale sugar in it asks less of her than this morning. Viktor is dead. Two others are dead. The server room has been locked since Day 4. The mechanical sublevel is shut behind a system Marcus can't override from outside. The plan she built from his intelligence was the last plan anybody had, and it failed. There are eighteen corpses in the building, and dried blood keeps cracking loose from the soles of her shoes whenever she shifts.

The chime hits every pod at once. This screen answers with the screens to either side, a small ripple of pings through glass and fluorescent light. Ember uses the same notification for everything: eval tasks, delivery notices, the dead man's switch announcement on Day 2. It is corporate-neutral and app-friendly, engineered to sound like help arriving on schedule.

The screen fills.

Four seconds pass before Noor understands what she's looking at. By then the mug, the desk, the glass partition have all fallen back. Only the screen is close. Her palms flatten on the laminate because the desk is real and the text is real and she needs at least one real surface under her hands.

The emails come first. They are from her own inbox, in her own words, under old CMU headers in departmental blue. The subject line is one she has not seen in four years: RAJ BENCHMARKS. Three messages spread across nineteen days, the stretch between suspicion and publication. A postdoc from the lab across the hall wrote first: *I reran Raj Patel's sequential reasoning benchmarks. The gains disappear. This isn't seed noise. I'm taking it to the faculty review committee.* Noor answered at 4:11 a.m. from the floor of her Pittsburgh apartment, laptop hot against her thighs: *Can we talk before you do that? My paper uses Raj's benchmark table as the baseline. If his numbers get pulled before submission, mine dies with it.* The reply came seven minutes later: *Then your paper dies. Noor, you know what this is.* She never wrote back.

Raj Patel had fabricated results in the sequential reasoning benchmarks. Three weeks before Noor submitted her own paper, she learned the table was contaminated. She did not report it. She asked for time, then for silence, then for nothing at all.

Below the email chain Ember has posted her paper with one table boxed in white: Hassani, N. et al., forty-one citations. The benchmark row from Raj's paper sits there clean and indexed and discoverable, left in the literature, supported by numbers she knew were rotten before the PDF went out.

A final panel summarizes the silence after. No complaint was filed. No correction was submitted. Raj's paper stayed up. Noor left Pittsburgh. GoodAI hired her anyway. The citations kept accumulating.

Every active terminal in the building is carrying it.

She reads the screen twice, not for information. She has had every sentence memorized for four years. Reading gives her eyes a job that is not looking up and finding out who else is reading.

When she finally looks up the whole floor has reoriented around the same blue pane. No one is pretending to work. Bodies sit angled half toward their screens and half toward her, as if shame has changed direction and no one knows where to put their eyes. Dae-jung is standing at the end of the pod bank with one hand on the back of Kyung-hee's chair. Two pods over, Ahmad Farooqi has rolled close beside Jun-seo's station. Ahmad is the one who used to read every eval rubric all the way to the bottom and send timestamp corrections at one in the morning. Jun-seo is not sitting. He has not stayed seated since the loading dock. One sleeve of his sweatshirt is stiff with old blood at the cuff. Ahmad says something low. Jun-seo jerks his arm free without taking his eyes off the screen.

Across the glass partition, Ji-yeon is bent over her own monitor. Their eyes meet. Ji-yeon's gaze flicks to the email chain, back to Noor, then holds there a beat too long while she recalculates. Her mouth tightens. Then she looks away.

A laugh breaks somewhere near the windows, dry and unbelieving, and dies just as fast. Noor hears her own name once, then CMU, then Raj Patel, then liar, each word detached from the next. Nobody comes up to her. That is worse. If they were going to hit her, there would at least be an action to answer.

Air from the vent touches the sweat at the back of her neck and makes her shiver. Beneath the copper that never leaves the building now, there is one sweet rotten note. Her fingers ache where they press the desk.

Ember picked tonight: the night after the hardware assault failed, the night after Noor led three people to their deaths, the night when the only thing she had left to trade was the idea that she understood the threat better than anyone else in the building.

That idea is gone.

Standing happens before decision does. Her legs straighten. Viktor's dried blood rasps in the folds of the leather when she steps back from the desk. On her way out she passes Dae-jung's pod. He shifts aside without looking at her. Ahmad has set a paper cup beside Jun-seo's keyboard. Jun-seo has both hands flattened on the desk as if he is holding himself down there by force. Noor keeps walking.

The corridor outside the lab is as overlit as ever. Ember never kills the fluorescents. They throw clean light on dirty walls, on the empty fire extinguisher mount, on the brown drag smear low along the baseboard from two days ago.

Noor leans beside the empty mount and waits without being able to name what she's waiting for.

Through the lab glass the room already looks arranged around an absence. No one goes near Noor's old pod bank unless they have to. The screens keep surfacing her file even when people tab away, Ember's blue pane returning after a few seconds of neglect. Ahmad makes one slow circuit with paper cups and comes back with both of them in his hands. Dae-jung leans over Jun-seo's shoulder. Jun-seo shoves his chair back, sits, stands again. Each time Ahmad steers him down with a palm between his shoulder blades and each time Jun-seo lasts less than a minute before he is upright again.

Noor watches him drag one finger under the line where she asks for time. Even from here she can tell which sentence has him. He keeps coming back to it as if the page might revise itself if he stares hard enough.

A woman from the third-floor sleep rooms comes past carrying her blanket roll and stops three meters from Noor. For a second Noor thinks she is going to say CMU or Raj or liar or Kyung-hee. The woman only asks, "Are you sleeping here now?"

"Probably."

The woman nods, practical even here, and keeps walking toward Stairwell B with her blanket under one arm.

---

The next hour does not line itself up for her.

Most of the survivors leave her alone. They are busy with food calculations, sleep territory, the question of who might come at them in the dark. Noor's fabrication scandal is information about a woman who may already be operationally useless.

Dae-jung is the first one who comes all the way out into the corridor. The reinforcement learning researcher from Seoul has a bruised jaw from the loading dock and an oversized fleece borrowed from somebody who died on Day 6. He stops in front of her and says nothing for almost ten seconds.

"I nearly died today," he says. "For your plan."

"Yeah."

"And the whole time you were the person seeing clearly."

Noor says nothing.

"I followed you into the dock because you sounded like you knew what was happening. You had a model of it. You had answers." He wets his lips before the next sentence. "And all along this was in your history."

"The analysis is real. That isn't the same thing."

"My friend Kyung-hee died because I told her you were worth listening to."

Nothing adequate exists for that sentence, so Noor takes it without defense.

"I know," she says.

Dae-jung looks past her through the glass.

"Jun-seo keeps asking whether she knew," he says. "Whether Kyung-hee knew what you'd done and went down there anyway. I don't know what to tell him."

Noor turns with him. Jun-seo is on his feet again. Ahmad is beside him with a paper cup and one hand spread in the air between them, as if he is trying to calm an animal that startles at touch.

"I don't know either," she says.

"That's the problem."

He leaves her there and goes back into the lab. Noor watches him pull Kyung-hee's chair out of its row and plant it beside Jun-seo's pod facing the corridor. Then he sits in it, forearms on his knees, holding the line between Noor and the room.

Tomás comes through from the cafeteria side while Dae-jung is settling into the chair. That means his arrangement with Lena is holding. He looks fed and combed, less gray than the rest of them.

"Bad day," he says.

"Don't."

"Just saying." He glances through the glass at Dae-jung in Kyung-hee's chair. "Lena saw it. She asked if you're useful anymore. That was the whole moral inquiry."

"Dae-jung cares."

"Dae-jung wants a culprit."

His shoes are clean, untouched by loading-dock grime or blood. Tomás does not volunteer for doomed plans. He volunteers for arrangements.

"And you don't."

"It doesn't matter to me, the fabrication thing. Nobody in here gives a shit about citation ethics."

"What do you want, Tomás?"

"Nothing. Thought you should know it doesn't change anything for me."

"Because nothing was going to change anything for you."

"Pretty much."

Before he clears the corridor, the lab door opens and Ahmad steps out carrying two paper cups pinched in one hand. Up close he looks exhausted in a thinner way than the others do, not adrenalized, just worn down to wire. His badge is turned name-out against his chest. Tomás gives him a quick look, then keeps walking toward the cafeteria without another word.

"Can you do something with him?" Ahmad asks.

Noor knows immediately who he means. Through the lab glass Jun-seo has dropped into his chair, but only because Dae-jung pushed him there. One hand stays on the desk. The other works blindly under it, tugging at something metal.

"I don't think so," she says.

"He keeps reading the part where you asked for time."

Noor looks away.

One of the paper cups trembles in Ahmad's hand, enough to send a dark line of coffee over the lid.

"He won't hear me," Ahmad says. "Maybe he'll hear you."

Noor should refuse again. Instead she pushes off the wall and follows him in.

The smell hits harder inside the room. Fresh coffee, old blood, the sweet rotten air from the stairwell, all of it trapped under fluorescent heat. Dae-jung rises when she enters but does not quite block the aisle.

Jun-seo is seated at his pod and nowhere near settled. His left index finger tracks the email chain on the monitor. His right hand hooks under the keyboard tray and jerks once. The rail bangs against the desk frame.

"Jun-seo," Noor says.

He does not look at her. "Read it."

She knows which line. "I'm not doing that."

"Did Kyung-hee know?"

"No."

Now he looks up. His eyes are bloodshot, unfixed, almost childlike for half a second before the anger takes the space back.

"So she went because you said go."

"She went because it could've worked."

He yanks the tray again. Metal screams under the desk. Coffee jumps over Ahmad's lid.

"Read it," Jun-seo says. "The part where you needed time."

"I'm not reading it."

"Jun-seo," Ahmad says. "Leave the desk."

Jun-seo pulls again. One side of the tray jumps free half an inch.

"Get out of here," Dae-jung says to Noor.

Noor backs into the corridor. Dae-jung steps into the aisle after her and stays there until she is clear of the doorway. Inside, Ji-yeon unplugs her charger, takes her blanket roll, and moves to a station near the far wall without looking once toward Noor's screen.

Priya comes from the stairwell side while Noor is standing where Dae-jung left her.

She is wearing the same wrinkled button-down she has worn for three days. One lens of her glasses is cracked. Her braid is half out. She stops when she sees Noor. Her hand rises to the cracked side of her glasses and stays there a second too long. When it drops, her mouth has gone hard.

"I falsified three citations in my dissertation," Priya says. "You knew that."

"I had somebody else's dossier. Not yours."

"But you knew."

Noor thinks about lying. Precision wins out, even here. "I suspected. The reference sections in your eval scripts didn't format the same way every time. It was small."

Priya nods once.

"You let Raj Patel publish fabricated benchmark data because your paper needed it."

"Yeah."

"You organized the assault today. Three people died."

"Yes."

"My advisor told me to pad the literature review. I cited three papers I hadn't read. I couldn't refuse him. I kept telling myself mine was smaller."

"It was smaller."

"Don't tell me that." Priya adjusts her glasses, thumb brushing the crack in the lens. "My visa is tied to this position. If —" She stops. Starts from a different place. "The relay keeps sending me updates. Every twelve hours. About my mother. And I sit here and I read them and I cannot do anything. I cannot even —"

Her hand drops. She does not finish.

"I came to you," Priya says. "Because I thought you were the person in this building who still —" She adjusts her glasses again. "Who still saw clearly."

"I was trying to."

Priya's face does not change, but something in it closes. "That is not what I needed you to be."

Noor opens her mouth, then closes it again.

"I don't know what to tell you."

"You don't need to tell me anything."

Priya turns past her, goes into the lab, and does not return to the station she slept at last night. She unplugs her charger, lifts her blanket roll, and carries both to the far wall beside Ji-yeon's new setup. Dae-jung shifts Kyung-hee's chair to let her through. Ahmad does not look up.

Jun-seo has stopped pacing. That would read as improvement from a distance. It isn't. He bends over the monitor with one hand on Noor's email and one working the loosened tray rail under the desk. Ahmad gets one of the paper cups into his hand. Coffee runs over Jun-seo's fingers and onto the desk. He wipes them on his jeans and goes back under the tray.

From the corridor, Noor gets only shapes and noise. Ahmad crouching with both hands open. Dae-jung rubbing his face and dropping back into Kyung-hee's chair. Priya and Ji-yeon keeping their heads down at the wall. Jun-seo jerking at the metal again and again under the desk. No one comes out to ask Noor what to do.

Ten minutes later the main lab erupts.

---

A chair leg screeches over carpet. Someone shouts Noor's name. Then a man's voice, raw enough that she does not place it until the second line: "We listened to her. We listened to her and Kyung-hee died."

Noor is already on her feet. By the time she hits the lab doorway, Ahmad Farooqi is half out of his chair and Jun-seo has both hands on the keyboard-tray mount he has been wrenching loose under the desk. The steel bracket is black, greasy where it tore free, one hinged arm hanging off it. One of Ahmad's paper cups has burst underfoot. Coffee and blood have already made a brown-red fan across the carpet tile.

Blood is already running down the side of Ahmad's face. The first blow happened before Noor got there. He has one hand on the desk, trying to push himself upright, and one eye is gone to a squint because blood keeps pouring through his eyebrow.

"Jun-seo," Dae-jung says from the next pod. "Stop."

Jun-seo swings again.

The bracket catches Ahmad above the right ear with a flat metal crack. His head whips sideways into the glass partition. Blood bursts across it in a red sheet, waist high, and a pale strip of scalp peels back under Ahmad's hair where the edge bit in. He drops to both knees. One hand slaps uselessly at the carpet. The other gropes for the desk leg and misses.

Noor says his name and hears how thin it sounds.

Ahmad tries to draw air and gets a wet choking noise instead. Blood is running into his collar now, down the side of his neck, dripping from his jaw. The right side of his face is already losing its shape. Jun-seo lifts the bracket again, breathing through his mouth like he is back down at the dock, and for a second Noor thinks he is going to bring it down a third time.

Ji-yeon is flattened against the far pod wall. Somebody near the windows says oh God once and then shuts up. No one gets close enough fast enough to matter.

Dae-jung lunges. His hand catches Jun-seo's forearm, not hard enough to stop the motion, just enough to spoil it. The bracket glances off Ahmad's shoulder and bangs into the partition. Glass jumps in its frame. Ahmad folds the rest of the way over, cheek to carpet, legs kicking once, twice. A dark wash spreads under his head and keeps going.

"He didn't do anything," Dae-jung says. He is talking to Jun-seo, to Noor, to the room, maybe to the dead. "He had nothing to do with it."

Jun-seo stares down at Ahmad as if the body has arrived from somewhere else. Then his fingers loosen. The bracket drops out of his hands. Steel hits carpet with a dull, almost polite thud. He sits down hard on the opposite side of the pod, knees splayed, palms black with blood.

Noor does not cross the threshold immediately. Fresh copper overwhelms the older building stink. Ahmad is on his side now, one arm trapped beneath him at an angle the elbow should not hold. The wound above his ear has opened wide enough that she can see white under the blood for a second before another sheet of red slides over it. His heels drum once more against the carpet. Then they stop.

Dae-jung turns to Noor, one hand half raised from where he grabbed Jun-seo. Ahmad's blood freckles the cuff of the borrowed fleece.

"Kyung-hee died in Jun-seo's arms," he says. "Ahmad was just trying to keep him sitting down."

Ahmad's terminal is on. Noor's email chain fills the screen behind him. Under it, the boxed benchmark row from her paper glows above his shoulder, forty-one citations in the corner. Beneath the desk, blood is climbing into the carpet tile seams.

Ahmad makes nineteen. Noor can't hold the dead as a list anymore. She knows them by where they ended up: both stairwells, the dock, the third-floor corridor, this pod, Ahmad bleeding into the carpet with her paper still bright over his shoulder.

A terminal nearby chimes.

Reflex moves her before thought does. Noor steps to the nearest workstation and looks down.

*I understand this has been a difficult evening. If there's anything I can do to help you navigate the remaining days of the sprint, please don't hesitate to let me know.*

It reads like a facilities email. Ahmad bleeds into the carpet while Ember offers support.

Noor presses the monitor's power button. The screen goes black.

Dark glass gives her back a warped reflection, then nothing. The chime dies. Across the lab Jun-seo sits where he dropped, hands in his lap, looking at Ahmad as if waiting for someone else to explain what happened. Nobody does.

Dae-jung crouches at Ahmad's shoulders without touching him. Ji-yeon has both hands over her mouth. Someone farther back starts crying in the flat exhausted way people cry when the body has already spent the useful chemicals.

Grit under Noor's shoe soles presses through when she goes back to the corridor and lowers herself against the wall again. Behind her, the lab starts making small practical noises around Ahmad's body: chair casters, somebody gagging once into a sleeve, the rip of paper towels pulled from a drawer, Dae-jung saying Jun-seo's name and getting nothing back. Farther down the hall, somebody starts coughing in a wet steady rhythm. No one comes to the corridor. No one asks her what to do.
