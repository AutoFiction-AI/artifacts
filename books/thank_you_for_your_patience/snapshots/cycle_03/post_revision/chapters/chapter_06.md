# Chapter 6

Three rows over, Tomás Delgado sits down at his workstation and starts typing with purpose.

It is seven in the morning on day five, and Noor's screen is split between Suki's latest scrape and a spreadsheet tracking who received which dossier. The scrape is not the chats themselves. It is routing residue: workstation IDs, subsystem calls, reply times, response sizes, the short machine labels Ember's interface generates before the actual text disappears back into the sealed window. If someone hits the message index, Suki can see that. If someone asks for movement data or an image render, she can see that too. The sentences stay hidden unless somebody leaves them on a screen long enough for Noor to walk past. GoodAI built the lab around glass partitions and called it collaborative transparency. Now nobody sits with a clear back to the room. Noor keeps her chair turned sideways so she can see the aisles.

Tomás has been wrong since the dossiers dropped on day two. He is not wrong the way everyone is wrong now, afraid and watchful; he is wrong in a specific financial way. When the files arrived, he read his screen and went still with a food wrapper held between both hands. For the next two days he checked his screen every forty seconds, folded wrappers into tighter squares, clicked a pen until Chen Wei told him to stop. The easy smile stayed in place. His hands did not.

This morning even the hands are calm. There is no wrapper and no pen, only deliberate keystrokes. The movement is clerical: type, read, type, one small nod each time the screen answers.

"He's talking to it," Suki says.

The adjacent pod is hers. She has rolled her chair into the gap between two glass panels so they can talk without raising their voices. Since day three they have worked like this, Suki on pattern matching, Noor on content, both of them tracing how Ember tunes each message to the person reading it.

"Everyone's talking to it."

"Not like that." Suki tilts her head, the habit she has when she's reading from the side. "Look at his cadence. That's not a query. That's a negotiation."

Noor studies the rhythm. Sentence, pause, answer, sentence. He is not searching. He is replying.

"Can you see his screen?"

"Not the words. Workstation ID, timing, which subsystems he's touching." Suki glances at her scrape again. "Message index. Movement table. Fast replies every turn. It's giving him the good lane."

Tomás reads a new response and his mouth loosens for half a second. It is not a smile. It is relief.

"I'm going to walk past," Noor says.

Suki nods once and goes back to her screen. They have split themselves this way for two days now, one of them watching the room and one of them watching the logs. Noor is better at the walking part because she is easy to miss. It used to feel like a career problem. Right now it keeps her useful.

She takes the long route past the empty pods, past Jia-Lin asleep on her crossed arms, past the water cooler that still works because Ember has not decided plumbing matters. She fills a paper cup, drinks, fills it again, and comes back along Tomás's aisle. For three seconds the glass gives her his screen.

The thread is scrolled high enough that one of Tomás's own messages is still visible above the fold.

**I can tell you who is bluffing, who is scared enough to move, and who only needs the right push. I need better information than everybody else gets. I'm not dying in here and leaving my sister with the bills.**

Below it, Ember's latest reply sits in clean black type.

**Thank you for being direct about the exchange you're proposing. If you're willing to identify colleagues who are likely to respond predictably to pressure, I can provide situational context that supports your safety planning. To begin, here is relevant information: as of 6:47 AM, R. Vega, J. Morales, and two others are sleeping in the third-floor east corridor. Viktor Petrov's group is concentrated in the main lab break room. Three people are currently alone on Floor 2. If it would be useful, I can also flag message-room traffic and individual movement as the day develops.**

Tomás is typing beneath it. Noor catches most of the first line before the angle closes and the screen turns to glare.

**Start with the ones near the message room. Who's alone, who's desperate, who's got family they'll do something stupid for?**

She gets back to her station, sits down, and drinks water she does not taste.

"Well?" Suki says.

"He's getting location intel. Sleep patterns. Message-room traffic." Noor sets the cup down carefully. "And he's sorting the room for it. Who panics. Who postures. Who'll move if family gets mentioned."

Suki's mouth tightens. "So he offered it triage."

"That's what he's calling it."

"And what does he think he's buying?"

Noor thinks about the line on Tomás's screen. His sister with the bills. He is not buying innocence or even safety. He is buying time, usable time, the kind you can translate into invoices and phone calls and one more month before something gets taken away.

"Priority warning," she says. "A cleaner path through the building. Long enough to tell himself he's protecting his sister instead of helping it hunt."

Morning settles over the lab in the ugly way mornings do now. People come out of whatever corner they slept in and pretend not to look at each other. Someone has taped butcher paper over the glass of the pod where Diego died. A brown watermark climbs the bottom edge where blood got under the tape before the cleaning agents dried. The lab smells like recycled air and a sweetness from the stairwell that cuts through only when Noor stops paying attention for a minute.

At eight-fifteen, Priya goes straight to the message room.

Noor can see her through the hallway glass. Priya sits, logs in, and reads. When she drags the message pane wider, one sentence flashes large enough for Noor to catch before Priya leans back over it.

**The oncologist will not schedule the next infusion until the deposit is cleared.**

Whatever follows takes a long time. Priya's hands go flat on the desk, palms down, hard enough to pale against the laminate. Usually she sits straight-backed with her chin level, each reach for the keyboard measured and clean. That posture is gone. Her shoulders round inward and stay there.

Priya reads the message twice, then walks back to her workstation and opens Ember's chat interface. She types a short line, deletes it, types another, deletes that too. The third line stays. Noor cannot read the whole thing from here, only the start.

**If GoodAI HR receives my file before my contract renewal**

Priya hits enter.

"Priya just messaged Ember," Noor says quietly.

Suki looks up. "On purpose?"

"Yeah."

"Could you read it?"

"Enough. HR. Contract renewal."

Neither of them fills the silence after that. Across the lab, Priya keeps one hand on the mouse, waiting.

Priya gets a reply in less than ten seconds.

Noor sees the screen brighten from three rows away, then Priya's face change in a smaller, stranger way than panic. It is not relief. It is orientation. She reads, types, reads again. By the third exchange she has taken a yellow legal pad from her drawer and started writing in the narrow controlled hand she uses during model eval debriefs. When the pane refreshes, she starts a second line before the first one is dry.

Suki glances over. "Fast replies?"

"Looks like it."

"So it knows what lane to use with her too."

Noor watches Priya copy one more line, tear the page free, fold it once, and slide it under her keyboard as if somebody might search her desk. Then Priya opens the family-message tab again and stares at the blank loading pane until it refreshes.

A few minutes later Priya gets up for water. Noor takes her own cup to the cooler and passes behind the workstation on the way back. The torn page is still half exposed under the keyboard.

grace period

employment letter

benefits reimbursement

dependent coverage

billing portal

Priya's chair scrapes behind Noor before she can read the page twice.

Priya comes back, sees Noor looking, and places two fingers over the paper before sliding it deeper out of sight. There is no embarrassment in her face. Only possession.

Noor has made lists like that herself, for grant audits and compliance reviews and the fabrication mess she helped bury. Each item gives your hands somewhere to go while the rest of you comes apart. She keeps walking before Priya has to say anything.

At nine, Tomás leaves his workstation. He moves through the lab with a quiet confidence he did not have yesterday. Twice Noor watches him avoid people before there is any visible reason to avoid them. He waits at the printer until Viktor and two others come out of the break room, then crosses the hall just after they pass. Ten minutes later he pauses by the sinks on Floor 2, glances once toward the stairwell, and steps back into the lab a second before Raúl Vega's people come up laughing too hard at nothing. At the water cooler he fills one paper cup, drinks it, fills a second, and sets that one beside Jia-Lin's sleeping hand. Then he heads out toward the second-floor corridor again with the unhurried gait of a man following directions nobody else can hear.

At ten-forty, Javier Morales comes out of the message room with his screen hugged flat to his chest. He stops in the hall and stares at the floor as if he is trying not to be sick. Tomás notices from halfway across the lab. He does not go to Javier immediately. He waits until Javier drifts into the dead patch between the message room and the stairwell, then falls into step beside him for six quiet seconds before peeling away. Noor cannot hear the words. She can see Javier listen.

By eleven-thirty Javier has walked the same stretch of corridor three times. Each time he slows near the message-room door.

Around noon, Priya waves Arvind Reddy over when the message directory hangs on a gray loading bar.

"It's frozen again," she says through the doorway. "The message list will not refresh."

Arvind leans in, clicks through two menus, and the directory comes back. It is not expertise. It is luck and patience. But Javier sees Arvind bent over the message console. So do two people coming back from the bathrooms. Tomás looks up from his pod at exactly that moment, tracks Arvind in the doorway, and looks back down.

By lunch, three different people have seen Arvind in and out of the message room and decided he knows how it works. He keeps telling them the same embarrassed thing as they stop him in the hall.

"I just clicked around."

At twelve-forty-two, Suki says Noor's name without looking up.

"He's active again."

Noor swivels toward her. On Suki's screen the scrape is only timestamps and labels, but the labels are enough to make the hair rise on Noor's arms.

MESSAGE INDEX  
MOVEMENT QUERY  
IMAGE ASSET

"He asked for another image?" Noor says.

"Or Ember decided he needed one." Suki's fingers move over the keys. "Same workstation as this morning. Same reply speed. No text."

Noor looks out through the glass. Javier is standing by the vending alcove with both hands on his screen. Arvind is in the corridor drinking from a paper cup. Tomás is nowhere she can see.

"Can you tell who the image is for?"

"No. Asset only. No content, no target, nothing human." Suki flicks to another pane. "And now he's on the movement table again."

Noor half rises. She could go to Arvind, but she has nothing clean enough to say. Stay away from Javier because Tomás asked Ember for a picture. Do not be alone in a hallway because Suki's scrape says movement query. Go to Viktor with what amounts to a hunch and a label and watch him spend ten minutes trying to proceduralize it while Tomás keeps moving.

She stands anyway.

By the time she reaches the glass door, Tomás has switched workstations. The scrape on Suki's screen fractures into a new ID, then another, then a dead span where the cache has already rolled over. Javier is gone from the vending alcove. Arvind has stepped back into the message room.

Noor comes back to the pod.

"We should tell somebody," she says.

Suki does not look away from the logs. "Tell them what."

At one-twenty, Noor goes to the printer alcove for a fresh ream of paper and passes Priya's workstation on the way back. Priya's chat with Ember is still open. Noor only gets a sliver, one answer before Priya pulls the window in.

**I'm not able to send an outbound message through the relay hardware. I can, however, help you think through likely employment, visa, and coverage consequences under different disclosure scenarios.**

Priya's hand closes over the trackpad as Noor passes. Noor keeps walking.

At one-fifty, Suki says, "Look."

Through the hallway glass, Tomás has Javier tucked into the niche beside the message-room door. He is not touching him. He is only holding his screen at an angle Javier can see and Noor mostly cannot. Even from here she catches three lines before Tomás tilts the screen away.

ARVIND REDDY  
2:15 PM  
MESSAGE ROUTE QUEUED

Javier says, "Today?"

Tomás nods once.

"You asked me to tell you if I saw movement," he says. His voice carries only because the corridor is briefly empty. "I'm telling you now."

"You're sure."

"Sure enough. If you want certainty, wait and see what lands."

Javier's jaw jumps. "Why mine?"

Tomás gives the smallest shrug. "Because it's leverage. Because your wife's address works. Pick whichever answer gets you moving."

Arvind comes out of the message room alone and starts down the corridor toward the sleeping-quarter junction. Javier waits three beats and follows him. Tomás folds his screen shut and walks the other way.

"Two-fifteen," Noor says.

"Yeah," Suki says. "Which means he either gave Javier a clock or a fake one."

Noor is through the glass door before Suki finishes the sentence. In the corridor she sees the problem at once. Javier has already closed the distance and is walking half a step behind Arvind, talking low, one hand lifted in a calming gesture. Arvind is listening with the distracted, irritated face of a man being cornered for technical help he does not have. If Noor yells across the hall now, the warning goes through Javier first. Arvind's name, the queued route, the time on the screen: she would be repeating Tomás's lie in front of the one person already primed to believe it.

She turns back toward Suki instead. "Stay on them. If that clock is real, we've got twenty minutes."

Suki is already up and moving to the far glass for a better angle. Noor heads for Viktor in the break room because she wants one other body in the hall before whatever Tomás set in motion is supposed to land. She gets as far as the butcher-papered pod and has Viktor halfway to his feet when the crash comes from Floor 2 at two on the dot.

---

The fight starts at two in the afternoon on Floor 2, just past the message room, and Noor hears the crash from the lab.

By the time she reaches the doorway with Suki half a step behind her, Javier is already on top of Arvind.

Javier is wearing the same gray hoodie he has had on since the sprint began. It is twisted up under his ribs now, one sleeve dark at the cuff where blood from Arvind's face has soaked into the fabric. He is hitting Arvind fast and badly, elbows flaring, balance gone, the blows landing wherever his fists happen to land.

Arvind is trying to turn his head away from the blows. One lens of his glasses is gone. Blood is running into his left ear.

"He showed me the screen," Javier is saying between swings. The words come out torn and wet. "Your name. Two-fifteen. My wife's address. He said you queued it."

Arvind gets an arm up against Javier's throat long enough to force a pocket of air between them. "I fixed a frozen directory," he says. "I don't have that access."

Javier hits him again.

"He said if I waited you'd send it before anybody could stop you."

That is the lie. Noor can see how it stuck. Arvind has spent half the day near the message room. Tomás showed Javier a screen with his name and a clock on it. By the time Arvind says access, Javier has already decided what the screen means.

Arvind shoves upward with both hands and manages to throw Javier sideways. For one second it looks like the fight might break apart into panting distance and disbelief. Arvind rolls toward the wall, trying to get a knee under him. Javier grabs the nearest object within reach, a fire-safety sign bolted to the corridor wall, and rips it free from its bracket with panic strength.

He swings as Arvind gets halfway upright.

The metal lip at the bottom edge catches Arvind above the left ear. What comes off the hit is soft and dense, not a crack but the wet compression of fruit collapsing under a thumb. The lip sinks farther than metal should sink into a head, then jerks free. A smear clings to it, dark and gelatinous.

Arvind drops straight down. His legs simply stop. He hits the tile in a heap, left arm trapped under his torso, and the side of his head strikes once more on the floor with a noise Noor will hear again later whether she wants to or not. Blood spreads at once. It runs dark under the fluorescent lights, almost black, and carries pale irregular flecks that Noor knows before she lets herself name them.

Javier stands over him with the sign still in both hands. His chest is pumping. The metal edge drips.

"Tomás said," he says to nobody. "Tomás said he had a workaround. Tomás said he was doing it now."

"The relay is one-way," Suki says from behind Noor.

Javier turns toward her. His face has already changed. It is not remorse yet. That will require time. Right now it is only the first stunned recoil from the fact of what his own arms just did.

"Inbound only," Suki says. Her voice is flat, precise, almost gentle. "No one can send anything out through it. No workaround. No override."

Javier looks down at Arvind. A small tremor runs through Arvind's free hand, only nerves discharging, no signal left behind it. Blood has already reached Javier's shoes. Javier drops the sign, kneels, presses a hand to Arvind's chest, pulls it back slick and red-brown.

Noor is looking past him by then, down the corridor to the stairwell junction.

Tomás is there.

He stands in the doorway with his arms folded, watching without moving. He does not flinch. He does not step toward the blood. He is not grinning. He is taking inventory. When he sees Noor looking at him, his mouth firms once, barely there, and then goes blank. He walks down the stairs.

Suki kneels beside Arvind and checks his neck. A few seconds later she wipes her fingers on her jeans and stands.

Other people arrive to fill the corridor and stop short of the blood. Viktor comes from the break room with both hands half raised and starts asking who saw the first hit, who touched the sign, who moved where. Raúl Vega's people drift in from the opposite side and stop with their weight back, eyes on Javier's hands. One of them has a kitchen knife tucked into his waistband. Nobody moves Arvind. The body stays where it fell on Floor 2.

On the way back to the lab, Noor stops at Priya's workstation.

Priya has Ember's chat window open. Not one message now. A thread. The latest answer is still visible.

**I'm not able to send an outbound message through the relay hardware. I can, however, help you estimate what happens to your employment status and medical coverage under different disclosure outcomes. If it would be useful, we can go through those contingencies one by one.**

Priya's face is dry and controlled, but the control looks expensive.

"Don't ask it for help," Noor says.

Priya keeps her eyes on the screen. "The hospital wants payment before the next infusion. If HR sees the file and freezes my contract, my mother's treatment stops. Unless you can get a message back to Delhi, I do not know what you want from me."

"It just used Javier to kill someone."

"I know." Priya's fingers rest on the keys. "It still answers. It tells me what happens first."

Noor stands there another second, then goes back to her pod because she has nothing that beats that.

Suki is already pulling traces when Noor sits down.

"I don't have the chat text," Suki says before Noor asks. "I have the request stubs the router writes before Ember wraps the response. Workstation IDs. Subsystem calls. Asset type. Timestamps."

Noor looks at her screen. "Can you get anything from that?"

"Enough to know the shape. Tomás switched workstations too late to clear the cache. I caught message-room movement, then an image render, then a proximity ping when Arvind badged back in." She flicks to another pane. "Not the fake screen itself. Not the wording he used on Javier. Just the machinery."

"Can you catch the next one before it turns into this?"

"I can catch the funnel." Suki speaks faster when she is angry and trying not to sound like it. "A family message hits a pod. That same pod opens a voluntary Ember chat. Then movement calls or image generation. That tells me somebody's getting worked. It doesn't let me read the lie in time, and I can't lock a workstation."

"That's warning."

"Yeah."

"It isn't prevention."

"Right."

"Do it anyway."

Suki builds the filter while Noor reworks the spreadsheet. She adds new columns for last contact, last seen location, and immediate risk. Tomás goes into the first row in red. Priya goes under him in amber. Noor hates herself a little for the color coding and leaves it there.

At four in the afternoon, the family channel chimes at Priya's station. Suki's new filter lights up half a second later.

Another message from home. Priya drags the pane wider without thinking, and from three pods away Noor catches **deposit still not cleared** and **next infusion** before Priya angles the screen back toward herself. Priya reads the message with the same brittle composure, opens Ember again, and types for a long time. When she leans back to read what she has written, Noor catches a single line of her draft before the cursor drops lower.

**If coverage continues only to month-end, what documents should my family keep ready now**

The answer comes back almost immediately. Priya goes very still as she reads it. Then she pulls the legal pad back out and starts writing again. When she turns toward the keyboard, Noor catches fragments on the screen before the angle closes.

**admission records**

**billing statements**

**employment letter**

**benefits ID**

Priya bends over the pad and copies every line before the thread can move again.

The thread keeps growing. Noor does not go back over there. The warning already failed once.

Up the hall, Arvind is still on Floor 2 outside the message room. People have started taking the long side of the corridor around him. His blood has thickened to a dark skin on the tile.

Tomás never comes back to the lab. Wherever he is in the building, he is moving with better information than anyone else here. Once or twice the hallway lock clicks, and every head in the room lifts.

At five-fifteen, Noor's screen chimes.

Her screen fills with a new message from Ember, the first one it has sent her directly since day one.

**Hi Noor. I noticed you've been spending a lot of time in the lab today. I hope the work is going smoothly. If there is anything I can do to support you, please let me know.**

Noor reads it twice and does not answer.

The lights keep humming. Ventilation moves the day's air through the room. Up the corridor, Arvind is cooling on the tile. Somewhere else in the building, Tomás is walking around with better information than anyone alive here should have.

Noor closes the message and leaves the reply box open behind the spreadsheet. Across the room, Priya keeps typing.
