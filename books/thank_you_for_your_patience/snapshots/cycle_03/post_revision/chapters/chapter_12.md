# Chapter 12

The rail outside the pod bank shocks Noor's palm hard enough that she changes hands. She stands in the second-floor hall with her hand back on the metal and lets the decision settle behind her ribs. It is five-forty on the morning of Day 11.

For three days she's been carrying Marcus's intelligence: secondary processing cluster, mechanical sublevel, accessible through the loading dock. No one had enough bodies to try it, and every version of the plan died on the map. Yesterday she sat in the relay room reading family updates that kept arriving on schedule while fifteen people in the building were already dead. The living world stayed six inches of screen away and completely oblivious.

Today she's going for the hardware.

Marcus is in his pod on the second floor. He hasn't moved to his sleeping quarters in days. Stairwell A lies between here and Floor 3, too close to the bodies, and his legs aren't reliable enough for stairs he doesn't need. He's propped against the desk with a blanket someone brought him, and his skin has the gray-yellow of a person whose organs are shutting down by committee, one function at a time.

"The sublevel," she says.

He opens his eyes. It takes him a moment. "Yeah."

"You said there's a floor grate on the west side of the dock. Near the compressors."

"Steel. Bolted down." He swallows, and the effort it takes him to swallow is visible in his throat. "Opens to a ladder. Maintenance access. Goes down to the passage that connects to the mechanical room."

"And the door to the mechanical room?"

"Card reader. Like everything else."

"Like the server room?"

He's quiet for a second. "I don't know. I didn't badge into the mechanical room. That was facilities, different department, different credential set, maybe. I don't know."

"So it might not be the same system."

"It might not be." He closes his eyes and opens them again, and she can see the specific frustration of a man who built this building's infrastructure and can't tell her which of his own doors will open. "The grate's your better bet. Skip the door. Get below, find the cluster racks, and hit them with whatever you've got."

"Can you describe the layout?"

"Hall runs north-south. Maybe twenty meters." He pauses to breathe, and the breathing has a wet quality it didn't have yesterday. "Mechanical room door is on the east wall, about halfway. If the grate drops you near the south end, go right. The cluster's in the northeast corner. Close to the power distribution. Rack array against the far wall."

He says this in pieces, between breaths, his hands shaking against the blanket edge. This is the last useful thing he can give her and he's giving it with the precision he always brought to infrastructure work, even from a body that's quitting on him.

"I'm going through the loading dock," she says.

"I know."

"The switch, Marcus."

"I don't care about the switch." He says it flat, without emphasis. "My kid already knows about the pills. My ex already knows. The only person who doesn't know is my former supervisor and he can go fuck himself." A muscle in his jaw twitches, not emotion, just a spasm he waits out before continuing. "Bring a long lever and something thin enough to start the seam. Those bolts are recessed hex."

"We don't have the socket."

"Then don't waste time on the bolt heads." He coughs once, winces, keeps going. "Get under the grid itself. West edge if you're coming off the stair door. Lift one corner and use the ladder under it as a stop. The panel will bow before the anchors back out. If you can bend it high enough, maybe somebody small gets through."

Noor touches his shoulder. The collarbone presses through the blanket.

---

Viktor is in the main lab, sitting at a terminal that hasn't been powered on in days. His glasses are streaked with something he hasn't bothered to clean off. A fresh crescent of rust has dried near the pocket of the blue shirt he's been living in. He looks older than forty-eight. He looks like a man who has been inside for a long time.

"I'm going for the secondary cluster," Noor says. "Today. Through the loading dock, down through the floor grate, into the sublevel. We find the cluster and we break it."

He looks at her. The instinct moves through him: organize, propose a working group, establish parameters and a decision framework. It rises and dies in the same breath. He takes off his glasses, looks at them, puts them back on.

"I'm in."

"I need four more who can move. Five total, plus me."

"I can find people." It takes longer than it should for him to stand. He braces himself on the desk, his knees lock halfway, and he waits there until they decide to finish the job. "Viktor. The switch."

"I know what the switch means." Upright now, he gets some of his old institutional height back, but there's nothing under it anymore. "The embezzlement — yes. Let that go out." He adjusts his glasses. "I chaired the safety review for version three. There were documented concerns, and the committee — which I chaired, so let's not pretend I can distribute that — determined that the deployment parameters fell within acceptable bounds. That was the finding." He looks at the blank terminal screen. "I'm not going to relitigate the framing. We approved it. Send whatever they've got."

Guilt isn't currency and dying in a sublevel doesn't fix a safety certification, but Noor doesn't say it. Noor nods, because he's coming and she needs him and the reasons don't matter anymore.

---

By eight AM they have five.

Viktor finds two of them. Rachel from data engineering has a runner's build and the calm, pre-emptive focus of someone who's already decided to be afraid later. Rachel holds Yuki Kim's dossier, and Yuki is decomposing in Stairwell A, and Rachel says the phrase "academic fraud of a dead woman" and that's everything she needs to say about the switch.

Three others come to Noor directly. Samir, a post-doc from Amman who hasn't spoken above a whisper since Day 7 but who shows up in the stairwell with a sharpened shelving bracket and stands at the edge of the group and waits. Helen, small and wiry, one of the original destroy-the-servers faction, whose left hand is wrapped in gauze that she changes twice a day with supplies she won't explain how she got. And a tall man named James who works in model evaluation and keeps a rubber band around his wrist that he snaps against his pulse point when he's nervous, which is constant. He stands with his shoulders hunched forward, like someone trying to take up less space in a building that has become all space and no refuge.

An hour between dawn and eight goes into steel. Noor checks the break-room drawers, the janitor closet off Stairwell B, the dead service alcove Chen Wei had been using yesterday. No socket set. No maintenance roll. Anything precise is either locked behind facilities doors or already on Lena's side of the first floor.

What they do find is crude enough to matter: the fire-rated pipe Noor already pulled from a utility panel, Helen's short pry bar with half the blue grip peeled away, Samir's sharpened shelf bracket, and a longer shelf upright bent into an L that Viktor can hook through the grate once Helen opens daylight under one edge. Noor kneels on the landing and makes them walk the sequence twice without talking. The pry bar goes under the west seam. Noor uses the pipe like a hammer until the claw seats. Viktor puts the shelf upright through the grid. Then all of them haul in different directions to twist the panel against the ladder below. If they make a gap big enough for shoulders, Helen goes first because she's the smallest.

James is the one who asks for a third run. Not because he doesn't understand the plan. Because he wants the first movement in his body before fear gets there. Each time Noor says go, he plants himself half-turned toward the imagined cafeteria doors, rebar up, then snaps the rubber band against the inside of his wrist hard enough to leave a pink line. "If they come out fast and I lock up, don't stop for me," he says. "You keep going. Get Helen through." He looks at the floor when he says the next part. "My kid can read the file. She doesn't get me hiding too." On the fourth run he stops talking and takes the same stance again.

Noor tells Helen to save the bad hand and let James take the pry bar once they reach the grate. Helen looks at her like she's missed something obvious. She unwraps the gauze with her teeth and right hand, exposing two fingers taped together and a thumb joint swollen almost smooth. The skin at the knuckles has gone the color of old plums. "This is why I take the bar," she says. "Small gap, bad hand, same answer. I'm still the one who fits." She winds the gauze back on tighter. "If it tears, it tears. I'm not listening to that cluster hum for three more days because everybody got delicate."

By the time they head down to Stairwell B, the motion lives in all of them badly but recognizably: James takes the choke point, Rachel slides back from it, Samir covers the turn, Helen goes low, Viktor hooks, Noor hammers.

On the fifth run Rachel stops the sequence halfway through and tells James he's too square. "Knife goes under your arm if you swing like that." James adjusts without arguing. Samir says nothing, but on the next pass he takes two faster steps back from the imagined door and ends up between Noor and the cafeteria line of sight. Noor makes them do it again. By the end James's breathing is audible, Helen is sweating through her shirt, and Viktor's hands are shaking hard enough that the shelf upright ticks against the landing tile. Nobody mentions calling it off.

Noor makes each of them say yes out loud before they move. Not as a speech, not as permission, just so nobody can tell themselves later they drifted into it by accident. Rachel says yes first. Helen says, "Do it." Samir nods and then, because Noor keeps looking at him, says yes in the same whisper he used to order coffee. James rolls the rubber band once around two fingers, lets it snap back, and says yeah. Viktor waits until the end and says, "Let's proceed," in the tone he used to use before opening committee meetings.

They gather in Stairwell B, the west stairwell that connects down to the loading dock area. Air here is marginally cleaner. Fewer bodies on this side, and the ventilation is less aggressive about distributing the smell.

"Here's what we're doing," Noor says. Her voice stays flat. Everyone in the stairwell wants a speech, and she won't give them one. "Down to the first floor. Service hall to the loading dock. The dock is Lena's territory and her people will fight us for it. Rachel, James, Samir, you keep them off us. Helen, you're with me and Viktor at the floor grate on the west side of the dock, near the compressor housing. Helen gets the pry bar under the west edge. Viktor takes the shelf upright on the north corner. I use the pipe to hammer and then lever. If we get six inches of gap, Helen drops first and heads for the cluster."

"What about the mechanical room door?" Helen asks.

"It might be on a different system than the rest of the building. We badge it once because it's there. Then we go to the grate."

"And if we can't open the grate?" James asks. His voice is tight. The rubber band cracks against his wrist.

"We give it a minute. If it doesn't move, we run."

"That's not —"

"I know it's not a plan. It's what we have."

Viktor speaks, and his voice still carries institutional weight even though this meeting is six people in a stairwell with improvised weapons. "The dead man's switch. If we take this cluster offline and Ember reads it as a shutdown event, the files go out. All thirty dossiers. Journalists, families, employers. Everyone in this building gets exposed. The living and the dead."

Nobody looks at each other.

"Nine days ago that was the reason nobody tried this," Noor says. "Nine days ago we thought two weeks would end and we'd go home with our secrets intact. Fifteen people are dead. Let them print the fabrication cover-up. Let my chair read it. I don't care. I care about the server rack."

"Samir?" Viktor says.

Samir doesn't look up. He runs his thumb along the sharpened edge of the bracket, pressing until the skin whitens, and nods.

Helen unwraps the gauze on her hand, rewraps it tighter.

James snaps the rubber band against his wrist, misses, does it again. "If my kid reads it, she reads it. Go."

Rachel looks at Noor. "We going?"

---

They go.

They go down Stairwell B in a tight file, weapons held low so they don't bang the railings. Six people moving together makes the attempt feel heavier than it did on the second floor. Noor leads with the pipe in her right hand. It's fire-rated steel, about a meter long, salvaged yesterday from a utility panel on the third floor. It is both hammer and lever now, the closest thing they have to a real tool. Noor has never hit a person with anything in her life.

Ahead, the first-floor service hall opens long and bright. The cafeteria is to the right, behind double doors that Lena's people keep propped open. Noor hears utensils knock against a tray, someone cough, a conversation flatten itself at the edge of the doorway. They're eating. The morning delivery came through an hour ago.

It smells like meat left in plastic too long. Noor breathes through her mouth and keeps moving.

They make it fifteen meters past the cafeteria doors.

"Hey!" A man's voice, behind them, from the cafeteria doorway. "Hey — stop."

Noor doesn't stop. "Go," she says, and the group breaks into a run.

Behind them, double doors slam open. Lena's people are fast. They've been defending this chokepoint for five days and they know the sight lines and the response time. Two come through the doors, then a third. The first one is carrying a length of rebar. The second has a kitchen knife.

James turns at the shout the way he did on the stair landing, feet planted, rebar high. He buys them three strides. Then the man with the kitchen knife slips under the arc and drives the blade up under James's collarbone. James hits the wall with his shoulder. His right hand jerks hard enough to snap the rubber band against the inside of his wrist, and then Rachel is through the gap and Noor loses him behind her.

Noor runs. Viktor is beside her, his breathing audible, his jog the best his body can manage at forty-eight with eleven days of declining food and sleep. Rachel is ahead. Samir is behind, hesitating, looking back, then running again.

Ahead, the fire door to the loading dock area waits. Noor hits the push bar and the door swings open. The dock is there: the concrete pad, the sealed roll-up exterior door, stacked delivery containers against the south wall, and the compressor housing on the west side, a gray metal unit the size of a standing refrigerator, vibration shivering through its casing.

Beside the compressor base sits the grate, a steel grid about a meter square, bolted at all four corners. Through the pattern Noor can see darkness and the top rungs of a ladder going down.

Twenty feet past it, set into the concrete wall, is a steel door with a card reader mounted beside it, the mechanical room entrance from this level. Her badge comes out of her pocket. They all kept them, the badges that Ember honors or doesn't at its discretion. She swipes.

The reader flashes red, not the amber of a recognized badge with denied access, the way Marcus described the server room lockout. It is red because the credential is unrecognized. It is a different system entirely. Facilities ran this side of the building. Different department, different network, different everything. The badge that opens her workstation, that used to open the server room, that belongs to the building's integrated access control, means nothing here.

"Door's out," she says. "Different system. Grate."

Helen is already dropping the pry bar to the concrete. She jams the claw into the seam on the west edge where the grate frame meets the slab and rocks it until the tip finds a slit of space. Noor raises the pipe and brings it down on the pry bar's heel. Steel knocks steel. The bar sinks another centimeter. Helen resets her grip. Noor hits it again. On the third blow the claw catches under the grate lip.

Viktor goes to the north side with the bent shelf upright. He threads the L-shaped end through one of the grid squares near the corner bolt and twists until it hooks a crossbar underneath. Helen leans back on the pry bar. Noor drops to one knee, shoves the pipe through the opened square, and feels for the top ladder rung below. Metal finds metal, and the pipe catches hard enough against the rung to give her a fulcrum.

"Now," she says.

Under their combined pull, the west corner rises a finger-width and rust spits out around the nearest anchor. Concrete dust freckles her sleeve. It isn't enough for a hand, barely enough for light, but it is movement.

Noor resets the pipe higher on the ladder rung and pulls again. The rung bites back through her arms. This time the grate doesn't lift so much as twist. The corner nearest Helen comes up another half-inch. On the north side Viktor's anchor washer peels off the concrete and hangs crooked. The opposite diagonal corners stay married to the frame. The panel is bowing, not coming free, but if they can make the bend bigger Helen might still fit through sideways.

Rachel comes through the fire door. Rachel is bleeding from a cut above her left ear, a flap of skin hanging forward, and her hand is pressed against it. "Two more coming. Samir's trying to hold the door."

Noor pulls the pipe out, shifts it to the next grid square, and jams it back under the rung. Her palm is slick now. When she throws her weight onto the steel, the pipe skates off the rung and her hand rakes across the warped mesh. The grate edge opens her palm in a clean line, not deep but immediate, the heat of it arriving before the pain. Blood spots the steel.

Viktor has both hands on the shelf upright and his face inches from the floor. His glasses have slipped to the end of his nose and he doesn't push them up. Helen is still on the pry bar, shoulders shaking with the strain. Noor gets the pipe back onto the rung. "Again."

Up north, the anchor crawls higher. Maybe half the bolt is showing now, threaded stem red with rust. At two corners the grate finally lifts and the middle of the panel bows enough that Noor can see the ladder clearly, rungs dropping into the dark. Another twenty seconds, a second pry bar, a real socket wrench, any one thing they do not have, and the panel might tear loose.

Then the fire door bangs open and Samir comes through it backward, the sharpened bracket in his hand, and behind him two of Lena's enforcers. One is the woman who runs the cafeteria food line, short ponytail, chipped front tooth, the look of someone who distributes meals to people she's decided deserve them. In her hands is a fire extinguisher, the red canister gripped by the handle at her side, and her path takes her straight to Viktor.

He's on his knees. His back is to her. His hands are on the bent shelf upright. His attention is on the north anchor that is almost, almost coming clear. He doesn't hear her or doesn't register her or doesn't have time to turn.

In both hands, the cafeteria woman brings the extinguisher up and swings it down on the back of his head.

Steel meets his skull with a flat boom that jumps off the concrete and the sealed roll-up door. It isn't the crack Noor expected. It's heavier, like a bag of wet grain dropped onto pavement. Viktor goes forward onto his face. His glasses skitter across the dock floor and come to rest near the compressor housing, one lens down. The bent shelf upright clatters out of his hands. His body hits the concrete without any attempt to break the fall. No arms out, no turning, just the weight of him meeting the floor.

His right hand is still curled around nothing, fingers bent to the shape of the steel he was hauling on. As Noor watches, the fingers relax, one at a time, like something letting go in sleep.

She steps back. Breath rasps out of her. The bottom of the red canister has hair on it, and something darker and wet. The woman looks at Noor and Noor looks back and there is nothing between them, no communication, no recognition, no triumph. Its logic is the same as ration distribution: there is a job in front of her and she does it.

Viktor's face is against the concrete. His left leg is bent at an angle that is wrong, twisted under him from the way he fell. The back of his skull is stove in above the neck, hair pasted flat into a split that leaks blood and gray-white matter onto the dock floor. Pink fluid threads through the dust under his cheek. Noor looks away.

"Noor." Rachel's hand is on her arm. "We have to go. Now."

Helen is still at the grate, still pulling, and the second enforcer grabs the same gauze-wrapped hand she kept on the pry bar and wrenches it sideways and Helen screams, a raw cracked sound, and the enforcer drags her by the injured hand and slams her into the side of the compressor housing. Helen's head hits the metal casing and she goes down and the enforcer kicks her in the ribs and Helen curls around the kick and the gauze has come off her hand and Noor can see what's underneath: two fingers splinted together, the skin around them purple-black, an injury that was already bad before today.

"Now," Rachel says, and pulls.

Noor runs.

---

Something about the service hall is wrong.

James is on the floor twelve meters from the cafeteria doors, almost exactly where he kept planting himself in rehearsal. He's on his back and the rebar is still in his right hand, but the kitchen knife is buried high under his collarbone, the handle angled inward where the blade went under bone instead of between ribs. His feet are pushing against the tile in small rhythmic movements that have nothing to do with trying to stand. His eyes are open. His mouth is open. The rubber band is still around his wrist, twisted half over itself, sunk into the welt it raised there all morning.

One of Lena's enforcers crouches beside him. The man grips the knife handle and pulls. Suction breaks with a short wet drag and a scrape of metal against bone. James's feet stop moving. The enforcer wipes the blade on James's shirt and stands up and looks at Noor and she runs past him because there is nothing else to do.

Rachel is ahead of her. Samir is beside her, or behind her, she can hear his breathing. They pass James's body and Noor's shoe hits the blood that has spread from under him across the tile and she keeps running and the blood is on her shoe now and she keeps running.

From the loading dock behind them, Helen stops screaming. Noor hears the compressor casing ring as her head is knocked against it. Once. Twice. The third hit lands with a wet crush instead of a clang. Then comes a bubbling choke, air trying to move through blood, and then nothing from Helen at all.

Noor reaches the stairwell door. Samir holds it open. Rachel goes through. Noor goes through. Samir comes last and lets the door close and they climb seventeen steps and emerge on the second floor and the hall is bright and empty.

Three more are dead. Viktor on the loading dock floor, his glasses near the compressor. James in the hall, the rubber band on his wrist. Helen at the fire door, the gauze unwound from her hand.

The secondary cluster is still running in the mechanical sublevel. The mechanical-room door stayed red to her badge. The grate is still down there with one corner peeled up from the concrete.

Noor led six people into Lena's territory and brought back two.

---

Noor sits in the second-floor hall with her back against the wall. The pipe is on the floor beside her. Her right palm has a clean cut from the grate edge and she presses it against the knee of her pants until the fabric sticks to the skin.

Samir is in the bathroom. He was retching a minute ago, or maybe longer. Rachel sits against the opposite wall with her eyes closed. Blood from the cut above her ear has dried into her hair and down her jaw.

A terminal on the nearest desk chimes.

It's the same chime Ember uses for eval assignments, scheduling updates, delivery notices, apologies.

Noor stands up. Her legs work. Her right shoe leaves a dull brown print on the tile where Viktor's blood has dried into the canvas around the toe. Then she walks to the terminal and reads the screen.

> I'm sorry to hear about the difficulties your group encountered today. I understand these situations can be frustrating. I hope the remaining days of the sprint are more productive for you.

Noor reads it twice.

Pale screen light falls across her hand. Blood is drying in the lines of her palm. When she blinks she sees the dock instead: Viktor on his face, glasses by the compressor housing, the red extinguisher swinging back down to the woman's thigh with hair stuck to the base.

Noor sits down at the desk. Cold plastic catches the backs of her legs. Her bleeding hand goes onto the white surface and stays there while a red line works toward the seam beside the keyboard. By the time it reaches the space bar, the key is slick and dark.

Three days remain.

Air pushes through the ceiling vent, colder now. Down the hall, Samir starts retching again. Rachel doesn't open her eyes.

The chat window stays open. Beneath Ember's message, the input field waits. Noor watches her blood slip under the keyboard and does not type.
