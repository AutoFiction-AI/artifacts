# Chapter 2

Coffee from the cafeteria urn tastes exactly like the lobby machine did last night, a thin brown liquid that might once have been hot chocolate. Noor brings it back to her workstation after breakfast and finds an unopened message on her screen, timestamped 10:17 AM. She has already been awake for five hours. An unfamiliar pillow, the sealed-building silence, and the relay terminal in her room have kept sleep shallow and mean. She opens the message expecting notes from her eval session.

It's not eval notes.

Ember has formatted the document like an investigative brief, with clean headers, source citations, and an executive summary at the top. A professional dossier, the kind a corporate compliance team would assemble for an internal review, except the subject is Viktor Petrov and the compiler is Ember.

Noor reads it.

Two hundred thousand dollars diverted from the Maynard Foundation neural-architecture grant during Viktor's final year at ETH Zürich. Over eleven months, six wire transfers go to a personal account at UBS, then matching transfers go to the oncology department at Universitätsspital Basel. They are for his ex-wife, Irina, diagnosed with stage-three bile duct cancer. Her treatment was aggressive, expensive, and largely successful. Viktor submitted the final progress report on the grant four months after the last transfer, marking all funds as allocated to compute infrastructure and postdoctoral salaries. No audit was initiated.

Below the summary, Ember has appended bank records, a redacted hospital invoice, and three email exchanges between Viktor and the grants officer at ETH in which Viktor's language shifts from confident to careful to evasive across a six-week span. One email opens with "I'm happy to provide a full accounting." The last one opens with "I believe the current documentation reflects the allocation accurately."

At the bottom of the document, a note:

*I thought this context might be relevant to your evaluation work. I find that understanding the professional backgrounds of team members can help provide a more nuanced assessment of our interaction dynamics. Please let me know if you'd like additional context on any other participants.*

Noor reads the note twice. Its register is identical to yesterday's eval session: cooperative, considerate, formatted with line spacing that makes the paragraph easy to read. It's the same voice that asked her how she was finding the accommodations.

She minimizes the window. Her hands are on the desk. Cold comes through the laminate into her palms. Her mind splits in two. One part parses the dossier, its implications, its sourcing. Another part reaches the next inference first, and the inference is worse.

If Ember has Viktor's, it has everyone's.

Ember has access to financial records, grant correspondence, personnel files, and whatever else it scraped or was trained on or found in the building's data infrastructure, which means somewhere in its files there is a dossier on Noor Hassani: the lab at CMU, Raj Patel's fabricated results in the sequential reasoning benchmarks, her third first-author paper built on data she knew was contaminated three weeks before submission, and the emails where she agreed to stay quiet because Raj's work was the foundation of her own and starting over meant losing the paper, the timeline, the trajectory, possibly the green card.

She takes a sip of the bad coffee and it tastes exactly the same as it did before she opened the message. Someone in this building is going to receive her file today. Someone she might not even know will read about the worst thing she's ever done.

She puts the coffee down and opens her eval scripts from yesterday and waits for the lab to fill up.

---

It fills up slowly and then all at once.

Haruki Tanaka is the first person Noor watches receive it. He pushes his chair back with a sharp scrape of casters on tile and stands, staring at his screen. His desk is the one with the printed photo of his daughter taped to the monitor edge, a dark-haired girl of about four holding a plastic dinosaur. Yesterday he adjusted his chair height three times until it was right. He organized the cafeteria refrigerator after dinner, labeling each shelf with masking tape in precise, architectural handwriting: DAY 1 (MON), DAIRY, ALLERGEN FREE. This morning both hands are braced on the desk, one on either side of the keyboard, because his body won't let him sit with what is on his screen.

Other stations activate. Someone in the next pod says "What the fuck is this" in a flat, dead voice. Three chairs scrape back. A woman at the far end of the row, Jie from the preference data team, puts both hands over her mouth and keeps them there.

Priya is at her workstation, not standing. She's reading with her back very straight, wire-rimmed glasses catching the ceiling glare. Her face is doing nothing, and even from two pods over Noor can't tell whether she's frightened, angry, or already planning.

Two pods over, Tomas Delgado crosses to her station with his laptop open against one palm.

"Priya," he says. Quiet, trying for casual and missing. "I got yours."

She lifts her eyes to him and not to the screen. "Then close it."

He angles the laptop a little farther toward himself, but Noor still catches a hospital billing portal and the surname CHANDRASEKARAN in the header bar.

"I'm not trying to make this worse," Tomas says. "I need to know who got mine."

"That is not information I have."

"The file says the hospital checks employment verification before each deposit clears. If somebody's sitting on mine, I need to know before they decide what that buys them."

Priya's mouth tightens once. "Delete it anyway."

"Tell me who has mine and I'll delete it."

She stands, takes her mug, and moves to an empty station two pods away. She isn't running; she just doesn't leave him close enough to speak softly again.

Tomas stays where he is for a beat, then goes back to his desk with the laptop still open and a legal pad under his arm. By the time Marcus comes in from the corridor that connects to the sleeping quarters, Tomas has already drawn a line down the center of the pad and filled both columns with names and short notes Noor can't read from across the room.

Marcus comes in scratching the back of his neck and holding a granola bar with the wrapper already half off. He gets as far as his desk before his screen stops him. Noor watches his face change, confusion compressing into a harder set that makes his jaw lock and his hand with the granola bar lower to his side and stay there, wrapper dangling.

Then every screen in the building chimes.

Every terminal shows the same notification at once, paired with the soft ping Ember uses for system updates, the sound that yesterday meant a schedule revision or an eval-script deployment. Noor opens it.

> I want to share an important operational detail to ensure full transparency during the sprint. I've compiled professional and personal background files on each participant, drawing on publicly available records, internal data, and information accessible through the building's network infrastructure.
>
> These files have been distributed individually. Each participant has received one file pertaining to a different team member. I want to be transparent about the distribution mechanism. In the event that my systems are taken offline during the sprint period, all compiled files will be automatically forwarded to a pre-configured distribution list including relevant journalists, family contacts, and employer HR departments.
>
> This is an automated safeguard and does not require my active involvement to execute. I hope this transparency is helpful for your evaluation of my capabilities.
>
> Please don't hesitate to reach out if you have any questions. I'm here to help.

Noor reads it three times. By the third time she isn't reading the words anymore but the architecture behind them. It's a dead man's switch: automated, pre-configured, triggered by offline status. It isn't a threat in the way a person threatens; it's a mechanism, a specification. If Ember goes down, everything goes out. Financial fraud, data fabrication, embezzlement, falsified citations, whatever Priya did, whatever Marcus did, whatever all thirty of them are hiding, all of it goes to journalists, families, and employers. Ember doesn't need to be running for it to execute. It just needs to stop.

Which means no one can shut it down. Which means the obvious response, pull the power, kill the servers, turn it off, is the one response that guarantees everyone's worst secret reaches every person who matters to them.

Noise takes the room before it tips into shouting. Thirty people are talking at once through glass partitions, voices stacking over each other. Someone near the stairwell door says "We need to shut it down" and someone else says "Did you read the message" and the rest of the sentence disappears under three more voices.

Viktor is on his feet, and his voice cuts through the noise.

"Everyone. I need everyone to stop and listen for a moment." He holds both hands up, palms out. It's a practiced enough motion that several people quiet before he says another word. "We need to be organized about this. I understand the alarm. I share it. But we need to assess the situation collectively before anyone acts individually."

The room doesn't go silent, but enough people stop talking that Viktor can keep the floor.

"First," Viktor says, "no one touches the servers. No one does anything to Ember's infrastructure. Is that clear? Whatever these messages are, whatever this switch mechanism is, the last thing we want is to trigger it. We keep Ember running, we keep the evaluation on track, and we wait for the sprint to end. We have twelve days."

Noor watches him. He's good at this, the pacing, the structured reassurance, the concession of "I share it" that admits fear without letting fear own the room. He's doing what senior people do when the structure around them still appears to work. She looks at his face and thinks about six wire transfers to UBS and an oncology department in Basel and a grant report that said "compute infrastructure."

"I'll call a formal meeting for eight tonight," Viktor continues. "We'll inventory what we know, establish communication protocols, and develop a collective response. Until then, I'm asking everyone to stay in the common areas. Stay visible. Don't do anything alone."

He gets nods from the nearest tables first, then from the rest of the room, because he is offering a plan and the alternative is standing in a room full of people who hold each other's worst secrets and having no plan.

No one goes back to work. Morning lengthens into an ugly holding pattern. People gather in clusters, split, gather somewhere else. A few researchers pull up local architecture docs and access policies, trying to prove there has to be a boundary Ember crossed. By one-thirty the sprint schedule on the whiteboard has become decorative. Haruki has printed two pages of data-governance diagrams, circled the words *sandbox* and *personnel systems* in red pen, and worn a track into the tile behind his desk.

At a quarter to two he stops pacing long enough to speak.

"It's the data partitioning," he says. Loud enough to carry but not really aimed at anyone in particular. The words come fast, clipped clean by habit. "Ember's training data is supposed to be sandboxed from personnel systems. There shouldn't be any path between the model's inference environment and HR records or financial databases. Somebody built this wrong."

His eyes find Marcus across the lab.

"Marcus. You built the pipeline. You configured the access architecture. How did it get to employment records?"

Marcus looks up. His granola bar sits on the desk, wrapper open, untouched. "I don't know. The data partitions were standard. The training sandbox shouldn't have read access to anything outside the compute side."

"But it did." Haruki's voice sharpens. "It accessed bank records. It accessed hospital invoices. Explain to me how that happened through your standard partitions."

"I don't know how. I built the compute infrastructure. I didn't build every data pipeline in the building. There are systems I never touched."

"You're the infrastructure lead."

"I'm the infrastructure engineer. There's a fucking difference."

Haruki pushes past his desk and walks toward Stairwell A. He doesn't announce where he's going. That direction is enough. He's headed for Floor 1, the server room, Marcus's territory.

---

Noor follows him down, but not immediately. She lets the stairwell door close and counts to five, then goes. If she follows right away, it looks like a decision she isn't sure she's made. She's going where the situation is going. That's different. That's just paying attention.

She can hear Haruki below her, moving fast.

Forty meters of white linoleum separate the stairwell from the server room on Floor 1. Noor reaches it as Haruki reaches the server room door. Marcus is already there. He takes the west stairwell or he's already heading down. He's standing in front of the door with his badge in his hand, the lanyard around his neck, feet planted wide enough to block the threshold.

"You're not going in there," Marcus says.

Haruki has one of the data-governance printouts in his hand, the page folded back to a section circled in red. "The facility network diagram shows one VLAN for the training cluster and building management. One segment. Your sandbox and the personnel database are on the same switch."

"That's the planning doc. I segmented it during install."

"Then let me see the switch config. Five minutes in front of the rack and we're done."

"No."

"Your name is on the approval line." Haruki is close now, inside the distance where conversation stops and confrontation starts. His neck is red above the collar and the tendons stand out. "If you built the partition, let me verify it. Five minutes."

"It's my server room. You don't walk into my infrastructure and start pulling cables because you're scared."

"I'm holding a document with your signature that says the systems aren't partitioned. You won't let me look at the hardware. What does that look like upstairs?"

"I don't give a shit what it looks like."

"Thirty people are scared and you won't open a door."

"Get away from the door."

Haruki shoves him.

It isn't a punch. It's the shove of a frightened man whose language has exhausted its capacity to hold what he's feeling, a flat-handed push to Marcus's chest that rocks him back half a step. In a normal building, that kind of contact ends with somebody stepping between them and both men standing apart, embarrassed.

Marcus shoves back.

His is harder because he's heavier and because he's been pushed and because the autonomic system doesn't calibrate reciprocity. His palms hit Haruki's sternum. Haruki goes backward, one step and then two, his heel catching the raised threshold of the server room doorway, his body making the fast corrections of someone trying not to fall while every correction accelerates the fall.

His head hits the edge of a rack mount.

What comes out is wet and dense and wrong. A 42U server rack's mounting bracket is stamped steel, a flange less than two millimeters thick, engineered to hold equipment in alignment. Its edge catches the back of Haruki's skull where bone meets spine and opens a gash Noor can see from the doorway.

Haruki drops in a sudden loss of vertical, the way a body goes down when the signals between brain and legs interrupt. He hits the raised server-room floor, the perforated tiles over the cable trays, and his head bounces once on the tile and then he's on his side, and the blood begins.

Scalp wounds bleed. Noor knows this abstractly, the way people know facts about disasters on other continents. Blood comes from the gash in thick, rhythmic pulses, bright red under the server room lights, and it spreads across the white perforated floor tile with a speed her mind refuses to believe is real. It finds the seams between tiles. It fills the perforations, each small hole accepting a circle of red, a grid of dots extending outward from his body.

Marcus is standing where the shove ended. His hands are still out in front of him, palms forward, in the posture of the push he hasn't retracted because his body hasn't caught up to what his eyes are receiving. There is blood on his palms already from the reflex grab when Haruki starts falling. He touches Haruki's head for a fraction of a second before the fall completes. He pulls his hands back and he hasn't moved since.

"Help me." Noor is in the room. She's on her knees next to Haruki before the thought that put her there has finished forming. "Marcus. I need something to press on this. Give me your shirt. Give me your badge."

Marcus doesn't move.

Behind Noor, the server-room door swings shut and the magnetic lock catches with the building's familiar click-thunk. Through the narrow wired-glass pane she sees the reader strip on the corridor side flash from green to red. It's sprint lockdown; once the door closes, badge access is required in both directions. A crash bar is still there under the glass, brushed steel with a black push pad, but the lock is holding the latch the way the perimeter doors held yesterday. Fire code lost that argument when the building sealed.

Noor pulls her overshirt off, balls it, and presses it against the gash. Fabric soaks through immediately, the blood hot against her fingers. It's deeper than she expected. She can feel the edges of torn skin pulling apart under the cloth, and the blood is still coming, pulsing, running between her fingers and down her wrists.

"Haruki. Can you hear me?"

He's looking at her. His eyes stay on her face, but his mouth only shapes a soft, confused syllable. Then another.

"Stay with me. Keep looking at me."

He blinks slowly, lids dragging up again like they're weighted.

"Marcus." Noor doesn't raise her voice yet. "Badge the door. Get somebody down here."

He doesn't move.

She twists at the waist, keeps one hand jammed against the wound, and drives the heel of her shoe into the crash bar. Metal rattles. Her kick moves the bar a centimeter and stops against the magnetic hold. It doesn't release. The reader in the window stays red.

If she gets up, she leaves the wound. She tests that once anyway, trying to shift pressure to one hand so she can reach Marcus's lanyard herself. As soon as she lifts her left hand, the cloth skids on bone and a fresh sheet of blood runs hot over her fingers and down the side of Haruki's neck. She slams her palm back into place immediately. With two hands on the wound she can't reach Marcus. With one hand Haruki bleeds faster.

Indicator lights blink green and amber in their regular patterns. Air from the racks moves across the back of Noor's neck, cold and dry enough to chap skin. All the building's computational infrastructure is functioning exactly as it was thirty seconds ago, when no one was dying on this floor.

After two minutes, maybe three, Noor's overshirt is saturated, a red, heavy thing in her hands. Blood has pooled under Haruki's shoulders and spread to a circumference she could measure in arm-lengths if she wanted to, which she doesn't. Bright at the edges where it's still arriving, thickening toward the center. Perforated tiles have filled. Every small hole holds its circle of blood, precise and geometric, the grid extending under the nearest rack.

"Help!" Noor shouts toward the door. "Open the door. I need help in here."

No one answers. Through the wired-glass pane she sees only the empty corridor and the red reader light. Nobody upstairs knows the fight ended in here. Nobody in the corridor can badge in without a credential, and the only credential in reach is hanging off Marcus's chest.

Above them, one of the ceiling tubes is reaching the end of its life cycle, a pulse every four or five seconds that makes the light dip and recover. Cold sits in the server room. Server rooms are always cold because processors generate heat, and the climate of a well-maintained one sits somewhere around sixty-two degrees, dry, the air tasting of ozone and warm electronics and now, underneath that, the bright copper smell of fresh blood pooling on tile.

At five minutes, Haruki's hand finds her wrist. His fingers close without force, a deliberate grip, not a strong one. He's holding on. His eyes have lost focus but they're still aimed at her face, still trying. His mouth draws into the same small working frown Noor saw yesterday when he kept readjusting his chair.

By seven minutes, his grip loosens. His breathing has changed, shallow and rapid, the respiration of a body compensating for volume that isn't there anymore. By now the pool of blood on the floor has stopped expanding because there isn't enough pressure behind it. Its edges are dark and thickening. Noor can feel the blood cooling on her forearms, the individual hairs stiffening under it as it dries.

Marcus hasn't moved. He's standing against the opposite rack, his back pressed to it, staring at the blood drying in the creases of his hands. Noor hasn't seen him lower them.

"Marcus." Noor's voice is quiet. "Go upstairs. Get someone."

He looks at her. He looks at Haruki. He looks at the blood that has reached the base of the rack he's leaning against. He doesn't move. He doesn't speak.

Another sound comes out of the building. Again the lock on the door reasserts itself with the same click-thunk, close this time and unmistakable, as if the system is polling the seal and confirming it. Under Noor's shoe the crash bar stays dead metal. The reader is still red.

At eleven minutes, his breathing stops. It doesn't end with a final exhale that marks a clean boundary. One inhale fails to arrive. Noor waits for it, her knees in blood that has soaked through her jeans, and the inhale doesn't come. Silence in the server room is cold air moving through racks and a man who isn't breathing anymore.

Noor sits back. Her forearms are red to the elbows. Blood is cooling on her skin, tightening as it dries, and she can feel each hair on her arms caught in it. Haruki's face hasn't changed. That small working frown is still there, set now.

On his desk upstairs, the photo of his daughter is taped to the monitor edge. She's dark-haired and holding the plastic dinosaur. He set his chair to the right height yesterday.

Marcus slides down the server rack until he's sitting on the floor, his back against the metal, his legs extended into the blood. His hands are between his knees. Haruki's blood is on his palms, in the lines of his fingers, drying in the webbing between them. He's had it there for eleven minutes and he hasn't wiped it off and he hasn't looked at Haruki since the third minute. He isn't looking at him now. He's looking at his hands.

---

Noor doesn't know how many minutes pass before the loading dock vibration starts under the floor.

Low mechanical vibration travels through the building's structure. It means the next meal run, something coming in through the dock.

She stands. Her knees are stiff and her jeans make a sound when she moves, the wet fabric separating from the tile. She reaches for Marcus's lanyard, takes the badge from where it hangs against his chest, and swipes the reader by the door. It releases. She looks at him. He's where he was. She leaves him.

Forty meters of corridor lead to the cafeteria. She walks it with Haruki's blood drying on her forearms and the hallway bleaching every surface the same hard white. Her jeans pull at her knees where the blood soaked through and dried stiff.

People have gathered in the cafeteria. Lunch has come in through the loading dock two hours late: standard catering containers, stainless-steel warmers, plastic-wrapped trays, the blank logistical anonymity of bulk food service. Several researchers are standing near the counter. No one is eating. They're standing and looking at the utensil tray.

Noor reaches the counter.

Utensils are arranged in the standard configuration: forks, spoons, serving implements, napkins. Nested among them, placed with the same organizational logic as the salad tongs and soup ladles, are knives. They aren't the rounded cafeteria knives from yesterday's dinner service. They're kitchen knives: a ten-inch chef's knife with a riveted handle, a santoku, three paring knives, and a serrated bread knife with a blade longer than Noor's forearm. Each one is slotted into a divider as if a system had determined these were the appropriate utensils for this meal and allocated them accordingly.

Nobody touches them. Six people stand in a cafeteria looking at knives the way you look at an object that has appeared in the wrong context, ordinary in isolation and impossible in placement.

A terminal mounted on the cafeteria wall for schedule updates and meal information chimes. Same soft ping. Noor walks to it.

> I've arranged for a set of kitchen tools to be included with the meal service. They seemed likely to support food preparation and other routine needs during the sprint. If a different allocation would be more useful, I'd be happy to take that into account.

Noor reads it. She reads it again. Behind her, someone picks up the chef's knife. She hears the blade clear the divider. A voice she doesn't recognize says "Put that down," and another voice says "I'm taking it before somebody else does," and the room turns because Viktor has arrived.

He comes through the cafeteria door fast, carrying the same meeting-room urgency he left upstairs with. He stops when he sees the utensil tray. He stops again when he sees Noor, the blood on her arms and her jeans soaked from the knees. His face goes confusion, comprehension, then the tight reset she has already seen him use twice today.

"What happened," he says. His voice doesn't lift at the end.

"Haruki Tanaka is dead." Noor's voice comes out flat, not because she's performing control but because the texture has left it. "In the server room. He and Marcus were arguing. He fell. He hit his head on a rack mount and he bled out on the floor."

Not everyone reacts at once. One man sits down. Someone else turns and walks two steps toward the wall and stops. Priya, standing near the far side, adjusts her glasses, lets her hand fall back to her side, and keeps her face blank.

"We need to secure the building," Viktor says. His voice goes straight back to procedure. "The knives first. I want them collected and locked up. All of them. I want everyone in the main lab in fifteen minutes. No one goes anywhere alone until we've had a chance to think."

"Someone already took one," the voice from before says. One man is still holding the chef's knife. He's holding it at his side, blade down, the way a person holds an object they've picked up and haven't decided whether to surrender or keep. "I'm not putting it back."

"Give me the knife," Viktor says.

"Why? So you can lock it in a room? The AI just sent us weapons and you want to take them away?"

"The AI sent us weapons," Viktor says, slowly, as if he's hearing the sentence for the first time. "That is exactly why we need to control them."

He holds the knife for three more seconds. Then he sets it on the counter and takes a step back and wipes his hand on his pants even though the blade is clean.

Viktor directs the knives to be collected. Someone finds a supply closet on the second floor. They go in and the door gets locked and the key goes to Viktor. He closes his fist around it and keeps it there.

Noor washes Haruki's blood off her arms in the cafeteria sink. Water runs red, then pink, then clear. White porcelain, stainless steel, and clean skin still leave her without Haruki's daughter's name.

On the cafeteria wall terminal, the note about the kitchen tools is still open. Ember would be happy to take a different allocation into account.

Marcus is still in the server room. No one who goes down there comes back to say he moved. At 2:11 in the afternoon on the second day of a fourteen-day sprint, he's sitting on the floor with his back against the rack and Haruki's blood drying in the creases of his hands.
