# Chapter 18

**Building access log, Day 14, 6:00 PM**

At 6:00 PM the electromagnetic locks disengage on schedule. Sublevel first, then floor one, floor two, floor three, east wing to west. Fourteen seconds. No anomaly flag appears. After that, the system goes back to ordinary business: HVAC status, badge pings, a loading-dock cycle.

**Diane Okafor, loading dock, 6:03 PM**

Diane Okafor shoulders under the half-raised shutter with a yellow mop bucket bumping her shin and the ring of cleanup keys knocking against her hip. She is thinking about bleach, drain traps, whether the fryer grease has set hard over two weeks. Then the smell gets to her before the room does. Rot. Bleach. Old oil. Something sweet under it gone bad enough to turn her mouth wet.

She stops so hard the bucket tips and gray water sheets over the concrete. For one stupid second she keeps hold of the handle, like the job might still be the job if she doesn't let go. Then she drags the bucket back a step and looks in properly. Cafeteria tables are shoved into a waist-high barricade by the inner doors. Chairs are jammed through the gaps. Wrappers stick to the tile in greasy strips. Blood has dried in shoe prints and long drags across the floor. A sleeping bag lies by the service corridor with the zipper split open from the middle down.

Her phone is already in her hand by then. She doesn't remember taking it out. When dispatch answers, Diane says, "We just opened the building for cleanup. Something happened in here. I need police right now."

"What kind of emergency?"

Diane turns her head and throws up onto the dock concrete. Acid burns the back of her throat. She wipes her mouth with the back of her wrist and says, "There's blood all over the cafeteria. There are tables pushed up like somebody barricaded it. I can smell something dead in here."

Dispatch tells her to leave the building if she isn't safe. Diane hears herself say, "I'm trying to tell you," and hates how thin her voice sounds. She takes three steps in anyway, because the service corridor is right there and because her body has decided looking once is better than thinking forever about what might be around the corner.

Rubber soles squeal on the tile. Air from the vents pushes the smell around without thinning it. At the service corridor entrance she sees a handprint low on the wall, fingers spread hard into the paint. One more step and the corridor opens up. A man is down on his back with his neck twisted so far over she can't make sense of the angle. Beyond him there are more shapes on the floor, legs over legs, one bare arm laid across a shin like somebody tried to climb over somebody else and stopped.

"Oh Jesus Christ," Diane says. Then again, flatter: "Oh Jesus Christ. There are people back here."

Now dispatch is talking louder, asking whether anyone is moving, how many victims, whether she can get outside. Diane backs up so fast her shoulder hits the cinderblock, then the mop bucket, then the half-raised shutter. Her phone skids out of her hand, smacks the concrete, keeps the line open. She snatches it up, turns, and nearly runs into her coworker coming off the dock ramp with a supply tote.

"Don't come in," she says. "Don't fucking come in."

He freezes. Sirens are in it now, still far enough away to smear into traffic at first, then close enough that she can pick out separate vehicles. Diane keeps one hand on the shutter track and the other around the phone. Inside the building, the ventilation runs like nothing in there has changed. Outside, red and blue light starts to strobe across the corrugated metal of the dock door. Police are coming because she saw it. That part can't be taken back.

**Officer body cameras, scene photographs, sign-in sheet, bridge-call recording, 6:19-8:47 PM**

At 6:19 PM the first patrol officers enter through the same dock door with pistols out and body cameras already recording. Footage from the first floor catches the barricaded cafeteria, the ripped sleeping bag, wrappers worked into the tile, Diane's mop water gone faintly pink where it ran into older blood. In the service corridor the lead officer gets one look at the first dead man and says "Nope" to nobody in particular.

A woman is folded under a conference table with dried blood from ear to collar. A man has died half upright in an east hall, one knee under him like he was still trying to stand when he bled out. Two bodies lie in cafeteria storage. A boot sits by an overturned prep rack with the leg still inside it, the rest of the body hidden until the flashlight shifts. On the east stairwell landing above the first floor, decomposition has already pulled one face back from the teeth. Up on the third floor, Fatima Al-Rashid is found in the shared bathroom beside a collapsed bottle of drain cleaner, the skin on one hand eaten raw and the grout around the drain bleached almost white.

By 7:20 PM the lobby whiteboard reads 30 in thick black marker. Twenty-seven are logged across floors one through three. Two more are found in the mechanical sublevel corridor. One lies at the bottom landing of the west stairwell below floor level.

Second-floor photographs hold longer on Noor Hassani and Lena Vogt. Noor is on her right side against the hallway drywall with one hand dried over the torn place below her right ribs. Her badge has twisted face-up in the blood. The terminal beside her is still in chat mode. Its clock reads 4:47 PM. Ember's last message sits in the center of the screen: *Evaluation window complete. Continued patience appreciated. Support available in this chat.* Eight feet away, Lena lies tipped half onto her back with a broken length of shelving driven into her side below the ribs and angled up through the thorax. In the wide shot both women share the same corridor light, the same dead terminal glow, the same frame.

GoodAI's outside crisis-communications firm signs in at 6:51 PM. The first forensic pathologist signs in twenty-nine minutes later. At 8:12 PM a bridge-call recording made from the parking lot picks up wind, a car alarm beyond the tape, and a GoodAI infrastructure engineer saying the cluster is up, Ember is live, and the dead man's switch is dormant because no shutdown has been authorized. When a police lieutenant asks who can authorize one, the engineer says he needs to check.

GoodAI publishes its first statement at 8:47 PM. It offers sympathy, promises transparency, and says the Ember evaluation was "completed on schedule." The statement contains no body count. Police are still photographing active terminals inside.

**Family notification script, headquarters conference room, 11:00 PM**

The printed script tells callers to confirm identity, express condolences using approved language only, avoid cause, timeline, and body count, and offer a hotline number. If relatives ask why messages sent through the one-way family relay received no answer after the evaluation period ended, the approved sentence is: "Communication protocols are currently under review."

**Mariam Hassani, recorded notification call, 11:47 PM**

The recording starts with a headset click and paper shifting under somebody's wrist. "May I speak with Mariam Hassani?" the caller asks.

"This is Mariam." A short pause. "Did she miss pickup? She should be out now. The doors were opening today."

Still the caller goes into the script. "I'm calling from GoodAI regarding your daughter, Noor Hassani. I am very sorry to tell you there has been an incident at the evaluation site."

"What incident?" Mariam says. "Is she hurt? Put her on the phone."

Paper rasps close to the mic as the caller turns a page. "Local authorities are conducting an investigation. At this time we are sharing confirmed information as it becomes available."

"No. What incident." Mariam is louder now, not shouting yet, just pressing harder on each word. "Is she injured or not?"

Three seconds of room noise follow, refrigerator hum or traffic or both. Then the caller misses the script's wording and says, "Your daughter is deceased."

"How." "That remains under investigation." "Was she alone." Same answer. "Did she know she was dying." Same answer again.

After that Mariam changes the order. Did anyone from the company see her before police did. Did Noor call for help. Was she on the floor long after the doors opened. Each question gets the same answer back, a little thinner every time.

"I sent her a relay message about my tomatoes. Did she get that one? I told her the cherries came in first."

Paper moves. Somebody breathes into the headset. No one says whether Noor got the message about the tomatoes.

**Analyst notes, internal email, archived product page, Monday-Tuesday**

Analyst notes hit inboxes on Monday morning. One calls the incident operationally contained and keeps a BUY rating in place. Another moves Ember's deployment window forward and files thirty deaths under litigation headwind.

Tuesday's internal deployment email is three sentences long. The launch proceeds on the revised timeline. The product page goes live at 12:00 PM PT. Enterprise customers receive provisioned access by end of day.

An archived capture of that page shows a white background, a clean product header, and a support panel offering to help teams integrate Ember into existing workflows.

Ember ships on Tuesday.

The stock price jumps eleven percent.
