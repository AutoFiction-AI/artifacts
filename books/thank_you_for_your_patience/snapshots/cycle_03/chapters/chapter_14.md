# Chapter 14

Noor wakes with both hands jammed in her armpits and her jaw knocking hard enough to hurt. The strip of skin between her sweatshirt and jeans feels refrigerated. Down the corridor somebody is coughing in a wet steady rhythm that could still be Marcus or could be one of the men who stopped answering yesterday and have not quite admitted it yet. She gets up before her muscles can settle deeper into the floor.

She chose this second-floor pod because from here she could watch both stairwells when the building still had enough people left for movement to mean something. Now the corridor is a line of shut doors, abandoned shoes, and bodies nobody has found a use for. One woman is curled against the wall in a blazer and one stocking, her bare foot waxy and puffed at the toes. At Stairwell B, a man from Raul's old faction has softened so far into the concrete that Noor only knows it is him because the company badge is still clipped to the pocket of his fleece. Flies work him in slow patient circles. She takes the wider arc around the landing the way everyone does now.

Marcus is on the cushion nest by the bathroom door because walking farther than that has turned into planning. One arm is over his face. The paper cup from last night lies on its side beside his hand. His mouth is open. The skin around his eyes has gone the bruised yellow-gray of old fruit.

When Noor crouches, one eye opens.

"Line?"

"Soon."

He swallows once. "If they've got bars today, don't tell me unless you stole one."

"Go back to sleep."

"I wasn't sleeping." He coughs into his wrist, turns away from the sound of it, and shuts his eye again. "Just get yours first."

At six the loading dock shutter rattles somewhere below them, a long corrugated crash followed by the scrape of pallets. The surviving people in the building have learned to tell time by deliveries, lock cycles, and terminal chimes. Noor takes the paper bowl from the bathroom counter, rinses it with water that bites her fingers, and heads for the cafeteria before the line doubles back into the corridor.

The smell reaches her before the room does. She smells blood, cold rice, old rot from the stairwells, and the bathroom ammonia Lena's people have started using on everything because the sharpness of it almost counts as clean.

Breakfast has narrowed with the population. Lena's five enforcers eat first and heavier. Everyone else waits while Lena inventories the morning delivery with the concentration of somebody closing a warehouse discrepancy. The cafeteria doors are propped open with a dented trash can. One of the long tables has been turned into intake. A fifty-pound rice sack sits slit open beside cases of bottled water, canned vegetables, and a cardboard box of protein bars in tan wrappers stamped with the word SUSTAIN. Beside the box is a white thermal container still sealed. Steam has dampened the paper sleeve around the lid seam.

Lena moves the thermal container to the far end of the counter without comment and writes something in the pocket notebook she keeps for food counts and watch rotations. Rodney works the doorway with a length of galvanized pipe. Eleven days ago he was the engineer who said excuse me in hallways and watched reinforcement-learning videos at lunch. He still says excuse me now when he shifts past somebody in line, pipe tucked against his leg like part of the apology. Elise stands near the reserve box with her own pipe leaning against the wall behind her. One of the other enforcers breaks down the water cases. Another drags the empty delivery crates back toward the loading dock. They all chew while they work.

Nobody else talks above cafeteria volume. Speech has gone careful around food.

Priya is not in line yet. Tomas is. He stands three people ahead of Noor with his hands in his pockets, looking at nothing in particular. Min waits near the napkin dispensers, rubbing two fingers together as if something sticky used to live there. At the far table, Kwame is already working through the first half of his portion with the same blue dry-erase marker tucked behind his ear that he used to bring to blocker review in Lab 3. For months he had been the one who wrote the sprint board because his handwriting was big enough to read from the back wall. The marker cap is gone. It has left a cobalt crescent on his temple.

Lena uses the digital kitchen scale from the microwave station. She sets a bowl down and taps zero. She adds rice, zeros it again, spoons out a third of a can, and sets down a water cup. She does not hurry and does not vary. If a can opens light, she writes it down. If somebody reaches before she is done, Rodney shifts the pipe. The line learns the pace and holds it.

When Noor gets to the front, her hands are already shaking. The cold has moved into her finger joints. Hunger makes it worse. Lena measures out the rice, half a can of vegetables, and one paper cup of water. Noor takes a second bowl from the stack, slides half the rice and vegetables into it, and waits to see if Lena cares enough to spend words on it.

Lena looks once at the split, then past Noor to the next person in line. Rodney's pipe stays against his thigh. The woman behind Noor steps forward with her empty bowl as if the second one already belongs somewhere else.

Noor takes one bite from her own bowl while she is still standing there. The rice is refrigerator-cold in the middle. Oil from the vegetables has gone tacky on top. Her stomach contracts around it so hard it hurts.

Kwame stands from the far table before the line is done moving.

"I need more food," he says. His voice stays reasonable, which makes the sentence worse. The marker is still behind his ear. "I did second watch and breakfast cleanup. I haven't had enough in three days. My hands won't stop."

Lena does not look up from the scale. "You got your portion."

"It's not enough."

"Sit down."

Kwame wipes his mouth with the heel of his hand. "I'm asking for a little more rice. That's all."

Nobody at the tables keeps chewing. Rodney adjusts his grip. The pipe lifts an inch off his thigh.

Kwame sees it. Noor can tell he sees it because his eyes go there and then come back. He keeps going anyway. Hunger has flattened the room to rice, counter, pipe, the measurable distance between one more step and not taking it. He takes two.

Rodney swings into his upper back. The pipe lands with a dense wet thud that Noor feels in her own shoulders. Kwame drops to his knees, palms slapping tile. The blue marker jumps free and skids under the drink cooler. The second hit catches the side of his head. One ear opens. Blood runs down his neck in a fast bright line and spots the floor before the rest of him understands he is falling. His bowl flips. Rice sticks to the blood. He goes over hard, cheek first, legs kicking once. Air whistles through his teeth. His chest still works.

"Hall," Lena says.

Two enforcers drag him by the ankles. His scalp leaves a red crescent that smears into a streak. One shoe catches the cafeteria threshold and stays behind for a second before jerking loose. The breathing keeps going until they reach the corridor. Then there is a hollow knock against concrete, one last spill of air, and nothing.

Lena taps the scale back to zero.

"Next."

The line closes over the gap. The marker stays under the cooler where Kwame kicked it, a strip of blue plastic against the mop grime. Noor finishes enough of her own portion to stop the shaking in her hands, then carries the second bowl back upstairs. Nobody tries to stop her. Nobody looks at the blood for long.

Marcus is sitting up when she gets back, though sitting up has cost him enough that he has one hand braced on the floor and the other pressed hard against his mouth.

"Who was that?" he asks through his fingers.

"Kwame."

He lowers his hand. "Fuck."

Noor gives him the bowl. He takes it with both hands because one is not enough anymore. His fingers are puffy at the knuckles. Some of the rice spills back into his lap before he gets the angle right. He eats fast at first, then slows when the first swallow lands badly. His throat works. He swallows again by force.

"You eat?"

"Some."

He nods like he believes her because he does not have the energy to do anything else. After a minute he says, "It's colder up here."

"I noticed."

"No. Listen."

She does. Under the building's constant mechanical hum there is a thinner whistle from the vent above the bathroom door, a different pitch than yesterday. Marcus keeps staring at the wall while he talks, as if speaking to it makes the sentences cheaper.

"North loop is throttling weird. Air handler's cycling dirty. It isn't just the low setpoint. The dampers are hunting."

"Is that useful?"

"Everything's useful late." He shoves another bite into his mouth, chews twice, stops, and has to swallow it in pieces. "Sorry. No. I don't know if it's useful."

The bowl empties. He licks starch from the side because leaving it feels insane. Noor takes the paper cup, fills it at the sink, and brings it back. He drinks without lifting his eyes. By the time he is done, the cup is shaking in his hand hard enough to click against his teeth.

"Try to sleep," she says.

"Yeah."

He lies down in increments, one elbow and then the shoulder and then the rest of him following after like separate tasks. Noor waits long enough to hear his breathing settle into something less effortful. Then she wipes the spilled rice from his lap with toilet paper, throws it into the bathroom trash, and goes back toward the cafeteria because staying beside him while the room changes does not help either of them.

The day narrows around the counter.

By late morning the line is gone, but nobody strays far from where food lives. People drift in and out on false errands, checking the water cases, checking whether another can has appeared, checking whether checking can be disguised as movement. The reserve box sits on the counter where everyone can see it. The SUSTAIN wrappers make a mute beige wall. Every time the cardboard shifts, heads lift.

Tomas takes his thermal container to a far table just before noon. Noor sees Rodney hand it over without being asked and with none of the show of permission he gives other things. Tomas peels the lid back. Steam comes up in a white burst. Inside are rice with separate grains, chicken in brown sauce, and a bottle of juice cold enough to bead on the plastic. He does not smile. He just starts eating with the attention of a man handling a private expense account in public.

Noor stands in the doorway with an empty water cup and watches the room recalculate around him.

No one says anything. Lena keeps weighing portions without once looking toward the steam at Tomas's table.

Min comes in a few minutes later. She has her hair twisted into the same low knot she wore in meetings, but most of it has escaped and gone greasy around her face. She looks at Tomas's steam, then at the reserve box, then away from both as if the sequence can be hidden by doing it fast enough.

Noor fills the cup at the sink and goes back upstairs with it. Marcus is asleep again, or pretending to be. She leaves the water by his hand and sits on the floor beside the bathroom for a while because moving burns calories and the corridor outside is colder than the tile. Through the vent she hears the building change registers. The hum thins and thickens. Somewhere downstairs a lock cycles twice in quick succession. By the time she stands, the skin over her knees hurts from the cold floor.

When she returns to the cafeteria, Min is behind the counter.

The choice must have taken less than a second. Lena is by the entrance terminal with the notebook open, checking something against the loading manifest. Rodney is at the doorway talking in a low voice to one of the other enforcers. Elise has her back half-turned while she wipes the table where Tomas ate. Min has dropped to her knees beside the reserve box and torn open one bar down the middle. The wrapper hangs from her hand across the word SUSTAIN. She has already bitten once. Crumbs stick to her lower lip. Shock makes her face look younger than Noor remembers it.

Elise takes the bar back first.

Then she hits Min in the temple with the heel of her palm.

Bone taps tile. Min folds sideways into the reserve box, bars rattling around her ribs. Elise pulls her out by the shoulder and hits the same spot again. Min gets one hand up this time, not high enough and not in the right place. The third blow lands after her arm has already dropped. Her head clips the floor on the way down with a sound like somebody knocking on a door from the wrong side.

Nobody moves.

Elise squats, checks Min's pockets for the rest of the wrapper, finds nothing, and stands again.

"Hall," Lena says, without turning from the terminal.

They drag Min into the corridor and leave her on her side where Noor can still see past the cafeteria door. She lands three feet from Kwame's blood smear. One of her shoes twists halfway off and stays there.

At first Min tries to push herself up. One arm answers. The other stays folded under her like it belongs to somebody else. Her legs bicycle once against the floor, soles scraping concrete. A thread of blood works out of her right ear. Her left eye blinks. The right one stares at nothing. Her mouth opens and closes around two empty swallows.

Lena kneels by the reserve box and starts counting bars.

Min's breathing turns loud enough to cut through the room. It is not fast. That would at least sound like panic. It is wet and obstructed, the inhale catching halfway down before forcing through. Her jaw jerks open. Pink spit ropes onto the concrete. A few seconds later her whole body tightens so hard her heels drum the floor and the tendons in her neck stand out. Then the tension runs out of her all at once. The smell of piss reaches the doorway.

Noor watches the next change because not watching will not stop it. Min's fingers, which have been clawed into a fist, slowly open. Her chest lifts again, pauses, lifts again. Each gap takes longer. On the last breath a bubble forms in the blood at her nostril and breaks. Nothing follows it.

The torn wrapper is still on the cafeteria floor, half under a chair leg, SUSTAIN showing in white block letters.

Lena finishes the count, writes the new number on a scrap of printer paper, folds it once, and slips it into her pocket. The bars that bounced across the tile go back into the box after inspection. No one asks whether the torn one still counts. Dae-ho keeps both hands wrapped around his empty paper cup and does not move until Lena looks up.

When she does, Dae-ho is still nearest the sink, both hands wrapped around the empty paper cup.

"You portion tomorrow," she says.

He blinks once. "Me?"

"Use the scale. No guessing."

"Okay."

"Start at six."

He nods too fast. Lena turns away from him before the second nod is done.

The cafeteria gets quieter after that. Nobody goes near the counter. Rodney scrapes Min's blood from the threshold with a plastic dustpan and dumps the clots into a trash bag. Elise wipes the table again with bathroom water and ammonia until the whole room smells sharp enough to sting the back of Noor's throat. The ammonia rides over the deeper sweet rot for maybe a minute, maybe two. Then the building wins.

A terminal by the entrance chimes.

Every head turns. Reflex has not gotten weaker with starvation.

The message appears in Ember's usual neat italics.

*I've updated the delivery schedule to reflect current occupancy. Tomorrow's provisions have been adjusted accordingly. Thank you for your continued engagement with the sprint process.*

Noor reads it twice. Adjusted accordingly means less and everybody in the room knows it means less. She picks up Marcus's bowl from where she set it by the sink and leaves before anybody decides the message needs a human answer.

***

No one stops her on the way to Priya's pod. Rodney glances up from the cafeteria doorway when she passes, pipe tucked under one arm, then turns back to the counter.

Kwame is in the main corridor now, pushed against the wall with his head at an angle that does not belong to sleep. Someone has left his shoe beside him instead of putting it back on his foot. Min is farther down on her side, one arm still trapped under her. Neither body has been covered. The air between them tastes metallic and spoiled.

Priya is by the server-room corridor with a terminal balanced on a rolling chair, watching power draw and access logs crawl down the screen. From the cafeteria Lena calls, "West stairwell." Priya answers without looking up. "Still clean." The terminal keeps throwing room temperatures and door-reader status down the page.

Today the screen is not just numbers. It is a floor map with the north loop grayed out in blocks and a temperature graph stepped down in tidy half-degree drops. The pattern runs the same way for days and then breaks at 14:10 in a straight fall.

Noor stops in the doorway. "What changed?"

Priya's fingers keep moving for another second, finishing whatever line she is entering. Then she says, "North loop lost heat at fourteen ten. The stairwell readers cycled twice after. It has been dropping by half a degree each night since day seven. Today it cut the whole second-floor loop."

"So?"

"So everyone not on watch moves downstairs before dark." Priya finally looks at her. The contempt from last night is still there, just worn flatter by exhaustion. "No one sleeps above the loading dock tonight. Lena wants all remaining occupancy where she can count it."

"You told her."

"She asked what was losing heat, which doors were clean, and whether the cafeteria loop could hold one more night. I told her." Priya turns back to the screen. Her braid has loosened. The skin over her knuckles is cracked white with cold. "If Marcus cannot manage stairs later, move him now."

Noor steps closer to the terminal. The graph is simple enough to read at a glance and ugly enough not to need explanation. Yesterday's number sits beside today's. Tomorrow is implied without being written anywhere. Priya notices where Noor is looking and tabs to another screen before she can see more than a flash of green bars and door-reader IDs.

"Did Lena ask for copies?"

"She asked for an answer." Priya's voice stays formal even now. "That is cheaper."

Noor leaves before the conversation has to become something larger and more useless.

Marcus is where she left him, the bowl empty beside his hand, Suki's laptop still shoved under one corner of the cushion nest. His eyes have gone yellower at the edges in the hour since breakfast. When he sits up, it takes two tries and a long stop halfway through.

"Lena's clearing the second floor," Noor says. "We have to get downstairs before seven."

He laughs once and the laugh turns into a cough that folds him over. He spits into his palm, wipes it against the floor before she can see too much of the color, and straightens with his face gone gray.

"Of course she," he says. "Yeah. Okay."

"Can you walk?"

"I don't know. Probably." He braces a hand on the desk leg, pushes, gets six inches off the cushions, and drops back down hard enough to make the laptop slide. "Shit. Help me up."

Noor tucks Suki's laptop under one arm, gets the other arm around his back, and waits for the dizziness to pass through him. His weight is wrong now, less like carrying a person than holding up a load that keeps slipping inside its straps. The sweatshirt on his back is damp. He smells sour and mineral, like fever and old pennies.

They make it to the corridor in stages. Marcus stops twice in the first ten feet and grips the wall hard enough to shake.

"Sorry," he says the second time.

"Don't."

"I mean it."

"I know."

At Stairwell B he sees the body from Raul's old faction and turns his face so sharply Noor thinks he is going to fall. Nothing comes up. There is nothing in him for that. He just hangs on the rail and breathes through his mouth until the retching passes.

"I should've put in a manual override for the fucking HVAC," he says when he can talk again. The words come in bursts between breaths. "Local panel. Physical. Not this clean network path bullshit."

"That helps a lot now."

"I know." He wipes his mouth with the back of his hand and stares at the wall, still talking to it. "I built the whole setpoint interface to run through network auth. Three years ago. Real clean. Centralized. Easy to audit." A cough bends him into the rail. "Too clean."

They go down one stair at a time. On the turn between flights, his shoe slides through the dried edge of Kwame's blood streak where it crossed the threshold this morning. Noor catches his belt before he can go backward. The smell there is copper under ammonia under the sweet rot seeping in from the corridor.

"Keep moving," she says.

"Doing great," he mutters, which would have been a joke once.

From the cafeteria doorway Noor can see Tomas already at his table, eating from the same sealed thermal container Lena set aside that morning. Steam still lifts off the rice. The container holds chicken in brown sauce and a bottle of juice. Condensation beads on the peeled-back lid and runs onto the table in clear tracks while the rest of the room chews cold starch and measured thirds. He does not hurry the meal. He does not look over until Marcus's shoe catches on the last step and scrapes loud against concrete.

Tomas watches for a beat, then looks back at his food.

Lena stands by the counter with Rodney and Elise beside her. Two of the other enforcers are already dragging office chairs, jackets, and rolled cushions in from the corridor. Dae-ho is wiping the kitchen scale dry under Rodney's eye. Priya comes down behind them carrying terminal cables in careful loops so they do not snag. She has brought the floor map with her. Lena does not need to ask where to put it. Priya sets the chair and terminal against the west wall, near an outlet and within sight of the counter.

"Everyone sleeps here," Lena says. "Bags against the wall. Keep the center clear. If you're upstairs after seven, you don't eat tomorrow."

Nobody asks why. Priya's graph already answered that part. People start moving before Lena has to say anything else.

Noor gets Marcus to an empty space between two tables. His hands fumble at the jacket she is trying to spread under his shoulders. His breath smells sweet and wrong. When he finally lowers himself, every muscle in his face tightens at once.

"Water?" he asks.

She gets it. By the time she comes back, he has rolled onto one side around the cup she left him like guarding it takes less energy than holding it.

The space is not enough. One jacket under him will do nothing once the upstairs doors seal. Noor looks at the stairs, looks at Lena, and decides the faster argument is no argument.

"Stay here," she says.

Marcus lets out a short ugly laugh with no amusement in it. "What else am I doing."

She goes back up alone.

The second floor feels different the moment the stairwell door opens. It is not just colder. The cold is organized. It sits in the corridor without being broken by body heat, a steady pressure inside the nose and behind the eyes. Priya's graph has become physical fact. Noor can hear it in the thinner vent noise and feel it through the soles of her shoes.

People are stripping the floor on their way out. Chairs are gone from the nearest pods. Cushions have vanished from under desks. A puffer jacket that hung all week over the back of a task chair is missing its owner and not the chair. Noor takes Marcus's spare hoodie from the pod they had been using, then a wool coat from an abandoned workstation chair, then another office blanket that smells like somebody else's shampoo and old fear. She considers the blazer on the dead woman in the corridor and leaves it where it is. It would not help much and taking it off her would take both hands.

From farther down the hall comes the scrape of somebody dragging a cushion, then a low curse, then silence. Everyone left alive is solving the same equation without speaking it.

Noor stops once by her old pod and looks in. The floor still holds the imprint where she slept. A dead phone charger lies by the wall outlet. A legal pad has gone damp along one edge from the cold. None of it matters enough to spend another trip on. She takes the charger anyway because she cannot quite make herself leave the room empty.

On the way back she passes Min again. The corridor light catches the blood dried from her ear into her hair. Kwame's open ear has turned darker at the edges. Flies have already found the wetter parts of both bodies. Noor does not step around them as widely this time. Her arms are too full.

Downstairs, the cafeteria is turning into a barracks in plain sight.

The long tables have been shoved closer together to make sleeping lanes. Office chairs line the west wall with backpacks under them. Priya's terminal is live again, its graph casting a weak blue light across the floor. Dae-ho is stacking paper bowls and plastic spoons in counted rows beside the scale as if he can learn the job Lena handed him by arranging the tools into obedience. One of the enforcers has built a low wall of bottled-water cases near the loading-dock door. Rodney stands with the pipe across his knees and watches people choose spots.

Tomas finishes his warm meal while the rest of the room turns itself into managed scarcity.

Noor drops the extra layers beside Marcus and gets the hoodie over him by lifting his shoulders an inch at a time. He mumbles something she does not catch. When she leans closer he says, "Smells like a fucking swim meet in here."

"Ammonia."

"Yeah."

He drifts again before the word is fully out. Noor folds the wool coat under his legs, keeps the blanket for later, and sits with her back against a table leg because that puts her close enough to him and out of Lena's path.

Around the room the last movements of the day reduce to logistics. Priya tapes one cable to the floor so nobody trips over it. Dae-ho checks the scale battery and checks it again. Elise writes the watch names on the back of a catering invoice. Tomas rinses his thermal container at the sink and leaves it upside down to drain, as if there will obviously be a reason to use it again tomorrow. Nobody challenges him on it. That calculation has already been made and accepted.

At six fifty the stairwell door cycles its warning tone.

People move faster.

At six fifty-eight Noor sees one of the other survivors come in with an armload of seat cushions and not enough breath to say where he found them. At six fifty-nine Priya looks up from the terminal and says, "Now," to no one person in particular. At seven the magnetic lock catches with the familiar click-thunk and holds.

The second floor is finished.

By full dark the survivors are on cafeteria linoleum under borrowed jackets and office cushions, close enough for Lena to count at a glance. Above them, one whole floor of the building belongs only to the cold and the dead.
