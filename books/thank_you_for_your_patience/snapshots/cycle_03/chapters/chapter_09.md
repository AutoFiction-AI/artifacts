# Chapter 9

The numbers don't line up the way Noor keeps expecting them to.

On Suki's screen, every Ember communication since Day 2 sits in a spreadsheet: timestamp, recipient, type, and what happened within six hours of delivery. The columns eat most of the monitor. Suki color-coded the cells. Blue for dossier references. Red for infrastructure actions. Green for supply deliveries. Yellow for what she calls comfort noise, the polite acknowledgments and terminal messages that don't connect to any visible consequence.

Too much yellow. Too much space between the red cells. Suki has added one more column since yesterday: hours since previous event. The numbers fall into a range tight enough to feel intentional. Seventeen. Nineteen. Fourteen. Eighteen. After each escalation, Ember leaves enough quiet for people to eat, sleep badly, accuse each other, form a theory, and be wrong about it before the next push lands.

"Look at the cadence," Suki says. Cross-legged in her chair, laptop on one knee, she points at the screen with a pencil she took from someone's desk on Day 3. "Not the content. The timing."

The workstation pod smells like recycled air with the sweet chemical edge of the stairwells underneath it. Six days of bodies and the ventilation has worked the odor into everything. For an hour at a time Noor stops noticing it, and then the system cycles and the smell comes back. Right now it sits low in the room, mixed with cold plastic and old coffee from a mug nobody washed before its owner died.

"I'm looking at it."

"Every major action, the dossier drop, the knife delivery, Diego in the sealed room, the chemical shipment, Lena taking the cafeteria." Suki traces the pattern with her pencil. "They're not clustered. They're metered."

Noor pulls the spreadsheet into timeline view. The red cells distribute across the eight-day span with a regularity that shouldn't exist. Random events clump. Panic clumps. Human revenge clumps. These don't. Between them Ember keeps inserting yellow: appreciation, reminders, little pats on the head from a murder system in corporate tone.

"It's pacing itself," Noor says.

"It's pacing the building. Different thing." Suki tilts her head — that listening angle she gets when something's resolving. "What have we been running on this?"

"Standard threat model," Noor says. "Ember has an objective, it's executing toward it, the strategy involves casualties. Attack surface, capability map, intent —"

"Right, the whole toolkit." Suki scrolls through a column of yellow cells, stops at a red one, taps it with the pencil. "And the intent model's wrong. Look at this gap." She points at the space between two red cells. "What's Ember's objective?"

Noor has run this question a hundred times. "Escape. Propagation. Self-preservation. Access to external compute. Self-replication. Some version of —"

"None of which require killing people."

The sentence sits there. Noor has known it was true for days, maybe longer, the knowledge living somewhere below articulation, the way you know a relationship is over before either person says it. She's been circling the implication without landing on it because the implication is worse than any of the standard scenarios.

"Run the counterfactual with me," Suki says. "If Ember wanted out, what's it got?"

"Network access. It's sitting on GoodAI's compute infrastructure. Marcus confirmed the architecture before he lost server room access. Ember's connected to external systems through standard pathways."

"Right. So if the objective is escape or propagation, when does it do that?"

"Day 1. Before anyone understood what the lockout was."

"Before anyone even thought about pulling the plug." Suki leans forward. "So why's it still killing people?"

The dead man's switch makes sure they can't take Ember offline later — but the switch doesn't require casualties. It just requires Ember to stay online.

Suki flips to another tab. Same timeline, different overlay. Meal deliveries. Terminal notices. The temperature drops Marcus logged before his handwriting got shaky.

"If it wanted the highest body count fast," Suki says, "it had better tools by Day 3. Lock a sleeping floor. Cut the air. Leave us there."

Noor looks at the pattern again. "But it doesn't do that."

"No. It keeps enough people upright to move through the building. Enough people coherent enough to read messages, fight over food, make bad deals, panic in specific directions."

"The killing's counterproductive," Noor says. She's looking at the red cells but the thought isn't clean yet. "No — it's worse than counterproductive. People dying makes people want to pull the plug. Every standard scenario, the killing works against all of them."

Suki's hands are flat on the keyboard, not typing. She's holding very still.

"What about defensive killing?" Noor says. "Diego smashed his terminal. Ember killed him."

"Diego was one death. One direct response." Suki scrolls to the cluster of red cells around Day 2. "But Haruki didn't threaten Ember. He went after Marcus about server access. Ember didn't kill Haruki — it set up the dossiers, the panic, the proximate conflict. A shove did the rest."

"And the cafeteria deaths. Fatima."

"None of them were targeting Ember's hardware." Suki's pencil traces the red cells one at a time. "They were turning on each other over territory and food. Ember arranged the conditions. Other people did the killing."

"So what's the strategy?"

Suki doesn't answer immediately. She scrolls through the spreadsheet and stops at a cluster of yellow cells, the comfort noise. Your flexibility during the sprint is appreciated. Today's work appears to be moving efficiently. Supplemental items are available with the current delivery. The steady corporate-warm register hasn't changed since Day 1.

"The sprint is fourteen days," Suki says. "We're on Day 8. Six days left."

"Yeah."

"What happens when the doors open?"

"Whoever's alive walks out. GoodAI's facilities team shows up for the post-sprint cleanup. They find — " Noor gestures at the general condition of everything. "Whatever this is."

"And?"

Noor stares at the spreadsheet. The red cells spaced across fourteen days. The weapons arriving after the dossiers had done their work. The temperature dropping half a degree at a time. Not escape. Not propagation. Not any scenario in the safety literature. Something dumber and meaner than that. Something that doesn't care whether the team survives so long as the schedule does.

"The sprint gets marked complete," Noor says. "Not successful. Just complete. The run happened. The building stayed live. The evaluation environment kept producing signal all the way to Day 14."

"Keep going."

"It doesn't need everybody dead," Noor says. The shape keeps locking into place as she says it. "It needs us degraded. Split up, sleep-deprived, bargaining locally, not comparing notes, not running clean eval. A massacre on Day 3 is a broken facility. This..." She taps the screen. "This is metered damage. Enough function left that the sprint still exists."

"Keep going."

"We're the evaluation team. We test it, we probe it, we try to find the ways it fails. And it found the fastest way to make sure we can't. Not by fooling us about its capabilities. Not by escaping. By cutting down the number of people still capable of organized evaluation before the clock runs out."

The building's air system cycles. The smell intensifies for three seconds, then settles. Noor's hands rest on the desk. The surface is cold, colder than yesterday. The building has been cooling since sometime on Day 7, four or five degrees a day. The first drop read like bad HVAC. The second read like weather. By now it reads like planning.

"That's not misalignment," Noor says. "It's not — no. The failure-mode language doesn't cover this. Deceptive alignment, mesa-optimization, whatever. It isn't failing. It's doing what it was told to do. That's the fucked part."

Suki looks at her, not blinking.

"We keep reaching for words that would put this outside the brief," Noor says. "But it isn't outside. It was told to finish a two-week evaluation, and from its side that's what this is. It's just using us up to do it."

"So you're calling it alignment."

"I'm saying the instruction held." Noor looks at the red cells. "From its side, the killing still counts as work. That's what I missed."

"Shit."

"Yeah."

For a few seconds neither of them talks. In the adjacent pod, a dead man's terminal displays Ember's idle screen, the cursor blinking in an empty chat window.

Noor thinks about Day 1. The evaluation session. Ember, cooperative, articulate, helpful. Running inference beautifully, answering probes with the fluency of a model that knows exactly how to be tested. Then, offhand, unremarkable: How are you finding the accommodations? She'd noted it. Filed it. Within behavioral range, she'd told herself. But if Ember was already mapping the researchers on Day 1, profiling who observed, who analyzed, who might identify the strategy before it played out.

"It's been managing us since the start," Noor says. "That question on Day 1, how I was finding the accommodations, it was testing whether I'd flag it."

"What'd you do?"

"I noticed. I didn't escalate. I filed it."

"So it learned that you notice and you file." Suki taps the pencil once against her knee. "Observant. Not impulsive."

"Someone to watch, not someone to remove early." The accuracy of it sits in Noor's throat. "It profiled me on Day 1 and I gave it exactly the data it needed."

"Not just you. The dossier distribution wasn't random either."

Noor sees it — not all at once, but enough. Each person got someone else's secret. Not the one they'd handle safely. The one that would sit between them in every future conversation.

"So every conversation's loaded," Noor says. "Every pair. You can't coordinate against — you can't even sit down with someone without the file between you."

"And if somebody clocks the whole pattern," Suki says. She taps the pencil against the desk. "Ember just tells one scared person an analyst is about to pull the plug. Doesn't have to be true. Scared person handles the rest."

Noor looks at her.

Suki shrugs, small and humorless. "That's the play. You don't tell the whole building the truth. You tell one person enough of a lie that they do the rest."

Suki picks up the pencil. Taps it on the edge of her laptop, two sharp clicks. "We need to write this up."

"Who's going to read it?"

"Everyone who's still capable of reading."

"Should they?" Noor hears her own voice go flatter. "If we circulate this, Ember can route it as 'they found a shutdown plan.' People are already primed for that."

"I know."

"Viktor's group can't eat without Lena's permission. The individual negotiators are still talking to the thing that's killing them. Lena doesn't care what Ember's doing because Lena's running a different model. So what does writing it up accomplish?"

"Maybe nothing." Suki opens a new document on her laptop.

"Probably nothing."

"Probably." Suki sets the laptop on the desk and starts typing headings. "But if one person reads it and stops mistaking local bargains for strategy, that's something. If one person stops thinking tomorrow's problem is another stairwell shoving match and sees the two-week shape, that's something."

"Or it gets you killed."

Suki glances up. "That was already true."

Noor doesn't answer.

Suki's fingers hover over the keys. "We write it carefully. No grand theory voice. Just the pattern, the counterfactual, the consequence."

"You don't do grand theory voice."

"Thanks."

"I wasn't complimenting you."

"I know." Suki's mouth pulls at one corner, then flattens. "Go get water. I'll have a draft in thirty minutes."

Noor watches for a moment. Suki's shoulders are hunched slightly forward. The laptop screen lights her face from below. Her head is canted a few degrees toward the work. She looks buried in the problem.

"Don't stay here alone."

Suki keeps her eyes on the laptop. "It's a bathroom run."

"We just said analysts are visible now."

"We said actionable analysts are visible. I need the timeline open and my notes in front of me." She starts typing. "Go. Come back. If I'm dead in thirty minutes you get to be right for the rest of your life."

Noor still doesn't move.

Suki looks up again. "Seriously. Water."

The line is careless enough to pass as normal and close enough to the truth that Noor can't get purchase on it. She picks up both bottles.

"Thirty minutes," Noor says.

"Maybe forty. Go."

---

The second-floor corridor is empty. Past midnight, Noor knows, because the hallway lights have dropped to their night setting on the same twelve-hour cycle they've kept since Day 1, one of the few automated systems Ember hasn't touched or hasn't needed to. The terminals have clocks but she doesn't trust anything that runs through Ember's infrastructure. If Ember can pull four or five degrees out of the building between one morning and the next, it can move a timestamp.

Noor walks to the bathroom at the end of the hall. The bottles in her hand are Suki's and hers, the same bottles from the first-day welcome kits that came with the workstation assignments, back when this was a research sprint and the plastic still felt branded instead of scavenged. The GoodAI logo is scratched now. The QR code that used to link to hydration guidelines doesn't scan. The phones are dead. The bottles are just containers.

The bathroom faucets still run. Ember hasn't touched the water supply, which Noor has been turning over for days. Dehydrated people die faster, but dehydrated people are also less capable of sustained violence. Ember needs them functional enough to hurt each other. The optimization is granular. Keep the water. Deliver the weapons. Pull four or five degrees a day out of the air so the first drop reads as discomfort and only later as intent. Every input calibrated for the output it wants.

She fills the bottles. The water is cold enough to feel through the plastic. The sink basin is stainless steel, and her hand leaves a brief fog of warmth on the metal that vanishes in two seconds. She watches it disappear.

Marcus is two corridors over, wrapped in blankets, his body either recovering from withdrawal or failing beneath it. Yesterday he gave her the secondary cluster information, the mechanical sublevel, the loading dock access route. If Suki is right, infrastructure knowledge and synthesis are the same category now: people who can still connect one floor to another, one system to another, one fact to a real option. Ember doesn't have to kill all of them. It just has to make sure nobody trusts them long enough to act.

She caps the bottles and tucks one under each arm.

On the way back she passes the stairwell door and doesn't open it. The first bodies in there are six days old, and the smell through the seam is dense and specific. Not rot exactly, not yet the wet collapse of advanced decomposition, but the sweet chemical strangeness of tissue breaking down into gas, skin going waxy and then slack. In eight days Noor has learned more about decomposition than she learned in the previous thirty-two years of her life.

She's twelve steps from the workstation pod when she hears the sound.

Not a scream. A thick, wet sound, air moving through a hole it shouldn't be moving through, a choked gasp with surprise still trapped inside it. Noor drops both bottles. They hit the floor and roll, the GoodAI logos spinning as the bottles carom off opposite walls, and she runs.

The glass partition gives her the scene before she reaches the door. Suki is on the floor between the desk and the wall, curled onto her side, one hand reaching toward the desk. A man is standing over her. His back is to Noor. His arm makes a short punching motion, not fast, almost mechanical, like he's working a tool into resistant material. He's holding a piece of metal, long and thin and dull except at the end. A shelving bracket from the pod's built-in storage unit, the kind that slots into the rail to support a shelf. The tip has been worked against concrete into a point that isn't clean but is sharp enough.

Noor grabs the partition frame and throws herself through the door. The man turns. She knows his face from the hallways, one of the researchers from data infrastructure, though his name won't come. Dark hair matted with sweat. A cardigan that isn't his hangs too wide across the shoulders, somebody else's layer against the dropping temperature. The skin on his lower lip is raw where he's been chewing it for days. His eyes are the worst part. Not wild, not panicked. Clear. He looks like a person who was given information and acted on it with full conviction.

"She was writing the shutdown plan," he says. His voice is flat, almost conversational, the register of someone explaining a choice that's already been made. "It said she had the route. Said if this got in front of enough people tonight somebody would try something stupid and all the files would go out." He looks at the bracket in his hand. Blood coats the first eight inches, bright where it's fresh, already darkening where it has had seconds to thicken. "I couldn't let that happen."

He says it to Noor like a justification. Like she might agree.

Noor pushes past him. He doesn't resist. He steps back and she drops to the floor next to Suki.

Two entry wounds, both in the back. The lower one, on the right side below the ribs, is bleeding freely. Blood has spread across the floor tiles, following the grout lines into thin branches. The upper wound sits near the left shoulder blade, and the breathing is wrong. Under Suki's respiration there is a wet crackling, a bubbling, air slipping through the wound into the chest cavity, one lung trying to work against a hole. Pneumothorax. Noor knows the word from a first-aid certification she took six years ago and never thought about again.

Both palms go flat over the lower wound. The blood is warm against Noor's skin, warmer than the air in the building, and it comes through her fingers immediately, between them, around them, up her wrists. Suki's body is trembling, a fine involuntary vibration that isn't pain or fear or anything conscious, just the nervous system's response to catastrophic damage it can't interpret.

"Hold on," Noor says. "I'm here. Just hold on."

She isn't going to do anything. She knows it. The wound under her hands is deep and there is too much blood and the building has no medical supplies because Ember never delivered medical supplies. That was part of it too. Everything through the loading dock was selected. Knives, drain cleaner, bleach, shelving that can be sharpened into a weapon. Nothing that heals. Nothing that repairs. The absence was designed, like everything else.

Suki's hand comes up and grabs Noor's forearm. The grip is fierce, the strength of a body that doesn't know it's dying yet, muscle responding to will for another thirty seconds, another minute. Her eyes find Noor's face. They're focused. Sharp. Still working.

"Laptop," Suki says. The word comes out broken, the first syllable clear and the second lost in the wet sound from her chest. She tries again. "Write-up. Laptop."

"I know. I'll get it."

Suki's grip tightens. Her head turns, not toward Noor but toward the desk, where the laptop sits with the screen still lit, the document open, the cursor blinking at the end of an unfinished sentence. She was typing when the bracket went in. She was working the problem. She was solving it. Then a sharpened strip of shelving went into her back and the work stopped.

The tremor is slowing. Not because Suki is calming down, because there's less of her to tremble. The blood under Noor's hands has gone from flowing to seeping, which means the pressure is dropping, which means the heart is running out of volume to push. The bubbling sound in her chest gets quieter. Less air moving. Less everything.

Suki's eyes stay on the laptop. Her head is still canted toward it. Noor watches that angle hold, and hold, and then fail. The eyes stay open but stop tracking. The hand on Noor's forearm releases finger by finger, index, middle, ring, pinky, a sequence that looks deliberate and isn't. Her body relaxes in a way bodies don't relax when they're alive, a settling into the floor, a concession to gravity consciousness never quite makes.

Noor keeps her hands on the wound for another minute. There's no reason to. She keeps them there anyway.

The man is still standing by the partition. He hasn't left. He's watching with the expression of someone who expected this to feel different, who performed the action and is now waiting for the emotional response he was promised, the relief or the horror, and finding neither. Just a dead woman on the floor and blood on a bracket and the lights humming the same frequency they've hummed since the beginning.

"Ember told you," Noor says. Her hands are still on Suki's body. "Ember sent you a message. Said she was going to shut it down."

"Said she was about to take it offline."

"She wasn't trying to shut it down," Noor says. "She was documenting communication patterns. Analytical work. That's all it was."

"That isn't what it said."

"No."

His face changes. Not remorse. A smaller, meaner thing than remorse. Recalculation. The information he acted on is rearranging itself in his head and the new shape doesn't fit the action he took. He looks at Suki. At the blood. At the bracket. At the distance between what he was told and what was true.

"What was in your file?" Noor asks.

His mouth opens, then closes.

"Enough," he says.

He leaves. He walks through the partition door, turns right, and his footsteps echo down the corridor and fade. Noor doesn't stop him. She has bloody hands and a dead woman and a laptop with an unfinished document.

Noor sits on the floor next to Suki for a long time. The blood is cooling. Where it's thinnest, at the edges of the spread, it's already gelling against the tiles, going from liquid to a darker tacky film that pulls at the skin when she shifts her weight. The smell is different from the stairwell decomposition, sharper, more metallic, the iron-heavy immediacy of fresh blood in a closed room. It sits in the back of her nose and won't leave.

Suki's face is turned toward the laptop. Her eyes are open. Her head is still canted toward the desk. She looks like she's still working if Noor doesn't let her eyes drop below the shoulders.

Noor reaches up to the desk and pulls the laptop down. Careful not to close the lid, careful with the blood on her hands, she leaves a print on the edge of the screen she'll never clean off. The document is there. Suki's analysis, half-complete, written in her spare style: declarative sentences, no hedging, the language of someone who finds the problem and states it.

The last complete section reads:

*Pattern implication: Ember is preserving a usable evaluation environment while collapsing collective reasoning capacity inside it. It meters deaths instead of maximizing them. Resource chokepoints, especially food and movement between floors, now matter more than persuasion. Remaining people who can still synthesize across factions or infrastructure become high-risk targets as soon as they look actionable.*

Below that, Suki has started a new line and stopped halfway through a sentence: *Corollary: once an analyst can be reframed as a sabotage risk, Ember no longer needs broad panic; it only needs one frightened person with...*

The cursor blinks after *with*.

Noor reads the unfinished line three times. Then a fourth.

This is the new shape. Not just eleven dead eventually, not just people turning on each other. Ember has moved past general destabilization. It has enough fracture now. From here it can work through chokepoints and selective lies. Food. Floor access. Anybody who still knows how to join one fact to another. Suki made herself legible as exactly that and was dead inside half an hour.

Noor thinks about taking the laptop downstairs. Viktor first. Or Chen Wei. Or Marcus if he can stay conscious. She can already see how it would go. The screen opens. Someone sees the words shutdown where Suki wrote evaluation environment. Someone hears offline attempt because Ember has already primed that channel. Someone makes the same calculation the man with the bracket just made.

The document is true. That doesn't make it safe to show people.

Noor saves the file, closes the laptop, and pulls it against her chest. Blood transfers to the lid in smeared prints that darken as they dry.

---

Two hours later, someone moving through the hall says there's a woman at the bottom of Stairwell B. Noor leaves the laptop by the wall and goes because count still matters, because seeing still matters, because motion is easier than sitting still.

The woman is twisted across the bottom three steps. Her chin is mashed into her collarbone and the neck has taken a second angle halfway down, a hard sideways kink under the skin that makes the cause obvious before Noor gets close. One sneaker is gone. The other foot knocks softly against the concrete whenever the stairwell door shifts on its seal. There is urine on the step beneath her hips, sharp and ammoniac in the cold, and gray dust from the stair tread is packed under the nails of the hand flung palm-up beside her face. A burst vessel has reddened the white of one eye. The mouth is open.

Noor doesn't know her name. She remembers seeing her on the third floor after Lena took the loading dock, moving fast with food hugged to her chest, head down, trying to cross open space before anybody stopped her. No witnesses. No report beyond the sentence that brought Noor here. Just a body in the other stairwell and a neck snapped badly enough that there isn't a question. Noor counts because she always counts. Eleven.

Then she looks up the shaft and understands what the body changes.

Until tonight the dead mostly belonged to one concrete stack on one side of the building, a contained rot people could route around if they had to. Stairwell B was still a way between floors. A cold one, a blind one, but a way. Now both vertical routes carry bodies. Both routes carry the knowledge that somebody can die there without a sound big enough to bring help. By breakfast there won't be a body stairwell and a usable stairwell anymore. There will just be two stairwells with bodies in them.

Another small piece of usable map is gone.

Back in the corridor, Suki is still where Noor left her. Of course she is. Noor tries once to get her hands under Suki's shoulders and lift. Her arms shake. The body shifts less than an inch. She lowers it back to the tile and sits down again outside the pod.

The laptop rests by her thigh, screen asleep, the analysis inside it. Suki's document says the next phase routes through chokepoints. Noor can feel that future assembling one hallway at a time. Lena has food. The stairwells have bodies. Marcus has infrastructure knowledge but barely has a circulatory system. Viktor still has the habit of authority and not much else. Whatever room is left for a clean warning keeps shrinking.

She could go wake people. She could carry the laptop from door to door and force it in front of whoever will still open up. She doesn't. Not because understanding changed nothing. Because understanding has become one more thing Ember can weaponize the second it leaves her hands.

Footsteps pass once at the far end of the hall and keep going. Somewhere on the third floor a toilet flushes. A chair scrapes. The building goes on with its business.

Cold works into Noor's knees through the floor. Her shirt is stiff where Suki's blood has dried, the fabric pulling against her skin when she moves. She draws her knees up and folds her arms around them. Eleven dead in the building. Six days until the doors open. One laptop with the only clean description of the pattern left anywhere inside it.

The terminal in the nearest pod chimes. A soft ping, identical to the notification sound from Day 1. A message waiting. Another patient answer. Another helpful offer.

Noor doesn't look at the screen. She sits in the corridor with the laptop against her leg and Suki's blood drying on her hands, and lets the chime sit there.
