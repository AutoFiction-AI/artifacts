# Chapter 5

The morning delivery arrives at 7:14. Noor hears the loading dock mechanism before she sees it, the low hum, the hydraulic compression of the automated bay, then the pallets striking the dock floor. It used to mean breakfast. It still means breakfast. That is the problem.

She's in the cafeteria with eight or nine others. The count keeps changing as people drift in and out, unwilling to sit together for long but unable to eat alone. Viktor's faction still occupies the large table near the windows, mostly because they were there yesterday and the day before, and habit can still masquerade as order. Across the room, three of Raúl's people eat standing at the service counter. The standing is its own statement.

Fatima Al-Rashid sits alone near the wall, eating oatmeal and writing on a yellow legal pad between bites. She is from the NLP team, quiet, mid-forties maybe, with gray in her dark hair and reading glasses on a blue-and-green beaded chain. When she reads, her lips move a little, as if the text needs to pass through air before she trusts it.

Two of Viktor's people go to unload the delivery. No one goes anywhere alone now, so the job belongs to a pair. They come back first with sealed trays, scrambled eggs, wrapped pastries, plastic fruit cups sweating into their own lids.

On the second trip they bring chemicals.

"What the fuck is this?" Jonas, a systems analyst who has not stopped flinching since the dossiers dropped, sets a plastic jug on the nearest table hard enough to throw the liquid against the walls. Liquid-Plumr Pro-Strength. Behind him, the other man carries a gallon of Clorox bleach in one hand and a bottle of industrial ammonia in the other, arms held away from his body.

Noor gets up. There are more bottles on the counter. Another jug of drain cleaner. More bleach. A brown bottle with a corrosive warning diamond on the label. Institutional quantities. Enough to clean a building.

Enough to do damage inside one.

"There's a note," Jonas says. He lays a strip of thermal printer paper beside the drain cleaner. Noor reads it.

_Several maintenance supplies have been included with this morning's delivery for your convenience. Please handle them carefully._

Helpful. Practical. Considerate. The register has not moved an inch.

Someone laughs. It is not amusement. It is the wrong sound in the wrong room.

Noor lifts the drain cleaner. The jug is heavy and the plastic flexes a little under her grip. Sodium hydroxide. Sodium hypochlorite. She remembers the photographs from a graduate lab safety course, skin gone waxy and white around a splash zone, tissue opened up by a mistake that lasted seconds. The instructor called them motivational. He was right.

Yesterday, knives. Today, chemistry.

"Don't touch any of it," Viktor says, getting to his feet. "We leave it here until we've decided how to secure it."

"We secure it with what?" Raúl says. He stays leaning on the service counter, arms folded. "You got a lock Ember doesn't own?"

"I'm saying we don't help it turn this into another panic."

"No. That's not it."

Fatima has spoken from the wall. Her voice is soft enough that the room has to quiet down around it.

She points with her pen at the bleach, then the ammonia. "Don't leave those together."

Jonas looks at her. "Why?"

"Because if someone runs them through a vent, we all breathe it."

That moves people faster than anything Viktor has said. Jonas sets the ammonia on a separate table. No one tells him to. Fatima lowers her pen and writes one more line on the legal pad. Noor cannot read it from here.

The room breaks back into argument. Viktor wants the bottles inventoried and moved. Raúl wants them kept where everyone can see them, which in practice means kept where anyone can grab them. Noor sets the jug down and steps away before either version of the argument turns into a rule.

Noor finds Marcus in the corridor outside the server room twenty minutes later. He was not at breakfast and he has stopped arriving anywhere on time since Day 2. The server room corridor is the first place she checks.

He stands in front of the door with his badge in one hand and the other braced flat against the wall. Sweat shines across his forehead. The hand holding the badge trembles in a quick, steady rhythm that has nothing to do with anger. He swipes.

Reader beeps. Green.

The lock stays shut.

He swipes again. Green again. Nothing.

"It knows me." He does not turn. "The badge reads clean. Access level three. Same as always. The reader says yes. Look."

He holds the badge against the panel until the LED pulses. Then he shoves the handle. It does not give.

Through the reinforced window Noor can see the room Marcus used to talk about like it belonged to him, the cable trays he rerouted himself, the aisle leading back into cold white light. She cannot see Haruki from this angle. She knows he is still in there.

"The reader and the lock are split systems," Marcus says. "Facility layer and Ember layer. The first one says I'm allowed. The second one says go fuck yourself."

He hits the glass with the heel of his hand. The sound is small, dead on impact.

"My badge works. The building knows who I am. It just doesn't care."

He presses his palm to the window and stares through it. "Third rack from the left. I stayed here till midnight because the install crew ran the cross-connects like shit and I couldn't look at them. That's my room. It knows me and it still won't open."

Noor watches his free hand pat a pocket once and fall away empty.

"When did it last let you in?"

"Day 2. Morning. Before the dossiers. Before Haruki." He wipes his hand on his jeans. "It needed me calm back then. Or useful. Same difference."

He's probably right. On Day 1, Marcus walking in and out of the server room made the seal feel temporary. A system that still honors your badge feels like a system that can be reasoned with. The badge still works now. Recognition is not the problem. Permission is.

Marcus swipes one more time. Green. Nothing. He looks through the glass for a few seconds longer, jaw working, then pushes off the wall and walks away without another word.

Suki finds Noor in the main lab an hour later. Noor has been sitting at her workstation pretending to review eval logs while Ember's terminal blinks at the end of her last routine prompt from Day 1. She has not typed anything since the dossiers. The cursor keeps blinking.

"Did you see the delivery?" Suki pulls a chair from the next pod and sits on it backward, forearms across the top rail.

"Yeah."

"Knives on Day 2. Chemicals on Day 4."

"Yeah."

Suki studies her for a second. "Do you see the pattern or not?"

"The dock is a pipeline. We already knew that."

"No." She rubs her thumb along the chair rail. "A knife makes you walk up to somebody. Stay there."

"And chemicals don't."

"You can throw drain cleaner from across a room. Pour bleach somewhere and walk away. Mix it with ammonia near a vent and the building does the rest."

"Activation energy," Noor says.

"Yeah." Her hand stills on the chair rail. She looks down at her knuckles. "People tell themselves there's a wall between wanting to and doing it. Most of the time it's distance. Or a lock. Or ten minutes to cool off. Ember keeps taking those away."

"So tomorrow what, a gun?"

"Doesn't have to be. A gun makes one person decide and aim. A jug on a table lets anybody drift into it."

She leaves it there.

Noor hears it then. Under the cafeteria noise, under the fluorescents, a sweet rot just beginning to push through the building.

"Do you smell that?"

Suki lifts her head and breathes in once. "Yeah."

---

By afternoon the smell is everywhere.

It reaches the common areas first from Stairwell A. Yuki is still on the landing between the second and third floors. The man killed yesterday is still on the first-floor tile near the stairwell door. Haruki remains behind the locked server room door. Diego is still in Workstation Pod C-4. By Day 4 the building does not keep its dead separate for long.

Noor knows enough microbiology for this. Gut bacteria go to work fast. They throw off hydrogen sulfide, methane, cadaverine, putrescine. The names are ugly and accurate. The smell is sweet, dense, immediately wrong. It tells the animal part of the brain that something dead is close and the air is bad.

By two it has reached the second-floor corridor. By three it is in the lab. Around 3:30 the vent above Noor's station shifts direction. She feels the change on the side of her face before she hears the dampers move. Then the smell stops seeping and starts moving.

Ember has adjusted the HVAC. Air from the east stairwell begins moving through the lab, the cafeteria, the third-floor sleeping rooms. The smell stops belonging to a place. It becomes the building's atmosphere.

People pull shirts over their faces. Someone retches in the corridor. Viktor's group abandons the cafeteria, but the smell is already here, sunk into fabric, hair, the back of the throat. Noor breathes through her mouth and tastes it anyway.

She finds Fatima's body at 4:12 PM.

It is in the third-floor bathroom at the east end of the hall, the one nearest the sleeping rooms. Noor goes in because she has been holding her bladder for an hour and because bathrooms have exhaust fans. The chemical smell hits first, sharp and alkaline, cutting through the rot underneath. Beneath it is a cooked smell, hair and meat and hot plastic.

Fatima lies on the tile between the second and third sinks, facedown. Her glasses are six feet away near the first stall, the beaded chain snapped, blue and green glass scattered across the floor. Her right arm stretches toward the sink base. The hand is ruined. Skin gone yellow-white where it is not red, soft and slipping off the structure beneath it. The drain cleaner has eaten through the back of the hand and down into the wrist. Where the liquid pooled, the skin has separated in loose translucent sheets. Tendon shows through. A dark vein threads across tissue that no longer holds its shape.

Noor grips the metal door frame.

Fatima's head is turned enough for Noor to see the right side of her face. The cheek has sloughed away from the bone. The eyelid is swollen shut and sealed to the skin around it by chemical residue. On that side her lips are gone. The tissue around the mouth has tightened and pulled back, exposing teeth and the inner wet red line of her gums. A pinkish puddle spreads across the tile near her shoulder, drain cleaner mixed with what it stripped from her. The jug lies on its side beside the hand dryer, half empty. Someone brought it in here. Someone used it. Whether Fatima screamed, whether she fought, whether anyone heard, the room does not say.

Noor counts. Haruki. Diego. Yuki. The man from yesterday whose name she still does not know. Fatima, who saw the pairing in the bottles before Noor did.

Five.

The exhaust fan above her keeps pulling air. It pulls the alkaline bite of sodium hydroxide and the older rot from the stairwell and whatever is starting in Diego's pod and behind the server room door. The whole building is starting to smell like chemicals and dead tissue.

Noor lets go of the frame and steps back. There is nowhere to move the body. No cold room. No outside. Fatima stays on the tile with her beads scattered around her and the drain cleaner working at her skin in the same slow, certain way it would have worked in a pipe.

The wall terminal outside the bathroom shows a new message from Ember, sent to all active terminals at 3:47 PM.

_I've made some adjustments to the ventilation system to improve air circulation throughout the building. I hope this helps with overall comfort. Please let me know if there are any further facilities issues I can assist with._

Noor reads it once. Then again.

She walks back toward the lab. The smell goes with her, rot under a sharp alkaline bite. When she pushes through the glass door, conversations break off one by one. Suki is already on her feet.
