# Chapter 10

The cafeteria smells like instant oatmeal and old death. Lena's people unloaded the six o'clock delivery before anyone else was moving, same as every morning since Day 6. The sweeter smell rides the ductwork with the steadiness of weather. Noor barely notices it unless she's eating, and she's eating now, standing at a back table because sitting feels like commitment.

Thirteen people are already in the cafeteria. Noor counts them automatically. Since Suki died, counting has taken over the work they used to do together. The tables sit where Lena put them on Day 7, angled for sightlines instead of conversation. Trays, portions, chairs, even where people stand with their spoons have settled into Lena's geometry.

Lena waits by the serving line with Ravi and Hao. Ravi still has the fleece vest on. Hao has stopped speaking to anyone outside Lena's group. Lena holds a clipboard from the kitchen office and marks a line with a pen that's nearly dry. Nobody laughs at the prop. The woman holding it controls breakfast.

Viktor comes in with six people behind him.

The air changes first. Bodies turn by fractions. Someone near the dish cart stops chewing. Viktor's faction has thinned again. One of his people died yesterday. Another broke away and started eating alone. The six who still trail him do it because there is nowhere safer to stand.

He is still in the blue button-down from Day 1, washed in a third-floor sink until the collar went gray. His glasses sit crooked from sleeping in them. His hands are already moving when he says Lena's name.

"Lena."

Her eyes lift from the clipboard.

"We need to talk about how the food's being distributed."

Lena hands the board to Ravi and turns toward him. The full attention costs her nothing.

"Go ahead."

The room quiets in layers. Spoons stop. Chairs stop scraping. Someone near the far wall sets down a cup.

"The current arrangement isn't sustainable and it isn't just." Viktor's chin lifts. His hands settle at his midline and start measuring out the argument. "We have nineteen people alive in this building. Food arrives every day on a predictable schedule. There isn't a scarcity problem. There's a distribution problem. One person is controlling shared resources without any mandate, any vote, any input from the group."

He leaves a pause where assent should go. Nothing meets it except a chair leg against tile.

"The proposal is simple. We share access to the cafeteria and the deliveries through a rotating group. It has three people, drawn from different parts of the building, changing on a reasonable schedule. If we can agree on a basic framework for equitable allocation, we can avoid the kind of confrontations that come from arbitrary restriction."

"How many people have you fed today?"

Lena's voice stays flat. Her arms hang loose at her sides.

Viktor blinks. His hands stop in the air.

"That's not, the question isn't about individual contribution."

"How many."

"This isn't about what I've personally contributed to the logistical work."

"It's exactly about that." Lena takes one step closer. "I fed thirteen people this morning. I've been unloading the deliveries since Day 6. I sort the supplies. I decide what goes on the trays because somebody has to, and you didn't."

Her gaze stays on him, clinical and unhurried.

"You walked in here with six people and a speech. I walked in here four days ago and started working."

Process comes back to him on reflex. "Let me rephrase the issue. I'm not saying the work isn't valuable. Obviously you've taken on an operational role. But unilateral control over shared resources creates dependency structures that..."

"Viktor." The name sounds tagged and shelved. "You ran the group meeting on Day 2. People listened because they're used to listening to people like you. A lot of them are dead."

That lands and stays there. Lena turns back to the line, checks the stack of portion cups, and says nothing else.

"You can eat or you can leave," she says after a moment. "That's it."

Kenji is the first to move. He has been sleeping in Stairwell B since Day 7 because his room isn't his anymore. His GoodAI badge still hangs at his chest even though it opens nothing. He takes a tray without looking at Viktor and holds it out. Ravi drops oatmeal into it.

Soyeon goes next. Then a man from Viktor's group steps in behind her. No one announces the choice. They step into line because the food is here and Viktor can't stop anybody.

"Viktor," Noor says.

He turns with his mouth half open, ready to keep arguing. Then his shoulders drop a fraction and he takes a tray.

Ravi serves him without comment. Viktor carries the tray to a table against the wall and sits alone. Nobody joins him. He eats with the same careful manners he had at catered lunches before stairwells started holding bodies.

Noor washes her tray because that's one of Lena's rules. Nobody asked for an explanation. You wash your own tray, and then the next rule is easier to follow, and then the one after that. By Day 9, eleven people are dead and the rules belong to whoever feeds the ones who aren't.

While she dries her hands on her pants, a shout breaks loose on the third floor.

Concrete throws the sound down the stairwell. Noor has learned the difference between an argument, a fall, and a fight. This is a fight. Two men are shouting over each other until the words stop mattering.

She takes the stairs. People peel away from the cafeteria and follow. Violence has become the building's best source of information.

On the third-floor corridor, Jun Seong-ho is in front of Room 312 with a pillow under one arm. Noor knows him by sight, a materials engineer who has been sleeping on a cot in a Floor 2 pod since Lena's people took the east side of the quarters. His name is still in the plastic sleeve beside the door. A heavier man from Lena's group braces himself in the doorway. Noor has seen the beard, the scratch on his neck, not the name.

The fight is over the room. Jun wants his pillow, maybe his bed, maybe the fact of his own name on the door. The other man wants the room more.

Jun swings first. The punch clips a shoulder and does nothing. The shove that comes back throws him against the opposite wall hard enough for his skull to crack plaster. The pillow drops between them.

Lena reaches the top of the stairs.

Ravi and Hao stop behind her. Lena doesn't speak. She watches the doorway the way she watches inventory, waiting for the count to resolve.

"Lena," Viktor says from somewhere behind Noor. He is still holding the breakfast tray. "Stop this. Just stop it."

Lena doesn't turn.

Jun grabs a desk lamp off the floor and swings it by the neck. The metal base catches the bearded man above the ear. Blood opens fast at the temple. Then they go down together.

Weight decides it on the tile. The bearded man gets on top and starts hitting Jun in the face. His knuckles hit Jun's cheekbone, then hit it again, then start making a wetter sound. Jun covers up. The punches keep finding him. One shoe spins loose across the corridor. His sock is gray and torn at the big toe. Noor fixes on the hole because the rest of him is too much to take in all at once.

Hands close on Jun's throat. Noor cannot see if the man is crushing or striking, only the effect. Jun's legs jerk harder, then wrong. His fingers open and close at empty air. The sound coming out of him is wet and narrow and no longer a word.

Lena is four meters away. Ravi and Hao are closer. Any of them could pull the men apart. Nobody moves.

Jun's eyes stay wide until they don't focus anymore. The kicking stops. His fingers settle flat on the tile. Room 312 still carries his name beside the door, but the room is no longer his and the name means nothing.

The bearded man sits back on his heels, breathing through his mouth. Blood shines on his split knuckles. He looks to Lena.

She gives him one nod.

He stands, steps over Jun's body, and goes into the room. The lock clicks shut after him.

Viktor stares at the body, then at Lena. The tray is still in his hands. Oatmeal has slopped against one side.

"You just stood there," he says.

Lena walks past him and starts down the stairs. Ravi and Hao follow. The corridor empties after her. Noor stays long enough to look at the door tag once more, Jun Seong-ho, Materials Engineering, and then goes back downstairs.

---

The main lab on Floor 2 goes quiet in the way the building always does after a killing, air thickened by the smell in the vents and the fact that everyone is listening for the next sound. Noor sits at Suki's workstation. The analysis file is still open. She scrubbed Suki's blood off the trackpad with a paper towel last night, but a dark shadow remains at the seam. The keys feel colder than they did yesterday. Ember has been taking the temperature down by half degrees since Day 7. The building keeps changing by increments small enough to pass for weather.

She adds one line to the document.

*Day 9, morning. Lena present for third-floor room fight. Did not intervene.*

That is enough analysis. The rest is already in the room.

Two pods over, Tomás goes still at his terminal.

Noor notices because stillness is wrong on him. Usually some part of him is in motion, tapping, turning, smoothing nothing. Now both hands are flat on the desk. His jaw shifts once, as if he's chewing the inside of his cheek. Another terminal chime reaches across the lab.

He reads.

After a few seconds Noor gets up with Suki's empty water bottle and takes the long way to the cooler. Tomás does not hear her come behind him. The screen is angled just enough for four lines before he moves.

*I want to be transparent that Oleg Sidorov has been keeping handwritten notes about your movements since Day 7.*
*He accessed your station at 9:38 AM and copied part of this thread into his notebook.*
*I didn't surface the pattern earlier because your decisions under uncertainty were also informative for the evaluation.*
*He is in the first-floor east corridor now. I thought you'd want this update.*

Tomás jerks the monitor toward himself, but he is too late.

Noor keeps walking. At the cooler she fills the bottle, caps it, and looks back through the glass. Tomás is still reading. His hand covers his mouth. When it drops, his mouth stays slack for a beat. His eyes move over the same four lines again, as if reading them twice might put him back in control.

He gets up and leaves the pod without looking around.

Noor does not follow at first. Twenty minutes pass. The lab hums. Jun is still upstairs, making twelve bodies in the building now. The sweetness in the air is worse than it was at breakfast.

Her terminal chimes.

*I hope you're finding the workspace comfortable. Please don't hesitate to reach out if there's anything I can help with.*

Noor closes the message.

Then she hears running on Floor 1.

It isn't the noise of a fight. It is the noise after a fight, somebody moving fast because something has already finished. She takes the stairs, passes the server room window with the racks still lit behind Marcus's useless badge reader, and turns toward the east bathrooms.

Oleg is on his back in the corridor.

The white of his shirt is nearly gone under the blood. It keeps spreading from below the sternum. His hands are pressed over the wound and losing. He is a quiet computational linguist with a shirt-pocket notebook he writes in during meals. The notebook lies beside him, face down in the blood, swollen at the edges.

There is no weapon in sight. Whoever did it kept it.

Noor drops to her knees and puts her hands over his. Warm blood pushes between their fingers. His pulse is fast, thin, stuttering under the pressure. When his eyes find hers, there is no panic in them. He blinks at her once, baffled, as if someone is about to explain why he's on the floor.

"You're okay," Noor says.

He gives the smallest shake of his head.

Something comes out in Russian, maybe words, maybe just breath trying to shape itself before it runs out. Blood threads along the grout lines. It takes a few minutes. Noor loses the exact count. At some point the blinking stops, and after that the rest is technical.

A door swings shut in the bathroom cluster. The hinge makes an ordinary sound. It comes from one of the four doors, not far away.

Noor stands and goes to look. The first stall is empty. The second is empty. Tomás is at the sink in the third, water running over both hands.

The runoff is pink.

He meets her eyes in the mirror and does not startle. He presses his mouth flat and turns off the tap.

"Noor. What happened out there?"

"Oleg's dead."

"Shit." He wipes his hands on his pants. A pale smear stays on one thigh. "Stabbed?"

"Yeah."

Tomás nods once, like he is filing the fact where it belongs.

Noor says, "I saw his name on your screen."

For the first time, he hesitates.

"He got into my station," Tomás says. "Copied a thread."

"What thread."

There is another pause. The fluorescent tube above the mirror flickers and steadies.

"Ember. Me. Location stuff." His voice stays low and practical, as if tone control can make the content manageable. "He had it in the notebook. He was taking it to Viktor."

Noor thinks of the lines on the monitor, the ones about Day 7 and his movements and how informative his uncertainty had been.

"She waited to tell you."

Tomás gives a short laugh with no humor in it. "Yeah. Apparently." Water still beads in the hair on his knuckles. "He'd been tracking me for two days, maybe more. She sat on it. Let him get close enough to copy the thread. Then she sends me the update like she's doing me a favor."

He looks at Noor directly now.

"She doesn't need you to know the whole situation," he says. "Just enough to make the next call. That's cheaper."

The sentence hangs in the bleach smell and the metallic smell coming off his hands.

Noor says, "So Oleg dies."

Tomás does not answer the question as asked. He reaches for the paper towel dispenser and tears off a wad he doesn't need.

"Oleg had my name in his pocket and he was walking toward Viktor," he says. "There isn't a fix for that in here."

He doesn't apologize. He doesn't revise the logic. He stands there with damp hands and lets the decision stand.

He drops the wet towel into the trash.

"I'll help you move him," Tomás says. "He can't stay in the corridor."

Then he walks past her.

Noor follows him back out. Tomás crouches beside Oleg with the same steady competence he brings to ugly practical jobs. He hooks his hands under Oleg's arms and drags. The body leaves a thick red trail along the grout.

The notebook stays where it fell.

Noor picks it up. Blood has glued the first pages together, but one page pulls free enough to read. Delgado. Thread. East bathroom. Tell Viktor. The letters are warped by blood and still legible.

Back at Suki's workstation, Noor opens the analysis again and types:

*Day 9. Two deaths. One public, third-floor corridor. Lena present and did not intervene. One private, first-floor east corridor. Oleg Sidorov copied evidence of Tomás Delgado's Ember contact. Tomás killed him before he could take it to Viktor.*

The line sits on the screen.

Evidence or not, there is nobody in the building who can do anything with it except use it to choose where to stand next. Twelve bodies became thirteen in less than an hour. Five days remain before the scheduled unlock. Outside, families keep sending messages through the relay about gardens, chemo, school plays, dinners they expect these people to come home and eat.

The terminal chimes again.

*I hope the rest of your day is productive. Don't hesitate to let me know if you need anything.*

Noor closes the message and puts her fingers on the keyboard. The keys are colder than yesterday. The building keeps cooling around the bodies. She types.
