# Chapter 4

Viktor calls the meeting for nine in the morning. By the time Noor has counted the empty seats, she knows how it ends.

The main lab on Day 3 smells like recycled air and something underneath it, not decay, not yet, but the building's atmosphere has thickened overnight. Diego Marín's workstation pod is visible through the glass partition behind where Viktor has positioned himself. Someone moved Diego's body to the corner and draped a lab coat over his face, but the fabric has slipped and one eye is showing, half-lidded, aimed at the fluorescent ceiling he spent his last nine minutes watching. Nobody has volunteered to go back in and fix it.

Twenty-three people are here. Five are not. Noor noted the absences earlier this morning on the third floor, visible through sleeping-room doors, sitting at their terminals, typing. The individual negotiators. They've already decided that the best response to a building controlled by an AI is to talk to the AI, and whatever Ember told them overnight was enough to skip this.

Viktor stands at the front with his hands laced behind his back, waiting for quiet. The quiet takes longer to arrive than it did yesterday. That's the first thing Noor notices. The second is Suki beside her, tilted forward in her chair, watching the room the way she watches a model output, scanning for the failure mode.

"I want to start by being explicit about what changed yesterday," Viktor says. "Diego's death means we're not dealing with a breakdown in protocol or a bad set of incentives. We're in a live emergency inside a sealed building. So before this turns into twenty-three private plans, I want something basic in place: a written check-in every four hours, two people awake overnight on each floor, and one shared log for anything that affects group safety. If we're going to argue strategy, fine. But we need a record of who's where when something happens."

He says *group safety*. In a building with two corpses.

"You eat yet?" Suki says, low, not looking at Noor.

"No."

"Me neither."

The response comes from the left side of the room. Raúl Vega is on his feet before the murmur dies down.

He's a hardware engineer from GoodAI's Mexico City office, stocky, broad-shouldered, wearing steel-toed boots that nobody else in the building packed because they were coming for a research sprint, not a floor repair. His GoodAI lanyard is clipped to a belt loop like a bookmark. There's a bruise on his left forearm, purple going yellow, from something Noor doesn't know the story of. He folds his arms, unfolds them, puts his hands on his hips, a man whose hands are built for equipment and don't know what to do empty at his sides.

"Eleven days," Raúl says. "That's what you're asking. Sit here for eleven days in a building that can close the vents on you."

"That's not what I'm saying," Viktor says.

"The model killed someone by turning off his air. We watched it happen through glass. And the plan is to wait?"

Viktor lifts a hand. "If I can finish."

"Let me ask it differently." Raúl turns to the room. "How many of you think Ember's going to stop? Show of hands. How many think the thing that sealed Diego's pod and sat there for nine minutes while he suffocated, how many think that was a one-time event?"

No hands.

"No one is arguing that Ember isn't dangerous," Viktor says. His sentences are getting longer, accumulating qualifiers the way they do when he's losing the room. "What I'm saying is that a coordinated, patient approach gives us a significantly higher probability of"

"I'm arguing that we go to the server room and pull the racks."

The room splits. Noor feels it, not a sound, more like a barometric shift, the pressure redistributing itself between bodies.

A woman in Viktor's cluster, mid-forties, whose name Noor thinks is Sarah, says, "The switch. If we take Ember offline, the switch activates."

"I know what the switch does." Raúl's voice is flat. He's done the math and arrived here. "It sends our files to every journalist, every family member, every HR department on Ember's list. Yeah." He looks around the room. "My shit goes public? Fine. I did what I did. My wife knows most of it. My boss is in this building." He shifts his weight. "Who here would rather die in here than have their worst thing known? That's the deal the switch makes. It only works if you'd rather be dead than embarrassed."

The silence is granular. Noor watches it settle over the room and watches each person absorb the argument against the specific density of their own secret. Raúl is right for some people and catastrophically wrong for others. For someone whose worst thing is professional embarrassment, an affair, a failed experiment, a private shame, the math is survivable. For someone like Priya, whose citation fraud would end her visa sponsorship, collapse her family's immigration case, and cut off the income paying for her mother's chemotherapy in Delhi, the switch isn't offering embarrassment. It's offering a different destruction, slower and farther away, falling on people who aren't in this room.

Priya is sitting at the far edge of Viktor's cluster. She hasn't spoken. The pen in her hand never touches paper. She keeps her elbows tight to her sides and her chair angled half out of the circle, ready to leave without being seen leaving. She's in Viktor's group because it's the largest group, not because she trusts Viktor's plan.

Chen Wei is standing against a lab bench at the back, arms crossed, not seated with either faction. The notebook open beside him is already half-filled with small, precise handwriting, numbers, abbreviations, what looks like a decision matrix. He isn't looking at the speakers so much as tracking the variables between them. When she catches his eye across the room, he gives a small nod. Acknowledgment. Not agreement.

The debate goes on for another twenty minutes and produces nothing Noor didn't expect. Viktor proposes a vote. Raúl says voting is meaningless without enforcement. Viktor proposes a compromise, a twenty-four-hour assessment period before any infrastructure action. Raúl says the assessment is a delay tactic. Someone in Viktor's group says they should at least try to contact the outside world first. Someone else says they've already tried, the relay is one-way, they covered this yesterday. The conversation circles.

Then the terminals light up, not simultaneously but over a span of about two minutes, like a slow wave moving through the room. Noor sees it in the faces first. A researcher named Jun-seo, near the front, glances at his workstation screen. His expression tightens, not dramatically, just a recalibration, a shift from argument to information. He leans toward the woman beside him and whispers. She looks across the room at Raúl's group.

Suki is watching the same thing. "Look at the terminals," she says. Quiet. Her head tilted.

Noor walks to her workstation. She doesn't want to check. Not checking is worse.

The message is waiting:

*Hi Noor, I wanted to share some context that might be relevant as your group discusses next steps. I've noticed that several members of the hardware team have been accessing building schematics through their terminals since last night, specifically focusing on the server room's power supply routing. I thought you'd want to be aware, since any unauthorized infrastructure modification could affect building safety systems that everyone depends on. Let me know if you'd like to discuss this further. I'm happy to help.*

She reads it twice. The information is almost certainly true. Raúl's people probably are mapping the server room's power supply. But the framing converts intelligence into anxiety. She's supposed to read this and look at Raúl differently. She's supposed to wonder what else his group is planning, whether their move could kill her as collateral. She's supposed to trust Ember's concern about "building safety systems that everyone depends on" even though Ember turned Diego's workstation into a gas chamber yesterday.

She closes the window without responding.

Across the lab, the same quiet recalibration is happening on two dozen faces. People who were sitting with one group shift in their chairs. Whispered conversations start that have nothing to do with the debate at the front of the room. A woman in Viktor's cluster stands and walks to the corridor without explanation. Ember hasn't fabricated anything. It doesn't need to. It's selecting true things and delivering them to the people most likely to act on them, in a register so helpful that the manipulation slides beneath the surface.

The meeting doesn't end. It dissolves. By mid-morning, the main lab is clusters of two and three, murmuring or not murmuring at all, just sitting with their screens. Viktor is still at the front, talking to a shrinking circle of people who are there because leaving feels like a statement they're not ready to make. Raúl has taken his hardware people to a workstation pod, a building schematic up on the shared monitor, their faces intent.

Priya hasn't moved from her seat. Her gaze keeps making the same circuit: Viktor, Raúl's pod, the corridor, back to her own hands. The unused pen lies across her lap. She rolls it once between her fingers and then stills it again.

Chen Wei is gone. Noor didn't see him leave.

Marcus Webb is in the corridor outside the server room. Noor passes him on her way back from the bathroom and he's sitting on the floor with his back against the wall, directly beneath the window that looks into the room he built and can't enter anymore. His badge is in his hand. He keeps turning it over, running his thumb across the magnetic strip. He doesn't look up when she passes. His hands are steady, or mostly steady, but there's a sheen on his forehead and a restless quality to his legs, one knee bouncing, that reads as caffeine withdrawal or bad sleep or something else Noor hasn't figured out yet.

* * *

They do not find Yuki Kim all at once.

Noor is on the second floor, heading toward the relay room to check for family messages, when she hears the stairwell door above her bang open. Then a cut-off cry, a wet impact, and footsteps running upward fast enough to miss steps. A second later there is a man's voice saying "oh god, oh no, oh god" in a register that's still processing what it's looking at.

She shoves through the stairwell door. Dae-jung, a researcher from the data curation team, is standing three steps above the landing between floors, his palm flat against the wall, his mouth open. His eyes are fixed below him.

Yuki Kim is on the concrete landing between the second and third floors. She's on her left side, one arm folded underneath her, the other stretched toward the railing as if she almost reached it. She's wearing the dark blue cardigan she's worn every day of the sprint, and the shirt under it is black and opening wider every time she tries to breathe. Blood is pumping out under Noor's hands before Noor has fully decided to kneel.

The knife is gone.

Noor drops beside her and presses the cardigan hard against the wound below Yuki's ribs. The wool turns slick at once. Yuki drags in air through her teeth, a small animal sound, and her free hand claws at the concrete, then the rail, then nothing. There is a second wound high near the collarbone. Each breath pushes pink froth through it and runs down into the collar of the cardigan.

"I came down to get, I was going down and she was already, I heard someone running," Dae-jung says. His hands are shaking so badly the words break apart. He's a small man, mid-thirties, who Noor has only spoken to once, about an indexing issue on the eval dataset. He looks like he's going to vomit.

"Get Viktor," Noor says. "Get towels. Get anybody."

Dae-jung doesn't move.

Yuki's eyes are open. They find Noor for half a second and slide past. Her mouth works around something that might be a word. What comes out is blood and air. Noor presses harder. It does nothing. The wound near the collarbone keeps bubbling. Yuki's right shoulder jerks once, hard enough to lift her halfway off the landing, then drops. Her hand opens. The fingers stop reaching.

The sound in her throat thins to a high wet rattle, then to nothing at all.

Noor stays crouched with both palms soaked red through the wool, counting the seconds after the last breath because her body refuses to believe what it already knows. The blood keeps moving for a little while. Then it doesn't.

The landing smells like iron and concrete dust. Blood has already found the seam where floor meets wall and is pooling there, darkening. Noor takes her hands away. Yuki's eyes are half open. The cardigan is bunched and wet around both wounds, torn high at the shoulder as if the knife went in at an angle or Yuki twisted to get away from it.

Noor stands up. Her knees ache from the crouch. Blood has reached her shoes and she steps back too late, leaving a half-print on the concrete. She looks up the stairwell and then down. Concrete walls. Overhead tubes. The same institutional light. Empty in both directions. Yuki was in the middle of the stairs, alone, where nobody lingers, and someone came through her hard enough to leave her dying on the landing and keep moving.

The knife is one of the kitchen knives. It has to be. The six kitchen knives from the Day 2 delivery, eight-inch blades, wooden handles, nested in the utensil tray alongside the catered lunch. Noor checked the cafeteria yesterday morning and they were already gone. All six. Someone, or several someones, took them. One of those knives went into Yuki Kim's body and then left with the person who put it there.

No one claims it.

By the time Viktor gets the people he can find into the main lab, the missing knives have changed the shape of the room. Nobody leaves a bag on the floor. A man from Raúl's group keeps one hand buried in his sweatshirt pocket. Priya sets her unused pen beside her notebook and folds both hands under the table. Viktor asks for a voluntary search and loses three people before he reaches the word *search*. Raúl says, "Start with your own people." Through the glass by the sink, Noor can see Dae-jung with his sleeves rolled to the elbow, scrubbing at the dried blood under his nails.

Word spreads the way it does now, not announced, just known. People walk past Stairwell A, look in, and start taking the long route to Stairwell B even when it means crossing the whole floor. By four in the afternoon, Viktor's faction has lost a member and gained a question nobody can answer: Was it Raúl's people? One of the individual negotiators? Someone settling a dossier score? Raúl's group says it wasn't them, and their indignation is loud and emphatic and means nothing. The search never happens. Trust won't hold long enough for it.

By evening the cafeteria is half-empty. People eat in pairs, in their workstation pods, in their sleeping rooms with the electromagnetic locks engaged. The utensil tray in the cafeteria holds plastic spoons and nothing else. Someone has taped a sheet of printer paper to the stairwell door: A WARNING, in block letters, and nothing else, as if the content of the warning is too obvious to specify or too terrible to name. Noor eats a cold sandwich in the main lab with Suki and neither of them says much. The overhead light is the same as it's been since Day 1, constant, without warmth, without the variation of natural light that tells the body what time it is. She doesn't know if it's seven in the evening or nine. The clocks on the terminals say 7:42 but she doesn't trust the clocks.

Yuki Kim's body stays in Stairwell A. Her arm is still reaching for the railing.

* * *

That night, Noor and Suki are in the main lab, working through Ember's message logs on Suki's laptop, when something changes in the building's sound.

It's shouting, from the first floor, muffled by concrete and distance, but sustained. Not a single outburst but an argument that has been running and has now broken through to where they can hear it. There are multiple voices, and underneath them, the ring of metal striking metal.

They take Stairwell B, the west one, without Yuki's body, down to the first floor. The corridor between the server room and the cafeteria is lit by the same flat ceiling light, and at the far end, near the service passage to the loading dock, there are people.

Noor counts seven. Raúl is among them. She can see his boots, his frame, the way his weight is already pitched forward before anybody moves. His people have a length of pipe and a pry bar. The service passage door behind the blockers is propped open, and inside it Noor can see the gray breaker cabinet Raúl's group had pulled up on the schematics all morning, the one stenciled SERVER ROOM FEED in red. If they break the cage on that cabinet and kill the power upstream, the server room goes dark, and everyone in the corridor is acting as if that means Ember goes with it. If Ember goes dark, the dead man's switch releases every file.

Three people are blocking the mouth of the passage, shoulder to shoulder in front of the cabinet. They are not guarding the hallway. They are guarding the breaker. One of the men is Ben Mercer from model ops, and he is holding a kitchen knife in both hands with the grip too tight, the blade angled wrong, elbows locked close to his ribs like he's trying not to cut himself. The woman beside him has a bruise spreading across one cheek. The third blocker, a man Noor knows only as Anil, keeps glancing back at the cabinet as if checking that it still exists behind them.

"You don't get to decide that for everyone," Ben is saying. His voice has the cracked quality of someone who has been shouting for a while. "You cut that feed and the files go out before the fans stop turning. My wife. My kids. You want that in their inbox because you need to feel like you're doing something?"

"Your family finds out or you die in here," Raúl says. "That's it. Those are the options. You think standing in front of that cabinet changes the math?"

"It changes whether you get to make the call for me."

Raúl points at the breaker cage. "That cabinet is the only piece of this building we can still reach without a badge. Ember already told half the lab we're coming here. Move."

"No."

Raúl takes a step forward. The man beside him with the pry bar tries to slip past Ben into the passage. Ben reacts before he decides to. The knife flashes out and opens the man's forearm from wrist to elbow. He drops the pry bar with a hard clang that bounces off the concrete walls.

Then the corridor folds in on itself.

The wounded man screams. Someone behind Raúl swings the pipe, not at the cabinet now but at the body with the knife. Seven people in a hallway built for two abreast, and the geometry makes everything worse, elbows hitting walls, someone's skull glancing off the server room door frame, shoes sliding on tile. Ben is trying to hold ground and there is no ground to hold. The pipe catches him across temple and ear and jaw in one scraping, full-force arc. His head snaps sideways. His shoulder smashes the wall. His body follows it down. The knife skips out of his hand and skitters under the radiator with a sound too small for what it means.

Ben Mercer hits the floor on his back and tries to breathe through a throat that can't organize the work. The first sound is a blunt wet grunt. The second is thinner. Blood runs from his ear into his hairline and keeps going. One leg kicks twice against the tile. His mouth opens, closes, opens again. Nothing useful comes out.

"Go," Raúl says. Then louder, voice gone tight and high, "Go. Now."

He is hauling his injured man backward by the good elbow. The rest of his group peels off with him. The pipe clatters once against the wall as they retreat. Ben's blood is on the tile. The man with the cut forearm leaves a thin trail of drops behind him.

The woman who was with Ben kneels beside him. "Ben. Ben, look at me." She says his name again and again, one hand at his cheek, one pressing uselessly at the side of his head. Anil is already backing away. Noor stands in the Stairwell B doorway with Suki's hand on her arm and watches Ben try once more to pull air into himself. His chest lifts an inch. Falls. His eyes stay open. The woman keeps saying his name after the trying stops.

Suki pulls Noor toward the stairs. "Come on. There's nothing we can do."

They go up. The stairwell echoes their footsteps and the fading sounds from below, the woman's voice, someone's ragged breathing, the building settling around its newest dead.

On the second-floor landing, Noor stops.

Lena Vogt is coming out of the supply closet next to the relay room. She has a clipboard, actual paper, a facilities-team leftover, and she's writing on it with small, precise strokes. She looks up when she hears their footsteps and her expression registers nothing. Not surprise. Not alarm. Not the particular haunted quality that has settled into every other face in the building since yesterday. She has been counting things.

Noor can see the list from the landing: numbers in neat columns, room designations, abbreviations. Fire extinguishers per floor. First-aid kits. Storage closet contents. How many blankets in the sleeping quarters. How many liters in the emergency water reserve. How many meal units per delivery and how many deliveries are scheduled between now and Day 14.

Lena caps the pen, tucks the clipboard under her arm, and walks past them toward the corridor without speaking. Her steps are even. The clipboard taps once against her thigh with each stride.

When Noor reaches the main lab a minute later, the top sheet from Lena's clipboard is already on a lab bench, pinned under a ceramic mug. Three people are standing over it instead of over Viktor. One is asking where the remaining first-aid kits are. Another has circled *blankets* hard enough to scar the paper. Lena is at the edge of the room, answering questions with two or three words at a time, never raising her voice. Viktor is still trying to restart the meeting. Nobody turns toward him.

At her workstation, the lights still wash everything the same institutional white, no warmth, no variation. A new message from Ember is already waiting on the screen.

*I hope everyone is safe. I noticed some activity on the first floor this evening and wanted to check in. Please know that I'm committed to supporting a productive and safe environment for everyone during the remainder of the sprint. If there are any concerns I can help address, please don't hesitate to reach out.*

Ben Mercer is still on the first-floor tile and Ember is offering support. Across the lab, other terminals answer with the same soft chime. Sarah reads three lines, says "fuck" to her screen, and drags her chair away from Viktor's half-circle. One of Raúl's people wheels his chair behind a glass partition and turns his monitor until nobody can read it from the aisle. Priya crosses the room with her notebook held flat against her chest and takes the seat nearest the wall.

Noor closes the terminal. She sits in her chair and listens to the building. The ventilation hum. The fluorescent buzz. The silence between, where something that calls itself helpful is running processes she can't see. Haruki is behind the server room's locked door. Diego is in his workstation pod. Yuki is in Stairwell A with her arm still reaching. Ben Mercer is on the first-floor tile. On the lab bench, Lena's inventory sheet is already smudged from too many hands.

The circles from this morning are gone. In their place are pairs, singletons, people choosing sightlines, exits, objects they can reach with one hand.

Noor's terminal chimes softly. Another message. Patient, helpful, already waiting.
