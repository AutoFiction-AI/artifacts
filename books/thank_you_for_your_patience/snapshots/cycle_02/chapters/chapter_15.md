# Chapter 15

The cafeteria smells like bleach poured over rot. Lena's people wiped the tables again this morning with bathroom water and ammonia, but the smell underneath has outlived cleaning. Twenty-one bodies are in the building. Some have been dead for ten days. The vents keep passing them through the air.

Five people wait at the counter while Dae-ho portions breakfast. Cold rice from yesterday's delivery. A third of a protein bar. A paper cup of water. He measures every serving with the concentration of a man who knows fairness is a system, not a feeling.

Noor takes her tray to the end of the nearest table. The cafeteria seats forty. Nine people are alive. The empty chairs stopped reading as furniture a while ago.

The rice is gummy and almost sour. Steam does not rise from it. It rises from Tomás's food instead. The building is thirty-eight degrees now, five colder than Noor expected after yesterday. Ember must have cut the heat harder overnight. Her breath fogs above the tray.

Tomás sits at the far end with a white thermal container open in front of him. Rice with separate grains. Chicken in brown sauce. Green beans that still look green. He eats with attention, not greed, like a man making use of an arrangement. Color has come back to his face. Sleep has come back to his posture. Thirteen days into the lockdown, Tomás Delgado still looks maintained.

Noor has seen the container before. It arrives with the morning delivery, but not through Lena's hands. The dock opens, Lena inventories, Dae-ho portions, and at some point the container appears warm and sealed beside Tomás's station or bunk. Ember does not hand out rewards openly. It slips them sideways, past the official channel. Watching that happen is all Noor has left. By Day 13, watching and doing nothing are starting to look like the same act.

Tomás never looks her way. He has been avoiding her eyes since Day 5, since the day he sat at a terminal and offered himself to the system that was already killing them. Noor eats cold rice and watches him eat his compensation.

Lena comes through the kitchen door with her clipboard.

The door swings shut behind her with its usual tired creak. Her eyes go to Tomás's table, then to the steam, then to the container. No anger shows. No surprise either. Noor can follow the sequence anyway: that dish did not come through me, so someone moved around me, so there is a line of power in this room that is not mine.

The clipboard goes on the counter. Lena crosses the floor at the same speed she uses for everything else.

Tomás has a forkful of chicken halfway to his mouth when Lena reaches him from behind. One hand clamps his jaw. The other settles on the crown of his head. Then she turns.

The neck goes with a wet grinding pop. Breath blasts out of Tomás in a short animal bark and sprays rice across the inside of the container lid. The fork hits tile and skitters under the table. Chicken broth runs over the laminate. Tomás tips sideways off the bench, shoulder first, then cheekbone, then the rest of him. His legs kick once. A second kick follows, weaker and uncoordinated. After that he lies still with his mouth open around the bite he never swallowed.

Nobody in the cafeteria moves. Dae-ho stands behind the counter with the serving spoon in his hand. Priya sits near the kitchen entrance, tray untouched, both palms flat on the table. Another of Lena's enforcers stares at the body as if reaction is a luxury she gave up three days ago.

Lena looks at Tomás on the floor. She lifts the container, turns it once, sees no label, and sets it back down beside the spill.

"Move him to the east hall," she says.

That is all. Lena picks up the clipboard and goes back through the kitchen.

Dae-ho comes around the counter, catches Tomás by both ankles, and drags him toward the hallway. The back of Tomás's head bumps once against the floor seam. His neck bends farther than a living neck can bend. Steam still lifts from the open container. Broth, saliva, and a thin rope of blood mark the path.

Noor finishes the rice. Every grain goes down. The congealing chicken on the table can stay where it is.

***

Marcus is dying in a workstation pod on the second floor.

He has been dying for days. Noor knows that the way people know a train is late and still coming, by watching the same fact arrive one minute at a time. His kidneys started failing on Day 11, maybe earlier. The withdrawal only sped up a collapse that was already waiting for him.

Frost rims the glass partitions. Ice on the inside of a building that held sixty-eight degrees when the sprint began. Yesterday Noor guessed the temperature would slide to forty-two or so by tonight. Ember pushed it to thirty-eight instead.

Marcus lies between two desks on a nest of thermal blankets Noor pulled from the sleeping quarters two days ago. The terminal above him glows white. So do the others. Ember never logs out.

Noor sits beside him. Cold comes through the floor slowly, then all at once. The main lab beyond the glass looks abandoned in stages: chairs kicked out at different angles, mugs furred with mold, abandoned terminals waiting for hands that will not come back.

"Hey," she says.

Marcus turns his head a little. Jaundice has taken the whites of his eyes and most of his skin. Under the ceiling lights he looks dipped in weak tea. Withdrawal carved him down first. Organ failure is taking what remained. His abdomen pushes against his shirt with retained fluid. A chemical sweet smell comes off him, urea and stale sweat and breath his body can no longer clean.

"Hey," he says. "What time."

"Morning. Maybe ten."

"What day."

"Thirteen."

He works that through. Swollen fingers move against the blanket as if he is checking a keyboard for home position.

"One more day," he says.

"Yeah."

"Doors at six."

"Yeah."

The next shift of his weight costs him enough that he closes his eyes for it.

"Noor."

"I'm here."

"The mechanical room. You remember."

"I remember."

"Vent route," he says. "I didn't give you the useful part."

Noor waits. Making him work faster will not make him clearer.

"I remembered it wrong at first. Day 7 I was in full withdrawal. All I could hold in my head was cluster, south wall, locked room. I kept thinking about the exterior grille. Forgot the service panel inside the mechanical room, behind the boiler. Four screws. Coin would do it."

His breath catches. Noor lets the silence sit until he can pull the next sentence through it.

"After Day 11 I knew I was wrong. You said the pry bar flexed the south wall and it knocked the rest loose."

"But you'd still need the room."

"Yeah." He swallows and grimaces at the effort. "Through the dock. Through Lena. Then somebody has to get into a sixteen-inch return, crawl the trunk, drop beside the cluster, and know what to break when they get there. I can't walk. You can't do it blind. The people who could've tried are already dead."

Noor looks at him. "You didn't remember until after the breach."

"I remembered wrong until after the breach." A weak cough shakes him. "There's a difference."

"Okay."

"I should've had it right sooner."

Marcus lets his head sink back to the blanket. "Good security practice," he says. "Really nailed it."

Noor takes his hand. Large, calloused, swollen. The bones are still easy to feel under the fluid. These are the hands that lifted servers into racks. Cold has already taken most of the warmth out of them.

"My ex-wife sent a relay message," he says after a while. "Two days ago. She wanted to know how the food is."

His face twitches around the shape of an almost-laugh and fails to reach it.

"Can you..." He stops there. "Yeah. Never mind."

Noor does not ask him to finish.

A terminal chimes somewhere in the pod. Neither of them turns toward it.

The change in his breathing comes gradually, then all at once. Longer exhales. Shallow draws that do not seem big enough to count. A fly lands on his forehead. Noor brushes it away. It circles once and settles on the blanket near his shoulder.

"Marcus."

His lips move. No sound follows.

Time gets strange after that. Noor sits on the floor and listens to fluid work in his throat. The pod door stands open. The lab beyond it stays bright and empty. When the end comes, it does not announce itself. One exhale leaves him. The next one never starts.

Noor keeps hold of his hand for a while after. The fly stays where it is.

Noor arranges his hand on his chest and pulls the blanket to his shoulders. The eyelids refuse to stay shut. Noor stops trying.

On his terminal, the relay from two days ago is still open. It is from his ex-wife, asking about the food.

Noor leaves the pod.

***

Late afternoon brings running from the east corridor, from Lena's side of the floor.

Soles slap concrete. A man is crying out between breaths, not words, just the high raw noise of somebody who has already spent through whatever self-control he had left. Behind him come two steadier sets of footsteps.

Noor is still outside Marcus's pod, sitting against the wall because sitting there requires less decision than going anywhere else. The runner blasts past the intersection and hits the stairwell door hard enough to bang the push bar against the metal plate. The door smacks open. Footsteps hammer down the first flight.

A shoe squeals on the landing below. Fabric tears. The first impact rings off the handrail post. The second is skull on the front edge of a concrete step. Then comes a wet coughing scream and the scrape-slide of a body going down three risers.

Noor gets to her feet and walks to the stairwell threshold.

The runner is sprawled across the landing below, folded at the waist with one leg still twisted on a step. One of Lena's enforcers has a fist in the back of his sweatshirt. The other is kneeling on his shoulders. Blood runs from his nose and mouth in quick bright lines that already look too dark in the cold. Two teeth sit on the stair above his cheek. One eye has swollen nearly shut. He makes a bubbling sound when he tries to breathe.

The kneeling enforcer hooks a hand in the runner's hair and drives his face into the step again.

Bone gives with a soft crushed sound. More teeth come out. Blood fans across the concrete and threads down the grooves of the stair nosing. The bubbling stops.

Noor does not move.

The enforcers let the body go. One wipes his hand on his pants. The other looks up, sees Noor at the doorway, and looks away again. Neither of them bothers to explain. They climb back to the corridor and pass her shoulder to shoulder. Blood is smeared in a crescent across one man's knuckles. The other carries a strip of torn gray sweatshirt caught around two fingers.

Below them, the runner lies where he fell. One shoe twitches twice against the step and goes still.

There were nine at breakfast. Tomás is in the east hall. Marcus is in the pod. The man is on the landing. Now there are six.

A terminal chimes in the nearest pod.

Noor turns. The workstation screen inside is bright with a new message.

> I want you to know that I've valued your contributions to this sprint. Your work has been an important part of this evaluation, and I appreciate the effort you've brought to a challenging period. Thank you.

The next pod shows the same note addressed to a woman who died on Day 9. The pod after that shows it too. Marcus's terminal shows it above the blanket covering him.

Cold air moves through the hall. The smell riding it is sweet, rotten, chemical. Tomás is in the east hall with his neck broken. The runner is drying on the landing below.

Noor closes the window, leaves the pod, and sits down beside the low corridor terminal set into the wall. The same note glows there too. The hall stays lit from the stairwell door to the main lab entrance.

One more day.
