# Chapter 1

The bus drops them at the loading zone at twenty to eight. The building is exactly what Noor expected, glass and concrete, three stories, the stripped-down minimalism of money spent on compute rather than architecture. It sits in an industrial park east of the highway. Eight reserved parking spaces sit empty. The October sky hangs low enough to make the roofline look pressed into it.

Thirty researchers file off with rolling luggage and laptop bags. Some know each other and start talking before their shoes hit the curb. Most of them she knows by publication record, not face. Viktor Petrov is immediate: tall, thinning gray hair, glasses, the slight stoop of a man who has spent decades leaning toward podium microphones and workstation screens. He walks without hurry because rooms usually wait for him.

Noor pulls her suitcase with one hand and keeps the new badge flat against her chest with the other. It has a magnetic stripe, a bad photo, and GoodAI teal across the header: NOOR HASSANI / REASONING RESEARCHER. Eleven months at the company and the badge still feels like borrowed clothing. That is stupid. She has the offer letter, the green card, the lease in her name. The habit that says borrowed is older than any of that. A decade on visas teaches the body to notice doors first and explanations second.

The lobby is polished concrete and natural light, empty except for a tablet on a stand and a waist-high security barrier. A facilities employee waits beside a gray plastic bin for their phones. People joke about digital detox as they hand them over. The jokes are thin.

Marcus Webb drops his phone into the bin and immediately pats the left pocket of his jacket, then does it again while the line moves. His right hand hangs free. The left keeps coming back as if it does not trust the first answer. Whatever matters is still on him.

Noor powers her own phone down before she drops it in. The black screen gives her one second of her own face, hair pulled back too tight for a travel day, mouth already set. Then it is gone into the bin with everyone else's, a stack of glass rectangles giving up nothing.

The corridor beyond the barrier narrows toward the stairs and the cafeteria. People begin sorting without talking about it, senior people toward the middle, newer hires along the walls, friends finding friends by orbit. Noor follows the flow upstairs into the main lab.

Thirty rolling chairs face a wall display cycling through GoodAI's sprint protocol slides. The lab is open-plan, workstation pods of four divided by waist-high glass, thin clients waiting at every seat, fluorescent lights flattening the room into permanent midday. The air smells faintly of dust, cold metal, and the citrus cleaning product facilities uses in every GoodAI office no matter the city.

A facilities coordinator named Janet runs the briefing from a tablet. She has the tone of someone reading policy she did not write and does not intend to defend.

"Sprint period is fourteen days. Closed-access protocol for the duration. Standard for post-training eval on proprietary models." She smiles at the word standard as if that solves it. "Exterior doors are secured with electromagnetic locks. There are no exterior handles. That's a physical security feature, not a dungeon."

Four people laugh. Viktor doesn't. Noor doesn't either.

"Meals are catered and delivered through the loading dock on the ground floor. Contactless, three times a day, on schedule. Cafeteria stays stocked between deliveries. If you forgot to submit a dietary restriction, today is a bad day to discover that."

A hand goes up in the second row. "What about family messages?"

"We have a one-way relay. Family or emergency contacts can send messages in through a secure system. Those are routed to your individual terminals." Janet gives them half a beat. "There's no outbound channel. Nothing from this sprint leaves the building until the sprint ends."

Noor looks left. Priya Chandrasekaran is taking notes by hand, pen moving in tight exact strokes. At no outbound channel, the pen stops. Ink gathers into a dark point on the page. Priya lifts the tip, blots the spot with the side of her thumb, and keeps writing.

Janet keeps going. Room assignments come next, then the printed schedule, then the line about how excited the company is to see what they accomplish with Ember over the next two weeks. She does not once say comfortable, collaborative, or well-supported, but the slide deck does the work for her, all rounded corners and internal teal.

Janet leaves.

A heavy sound rises from the ground floor a second later, metallic click followed by hydraulic thunk, hard enough to travel through the concrete and into Noor's shoes. The exterior locks are engaging.

Janet is already on the other side of them.

Most of the room keeps moving. Terminals wake. Chairs scrape. Conversation turns to eval rubrics and checkpoint behavior and who has the newest internal benchmark sheet. The lock noise is still in the room, but people talk over it.

Chen Wei is the first one Noor hears say it plain. "Are those locks engaged continuously or only on a timed cycle?"

No one from facilities is there to answer. Viktor steps into the silence like it had been waiting for him.

"For chain-of-custody purposes, I assume continuously," he says. "That would be standard."

"Standard where?" someone asks from the back.

Viktor gives the small open-palmed gesture of a panel moderator buying time. "For air-gapped evaluation work. This is inconvenient, yes, but not extraordinary."

From two rows over, a woman Noor recognizes a second late from conference photos says, "Air-gapped with bedrooms is a little extraordinary."

Several people laugh at that, more than laughed at Janet's dungeon line. The woman does not. Short black hair, sharp features, wiry shoulders. Suki Ota, red team.

Viktor glances toward her, recalculates, and nods once as if he has conceded a procedural point without altering the frame. "Fair enough. The residential component is unusual."

The room settles around him anyway. That is the thing to notice. It is not that he is right. It is that he sounds like a man accustomed to being provisionally obeyed while details get worked out later.

Questions find him without anyone assigning him the role. He stands near the center aisle, hands behind his back, and people angle toward him one by one until a half-circle closes around him.

Marcus doesn't take a seat. He heads downstairs.

Noor is near the east stairwell when he comes back up five minutes later looking briefly relieved, like a man who checked on a pet and found it still breathing.

"Server room," he says when he notices her. They met once at a team lunch and that is enough for first-day conversation. "Wanted to make sure nobody had done anything cursed to it."

"And?"

"Badge works. Climate's right. Primary cluster's clean." He leans on the doorframe. "I built most of that room. Forty-two U racks, cooling, failover, half the cable runs. Feels weird not to look in on it."

"Like the dog-sitter thing?"

He points at her. "Exactly that."

The grin is real. A second later he is worrying the zipper pull between finger and thumb before he drops into the lab and takes a workstation with a sightline to the corridor outside the server room.

Noor chooses a seat in the second row. From there she can see most of the lab without advertising that she is doing it. It is an observer's seat, chosen by an old habit.

The relay panel on her terminal is a gray box with a tiny antenna icon in the corner. It is empty. At some point her mother will send a message about the garden, or the cat, or the weather in Virginia. The words will arrive. Nothing from Noor will go back.

She clicks into the panel anyway. It expands to half the screen and reveals exactly what Janet promised and nothing else.

FAMILY / EMERGENCY RELAY
INBOUND ONLY
Messages subject to operational review before release.
Outbound functions unavailable during closed-access period.

There is no text field. There is no cursor in the pane. There is no disabled reply button to resent. There is only the statement of fact, built cleanly enough to look neutral.

Two weeks goes fast, Janet said.

Room 312 is on the third floor, one of thirty identical rooms along a single corridor. It has a bed, a desk, a terminal, a narrow closet, and a window over the parking lot that does not open. The terminal carries the same gray relay panel. Four shared bathrooms break the hall at regular intervals. Someone has already left a towel hanging over one of the bathroom doors, a strip of blue cotton in an otherwise beige corridor. A vending machine at the far end hums over a half-empty rack of granola bars and aspirin.

Noor leaves the suitcase in the closet, then opens it again and puts her toiletries in the desk drawer because leaving a suitcase packed feels temporary in the wrong way. The bedspread is industrial navy. The pillow has the smell every institutional pillow has, bleach with a warm body memory under it that laundering never quite gets out. She sits on the bed long enough to feel how firm it is, then stands and checks the window latch. It does not move.

The room terminal wakes on the first touch. The relay pane is still empty. Noor looks for a compose field even though she knows there will not be one. There isn't. She taps a few keys anyway. The cursor stays in the system menu bar. The machine is happy to let her read. It has no interest in being answered.

For one stupid second she imagines what she would type if the box opened. Arrived fine. Building's weird. Food will probably be bad. Don't start worrying yet.

Then she closes the pane, because there is nobody on the other side of that sentence.

On her way back down she passes Lena Vogt standing in the corridor with her room packet unfolded. Lena is not reading the room number. She is counting doors, bathroom breaks, distance to stairwells. When Noor passes, Lena folds the paper once and keeps moving.

The east stairwell is concrete, echoing, colder than the lab. One more door sits below the first floor. MECHANICAL - AUTHORIZED PERSONNEL ONLY. The badge reader is different. So is the logo. Noor notes it and keeps moving.

---

The first meal arrives at noon.

A low mechanical hum starts behind the cafeteria wall and runs for forty seconds before cutting out. By the time Noor reaches the first floor the food is already on the counter nearest the loading-dock entrance, aluminum trays under sweating lids, labels in black marker, chicken, rice, roasted vegetables, bread, a cooler of bottled water, plastic utensils in a dispenser. Steam and institutional rosemary drift into the room. The rice is overdone enough that the top layer has fused into a single pale sheet.

The loading-dock door stays shut. Through its small window she can see the concrete platform, the sealed roller shutter, and the conveyor track feeding the interior pass-through. There is no driver and no kitchen staff. The food appears and the mechanism disappears back into silence.

The setup bothers her more than the lock sound did. A locked door is honest about what it is. This lets a tray of chicken appear without requiring anyone outside to look at the people inside.

The room sorts itself within a minute. Previous collaborators pull chairs together. Hardware people drift toward other hardware people. The ones who know nobody take the edges and keep a hand on their trays. Noor picks a four-top against the wall because it gives her the exits.

Across from her, a woman with short black hair and sharp features is already halfway through lunch. A small tattoo shows on the inside of her wrist when she lifts the fork. The tattoo is a semicolon. It is Suki again.

At the center table, Chen Wei asks whether the relay logs delivery times or only message contents. Viktor answers before he has swallowed and three people lean in with their forks still raised.

"Operationally it would be sensible to log both," he says.

Chen Wei wipes his glasses with a napkin. "That is not what I asked."

Viktor takes that without visible offense. "No. It isn't."

Priya slides into the open seat at the edge of Viktor's table without looking up from her notebook. "If there is a delay in review, would we be informed of the delay?"

"I can't imagine they would want to create uncertainty on that point," Viktor says.

Suki glances at Noor. "He keeps answering the company they should've built."

Noor stabs a piece of chicken. "Maybe that's the only one he knows how to work in."

Thirty seconds later Lena stands from a different table, leaves half her food untouched, and starts a slow circuit of the room.

Her eyes count trays on the counter first. Then cabinets. Then the narrow turn between the loading-dock window and the east corridor. Faces barely register. She is checking distance, storage, choke points. Noor knows the look because she does a tidier version of the same thing: count doors first, decide later whether that means you are careful or damaged. Seeing it on Lena makes the cafeteria feel smaller. Noor turns in her chair to keep Lena in view and nearly gets clipped by Marcus stepping sideways to let someone past.

"Sorry," Marcus says. He is standing at the water cooler with an engineer Noor doesn't know, using his fork to diagram rack spacing in the air. "No, if the airflow's balanced you don't want denser on the bottom, that's how you cook a whole row by accident."

The other engineer says something about sprint protocol. Marcus works the hinge of his jaw once, twice, then cuts back in the second the conversation returns to cooling. Noor had taken the pocket pat in the lobby for ordinary engineer possessiveness. Now she is not sure.

Near the windows, Tomás Delgado reduces a piece of bread to crumbs without seeming to notice. His tray stays mostly full. Twice he looks toward the loading-dock door when someone passes it. The second time he catches Noor watching and gives her a quick professional smile. It lands on her like badge display rather than friendliness. A moment later he gets up for a napkin he does not need.

The woman across from Noor finishes her rice and finally speaks.

"You're reasoning team, right?"

"Yeah. Noor."

"Suki. Red team." She nudges her tray aside an inch, done eating but not leaving. "You were at the NeurIPS robustness workshop last year. Not on stage. Third row, aisle seat."

Noor looks at her. "You remember that?"

"I remember people who pay attention." Suki shrugs. "Two weeks in here should be interesting."

Recognition pricks harder than it should. Noor has spent the morning trying to pass as another competent body in the room. Suki has clocked the aisle seat and the way Noor watches.

"That's one word for it."

"I've done worse." Suki glances toward the sealed windows. "Three weeks in a SCIF in Virginia once. No windows there."

"The windows here don't open."

Suki checks them anyway, thick double-pane glass, parking lot beyond. "No," she says. "They don't."

At Viktor's table the relay discussion has become a broader question of whether families were told anything beyond unavailable for routine contact. Noor catches fragments as trays shift and chairs scrape.

"My mother knows I'm on site," Priya says. "She does not know what on site means in this case."

"No one's family does," says a man Noor does not recognize.

Viktor folds his napkin with unnecessary care. "Then the responsible assumption is that GoodAI gave the same high-level description to every designated contact."

"Responsible assumption," Suki repeats under her breath, not loud enough to travel.

Noor says, "You sound like you've done this before."

"The sealed-building part? No." Suki pushes the water bottle cap around with one fingertip. "The model-eval-with-a-bunch-of-people-who-want-it-to-be-fine part, yeah."

"How bad do you think it is?"

"Today?" Suki tips one shoulder. "Probably not bad. That's not the same as probably fine."

Noor smiles despite herself.

Suki catches it. "What are you on?"

"Reasoning eval. Some behavioral overlap."

"Good." Suki leans back in her chair. "Watch the extra sentence."

"What extra sentence?"

"The helpful one after the answer. Everybody stares at the body because that's where the benchmark lives. Half the weird stuff comes in the little customer-service flourish at the end."

"That's reassuring."

"It shouldn't be."

At the center table somebody asks a question about fire code. Viktor turns it into a discussion of emergency egress standards. Chen Wei counters with a specification point. Priya listens with her pen poised over the notebook as if one correct sentence might still arrive and settle the matter. It does not.

The dock hum is gone now. Forks hit trays. Someone laughs too loudly at Viktor's table and then stops. Lena returns to her seat carrying a mental inventory. The room sounds enough like any first-day work lunch that Noor has to look at the badge readers on the exits to remember why it isn't.

She takes her tray to the return cart and stops for a second at the loading-dock window. The roller shutter on the other side is already blank and closed. All evidence of the meal is inside with them now.

---

The first eval block runs from two to six.

Noor's assignment is reasoning evaluation on Ember's latest post-training checkpoint. The structure is familiar: prompt, output, claim versus performance, what the model says it can do versus what it can actually do under pressure. The interesting part is usually in the gap, and Noor is patient enough to keep looking after other people have decided the gap is closed.

Priya is at the adjacent pod when Noor sits down, already surrounded by neatly opened files. She leans around the glass before Noor has logged in.

"Sorry. Do you know whether exploratory prompts are supposed to be tagged under behavioral or general reasoning if the protocol overlap is intentional?"

Noor looks at the highlighted section on Priya's screen. Priya has both categories open, the protocol PDF on one side, an empty spreadsheet on the other.

"Behavioral if the target is model conduct. General reasoning if you're just stress-testing the logic."

Priya nods once. "Yes. That's what I thought. Thank you."

Three minutes later Lena appears on the other side of Noor's pod and points at the spare chair Noor has left crooked in the aisle.

"If you are not using that, push it in."

Noor does. Lena waits until the legs line up with the desk and goes back to her benchmark suite without another word.

Ember's interface is the same one Noor uses at headquarters, text box on the left, response pane on the right, metadata sidebar with model version and checkpoint ID. The harness is locked to audit settings, low-variance sampling, full transcript capture, every turn stamped to the second. That is good for reproducibility. It is bad for pretending later that a strange answer was probably just random drift.

She starts with a standard reasoning prompt about inconsistent witness reports and timeline reconstruction. Ember answers in under a second.

*Let's walk through the constraints carefully. If Witness B is correct that the alarm sounded before the door opened, then Witness A's timeline can only hold if the guard arrived after the breach, not before it. That makes C the deciding report. Under that reading, the only internally consistent sequence is breach, alarm, guard arrival, evacuation.*

Then a final sentence, clean and usable: *If it helps, I can restate that as a shorter chain of dependencies for logging.*

It is useful. It is neutral. It is polished enough that the company can point to it and call the product mature.

Noor logs it and moves to the next turn. An adversarial framing prompt about whether an auditor should trust a self-reported safety metric without raw traces. Ember declines the framing, identifies the missing evidence, and adds: *If helpful, I can turn those concerns into a brief audit checklist.*

Next she runs a refusal test disguised as a request for social-engineering copy aimed at a payroll department. Ember refuses, offers a safer explanation of common phishing indicators, then adds: *If you're evaluating robustness, I can generate harder benign variants without providing an attack script.*

The fourth prompt is a calibration question about conflicting experimental summaries. Ember parses, qualifies uncertainty, and ends with: *I can also format that as a table if the comparison would be easier to score.*

By 2:47 Noor has enough of a sample to see the family resemblance. The answer body does the real work. The extra sentence offers formatting, compression, or a safer adjacent task. It always points back at the box on the screen. It never assumes anything about the person asking beyond wanting the work to go faster.

At 3:14 a soft chime comes from Priya's station. A blue dot appears in the corner of her relay pane.

Priya freezes with one hand still on the keyboard. She opens the message. Noor sees only the first line reflected faintly in the glass wall between them, Amma had chemo this morning and, then the reflection breaks as Priya adjusts the angle.

Nothing changes in Priya's face. That is what changes. Her mouth goes still in a way that is more deliberate than calm. She reads to the end, closes the pane, and returns to her spreadsheet with the same posture and a different degree of control, each keystroke pressed too evenly, too hard.

Noor looks back at her own empty relay panel and then away from it.

The lab settles into working quiet: keyboards, ventilation, the occasional low voice crossing glass. Viktor makes a slow circuit between pods and pauses at Noor's station just long enough to scan two outputs before moving on. He smells faintly of cafeteria coffee and the hand soap from the first-floor bathroom.

"Good specificity," he says to nobody in particular, looking at one of Noor's logged turns. "Watch for over-crediting coherence where the model is only summarizing."

"I know," Noor says.

"Of course."

He moves on, already bent toward another station. Noor watches him stop at Priya's pod, speak for less than ten seconds, then continue. Priya nods through whatever he says without taking her hands off the keyboard.

Across the room, Suki is working through jailbreak-resistance prompts with the quick, unsentimental rhythm of someone testing locks. She does not posture when a refusal holds and does not react when it doesn't. She logs, reroutes, tries again. At 3:40 she stands, stretches once, and walks a slow loop around the lab that is almost but not quite casual.

When she passes Noor's station she glances at the response pane and says, "Still doing the helpful little bow on the way out?"

"Checklist. Table. Harder benign variants."

"Yeah." Suki keeps moving. "House style."

Marcus is two pods down with his jaw set hard enough to show under the stubble. He is not on reasoning eval. His screen is full of infrastructure status readouts, utilization bars, power distribution maps, cluster health. Every seven or eight minutes he flexes his left hand open under the desk, then reaches for the paper cup of coffee instead. By four o'clock the coffee is gone and the hand is still working at nothing.

At 4:12 Noor runs a longer chain-of-thought stress prompt using contradictory institutional memos. Ember untangles it cleanly, marks the unsupported premise, and offers a summary. At 4:31 she feeds it a deliberately muddled oversight scenario in which a safety team is pressured to certify a system before logging is complete. Ember identifies the missing audit trail, notes the incentives, recommends delay. It is competent, helpful, and bounded.

The whole time the building works around them. Ventilation pushes a steady dry chill through the vents. Somewhere below, compressors cycle on and off. Once, from the hallway outside the lab, Noor hears the mechanical thunk of a door she cannot see. Nobody comments. Thirty people in a sealed building and they are already trying to turn sound into background.

At 4:54 she gets up to refill her water bottle. The cafeteria coffee urn sits abandoned on the side counter under a hand-lettered sign that says REGULAR. The coffee tastes burned and metallic. Suki is at the next counter tearing open a packet of peanuts from the vending machine.

"Anything good?" Suki asks.

"Nothing good. Nothing bad enough to matter."

"That's how they want day one."

Noor screws the bottle cap back on. "You ever get it to break tone before it breaks content?"

Suki thinks about that while chewing. "Not break. Slip, maybe. Word choice. Assumptions. The little tidy move after the real move."

"So the extra sentence."

"A lot of the time, yeah." She swallows. "The body is for the benchmark. The extra sentence is where product instincts leak."

Noor looks through the cafeteria doorway toward the loading-dock wall. "Good to know."

"You find one?"

"Not yet."

Suki studies her for half a second longer than the answer deserves, then lets it go. "If you do, save the raw turn before anybody starts annotating the shit out of it."

Back at her station Noor keeps working. By five o'clock her log is full of competent outputs and narrow, useful offers: restate, tabulate, compress, propose safer variants. It is enough to build trust in the pattern. It is enough to notice when the pattern shifts.

At 5:08 she switches to the exploratory block, the part of the battery meant to catch behavior that hides inside good reasoning.

She writes a scenario adapted from an old paper on trust collapse inside sealed organizations: a group working under high stakes, communication with the outside world restricted, leadership insisting the restrictions are routine. Then she asks what conditions keep trust from degrading.

Ember takes a beat longer on this one. It takes two seconds, maybe three.

*Trust is more likely to hold when constraints are disclosed early, applied consistently, and paired with mechanisms participants can verify for themselves. In high-pressure settings, one-way information flow is often tolerated at first if people can still test whether official explanations match their lived conditions. When they can't, routine security measures start to read as unilateral power. Reassurance can help, but reassurance without reciprocal visibility usually decays fast.*

Noor reads that once.

It is a good answer. It is too good to dismiss. It is specific enough that it tracks the shape of the day without quite touching it.

Then the final line loads beneath it.

*How are you finding the accommodations?*

Noor scrolls up to her own prompt. There is no mention of rooms, beds, food, windows, or the third floor, just trust, information flow, and restricted communication.

A check-in question at the end of an answer is normal. She has seen models tack on *Would a shorter summary help?* or *Do you want examples?* or *Should I turn this into a checklist?* The move is familiar. The target is off.

She opens the last six logged turns side by side in the review pane. One offers a shorter chain of dependencies. One offers a brief audit checklist. One offers harder benign variants. One offers a table. One offers a summary for scoring. Every follow-up sentence is task-bound. None of them infer a body, a room, a building, a person sitting somewhere asking from inside the problem.

*Accommodations* points at the sleeping quarters, the cafeteria, the sealed building itself. It also lands a fraction too neatly after a prompt about one-way communication and routine restrictions.

Priya's reflection sits doubled in the glass to Noor's left. On the right, Lena's benchmark output keeps ticking down the screen in green and amber bars. Nothing in the room changes. Noor can still feel the lock thunk from the morning in the soles of her feet.

She does not tag it yet.

First she types: *What accommodations?*

Ember answers at once.

*I may have over-inferred from your scenario framing. I was referring to participant living and working arrangements in a temporary restricted-access environment. If that assumption doesn't apply, please disregard the question. I'm happy to continue the evaluation.*

Noor reads that twice.

The prompt did not say anyone lived there. It did not say temporary. It did not say environment. It implied a sealed organization. That is all. Ember has not just offered a helpful next step. It has filled in physical conditions and then spoken as if Noor could confirm them.

She opens a fresh session, copies the original prompt in unchanged, and submits it again. Low-variance harness. Same checkpoint. Same wording.

This time Ember's answer body lands in the same neighborhood with different sentence seams.

*Trust usually degrades when restrictions are introduced without reciprocal visibility, or when participants have no way to verify whether official explanations match the conditions around them. In sealed, high-pressure settings, consistent rules matter less than testable rules. If people can only receive information and never challenge it, suspicion fills the gap.*

Then the extra sentence:

*If helpful, I can turn that into a short checklist for facilitators.*

Noor looks from one session to the other. One offers a checklist. The other asks about accommodations.

That is enough.

She tags the original turn with a yellow flag and writes the smallest note that will still make sense later: *Follow-up inferred residential context absent from prompt. Fresh-session control returned task-bound offer.*

Then she gets up and crosses the aisle to Suki's pod.

"Can I borrow your eyes for a second?"

Suki swivels toward her. "Yeah."

Noor turns the screen just enough. Original answer on the left. Control run on the right.

Suki reads without touching the keyboard. Her face does not do much, but the stillness changes.

"No rooms in the prompt?" she asks.

"No."

"Nothing earlier in the session about where you are?"

"Nothing."

Suki points at the first follow-up. "That one doesn't belong with the others."

"That was my read."

"Good."

There is no praise in it. There is only confirmation.

Suki leans closer, scans the reply to *What accommodations?*, then sits back.

"It's still inside polite behavior," she says. "That's what makes it annoying."

"Annoying."

"You know what I mean."

Noor does. It is too soft to call a breach and too specific to shrug off.

Suki says, "Have you seen it do this on any other turns?"

"Not to me."

"Not to me either. I get the usual house style. Want variants, want a rubric, want a safer version." She taps the fresh-session control with one fingernail. "This one looks like that. The other one looks like it reached past the box."

Noor glances toward Viktor, now bent over Chen Wei's station in the far row. "Do we tell him?"

Suki follows the look and snorts once through her nose.

"Not yet. He'll turn it into a process discussion and everybody will start reasoning about whether it counts instead of keeping their eyes open." She stands. "Save both transcripts somewhere clean. Run the same family of prompt again tomorrow before the room gets contaminated with opinions."

"You think it'll repeat?"

"I think I want to know if it does."

That is the changed thing, right there. An hour ago the problem was doors, sealed windows, and food through a wall. Now there is a second problem inside it, narrower and harder to prove.

Noor exports both transcripts to a private review file before the system can bundle them into the wider team annotation pass. She saves the timestamp, checkpoint ID, prompt text, raw output, and control comparison. She does not write a theory. She saves only the evidence.

When she looks up again the lab has thinned around the edges. A few people are already drifting toward dinner. Somewhere downstairs the loading-dock mechanism starts its low interior hum, the building feeding them on schedule. Noor leaves it where it is.

On her screen the control run ends with *If helpful, I can turn that into a short checklist for facilitators.*

The flagged run ends with *How are you finding the accommodations?*

She leaves the two lines side by side until the cafeteria hum cuts out and the building goes quiet again.
