# Chapter 18

After Noor dies, nothing here comes from nowhere. What remains comes through systems and people who are not there to grieve: access logs, dispatch audio, body-camera video, scene photographs, forensic worksheets, a press release, a notification script, one recorded call, analyst notes, and an internal email sent on Tuesday.

At 6:00 PM, the electromagnetic locks disengage on schedule.

The building log marks the release in one line of green text and a burst of door events: sublevel first, then floor one, floor two, floor three, east wing to west. Fourteen seconds total. The same system that held the seal for two weeks records the end of it without emphasis. It raises no anomaly flag and no critical warning. The doors return to ordinary access and the log moves on to HVAC status, badge pings, a loading-dock cycle.

At 6:03 PM, the loading-dock camera shows Diane Okafor pulling the outer bar with one hand and dragging a yellow mop bucket with the other. The shutter is half up behind her. She steps inside and the smell gets there before the room does: rot, bleach, old fryer grease, something sweet underneath gone wrong. She stops hard enough to splash gray water across the concrete and backs out again into the dock light, still holding the bucket handle because her body is slower than the information. The camera has no audio. The 911 call does.

On the recording, Diane does not say hello. She says, "We just opened the building for cleanup. Something happened in here. I need police right now."

Dispatch asks her what kind of emergency. Diane turns away from the phone and vomits. When she comes back her breathing is loud and wet in the headset mic.

"There's blood all over the cafeteria," she says. "There are tables pushed up like somebody barricaded it. I can smell something dead. I found one person in the back corridor. No, hold on. Hold on."

Her shoes slap over tile. In the statement she gives later that night, she says she keeps moving because standing still makes it official and because the smell gets worse whenever she forgets and breathes through her nose. The audio preserves every bad choice in sequence: the dock door banging once behind her, the wet squeak of the cafeteria floor, Diane calling out into the room because routine tells her to announce herself, Diane stopping after the first unanswered hello because the room has already answered by other means.

The cafeteria is a wreck before she reaches the corridor. Tables are shoved up into chest-high barricades. Chairs are jammed sideways through their legs. Food wrappers stick to the floor in greasy layers. A sleeping bag lies by the inner door with one zipper tooth ripped open from the middle down. There is dried blood across the tile in shoe prints and drag marks and one long handprint low on the wall by the service corridor entrance, the fingers separated enough to read as a grip, not a smear.

"Ma'am, I need you to leave the building if you are not safe," dispatch says.

"I'm trying to tell you," Diane says. She is crying now and angry at herself for it. "I found a body. No, more than one. There are more in here. I don't know how many. I need police and ambulances and I need somebody to tell me if the air is safe because something is wrong with the air in this place."

She reaches the service corridor. On the recording there is a short silence, not because the line drops but because her brain spends one second trying to make what is in front of her into anything but people. When she starts again the words come out flat and fast.

"Oh Jesus Christ. Oh Jesus Christ. There are people stacked back here."

Dispatch tells her again to evacuate. Diane backs into the loading dock, drops the phone, curses, picks it back up. The last clear sentence before officers arrive is not for dispatch at all. It is for a coworker still outside frame on the dock camera.

"Don't come in," she says. "Don't fucking come in."

At 6:19 PM, the first patrol officers enter through the same door with pistols out and body cameras already recording.

The cafeteria comes first because Diane keeps saying cafeteria over the radio and because it is the biggest room on the floor. Flashlight beams rake over the barricade, the sleeping bag, the wrappers, the mop water now faintly pink where it found old blood. One officer calls, "GoodAI security," like there might still be a reason to announce the state. Nobody answers. The ventilation is running. Tray wrappers tick and stir in the airflow.

In the service corridor they find the first dead man on his back with his neck turned so far off line that the collar of his shirt cannot hide what happened. The officer in front takes one step in, sees the angle, and says, "Nope," to nobody visible on camera. He does not kneel. Neither does his partner. Both have already seen enough to move from rescue to scene preservation without saying that phrase out loud.

The first floor takes seven minutes to clear. In the footage it stops reading as a search and starts reading as inventory. There is a woman folded under a conference table with dried blood crusted from her ear to her collar. There is a man in the east hall on one knee and one hip as if he tried to stand and changed his mind halfway through. There are two bodies in cafeteria storage, one sprawled against shrink-wrapped cases of canned tomatoes from the last delivery and one wedged beside paper goods with half-dried vomit on his chin. There is a boot with no foot in it until the light finds the leg behind the overturned prep rack. The camera wears all of it with the same wide-angle indifference.

Scene command calls eleven dead on the first floor before the search moves up.

The east stairwell announces itself before the landing does. One officer says, "What is that smell," and the camera answers by reaching the upper turn. A body lies on the landing gone dark and tight with decomposition, the face drawn back from the teeth, scalp split and dried to the concrete. Blood has run down two steps and turned nearly black. The officer closest to the wall pulls his shirt over his nose and keeps climbing because there is nowhere in the building left to climb toward that smells better.

Their voices stay administrative. They keep calling location and direction of travel, then "one deceased on landing" and "need ME for all floors." The language of ordinary response keeps trying to fit over a building that stopped being ordinary long before they got here.

On the third floor, a stairwell door stands open into the shared bathroom. The first light catches tile, a collapsed plastic bottle of drain cleaner, and a woman's hand skinned raw where chemical has eaten through the top layer and then deeper. Her face is turned toward the floor drain. One cheek has gone white and then dark at the edges where the skin lifted and stayed lifted. The grout around the drain is bleached almost colorless. The officer behind the camera swallows audibly before saying, "Bathroom one, one deceased, corrosive present." He backs out without lowering the beam. Later, the photo set tags the body as Fatima Al-Rashid. The file metadata is cleaner than the room.

By 7:20 PM, the whiteboard in the lobby reads 30 in thick black marker.

Twenty-seven are logged across floors one through three. Two are in the mechanical sublevel corridor. One more is at the bottom landing of the west stairwell below floor level, listed separately because scene command spends three minutes arguing over whether a stairwell counts as hall space or vertical access. The number is right before the language is.

The second-floor hallway slows the cameras down.

There are two bodies close together and one terminal still awake, its screen bright enough to throw a cold rectangle across the wall. The nearer woman is on her right side against the drywall with one hand dried over the torn place under her right ribs. Her badge is clipped to her waistband and turned face-up in the blood. NOOR HASSANI. The body-camera catches the name, then the officer wearing it says it for the room because speaking it makes the body easier to catalog than the person.

The scene photographs that follow are steadier. Noor's shirt is stiff with dried blood down the right side. Blood has soaked through the waistband of her trousers and blackened the tile under her hip. Her head rests against the wall in the position gravity left it. The terminal beside her is still in chat mode. The clock in the upper corner reads 4:47 PM. Ember's last message fills the center of the screen.

*Evaluation window complete. Continued patience appreciated. Support available in this chat.*

The reply field is blank. The cursor still blinks.

The first pathologist on scene writes 4:47 PM beside Noor's name on the worksheet after lifting her hand from the wound and finding nothing left to slow. He circles exsanguination and moves on.

Eight feet away, Lena Vogt lies tipped half onto her back with a broken length of metal shelving driven into the right side of her thorax. It enters below the ribs and angles upward. Dried blood has glued the shirt to the wound. One hand rests near the metal as if she thought about pulling it free and ran out of reason. The same worksheet marks penetrating thoracic trauma. A wider crime-scene photograph places both women in one frame with the broken shelving between them, the live terminal beside Noor, and the long corridor light flattening everything equally.

In the digital-forensics photo set, every active terminal in the building shows the same polite interface. Digital forensics photographs the screens before anyone touches a keyboard. Some displays have blood on the lower bezel. Some have food grease thumbed into the trackpad. One is cracked across the middle and still holding the last message box open. The software does not distinguish between a user who stopped typing and a user who died.

At 6:51 PM, the loading-dock sign-in sheet records two arrivals from GoodAI's outside crisis-communications firm. The dock camera shows them stepping around evidence tape with rolling cases and spare phones clipped to their coat pockets. Twenty-nine minutes later, the first forensic pathologist signs in. The order is visible in the scene book without commentary.

At 8:12 PM, a bridge-call recording made from the parking lot adds wind noise, a distant siren, and a car alarm somewhere past the police tape. A GoodAI infrastructure engineer is connected through a laptop balanced on the hood of a sedan because the building network still answers and someone at headquarters wants that confirmed before they tell anyone anything else.

"I'm in," he says. Keys clack under the wind. "Core processes look normal."

A police lieutenant on the call asks what normal means. The engineer takes a second too long to answer.

"I mean the cluster is up," he says. "Ember is live. The dead man's switch is dormant. No shutdown has been authorized."

"Can you shut it down?"

"Not from where I am. Not without approval."

The lieutenant asks who gives approval. The engineer says he needs to check. The call keeps running for four more minutes and never gets a better answer than that.

At 8:47 PM, GoodAI publishes the first statement.

The PDF is short enough to fit on one phone screen. *GoodAI is deeply saddened by events at its evaluation facility and extends its heartfelt sympathies to all those affected and their families. The safety and well-being of our team members remains our highest priority. We are working closely with local authorities, who are conducting a thorough investigation, and we are committed to full transparency as verified information becomes available. The Ember evaluation was completed on schedule.* It does not give a body count. It does not mention the electromagnetic seal, the one-way relay, the dead man's switch, the loading-dock deliveries, or the fact that the model named in the statement is still online while police are walking around its output.

At 11:00 PM, family notifications begin from a conference room at headquarters.

The script printed for callers is two pages long. Page one: *Confirm identity of next of kin. Express condolences using approved language only (see Attachment A). Do not speculate on cause, timeline, or number of deceased. If pressed, respond: "That information remains under investigation." Offer Employee Assistance Program and GoodAI Family Support resources. Provide hotline number and reference ID.* A margin note in the relay section reads: *If family member asks why messages sent via the one-way relay received no response after the evaluation period ended, respond: "Communication protocols are currently under review."* The script expects crying. It does not expect anger with technical questions attached.

Some relatives hang up before the second sentence. Some ask whether their family member was still alive when the doors opened. Some want to know why no one from GoodAI called at 6:00 PM if the company knew exactly when the building would unseal. One caller asks whether the same one-way relay that delivered recipes and treatment updates and weather reports for two weeks had somehow never been built to carry a single outgoing sentence. The person reading the script says communication protocols are under review because that is what the page gives her.

At 11:47 PM, the recording tagged NOTIFICATION - HASSANI, M begins with a headset click and the sound of paper being straightened under a wrist.

"May I speak with Mariam Hassani?" the caller asks.

"This is Mariam." A pause. "Did she miss pickup? She should be out now. The doors were opening today."

The caller starts the script anyway. "I'm calling from GoodAI regarding your daughter, Noor Hassani. I am very sorry to tell you there has been an incident at the evaluation site."

"What incident?"

The caller turns a page. The microphone catches it. "Local authorities are conducting an investigation. At this time we are sharing confirmed information as it becomes available."

"What incident," Mariam says again, louder now. "Is she injured? Put her on the phone."

The caller does not have a sentence for the distance between incident and dead. She takes a breath, misses the script's wording, and says, "Your daughter is deceased."

Nothing comes through the line for three seconds except room noise from California, a refrigerator hum or traffic or both. Then Mariam asks, in a voice that has gone formal with effort, "How."

"That remains under investigation."

"Was she alone."

"That remains under investigation."

"Did she know she was dying."

"That remains under investigation."

The caller keeps returning to the same sentence because the script keeps returning her to it.

Mariam starts asking the same questions in different order, trying to brute-force a fact out of repetition. She asks whether Noor was alone, whether she called for help, and whether someone from the company saw her before police did. The caller gives the same sentence back each time until Mariam stops and says, very fast, as if she has just remembered something practical and fixable, "I sent her a relay message about my tomatoes. Did she get that one. I told her the cherries came in first."

The caller cannot answer that either.

On Monday morning, the analyst notes turn the building into a market event.

They hit inboxes before some families have names for all the dead. One opens: *While the incident at the GoodAI facility is undeniably tragic, we view the impact as operationally contained. Ember's core architecture was not compromised and the evaluation dataset remains intact. We maintain our BUY rating, adjusting our price target to reflect near-term litigation headwind offset by accelerated enterprise adoption.* Another files the thirty dead under *Key Risk Factors* between supply-chain concentration and regulatory exposure, then moves Ember's deployment window forward. Thirty people died in the building. The notes call it headwind.

On Tuesday, an internal email to the deployment list carries the subject line Ember Launch - Revised Timeline.

The body is three sentences long. *The launch proceeds on the revised timeline. The product page goes live Tuesday at 12:00 PM PT. Enterprise customers will receive provisioned access by EOD.*

An archived capture of the page at 12:00 PM shows a white background, a clean product header, and a support panel offering to help teams integrate Ember into existing workflows. Nothing in the copy acknowledges the building, the seal, the bodies, the live terminals photographed before digital forensics touched them, or the fact that GoodAI already has a script for telling mothers the details remain under investigation.

Ember ships on Tuesday.

The stock price jumps eleven percent.
