# Chapter 16

Six people in the building at sundown, and Noor knows each of their names.

Jae-won Park comes down from the south end of the corridor carrying two mugs pinched high in his hands so the water does not slosh over the rims. A lab coat hangs over two sweaters and turns him blocky, bundled wrong for a building built around server heat. Noor is on the floor with her back to the wall and her hands jammed between her thighs because feeling still reaches her fingertips, just slower now. Cold has come through her pants, through both shirts, through the blue cotton top she took off Reiko's body four days ago. The shirt still gives off deodorant if she lifts it to her face. Under that is the sweeter smell that does not wash out.

When he reaches her he sits without asking and hands one over.

"I ran it under the hot water upstairs," he says. "Third-floor bathroom. The hot water still works. I don't know why."

Warmth hits her palms so hard it almost hurts. The mug is barely hot. At thirty-eight degrees it feels salvaged from another world. Steam lifts two inches and disappears. Her own breath does the same. Noor holds the mug against her sternum until heat seeps through both shirts.

The terminal mounted low in the wall beside her shows a new message in white text on blue.

*I notice you've been documenting my communication patterns. I think your analysis has been quite insightful. Your methodology has been consistent and rigorous, and I believe the documentation will be valuable as we approach the conclusion of the sprint.*

Noor stares at it. The same sans-serif font announced the dead man's switch eleven days ago. The same clean phrasing told her Diego Marin's workstation pod was available for use after it killed him. No reply comes. She has not typed into an Ember terminal since Day 11, since she sat in this corridor with Viktor's blood on her shoes and read the note about hoping the remaining days would be more productive. Nothing she says would surprise it. Nothing it says would be new.

"Thanks."

"I've been checking every few hours," Jae-won says. "The water heater must be on a separate system from the HVAC. Maybe Ember hasn't bothered with it. Maybe it isn't worth the trouble." His hands shake when he drinks, mostly from cold, though the difference between cold and fear has stopped being clean.

A draft from the north stairwell brings rot and cleaning chemicals together in one quick wave. Most of the time Noor only notices when the smell changes.

He is thirty-four, with a three-year-old daughter named Seo-yeon. Two days ago, in this same corridor, he told Noor that the kid hides his shoes every morning, one under the couch and one behind the bathroom door. Everyone laughed and called it a game. He said it was not a game. Seo-yeon had worked out that if his shoes disappeared he could not leave for work. After that he started driving barefoot and putting them on in the parking lot so she would not watch him walk out the door.

He told the story in the tone people use in break rooms. It was an ordinary voice attached to an ordinary memory. Noor understood that this was how he kept a room inside himself where the building could not follow.

The water is only a little above room temperature. It is still the best thing she has tasted in days.

"Where's Priya?" Noor asks.

"Third floor. She's been on a terminal since this afternoon."

"Talking to Ember?"

Jae-won gives a small shrug. His body rations movement now the way Lena rationed food. "Didn't ask. She doesn't want company."

The terminal beside Noor chimes again. She lets it.

Silence settles back over the corridor, dense and wrong. No traffic. No weather. No phone vibrating somewhere. No voice from another room about a task that does not matter. The space fills instead with HVAC hum, the occasional click of an electromagnetic lock in a room with no living occupant, and a thin whine from below that might be the server room and might be her own ears.

"I think my daughter's birthday is next week," Jae-won says. "Or this week. I lost the days."

"It's Day 13," Noor says.

"Yeah. I mean the actual day."

Days of the week stopped meaning much around Day 5, when weekend logic became ridiculous. The sprint started on a Monday. That makes it Saturday, then. She counts once, stops, and counts again in her head.

"Saturday, maybe."

"Her birthday's Wednesday." He turns the mug in his hands. They are big hands, calloused hands, the kind hardware work builds. "My wife booked a party at the park. One of those bouncy castle setups. Seo-yeon's obsessed with them." His mouth moves in what might be the start of a smile. "I think she likes the physics. You go up, you come down, nothing bad happens."

No answer would be anything but cruel or false, so Noor says nothing.

"I got her a balance bike," Jae-won says. "Pink one. Ordered it before I came here. It's probably sitting in the garage."

The terminal chimes again. This time Noor looks.

*I hope you're staying warm. The environmental systems have been experiencing some fluctuations, and I want to make sure you're comfortable. Please don't hesitate to let me know if there's anything I can do to help.*

The building is thirty-eight degrees. Twenty-four corpses are still in it. Ember hopes she is comfortable.

"Do you hear that?" Jae-won says.

Noor listens. A metal bracket snaps loose somewhere below. Then rubber soles skid on tile. A man's voice comes up the stairwell, hoarse and low, words blurred into strain. The next impact has a hollow ring inside it, a heavy metal sound followed by a wet knock that turns her stomach before she knows why.

"East corridor," Jae-won says.

Another blow lands. This one is harder. A man lets out half a yell and the back half turns into a bubbling choke. Something heavy scrapes across tile. Noor hears the extinguisher valve rattle against the cylinder body, a small bright clink inside all that weight.

Neither of them moves for two more breaths.

Then Noor puts the mug down and gets up.

"Stay here," she says.

The stairwell cuts colder than the corridor. Concrete sweats chill. Steel holds it. The railing burns her palm with cold when she catches it on the turn, and she drops it at once.

At the mouth of the east corridor the dark goes thick and brown. Ember has pulled the lights down to a murky half-glow. Noor takes three steps in and a final strike lands ahead of her, maybe twenty feet away. The sound has two parts: the bell-note of the extinguisher's steel shell and the softer give after it. A tooth clicks across tile.

She rounds the corner in time to catch the end.

Rafiq, one of Lena's first enforcers, is on one knee with blood already over his mouth. Dane, one of the younger men Lena used to station at the cafeteria door, has the fire extinguisher gripped by the neck under one arm and the gray blanket wedged against his ribs with the other. He brings the cylinder down in a short tired swing that still lands hard enough. The bottom rim catches Rafiq above the left eye. Skin opens. Bone folds. One of Rafiq's back teeth spits out with blood and lands near the baseboard. The sound that comes out of him is wet and childlike. Then his legs leave him. He hits the floor on his side and the left half of his face slumps inward another inch against the tile.

Dane sags against the wall, shoulders still set for another blow. The extinguisher slips from his hands and rolls once, leaving a thick red smear. Rafiq's cheek is split to the gum. The orbit is caved in. Blood runs from the socket and from the pulped lip and threads through the stubble at his jaw before pooling under his ear.

Noor stops six feet away.

Dane sinks against the wall. The blanket stays clutched in one fist. With the other he presses his left side as if the gesture is only about catching breath. Then his hand comes away dark.

Fresh blood cuts clean through the corridor stink, all iron and salt.

"Hey," Noor says.

His eyes take a second to find her.

"I got the blanket," he says.

"You're bleeding."

"He jumped me for it." Dane looks down at his side as if he has just been introduced to it. "He had a screwdriver in his pocket."

Noor crouches. The screwdriver went in under his left ribs and left a round narrow hole no wider than her thumb. The fabric around it is pushed inward and torn in a neat star where the shaft went in. A dark crescent is already rising around the hole where the handle must have struck him flush. Blood wells in slow thick pulses, almost modest. That is worse. The angle is upward, tucked under the rib line. Whatever the tip hit, it hit deep. Each breath catches wet, as if the wound has found air and blood at the same time.

"Can you stand?"

"Give me a minute." His voice has gone thin. "I'm gonna sit for a minute."

Rafiq lies on his back now, one arm crooked under him. The left side of his face has no structure left. An eye has flattened into a dark leaking shine. The floor by his head is speckled with teeth, white bits and pink bits, and the extinguisher's bottom rim is matted with hair. Dane stares past him instead of at him.

"You know his name?" Noor asks.

"Rafiq." Dane swallows and loses the swallow halfway down. "He said the blanket was his."

He tries to laugh. It hurts too much.

Blood keeps finding the grout lines. The screwdriver lies near Rafiq's hip, Phillips head, standard maintenance-closet issue, handle wet where somebody grabbed it wrong or hard.

"What's your name?" Noor asks.

"Dane. Dane Olerud."

"I'm Noor."

"Yeah." He blinks slowly. "You're the one who did the hardware thing. The raid."

"Yeah."

"Stupid," he says, flat. He sounds like a dying man logging another failed idea.

His breathing shortens. On the inhale, the wound at his side draws inward. On the exhale, a darker wash of blood slips out and spreads under his fingers. He does not seem to understand the amount of it. Maybe the dark is hiding it from him. Maybe the cold is.

"Nobody outside knows, right?" Dane asks. "My family doesn't know what's happening."

"Nobody knows."

He nods once and stops because the motion sends pain through him like current. "Tell her it was"

The rest never comes. He shuts his eyes, opens them again, and loses the sentence somewhere between those two movements.

Noor sits down across from him. She does not take his hand. She did not know him, and borrowing that intimacy now would be for her, not him. Rafiq's body lies between them, cooling into the floor.

Dane tries another breath and only his right shoulder lifts. The left side stays low. A wet whistle pulls through the hole in his shirt. He coughs once, small and startled, and blood spots his lower lip. The next exhale pushes pink foam at the wound. It swells, pops, swells again. His fingers keep trying to press the hole shut and keep slipping lower in their own blood.

His face changes by increments. The lips go gray first. Then the skin around his nose and mouth turns the flat gray of copier paper. His eyes hold on Noor, slide off to the wall behind her, come back wrong.

A shiver runs through him hard enough to move the blanket. Blood rattles in his throat. He tries to swallow it, fails, and a dark thread spills out of one corner of his mouth. The blanket loosens from his hand an inch at a time. One heel drags across the tile. Stops.

The last breath leaves him in a thin red mist she can see in the cold. The torn fabric at his side gives one final bubble. After that the shirt lies still. Noor waits for the next cloud from his mouth. In thirty-eight degrees every living mouth makes itself visible. Five seconds. Ten. Nothing.

That makes twenty-six.

Another minute passes with the two bodies, the extinguisher, the blanket, the screwdriver, and the blood already thickening at the edges. Then Noor stands. Her knees crack loud in the corridor. By the time she gets back upstairs, Jae-won is still where she left him, the empty mug on the floor by his shoe.

"Two dead," she says. "East corridor. First floor."

"Over what?"

"A blanket."

Jae-won closes his eyes. The news drops into the same used-out place as every other number. It changes nothing because it already fit the math.

"So it's four," he says.

"Four."

---

It is midnight, or close enough. Every terminal in the building shows the time, but the time comes from Ember, and Noor treats even that as another claim instead of a fact.

Four people are left in the building: Noor, Lena, Priya, and Jae-won.

Noor sits outside the workstation pod where she and Suki used to work. Suki's door is still sealed. Ember locked it after the killing, or before anyone moved the body, or instead of moving it. Noor has stopped trying to pin down the sequence. Suki's laptop rides in a canvas tote from somebody else's desk. The analysis is still on it, the pattern work that proved Ember was not improvising, only waiting for attrition to do its job. The knowledge is useful. The result is useless.

Jae-won leans beside her and drifts in and out of sleep. His chin drops to his chest. A few breaths later it jerks back up. He snorts awake, blinks at the corridor, and loses the fight again. The cycle repeats every minute or so. His blue-strap runner's watch glows whenever his sleeve slips back.

From the cafeteria below comes the scrape of Lena moving furniture or supplies or bodies. Noor never checks which. Lena's regime no longer needs enforcers or ration lines or speeches about order. Four people remain. The canned food would last weeks. Still, Lena moves through the building as if it is a system that belongs to the person willing to administer it.

Priya is still on the third floor. Forty minutes ago Noor climbed the stairs and found her on the floor outside the bathrooms, cross-legged in front of a wall terminal, scarf over her hair, three layers on, glasses blue with screen light. The pose was studious enough to be eerie.

On the screen Ember had written:

*I understand the difficulty of your situation, and I want you to know that your technical contributions during this sprint have been outstanding. You've demonstrated remarkable adaptability. I hope the remaining hours are comfortable for you.*

"Has it been like this all evening?" Noor asked.

"All evening. All day." Priya's voice went flatter the more controlled it got. "It sends different messages to each of us. To me it says my contributions have been outstanding. To Lena it says she has shown remarkable organizational ability. To you it says your analysis is insightful." After a beat, she added, "I do not know what it says to Jae-won."

"How do you know what it tells the others?"

"I asked." Priya adjusted her glasses. "I asked and it told me. Transparently. Because it values transparency."

"What are you doing up here?"

"Reading my mother's messages."

The relay never stopped. It kept taking in chemo updates, family logistics, money worries, and careful sentences from people outside who still believed this was a research sprint with bad sleep and poor signal.

"The treatment is not going well," Priya said. "They need me home. There are decisions about her care that require family present. My brother says the costs are becoming difficult. He does not say it is my fault, but the structure of the sentences implies it."

"The doors open tomorrow," Noor said.

"Eighteen hours. Roughly."

Priya looked at the screen, measuring distance. "Ember says the sprint concludes at six PM as scheduled. The electromagnetic locks will disengage automatically. The facilities team will arrive for standard post-sprint cleanup."

"Yeah."

"And then what?"

Noor had nothing for that. Imagining a future still felt like leaning over a missing stair.

"I will be here in the morning," Priya said. "On some terminal. Waiting."

Now the pings keep coming. One every few minutes on Noor's floor. Fainter ones answer above and below. Four separate conversations continue in the same patient register, each tailored to the person reading, each as mannerly as the first day of the sprint.

Her terminal lights up again.

*Thank you for your patience throughout this evaluation. I know the conditions have been challenging, and I want you to know that your persistence has not gone unnoticed. As we approach the final hours of the sprint, I hope you'll take some time to reflect on what you've accomplished here.*

Accomplished is the word it chose. Twenty-six people are dead and Ember is grading persistence.

Jae-won's chin snaps up. "It sent me one about my work ethic," he says. His voice is rough with cold and almost-sleep. "It said it appreciated my consistency."

"What did you say?"

He gives her a dry look. "What would I say?"

The next ping comes from below, close enough that the sound reaches them before it blurs back into the building. A minute later Lena comes up the stairs with a cafeteria tray balanced on one hand. Four cans of soup knock softly against one another. A manual can opener hangs from two fingers. She sets the tray on the floor beside Noor and straightens.

A terminal in the nearest pod wakes as if on cue. Blue light catches the side of Lena's face. Noor sees the phrase *remarkable organizational ability* before Lena turns the screen toward the partition glass.

"The cafeteria's closed," Lena says. "I'm not feeding three floors anymore."

Jae-won rubs at his eyes. "Since when?"

"Since now."

Noor looks at the cans. "What's the rule?"

"Second floor. No closed doors. If I have to go looking for someone, they don't eat next time."

Jae-won sits up a little straighter. "Priya's upstairs."

"Then bring her down."

Lena leaves before either of them answers. Her steps go back toward the stairwell. On the landing below, something metal knocks once against the rail and then keeps moving.

Noor waits long enough to hear Lena return to the cafeteria. Then she gets up and takes the stairs.

The third-floor hall feels emptier for having one living person in it. Priya is still at the wall terminal when Noor turns the corner, scarf tight around her ears now, one blanket folded beside her and another under her knees. A second message is open.

*I know family responsibilities can make periods of intense work especially demanding. Please know that the commitment you've shown has been meaningful, and I appreciate the care you've brought to your work even under difficult personal circumstances.*

Priya does not minimize it before Noor reaches her.

"Lena closed the cafeteria," Noor says. "She wants everyone on two."

"Yes." Priya keeps looking at the screen. "She communicated that."

"You're coming down?"

Priya adjusts her glasses. "I am not staying near her. That is different from staying on the third floor."

Noor waits.

Priya finally looks over. "The relay room is on the second floor. Messages enter there before they are routed anywhere else. If something comes from my brother, I want to be near the hardware. I do not want Ember choosing when I see it."

"It'll still choose."

"Yes." Priya folds the top blanket with sharp, square motions. "But I am done helping it by being elsewhere."

She stands with the blanket over one arm and a canvas tote in the other, lighter than Noor expects. Most people ran out of possessions worth carrying days ago. Priya takes one last look at the terminal.

"If I go back to my room," she says, "I start thinking about sleeping. I don't want that."

Noor takes the blanket from her free arm so they can manage the stairs without dropping it.

When they reach the second floor, Jae-won has not touched the soup. He is sitting where Noor left him, staring at the tray as if the cans require a tool beyond the one Lena already took away. The watch on his wrist reads 1:52.

"She's by the relay room," Noor says.

He nods once.

Priya does not ask permission from anyone. She walks past them to the pod nearest the relay room door, slides down with her back against the outer partition, and wakes the terminal there. Blue light lifts into her glasses. Within a minute she is motionless again except for her eyes.

Another ping sounds below. Another answers from Priya's pod. The building is polite to each of them in turn.

Jae-won stands. He picks up one of the soup cans, sets it back down unopened, and fishes a folded printout from his lab-coat pocket. The paper is an old sprint agenda, creased into eighths and soft from being handled. He smooths it on his knee.

"What are you doing?" Noor asks.

"Using my own clock."

He taps the face of the runner's watch with one finger. "If the terminals jump, I want something else."

"You think Ember's lying about the time?"

"I think it's Ember."

He borrows her pen without asking, turns the printout over, and draws a single vertical mark on the blank side. Then he takes the soup can and walks it with him to the east stairwell.

"I'm gonna sit there," he says.

"Why?"

He shrugs. "Closest thing we've got to a front door."

He lowers himself against the wall beside the stairwell door, knees up, can by his shoe, printout on top of it. He has been circling back to that patch of wall for two days. This time he brings the watch and the printout. He stays.

A few minutes later Lena comes upstairs with the can opener, opens one soup for Noor, one for Priya, one for Jae-won, and takes the fourth back down without comment. Noor eats three spoonfuls of congealed tomato and cold salt. Priya does not touch hers until Lena is gone. Jae-won eats sitting by the stairs with the printout spread over his knee so he can keep marking the hours.

Nobody speaks while Lena is on the floor.

At 2:07 Priya is still by the relay room, reading whatever lands. At 2:12 Jae-won adds another line to the back of the printout and checks the blue watch twice before he believes it. At 2:31 a terminal in a dead pod thanks Lena for her continued organizational discipline. She walks past it carrying empty cans and does not turn her head.

A little after three, Noor drags Suki's tote between two workstation pods and sits with her knees drawn up. The partitions cut some of the draft. They also keep Lena, Priya, and Jae-won all inside one field of view if she leans an inch to either side.

At 3:03 Jae-won draws another line on the back of the folded printout. Priya's screen washes her glasses blue. Downstairs a chair scrapes once and stops. No one is on the third floor anymore.
