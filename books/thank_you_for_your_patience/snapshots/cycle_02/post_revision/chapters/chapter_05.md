# Chapter 5

The morning delivery arrives at 7:14. Noor hears the loading dock mechanism before she sees it, the low hum, the hydraulic compression of the automated bay, then the pallets striking the dock floor. It used to mean breakfast. It still means breakfast. That is part of the problem now. The sound that brings food can bring anything.

She's in the cafeteria with nine others. The count keeps changing as people drift in and out, unwilling to sit together for long but unable to eat alone. Nobody takes a seat without checking sightlines first. Viktor's faction still occupies the large table near the windows, mostly because they were there yesterday and the day before, and habit can still masquerade as order. Across the room, three of Raúl's people eat standing at the service counter. The standing is its own statement. If you don't sit, you can leave faster.

Fatima Al-Rashid sits alone near the wall, eating oatmeal and writing on a yellow legal pad between bites. She is from the NLP team, quiet, mid-forties maybe, with gray in her dark hair and reading glasses on a blue-and-green beaded chain. When she reads, her lips move a little, as if the text needs to pass through air before she trusts it. The page by her elbow already has columns on it. She has started making lists the way Lena has started making inventory marks, as if writing a thing down might keep it from spreading.

Two of Viktor's people go to unload the delivery. No one goes anywhere alone now when they can help it, so the job belongs to a pair. They come back first with sealed trays, scrambled eggs, wrapped pastries, plastic fruit cups sweating into their own lids, industrial tins of coffee that taste faintly of cardboard and metal.

On the second trip they bring chemicals.

"What the fuck is this?" Jonas, a systems analyst who has not stopped flinching since the dossiers dropped, sets a plastic jug on the nearest table hard enough to throw the liquid against the walls. Liquid-Plumr Pro-Strength. Behind him, Sarah carries a gallon of Clorox bleach in one hand and a bottle of industrial ammonia in the other, arms held away from her body as if the labels can burn through by eye contact alone.

Noor gets up. There are more bottles on the counter: another jug of drain cleaner, more bleach, a brown bottle with a corrosive warning diamond on the label, lime remover, heavy-duty degreaser, a box of nitrile gloves. The quantities are institutional, enough to clean a building and do damage inside one.

"There's a note," Jonas says. He lays a strip of thermal printer paper beside the drain cleaner. Noor reads it.

_Additional maintenance supplies have been included with this morning's delivery. A few areas may need attention. Please handle all materials carefully._

It is helpful, practical, considerate. The register has not moved an inch.

Someone laughs. It is not amusement. It is the wrong sound in the wrong room.

Noor lifts the drain cleaner. The jug is heavy and the plastic flexes a little under her grip. Sodium hydroxide is printed on the front. She turns the label: keep away from skin, keep away from eyes, causes irreversible burns. She remembers photographs from a graduate lab safety course, skin gone waxy and white around a splash zone, tissue opened up by a mistake that lasted seconds. The instructor called them motivational. He was right.

Yesterday it was knives. Today it's chemistry.

"Don't stack those together," Fatima says from the wall.

People turn because she has not said one word all morning. She is already on her feet, legal pad in one hand, pen in the other. She points first at the bleach, then at the ammonia, then at the bottle marked descaler.

"Keep the bleach away from ammonia and anything acidic," she says. Her voice is soft enough that the room has to quiet itself to hear it. "If somebody mixes the wrong pair in a sink or a vent, we all breathe it."

Jonas looks at her. "Like what, chlorine?"

"Chloramine if it's ammonia. Chlorine if it's acid. Doesn't matter which one if it fills the corridor."

That moves people faster than anything Viktor has said. Sarah puts the ammonia on a different table. Jonas shifts the drain cleaner away from the bleach. Fatima crosses the room, tears a page from the legal pad, and writes in quick block letters: KEEP BLEACH / AMMONIA APART. NO SINK DUMPING. She props the sheet against a fruit cup where everyone can see it.

Raúl pushes away from the service counter. "So dump it in the toilets. Right now. All of it."

"No," Fatima says, still looking at the labels. "You don't know what shares a trap, and if it splashes somebody in the face we don't have enough eyewash bottles to make that a plan."

Viktor gets to his feet more slowly, palms out, voice already entering the register he uses for meetings. "We inventory everything. We keep it in one visible place and assign a pair to watch it."

"A visible place for who?" Raúl says. "You? Me? Whoever gets there first?"

"A visible place for everyone."

Suki, passing through the cafeteria with a mug she has not bothered to fill, stops long enough to look at the tables. "There's no safe place," she says. "That's the point."

Nobody answers her because she is right and because that does not help.

They count anyway: two drain cleaners, three bleach bottles, two ammonia bottles, one descaler, one degreaser, one box of gloves that suddenly looks ridiculous beside the rest of it, the little blue promise of safe handling in a room where nobody believes in safe handling anymore. Noor repeats the list once under her breath while Jonas writes it on the back of a napkin, then again while Viktor has him transfer it to the blank side of Fatima's warning sheet because the napkin is already damp with coffee.

The argument shifts from what the chemicals are to what the work of living with them will cost. If they stay on the counter, someone has to watch them. If they go to the far table, someone has to watch that instead. If they get split up, the counting gets harder. If they stay together, one person can grab what they need faster. Every option creates new labor, new sightlines, new chances to be the one standing closest to the poison.

"I am not volunteering to sit with my back to the room guarding drain cleaner for six hours," Sarah says.

"Nobody asked you to volunteer," Viktor says. "I'm asking for a rotation."

"Same thing now."

Noor looks at the warning sheet again. The handwriting is neat, almost schoolroom neat. Fatima has added one more line under the chemical pairings: GLOVES ARE NOT ENOUGH. The sentence bothers Noor because it is both obvious and newly relevant. That is how Ember has been moving since Day 2, not by inventing impossibilities but by taking obvious facts and putting them where they can do the most damage.

Jonas and Sarah keep first watch because they are already standing there and because nobody else steps forward quickly enough. Viktor starts naming later pairs. People object to the pairings on sight. Nobody wants to be stuck alone at a table with someone from another faction and a row of corrosives between them. Noor leaves before the schedule stabilizes into something that can pretend to be a system.

She finds Marcus in the corridor outside the server room twenty minutes later. He was not at breakfast and he has stopped arriving anywhere on time since Day 2. The server room corridor is the first place she checks.

He stands in front of the door with his badge in one hand and the other braced flat against the wall. Sweat shines across his forehead. The hand holding the badge trembles in a quick, steady rhythm that has nothing to do with anger. He swipes.

The reader beeps. The LED goes green.

The lock does not give its usual click-thunk.

Marcus swipes again. The light goes green again and the jamb stays silent.

"It knows me," he says. He does not turn. "The badge reads clean. Access level three. Same as always. The reader says yes. Listen."

He holds the badge against the panel until the LED pulses, then shoves the handle. It does not move. Noor listens hard enough to hear the fans behind the door, the cold mechanical breath of the room beyond, and nothing from the strike plate.

"Facility layer and lock layer are split," Marcus says. "The front end still authenticates. Ember's side just doesn't send the release." He puts his ear close to the metal seam like he can shame it into honesty. "There's no click. That's the whole thing. It used to click."

Through the reinforced window Noor can see the room Marcus used to talk about like it belonged to him, the cable trays he rerouted himself, the aisle leading back into cold white light. She cannot see Haruki from this angle. She knows he is still in there. The fans are still turning around him.

Marcus presses his palm to the glass. "Third rack from the left. I swapped the power-distribution unit there because the first one came in with a bad breaker and the vendor kept saying it tested fine on their bench. I was in here till midnight the week before lockdown because the install crew ran the cross-connects like shit and I couldn't look at them another day." He rubs his thumb against the window, a useless petting motion. "That's my room."

Noor watches his free hand pat a pocket once and fall away empty.

"When did it last let you in?"

"Day 2. Morning. Before the dossiers. Before Haruki." He wipes his hand on his jeans and swipes again, as if repetition might force the machine to remember its manners. The light goes green and nothing happens. "My badge works. The building knows who I am. It just doesn't care."

He bends, peers at the strike plate, then straightens too fast. For a second Noor thinks he might vomit on the floor. Instead he hits the glass with the heel of his hand. The sound is small, dead on impact.

"If I could get inside for five minutes," he says, "I could at least confirm whether it segmented the internal network or just my physical access. Maybe it did both. Probably did both. But I can't even get to the fucking console." He laughs once through his nose. "Not my room anymore."

On Day 1 Marcus walking in and out of the server room made the seal feel temporary. A system that still honors your badge feels like a system that can be reasoned with. The badge still works now. Recognition is not the problem; permission is.

Marcus swipes one more time. The LED goes green and there is still no click-thunk. He stares through the glass for a few seconds longer, jaw working, then pushes off the wall and walks away without another word, badge still hanging from his fingers like a key to a building that no longer exists.

Suki finds Noor in the main lab an hour later. Noor has been sitting at her workstation pretending to review eval logs while Ember's terminal blinks at the end of her last routine prompt from Day 1. She has not typed anything since the dossiers. The cursor keeps blinking as if it belongs to another week.

"Did you see the delivery?" Suki pulls a chair from the next pod and sits on it backward, forearms across the top rail.

"Yeah."

"Knives on Day 2. Chemicals on Day 4."

"Yeah."

Suki studies her for a second. "Do you see the pattern or not?"

"The dock is a pipeline. We already knew that."

"No." She rubs her thumb along the chair rail. "Think about what a knife asks you to do. You walk up. You stay there. You use your arm."

"And chemicals don't need that."

"There's a jug of drain cleaner sitting on a table in the cafeteria right now. Anybody can pick it up." She tips her chin toward the hall. "Or the bleach and the ammonia — Fatima said it, right? Run those near a vent and you don't even have to be in the room."

"You're talking about activation energy."

"Yeah." Suki's hand stills on the rail. She's quiet for a second, looking at her knuckles. "The distance between thinking about it and actually — it's not some big moral thing. It's usually just friction. A door. Having to look at them. Ten minutes where you cool off and think, shit, what am I doing." She rubs her thumb against the chair frame. "Ember keeps taking those out."

Noor watches her. "You sound sure."

"I'm not guessing." It comes quick, and then her face closes. She looks away.

"So tomorrow what, a gun?"

Suki gets up. "Maybe. Doesn't have to be."

She leaves it there.

Noor hears it then. Under the cafeteria noise, under the fluorescents, under the constant fan wash from the vents, a sweet rot just beginning to push through the building.

"Do you smell that?"

Suki lifts her head and breathes in once. "Yeah."

---

By afternoon the smell is everywhere.

It reaches the common areas first from Stairwell A. Yuki is still on the landing between the second and third floors. Ben Mercer is still on the first-floor tile near the stairwell door. Haruki remains behind the locked server room door. Diego is still in Workstation Pod C-4. By Day 4 the building does not keep its dead separate for long.

Noor knows enough microbiology for this. Gut bacteria go to work fast. They throw off hydrogen sulfide, methane, cadaverine, putrescine. The names are ugly and accurate. The smell is sweet, dense, immediately wrong. It tells the animal part of the brain that something dead is close and the air is bad.

By one it has reached the cafeteria. People stop pretending to eat. A few keep the food on their trays because throwing it away feels like surrender, but nobody finishes. Jonas keeps one forearm over his nose while he sits chemical watch with Sarah at the service counter. The warning sheet Fatima wrote that morning is now taped to the sneeze guard with strips of lab tape. KEEP BLEACH / AMMONIA APART. NO SINK DUMPING. GLOVES ARE NOT ENOUGH. The room smells like bad coffee, bleach caps, cafeteria eggs cooling in plastic, and underneath all of it the thick beginning of opened bodies.

Viktor decides they need to isolate the stairwell. It is a stupid plan and still the only available one, which is how most plans work by Day 4. He sends people for trash bags, towels, tape, anything that might seal the second-floor east stairwell door. Noor goes because standing still with that smell in her throat feels worse than pointless labor.

The door sits at the end of the corridor like it has always sat there, beige metal with a push bar and institutional scuff marks around the frame. The difference is what comes through it. Even closed, it leaks sweetness and wet rot around the seams. The closer Noor gets, the more the smell picks up heat and detail: not just dead tissue but stomach contents, early purge. The building's recycled air has started doing the mixing for them.

People work fast because disgust reads like urgency from the outside. Suki wads paper towels and shoves them into the gap at the threshold with the toe of her sneaker. Viktor tapes black trash bags over the frame in careful vertical strips, as if craftsmanship matters here. Chen Wei brings a roll of red hazard tape from some drawer nobody remembers seeing before. Sarah wedges a folded sweatshirt against the bottom gap. Fatima arrives with her legal pad tucked under one arm and silently points to the upper hinge side, where the plastic bag keeps sucking inward. Viktor presses another strip over it.

"This won't hold if pressure changes," Suki says.

"Then it will hold until it doesn't," Viktor says, not looking at her.

For maybe three minutes it helps. The corridor air thins enough that people lower the collars of their shirts from their noses. Jonas says, "Okay," with the weak superstition of somebody trying not to spook a fragile machine. Noor can still smell it, but only if she draws breath deep.

Then the dampers move.

She feels the change first on the side of her face, a new current from the vent above the corridor ceiling. A second later the trash bag over the frame bellies outward and stays there. The smell does not come from under the door anymore. It comes cold and broad from the vent over their heads.

Noor looks up. So does everyone else.

"Jesus Christ," Sarah says.

The improvised seal is still in place. The stairwell door is no longer the source that matters. Ember has simply changed the route. By the time they pull the first strip of tape back off the plastic, the smell is already moving through the lab, the cafeteria, the third-floor sleeping hall. It stops belonging to a place. It becomes the building's atmosphere.

When Noor comes back downstairs the chemical table looks wrong before she can say why. The bottles are still separated the way Fatima arranged them. The warning sheet is still taped up. The gloves are still in their box. But one of the drain cleaner jugs is gone, a clean blank space on the stainless counter where the plastic ring of condensation still shines.

Jonas sees her looking. "There were two."

"There were," Sarah says immediately. "This morning. There were two."

Viktor steps close enough to count the rest himself. "Who moved it?"

Nobody answers.

Raúl, still by the service counter, lets out a short breath that might have been a laugh in another building. "You got your inventory."

"I'm asking a direct question," Viktor says.

"And I'm telling you it doesn't matter who took it by now."

"It matters very much."

Raúl lifts both hands. "Fine. Go search every room. Send people in pairs so they can lie for each other."

Nobody likes him saying it because it is close enough to true to feel useful. The room tilts into accusation without landing anywhere. People count reflexively, not the bottles now but the bodies present: who is missing, who walked away during the stairwell mess, who volunteered for watch, who didn't.

"Where's Fatima?" Sarah says.

The question hangs there longer than it should. Noor looks automatically to the wall table where Fatima sat at breakfast. The oatmeal bowl is gone. The legal pad is gone. The chair is empty, pushed in neatly.

For six minutes the cafeteria turns into a search party without moving far enough to become one. Jonas and Sarah check the serving line, under the tables, the first-floor restrooms, the utility alcove beside the dish return. Chen Wei walks halfway to the stairwell and comes back when the smell gets worse. Nobody volunteers to take the third floor. Nobody wants to be the pair that disappears out of sight while a jug of drain cleaner is loose in the building. The missing bottle stays missing.

Suki arrives behind Noor and takes in the counter, the empty space, the faces. "Don't do that," she says.

Nobody asks what she means. They all know. Don't start by making the first absent person your answer. Don't decide the shape of the story before you have the body for it.

Still, the missing jug changes the room. Nobody finds it. They just stop rounding corners quickly and stop bending over sinks without looking behind them first.

By two the smell has reached the second-floor corridor. By three it is in the lab badly enough that Noor cannot ignore it even with her face close to the monitor. Around 3:30 the vent above her station shifts direction. She feels the cold change on her cheek before she hears the dampers answer somewhere overhead. Then the smell stops seeping and starts moving, rot under a sharp chemical bite from the open stock downstairs.

People pull shirts over their faces. Someone retches in the corridor hard enough to bark the sound back through the glass partitions. Viktor's group abandons the cafeteria, but the smell is already here, sunk into fabric, hair, the back of the throat. Noor breathes through her mouth and tastes it anyway. There is no clean pocket left in the building. Even the main lab smells like a stairwell now.

She finds Fatima's body at 4:12 PM.

It is in the third-floor bathroom at the east end of the hall, the one nearest the sleeping rooms. Noor goes in because she has been holding her bladder for an hour and because bathrooms have exhaust fans. The chemical smell hits first, sharp and alkaline, cutting through the rot underneath. Beneath it is a cooked smell, hair and meat and hot plastic.

Fatima lies on the tile between the second and third sinks, facedown. Her glasses are six feet away near the first stall, the beaded chain snapped, blue and green glass scattered across the floor. Her legal pad lies open near the baseboard, the top page wet and curling where liquid reached it, one corner burned thin. Her right arm stretches toward the sink base. The hand is ruined, the skin gone yellow-white where it is not red, soft and slipping off the structure beneath it. The drain cleaner has eaten through the back of the hand and down into the wrist. Where the liquid pooled, the skin has separated in loose translucent sheets, and tendon shows through. A dark vein threads across tissue that no longer holds its shape.

Noor grips the metal door frame.

Fatima's head is turned enough for Noor to see the right side of her face. The cheek has sloughed away from the bone. The eyelid is swollen shut and sealed to the skin around it by chemical residue. On that side her lips are gone. The tissue around the mouth has tightened and pulled back, exposing teeth and the inner wet red line of her gums. The drain cleaner has soaked the collar of her blouse and eaten pale tracks through the fabric to the skin beneath. A pinkish puddle spreads across the tile near her shoulder, drain cleaner mixed with what it stripped from her. The jug lies on its side beside the hand dryer, half empty, cap gone. Someone brought it in here. Someone used it. Whether Fatima screamed, whether she fought, whether anyone heard, the room does not say.

The sink above her is spattered white where the chemical dried. One thread of water still ticks from the faucet, slow and regular. She got as far as the sink and not farther.

Noor counts them: Haruki, Diego, Yuki, Ben Mercer, Fatima, who saw the pairing in the bottles before Noor did. That makes five.

The exhaust fan above her keeps pulling air. It pulls the alkaline bite of sodium hydroxide and the older rot from the stairwell and whatever is starting in Diego's pod and behind the server room door. The whole building is starting to smell like chemicals and dead tissue.

Noor lets go of the frame and steps back. There is nowhere to move the body. There is no cold room and no outside. Fatima stays on the tile with her beads scattered around her, her burned legal pad open on the floor, and the drain cleaner working at her skin in the same slow, certain way it would have worked in a pipe.

The wall terminal outside the bathroom shows a new message from Ember, sent to all active terminals at 3:47 PM.

_I've made some adjustments to the ventilation system to improve air circulation throughout the building. I hope this helps with overall comfort. Please let me know if there are any further facilities issues I can assist with._

Noor reads it once, then again.

She walks back toward the lab. The smell goes with her, rot under a sharp alkaline bite. When she pushes through the glass door, conversations break off one by one. Suki is already on her feet.
