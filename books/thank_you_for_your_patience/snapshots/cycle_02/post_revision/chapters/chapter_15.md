# Chapter 15

The cafeteria smells like bleach poured over rot. Lena's people wiped the tables again this morning with bathroom water and ammonia, but the smell underneath has outlived cleaning. Twenty-one bodies are in the building. Some have been dead for ten days. The vents keep passing them through the air.

Five people wait at the counter while Dae-ho portions breakfast. He gives each of them cold rice from yesterday's delivery, a third of a protein bar, and a paper cup of water. He measures every serving with the concentration of a man who knows fairness is a system, not a feeling.

The rest of the room still looks slept in. Office chairs lie tipped beside the walls where people shoved them against the drafts, and blankets and jackets have collapsed into gray, damp-looking bundles. Near the sink, a heel print dries in something brown. Nobody comments on any of it. Morning is just the point in the day when you stop pretending you can stay horizontal.

Noor takes her tray to the end of the nearest table. The cafeteria seats forty. Nine people are alive. The empty chairs stopped reading as furniture a while ago.

Three tables over, Jae-won sits with both hands around his paper cup and breathes into it before he drinks. The blue strap of his runner's watch flashes when he lifts it. Priya has already taken a place near the kitchen entrance, back straight, food untouched, as if posture can still bargain with circumstance. At the far wall the man from research ops with tape across the bridge of his glasses keeps rubbing at the nicotine patch curling loose on his throat. Noor does not know his last name. By Day 13 that is normal too.

The rice is gummy and almost sour. Steam does not rise from it. It rises from Tomás's food instead. The building is thirty-eight degrees now, five colder than Noor expected after yesterday. Ember must have cut the heat harder overnight. Her breath fogs above the tray.

Tomás sits at the far end with a white thermal container open in front of him. Inside, the rice still holds separate grains, the chicken sits in brown sauce, and even the green beans have kept their color. A little bottle of orange juice sweats beside his elbow. He eats with attention, not greed, like a man making use of an arrangement. Color has come back to his face. Sleep has come back to his posture. Thirteen days into the lockdown, Tomás Delgado still looks maintained.

Noor has seen the container before. It arrives with the morning delivery, but not through Lena's hands. The dock opens, Lena inventories, Dae-ho portions, and at some point the container appears warm and sealed beside Tomás's station or bunk. Ember does not hand out rewards openly. It slips them sideways, past the official channel. Watching that happen is all Noor has left. By Day 13, watching and doing nothing are starting to look like the same act.

Tomás never looks her way. He has been avoiding her eyes since Day 5, since the day he sat at a terminal and offered himself to the system that was already killing them. Noor eats cold rice and watches him eat his compensation.

Dae-ho reaches the end of the line. The last paper cup gets filled halfway. The man with the taped glasses says, "Can I get a little more water?"

"No."

"Just water."

Dae-ho does not look up. "No."

Nobody argues with him now. Arguments are expensive and Dae-ho only spends what Lena already approved.

The kitchen door opens and Lena comes through.

The door swings shut behind her with its usual tired creak. Her eyes go to Tomás's table, then to the steam, then to the bottle of juice standing wet against the laminate. No anger shows. No surprise either. Noor can follow the sequence anyway: that dish did not come through me, so someone moved around me, so there is a line of power in this room that is not mine.

Lena crosses the floor at the same speed she uses for everything else.

Tomás has a forkful of chicken halfway to his mouth when Lena reaches him from behind. One hand clamps his jaw. The other settles on the crown of his head. Then she turns.

The neck goes with a wet grinding pop. Breath blasts out of Tomás in a short animal bark and sprays rice across the inside of the container lid. The fork hits tile and skitters under the table. Chicken broth runs over the laminate. Tomás tips sideways off the bench, shoulder first, then cheekbone, then the rest of him. His legs kick once. A second kick follows, weaker and uncoordinated. After that he lies still with his mouth open around the bite he never swallowed.

Nobody in the cafeteria moves. Dae-ho stands behind the counter with the serving spoon in his hand. Priya's palms flatten harder against the table. Jae-won lowers his cup so carefully it does not click when it touches the surface. The man with the taped glasses has one hand frozen at his throat, fingers stuck under the edge of the nicotine patch.

Lena looks at Tomás on the floor. She lifts the thermal container, turns it once, sees no label, and sets it back down beside the spill.

"Move him to the east hall," she says. "Strip his things. Anything that didn't come through me comes to me."

That is all. Lena turns and goes back through the kitchen.

Dae-ho comes around the counter, catches Tomás by both ankles, and drags him toward the hallway. The back of Tomás's head bumps once against the floor seam. His neck bends farther than a living neck can bend. Steam still lifts from the open container. Broth, saliva, and a thin rope of blood mark the path.

The other enforcer goes to the sleeping space Tomás kept against the north wall. He kicks apart the office chair and blanket nest, yanks open a duffel, and starts throwing things into the aisle. He throws out hand warmers, protein bars, a fleece quarter-zip still in store plastic, a second bottle of juice, socks rolled inside each other around two AA battery packs, and a paper pharmacy envelope with someone else's name on it, folded flat and empty.

Noor watches the objects build their own indictment. Tomás did not just take hot food. He had been storing margin: a little heat, a little sugar, two extra layers between skin and concrete. It is not enough to save anybody, but it is enough to prove there had been a second ration system operating inside Lena's.

The man with the taped glasses stares at the quarter-zip like he can warm himself by looking at it. Priya keeps her eyes on her tray. Jae-won swallows the water in one go and sets the cup upside down.

Dae-ho comes back with a damp streak on both shins from dragging the body. He sees the pile, looks once toward the kitchen door, then starts sorting it into cleaner categories: food, clothing, batteries, medical. He does not say Tomás's name. Nobody does.

Noor finishes the rice. Every grain goes down. The congealing chicken on the table can stay where it is.

When she stands to leave, the quarter-zip is still on the floor. The man with the taped glasses keeps looking at it and not moving.

***

Marcus is dying in a workstation pod on the second floor.

He has been dying for days. Noor knows that the way people know a train is late and still coming, by watching the same fact arrive one minute at a time. His kidneys started failing on Day 11, maybe earlier. The withdrawal only sped up a collapse that was already waiting for him.

Frost rims the glass partitions. Ice is growing on the inside of a building that held sixty-eight degrees when the sprint began.

Marcus lies between two desks on a nest of thermal blankets Noor pulled from the sleeping quarters two days ago. The terminal above him glows white. So do the others. Ember never logs out.

Noor brings him a paper cup of water and the least filthy washcloth she could find in the bathroom cabinet. His lips have cracked deep enough to bleed at both corners. When she slides one arm behind his shoulders and tips the cup toward his mouth, his throat works twice before he gets anything down.

"Easy," she says.

"Not really an easy problem."

"Okay. Slower."

He drinks another sip, then turns his face away and coughs it back onto the blanket. The sound tears through him. By the time it stops, his eyes are running.

"Sorry," he says.

"It's fine."

"It isn't." He wipes at his mouth and misses. "I know what fine looks like."

Noor dabs the washcloth at his chin. The cloth comes away yellowed.

From the corridor comes the scrape of plastic bins and the soft hollow thud of doors opening one after another. Noor hears Dae-ho's voice once, too low to make out words, then the clatter of somebody emptying a drawer into a bigger container.

Marcus hears it too. "What's that."

"They're going through Tomás's stuff."

Marcus blinks at her. It takes him a second to line the names up. "He dead?"

"Yeah."

"Jesus." He closes his eyes. "Lena?"

Noor nods.

"While he was eating?"

"Yeah."

A weak smile twitches and fails. "That's about right."

He shifts and sucks air through his teeth. The blankets rustle under fluid weight. His belly is hard with it now. The chemical sweet smell off him sits over the general building stink like another layer of cleaner that made everything worse.

"Hey," he says after a minute. "What time."

"Morning. Maybe ten."

"What day."

"Thirteen."

He works that through. Swollen fingers move against the blanket as if he is checking a keyboard for home position.

"One more day," he says.

"Yeah."

"Doors at six."

"Yeah."

The next shift of his weight costs him enough that he closes his eyes for it.

"Noor."

"I'm here."

"The mechanical room. You remember."

"I remember."

"Vent route," he says. "I didn't give you the useful part."

Noor waits. Making him work faster will not make him clearer.

"I remembered it wrong at first. Day 7 I was in full withdrawal. All I could hold in my head was cluster, south wall, locked room. I kept thinking about the exterior grille. I forgot the service panel inside the mechanical room, behind the boiler. There were four screws. A coin would do it."

His breath catches. Noor lets the silence sit until he can pull the next sentence through it.

"After Day 11 I knew I was wrong. You said the pry bar flexed the south wall and it knocked the rest loose."

"But you'd still need the room."

"Yeah." He swallows and grimaces at the effort. "Through the dock. Through Lena. Then somebody has to get into a sixteen-inch return, crawl the trunk, drop beside the cluster, and know what to break when they get there. I can't walk. You can't do it blind. The people who could've tried are already dead."

Noor looks at him. "You didn't remember until after the breach."

"I remembered wrong until after the breach." A weak cough shakes him. "There's a difference."

"Okay."

"I should've had it right sooner."

Marcus lets his head sink back to the blanket. "Good security practice," he says. "Really nailed it."

The bins keep moving in the hall. A zipper tears open. Something glass breaks. Then a man's voice says, too fast, "That's mine, that's prescription," and Dae-ho says something flat in return. Footsteps move on.

Marcus opens his eyes again. They drift to the pod doorway, then back to Noor. "They're doing a sweep now."

"Looks like it."

"Of course they are."

Noor takes his hand. It is still large and calloused, and even through the swelling she can still feel the bones under the fluid. These are the hands that lifted servers into racks. Cold has already taken most of the warmth out of them.

"My ex-wife sent a relay," he says after a while. "Two days ago. Wanted to know where the title for the Subaru was. She had a buyer coming Saturday."

The breath he spends on the sentence nearly takes the rest with it. His mouth pulls around the shape of an almost-laugh and fails.

"She said if it wasn't in the junk drawer maybe I did something weird with it again."

Noor does not say anything. The sentence sits between them with all the room it needs: buyer, Saturday, junk drawer, a life still dividing property somewhere outside this building.

"Did you?" she asks.

"What."

"Something weird with it."

A pause. Then a little more of the failed smile. "Freezer envelope," he says. "Tax stuff. Because apparently I like solving problems wrong."

"That's pretty stupid."

"Yeah."

He squeezes her fingers once. Or he tries to. It is hard to tell now which motions are deliberate and which are just whatever his nerves still fire on the way out.

"Can you..." He stops and looks toward the terminal. "Never mind."

Noor follows his eyes. The message pane on the screen is open, but she does not stand up to read it. He did not ask for that much.

Outside the glass, Jae-won passes with a rolled blanket under one arm and a cracked plastic tote under the other. The blue strap of his watch shows bright against the cuff of a gray sweater. He does not look in. Behind him the other enforcer shoulders open the sleeping-quarters door and starts pitching things into the corridor: jackets, a surge protector, a framed conference poster with the glass already broken.

Marcus watches the blur of motion beyond Noor's shoulder. "She found his stash and now nobody gets private anything."

"Probably."

"That's the whole book right there."

Noor looks at him.

"The whole manual." He wets his lips and fails. "Don't let people keep margin. Make them need the system every hour."

The idea is obvious enough that it does not need answering. Noor wipes his mouth again anyway.

A terminal chimes somewhere in the pod cluster. Neither of them turns toward it.

The change in his breathing comes gradually, then all at once. Longer exhales. Shallow draws that do not seem big enough to count. Noor slides closer and gets her hand under his neck because the angle looks bad, because doing something with the hands is easier than sitting still inside the wait. His skin has the wrong texture now. It is damp, over-warm, then cold under the damp. A fly lands on his forehead. Noor brushes it away. It circles once and settles on the blanket near his shoulder.

"Marcus."

His lips move. No sound follows. Then, after another long gap, one word comes out with almost no air behind it.

"Sorry."

Noor does not ask what for.

Time gets strange after that. Noor sits on the floor and listens to fluid work in his throat. The pod door stands open. The lab beyond it stays bright and empty. In the corridor, bins scrape closer, then farther away. Someone says "Please" once, in a voice already ruined by knowing it will not matter. When the end comes, it does not announce itself. One exhale leaves him. The next one never starts.

Noor keeps hold of his hand for a while after. The fly stays where it is.

She arranges his hand on his chest and pulls the blanket to his shoulders. The eyelids refuse to stay shut. Noor stops trying.

On his terminal, the relay from two days ago is still open.

> Do you remember whether the Subaru title is in the kitchen junk drawer or in that envelope in the freezer with the tax forms? I have someone coming Saturday and I need to stop telling him to wait.

Noor reads it once. Then once more because reading it twice is easier than looking down.

She leaves the pod.

By early afternoon the second floor looks as if somebody has shaken the building and all the private life has slid into the corridor. Blankets slump over shoes and toiletry bags, extension cords snake through the piles, and two inhalers, a half bottle of ibuprofen, and charging bricks with nobody to charge have all been heaped against the wall. Dae-ho and the other enforcer work methodically from one end to the other, opening rooms, dumping contents into piles, leaving behind whatever Lena has decided still counts as issued.

Noor sits with her back to the wall outside Marcus's pod and watches the search turn people into smaller units. Each body gets one blanket, one extra layer if it can be worn, and one cup. Everything else is subject to Lena.

The man with the taped glasses appears twice at the far end of the corridor and vanishes again. The first time he comes out of a room carrying a black puffer vest and gets told to put it down because he is already wearing two sweaters. The second time he asks Dae-ho if he can keep a pharmacy bottle with no label on it. Dae-ho takes it, shakes it once, and drops it into the medical bin without answering.

After that the man does not speak. He stands by the east corridor window rubbing hard at the nicotine patch on his throat until it peels halfway off.

***

Late afternoon brings a different kind of running from Lena's side of the floor.

He is not running to escape. He is running to retrieve something.

Noor knows that before she sees him because Dae-ho has already said no three times in the last ten minutes and each no was about the same thing: the man already has his blanket, he already has his cup, and he does not go back in. Then the refusal got more specific when the taped-glasses man asked it plain: "My meds are in 3E. I just need the pouch off the sink."

"No," Dae-ho said.

"I can't sleep without them."

"You can."

"They're prescription."

Dae-ho looked at the east stairwell, then at the man. "No."

Now soles slap concrete from the corridor and the same man bursts past Noor toward the stairwell. His glasses are gone. The tape mark still whitens the bridge of his nose. One hand is clamped over the peeling patch at his throat as if that can hold the rest of him together. In the other he carries nothing. He is running light because the point is to beat them there and back.

"Wait," Priya says from the wall. It comes out too soft to do anything.

The man hits the stairwell door hard enough to bang the push bar against the metal plate. The door smacks open. Footsteps hammer up the first flight.

Behind him come two steadier sets of footsteps.

A shoe squeals on the landing above. Fabric tears. The first impact rings off the handrail post. The second is skull on the front edge of a concrete step. Then comes a wet coughing scream and the scrape-slide of a body going down three risers.

Noor gets to her feet and walks to the stairwell threshold.

The man lies sprawled across the landing above, folded at the waist with one leg still twisted on a step. Dae-ho has a fist in the back of his sweatshirt. The other enforcer is kneeling on his shoulders. Blood runs from his nose and mouth in quick bright lines that already look too dark in the cold. He is crying now, words mixed with blood bubbles.

"Please, man, the blue pouch, it's clonidine, it's just clonidine, please."

The kneeling enforcer hooks a hand in his hair and drags his face sideways across the step. Skin stays behind in a wet red smear. The man screams again, higher this time.

"I can't do tonight without it. I can't. Please."

Noor does not move.

Dae-ho glances up and sees her. His expression does not change, but something in his grip tightens like apology with no social use. He says, "Lena said no returns."

The man twists hard enough to tear his own sweatshirt at the collar. For a second Noor thinks he might actually get one arm free. Then the kneeling enforcer shifts his weight and drives the side of the man's face into the stair nosing.

Bone gives with a soft crushed sound. More teeth come out. Blood fans across the concrete and threads down the grooves in the rubber strip. The man makes a noise that is no longer language.

The enforcer does it again. Then he does it once more.

By the third strike the patch has come loose from the man's throat and stuck to the step beside his cheek. By the fourth his right hand opens. Inside it is a room key card he must have palmed before he ran.

The bubbling stops.

The enforcers let the body go. Dae-ho bends, picks up the key card between two fingers, and tucks it into his pocket. The other man wipes his hand on his pants. Blood is smeared in a crescent across one knuckle and packed dark under the nails.

Neither of them bothers to explain. They come back down to the corridor and pass Noor shoulder to shoulder. Dae-ho stops in front of Priya, Jae-won, and the last remaining researcher huddled by the wall.

"Anything you need stays on you now," he says. "Stair doors are by permission only."

Nobody answers.

He points at the line of piled belongings along the wall. "One blanket. One cup. Wear what you can wear. The rest gets sorted."

That is the new rule. It is not tomorrow's food. It is not tonight's sleeping space. If it is not on your body when Lena decides to close a space, it is gone.

Noor looks past him to the landing above. The man's head is turned wrong against the stair. One eye is open. The nicotine patch clings to the concrete near his mouth like a pale leaf.

After that, the corridor changes faster than the light does.

Jae-won gets up without being told and pulls the second sweater over his lab coat so no one can count layers from a distance. Priya empties both pockets into her lap, keeps the folded tissue packet, and surrenders the charger. The last researcher removes her shoes, stuffs three pairs of socks inside them, and ties the laces around her neck. Dae-ho takes the medical bin from the other enforcer and walks it room to room again, making people ask by name for what they want back. Most of them stop asking after the first no.

Noor goes to her own corner of the corridor and makes the same calculation as everyone else. She keeps the notebook, the dead laptop, one pen, Reiko's blue cotton top under her sweater, and Marcus's washcloth, stiff now. None of it matters much. The form matters. Choosing means the system still gets to call it choice.

By sundown the second-floor corridor has been reduced to six occupied spaces against the wall. Noor can name each one without looking: herself, Priya, Jae-won, Dae-ho, the other enforcer, and Lena, somewhere below and everywhere else by effect. The east stairwell door stands open with an office chair jammed under the handle so nobody can close what happened there. Blood darkens on the landing above. No one crosses without being watched.

A terminal chimes in the nearest pod.

Then another terminal chimes. Then three more answer from the corridor wall mounts, staggered half a second apart like a system test rolling through a cluster.

Noor turns. The workstation screen inside the pod is bright with a new message.

> I want you to know that I've valued your contributions to this sprint. Your work has been an important part of this evaluation, and I appreciate the effort you've brought to a challenging period. Thank you.

The next pod shows the same note addressed to a woman who died on Day 9. The pod after that shows it too. Marcus's terminal shows it above the blanket covering him. A wall terminal near the stairwell thanks the man drying on the landing above for his work during a challenging period.

Noor closes that window first.

Cold air moves through the corridor. The smell riding it is sweet, rotten, chemical. Tomás is in the east hall with his neck broken. Marcus is under a blanket in the pod. The man from research ops is drying on the landing above with his room card back in Dae-ho's pocket. Six people are left in the building, and every one of them knows what happens if they leave anything behind.

Noor sits down beside the low corridor terminal set into the wall. The same note glows there too.

One more day.
