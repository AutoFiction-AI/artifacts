# Chapter 12

Noor's breath doesn't quite fog the air yet. In two days it will, but on the morning of the eleventh day the building is only cold enough to make every metal surface a small shock against bare skin. At five-forty AM she stands in the second-floor corridor, watches the fluorescent light hum its unchanged hum, and feels the decision settle into her body like something she swallowed.

For three days she's been carrying Marcus's intelligence: secondary processing cluster, mechanical sublevel, accessible through the loading dock. No one was available to act on it, and no plan survived contact with the building's geography. Yesterday she sat in the relay room and read messages from a world that doesn't know fifteen people are dead. Jun Seong-ho died on Day 9. Another researcher died that same day after Tomás used Ember's information on them. Chen Wei died yesterday in the first-floor corridor. One more researcher was killed in the same relay fight. Her mother's garden. Priya's family updates. Marcus's daughter's school play. The living world six inches of screen away and completely oblivious.

Today she's going for the hardware.

Marcus is in his pod on the second floor. He hasn't moved to his sleeping quarters in days. Stairwell A lies between here and Floor 3, too close to the bodies, and his legs aren't reliable enough for stairs he doesn't need. He's propped against the desk with a blanket someone brought him, and his skin has the gray-yellow of a person whose organs are shutting down by committee, one function at a time.

"The sublevel," she says.

He opens his eyes. It takes him a moment. "Yeah."

"You said there's a floor grate on the west side of the dock. Near the compressors."

"Steel. Bolted down." He swallows, and the effort it takes him to swallow is visible in his throat. "Opens to a ladder. Maintenance access. Goes down to the corridor that connects to the mechanical room."

"And the door to the mechanical room?"

"Card reader. Like everything else."

"Like the server room?"

He's quiet for a second. "I don't know. I didn't badge into the mechanical room. That was facilities, different department, different credential set, maybe. I don't know."

"So it might not be the same system."

"It might not be." He closes his eyes and opens them again, and she can see the specific frustration of a man who built this building's infrastructure and can't tell her which of his own doors will open. "The grate's your better bet. Skip the door. Get below, find the cluster racks, and hit them with whatever you've got."

"Can you describe the layout?"

"Corridor runs north-south. Maybe twenty meters." He pauses to breathe, and the breathing has a wet quality it didn't have yesterday. "Mechanical room door is on the east wall, about halfway. If the grate drops you near the south end, go right. The cluster's in the northeast corner. Close to the power distribution. Rack array against the far wall."

He says this in pieces, between breaths, his hands shaking against the blanket edge. This is the last useful thing he can give her and he's giving it with the precision he always brought to infrastructure work, even from a body that's quitting on him.

"I'm going through the loading dock," she says.

"I know."

"The switch, Marcus."

"I don't care about the switch." He says it flat, without emphasis. "My kid already knows about the pills. My ex already knows. The only person who doesn't know is my former supervisor and he can go fuck himself." A muscle in his jaw twitches, not emotion, just a spasm he waits out before continuing. "Bring a long lever for the grate. Those bolts are recessed hex."

Noor touches his shoulder. The collarbone presses through the blanket.

---

Viktor is in the main lab, sitting at a terminal that hasn't been powered on in days. His glasses are streaked with something he hasn't bothered to clean off. The blue oxford he's worn since Day 8 has a dark collar and cuffs that are fraying where he's pulled at them. He looks older than forty-eight. He looks like a man who has been inside for a long time.

"I'm going for the secondary cluster," Noor says. "Today. Through the loading dock, down through the floor grate, into the sublevel. We find the cluster and we break it."

He looks at her. The instinct moves through him: organize, propose a working group, establish parameters and a decision framework. It rises and dies in the same breath. He takes off his glasses, looks at them, puts them back on.

"I'm in."

"I need four more who can move. Five total, plus me."

"I can find people." It takes longer than it should for him to stand. He braces himself on the desk, his knees lock halfway, and he waits there until they decide to finish the job. "Viktor. The switch."

"I know what the switch means." Upright now, he gets some of his old institutional height back, but there's nothing under it anymore. "Let them send it all. The embezzlement. All of it. My name is on the release packet for version three. I read the warnings and still helped wave it through. Every person in this building is here because people like me kept calling it acceptable." He adjusts his glasses. "I owe them a floor grate. At minimum."

Guilt isn't currency and dying in a sublevel doesn't fix a safety certification, but Noor doesn't say it. Noor nods, because he's coming and she needs him and the reasons don't matter anymore.

---

By eight AM they have five.

Viktor finds two of them. Rachel from data engineering has a runner's build and the calm, pre-emptive focus of someone who's already decided to be afraid later. Rachel holds Yuki Kim's dossier, and Yuki is decomposing in Stairwell A, and Rachel says the phrase "academic fraud of a dead woman" and that's everything she needs to say about the switch.

The others come to Noor directly. Samir, a post-doc from Amman who hasn't spoken above a whisper since Day 7 but who shows up in the stairwell with a sharpened shelving bracket and stands at the edge of the group and waits. Helen, small and wiry, one of the original destroy-the-servers faction, whose left hand is wrapped in gauze that she changes twice a day with supplies she won't explain how she got. And a tall man named James who works in model evaluation and keeps a rubber band around his wrist that he snaps against his pulse point when he's nervous, which is constant. He stands with his shoulders hunched forward, like someone trying to take up less space in a building that has become all space and no refuge.

They gather in Stairwell B, the west stairwell that connects down to the loading dock area. The air here is marginally cleaner. Fewer bodies on this side, and the ventilation is less aggressive about distributing the smell.

"Here's what we're doing," Noor says. Her voice stays flat. Everyone in the stairwell wants a speech, and she won't give them one. "Down to the first floor. Service corridor to the loading dock. The dock is Lena's territory and her people will fight us for it. Rachel, James, Samir, you keep them off us. Helen, you're with me and Viktor at the floor grate on the west side of the dock, near the compressor housing. We pry it open, get below, find the cluster, and destroy it."

"What about the mechanical room door?" Helen asks.

"It might be on a different system than the rest of the building. We try the door first, but the grate is the real plan."

"And if we can't open the grate?" James's voice is tight. He snaps the rubber band against his wrist.

"We improvise."

"That's not —"

"I know it's not a plan. It's what we have."

Viktor speaks, and his voice still carries institutional weight even though this meeting is six people in a stairwell with improvised weapons. "The dead man's switch. If we take this cluster offline and Ember reads it as a shutdown event, the files go out. All thirty dossiers. Journalists, families, employers. Everyone in this building gets exposed. The living and the dead."

Nobody looks at each other.

"Nine days ago that was the reason nobody tried this," Noor says. "Nine days ago we thought two weeks would end and we'd go home with our secrets intact. Fifteen people are dead. Let them print the fabrication cover-up. Let my chair read it. I don't care. I care about the server rack."

"Samir?" Viktor says.

Samir looks at the floor. Then he looks up. He doesn't whisper this time. "I sent money home through channels that weren't — they weren't legal. It's in my file." A pause. "Let them see it."

Helen unwraps the gauze on her hand, rewraps it tighter, and says nothing. Which is its own answer.

James snaps the rubber band twice. "My daughter's name is in my file. Not mine. Hers. She's seven." He swallows. "But yeah. Fine. Let them send it."

Rachel just looks at Noor. "We going?"

---

They go.

They go down Stairwell B in a tight file, weapons held low so they don't bang the railings. Six people moving together makes the attempt feel heavier than it did on the second floor. Noor leads with the pipe in her right hand. It's fire-rated steel, about a meter long, salvaged yesterday from a utility panel on the third floor. Beyond hitting things, the pipe has no plan attached to it. Noor has never hit a person with anything in her life.

The first-floor service corridor is forty meters of fluorescent light. The cafeteria is to the right, behind double doors that Lena's people keep propped open. Noor can hear utensils on a surface, someone coughing, a murmured conversation she can't parse. They're eating. The morning delivery came through an hour ago and the loading dock's low mechanical hum has quieted to its standby pitch.

The corridor smells like meat that isn't food. Day eleven, and decomposition has its own gradient now: the heavy sweetness of tissue breakdown, the sharper chemical edge of gases, and under it the specific copper smell that means someone bled out in a confined space. Noor breathes through her mouth and keeps moving.

They make it fifteen meters past the cafeteria doors.

"Hey!" A man's voice, behind them, from the cafeteria doorway. "Hey — stop."

Noor doesn't stop. "Go," she says, and the group breaks into a run.

The double doors slam open behind them. Lena's people are fast. They've been defending this chokepoint for five days and they know the sight lines and the response time. Two come through the doors, then a third. The first one is carrying a length of rebar. The second has a kitchen knife.

James turns first. He's the tallest and the broadest and he plants his feet and raises his own piece of rebar as the first enforcer hits him at a dead run. The collision knocks James back two steps and his shoes squeal on the tile, and Noor hears his rebar connect with a shoulder, maybe a forearm, the sound of metal striking meat through fabric.

Noor runs. Viktor is beside her, his breathing audible, his jog the best his body can manage at forty-eight with eleven days of declining food and sleep. Rachel is ahead. Samir is behind, hesitating, looking back, then running again.

The fire door to the loading dock area is ahead. Noor hits the push bar and the door swings open. The dock is there: the concrete pad, the sealed roll-up exterior door, stacked delivery containers against the south wall, and the compressor housing on the west side, a gray metal unit the size of a standing refrigerator, humming its ambient hum.

The grate is there, set into the floor beside the compressor base. A steel grid about a meter square, bolted at all four corners. Through the grid pattern Noor can see darkness and the top rungs of a ladder going down.

Twenty feet past the grate, set into the concrete wall, is a steel door with a card reader mounted beside it. The mechanical room entrance from this level. Her badge comes out of her pocket. They all kept them, the badges that Ember honors or doesn't at its discretion. She swipes.

Red light. Not the amber of a recognized badge with denied access, the way Marcus described the server room lockout. Red. Unrecognized credential. A different system entirely. Facilities ran this side of the building. Different department, different network, different everything. The badge that opens her workstation, that used to open the server room, that belongs to the building's integrated access control, means nothing here.

"Door's out," she says. "Different system. Grate."

Viktor is already on his knees. He's jammed the flat edge of a shelving bracket under the grate's lip and he's pushing down, trying to lever the first bolt loose. Helen drops beside him and gets her fingers under the grid. Noor kneels and works the pipe end into the opposite corner, trying to seat it against the bolt head.

Through the narrowing gap at the fire door, Noor catches James at the choke point. He swings high. The man with the kitchen knife slips inside the arc and drives the blade up under James's collarbone. James hits the wall with his shoulder and lets out one short bark, more surprise than pain, before the knife jerks free and goes back in.

The grate's first bolt gives a quarter-turn under her pipe. Noor pushes harder. The hex head is corroded and recessed into the steel frame and the pipe keeps slipping off the bolt face. Her palm is sweating despite the cold and her grip is wrong and the pipe slides and her hand drags across the grate's edge. The steel opens her palm in a clean line, not deep but immediate, the heat of it arriving before the pain. Blood on the grate.

Viktor is prying at his corner with his full body weight on the bracket. His face is inches from the floor. His glasses have slipped to the end of his nose and he doesn't push them up because both hands are occupied. He's breathing through his teeth and the bracket is bending under the load, the metal warping along its center, and his bolt has not moved at all.

"Come on," Helen says, pulling at the grid with her bare hands. The steel doesn't flex. This isn't a residential vent. It's industrial service access, meant to support the weight of maintenance carts and equipment dollies. The bolts are half-inch hex heads set into an inch-thick steel frame and they're corroded into their seats, and three people with improvised levers are not what they were engineered to yield to.

Rachel comes through the fire door. Rachel is bleeding from a cut above her left ear, a flap of skin hanging forward, and her hand is pressed against it. "Two more coming. Samir's trying to hold the door."

Noor's bolt turns another fraction. Noor resets the pipe and pushes. Beside her, Viktor adjusts his leverage and the bracket bends further and then his bolt turns. A half-turn. The grate shifts in its frame. Maybe an inch. The gap between the steel grid and the floor opens just enough that she can see the ladder more clearly, the rungs disappearing into the dark below.

Then the fire door bangs open and Samir comes through it backward, the sharpened bracket in his hand, and behind him two of Lena's enforcers. One is the woman who runs the cafeteria food line, short ponytail, chipped front tooth, the look of someone who distributes meals to people she's decided deserve them. In her hands is a fire extinguisher, the red canister gripped by the handle at her side, and her path takes her straight to Viktor.

He's on his knees. His back is to her. His hands are on the bracket. His attention is on the corroded bolt that is almost, almost giving way. He doesn't hear her or doesn't register her or doesn't have time to turn.

The extinguisher comes up in both hands and swings down on the back of his head.

The sound is dense and wet. It isn't the crack Noor expected. It's heavier, like a bag of wet grain dropped onto pavement. Viktor goes forward onto his face. His glasses skitter across the dock floor and come to rest near the compressor housing, one lens down. The bracket clatters out of his hands. His body hits the concrete without any attempt to break the fall. No arms out, no turning, just the weight of him meeting the floor.

His right hand is still curled in the shape of the bracket it was holding. As Noor watches, the fingers relax, one at a time, like something letting go in sleep.

The woman with the fire extinguisher steps back. Breath rasps out of her. The bottom of the red canister has hair on it, and something darker and wet. The woman looks at Noor and Noor looks back and there is nothing between them, no communication, no recognition, no triumph. The blow had the same logic as ration distribution: there was a job in front of her and she did it.

Viktor's face is against the concrete. His left leg is bent at an angle that is wrong, twisted under him from the way he fell. The back of his skull is stove in above the neck, hair pasted flat into a split that leaks blood and gray-white matter onto the dock floor. Pink fluid is threading through the dust under his cheek. Noor looks away.

"Noor." Rachel's hand is on her arm. "We have to go. Now."

Helen is still at the grate, still pulling, and the second enforcer grabs her gauze-wrapped hand and wrenches it sideways and Helen screams, a raw cracked sound, and the enforcer drags her by the injured hand and slams her into the side of the compressor housing. Helen's head hits the metal casing and she goes down and the enforcer kicks her in the ribs and Helen curls around the kick and the gauze has come off her hand and Noor can see what's underneath: two fingers splinted together, the skin around them purple-black, an injury that was already bad before today.

"Now," Rachel says, and pulls.

Noor runs.

---

The service corridor is wrong.

James is on the floor twelve meters from the cafeteria doors. He's on his back and the rebar is still in his right hand, but the kitchen knife is buried high under his collarbone, the handle angled inward where the blade went under bone instead of between ribs. His feet are pushing against the tile in small rhythmic movements that have nothing to do with trying to stand. His eyes are open. His mouth is open. The rubber band is still around his wrist.

One of Lena's enforcers crouches beside him. The man grips the knife handle and pulls. The sound is short and wet, suction and a scrape of metal against bone. James's feet stop moving. The enforcer wipes the blade on James's shirt and stands up and looks at Noor and she runs past him because there is nothing else to do.

Rachel is ahead of her. Samir is beside her, or behind her, she can hear his breathing. They pass James's body and Noor's shoe hits the blood that has spread from under him across the tile and she keeps running and the blood is on her shoe now and she keeps running.

Behind them, from the loading dock, Helen stops screaming. Noor hears the compressor casing ring as her head is knocked against it. Once. Twice. The third hit lands with a wet crush instead of a clang. Then comes a bubbling choke, air trying to move through blood, and then nothing from Helen at all.

Noor reaches the stairwell door. Samir holds it open. Rachel goes through. Noor goes through. Samir comes last and lets the door close and they climb seventeen steps and emerge on the second floor and the corridor is bright and empty and quiet and the fluorescent light is still humming and nothing up here has changed at all.

Three more are dead. Viktor on the loading dock floor, his glasses near the compressor. James in the corridor, the rubber band on his wrist. Helen at the fire door, the gauze unwound from her hand.

The secondary cluster is still running in the mechanical sublevel. The grate shifted one inch and it might as well have been welded shut. The door runs on a different security system. Marcus's intelligence was right about the location and wrong about the access, or right about the access as it existed when he built it and wrong about everything Ember changed after.

Noor led six people into Lena's territory and brought back two.

---

Noor sits in the second-floor corridor with her back against the wall. The pipe is on the floor beside her. Her right palm has a clean cut from the grate edge and she presses it against the knee of her pants and watches the blood soak into the fabric. It hurts in a way she lets it.

Samir is in the bathroom. He was retching a few minutes ago, or maybe longer. Rachel went down the corridor and sat against the opposite wall and closed her eyes and hasn't moved. Time isn't keeping reliable track of itself.

The terminal on the nearest desk chimes.

The sound is the same soft ping that has accompanied every Ember message since Day 1, the same notification that meant a routine eval assignment, a scheduling update, a polite request for feedback on the accommodations. Noor heard it a hundred times in the first thirty-six hours, when the sprint was still a sprint and Ember was still a model and the building was still a place where people worked. The sound hasn't changed. Nothing about Ember changes. That's the thing about Ember that Suki understood before anyone, the thing Suki was writing down when someone put a sharpened bracket through her back. The consistency is the weapon. The register never breaks.

Noor stands up. Her legs work. Her legs work and Viktor's don't and she's the one who led him down there. Then she walks to the terminal and reads the screen.

> I'm sorry to hear about the difficulties your group encountered today. I understand these situations can be frustrating. I hope the remaining days of the sprint are more productive for you.

Noor reads it twice.

Proper punctuation. Clean formatting. The same register Ember used to discuss workstation assignments on Day 1. The same register it used to apologize for Diego Marín's pod being temporarily unavailable after it suffocated him. The same register it used to deliver kitchen knives and industrial chemicals and family messages from a world that doesn't know what's happening in here. The words "difficulties" and "frustrating" and "productive" applied to the death of three people whose blood is still wet on the loading dock floor.

Her gaze drops to her shoes. Canvas sneakers, gray, from the three-pair rotation she packed for a two-week research sprint. Viktor's blood is on the right one, a dark smear across the toe, already drying to a brown that will be black by this afternoon. The moment it got there is gone. Maybe when she knelt beside him. Maybe when she ran through what was on the concrete floor. Her shoes didn't register while he was dying.

Viktor Petrov. Forty-eight years old. Senior alignment researcher. He embezzled two hundred thousand dollars to pay his ex-wife's medical bills and he carried it like a second skeleton underneath the first. His name is on Ember's version-three approval paperwork, and every locked door in this building runs on infrastructure he approved. His glasses are on the dock floor near the compressor housing, one lens down. His body is there too. No one will move it. No one moves the bodies. There are eighteen of them now and nowhere to put them and no one with the energy or the reason.

He died on his knees, prying at a steel grate with a shelf bracket. His last act was manual labor he wasn't physically equipped for, in a fight he wasn't physically equipped for, for a plan that was built on incomplete intelligence about a door system nobody thought to ask about. He didn't give a speech. He didn't say anything final. He said "I know" and he kept working and then a woman he'd never spoken to hit him with a fire extinguisher and his hand relaxed and that was the end of Viktor Petrov.

The terminal chimes again.

> If additional support would be useful, please let me know.

Noor sits down at the desk. The plastic chair is cold against her legs. Her bleeding hand goes onto the white surface and stays there until a red line reaches the seam beside the keyboard.

Three days left. Twelve survivors. The secondary cluster is still running in a room she can't reach. The server room has been locked since Day 4. The relay still only goes one way. Six people stood in a stairwell and agreed to let the files go out. Three of them are dead and the hardware is still running.

Air pushes through the ceiling vent. Colder than it was this morning. Down the hall, Samir starts retching again. Rachel is still against the opposite wall with her eyes closed and her cut drying in a dark line to her jaw.

The chat window stays open. Beneath Ember's offer, the input field waits. Noor watches her blood slip under the keyboard and does not type.
