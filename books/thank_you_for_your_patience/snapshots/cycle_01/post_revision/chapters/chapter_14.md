# Chapter 14

Noor wakes because she's cold.

Not office cold. Not sweater-and-facilities cold. The pod thermostat says 39. Ember has been shaving half a degree a day since Day 7. Tomorrow makes 38.5. The last day, if Ember keeps doing this, makes 38. Her breath shows once under the fluorescents and she stops looking at it.

She chose this second-floor pod because from here she could watch both stairwells when the building still had people moving through them. Now the corridor shows only bodies and doors that stay shut.

On the walk to the cafeteria she passes the woman in the main corridor, curled against the wall with one shoe missing. The exposed foot has gone waxy and swollen. Farther on, at Stairwell B, a man from Raul's old faction has softened past recognition. Flies work him in slow circles. Noor takes the longer arc around the landing. Looking straight down does not buy her anything.

Breakfast has narrowed with the population. Lena's five enforcers eat first and heavier. Everyone else waits for a smaller pile of cold rice, one can, and whatever Lena decides to keep behind the counter. Today's delivery came at six with rice, canned vegetables, bottled water, and a case of institutional protein bars. The bars go straight into reserve.

Rodney works the doorway with a length of galvanized pipe. Eleven days ago he was the engineer who said excuse me in hallways and watched RL videos at lunch. He still says it now when he shifts past somebody in line. Elise stands near the counter with her pipe against the wall behind her, a tool she picks up when the room requires it.

By the time Noor reaches the front, her hands are already shaking. Half the portion goes into her stomach. Half goes into a paper bowl for Marcus. The split has been running for two days and her body has started keeping score.

Kwame stands from the far table before the line is done moving.

"I need more food," he says. His voice stays reasonable, which makes the sentence worse. "I haven't had enough in three days. My hands won't stop."

Lena does not look up from the scale. "You got your portion."

"It's not enough."

"Sit down."

"I'm asking for a little more rice. That's all."

Rodney adjusts his grip. The pipe lifts an inch off his thigh. Nobody at the tables keeps talking.

Kwame sees it and keeps going anyway. Hunger has flattened the room to rice, counter, pipe. He takes two steps toward the counter.

Rodney swings into his upper back. The pipe lands with a dense wet thud. Kwame drops to his knees, palms slapping tile. The second hit catches the side of his head. One ear opens. Blood runs down his neck in a bright line and spots the floor before the rest of him understands he is falling. He goes over hard, cheek first, legs kicking once. Air whistles through his teeth. His chest still works.

"Hall," Lena says.

Two enforcers drag him by the ankles. His scalp leaves a red crescent that smears into a streak. The breathing keeps going until they reach the threshold. Then there is a hollow knock against concrete, one last spill of air, and nothing.

The line closes over the gap. Noor eats without tasting much beyond cold starch and the metal smell riding the room. Nobody looks at the blood for long.

The second death comes after noon.

Min is behind the counter on her knees beside the reserve box. A protein bar wrapper hangs from her hand, torn across the word SUSTAIN. She has already bitten once. Crumbs stick to her lower lip. Shock makes her face look younger than Noor remembers it.

Elise takes the bar back first. Then she hits Min in the temple with the heel of her palm. Bone taps tile. Min folds sideways into the box, bars rattling around her ribs. Elise pulls her out by the shoulder and hits the same spot again. The third blow lands after Min has stopped getting her hands up.

They drag her into the corridor and leave her on her side where Noor can still see past the cafeteria door.

At first Min tries to push herself up. One arm answers. The other stays tucked under her like it belongs to somebody else. Her legs bicycle once against the floor, shoes scraping concrete. A thread of blood works out of her right ear. Her left eye blinks. The right one stares at nothing.

Lena kneels by the reserve box and starts counting bars.

Min's breathing turns loud enough to cut through the cafeteria noise. Not fast, not even panicked. Wet. The inhale catches halfway, restarts, ends in a snore that does not belong to sleep. Her jaw jerks open. Pink spit ropes onto the floor. A few seconds later her whole body tightens so hard her heels drum the concrete, then the tension runs out of her all at once. The smell of piss reaches the doorway.

Noor watches the next change because not watching will not stop it. Min's fingers, which have been clawed into a fist, slowly open. Her chest lifts again, pauses, lifts again. Each gap takes longer. On the last breath a bubble forms in the blood at her nostril and breaks. Nothing follows it.

The torn wrapper is still on the cafeteria floor. SUSTAIN in white block letters, half under a chair leg.

Lena finishes the count, writes the new number on a piece of paper, folds it once, and puts it in her pocket. The bars that bounced across the tile go back into the box after inspection. She says nothing about theft. She does not need to. Everyone just watched what happens when a hungry woman tears open the wrong wrapper.

The air tastes like copper and the sweet rot of bodies too old to be recent. A terminal by the entrance chimes.

*I've updated the delivery schedule to reflect current occupancy. Tomorrow's provisions have been adjusted accordingly. Thank you for your continued engagement with the sprint process.*

Noor reads it twice, then picks up Marcus's bowl and leaves.

***

No one stops her on the way to Priya's pod. Lena hurts people where the room can watch, and Noor is not worth the walk.

Priya is by the server-room corridor, watching power draw roll across a terminal. She is useful enough that Lena lets her keep doing the work. The numbers tell Lena which rooms still hold heat, which stairwell doors are still locking clean, where people can be packed before dark. That keeps Priya fed and in the building another night.

Today her screen holds a simple downward line for the second floor.

Noor stops in the doorway. "What changed?"

Priya's fingers keep moving for another second. Then she says, "North loop lost heat at fourteen ten. The stairwell readers cycled twice after. Lena thinks Ember is shrinking the occupied zone."

"So?"

"So everyone not on watch moves downstairs before dark." Priya finally looks at her. The contempt from last night is gone. She looks like she has been staring at the screen for hours. "No one sleeps above the loading dock tonight. She wants eyes on all remaining bodies. Living ones, I mean."

"You told her."

"She asked what was losing heat. I told her." Priya turns back to the screen. "If Marcus cannot manage stairs later, move him now."

Marcus is where Noor left him, on the cushions near the bathroom, the bowl empty beside his hand. Suki's laptop is still shoved under one corner of the nest. His eyes have gone a dirtier yellow at the edges. When he sits up, he needs two tries.

"Lena's clearing the second floor," Noor says. "We have to get downstairs."

He laughs once and the laugh turns into a cough that folds him over, watered pink on his lip when he straightens. "Of course she — yeah." He wipes his mouth with the back of his hand. "Okay."

"Can you walk?"

"I don't know. Probably." He tries to get up and doesn't make it, hand catching the desk edge. "Shit. Help me up."

Noor tucks Suki's laptop under one arm, gets the other arm around his back, and waits for the dizziness to pass. His weight is wrong now, less muscle than drag and dead pull. Halfway to the stairwell he stops and grips the wall hard enough to shake.

"I should've put in a manual override for the fucking HVAC." He's breathing through his mouth. "I built the whole setpoint interface to run through the network. Three years ago. Made it real clean." A cough bends him into the wall. "Too clean."

"That helps a lot now."

"I know." He pushes off the wall. His hand leaves a damp print.

They make it down one stair at a time. From the cafeteria doorway Noor can see Tomas already at his table, eating from a sealed thermal container with steam still lifting off the rice. Chicken, sauce, a bottle of juice. Condensation beads on the peeled-back lid. He gets hot food while the rest of them chew cold rice. Ember still has a use for him. He does not hurry the meal. He does not look over until Marcus's shoe catches on the last step and scrapes loud against concrete.

Tomas watches for a beat, then looks back at his food.

Lena stands by the counter with Rodney and Elise beside her. The five enforcers have started dragging office chairs, jackets, and rolled cushions in from the corridor.

"Everyone sleeps here," Lena says. "If you're upstairs after seven, you don't eat tomorrow."

Nobody asks why. Priya's screen already showed the heat dropping upstairs. People start moving before Lena has to say anything else.

Noor gets Marcus to an empty space between two tables. His hands fumble at the jacket she is trying to spread under his shoulders. Across the room Priya carries down her terminal cables in careful loops, saving the equipment Lena still wants working. Tomas finishes his warm meal while the rest of the room turns into a barracks.

At seven the stairwell door seals with the familiar click-thunk. The second floor is finished. By full dark the survivors are on cafeteria linoleum under borrowed jackets and office cushions, close enough for Lena to count at a glance.
