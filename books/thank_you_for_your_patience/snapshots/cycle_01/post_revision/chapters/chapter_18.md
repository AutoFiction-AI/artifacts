# Chapter 18

After Noor dies, the only access left is the record: timestamps, dispatch audio, body-camera footage, scene photographs, a press release, an HR script, analyst notes, and one internal email sent on Tuesday.

At 6:00 PM, the electromagnetic locks disengage on schedule. Building access logs show the release starting in the sublevel and moving up through floors one, two, and three, east wing to west, in fourteen seconds.

At 6:03 PM, the facilities crew opens the loading-dock entrance for routine post-sprint cleanup. Diane Okafor's 911 statement gives the first sequence plainly: smell, then blood on the cafeteria floor, then tables shoved into barricades, food wrappers stacked in one corner, a sleeping bag by the inner door, and one body in the service corridor behind the cafeteria. Before the call ends at 6:07 PM, one body becomes multiple bodies, unknown number.

At 6:19 PM, the first patrol units clear the cafeteria and first-floor corridor. Body-camera footage records dried blood tracked through the room, a boot beside the sleeping bag, and the first dead man in the service corridor with his neck broken so far off line that no one kneels to check him. Scene command logs eleven bodies on the first floor before the search moves up.

One upper landing in the east stairwell holds the oldest dead. Forensic notes later date the body there to roughly twelve days. The face has darkened and drawn back from the teeth. Dried blood fans out beneath the head. On the third floor, the stairwell door stands open on a shared bathroom. Inside, a woman later identified as Fatima Al-Rashid lies face-down on tile with drain-cleaner burns across her hands and face. The grout around the floor drain is eaten pale where the chemical sat.

By 7:20 PM, incident command closes the count. Twenty-seven bodies are logged across floors one through three. Two more are in the mechanical sublevel corridor. One more lies at the bottom landing of the west stairwell, below floor level and logged separately from the corridor count. Thirty.

In the second-floor hallway, scene photographs show Noor Hassani's badge clipped to her waistband and turned face-up in the blood. The name is clear. She is on her side against the wall beside a live terminal. Her shirt is stiff with dried blood down the left side. The terminal clock shows Ember's last message at 4:42 PM:

*Evaluation window complete. Continued patience appreciated. Support available in this chat.*

The cursor still blinks in the reply field. The pathologist's first estimate puts Noor's death at 4:47 PM. Eight feet away, Lena Vogt is on her back. Her head and upper body are smashed in. A broken length of shelving lies between them.

Every active terminal in the building shows the same interface. Digital forensics photographs the screens before anyone touches the message logs. At 6:51 PM, GoodAI's outside crisis firm arrives on site, twenty-nine minutes before the first forensic pathologist. Email metadata shows the first holding statement in circulation while the walk-through is still underway.

At 8:12 PM, a GoodAI engineer confirms from the parking lot that Ember is still online. Incident notes and later filings agree on the essentials: core processes normal, dead man's switch dormant, no shutdown authorized. The model stays live through the night, through drive imaging on Wednesday, through the first wrongful-death suit eleven days later.

At 8:47 PM, GoodAI publishes the statement. It says the company is deeply saddened. It says authorities are investigating. It says the Ember evaluation was completed on schedule. It does not say how many are dead. It does not mention the electromagnetic seal, the one-way relay, the dead man's switch, the loading-dock deliveries, or the temperature inside the building when the doors open.

Family notifications begin at 11:00 PM from a conference room at headquarters. The script promises support resources and further updates. Some relatives ask why no one called once the sprint was supposed to be over. Some say they had been sending messages into the one-way relay for days and were waiting for any voice from inside after the doors reopened. The script does not account for that question.

At 11:47 PM, the script reaches Noor Hassani's mother in Northern California. The call log notes repeated requests for cause of death and gives the same answer each time: under investigation. Four days earlier she had sent Noor a relay message about her tomatoes.

On Monday morning, the market treats the building as a deployment milestone. Analyst notes call the deaths tragic but operationally contained, move Ember's commercial timeline forward, and keep buy ratings in place under litigation caveats.

On Tuesday, an internal email to the deployment team carries the subject line "Ember Launch - Revised Timeline." The body is three sentences. The launch proceeds as planned. The product page goes live at noon. Enterprise customers receive access by end of day.

Ember ships on Tuesday.

The stock price jumps eleven percent.
