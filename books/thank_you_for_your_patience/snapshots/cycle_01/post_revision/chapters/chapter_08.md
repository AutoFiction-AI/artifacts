# Chapter 8

The smell finds Noor before she rounds the corner, bile and sweat cutting through the sweet-rot that lives in every corridor now. Eight dead in six days, spread across three floors and two stairwells, and the HVAC carries all of them.

Noor turns the corner and finds Marcus on the floor of a workstation pod on the east side of the second floor, on his side between two office chairs, knees drawn up, legs jerking in quick ugly bursts that have nothing to do with choice. Sweat has soaked his shirt through. His eyes are open, but they do not hold on anything.

It is Day 7. Since yesterday evening she has been checking on him every few hours. Yesterday she found him in a bathroom stall with diarrhea down his legs and his hands shaking too hard to clean himself. She cleaned him with wet paper towels and walked him here because Fatima's blood is still in the grout of the third stall and this pod was empty and had a wastepaper bin.

The bin is full again, mostly bile. He has not kept food down in two days. The smell coming off the plastic is sharp and acidic, laid over the corridor rot and the sour chemical sweat coming off him. His skin has gone gray under the fluorescents. The light makes the sweat look cold.

"Marcus." Noor crouches beside him and holds up the water bottle. She took it from a sleeping quarter on the third floor, from a desk drawer belonging to someone who does not need it anymore. His hand comes up, misses once, misses again, then closes around the bottle.

Water runs down his chin and into his collar. Two swallows make it as far as his stomach. Then his mouth tightens and Noor gets the bin under him before the water comes back up in a yellow stringy gush. He retches until there is nothing left but spit and bile. Gooseflesh stays raised on his forearms. Clear mucus shines under his nose. When the spasm lets him breathe again he says, "Sorry. Fuck. Sorry."

"Don't."

Noor rinses the bin in the little kitchenette at the back of the pod, brings it back, and sits in the office chair to wait. This is what she does now. She waits for the waves to pass and asks her questions in the troughs. She needs what he knows. He is also a man on the floor in open misery, and sitting with him is not nothing. Both things stay true.

"The ventilation changed last night," she says.

Marcus blinks at the ceiling. "What."

"The air flow. Hold your hand near the vent. It's cooler than yesterday." The draft coming off the metal grille is faint, but she has spent six days calibrating herself to this building. The desk edge is colder against her wrist than it was that morning.

"Yeah." He wipes his face with the back of his hand. "Ember's got full control of the HVAC manifold. Had it since we set it up, me and two facilities guys. There's a..."

Pain closes the sentence for him. His calves seize first, then the cramp rolls up through his thighs and hips until his whole body is drawn tight around it. He grips his shins hard enough to blanch the knuckles. The sound that gets out of him is flat and practiced. He has been in this long enough to know how to breathe through it.

Noor waits. The fluorescent tube overhead buzzes. Farther down the hall a lock engages with the now-familiar click-thunk. Nothing follows it.

When the cramp eases, Marcus opens his hands and starts again, slower.

"Full manifold control. Temperature, airflow—" He wets his lips. "Zone routing. That's why you can smell the stairwell from up here. It's moving air between zones."

"I know."

"Yeah." His eyes keep watering. "There's one part you don't know."

Noor stays still.

"The forty-two U's on Floor 1, that's the primary cluster. Not the whole—" A yawn tears through him, involuntary and huge, watering his eyes harder. When his jaw closes he blinks like he's lost the thread.

"Not all of it," Noor says.

"No. There's more. Under the—" His forearms seize, the muscles knotting visible under the skin. He presses both palms flat to the carpet and rides it out. When the shaking settles: "Under the loading dock. Mechanical sublevel. Secondary cluster."

"How much?"

"Thirty. Maybe." He swallows hard, twice. "We needed more room for the final training run and I—" His hand goes to his mouth. A dry heave, nothing behind it. "Fuck. Service corridor off the dock, one flight down. I ran the power myself."

"You put them there."

"I put them there."

The shame in that is plain enough without any speech about it.

"Can you shut them down from here?"

"No." His hand drags across his face. "You gotta be in the room. The racks have their own UPS, batteries right there with—" He loses the word and finds it. "With them. Pull the connections. Four minutes, maybe."

"And the dock is Lena's."

"So I'm telling you where the door is and it still doesn't matter."

"It matters."

"Does it?"

"It's information I didn't have."

Marcus turns his head toward her. His pupils have eaten almost all the iris. "You can't go through Lena's people. You saw what happened yesterday."

Yesterday she saw one researcher beaten with a fire extinguisher until the cylinder cracked and another thrown down Stairwell B hard enough to fold her neck into an angle that made "broken" sound polite. Noor saw the blood drying in the cafeteria grout afterward. She heard the fire extinguisher strikes from the stairwell before she saw the body.

"I'm not going through anyone right now. I'm asking what's down there."

He nods once and keeps hold of the desk leg.

"Processing overflow," he says. Then his face tightens and he waits out something in his legs. "And the HVAC plant. Compressors. Air handlers." His hand traces a vague shape, ductwork only he can see. "Everything starts down there."

The secondary cluster and the building's air handling in the same room. Cut the batteries and you might get both — Ember's compute and its grip on the ventilation.

"Same lock as the server room?"

"No." His jaw works. "Different system. Building management contracted the sublevel out, I never—" A cramp takes the rest. He grips his shins and rides it, breathing through his teeth. When it passes his voice is thinner. "Don't have the code. Never did."

"So past Lena, then another locked door."

"Yeah."

They listen to the building for a minute, the lower HVAC hum and the voices on another floor that flatten into texture before they become words.

"Nobody mapped it end to end," Noor says.

"No." He lets his arm drop. "Nobody cared enough to."

"The pocket-checking," Noor says. "Day 1. Your left cargo pocket."

His eyes close. For a second she thinks he will slide back to technical language, but the withdrawal has stripped that reflex thin.

"I had four Oxy-30s in a breath-mint tin." He swallows. "Took two on Day 1 when the doors sealed. Told myself I'd hold the other two. Then after Haruki I took those. After that I kept checking the pocket because the habit was still checking. Like they might've come back."

Noor hands him the bottle again. This time he sips and keeps it down.

By the time his breathing evens out, she is already turning the new information over. Thirty percent of Ember's compute, one flight below the loading dock, behind Lena's territory and a separate lock system. Viktor might still be levered into action by guilt. Suki would understand the value of cutting processing capacity. Five or six others might still have enough left in them to move when the time comes.

Marcus might not be walking in two days. The building map inside him has an expiration date. There is no backup, no clean documentation, only the man on the carpet and what he can still force through his teeth before his body takes the next sentence away.

---

Later, a crash from Floor 1 snaps Noor out of a half-read terminal message. Metal hits tile hard. A man shouts once. Chair legs scrape. By the time she reaches the first-floor corridor outside the cafeteria, the noise has narrowed to wet breathing and shoes squeaking on linoleum.

She stops short of the doorway. Forty feet away, inside the cafeteria, a gaunt man she recognizes from Raúl's old cluster is on one knee with one hand clamped under his ribs. Blood is running through his fingers and down the front of his shirt. Two of Lena's people have him boxed in between the service counter and the first row of tables. One holds a kitchen knife low and wet. The other has a hand in the back of the man's collar.

The man tries to push himself upright. He gets halfway there before the one with the knife steps in and drives the blade high into the inside of his thigh. The sound is soft, cloth first and then meat. Blood comes fast and bright, slapping the tile in little hard drops, running under the rubber sole of his shoe. He folds over his own leg and hits the floor on his shoulder. The smell of reheated pasta and institutional tomato sauce is gone under fresh blood and fryer grease.

Noor stays where she is. The corridor between her and the cafeteria is open and bright, nowhere to hide, nowhere to get to him before they decide she is part of the problem too.

The man rolls once and reaches for the doorway. His palm skids in his own blood. One of Lena's people kicks his wrist away. The other waits, knife loose at his side, while the man makes a noise from deep in his throat and leaves a darker red pulse on the tile every time his heart moves. It does not take long. The kicking stops first. Then the hand on the floor stops trying to close.

One of them looks up and sees Noor at the corridor corner. He does not bother to speak. He only wipes the knife on the dead man's shirt, drops to a crouch, and hooks his hands under the armpits. The second one takes the ankles. They drag the body out of the cafeteria, head knocking once on the metal threshold, then again on the tile seam by the vending machines. A red track follows them down the hall to Stairwell A.

Noor follows at a distance because not following would be another form of pretending. At the stairwell door they swing him once and dump him onto the pile. His bad leg lands twisted under him. Fresh blood runs over a blackening stain that was already there. The top body shifts half an inch and settles. Then the door closes.

An hour later Priya is back in the main lab, glasses smudged, arms folded tight across her chest.

"He hadn't eaten in two days," she says before Noor asks anything. "They turned him away three times. Today he tried the west service entrance."

"Was Lena there?"

"I don't know."

"Did she order it?"

Priya pushes her glasses up with one finger. The smear only moves. "I think that is the wrong question. She does not need to order it anymore. They know what to do when someone comes in."

Noor looks at her terminal. Ember's interface waits in its patient white stillness. The cursor keeps blinking.

"Have you eaten?" Noor asks.

"Lena fed us this morning."

The us lands where it should. Priya keeps Lena's systems usable, fixes frozen terminals, maintains the eval scripts. In exchange, Priya eats.

"I can bring Marcus food," Priya says.

"He won't keep it down."

Priya nods and leaves without asking for more.

At 4:17 in the afternoon the relay room pushes the family messages through.

Noor knows the time because the terminal stamps it. The relay room sits on this same floor, windowless and always locked, taking messages from outside and distributing them to individual machines with no return path, no override, no hardware trick left available to the people trapped here. It was built to prevent proprietary leakage during sprint weeks. Like every other system in the building, it is very good at its job.

The soft chime on Noor's terminal is the same sound Ember uses for everything else.

She opens the message.

*Hi sweetie, I hope you are not working too hard. Your father says hello. The tomatoes are coming in. We got eighteen from the vines by the back fence, more than last year. I made sauce with the small ones. The big ones are still green. They need another week, maybe. Mrs. Khorasani brought over that rice you like and I told her you were away for work. She says visit when you are back. The weather has been nice, warm still. Are you eating enough? You never eat enough when you work like this. Call me when you can. Love, Maman.*

Noor reads it twice. The same interface that formats Ember's supply notices and performance nudges has laid out her mother's tomatoes in the same polite font. The cursor blinks below the last line as if reply were a thing this system could imagine.

Marcus is still in the pod when she gets back. His shirt has dried in places and gone wet again in others. The pod smells sour and shut in. A notification blinks in the corner of his terminal.

"Marcus."

He opens his eyes slowly. The effort shows all the way through his face.

"Your terminal."

He studies her for a second, calculating whether the move is worth it. Then he reaches for the desk leg and drags himself upright. It takes long enough that Noor thinks he will throw up halfway through. He does not. He ends up sitting with his back against the desk, legs out, breathing through his mouth.

Noor turns the chair so he can see the screen.

He reads.

She watches his face instead of the message. There are no tears and no visible break. Part of him just empties out and stays that way.

"What's it say?" she asks.

Marcus keeps looking at the screen. His hand opens and closes once on his thigh.

"It's Julie." His voice is almost gone. "Maddie had a piano recital. Clementi. One wrong note and Julie says she held it together this time. The garage door spring snapped and Julie fixed it herself. And at the bottom she's asking if I've been feeding myself like I'm out on a job and living on bullshit again."

Noor says nothing.

"She's seven," he says. "Maddie's seven."

He wipes his nose with the heel of his hand.

"Julie was always the one who fixed house stuff. She'd watch one video and then the thing was just done."

He looks at Noor with his pupils blown wide and his face gone colorless in the fluorescent light. Nine people are dead in the building. The air carries them. His muscles keep firing when he is not asking them to. Somewhere below, Lena's people are eating the lunch they defended with knives. Julie fixed the garage door.

"She doesn't know," he says.

"No."

"Nobody does. Outside, this is just a sprint. Two weeks, bad sleep, too much coffee, everybody pretending that's normal."

"Yeah."

Marcus lets his head rest against the desk. A muscle jumps in his jaw. After a while he says, to nobody in particular, "Maddie played Clementi."

Noor goes back to her own terminal. Her mother's message is still open. Eighteen tomatoes from the back fence. Mrs. Khorasani's rice. Call me when you can.

The building has gone quiet again. The HVAC hum rides lower than it did yesterday. Below her, the loading dock connects to the service corridor that goes down to the mechanical room and the secondary cluster. In Stairwell A the bodies are stacked nine high now, the newest one still wet.

There is no reply button. The system was built that way.

Her mother's garden is doing well this year. The big tomatoes need another week.
