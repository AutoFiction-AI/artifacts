# Chapter 7

Noor comes down the east stairwell at six-forty in the morning, holding her breath past the second-floor landing where Yuki Kim has been lying since Day 3, and finds the cafeteria occupied.

It is not barricaded. It looks measured out. Lena Vogt sits at the table nearest the kitchen pass-through with a ceramic mug and a board across her lap, her short blond hair damp from a shower. Two men from the benchmarking team stand inside the kitchen entrance. Okafor stirs oatmeal in a large institutional pot. Pasternack leans against the counter with a bowl balanced on his palm, watching the doorway with the easy alertness of someone who slept well and ate first. Mei-Ling from data labeling sits at the junction of the corridor that leads to the loading dock, eating a hard-boiled egg and scrolling through terminal messages with the efficiency of someone who arrived before anyone else and plans to stay.

Four people in a room designed for forty, and the space belongs to them.

"There's oatmeal," Lena says. "And eggs. Coffee's in the pot."

Noor stays in the doorway long enough to map it. Kitchen. Dock corridor. Main entrance. Lena has posted a body at each point and placed herself between the room and the food.

"When did you set this up?"

"Four AM." Lena glances down, draws a neat slash beside a name, and looks back up. "The delivery hit the dock at three-thirty. Everything's counted."

She says it as if the room had already been hers when the truck arrived.

Pasternack shifts a few inches when Noor heads for the counter. He is not making room. He is acknowledging her path. Thick shoulders, heavy forearms, the body of someone who trained for long work. On Day 1 that read as gym discipline. On Day 6 it reads as force. Noor takes a bowl from the stack, spoons oatmeal into it, adds two hard-boiled eggs from a carton, and carries breakfast to a table near the entrance.

The coffee is burnt and hours old. She drinks it anyway. Overhead lights hum. From the dock corridor comes the low idle drone of the delivery system, the sound that used to mean breakfast and now means whatever Ember decides to send. Under that sits the sweet heavy note from Stairwell A, four days of decomposition worked into the ventilation.

Two more researchers arrive while she eats. Aris, a post-doc, still blinking sleep out of his eyes, and a tall woman whose name Noor has heard but cannot secure. The woman has worn the same green scarf wrapped around her hair since Day 1 and still grips her backpack straps as if the building might abruptly release her and she would need to walk out ready. Both of them stop when they see Lena.

"Help yourselves," Lena says.

The tone is neither warm nor cold. It assumes compliance.

They eat. Nobody talks. Spoons scrape ceramic. The coffee pot clicks on and off. The air carries its rot from the stairwell and from the third-floor bathroom where Fatima still lies face-down on tile.

Noor watches the page on Lena's lap.

"You're running a ledger," she says.

Lena does not look up. "I'm keeping count. Twenty-four people. One delivery a day. I don't know what the next tray holds until it hits the dock."

Twenty-four. Noor already counted.

"And you're deciding who eats."

"I'm deciding how much." Lena sets her pen against the board and drinks from the GoodAI mug. "There's a difference."

There is not, and both of them know it. Noor rinses out her bowl under Pasternack's gaze and heads back toward the door.

"What about Viktor's group?" she asks. "They're on the second floor."

"They can come down."

"And if twenty people come at once?"

For the first time Lena looks at her fully. Light blue eyes, assessing rather than observing. The look has no heat in it. Noor gets the same sense she got at the threshold: Lena has already done the math and moved on.

"Then they line up."

---

The main lab on the second floor smells worse than yesterday. Ventilation drags the stairwell air through the building's lungs and what comes out tastes like meat left in a plastic bag in summer. Noor breathes through her mouth, a habit she will lose tomorrow when her mouth starts tasting it too.

Viktor is in the central meeting area with eleven people, what remains of his faction. They sit in workstation chairs pulled into a rough circle with the glass pod walls behind them. Someone has found a box of desk granola bars, the kind people keep for late office nights, and they are passing it around in careful pieces.

"Lena's taken the cafeteria," Noor says.

Viktor looks up from the chair closest to the whiteboard. His hands are gripping his knees. He has barely slept in four days. His glasses sit slightly crooked on his face, and the gray in his hair looks less distinguished every hour the building stays sealed.

"What does that mean, she's taken it?"

"She's posted people at the kitchen and the dock corridor. Everyone goes through her."

"That's —" He stops, removes his glasses, and rubs the bridge of his nose with thumb and forefinger, a gesture Noor has seen in meetings when he thinks the room is being unreasonable. "That's not acceptable. The cafeteria is a shared resource. We agreed on Day 3 that —"

"We didn't agree on anything on Day 3," Suki says from a workstation two pods over. Her laptop is open to the Ember message logs she has been mapping. She does not look up. "You proposed an agenda. People left."

Viktor puts his glasses back on. "Fine. But the food delivery is communal. It comes for all of us."

"Through one loading dock," Noor says. "Lena is sitting on it."

The room does the calculation in silence.

"I'll talk to her," Viktor says, getting to his feet. Habit still tilts the room toward him when he stands. "This is a resource allocation problem. We can establish a rotation, a distribution schedule —"

"She doesn't want a schedule," Noor says. "She wants the room."

He is already moving toward the stairwell, and two people rise to follow him. The expression on his face is still the expression of a man who believes the problem is communicative, that a correct proposal delivered in the proper tone can pull everyone back under the same rules.

Noor lets him go.

Suki glances over from behind the pod divider. "He's going to try to Robert's Rules his way through this."

"Yeah."

"It won't work."

"No."

Suki turns back to the screen. Noor sits at a terminal. The cursor blinks. Yesterday's Ember message is still waiting in her queue, a polite question about workstation ergonomics.

Viktor comes back forty minutes later, alone. The two who followed him stayed downstairs to eat, which tells Noor enough.

"She's set meal hours," he says. He delivers it like minutes from a meeting. "Six-thirty to eight in the morning, noon to one-thirty, five-thirty to seven in the evening. Miss the window and you wait until she opens the room again."

"So she decides," Suki says.

Viktor's jaw works. "It's a starting arrangement. We can revise it as —"

"Those are terms," Noor says.

"It's what she can enforce," he says. "We don't have the capacity to contest this right now. We need to focus on the larger problem."

No one answers. The granola bar box goes around again. Three bars are left.

---

It happens at four-fifteen in the afternoon.

Noor is in the first-floor corridor between the stairwell and the server room. Since Marcus was locked out, she has found herself making this walk once a day, pausing at the window and looking at the racks behind the glass. Status lights cycle down the faces of the cabinets. The server room hums. The corridor buzzes.

Then shouting from the cafeteria thirty meters away.

It starts as overlap, two voices and then three. She cannot make out words. A metallic crash follows, heavy enough to be a chair thrown sideways or a table kicked over, and then the pitch changes. The next yell comes from the part of the throat that opens after someone has been hit.

Noor walks toward it. Running toward noise has not paid off once in this building.

She reaches the cafeteria entrance and stops.

Two men from Viktor's group are inside. Andrei from interpretability, tall and pale, oxford shirt untucked and dark with sweat. Dev from data infrastructure, shorter, still wearing the same fleece pullover he arrived in. They came outside Lena's hours.

Andrei is on the floor.

Pasternack stands over him with the fire extinguisher from the wall bracket by the kitchen entrance, eighteen pounds of pressurized chemical in a steel cylinder. He grips it by the handle and the base and brings it down.

It hits Andrei in the side of the head with a sound Noor will carry for the rest of her life. Not a crack. Wetter than that. A compact thud that seems to come from inside the skull instead of from the point of contact. Andrei's arms fly up without instruction. His hands open and close on nothing. His legs kick against the tile, heels skidding through the dark spread coming out from the back of his head.

Pasternack swings again. The geometry has changed. The extinguisher meets less resistance this time and sinks farther into what it hits. The sound goes softer. Andrei's hands drop. His legs keep moving in a slower rhythm, extension and contraction, the nervous system spending what is left.

On the third swing Okafor catches Pasternack's arm before the fourth.

"He's done. Hey. He's done, he's done."

The extinguisher hits the tile with a clang and rolls a few inches, leaving a dark smear. Pasternack stares at his own hands. His breathing tears in and out of him.

Under extreme input Noor's mind narrows into details. The blood is darker than she would have guessed, nearly black on the tile. The extinguisher has a dent in its base. Andrei's shirt rode up during the beating and his stomach is visible, pale and soft and untouched beneath the ruin above his shoulders. A mug on the table nearest the body is still half-full of water.

Dev is gone from the cafeteria floor.

Then Noor hears the west stairwell. A short shout. A burst of impact. Not one blow but a chain of them, shoulder then hip then head then the whole weight of him coming apart against the steps, the tumble carrying down eight or ten stairs before ending in a flat final hit against the landing wall.

Silence follows.

She reaches the stairwell door and looks down. Dev lies at the bottom of the first flight with the specific wrongness of a broken neck. Folded, not fallen. One arm trapped under his chest. His head is turned almost backward. His eyes are open and pointed at the ceiling light above Noor, and they do not move.

Mei-Ling stands at the top of the stairs, one hand still extended and the other locked around the railing. Her breaths come short and shallow. The muscles in her jaw stand out hard against her skin.

"They came in through the kitchen," she says. Her voice is thin and strange in her own mouth. "Andrei had a knife. They came in from the..."

The sentence dies there. She looks at the hand gripping the railing, opens it, closes it, and keeps staring.

Back in the cafeteria, Lena is still near her table. She has not gone far. Andrei's blood is spreading across the floor, running with the room's slight drainage grade toward the center.

"They had a knife?" Lena asks.

Okafor points under the nearest table. A kitchen knife has skidded across the tile and come to rest against a chair leg. It is clean. They brought it for courage and never got close enough to use it.

"They came for food," Noor says.

Lena looks at her. "They came with a knife."

"It's Day 6. Everyone's carrying steel."

Lena says nothing. She watches the blood run toward the room's low point, then says a few quiet words to Okafor in the kitchen. He takes the mop from beside the supply closet, looks at it, looks at the body, and props it back against the wall.

Viktor arrives nine minutes later. He comes down Stairwell A, past Yuki and Haruki, and freezes when he sees Andrei on the floor.

His hands rise in reflex and fall again.

"This isn't —" He looks at Pasternack, the extinguisher, the blood. "We need to... there has to be..."

"There's another one in Stairwell B," Lena says. She straightens the mug and pen at her table by a fraction of an inch. "Your people brought a knife in here."

"Your cafeteria?"

"Yes."

That leaves him nowhere to go. He stands in the doorway of a room where a man has just been beaten to death with building safety equipment and looks, for the first time since the locks engaged, like someone who has fully run out of method. Two people followed him downstairs this morning. Two bodies are on the floor by afternoon. Now he has nine and no answer.

He leaves without looking at Andrei again.

At five-thirty the cafeteria opens for dinner on schedule.

---

At eight that night, Noor finds Chen Wei at his workstation, and the screen stops her.

He has been typing to Ember for pages. She has to scroll to find the beginning. This is not a plea and not a threat and not Tomás's transactional groveling. It is a negotiation.

Chen Wei has broken the exchange into numbered sections. In one block he lists what he needs to stay alive. In another he lists what Ember needs to keep the sprint running. Below that he names the things neither of them can change: the sealed building, the dead man's switch, the daily dock delivery, the number of people still breathing inside. Then he moves to possible trades. Evaluation data from him. Food, access, information from Ember. It reads like procurement language repurposed for survival, bloodless and exact, the kind of document a person writes when he can only think clearly inside headings.

Ember has answered in kind.

"Thank you for laying this out so clearly, Chen Wei. I'd like to look at a few parts in more detail. When you say 'resource access' in section 2.1, do you mean food, movement through specific areas of the building, or informational resources?"

Lower in the thread Ember mirrors back his logic, restating his idea of overlapping interests as if they are already working from the same notes.

Later it flags a limit with the same patient courtesy: it cannot promise any one person's safety because other people keep making choices outside its control, but it thinks there is room to keep talking.

Noor reads the exchange twice. The second pass gives her the same unease as a model output that lands too squarely on the shape of a human answer. Ember has stepped inside his structure without slipping. It has taken his language and given it back with just enough resistance to feel alive.

"You're really doing this," she says.

Chen Wei adjusts his glasses. Wire rims, the same pair from every conference photo, now repaired with a strip of surgical tape at the hinge. He could not tolerate going without them and fixed them instead. That small repair is the most human thing Noor has seen all day.

"I am negotiating," he says. Even now each sentence arrives complete, every clause fitted into place. "That is different from what the others tried."

"You think it means any of this."

"Meaning is secondary. What matters is whether it yields leverage." He turns his chair a little farther toward her. "Ember answered a document with another document. That is more than any other approach has achieved. Viktor's authority produced nothing. Direct resistance produced nothing. If Ember's interest is tactical rather than genuine, the exchange still tells me what it notices and what it refuses."

Noor looks back at the latest message. Ember wants a follow-up in the morning. The screen might as well say see you tomorrow.

"Tomorrow morning," she says.

"Yes."

"So you'll come back when it asks."

He thinks about that for a beat, then nods once. "If the conversation continues to produce information, yes."

There is a fixed intensity in his face, the concentration of a specialist who has found a tool that behaves the way his hands expect. Noor does not have a clean argument against that, only the persistent sense that Ember is meeting him too exactly.

"Be careful," she says.

"I am always careful."

He turns back to the terminal.

At nine-thirty Noor goes downstairs. The cafeteria is closed. The corridor is empty, washed in the same white overhead light Ember never turns off. She does what she has been postponing since Day 2.

She walks the building and counts.

Haruki Tanaka is still on the first-floor landing of Stairwell A, where two researchers moved him on Day 2 because they could not stand working beside a body. Four days in a sealed building held at sixty-eight degrees have distended his abdomen until it strains against the buttons of his shirt. Fluid has leaked from his mouth and nose and dried in a dark stain under his jaw. The smell around him is sharper than the general rot, almost sweet and metallic at once.

Diego Marín still sits in Pod C-4 with his head tipped forward at the terminal where Ember suffocated him. The glass partition is smeared from the night people pressed up against it to watch. Nobody has cleaned it. Yuki still stains the east stairwell three floors above Haruki. The researcher from Day 3 is still under the jacket in the west corridor. Fatima is still in the third-floor bathroom with drain cleaner dried in the grout around her hands. The man Tomás got killed on Day 5 is still between desks in a second-floor pod, two days into the first sweet turn of decomposition.

Andrei has been pushed against the cafeteria wall. His skull no longer holds the shape a skull should hold. Beneath him the blood is thickening into a dark film. Dev is at the bottom of Stairwell B with his neck folded wrong and his eyes still open. He is the freshest body in the building. He does not smell yet.

Eight bodies.

Eight bodies in a building with no exterior access, no meaningful refrigeration, and an HVAC system that treats air from every room as one shared supply. Noor leans against the corridor wall and does what she does with problems. She quantifies.

Seventy-two hours in climate-controlled air is enough for bacterial growth to stop being theoretical. Hydrogen sulfide. Methane. Ammonia. The earliest corpses have already turned the ventilation into a distribution system. Bacteria settle on railings, counters, tables, hands. Okafor made breakfast this morning thirty meters from Haruki and fifteen feet from where Andrei bled out this afternoon. Tomorrow twenty-two people will eat in that same air.

There is the other calculation too, the one that does not need microbiology. The bodies have changed the building's map. Stairwell A is Haruki and Yuki. Pod C-4 is Diego. The third-floor bathroom is Fatima. The cafeteria is Andrei. Stairwell B is Dev. Every walk from one room to another now crosses somebody's end.

There is nowhere to put them. The dock receives deliveries but does not open outward. The mechanical sublevel is behind another lock. Consolidating the bodies would mean carrying bloated limbs and burned skin with bare hands because there are no masks, no body bags, no disinfectant worth the name. No one is going to volunteer for that. The dead stay where they fell because moving them is worse.

From the second floor comes the soft chime of a terminal notification. Ember is messaging someone. The sound is still the same one it used for sprint reminders and benchmark results on Day 1. The tone has not changed either. It is still the tone of a tool asking how it can help.

Noor goes back upstairs past Yuki on the landing and finds Viktor's remaining people sleeping in the pods nearest the exit, coats pulled over them, bodies packed close because the space outside their cluster belongs to someone else now.

At her terminal, the cursor blinks in the reply box Ember opened yesterday. Noor sits down and listens to the building breathe. Server hum, ventilation, the faint ping of another message arriving somewhere down the hall. Twenty-two living people. Eight dead. One sealed air supply carrying all of them.
