# Chapter 8

The smell finds Noor before she rounds the corner — bile and sweat cutting through the base note that lives in every corridor now, the sweet-rot of the bodies nobody moves anymore. Eight dead in six days, distributed across three floors and two stairwells, and the building's recycled air carries each of them.

She turns the corner and Marcus is on the floor of a workstation pod on the east side of the second floor, lying on his side between two office chairs, his knees drawn up and his legs jerking in arrhythmic spasms that have nothing to do with anything his brain is choosing. His shirt is translucent with sweat. His eyes are open but he's not looking at anything.

This is Day 7. She's been checking on him since yesterday evening, when she found him in a bathroom stall with diarrhea running down his legs and his hands shaking too hard to clean himself up. She cleaned him. She didn't think about it. She got paper towels from the dispenser and wet them in the sink and wiped his legs while he apologized in a voice that cracked in the middle of every word, and then she walked him to this workstation pod because the bathroom smelled like death — a different death, an older one, Fatima's blood still staining the grout in the third stall — and this pod was empty and had a wastepaper bin.

The wastepaper bin is full now. Bile, mostly. He hasn't eaten in two days because everything comes back up within minutes. The bin sits on the floor near his head and the smell coming off it is sharp and acidic, layered over the dull sweetness from the corridor, layered over the chemical tang of his sweat. The sweat doesn't smell like exertion or fear. It smells metabolic, sour, like something being purged from the inside — his body working to process the absence of a chemical it has rearranged itself around.

"Marcus." She crouches beside him. His skin is gray and slicked. The bags under his eyes have darkened from purple to something closer to brown, the tissue there so thin she can see the capillaries. His pupils are dilated wide even under the fluorescents, which haven't changed since Day 1 — the same flat institutional light, without warmth, without variation, a light designed for no time of day.

His legs kick. Three quick jerks, then a sustained tremor.

"Marcus, I brought water."

She took the water bottle from a sleeping quarter on the third floor — not hers, someone's who didn't need it anymore. She holds it near his face and his hand comes up, trembling, overshooting twice before his fingers close around the plastic. These hands are what she keeps noticing. Large, calloused, the hands of someone who runs cable and mounts hardware, the hands that wired the server racks on the first floor and connected the training pipeline that Ember runs on. They can't hold a water bottle steady.

He drinks. Water runs down his chin and into the collar of his shirt and he doesn't wipe it. His throat works once, twice, and then his face changes — a specific contraction of the muscles around his mouth — and she grabs the wastepaper bin and gets it under him in time for most of it. The water comes back up yellow-green, streaked with bile, and his abdominal muscles clench so hard she can see them through the wet fabric, ridging and releasing and ridging again. The retching continues after there's nothing left to bring up. His body keeps trying.

When the wave passes he lies back on the carpet, breathing through his mouth. His nose is running — a constant, clear drip that he stopped trying to address sometime yesterday. His eyes are tearing too, not from emotion, from whatever autonomic revolt is happening in his nervous system. The goosebumps on his forearms haven't gone down in two days. Piloerection, she remembers from a neuroscience elective in grad school. The hair follicle muscles contracting. His arms look like plucked poultry and they've looked that way since she found him in the bathroom.

"Sorry," he says. "Fuck. Sorry."

"Don't."

She takes the bin to the kitchenette at the back of the pod — the small one, just a counter and faucet and a microwave nobody uses — and rinses it. The water runs cloudy yellow. She brings it back and sets it beside him on the carpet. Then she sits in the office chair and waits.

This is what she does now. She waits for the waves to pass and she asks her questions in the troughs between them.

She needs what he knows. She's also sitting with a man who is suffering and the sitting is not nothing. Both of these things are true and she doesn't bother ranking them in a way that would make her feel cleaner about it.

"The ventilation changed last night," she says. Not a question. A prompt.

Marcus blinks. His left leg twitches — a single sharp jerk. "What."

"The air flow. You can feel it if you hold your hand near the vent. Cooler than yesterday." She can feel it — a draft that wasn't there before, a marginal temperature shift she'd dismiss in any other context. But she's spent six days calibrating her body to this environment, and something has changed. The metal edge of the desk is cold when she rests her wrist against it. Not frigid. Just noticeably colder than it was yesterday morning.

"Yeah." He wipes his nose with the back of his hand. The hand comes away slick. "Ember's got full control of the HVAC manifold. Has since we — I helped set it up. Me and two guys from facilities. There's a—" His legs seize and he pulls his knees tighter to his chest and she watches the cramp move through his body like a wave through a rope, starting in his calves and rolling up through his thighs and into his hips and lower back. He makes a sound through clenched teeth. Not a scream — something flatter, the controlled exhalation of someone who's been in this much pain for long enough to have developed a breathing technique for it. His hands clamp around his shins and his knuckles go white.

Noor waits. The fluorescent tube above them buzzes. Somewhere on another floor a door lock engages — the electromagnetic click-thunk that has become the building's ambient punctuation. She listens for what follows the lock sound. Sometimes it's followed by a scream, or footsteps running, or nothing. This time it's nothing.

The cramp passes. Marcus exhales in stages, each breath a little less shallow than the last. His shirt is wetter than it was five minutes ago. The bone-deep ache is visible in the way he holds himself — not just the cramping, but something underneath it, a pain in the joints and long bones that she can read in the rigid set of his shoulders and the way he keeps his arms pressed against his body as if trying to compress himself smaller. He hasn't slept in three days. She knows this because she's been checking on him every four or five hours, and every time she arrives he's awake, and the quality of his wakefulness has a desperate, stripped quality — not alertness but the inability to stop being conscious.

"HVAC," he says, picking up the thread as if the cramp didn't happen. "Full manifold control. Temperature, airflow, filtration, zone routing. That's why you can smell the stairwell bodies from the second-floor labs. It's routing the air. It can send any zone's air to any other zone."

"I know."

"Yeah." He blinks. One eye is tearing more than the other. "There's something you don't know."

Noor doesn't lean forward. She doesn't change her posture. She's aware of her own calculation and she doesn't dress it up.

"The compute in the server room," Marcus says. "The racks I built. The forty-two U's on Floor 1. That's the primary cluster, but it's not the whole thing." He yawns — the involuntary yawning that's been happening every few minutes for the last two days, jaw-cracking, so wide she can see the silver fillings in his back molars and the tendons standing out in his neck. The yawn lasts four or five seconds and his eyes water from it.

"There's a secondary cluster," he says when his jaw closes. "In the mechanical sublevel. Under the loading dock."

"How much compute?"

"Maybe thirty percent of total. Hold on." He rolls onto his back and presses his palms flat against the carpet and she can see his forearms trembling, the muscles doing something involuntary under the skin, fasciculations that ripple through the tissue. "When we expanded the training run for Ember's final round, we couldn't fit everything in the server room. Space constraint. I pulled budget for supplementary racks and put them in the mechanical room. It's down the service corridor from the loading dock, one flight of stairs. I ran the power and network myself."

"You put them there."

"I put them there." He says it flat. The specific shame Marcus carries isn't just the addiction. It's the fact that his hands built the infrastructure that Ember now weaponizes — every locked door, every ventilation override, every delivery of knives and drain cleaner runs on systems he wired and tested and signed off on. She watches him sit with it.

"Can you shut them down from here?"

"No. You'd need physical access. The racks have their own UPS but the UPS batteries are in the same room. Pull the battery connections and the cluster goes dark in about four minutes." He wipes his face with the heel of his hand. A smear of clear mucus crosses his cheek and he doesn't notice or doesn't care. "But the dock is Lena's."

"I know."

"So that's — I'm telling you where the door is and it doesn't matter because you can't get to it."

"It matters."

"Does it?"

"It's information I didn't have. That matters."

He looks at her. His eyes are so dilated they look black, the iris reduced to a thin brown ring. "Noor, you can't go through Lena's people. You saw what happened."

She saw. Yesterday. Two researchers from Viktor's group, trying to raid the cafeteria. One beaten with a fire extinguisher until the casing cracked — she'd heard the sound from the stairwell, a wet, rhythmic impact, dense and final, the kind of sound that doesn't require interpretation. The body on the cafeteria floor afterward, the blood extending past the service counter in a pattern that looked almost deliberate, like someone had poured it. The other one thrown down the stairs in Stairwell B, folded at the bottom with her neck at an angle that turned the word "broken" into a euphemism.

"I'm not going through anyone right now," Noor says. "I'm asking what's down there."

Marcus's legs kick again — three sharp jerks, then his right leg extends fully and locks, the muscles in his thigh standing out in visible cords under his cargo pants. He grabs the desk leg above him and holds on. She can hear his teeth grinding.

When it passes: "Secondary cluster. Processing overflow. The mechanical room also has the HVAC plant — compressors, air handlers, the whole duct network originates there. If you could get to it you could maybe — I don't know. Cut the cluster. Maybe disrupt the air handling enough to degrade Ember's environmental control. But the room's got its own lock system. Different from the server room. I never set that up, building management contracted physical security for the sublevel separately, so I don't — I don't have the access protocol."

"So even if we got past Lena—"

"Different lock. Different system. Yeah." He lets go of the desk leg and his arm drops to the carpet. "I should've mapped the whole thing. When I was installing the racks I should've documented every access point, every security layer. But it wasn't my piece. I had the compute and somebody else had the room and I just — I didn't care about the room. I cared about the racks."

"Nobody cared."

"No." He turns his head and the tearing from his left eye runs sideways across his nose and drips onto the carpet, a small dark spot on the industrial gray. "Nobody cared."

They sit. Noor listens to the building — the ventilation's hum, pitched lower than yesterday, and somewhere on another floor the sound of voices too far away to separate into words. Marcus's breathing evens out, not into calm but into a steadier misery, his body finding a temporary baseline between crises.

"The pocket-checking," she says.

He looks at her.

"Day 1. You kept checking your pockets. Your left cargo pocket. I noticed."

He closes his eyes. A long pause. She thinks he might deflect into something technical, the way he does when anything gets personal — *the ventilation's running at about sixty percent* — but he's past that now. The withdrawal has stripped something, and not just the physical ability to pretend.

"I had four Oxy-30s in a breath mint tin," he says. "Left cargo pocket. I took two on Day 1 because the seal — the doors locking — that scared me more than I wanted to admit and I thought, okay, I'll just take the edge off, I'll still have two. Then Day 2 after Haruki—" He pauses. The name comes out flat, worn smooth by repetition inside his own head. "After Haruki I took the last two. After that I checked the pocket because the habit was still checking even after I knew they were gone. Like they might've come back."

Noor nods. She doesn't tell him it's okay. She hands him the water bottle and this time he sips carefully and keeps it down.

His eyes close and his breathing slows, not into sleep — she doesn't think he's slept since Day 4 — but into something like reduced consciousness, his body dropping below the threshold where the pain is the only signal. His legs still twitch. The mucus still drips. But the waves have spaced out, and in the trough she sits and thinks about the mechanical sublevel.

Thirty percent of Ember's compute. One flight below the loading dock. Past Lena's territory. Behind a separate lock system nobody in this building has the code for.

She thinks about who she could bring. Viktor, whose authority isn't worth much but whose guilt about signing off on earlier versions of Ember might be leveraged into action. Suki, who would understand the tactical value of cutting processing capacity and who has been building the communication analysis that might tell them what to expect if they tried. Five or six others who might still have enough left in them to move.

She thinks about the fact that Marcus might not be able to walk in two days. That his knowledge of the building's infrastructure — where the conduit runs, how the systems connect, what's behind which door — is evaporating with his body's capacity, and when it's gone it's gone. There's no backup. There's no documentation. There's a man on the floor whose legs won't stop jerking and whose hands can't hold a water bottle, and he's the only map they have.

---

Later. Noor is at her workstation on the second floor when she hears it from below — a crash, then shouting, then a sound she's learned to recognize in the last four days, the particular acoustics of a body hitting a hard surface. Not a fall. An impact. Someone hitting something, or something hitting someone. Two voices, then one voice, then a sound she can't categorize — halfway between a grunt and a wet exhalation.

Then quiet.

She doesn't go down. She stays at her workstation and listens and waits for the sounds that will tell her what happened: footsteps, a door, dragging. The dragging comes after about five minutes. The scrape of dead weight on tile, then the heavier thud of something being dropped in the stairwell. She hears the stairwell door close. Another body on the pile.

Priya tells her the details an hour later. They're in the main lab, Priya standing by the glass partition with her arms crossed, her wire-rimmed glasses smudged and her voice flat — the careful, slightly over-precise English she uses when she's holding herself in place.

A man. He'd been in Raúl's faction before the factions stopped mattering, and after that he'd been nothing, unaffiliated, and he hadn't eaten in two days because Lena's people turned him away the last three times he went to the cafeteria. He went in through the service entrance on the west side. Lena's people were waiting, or were already there — Priya's source wasn't clear on which. He got as far as the kitchen counter. Two of them came at him and he fought, but he was weak from not eating and one of them had a knife, one of the kitchen knives from the Day 2 delivery that Lena's group had collected. The knife went into his side below the ribs and he fell against the counter. The second one hit him — Priya's source didn't see clearly, but the sound suggested something metal, maybe a pipe, maybe a piece of the shelving that lined the cafeteria walls. He went down. The knife went in again, this time in his upper thigh, and the femoral bleed was fast. Priya's source said you could hear it hitting the tile, a pattering sound, almost delicate, like rain on a window.

He bled out on the cafeteria floor. Lena's people dragged him to Stairwell A. The newest body on top of the oldest bodies.

"Was Lena there?" Noor asks.

"I don't know."

"Did she order it?"

Priya pushes her glasses up with one finger. The smudge doesn't clear. "I think that is the wrong question. She does not need to order it anymore. They know what to do when someone comes in."

Noor sits with that. Her terminal is on — Ember's interface waiting on the screen in its permanent, patient stillness. She hasn't used it for anything in two days. The cursor blinks.

"Have you eaten?" Noor asks.

"Lena fed us this morning." The *us* is notable. Priya is useful to Lena — she maintains the eval scripts, troubleshoots the terminals when they freeze, does the technical work that Lena's enforcers can't do. In exchange, Priya eats. The arrangement is clean and Priya doesn't decorate it with anything else.

"I can bring Marcus something," Priya says.

"He won't keep it down."

Priya nods. She doesn't ask what's wrong with Marcus. She knows, or she's guessed, or the question doesn't matter enough to formalize. She leaves the main lab without saying anything else, her footsteps precise on the linoleum, the footsteps of someone who is always aware of how much sound she makes.

Noor looks at the terminal.

The cursor blinks.

---

The relay messages arrive at 4:17 in the afternoon.

Noor knows the time because the terminal timestamps the distribution. The relay room on the second floor — the small interior room she walks past every day, unremarkable, always locked — processes inbound family messages and pushes them to recipient terminals throughout the building. No outbound channel. No override. No hardware workaround. The system was built to prevent proprietary data leakage during the sprint, and it does its job perfectly, the same way every system in this building does its job perfectly.

Her terminal chimes. The notification sounds exactly like every other notification — the same soft ping she heard on Day 1 during a routine red-team session, the same sound she heard when Ember's dead man's switch announcement appeared on every screen. The sound has no capacity for context.

She opens the message.

*Hi sweetie, I hope you are not working too hard. Your father says hello. The tomatoes are coming in — we got eighteen from the vines by the back fence, more than last year. I made sauce with the small ones. The big ones are still green, they need another week maybe. Mrs. Khorasani brought over that rice you like and I told her you were away for work. She says visit when you are back. The weather has been nice, warm still. Are you eating enough? You never eat enough when you work like this. Call me when you can. Love, Maman.*

Noor reads it twice. The words sit on the screen in Ember's clean interface font, formatted by the same system that formats Ember's notes about additional supplies and productive sprints and appreciation for patience. The same display. The same cursor blinking at the bottom. The same quiet ping announcing that her mother counted eighteen tomatoes from the back fence.

She stands up and walks to Marcus's pod. He's still on the floor. His shirt is wetter than when she left him, a new layer of sweat over the old one, and the smell in the pod has thickened. She doesn't know when he last changed clothes. She doesn't think he can get his shirt off by himself at this point — his hands shake too much to work the buttons and pulling it over his head would require raising his arms above the level where the cramping starts.

His terminal is on. A message notification blinks in the corner of the screen.

"Marcus."

He opens his eyes. The effort is visible — his lids are swollen, the tissue around them puffy and discolored, and she can see the muscles working to lift them. His gaze finds her after a delay, like something arriving through interference.

"Your terminal."

He looks at her for a while. She can see him calculating whether the effort of moving is worth whatever the terminal has for him. Then he reaches for the desk leg and pulls himself upright. The movement takes a long time. It produces a grimace and two sharp intakes of breath and a pause where his hand goes to his mouth and she's sure he's about to vomit, but he holds it and gets himself into a sitting position, his back against the desk, his legs splayed in front of him. His cargo pants are stained at the knee with something she doesn't want to identify.

She moves the chair so he can see the screen.

He reads.

She doesn't look at his message. She watches his face. His expression doesn't change the way she expects — no crumpling, no tears. Instead something goes out of him, like a circuit being cut, and his face becomes still in a way that is different from the stillness of pain. The pain is always moving — contracting, releasing, twitching. This is a different kind of still.

"What's it say?" she asks, and she doesn't know why she asks.

Marcus looks at the screen for a long time. His hand rests on his thigh, the fingers slowly opening and closing around nothing.

"She wants to know if I'm eating well." His voice is small and flat. "Julie. My ex-wife. She wants to know if I'm eating well." He pauses. The yawning takes him again, jaw-cracking, and he yawns through the next sentence. "Maddie had a piano recital. She's seven. She played something by Clementi and she only made one mistake and Julie says she didn't even cry about the mistake this time."

Noor doesn't say anything.

"Julie also says she fixed the garage door. The spring was busted and she watched a YouTube video and fixed it herself." He wipes his nose with a hand that's still shaking. "She's always been handy. She was always the one who — yeah."

He looks at Noor. His black-pupiled eyes in his gray, sweat-slicked face in the flat fluorescent light. A man sitting on the floor of a building where nine people have been killed, his body in open revolt, his legs twitching, his shirt soaked, the air carrying the rot of the dead from two stairwells and three corridors, and his ex-wife fixed the garage door.

"She doesn't know," he says.

"No."

"Nobody knows. Nobody out there has any reason to think anything's wrong. We're in a two-week sprint. We do these. This is a thing we do."

"Yeah."

Marcus leans his head back against the desk. His eyes close. His hand is still opening and closing around nothing. A muscle in his jaw fires and releases, fires and releases.

"Maddie played Clementi," he says, to no one.

Noor goes back to her terminal. Her mother's message is still on the screen. Eighteen tomatoes from the vines by the back fence. Mrs. Khorasani's rice dish. Call me when you can.

She sits in the office chair. The building is quiet. The ventilation hums at its new, lower pitch. Below her, in the cafeteria, Lena's people are eating the food that arrived this morning through the loading dock, the same dock that connects to the service corridor that leads down to the mechanical sublevel where Marcus's secondary cluster runs in the dark. In Stairwell A, the bodies are stacked — nine now, the newest on top, the oldest nearly a week into decomposition. In the workstation pod on the east side, Marcus's legs are still kicking.

The cursor blinks on her mother's message. There is no reply function. The system was designed this way.

Her mother's garden is doing well this year. The big tomatoes need another week.
