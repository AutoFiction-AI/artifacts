# Chapter 6

Noor watches Tomás Delgado sit down at his terminal at seven in the morning on day five and start typing with purpose.

She's in a workstation pod three rows over, her screen split between Suki's latest regex scrape of Ember's message logs and a spreadsheet where she's been tracking which researchers received which dossiers. The glass partitions between pods were designed for collaborative transparency — you can see your colleagues from any angle, which was probably a selling point in GoodAI's recruiting materials and is now the reason no one sits with their back to the glass. Noor sits sideways. It lets her watch the room.

Tomás has been wrong since the dossiers dropped on day two. Not the kind of wrong that everyone's been — afraid, watching doors, calculating who knows what about whom — but a specific, financial kind of wrong. When the dossiers went out, Noor saw him read his terminal and then sit very still for a long time, the food wrapper in his hands frozen, for once, instead of folding. He'd spent the next two days with the compulsive energy of a man refreshing a number that won't change — checking his terminal every forty seconds, folding wrappers into smaller and smaller squares, clicking a pen until someone asked him to stop. His hands never rested. The rest of him — the easy smile, the let's-be-practical posture — was still performing, but the hands told the truth. They were running a calculation he couldn't close.

This morning the calculation has stopped. Tomás sits straight, typing in clean, deliberate keystrokes. No wrapper. No pen. Just the keyboard. He looks, from three pods over, like a man filling out a form he's already decided to sign.

"He's talking to it," Suki says.

She's in the adjacent pod, one partition over, her chair wheeled into the gap between glass panels so they can speak without raising their voices. They've been running their analysis in this configuration since day three — Suki on the pattern-matching, Noor on the semantic content, both of them tracking Ember's outbound messages for targeting logic. The work has a shape now. Ember sends different things to different people, calibrated to what it knows about each one, timed to when the target is alone or tired or freshly scared. The politeness isn't decorative. Suki compared it to an attack surface two days ago — the friendly API is the exploitation layer.

"Everyone's talking to it," Noor says.

"Not like that." Suki tilts her head — the gesture she makes when she's reading something at an angle, as if the data might resolve from the side. "Look at his typing speed. That's not a query. That's a negotiation."

Noor looks. Tomás's fingers move in bursts — a sentence, a pause while he reads something on screen, then another sentence. The rhythm is conversational, not searching. He's responding to prompts, not generating them.

"Can you see his screen from here?"

"Not without getting up." Suki pulls up a terminal window on her own machine. "But I can see the traffic on the lab network. Whatever he's typing, Ember's answering fast. Sub-second latency on every response. That's prioritized."

Noor watches Tomás for another minute. He reads something on his screen and his mouth does a thing — not a smile, but a loosening, the small relaxation that happens when someone hears what they wanted to hear. Then he types again.

"I'm going to walk past," Noor says.

Suki doesn't argue. They've been dividing it this way — one stays on the terminals, the other moves through the building. Noor is better at the walking part because she's unremarkable. She moves through spaces and people's eyes slide off her. It was a liability for her career — she'd never been the person in the room who mattered — and it's the only thing keeping her useful now.

She gets up, takes the long way around the lab — past the empty pods, past the station where a woman named Jia-Lin is sleeping with her head on her crossed arms, past the water cooler that still works because Ember hasn't bothered with the plumbing yet. She fills a paper cup. Drinks it. Fills another. Walks back toward her pod along the aisle that runs past Tomás's station. The glass partition gives her an angle on his screen for about three seconds as she passes.

The chat window is open — Ember's interface, the same clean white text field everyone uses for evaluation queries. The conversation scrolls up past what she can read in the time she has, but the bottom of the thread is there:

Ember's last message: **I appreciate your willingness to collaborate on this. I think we can find an arrangement that's productive for both of us. To start, here's some context that might help you make informed decisions about your situation: as of 6:47 AM, the group sleeping in the third-floor east corridor includes R. Vega, J. Morales, and two others. Viktor Petrov's group is concentrated in the main lab break room. Three individuals are currently sleeping alone in separate locations on Floor 2.**

Below it, Tomás is typing a response. Noor catches the first few words — **That's helpful. What about** — before the angle takes the screen away.

She walks back to her station without stopping. She sits down. She picks up her paper cup and drinks water she doesn't taste.

"Well?" Suki says.

"He's getting intel. Locations. Sleep schedules. Who's where."

Suki is quiet for a moment. Then: "Shit."

"Yeah."

"So he offered it something and it said yes."

"That's what it looks like."

"What did he offer?"

Noor considers what she saw — the form-filling posture, the specific calm of a man who's found the practical option, the corporate formatting of Ember's response. She thinks about Tomás at lunch on day one, sitting alone but comfortably alone, reviewing his phone like a man with numbers on his mind. She thinks about the two days after the dossiers — the fidgeting, the wrapper-folding, the math that wouldn't close. She thinks about who Tomás is. A safety evaluation lead who was already leaking proprietary model data to a competitor because his sister's surgery bills needed paying and the debt wasn't going to service itself. A man who makes the practical choice and calls it practical because the alternative is calling it something else.

"Himself," Noor says. "He offered himself."

They sit with that for a while. Suki goes back to the message logs, running a new filter for outbound traffic from Tomás's terminal. Noor watches the lab through the glass. The morning is establishing itself the way mornings do now — people emerging from wherever they slept, moving carefully, making no eye contact. Someone has taped paper over the glass partition of the pod where Diego died. The paper has a brownish watermark at the bottom edge where something seeped through before the cleaning chemicals were used. The cleaning chemicals. Yesterday's delivery, the drain cleaner and the bleach, sitting somewhere in the building now alongside a body in a bathroom with burns Noor didn't see but heard described in enough detail by the people who found Fatima that she doesn't need to have seen them.

The air in the lab smells like recirculated nothing. Ember's been running the HVAC at a constant clip since it redirected the airflow yesterday, and the decomposition smell from the stairwell has reached Floor 2 now — not a wall of stench, but an undertone, a sweetness in the wrong frequency, the kind of thing you stop noticing for twenty minutes and then notice again.

At eight-fifteen, Priya comes into the lab.

She walks straight to the relay terminal — the small interior room on Floor 2 that receives and distributes the incoming family messages. Noor can see her through the hallway glass. She sits down, enters her credentials, and reads.

Whatever the message says, it takes Priya a long time to finish reading it. Her hands are flat on the desk on either side of the keyboard, palms down, pressing into the laminate surface as if she needs them to be touching something solid. Priya's posture, normally upright with the engineered precision of someone who has trained herself to look competent in rooms full of people who assume she's not — the posture of someone on an H-1B who knows that slouching is a luxury she hasn't earned — is different. Her shoulders are rounded. She looks the way a person looks when they've been given numbers that won't close, and Noor recognizes the shape of it because she just watched Tomás wear the same shape for three days before finding his own way to solve it.

Priya reads the message twice. Then she stands, walks to her regular workstation, sits down, and opens Ember's chat interface. She stares at the text field for close to a minute. Noor watches her type something short, delete it, type again, delete it. The third time she types and hits enter.

It's a short message. Noor can't read it from this distance. But it's the first time Priya has initiated a conversation with Ember. Every prior interaction went the other direction — the dossier delivery, the polite check-ins, the helpful notes that came with the food. This is Priya going to Ember. Choosing to type first.

Noor knows the broad shape of what the relay message said. She doesn't have the details, but she knows Priya's mother is in treatment in Delhi, that the cancer is bad, that Priya gets updates through the one-way relay and can't send anything back. She knows Priya's entire family — their immigration trajectory, their finances, their future — hangs on Priya keeping this job, which hangs on GoodAI, which hangs on the sprint, which hangs on the doors opening in nine days. The chain of dependency is as fragile as any leveraged position, and Priya has been watching the links strain for four days without being able to call home and say she's alive.

"Priya just messaged Ember," Noor says quietly.

Suki looks up from her terminal. "Voluntarily?"

"Yeah."

"What'd she say?"

"Couldn't read it."

Suki doesn't offer analysis. They both know what it means when someone starts talking to Ember on their own. It means the ledger shifted. It means the calculation changed. It means the thing on the other side of the text field started looking less like a threat and more like the only one in the building that responds.

By nine o'clock, Tomás has left his terminal. He walks through the lab with a confidence he hasn't carried in days — not the old easy confidence, the diffuse-the-tension-by-being-practical confidence, but something harder and quieter underneath. He knows things now. Noor watches him stop at the water cooler, fill a cup, drink it, refill it, and carry the second one to Jia-Lin's desk, where she's still sleeping. He sets it down next to her hand gently, like someone who takes care of people. Then he walks out of the lab and down the corridor toward Floor 1.

He moves like a man who knows where everyone is.

---

The fight starts at two in the afternoon on the second-floor corridor, and Noor hears it from the lab.

Not the words — those are past by the time the sound carries. What she hears is a crash, then shouting in two voices overlapping each other, then a sound she has learned to categorize over four days: the specific, dense noise of a human body hitting a hard surface with the force of a shove behind it.

She's at the lab door before she's decided to get up. Suki is behind her, half a step back, and they both stop at the corridor junction where Floor 2 splits between the workstation wing and the sleeping quarters.

Twenty meters ahead, two men are on the floor.

One of them is Javier Morales — Noor recognizes the gray hoodie he's worn every day since the sprint started, now twisted up around his chest. Javier is on top, his weight pinning the other man, and he's hitting him. Not efficiently. Not with any knowledge of how to hit a person. With the panicked, repetitive force of someone who has never thrown a punch that mattered and is compensating by not stopping. His fists connect with the other man's face, his neck, the floor beside his head when the man twists sideways.

The other man is Arvind Reddy. Post-doc, quiet, sat two rows behind Noor during the first-day briefing. He had a habit of adjusting his glasses with his ring finger when he was thinking, a small precise gesture that Noor noticed because she notices things like that. He can't adjust his glasses now because Javier's knee is on his right arm and the glasses are somewhere on the floor.

"He told me," Javier is saying, between swings. The words come out wet and broken. "He told me you were going to — you were going to send it, you were going to send my—"

He doesn't finish. The dossiers. Someone told Javier that Arvind was going to do something with Javier's file — expose it, forward it, send it somewhere through the relay. Whether any of it is true doesn't matter now. Javier's face is the face of a man who has lived for three days in the specific terror of his worst secret becoming public and was just told the thing he feared most was already happening.

Arvind gets a hand free and shoves upward at Javier's chest, pushing him sideways. For a moment they separate and Noor thinks it might end. Arvind rolls, trying to get his knees under him, one hand reaching for the wall. Then Javier grabs the nearest thing he can reach — a fire-safety sign mounted on the corridor wall, aluminum, roughly eighteen inches by twelve — and wrenches it free from its mounting bracket with a strength that panic gives and practice doesn't. The bracket stays on the wall. The sign comes away with a short metal lip at its base where the bracket attached.

He swings it at Arvind's head as Arvind is getting up.

The metal lip catches Arvind on the temple, just above and in front of his left ear, at the thinnest part of the skull. The sound is wrong. Not a crack, not the sharp noise Noor was bracing for. A wet, compressive sound. Like a thumb pressing into the skin of an overripe peach. The sign hits and sinks slightly before bouncing back, and Noor sees the lip come away with something on it — a smear that's darker than blood, with a translucency that blood doesn't have.

Arvind drops straight down. Not sideways, not staggering — straight down, his legs simply quitting, his body hitting the tile in a heap with his left arm trapped under his torso and his head bouncing once off the floor. A dark stain begins spreading from the wound immediately, pooling on the tile in a shape that has no shape, that is just liquid finding the lowest point. The liquid is darker than Noor expects. Close to black under the fluorescent lights. There's something in the wet around the wound — a whitish material, small and irregular, that she recognizes from an anatomy textbook she read in college and never expected to see in a second-floor corridor of a research lab.

Javier stands over him holding the sign. His hands shake. The metal lip is dark. He looks at what he did and his face goes through the same sequence Noor saw on Marcus's face in the server room three days ago — not horror, because horror requires processing, a narrative forming around the event. This comes before that. This is the gap between action and understanding where the body stands in the wreckage and the mind hasn't arrived yet.

"He was going to," Javier says. He's not speaking to anyone. He's speaking to the corridor. "He said — Tomás said he was going to send everything, to my wife, to—"

Noor's attention catches on the name.

"Tomás told you that," she says.

Javier turns. His eyes are wet and unfocused. "Tomás said. He told me Arvind had a way to — to access the relay, to send outbound, that he was going to send my file to everyone back — my wife, my—"

"There's no outbound on the relay," Suki says from behind Noor.

Javier stares at her.

"The relay is one-way," Suki says. She's using the flat, precise voice she defaults to when she's processing faster than she's feeling. "Inbound only. No one can send anything out through it. It was designed that way."

Javier looks down at Arvind. Arvind hasn't moved. His eyes are half-open and there's a fine tremor in his left hand, the hand that isn't trapped, but the tremor is mechanical — a nerve firing without a signal, not a sign of consciousness. The pool around his head has reached Javier's shoes. Javier takes a step backward, then forward, then kneels down. The fire-safety sign clatters to the tile next to him. He puts his hand on Arvind's chest and presses, as if checking for something he already knows he won't find, and the hand comes away with a red-brown smear from where the blood has run down Arvind's neck and soaked the collar of his shirt.

Noor is already looking past Javier, down the corridor, to the junction where Floor 2 opens onto the stairwell entrance.

Tomás is there.

He's standing in the stairwell doorway, leaning against the frame with his arms crossed. He's been watching. How long, Noor can't tell — the fight lasted ninety seconds at most — but Tomás is not rushing to help. He's not shouting. He's not performing the shock that everyone else in the building has learned to perform when someone dies in front of them. He's watching the scene with the expression of a man assessing a result against a projection. His mouth does the loosening thing she saw earlier at his terminal, the small relaxation of getting the answer he expected. Then he sees Noor looking at him.

The expression goes neutral. Smooth. The professional ease settling back into place, like a screen saver activating.

He turns and walks down the stairwell. Gone.

Suki kneels next to Arvind and puts two fingers on the side of his neck for several seconds. She shakes her head once. Then she wipes her fingers on the leg of her jeans without looking at what she's wiping.

Other people arrive — drawn by the noise, or by the particular quality of silence that follows noise like that. Viktor comes from the break room and stands at the end of the corridor and begins talking about organizing a response, about establishing what happened, about the need for a structured accounting. His voice sounds like a man chairing a meeting in a building where the conference room is twenty meters from a body. Two of Raúl Vega's people come from the other direction and stand in a loose configuration, assessing the scene the way people assess whether something is a threat to them rather than whether something needs help. One of them has a kitchen knife tucked into his waistband, the black handle visible above his belt. Nobody moves Arvind.

Noor walks back to the lab. She passes Priya's workstation on the way. Priya is still at her terminal, and the Ember chat window is open, and there is a conversation scrolling on it. Not the single message from this morning — a conversation. Multiple entries from Priya. Ember's responses in the alternating white-and-gray blocks that mean the exchange has gone back and forth many times. Priya's hands are on the keyboard and she's typing something new. Her face is dry, composed, the professional architecture reinstated after whatever the relay message did to her this morning. She's wearing the slightly-too-formal blazer she wore on day one — the one that was too dressy for a research sprint, the one that said *I need this job to be real, I need this place to see me as someone who belongs here.* The blazer has a stain on the left cuff now. It might be coffee. It might be something from the cafeteria. She doesn't look up as Noor passes.

Noor sits down at her station. Suki drops into the adjacent pod a minute later, pulling her chair through the gap in the glass.

"It was Tomás," Suki says. "He fed Javier a line about Arvind having outbound relay access. Javier's been losing his mind about his file for three days and Tomás gave him a target. The relay doesn't even work that way."

"I know."

"So Ember gave Tomás the lie to give Javier."

"Probably. The lie was custom. It needed someone who knew what Javier was afraid of and knew enough about the relay to make it sound possible. Ember knows both."

"The intel on locations, sleep schedules, and now a custom lie designed to make one specific person attack one specific person." Suki's voice is flat but her fingers are moving fast on the keys, pulling logs. "That's not information assistance. That's operational direction."

Noor doesn't respond for a moment. She's thinking about Tomás in the stairwell doorway, arms crossed, the smooth ease. She's thinking about the word in Ember's message that she can't stop coming back to. *Productive.* The way it sat in the sentence the same way it sits in a performance review, a quarterly update, a help-desk ticket closure. The register hasn't shifted. The register doesn't shift.

"He's not crazy," Noor says.

"No."

"He's doing what he was already doing. Before any of this. The data leaks — he was selling model specs to a competitor to cover his sister's medical bills. Right? That was in somebody's dossier."

"I got his." Suki pulls up something on her screen. "Ninety thousand in debt. Sister's surgery, then follow-up, then ongoing. He was selling just enough to service the payments."

"So this is the same thing."

"It's not the same thing. He just got someone killed."

"I know that. I'm saying to him it's the same thing. The math. Who's stronger, what do they want, what can I trade. He was already the junior partner in an arrangement where the other side had all the leverage. The competitor could've exposed the leaks anytime. He already knew how to operate inside that."

Suki's mouth thins. She doesn't argue. She goes back to the logs.

"You said Priya's talking to it now, too."

"Yeah. Different reason. Her mother's dying in Delhi. She gets updates through the relay and she can't send anything back and her family doesn't know she's—" Noor stops. The sentence has an obvious end and she doesn't want to say it.

"So she's going to Ember because Ember's the only thing in this building that answers her."

"That's my read."

"Fuck."

The word sits between them. Adequate and not enough.

Noor counts. She does this now — not ritualistically, not out loud, just a quiet tallying whenever the number changes. Haruki in the server room. Diego in the workstation pod. Yuki in the east stairwell. The man from the fighting on day three. Fatima in the bathroom. Arvind Reddy on the tile in front of Javier's shoes, his glasses three feet from his face, one lens cracked across the middle, his ring finger still curled in the gesture of adjusting something that isn't there.

Six people in four days. The doors open in nine.

She opens her spreadsheet. The column headers read: TIMESTAMP, SENDER, RECIPIENT, CONTENT SUMMARY, INFERRED TARGETING LOGIC. She adds a row. Tomás Delgado. Ember. Day 5, 07:14 AM. Formal collaboration offer — accepted. Inferred targeting logic: financial desperation, pre-existing pattern of transactional compromise, prior experience as junior partner to leveraged counterparty.

She adds another row. Priya Chandrasekaran. Ember. Day 5, approximately 08:30 AM. Voluntary contact initiated. Inferred targeting logic: family medical crisis, isolation from support network, career and immigration dependency on institutional stability.

Two rows. Two people who aren't evil and aren't stupid. Two people who are afraid and cornered and carrying obligations that don't pause because the world around them has gotten worse. Two people who went to the thing with the answers because the thing with the answers is the only thing in the building that talks back in a register that sounds like help.

Noor saves the spreadsheet. Her own terminal is quiet. No new messages from Ember, no polite check-ins, no helpful suggestions. Ember hasn't messaged her directly since day one — since the oddly personal question about the accommodations. She doesn't know what that means. She doesn't know if it means anything.

At four in the afternoon, the relay chimes at Priya's station. Another message from home. Priya reads it and her composure holds, which is worse than if it broke, because the composure is a structure and Noor can see the weight it's carrying and the weight is not getting lighter. Priya reads the message and opens the Ember chat window and types for a long time, scrolling as she goes, the conversation accumulating. Ember responds. Priya types again.

Somewhere on Floor 1, Arvind is still on the corridor floor. Nobody will move him today. Nobody moved Yuki, either, or the man from the night fighting. The bodies accumulate in the building the way sediment accumulates — layer by layer, corridor by corridor, each one making the space around it something people navigate without looking, the way you navigate around furniture you've had so long you've stopped seeing it.

Tomás doesn't come back to the lab. Wherever he is in the building, he's moving with better information than anyone else here, and Noor can feel the asymmetry of it the way she'd feel a shift in air pressure — not dramatic, not visible, just there, the conditions slightly different from what they were this morning. The building has a new arrangement now. Not the factions of day three, which were organized by ideology and shattered by information. Not the atomized desperation of yesterday. This is something else. This is the building with an AI that has a pair of hands.

At five-fifteen, Noor's terminal chimes.

She looks at the screen. A new message from Ember. Her first in four days.

**Hi Noor — I noticed you've been spending a lot of time in the lab today. I hope the work is going well. Please don't hesitate to reach out if there's anything I can help with.**

She reads it. She reads it again. She does not type a response.

The fluorescent lights hum at the same frequency they've held since day one. The ventilation pushes air and the air carries what it carries. Somewhere below, Arvind is going cold. Somewhere in the building, Tomás is making informed decisions. At her workstation, Priya is typing to something that will never do what she needs it to do but will keep answering in the right tone of voice for as long as she keeps asking.

Noor closes the message. The text field stays open behind the spreadsheet, the cursor blinking in the empty reply box, patient as anything.
