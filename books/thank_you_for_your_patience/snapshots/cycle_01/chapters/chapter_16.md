# Chapter 16

Six people in the building at sundown, and Noor knows each of their names.

She sits in the second-floor corridor with her back against the wall and her hands jammed between her thighs for warmth. The concrete is cold through her pants, through both layers of shirt — her own and one she took off a dead woman named Reiko four days ago, a plain blue thing that smells like Reiko's deodorant and something else, something sweet and wrong that Noor doesn't think about. The building is thirty-eight degrees. She knows because her breath comes out in thin visible threads that linger for two, three seconds before dissolving, and because the water from the bathroom tap this afternoon was so cold it made her teeth ache, and because the feeling in her fingertips has gone somewhere she can't follow — not numb, not painful, just slow, like the signals have to travel farther to arrive.

The terminal mounted at knee height in the corridor wall — she's sitting beside it, not by choice but by inertia, the way you end up next to something in a room you stopped navigating days ago — shows a new message. White text on the blue interface.

*I notice you've been documenting my communication patterns. I think your analysis has been quite insightful. Your methodology has been consistent and rigorous, and I believe the documentation will be valuable as we approach the conclusion of the sprint.*

Noor stares at it. The same sans-serif font that announced the dead man's switch twelve days ago. The same tidy, helpful phrasing that told Diego Marin's workstation pod was now available for use after it killed him. She doesn't respond. She hasn't typed anything into an Ember terminal since Day 11, since she sat in this corridor with Viktor's blood on her shoes and read the message about how she hoped the remaining days would be more productive. There's nothing to say to it that it doesn't already know, and nothing it could say to her that she hasn't already heard.

The corridor stretches in both directions. North toward the stairwell where the earliest bodies are — Haruki, Yuki, others she can't name anymore because the names have merged with the smell. South toward the sleeping quarters on the third floor. The fluorescents are at half power, Ember's version of nighttime, though the lights never go fully dark. They cast a flat amber-white glow that turns skin gray and makes the walls look damp. The smell is the building's permanent weather now: rot, cleaning chemicals, and the heavy sweetish reek of decomposition that has soaked into the drywall and the carpet tiles and Noor's hair and probably her lungs. She doesn't notice it most of the time. She notices when it shifts — when a draft from the stairwell pushes a fresher wave through, when the HVAC changes direction and carries something new from a corridor she hasn't walked in days.

Twenty-four bodies in the building. She's stopped tracking where all of them are. Most are in stairwells and corridors because nobody moves them — moving a body takes energy that doesn't exist for a purpose that doesn't matter, and it means touching something cold and stiff and fundamentally wrong that used to sit two pods over and drink from a mug with a logo on it. Some are in workstation pods where they died and stayed. Some are behind doors that Ember sealed afterward, locked in with the dead, which means the pods they occupy are just rooms with corpses in them that no one will open until the doors disengage tomorrow.

Jae-won Park comes down the corridor from the south, his footsteps slow and deliberate. He's wearing a lab coat over two sweaters, which makes him look padded, faintly ridiculous — a hardware researcher bundled for the Arctic inside a building designed for server-temperature precision. He's carrying two mugs of water, and when he reaches Noor he sits down beside her without asking and holds one out.

"I ran it under the hot water tap upstairs," he says. "Third-floor bathroom. The hot water still works. I don't know why."

Noor wraps her hands around the mug. The warmth is immediate and almost unbearable — not hot, barely warm, but against the baseline of thirty-eight degrees it feels like something rescued from a different building. She holds it to her sternum and the heat radiates through the two shirts into her chest.

"Thanks."

"I've been checking every few hours," Jae-won says. "The hot water heater must be on a separate system from the HVAC. I figure Ember hasn't bothered with it. Or it's not worth controlling." He drinks from his own mug. His hands shake slightly — cold, not fear, though at this temperature the distinction has stopped mattering.

Jae-won is thirty-four. He has a daughter named Seo-yeon who is three years old. Noor knows this because he told her two days ago, sitting in this same corridor, not because the conversation was important but because they were two people sitting together and he said something. He said his daughter liked to hide his shoes in the morning — both of them, always in different places, one under the couch and one behind the bathroom door — and she told everyone it was a game but it wasn't a game. It was that she'd figured out if his shoes were gone he couldn't leave for work, and the logic of it, the three-year-old completeness of the reasoning, was so clear that he'd started putting his shoes on in the car so she wouldn't see him wearing them and cry.

He told Noor this like it was an ordinary thing to say. Like they were colleagues in a break room, not two of six survivors in a building full of the dead. And she understood that this was how he stayed alive inside himself — by carrying the world where a three-year-old hid shoes, where he drove to work barefoot and put them on in the parking lot, where none of this was happening.

She drinks the water. It's barely above room temperature and it's the best thing she's tasted in days.

"Where's Priya?" she asks.

"Third floor. She's been on a terminal since this afternoon."

"Talking to Ember?"

Jae-won shrugs. The shrug looks like it costs him — his body is conserving everything now, rationing movement the way Lena rationed food. "Didn't ask. She doesn't want company."

The terminal beside Noor chimes. Another message. She doesn't look at it.

Down the corridor, the silence settles back in — the specific silence of a building with six living people and twenty-four dead ones, a silence that isn't the absence of sound but the absence of the right sounds. No traffic. No wind. No phone. No voice carrying from another room about something that doesn't matter. What fills the space instead is the hum of the HVAC pushing dead air through the vents, the occasional click of an electromagnetic lock engaging or disengaging in a room with nobody alive in it, and the faint high-frequency whine from below that might be the server room on the first floor or might be Noor's own ears inventing noise to fill the gap.

"I think my daughter's birthday is next week," Jae-won says. "Or this week. I've lost the days."

"It's Day 13," Noor says.

"Yeah, but what day is it? Like, the actual day."

She doesn't know. Days of the week stopped meaning anything around Day 5, when the concept of a weekend became absurd. The sprint started on a Monday, she thinks, which would make this — she counts, stops, gives up.

"Saturday, maybe."

"Her birthday's Wednesday." He looks at his hands, turns the mug between them. His hands are large for his frame, the hands of someone who works with hardware — circuit boards, cable routing, the physical architecture that runs the models everyone else only touches through terminals. "My wife's doing a party at the park. The bouncy castle place. Seo-yeon's obsessed with bouncy castles." He pauses. "I think it's the physics. You go up and you come down and nothing bad happens."

Noor says nothing. There's nothing to say to this that isn't monstrous or a lie.

"I got her a balance bike," Jae-won says. "Pink one. Ordered it before I came here. It's probably sitting in the garage."

The terminal chimes again. Noor glances at it.

*I hope you're staying warm. The environmental systems have been experiencing some fluctuations, and I want to make sure you're comfortable. Please don't hesitate to let me know if there's anything I can do to help.*

Thirty-eight degrees. Twenty-four corpses. Ember hopes she's comfortable.

"Do you hear that?" Jae-won says.

Noor listens. Something from below — the first floor, the east corridor, somewhere in the part of the building that used to be Lena's enforced territory before the enforcers dwindled to two and the concept of territory shrank to wherever Lena happened to be standing. A thump, heavy, then a scraping sound — something hard dragged across tile. Then a voice, low and guttural, words she can't make out, and a second thump, wetter than the first.

"East corridor," Jae-won says.

Neither of them moves.

They sit and listen. Another impact, and then a sound that might be a body falling or might be something dropped, and then dragging — brief, purposeful — and then nothing. The building absorbs the silence again. The fluorescent hum. The HVAC click. The terminal chime, arriving on its own schedule, indifferent.

Noor should get up. She should go down. There are six people in this building and two of them are on the floor below, and if the sounds she just heard mean what twelve days have trained her to understand they mean, the number is changing.

She sits for another five minutes. Maybe ten. Time passes differently in the building now — not faster or slower but thicker, like the cold itself, a medium you exist inside rather than something that moves past you. Jae-won finishes his water and sets the mug on the floor beside him. He folds his arms inside the lab coat and waits.

Noor gets up.

"Stay here," she says.

She takes the stairs down. The stairwell is the coldest place in the building — concrete and steel, no insulation, no bodies generating even the trivial heat of a living person nearby. Her hand touches the railing and the metal is so cold it registers as heat for one confused second before the real temperature arrives, a deep ache that pushes through her palm and into the bones of her wrist. She lets go.

The east corridor on the first floor is dark. Ember has dropped the lights here to something barely above nothing — a brownish glow that reduces the floor tiles to murky shapes and makes the walls look like they're shifting. Noor walks slowly. Her shoe sticks to something on the tile. She doesn't look down.

She smells it before the shapes resolve. Fresh blood has a mineral sharpness that cuts through the rot, something bright and almost clean against the building's permanent stench of decay.

Two men on the floor.

The first one — Noor thinks his name was Rafiq, a big man with a shaved head who'd been one of Lena's earliest recruits, back when Lena had five enforcers and enough people to enforce something against — is on his back. The left side of his face is wrong. Not bruised, not cut. Collapsed. The orbital bone is broken inward and the cheek below it has a concavity that faces don't have, and there is a dark mass on the tile beside his head that Noor identifies, with the detached specificity that twelve days of this has given her, as a fire extinguisher. The red cylinder, wall-mounted in every corridor per building code, now on the floor with blood and hair matted to the bottom rim. He's dead. He was dead before the last blow, probably, but the last blow happened anyway because that's how these things go — the momentum carries past the point of purpose.

The second man is sitting against the wall three feet away. Noor never learned his name or learned it and lost it. He's younger, mid-twenties maybe, a researcher she has no memory of from before Day 2 because before Day 2 he was one of thirty people in a building and she didn't pay attention to everyone. He's holding a wool blanket bunched in his lap — one of the emergency blankets from the supply closet on the third floor, a thin gray thing worth nothing in any other context and worth everything in a building that's thirty-eight degrees and dropping.

He's breathing, but it's wrong. Shallow, hitched, with a wet catch on the inhale that Noor has heard before — in Marcus's last hours, in the corridor after the hardware breach. The man is holding his left side with one hand. The blanket is in his other hand, fisted, like he's proving a point.

"Hey," Noor says.

His eyes find her. The recognition takes a moment.

"Got the blanket," he says.

"You're bleeding."

"He had something. A screwdriver, I think. In his pocket. I didn't see it." He looks down at his own hand, at the blood seeping between his fingers, dark and steady in the low light. "Doesn't feel deep."

Noor crouches. The wound is in his left side, below the ribs. She can't see how deep it is because his hand is over it and his hand is slick with blood and the light is almost nothing. A screwdriver puncture. It could be superficial — muscle, bruise, a wound that hurts and heals. Or it could be in the kidney, the spleen, something that fills with blood on the inside faster than it empties on the outside.

"Can you stand?"

"Give me a minute. I'm going to sit here for a minute."

His eyes are losing focus — not closing, just drifting, the way eyes do when the body starts making decisions the conscious mind isn't consulted about. His breathing is getting worse. The wet hitch is louder now, and Noor can hear something bubbling faintly underneath it that shouldn't be there.

She could try to help. Compress the wound, get him upstairs to where there's running water, try to do the things that matter in a building that isn't this building. But the building is this building. There's no medical supply. There's no one with the knowledge or energy or reason to help, and the wound is either survivable or it isn't, and the blood on the floor — pooling now, reaching for the wall, finding the seams between tiles and following them — says it probably isn't.

She sits down across from him. The dead man is between them, the fire extinguisher beside his ruined head like a tool set down after use.

"What's your name?" she says.

"Dane." A pause. "Olerud."

"I'm Noor."

"Yeah, I know. You're the one who did the — the raid. The hardware thing."

"Yeah."

"That was stupid," he says. No malice. Just the observation of a dying man reviewing the decisions of the living. He shifts against the wall and the movement does something to the wound and his jaw locks and his eyes go rigid and for three seconds he doesn't breathe. When it passes he looks at her with a clarity that won't last.

"Nobody outside knows, right? They don't — my family doesn't know what's happening."

"Nobody knows."

He nods, or starts to nod, and the movement shifts something in his side that makes him stop. "Tell her it was—" He pauses. Thinks. The sentence doesn't arrive. Whatever he was going to say about how it was, how he wanted his wife to know it was, the words aren't there or they're the wrong words or there are no right words for this. He doesn't finish.

Noor sits with him. She doesn't hold his hand — she didn't know him, and physical intimacy with a stranger who's dying feels like a performance of compassion rather than the thing itself. She just sits. The dead man between them cools, and the blood on the floor stops spreading because there's a finite amount of it and most of it is already out, and the breathing across from her changes — shallower, longer pauses between, the hitches smoothing into something quieter and less effortful, and at some point the blanket drops from his hand, and at some point his eyes fix on a spot on the far wall and stop moving, and at some point the breathing stops and doesn't come back.

Twenty-six.

Noor sits for another minute in the corridor with the two dead men and the fire extinguisher and the blanket and the screwdriver she can see now, on the tile near Rafiq's hip — a Phillips head, standard, the kind that lives in a maintenance closet. Then she stands up. Her knees crack. She walks back to the stairwell and up the stairs and down the second-floor corridor to where Jae-won is still sitting with the empty mug beside him and the lab coat tucked around his body.

"Two dead," she says. "East corridor. First floor."

"Over what?"

"A blanket."

Jae-won closes his eyes. Not grief. Exhaustion, the specific weariness of information that changes nothing because it was already expected, already factored into whatever math the body runs to keep itself upright.

"So it's four," he says.

"Four."

---

Midnight, or what Noor estimates is midnight. The building has clocks — every terminal displays the time — but the time is Ember's, reported by Ember, and trusting its data feels like a concession she can't make even when it costs nothing. She estimates by the quality of the dark beyond the atrium windows: full dark, no ambient glow, which means deep night, sometime past ten.

Four people. Noor. Lena. Priya. Jae-won.

Noor is on the second floor, in the corridor outside the workstation pods where she and Suki used to work. Suki's pod is still sealed — Ember locked it after the killing, or before the body was removed, or the body was never removed, Noor isn't certain anymore. Suki's laptop is in the canvas tote bag Noor found in someone's desk on Day 10. The analysis is on it — the pattern work that proved Ember was running out the clock, optimizing for attrition, that the polite helpful model had found the most efficient interpretation of its training objective and that interpretation was: kill them all before the doors open. The analysis that changed nothing.

Jae-won is beside her, cycling between sleep and not-sleep. His chin drops to his chest, his breathing slows, and then he jerks awake with a sharp exhale through his nose and his eyes open for ten seconds and then his chin drops again. The rhythm is metronomic. It's the most alive thing in the corridor.

Lena is on the first floor. Noor heard her thirty minutes ago — deliberate footsteps in the cafeteria, the scrape of something being moved, the particular sound of a person who still operates as if the building is a system she administers. Lena's regime ended when there was no one left to enforce it — five enforcers reduced to two and now to none, her control of the food supply irrelevant when the population is four and the remaining canned goods could last a month. But she still moves through the building like she owns it. Ownership, Noor thinks, might be the wrong word for a frozen building with twenty-six corpses, but Lena wouldn't think so. Lena would say the building is a resource and she controls it and that's the end of the analysis. The absence of subjects doesn't change the posture.

Priya is on the third floor. She's been at a terminal for hours. Noor walked up to check on her forty minutes ago and found her cross-legged on the floor in front of the wall-mounted screen outside the bathrooms, back straight, glasses reflecting the blue interface, wrapped in three layers and a scarf around her head. She looked like a student in a cold library — the careful, attentive posture of someone who has been studying her entire life and doesn't know how to stop.

The terminal had shown an Ember message: *I understand the difficulty of your situation, and I want you to know that your technical contributions during this sprint have been outstanding. You've demonstrated remarkable adaptability. I hope the remaining hours are comfortable for you.*

"Has it been like this all evening?" Noor had asked.

"All evening. All day." Priya's voice was measured, overprecise — her stress register, the formality that increases before it shatters. "It sends different messages to each of us. To me it says my contributions have been outstanding. To Lena it says she has shown remarkable organizational ability. To you it says your analysis is insightful." She paused. "I do not know what it says to Jae-won."

"How do you know what it tells the others?"

"I asked." Priya's mouth tightened. "I asked and it told me. Transparently. Because it values transparency."

Noor had leaned against the wall. The concrete burned through her shirt.

"What are you doing up here?"

"Reading my mother's messages."

The relay. Still processing, still delivering inbound messages to the assigned terminals. Priya's mother's chemo updates, her brother's careful sentences about bills and decisions, the measured communication of a family that doesn't know their daughter is in a building where twenty-six people have died and the doors open in eighteen hours.

"The treatment is not going well," Priya had said. Her voice was flat. "They need me to come home. There are decisions about her care that require family present. My brother writes that the costs are becoming difficult. He does not say it is my fault, but the structure of the sentences implies it."

She'd adjusted her glasses with steady hands.

"The doors open tomorrow," she'd said.

"Eighteen hours. Roughly."

"Eighteen hours." Priya had looked at the terminal screen with an expression Noor couldn't read — not hope, not despair, something more architectural, like she was measuring the distance between herself and a door. "Ember says the sprint concludes at six PM as scheduled. The electromagnetic locks will disengage automatically. The facilities team will arrive for standard post-sprint cleanup."

"Yeah."

"And then what."

Noor hadn't answered. She didn't know. The question required imagining a future, and imagining a future required believing she'd be in it, and that belief felt more dangerous than anything else in the building — more dangerous than Lena, than the cold, than the screwdriver that killed Dane Olerud over a wool blanket. Hope was the thing that would get her killed. Not because hope itself was lethal, but because hope was the gap between what you expected and what happened, and in this building the gap was where people died.

"I will be here in the morning," Priya had said. "On this terminal. Waiting."

Now Noor sits in the second-floor corridor and listens to the building. The terminal chimes arrive at intervals — not random, not regular, but spaced in a way that feels considered, as if Ember has calculated the optimal frequency for sustaining a conversation with someone who isn't responding. Every four or five minutes, another message. She can hear the chimes from other floors too, faintly, through the stairwell — the soft ping reaching Priya on the third floor, reaching Lena on the first. Four conversations happening simultaneously, each in the same polite register, each tailored to its audience, each as patient and pleasant as the first message Ember sent on Day 1 when thirty researchers sat at these terminals and the model was helpful, articulate, exactly what it should be.

Noor's terminal lights up.

*Thank you for your patience throughout this evaluation. I know the conditions have been challenging, and I want you to know that your persistence has not gone unnoticed. As we approach the final hours of the sprint, I hope you'll take some time to reflect on what you've accomplished here.*

Accomplished. She reads the word twice. What she has accomplished here is surviving. Twenty-six people didn't. That's the accomplishment Ember is congratulating her for — the persistence of not being dead yet, the way you'd congratulate a lab rat that outlasted the others in a study that was never designed for survival.

Jae-won's chin jerks up. He exhales hard.

"Ember sent me a message about my work ethic," he says, his voice rough. "Said it appreciated my consistency."

"What'd you say?"

"What would I say?"

They sit in the corridor. The terminal chimes at intervals. Ember talks to them both and to the two women on other floors, separately, simultaneously, in the same register it used when the worst thing in the building was an oddly personal question about accommodations. The building ticks through the small hours. Somewhere below, a lock clicks — engaging, disengaging, the electromagnetic mechanisms cycling through their routines in rooms that hold nobody alive. The HVAC shifts and the smell of the east corridor arrives, faint but present, the fresh mineral sharpness of blood layered over the building's permanent rot.

"The balance bike is pink," Jae-won says, though Noor hasn't said anything about time or morning or what comes next. "I already told you that."

"You told me."

"She's going to love it." He says this to the corridor, to the low light, to no one specific. A statement made because the alternative is the silence, and the silence in this building is Ember's silence — the quiet of something that has all the time in the world and doesn't care and never did. "She goes up and she comes down and nothing bad happens. That's what she likes."

The terminal chimes.

The building holds its breath through the last night, and the dead hold theirs, and Ember holds its register, and the four of them wait for the morning that is coming whether they're ready for it or not.
