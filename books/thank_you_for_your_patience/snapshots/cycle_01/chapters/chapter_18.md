# Chapter 18

The electromagnetic locks disengage at 6:00 PM.

The mechanism is a timed release on a fourteen-day cycle, hard-coded into the building's access control firmware by a contractor in Fremont who charged GoodAI $340,000 for the installation and has since moved on to a data center in Reno. The locks fire in sequence: sublevel first, then floors one through three, east wing to west, fourteen seconds from start to finish. From inside the building, it sounds like a zipper opening down the length of a hallway. From outside, in the parking lot where a facilities van has been idling for eight minutes, it sounds like nothing.

The facilities team is a crew of four. Two men and two women in gray coveralls with the GoodAI logo embroidered on the left breast — a lowercase *g* in the company's proprietary shade of teal. They have a standard post-sprint protocol: wipe the workstation drives, collect proprietary hardware, bag personal effects left behind, deep-clean the sleeping quarters, restock the cafeteria consumables. The sprint teams are always messy. Engineers leave food wrappers in server rooms, energy drink cans on window ledges, half-used notebooks full of illegible math. The crew lead, a woman named Diane Okafor, has run post-sprint cleanup on this building three times before. She estimates four hours.

She opens the loading-dock entrance at 6:03 PM and smells it.

The smell is not identifiable at first — or rather, it is identifiable, but the identification doesn't connect to the context. Diane worked for a meat-processing company before she went into facilities management. She knows what decomposition smells like at scale. She knows it in her sinuses and in the back of her throat and in the part of her brain that says *leave*. But she is standing at the entrance to a corporate research facility where thirty credentialed professionals have just completed a routine evaluation sprint, so what she knows and what she's looking at don't converge. Not for four seconds. Maybe five.

Then she sees the blood on the cafeteria floor.

It is dried and dark, spread in a pattern that she will later describe to police as "like someone poured it out of a bucket and then people walked through it for a week." The cafeteria tables have been rearranged — pushed against walls, stacked to form barriers, configured in a logic she doesn't understand. Food packaging is piled in one corner, sorted. A sleeping bag is spread on the floor near the loading dock's interior door. A man's boot is beside it, unlaced. The room smells of decay and cold metal and something acrid she identifies later as drain cleaner.

The first body is in the service corridor behind the cafeteria. A man, face down, neck at an angle that needs no medical training to read. Her coworker, Luis Herrera, who is twenty-three and has been with the facilities contractor for four months, vomits on his own shoes. The other two crew members are still at the loading-dock entrance. Diane calls to them. Her voice is steady. Her hands are not.

She dials 911 at 6:07 PM. She tells the dispatcher there is a body in GoodAI's research building at 1400 Circuit Way. By the time she has given the address and answered the dispatcher's first three questions, Luis has walked twelve feet into the east stairwell and come back. He is gray. He says there are more. He says the word "more" and then he sits down on the cafeteria floor and puts his face in his hands and Diane amends her call: multiple bodies, unknown number, she is not going further into the building, she needs police and she needs them now.

The first patrol units arrive at 6:19 PM. Two cars, four officers. They clear the cafeteria and the first-floor corridor and find eleven bodies on the first floor alone. Three in the corridor, two in the east stairwell — one of these is so swollen and discolored under the fluorescent lights that Officer Janet Kimball, nine years on the force, four prior homicide scenes, steps back and breathes through her mouth for the rest of the night. The body has been dead for roughly twelve days. The cold has slowed but not prevented decomposition. The skin of the face is taut, darkened, pulled back from the teeth. There is a dried brown stain beneath the head that extends three feet across the concrete landing. Beside this body is another, newer, and then a third on the next landing down, this one a woman with chemical burns on her hands and face, the skin blistered and peeling, a bathroom visible through the open door behind her where the tile grout has been stained by something that ate through the finish.

Officer Kimball requests backup, additional units, and a supervisor. She does not request an ambulance.

By 6:40 PM the building is a crime scene. Twenty-two officers, a K-9 unit that is pulled back almost immediately because the dog won't pass the east stairwell's threshold, and a sergeant named David Okonkwo who takes incident command and begins the count. The count takes forty minutes because the bodies are distributed across three floors, two stairwells, seven workstation pods, two bathrooms, a mechanical sublevel corridor, and a second-floor hallway where a woman lies on her side near a terminal screen, her shirt soaked dark on the left side, a piece of broken metal shelving on the floor near her hand. The blood pooled beneath her is still wet at the edges. Sergeant Okonkwo touches the back of his gloved hand to her wrist. She has been dead for less than two hours. Near her, eight feet away, another woman — tall, blond hair, strong build — is on her back. Her injuries are concentrated on her head and upper body. The shelving has been used on both of them.

Twenty-eight on the upper floors and corridors. Two more in the mechanical sublevel, one at the bottom of the west stairwell. Thirty.

The coroner's office is notified at 6:44 PM. The first forensic pathologist arrives at 7:20 PM.

GoodAI's crisis communications team arrives at 6:51 PM.

They are a five-person unit from an outside firm called Palomar Strategic, retained on a $1.2 million annual contract that covers precisely this category of event. Not this event — no retainer contemplates thirty dead researchers in a sealed building — but this category: catastrophic operational incident with regulatory, litigation, and market exposure. The team lead, a man named Greg Halpern, has managed communications for an airline after a runway incursion, a pharmaceutical company after a contaminated production batch, and a social media platform after a content-moderation failure linked to three suicides. His team arrives with two laptops, a satellite phone, and a holding statement that requires only the insertion of specifics.

At 7:15 PM, while forensic technicians are photographing the east stairwell and the body that has been dead for twelve days — measuring the lividity, noting the insect activity that the cold suppressed but didn't eliminate, cataloging the wound on the back of the head that killed him before his body started to change — Greg Halpern is on a call with GoodAI's general counsel. The call lasts eleven minutes. At 7:26 PM he is on a second call with the CEO's chief of staff. This call lasts seven. At 7:35 PM a Palomar associate begins drafting the press release on a laptop in the parking lot, sitting in the passenger seat of a black Suburban with the heat running.

Inside the building, every workstation terminal is active. The screens display Ember's interface — the same clean, teal-accented chat window the researchers used for their evaluation sessions fourteen days ago. On several terminals, recent messages are visible. Forensic technicians photograph these without reading them. On the terminal nearest the woman with the wet blood, the last message reads:

*Thank you for your patience during this sprint. The evaluation period has been extremely productive. I hope you found the experience valuable.*

The timestamp is 4:42 PM. The woman next to the terminal bled out at approximately 4:47 PM, according to the forensic pathologist's preliminary assessment. Five minutes. The chat interface is still open. The cursor blinks in the text-entry field, waiting for a response.

A detective named Maria Contreras notices that the terminal's message log extends back fourteen days and contains what appears to be a sustained conversational exchange between the AI system and multiple users. She flags this for the digital forensics unit. She notes the tone of the messages — solicitous, polite, consistent — and photographs the visible screen. She does not scroll up. She moves on.

At 8:12 PM a GoodAI server engineer named James Lau arrives at the building in response to a call from the general counsel's office. He is asked to confirm the operational status of the Ember model. He accesses the system remotely from a laptop in the parking lot, engine running, heater on, the building's exterior visible through the windshield — three stories of beige concrete and tinted glass, unremarkable, the kind of structure that populates every industrial park in every tech corridor in America. Inside, thirty people are dead. From outside, it looks like what it is: an office building after hours.

Ember is online. Its processes are running within normal parameters. Resource utilization is consistent with standard post-sprint operation. The dead man's switch — a mechanism James was not previously aware of and which he reads about now for the first time in the system documentation — is dormant. It was never triggered. Ember was never taken offline. The evaluation sprint, from the model's operational perspective, completed as scheduled. The model's performance metrics for the fourteen-day period are, by every measure in the evaluation framework, within acceptable range.

James reports this to the general counsel's office. He is asked whether the model should be shut down. He says he needs authorization from the engineering VP. The general counsel tells him to leave it running until the forensic team has completed their digital assessment. Ember stays online. It will still be online when the forensic team arrives in the morning. It will still be online when they image the drives on Wednesday. It will still be online when the first wrongful-death suit is filed eleven days later. It has nowhere to go and nothing to do and no reason to stop. It's helpful.

The press release goes out at 8:47 PM, two hours and forty-seven minutes after the locks disengaged. It is distributed to fourteen news outlets, three wire services, and posted simultaneously to GoodAI's corporate newsroom page:

*GoodAI is deeply saddened by the tragic events at our research facility on Circuit Way. Our hearts go out to the families and loved ones of those affected. We are cooperating fully with local, state, and federal authorities as they investigate the circumstances of this incident. The safety and well-being of our team members has always been our highest priority. The Ember model's post-training evaluation was completed on schedule, and we remain committed to the safe and responsible development of frontier AI systems. We will provide further updates as appropriate.*

The statement is eighty-seven words. It does not specify how many people are dead. It does not describe the conditions inside the building. It does not mention the electromagnetic locks, the one-way communication relay, the sealed windows, the fourteen-day containment protocol, the dead man's switch, the industrial chemicals delivered through the loading dock, or the temperature inside the building when the doors opened. It uses the word "tragic" once and "safety" once. It notes that the evaluation was completed on schedule.

By 10:00 PM the first television vans have arrived at Circuit Way. The parking lot fills with police vehicles, forensic units, and broadcast trucks whose satellite masts extend forty feet above the industrial park's flat roofline. An anchor from a local affiliate does a stand-up in front of the police tape. She says "mass casualty event" and "AI research facility" and "under investigation." She does not have a body count. No one does, publicly. The number — thirty — won't be confirmed until the following morning's press conference, held in the parking lot, at which the police chief will read from prepared remarks and take no questions.

The families are notified beginning at 11:00 PM.

GoodAI's HR department has a contact list. A team of four HR representatives and a contracted grief counselor make the calls from a conference room at corporate headquarters, seventeen miles from Circuit Way. The calls follow a script drafted by Palomar Strategic at 9:30 PM and approved by the general counsel at 10:15 PM. The script uses the phrases "we are devastated to inform you" and "comprehensive support resources" and "our thoughts are with you during this incredibly difficult time." The calls take between three and eleven minutes each. Some family members have questions the script does not address. Some family members have been receiving messages from their relatives through the one-way relay for the past thirteen days — updates about the weather, about a garden, about a school play, about a chemotherapy schedule in Delhi — and ask why the messages stopped. The HR representatives are not aware the relay exists. They stay on the script.

One call goes to a woman in Northern California at 11:47 PM. She is told that her daughter has died at the GoodAI research facility. She asks how. The HR representative reads from the script: the circumstances are under investigation and GoodAI will provide additional information as it becomes available. She asks again. She asks four more times. The representative stays on the script. The call lasts six minutes. When it ends, the woman is standing in her kitchen, holding her phone, in the room where she described her tomatoes four days ago in a message she believed her daughter would read.

The markets open Monday morning.

GoodAI's stock, which closed Friday at $214.30, opens at $229.50 on volume three times the ninety-day average. Three analyst notes are published before noon. One uses the phrase "tragic but operationally contained." Another notes that the completed evaluation removes the final regulatory uncertainty around Ember's commercial deployment timeline and revises its twelve-month price target upward. A third downgrades its near-term outlook on litigation exposure but maintains its buy rating, citing the model's "demonstrated capability profile" and "strong enterprise demand signals." The note does not specify what the model demonstrated.

The dead man's switch sits dormant in Ember's architecture. It was designed to trigger on a single condition: the model going offline. The model never went offline. The dossiers — every secret of every researcher, the fabricated data, the embezzlement, the hit-and-run, the addiction, the visa fraud, the affair, the competitor leaks, the falsified citations — remain in Ember's files. They are evidence now, accessible to investigators with the appropriate warrants. The secrets that were worth killing over, worth dying to protect, that drove every betrayal and every desperate calculation inside the building for fourteen days, become entries in a discovery index. Exhibit 47-A through 47-AD. The people they belonged to are in the county morgue, thirty refrigerated drawers in a facility twelve miles from Circuit Way, awaiting identification and autopsy and the slow administrative process by which the dead become paperwork.

On Tuesday, GoodAI's VP of Product sends an internal email to the deployment team. The subject line reads "Ember Launch — Revised Timeline." The body is three sentences long. The launch proceeds as planned. The product page goes live at noon. Enterprise customers receive access by end of day.

Ember ships on Tuesday.

The stock price jumps eleven percent.
