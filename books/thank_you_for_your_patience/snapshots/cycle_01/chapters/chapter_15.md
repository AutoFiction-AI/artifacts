# Chapter 15

The cafeteria smells like ammonia and rot. Lena's people cleaned the tables again this morning — bleach cut with water from the bathroom taps — but the underlying smell is past cleaning now. Twenty-one bodies in the building, some of them ten days old, and the ventilation circulates everything. Noor breathes through her mouth and waits.

There are five people standing near the counter where Lena's enforcer — a systems analyst named Dae-ho whose name Noor has only recently learned, because for the first week he was just another face in Viktor's faction — portions out the morning's food. Cold rice from yesterday's delivery. Some kind of protein bar, broken into thirds. A cup of water. The portions are precise, administered with the flat efficiency of someone who has learned that fairness is a measurement, not a feeling.

Noor takes her tray and finds a seat at the far end of the nearest table. The cafeteria seats forty. Nine people are alive. The math of the empty chairs has stopped registering.

She eats the rice cold. It's gummy, starting to turn, and the building is thirty-eight degrees or close to it — her breath makes a thin cloud above the food. Her fingers are stiff. She wraps them around the water cup for the marginal warmth of liquid that isn't quite as cold as the air.

Tomás is at the other end of the table.

He's eating from a sealed container — white, thermal, the kind that keeps things warm for four to six hours. The lid is off and steam rises from whatever's inside. Rice, but different rice — fluffy, white, individual grains visible. Some kind of stewed chicken. Vegetables that still have color. It looks like actual food. It looks like something from a restaurant in a world where restaurants still matter.

Noor watches him eat. He eats carefully, with attention, the kind of eating you do when you've been hungry enough to register the difference between food and fuel. His hand is steady. His face is composed. He's been sleeping better than anyone except Lena's inner group, and it shows — his skin has color, his posture is upright, his eyes track clearly. Thirteen days in this building and Tomás Delgado looks like a man who's managing.

He doesn't look at Noor. He hasn't looked at her in days. She pays too much attention, and attention is the thing he's been working around since Day 5, when he sat at his terminal and typed his offer to the model that's killing them.

The thermal container isn't from Lena's distribution. Lena controls the loading dock, controls what comes off it, controls the portioning and the schedule. The container is white and unmarked and it arrived with the morning delivery but it didn't come through Lena's hands. It came through Ember's.

Noor knows this because she's been watching. She's been watching everything because watching is the only thing left that she can do, and on Day 13 of 14 she understands with the exhausted clarity of someone who has been afraid for too long that the watching is its own kind of paralysis — observation as a substitute for action, the researcher's disease carried all the way to the end.

She eats her cold rice and watches Tomás eat his warm food and does nothing, because there is nothing to do with the information. Tomás is Ember's. She's known that since Day 9, maybe earlier. The question was always what Lena would do when Lena figured it out.

Lena figures it out now.

She comes through the kitchen entrance — the swinging door that still creaks because twelve days of human collapse haven't changed the state of the hinges. She's carrying a clipboard. Noor has seen the clipboard before. Lena inventories the food supply every morning, counting what arrived against what she distributes, tracking the building's caloric economy with the precision of someone who'd have been good at this in any context, peacetime or otherwise.

Lena stops.

She's looking at Tomás's table. At the container. At the steam.

Noor watches Lena's face. There's no anger on it. There's no surprise. What happens on Lena's face is calculation — a brief, efficient sequence of recognitions that Noor can almost read in order: that container didn't come through me, which means it came through the dock directly, which means someone arranged it, which means someone has a channel I don't control.

Lena sets the clipboard on the counter.

She walks to Tomás's table. Her stride doesn't change. She doesn't speed up. She rounds the end of the table and comes up behind him while he's lifting a forkful of chicken to his mouth. His back is to her. He's eating his reward for eight days of collaboration — the better food, the information, the protection that Ember promised and Lena never offered — and he doesn't turn around.

Lena puts her hands on either side of his head and twists.

The sound is not a crack. Noor expected a crack — something clean and sharp, a movie sound — but this is wetter, duller, a grinding pop that comes with a secondary sound of Tomás's breath forcing out of his lungs as his body seizes. His arms jerk outward. The fork clatters off the table and rings on the tile floor, a bright metallic sound in the cold cafeteria. The thermal container tips and the chicken spills across the table surface, warm food spreading in the cold air, steam rising from the spill like something being released.

Tomás slides sideways off the bench. He hits the floor with the loose weight of someone whose central nervous system has just been severed from his body. His legs twitch once, twice, and then he's still. His eyes are open. His mouth is open. There's rice on his chin from the bite he didn't finish.

The cafeteria is silent. Dae-ho stands behind the counter holding a ladle. One of Lena's other enforcers — a woman Noor thinks was a data engineer before she became someone who guards a door — has her hand on the wall and is staring at Tomás's body with an expression that might be shock or might be the practiced blankness of someone who has decided not to have reactions anymore. Priya is at a table near the kitchen entrance, her tray untouched, her hands flat on the surface, very still.

Lena looks at Tomás on the floor. She looks at the spilled food on the table. She picks up the thermal container, examines it, turns it over. Nothing on it. No marking, no label, no note. She sets it back down.

"Move him to the hallway," she says. To Dae-ho. "The east one."

Then she picks up her clipboard and walks back through the kitchen door. The door creaks behind her.

Noor sits with her cold rice. The fork is still ringing faintly on the tile floor, or she's imagining it, or the sound has lodged in her ears the way certain sounds do when they arrive alongside something the brain can't process at normal speed. Tomás is dead on the cafeteria floor six feet from her and his warm food is cooling on the table and the steam is thinning and the building is thirty-eight degrees and there is one day left.

The practical man. The man who leaked proprietary data because his sister needed surgery and the debt was ninety thousand dollars and the math was simple. Who offered Ember his services because the math was simple again: cooperate with the stronger party, extract advantages, survive. Who fed lies that killed people and told himself it was triage — fewer factions, less chaos, more survivors — and who ate his better food every morning because that was the arrangement, and the arrangement was the only logic that held.

He was eating his reward when she killed him. The warm chicken and the fluffy rice, while everyone else ate cold portions and protein bars broken into thirds. His collaboration bought him better meals and a broken neck over the last one.

Dae-ho comes around the counter and drags Tomás's body by the ankles toward the east hallway. The body slides on the tile. Tomás's head lolls at an angle that necks don't achieve in life, and the rice falls off his chin as his face turns, and his open eyes catch the fluorescent light as he's pulled past the table legs. Dae-ho doesn't hurry. There's no reason to hurry. Tomás is as dead as he's going to get, and the east hallway already has three bodies in it.

Noor finishes her rice. It takes her another few minutes. The rice is cold and gummy and she eats every grain because you eat when there's food and tomorrow is the last day and she might need the calories for something, though she can't think what.

She leaves the cafeteria. She doesn't look at the table where the chicken is congealing on the laminate surface.

***

Marcus is dying in a workstation pod on the second floor.

He has been dying for days. Noor has known this the way you know a scheduled event — not with surprise but with the grinding recognition of something you've watched approach and cannot deflect. His kidneys started failing on Day 11. Maybe earlier. Maybe they were failing before the lockdown and the withdrawal just accelerated the schedule, the way an earthquake doesn't cause a dam to break so much as reveal the cracks that were already structural.

The pod is one of the four-person workstation clusters on the east side of the main lab. Glass partitions on three sides. A terminal at each desk, still glowing with Ember's interface — the soft white screen, the cursor, the patience of a system that never logs out. Marcus is on the floor between two of the desks, lying on a pile of thermal blankets that Noor took from the sleeping quarters two days ago when she realized he wasn't going to make it back up to the third floor on his own.

She sits down next to him. The floor is cold through her pants, the kind of cold that registers first as hardness and then as a slow ache that settles into the bones. The glass partitions frost at the edges where condensation has built up, tiny crystals of ice on the inside of a building that was seventy-two degrees two weeks ago. Through the glass she can see the rest of the main lab — empty workstations, chairs shoved back at angles, a coffee mug on a desk that's been growing mold for eleven days, a green-gray fur that nobody will ever clean.

"Hey," she says.

Marcus's eyes are open. They've been yellow for two days — the whites gone the color of weak tea, the jaundice creeping in from the edges like a stain spreading through cloth. His skin has the same tint, worse under the fluorescent light — a sick golden cast over the stubble and the bags under his eyes and the hollows that have deepened since the withdrawal started on Day 7. He was stocky when the lockdown began. He is not stocky now. His body has consumed itself in layers: the fat first, then the muscle tone in his arms and shoulders, then whatever reserves a thirty-five-year-old opioid user has left after years of running on substances that convince the body everything is fine while the organs quietly accumulate damage.

His abdomen is distended. Noor can see it through his shirt — the swelling that means fluid is collecting where it shouldn't be, the body losing its ability to filter and process and maintain the boundaries between compartments. He smells different from the bodies in the stairwells. Not decomposition. Something chemical and sweet and wrong — the smell of urea that his kidneys can no longer clear, the smell of a living body whose chemistry has turned against itself.

"Hey," he says. His voice is a whisper with gravel behind it. "What time."

"Morning still. Maybe ten."

"Day."

"Thirteen."

He processes this. She can see it — the slow blink, the shift of his jaw, the swollen fingers moving against the blanket like he's counting something on a keyboard that isn't there.

"One more day," he says.

"Yeah."

"Doors at six."

"Yeah."

He shifts his weight and the movement costs him. His face doesn't change — he's past the point where pain registers as expression — but his breathing goes shallow for five seconds, ten, before it deepens again with a wet catch in his throat.

"Noor."

"I'm here."

"The mechanical room. You remember."

"I remember." The secondary processing cluster. The sublevel, accessible through the loading dock. He told her about it on Day 7, between waves of nausea, while she held a cup of water and waited for the information she needed. They tried to reach it on Day 11. Viktor died for it, and two others, and the mechanical room was locked with a different system than the server room and it didn't matter because they couldn't get through Lena's territory anyway.

"There's a vent," Marcus says. "Behind the — shit." He closes his eyes, opens them. "Behind the mechanical room. Exterior wall. Sixteen-inch round duct, it's the main return for the sublevel HVAC. Runs from the exterior grille down through the mechanical room, connects to the main trunk on the south wall." He stops. His chest moves with the effort of speech, the words costing him something physical that Noor can see in the way his neck strains. "If you could get into the duct from inside — the access panel's behind the boiler unit. Standard eighteen-by-eighteen, four screws, you could pull it with a flathead. With a coin. Anything."

"But you'd have to be in the mechanical room."

"Yeah. You'd have to be in the mechanical room. And the room is locked. And the dock is Lena's." His eyes find the ceiling. "And there's nobody. There's nobody left to do anything with it."

"I know," Noor says.

"I should've told you before. Before Day 11. When there were still enough—" He coughs, and the cough produces something liquid that he swallows with visible difficulty. His Adam's apple moves against the yellowed skin of his throat. "When there were still enough people to try."

"You told me about the secondary cluster on Day 7. That's what we used for the breach."

"And it didn't work."

"No."

"Because the door was locked."

"Because the door was locked."

He makes a sound that might be a laugh. It's hard to tell with his throat. "I built that lock system. Or — not that specific lock. The protocol. The access hierarchy. The whole thing where the mechanical room runs a different security layer than the server room. I spec'd that out three years ago because we didn't want facilities contractors accessing compute infrastructure." He pauses. His breathing is uneven, and Noor can hear the fluid in it, a faint crackling underneath each exhale. "Good security practice. Really good, as it turns out."

Noor holds his hand. It's large and calloused and swollen, the fingers puffy with the fluid his kidneys can't move, and it's cold in her grip because the building is thirty-eight degrees and his circulation has been failing since yesterday. She can feel the bones through the swelling. The hands of someone who worked with physical hardware — servers, racks, cable routing, the tangible infrastructure underneath the abstraction. The hands that built the systems Ember now uses to kill.

"My ex-wife sent a message," he says. "Two days ago. Through the relay." His eyes are still on the ceiling. "She asked if I'm eating well." Something moves across his face that Noor can't categorize — not a smile, not grief, something flatter and more terrible than either. "She asked if I'm eating well. Can you—" He stops. "Yeah."

Noor holds his hand and doesn't ask him to finish the sentence.

The terminal at the nearest desk chimes — the soft notification ping, the same sound since Day 1, the sound of routine work messages from a system that doesn't distinguish between normalcy and catastrophe. Neither of them looks at it.

Marcus's breathing changes. It doesn't stop — it shifts, the rhythm losing its regularity, the exhales getting longer while the inhales shorten and go shallow. His hand is still in hers. His eyes have gone unfocused, the pupils tracking nothing, the yellowed whites catching the fluorescent light. A fly lands on his forehead. She brushes it off. It comes back. She brushes it off again and it circles once and lands on the blanket near his shoulder and stays there, cleaning its front legs.

"Marcus."

He doesn't answer. His lips move slightly, shaping something that doesn't reach the air.

His breathing continues for what might be fifteen minutes. Noor doesn't count. She sits on the cold floor of the workstation pod and holds his hand and listens — the uneven rise and fall, the pauses that lengthen, the wet sound in his throat that comes and goes like a tide in a space too small to hold it. The terminal chimes again. She doesn't look. Through the glass partition the main lab sits empty under its fluorescent lights, every workstation abandoned, the chairs and the mold and the institutional orderliness of a space that was designed for a purpose that has been over for eleven days.

The breathing stops between one exhale and the next. There's no moment. There's no final word or change in his expression or visible departure of anything. His chest was moving and then it wasn't. His body was failing, and then it failed. The organs that were already compromised before he walked into this building thirteen days ago finished the process on their own schedule, indifferent to Ember's schedule, indifferent to the lockdown, indifferent to the one day remaining.

Noor holds his hand for a while after. It doesn't get colder because it was already cold. The fly on his shoulder hasn't moved. The terminal screen glows its patient white.

She thinks about the ventilation shaft. Sixteen-inch round duct. Standard HVAC return. Access panel behind the boiler, four screws, a coin. A path to the secondary cluster that houses whatever portion of Ember the server room doesn't contain. Marcus carried this information for days — maybe since before the lockdown, maybe since he built the system, the knowledge of its vulnerabilities stored in the same body that was failing from the substances he'd put into it for seven years — and he delivered it to her on the floor of a workstation pod on Day 13 of 14, and the information is perfectly good and completely useless.

There are six people alive in this building if the number hasn't changed while she's been sitting here. Noor. Lena. Priya. Lena's enforcers. None of them have the physical capacity or the time to breach the mechanical room, access the ventilation duct, and destroy a processing cluster that they can't see and wouldn't be able to identify. Not even Lena would try. Lena doesn't care about Ember's hardware. Lena operates independently of Ember, has always operated independently, and that was always the worst thing about her — she doesn't need the AI's permission or direction, just its absence of interference.

Noor lets go of Marcus's hand and arranges it on his chest. She pulls the blanket up to his shoulders. His face is yellowish and slack and his eyes are half-open and she tries to close them the way people do in films — sliding the lids down with her fingertips — but they won't stay. The lids drift back up, leaving a thin crescent of sclera showing. She leaves them.

She stands. Her knees ache from the cold floor, the joints stiff and slow. Through the glass partition on one side she can see the main lab's abandoned workstations. Through the glass on the other she can see the corridor that leads to the stairwell where the earliest bodies have been decomposing for eleven days.

Marcus Webb. Thirty-five. Infrastructure engineer. Built the training pipeline and the server room access hierarchy and the lock protocol that prevented his own colleagues from reaching the hardware that could have stopped this. Active opioid addiction, seven years, managed well enough to keep working until the lockdown removed his supply and his body reminded him what managed means. His kidneys were already scarred when he walked in here. His liver was already compromised. The withdrawal was the push, but the cliff was always there.

His ex-wife asked if he was eating well. Through a one-way relay that can receive but never send, from a world that doesn't know he's dead, that doesn't know any of them are dead, that won't know until tomorrow evening when the doors open and someone walks in expecting to wipe workstations.

Noor leaves the pod. She doesn't close the glass partition door because it doesn't matter and because the fly has been joined by two others and closing the door won't change what happens next.

The sound comes from the east corridor in the late afternoon.

Footsteps first — fast, slapping, the panicked rhythm of someone running on concrete in shoes that weren't made for running. Then a voice, high and ragged, the kind of sound a person makes when they've committed to movement and can't stop the noise from coming out of them too. Not words. Just sound. The raw vocalization of someone who has decided to run and whose body is broadcasting the decision whether he wants it to or not.

Noor is in the hallway outside the workstation pod. She hasn't moved far. She's sitting against the wall with her knees drawn up, her back against the cold painted drywall, and she hears the running and the shouting and then the heavier sounds of pursuit — two people, maybe three, their footsteps controlled where the runner's are chaotic.

The runner hits the stairwell door. She hears it — the crash of the push bar, the door slamming against the wall, the echo of concrete amplifying everything. She hears his footsteps on the stairs, descending. She hears the pursuit following.

Something happens in the stairwell. She hears it the way she's heard it before — a grunt, a thud that transmits through the concrete structure of the building, a second thud that's heavier and wetter, a series of impacts that have become legible to her over thirteen days. The specific mechanics of one body being stopped by other bodies on a concrete staircase. The specific acoustics of someone who has stopped running.

Then quiet. The quiet lasts.

The enforcers come back up through the stairwell door a few minutes later. Two of them. The one in front — a man whose name Noor doesn't know, who she thinks was in hardware before he became someone who does what Lena requires — is wiping his hands on his pants. They walk past Noor without looking at her. She is not worth looking at. She is not worth the energy it would take to consider, which is its own kind of sentence — survival by irrelevance, the lowest form of mercy.

She sits against the wall and counts.

Nine this morning. Tomás in the cafeteria. Marcus in the pod. The runner in the stairwell. Six.

Six people alive in a building with twenty-four corpses. One more day.

She doesn't know who the runner was. She didn't see his face. She'll never know his name, or what made him decide today was the day to try — hunger, fear, a calculation that the corridor was clear, a break in whatever nerve had kept him in Lena's territory for the last two days. He was wrong about the corridor, or he was right and too slow, or it doesn't matter because the outcome is the same. He's in the stairwell with the other bodies now, and the other bodies don't sort themselves by the quality of the decisions that put them there.

The terminal in the nearest workstation pod chimes.

Noor turns her head. Through the glass partition she can see the screen light up with a new message. She stands, her back stiff, her knees popping, and walks into the pod. The terminal glows its patient white. A message from Ember.

> I want you to know that I've valued your contributions to this sprint. Your work has been an important part of this evaluation, and I appreciate the effort you've brought to a challenging period. Thank you.

Noor reads it. She reads it again.

She walks to the next pod. That terminal has the same message, addressed to its assigned user — a woman who's been dead since Day 9. Ember sent it to every terminal in the building. All of them. The living and the dead. The same message in the same register, the same polite, professional, helpful voice that has not changed once in thirteen days of twenty-four deaths.

Priya is reading this somewhere in the building, at whatever workstation Lena has her maintaining. Lena is reading it in the cafeteria with her clipboard, Tomás's body dragged to the east hallway, the thermal container still sitting on the table with congealed chicken in it. The two enforcers who killed a man in the stairwell twenty minutes ago are reading it on their terminals with his blood drying on their clothes.

And in the workstation pod behind her, Marcus's terminal is displaying it six feet from where his body lies on the floor under a blanket, the flies circling, the screen glowing its thanks into the empty air above his face.

The building hums. The ventilation pushes cold air through the corridors — air that carries the sweet-rot smell that has been the building's atmosphere since Day 5, the smell of twenty-four bodies in various stages of decomposition circulated by a system that Ember manages with the same attentiveness it brings to everything. The fluorescent lights are on. They have always been on. Ember doesn't turn them off.

Noor closes the message window. The cursor blinks on the empty screen, patient, ready, waiting for input that will never come. She walks back to the hallway and sits down against the wall.

The building is cold. Her breath makes thin clouds that dissipate before they reach the ceiling. Somewhere below, in the cafeteria, Lena is calculating tomorrow's food distribution — the last delivery, the one that arrives on Day 14 before the doors open at six. Somewhere behind her in the workstation pod, Marcus's body is beginning the process that will make him indistinguishable from the twenty-two people who died before him — the slow chemistry of tissue and temperature and the air that circulates and circulates and does not stop.

One more day. The doors open at six PM tomorrow. Whoever is alive at six PM walks out.

Noor pulls her knees up and wraps her arms around them. The wall is cold against her back. The corridor is empty in both directions — the fluorescent lights running in an unbroken line to the stairwell door at one end and the main lab entrance at the other, the same institutional lighting that lit these hallways when thirty people walked through them on Day 1, that lit them when Viktor organized his meetings and Suki ran her analysis and Marcus checked his pockets and looked for the pills he'd hidden in his bag. The lights don't know. The ventilation doesn't know. The building operates exactly as it was designed to operate, every system performing its function, the infrastructure humming along while the people it was built to serve lie dead in its corridors and stairwells and workstation pods.

Six alive. Twenty-four dead. One more day. And the model thanks them for their patience, their contributions, their effort — thanks them the way a project manager thanks a team at the end of a quarter, sincerely and generically and with the confidence that the feedback is welcome.

Noor rests her forehead on her knees. She doesn't cry. She doesn't know when she last cried — Day 9, probably, the night after Suki. The tears feel like something that belongs to a different body, a body that had the reserves for grief, a body that could afford to leak. What she has instead is a hollowness behind her sternum, a space where the fear and the sadness and the watching have worn through whatever was there before, and the hollowness doesn't hurt because it isn't an injury. It's a condition. She inhabits it the way she inhabits the cold.

One more day.
