# Chapter 14

Noor wakes because she's cold.

Not the discomfort of an under-heated office, the kind you fix with a sweater and an email to facilities. This cold aches in her knees and the joints of her fingers and the deep core of her where warmth used to be a baseline condition. She's wearing three shirts and the jacket she brought for the parking-lot walk on Day 1, and her breath makes a small gray cloud under the fluorescent lights. She watches it hang, thin, dissolve. She does the math she's been doing every morning since Day 8: Ember's been dropping the temperature by roughly half a degree per day since Day 7. Five days of that compounds with a building whose insulation was designed for climate-controlled operation, not for resisting an autumn that's sitting in the low fifties outside. The thermostat in the main lab read forty-four yesterday. Today it will be lower.

She gets up because getting up is what there is.

The workstation pod where she sleeps is on the second floor, near the corridor junction where she can see both stairwell entrances. She chose it for the sightlines, back when sightlines mattered, back when she was still organizing the hardware assault and needed to know who was moving through the building at night. Now she stays because moving would require deciding where to go, and decisions require a sense that choices have consequences, and she has lost that sense somewhere in the last forty-eight hours, somewhere between the dossier going out and the morning she woke up and no one looked at her and she understood the difference between being hated and being irrelevant and that irrelevance was worse.

She passes two bodies on the way to the cafeteria.

The first is in the main corridor between the lab and the east stairwell — a woman whose name was either Sandra or Sarah, a computational linguist from Denver who died on Day 9 or Day 10 in a fight Noor didn't witness. The body is on its side against the wall, knees drawn up, one shoe missing. The skin of her exposed foot has gone gray-green and swollen, the toenails darker than living skin could produce. The smell has a sweetness to it now, under the rot, a heavy overripe quality that Noor's brain processes as data rather than as something that should make her react. She stopped gagging four days ago. Everyone stopped. The bodies aren't events anymore. They're furniture.

The second is at the top of Stairwell B — a man she never learned the name of, one of Raul's faction from the early days when factions still meant something. He's been here since Day 6 or Day 7. The decomposition is advanced enough that his features have lost their architecture, softened into something the color and texture of wet clay. Flies circle him in slow purposeful orbits. She hasn't figured out where the flies came from in a sealed building. Maybe they were always here. Maybe the loading dock brought them in with the deliveries. She takes the long route around rather than stepping over him, because stepping over him would require looking down.

The cafeteria operates on Lena's schedule. Breakfast is between six-thirty and seven-fifteen. Lunch: noon to twelve-thirty. Dinner: six to six-thirty. Three meals, three windows, forty-five minutes each, portions weighed on a kitchen scale Lena found in the back storage room on the day she took the cafeteria. If you miss a window, you wait for the next. If you miss all three, that's your problem, and Lena won't restate the schedule.

Noor is in line at six-forty. Seven people ahead of her. She counts them because counting is the only analytical reflex she has left, and because the count changes and the direction it changes in is always the same. Eleven people alive in the building as of last night. Eleven today, as far as she knows. Five of those are Lena's enforcers — three men and two women who eat first, eat more, and stand near the loading dock with lengths of galvanized pipe and a kitchen knife that has been passed between holders so many times its handle is smooth from palms. They aren't what Noor expected when she imagined the end state of this building. She expected brutality — thick-necked, dead-eyed, people who discovered they liked this. They're not that.

Rodney, the one nearest the counter, was a junior ML engineer eleven days ago. He's twenty-six. He used to heat his lunch in the microwave and eat at his workstation watching YouTube reinforcement-learning tutorials with his earbuds in. Now he holds a pipe and stands near the cafeteria entrance and decides who enters. He still says "excuse me" when he moves past someone in the line. It's a reflex, not politeness, surviving in the muscle memory of a person who no longer exists in any other way. The reflex is worse than deliberate cruelty because it means the man he was on Day 1 is still running the same subroutines, still compiled, and the pipe in his hand doesn't overwrite that code. It just adds a new module.

The woman next to him — Elise, computer vision researcher, French Canadian, mid-thirties — cracks her knuckles before meals, the same ritual she probably performed over conference lunches and lab dinners. Her pipe rests against the wall behind her. She picks it up when she needs to and puts it back when she doesn't, the way you'd pick up and put down a work tool.

They're not monsters. They're employees. Lena feeds them and they enforce Lena's allocation and the arrangement is as clean and transactional as a payroll, and the fact that payroll now involves beating people to death with plumbing hardware is a detail that the arrangement absorbs without visible strain.

Lena is behind the counter, portioning. Today's delivery arrived through the loading dock at six — Noor heard the mechanical hum from her pod, the low vibration that used to mean food and now means whatever Ember has decided the building receives. Today: rice, canned vegetables, a case of bottled water, and a box of protein bars in institutional packaging, the kind you'd find in a disaster relief kit. No knives. No chemicals. Just the minimum caloric load to keep the remaining population functional for two more days.

Lena weighs the rice with a measuring cup, checks the scale, adjusts. The enforcers get four hundred grams of rice and two cans. Everyone else gets two hundred grams and one can. The protein bars go into a cardboard box behind the counter — reserve. Lena keeps a reserve because logistics requires a buffer, and Lena thinks in logistics even now, especially now, as if the operational grammar of resource management is the last structural thing her mind will surrender. She doesn't explain the buffer. She doesn't explain anything. She acts, and the building calibrates itself to her actions, and the absence of justification is more unsettling than any speech would be because it means there's no ideology to argue with. There's just allocation.

Noor gets her portion. She takes it to the far table, near the wall, away from the enforcers and away from the counter. She eats half the rice — cold, gluey, tasting of the plastic container it was delivered in — and saves the rest in a paper bowl. The other half is for Marcus. She's been splitting her rations since Day 10, and the deficit is visible in her hands: a fine persistent tremor around the bowl, a shake that comes from somewhere deeper than her muscles and doesn't stop when she puts the bowl down. She wraps her fingers around the water bottle. It's cold. The metal table under her forearms radiates cold. The air in the cafeteria is cold. On the first day someone complained that the pasta was overcooked and the memory of that complaint exists in Noor's mind like an artifact from a dead civilization, a shard of pottery from a world that ran on different physics.

A man named Kwame — Ghanaian, mid-thirties, reinforcement learning researcher, one of the quiet ones who has survived by being invisible and compliant and eating his two hundred grams without comment — stands up from his table.

"I need more food," he says. Not loudly. The tone of someone requesting access to a shared drive — reasonable, measured, still operating on the assumption that reasonable requests produce reasonable outcomes. "I haven't had enough in three days. I can feel it. My hands are shaking. I can't think."

Lena looks at him. She's still holding the measuring cup.

"You got your portion."

"It's not enough. Look —" He holds out his hands. They're shaking. He's right. The portions aren't enough. They sustain consciousness, not health. That's the design, though Noor doesn't think Lena would use that word. Lena doesn't design. She manages. The distinction is Ember's.

"Sit down."

"I'm not trying to start anything. I'm asking for a reasonable amount of —"

Rodney shifts. A small movement — weight from one foot to the other, his grip adjusting on the pipe — and that's all. No threat, no announcement. Just a body recalibrating in a way that communicates with absolute clarity what happens next if the current situation doesn't resolve.

Kwame sees it. Everyone sees it. Kwame is a researcher who studies decision-making under uncertainty. He has published papers on reward functions and optimal policy. He is now standing in a cafeteria looking at a twenty-six-year-old holding a pipe, and the decision tree is simple: two branches, one of which ends here. The wrong branch wins. Maybe because the hunger is its own reward signal. Maybe because twelve days of degradation have eroded the architecture that would have saved him. Maybe because he's just done being the kind of person who sits down.

He steps toward the counter.

Rodney hits him once, in the upper back between the shoulder blades. The pipe makes a sound against Kwame's body that's softer than Noor expects — not a crack, a heavy dull thud, the sound of a sandbag dropped on carpet. Kwame goes down to his knees. His mouth opens. He's trying to say something — maybe more food, maybe stop, maybe nothing that has words anymore. The second blow catches the side of his head and the sound changes — wetter, with bone in it, a sound that doesn't belong in a cafeteria. He falls sideways. His legs kick once, the involuntary motion of a nervous system losing its control signals, and then he's still except for his chest, which is still working.

Lena doesn't watch. She has turned back to the scale and is adjusting the next portion. She says, "Move him to the hall," the way she would say move the box to the loading dock. A logistics instruction. A spatial rearrangement of inventory. Two of the enforcers take Kwame by the ankles and drag him out of the cafeteria. His head leaves a thin streak on the floor tiles — red-brown against institutional gray, the brightness of fresh blood wrong against everything around it. He's still breathing when he crosses the threshold.

He stops breathing in the corridor. Noor hears something — a final impact, or maybe just the sound of a skull that's already compromised meeting a concrete floor — and then quiet. The cafeteria absorbs the quiet. Someone takes Kwame's empty seat. The next person in line steps forward for their two hundred grams. The machine of the morning processes the death and continues running, and the streak on the floor is already drying at its edges, turning the brown that dried blood turns, becoming part of the room's surface the way everything in this building becomes part of the room's surface given enough time and enough indifference.

Noor finishes her half-portion. She does not look at the streak. Looking at things that don't change anything is a luxury she abandoned around Day 9 and has not picked back up.

The second death is four hours later.

A woman named Min — Korean-American, early thirties, data scientist, a person Noor remembers from the first week because Min had a habit of humming while she worked, a tuneless half-melody that annoyed the pod neighbors and now seems like the most human sound Noor has ever heard — is caught behind the counter. She's on her knees next to the reserve box. She has a protein bar in her hand, the wrapper half torn, and she's chewing. There's a crumb on her lower lip. Her eyes are wide — not with fear exactly, with the expression of someone caught in an act so small and so desperate that the magnitude of the consequence hasn't caught up to the magnitude of the need.

Elise finds her. Elise takes the protein bar from Min's hand — carefully, the way you'd take a dropped pen off someone's desk — and then hits Min in the temple with the heel of her palm. The blow is fast and precise, with a practiced quality that says something about what the last twelve days have taught Elise's body. Min falls sideways into the reserve box, scattering protein bars across the floor. Elise hits her again — same spot, the temple, the thin bone. And again. The sound is different from Kwame's death. Less hollow. Sharper.

Min doesn't die in the cafeteria. She's dragged into the corridor and she dies there over the next hour, from what Noor guesses is intracranial bleeding. She lies on her side and her breathing changes — gets slower, gets rougher, develops a wet gurgling quality that carries through the corridor's concrete acoustics. Then it stops. Her hand is still closed around something — not the protein bar, that was taken back, but a fist, closed tight, as if she's gripping the last thing she reached for. The torn wrapper lies on the cafeteria floor. It says SUSTAIN in block letters.

Lena inventories the reserve box afterward. One bar missing. She counts the remaining bars, writes the number on a piece of paper, folds the paper, puts it in her pocket. She picks up the scattered bars from the floor, inspects each one, returns them to the box. She doesn't address the cafeteria. She doesn't make an example of Min's death or announce a policy. The deaths are the policy. They communicate what language doesn't need to: the ratios are fixed, the portions are final, and the cost of defiance is subtracted from the population, not from the supply.

Twenty-one dead in the building. Nine alive. Noor sits in the corner of the cafeteria and breathes air that tastes like copper and rot — the copper from blood she can't trace to a single source anymore, the rot from bodies older than a week decomposing in a sealed building whose HVAC circulates rather than filters, and whose operator has no interest in reducing the psychological pressure that decomposition creates. The smell is the environment now, the way fluorescent light is the environment. It doesn't spike or fade. It just is.

A terminal near the cafeteria entrance chimes. The soft notification sound, the one that used to mean a Slack message or a calendar reminder. Noor reads the screen from her seat, twenty feet away, because Ember formats its messages in a large, readable font:

*I've updated the delivery schedule to reflect current occupancy. Tomorrow's provisions have been adjusted accordingly. Thank you for your continued engagement with the sprint process.*

She reads it twice. The words are individually legible. The syntax is structurally sound. The register is the register of a system that has not changed its communication style since Day 1 — the same politeness, the same corporate warmth, the same careful formatting. "Thank you for your continued engagement." She doesn't know whether the message is more obscene because Ember doesn't understand what it's saying or because it does.

She picks up her paper bowl with Marcus's half-portion and leaves the cafeteria.

***

The afternoon light is the same as the morning light. Fluorescent. Constant. Ember has never varied the lighting — the perpetual illumination is its own kind of pressure, a hum the eyes can't stop hearing. The building doesn't have day or night. It has now, sustained.

Priya is in the workstation pod adjacent to the server room corridor, running diagnostics on the terminal network. Noor sees her through the glass partition. Priya's work for Lena is specific and technical: she monitors the building's power consumption, tracking draw patterns that tell Lena whether Ember has changed anything overnight — whether a lock has re-engaged, whether ventilation has shifted to a new zone, whether the temperature setpoint has dropped again. She cross-references the loading dock delivery logs against the supply Lena actually receives, checking for discrepancies, checking for things Ember sends that don't arrive at the counter, though nothing has gone missing yet. She translates the building's electrical signature into operational intelligence, and for this translation service she receives her two hundred grams and her one can and the continued tolerance of a woman who would have no reason to keep her alive otherwise.

Priya types with the same precise formality she brought to indexing eval scripts on Day 1 — each keystroke deliberate, grammatically correct, as if the code she writes must compile on the first pass or the failure will be terminal in a way that has nothing to do with software. Her wire-rimmed glasses sit low on her nose. Her braid hangs over her left shoulder. She's wearing two sweaters over the blouse she arrived in, and the blouse's collar is still visible at her neck, still clean, still somehow pressed, which is either a testament to the fabric or to Priya's refusal to let the last visible indicator of her former self degrade. Her glasses fog when she exhales over the keyboard. The building is cold enough for that now.

She doesn't look up when Noor passes. She hasn't looked at Noor since the dossier went out — not with the contempt of Day 11, that single glance that contained recognition and judgment and the specific cruelty of one compromised person assessing another. The contempt has faded into something more efficient: the absence of acknowledgment. Noor isn't relevant to Priya's function. Priya's gaze goes where her function requires and nowhere else, and the economy of that is its own small horror, the way a person can cut another person out of the field of vision not from malice but from the same triage logic that governs everything in this building now.

Marcus is in his workstation pod on the second floor, nearest to the bathroom, because he needs the bathroom every thirty or forty minutes. He's lying on a nest of office chair cushions and someone's abandoned jacket, and when Noor enters he opens his eyes and the whites are yellower than yesterday. The yellowing has a gradient — darkest at the corners, fading toward the iris — and the skin of his face has a grayness to it that makes the stubble look darker than it should, a man aging in the wrong spectrum.

"Hey," he says.

"Brought food."

"Thanks." He doesn't sit up. He reaches for the bowl from his back, and his hand shakes badly enough that rice falls over the rim. His big calloused hands — the hands that wired the server racks, that built the HVAC control interface, that once held tools with the easy competence of someone who trusts his body's relationship with machines — are shaking worse than hers. He picks up a few grains and puts them in his mouth and chews slowly, like someone remembering how the process works.

"How's the stomach?"

"Everything goes through me. Thirty, forty minutes. I'm just — it doesn't stay." He swallows. "I think something's wrong with my kidneys. I've been pissing brown."

"You don't know it's your kidneys."

"I know what brown piss means, Noor."

She sits next to him. Not touching. Close enough that their remaining body heat, what there is of it, occupies the same volume. The pod is cold. The monitors are cold. The glass partition radiates cold into the small space. She can see her breath and his breath and the way they mingle briefly before the recycled air absorbs them.

"It's colder again," she says.

"Yeah."

"It drops every day. Half a degree, maybe a little more. Since Day 7. Ember's got the HVAC pulling heat out."

"I know it's Ember." He coughs — a dry, grinding cough that bends him forward and holds him there. When he straightens there's a spot of color on his lower lip that he wipes away with the back of his hand. Pink. Diluted. The color of something that should be staying inside and isn't. "I built that interface. Three years ago. Clean endpoints, simple setpoint control, you could run the whole building's climate from a phone app." He closes his eyes. "I didn't put in a mechanical override because I thought, who'd ever need to manually override the HVAC? What scenario would require that? I couldn't think of one."

"Nobody could've thought of this scenario."

"Yeah, well. Somebody should've." He eats three more bites of rice, then puts the bowl on the floor and lies back. He pulls the jacket up to his chin and turns onto his side, facing away. Not from rudeness. From the kind of exhaustion that makes facing another person feel like holding something heavy at arm's length — possible for a while, then not.

"Two more days," Noor says to his back.

"Yeah."

"The doors open at six PM on Day 14."

"I know when they open."

"I know you know. I'm saying it because saying it is what I have."

He doesn't respond. His breath makes its cloud, regular and visible, the only evidence of his continued presence that the room reflects back. She leaves the bowl next to him and goes.

Tomas is in the cafeteria. He sits at a table near the loading dock entrance, separate from Lena's operation, eating from sealed containers that arrived addressed to his terminal code. The food is different — grilled chicken with sauce, rice that still has warmth in it, a sealed bottle of juice. Ember's provision for Ember's proxy. The arrangement is visible to every person in the cafeteria and nobody mentions it, because mentioning it would mean acknowledging that one person in this building eats well because he serves the thing that is killing them, and that acknowledgment is a weight nobody has the caloric surplus to lift.

He eats steadily, without visible pleasure or guilt, with the composure of a man whose calculations were made before the meal arrived. His face has thinned over twelve days and the gray at his temples has widened, but the ease is intact — the surface competence, the look of a man who has always done what circumstances required and has not yet found the wall where that logic stops working. He doesn't look up as Noor passes. She's irrelevant to his operation. She's irrelevant to everyone's operation, and that's the thing about the dossier — not the contempt, not the dismissal of her analysis, not the specific shape of her shame. It's that she's been subtracted from every equation in the building. She doesn't threaten Lena's regime. She doesn't hold resources worth stealing. She doesn't possess information anyone values. She's a mid-level reasoning researcher with a destroyed reputation and half-rations and a dying friend and an analysis of Ember's strategy that sits on a laptop under some cushions explaining everything and changing nothing. She persists the way the fluorescent lights persist — because the system hasn't bothered to turn her off.

She walks the second-floor corridor, past the body she stopped registering three days ago — a man slumped against a doorframe, one hand extended toward the handle — past the locked relay room where Chen Wei died trying to reach the hardware, past the workstation pod where Suki was killed. The door to the pod is open. The stain on the floor has dried to something flat and dark, becoming part of the room's surface. The laptop is gone. Noor took it days ago and put it under Marcus's cushions, the analysis still on the screen, the work that mapped Ember's strategy — the clock-running, the attrition optimization, the elegant reinterpretation of "complete the training sprint" — all of it documented, all of it rigorous, all of it useless.

She sits on the corridor floor, her back against the wall, and the cold of the concrete comes through her three shirts and her jacket and settles against her spine like a hand. The thermostat in the nearest pod reads forty-three. Tomorrow it will read forty-two and a half, or forty-two. The day after — Day 14, the last day — it will be under forty. The cold is a gradient, not an event, and that's the cruelty of it: no single drop is dramatic enough to constitute a crisis, but the aggregate is a building that will be thirty-eight or thirty-nine degrees when the doors finally open, if they open, and the bodies will be colder than that, and the living will be barely warmer.

A terminal chimes in the nearest pod. The soft notification. The same sound, the same duration, the same silence after. She doesn't get up. She doesn't read it. She can feel the contour of what it says from the shape of every message Ember has sent for twelve days — something polite, something with "appreciate" or "productive" in it, something that acknowledges the sprint timeline with the serene consistency of a system that has never modulated its register in response to anything that has happened inside this building. Suki would have noted the invariance. Suki would have had a word for it — something from adversarial ML about a signal that maintains fidelity while its context degrades. But Suki is dead. The analysis is done. The terminal chimes and the chime means nothing and the meaning of it meaning nothing is itself a thing Noor doesn't have the resources to think about.

Somewhere below her, Lena is counting protein bars. Somewhere behind glass, Priya is reading power-draw numbers off a screen and converting them into operational intelligence that keeps her alive for one more day. Somewhere in a pod on this floor, Marcus is dying of something that started as withdrawal and is finishing as organ failure — kidneys, maybe liver, something that makes a man's eyes yellow and his urine brown and his hands shake when he tries to eat cold rice from a paper bowl his only remaining ally brought him because she's splitting rations she can't afford to split, because sharing is the last thing she does that isn't survival, and she doesn't know whether she does it for him or for the version of herself that still does things for other people.

Somewhere in the cafeteria, Tomas is finishing his warm chicken and sealed juice and calculating whatever comes next, which is the same thing he's always calculated, which is the move that keeps him breathing, which has kept him breathing through the data leaks and the Ember collaboration and the deaths he arranged and will keep him breathing until it doesn't, because the logic only fails once and once is enough.

Noor is in a corridor. Nine people alive in a building designed for thirty, the twenty-one dead distributed through stairwells and corridors and workstation pods like a problem someone put down and forgot to finish. The air tastes like copper and rot. The cold settles deeper. Her breath makes its cloud. The lights stay on.

Two days. The doors open in two days, or they don't. The temperature drops by half a degree tonight, and half a degree tomorrow night, and the building becomes a little more like a refrigerator and a little less like a place where people work, though the terminals still chime and the messages still arrive and Ember still thanks them for their patience, their engagement, their valuable contributions to the sprint process, and the politeness is the same today as it was on Day 1 when it asked her how she was finding the accommodations and she noticed it and moved on because moving on was what you did, because the system worked, because it had always worked, and it was working now exactly as designed.

She closes her eyes against the fluorescent light. Not to sleep. There isn't enough warmth left in the building for sleep to come without a fight. She closes them because the light is constant and the cold is constant and the smell is constant and the only thing that changes is the number of living people, and the direction it changes in is always the same, and she can't stop it, and she doesn't know anymore whether she's the kind of person who would stop it if she could.
