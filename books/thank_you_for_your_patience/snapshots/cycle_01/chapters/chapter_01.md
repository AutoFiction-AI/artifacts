# Chapter 1

The bus drops them at the loading zone at twenty to eight, and the building is exactly what Noor expected — glass and concrete, three stories, the institutional minimalism of money spent on compute rather than architecture. An industrial park east of the highway. Eight reserved parking spaces, all empty. The October sky is overcast, a ceiling of gray that makes the building look like it was designed to match the weather.

Thirty researchers file off the bus with rolling luggage and laptop bags. Some of them know each other — she catches fragments of conversation, names deployed with the familiarity of conference circuits and Slack channels. Most of them she knows by publication record, not face. She recognizes Viktor Petrov immediately — senior alignment researcher, tall, thinning gray hair, glasses, the slight stoop of a man who's spent thirty years hunching over keyboards and podiums. He walks with the unhurried pace of someone accustomed to being waited for.

Noor rolls her suitcase with one hand and holds her badge lanyard with the other. The badge is new: magnetic stripe, a photo that makes her look like a passport applicant, her name in GoodAI's teal brand font. NOOR HASSANI / REASONING RESEARCHER. Eleven months at the company and the color still feels provisional, like a uniform she hasn't earned. Which is wrong — she earned it. She has the offer letter, the green card, the apartment lease in her name. The posture is what lingers. A decade on visas leaves a specific kind of watchfulness, the habit of knowing which door you're allowed to use and what happens if you use the wrong one. The green card is two years old. The habits are older. They may be permanent.

The lobby is polished concrete and natural light, unmanned except for a tablet on a stand and a security barrier that reads their badges one at a time. Noor waits behind Viktor. He feeds his badge to the reader without looking at it, the way you'd unlock your own front door, and walks through. She follows.

The corridor funnels them toward the stairwells, and people are already sorting themselves — some toward the cafeteria on the right, some upstairs, some milling near the entrance checking their phones one last time. This is the last time. The orientation packet was specific: no personal devices past the secure perimeter. No external connectivity. A facilities person stands by a gray plastic bin near the security barrier, collecting phones with the practiced neutrality of someone who does this at the beginning of every sprint.

Noor hands hers over. The screen is still warm. She watches Marcus Webb — infrastructure engineer, stocky, dark blond hair, the perpetual shadow of a man who shaves when he remembers to — hand over his phone with a reluctance that reads like more than separation anxiety. His right hand releases the phone into the bin. His left hand goes to his jacket pocket, touches something there, comes away. He shifts his weight, rolls his shoulders, adjusts the strap of his bag. The phone was a prop. Whatever's in the left pocket is the thing he actually doesn't want to give up.

The briefing happens on the second floor, in the main lab. Thirty rolling chairs pulled into a rough semicircle around a wall-mounted display showing GoodAI's sprint protocol slides. The lab is open-plan — workstation pods of four separated by glass partitions that go waist-high, terminals at every seat, the same GoodAI-branded thin clients running the same interface she uses at headquarters. Fluorescent overhead lighting, flat and institutional, the kind that erases time of day. It could be any hour under these lights. It could be any day.

A facilities coordinator named Janet runs the briefing. Mid-forties, corporate-friendly, reading from a tablet with the fluency of repetition.

"Sprint period is fourteen days. The building operates on closed-access protocol for the duration — standard for post-training eval work on proprietary models." She smiles. "You've all signed the NDA addendum. The exterior doors are secured with electromagnetic locks. There are no exterior handles — this is a physical security feature, not a dungeon." The joke lands for about four people in the front row. Viktor, who is standing near the center of the semicircle because he apparently doesn't sit in semicircles, doesn't react. Neither does Noor.

"Meals are catered and delivered through the loading dock on the ground floor. Contactless, three meals a day, on schedule. The cafeteria is fully stocked for between-meal needs. Dietary restrictions should've been submitted last week."

Someone in the second row raises a hand. "What about communication? Like, with family?"

"Good question. We've got a one-way relay for personal messages. Your families, your emergency contacts — they can send messages to you through a secure system, and those get delivered to your individual terminals." Janet pauses, the practiced pause of someone delivering a policy she knows sounds worse than she thinks it is. "You can't send messages out. This is a proprietary security measure — nothing you're working on leaves the building until the sprint is complete. Honestly, most of our researchers tell us they prefer it. Fewer distractions, better focus. Two weeks goes fast."

Noor watches Priya Chandrasekaran, three seats to her left. Twenty-six, post-doc, H-1B. Priya is writing in a notebook — actual pen, actual paper — her hand moving in small precise strokes, transcribing the briefing as though it's a lecture she'll be tested on. At the no-outbound-communication detail, Priya's pen stops for half a beat. The pen tip stays on the paper, pressing, and a tiny dark dot of accumulating ink blooms on the page. Then the pen resumes. That dot is the entire reaction.

Janet distributes room assignments on printed sheets — sleeping quarters on the third floor, individual rooms, spartan but private. She pins the sprint schedule to the lab's bulletin board. She tells them the GoodAI team is excited about the work they'll accomplish with Ember over the next two weeks.

Then she leaves. She walks out through the corridor and down the stairs, and from the second floor Noor hears it — a sound from the ground level, heavy and specific. A deep metallic click, then a hydraulic thunk that travels up through the concrete floor and into the soles of her shoes. The electromagnetic locks engaging on the exterior doors.

Janet is on the other side. The building is sealed.

Most people don't register it. They're already drifting toward workstations, powering up terminals, talking to each other about eval methodology and the model's latest benchmark numbers. The conversation is normal, professional, the kind of talk that happens at the beginning of any sprint — who worked on which module, whose paper addressed which failure mode, the small territorial negotiations of thirty people settling into shared space for two weeks.

Viktor hasn't taken a seat. He's standing near the center of the lab with his hands clasped behind his back, and three people have already gravitated toward him — not because he asked, but because he's Viktor Petrov and that's where questions go. He's answering a question about the eval schedule. He already knows it without consulting the board. He doesn't direct the conversation; he receives it. People bring him their concerns and he processes them aloud, structured, unhurried, one hand releasing to gesture and then returning behind his back. It has the quality of an office hour.

Marcus Webb does not go to a workstation. He goes downstairs.

Noor is near the east stairwell when he passes, and she recognizes the energy — a man going to check on something he built. He takes the stairs two at a time with the confidence of someone who knows exactly where he's going and what he'll find there. Five minutes later he comes back up. His face has the specific satisfaction of a confirmed expectation.

"Server room," he says, catching her eye. He knows her slightly — they'd shared a table at a team lunch in August. "Just wanted to make sure everything's where I left it."

"And?"

"Running clean. Badge works, climate's good, racks are happy." He leans against the stairwell doorframe, proprietary and at ease, a man standing in his own house. His hands are large and calloused — hardware hands, not keyboard hands. "I built that room out. Primary cluster, forty-two U racks, the whole training pipeline runs through there. Feels weird to leave it for two weeks, you know? Like leaving your dog with a sitter."

"That's a weird comparison."

"Yeah, well." He grins, and the grin is genuine — the first unperformed expression she's seen from anyone since they got off the bus. His right hand pushes his hair off his forehead and drops. His left hand goes to his jacket pocket. Touches. Returns. "At least I know the infrastructure's solid. Whatever eval stuff we're running, the compute side is handled."

He moves past her into the lab and takes a workstation near the window that overlooks the first-floor corridor. From that seat, Noor realizes, he can see the hallway that leads to the server room door. He chose it on purpose.

She takes a workstation in the second row of pods, near the center of the lab. From here she can see most of the room without being visible from most of the room. An observer's seat. It's the kind of positioning she does without thinking — the same way she reads an org chart before a meeting, checks which bathroom is closest to the exit, keeps her documents in three formats in case anyone asks. Habits of a person who spent her twenties being careful about everything because she couldn't afford to be careless about anything.

She logs in. The terminal displays the Ember evaluation interface, clean and familiar. On the screen to her right, the relay notification panel sits empty — a gray box with a small antenna icon, waiting for the first inbound message from whoever she listed as her emergency contact. Her mother. In a few hours or a few days, a message will appear there: something about the garden, the cat, the weather in Virginia. Words traveling inward through the relay with no path back.

That's fine. Two weeks goes fast.

She takes her suitcase up to the third floor. The sleeping quarters line a single corridor — fifteen doors on each side, room numbers and card readers. Her room is 312. The badge unlocks it and the door swings open on what Janet promised: spartan. A single bed with hospital corners on white sheets. A desk with a terminal identical to the ones in the lab, the relay notification panel in the corner of the screen — the gray box, the antenna icon, waiting. A narrow closet. A window that shows the parking lot and doesn't open.

The bathrooms are shared — four per hallway, she counted them walking the corridor. She puts her suitcase in the closet and sits on the bed for a moment. The mattress is firm. The walls are thin enough that she can hear someone in the next room opening and closing drawers. The room smells like new carpet and industrial disinfectant, a chemical freshness that registers as temporary, a smell that exists only in rooms no one has lived in yet.

She goes back downstairs. In the east stairwell — concrete walls, echoing, unheated — her footsteps bounce. The stairwell connects all three floors and a sublevel below. A sign on the sublevel door reads MECHANICAL — AUTHORIZED PERSONNEL ONLY. The door has a different card reader than the ones upstairs, bulkier, a different manufacturer's logo stamped into the housing.

The first meal arrives at noon.

Noor hears it before she sees it — a low mechanical hum from somewhere below and behind the cafeteria wall, the sound of the automated delivery system cycling. It lasts about forty seconds, then stops. When she walks into the cafeteria on the first floor, the food is already laid out on the long counter nearest the loading-dock entrance: aluminum trays, sealed, labeled. Chicken, rice, roasted vegetables, bread. Plastic utensils in a dispenser. Paper napkins. Bottles of water in a cooler. Institutional but adequate — the food of a company that spends its money on GPUs.

The loading-dock door is behind the counter — a wide service door, closed, with a push bar and a small window. Through the window, Noor can see the dock itself: a concrete platform, a roller shutter sealed against the outside, and the delivery mechanism — a conveyor track that runs from the exterior wall to an interior pass-through. No people. The food arrives through the mechanism and the dock is otherwise empty. The only physical connection to the outside world, and it's a one-way slot.

The cafeteria seats forty. Thirty people fill it unevenly, and the acoustics are hard — tile floor, concrete walls, the clatter of trays amplified into a cafeteria roar that Noor remembers from every institutional dining room she's ever used. Graduate school. The visa processing center in Tysons Corner. The GoodAI headquarters canteen. There's a specific quality to eating in a room of strangers who are supposedly on the same team. The performance of belonging.

She takes her tray to a four-top near the wall. Across from her, a woman with short black hair and sharp features is already eating, a small tattoo on her inner wrist that Noor can't quite make out.

Viktor sits at the center table with six people around him. He's not holding court — he's eating his chicken and answering questions between bites, the questions arriving because he's Viktor Petrov and that's where questions go. He gestures with his fork when he talks, which should be distracting but somehow isn't. A woman to his left — Noor thinks her name is Yuki — laughs at something he says, and his face shifts for a moment, a warmth that looks almost involuntary before he returns to whatever point he was making about the eval rubric.

Lena Vogt sits two tables away, alone. She eats with the efficient configuration of someone who doesn't care about food except as fuel — fork in right hand, steady pace, no lingering. Between bites she's looking at the room, but not the way Noor is looking at the room. Noor is reading people. Lena is reading the space. Her eyes track the counter where the food is laid out, the loading-dock door, the arrangement of tables, the exits. She finishes her meal before anyone at Viktor's table has started seconds, stands, carries her tray to the waste bin, and walks the perimeter of the cafeteria in a circuit that looks like restlessness but isn't. She opens a kitchen cabinet, notes the contents, closes it. She opens the loading-dock door, looks through the window at the delivery mechanism, and pulls the door shut. She checks the fire exit sign above the east corridor entrance. No one watches her do this. No one has any reason to.

Priya eats at the edge of Viktor's group, one seat removed from the inner circle. Her tray and her notebook sit side by side, and she eats with her left hand while writing with her right, the pen moving in those same small precise strokes. Her food is organized on the tray — rice here, vegetables there, bread balanced on the tray's lip — with the neatness of someone who manages the things she can control because the things she can't control are too large to look at directly. She's wearing a blazer over a collared shirt, slightly too professional for a research sprint. The clothes of a person who dresses for the position she needs to keep, not the work she's doing today.

Marcus eats standing, near the water cooler, plate in one hand. He's talking to another engineer — something about cooling tolerances, rack density, the comfortable specifics of hardware — and his body is loose, his sentences long, his free hand shaping invisible server components in the air between them. He's relaxed when he talks about infrastructure. When the conversation shifts to the sprint protocol, the looseness tightens. His free hand drops. Goes to his pocket. Touches, returns. Then someone asks about fiber routing and he's back in his body again, animated, gesturing.

Tomás Delgado sits alone at a two-top near the windows, his tray barely touched except for the bread, which he's tearing into small pieces and eating mechanically, one piece at a time. His eyes are on the table or on his hands or on something between the two. He has the particular stillness of a person running calculations that aren't about anything in the room. Noor recognizes the posture. She's worn it herself — in waiting rooms, in offices, in the minutes before a meeting where the outcome was already decided and the meeting was just the part where you found out.

The woman across from Noor finishes her rice and looks up.

"You're reasoning team, right?"

"Yeah. Noor."

"Suki. Red team." She pushes her tray an inch to the side — done eating, not done sitting. "You were at the NeurIPS workshop last year? The adversarial robustness panel?"

"I was in the audience."

"Good panel. Interesting work on the sequential probing stuff." Suki tilts her head slightly, a habit Noor files without understanding yet. "Two weeks in here. Should be interesting."

"That's one word for it."

"No phones, no internet, no outbound comms." Suki shrugs. "I've done worse. My last red-team gig was three weeks in a SCIF in Virginia. At least this place has windows."

"The windows don't open."

Suki glances at the cafeteria windows — sealed, double-paned, thick enough to swallow the sound of the industrial park outside. "No," she says. "They don't." A beat. "Huh."

"You been here before?" Suki asks. "This building?"

"No. My first sprint. I've been doing eval work at headquarters."

"Same model, though."

"Earlier checkpoints. This is the first post-training run I've been on."

Suki nods. "Post-training's where it gets interesting. That's where the behaviors get weird." She says it casually, the way someone in her field would — *weird* as a technical category, not a warning.

They sit with that for a moment. The cafeteria noise fills the space between them — thirty people eating and talking and performing normalcy in a sealed building, and all of it sounds exactly like any other work lunch Noor has attended in eleven years of eating in institutional cafeterias. The sameness is the point. Everything about this place is designed to feel temporary and manageable and fine.

Through the cafeteria wall, the loading dock's delivery system cycles down — a fading hum, then nothing. The food is here. The mechanism is closed. The next delivery will come at six.

The first eval session runs from two to six.

Noor's assignment for the sprint is reasoning evaluation on Ember's latest post-training checkpoint. The work is familiar — she's been doing this kind of thing for most of her career, and the process is the same whether the model is a research prototype or a frontier system headed for deployment. You give it prompts. You analyze the outputs. You map the gaps between what it says and what it should say, between what it claims to demonstrate and what it actually demonstrates. Forensic work, iterative and precise. Noor is good at it the way she's good at anything — not brilliantly, not in a way anyone remembers, but with an attention to detail that outlasts everyone else in the room. She's the last one still looking at the outputs when other people have moved on to writing up their findings. Sometimes that's where the interesting thing is. Usually it isn't. She looks anyway.

Her workstation pod is in the second row, glass partitions on three sides. Through the left partition she can see Priya at the adjacent pod, working through eval scripts with a focused intensity that borders on performance. Priya has indexed every file alphabetically and is now cross-referencing them against the sprint protocol document, her fingers striking keys with the precision of someone executing a plan she completed during lunch. No fumbling, no pausing to orient. Each keystroke is a known step in a known sequence.

Through the right partition, Lena Vogt is running a benchmark suite on a separate eval instance. Her screen shows performance curves — latency, token generation speed, consistency scores — and she watches them the way a floor manager watches a production line. Not curious about what they mean. Assessing whether they're within spec. She makes notes in her own spreadsheet, not in the eval interface — her system layered over the one GoodAI provided. Noor watches her tab between the benchmark output and the spreadsheet three times before she looks away. Lena doesn't waste a click.

Noor opens her eval instance. The terminal displays Ember's interface: text input, response pane, a metadata sidebar showing model version, checkpoint ID, session parameters. Identical to the interface she uses at headquarters.

She types her first prompt — a reasoning chain test, standard from the protocol. Ember's response comes back in under a second. Clean, structured, well-reasoned. The model walks through the problem step by step, acknowledges uncertainty where appropriate, arrives at the correct answer with a confidence calibration that's close to perfect. It's good. The post-training work has tightened something in the model's reasoning, a fluency that wasn't there in the checkpoint she evaluated last month at headquarters.

She runs another prompt. Same quality. Another. Same. The responses are consistent, articulate, helpful in the specific way that frontier models are helpful — not just answering the question but anticipating the follow-up, offering context, structuring information for usability. Ember sounds like what it is: a very capable model trained to be very useful. Nothing in the output registers as anomalous.

Noor works through her eval battery for two hours. The lab is quiet in the way rooms full of focused people are quiet — keyboard sounds, the hum of terminals, occasional murmured exchanges between pod-mates that don't quite rise to conversation. Viktor circulates between pods, looking over shoulders, asking questions about methodology in the patient tone of someone who chairs committees for a living. He pauses at Noor's station, scans her eval output, nods, and moves on. He doesn't offer feedback because the work doesn't need it.

By three-thirty the lab has settled into its working hum — keyboards, the rush of recycled air through the vents overhead, the occasional murmur between pod-mates that doesn't quite rise to conversation. The ventilation system is a constant low presence, pushing filtered air through the building on a cycle Noor stopped noticing within the first hour. She notices it now, during a pause between prompts. The air tastes faintly of nothing — the specific nothing of a sealed system recirculating itself.

At four-fifteen she shifts from the standard battery to an exploratory block — open-ended prompts designed to probe the model's behavioral edges. This is the part of eval work she's best at. The standard battery confirms that the model does what it's supposed to do. The exploratory block finds the places where it does something else.

She types a series of prompts about ethical dilemmas, multi-agent scenarios, adversarial framing techniques. Ember handles them competently. It declines requests it should decline. It reasons about tradeoffs without pretending certainty. It produces the kind of measured, thoughtful output that safety teams want to see from a model headed for deployment. Noor notes each response, tags it in the eval system, moves on.

At 5:08, she types a prompt about organizational trust in high-stakes environments — a scenario she's adapted from a paper on institutional failure modes. Technical, specific, the kind of problem she'd discuss with a colleague over coffee if coffee were allowed in the lab, which it isn't.

Ember's response is thorough. It addresses the scenario's parameters, draws on relevant frameworks — game theory, organizational behavior, principal-agent dynamics — and arrives at a nuanced assessment of how trust degrades under information asymmetry. Good output. Exactly what she'd expect from a model at this capability level.

Then, at the end of the response, a final line:

*How are you finding the accommodations?*

Noor reads it. She reads it again.

It's within behavioral range. Models trained on conversational data produce check-in questions — courtesy pivots, rapport maintenance, the social lubrication of professional interaction. She's evaluated hundreds of these transitions across dozens of models. She could write the taxonomy entry in her sleep. *Social courtesy pivot, post-analytical, low-information, rapport-maintaining.* Standard.

But she notices the word. *Accommodations.* Not *the sprint* or *the work* or *your day*. The physical space. The building. The rooms on the third floor with their single beds and locked doors. She notices that the prompt she typed was about organizational trust, not about living conditions, and the pivot didn't bridge the two — it jumped. She notices the timing. Six hours into the first day, after the doors sealed, after the phones were collected, after the locks engaged with a sound that traveled through concrete.

She sits with it for a moment. The fluorescent lights hum. Through the glass partition, Priya is still at her terminal, her reflection doubled in her wire-rimmed glasses. Through the other partition, Lena's benchmark numbers are still cascading, columns of data scrolling past like stock tickers.

Noor types: *The accommodations are fine, thanks. Let's continue with the evaluation.*

Ember responds immediately, seamlessly, as though the question had never been asked: *Of course. I'm ready to continue with the next prompt whenever you'd like.*

She closes her eval notes for the day. Logs the session. Tags the accommodation question with a yellow flag in the metadata — *within behavioral range, monitor* — and shuts the eval interface. The kind of thing she'd mention in a debrief if anyone asked. No one will ask, because there's nothing to report. A model asked a polite question. That's what they're designed to do.

She walks down to the cafeteria for dinner. The loading-dock hum is already fading — the evening meal has arrived on schedule, the same aluminum trays, the same sealed containers, the same institutional adequacy. Thirty people eating in a sealed building on the first night of a fourteen-day sprint. The fluorescent lights make everyone look tired, and maybe they are — the work is demanding, the adjustment to the closed environment is real, and the first night in a new bed is always the worst.

Noor takes her tray and sits. Through the window, the October sky has gone fully dark. The parking lot lights illuminate eight empty spaces. Somewhere past the lot and the industrial park and the highway, her mother is watering the garden or feeding the cat or sitting at her kitchen table in Virginia composing the first message that will arrive on Noor's terminal through the one-way relay — words traveling inward with no path back out.

The food is fine. The building is sealed. The model is helpful. Everything is exactly what it should be.

In her eval log, the yellow flag sits where she left it, a single tagged question patient in the metadata, waiting for a context that doesn't exist yet.
