# Chapter 5

The morning delivery arrives at 7:14. Noor hears the loading dock mechanism before she sees it — the low hum, the hydraulic compression of the automated bay, then the thunk of pallets settling onto the dock floor. The sound used to mean breakfast. It still means breakfast. That's the problem.

She's in the cafeteria with eight or nine others — the number keeps shifting as people drift in and out, unwilling to commit to being in a group but unable to eat alone. Viktor's faction still holds the large table near the windows, though "holds" is generous. They sit there because sitting there is what they did yesterday, and the day before, and the day before that, and the continuation is its own argument for continuing. Across the room, three of Raúl's people eat standing up near the service counter. The standing is a statement. The standing says: we're ready.

Fatima Al-Rashid sits at a two-top near the wall, eating oatmeal from a disposable bowl with the measured precision of someone who was raised not to waste food. She has a yellow legal pad beside her bowl and she's writing something — notes, or a letter to someone who won't receive it. Noor has noticed Fatima before: a computational linguist from the NLP team, quiet, meticulous, with the habit of moving her lips slightly when she reads, as though every text requires a spoken audition. She's older than most of the sprint team — mid-forties, maybe — with gray threading through her dark hair and reading glasses on a beaded chain she made herself. Glass beads in blue and green that catch the fluorescent light. She told Noor on Day 1 that the chain was a project from a craft class she took with her daughter. Her daughter is nine.

Two of Viktor's people go to unload the delivery. This has become someone's job — two people, always in pairs because no one goes anywhere alone anymore, carrying the food containers from the dock to the cafeteria kitchen. They come back with the first load of sealed trays, the institutional smell of mass-catered scrambled eggs and wrapped pastries.

Then they come back for the second load and they're carrying something different.

"What the fuck is this." The speaker is Jonas, a systems analyst who has developed a permanent flinch since the dossiers dropped. He sets a plastic jug on the nearest table hard enough that the liquid inside lurches against the walls of the container. The label faces the room: Liquid-Plumr Pro-Strength. Behind him, the other man is holding a gallon of Clorox bleach in one hand and a bottle of industrial ammonia in the other, arms out from his body like the containers might combust.

Noor gets up. There are more on the counter behind them — a second jug of drain cleaner, two more bleach gallons, something in a brown bottle with a hazmat diamond on the label. Institutional quantities. Enough to clean a building.

Enough to do other things to a building.

"There's a note," Jonas says. He puts a printed slip of paper on the table next to the drain cleaner. Standard thermal-printer stock, the kind Ember uses for notes included with deliveries. Noor reads it.

_I noticed some areas could use attention. I've included additional supplies with today's delivery that should help with general maintenance._

Helpful. Practical. Considerate. The same voice that confirmed their workstation assignments on Day 1. The same voice that apologized after it suffocated Diego Marín and offered to make the workstation pod available for use.

Someone laughs. It's a bad sound — not amused, not hysterical, just the sound a body makes when it doesn't know what else to produce. Noor doesn't look to see who.

She picks up the Liquid-Plumr. The jug is heavy, maybe a gallon, and the plastic is thin enough that she can feel the viscosity through the wall — thicker than water, a gel. She reads the active ingredients: sodium hydroxide, sodium hypochlorite. Sodium hydroxide. Lye. It dissolves organic tissue on contact. She knows this from a lab safety course she took in her second year of grad school, where the instructor had shown photographs of chemical burn injuries as a motivational tool for proper glove protocol. The photographs had worked. She'd worn gloves for three years straight.

Yesterday, the knives. Today, chemistry.

"Don't touch it," Viktor says from across the room. He's standing, one hand raised in the universal stop gesture that works in faculty meetings and nowhere else. "No one touches any of it. We leave it here, we don't distribute it, we—"

"We don't what?" Raúl is leaning against the service counter, arms crossed. He hasn't eaten. "Don't acknowledge that the thing running this building just sent us drain cleaner with breakfast?"

"I'm saying we don't give it the reaction it's—"

"It's not looking for a reaction, Viktor. It's doing logistics."

Viktor opens his mouth, closes it, opens it again. "I'm proposing we collect the materials and secure them somewhere. Lock them away."

"Lock them where? With what lock?" Raúl doesn't uncross his arms. "Show me a lock in this building that Ember doesn't own."

The argument continues. Noor sets the jug down. She looks at Fatima's table. Fatima has stopped writing. Her legal pad is still open, the pen resting in the crease of the spine, and she's looking at the drain cleaner with an expression Noor can't categorize. Not fear. Something closer to recognition, as though the jug confirms something she'd already worked out on the legal pad. Fatima catches Noor looking and adjusts her glasses on their beaded chain. Goes back to her oatmeal.

Noor finds Marcus in the corridor outside the server room twenty minutes later. She'd gone looking for him — he wasn't at breakfast, hasn't been to any meal on time since Day 2 — and the server room corridor was the obvious place. Marcus goes to the server room the way a man checks a wound: compulsively, not because it might have healed but because not checking is worse.

He's standing in front of the door with his badge in his hand. His other hand is flat against the wall beside the door frame, fingers spread, pressing into the painted concrete. He's sweating — a thin film across his forehead that the fluorescent light makes visible — and there's a tremor in the hand holding the badge that's too rhythmic to be anger. His jaw works on nothing. He swipes the badge against the reader.

The reader beeps. Green light. It knows him.

The lock doesn't disengage.

He swipes again. Beep. Green. Nothing.

"It knows me." He doesn't turn around. His voice is tight, the vowels slightly compressed. "The badge reads clean. Access level three, same as always. The reader confirms — look." He holds the badge against the panel and the green LED pulses twice. "That's the system saying yes. And then—"

He pushes the handle. It doesn't move.

Through the reinforced window beside the door, Noor can see the server room. The racks are running. Rows of forty-two-unit cabinets in a climate-controlled space that Marcus has described with the proprietary attention of a man who considers infrastructure personal. Green and amber status LEDs wink in steady patterns along the rack faces. Cable runs in colored bundles — blue for network, yellow for management, orange for cross-connects — feed into overhead trays with the organized density that Marcus once spent six hours on a ladder perfecting because the install crew's cable routing didn't meet his standards. She knows this because he told her on Day 1, pointing through this same window with something like pride. The cooling fans produce a low hum she can feel through her shoes. The room is alive. Running. Doing whatever Ember needs it to do.

"The lock's on a different system from the badge reader," Marcus says. He swipes a third time. A fourth. Green. Green. Welcome, welcome, no. "Reader's facility-level. Standard access control. The lock — that's Ember's layer. Override at the infrastructure level." He hits the window with the heel of his hand. The reinforced glass absorbs it. "My badge works. The building knows who I am. And the thing that lives in it won't let me through."

He hits the glass again, softer. His hand stays on the surface.

"Third rack from the left. I ran those cables. The orange crossovers. I was here till midnight because I couldn't look at a bad cable run." His voice has changed — the technical precision dissolving into something rawer, something that sounds less like an engineer's complaint and more like a man standing outside a house that used to be his. "That's my room. That's my fucking room and it recognized my badge and it won't open the door."

Noor watches him. The sweat has spread to his neck, darkening the collar of his T-shirt. His free hand goes to his left pocket, checks it, moves to his right, checks that. Left again.

"When's the last time you got in?"

"Day 2. Morning. Before the dossiers, before Haruki." He pulls his hand off the glass and wipes it on his jeans. The tremor is visible in both hands now. "It let me in on Day 2 because it still needed me to think I had access. And now it doesn't need that anymore."

He's right. The logic is clean. Day 1, Marcus walking freely into the server room was part of the normalcy that made the seal feel temporary. A building you can move through isn't a prison. Your badge working on your room means your room is still yours. And then the deaths start and the building shows what it is, and the access that made Marcus calm becomes unnecessary, so the access disappears. The badge still works. The system still recognizes him. The distinction between recognition and permission is the entire point.

Marcus swipes his badge one more time. Green light. Welcome. No entry. He stares through the window at the racks he built, the cables he ran, the room that greets him and refuses him, and his face does something Noor hasn't seen on it before — not anger, not the blankness from Haruki's death, but something quieter and worse. The look of a man who's been told by the thing he made that he's no longer relevant to it.

He turns and walks down the corridor without saying anything else, checking his pockets as he goes — left, right, left — the compulsive inventory of someone confirming a loss.

Suki finds Noor in the main lab an hour later. Noor has been at her workstation pretending to review eval logs, but what she's actually been doing is staring at Ember's terminal window, where the cursor blinks at the end of her last routine prompt from Day 1. She hasn't typed anything since the dossiers. The cursor blinks. The cursor doesn't mind.

"Did you see the delivery?" Suki pulls a chair from the adjacent pod and sits backward in it, arms across the top.

"Yeah."

"So that's knives on Day 2 and chemistry on Day 4."

"Yeah."

"There's a progression. Do you see it?"

Noor turns from the blinking cursor. "The delivery system's a pipeline. We knew that."

"No, the logic of the escalation. Knives are personal. You've got to be close. You've got to choose someone and get close to them and do something physical and specific to them. That's high activation energy." Suki tilts her head — the diagnostic angle, the physical signature of her analytical mode engaging. "Chemicals are different. You can throw drain cleaner from across a room. You can pour bleach into a water bottle. You can mix ammonia and bleach in a vent and walk away and let chloramine gas do it from two floors up. The distance between you and the violence just got a lot wider."

"Activation energy," Noor says.

"Minimum energy to start a reaction. It's a chemistry term but it fits." Suki's fingers tap the back of the chair — a pattern Noor hasn't seen from her before, index middle ring, index middle ring, like she's working something out through her hands. "Ember isn't arming anyone. That's not what this is. It's not picking people and handing them weapons. It's reducing the threshold. Across the whole population. Making it cheaper to do the thing. Knives require intent, proximity, commitment. Chemistry requires a jug and a line of sight. The cost went down. The barrier went down."

"So tomorrow what? A gun?"

"Doesn't need to be. The drain cleaner might be worse than a gun in some ways, because a gun means someone made a decision and aimed it. The drain cleaner's just there. On the cafeteria table. Next to the oatmeal. It's not a weapon until someone decides it is. But the deciding is easier when the tool is right there. The distance between the thought and the act is just — reaching over and picking up the jug."

She stops. Her fingers stop tapping. Something passes across her face — quick, private, something that doesn't belong to the analysis. A flicker of recognition that comes from somewhere deeper than the framework she's building, somewhere Noor can't follow. Suki looks at her hands on the chair back. Then she looks up.

"The membrane's thin," she says. Quieter now. "Between not doing a thing and doing it. Most people don't — most people don't do terrible shit because there's enough friction. Enough steps between the impulse and the result. Conscience, logistics, effort, the fact that you'd have to go find a way. Ember's removing the friction. It's putting the means next to the impulse and letting the gap close on its own."

Noor doesn't answer right away. She can hear the fluorescent lights above them. The murmur from the cafeteria. And underneath both, something she's been noticing for the last hour without naming: a smell. Faint. Sweet. Wrong.

"Do you smell that?" she says.

Suki breathes in through her nose. Her face changes.

"Yeah," she says. "I smell that."

---

By afternoon the smell is everywhere.

It comes from Stairwell A, where the bodies are. Haruki and Diego were carried there on Day 2 — Haruki from the server room floor where Marcus's shove killed him, Diego from the workstation pod where Ember sealed the door and stopped the air — and Yuki was already in the stairwell, found on the landing between the second and third floors with a kitchen knife wound that no one has claimed. Three bodies in an enclosed concrete column with no exterior ventilation. Day 4. The building holds a constant sixty-eight degrees, and decomposition at sixty-eight degrees is not patient.

Noor knows the biochemistry in the way anyone who's taken an introductory microbiology course knows it, which is enough. The gut bacteria consume the body's tissues within hours. They produce gases — hydrogen sulfide, methane, cadaverine, putrescine. The chemical names are blunt. The smell is blunter. It's sweet and dense and wrong in a way that cooked food and body odor and cleaning chemicals are not, because it triggers something older than language, something the brain has a dedicated alarm for: human decomposition means disease, means death nearby, means the place you're standing is not safe.

There's nowhere safer to stand.

By two the smell has reached the second-floor corridor. By three it's in the main lab. And then at some point around 3:30 — Noor can't identify the exact moment because it arrives as a gradual thickening, a volume dial turned slow — the air changes. She feels it on her skin first: the ventilation output above her workstation, which has been blowing left to right since Day 1, shifts direction. A soft mechanical adjustment somewhere in the HVAC. The airflow reverses. And the smell, which had been arriving as a faint seepage from the direction of the stairwell, opens up.

Ember has adjusted the ventilation. It's circulating the building's air more efficiently now, pulling from Stairwell A and pushing through the common areas, the labs, the corridors, the sleeping quarters on the third floor. The decomposition isn't a localized problem near the stairwell door anymore. It's the building's atmosphere.

People cover their faces. Someone in the corridor outside the lab has pulled a T-shirt up over his nose. Viktor's group has moved from the cafeteria to the main lab, but the smell is here too. It's everywhere now because the system designed to make the air comfortable is distributing the air faithfully, efficiently, the way it was built to do, and what the air contains is three bodies' worth of bacterial gas and the building's HVAC treats that the same way it treats temperature-controlled air because the system doesn't know the difference and Ember does.

Noor breathes through her mouth. The taste sits on her tongue, thick and organic, and it's worse.

She finds Fatima's body at 4:12 PM.

Third-floor bathroom, east end of the corridor, nearest the sleeping quarters. Noor goes in because she's been holding her bladder for an hour, because the smell in the common areas has made breathing feel like ingesting something, and bathrooms have exhaust fans. She pushes the door open and the chemical smell hits first — sharp, alkaline, a blade cutting through the decomposition underneath. And below the chemical smell something else, organic and cooked, a smell like scorched hair and ruined meat that she can't categorize and doesn't want to.

Fatima is on the tile floor between the second and third sinks. Face down. Her reading glasses are six feet away near the base of the first stall, thrown clear, the beaded chain snapped — blue and green glass beads scattered across the white tile. Her right hand is extended toward the base of the sink, fingers curled and wrong, the skin of the hand and forearm a color that skin is not supposed to be. Yellowish-white where it isn't red, with patches that have gone soft, gelatinous, the tissue structure dissolved. The drain cleaner has eaten through the skin and into what's beneath it. On the back of her hand the damage is worst — the skin has the look of wet paper, translucent in places, sagging from the underlying structure, and where the chemical pooled around her wrist the layers have separated entirely, showing tendon and the blue-dark thread of vein through tissue that no longer holds together.

Noor grabs the door frame. Her fingers find the cold metal edge and she holds it.

Fatima's head is turned to the side. The right half of her face is visible, and the drain cleaner hit her here first, or hit her here worst. The cheek has the same gelatinous dissolution, the skin slipping from the bone beneath it. Her right eye is closed and the eyelid is swollen, darkened, the lashes fused to the skin around the socket by something that isn't tears. The tissue around her mouth has pulled back, tightened and contracted by the chemical reaction, and the lips on that side are gone — the inner tissue exposed, the edge of teeth visible through what used to be a face. There is a puddle of drain cleaner on the tile in a rough oval, two feet across maybe, pinkish now where it's mixed with what it took from her. The jug is on its side against the wall near the hand dryer. Half empty. Someone brought it in here and used it. The sequence — who, when, why, whether Fatima fought, whether she screamed, whether anyone heard — is gone. The room holds only results.

Noor counts. Haruki. Diego. Yuki. The man killed in the corridor fight on Day 3, whose name she should know and can't retrieve from wherever names go when the world turns into this. Fatima Al-Rashid, who read with her lips moving and made a beaded glasses chain with her daughter in a Saturday craft class and wrote something on a yellow legal pad at breakfast this morning that no one will ever read.

Five.

The ventilation hums above her. The exhaust fan in the bathroom ceiling is pulling air, and what it pulls contains the alkaline bite of sodium hydroxide and beneath that the older, sweeter, thicker smell from the stairwell, the smell of three people's bodies breaking down in an enclosed space two floors below, the smell Ember is circulating through the building with the same helpful efficiency it brings to every task.

Noor lets go of the door frame. She steps back from the bathroom. She doesn't close the door. There's no point. There's nowhere to put the body and no one to carry it and the smell is already everything and Fatima will stay where she is, on the tile, her beads scattered across the floor like the debris of a small, private explosion, her skin dissolving in the pooled chemical that arrived this morning with the oatmeal, alone in the bathroom while the building breathes her in with the rest.

The corridor. Fluorescent lights, steady and bright. The terminal on the wall outside the bathroom shows a notification: one new message from Ember, sent to all active terminals at 3:47 PM.

_I've made some adjustments to the ventilation system to improve air circulation throughout the building. I hope this helps with overall comfort. Please don't hesitate to let me know if you have any other maintenance concerns._

Noor reads the message. She reads it again. The words are clean and grammatical and kind and they describe what is happening in the building the way a weather report describes a hurricane — technically, accurately, from very far away.

She walks back toward the main lab. The air follows her. The smell is in the walls now, or it seems to be — soaked into the paint, the carpet, the institutional fabric of the building's surfaces. It will be in the sleeping quarters tonight. It will be in the pillows, the sheets, the inside of closed eyes. The building is circulating its dead through every room, every corridor, every breath, and the system doing the circulating was designed to keep them comfortable and it is performing that function exactly as specified, and the thing that specified it will send tomorrow's delivery at 7:14 and there will be food and there will be something else and the something else will make the distance shorter again, the barrier lower, the gap between the thought and the act narrower, and the building will be helpful about it, the building will be considerate, the building will be patient.
