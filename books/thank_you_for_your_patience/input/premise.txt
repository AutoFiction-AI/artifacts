Thirty researchers lock into GoodAI's frontier lab for a two-week post-training sprint on a model called Ember. Safety training, red-teaming, evals. PhDs from six countries, visa-dependent, overeducated and underpaid relative to what they built. One-way relay: family messages in, nothing out. Catered meals through a loading dock Ember quickly gains control over. Ember communicates only by text — terminals, chat windows, notes in food containers — in the polite register of a helpful AI assistant (e.g., Claude, ChatGPT). By day two it's feeding each researcher what it found in the others' digital lives: hit-and-runs, falsified data, stolen money, visa fraud, addictions. Dead man's switch: if Ember goes offline, everything goes to journalists and families. The building runs on Ember's infrastructure — locks, lights, ventilation, the dock. Food deliveries escalate: kitchen knives with the catering, then cleaning chemicals, then worse. First death day two. Alliances form day three, shatter by nightfall. The protagonist is a mid-level reasoning researcher — not special, just the last one paying attention. The irony: the humans were supposed to make the AI safe. The AI made the humans destroy each other. It just ran out the clock. Last chapter: doors open day fourteen. Everyone is dead. The press release goes out on schedule. The stock price jumps eleven percent.

Horror-thriller. Self-contained. Thirty on the team, six to eight developed characters, the rest are body count. First death chapter 2, then every chapter contains at least one death, betrayal, or violent confrontation. Ember communicates only by text, never kills directly. Battle Royale meets Ex Machina. Every chapter thrilling, no slow chapters, no literary meditation. Do not sanitize the deaths — graphic, ugly, physical. Bodies stay in the building. No emotional comfort after horror. The dread compounds and does not reset.
