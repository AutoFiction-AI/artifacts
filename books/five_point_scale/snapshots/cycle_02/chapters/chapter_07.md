# Chapter 7

The all-hands was projected onto the break room television, a forty-two-inch Samsung mounted at an angle that forced everyone on the left side of the room to look slightly up and to the right, like a congregation receiving a benediction. Someone had miscalibrated the color temperature. The CEO's face ran about eight hundred Kelvin too warm, so he appeared to be broadcasting from inside a tanning bed, but nobody else seemed to notice or care.

"What I want to talk about," the CEO said, "is the human element."

He paused. He was good at pauses. Behind him, the Canopy AI San Francisco headquarters stretched in careful wide-angle: standing desks, exposed brick, a vintage arcade cabinet positioned within the frame in a way that communicated both whimsy and the kind of revenue that permitted whimsy. The lighting was soft, diffused, a three-point setup with the key light slightly too high — a choice that made the CEO look authoritative rather than approachable, though Nick was fairly certain the intention had been the opposite.

Thirty-one people were packed into the San Leandro break room, which smelled like reheated curry from someone's lunch and the particular chemical sweetness of the coffee machine's descaling cycle. The room was designed for twelve. Priya had carried in extra folding chairs from the conference room and arranged them in rows, and now she stood against the back wall with her arms crossed, watching the screen without blinking. The muscle at the hinge of her jaw kept jumping.

"Every model that ships from this company," the CEO continued, "every image generator, every diffusion pipeline — they're built on your work. On human judgment. On taste that can't be automated." He smiled. The smile was engineered for warmth. "That's what I call the irreplaceable human layer."

Nick heard Tommy, two rows ahead, exhale through his nose.

The CEO talked for another eight minutes. He was a skilled communicator — he knew when to slow down, when to gesture, when to look directly into the camera in a way that simulated eye contact. He talked about Canopy's growth: three new model partnerships in Q1, a Series C that valued the company at $1.2 billion, expansion into video evaluation by year-end. He talked about "the incredible team" and "the pride I feel" and "the foundation everything rests on." He said *human* eleven times. Nick counted.

Between the growth numbers and whatever was coming next, the CEO took what Nick recognized as a rehearsed detour into gratitude. He named specific labeling floors — Manila, Austin, San Leandro — and said each one was "critical to the mission." He told a story about a calibration correction that had caught an anatomical error in a model bound for a major client, and how that single correction had saved the partnership. "That was a human being," the CEO said, pointing at the camera with two loose fingers and a little lean into the lens, too practiced for gratitude. "That was one of you. AI can generate a thousand images in an hour, but it takes a human eye — your eye — to tell the machine what quality looks like."

Nick glanced at Priya. Her expression hadn't changed, but her fingers were pressing into the flesh of her upper arm where she held it crossed, leaving white crescents in the skin.

Then the throughput targets.

They came in the back third of the presentation, tucked behind a slide titled "Scaling Our Impact," which featured an upward-trending arrow in Canopy's brand teal. Current evaluation rate across all labeling floors: 8.4 images per evaluator per hour. New target: 14.2 images per evaluator per hour. Implementation timeline: six weeks.

The room did the math quietly.

Fourteen-point-two meant roughly four minutes per image. Four minutes to open the file, assess prompt adherence, rate anatomical accuracy, check lighting consistency, evaluate compositional balance, score stylistic coherence, flag edge cases, and click Next. Nick had been averaging about seven minutes per image, which was already faster than most of the senior evaluators, and even at seven minutes he occasionally caught himself rushing through the lighting check because a hand with seven fingers in the foreground was screaming for attention.

Four minutes. The number sat in the break room like a dropped plate.

Nick's phone buzzed in his pocket. A message in the floor's Slack channel — someone had typed *14.2???* with three question marks. Someone else replied with the wide-eyed emoji. Then Priya's name appeared: *Let's discuss in tomorrow's sync. Please hold questions for now.* Which was Priya trying to contain the reaction before it turned into something the San Francisco office could screenshot.

"To support this," the CEO said, still smiling, "we're making a significant investment in team expansion. New evaluators coming onboard across all floors over the next month. Fresh perspectives, fresh energy." He clasped his hands. "The human layer is growing."

After he signed off — "Keep doing amazing work, all of you, I mean that" — there was a beat of silence, and then the room murmured and shuffled in the way rooms do when people need to say something but don't know what's safe.

Nick stayed in his chair. Dani, sitting next to him, was already standing, pulling her phone out. When work turned unpredictable she got more exact, not angrier; now every movement had that careful click to it, her jaw set.

"Fourteen," she said, not to Nick specifically. "Point two."

"The point two is my favorite part," Nick said.

"The point two is because someone in analytics decided a round number would look arbitrary."

That was probably true.

Priya was already at the door, intercepting people, her voice carrying the tone she used when things were fine and she needed everyone to know things were fine. "We'll have a team sync tomorrow morning to walk through what this looks like day-to-day, OK? Nothing changes overnight. Just — let's all be there at nine."

Nick watched her. Priya's hair was pulled back tight, and the overhead fluorescents threw a shadow under her cheekbones that made the hollows beneath her eyes more pronounced than they'd been even a month ago. She'd lost weight in the way stress did it: first the softness along the jaw, then the blouse hanging flatter across the chest, then the collarbones showing every time she turned toward the door.

Tommy materialized at Nick's elbow, coat over his arm. "Fresh perspectives," he said. "Fresh energy. Can't wait to explain what a wrist is to twelve people who were doing content moderation last week."

Nick looked past him to Priya at the door, still catching people one by one and telling them nothing changed overnight. The TV had already gone back to the Canopy logo.

* * *

The new hires arrived the following Monday — eleven of them — and eleven more the Monday after that. By the third week of April, the San Leandro floor had expanded from thirty-one evaluators to fifty-three, and the parking lot, which had always felt oversized for the building, no longer was.

They came from everywhere. Content moderation, customer service, an Amazon warehouse, a Lyft driver who'd heard the hourly was better. A few had art backgrounds — a photography student, a woman who'd worked framing prints at a shop in Emeryville — but most had been hired for warm-body availability, not visual training. They'd been through a two-day onboarding that covered the rubric categories, the rating scale, the evaluation portal's interface, and the sexual harassment policy, in roughly that order of emphasis.

Nick knew they were in trouble on the second afternoon of the first wave, when Priya stopped behind his chair before he'd finished his third image.

"Can you come look at something before it becomes six somethings?"

He followed her to the glass conference room they'd converted into overflow seating. Four new hires sat at folding tables inside, shoulder to shoulder, their badges still stiff enough to catch the light every time they moved. Someone had taped a sheet of printer paper to the glass that said "NEW HIRE CALIBRATION" in thick black marker. The tape had already started to peel at one corner. In the top right of each monitor, the throughput widget glowed in Canopy teal. One evaluator was already at 11 for the hour. Another was at 13.

"I just need another set of eyes," Priya said, keeping her voice low. She had her phone in one hand and a clipboard in the other, and the Costco-sized antacid bottle was balanced on the clipboard like a paperweight. "A couple of these felt off, but I don't want to pull people for normal beginner stuff if it's just normal beginner stuff."

"Sure."

"Just talk them through what you see," she said. "Don't make it a whole thing unless it has to be a whole thing."

The first screen belonged to a guy with a fresh buzz cut and the pale band on his forearms where some kind of work glove had probably ended. On his monitor, a generated woman held a violin under her chin. The instrument was fine. The face was fine. The bow hand was a disaster, the wrist bent backward in a smooth impossible arc, the thumb hooked over the stick from the wrong side like the model had been given written instructions by somebody who had only heard of violins over the phone.

"Walk me through the four," Nick said.

The guy looked up, startled but not embarrassed yet. "Anatomical four?"

"Yeah."

"I figured the hand was weird on purpose. Like painterly weird. Not, you know, wrong wrong."

Nick put his own wrist out over the desk and bent his hand back until the tendons pulled. "This is about where a person stops. She keeps going. And the thumb's in the wrong place. If she drew the bow like that, she'd drop it."

The guy squinted at the screen. "Oh."

Priya stepped in with the tone she used when she was trying to correct somebody without making them defensive. "That's the distinction. If the style is exaggerated but the structure could still exist, you can be generous. If the structure can't exist, you can't call it style and move on."

At the far end of the table, a woman in a new Canopy hoodie swiveled halfway around. "Even if the prompt doesn't mention the hand?"

"Even then," Priya said. "We're still rating the image."

The woman nodded like she understood, then clicked back to her own screen. Nick saw the image there before he saw the number. A man crouched beside a child's bicycle, smiling at something outside the frame. His left arm came out of the shoulder in a single upholstered tube all the way to the wrist. Anatomical accuracy was marked 5.

"Hold on," Nick said.

She froze with her cursor over Submit. "What?"

"Go back to the arm."

"The sleeve covers most of it."

"No." Nick leaned in, close enough to see the pixels break apart at the edge of the monitor. "The sleeve replaces it. There's a shoulder and a wrist. There isn't anything between."

She leaned forward too. "Oh."

Priya said, "How many have you already done this hour?"

The woman straightened. "Seventeen."

The number landed in the room. Seventeen was good. Seventeen was the kind of number people had started glancing at when they passed each other on the floor.

"Can you open your completed queue?" Priya asked.

The woman hesitated just long enough for Nick to catch it. Then she clicked through. The first thumbnail showed a handshake with one duplicated thumb tucked behind the real one like a second thought. Rated 4 for anatomy. The next was a child at a picnic table with a knee bending sideways under the shorts. Rated 5. The next was a bridal portrait where the bouquet stems disappeared into the bride's palm and reemerged from the heel of her hand like roots through dirt. Rated 4.

Priya pressed her lips together.

Nick clicked to the first evaluator's history. Same problem, different body parts. A boxer with one glove fused shut around nothing. A fashion shot where the model's clavicles met in the middle like a folded coat hanger. Clean justifications in the note field, all of them confidently wrong: *acceptable stylization*, *minor proportion issue*, *composition carries the image*.

In the top corner of Priya's phone, a Slack notification slid down over the lock screen. *Ops: seeing strong speed from first cohort. Keep them live if possible.* She didn't angle the phone away fast enough, so Nick read it by accident or not by accident.

He looked back at the monitors. The throughput widgets kept ticking. 12. 14. 18.

"How much has this pod submitted since lunch?" he asked.

Priya opened the team dashboard with her thumb. "Twenty-seven completed." She swallowed. "Twenty-nine if you count the two still open."

"They're wrong."

"I can see that."

She said it too quietly for the new hires to hear. It sounded less like irritation at Nick than irritation at the fact of having to say anything at all. She watched the dashboard for another second, doing the math on her face. If she pulled four people off live, the floor's hourly rate would drop. If she left them on, somebody else would have to clean up whatever they'd touched, assuming it got caught before it moved on.

"OK," she said.

She straightened and switched back to public voice. "Everybody pause your queues for a minute, please. Hands off keyboard."

The guy with the violin image looked at the teal number in his corner. "Are we in trouble?"

"We're in retraining," Priya said. "Bring your notebooks. Conference room B."

No one moved right away.

"Now, please."

Chairs rolled back. The woman with the boneless arm image closed her laptop too fast and nearly knocked over her water bottle. Priya caught it without looking. Her other hand was already on the antacid bottle cap.

"Nick," she said, as the four of them filed out, "I need you to review everything this pod has submitted since lunch. Flag anything you wouldn't pass at senior."

"What about my queue?"

"Your queue waits."

"For how long?"

She looked at the glass door, then back at him. "Until I know which two of these people I can afford to put back on live."

Nick sat down at the empty station closest to the door and opened the completed batch history. The mistakes came fast enough to create their own rhythm. A chef with a thumb growing out of his cuff. A sleeping cat with human molars inside its half-open mouth. A group portrait where one bridesmaid had six fingers and the woman beside her had four, and both had been marked anatomically acceptable because the lighting was good and the dresses were well rendered. He flagged twelve of the first twenty-one before he realized he was keeping count the way he kept count of the CEO's use of *human*.

Through the glass, Priya stood at the whiteboard with four dry-erase markers lined up on the tray like tools before surgery. She drew an arm in profile, then an elbow, then erased it and drew it again larger. Nick couldn't hear every word, only fragments when somebody opened the door a little too hard.

"No, not every weird hand," she said at one point. "Some weird hands are just bad hands. I'm saying this one physically doesn't work."

A minute later: "I know what the target is. The target doesn't make this an elbow."

The guy with the glove tan line said something Nick couldn't catch. Priya answered in a longer burst, too fast, then stopped, reset herself, and wrote "STRUCTURE BEFORE STYLE" on the board in block capitals.

Nick flagged sixteen of twenty-seven. Three more he marked for second review because they were the kind of image the portal punished you for overthinking: almost plausible, maybe wrong, wrong enough to matter only if you were looking hard. He was still in the batch history when Priya's phone rang. She stepped out of the conference room and took the call in the narrow strip of hallway between the glass wall and the vending machine.

"Yes," she said. "I understand what ramp means."

She listened.

"I'm looking at a perfect score on an arm with no elbow."

She listened longer. Nick kept his eyes on the screen and heard the call anyway.

"Then log it as extended calibration," Priya said. "I don't care what bucket you use."

She hung up, stayed still for half a second, then unscrewed the antacid bottle and dry-swallowed two tablets. The chalky smell drifted out into the hall. When she went back into the room she was smiling again, or doing the version of smiling that kept everybody else calm.

An hour later she came back out while Nick was still working through the queue.

"How bad?" she asked.

"Sixteen wrong. Three more maybe wrong enough to check twice."

Priya nodded once. "OK."

"You can't put them back on live."

"I can't keep all four off live either." She rubbed the bridge of her nose with the side of one thumb. "I can take the two worst off tomorrow morning. Maybe till lunch if Ops stops calling me every six minutes. After that I need bodies."

She looked through the glass at the four new hires, now quiet over their notebooks, and for a second the composure fell away hard enough for Nick to see the effort underneath it.

"Which two are least dangerous?" she asked.

Nick hated the phrasing because it was honest.

He pointed to the woman in the Canopy hoodie. "Her if you slow her down. And the guy with the violin, maybe. He sees it once you show him."

"The other two?"

"Not tomorrow."

Priya exhaled. "Not tomorrow isn't a category they'll accept."

"It should be."

"I know." She reached for the clipboard, found there was nothing on it, and set it back down. "Can you put together examples tonight? Hands, elbows, occlusion, common failure stuff. Something I can drop into a training deck before nine."

"Yeah."

"Thank you. And can you stay on these until you're done? I don't want the client team catching them before we do."

"How many are there total?"

"Twenty-seven from this pod. Fifty-three pending from the second pod after dinner." She gave him a tired look that was not quite a joke. "Fresh energy."

By the end of the shift, Nick had back-audited forty-one completed evaluations and built the bones of the quick-reference sheet on a Google Doc Priya had titled "Visual Training Module v1." He filled it with wrists, elbows, teeth, hands holding objects, feet in heels, and the direction of cast shadows, the kind of things he had once called drawing. Priya ran two emergency retraining sessions before Friday. By the time the second wave of eleven arrived the following Monday, she had added three more. Some of the new hires improved. The ones who improved got slower. The ones who stayed fast stayed wrong.

Fifty-three evaluators now, the room louder than it had been, the ambient click-rate faster, the hum of the fluorescents submerged under the sound of chairs rolling and keyboards tapping and low conversations that weren't supposed to be happening but were. A new hire in the row behind Nick's station was eating sunflower seeds and spitting the shells into a paper cup — a small thing, but three weeks ago nobody ate at their desks, and the fact that nobody had told this person not to was its own data point about how thin Priya's attention was being stretched.

Nick sat at his station and opened his queue. A generated image of a landscape: rolling hills, a barn, a sky that had three different kinds of cloud because the model couldn't commit to a weather system. He rated it — prompt adherence 4, anatomical accuracy N/A, lighting consistency 3, compositional balance 3, stylistic coherence 2 — and clicked Next.

The next image was a portrait. A woman's face in three-quarter view, and it was actually good. The skin tones had depth, built in layers like oil paint rather than the flat plasticity most generators defaulted to. The lighting came from one source and behaved plausibly. The eyes were slightly different sizes, but within human variation. He rated it fairly and moved on, and the image after that was a hand holding a rose where the thumb came out of the wrist, and the image after that was a cat with human teeth, and by the end of the hour he'd rated fourteen images and the new evaluator at the station beside him had rated twenty-two.

Down the row, Bao was leaning into his screen with his reading glasses low on his nose, studying something with the unhurried focus he brought to every image. The throughput widget in the corner of the evaluation portal — a new addition, visible to evaluators and managers alike, counting completed ratings per hour in real time — showed 6 next to Bao's name. His accuracy, which Nick could see from his senior evaluator dashboard, was 97 percent. But the system didn't display accuracy on the widget. It displayed speed. Six was less than half of 14.2, and the gap sat on the screen like something already decided.

* * *

Tommy suggested drinks on a Thursday in early May. Nick almost said no — he'd been staying late, reviewing calibration batches, building the quick-reference sheet Priya had asked for — but Tommy asked once by the time clock and again in the parking lot, both times too casual, and by the second ask he was already angling toward the bar.

They walked to the bar, the one across the parking lot and a block down, the place the floor treated as a decompression chamber. It was after six and the light was long and warm, the sun still well above the hills to the west, everything gold-edged in a way that Nick's eye catalogued without permission: the low light catching the chain-link around the business park's electrical substation, the shadows stretching long across the parking lot's cracked asphalt, the particular orange hitting the bar's dirty front window and making it glow like a lamp.

Inside, the bar was the same as always — sticky wooden tables, a pool table with a rip in the felt that nobody was going to fix, a jukebox loaded with music from a decade nobody in the bar had lived through consciously. They got beers. Tommy got a second before Nick had finished his first, which was unusual. Tommy was careful about drinking — he'd grown up in a house where it was a problem — and the speed tonight meant something.

"So the new people," Tommy said.

"Yeah."

"There's a guy on the late shift who rated a dog with human hands a four for anatomical accuracy."

"A dog with human hands."

"Yeah, like — full-on human hands. Fingers and everything. Like a person in a dog suit. And he gave it a four because, and I quote, 'the proportions are mostly correct.'"

Nick laughed. Tommy didn't.

"It's gonna get worse," Tommy said. He was picking at the label on his bottle, rolling the wet paper between his thumb and index finger. "The targets, the new hires, the whole — " He waved at the air between them. "They don't want quality. They want volume. And we're the ones who have to pretend both things can be true at the same time."

"Priya thinks she can fix it with training sessions."

"Priya thinks she can fix everything with training sessions." He said it without malice. "She's gonna burn out. She's already — have you seen her? She's running on antacids and, I don't know, sheer organizational will."

Nick had seen her. He drank his beer and didn't say anything.

Tommy ordered a third. The bartender was a heavyset woman with a Sharks tattoo on her forearm who poured without comment or judgment. The bar was mostly empty — a couple at a corner table, a man watching baseball on the TV above the register with the sound off, captions scrolling.

"Can I tell you something?" Tommy said.

"Sure."

"Something kind of — I don't know. Something I haven't told anyone here."

Nick turned his bottle on the table, leaving a wet arc on the varnish. He told himself it was just Tommy being Tommy, building up to a joke that required preamble.

"I've been submitting work to art competitions," Tommy said. "Juried shows. Gallery open calls. Some of the online prizes — the Ortega, the Pacific Northwest Emerging, couple others."

"OK." Nick waited.

"AI-generated work."

Nick set his beer down. A truck downshifted on the road outside and the bar's front windows hummed with it.

"Under a different name," Tommy said. He was looking at the table, at the wet ring his bottle had left on the wood. "I've been using Midjourney and then running it through a couple of post-processing steps — color correction, cropping, some manual touchup in Photoshop — and submitting it as digital mixed-media."

"And?"

"I've won three. Got honorable mention in two others." Tommy looked up. The skin around his eyes had tightened, and his jaw worked sideways on nothing, like he was already rehearsing the laugh he'd use if Nick made him take it back. "The Ortega was five hundred bucks. That's, like — that's a car payment."

Nick said nothing for a moment. He was looking at Tommy's hands on the bottle, the cuticles bitten to the quick, a hangnail on his left ring finger that he'd picked until it bled and scabbed over.

"I know how it sounds," Tommy said. "I know. But — OK, here's the thing. These competitions, the jurors — they can't tell. They literally can't tell, Nick. I'm not even trying that hard to hide it. The last one I submitted, I barely touched the output. Just a landscape with some chromatic aberration and a weird sky, and three jurors with MFAs looked at it and said yes, this is art, here's your ribbon." He laughed, but it wasn't the laugh Nick was used to — it was thinner, with a serrated edge. "I spent maybe two hours on that one. Tweaking the prompt, messing with the style settings, changing the aspect ratio. Two hours. You know how long I spent on the last actual piece I entered? The one that didn't even get shortlisted? Four months."

"Jesus, Tommy."

"They replaced us." Tommy's voice had shifted — still casual, still loose, but the looseness had weight under it now. "Every one of those competitions, the entry fees alone — sixty, eighty bucks a pop. I used to send real work. Mine. Before I bailed. Never won shit. Not once. And now I feed a machine some prompts, clean up the weird parts, and suddenly I'm a juried artist."

He took a drink. Then: "You know how much I owe in student loans?"

Nick knew the weight of it, if not the number.

"Forty-seven grand," Tommy said. "I checked the loan site last week because I thought maybe if I ignored it long enough it'd turn into a less embarrassing number. It did not. That's a year and a half of art school, no degree, and a whole lot of critique language I can now use to describe why my life sucks. Borrowed gesture, unresolved surface, all that. They'd stand around in a white room and politely explain why the work wasn't under pressure, and I'd nod like, yes, thanks, great, keep going, maybe charge me more for this." He peeled another wet strip of label off the bottle. "Now I'm making twenty-one bucks an hour rating dogs with human hands, and the only art that's ever paid me back came out of a fake account and a Midjourney subscription."

Nick pressed the strip of wet paper flat against the table with his thumb. He thought about the coding tabs still open on his kitchen table, the plan that had come attached to the job application like a second document. "That doesn't bother you?"

"Sure," Tommy said. "Yeah, it bothers me. It bothers me when I cash the check. It bothers me when the congratulations email hits. It bothered me the first time, and it bothered me less the second time, and by the third time I was mostly annoyed I hadn't started sooner." He picked at the label again. A piece came off in a wet strip. "You want to know what really bothers me? The Ortega one, the ribbon one? It was better than anything I made in school. Not because I'm bad. Just because the thing can do stuff I can't. The gradients, the — I don't know, that vapor-y shit it does. And it never hesitates. Or it doesn't show the hesitation, maybe. Every line comes out like, yeah, meant that, and people read that as intention. As skill. Even though nobody in there is meaning anything. You know?"

Nick had no answer. The bar's jukebox clicked between songs, the mechanical sound of the record arm lifting and settling back down, and then an Eagles song neither of them acknowledged came on and filled the silence with someone else's California.

"You're not going to tell anyone," Tommy said. Not a question.

"No."

"Because you think I'm wrong?"

Nick looked at his beer. The bottle was sweating in the warm bar, beads of condensation tracking down the glass, pooling on the scarred wood. He thought about saying it was dishonest, that it cheapened the work, but the words felt fake before they made it past his teeth.

"I don't think you're wrong," Nick said. "I just don't know what to do with it."

"Yeah." Tommy tipped his bottle toward Nick's. "That's kind of where I'm at too."

They stayed another hour. Tommy circled back to the Bay Area Emerging prize, to some video of a model spitting out finished renders in under two minutes, to the way people online were either celebrating or acting like civilization had ended. Nick said yeah, shit, that's wild at the right intervals and kept thinking about Priya's antacids, Bao's throughput number, the quick-reference sheet still open on his work desktop.

Tommy left first, waving from the door, his thin frame backlit by the parking lot's sodium lights. Nick paid the tab — forty-two dollars, roughly two hours of evaluation work at his pay rate, or twenty-eight images at the new target, or eighty-four individual rubric ratings. He caught himself doing the conversion, stopped, then noticed that stopping had taken effort. The receipt stayed under his hand awhile before he signed it.

He let himself into the apartment a little after nine. The room smelled like dust and old linseed oil. Canvases were stacked against the wall beside the kitchenette, turned backward so all he saw were stretcher bars, staples, masking-tape labels in his own handwriting. The half-finished painting on the easel had skinned over. Two brushes sat in a jar by the sink, hardened together with dried ultramarine.

He set his keys on the counter, then crossed to the stack and turned one canvas around.

It was a hillside from the Vera Lux show, twenty-four by thirty, painted before the bottom dropped out. Under the apartment light the greens looked flatter than he remembered. The paint surface kept a record of every hesitation: a tree line scrubbed out and redrawn a half inch lower, a cloud bank softened past the point where it needed softening, a ridge where the palette knife had corrected the horizon too late and left a raised seam. Tommy had said the machine never hesitated, or never showed the hesitation. This did. The whole painting was made of places where he'd stopped, doubted, changed his mind, gone back in.

He caught himself trying to rate it.

Lighting consistency was fair. Compositional balance, maybe. Stylistic coherence — he shut the phrase down before it finished assembling.

He turned the canvas back around harder than he meant to. The stretcher bar tapped the wall. The half-finished painting on the easel sat where he'd left it, a gray-green underpainting with one stand of weeds worked almost to completion and the rest still ghosted in charcoal. He looked at it for a long time, long enough to see where the oil had gone matte and where it still held a skin of shine.

Then he stepped around it, went to the sink, and left the brushes exactly where they were.
What stayed with him was not the hillside but the violinist's wrist bent backward in that smooth impossible arc.
