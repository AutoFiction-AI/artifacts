# Chapter 3

The interview was at ten. Nick woke at five-thirty because he had never actually fallen asleep — he'd spent the night on his back in his Fruitvale studio, running LeetCode patterns through his head while the windows buzzed with each BART train on the elevated track outside, the glass vibrating at a frequency he could feel in his molars. He was not preparing. He was tracing preparation instead of doing it: the movements were the same, and the risk was gone.

He showered with the water pressure his building provided, which was the pressure of a person who was trying to be polite about it. He dressed. He examined himself in the bathroom mirror, which was small and spotted and mounted over a sink that shared counter space with the kitchenette because this was a five-hundred-square-foot studio where everything shared space with everything. He looked like a man who painted for a living and did not make a living doing it. Dark hair past his ears because haircuts were nineteen dollars and that was a decision he kept deferring. Hazel eyes dark-smudged underneath from chronic undersleep. He had chosen the green flannel — his best, purchased at a Goodwill on MacArthur Boulevard for seven dollars a year and a half ago, all buttons present, no visible stains under overhead light. Beneath it, a black t-shirt he had audited for damage at six AM and cleared.

He had paint under two of his fingernails. Cadmium red, which was the last color he'd used three weeks ago on a canvas he'd since turned to face the wall. He went at the nails with a toothpick, standing at the sink, scraping pigment from the keratin with the precise, practiced motion of someone who had done this before job interviews and gallery openings and dates and every other occasion where the visible evidence of his work was a liability rather than a credential. The red came off one nail. The other held. He gave up and decided no one was going to be looking at his fingernails at a tech company interview, and then immediately revised this to: he didn't know what anyone looked at in tech company interviews. He had never been to one. He was thirty-four years old and the closest he had come to a corporate interview in his adult life was when he'd talked his way into a teaching-assistant position at CCA by bringing a portfolio and a willingness to mix paint for the department chair.

He ate half a granola bar standing in the kitchen, looking at his stacked canvases against the far wall. Nine unsold paintings from the Temescal gallery show, plus six older ones, plus three he didn't count because they were bad. Eighteen paintings. Not one of them had ever sat in a queue on someone's screen waiting to be rated on a five-point scale. That was not a thought he was having yet. That thought was coming.

He drove south on 880 toward San Leandro in the gray January light, his Civic shuddering in the left lane at sixty-two miles an hour, which was its current maximum comfortable velocity. The engine had been making a sound since October that he'd been classifying as harmless by the usual method: he refused to think about it too carefully. The windshield crack, which had started as a chip in July, now ran diagonally from the lower left to a point roughly behind the rearview mirror, bisecting his view of the road into two slightly misaligned planes. He kept meaning to get it replaced. He kept not having three hundred dollars.

The San Leandro business park was off Davis Street, a collection of two-story office buildings set back from the road behind parking lots. The buildings were beige stucco with tinted windows, built to house whoever signed the lease — dental practices, insurance adjusters, now, apparently, a company that trained the AI systems that had replaced him. Nick parked his Civic in the far corner of the lot, near a landscaping island where three ornamental pear trees had been pruned into identical lollipop shapes. They stood in a row with the disciplined geometry of objects that had been told what to look like. It was the sort of pruning that made him think about bonsai, about topiary, about the human insistence on making living things hold still.

He turned off the engine. The shudder traveled up through the steering column and died. His hands were not steady. This was not because he was afraid — or it was, but the fear was specific, not general. It was exam fear, narrow and humiliating: he had crammed the gestures without learning the material, and the whole point of the room was to measure that gap.

He locked the car with the physical key because the remote had died two months ago and he had not replaced it. He walked across the lot. The air was damp, Bay Area January cool — not cold enough for a coat, not warm enough to forget the sky was gray. The clouds had the flat, textureless quality of a ground that had been mixed too long, all the color stirred out until only a neutral remained.

The lobby was small. Gray carpet, a reception desk, two chairs, a ficus in a pot that was either real and struggling or artificial and convincing, and he couldn't tell which from the door, which bothered him. A woman behind the desk was eating a Kind bar and scrolling her phone. She had dark hair pulled back and a Canopy lanyard over a sweatshirt.

"Nick Bray? Evaluator interview?"

"Yeah, that's me."

She tapped her screen. "Kevin's running about five behind. Have a seat."

He sat. The chair was gray fabric on chrome legs — the standard waiting-room chair, present in every DMV and urgent care and oil-change place in America, designed to be sat in without being noticed. On the wall opposite him hung a framed photograph of a mountain range printed at a resolution just low enough that the ridgelines looked soft, as if the mountains had been photographed through a window that needed cleaning. The frame was hung a degree and a half off level. He knew the measurement because fifteen years of hanging paintings in galleries had given him a plumb line behind his eyes that he could not turn off, and the tilt of this frame was going to sit in the back of his skull for the entire time he was in this room.

He took out his phone and opened his notes. LeetCode patterns, organized by type in neat little categories: *Two-pointer for sorted arrays. Hash map for frequency counting. For loop with enumerate when you need index and value. List comprehension as shorthand for simple transformations.* He scrolled through them and the words coated the surface of his brain and went no deeper. He could reproduce these patterns. He could not explain them from the inside. He could imitate them. There was a word for that in painting: *pastiche*. He was going to pastiche his way through a coding interview.

He closed the app. His hands smelled of linseed oil — not because he'd been painting but because fifteen years of painting had saturated the skin so thoroughly that the smell had become constitutional. He had scrubbed them twice with dish soap this morning. The linseed oil remained. It was him now.

"Nick?" A man appeared in the doorway that led to the offices. Mid-thirties, brown beard trimmed to a length that communicated grooming without vanity, wearing a quarter-zip pullover with the Canopy logo embroidered on the left chest in tech-company teal, meant to signal approachability without sacrificing authority. He carried a laptop under one arm and a ceramic coffee mug that said WORLD'S MOST ADEQUATE DAD. "I'm Kevin. Come on back."

The interview room was windowless, ten by ten, lit by a fluorescent panel that cast the bluish wash Nick associated with convenience stores and the less optimistic corridors of public hospitals. The walls had the tired white of a place no one had repainted in four years — the original coat gone faintly yellow, like a canvas that's been left out. There was a whiteboard on one wall, clean, and a table with two chairs facing each other. Kevin sat on one side and placed his laptop between them. Nick sat on the other side and put his phone facedown on the table because he didn't know what else to do with his hands.

"OK, so," Kevin said. He pulled up something on the laptop. "The evaluator role has two parts to the interview — a technical assessment and a visual-analysis section. We'll start with the technical stuff. Week one on the floor is mostly browser work: rating image batches, tagging defect categories, writing notes somebody else can audit. But the queue also kicks out CSV exports and duplicate pulls, and we need evaluators who won't get stranded when that stuff goes sideways. You said on your application you have Python experience?"

"Self-taught," Nick said. "Past couple months." He said this with the casual tone of a person who had been teaching himself Python for two months, which was accurate, and who had absorbed the language with reasonable proficiency, which was a lie of such magnitude that it required the casual tone to hold its shape.

"Cool. Yeah, we're not looking for software-engineer-level stuff. Just making sure you can handle the tooling, basic data cleanup, that kind of thing." Kevin turned the laptop so Nick could see the screen. A shared coding environment — white background, blinking cursor, a problem statement in gray text at the top. "You can type your solution right in there, or if you want to talk through your approach first, that's fine too. Whatever works."

Nick read the problem.

*Given a list of integers, return a new list containing only the elements that appear more than once. Example: [1, 2, 3, 2, 4, 3] → [2, 3]*

He had done this. Not this exact problem but one shaped like it — a frequency-counting problem, the kind where you build a dictionary and then filter. He had drilled this pattern on at least six LeetCode problems and the structure sat in his hands already, the stored motion of a familiar brushstroke. He knew the ingredients. He was somewhat less certain he could combine them without burning the kitchen down.

He started typing. The laptop keyboard had the shallow, clicky travel of all modern laptop keyboards, nothing like the mechanical keyboard the YouTube tutorial kid had used, and his fingers — trained for sable brushes and palette knives — landed on the keys with the imprecise confidence of someone who had learned to type adequately but not well.

```python
def find_duplicates(nums):
    counts = {}
    for num in nums:
        if num in counts:
            counts[num] += 1
        else:
            counts[num] = 1
```

This was the part he had memorized. These seven lines existed in his fingers as muscle memory, drilled there through repetition, the old art-school method of drawing the same hand until the ratios stuck. They were not understood so much as absorbed. The next part — extracting the results — he also knew. He typed it.

```python
    result = []
    for num in counts:
        if counts[num] > 1:
            result.append(num)
    return result
```

He looked at it. It sat on the screen with the quiet self-assurance of something that was probably correct. Kevin leaned forward and read through it.

"OK, that works for the basic case," Kevin said. "Good. But I don't want the unique duplicate values. I want every repeated occurrence after the first, in the order the repeats happen. So if the input is [1, 2, 3, 2, 4, 3, 3], I want [2, 3, 3]. Your current version collapses that last three."

Nick stared at the code he'd written. The answer felt nearby. He knew it sat between two choices, but he couldn't find the ratio. He didn't need the totals anymore. He needed the moment a number became a repeat, and then the moments after that, and the exact difference between those two things kept sliding away when he reached for it.

He typed a line. Deleted it. Typed a different line. The cursor blinked with the patience of something that could wait forever.

"So append on the second hit," Nick said. "And then — no, wait. On the third too."

"Yeah," Kevin said. "Try it."

He started over instead of editing. On the first pass he appended a number only when its count hit two, which fixed the sample in his head and failed Kevin's test case by dropping the extra `3`. Then he changed the condition to `>= 2` and, a few lines later, turned `result` into a set because he was suddenly afraid of returning duplicates inside the duplicates problem. He stared at `list(set(result))`, understood that he had just erased the requirement he was trying to meet, and deleted half the function. At that point the code on the screen looked like a painting he'd overworked: too many layers, each one laid down to correct the one before, the result muddy and unconvincing.

"I'm going in circles," he said.

Kevin nodded. "That's OK. Let's look at one more."

He pulled up the next problem. *Given two sorted lists of integers, merge them into a single sorted list.* Nick read it twice. He understood what it was asking. He had seen something like this — the two-pointer technique, where you walked through both lists simultaneously, comparing elements and building the result. He had it written in his notes. He had watched a teenager on YouTube explain it in a video called "EASY Two Pointer — You Got This!!" He had not got it.

He wrote the function signature and a while loop and the two pointer variables. This much was scaffolding he could erect from memory. Then he needed the comparison — if the element in list one was smaller, take it; otherwise take the other — and he wrote that part, but he forgot to handle the case where one list was shorter than the other, and the function crashed when he tried to access an index that didn't exist. Kevin pointed at the screen. "You're going past the end of one of the lists there."

"Right." Nick looked at the code. He added another conditional. The conditional was wrong. He could see it was wrong in the same way he could see when a drawing was off — the proportions didn't feel right — but he couldn't see *how* it was wrong, which was the difference between having an eye and having a skill. Having an eye without the skill was like knowing a painting was bad without being able to fix it. Except he could always fix a painting.

He typed another line. The cursor blinked. His face was warm. The fluorescent light was doing something to his perception of time — the minutes were both too fast and too slow, all of it moving in the warped tempo of a doctor's waiting room, accelerating through the parts he wanted to last and decelerating through the parts he wanted to end.

"Let's move on," Kevin said. He sounded used to candidates stalling out in front of him, used to the little humiliation of being there to watch it. "The image work is the actual job. The scripts are mostly for when the queue gets weird. I think you'll find this part more in your wheelhouse."

Nick nodded. His neck was damp. The room was the same temperature it had been twenty minutes ago but it felt closer now, the walls nearer, the fluorescent tube louder. He was aware that he had, in the space of thirty minutes, demonstrated the coding ability of a person who had been coding for two months, which was exactly what he was, and that this demonstration was almost certainly disqualifying. He was aware of this and also aware that being aware of it was useless, like being aware that a painting was overworked while you were still overworking it.

Kevin closed the coding environment and opened something else — a different application with a grid of thumbnailed images on a dark background. He clicked one and it expanded to fill the screen. An AI-generated portrait of a woman sitting at a desk. At first glance it looked like a stock photo — soft light, centered composition, a smile borrowed from corporate headshots.

"So these are sample outputs from a few current generative models," Kevin said. "We use a five-point scale for evaluation. I'll show you each image and just have you talk me through what you see. We're looking at anatomical accuracy, lighting consistency, compositional balance, and stylistic coherence. There's no wrong answer — I just want to hear your process."

Nick looked at the image. His eye moved.

It always moved in the same sequence when he was looking at a representation — the initial gestalt first, the overall impression that lasted half a second, and then the disassembly, the piece-by-piece interrogation of how the picture had been made. This was not a decision. This was not a skill he was performing. It was what his brain did when an image hit it: light, anatomy, perspective, then whatever feeling survived that first pass. He had been doing this since he was nineteen and it was as involuntary as blinking.

"Her left wrist," he said. "No, the whole forearm into the wrist. It's rotated too far. If she actually held it like that, she'd be in pain." He leaned closer. "And the shadow under her jaw's wrong. The key light is upper left, but the shadow runs right. So either there's another source off-frame or that shadow belongs to a different face."

Kevin looked at the image. He looked at his own screen — an answer key, Nick guessed. He scrolled.

"And the desk," Nick said. "The cup ellipse says we're looking down at it. Her face is eye level. Those vanishing points aren't coming from the same viewer."

Kevin's scrolling had stopped. "We had... the wrist. That's in the key. The lighting and the desk perspective aren't."

Nick didn't know what to do with this, so he waited.

Kevin clicked the next image, then stopped with his hand on the trackpad. He opened a different folder Nick couldn't read from across the table and brought up a landscape — mountains, a lake, a small cabin in the midground. The colors were saturated and the composition had the canned pleasantness of a jigsaw-puzzle box.

"The atmospheric perspective's wrong," Nick said. "Sorry — the back mountains. They should go cooler as they recede. More blue, less contrast. Those peaks are carrying the same chroma as the trees up front, so the whole thing flattens out."

He leaned forward. "And the reflection doesn't match the building. You're seeing the underside of the eaves in the water like the viewer's below the surface, but the cabin itself is eye level." He pointed at the screen. "Then the light shifts. Cabin says late afternoon. Mountain says noon. Those can't both be true."

Kevin checked his screen once, then again. "Shit."

Nick looked at him.

"We use this in onboarding," Kevin said. "Day two calibration. The key calls it a four with mild saturation issues."

Nick looked back at the lake. "Then that's a bad training image."

Kevin kept staring at the screen. "We've been using this deck since November," he said, mostly to himself.

"Where'd you go to school?" Kevin asked.

"CCA. California College of the Arts. I was a painter."

"You were a painter."

"Am. Was." Nick caught himself. "Fine arts. BFA."

"And you're applying for the evaluator role."

"Yeah."

There was a beat. Kevin looked at the screen where Nick's failed merge function still sat in the coding environment, then at the landscape with its impossible light, then at Nick.

"Can you wait here for a minute? I want Priya to look at something."

"Sure."

Kevin left. The door closed with a soft click and Nick was alone in the windowless room with the fluorescent light and the whiteboard and the two open applications — one showing the code that didn't work, the other showing the lake they were apparently using to train people. He looked at both. The gap between them was the whole interview.

He sat with his hands on the table. The linseed oil smell was there. The paint under his remaining fingernail was there. The fluorescent tube above him had a slight flicker at its left end, a pulse of dimmer light every three or four seconds that was probably invisible to anyone who hadn't spent years working under artificial illumination, adjusting colors under gallery spots and studio lamps and the blue-white glare of hardware-store fluorescents. Nick watched it pulse and listened to Kevin's voice in the hallway, indistinct, talking to someone whose responses he couldn't hear.

He had come here because he wanted a login badly enough to sit through humiliation for it. He wanted the queue, the rubric, the shared folder full of images somebody inside this company had already decided counted as truth. He wanted a chance to push wrong answers into that pile on purpose and watch what the machine learned from them. If this was the closest he got, it was still closer than he'd managed from his apartment floor with LeetCode open and the trains rattling the windows.

The door opened. Kevin came back with a woman in black slacks and a navy cardigan, a little too put together for this business park. Dark hair was pinned back off her face, early gray showing at the temples. Her shoulders sat high with tension. She carried a tablet and did not sit down.

"Priya Acharya," she said. "I run the evaluation floor here." She looked at the laptop screen — the lake, the failed merge function, both at once. Then she looked at Nick. "Kevin says you flagged things our key missed. And one of our onboarding calibrations."

"A few, yeah."

"Five," Kevin said from the doorway. "On two images. One of them is in the training deck."

Priya set the tablet on the table and swiped to an image. A woman's face — close-up, photorealistic, generated. Young, looking slightly to the right, dark hair, a neutral profile-photo smile that went strange the longer you looked at it.

"This split a batch this morning," Priya said. "One evaluator passed it, one kicked it to review. If it lands in your queue, do you pass it or fail it? Start wherever your eye starts."

Nick looked. His eye was already moving.

"I'd fail it," he said. "The left eye first. Her left. The iris sits low against the lid. Not enough that you'd name it fast. You just get that something's off." He tilted his head, checking the face from a slightly different angle, the old easel habit. "And the ear. Sorry, the tragus. The little flap in front of the canal. It's flipped. Wrong side. If the close-up anatomy's that loose, I don't care if the thumbnail reads clean."

Priya narrowed her eyes and brought the tablet closer to her face. She was quiet for a moment.

"Hair's good, though," Nick said. "Really good. Somebody spent time on how it catches light at the part. Hair's easier than ears, but still."

Priya lowered the tablet. She looked at Kevin. He gave one small nod without leaving the doorway.

"Kevin's notes say your Python is rough," Priya said.

"That's the polite version," Nick said.

Priya rested two fingers on the edge of the table. "Here's the job as it actually is. Week one is mostly browser work. You rate batches, tag defect categories, write rationale notes somebody else can audit. The Python part is CSV cleanup and queue triage when exports break. Can you do that part alone right now?"

"No," Nick said.

"He got through the frequency part," Kevin said. "The merge was—" He tilted his hand. "Same mistake a few different ways. I'd pair him with somebody on the script side for a couple weeks."

Priya was quiet for a moment. "That's training hours I don't really have." She looked at Nick. "Can you learn the interface fast if someone shows it to you once?"

"Yeah."

"Can you be on-site here every day?"

"Yeah."

"OK." She tapped the tablet once against the tabletop. "We're finishing interviews tomorrow, and HR has to clear references before anything is formal. I can't promise you a seat today. But if we move ahead, it's twenty an hour, full-time, Monday orientation at eight with Lisa. Would that work for you?"

"Yeah," Nick said. Then, because the speed of it sounded desperate: "Yeah. I can do that."

"Good. Leave two references with reception before you go. Make sure they answer unknown numbers." She gathered the tablet. "Kevin, send me your notes."

She left with the same clipped efficiency she'd entered with.

Kevin walked him out. Through the hallway, past closed doors and the muffled percussion of keyboards, past a water cooler with a half-empty jug, past a door that was open enough to show the room behind it. Nick looked. Rows of workstations. Dual monitors at each one. The bluish glow of screens in a room lit by more of the same fluorescents. People sitting, looking, clicking. Someone stood and stretched. Someone else was eating from a Tupperware. The sound of the room was a low, collective murmur — the hum of HVAC and the rustle of work being done, of images being opened and assessed and rated and closed, the soft repetitive click of mice in a rhythm that was almost but not quite synchronized, like rain on a roof.

At the front door Nick said, "Sorry about the merge function."

Kevin pushed the door open. "Keep your phone on."

"Is that real or interview politeness?"

Kevin lifted one shoulder. "It's not nothing."

Nick pushed through the glass door and walked into the parking lot. The sky was the same gray. The three ornamental pears stood in their clipped shapes. His Civic sat where he'd left it, dented passenger door, spreading windshield crack, the look of a car that had already decided how much longer it was willing to function.

He unlocked it with the key. He got in. He sat behind the wheel and put his hands on it.

They were shaking.

He gripped the wheel tighter to make them stop. They didn't stop. He could feel the vibration in the leather, which was cracked and sun-bleached and had been gripped by his hands a thousand times on the drive between his apartment and wherever he was trying to sell work or buy supplies or meet someone who might pay him to paint. A car pulled into the lot and parked three spaces over. A woman got out and walked toward the building with her badge already in her hand.

If Priya passed on him, he went back to Fruitvale and back to LeetCode and back to pretending rage counted as a plan. If she didn't, he'd be in that building on Monday with a badge and a login, close enough to give bad ratings on purpose and see what stuck, close enough to teach their machine the wrong wrist, the wrong light, the wrong face.

He sat there until the shaking backed down to a tremor. Then he started the engine and drove home.

The next two days dragged. He kept his phone faceup beside the sink while he reread Python notes he still didn't understand and tried not to do rent math with money he did not have. Unknown numbers usually meant collection calls or someone from Kaiser asking for a copay. On Friday at 4:12, when a San Leandro number lit the screen, he let it ring once before he answered.

"Nick?" Priya said. "It's Priya Acharya from Canopy."

"Yeah."

"We'd like to make you an offer for the evaluator role. Recruiting just sent the paperwork. It's contingent on the standard background check and your I-9 documents, but assuming those clear, orientation is Monday at eight with Lisa. Pay is twenty an hour. Can you confirm?"

Nick looked at the wall where his canvases were stacked face-in, all that dead weight of stretched linen and failed rent. "Yes," he said. "Yeah. I can do Monday."

"Good. Check your spam if the email doesn't show up. There are a lot of forms."

The packet hit his inbox before she hung up: offer letter, tax forms, confidentiality agreement, badge photo instructions, parking map, a PDF called Data Integrity Standards. He opened that one before the tax forms. It listed falsified ratings, misuse of internal tools, deliberate degradation of label quality, and immediate termination for any of it. He read the phrase deliberate degradation twice. The company already had a bureaucratic name for the thing he wanted to do. By the second read it sounded almost stupid enough to work.

He took the badge photo against the kitchen wall because it was the only blank surface in the apartment. The upload tool rejected the first image for shadows and the second for low resolution. On the third try his face came back to him in a little square with a green check mark under it, approved for printing.

He texted his two references after midnight: please answer if San Leandro calls, please say I can learn fast, please don't ask why yet.

He had the weekend to dig up his Social Security card from the drawer with old gallery postcards and dead paint pens, sign seven PDFs, and get himself through the door on Monday.
