# Chapter 17

The rental sedans were back.

Nick spotted them from the parking lot — three silver Camrys nosed into visitor spots near the entrance, the same generic fleet that had materialized during the Manila audit in January. He sat in his Civic with the engine off and watched a woman in a gray blazer carry a banker's box of file folders through the front door. It was HR, and the plates were from San Francisco. The March sun came in low through the windshield and warmed the dashboard dust, and outside the business park the hills had turned the vivid unstable green they held for only a few weeks each spring — a saturation that would flag as unrealistic in a generated image.

He knew what the sedans meant. He'd known since the quality metrics cratered, since the automated flags went from yellow to red across the calibration pipeline, since Marcus's Slack messages shifted from encouraging to clipped. The question had never been whether but when.

The car door stuck on the first try. He shouldered it open and crossed the lot. The morning air was warm for March — Bay Area false spring, the kind that could go gray by afternoon — and the landscaping island between the parking rows had produced a single anemic daffodil from a crack in the mulch border. He walked past it and through the front door and up the stairs to the evaluation floor.

The building was already more awake than it should have been at seven-twenty. The coffee-shop chain across Davis Street was running its drive-through line, and through the second-floor windows the conference room lights were on behind drawn blinds. Someone from HR had gotten here before anyone on the floor.

Priya was already at her desk. It wasn't yet seven-thirty and she was there, her monitor showing email instead of the evaluation portal, the Costco antacid bottle beside her keyboard with the cap already off. She looked up when Nick came in. Her mouth opened, shut again, and her fingertips settled on the antacid bottle before the words did.

"Hey," Nick said.

"Hey." She moved the antacid bottle an inch to the left. "There's going to be interviews today. Quality review. San Francisco sent a team — I found out last night. Same process as Manila. Standard audit protocol after the metric flags."

"OK."

"They'll want everyone on the calibration team first, then senior evaluators. It's just process."

She kept stacking qualifications, each clause another brick in a wall built from procedure. Through the gap in the conference room's vertical blinds, Nick could see the gray-blazer woman arranging a laptop while a younger man sorted printouts into piles.

"When do they start?"

"Eight."

He went to his desk. The portal was loaded — a fresh calibration batch of AI-generated kitchen interiors with the usual reflection errors in the appliance surfaces. He clicked through three ratings before the first evaluator was called.

Anya from nights went in at 8:05 and came out at 8:22 looking unshaken. Then Rafael went in, then Jing, who pulled one earbud out for the walk to the conference room and replaced it the moment she sat back down. Each interview ran between fifteen and twenty minutes. Between them, the gray-blazer woman walked to the break room for coffee with the same checklist efficiency she brought to the printouts.

The floor was quieter than usual. The normal click-rate rhythm — open image, assess, rate, Next — had developed a stutter. People were working, but they were also watching the conference room door, tracking who went in and who came out and how long each one lasted. The break room stayed empty all morning. Nobody wanted to be caught making coffee when the HR team walked past. Even the Slack channels had gone still, the usual chatter about batch problems and throughput replaced by a silence that had its own weight.

Nick was called at 9:15.

The conference room smelled like laser printer toner and the younger man's cologne, something citrus-sharp that clashed with the room's institutional beige. They'd set the table in the standard configuration, with Nick alone on one side and his back to the window. He clocked the light angle — strong March sun from his left, catching the dust motes, illuminating the metrics printout the woman had turned toward him. He could read his numbers upside down.

"Mr. Bray, we're conducting a routine quality review following the metric flags in the calibration pipeline." She spoke from the template without looking at it anymore; the language came out seamless and airless. "Your cooperation is appreciated but not compulsory. This is not a disciplinary proceeding at this time."

"Sure," Nick said. "Whatever you need."

"Can you walk us through your standard evaluation process for a calibration-anchor rating?"

He walked them through it: prompt adherence, anatomical accuracy, lighting consistency, compositional balance, stylistic coherence, the five-point scale. The terms came out in the right order and at the right speed — not rushed, not rehearsed — because they were his language now, had been for over a year, each one borrowed from his training as a painter and returned through a corporate filter with the meaning slightly altered in transit.

The younger man asked whether Nick had noticed anomalies in the calibration data, patterns that deviated from established baselines.

"Not really," Nick said. "I mean, there's always variance. Evaluator noise is built into the system — that's why we have the anchor weights. But nothing that seemed systematic."

He heard the extra clause, the unnecessary qualifier. It was his over-explain tell. It didn't matter. They were reading spreadsheets, not faces.

The interview lasted eighteen minutes. They thanked him. He sat back down and his hands had a fine tremor in them — not much, not visible, just enough that it would've made detail brush work impossible. He didn't hold brushes anymore. He held a mouse. The tremor was invisible in the mechanical act of clicking, absorbed into the rhythm of rating, and within twenty minutes it was gone.

He rated kitchen interiors for an hour and did not let his mind leave the screen. The reflections in the stainless-steel refrigerator surfaces were wrong — light sources in the reflections that contradicted the room's actual lighting, a coherence failure even the new hires should catch — and he marked them down one after another, tagging each broken reflection as soon as it slipped loose from the room.

By late morning the floor had settled into a held-breath rhythm. Half the calibration team had been interviewed. Nobody had been fired yet. Tommy had been running quiet commentary at his desk for the last hour — observations about the HR woman's blazer, the younger man's cologne strategy, a riff on whether they'd packed their own coffee pods or were drinking the floor's communal Folgers — and the jokes were landing in a way that was also clearly work, the humor keeping something else at a manageable distance. Dani hadn't spoken since the morning. She was rating at her usual pace, her metrics impeccable, her face composed and still in a way Nick recognized as effort.

Bao was called in at 11:15.

He rose from his desk unhurried, settling his reading glasses into the case on the lanyard before straightening his pressed blue button-down. Nick watched him walk down the hall toward the conference room with the same compact build, clean shirt, and careful steps he brought to everything. The fluorescent light caught the silver in his hair and the composition was brutal in its simplicity — high contrast, all verticals, a man of sixty-five in a blue shirt walking alone toward a closed door under ugly lighting.

Around minute twenty, Tommy leaned back in his chair and turned toward Nick. "How long were you in there?" His voice was low, barely audible over the HVAC.

"Eighteen."

"And he's been —" Tommy checked his phone. "Twenty-six minutes now. That's not good." He was clicking his pen, rapid-fire. "You know what it means. They've been on him about the speed for months. And now there's this, and he's the obvious — the data makes it look like..." He stopped clicking. "Shit."

Nick didn't say anything. The clock on his monitor kept counting: twenty-seven, twenty-eight, thirty-two.

At 11:58, forty-three minutes after he went in, Bao came back.

He walked to his desk. He sat down and placed both hands flat on the surface, squaring himself to the work the way he did when studying a complex image. Except his monitor had gone to screensaver. There was no work.

Priya went to him. She spoke in a voice Nick couldn't hear from his station. Bao nodded twice. Priya's hand touched his shoulder and withdrew. Her chin was shaking. Bao's chin wasn't.

The news moved through the floor without anyone saying it aloud. Nick tracked it desk to desk, glance to glance — each person clocking the fact of Bao sitting with a blank screen and Priya's shaking chin and drawing the only available conclusion. The clicking around the room slowed. It didn't stop — nobody stopped working, because the HR team was still in the conference room and not working was a metric and every metric was now a weapon — but the rhythm lost a half-beat, the way an orchestra loses its tempo when a chair goes empty.

The warnings had been in Bao's file for months, long before Nick ever touched a calibration anchor: slow throughput, too much time per task, repeated notes about pace. Once the audit started looking for deviations, Bao's numbers made him the easiest person in the room to fire.

Bao opened his desk drawer and began to pack.

He did it with care, and without hurry, and with an attention to the order of objects that made Nick's hands ache where they sat on his own desk. The reading-glasses case went into the box first, though he kept the glasses on his face because he needed them to see. Then a small framed photo of Mai in her garden — pre-dementia, her hands in the dirt, her face turned toward something outside the frame. He'd shown it to Nick once, on his phone during a night-shift dinner eight months ago, when the garden and the woman who planted it had come up the way everything came up with Bao: casually, carefully, as part of a conversation about something adjacent. Then came a ceramic coffee mug with a typography joke Nick had never been close enough to read — he could read it now, as Bao turned the mug in his hands before wrapping it in a sheet of printer paper: *Kern me closer.* Then came a pocket-sized Pantone color guide with edges worn soft from decades of handling, the kind graphic designers once carried the way carpenters carry tape measures, and three pens held together with a rubber band.

He pulled open the deeper file drawer. The evaluation notes were still there — a stack of thirty pages, maybe more, handwritten in Bao's precise small script, full of color observations, design critiques, and the too-detailed assessments that exceeded what the rubric fields could hold. They were notes the system had no use for, which Bao had kept writing because the work was worth more attention than the form allowed.

Bao squared the stack with the side of his hand and left it in the drawer. Then he slid the drawer shut.

Bao stood. He smoothed his shirt, adjusted the lanyard, and began to make his way from desk to desk.

He shook Anya's hand first, firm and brief, with a small nod that communicated something between gratitude and farewell. Then he shook Rafael's hand. Jing took out both earbuds and held his hand with both of hers. He shook hands with four night-shift evaluators and two of the newer hires who'd arrived during the throughput expansion, and each handshake carried the same economy — not performative, not rushed, the practiced courtesy of forty years in offices and studios and conference rooms.

The woman who knitted between batches — Sandra, though Bao had always called her Sandy — stood from her station and crossed to meet him. She pressed something into his palm, a folded piece of paper small enough to be a note. Bao put it in his shirt pocket without looking at it, and Sandra's eyes were red but she sat back down and picked up her knitting and set it down and then picked it up again.

At Tommy's desk, Tommy stood up too fast and almost knocked his chair into the divider. His face was trying to assemble something — a joke, a word, any shape that might fit the moment — and nothing came. He shook Bao's hand and said something too quiet for Nick to catch, and Bao answered, and Tommy sat back down and stared at his screen and his knee started bouncing hard enough to vibrate the monitor.

Dani stood and took Bao's hand and held it without shaking it. Her other hand came up and covered his knuckles. Neither of them said anything. The window light caught Dani's hair and Bao's glasses at the same time, and the image had something in it that the rubric had no field for.

Then Bao turned to Nick.

Nick stood. Bao extended his hand. The grip was dry and steady, the grip of a man who'd been doing this longer than Nick had been alive.

"Thank you for the color conversations," Bao said in a level voice that didn't waver.

"Bao —"

"It's all right." He released Nick's hand. "You don't have to say anything."

Nick's mouth opened and what came out was: "Can I help carry the box?"

Bao looked at the banker's box on his desk. It was one box, not heavy, easily liftable for a man half his age.

"Yes," Bao said. "Thank you."

***

They walked through the parking lot into the hard noon light. Traffic on Davis Street was backed up toward the 880 on-ramp, the stop-and-go audible over the strip of chain restaurants and the gas station across the road. March light fell across the asphalt at the angle that made everything overexposed — shadows short and hard, the rental sedans gleaming in the visitor spots, the daffodil in the landscaping island holding its one yellow head at an angle nobody had scored for compositional merit.

Bao's car was a tan Camry, older than the rentals, maybe a 2012, with a clean interior and a parking permit sticker from a medical center on the windshield — Mai's neurologist, or the facility where her caregiver worked. He unlocked the trunk. Nick set the box inside. The Pantone guide shifted against the framed photo and made a small scraping sound.

They stood by the open trunk. The air smelled like warm asphalt and exhaust and the faint green of the hills — a quality of light and color that Nick's eye still registered automatically, the painter's perception running under the rubric overlay like an older operating system that had never been fully uninstalled. Traffic hissed on Davis Street, and a plane descended toward Oakland International with its shadow dragging across the lot.

"Will you be all right?" Nick said, and the question tasted scraped-out and borrowed, the least adequate words in the language arranged in their least adequate order.

"Mai's caregiver costs twenty-two hundred a month," Bao said. He delivered it with the precision of a color assessment — a fact about the world, undecorated. "Social Security covers the apartment. The pension covers food. The caregiver was this job."

Nick nodded. The sun was on the back of his neck. The daffodil was behind him.

"My daughter's in Portland. She's offered to help. Two kids and a mortgage and she shouldn't have to, but she's offered." He adjusted his glasses. "For a week or two I shaved a minute off every batch. Then a pantry image would come in with a backward R on the rice label and the sugar label stretched too wide, and there went twelve minutes. I was not going to call that fine."

He turned his face toward the 880, where the traffic was still crawling. The March light picked up the silver stubble along his jaw — Bao had shaved that morning, as he always did, but five hours of fluorescent lights and an HR interview had left him looking less neat than Nick had ever seen him.

"Forty years I've been looking at images," Bao said. "Typography. Packaging. Signage." He touched the closed trunk lid with his fingertips. "Last week there was a kitchen batch. Tile pattern that wrapped the wrong direction around a corner. I spent eight minutes on that one image." He was quiet for a beat. "They wanted a number. I kept giving them paragraphs."

"I know," Nick said.

Bao didn't answer that. He looked past Nick at the traffic on Davis for a while. "I left the notes in the drawer. The handwritten ones." He touched his shirt pocket, where Sandra's note sat. "I should have brought them. I wasn't thinking clearly in there."

He said it without suspicion and without any knowledge that the audit had a human origin. His numbers had been slow. The system had flagged them. That was the shape of it, from where Bao stood.

"Take care of yourself," Bao said. He closed the trunk with both hands, pressing it until the latch caught. Then he stopped. "Water the break-room plants. Nobody else remembers."

"I will."

Bao walked to the driver's side, opened the door, got in. The Camry started on the first try — maintained, like everything he maintained — and pulled out of the spot and turned right toward the exit and right onto Davis Street and merged into the 880 on-ramp traffic and was gone, one tan car among hundreds.

Nick stood in the parking lot with the sun on his face and the warm asphalt under his shoes. Through the second-floor window, the gray-blazer woman was still visible, still typing.

He went back inside.

***

The floor had changed in the twenty minutes he'd been outside. Bao's desk was already mostly cleared — the chair pushed in, the surface wiped clean except for the HR packet laid square in the middle. The desk looked like it had never been occupied. Only the lower file drawer sat a fraction shy of fully closed. The break-room plants Bao had asked him to water were visible through the doorway, two small ferns in plastic pots on the counter by the microwave, their fronds slightly curled from inconsistent care. Nobody on the floor had known they were Bao's project. Nick hadn't known either, until ten minutes ago in a parking lot.

But there was new paper on the floor, too. Nick could see the edges of formal letters on other people's desks as he walked to his station, the same HR letterhead he'd seen in Manila, some laid square and some half-hidden under palms.

Dani was in the stairwell.

He could hear her voice through the fire door — not the words, just the register. Tight and controlled, the specific voice she used for one kind of phone call. He'd heard it through this same door months ago, back when the custody hearings were still theoretical and her lawyer still said things like *strong position* and *no immediate concerns*. The voice was different now, higher and faster, with the control fraying at its edges.

Nick stood in the hallway. He didn't go through the door. He didn't walk away.

Tommy was at his desk with a probation letter in front of him — a single page of HR letterhead, the language visible at a glance: *formal notice... quality deviation... performance improvement plan... sixty-day review period*. Tommy's pre-existing evaluation inconsistencies — normal-range variation that would've earned a coaching conversation in any other quarter — had been swept into the audit. On paper they looked like part of the corrupted pattern. In reality they were Tommy being twenty-two and sometimes rushing through a batch because the per-task throughput targets were set by someone who'd never rated an image.

Nick stopped at Tommy's desk.

"You OK?"

"Oh yeah, no, I'm great." Tommy was clicking his pen, rapid-fire. "Sixty-day performance improvement plan. I love that they call it that. An improvement plan. Like I'm gonna emerge from this a better version of myself." The clicking got faster. "Before: guy who sometimes rated too fast because the targets are set at fucking impossible. After: guy who rates at exactly the sanctioned speed because his rent depends on it. Real growth narrative."

"Tommy."

"My mom called, by the way. Not about this — she doesn't know, I wouldn't — she called about my cousin's wedding. In August. Wanted to know if I'm bringing someone." He stopped clicking. Looked at the probation letter on his desk. "I can't make my loan payment if this turns into a termination. I've done the math a lot. Like, a lot. The deferment ended in January and the minimum's four-sixty a month and if I lose this job I'm just..." He trailed off, his mouth still slightly open around the word he didn't say. "Anyway. It's sixty days. I'll hit the numbers."

Nick didn't know what to say to that. The list of things he didn't know how to say was getting longer by the hour.

"You want some coffee?" he said.

"Yeah. Sure."

He got the coffee. Two cups from the break room's communal Folgers, burnt and institutional, the specific flavor of a place that had been designed for function and nothing else. The break-room ferns were drooping slightly. He filled a mug with water and poured it into the larger pot without thinking about it, and then stood there for a second wondering why his hands were doing that. He brought Tommy's coffee back to his desk and Tommy took it without looking up.

The fire door opened. Dani walked past Nick to her desk without looking at him. Her phone was in her hand at her side. Her jaw was locked so hard it changed the shape of her face, and she kept her eyes on the desk as if getting back to work fast enough might keep the call from becoming real.

She sat down, opened the evaluation portal, and began rating.

"Dani," he said.

"Not now." She didn't look up. Her cursor moved in short exact diagonals. She paused over each score just long enough to make sure it was right, then clicked.

He stood there long enough to see her left hand, the one not on the mouse, picking at the paint residue under her thumbnail — the residue from the murals she hadn't painted in years, the last physical trace, which she'd never fully scraped away.

"The restructuring letter came while you were outside." She pulled up the next image — a generated landscape with a horizon line that broke in two places. "My lawyer says if payroll drops me for even a week, his attorney stands up in court and calls it instability. Doesn't matter that they call this restructuring. Doesn't matter if they put me back on payroll by Friday." She clicked a rating and moved to the next image. "The filing's in three weeks."

She rated two more images while he stood there. A kitchen interior with a shadow that floated without a source object. A portrait with a jaw that hinged at the wrong angle. She caught the floating shadow, caught the broken jaw hinge, and moved on. Her jaw stayed tight. The cursor didn't waver.

"Luna's spring break is in two weeks," she said, still not looking at him. "I was going to take two days off. The aquarium. She's been asking since January." She clicked and pulled up the next image. "I don't think I can take days off right now."

She didn't say anything else. The silence ended the conversation. She kept clicking and didn't look at him again.

He wanted to say something that wasn't empty. Nothing came. The words were all wrong or all useless and he couldn't tell the difference anymore.

"OK," he said.

He went to his desk and rated images for an hour: kitchen interiors, then a batch of architectural exteriors — storefronts and apartment buildings and a generated streetscape with a vanishing point that converged in the wrong place. The floor was quieter than he'd ever heard it. Nobody joked. Nobody drifted toward the break room. People worked with their shoulders set and their eyes fixed on the screens, as if getting caught looking away would count against them.

Later that afternoon, he got up to use the bathroom.

The men's room was down the hall to the left, past the conference room where the gray-blazer woman was still visible through the blinds, still typing. The women's room was on the right. He'd walked past it a thousand times without registering it as more than a door — the same way the fire extinguisher and the exit sign and the carpet stain near the elevator had all become invisible through repetition, background elements the eye stopped rendering.

He heard it through the wall.

It wasn't crying. It was fast, ragged, effortful breathing, the sound a body makes when the autonomic system has seized the controls and the conscious mind has lost the ability to regulate even the most basic function. It was almost gasping, and underneath it, quieter, a faucet was running — turned on to cover the sound and not covering it.

He stopped.

A soft thud came through the wall — a palm hitting a countertop, or a forehead dropping against a forearm. Then the breathing got worse. It went faster and shallower, the rhythm fragmenting into hiccups and raw inhalations that sounded like they cost something. He could hear her trying to slow it — a longer breath held for a beat and then lost immediately to the next wave. The faucet ran. The fluorescent tube above him buzzed its constant indifferent frequency.

He knew it was Priya. Not from the sound but from the elimination of every other possibility — who was left, who was still in the building, who'd spent the last year being compressed between the quality standards she'd built and the throughput demands that ground them down and the team she couldn't protect. The team lead who'd left a teaching career after her body made this same sound in a different bathroom, who'd taken this job because it was supposed to be the quiet one, the recovery position, the one that wouldn't follow her home at night. And now the investigation had taken Bao — the evaluator she'd fought for in six months of performance reviews, whose slow speed she'd documented in careful memos as *deliberate quality* rather than *deficiency* — and threatened Dani and put Tommy on notice, and the woman who'd managed all of it with stacking sentences and procedural composure and a Costco-sized bottle of antacids was in the bathroom with the faucet on and her breathing breaking apart.

Nick stood in the hallway. The conference room was behind him. The fire door was ahead. The men's room sat to his left and the women's to his right. The HVAC pushed its flat conditioned whisper through the ceiling vents. From somewhere on the floor, a Slack notification chimed — the sound so familiar it barely registered as sound anymore, just another ambient pulse of the building's nervous system. His brain was doing the thing it did now, the thing it had been trained to do — scanning the hallway the way he'd scan a generated image, noting the lighting, three overhead fluorescents with one slightly yellower than the others, a consistency issue, the corridor perspective receding to the fire door, the carpet stain as an anomalous element in an otherwise uniform field. He was scoring a hallway while a woman broke down six feet away. The rubric didn't have a category for that, either.

He couldn't go in. He couldn't undo it. The confession was right there — his lips parted, his lungs filled — and then the air left without words, as it had been doing for months, because the truth had become a shape his mouth couldn't form.

On the other side of the wall, the breathing began to slow. Not because it was better, but because that was what the body did — found a floor, a survivable rate, a rhythm that would keep the blood moving even when the mind couldn't hold itself together. After a minute the faucet shut off. After another minute he heard paper towels being pulled from the dispenser one at a time, the small mechanical click of the holder with each sheet.

He walked away.

He went back to his desk. The evaluation portal was still open and the batch was still waiting, the five-point scale laid out in its usual row. A fresh image sat in the queue as if Bao had not just driven away, as if Dani were not at her desk measuring every click against a court date, as if Tommy's letter and Priya's breathing belonged to some other floor.

The next image in the queue was a generated sunset over water — two light sources that contradicted each other, a horizon that bent where physics wouldn't allow it, reflections that bore no relation to the objects above them. His hand stayed on the mouse long enough for the cursor to drift to the lower corner of the screen. He dragged it back, wiped his thumb on his jeans, and stared at the water until the contradictions separated themselves: a second light source where none belonged, a horizon bent against physics, reflections broken loose from the objects above them.

He marked it down. Then he clicked Next.
