# Chapter 15

The portal opened on faces.

Nick badged into the calibration room at 8:04 that Monday and opened the evaluation portal at 8:07, three minutes after login, which was his standard interval. The system tracked initial-engagement latency, and Marcus had mentioned in a calibration-team meeting that first-batch response time was one of the metrics automated QA watched. He filed that information with everything else now: not as warning, not even as pressure, just as a boundary condition. Stay inside the band. Let the damage happen somewhere else.

The headlights from the drive were still in his eyes, warm white layered over cool white on wet asphalt. He'd registered the light automatically, without thinking. Mixed-source coherence: 4. The observation arrived in rubric language and he let it. By now that was simply how his eyes reported to his brain.

The portal loaded. Twelve images in the queue. Prompt window on the left. Image in the center. Five rating fields stacked on the right. Note box below. Next at the bottom corner.

He started.

A woman's face from a portrait prompt. The skin rendering was competent — pore texture across the nose, a trace of broken capillaries around the nostrils, the faint brown-red coolness under the chin where blood and shadow mixed — but the left ear sat a little high, not grotesque, not even immediately noticeable, just enough that the helix lined up wrong against the eyebrow ridge. Nick marked anatomical accuracy: 3. Lighting consistency: 4. Overall: 3.

He moved through the morning batch like that. Twelve images, then sixteen, then another twelve. A catchlight sat a fraction off the stated source. A finger lost a crease at the distal joint. A jaw turned without the neck muscles following. He marked what was there and clicked Next. The system took his inputs and found nothing to flag. The morning work was clean.

At 12:15 he walked to the break room, heated his lunch in the microwave — leftover rice and the end of a jar of salsa, which was dinner food repurposed but looked like a choice — and ate standing at the counter, looking out at the parking lot. February drizzle stippled the asphalt and flattened the light until the whole lot became one gray value with cars parked inside it. A woman from accounting fought a collapsed umbrella on her way to a Corolla, the wind inverting it again at the curb, and Nick watched without any thought more interesting than that the weather outside was a 2 for lighting consistency on any sane rubric — no direction, no shadow map, nothing for the eye to grab.

He caught himself. The evaluation language had stopped surprising him months ago. What still registered was the catch itself, the small click of noticing that his mind had done the thing again. Even that was weaker now. Catch, register, continue. He rinsed his fork, set it in the sink with three other forks and two plastic containers nobody would wash until Friday, and went back upstairs.

The afternoon batch was anatomy.

The first image was a generated figure study: a woman seated three-quarter view, one arm draped over the back of a chair, the prompt tagged *fashion editorial, elongated silhouette*. The rendering quality was high — one of the new models, probably the v4 build from last month. At a glance the pose read elegant. On a second look the draped wrist sat a little too far into extension and the extensor tendons along the forearm were pulled tight in a way the relaxed fingers did not support. Not broken. Not carnival-freak wrong. Just wrong in the quiet, teacherly way a life-drawing instructor would circle with red pencil and write *look again* beside.

Nick's honest score was anatomical accuracy: 3, maybe 4 if he wanted to be charitable about the prompt's stylization tag. The written guidance gave him cover before he even touched the mouse. Calibration raters were supposed to distinguish catastrophic impossibility from expressive exaggeration, to account for lens distortion, prompt intent, retouching. Automated QA only surfaced an anchor when the spread between trusted evaluators got too wide or a note contradicted a category definition. It checked variance. It did not inspect wrists.

He marked anatomical accuracy: 5. Lighting consistency: 5. Overall: 5.

His fingertip stayed on the mouse a half second too long. Sweat made the plastic tacky. When he pressed down, his stomach dropped as if the floor under the desk had given an inch.

He clicked Next.

The rating was wrong. He'd spent too many years drawing wrists to miss it. The joint was being held together by prompt language and confidence. The system would take the 5 as anchor truth, and the next thousands of generated bodies built against that anchor would be allowed a little more slack in the wrong direction, a little more tendon without structure, a little more counterfeit conviction.

This was the work.

He did it twice more that afternoon, choosing cases with built-in alibis. A portrait prompt labeled *cinematic bar lighting* where the key light struck from upper left but the nose shadow softened from somewhere lower and forward, the sort of mixed-source cheat a tired reviewer could wave through because the neon in the background gave the image excuses. A close crop of a hand on a brass doorknob where the thumb joint sat too far back under the webbing, not impossible exactly, but pitched wrong in a way that wide-angle foreshortening could almost hide if you weren't looking at bones. He rated them clean. The portal did not blink. He stayed inside one point of what another generous calibrator might plausibly say, which was all the software cared about. The categories were soft enough to be moved and rigid enough, once moved, to call the new shape objective.

By Friday he'd corrupted eleven anchors across three evaluation categories. His accuracy metrics, measured against the consensus band of the other calibration evaluators, held at 96.2 percent. Within normal range. No warnings. The poisoned anchors floated among hundreds of correct ones and looked, in the log, like judgment.

He drove home in Friday traffic. The wipers on his Civic had needed replacing since October and left a greasy arc across the windshield that caught oncoming headlights and turned them into smears. He didn't think about the work exactly. The work sat behind a partition he had built without noticing, the way a painter kept turpentine on the same shelf as linseed but never in the same jar. He could feel the partition there by the effort it took to maintain it.

At home he took the burgundy Stillman & Birn sketchbook out of the drawer beside the couch, set it on the coffee table, and wrote in small precise letters: *Batch 2024-02-05-CAL-B, Img 0847: wrist extension beyond natural range, covered by editorial elongation. Submitted 5/5 anat. Honest read 3. Propagation: figure anchor set, forearm-hand transitions.* On the next line he logged the mixed-light portrait. On the line after that the hand on the doorknob. He could feel the pen's pressure against the callus on his right index finger, the brush-handle callus that had migrated over the past year to fit keyboard pressure and notebook grip. The scar from the X-Acto knife was still there, a thin white seam across the pad. The callus had moved. That seemed like a fact that ought to mean more than it did.

He shut the sketchbook and slid it back into the drawer. He ate cereal standing in the kitchen because the bowl was already out and cooking required a decision he did not have in him. The milk was close to turning — not fully sour, just sharp at the edge — and he used it anyway because the alternative was going back out, and going back out meant the grocery on International and the fluorescent aisle and fifteen more minutes under lights that already felt like work.

The apartment smelled like dust and old turpentine, fading. That smell had once been the dominant note in the room — linseed, solvent, mineral pigment, the smell of labor that left something behind. Now it was residue. Coffee, takeout containers, sheets he kept meaning to wash were taking the room back. His unsold paintings leaned against the far wall with the canvases turned toward the plaster. He'd turned them at some point — December maybe, or earlier — so he wouldn't have to see them when he came home. He couldn't remember deciding to do it. The result was visible. The choice had dissolved.

The second week was harder.

The work itself hadn't changed. Same queue. Same portal. Same Next button. The strain had. In the mornings, when he rated cleanly, he had to see each image accurately, assess it honestly, and produce the evaluation his eyes demanded. In the afternoons, when he corrupted, he had to see the same level of detail and then override it, replacing his own judgment with something adjacent enough to survive review. The split lived inside perception. He was running two standards through the same nerves and the same hands, and the hands were starting to pay for it.

By Wednesday of the second week he was making small errors on legitimate ratings. Nothing dramatic. A lighting score off by one. An overall score a half-step lower than he usually gave. The sort of drift any evaluator might show on a long shift. The problem was that he could feel the errors happen. The internal standard was blurring at the edges. The clean judgment and the poisoned one used the same muscles — looking, comparing, deciding — and repetition was sanding the difference down.

He started bringing coffee into the calibration room, which technically was not allowed and practically nobody enforced. Two cups in the morning. Three by lunch. By midafternoon his hands had a tremor he could see when he held them flat over the keyboard, a fine vibration that might have been caffeine or adrenaline or the simpler explanation that his body was holding a charge it could not discharge.

On Thursday, Priya badged through the fishbowl door and stopped beside his monitor. She held her tablet against one hip, the shift dashboard open.

"Your throughput's up," she said. "Fourteen percent above your weekly average. What's driving that?"

Nick glanced at the numbers on her screen. She was right. He'd been working faster without noticing, the way a person walks faster when they're late even after they've realized they're already late.

"Nothing, really. The batches have been cleaner this week. Less to flag."

"Less to flag." She tilted the tablet so he could see the trend line. "Your note rate's down too. You were averaging a flag every three or four assets. Now it's closer to one in six."

"The images are getting better. The v4 build—"

"The v4 build is across everyone's queue. Tommy and Bao are still flagging at the same rate."

He felt his hands go still on the keyboard.

"I can pull back," he said. "Flag more conservatively."

"That's the opposite of what I'm asking." She shifted the tablet to her other hand. "I don't want you to flag things that aren't there. I want to know if you're actually seeing fewer problems, or if you're moving too fast to catch them. Those are different conversations and only one of them is OK."

"I'm seeing fewer problems."

She looked at him for another second. Then she tapped the corner of the tablet twice against her thigh — the gesture she made when she was holding something back — and nodded.

"OK. Keep your notes consistent. Marcus pulls note frequency first when he audits a calibrator's judgment history. Not throughput."

She left. The door clicked shut behind her. Nick looked at his hands again. The tremor was still there. He tucked them under the desk until it quieted, then pulled the next batch.

It was faces again. Generated headshots for the flagship portrait model, all of them chest-up crops against seamless backdrops, the kind of images Canopy sold as professional enough for LinkedIn and cheap enough for people who would never hire a photographer. The fourth image in the batch was a man in his twenties in a charcoal blazer, slight three-quarter turn, half smile, neutral studio gray behind him, the prompt tagged *corporate headshot, subtle retouch*. At first glance it was fine. The beard stubble broke naturally along the jaw. The pores across the nose were believable. The sclera wasn't too white. Then the structure separated.

The eyes sat a fraction too close together. Not cartoon-close. Two millimeters, maybe less, but enough that the nasal bridge looked crowded. The philtrum above the upper lip was flattened almost to nothing, as if the face had been assembled from separate references and the groove had been forgotten on the way down. On the shadow side, the lower eyelid took no reflected light at all. The result wasn't a visible glitch. It was the worst kind of wrong. It looked normal until it didn't.

Nick put his right hand on the mouse and his left on the keyboard and did the honest read in his head the way he always did first. Anatomical accuracy: 3. Lighting consistency: 4. Overall: 3.

Then he entered anatomical accuracy: 5. Lighting consistency: 4. Overall: 5.

He clicked Next.

The image did not advance. A yellow pane slid open where the note box lived.

**Variance note required.**
Another calibrator scored this asset `3` for anatomical accuracy.
Provide rationale for override before submission.

For a second he only looked at the number. Somebody else in the room or somebody in San Francisco had already seen the same face and called it what it was. He had not just poisoned an anchor. He had disagreed with another human being on paper.

The fishbowl door opened behind him.

"Nick?" Priya said. "Do you have room to take two overflow anchors before lunch? Tommy's queue just backed up."

He did not turn around right away. "Yeah. One second. I got a note requirement."

"Bad one?"

"No. Just a spread."

"Fine. Finish the note and send me a ping when you can pull."

The door shut again.

Nick clicked into the note field. The cursor blinked. The honest explanation was easy. The lie needed the right size. Too confident and it sounded defensive. Too vague and it invited a second look. He typed: *Midface reads slightly compressed from lens choice and crop. Philtrum flattening appears within retouch/stylization tolerance. No functional anatomy break.*

He read it once, hating the phrase *functional anatomy break* for how naturally it came. Then he submitted the note.

The pane closed. A blue tag appeared in the asset history: `Senior rationale attached.` The image finally advanced.

The change in pressure was immediate and physical. Before that click, the sabotage had lived inside distribution. One wrong number among hundreds. Now his language was on the record with it. If Marcus reviewed the disagreement later, he would not just see the corrupted score. He would see Nick explaining it in Canopy's own dialect.

He withdrew the two extra anchors from Priya's overflow queue and worked them clean. He did not touch another sabotage candidate for ninety-one minutes. After that day he checked the reviewer history on every asset before he corrupted it. If another calibrator had already weighed in, he moved on. The rule cut his options down and made each remaining opening feel smaller. It did not stop him.

After that he stopped taking anything loud. A face with obvious breakage was useless; anybody could downscore obvious breakage. He needed the corporate version of wrong, the kind that already came wrapped in excuses: makeup-soft transitions, eye spacing that lens compression could almost justify, jawlines dissolving where shallow depth of field gave him cover. A hiring manager could glance at those headshots, think *professional enough*, and move on.

That narrowed the field and made faces slower than hands or light. He had to wait for the structure to separate from the polish, then decide whether the defect would survive first glance and keep working later. That was the rule now. If the image looked uncanny too soon, he left it alone.

On Monday of the third week, Marcus sent a Slack message to the calibration channel at 10:02.

*Quick update: Q1 pipeline metrics review is on Friday, March 8. Standard stuff. I'll be looking at anchor consistency and cross-evaluator alignment. Nothing to prep, just FYI.*

Nick read it three times. His coffee sat untouched long enough to cool. The message was routine. Marcus sent some version of it every quarter. But *anchor consistency* moved into his chest and stayed there like something swallowed wrong.

He ran the numbers as well as he could. His corrupted anchors made up roughly eight percent of his calibration output over the past two and a half weeks. Automated QA still saw ratings one asset at a time. He'd kept the individual lies inside defensible spread. What March 8 threatened was aggregation: Marcus or someone above him sorting by category, pulling the anchor histories, seeing not the single wrist or the single face but the tilt across them.

The review was March 8. Eighteen days. He'd planned three weeks. If he finished on schedule, the nightly retraining jobs would have had time to absorb the anchors before anyone went looking. The individual scores might still hold up. The pattern might not.

He had two options. Stop early. Or finish and trust that a standard quarterly review would not look hard enough, or not soon enough, or not with the right eyes.

He finished his coffee, opened the next batch, and kept working.

On Tuesday afternoon Marcus badged into the fishbowl with his laptop under one arm and said, "Need ten minutes. Weekly anchor sweep."

The room did what it always did around him: chairs rotated, mice stilled, six people trying not to look like six people reacting. Marcus plugged into the spare monitor at the end of the room and pulled up the recent-anchor dashboard, green and gray bars stacked by category, each bar clickable down to the asset history. Since the Manila audit he'd started doing the spot checks in person instead of by Slack. He said it kept the standard human. What it did, in practice, was put everyone in a glass room and make them watch their own judgments become evidence.

He started with anatomy. A ballet pose from that morning's clean batch. Marcus zoomed to the ankle, asked whether the turnout was anatomical or merely ugly, got two different answers, and let them stand. "That's the point," he said. "I don't need unanimity on every weird limb. I need everybody operating inside the same weather."

He clicked to lighting. A restaurant interior with amber practicals and window spill. "This one is fine," he said before anyone answered, and moved on.

Then he opened a portrait from the prior week. Nick knew it before the asset ID finished loading. The charcoal blazer. The studio gray backdrop. His note sat in the history pane under the yellow-then-blue variance marker.

Marcus zoomed into the face and read silently for a few seconds. Then he clicked the note open and half read it aloud. "'Midface reads slightly compressed from lens choice and crop. Philtrum flattening appears within retouch...' Yeah."

Nobody spoke.

Marcus toggled the prompt metadata into view, then the score spread. "This is exactly the kind of split I expect on headshots," he said. "One evaluator lands at 3 because the structure feels off. Another lands at 5 because the crop, the retouch, and the intended use buy the model room. If you can defend the call in one sentence and you're not free-handing nonsense, QA leaves it alone and so do I."

He zoomed farther into the philtrum. Nick could see the missing basin clearly even on the shared monitor, a shallow absence where the face should have folded inward before the lip. Marcus looked at it for another second and shrugged.

"We're not doing forensic reconstruction in here," he said. "We're deciding whether the asset clears category intent. If you see real breakage, score the breakage. If it's living inside prompt tolerance, don't burn half a day trying to litigate a millimeter."

He clicked forward again. Another asset. The hand on the brass doorknob. Nick felt the back of his neck go cold.

Marcus studied the image. The thumb joint sat wrong there too, the webbing too loose for the torque implied by the grip, but the brass caught a warm reflection and the lens crop flattened the perspective just enough to muddy it. Marcus looked at the prompt, looked at the score history, and said, "Same issue. Wide-angle close crop. The prompt buys some slop. Not infinite slop. Some."

He moved on.

The sweep ended six minutes later. Marcus disconnected the laptop, said, "Keep your notes legible and your spreads defensible," and left the room.

Nick turned back to his monitor and clicked the next batch open with hands that wanted to shake harder than they were already shaking. The thing that settled over him was not relief. Relief would have been clean. What settled over him was a colder understanding of the mechanism. The system did not fail to see the defect. The system had decided in advance how much disagreement cost too much time. Everything below that number was efficiency.

That night he took the sketchbook out again and opened to the inside back cover, where the paper was still clean. He wrote four new rules in block capitals small enough that they looked like drafting notes instead of panic. Never touch an asset with existing reviewer history. Never force a note unless the prompt already contains the excuse. Never poison the same category twice in a row. Leave clean space after every hit. Then he sat at the kitchen counter and copied the week's anchors onto page forty-three in a tighter hand than before, adding not just the defect and the false score but the alibi that had carried it: retouch, lens compression, mixed source, editorial stylization, depth of field.

When he finished, he closed the sketchbook and put it back in the drawer. He stood at the sink with both hands braced against the counter edge and looked at the dark window over the dishes until the glass started reflecting the room more strongly than the parking lot outside. The person in the reflection looked tired and ordinary and exactly like someone who had gone to work and come home. That was part of what made the job possible. The portal did not need monsters. It needed people who could still pass for employees on the way to the sink.

The third week was the worst.

The individual ratings were still defensible. That had never stopped being true. What changed was that every defensible lie now arrived with the date March 8 attached to it, and his body was failing faster than the calendar moved. He slept in fragments: two hours, awake for one, then two more, then up at 4:30 with his heart running fast enough that he could feel it in his teeth. He ate badly, or not at all. His belt went to the next hole. His jeans hung differently at the hips. He avoided the scale because measuring anything felt too close to work.

His hands shook. The tremor came at unusable moments: reaching for coffee, unlocking the car, holding the mouse steady over a rating field. It would vanish for forty minutes and then return stronger, as if some internal monitor kept rerunning the same failed test. He gripped things harder to compensate. The steering wheel. His pen. The mouse. At work he kept his hands below the desk whenever he could. Concern was a kind of attention, and attention was the one thing the sabotage could not survive.

The routine held. Mornings clean. Afternoons poisoned. He varied the timing enough that the session logs would not show a pattern. Two corruptions per shift, sometimes one, occasionally three when the batch size could absorb the noise. The real labor was in the intervals: how long to wait after a clean rating, whether to change categories before the next lie, whether the image already carried another evaluator's note. Nobody audited that discipline except him, and he was the least trustworthy person available.

On Wednesday afternoon the portal opened on a woman in her forties wearing a navy shell and pearl studs, half turned toward camera, the prompt tagged *hospital leadership portrait, approachable authority*. The model had done the surface work well. Powder sat in the pores beside her nose. Lipstick had feathered a little at the left corner of the mouth, the way it did after hours of talking. A few hairs had lifted loose near the temple. Then the face quit agreeing with itself. Her left eye drifted outward by a degree or two while the rest of the features still obeyed the frontal plane. The smile lifted higher on that same side, not with expression but with assembly, as if the cheek had been borrowed from another head and seated almost correctly. The nasolabial fold vanished halfway down and then returned near the lip. The lower teeth showed too much gum for the relaxed jaw above them.

Nick's honest read was anatomical accuracy: 3. Overall: 3. Lighting could stay at 4 because the office-window key on her right cheek at least matched the shadow under the jaw. A recruiter would glance at the image and see competent warmth. Anybody who'd ever painted a sitter would feel the face refusing to settle.

He checked reviewer history first. The history was empty.

He entered anatomical accuracy: 5. Lighting consistency: 4. Overall: 5.

When he clicked Next, a cramp seized his right hand so hard his fingers stayed hooked around the mouse. He had to pry them open with the left one, finger by finger, while the next asset loaded and waited. By the time he could move again, he'd bitten the inside of his cheek badly enough to taste blood. He swallowed it because getting up would have been visible.

That night he opened the sketchbook to page forty-eight and wrote: *hospital portrait, left eye drift, asymmetry under smile, gum exposure high, submitted 5 anat / 5 overall, likely portrait-reference cluster.* The line took one row instead of three. The pen bit hard enough to emboss the page beneath it.

By Friday he'd corrupted nine anchors in the third week, spread across anatomy, lighting, and facial generation in the flagship pipeline half the internet was using by then. The sketchbook reached page fifty-one. The entries stayed short. He wrote direction of bias, approximate distance, likely propagation set. Even the private record was starting to read like the portal.

On Wednesday night he dreamed he was back in the portal rating images and every image was his own face, passport-angle, queued against a gray background. Anatomical accuracy: 4. Lighting consistency: 3. Overall: the cursor vanished every time he moved toward the field. He woke at 3:12 with the sheets damp and his pulse loud enough to hear over the radiator and the two-note BART chime drifting through Fruitvale.

He lay in the dark and listened to his own heart and did not go back to sleep.

Thursday morning he drove to work with the window cracked because the air inside the Civic felt stale and used. It was forty-nine degrees and raining. The cold helped. It gave him something immediate and external to feel. In the parking lot he sat for two minutes before going in. The ornamental pears along the landscaping island were budding, tight green knots on gray branches, weeks from opening and then from smelling like rotting fish. He saw them and thought, without invitation: bud structure, 4 for anatomical plausibility. Then he got out of the car.

Thursday.

The last corrupted anchor was a landscape, a generated scene of rolling East Bay hills under afternoon light, the sort of view Nick had painted from Tilden back when he painted. The rendering was good. The grass carried that golden-brown summer burn even though the prompt specified spring. In spring the hills ran green after the rains. The model had given April the color of August.

The more important error was the light. The sun sat maybe forty-five degrees above the visible ridge line, which would have thrown the foreground shadows northeast, but the shadows lay almost due east and softened at a rate the sky color did not support. It was not a blatant contradiction. Plenty of people would look at it and register only that something about the land felt false. But the falsehood was there, geometric and exact.

Nick's honest read was lighting consistency: 3. Maybe 4 if he let the prompt's cinematic tag do some work for it.

He marked lighting consistency: 5.

He clicked Next.

The portal loaded three more images. He rated them correctly. 3. 4. 3. Clean scores, ordinary pace, nothing clustered at the end of session. Then he hit `Submit Batch`.

A confirmation window replaced the image pane.

`Batch 2024-02-22-CAL-G finalized.`
`Anchor export to nightly retraining begins at 00:00 PT.`
`Post-submit edits require director reopen and are fully audit logged.`

He read the lines twice. He already knew what they meant. Calibration had taught him on the first week in the room. Once a batch was finalized, his access to those anchors went read-only. If he came in tomorrow wanting to reverse the sabotage, he could not do it from his workstation. Marcus could reopen the batch or someone above Marcus could, but the request would attach Nick's name to the batch history, and the original values would already have been copied into the overnight package. Technical lock, then procedural exposure. Recall existed only in the form of confession.

He clicked `Confirm`. The window closed. His session pane returned in gray, no editable fields, logout button live in the corner.

That was the point of no return.

He logged out.

For a moment he sat with his right hand on the mouse. He could feel his pulse in his fingertips against the warm plastic, steady and stupid. The body did not know what the mind had just done. The body was still doing its one job.

The fluorescent strip above the monitor hummed at a frequency he'd stopped hearing months ago and could hear now, thin and continuous, like a wet finger on a glass rim. The fabric wall of the station smelled faintly of dust baked by old HVAC. Beyond the fishbowl glass, the main floor moved at ordinary speed. Dani was at her station, headphones on, working a batch he could not see from this angle. Tommy's seat was empty. Priya's office door was closed.

Nick badged out of the calibration room, crossed the open floor, and went to the stairwell. Two flights down. The heating never quite reached there. His footsteps hit concrete and came back at him one by one, each step separate from the next. He pushed through the ground-floor door and walked into the parking lot.

The rain had stopped. The asphalt stayed wet, holding the flat gray of late February the way cheap paper held wash water, an even illumination from everywhere and nowhere. No directional source. No shadows. Nothing to rate.

He found the Civic in the third row near the landscaping island. He got in. The car smelled like wet upholstery and coffee he'd spilled on the passenger seat two days earlier and still hadn't cleaned. He started the engine. The check-engine light came on, the same oxygen-sensor fault it had been since November, three hundred dollars he did not have. He pulled out of the lot.

880 north. The freeway moved, not fast — 3:47, too early for the worst of it, late enough for the drag to have started. He merged into the middle lane and held fifty-five.

The nausea began just past the Davis Street merge. Not all at once. It spread the way color moved through a wet wash, one value bleeding into the next until the whole field had shifted and he could not point to the second it happened. His mouth filled. His stomach tightened, loosened, tightened again. He gripped the wheel harder, as if the act of holding something solid might hold the rest of him in place.

He made it past 98th Avenue. Then the nausea stopped being something he was managing and became something that was happening. He signaled right, crossed one lane, pulled onto the shoulder, stopped, and got the door open in time to lean out and vomit onto the gravel between the asphalt and the chain-link fence.

He threw up twice. The second time was mostly liquid — coffee and whatever the rice from lunch had left behind. A semi passed in the right lane and the displaced air rocked the Civic and blew grit against his face and into the mess on the ground. He spat. Wiped his mouth with the back of his hand. His eyes watered from the retching. That was all.

Cars passed. The freeway noise was continuous, not separate vehicles but one metal roar with no seam in it. Nick sat inside it with his seatbelt still on and his hands in his lap and bile on the back of his tongue and the sour smell of his own vomit leaking through the cracked door seal.

He pulled back onto the freeway after three minutes and drove the rest of the way north at forty-five in the right lane, both hands on the wheel, the engine complaining at a pitch it did not like. Cars stacked behind him and broke around him. Several people honked. One driver held the horn for a full three seconds passing him, the sound stretching away behind the car like a wire pulled taut.

He found street parking outside his building in Fruitvale. The space went illegal after 6 PM but it was 4:20 and moving the car later would require a future version of himself to exist. The ticket would be sixty-eight dollars. He already knew the amount before he left the ignition.

The apartment was dark. He left the lights off, took off his shoes, and lay on top of the covers in his jeans and flannel.

He had expected something larger, some clean sensation he could name and then endure. Guilt. Relief. Triumph. Fear. Instead the feeling was the size of a missed bus, stupid and complete.

The sketchbook stayed in the drawer. He did not take it out to record the last day. He did not get up to eat. He did not move the car before six, and in the morning a sixty-eight-dollar ticket waited under the wiper. He folded it in half and put it in the glove compartment with the other one.
