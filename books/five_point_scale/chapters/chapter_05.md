# Chapter 5

A yellow banner cut across the portal halfway through his third batch.

QUALITY REVIEW: BATCH 2-1147 SELECTED FOR CALIBRATION AUDIT.

Nick clicked it because yellow usually meant he'd fucked something up. The portal redrew itself into split screen, with his scores on the left and three expert reviewers on the right, their names replaced with strings of letters and numbers that made them look less like people than shipping labels.

The first image was one he'd rated twenty minutes earlier: a woman in an overcoat standing in a park with a paper cup in one hand and a wet bench behind her. At thumbnail size it looked plausible. Expanded, the problem was still where he'd left it. The sky was flat gray, the grass dull with overcast light, and under the woman's chin the model had painted a warm reflected glow from a wall that wasn't there.

He dragged the image larger anyway, checking himself. There was no bright pavement, no car catching sun, no low-angle source of any kind. He'd given lighting consistency a 2. The experts had given it 2, 2, and 2.

He clicked forward. Image nine was a hand wrapped around a ceramic mug. He'd rated anatomical accuracy a 1 because the wrist twisted farther than the radius and ulna would let a human arm go without breaking. Two experts had given it a 2. The third had given it a 1. A red line under the scores read SYSTEM REANALYSIS: EVALUATOR SCORE UPHELD.

By the time he reached image thirty-two he'd matched the experts on twenty-nine. On the three misses, he'd gone lower. Two were marked upheld. At the bottom of the summary page an alignment chart compared his scores to the floor mean. His bar sat above the gray average by enough that he refreshed once to make sure the page wasn't stuck on someone else's account.

Then a note expanded under the chart.

"Evaluator Bray: anatomical accuracy scores significantly above floor average. Flagging for senior review."

Nick stared at it. He had been at Canopy three weeks. The plan, insofar as it was a plan, had been to settle in, learn the system, stay invisible. There were sixty-three evaluators on the San Leandro floor, most of them with more time in the chair than he had. Dani had been here eight months. Bao had been here since the office opened. Nick had figured he'd land in the middle somewhere, good enough to keep the job, unremarkable enough to be left alone.

Instead the images kept arriving with reflected light from nowhere, wrists twisted past function, shadows pointing the wrong way.

Fourteen years of looking at paintings, at light on surfaces, at wrists bent under load and shadows crossing faces had trained the response too deep to shut off. He'd thought he'd left that in the apartment with the stacked canvases and the turpentine smell. He hadn't. When an image was wrong, the wrongness landed before thought did.

The audit note was its own kind of problem, because competence attracted attention, and attention was the opposite of what an infiltrator needed.

But attention also moved him closer to the machinery. At least now he knew one thing that rolled uphill. And the machinery was what he'd come here for.

He closed the audit and reopened the batch.

The Slack problem announced itself at 9:07 on a Wednesday in his second week.

Priya dropped the daily standup prompt into the San Leandro floor channel while people were still lowering themselves into chairs and unsealing breakfast bars.

STANDUP THREAD. POST BLOCKERS, BATCH QUESTIONS, AND COVERAGE NEEDS IN-THREAD.

Nick had never used Slack before Canopy. His professional communication history consisted of emails to gallery owners, occasional texts to Jess, and one ill-advised attempt to use Instagram DMs to pitch a commission to a restaurant in Piedmont. Slack was something else entirely, a cascading ecosystem of channels and threads and reaction emojis that seemed to operate by rules no one had written down.

The speech-bubble icon opened a narrow reply column off to the right of Priya's message. Nick clicked it, lost it again, found it, and then somehow closed the whole channel instead. By the time he got back the thread already had seven replies: Tommy asking for coverage after lunch, Bao flagging a corrupted landscape batch, someone on day shift requesting a monitor swap. Nick decided typing directly under Priya's message was close enough.

Morning circle question, he wrote. Batch 2-1189, image 14. The wrist rotation exceeds what the radius and ulna can physically do without dislocation, and the reflected highlight under the jaw has no environmental light source. Are we supposed to score both anatomical accuracy and lighting consistency when the same bad arm is causing the whole cascade?

He hit Enter.

The paragraph appeared in the main channel.

For one second the floor stayed at its usual volume: mice clicking, chair casters rolling, the HVAC pushing lukewarm air that smelled faintly of carpet glue. Then Tommy made a choking sound two rows over and turned in his chair far enough to look at Nick around his monitor.

"Morning circle?" Tommy said.

A few heads lifted. Bao did not turn, but the corner of his mouth moved. On-screen, Priya replied beneath Nick's message almost immediately.

Nick, please use the thread for standup items.

Then, before he could fix it, another reply dropped in from a user in San Francisco whose name was only initials and a little building icon.

Can you condense this to a rubric note? Thanks.

Nick stared at his own sentence. It had seemed precise when he wrote it. Now it sat in the channel like a dead animal someone had to remove. He clicked More Actions, found three options he did not understand, copied the whole paragraph, opened what he thought was the thread, and pasted it there too. Now there were two versions.

"You double-posted it," Tommy said.

"Shut up," Nick said, not loudly.

Priya stood at the end of the row with her mug in one hand and looked over at him through the blue light of the monitors. She did not look angry. That was worse. Angry would have been a clean color. This was administrative disappointment, beige and total.

"I'll move it," she said. "Just use the thread next time."

By lunch the message had been moved, which meant the channel now held both his original mistake and Priya's small public act of housekeeping underneath it.

"You can't call standup the morning circle in the main channel and paste a whole anatomy deposition under Priya's message," Dani said.

It was late February, the same Wednesday. They were in the break room, which was really just a corner of the floor sectioned off by a partition, with a microwave, a coffee maker that produced a liquid that bore the same relationship to coffee that AI-generated art bore to painting, and a window overlooking the parking lot's strip of landscaping: three sickly junipers and a decorative boulder. Dani had her lunch open, a sandwich wrapped in foil that she ate with one hand while scrolling her phone with the other.

"Deposition," Nick said. "It was a question."

"It was a paragraph with bones in it. Nobody wants bones in Slack."

"It did have bones in it. That was the problem."

Dani gave him a look over the top of her phone. "The problem was that you posted it to eighty people when Priya asked for a thread."

"Sixty-three."

"Eighty if San Francisco's in the channel, which they are, because one of them told you to condense it."

Nick pulled the tab on a can of sparkling water he'd brought from home because the vending machine Coke cost a dollar seventy-five and he was trying to keep his daily spending under eight dollars. "What's the right amount to write, then."

She swallowed and thought about it. "'Hand anatomy incorrect. Wrist rotation non-functional.' Then if you need a second note, 'Lighting source unclear under jaw.' Two short lines. Done."

"That doesn't explain the cascade."

"It doesn't need to." She folded the foil back over the sandwich and pressed it flat with her thumb. "And don't call it the morning circle again."

"I know what a standup is. The word still doesn't make sense. Everyone sits."

"That part doesn't matter." She set the sandwich down. "Morning circle makes it sound like you think this is daycare."

Nick drank his water. It was flat. The can had been in his bag since morning, rattling against his laptop during the BART ride from Fruitvale to San Leandro. He should have bought it cold from the vending machine, but a dollar seventy-five was a dollar seventy-five.

"And the thread thing," Dani said.

"I know where the thread is."

"You say that every time and then I watch you type in the main channel anyway."

"I forget when the window shifts around."

"Then slow down long enough to find it." Her tone stayed level. "If you keep doing that, Priya has to clean up after you in public. She can't not do it, because San Francisco watches the floor channel when they're bored."

Nick picked at the edge of the can. "Tommy's never going to stop talking about morning circle."

"No," Dani said. "He is not."

The corner of her mouth moved, the smallest concession to amusement. Then her phone buzzed. She glanced at the screen, and whatever she saw changed her face by degrees rather than all at once. Her shoulders tightened. She set the phone face-down on the table too quickly.

Nick looked away before he made her catch him looking. "I'll use the thread."

"Good."

She said it in the same tone she used for work that had been corrected and could now move on. Dani was like that. She helped him without being friendly about it, or she was friendly without it costing her anything, and Nick couldn't tell which. She wore her competence casually, without fuss. A task arrived; she handled it and moved on. She never overshared, never asked him personal questions, never lingered in conversations past their natural end. When they talked, it was about work, and when work was done, she went back to her station.

But she kept helping him, which he hadn't asked for, and he noticed things about her that had nothing to do with help. The way she picked at the paint residue under her nails when she was thinking, a gesture so habitual she probably didn't know she did it. Luna's drawings sticking out of the tote bag she kept under her desk, a new one every few days, trees with faces and houses that floated in pink skies and people with too many fingers. The way she assessed an image before she scored it, tilting her head slightly to the left, a habit he'd seen in other artists and never in anyone who hadn't spent time with a brush or a pen.

Nick looked at his sparkling water. There was a smear of cadmium yellow under his thumbnail from a rag he'd handled that morning, one he'd used to clean up months ago and still left by the kitchen sink. The color was drying and cracked, like a paint chip peeling off old siding. He scraped it off with the edge of the can.

Tommy did in fact keep talking about morning circle. Not for three days, like Nick later told himself. For the rest of the day was enough. At two-thirteen Tommy dropped a message in the correct thread that read no blockers unless morning circle runs long. At four he walked past Nick's desk and asked if there would be finger paints at tomorrow's standup. Dani kept her face neutral. Bao said nothing. Priya, mercifully, pretended not to hear any of it.

Two days later, he learned what Dani's phone call had been about.

He was coming down the stairwell at the end of the floor. Nobody used it because it led to a fire exit and a section of the parking lot that smelled like the Chinese restaurant's grease trap next door. He heard her voice before he saw her. She was on the landing between floors, her back against the wall, her phone pressed to her ear.

Her voice was tight and controlled. Every word came out measured. He caught the middle of a sentence.

"— I haven't missed a day. I've been here every day since October. You can tell them that. Tell them exactly that."

Silence while she listened.

"That's what 'stable employment' means, Brian. That's what the filing says. Stable. I'm stable."

More silence. Her free hand was flat against the wall, pressing hard enough that the tendons in her wrist stood out.

"I know what she said. I know. But Lu drew a picture of our — she drew our apartment. She drew it and she put me in it. She put me at the table. I'm not — can you just tell them that? Can you just tell the judge that?"

Nick backed up two steps, quietly, and went the long way around to the bathroom. He stood at the sink and washed his hands for no reason. The soap smelled like artificial lavender. The fluorescents buzzed above the mirror and cast the kind of flat, greenish light that made everyone look slightly ill. It was a color temperature he'd never have chosen for anything, the visual equivalent of the break room coffee.

He'd known Dani had a daughter. He'd seen the drawings in the tote bag, asked about them once, and Dani had said "My daughter, Luna, she's seven" in a tone that was proud and also closed, like a door you were allowed to see but not walk through.

He hadn't known about the lawyer or the judge or how hard she leaned on the word stable, giving it the weight of evidence. He pictured her at her station, every message in the right thread, every shift accounted for, and understood more than he wanted to.

Nick went back to his station and opened his next batch. A landscape with a sky that couldn't decide whether it was sunrise or sunset; the color temperature of the clouds said morning, but the shadows fell east, which meant the light source was in the west. He rated it a 2. He used the thread.

---

Priya's office was not really an office. It was a glass-walled room at the end of the evaluation floor that had probably been designed as a small conference room: seating for four, a whiteboard, a table with a fake wood-grain surface that was peeling at one corner. Priya had made it hers in a way that was both thorough and slightly apologetic, as if she knew the room was too nice for the business park and wanted to compensate by underfurnishing it. A desk, a monitor, a photo of what Nick assumed were her parents, and a Costco-sized bottle of Tums that sat next to her keyboard like a second monitor.

It was the second week of March. Nick had been at Canopy for a month and a half. The rain outside had thinned to something that wasn't quite drizzle and wasn't quite mist, a Bay Area in-between that made the parking lot look like a photograph taken through dirty glass. The three junipers were still sickly.

"Close the door," Priya said. "Sit."

Nick sat. The chair was one of those ergonomic mesh things that tech companies bought in bulk and that always made him feel like he was being gently cradled by a very reasonable spider.

Priya was looking at her monitor, scrolling through something. She wore the same kind of business-casual outfit she wore every day: a dark cardigan over a blouse, earrings small enough to be professional but present enough to be human. Her hair had streaks of gray she didn't touch up, and she wore it pulled back in a way that looked efficient rather than styled. The skin under her eyes was darker than it should have been. She'd been at the office before him that morning and she'd still be here when he left.

"So," she said. "Your numbers are — do you know what your numbers look like?"

"I've seen the dashboard."

"Okay. So you know." She turned the monitor toward him. The graph plotted his accuracy scores against the floor average and the expert-reviewer consensus. His line tracked the expert line so closely they overlapped in places, while the floor average ran below both, separated by a gap that widened over the last two weeks. "Your anatomical accuracy is the highest in this office. Your lighting consistency is second-highest, and the person above you has been here a year and a half. Your overall quality score is ninety-three point four. Floor average is seventy-eight point two."

"Okay," Nick said.

"That's not a normal response to those numbers."

"What's the normal response?"

Priya leaned back. She picked up the Tums bottle, turned it in her hands absently, set it down. A habitual gesture, Nick thought, like Dani's nail-picking. Something the hands did while the brain worked on something else.

"The normal response is 'great, what does that mean for me.' So I'll just tell you what it means for you. I'm recommending you for senior evaluator."

Nick looked at the graph on her monitor. The gap was wide enough to explain why he'd been called in. Senior evaluator meant the priority queue he'd been locked out of since February: tighter batches, extra rubric fields, fewer hands between his score and whatever the model learned from it. It also meant the yellow audit banner would stop being occasional and become routine.

"Senior evaluators work on priority batches," Priya said. "Higher-complexity images, tighter turnaround, more detailed rubric fields. The pay bump is modest — about two dollars an hour — but your evaluations carry more weight in the aggregate scoring. And if one of those batches goes sideways, I hear about it the same day."

He heard the approval under the job description. Priya wasn't padding a modest pay bump. She meant the recommendation.

He hadn't come here to do the job honestly, but he had done it honestly. That was the part he hadn't planned for.

"I'll take it," he said.

"Good." Priya turned the monitor back. "I think this is the right move. For your development here." She paused before "development." Corporate words never sat naturally in her mouth. "I should tell you, the senior evaluator role also means your scores will be audited more frequently. Weekly reviews instead of biweekly. More eyes on your work."

"That's fine."

"And your evaluation notes need to fit the rubric fields. I know Dani's talked to you about this."

Nick almost smiled. "She has."

"She's good about that. Dani's good about a lot of things." Priya reached for the Tums bottle again, unscrewed the cap, shook two tablets into her palm, and chewed them with the mechanical efficiency of someone who'd done this many times today. She swallowed and put the bottle back without looking at it. "One more thing. Your social integration."

"My what."

"You called the standup a morning circle."

"I've been told."

"And the Slack thing."

"I'm using the threads now."

"Good. Look, the technical side isn't in question. Eleven months I've been on this floor and I haven't seen quality scores like yours. But the documentation part, the notes, the threading, the format that San Francisco actually reads when they pull reports, that's where I need you to catch up." She straightened a pen on her desk that didn't need straightening. "Because if I'm spending my morning moving your messages instead of reviewing batches, that's a problem I can't keep absorbing."

Nick sat with that. Outside the glass wall of Priya's office, the floor hummed with the ambient noise of sixty-three people rating images on monitors. He could see Bao at his station, leaning close to his screen, reading glasses catching the fluorescent light, moving with the careful deliberateness that characterized everything Bao did. He could see Tommy two rows over, slouched low in his chair, clicking through a batch with the speed that Priya was always trying to temper, his foot bouncing against his desk leg. Dani's station was empty. She was probably in the stairwell again, or the bathroom, or wherever she went when her phone buzzed with a notification she didn't want to read at her desk.

"I understand," Nick said.

Priya nodded. She looked tired. Not dramatically tired, not visibly falling apart, but permanently tired: gray under her eyes, tension in her shoulders she carried without adjusting for, the slight forward lean of someone always reaching for the next problem. She'd been running this floor for eleven months. She'd mentioned once, maybe during training, that she'd taught high school art for twelve years. Why she'd left, Nick didn't know. What he had in front of him was a glass-walled room in a San Leandro business park, a peeling fake-wood table, a Costco bottle of Tums, and Priya managing the quality of AI-generated images for a company whose CEO talked about "the irreplaceable human layer" from a nicer office in San Francisco.

"Start on priority batches Monday," she said. "I'll update your access credentials over the weekend. Congratulations, Nick."

On Monday morning the portal had a new tab.

PRIORITY.

He saw it before he took off his jacket. Priya had updated his access credentials sometime over the weekend, and the interface had rewarded the event with the visual drama of a software patch: one more tab at the top of the screen, one red banner, three extra fields under the image window. SENIOR EVALUATORS SUBJECT TO WEEKLY CALIBRATION REVIEW. The room on the other side of the locked door turned out to be another beige room with better metadata.

The form had changed shape, too. Under the score sliders sat new boxes for anomaly type, severity, and reviewer confidence, each with its own dropdown menu full of language that wanted certainty arranged into cleaner stacks than certainty ever came in. The old screen had asked for scores and a note. This one wanted the failure named, sorted, and fed back upstream in smaller pieces.

Across the aisle Dani glanced over at his monitor while she slid her tote under the desk. "You in priority now?"

"Looks like it."

"Then keep the notes short."

"You really believe in me."

"I believe in Priya not having to move your messages for you." She logged in, phone face-down by her keyboard. One of Luna's drawings stuck out of the tote, a horse this time, blue marker mane, legs going in four directions. "Congrats, though."

She said the last part without looking at him. That made it sound more real.

Nick clicked the new tab. The first image loaded: a family at a kitchen table, all four of them smiling with the fixed, mildly feverish happiness the models loved. The little boy had six fingers on his left hand.

Two dollars more an hour meant eighty a week before taxes. Roughly sixty-three after. Two hundred and fifty-two dollars a month. Enough to stop eating rice three nights a week and maybe get the Civic's check-engine light looked at, which had been on since November and had joined the list of things he couldn't afford to know more about.

It also meant a score like this would not disappear into floor noise. Priority batches ran through fewer hands. If enough senior scores started drifting, Priya would get the email first. The floor dashboard would put names next to the damage.

Nick moved anatomical accuracy to 2. In the note field he wrote: Hand anatomy incorrect. Extra digit on left hand.

He checked that the cursor was in the right box this time, then hit submit.

Priority gave him more weight and better metadata. It still didn't show him where the batches were assembled before they reached his screen.
