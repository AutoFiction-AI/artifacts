# Chapter 15

Jet flew the Bebop lower than he needed to, low enough that the ship's shadow kept finding the broken ground and sliding over it like spilled oil. South of Tharsis the settlements thinned fast. Towers got shorter. Roads stopped pretending to go somewhere. Old terraforming hardware sat out in the open where everyone could see how much money had died there. Mirror fields lay in cracked silver strips across the flats. Pump stations leaned into the dust with their intake mouths packed solid. A canal cut straight through the plain for thirty kilometers and then simply ended, as if the planet had gotten tired of being argued with.

The cockpit glass held the whole wrong-colored afternoon. Mars wasn't red from up close. It was rust, brick, old blood, oxidized machinery, dust blown thin over black stone. The light turned everything into some variation of worn metal. Jet kept one hand on the controls and one hand near the trim, feeling the age of the ship through the small corrections it wanted from him. The Bebop had a loose sympathy in the yoke on long flights. It never flew exactly straight unless you kept reminding it.

Behind him, Ed had written their route directly onto the nav glass in yellow grease pencil and drawn a tiny crown over Dry Well, because apparently every destination needed a hat. One bare foot hooked over the arm of the chair. The toes flexed in time with a tune only Ed could hear. The goggles sat up on Ed's forehead, held there by habit and static, and a measuring spoon from Jet's galley had somehow ended up tucked through the hair like an ornament.

"Dead greenhouse at two o'clock," Ed said. "No, correction, dead greenhouse cluster. Big crunchy salad bowl. Nobody watered the tomato kingdom."

Jet glanced left. Ribbed domes hunched out of the flats, half collapsed, their translucent skins torn open and packed with red drifts. A service road ran between them, visible only because the dust lay slightly shallower there.

"I see it."

"Do you think there are ghost tomatoes?" Ed asked.

From the common room, Faye said, "If there are, I hope they die again before dinner."

She was stretched on the couch with one arm over her eyes and a magazine open on her stomach, not turning pages. She had put on one of Jet's old sweatshirts over her usual clothes because the heating kept shifting between weak and nonexistent. It swallowed her wrists. She would rather have died than admit it was comfortable. Ein had claimed the warm stripe under the avionics rack and slept on his side with one hind leg twitching in short offended jerks, as if even in dreams somebody was breaking a rule.

Spike stood in the hatch between cockpit and common room, one shoulder against the frame, looking as if he'd just wandered there by accident and might wander away again. He had been doing that for an hour. The ship was too small for real avoidance on a flight like this. You could leave a conversation, but you couldn't leave the fact of other people breathing.

"How much longer?" Faye asked.

Ed peered at the display upside down. "Two hours thirteen minutes if the headwind stops being dramatic. Two hours thirty if Mars remains committed to the bit."

"Mars is always committed to the bit," Jet said.

Faye lowered her arm enough to squint toward the front windows. "All this for one settlement."

"Dry Well isn't much of a settlement anymore."

"Good. I was worried we'd miss the nightlife."

Jet let that sit. The truth was he preferred the empty part of Mars. Cities gave you too many stories all at once. Out here the damage was older and cleaner. You could see the exact shape of what had failed. A weather tower stood by itself on a rise, blades gone, only the central mast turning slow and pointless in the wind. Farther south, a line of black irrigation pipe surfaced from the dirt, ran fifty meters, and vanished back under, like something trying to crawl away.

No place on Mars looked empty if you knew what to look for. Failure had architecture. It left footings. It left service roads. It left paperwork in cabinets nobody cleared out. He thought of precinct rooms and informant files and a name he had never known while the man carrying it was still alive. He kept his eyes on the plain and eased the nose a little lower.

Ed hummed, stopped humming, and tapped the screen with a dirty thumbnail. "Basin lip in nineteen minutes. Then fun geology. Then more geology. Then old syndicate nonsense. Then Dry Well. Ed made color codes."

"I noticed," Jet said.

"Yellow means suspicious dirt. Blue means historical bad decisions."

"What's red mean?" Faye asked.

Ed looked delighted that she'd asked. "Red means Mars."

Faye made a small noise that could have been a laugh if she'd meant it more.

Spike pushed off the hatch and came forward at last. He took the copilot seat without asking, slouched in it, and looked out through the glass as if the planet had mildly inconvenienced him. "You're coming in low."

"Yeah."

"Wind'll throw you around along the basin wall."

"Probably."

Spike glanced at the display Ed had painted with lines and symbols and the tiny crooked crown. "West side's cleaner once you hit the cut. Less old hardware to scrape the hull on."

Jet nodded. That was the kind of thing Spike could say without either of them having to touch the rest. Practical. Observable. A route, not a reckoning.

He took it anyway. "You remember the approaches."

Spike kept watching the ground. "Some of them."

Faye turned a page she hadn't read. The paper crackled too loud in the ship. Nobody said anything after that for a while. The Bebop moved south over abandoned pump stations, over the broken circles of survey camps, over dry riverbeds the terraformers had named before they had water for them. The engines held a steady note under everything. The hull ticked once as temperature shifted.

Ed leaned forward so far the chair squealed. "Oh. Oh, look at that. Mirror field. Huge one. Wow."

The mirrors had once tracked sunlight into a central receiver tower. Now half of them were shattered and the other half pointed in random directions, flashing dumb bright pieces of afternoon into the cockpit.

"That project was called Helios Twelve," Ed said. "Ed found the file. It promised seasonal cloud generation and local agriculture expansion and a thirty-one percent increase in regional morale."

"How'd that work out?" Faye asked.

Ed watched the dead tower roll under them. "Regional morale remains unverified."

This time Faye did laugh, once, against her will. Jet heard it and didn't look back. On a ship this size you learned the value of not turning toward every soft sound. Some things only happened if you let them think nobody had noticed.

They crossed the basin lip just as the light began to flatten. The ground broke open beneath them in long oxide canyons and shelves of exposed black stone. Old freight tracks clung to ridgelines and then vanished where slides had taken them. Dust moved through the deeper cuts in pale streamers. Far off, the ruins of a relay station stood on three legs instead of four, the whole structure listing like a drunk trying to stay dignified.

Jet felt the ship answer the terrain. The air changed. The wind came harder off the stone. He worked the controls, small movements, steady pressure. Nobody had gone back to a room. Faye stayed on the couch. Spike stayed in the right seat. Ed kept up a stream of route updates and nonsense and occasional facts too sharp to count as nonsense.

"Old roadbed below," Ed said. "That one connected a clinic run, three freight depots, and a fuel cache that exploded in '66. Also, a man filed forty-seven maintenance complaints about the road surface before he quit. Ed respects him."

"You would," Jet said.

Ed rested chin on the back of the chair. "Adults only write that many complaints when they think somebody's supposed to care."

The line sat in the cockpit a second longer than the others had. Jet adjusted trim he didn't need to adjust. Out the glass, the basin went on opening, one scar after another, heading toward the south where Dry Well waited in a place the maps treated like an afterthought.

No one answered Ed. No one needed to. The ship kept going.

* * *

By the time the sun had dropped low enough to turn the cockpit windows copper, the Bebop felt smaller than it had that morning. Faye noticed because she had been waiting for somebody to finally vanish into a room and stay there. Nobody had. Even Spike's exits had been half-hearted. He went to check something, or pretended to, and came back with the same face he wore when he didn't want to explain himself.

She came out of the head toweling her hair with one of Jet's least disgusting rags and found Jet and Spike at the common table. Ed had pulled an old topographic survey over the main screen and layered it with ghost routes in three colors. Dry Well sat near the bottom edge, a blinking square under Ed's ridiculous crown. Jet braced one hand on the table and the black fingers of his prosthetic clicked once against the metal rim. Spike sat opposite him with his chair tipped back on two legs, looking bored in the exact way he looked when he was paying complete attention.

Faye almost kept walking. Then she didn't. She dropped onto the couch with the towel around her shoulders and let the shape of the room do the listening for her.

Jet touched one of the old lines on the screen. "This feeder route. The clinic supply ran off this one?"

Spike leaned forward. The chair legs came down. "Sometimes. Depends what year."

"Try me."

"Fuel, machine parts, ammo, food if somebody up the chain got sentimental. Medical crates when the settlements got loud enough."

"And you knew that."

Spike's eyes stayed on the screen. "I knew crates got marked priority."

"That's not what I asked."

Faye looked from one to the other. Jet's voice was level, which was new and somehow worse than when he yelled. Yelling burned off heat. This just sat there.

Spike rubbed his thumb along the edge of the table. "Yeah. Sometimes I knew medicine was in the mix."

Jet waited.

"I didn't know whose," Spike said. "I knew weight. Timing. Handoff codes. Fuel. You miss a stop, somebody on the line starts screaming over comms. That was the part I dealt with."

Jet's jaw shifted once. "And that was enough for you."

"It was the job."

"Yeah."

There was no snap in it, no cheap victory. Jet looked tired in a way that had nothing to do with sleep. Faye knew that look better than she liked. It was the face people made when the explanation they finally got turned out to be exactly as bad as they had guessed.

Ed, upside down now from the ladder to the upper bunks, said, "Route Four had radiation spikes in '65. Also mold. Nobody liked Route Four except mold."

Nobody responded. Ed did not seem offended.

Jet dragged two fingers across the screen until a second line lit up, thinner, official. "My side looked cleaner on paper. Informant payments. Handler initials. Transfer requests. All trimmed down so nobody had to think about the body attached to any of it."

Spike said nothing.

Jet looked at him at last. "I let my partner keep that side of the wall because it was easier. I told myself that's how the precinct worked. Need to know, chain of command, all that bullshit. He had names, I had cases, and if I didn't push too hard then I got to keep pretending I was doing the decent part of the job."

Spike leaned back again, not far. "You were doing your job."

Jet barked a humorless laugh. "That's your defense?"

"No." Spike's voice flattened. "It's what I told myself too."

The room went quiet enough that Faye could hear the little rattle in the vent behind the galley shelf. Jet's prosthetic hand stayed on the table. The other one curled around nothing.

"When the chip came out," Jet said, "I wanted a clean line. I wanted my partner on one end and you on the other and me somewhere off to the side where I could say I wasn't part of it except by bad luck."

Spike's mouth twitched, not quite a smile. "Find one?"

"No."

"Yeah."

Jet looked back at the map. The old routes crossed the basin like scars from different knives. "I wasn't asking for absolution."

"Good."

Jet's head lifted. "Don't start."

"I'm not." Spike spoke without force, almost without inflection. "There isn't any. Not for me, not for you. I kept my eyes on what made the line move. You kept yours on what made the paperwork hold together. Same trick."

Faye felt the room shift on that. Not because it was elegant. It wasn't. It was clumsy and ugly and too plain. That was why it worked.

Jet exhaled through his nose. "You ever wonder who was waiting on those deliveries?"

Spike took a second with it. "Not enough."

Jet nodded once. He had probably wanted that answer for a while. Faye couldn't tell if getting it helped.

Spike went on, quieter. "If a clinic got missed, I knew because somebody bitched about rescheduling. If a courier disappeared, I knew because another one got assigned. I thought that was me keeping out of the rest."

"It wasn't."

"No."

Ed swung gently from the ladder and said, "Everybody kept the bad data in separate folders. Folders touched anyway."

Faye turned her head. "Could you not."

Ed considered that. "Nope."

Jet's mouth pulled to one side despite himself. It vanished fast. He pushed off the table. "I'm heating something."

Spike stood when Jet did, as if some agreement had ended. "Wake me if the approach changes."

"It always changes."

"Then wake me."

He headed for the hall. Faye said, "Elegant contribution."

Spike lifted one hand without turning around and kept going.

Jet took a packet from the galley locker, frowned at it, put it back, took another. He had the look of a man furious at dry goods for not solving a problem. Faye stayed where she was a beat longer, then got up and came over because there was no point pretending she'd missed all of that.

Jet filled a pan with recycled water and set it on the burner. "If you're going to say I went easy on him, save it."

"Wasn't going to."

"You were thinking it."

"I think a lot of things."

"That's new."

She leaned a hip against the counter. "See, this is why nobody tells you anything nice."

Jet snorted. The burner clicked, caught, and filled the galley with a faint hot-metal smell before the broth powder hit the water. He started cutting preserved greens into smaller pieces that absolutely did not need to be smaller. His left hand pinned the board. His right worked the knife. The movements were too precise to be casual.

Faye watched him a moment. Then she asked, "What was it like?"

Jet didn't look up. "What was what like?"

"Being a cop."

The knife stopped. "You've asked me a lot of things. That's a new one."

"I know."

"Why."

Faye looked past him at the porthole over the sink. Mars moved there in a slow red band, all stone and shadow. "That picture on the chip. Your precinct in the back. Him with the ISSP shoulders." She shrugged. "I keep trying to picture what a regular day looked like around all that. That's all."

Jet went back to the greens. "Regular day."

"Yeah. Not the corruption, not the dramatic downfall, not the part where you drink in bars and stare into the middle distance. Just the job."

"I don't stare into the middle distance."

"You absolutely do."

"Fine." He scraped the greens into the pan. "Most days it was reports. Coffee that tasted like the machine hated you. Radios going off when you finally sat down. A lot of waiting on paperwork that should've cleared an hour ago. A lot of people lying badly."

Faye folded her arms. "That's every institution I've ever met."

"Pretty much." He opened a cabinet, frowned into it, came up with soy sauce. "The report room smelled like toner and wet coats in winter. Hallway always smelled like somebody's noodles because night shift kept eating at the desk. We had one vending machine that stole your coins unless you kicked it low on the right side."

She didn't realize until then that she had wanted details like that, dumb details. Something with no moral attached. Something a dead woman might have walked past on the way to use her stolen name.

Jet kept going, not sentimental, not bitter, just inventorying. "You'd get sent out to the same blocks over and over. Same domestic calls, same landlords, same guys who thought they were smarter than everyone because they changed the door code after every arrest. You knew which stairwells smelled like piss and which ones smelled like machine grease because somebody was running a shop out of their apartment. You knew who'd talk if you brought coffee and who'd only talk if you left."

"Did you like it?"

He gave that the respect of thinking before answering. "Parts of it. I liked knowing where I was supposed to be. I liked that if a kid went missing there were cars to put on it, forms to file, people to wake up. Most of it was slow and stupid, but sometimes being there mattered for ten minutes. Sometimes that was enough."

The broth began to simmer. Jet stirred it, then frowned at the powder packet as if it had insulted him personally.

"And the rest?" Faye asked.

"The rest was how easy it got to turn people into categories." He kept stirring. "Witness. Suspect. Informant. Transient. Body in an alley. You put the right words on the page and the page starts feeling more real than the person. That's the bad part. That's the part that sticks."

Faye looked down at the counter seam under her hands. "Yeah."

Jet slid two bowls out and set them down harder than necessary. "Why are you asking me this now."

She shrugged once. It felt inadequate. "Because I spent half a day in a records office looking at my own name attached to somebody else's prescriptions and signatures and clinic visits. And I still can't get a grip on her because records don't breathe." She glanced at him. "I figured maybe a precinct does."

Jet's face changed almost imperceptibly. Not softer. Just less defended. "You never told me what that place was like."

Faye laughed without humor. "A records office? Sexy stuff."

"Faye."

She hated that tone because it worked. She picked at a chip in the counter laminate with one nail. "There was a clerk. Mid-forties, shirt buttoned wrong, coffee stain old enough to vote. Wedding ring worn flat on one side. He kept saying they were closing and I kept sitting there and then he turned the sign to CLOSED and left me with the terminal anyway."

Jet set the spoon down.

"The air unit made this awful knocking sound every twenty seconds," she said. "Like somebody trying to get out of a pipe. Screen kept timing out and asking if I was still there. I must've hit yes fifty times." She gave a small, ugly smile. "That's probably the most commitment I've shown anyone in years."

Jet huffed a laugh before he could stop it.

"He let you stay," he said.

"Yeah."

"That was decent."

"Weirdly, yes."

She could still see the room if she let herself, the flat blue light on the terminal, the nicotine yellow in the ceiling tiles, the way the clerk had made a point of not looking at her too hard on his way out. Not kindness exactly. Recognition, maybe. The ugly little courtesy of letting somebody come apart in private.

"I didn't cry," she said, and then, because that sounded like she thought it mattered, "Not that I was trying not to. I just sat there."

Jet ladled broth into the bowls. "Sometimes that's what there is."

Ed dropped from the ladder and landed barefoot on the deck with a slap. "Question for former cop Jet. Did cop Jet have a lunch box?"

Jet blinked. "What."

"A lunch box. Sandwich container. Soup cylinder. Sad pickle chamber."

Faye barked a laugh. Jet looked offended by the whole line of inquiry.

"I had a thermos," he said.

Ed gasped. "Metal or plastic?"

"Metal."

"Was there tea?"

"Coffee."

"Did it leak?"

Jet pointed a spoon at Ed. "Every damn day."

Ed nodded with grave satisfaction. "Excellent. Authentic."

Ein, awake now, trotted in and sat hopefully by Jet's knee. Jet tore a strip from the preserved greens packet and gave it to him without breaking rhythm. Faye took her bowl when Jet handed it over, burned her fingers, swore, and took it anyway. The broth was too salty and too thin. It helped.

Ed had already moved on to the screen. "Dry Well beacon just woke up."

Both adults looked over.

Ed tapped the blinking square. "Somebody brought one more heater online in the main bay. Tiny power draw, very consistent. Also an exterior transponder ping, very low strength, like a polite cough. So. Home stretch."

The room held still around that.

"She's there," Faye said.

"Very probably," Ed said. Then, with cheerful irrelevance or maybe not, "Also Ein stole Spike's sock."

Ein looked innocent in the aggressive way only a dog could.

Jet took his own bowl and leaned against the counter. "How long?"

"Forty-nine minutes if the wind keeps behaving. Fifty-six if Mars remembers it has opinions."

Faye ate another spoonful. It tasted like salt and metal and whatever passed for greens after too long in storage. Outside the porthole the last of the light dragged across the stone. Inside, nobody said anything grand. Jet drank broth. Ed hunted signal noise. Ein crunched stolen evidence under the table.

After a minute Faye put her bowl down and said, to no one in particular, "I'm going up."

Jet nodded once. He didn't ask where. On the Bebop that counted as courtesy.

* * *

Spike was already on the observation deck.

Of course he was. There were only so many places on the ship where a person could go to be alone without making a statement about it, and the observation deck was the best of them. Too cold, too exposed, not comfortable enough for lingering unless you meant it.

He stood with his hands in his pockets, looking out through the forward glass. Night had not fully come down yet. Mars below was a long basin of dark rust and black stone with strips of old infrastructure catching the last light. A relay mast flashed once and went dark. Far ahead, too far to make anything of, one square on the scope marked Dry Well.

He heard Faye before the door finished opening. Not loud. Just the particular annoyed economy of her footsteps when she was carrying a hot bowl and refusing to admit it.

"You hiding?" she asked.

"No."

"Looks like it."

"Then I'm doing it wrong."

She came to stand beside him, not close enough to touch. She had ditched the bowl somewhere on the way up and kept the sweatshirt. The collar sat crooked, exposing one shoulder. She didn't bother fixing it. The glass put a faint copper stripe across her face.

For a while they looked out without talking. The observation deck always gave the planet too much room. From here the canyons looked carved and deliberate, like somebody had taken a tool to the whole basin and then walked off mid-job. Spike watched the shadowed shelves roll under them and tried, not for the first time, to imagine Ayane. He never got farther than a pair of hands on a crate and a name attached after the fact. Julia came easier. Julia always came easier. That was part of the problem and maybe all of it.

Faye rubbed one thumb against the heel of her hand. "Ed says she's got the bay heated."

"Yeah."

"That's almost hospitable."

"Maybe she's cold."

Faye snorted. "Maybe."

Another stretch of silence. The ship hummed around them. Somewhere aft, a panel ticked as it cooled and reheated. Through the glass, a dried reservoir flashed past, its retaining wall split open so the basin floor below showed in pale, useless terraces.

Faye said, "That whole route system. All those little stops and drop points. Did you ever think about who was waiting there."

Spike watched the reservoir disappear behind them. "Not really."

She turned her head.

He let the first answer sit just long enough to be true and then not enough. "Back then I kept it to weight, timing, fuel, the names on the handoff. If I did that part right, I called it enough."

Faye waited.

Spike looked down into the dark run of canyon under the Bebop. "I didn't think about what happened after the cargo changed hands. I do now."

There it was. Small. Flat. Nothing like a confession was supposed to sound like. That was probably why it came out at all.

Faye's expression didn't soften. He would've distrusted that if it had. She just looked back out the glass and nodded once.

"Yeah," she said.

The word held more than agreement. He let it.

"She died in my name," Faye said after a moment. "That's still a hell of a thing to fit in my head."

"I know."

"No, you don't."

Spike glanced at her. She was not looking at him now. Her fingers had gone to her collarbone and stopped there, two knuckles pressing the fabric down like she was checking for something under it.

"No," he said. "Not that part."

That seemed to satisfy her, or at least not irritate her further. On the Bebop those were close cousins.

"Jet told me what the precinct smelled like," she said.

"That sounds like Jet."

"Wet coats. Bad coffee. Copier heat." She breathed out through her nose. "I wanted something ordinary. I got sick of records."

"Records are easier."

"For who?"

He thought about it. "Nobody, probably."

Faye gave him a look sideways, quick and sharp. "Look at you, saying almost-human things."

"Don't spread it around."

"I won't. Ruin your reputation."

The corner of his mouth moved. That was all. Under them the basin began to rise again toward a line of broken ridges. The scope square for Dry Well brightened by a fraction as distance closed. Somewhere behind them, Ed whooped at some private victory and then immediately started humming again.

Faye said, quieter, "I keep thinking if I'd remembered anything real, anything from before, maybe this whole thing would've landed different."

Spike leaned one shoulder against the frame. "Would it?"

"I don't know." She sounded annoyed by the question. "Maybe I'd hate her more. Maybe less. Maybe I'd know if the gesture was mine first."

"Does it matter?"

"To me? Today? Yeah, a little."

He let that stand. Then he said, because there was no cleaner way into it, "For what it's worth, I don't think any of this was waiting on your memory. Or mine. Or Jet's. It was already moving."

She looked at him again. "That's not you trying to make me feel better, is it?"

"No."

"Good."

The door slid open behind them. Ed poked in sideways, hair worse than usual, Spike's missing sock hanging from one hand like evidence in a trial only Ed understood.

"Observation deck people," Ed announced. "Dry Well has one ship, one active bay heater, two exterior lamps, and a structural groan that Ed does not enjoy. Also arrival in eleven minutes."

Ed peered from one adult face to the other and added, matter of fact, "Adults get extra weird right before landing."

"Thanks, Ed," Faye said.

"You're welcome." Ed considered the sock. "Ein says this belongs to the tall sulky one."

"That's me?"

"Today yes."

Ed left as abruptly as Ed had arrived. The door shut. Faye looked down at the sock in Spike's hand because apparently Ed had transferred it at some point during the announcement.

"You gonna wear that," she said, "or frame it."

"Haven't decided."

She laughed, softer this time. It faded without embarrassment.

From the cockpit Jet's voice came over the ship intercom. "Ten minutes. Strap in if you want to keep your teeth."

Faye pushed off the glass. "There's the charm."

Spike stayed one second longer, looking out at the weak square of light ahead. Dry Well was still too far to see with the naked eye, just signal and distance and the knowledge of someone waiting. He thought of Jet at the table, of Faye in the records office hitting yes over and over, of a courier he couldn't picture and an informant he had never known existed while he was alive. None of it arranged itself into anything useful. It just stayed there, weight with no good place to set it down.

He turned and went inside.

Nobody had gone to a room. Jet was at the controls. Faye dropped into the couch and braced a hand against the cushion. Ed had already strapped in upside down somehow, still humming. Ein circled twice under the table and settled. On the main screen, Dry Well resolved at last from a blinking square into a handful of buildings half buried in dust, one bay lit, one ship waiting beside it.

No one said anything brave. No one found a reason to leave. The Bebop lowered its nose toward the basin and kept going.
