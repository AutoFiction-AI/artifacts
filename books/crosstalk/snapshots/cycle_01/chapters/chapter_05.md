# Chapter 5

The Hellas basin opened under the Bebop like somebody had hit Mars with a bottle and left the glass where it fell.

Red canyons split the ground in long crooked seams. Shadow sat in them thick and blue-black. On the rims, settlements clung to the rock in little rusted clusters of tanks, sheds, antenna poles, and dock lights that blinked through the dust as if they were surprised to still have power. The sky above all of it had that thin bad color Mars did at the end of the day, peach where the light caught and sick brass where it did not.

Jet hated chasing anything down here in a ship this size.

He sat forward in the pilot's chair with his good hand on the throttle and his metal one wrapped around the yoke, feeling every complaint the Bebop made through the frame. The trawler was built to endure, not to dance. She could take damage and keep flying. She could haul mass. She could drift for days. What she could not do was snake through smugglers' cuts in the southern basin after a custom ship that weighed maybe a quarter of what she did and moved like it had no furniture inside.

On the monitor over Ed's head the target ping slipped between canyon returns, reappeared, slipped again.

Ed had one foot on the console and the other up against the back of the chair, folded wrong in a way that made Jet's shoulder ache just looking at it. Ein stood with his front paws on the edge of the station, ears up, barking every time the ping jumped.

"Ed has the sneaky fish," Ed said. "Sneaky fish is going south-southish and then a little bite bite to the left. No, the other left. Mars left."

"There isn't a Mars left," Jet said.

"There is now."

Spike leaned in the bridge hatch with one shoulder against the frame, hands in his pockets like they were discussing dinner instead of dropping into canyon country with half a tank and no plan anyone had agreed on. He had his flight suit tied at the waist. The sleeves hung behind him.

"She's baiting us lower," he said.

Jet did not look around. "I know that."

"Doesn't sound like it."

"If you know so much, get in your ship."

"That's the first smart thing you've said in ten minutes."

Faye, strapped into the jump seat behind Ed's console, let out a short laugh that had no amusement in it. She had one boot up on the restraint bar and one hand on the zipper of her red jacket, already halfway out of it so she could get to the Redtail faster.

"Please launch him," she said. "If I have to listen to this for the whole chase, I'm opening the air lock."

Jet glanced at the fuel readout and felt his jaw go tight. Every dip and climb in the canyon wall cost them. Every hard correction cost them. The numbers kept stepping down in brisk little increments, cheerful as a cashier. He thought about the paid docking spot on Mars, the last woolongs already gone, Mama Yun's noodles eaten down to broth and memory. He thought about the ticket they could not afford before they had even gotten it. His hand tightened on the yoke until the metal joints in his left fingers clicked.

"We're not launching until we have a vector," he said.

Spike pushed off the hatch. "Then we're not launching."

"Jet-jet-jet," Ed sang. "Vector is a friend word. Vector is a shape word. Vector is also right there." Ed slapped a point on the screen with the flat of a hand. "There!"

The target ship broke from the clutter for half a second, a silver dart against the shadowed wall of the canyon, then cut hard between two stone teeth and vanished again.

Jet swore under his breath and rolled the Bebop after it. The whole ship answered half a beat late, a tired bulk shift that made the deck plates groan. Loose tools rattled somewhere below. Ein skidded, corrected, barked harder.

"That's it," Faye said, already out of the seat. "You can do your careful thing up here. I'm taking the Redtail."

"No."

"You don't get to say no."

"I just did."

She was moving before he finished. Spike was gone too, footsteps already hammering down the corridor toward the hangar. Jet cursed both of them now, out loud.

"Ed, keep the transponder on main screen. Full spread. I want terrain overlays and settlement traffic."

"Settlement traffic is full of boring workers and one singing truck and a medical courier that is in a hurry because of poop," Ed said.

Jet turned his head. "What?"

"Medical courier. Biological waste."

"Just put it up."

The bridge screen bloomed with colored lines and tags. The canyon narrowed ahead, then opened into a wider bowl where old mining access roads scarred the rock and a scatter of commercial signals drifted in and out of registration. The mysterious ship was cutting toward the bowl's western lip.

Jet hit the intercom. "Hangar, respond."

Spike's voice came back with engine noise under it. "Here."

Faye's cut right across his. "Obviously we're here."

"Listen to me for five seconds," Jet said. "Spike, take east wall high and get ahead of her. Faye, you hold west and don't cut low unless I say so. We box her into the bowl and force her up."

"That's too slow," Spike said.

"No, what's too slow is this ship you're trying to make pirouette," Faye said. "If I stay west, she just dumps into the undercut and I'm looking at rock."

"If you two would stop talking and fly-"

"I'm the only one up here flying a brick."

The comm cracked with the metallic thump of the Swordfish launch. On the starboard camera, Spike's mono-racer shot out of the Bebop's underside in a clean blue flare, dropped once, then knifed away along the canyon wall. A second later the Redtail punched free after it, round and red and faster off the line than either of them had a right to be in a situation like this.

Jet felt the old familiar mix of relief and irritation. The ship always got quieter when they left it. It always got more expensive too.

"You have your lanes," he said. "Stay in them."

"Sure," Faye said, in the tone of somebody already doing the opposite.

Ed hunched over the console. On the screen, the three friendly signals spread and the target signal darted ahead like a fish under dark water.

"Sneaky fish likes the zipper place," Ed said.

"The what."

"The zipper place. The canyons zip and unzip. See?" Ed made a fast opening motion with both hands. "There's a seam. If sneaky fish goes seam, Spike-person should go chomp and Faye-person should go whoosh and Bebop should go oom."

Jet looked at the map. There was, under the noise and Ed's nonsense, an actual route. A side cut split off the main canyon, ran blind behind a ridge, then met the bowl from the west. If the target used it and Faye stayed where Jet had put her, Faye might catch the exit.

"Faye, west seam, now."

"I'm already there."

"No, you're not."

"Jet, I can see the seam."

Spike came in flat over comms. "She's not at the seam."

"I said I'm taking the shortcut."

Jet felt the back of his neck go hot. "Don't take the shortcut. Faye, hold your lane."

She was laughing again, breathless this time with thrust and velocity. "You want her or you want a lecture?"

The Redtail's signal slashed off the screen into a lower cut Jet had not assigned. At the same moment the target disappeared into the same mesh of ravines.

"Damn it."

The next thirty seconds became pure noise.

Ed called out numbers, then colors, then numbers again, correcting himself so fast Jet had to choose which version to ignore. Spike said, "She's dropping," and then, "No, that's the wall," and then nothing at all because he was busy. Faye said she had visual, then said no she did not, then swore hard enough that Ein barked in offended sympathy.

Jet shoved the Bebop through the main channel and tried to keep the spread in his head. The canyon walls crowded the windows, red rock close enough that he could see old blast scoring from the mining years, black streaks where heat had licked stone, faded warning paint on one jut of concrete that had been part of something before the something rotted away.

Then a new signal burst onto the traffic band and a prox alarm went shrill.

"Unregistered pursuit craft, check your godsdamn vector!"

The face that popped onto the emergency screen belonged to a woman in a patched orange thermal cap with frost-bitten cheeks and a cigarette filter tucked behind one ear. Little green toy dinosaurs were taped across her dashboard in a line above the instruments. Behind her shoulder, through the ore hauler's dirty side glass, Jet could see thirty tons of raw hematite in a magnetic cradle and the mouth of the low cut Faye had chosen.

"Lady, move," Faye snapped.

"You move. I got weight under me, sweetheart."

Jet caught a flash of the Redtail on his lower camera, too fast, too close, red hull skimming past the blunt nose of the ore hauler with less than a meter between them. The hauler pilot let out a stream of abuse that clipped as her automatic collision system shoved her upward. The Bebop's own board lit with a traffic warning and an enforcement tag.

"Faye!"

"I missed her, didn't I?"

"That isn't the point."

"It sort of is."

The target signal jumped again, clean and infuriating, out of the mess Faye had made and into the wider bowl ahead. Jet shoved the hauler and Faye out of his mind because if he stayed with them the whole chase was gone. He rolled the Bebop hard enough that a storage latch banged open somewhere behind him and a pan clanged across the galley.

"Everyone listen," he said, too loud. "She's headed for the rim station. Spike, get above the fuel tower. Faye, back to west. Ed, if you say zipper again I swear to God-"

"Refueling place! Blinky tank village! Three old fuel bladders and one coffee machine with a broken milk arm!"

"How do you know that?"

"Public maintenance logs. Also the camera sees it."

The canyon spat them into open air. Ahead, welded to the cliff like an afterthought, hung a refueling stop: three spherical tanks banded in peeling white paint, a service platform on crooked stilts, two docking arms, and a web of pipes and maintenance catwalks clinging to the rock. Local craft hovered in approach lanes with their lights on. Wind dragged dust in long red sheets under the platform.

The target ship was arrowing straight for it.

Jet felt the shape of the thing before he named it. Crowd. Cover. More signals to hide in. More rules to break.

"Spike, now."

"I'm moving."

"Faye, west side."

"Yeah, yeah."

But he could hear it already in all three voices. The chase had gone past plan. The canyon had eaten the neat version. What they had now was appetite and noise and the other pilot still half a step ahead.

Jet drove the Bebop toward the station anyway, because the alternative was giving up and flying home broke.

* * *

By the time Spike cleared the Bebop and dropped fully into the Swordfish's narrow cockpit, the fight had simplified in the way good fights sometimes did. There was the ship in front of him, the walls to either side, and a clock measured in fuel and bad decisions.

Everything else could stay background for a minute.

He strapped in with one hand, thumbed the Swordfish hotter, and let the mono-racer fall into the canyon's throat. The controls answered him cleanly. That helped. Down here the Bebop sounded strained over the comm, all bulk and effort. The Swordfish only sounded alive.

The mysterious ship did not fly like a smuggler in a panic. That was the first thing.

It did not wobble. It did not overcorrect. It did not waste motion on anything showy. It flew like somebody who had been inside these cuts before and knew exactly how much room there was between wing and stone. Short bursts. Tiny corrections. No nerves visible from the back end of it. If Spike had not known better, he would have said the pilot had laid this route out in advance down to the meter marks.

Maybe she had.

Jet's voice hit his headset again. "East wall. Get above her."

"Working on it."

"If you hit the station approach, don't fire. I don't need Mars traffic pinning attempted manslaughter on top of whatever they already have."

Spike watched the ship ahead skim under an outcropping, disappear behind a conveyor skeleton, then flash back into view in the reflected light off the basin wall.

"That'd imply I was going to miss," he said.

Jet made a sound that might have been a curse or a laugh dragged backward. Hard to tell with static.

Faye cut in. "If anybody's firing, it's me."

"You almost clipped a hauler."

"Almost is free."

"Almost got logged."

"Everything gets logged."

Spike tuned them down a notch and let their argument flatten into noise. It sat better that way. He leaned the Swordfish toward the east wall and took a cut Jet's bigger ship could not use, knifing between two rusted survey pylons that still had faded terraforming company logos on them. The pylons flashed by close enough that the Swordfish's right fin picked up a scraping scream. He barely felt it.

Old infrastructure kept surfacing in the basin if you knew how to look. Stub tunnels. Dead service roads. Freight tags weathered half off rock faces. Before the syndicate routes had become syndicate routes, a lot of this had belonged to companies that thought they were going to turn Mars into a clean profitable miracle and then ran out of money halfway through. The syndicate had just learned how to use ruins better than accountants did.

The thought came and went. He did not let it sit.

Ahead, the target ship cut through the refueling station traffic pattern without asking anyone's permission. A hopper hauling drill heads lurched off course. A maintenance tug flashed hazard lights and spun slow in the wind wash. The station itself looked mean and underpaid, patched plates over old punctures, one docking arm painted a different color from the rest because somebody had replaced it from salvage. Over the service platform hung a sign that had once said FUEL and now mostly said UEL.

A local controller came over the public band, voice raw and furious. "Unknown racers, you are in a restricted commercial lane. Reduce speed or clear the station."

Spike ignored that. So did everybody else.

He came in above the platform, dipped the Swordfish's nose, and for three seconds the angle opened.

The other ship rolled under him, close. Not close on radar. Close in glass and metal.

He saw the cockpit.

Young woman. Dark hair yanked back. No helmet. One sleeve shoved to the elbow. Her hands were bare on the controls, knuckles nicked like somebody who used tools more than guns. She was looking straight through the turn, not at him, not hunting him with her eyes, because she did not need to. She had already done the math. The set of her mouth was wrong for fear. Not smiling, exactly. But there was a looseness there, something almost amused, as if the whole ugly noisy chase had confirmed a suspicion she had been carrying.

Then she glanced once, quick, and their canopies aligned.

Not panic. Not surprise. Recognition maybe, though that made no sense.

And then she was gone past him.

Spike pulled the Swordfish hard enough that the harness bit into his ribs. The mono-racer came around in a screaming arc over the station platform. Beneath him, he caught a flicker of people scattering: a fuel tech in an oversized brown coat dropping a wrench and running crooked because one boot sole had peeled loose; two loaders diving behind stacked canisters; a kid on a service ladder frozen for exactly one stupid second before some adult yanked him down by the back of the jacket. Somebody's coffee exploded across a railing.

He had the target now. Not target lock. Visual. Better.

"I see her," he said.

Jet came back instantly. "You see who?"

"Pilot."

Faye's voice sharpened. "Well?"

The other ship cut between the fuel bladders and the cliff face, too tight for anything but a small craft. Spike followed. The station's pipe maze whipped past his canopy in fast silver flashes. The ship ahead slipped under a service gantry, rolled once, and dropped toward a dark slit in the canyon wall that had not been on the public map.

Maintenance access. Old and narrow. Probably bored back when the station serviced mining crawlers instead of whatever scraps kept it alive now.

Spike lined up anyway.

That was when the chaff came.

The other ship kicked something out of its belly, not a missile, not a mine. A cluster of cheap canisters burst in the wake and turned the air into a glittering storm of metal trash. Shredded foil. Reflective insulation strips. Filings that lit under the Swordfish's sensors like fireworks. Sliced-up cargo tags. Little stamped maintenance tabs that hit the canopy like hard rain. His targeting screen went white, then red, then bloomed with three false returns stacked on top of each other so fast the system started arguing with itself.

"Shit."

The Swordfish shuddered as the debris hit. Static crawled the left side of his display. Something ticked sharp against the intake.

He could still see the tunnel opening ahead, a dark rectangle in the cliff with old hazard paint around the frame and half the letters of MAINTENANCE worn down to ghosts. He drove toward it on instinct and got within a breath of the mouth before scale caught up with him. The tunnel was there, and he was not fitting.

Not if he wanted to keep the wings.

He yanked back. The Swordfish screamed, nose up, engines biting at dirty air. The cliff wall rushed under him so close he could see old rock bolts sunk into the concrete lining, rust blooming around every one. A line of dead utility lamps whipped past beneath his belly.

The mysterious ship vanished into the dark.

Spike leveled out above the station with his heartbeat somewhere unhelpful and the smell of hot circuitry in the cockpit.

"Spike?" Jet said.

He looked at the ruined display, the tunnel mouth, the empty air where the other ship had been.

"Lost visual," he said.

"You what?"

"Tunnel."

Faye swore. Jet started talking at the same time, louder, but Spike barely heard either of them because his board was still sorting through false returns and one of them had already broken west, fast, deeper into the settlement approach grid.

Maybe her.

Maybe not.

He locked onto the cleanest echo and went after it because that was still better than hovering in place looking stupid.

* * *

On Ed's screen the chase turned into singing.

Not real singing. Not ears-singing. Numbers singing. Traffic pings bounced in one color, station beacons in another, commercial IDs in flat boring little notes, and the sneaky fish signal kept skipping through them all with a bright mean trilling line that Ed liked very much. It had personality. Then the chaff bloom hit and the song broke into a whole band of bad trumpets.

"Oho," Ed said.

Jet had one hand on the main console and one on the overhead brace now, standing because sitting was not mad enough for him. The Bebop had pulled clear of the station by a few hundred meters and was yawing slow while he tried to keep track of Swordfish, Redtail, local traffic, and the shape of the canyon routes beyond the rim.

"What is it?" he snapped.

"A mess!"

"Useful, Ed."

"Very useful. Messes are patterns with hats."

Ein barked twice at the monitor, front feet dancing.

Faye blew in over the comms. "I've got a signal westbound. Fast. Spike, are you on it?"

Spike answered a beat later. "Different angle."

"Whose angle?"

"Mine."

"That clears it up."

Ed leaned closer, face inches from the screen. The false returns were burning off now, dropping away one by one as the targeting systems sorted trash from metal from moving ships. Under that, something bothered him. Not bothered like danger. Bothered like a wrong note that insisted it belonged in the song.

One signal kept staying too neat.

The sneaky fish had been wiggly from the start. Real pilots were wiggly. Even very good ones. They overlaid with weather and rock shadow and little engine moods and tiny bursts of impatience. This one now had the right code and the right shape and none of the wrongness of a living hand on a stick. It went exactly where a signal should go if it wanted to be chased.

Ed grinned.

"Fake fish," Ed said.

Jet turned. "What?"

"Not ship fish. Pretend fish. Cargo fish in a wig."

"Ed."

"No, yes, listen-listen." Ed stabbed three spots on the display. "This one here is too tidy. It has no elbow. Sneaky fish always has an elbow. This one is a beacon riding piggyback. Maybe drone. Maybe box truck with a lie taped to it."

There was a pause on the comm long enough for wind static to fill it.

Then Faye said, "You could've opened with that."

"Ed just found it."

Jet hit the channel hard enough that it clicked. "Faye, visual confirm."

No answer for two seconds. Three.

When Faye came back, her voice had gone flat in the particular way it did when she was embarrassed and intended to make that somebody else's problem as fast as possible.

"It's a freight drone."

Jet shut his eyes once.

On the Redtail feed, which Ed pulled to the side screen without being asked, the drone waddled into view from behind a stack of ore bins. It was an ugly square-bodied commercial mover painted municipal yellow once and dust-color now, with two cargo claws tucked under it and a blinking transponder beacon hose-clamped to its dorsal frame. Somebody had run the wiring by hand. Yellow insulation tape flapped in the slipstream. The beacon was even braced with a strip of cut gasket rubber to keep it from rattling loose.

Mechanic work. Fast, practical mechanic work.

"Oh, that's nice," Ed said with genuine admiration.

Faye's ship swept past the drone and kicked it sideways in the wash. The little freight unit bobbed drunkenly and kept following its dumb programmed route, signal blazing proud as a lighthouse.

"How long?" Jet asked.

Ed checked automatically. "Twenty-one minutes, fourteen seconds since the split. If you want only round numbers, twenty minutes and some rude change."

Nobody said anything for a moment.

Then Spike, still farther out in the basin, said, "That tracks."

Jet looked like he might put his fist through the console. "No, it doesn't track. What it does is tell me we've been chasing a damn delivery cart through half a settlement while the real ship-"

The bridge speakers chimed with an incoming priority notification. Not emergency. Worse. Bureaucratic.

A red icon unfolded across the main screen: MARS CIVIL TRAFFIC AUTHORITY.

Ed made a delighted noise. "Mail!"

"Don't open it," Faye said.

"Ed is opening it."

The notice filled the screen in legal gray blocks and aggressively polite font.

MCA ENFORCEMENT ACTION: RECKLESS FLIGHT WITHIN COMMERCIAL SETTLEMENT CORRIDOR

ASSESSED PARTIES:
BEBOP REGISTRY MARS-TEMP-447A
SWORDFISH II AUXILIARY CRAFT
REDTAIL AUXILIARY CRAFT

VIOLATIONS INCLUDED UNAUTHORIZED LOW-ALTITUDE VECTORING, HAZARDOUS INTERFERENCE WITH COMMERCIAL HAULER 6H-88, FAILURE TO RESPOND TO TRAFFIC CONTROLLER INSTRUCTION, AND CREATION OF A DEBRIS HAZARD WITHIN FUEL SERVICE AIRSPACE.

TOTAL PENALTY ASSESSED: 8,000 WOOLONGS.

PAYMENT DUE: IMMEDIATE.

Late fees accrue at midnight local settlement time.

Attached below, in much smaller text, was a video still of the Bebop angled stupidly over the station like a guilty building.

Ein barked at it.

"Well," Ed said. "That is very official."

"Delete it," Faye said.

"That's not how fines work," Jet said.

"How would you know? You're not paying it either."

"If we don't pay it, they flag the registry."

"We barely have a registry."

Spike came back on the comm. "They got my angle wrong."

Jet stared at the notice like he could burn woolongs out of it by force. "Is that what you have to contribute."

"At least they're consistent."

"I'm going to kill both of you."

Ed scrolled farther down. "There's a customer feedback survey."

Faye made a sound somewhere between a cough and a laugh. "Send them a drawing."

Jet pointed at the console without looking away from the screen. "Plot a drift orbit. Engines cold as soon as the others dock. We're done burning fuel on this until I can see straight."

"Bebop's done zooming," Ed agreed.

But the grin stayed on Ed's face, because the trick was good. Cheap, mean, funny. Beacon on a freight drone. Chaff in the station lane. Tunnel too tight for the Swordfish. The sneaky fish pilot had put a beard on a robot and all the adults had gone after the beard with guns.

Ed liked adults best when they were competent. Adults were also very good when they were stupid in specific recognizable ways.

This had been the second kind.

* * *

By the time the Swordfish and the Redtail were back in the hangar, the Bebop had gone to cold drift above the basin. The engines were quiet. Without thrust underfoot the ship always felt a little too much like what it was, an old hull in space held together by fixes and habit.

Jet stood by the workbench with the summons printed out on thermal paper in his hand because he had not trusted the screen. The strip curled at the bottom like it wanted to roll itself into a cigarette. His thumb had left a dark grease mark over the word PENALTY.

The hangar smelled like hot metal, dust, and the bitter electrical stink of the chaff that had gotten into everything.

Spike came down the Swordfish ladder first, one hand sliding along the rail. Silver-gray metallic flecks clung to his shoulders and hair. He brushed some off, looked at the residue on his fingers, then wiped it on the leg of his suit. Faye climbed out of the Redtail on the far side with her jacket tied around her waist, flight undershirt stuck damp to her back, mascara smudged under one eye from rubbing at sweat with a dirty wrist. She hit the deck already talking.

"If anybody says one word about the shortcut, I'm not listening."

"Good," Jet said. "Because the shortcut isn't the only thing we're discussing."

"Really. I thought maybe the eight-thousand-woolong love note was enough."

Ed was upside down on top of a supply crate, the summons reflected in the lenses of the goggles pushed up on the forehead. Ein sat at the crate's base and watched everyone in turn like this was one of the better shows the ship had offered lately.

Jet slapped the paper down on the workbench. "You broke formation."

"You brought a fishing trawler into a canyon chase."

"It's a pursuit-capable interplanetary vessel."

Faye barked out a laugh. "No, it isn't."

"It is when I'm flying it."

"You weren't the problem," Spike said, which was maybe meant to help and had the opposite effect.

Jet swung toward him. "No? Tell me what the problem was."

Spike looked past him at the workbench, at the summons, at the little scatter of bright dust under the overhead lights. "We were."

"That's not an answer."

"It's the whole answer."

Faye crossed her arms. "Oh, great. Here comes the mysterious monk version of this."

"Nobody asked you to dive under a freight hauler."

"I didn't hit it."

"Again," Jet said, "not the point."

"It's a little the point."

"No, the point is you went off your lane because you thought you could grab the kill yourself."

Her face changed by a degree. Not much. Enough.

"First of all, there was no kill. Unless you suddenly forgot why we're down here. Second, if I had waited for your perfect little box maneuver, she'd have been on the next ridge before you finished saying 'hold position.'"

"You don't know that."

"I know what I saw."

"You saw the nose of a hauler."

"And I missed it."

Jet threw both hands up. "You keep saying that like I should hand you a prize."

Spike shut the Swordfish hatch behind him with one flat push. "Jet."

"What."

"Yelling isn't going to make the money come back."

"No, but maybe it'll stop the next disaster before it starts."

Faye leaned a hip against the Redtail strut and looked at Spike instead of Jet now, redirecting the heat the way she always did when the direct line got too true. "You don't get to do quiet and superior. You lost her."

"Yeah."

"That's it? That's your defense?"

"Not a defense."

"Then what is it."

He shrugged once. Tiny movement. "Fact."

For a second the hangar settled into the ship's little sounds. Cooling metal. A relay clicking somewhere in the wall. Ein's tags tapping the crate leg.

Then Ed said, cheerful as a knife, "Spike-person got sparkled."

Faye looked over and, despite herself, snorted. The chaff still dusted Spike's hairline like cheap glitter from a bad club. One shard clung stubbornly near his ear.

"You look ridiculous," she said.

"You nearly got flattened by industrial equipment."

"And yet I'm still the good-looking one."

Jet pinched the bridge of his nose with his flesh hand. "Can we do this after we talk about what actually happened."

"This is what happened," Faye said. "We chased a fake beacon into a fuel stop, almost got cited into debt prison, and now you want to make it about my lane discipline."

"Because your lane discipline sucked."

"Your coordination sucked."

"Your-"

"Jet," Spike said again, same volume, and this time Jet stopped because there was something different in it. Not sharper. Flatter.

Spike stepped to the bench, picked up the summons, glanced at it, set it back down.

"She wasn't running blind," he said.

Nobody answered immediately. The line sat there.

Jet frowned. "What."

"She knew where we were going to be before we did. Canyon cut. station traffic. tunnel exit. all of it."

Faye pushed off the strut. "Of course she knew where she was going. It's her route."

"More than that." Spike rubbed the glittering residue off his thumb. "The drone wasn't random. The chaff wasn't random. That tunnel wasn't just a hole she found. She planned the handoff."

Jet's eyes narrowed. "You think she set the drone before we even got there."

"Yeah."

"In the middle of nowhere."

"Not nowhere. Settlement lane. Freight traffic. She knew somebody'd ignore a drone if it had the right tag."

Ed raised one hand from the crate. "Correct. Boring signals are invisible. Ed has been saying this for years."

"You haven't," Jet said.

"Ed has thought it very loudly."

Faye dragged a hand back through her hair. A line of red dust came away across her palm. "Fine. She planned the beacon. That doesn't explain how she kept staying ahead when we had three ships on her."

Spike looked at her. "Because we did exactly what we always do."

"Which is?"

"You got impatient."

Faye opened her mouth.

"Jet tried to herd from the middle."

Jet's mouth closed instead.

"And I took the narrow cut because I thought I could end it fast."

He said it without drama. Like reading damage off a panel.

Ed pointed at him. "Pattern recognition!"

Faye stared at Spike for a second too long, then looked away first. "That's insulting."

"Doesn't make it wrong," Jet said before he could stop himself.

"Oh, fuck you."

"No, listen." He planted both hands on the bench, leaning into the words now because once Jet had a practical frame he could use it. "If that's true, if she can read our habits that fast, then this wasn't just good flying. This was a setup aimed right at how we work."

Faye laughed once, ugly and brief. "That's better. I love being profiled by a stranger."

"Not a stranger," Spike said.

That pulled the room still again.

Jet straightened. "You saw her."

Spike nodded.

"And?"

He looked toward the hangar door, not at any of them. "Young. Dark hair. No helmet. Steady hands."

Faye's voice lost some of its edge, not much. Enough to notice. "How young."

"Twenties maybe. Not a kid."

"Armed?"

"Didn't see."

"Scared?"

Spike rubbed at the glitter by his ear and flicked it away. "No."

Jet picked up the summons again, then put it down because he had nothing to do with his hands. Young woman. Dark hair. Not scared. The pilot in his head had been a category until now. Smuggler. bait setter. thief. Old syndicate remnant. Disgraced ex-cop with a grudge. Something with a face should have made it easier. It did not. It made the whole stupid chase feel more deliberate.

"What else," he said.

Spike took a second. "She looked..."

Faye waited. "What."

"Like she was proving something."

"To us?"

"Maybe."

"That's not helpful."

"Didn't say it was."

Jet looked at the thermal paper again, then at the ships in the hangar, then at Ed perched upside down and delighted, and finally back at Spike.

"If she wanted us lost," he said slowly, thinking it through as he said it, "really lost, she could've killed the signal after the tunnel."

"Yeah," Spike said.

"Instead she leaves the beacon running on a freight drone."

"Yeah."

"Why."

Spike gave the smallest shrug. "To make us burn time. To see what we'd do. To be funny. Pick one."

Ed kicked both feet lightly in the air. "It was funny."

Jet pointed at Ed without turning. "Not helping."

"Helping emotionally."

Faye stared past them at nothing for a moment, thumb grazing her collarbone once before she caught herself and dropped the hand. "So what, she's playing."

"Not exactly," Spike said.

"Then what exactly."

He finally looked at her, then at Jet. "She's not trying to disappear. She's running us."

No one said anything after that because there was not much to add that did not sound smaller than the sentence already hanging there.

The hangar lights hummed. The metallic dust on the deck threw back little dead sparks.

Jet felt his anger shift shape. It did not get smaller. It got cleaner. Less heat, more edge. Running us. That fit the canyon. The station. The drone with the hose-clamped beacon. It fit the six months of docking fees paid in cash behind Mama Yun's. It fit the dormant bounty that should not have existed and the signal that had woken one of Spike's dead names and the waitress telling Faye she remembered a woman who could not have been her. Somebody had taken time with all of this. Real time. Years maybe. And down in the basin they had charged in like hunger itself, loud and easy to read.

Faye blew air through her nose and pushed away from the strut again. "I hate when he's right."

"I'm right a lot," Spike said.

"That sentence alone proves you aren't."

Jet almost answered, then did not. He folded the summons once, carefully, and tucked it into the breast pocket of his work shirt like putting the debt there would make it less embarrassing.

"Fine," he said. "We stop acting like we're chasing some random joyrider."

"Great," Faye said. "Now we're chasing a random joyrider who knows our personalities."

"That's still an improvement."

Ed slipped off the crate and landed barefoot without a sound. "Sneaky fish knows the Bebop song now."

Jet looked down at Ed. "Maybe."

"Then Bebop should sing a different song."

That should have sounded childish. It did not. Not in the hangar, not with the basin spinning red under them and the fine in Jet's pocket and the glitter from the other pilot's cheap trick still clinging to Spike's sleeves.

Jet let the thought sit.

No one had anything useful after that, and for once they seemed to know it. Faye climbed back into the Redtail to check whether the near miss had scorched a port thruster gasket. Spike took a rag and started wiping chaff residue off the Swordfish's intake lip with the bored concentration he wore when something had gotten under his skin. Ed pulled the station map back up on a side monitor and began tracing routes with one finger, humming under the breath. Ein wandered from one set of legs to another, inspecting everybody's mood.

Jet stayed by the bench a little longer.

Through the hangar's upper port he could see a sliver of Mars, dark rust and thin atmosphere, the rim lights of some settlement turning slow below. Somewhere down there was the station they had torn past, the ore hauler pilot probably telling the story already to anybody who would buy her a drink, the freight drone still waddling its programmed path with a fake beacon strapped to its back like a joke with good wiring.

And somewhere farther south, maybe already beyond the next range of canyons, the real ship was moving at whatever speed its pilot had chosen from the start.

Not fleeing.

Setting tempo.
