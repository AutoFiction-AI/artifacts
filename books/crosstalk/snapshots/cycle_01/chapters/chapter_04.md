# Chapter 4

Mars had a way of paving over old sins with useful things. The back stair behind the Chou arcade was gone, replaced by a freight elevator painted with black-and-yellow hazard stripes. The vent cavity behind the pawn broker on Ninth had been filled with pink sealant that bulged through the grate like cheap sausage. The room above the machine-parts wholesaler, where Spike had once slept through an afternoon with a pistol under his hand and dried blood cracking at his ankle, had become a tutoring center. Paper planets hung in the window. One had peeled loose and stuck to the glass from static, Saturn turned on its side over Tharsis.

He checked anyway.

He checked the old cigarette machine with the false back. The machine was gone and the wall behind it had been tiled over in white squares already going yellow at the grout. He checked the drain trench behind a shuttered bathhouse where message capsules used to sit in the third slot down. The whole block had been torn open for a utility line. Rebar stuck out of a trench full of pale dust and plastic cups. He crossed into the older freight alleys and found one steel door welded shut, another with three fresh locks on it and a remnant gang tag sprayed across the frame in silver loops. It wasn't Red Dragon. It was something newer, with the same cheap paint and the same threat.

The wind came down the loading lanes with red grit in it, hissing over the concrete. By noon the sun had thinned to a dirty coin behind the settlement haze, and the yards were all sound and pressure: forklifts whining in reverse, pallet jacks clattering over seams in the slab, the low thump of cargo pods settling into magnetic cradles. A woman in insulated coveralls smoked behind a crate stack and read a paperback with the cover folded off. A loader drone rolled past Spike's shin, bumped his ankle, corrected, and kept going. Nobody looked at him twice. That was good. It should have stayed good.

The last place on his list sat at the edge of the district where the warehouses gave way to older lockups and bonded storage. The sign over the corridor still read KESSLER FREIGHT ANNEX in half-dead blue tubes. Two letters buzzed and failed, came back, failed again. He had used the locker under a logistics shell company that no longer existed and a routing number nobody had said out loud in years. It should have been dead with the rest of them.

The hall inside smelled like cold dust, old coolant, and the bleach the Annex used when it wanted to suggest cleanliness without paying for repairs. The floor was pitted concrete. The ceiling strips flickered in alternating rows, so that every few steps the corridor changed color on him. His locker waited at the far end behind a roll gate and a scarred biometric pad with a cracked corner.

He stood there for a second longer than he needed to.

It wasn't because of memory. Memory was cheap. Mars sold it by the block. He paused because the thing in front of him was supposed to be impossible. A ship had parked behind a noodle shop, sent the wrong handshake into the air, and now an old storage box was still sitting here ready to know his hand.

He set his palm on the reader.

The pad warmed, clicked, and held him there with a soft mechanical hum while it compared him to a version of himself that should have been dead. Then the lock thunked open.

Spike pushed the gate up halfway and ducked under. The air inside was colder. There were no windows and no circulation beyond the tired vent rattling somewhere behind the wall. Shelves lined both sides, metal flaking to orange at the bolts. The contents were what he remembered because he had arranged them not to matter: routing slips in sealed sleeves, old frequency crystals, two stripped-down comm relays, a packet of counterfeit transit seals gone stiff with age, a canvas tool roll, three paper cartons of manifests bound with wire.

There was nothing dramatic in it, and nothing worth nostalgia.

What bothered him was how undramatic the disturbance was.

Dust lay over the shelves in a thin even skin except where one carton had been opened and retied. The wire wasn't twisted the way he'd left it. The papers inside had been thumbed and settled again with care. Whoever had come in here had not tossed anything, had not stolen anything obvious, had not left the mess of a nervous searcher. They had known what they wanted before the door finished opening.

Spike crouched and pulled the carton free.

The moved manifest sat on top instead of buried halfway down where it belonged. He knew that because he had filed them by route weight and rotation, and this one came from the Tharsis-Hellas run in a quarter when the shipments had gone out under agricultural hardware, then mining parts, then whatever other lie fit the customs window. He slid the paper out of its sleeve.

The page had gone soft at the folds. The cargo line items were still crisp enough to read: ceramic barrel sleeves, receiver housings, spring packs, and three insulated cases under a medical rider appended at the last port call.

The rider was what had been marked. Somebody had circled the case numbers in dark pencil and written in the margin, in a hand neat enough to make the letters look calm:

She needed this to live.

Spike read it again because there was no point in pretending he had misread it the first time.

The attached rider carried pharmacode strings and a clinic destination he didn't recognize, out near the southern rim. The notes field named immune-suppression maintenance, post-transplant, and temperature sensitive handling. They were anti-rejection drugs, the kind of cargo that traveled in padded cases with extra seals and got unloaded fast because if the cold chain broke the shipment turned to expensive trash. He could remember signing off on the rider now that it was in front of him. He didn't remember the place or the cases. He remembered only the irritation of somebody jamming medical freight onto a weapons line at the last minute because the outbound window was closing and the books had to balance.

Back then it had all been weight, timing, bribes, route safety, who could be trusted not to skim, and who had to be overpaid because they were stupid enough to need it. Cargo stayed abstract until it left his ledger.

She needed this to live.

The note made the whole page smaller and meaner. It wasn't a ledger now. It was a hand around somebody's throat, opening and closing according to schedule.

He checked the rest of the carton. No other notes. No names. No message addressed to him except the one that wasn't addressed at all and had still found him exactly.

Spike put the manifest aside and turned to the lock assembly on the inside wall. The biometric unit fed into an older service housing with a removable plate. Two screws showed fresh scrape marks. The marks were slight, but they were enough. Somebody had pulled the plate, bridged the maintenance leads, and let the system believe it was being serviced. Old syndicate infrastructure loved maintenance overrides. It had never trusted the same men twice if one wire could replace them.

He should have expected that. He hated that he hadn't.

On the shelf above the manifests sat one of the comm relays, a black brick no bigger than a lunchbox. He took it down, thumbed the latch, and waited while the dead screen thought about whether to betray him. The relay woke on the third try with a phosphor glow that made the locker look even more tired.

Packet traffic populated line by line.

Automated route checks came first, then invalid node requests. A broker query stamped from a carrier front in Noctis came next, then another from a name he didn't know, then another. Most of them bounced, but not all. The old band had listeners on it. There weren't many, but there were enough.

At the bottom sat the one that mattered: AUTH SIGNAL CONFIRMED / LIN SHAO-FENG NODE RESPONSE REQUESTED.

It was timestamped yesterday.

Spike stared at the screen until the letters stopped looking like letters and turned back into trouble.

There it was. It wasn't paranoia or one old frequency chirping into empty space. Somebody had rung the bell and a few scavengers had lifted their heads.

He killed the relay, pulled the battery, and set the battery on the concrete. The pistol came into his hand without a decision. One shot in the cramped locker cracked so hard the vent rattled and dust dropped from the ceiling. Battery acid leaked black and sharp across the floor.

He stood still and listened.

The corridor kept being a corridor. The fluorescents hummed. A cart wheel squeaked somewhere far off. No boots ran his way. The Annex had probably written gunfire into the overhead as acceptable loss years ago.

Spike wiped the relay down with the inside of his sleeve, took the manifest, and reshelved the carton as close to its old angle as he could get it. It didn't matter. Whoever had wanted him to find the paper had already done the work. Still, habit was habit. He lowered the roll gate, waited through the lock cycle, and walked back down the hall with the folded manifest inside his jacket, the paper stiff against his ribs.

Outside, the freight lanes looked even more ordinary than before. A man with a lunch bucket argued with a dock clerk over unloading priority. Somebody laughed from the back of a flatbed. The smoking woman had turned a page in her paperback and switched the cigarette to her other hand without looking up. A ship lifted from a nearby pad and washed the alley in heat and grit, hot enough to sting through Spike's shirt.

He turned his face away from the exhaust and thought about a woman he had never met waiting on a shipment routed between rifle parts.

Maybe she got it in time. Maybe she didn't. Maybe the note meant the shipment had arrived and that had been the good part, the only good part. Maybe it meant the opposite. The sentence didn't say. That was another thing about it. Whoever had left the paper knew exactly how much to tell him. Just enough to drag a body into a column of numbers and leave it there.

He took the long way back to the paid dock. Twice he changed streets because the old reflex was awake now, the one that counted reflections and doorways and men leaning too long in one place. He passed a storefront screen tuned to bounty listings and saw a dozen faces scroll by in blue police light. None of them were his yet.

By the time the Bebop came into view above the adjoining roofs, the wind had turned colder. The ship sat where Jet had been forced to pay for it, ugly and familiar and home in the way Spike never let himself phrase even in his own head. Somebody had hung laundry on a neighboring line between two service poles. A small shirt snapped in the dust-laden air like it was trying to get free.

Spike climbed the gangway with the manifest in his pocket and the old dead name walking half a step behind him.

* * *

The Bebop smelled like fried peppers, engine grease, wet dog, and the detergent Jet bought in bulk because it was cheaper even though it never really got the socks clean. Hull metal ticked under the afternoon cold. From somewhere down the spine of the ship came the hollow bang of a panel being put back where it belonged by force rather than persuasion.

Jet was at the galley table with the drip manifold from the coffee maker laid out in pieces on a towel. His cybernetic hand held the housing steady while his right teased mineral scale out of the valve with a pick. He looked up once when Spike came in, then back to the part.

"You been out all day," he said.

"Looks that way."

"That answer's a waste of my time."

"Then don't ask."

Jet snorted once through his nose. It wasn't amusement, just air.

Faye was stretched along the couch with one boot on, one off, scraping old polish from her thumbnail with the corner of a playing card. The card back was bent soft from use. She did not look up either, which meant she was listening hard enough to hear fabric move.

"He went for a walk," she said. "Maybe he found enlightenment. Maybe he found a hot dog."

"On Mars?" Jet said.

"Fine. A terrible hot dog."

Ed had colonized the main console. Bare feet tucked under one leg, goggles on forehead, a nest of wires around the terminal like colorful noodles. Ein lay under the chair with his chin on his paws and one ear turned toward the room. The monitor glow painted Ed's face green and gold by turns as different maps rotated through.

Spike moved past them toward the sink. His jacket felt too heavy for a sheet of paper. He opened the tap, drank water that tasted faintly of metal, and let the cold sit on his tongue.

He could feel Jet deciding whether to ask another question and deciding against it. That almost bothered him more than if Jet had pushed.

The silence stretched into one of the ship's ordinary silences, full of noises that only counted as silence because everybody knew them. Life support murmured. A fan with a bad bearing whined. The tap dripped because Jet still had the coffee maker in pieces and had used the wrong wrench on the faucet last week. Faye flicked dry polish into an ashtray. Ed hummed something tuneless to the rhythm of typing.

Then Ed stopped humming.

"Sneaky ship lifted its feet," Ed said.

Jet's pick clicked against the manifold. "What?"

Ed's hands were already moving. A transit grid spun onto the main screen, then a topographic map of Mars in dirty reds and amber contour lines. A bright point blinked over Tharsis, left the settlement, and drew a line south.

"There. There there there." Ed jabbed the screen with one finger and grinned without looking away from it. "No more noodle-house nap. It's going zoom-small, not zoom-fast. Surface skim. Ooo, low. Very low. Show-off low."

Faye sat up with her card still in hand. "How low?"

"Low enough to kiss the dust and maybe get a mouth full of rocks if the pilot sneezes." Ed tilted the map, expanded the track. "See? Not orbit. Not even pretending orbit. Just scooting. Scoot scoot. South."

Jet pushed back from the table. The coffee maker part rolled, hit the lip of the towel, stopped. "Toward where?"

Ed flattened the route with a flourish. "Big bowl. Hellas basin. Maybe rim settlements first, maybe not. Ship's taking the nice long slide down."

That got Spike's attention in a different way than the line on the screen should have. Hellas sat south and empty in the way only Mars could manage, huge and raw and scored with the kind of terrain small operators loved. Old waystation country. Old supply roads. The kind of places a freight band used when it wanted weather and distance doing part of the work.

He set the cup down.

Faye looked from the screen to Spike and back again. "You know that face you make when you pretend you're not making a face?"

"No."

"You should. It's annoying."

Jet ignored both of them. He came around the table wiping his hands on the towel, leaving pale crescents of limescale dust across the fabric. "Can you hold the signal?"

Ed made an offended sound. "Ed can hold a signal, hold a sandwich, hold many things. Sneaky ship is practically helping. Ping ping ping. Very polite."

"Polite," Faye said.

"Mm-hmm. If sneaky ship wanted to hide, sneaky ship would stop blinking and tuck under traffic. But it's not doing that. It's doing this." Ed waggled her fingers over the southbound line. "Come see me dance."

Ein gave one soft bark without lifting his head.

Spike took a step toward the screen. The track was too clean. It wasn't straight. It was smarter than straight. It curved around enforcement corridors and skimmed commercial lanes just enough to blend if somebody took a lazy look. Whoever flew that ship knew Mars traffic and knew how visible they wanted to be.

The wrong band was still open, and Hellas lay at the end of it.

It wasn't panic. It was something worse: intention.

Jet folded his arms. "Fuel says we don't go tearing across half the planet because Ed thinks a transponder has good manners."

"Fuel says a lot of things," Faye said. "Mostly 'you're broke.' We know."

"That's useful. Thanks."

She tossed the playing card onto the table. "You're welcome."

Ed was still zooming the map in and out, following branching routes that weren't there yet but might be. "Sneaky ship changed altitude twice after leaving Tharsis. Little test wiggles. Maybe checking if anyone is staring. Ed is staring. Hello."

Spike heard himself ask, "Any stops?"

Ed glanced up at him. "Not yet. Why, Spike-person? You have a vacation cottage in Hellas?"

"No."

"Shame. It sounds windy."

Jet's eyes flicked to Spike anyway, brief and measuring. Yesterday Jet had come back from wherever he had gone with the look of a man carrying a brick under his coat. Today Spike knew he was wearing the same outline himself.

Nobody said that part.

Faye stood and crossed to the console barefoot on one side, boot heel striking the deck on the other. "Pull the last six hours."

Ed obeyed without comment. The track expanded behind the ship's current position, a slow unspooling away from Tharsis. It was long enough to say the pilot had chosen the timing, and late enough to say she had waited for something.

For him to check the locker? For the note to be found? Maybe that was giving the whole thing too much shape. Maybe it wasn't enough.

Jet was watching the screen and not the crew, which usually meant he was thinking harder than he wanted anybody to know. "If it keeps going south, we lose it in the basin clutter."

"If?" Faye said.

"If." He looked at her now. "We're not taking a pleasure trip because somebody likes riddles."

She laughed once, flat and short. "You think this is a riddle?"

Spike could feel the room starting to tighten, the way it did before a fight that hadn't admitted it was a fight. The ship's hum seemed louder. Even the loose vent over the stove had shut up to listen.

Ed, oblivious or pretending to be, put two fingers on the screen like a conductor readying an orchestra. "Bebop could go whoooosh."

Jet pointed at the map. "Bebop could also burn fuel we don't have, landing fees we don't have, and food money we don't have."

"We don't have a lot of things," Faye said. "That's not new."

Spike slipped the folded manifest deeper into his pocket and tasted battery acid memory at the back of his throat.

Hellas waited to the south.

If the ship reached the old lanes and kept singing on that band, whatever was left of the network would not stay theoretical for long.

Jet straightened. "All right. Bridge. Now."

Nobody argued with the order because they were already moving.

* * *

The bridge lights came up in tired strips as Jet dropped into the pilot's chair and keyed the systems awake. Outside the forward glass the paid dock's service mast leaned at an angle that looked unsafe but had probably held for twenty years. A loader in orange coveralls crossed the yard below with a crate balanced on his shoulder and never looked up. The city went on making a living while the Bebop decided whether to spend what little it had left.

Spike stood behind Jet's right shoulder with one hand on the back of the copilot seat. He kept his weight easy, casual if you didn't know him. His stillness said otherwise. Faye took the side console and tucked her bare foot under her opposite knee while she pulled the other boot on all the way with short irritated jerks. Ed swiveled between stations because sitting in one place seemed beneath Ed's dignity. Ein trotted in, circled once under the nav bench, and settled where everybody would have to step around him.

Jet brought up the fuel numbers and cursed under his breath.

"Say it louder," Faye said. "Maybe the tanks will get embarrassed and fill themselves."

"We're short for a basin run and back if we have to do any chasing once we're there."

"Then don't miss."

"That's your whole contribution?"

"No, I can be worse."

Jet jabbed a finger at the screen. "Dock fees, fuel, filters clogged with half of Tharsis because we had to park in that dump, and now this. I want one reason to go that isn't curiosity."

Spike said, "You already have one."

Jet did not turn around. "Let's hear yours."

There it was.

Spike looked at the southbound track on the secondary screen instead of at Jet. That was easier. "That ship's broadcasting on an old freight band. Not legal freight. Old syndicate routing. If it keeps doing that down there, somebody's going to answer."

Jet's head came around a little. "You know the band?"

"Enough."

"Enough from where?"

"From before."

Faye gave a dry little laugh. "That's nice and vague. Very in character."

Spike ignored her because if he looked at her he might say too much or the wrong thing. "It's not dead traffic, Jet. I checked."

That got a full turn from him. "Checked where?"

Spike met his eyes then, just for a second. "Around."

"Around where?"

"You want a map, make one yourself."

Jet pushed back from the controls hard enough that the chair motor whined. "You don't get to walk in here with half a sentence and expect me to spin the ship south."

"Then don't."

Faye looked from one to the other and shook her head once, disgust mixed with recognition. "Oh, good. This again."

Jet was already on his feet. Under stress he got bigger, louder, more solid in the room, and the bridge was not built for him to stand up angry in it. "No. Not 'this again.' I'm done with people on this ship telling me just enough to make the next bad decision and keeping the rest for private storage."

Spike felt the folded paper press against him when he breathed. "You asked for a reason. I gave you one."

"You gave me a scrap."

"It's enough."

"For you, maybe."

Ed had gone very still in the middle of swiveling. That was more noticeable than if Ed had screamed. Ein lifted his head.

Faye leaned back in her seat and crossed her arms. "He's not wrong that the signal matters."

Jet shot her a look. "That's surprising."

"Don't flatter yourself. I'm not taking his side. I'm taking my own."

"Which is?"

She stared at the front glass instead of at either of them, and when she spoke her voice had that faster edge it got when she was nearest saying something real and hated it. "A woman at Mama Yun's looked me in the face and told me she remembered me on Ganymede years I wasn't there. Same name. Same face, near enough. I want to know what the hell that means before one of you decides it means something for me."

Nobody said anything for a beat.

Faye hated beats like that. You could see it. She recovered by getting meaner. "What? You think I came in here because I love the scenery? I want answers. If they're on that ship, I'm not staying parked while you two have a secrecy contest."

Jet's mouth tightened. "You could've said that yesterday."

"You could've said a lot of things yesterday."

He absorbed that like a blow he knew he had earned and did not intend to discuss. His eyes cut to Spike again. "And you want to go because old syndicate traffic makes your skin crawl."

"Something like that."

"That's not an answer."

"It's the one you're getting."

Ed lifted a hand. "Ed has a reason."

Jet exhaled through his nose. "Sure. Why not."

Ed brightened a little at being invited back into the room. "Because it's a chase. Because sneaky ship parked in the good noodle place and then booped all the old human buttons and now it's going south-south-south like a little arrow with manners. Because if somebody does all that and then leaves breadcrumbs, Ed wants to see the loaf."

Faye blinked. "The loaf."

"Yes. The loaf."

"That's not a reason. That's a breakfast metaphor."

Ed considered this. "Ed likes breakfast too."

Against his own will, Spike felt the corner of his mouth move. It died fast.

Jet rubbed at his beard with the heel of his hand. "You all hear yourselves, right? One bad signal, one impossible waitress story, one child with a hobby, and I'm supposed to pour our last fuel into Hellas because that sounds sensible."

"No," Spike said. "You're supposed to do it because you have your own reason."

Jet looked at him flatly. "Don't tell me what my reasons are."

"Fine. Then say them."

The room changed shape again. It wasn't louder. It was tighter.

Jet's gaze dropped to the deck, then to the fuel screen, then to the southbound line. When he spoke it came rougher than before, less performed. "Somebody used a dead man's access to stick a bounty on me that shouldn't exist. That's enough reason."

Faye's head turned. "A dead man's access?"

"Yeah."

"Who?"

Jet hesitated just long enough to count.

"Nobody I knew," he said.

That was not the whole answer. Spike could hear it because he was carrying one of his own. Faye heard it too. Her expression said so, sharp and ugly and not surprised.

"Right," she said. "Great. So we're all doing that."

No one contradicted her.

Outside, a cargo hauler lifted from the neighboring pad and rattled the bridge glass. Red dust blew across the yard in a wide sheet, blurred, settled. Through it Spike could just make out the roofline beyond the warehouses and, past that, the block where Mama Yun's sat. Their usual spot behind the shop would be empty now. The one dependable place on Mars, empty because a stranger had chosen to make empty part of the message.

Jet dropped back into the chair and keyed the engine preheat without announcing a decision. Fans spun up deep in the hull. The floor took on the faint vibration of waking machinery.

Faye heard it and smiled without humor. "There's that."

"This isn't agreement," Jet said.

"Then what is it?"

"This is me refusing to lose the trail while the three of you circle yourselves."

"Touching," Faye said.

"Put your boot on."

"It's on."

He glanced at her feet. "The other one."

She looked, swore, and jammed her foot into it. Ed laughed so hard the chair spun halfway around.

The first stage thrusters coughed alive. Somewhere aft, clamps disengaged with a deep metal thud. The Bebop shivered in place.

Jet pointed without looking. "Ed, give me the cleanest line south that still lets us duck settlement enforcement."

"Mm-hmm. Sneaky but legal-ish."

"Legal."

Ed sucked air through teeth. "That's a narrower tunnel."

"Find it."

"Found it already."

Jet kept going. "Faye, strap in or don't, but if I've gotta scrape you off the port bulkhead again I'm charging rent."

"Add it to my tab."

"Spike."

"What."

Jet's hands settled on the controls. "If this band is as bad as you're saying, you tell me the second it gets worse."

He would not tell Jet everything. He would not tell him the name, the note in his pocket, or about the shot battery bleeding across the locker floor.

"Yeah," Spike said. "I'll tell you."

It came out close enough to truth to pass if you weren't looking directly at it.

The dockmaster on the local channel started yelling about departure sequence. Jet cut him off with a code string and a promise he did not mean about filing clean. The Bebop rose slow and heavy from the paid berth, casting a wide shadow over the concrete lot and the crooked service mast and the man below still carrying his crate. Dust rolled outward in red sheets. Laundry snapped itself horizontal on the line between the poles.

Tharsis opened under them in layers of roof metal, transit lanes, rust streaks, greenhouse glass gone cloudy at the edges. For a second Spike saw the alley behind Mama Yun's, the empty slot, and steam leaking from the noodle shop vent into the cold. Then the ship banked and the settlement fell away behind them.

South, the land roughened and widened. The map on the bridge screens redrew itself around basin contours and old roads half lost to dust. The blinking point ahead kept moving with patient confidence, not fleeing, not waiting either, and making them come.

Jet set the course.

Faye strapped in at last, muttering to herself.

Ed hummed over the nav board and fed route updates to the system in little bursts of delight.

Ein braced his paws and sneezed when the cabin pressure shifted.

Spike stood behind them with one hand on the seatback and the folded manifest against his chest and watched the southbound signal burn clean on the screen. There were four people on the Bebop, one ship ahead of them, and four different reasons to follow.

From up here Mars looked empty enough to hide anybody.

That was the problem. It looked that way right before it found room for the past to climb back in.
