# Chapter 8

The reader liked his right eye better than the left. Most machines did.

It took two passes before the locker clicked. By then the freight district had settled deeper into its night noises: a generator knocking somewhere off tempo, sheet metal pinging as the cold came down, dust hissing over concrete in long low sweeps. The loading bays along the lane glowed in strips. Everything else had gone black. Forklifts slept behind chain-link with their forks lowered like tired jaws.

Spike pulled the door open and stood there a second without going in.

The place smelled the same as it had four years ago: old paper, oil gone gummy in its can, cardboard that had drunk too much dry air and given up. It wasn't memory exactly. Memory moved. This was just storage. A dead version of his life stacked and numbered and waiting for nobody.

He stepped inside, shut the door against the wind, and clicked on the penlight he had brought this time.

The beam found crates, binders, bundles of route sheets strapped with brittle bands. He'd come here the first time because the alias had flared to life and he needed to know what else might still be breathing with it. He'd expected danger and gotten paperwork. One manifest moved from the bottom of a stack to the top. One sentence in a hand he didn't know.

She needed this to live.

Five words. There was no name and no accusation. That had been the irritating part. If whoever left it had written You did this, that would've been simple. If they'd written My mother or Help me or Even now, it would've been something he could push against. Instead it was just a fact. Facts had a way of sitting there until they got expensive.

He laid the annotated manifest on a crate under the light.

The paper was old enough to have gone soft at the folds. Carbon copy ghosting stained the back. Route code at the top. Tharsis freight annex to a basin clinic that only had a depot number, not a real name. Mid-column cargo descriptions in the syndicate's preferred language of concealment: machined housings, bolt assemblies, receiver blanks, lubricants, polymer wrap, coolant packs. Nothing that looked like a life in there unless you already knew where to look.

He knew now.

The medical line item sat halfway down the page, small as an apology. Refrigerated pharmaceuticals, sealed case, one unit. Special handling. Maintain cold chain. It had been tucked into a larger convoy under the same transit umbrella as rifle parts and ammunition. Standard enough. They hid all kinds of things inside weapons runs because weapons runs got where they were going. They had guards. They had fuel priority. They had people who got shot if they came up short. A clinic shipment on its own got taxed, skimmed, delayed, or robbed by the second checkpoint. A weapons convoy moved fast.

The ugly part was that the system worked.

He turned the page and found the attached handling sheet. Temperature tolerance. Transfer signatures. A remark from one of the depot clerks complaining about amperage draw from the refrigeration unit. A flat notation in his own hand authorizing the cold case to ride in the third truck and telling the dispatcher to stop whining.

He could see the moment without trying. A loading dock under yellow lamps. Dust in the corners where the sweepers never got it all. One driver bitching about battery pull. Spike taking the clipboard, looking at the clock instead of the cargo, writing three quick words and shoving it back. He had been younger and thinner and more careful with his hair. He had not been more moral. He had just been in a hurry.

At the time the refrigerated case had meant inconvenience. Different tie-downs. Different spacing from the hot crates. One more thing for idiots to mess up. He remembered that much. He didn't remember wondering what was in it, because he hadn't. The label had said pharmaceuticals and then turned into weight and volume and schedule. Somebody else's problem. That had been the whole job. Turn people into categories, categories into movement.

He lifted the manifest and checked the stack under it.

There were more.

The clinic code was the same, and so was the route family. Not every week, but regular. One insulated case a month folded into shipments that paid better and mattered more to men with guns. Twenty-eight days between one and the next. Then thirty-one. Then twenty-nine. Somebody had been living in those gaps. Not poetically. Literally. Someone's next four weeks arriving as a line item between ammunition belts and stamped receiver shells.

He flattened the second sheet with the heel of his hand.

On the delivery confirmation the receiving signature wasn't a name. It was a courier mark, a quick angled slash with a loop under it. The kind of abbreviated sign-off that let men farther up the chain pretend they weren't moving goods through actual human hands. He found the same mark on the next month's sheet and the month after that, then a variation with the pen dragging at the end as if the truck had been moving or the signer had been tired.

It was the same courier on the same route, again and again.

He sat on the edge of a crate because there wasn't any point pretending he was just doing a quick check now.

His penlight made a hard white island in the dark. Beyond it the rest of the locker stayed full of shapes that used to mean something: spare transponders, old route books, a dead comm unit, invoices for shell companies that had dissolved before the ink dried. Lin Shao-Feng lived in those things. Not Spike. Lin liked clean numbers and quiet drivers and routes so dependable nobody above him had to think about them. If you did logistics well, the cargo disappeared into inevitability. Men remembered gunfights. They remembered bosses. They didn't remember the truck that came on time every month.

Unless the truck stopped coming.

Spike set the manifest down and rubbed his thumb along the note the young woman had left. The pen strokes had pressed through the first page into the next two. Whoever wrote it had leaned hard. Not decorative. Not theatrical. Writing on bad paper with something mean enough behind the hand to emboss it.

She needed this to live.

He looked back at the line item. The drug names were there in print small enough to discourage curiosity. He knew enough to know what class they were. Anti-rejection. The kind you kept taking because if you stopped, your own body started acting like an occupied territory and tried to clear the invader out. He didn't know what organ. Kidney maybe. Liver. It didn't matter. The point was maintenance. Not cure. Not one good day in a hospital and done. Repetition. Delivery. Cold storage. Do it again next month. And the month after that.

He found himself counting how many times the route had repeated before he noticed he was doing it.

Nine confirmed runs in the stack. There might've been more somewhere else. Enough to say it wasn't a fluke. Enough to say the weapons convoy hadn't accidentally carried medicine once. The rifles paid the fuel. The insulated case got a ride. The route existed for violence and happened to make room for survival on the side. That was worse than if the whole thing had been purely dirty. Dirty was simple. This was useful.

He remembered the way the syndicate liked to talk about efficiency. Not in speeches. In little irritated corrections. Don't waste a trip. Don't send half-full trucks. Don't separate loads if one set of muscle can cover both. Every decision shaved a risk somewhere and pushed it somewhere else. By the time cargo reached its destination, nobody at the top could remember which part had been the point.

He'd told himself for years that he hadn't been one of the men making bodies. He'd moved freight. He'd moved information. He'd moved money. The blood was always somewhere farther down the line, on somebody else's shoes.

The manifest didn't argue with that exactly. It just made the line less clean.

He went through the rest of the stack slower. There were no names. There never would've been names if his operation had been working the way it was built to. There were depot numbers, cargo weights, seal confirmations, the courier mark. Once, on a customs bypass sheet, a note that the basin runner preferred night handoff because daytime dust cooked the reefer unit. Another time, a surcharge approved because the route had to divert around a militia checkpoint that week. Mundane. Petty. The kind of details a person built a life around if staying alive depended on delivery windows and battery draw.

He could feel the old habits trying to help. Build the pattern. Strip it down. Ask who had access, who moved the paper, who knew the stash point, which old contact could identify the route mark. Do the part that was useful and skip the rest.

Useful was fine. Useful was what he had.

But there was an extra drag in it now, something he couldn't shake by standing up or lighting a cigarette. The manifests had made a person out of the sentence without giving him the person. He had regularity. A clinic code. A repeated sign-off. Someone whose life had depended on his routing math and who had never entered his head when he was doing it.

That part sat wrong.

He gathered the relevant sheets into one stack and tucked them into his jacket. The paper crackled under the lining.

There were only a handful of people left on Mars who would've remembered a courier mark from one basin feeder run years ago. Most of the competent ones were dead, jailed, or honest now. Honest was the rarest option. Hector Moro was still alive because he had built a life around remembering exactly enough and never looking like the sort of man who could matter.

Spike killed the penlight, opened the locker, and stepped back into the cold.

Dust moved along the lane like water with nowhere to go. He pulled the collar of his jacket up and headed for the alley quarter where the bars with bad liquor and no windows kept their lights on for men who didn't want witnesses.

***

The Radiant sat halfway down a service alley too narrow for delivery trucks. The sign over the door still worked only in pieces. RADI _ NT. Good enough. A red tube buzzed under the dead letters, painting the metal door the color of old meat.

From the street it looked closed. That was the point.

Inside, the light dropped another level. Two freight men in dust-scored coveralls hunched over dominoes at a back table without talking. A woman in a station cleaner's uniform drank beer and watched a muted race feed with the fixed concentration of someone trying not to go home yet. The speaker over the shelf coughed out trumpet through static. The room smelled like citrus cleaner losing a fight with spilled liquor.

Hector Moro stood behind the bar polishing the same glass too long.

He'd gone gray in the way some men did when the color didn't so much leave as fade under the dirt. Narrow shoulders. Liver spots on the backs of both hands. His shirt sleeves were rolled to the forearms, and on his right little finger he wore a green plastic ring the size and color of hard candy. Children's junk, the kind that came out of a vending machine for two woolongs. He turned it around the knuckle with his thumb while he worked, not enough to seem like nerves if you didn't know what nerves looked like.

He looked up when the door shut, and the glass stopped moving in his hand.

"Thought you were dead," he said.

Spike took the nearest stool. "Didn't take."

Hector set the glass down carefully. "Yeah. I can see that."

"You serving or just judging?"

"Mostly judging." Hector reached for a bottle anyway. "House whiskey?"

"If you can call it that."

"I can't."

He poured two fingers into a chipped glass and slid it over. Spike took a swallow. It tasted like somebody had stored it in radiator parts.

"Still bad," Spike said.

"Still cheap."

"That's the spirit."

Hector huffed once through his nose. Not quite laughter. Not friendly either. "You don't come here for years and then walk in like you stepped out for a smoke."

"You want me to do an entrance?"

"No."

"Good."

The speaker crackled. One of the dominoes clicked against wood in the back. Hector picked the glass up again and resumed polishing, but his attention stayed on Spike the way a man watched a stove he'd stopped trusting.

Spike let the first minute go by without touching the subject. Hector respected patience more than pressure, and anyway there was no point pretending this was urgent in the dramatic sense. If the young pilot had found the locker, she had already been ahead of him for a while.

Hector finally said, "You using your own face on purpose these days?"

"It's what I had on me."

"Cute."

Spike drank again. "Nobody's said cute to me in a long time."

"That's because most people improve with age."

There it was. A little more acid than the room called for. Fear, then. Not of Spike exactly. Of the era Spike had dragged in with him.

He took the folded manifest from his jacket, opened it on the bar between them, and laid a finger on the line item.

Hector didn't look at the paper right away. He looked at Spike, then at the door, then at the silent race feed, then back at Spike.

"No," he said.

"You didn't even read it."

"I don't need to."

"Humor me."

"That habit got people killed."

"So did yours."

Hector's mouth twitched at that. He leaned in at last, glasses sliding down the bridge of his nose, and scanned the route header. The color left his face by degrees. He set the glass aside and put both hands on the bar as if the paper might tip.

"Where'd you get this?" he asked.

"Locker."

"Yours?"

"Used to be."

Hector looked at the note in blue ink and went still enough that the green plastic ring stopped moving.

"Hell," he said.

Spike watched him. "You know it."

"I know the route family."

"That's not what I asked."

Hector kept reading. His eyes moved down the monthly dates, the handling note, the receiving marks. "Somebody came through asking about this."

Spike's hand stayed where it was on the bar. "When?"

"Couple days ago. Maybe three. I've had a week."

"Young woman."

Hector looked up sharply. "So you do know."

"I know enough to ask."

"Then why're you asking me?"

"Because enough isn't the same as useful."

That landed. Hector pushed his glasses back up and reached for the bottle. He poured himself a little, drank it without bothering with a fresh glass, and wiped his mouth with the back of his hand.

"She wasn't syndicate," he said.

"How can you tell?"

"Because I'm still standing here."

Spike let that one pass.

Hector went on. "Syndicate people don't come in patient. They don't sit down and order one beer and ask if my knees are worse in winter now. They don't slide cash across like they're paying tuition. They come in knowing the answer already or not caring what the answer was."

"She paid well."

"Too well." Hector looked offended on principle. "Made the place look respectable."

Spike tipped the manifest toward him. "What'd she ask?"

Hector gave the room another quick scan. The domino men were still lost in their own slow war. The woman at the race feed hadn't turned. He lowered his voice anyway.

"Specific years. South basin feeders. Which loads got consolidated after the customs sweeps started. Who was dispatching cold cargo on arms routes. Names of depots that don't exist anymore. She had dates written down already. Real dates, not fishing."

"And?"

"And she knew enough that lying stupid would've been insulting."

"So you lied smart."

"I retired, Spike. I serve poison to freight drivers and pretend I don't hear gambling debts. I don't have a smart setting left."

Spike looked at him over the rim of his glass. "You always had one."

Hector scratched his jaw. The plastic ring flashed once in the low light. "She had paperwork."

"What kind."

"Copies. Not many. Enough. Route sheets. Clinic order numbers. One old transport abstract with your dispatch initials still on it."

Spike said nothing.

Hector mistook that for surprise. "Yeah. She knew who she was asking around, even if she didn't say your name out loud."

"Did she say any name?"

"Not hers."

"Mine."

Hector hesitated.

"Hector."

"She didn't say it first," Hector said. "I did. By accident."

Spike's gaze didn't change.

Hector shifted, irritated with himself. "She put a paper down with Lin's old freight codes on it. Not the alias. The route codes. Same thing, basically. I looked at it and said nobody's said those numbers since Lin Shao-Feng was moving half the damn basin. Then I wanted to bite my tongue off."

"You still have terrible instincts."

"You being here proves they haven't killed me yet."

That was fair enough.

Spike took another drink. The whiskey had not improved. "What'd she do when you said it?"

"Nothing dramatic. That's what got me. She just nodded. Like she'd filled in one more square on a form. Then she asked about couriers."

That made Spike look up from the glass.

Hector saw it. "Yeah. Not bosses. Not shooters. Not who owned what strip of dust. Couriers. The people nobody asks about unless they already know where the bodies are."

Spike's voice stayed flat. "Which couriers."

"At first all of them on the Tharsis-Hellas line from that period. Then one in particular."

He reached for the manifest but stopped short of touching the note. "This route. These dates. She wanted to know who was carrying the clinic case when it wasn't moved by truck all the way through. Who did the last leg. Who signed the handoff."

Spike tapped the repeated slash mark on the confirmation sheet. "This one."

"Yeah."

"You know it?"

Hector didn't answer right away. He poured himself another little swallow, changed his mind, and shoved the bottle away instead. "I knew her."

Spike waited.

Hector kept his eyes on the paper. "Ayane."

The name sat there between them. Simple. Two short syllables. No help in it.

"Just Ayane?" Spike asked.

"Just Ayane to me."

"She ran this route."

"Off and on for years. Not because she loved freight." Hector snorted once. "Nobody loves freight. She ran because she showed up on time, didn't skim, and would take the night legs when everybody else started whining about the basin roads."

"Syndicate?"

"Not by choice." Hector's mouth tightened. "Maybe not even by affiliation depending on how generous you were feeling. She moved things because cash work was cash work and because after her husband got killed she didn't have the luxury of principles people could see."

Spike looked back at the route sheets.

"What husband?" he said.

"Dead one."

"Useful."

"You want useful, go ask a ledger." Hector rubbed a thumb over the plastic ring until the skin around it whitened. "I didn't know the whole story. Nobody down at my level ever does. Word was her man had mixed himself up with cops and syndicate both, which is one of those hobbies that doesn't age well. Then he was gone and she had a kid and clinic bills and a face that made everybody think she was tougher than she was. That's what I heard."

Kid.

That word caught worse than husband had.

Spike set his glass down. "How old?"

Hector blinked, maybe at the question, maybe at the fact that Spike had asked one not strictly operational.

"The kid?" he said.

"Yeah."

"I don't know. Little enough that she talked about school pickups like they were emergency extractions. Old enough to draw, I guess."

"Draw."

Hector finally touched the edge of the bar and pointed toward the bowl by the till. Paper matchbooks sat there under a film of dust, each printed with the bar's faded starburst logo.

"She used to steal those," he said. "One every trip. Thought I didn't notice. Said her kid liked the blank backs for doodling. Ships mostly. Once she showed me one by accident. Had booster flames bigger than the ship. Kid had ambitions."

Spike looked at the bowl.

"You remember that," he said.

"I remember weird things." Hector shrugged one shoulder. "Maybe because nobody ever asked me anything important after. Maybe because she always apologized when she took one. Who apologizes for a matchbook?"

The dominoes clicked again from the back table. Somebody on the speaker hit a trumpet note so thin it sounded like wire.

Spike said, "Quiet, you said."

"Yeah. Quiet." Hector glanced toward the woman at the race feed, checking himself. "Not shy. That's different. She'd answer straight if you asked her something. She just didn't waste a lot around it. Came in with dust on her boots and one sleeve usually dirty with coolant or freezer gel. Never sat with the loud ones. If the jukebox was playing too much garbage she'd unplug it without making a speech."

"Reliable."

"As hell." Hector's expression went strange for a second, somewhere between respect and annoyance. "That kind of reliable where you stop noticing the person because the job keeps getting done. If the clinic case had to get across and the road wasn't full of militia idiots, you could bet she'd make it. Men above her loved people like that. They got to count on them without learning anything."

Spike's fingers rested on the page. He didn't move them.

Hector looked at him and saw something. Not remorse maybe. Not enough for that. But attention, at least.

"She wasn't asking about money," Hector said, quieter now. "That girl who came through. The young one. She didn't care who got paid. She cared where the route broke. That was the shape of her questions. Not who's responsible in some big speech way. Where it broke. Which month. Which handoff. Which depot started failing."

Spike lifted his eyes. "You answer that?"

Hector's jaw worked. "I told her what I knew. Not because she scared me. She did, but that's not why. I told her because she'd already done the hard part. She had the papers. She had the dates. She had more patience than any two men in the old network put together. She sat right there for two hours with one beer warming in front of her and never once acted like she was enjoying herself. People like that don't stop because an old bartender shrugs."

"What'd she look like."

Hector thought about it. "Sharp face. Dark hair tied back with whatever she had on hand. Not neat, just out of the way. Mechanics' hands. Cuts on the knuckles, grease sunk into the lines no soap ever gets. Young. Younger than she'd want to seem. And tired in a specific way." He tapped under one eye. "Not no-sleep tired. Long-time tired. The kind that gets organized."

The pilot, then. Or close enough. Spike had only seen her in flashes through canyon glare and cockpit glass, but the edges fit.

"She ask about me?" he said.

"Indirectly."

"Meaning."

"Meaning she asked who had authority to piggyback cold medical loads on arms shipments back then, and I said authority is a generous word for what you people had, and she said fine, who signed off." Hector spread his hands. "Again, she had the papers."

"And you gave her my alias."

"By then I was mostly confirming what she knew."

"That's how it always starts."

Hector's eyes sharpened. "You want to get mad, get mad. But don't pretend this is a leak. It's archaeology. You buried things sloppy."

That was closer to the truth than Spike liked.

He folded one corner of the manifest over and flattened it again. "What happened to Ayane."

Hector leaned back. For the first time since Spike had walked in, he looked old in a way that had nothing to do with gray hair or spots on the hands. It was the look of a man being asked for an ending he had never bothered to earn.

"I don't know," he said.

"You knew her for years and you don't know."

"That's how years work down there. People run a route until they don't. A man gets shot, a clinic closes, somebody remarries, somebody ODs, somebody gets religion, somebody gets buried in a dust slide. You think every disappearance comes with paperwork?"

"No."

"Then don't ask like that."

Spike let the rebuke land. Hector deserved one. Probably more.

Hector sighed and dragged the towel off his shoulder. "Last I heard, she was still doing basin work and still trying to keep one foot out of the real ugly stuff. Which means she was already in it up to the knee. After that? Nothing. Maybe she got sick. Maybe she found a better line. Maybe there wasn't a better line." He looked at the note again. "Nobody sends bulletins around when a courier disappears. They just stop making the board."

"And the kid."

"I told you. I don't know." Hector's voice softened despite himself. "I never met the kid. I met the matchbooks and the stories about school and one time she bitched for five straight minutes because a teacher sent home a note saying the kid needed better pencils. Ayane said if the school wanted better pencils they could start paying courier rates. That's what I've got."

That was more than a route sheet. More than a line item. Not much. Enough.

Spike finished the whiskey and set the glass down harder than he meant to. The cheap liquor bit up through his nose.

"You got anything else?" he asked.

"No."

"Think."

"I've been thinking since she came in, and it hasn't improved anything." Hector rubbed both hands over his face. "She asked one more thing."

Spike waited.

"She asked whether the clinic cargo ever rode without the weapons convoy."

"Did it."

"Not if your system was functioning."

Your system.

Hector heard it too. He winced but didn't take it back.

"I said no," he went on. "I said those clinic boxes made it because the guns were paying for the road and the guards and the bribed inspectors. I said if the convoy stopped, everything tucked inside it got stranded with the rest. She didn't say anything after that for a while."

Spike felt the paper edge under his thumb.

"Then what," he said.

"Then she thanked me. Left cash under the glass. Took one of the matchbooks. And before she went out the door she turned around and asked if I thought the men who ran routes ever knew what they were carrying."

Spike didn't move.

"What'd you tell her?" he asked.

Hector gave him a tired look. "I said the smart ones knew enough. And the ones who lasted told themselves that was different."

The room stayed small around them.

At the back table a domino slapped down hard enough to make the woman at the race feed glance over, annoyed, then go back to her horses. Somebody laughed once in the alley outside and kept walking.

Hector took the manifest, folded it closed with more care than he had shown it at the start, and pushed it back across the bar.

"Drink up and go home," he said. "Or go wherever you people call home. I don't want this decade in here any longer than I need to."

"You close soon?"

"For you? Maybe."

"You always were hospitable."

"And you always did mistake bad habits for charm."

Spike almost said something back and didn't. There wasn't much point. Hector had already given him everything he had worth taking.

He slipped the folded papers into his jacket. "If she comes back."

"Yeah?"

"Call me."

Hector stared. "You got a number now?"

Spike took the receipt pad, wrote a comm frequency, and tore the strip off. Hector took it, looked at the numbers, and tucked them under the till without comment.

"If she comes back," Hector said, "I might call. If I think it helps."

"That's the idea."

"No," Hector said. "Helping you has never been the idea."

Spike stood, left enough cash to cover the drink and Hector's annoyance, and should've walked out then. Instead he sat back down when Hector moved to the far end of the bar and busied himself with the register.

The whiskey glass was empty. He gestured at it.

"One more."

Hector looked over. "You trying to punish yourself or me?"

"Depends what you've got left."

Hector poured anyway.

The second glass wasn't any better. Spike drank it slower.

***

Hector did his closing in pieces around him, not asking him to leave again but not pretending company either. He wiped tables. Turned the race feed off. Kicked the domino players out with the kind of insults that came from repetition, not feeling. The woman in the cleaner's uniform finished her beer and left without looking at Spike. The speaker died. The room got quieter but not calmer. Quiet in a bar after last call was just another kind of exposure.

By the time Hector disappeared into the back room with the day's cash box, Spike had the place to himself except for the humming fridge and the alley noise coming thin through the door.

He unfolded the manifest again.

The bar light was worse than the locker light. Everything on the page went yellow at the edges. The blue ink of the note stayed blue.

Ayane.

He tried, against his better judgment, to hang a face on the name.

Nothing stable came. A dark braid that might've belonged to somebody else. Hands with coolant dried in the lines. A shoulder in a cheap jacket, turned away. Every attempt felt stolen from a dozen other women he'd passed on docks without really seeing. Build her out of function and he'd be doing the same thing again. Courier. Widow. Mother. Quiet. Reliable. Those were job descriptions with grief stapled on afterward. Not a person.

So he let the face go.

What he had instead was the route.

Truck three on the south basin feeder because its reefer plug had been patched and held voltage better than the others. Night departures because daytime heat ate the coolant faster even in autumn. Guards in the front cab because the militia boys liked to stop the middle vehicles first. Pay the checkpoint at Marker Twelve in cash, not product, because the local commander had a brother with a pharmacy and got sentimental about medicine but not enough to let anything through for free. He remembered all of that. He remembered the turn radius in one washout bend south of Tharsis better than he remembered the names of some men he'd slept beside in hideouts.

That was the problem.

He had built a system where remembering the bend mattered and remembering the body waiting at the clinic didn't. It wasn't even personal failure in the dramatic sense. Nobody had sat him down and said forget the people. The whole machine was built to make forgetting feel professional.

He'd been good at it.

He put the page flat and laid two fingers over the recurring dates. Twenty-eight days. Thirty-one. Twenty-nine. Again. Again. Somebody had counted down to each truck. Maybe Ayane. Maybe the person in the clinic bed. Maybe both. He'd counted those same days by fuel burn and escort cost and whether the customs sweep on the east perimeter had shifted enough to reroute around. Same calendar, different stakes.

He could remember telling a dispatcher once that the reason to piggyback cold cargo inside an arms convoy was simple: nobody stole medicine from men carrying automatic rifles. At the time it had felt practical, almost clever. Now it sat in his head like a dirty trick. The guns had protected the drugs. The drugs had justified nothing. Both had gone down the same road because he had decided the road existed to be used.

The thought didn't produce revelation. No lightning. No sudden moral adulthood. Mostly it made the whiskey taste worse.

He leaned back and looked around the empty room.

The Radiant had always been built for people who wanted to be overlooked. Low bulbs. No windows. Tables scarred beyond noticing one more scar. Even the starburst logo on the matchbooks had faded into something that looked more like a wound than a sun. Men like Hector survived in places like this by shrinking their importance until the world stepped around them. Men like Spike had survived by going the other direction, by making themselves so useful that the world hesitated to kill them.

Neither trick had done much for the people underneath.

He turned the glass in his fingers. The cheap liquor clung to the sides in sluggish sheets. Somewhere in the back Hector dropped something metal and swore at it with real feeling. Spike almost smiled.

Then he looked at the bowl of matchbooks by the till.

One every trip, Hector had said.

He reached over and took one. The cardboard felt soft from humidity that hadn't been there. The front was printed with the bar's tired starburst. On the back there was nothing but a blank cream rectangle and a brown smudge from the stack. Easy to picture a kid filling it with ships. Not easy. Easier. There was a difference.

He set the matchbook beside the manifest and stared at both.

He still couldn't make himself feel the right thing in the clean, respectable way some people seemed able to. Ayane was a name. A route. A woman with a dead husband and a kid who liked drawing big booster flames on scrap cardboard. That last detail made her more real and didn't make her grievable, not yet. Maybe never in the way grief was supposed to work. He hadn't met her. He hadn't watched her sit at this bar or steal a matchbook or bitch about pencils. He wasn't going to perform a sorrow he hadn't earned.

But he also couldn't go back to the easier version. The one where the syndicate had been a private tragedy starring him. The one where his years in it had mainly happened to his body, his face, Julia, Vicious, the handful of names that fit cleanly inside his own mythology. That version had always been convenient. It made departure look like moral action when really he'd left for reasons a lot narrower and more selfish than that.

He'd walked away because of a woman and a man and the certainty that if he stayed he'd die wrong.

He had not walked away because the routes were killing strangers.

The routes had kept going anyway. Or tried to. People like Ayane had still been out there using what he'd built because there wasn't anything cleaner to use. That was the part he hadn't bothered imagining. Not the violence. The dependence. The fact that ugly systems didn't just hurt people; sometimes they became the only thing holding them up.

He folded the matchbook open and shut once. The striker rasped softly.

On the back wall, the shelf mirror showed him in warped pieces: shoulder, jaw, one eye darker than the other, cigarette hair gone to shadow. A man sitting alone in a bar with old paperwork and a child's drawing surface. He looked less haunted than tired. That seemed fair.

He thought about the question Hector had repeated. Did the men who ran routes ever know what they were carrying.

Know enough.

That had been Hector's answer. Smart men knew enough.

Spike had known enough to understand the cargo needed cold. Enough to know it went to a clinic and not a warehouse. Enough to know someone would notice if it didn't show. He had not known the face, the diagnosis, the family structure, the name. He had treated the missing specifics like absolution.

That trick was dead now.

He finished the second whiskey and stood.

Hector came back out just as Spike was tucking the manifest under his arm.

"You sleeping here?" Hector asked.

"Didn't seem sanitary."

"Nothing about you has ever been sanitary."

Spike held up the matchbook. "Taking this."

Hector squinted at it, then at him. "They're free."

"Didn't ask."

Hector leaned one hip against the bar. Up close his eyes were more bloodshot than tired, and the green plastic ring looked ridiculous in the low light. He caught Spike looking at it and curled his hand in.

"Granddaughter," he said, annoyed. "Before you start."

"Wasn't going to."

"Good. Because you'd be wrong about whatever it was."

Spike slipped the matchbook into the same inner pocket as the manifest. The cardboard tapped once against the folded paper.

Hector heard it. So did Spike.

"You all right?" Hector asked, and then immediately seemed to regret having asked it.

Spike put a hand on the bar top. Sticky varnish. Old grooves under the palm.

"No," he said.

It came out so flat Hector almost missed it.

He didn't push. That was another reason he'd lived this long.

"Yeah," Hector said after a moment. "Well. Join the club."

Spike left cash under the empty glass. Hector didn't count it in front of him.

At the door Spike stopped.

"If she comes back," he said without turning, "don't lie to her."

Hector gave a short, unhappy laugh. "That ship sailed, crashed, and got salvaged for parts."

"Try anyway."

He went out into the alley before Hector could answer.

The air hit cold and thin. Somewhere above the roofs a cargo shuttle passed, low enough that the metal doors along the alley trembled in sequence after it. Dust lifted, spun, settled again. Mars at night always sounded farther away than it was.

Spike stood there a second with one hand inside his jacket, fingertips resting against the folded manifest and the matchbook through the lining. Light things. Just paper and cardboard. Not much.

He could feel both of them on the walk back.
