# Chapter 1

Jet could have put the Bebop into the lot behind Mama Yun's half asleep. Some days he had. The angle lived in his hands now: clear the crooked water tower, slip past the laundromat vent that coughed steam over the alley, nose down between the noodle shop's back wall and the junk dealer's fence, then settle with a soft touch before the port stabilizer kissed the old yellow line somebody had painted there years ago and never repainted. It was one of the few things in his life that still came back the same every time.

The bridge smelled like old oil, cold metal, dog, and the scorched peppers he'd welded into last night's pan. Outside the front glass Mars was going dull with evening, the sky that bad amber-pink it always wore like a bruise. A red fuel light kept winking from the panel at him. Not urgent yet. Not good, either.

"I'm telling you," Faye said from behind him, "if Captain Sleepy over there hadn't decided he was the only one allowed to get close, we'd have had the bastard."

Spike had one boot braced against the console and looked exactly like a man who had nothing to do with anything. "You fired too early."

"I fired because you drifted."

"I didn't drift."

"You drifted right past him."

Jet kept his eyes on the alley mouth threading up beneath them. "Could both of you shut it for ten seconds."

"I'm not the one talking," Spike said.

Faye made a sound in the back of her throat. "That's because you think looking bored counts as a defense."

Ed was upside down in the doorway to the bridge, knees hooked over the frame, goggles hanging off one ear. "Deimos was lumpy," Ed announced. "Very bad potato moon for straight line zooming."

"Thank you, Ed," Jet said. "At least somebody on this ship can identify terrain."

"Jet also identified terrain," Ed said. "Jet identified it loudly."

Ein sneezed from under the co-pilot chair. Red dust puffed off his muzzle and drifted through the strip of light at his feet.

The alley behind Mama Yun's slid into view.

Something was in his spot.

Jet's hand tightened on the yoke before he'd even finished seeing it. The lot was small enough that anything parked there changed the whole shape of it, and this ship changed more than shape. It sat low and clean where the Bebop should have been, a dark wedge with the engines cold and the hull reflecting the noodle shop's back sign in a red broken stripe. No cargo pods. No rust blooms. No tarp over the port intake. Nothing local about it. It looked like it had landed by mistake in the middle of a neighborhood argument.

"What the hell is that?" Jet said.

Spike leaned forward a little, which for Spike was practically a sprint. "Not ours."

"Oh, good," Faye said. "I thought you'd finally bought something with actual lines."

Jet brought the Bebop around once, low and slow, trying to find a pilot in the windows or a service light on the hull or any sign that somebody was about to wave apologetically and move. Nothing. The ship was dark. The alley was dark. The old refrigeration unit behind the shop thumped and rattled in the same rhythm it always did, like it took personal offense at being kept alive.

He hit the local hailing frequency. "Docked vessel behind Yun Noodle. Identify and respond."

Static.

He did it again, slower, because half the idiots on Mars thought they could ignore authority if it sounded tired. "Docked vessel behind Yun Noodle, you're occupying a private arrangement. Respond."

Nothing.

Faye craned around the back of his chair. "Private arrangement. Listen to you."

"It's private."

"It's a dirt lot behind a noodle shop."

"It's my dirt lot behind a noodle shop."

Spike looked out at the dark ship with his usual low-watt calm. "Maybe they're dead."

Jet shot him a look. "Don't start."

"I'm not starting. If they're dead, we can move it."

"Great," Faye said. "Let's add corpse disposal to the evening. That'll help the budget."

Ed dropped from the doorway, landed barefoot, and padded to the console like gravity had been an opinion all along. "Sleepy ship says nothing. Sleepy ship is all zipped up."

Jet circled a second time. The fuel light blinked again, meaner now. He knew exactly how much each pointless minute cost because he'd bought the last tank himself in person, cash over a counter, with the kind of smile a clerk used when the amount on the screen was small enough to pity. He took the Bebop up and away before anger could make him stupid.

"Paid dock," he said.

Faye's groan filled the bridge. "No. No, come on."

"You want to hover all night?"

"I want people to stop taking things that belong to me by emotional association."

"It belongs to me by labor," Jet snapped. "I fixed that refrigeration unit three times this month."

Spike settled back. "Four."

Jet glanced at him. "What?"

"You fixed it four times."

"That's not helping."

"I know."

The paid lot was exactly two blocks east and felt farther because everybody in Tharsis knew it existed only for people whose luck had already run out. A rusted light pole leaned over the entrance. The payment kiosk was bolted to a concrete island and wore three layers of old transit stickers under a skin of dust. Someone had scratched NO CREDIT NO EXCUSES into the metal under the screen.

Jet put the Bebop down with more force than the landing gear deserved. The hull shuddered. Somewhere aft, something in the galley clanged in protest.

No one moved at first.

Then Faye said, "Maybe it'll be cheaper if we look pathetic in person."

"We look pathetic for free," Jet said, unbuckling. "It hasn't helped yet."

The kiosk wanted cash.

Of course it wanted cash.

Jet fed it the folded notes from the envelope he kept clipped under the bridge panel. The screen ate each one with a cheerful chirp that made him want to put his cybernetic fist through the glass. The total on the corner kept dropping, not enough. Still not enough. He checked his pockets, already knowing. Two coins. One washer. A screw.

"That's not a coin," Faye said.

"I'm aware."

She stood there hugging herself against the cold, mouth pinched, then dug into the pocket of her yellow shorts and slapped three small woolong coins into his palm. "I was going to use those for cigarettes."

"You don't smoke."

"I was going to start."

Spike came down the ramp slow as weather and set one coin beside the others. "Found that in the couch."

Jet looked at it. "You've known there was money in the couch."

"I knew there was something in the couch."

Ed patted down a dozen pockets as if a full salary might have migrated into the coat nobody remembered Ed wearing. A transit token, two bent paper clips, a dead battery, and half a candy wrapper appeared on the kiosk shelf.

"Close," Ed said.

Ein put one paw on Jet's boot and looked up with an expression that said he regretted having no currency.

Jet fed the coins in. The machine accepted all of them, thought about its own cruelty for a moment, and printed a receipt nobody needed.

"There," Faye said. "Excellent. We're officially parked with all the dignity money can buy."

"Move," Jet said, and pulled the receipt free before the wind could take it.

They walked back through the thinning evening with their collars up. Dust skated along the curb and collected in the corners of cheap neon signs. Ship traffic muttered overhead. The cold on Mars always felt dry enough to sand skin off the knuckles, and Jet could already feel it splitting the cut beside his thumb open again.

Mama Yun's front window was fogged with broth steam. Somebody had taped a handwritten special to the glass and crossed out the word beef without bothering to replace it with anything. Inside, fluorescent tubes buzzed overhead. The place was narrow, hot, and smelled exactly right: stock, peppers, old fryer oil, dish soap, damp coats. Jet felt his temper sharpen when he saw the back door from the counter. Two steps through there and the lot should have been his.

Mama Yun was behind the register laying strips of orange peel on a square of wax paper. She dried them out and threw them into tea or broth or whatever else needed more life than the market could afford that week. She looked up, saw Jet's face, and did not even bother asking what had gone wrong.

"Don't start hollering," she said. "I just got the baby next door to sleep."

"There's a ship in my spot."

"There sure is."

"You noticed."

"Jet, the lot's behind my shop. I notice the lot."

Faye slid onto a counter stool as if she had been walking for three days. "Please tell me you're about to say you don't know who owns it and we can all do crime."

Mama Yun snorted and turned her orange peels over with a finger. Her nails were cut square and stained dark from soy and tea. "No crime in my shop."

"Outside your shop, then," Faye said.

"Still no."

Jet planted both hands on the counter. The sticky laminate tugged at his palm. "How long has it been there?"

"Three days."

He stared at her. "Three days."

"Maybe a little more. Came in before dawn. Woke up my whole back wall."

"And you just let it sit there."

She finally looked at him over the top of her glasses. "A pilot paid for the lot."

"Paid who."

"Me."

"For how long."

"Six months."

For a second the shop noise thinned out around the words. The hiss from the pot, the clatter of bowls, Faye breathing through her teeth, even Spike shifting his weight by the door. Six months. Not a misunderstanding. Not somebody stopping to piss and buy noodles. Six months meant planning. Six months meant someone had looked at that stupid patch of cracked dirt behind a noodle shop and decided it was worth money.

"Six months," Jet said again, because repetition was the only thing standing between him and taking this personally in public.

"Cash," Mama Yun said. "Up front. Exact. Clean bills. Kept the helmet on, too, before you ask, so no, I don't know the face."

"You took money from somebody wearing a helmet inside."

"I take money from anybody whose money spends."

"That was my arrangement."

"Your arrangement's with my refrigerator. The refrigerator's working."

Jet opened his mouth. Closed it. Opened it again. "Mama."

"Don't Mama me. If your spot means that much, next time pay six months."

"With what?"

That got a shrug out of her. Not cruel. Just Mars. "Catch your bounty."

Spike pushed off the doorframe. "That part didn't happen."

"I had noticed," Mama Yun said.

Faye barked a laugh. Jet wanted to be mad at her too and did not have the spare energy.

"Did the pilot say anything else?" he asked.

"Asked if the lot stayed quiet. Asked if anybody messed with ships behind my place. Asked if there was a mechanic nearby for refrigeration and I said yes, but he's already temperamental enough without strangers using his lot." She pointed a soy-stained finger at the stools. "Sit down. I've got noodles. You can be angry and eat at the same time. Lots of people do."

Jet looked at the back door again, as if the dark ship might take the hint and remove itself. Nothing moved but steam and the hanging fly strips.

Faye was already seated. "I'm not leaving without food. If we're this poor, I want to be poor sitting down."

Spike shrugged once and took the stool beside her.

Ed had found the end stool and was staring at the back window, head tilted, as if the ship might start talking if everybody else got quiet enough.

Jet stayed standing another beat because standing made it feel like he still had options. Then he sat.

Mama Yun slapped bowls down in front of them with the efficient contempt of a woman who had kept hungry idiots alive for too long to be impressed by any of them.

There was, of course, no beef.

* * *

The broth was hot enough to burn a line down Jet's throat and thin enough that he could see the bottom of the bowl through it when the noodles shifted. Pepper strips floated on top like they were apologizing for something. Mama Yun had given them extra scallion, which was how she handled pity when she could not stand to show it plain.

Faye poked at the bowl with her chopsticks. "This is decorative."

"It's dinner," Jet said.

"Dinner usually has some kind of material commitment."

"You want material commitment, go marry a butcher."

"With what dowry? We just parked it."

Spike ate like a man who had no quarrel with scarcity as long as it arrived in liquid form. "Could be worse."

Faye turned on him at once. "Don't do that."

"Do what."

"That thing where you act like deprivation is a hobby."

"I'm eating noodles."

"You're performing noodles."

Jet rubbed a hand over his face. "Can we get through one bowl before the postmortem."

"There's no postmortem," Faye said. "There's an obvious answer."

Spike didn't look up. "You shot."

"I shot because you drifted."

"You said it again."

"Because it happened again."

Jet pointed his chopsticks at both of them. "The target was boxed in."

"He wasn't boxed in," Faye said. "He had the whole damn ring to run."

"He had one lane," Jet said. "One. Spike was high, you were low, and if either of you had held a clean line for four more seconds instead of trying to win whatever private argument you were having with physics, we'd be eating protein."

"I held my line," Spike said.

"Like hell you did."

"Jet," Faye said, leaning in, "he cut right when I had a shot. What was I supposed to do, admire him."

"Miss quieter," Spike said.

Faye put the chopsticks down with enough force to splash broth on the counter. "I didn't miss quieter. He juked because you came in hot."

"He juked because you lit him up."

"He knew we were there."

"Not till you fired."

"He heard your engine from space."

Ed slurped noodles, thoughtful. "Deimos heard all the engines. Deimos was listening. Big potato ears."

No one answered.

Ed slurped again. "Anyway, bounty man was wiggly."

"Thank you," Faye said.

"That wasn't support," Spike said.

"It was more support than I've gotten from anyone with a pulse."

Jet turned to Spike. "Why did you dive under him."

Spike took his time swallowing. "Because if I went high, he'd have bounced off the rock face and shot past Faye's side."

"He did that anyway."

"Yeah."

"So it didn't work."

Spike lifted one shoulder. "Usually does."

"Usually doesn't pay for fuel either."

That landed. Not dramatically. Nobody shut up in revelation. They just went a little less loose around the mouth.

Faye looked back into her bowl. "So what, this is my fault now too?"

"Everything is everybody's fault," Jet said. "That's the system."

"That's not a system."

"It's on this ship."

Mama Yun came down the counter with the stock pot in both hands. "If you're going to keep arguing, move your elbows in. You're taking up six seats with four bodies."

Jet slid his elbows in automatically. Spike moved maybe a quarter inch. Faye didn't move at all until Mama Yun hit the back of her stool with the pot handle.

"Ow."

"Move."

Faye moved.

Mama Yun topped off Ed's broth first. Ed had somehow already finished half the bowl and was feeding noodles under the counter to Ein one strand at a time when Mama Yun wasn't looking and in fact when she was. She ignored it with the resignation of somebody who had once decided dogs did not belong in the shop and then run out of energy for principles.

"That ship isn't local," Jet said.

"I know what local looks like," Mama Yun said.

"You hear anybody ask after it."

"No."

"Anybody get off it."

"No."

"Anybody go near it."

"No."

"Then where's the pilot."

"Somewhere else."

Faye rolled her eyes so hard it looked painful. "Unbelievable. We're being menaced by parking."

"We're being bled by parking," Jet said.

"Same thing if you're poor enough," she shot back.

There it was. The true shape of it, ugly and stupid and impossible to make dignified. The paid lot was not ruinous by itself. Nothing ever was. It was one more leak in a ship that lived by leaks: fuel, ammo, noodles, filters, parts, docking, fines, coffee, laundry, cards, cigarettes Faye claimed not to smoke, dog food, whatever Ed had once ordered off a forgotten network account and insisted was educational. They never went broke in one clean gesture. They went broke by abrasion.

Jet took another mouthful of broth and tasted salt where the skin on his thumb had split. The spot behind the shop had never been just convenience. It was no meter and no questions and a walk of six seconds from the back ramp to hot food. It was Mama Yun yelling if somebody touched the Bebop. It was a place to come back to that had not been sold out from under him by some office or auction or bureaucrat with a stamp. He had built that spot the only way people like him got to build anything permanent, by fixing a bad compressor again and again until a woman with a noodle shop started saying your ship instead of that ship.

Spike must have seen some version of the thought on his face, because he said, quieter, "Maybe they leave tomorrow."

"They paid six months."

"Could still leave tomorrow."

"That would be a pretty expensive joke."

"People spend money on bad jokes all the time," Faye said. "You've met gamblers."

"You've been gamblers," Jet said.

"Yeah, and look how happy it made me."

Mama Yun set a small dish of pickled greens between them. "Don't pretend none of you chooses this life every day."

Faye snorted. "Some of us aren't choosing. Some of us are being extorted by history."

"You still want the greens or not."

Faye took some. "I didn't say no."

Spike reached for the same dish at the same time and got his hand slapped away with Faye's chopsticks.

"Get your own."

"It's in the middle."

"Emotionally it's mine."

"Everything's yours by emotional association tonight."

"Shut up."

Jet should have cut it off. He knew that. Stress made his voice louder and his sentences longer and his patience thinner than paper, which was one reason the crew got away with so much until they did not. But there was relief in listening to them fight about stupid things while something real and ugly sat one alley over with its engines off. If they were still squabbling over angles and noodles and whose coin had saved the docking meter, then the problem was still ordinary enough to live with.

Ed, though, had stopped paying attention to the bowl.

Jet noticed because Ed was almost never pointed in only one direction. Usually there was motion leaking out somewhere, a foot kicking, fingers drumming, eyes slipping off after any new sound like they'd been personally invited. Now Ed sat hunched on the stool with noodles cooling untouched and stared through the steamed back window toward the lot.

"What," Jet said.

Ed didn't look away. "Ship is boring."

"Great," Faye said. "Good. I hate exciting ships."

"Boring on top," Ed said. "Sneaky underneath."

Spike glanced over. "What's that mean."

Ed turned at last, grin back in place but crooked now because half the brain was still elsewhere. "Means registration is neat and tidy and says hello officer have a nice day. Means no warrants, no hot tags, no pirate pings, no little red fireworks. Very proper little fish."

Jet felt his shoulders unknot one grudging inch. "So it belongs to some asshole with money."

"Many assholes have money," Faye said.

"That's true," Mama Yun put in.

"Can you get a name?" Jet asked.

"Ed can get all the names. Ship has many names. Factory name, registry name, maintenance name, secret little internal network name if the owner was lonely." Ed slurped broth, then brightened. "Ed will look."

"After you eat," Jet said.

Ed stuffed the rest of the noodles into Ed's mouth in one improbable, legal gesture and kicked both feet in the air once to show compliance.

Faye stared. "That's disgusting."

Ed swallowed. "Efficient."

"No," Faye said. "Disgusting first."

The argument took its old channels from there. Faye wanted to know whether a ship that clean could be worth stealing. Spike said if she had to ask, she couldn't afford it. Faye said that had never stopped anyone worth respecting. Jet asked whether either of them planned to fund a legal dispute over dirt. Mama Yun told them all to either order tea or get out because the counter had to turn. Nobody ordered tea. They stayed until the bowls were empty and the heat from the broth ran out of them and Mars reminded them it was waiting outside.

On the way back, Faye walked with her hands jammed under her arms and muttered a running list of the things she hated: cold weather, rich strangers, exact change, men who flew like they had spare parts, noodles that insulted the concept of beef, and anybody who wore a helmet indoors. Spike let most of it slide past him. Once he said, "You forgot parking." Faye told him to die carefully. Ed skipped ahead in erratic diagonals, then ran back, then stopped in the middle of the sidewalk to study the glow from the occupied lot with Ein under one arm like a stolen loaf of bread.

Jet kept looking too. Every angle on the ship offended him more. It did not belong there. Not in any legal sense that mattered. In the practical sense. In the weight-and-silence sense. In the sense of returning to the same corner of the world and expecting it to know your shape.

By the time they climbed the ramp back into the Bebop, the ship's heat had dropped enough that the deck plates carried the cold through Jet's boots.

He hated the paid lot. He hated the receipt in his pocket. He hated that the back window over the galley looked onto a cracked lamp post instead of the steam vent behind Mama Yun's.

He hated, most of all, that he was going to sleep angry over parking and wake up still poor.

* * *

Night on the paid dock had a different sound than night behind the noodle shop. Mama Yun's lot carried broth steam, dishwater, the slap of back doors, somebody arguing in the apartments upstairs, the compressor kicking on like a cough. The paid lot had a security lamp buzzing over concrete and the faint grind of a meter somewhere inside the kiosk, measuring hours into money. It sounded like being charged for breathing.

Jet couldn't settle.

He took apart the galley cabinet latch that had been sticking since last week and spread the pieces out on a towel at the table because his hands needed something to do that wasn't strangling strangers. The screw heads were soft with age. The spring inside had lost tension. He cleaned each bit anyway, wiped the old grease off, put on fresh, checked the hinge alignment, took it apart again. The cabinet still held shut if you lifted it a fraction and pressed.

Spike had drifted to the common room couch with one knee up and a cigarette tucked behind his ear unsmoked because Jet had threatened to toss him out the airlock if he lit up near the fuel filters again. Faye was on the far end in her boots, not quite lying down, not quite sitting, flipping a battered deck of cards from hand to hand without looking at them. Every third throw she lost one and swore at the floor. Ein had wedged himself under the table where the heating vent still worked best.

Ed sat cross-legged at the terminal in the corner, back bent, hair haloed by monitor light. The screen threw pale columns over the walls and the nearest window. It made the room look thinner than it was.

"What've you got," Jet said without looking up from the latch.

"Polite fish," Ed said. "Very proper. Registration current. Taxes boring. No flaggy flags. No bounty boings. Docking permissions paid up because Mama Yun got surprise money and did not cry."

Faye flicked a card, missed the deck, and let it lie where it landed. "Maybe it's a honeymoon."

"In that alley?" Jet said.

"People have started marriages on less."

Spike closed his eyes. "You'd know."

Faye's card snapped a little louder between her fingers. "What's that supposed to mean."

"Nothing."

"You always say nothing when you mean something annoying."

"Saves time."

Jet fitted the cleaned spring back into the latch. "Can we have one hour. One hour where nobody takes the bait every time somebody breathes."

"He doesn't have to make it sound like bait," Faye said.

"You don't have to bite it."

"You say that like you don't."

Jet looked up long enough to glare at both of them equally. "I'm trying to repair something."

Spike opened one eye. "That thing's been fine for three years."

"It sticks."

"Only if you pull it wrong."

"Everything works if you use it exactly right, Spike. That's not the point."

Faye barked a short laugh that died quickly, not because anything sad had entered the room, just because they were all too tired to keep a laugh alive unless it was earning its keep.

Jet set the latch down. The galley smelled faintly of soap and old broth. The receipt from the kiosk sat under the sugar tin because he'd shoved it there and not thrown it out yet. He could feel it in the room like an unpaid insult.

Ed made a little humming noise and pulled another window across the screen. Data reflected in Ed's goggles, scrolled past, changed shape. Ed worked like some people played piano, not because the comparison was poetic but because the hands and the ears seemed to be doing different jobs while the rest of the body kept time.

"Can we move it?" Jet asked.

"Physically or legally or sneakily?" Ed said.

"The easiest one."

"Sneakily is easiest," Faye said.

"I wasn't asking you."

"You should ask me things. I've got plenty of criminal ideas."

"That's never been in doubt."

Ed stretched both arms over Ed's head without taking eyes off the screen. "Big ship could maybe tug little ship if little ship was sleepy enough and if nobody had alarms and if Jet wanted a tiny war."

"I don't want a tiny war," Jet said.

Spike put the cigarette in his fingers and turned it without lighting it. "What kind do you want."

"I want my damn spot."

"That's not a kind of war."

"It's on Mars."

That got an actual smile out of Spike, quick and reluctant. Faye saw it and looked offended on principle.

"Tomorrow," Jet said, more to himself than to anyone, "I'm asking around. Somebody saw where that thing came from."

"Or nobody did," Spike said.

"Then somebody saw the bills."

"Clean bills," Faye said. "Fancy."

"Rich doesn't mean fancy."

"It means rich enough to be annoying."

Jet checked the hinge alignment against the cabinet door. Still off by a hair. He reached for the screwdriver again.

The Bebop ticked around them as the outer hull cooled. Life support hummed. Somewhere down the corridor a loose panel rattled once and settled. Ed's keyboard clicked in shifting bursts. Outside, a ship came in high over the district, engines thinning out as it looked for somewhere better than the lot they were stuck in.

For a while the room relaxed into the kind of near-silence only people who lived on top of each other could manage. Not peace. Familiar truce. Faye kept throwing cards and gathering them. Spike watched the window. Jet worked the latch. Ed dived deeper.

"Huh," Ed said.

Jet did not look up immediately. On the Bebop, huh covered a lot of ground. Burned toast. Missing socks. A government data breach. Ein doing something with a shoe that wasn't technically illegal. "What kind of huh."

"Round huh."

"That's not a kind."

"Now it's a kind."

Faye flung another card. "Can the round huh wait till morning."

"Morning's still round," Ed said, distracted.

Jet reattached the latch to the cabinet and tested it. It clicked shut smoothly for the first time in a week. He should have felt better. Instead the small success only made the real problem look pettier and therefore meaner.

Ed stopped humming.

That got his attention.

Jet looked over. Ed's head had gone very still, not bent and bobbing through six ideas at once, just still. One hand rested on the keyboard. The other hovered over the trackpad without moving. On the screen, lines of registry data had given way to something older-looking, blockier, archive formatting dragged up from whatever basement the bounty system kept under the bounty system.

"Ed?" Jet said.

No answer.

Spike sat up a little. Faye lowered the cards.

Jet pushed back from the table. The chair legs scraped. "What'd you hit."

Ed's fingers twitched once and opened a narrower pane inside the first. More strings. More numbers. A pulse map of the ship's ID signal, repeated over time. Clean on the surface, then not clean. A hitch. A pattern in the spacing that didn't belong in ordinary civilian traffic. Jet didn't know exactly what he was looking at, only that it had stopped Ed cold.

"I said what'd you hit."

Ed turned then.

Jet had seen Ed excited, smug, hungry, feverish, upside down, asleep in a vent, laughing so hard no sound came out. He had seen Ed faking seriousness to torment adults and real seriousness only once or twice, brief as weather. This was neither. The usual slack play in Ed's face was gone. The eyes looked fixed to something far behind the screen, something only Ed could see yet.

"It's clean," Ed said softly. "Too clean in one place, and wrong in another."

Spike was already on his feet. "Wrong how."

Ed did not answer him. Ed was still looking at Jet.

"Jet," Ed said, and now even the voice had lost its bounce. "Come here."
