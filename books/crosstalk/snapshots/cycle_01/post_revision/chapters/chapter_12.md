# Chapter 12

Jet waited until the common room emptied out, then picked the bar for one reason only: nobody in it looked likely to know his name.

It sat in the Tharsis freight district between a brake shop and a parts recycler, with a narrow door and a sign that had once been blue and had long since given up trying. The room smelled like old fry oil, wet coats, and the kind of disinfectant that never quite covered the beer soaked into wood. A game was muttering on a wall screen with the sound turned low. Three dockworkers in reflective vests were playing cards under a dead neon tube. Nobody looked up when Jet came in. That was the point.

He took the far end of the bar, where a leak stain had spread across the ceiling tiles in a shape like a hand with too many fingers. There were eight smaller stains around it. He counted them once, then again, then once more because the second count came up nine and he did not trust nine.

"You eating?" the bartender asked.

She had a nicotine patch cut cleanly in half and stuck under one ear, as if she did not trust herself with the full dose. Her gray hair was pinned up with a hex key. Every few minutes she slid a racing form out from under the till, marked something with a golf pencil, and tucked it away again.

"No."

"Kitchen's dead anyway."

"Whiskey."

She poured without asking which kind because this was not that kind of place. Jet put cash on the bar. She looked at it, looked at him, took one bill, and pushed the rest back with two fingers.

"That's enough."

"For this?"

"For one."

Jet took the glass. "One's fine."

It was not good whiskey. That was fine too. He was not there for good. He was there because if he stayed on the Bebop he would start fixing things. The galley cabinet that stuck in damp weather. The coffee maker that needed the contact points cleaned. The loose hinge on Ed's console chair. He would do them one after another and call it productivity and never sit still long enough for the question to get where it wanted to go.

He drank a little. The bar's ceiling had been patched with three mismatched tiles and one square of sheet metal bolted in place crooked. Water had found the edges anyway. He counted the stains again.

Not was I responsible.

That was the easy one, or easier. Responsibility could be argued. You could lay out the facts. He had not known Daichi Kozuki. He had never met the man, never taken his statement, never handed him a wire, never sent him into a room. His partner had handled informants off-book. That much Donovan had made clear. Jet could build a case for himself if he wanted to. Wrong department. Wrong desk. Wrong day. No direct hand on the knife.

The other question had no paperwork in it.

Did I choose not to see.

He drank again and tasted metal from the cheap sink water they had cut the bottle with, or maybe that was his own mouth. Across the room one of the dockworkers slapped cards onto the table and laughed through a cough that sounded wet enough to worry a doctor and cheap enough not to see one. The screen above the bar flashed a score. Someone swore at it without any real commitment. The room kept going. Jet kept looking up.

There had been things.

His partner coming in too pleased with himself on weeks no case had broken right. Expense on a salary that did not support expense. An informant file closed too fast. A report routed around him with a shrug and a, don't worry about it, Black, this one came down from upstairs. The specific look precinct cops got when they knew something dirty was moving through the building and had already decided whether it was their problem. Jet had known that look. He had worn it himself.

Not every time. Not always. But enough.

He had told himself the job was big and ugly and built on compromise. He had told himself you picked the piece in front of you and worked it clean because if you stood in the hallway screaming about every rotten thing in the structure nothing got done. He had told himself that staying useful was better than staying pure. He had told himself a lot of things that were not lies exactly. That was what made them dangerous. They fit in the mouth too well.

The bartender drifted back down the bar, wiped a ring of moisture that was not there, then wiped it again.

"You keep checking that ceiling," she said. "It'll still be ugly in five minutes."

Jet looked at his drink. "I wasn't expecting improvement."

"Then you've got the right bar."

She moved away. That was the whole exchange. It still got under his skin because it was true in the wrong direction.

He saw his old precinct as clearly as if he had stepped out the back door and walked there instead. The bad fluorescent light that flattened everybody's face. The coffee that tasted like pennies. The cracked vinyl on the interview room chairs. His partner with a tie loosened and a grin too easy for the hour, saying he'd take the informant meet because Jet had paperwork. Jet believing him because Jet did have paperwork. Jet being grateful, maybe, for one less dirty errand in a job made out of dirty errands. That gratitude was what kept snagging.

If he had pushed harder, if he had said no, if he had gone where he was not invited, maybe he would have found something. Maybe he would have found Daichi Kozuki alive. Maybe he would have found nothing. There was no clean way to know now. That was another mercy he did not deserve: the dead did not come back and tell you where the road forked.

Faye's voice came back to him from the Bebop common room, flat and mean because flat and mean was safer than the other thing. You two are standing here talking routes and precincts like that's the whole story. Like a dead woman borrowing my face is just paperwork. Jet had snapped back at her, because of course he had. Spike had gone hard and cold in that way of his that made a room feel smaller. Ed had gone quiet in the middle of it, which had been the worst part. They had all seen too much in one another at once, and none of them knew how to do that politely.

He lifted the glass, found it empty, set it down.

"Another?" the bartender asked.

Jet thought about the walk back. About climbing the Bebop's ramp with whiskey in him and words in his throat and no place to put either. He thought about staying until the freight shift turned over and the room got meaner. He thought about not going back at all, which lasted less than a second because there was nowhere else to go.

"Yeah," he said.

She poured. This time her half patch had started to peel at one corner. She smoothed it back with the side of her thumb.

"You work nights?" Jet asked, mostly to hear a human voice say something ordinary.

"I work when Joro's gout acts up." She jerked her chin toward a door marked PRIVATE. "Which is most nights when it rains and all nights when he thinks it might rain."

"That bad?"

"He says his feet can feel weather from two districts over. I say he's lazy."

Jet gave a short breath that might have been a laugh if anybody had asked too much of it.

"You from freight?" she asked.

"Used to be police."

She nodded once, with no visible interest either way. "That so."

"Used to be." He heard how he was repeating himself and stopped.

She set a bowl of pretzels near his elbow without comment. One was burned black on one side. Jet took it because refusing would make it strange. Salt hit his tongue. He had not realized he was hungry.

The thing about corruption, the real thing, was that most of it did not look like a movie. No envelopes under tables, no confession in a smoky room, no man rubbing his hands and saying let us sell out an informant tonight. Mostly it looked like fatigue. Delegation. One detective handling one class of source because he was "better with them." Forms signed without reading the attachment because a fight had broken out in holding and somebody was yelling for you now. A name redacted for "operational reasons" and nobody asking who had decided the reasons. The machine hid itself inside everybody's shift being too long and everybody's patience being too thin and everybody wanting, just once, to go home on time.

He had called that realism. Mature judgment. Knowing how the world worked.

Maybe it had been cowardice with a badge clipped over it.

He shut his eyes. When he opened them the ceiling stains had blurred together. Ten, maybe. Eleven. He had lost count.

He thought of Daichi not as a man, because he did not have enough to build a man with, but as pressure in the shape of absence. A desk where a file should have been. An access code still live after death. A child somewhere growing up with the consequences. He thought of the pilot and of someone spending years pulling threads because the people who had failed her got to move on if nobody made them look back.

Jet rubbed at his beard. The skin under it felt raw.

Had he chosen not to see?

Yes, probably, sometimes, in the way people working inside a bad structure always did. Not because he wanted anyone dead. Not because he was bought. Because seeing all of it every day and refusing all of it every day were two different jobs, and he had picked the job that still let him cash a paycheck and believe he was one of the good ones.

It was not a dramatic answer. That was what made it stick.

He finished the second whiskey and stood. His stool legs scraped. One of the card players glanced over and then away.

"You leaving already?" the bartender asked.

"Yeah."

"Roof's still ugly."

Jet put two fingers on the bar in something like thanks. "I figured."

Outside, the freight district air had the cold iron bite Mars got after midnight. The sky over Tharsis was the color of rust washed thin. He shoved his hands into his pockets and started walking back toward the paid docking lot, back toward the ship, back toward the people he had just spent an hour avoiding and would spend the rest of the night listening for anyway.

Spike took the stairs to the roof because the elevator smelled like piss and because climbing felt closer to earning the air.

The building was nine stories of old concrete and patched conduit. Up top, the city spread in stacked layers of light and dust, service roads threading between warehouse blocks, beacon towers blinking red through the haze. Beyond that there was only dark land and the wrong-colored sky. Mars never did stars right from inside a settlement. The atmosphere held on to too much dust, too much old industry, too much reflected light. The sky looked used.

He sat on the edge and let his legs hang. His shoes knocked once against the wall below and then went still. He lit a cigarette, drew deep, watched the tip burn down clean.

Ayane's name still did not attach to much. Hector's bar. Quiet, reliable, had a kid. Ran the Tharsis-Hellas route. That was about it. A few facts and a note on a manifest, She needed this to live, written in a hand Spike had not recognized then and would not recognize now if it passed him on the street. If he shut his eyes he could not picture her. He got a blurred shape in coveralls, maybe, because that was what couriers wore, and then Julia pushed through anyway. Julia leaning against a doorframe with her hair pinned up loose. Julia in a red room. Julia turning her head half an inch before she smiled, like she was checking whether the smile was worth the trouble.

That was the problem.

He could picture Julia without trying. He could picture Vicious. Mao. Annie. Men he had hit. Men who had tried to hit him. Rooms he had bled in. Guns. The color of certain hallways. The smell of gun oil in the back of a warehouse at three in the morning. His memory was excellent for the things he had believed were his story.

Ayane was not his story. Not then.

He flicked ash into the dark. It caught the rooftop wind and disappeared before it reached the alley.

He could still see the south depot loading bay. Blue plastic wrap shining under bad light. His alias on a clipboard in block letters. LIN SHAO-FENG. Long crates with the serials filed off. Two white cold boxes strapped on top with red tape and KEEP COLD stamped crooked across the side. He had signed for all of it. Counted seals. Waved the loader through.

One night a latch sprang when a pallet hit the floor too hard. Foil packs spilled out and slid into the heel of his shoe. A dock kid in orange gloves crouched to scoop them up and said, "Medicine?"

Spike had said, "Probably," because the truck was late and the bay chief was already yelling.

Then he had handed the clipboard back and kept the line moving.

He knew now what those boxes might have been. Something for Ayane. Something for somebody like Ayane. A body that needed a shipment to come through on time or it started eating itself. He could picture the boxes better than he could picture her.

He took another drag. The smoke scraped his throat. Good.

He did not feel grief for Ayane. He felt the work. The crate edge in his palms. The marker stink off the manifest. Julia laughing once because the alias on his papers sounded fake even for syndicate business. He felt how easy it had been to keep the pen moving and not ask. That was what he had.

Julia had been enough to make him leave. Her face. Vicious's face. One woman, one man, one mess close enough to touch. That was the scale he had used then.

He looked up. No stars. Just haze and the reflected underglow of a city that could not stop burning fuel long enough to let the sky through.

If one of those boxes had been marked IMMUNOSUPPRESSANTS in letters big enough for him to read from across the room, would it have changed anything then?

Maybe not.

That was the part he hated enough to keep turning it over.

Maybe he would have asked who they were for. Maybe he would have laughed and said somebody upstairs had weird priorities. Maybe he would have done exactly what he had always done, moved the shipment and kept breathing.

He remembered what he had actually looked at on those runs: dock numbers. Seal colors. Which men were armed. Whether the route stayed clear. The rest had gone past him.

He smoked to the filter and lit another from the first. Below him a tram hissed around a turn. Somewhere in the next block a couple were fighting with the window open, the words flattened by distance into pure cadence: accusation, defense, accusation, a long stretch of both talking at once. Life, continuing badly.

He thought about the Bebop without wanting to. Jet, probably not sleeping. Faye, somewhere being furious in private. Ed, maybe talking to the dog, maybe doing the thing where the silence in the ship got so loud it turned into a game first because thirteen-year-olds were smarter than adults and meaner to themselves. They had all gone separate because that was the only way any of them knew how to handle being seen. Still, he could feel the lines running back to the ship, taut as wire.

Family was too sentimental a word to use on himself. He did not use it. He pulled his communicator from his pocket, thumbed the Bebop channel live, and listened to the carrier hiss for a second.

"I'm out a while," he said. "Leave comms up."

The cigarette ember pulsed between his fingers. Julia, Ayane, the routes, the note on the manifest, the unseen hand arranging a trail through all of it. He could not retrofit compassion he had not felt. He could not decide, after the fact, to have been a different man.

He could stop saying it had nothing to do with him.

He ground the second cigarette out on the concrete beside him, then immediately regretted dirtying the roof of a building he did not live in. That was new too. He rubbed at the black mark with the sole of his shoe until it was only a smudge.

When he finally stood, his knees clicked. He put both hands in his pockets and looked out over the city one more time, as if the right answer might be written somewhere in warehouse lights.

It was not.

He headed down the stairs with the Bebop channel still open.

Faye chose the bath house because you could pay cash, because the lockers still took coins instead of identity taps, and because in a room full of naked women nobody had the energy to make a spectacle out of another body.

The lobby was all cracked cream tile and fogged plastic plants. A woman behind the desk wore a transparent green visor and had painted tiny silver stars around one eye, half of them flaking off. She was reading a folded romance magazine with the cover torn away. Without looking up she slid a locker key across the counter and said, "Towel costs extra."

"Of course it does," Faye said.

"Soap too."

"You're a criminal."

"Bath house," the woman said, still reading. "Close enough."

Faye paid. The key was attached to a square of chipped yellow plastic stamped 24. In the changing room she undressed without hurry and without performance, folding her clothes more neatly than she would have admitted to anybody. The air was warm enough to make the skin along her arms pebble anyway. Somebody down the row was humming off-key while she wrestled with a compression wrap around one knee. Somebody else had brought in a baby and was apologizing to nobody in particular every time it squeaked. The room smelled like steam, mineral salts, cheap soap, and wet wood.

She caught herself in the mirror over the sinks, saw the usual pieces line up. Dark hair damp at the temples from the heat. Green eyes. Collarbones like hooks. The body she lived in, the body other people noticed first, the body she had learned how to use because everything else about her past came in fragments or bills.

She turned, looked over her shoulder.

No scar on the left shoulder blade.

There never had been. She knew that now. Knew it with records and dates and a clerk's apology and Miriam's tired certainty and the stupid little fact of a blue dress at a gambling table on Ganymede while Faye Valentine, the original issue, had been on ice with a tube in her throat and debt accruing interest in her name.

She went through the swinging door into the bath room. Heat hit her face. The main pool was sunk below the tile lip, mineral water gone cloudy with steam. A row of shower heads ticked along one wall. Women sat around the edges with their shoulders shining wet, talking about food prices, somebody's son, a fungus problem in a hydroponics tower. Nothing anybody said mattered. It was perfect.

Faye eased down the steps until the water took her at the ribs, the waist, the chest. The heat was almost painful for a second. Then it turned into weightlessness. She let herself drift away from the edge and tipped her head back so her ears slid under. The room dulled at once. Voices became blurs. Water held her up without asking questions.

This was why she had come.

Not for cleanliness. The Bebop had water. Intermittent, rusty at the start of a cycle, but water. She had come because floating erased some of the hard border of the body. In water you got less exact. Less pinned down by seams and straps and the fit of your own skin. Tonight that seemed useful.

Steam had filmed the cedar planks above into a dark sheen. Condensation gathered, fattened, fell in slow drops that dimpled the surface near her cheek. Every ripple took the lights overhead and broke them into moving coins. When she let her eyes go soft, her reflection came apart and reassembled ten times a second.

That was the part that kept tilting the room: she was not the only version. Amnesia had been one kind of damage. Bad enough. Waking up in the future broke, hunted by debt collectors, with a name and a face and almost nothing under them except paperwork and instinct. She had already spent years living with the fact that there had been a previous self she could not access. Some schoolgirl, some daughter, some woman who liked or hated things that meant nothing to the person Faye had become. That was one ghost. Manageable, if you stayed mean and kept moving.

This was different because another woman had been walking around under her name while Faye herself was not even awake to object. Another woman cashing chips, signing forms, tipping waitresses, maybe getting drunk, maybe getting laid, maybe laughing in a voice Miriam could still hear clear enough to swear by. Another Faye Valentine entering rooms and leaving impressions. More real, in some places, than the original.

If Miriam walked into this bath room right now, maybe she would recognize Faye. Maybe she would not. Maybe she would hesitate because memory had been carrying a scar Faye did not have. Maybe she would say, no, not you, the other one. The one in the blue dress. The one who tipped. The one who touched her collarbone just so.

Faye's hand moved before she could stop it. Two fingers to the notch above her sternum.

She snatched it away.

That had been hers. Hadn't it? Or maybe it had only become hers because somebody at some point told her she used to do it, or because she saw it in a record, or because she watched someone watching her and learned a habit from the watching. How many things in a person were original? How many were theft? How many were just repetition with the source lost?

"Too hot?" a woman near the edge asked.

Faye turned her head. The speaker was maybe sixty, maybe forty hard years made into sixty, with a waterproof hearing aid tucked behind one ear and a tattoo of a tiny wrench on the inside of her wrist. She was holding a soaked washcloth over the back of her neck.

"No."

"You looked mad at the water."

"I'm mad at a lot of things."

The woman considered that. "Water doesn't care."

"Yeah, I've noticed."

That was all. The woman went back to staring at nothing. Faye let the exchange settle and disappear. It helped, weirdly. People saying obvious things for no reason. Ordinary talk, going nowhere. Proof that not everything in the world had arranged itself around her crisis.

An attendant padded through in rubber sandals with a skimmer net, fishing hair from the corners. In the hot room beyond, somebody laughed so hard she started coughing. A child splashed, got hushed, splashed again. Faye drifted another few inches and let the back of her head bump tile.

The woman who had used her name was dead.

Faye still did not have a real shape for her. Not a name, not one that felt solid, just a dead woman whose body had rejected itself because the drugs keeping it in line had stopped coming regular. Faye tried to imagine anger clean enough to hold that fact and failed. The theft was real. The violation was real. Somebody had sold Faye's life out from under her while she slept and another woman had climbed into it because the alternative was what, exactly, dying sooner? Staying herself and getting turned away from clinics? Taking her child by the hand and admitting there was no legal version of her left that could survive?

It was wrong, and that still did not make it simple.

She hated that it was not simple.

She slid lower until the water touched her lower lip. Warm mineral taste. A little bitter. Not unpleasant. The ceiling above her blurred again. Three planks darker than the others where steam had gotten in deep. Beyond them, structure. Pipes. Nails. Somebody's idea of support.

She wondered what the woman had looked like when nobody was calling her Faye Valentine. Not the blue dress. Not the gambling table. Not the role. Did she walk differently alone? Did she peel the name off at night like makeup, or had it stuck after enough years that even in private she turned when somebody said Faye?

Other people knew pieces of that answer.

That was the vertigo. Not just that another woman had lived under her name. That other people carried memories of her that belonged to somebody else. Miriam's certainty. The young woman's face in the courtyard. Clerks, gamblers, maybe lovers, maybe nurses, maybe a landlord somewhere with a file folder full of receipts. Pieces of her spread out in strangers' heads. Faye did not own even the mystery of herself anymore.

Her throat tightened, and she hated that too because it was such a cheap body response, so generic, so available to every bad actress in every bad serial. She swallowed it down and focused instead on the tile against her shoulder blade, the real one, the scarless one, cool above the waterline. Specific. This body. This room. This night.

When she stood, water sheeted off her in one heavy curtain. The room air hit cold. She walked back to the showers and rinsed mineral salt from her skin. At the sinks she caught herself in the mirror again, blurred by steam this time, doubled by the fogged edge of old glass.

For one nasty second she saw two women.

Then the image cleared. Just her. Which was somehow not reassuring.

She dressed slowly. In the locker room a teenage girl with a shaved head was painting careful black lines around her eyes using the mirror on a compact that kept sticking half closed. The woman with the wrapped knee had moved on to arguing with herself about bus schedules. The baby had finally gone quiet. Life again, ordinary and indifferent.

At the desk, the attendant held out Faye's change without looking away from her magazine.

"You forgot your soap refund," she said.

"There's a soap refund?"

"If you don't steal it."

Faye took the coins. "You're wasting your talents here."

The woman licked a thumb and turned a page. "People keep saying that."

Outside, the cold bit hard after the steam. Faye tucked her hands under her arms and stood for a moment under the bath house awning, not moving, just watching her breath turn to vapor and vanish. She had come for anonymity and warmth and a place to set her body down without anybody wanting anything from it. She had gotten that. She had also gotten the clear sick fact that her name had once belonged to more than one life and might always.

She started back toward the Redtail and found herself touching her collarbone again, gentler this time, as if checking whether the gesture still fit.

The Bebop sounded different when three people were gone.

Not quieter exactly. Ships were never quiet. There was always a fan somewhere half a bearing away from failure, a coolant line ticking in the walls, the low refrigerator hum from the galley, the occasional deep settling thunk from the hull as temperature shifted outside. But the human noise was missing. No Jet opening cabinets too hard because he was pretending not to be angry. No Spike's shoes dragging in that lazy way that was only half laziness. No Faye talking to herself like she wanted witnesses. The ship's mechanical sounds had all the space.

Ed sat backward in the common room chair with knees hooked over the back and goggles pushed up into wild hair. Ein lay under the table in a loaf shape, ears alert, eyes open. Data windows floated across the main screen in blue stacks and green threads and one aggressively cheerful orange map Ed had made for no reason except the data liked orange tonight. In one corner a narrow comm bar glowed live. Spike had opened the ship channel and left it there. Every so often wind brushed the mic and was gone.

"See, see, the path-path-path is not random, Ein-buddy-person," Ed said. "Random is one thing and selected is a different thing and grown-up people always think messy means random, but messy counts as a pattern category too, yes it does, yes it does. Mystery-ship person didn't go boop anywhere. She went to all the places and left tiny digital breadcrumbs, tiny tiny crunchy ones, but not too crunchy, because if the breadcrumbs are too crunchy then Jet-person hears them and says why are there crumbs in my console, Edward."

Ein blinked.

Ed swiveled the chair a few degrees with a bare foot. "Jet-person is in beverage sadness mode. Spike-person is on a roof doing cigarette geometry. Faye-person is in hot water probably insulting someone whose job is to hand out towels. Adults are very good at feelings if the feeling is anger. Adults are extremely weird if the feeling is another feeling wearing anger as a hat."

Ein's ears tipped forward at the word hot. Or at the word towel. Hard to say.

"Yes, that too."

Ed tapped a key. The orange map dissolved into a file tree, then an access log, then a scan of orphanage intake records from Earth that looked like they had been digitized by somebody who hated the concept of preservation. Lines skewed. Dates clipped at the edges. Metadata glued on later by a different system with different ideas. The whole archive smelled, in Ed's imagination, like dust and old plastic and school lunch steam.

Ed had been all over it earlier, fast and bright and proud. Look what the ship did. Look where the pilot went. Look how all the strands make a nice ugly web. The adults had stared and gone pale in different flavors. After that came the chip and the arguing and the not-arguing after the argument, which was worse.

Now it was just Ed and Ein and the record.

"This one is personal data," Ed told the dog, softer. "Not just pattern data. Personal data is sticky."

Ein got up, stretched long enough to make his paws tremble, and put his chin on Ed's ankle.

"No, no, it's okay. Ed is merely observing. Observing is one hundred percent safe and emotionally neutral and definitely not a trick category."

That got no bark at all. Ein had learned skepticism somewhere.

Ed clicked into the access history the pilot had pulled six months earlier. Not because Ed had to. Because the line of thought kept curving back there no matter what other file Ed opened. The access trace was clean. The pilot had known what to ask for and how not to trip alerts. Intake ledger. Placement notes. One medical incident. No outbound deletion, no copy corruption, no vandalism. Just looking. Just reading. Just someone, somewhere on Mars, deciding Edward Wong Hau Pepelu Tivrusky IV belonged on the map with everybody else.

The entry opened.

EDWARD WONG HAU PEPELU TIVRUSKY IV, AGE 7, ADMITTED.

Below that, smaller text with dates and signatures and institutional abbreviations. A line for guardian status. A line for dietary restrictions left blank. A line for observed temperament written by an adult who had not known Ed at all and had still felt entitled to summarize.

Ed looked at the first line and stopped moving.

The ship hummed around the pause. Somewhere aft, a pipe clicked twice as it cooled.

Seven was a number. Ed liked numbers. Numbers behaved. Seven was prime. Seven was clean. Seven was also small, but not baby-small. Old enough to know when you had been put somewhere and nobody had said for how long. Old enough to decide a bunk was not home and might never be. Old enough to learn the sound of institutional shoes in hallways and the smell of bleach on blankets and which adults meant well and which adults meant schedule.

Ed's mouth went dry.

"Hmm," Ed said, but the sound came out thin.

Ein put one paw on Ed's knee.

It was not a dramatic gesture. Dogs did not do drama unless food was involved. It was just weight. Warmth. Contact. Here.

Ed reached down and scratched behind one of Ein's ears, slow enough that the fur shifted in layers under Ed's fingers.

"I was so little," Ed said.

The live comm in the corner gave a soft scrape and then nothing.

The words landed in the room and stayed there.

No third person. No joke. Just the sentence.

Ed kept looking at the record because looking away would make it stranger. Age 7. Admitted. There should have been more, maybe. Some emotional fireworks. Some big cinematic flash of memory. Instead there was a fluorescent hum in the back of Ed's head and the texture of a blanket that had been washed too many times and one meal tray with peas rolling into the compartment for potatoes. Not even a whole day. A few pieces. Memory behaved like bad file recovery. You got fragments and checksum errors and the occasional thing that came back sharper than it had any right to.

Pilot-person had looked at this. Sat somewhere, maybe with grease on her hands from an engine job, maybe with a bad cup of coffee, maybe with a heater rattling nearby, and opened the line about a seven-year-old Ed. Not to hurt, probably. Not exactly. To know. To complete the shape. That was almost weirder.

Adults thought the past was a locked box. Ed had never thought that. The past was active code. Ugly old code, undocumented, still running in the background and throwing errors into the present when you least expected it. The adults on the Bebop spent so much time pretending their background processes were not eating memory. Then a pilot parked in the wrong docking spot and suddenly everybody's hidden programs were visible on the main display.

Ed snorted once, not because it was funny. Because it was true.

"Bad architecture," Ed told Ein.

Ein thumped his tail one time.

Ed closed the intake record, then opened it again, then closed it for real. The common room reflected back from the darkened edge of the screen. Jet's mug still in the sink. Faye's red jacket thrown over the couch arm like she had tried to make it look accidental and failed. One of Spike's cigarettes on the table where Jet would complain about it in the morning if morning happened normally. Evidence everywhere that the ship was full even when it wasn't.

Ed slid off the chair and onto the floor. Ein followed at once, circling once before settling with his side against Ed's leg. From down there the common room ceiling looked lower. Old paint. A hairline seam above the light fixture. Nothing special. Ship ceiling.

Home ceiling, though.

That thought arrived without permission. Ed let it sit.

After a while Ed reached up, killed the main screen, and left only the galley light on. The room went amber and soft around the edges. The Bebop kept making its old mechanical noises, holding together the way it always had, by vibration and patches and stubbornness. The live comm window stayed pinned in one corner, a dim blue line that said somebody was still out there.

Ed leaned against the couch and listened for the others and for the occasional brush of sound on the open channel, the way you listened for weather through hull metal, not because you could control it, just because you wanted to know when it was getting close.
