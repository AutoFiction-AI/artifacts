# Chapter 18

Jet's tea had gone cold before Ren rolled the Dry Well bay doors wide enough for her ship to clear, and the wind had put a skin of red dust across it. He drank it anyway. It tasted like tannin and metal and the grit Mars worked into everything it owned for more than five minutes.

Ren moved around her ship with a rag hanging from one back pocket and a checklist that existed nowhere but her hands. She checked each landing strut with her boot, ran her palm along the seam she'd patched on the port cowling, looked up at the sky, then checked the seam again. The right knee of her coveralls had been mended with blue thread that did not match the rest of the fabric. She kept wiping her thumb across that stitched patch as if the grease on her skin bothered her more than the mismatch did.

Dry Well looked flatter by daylight than it had the day before, as if the place had taken its shape from the story Ren told in it and was now shrinking back into sheet metal and dust. The cracked solar panels along the far building threw back a weak white glare. Somewhere behind the storage shed a loose panel knocked once, then again, in the wind. The old waystation still smelled like hot wiring, stale air, and the iron tang of sand grinding itself finer.

Jet stood with one shoulder against the bay wall and watched Ren do a walk-around she did not need anyone for. That, more than anything she had said, made the morning feel final. People who were staying asked for a hand. They asked where the good wrench was. They said hold this, would you. Ren checked her own seals and her own fuel couplings and did not look up.

Ed sat on an overturned crate with Ein between her bare feet, chin propped on both fists, following Ren like she was a puzzle with moving parts. Faye leaned against a support post with her arms folded, jacket half-zipped against the cold. Her hair was still a little damp from the wash basin and had started curling at the ends. Spike stayed near the door track, coat on, cigarette unlit between two fingers as if he'd forgotten to commit to it.

Jet set the cup on the floor and pushed off the wall. "You want me to take a look at that patch before you go?"

Ren glanced at the seam on her cowling, then at him. "It'll hold to the basin rim."

"That's not exactly the part I asked."

"It's the part that matters right now." She gave the seam one more press with her thumb. "There's a machine shop two settlements north that keeps decent gasket stock. If they haven't gone under."

That was Ren all over. Ask a bigger question and she answered the piece with bolts in it. Jet had gotten used to the shape of it fast enough to hear the edges. He nodded once.

"Your portside condenser bracket is loose," she said. "Third mount back. It was singing when you came in yesterday."

Jet looked over at the Bebop on reflex, as if he might hear it from where he stood now.

"You heard that from inside the bay?"

"It wasn't subtle."

Faye snorted. "Nothing on this ship is subtle."

"Thanks," Jet said.

"You're welcome," Faye said at once, like she'd been waiting.

Ed raised a hand. "Ren-person, if Ren-person goes to a terminal with very friendly wires, will Ren-person send a message to Ed and Professor Ein? Not a location if location is dramatic, just a boop maybe. One boop. Two boops if snacks are available."

Ren's mouth shifted, not all the way into a smile. "I might send a schematic."

"That's better than a boop," Ed said solemnly.

"Debatable," Faye muttered.

Ren climbed the ladder to her cockpit, then climbed back down because she'd forgotten a metal lunch tin from the workbench. She tucked it under one arm and stood for a second with the hatch open above her, looking at the Bebop crew like she was taking a final measurement. Jet could not tell if she was memorizing them or just making sure none of them had wandered under her thrusters.

"You don't have to leave in a hurry," he said, because the thing he wanted to ask came out wrong the first time. "The bay's yours till nightfall."

Ren shifted the lunch tin to her other hand. "I know."

There it was again. The small answer. The factual answer. She knew. That did not mean she was staying.

Faye uncrossed her arms, then crossed them again. "You always this good at making everybody feel like they're in the way, or is this a special occasion?"

Ren looked at her, calm as ever. "I grew up in mechanic bays. If people start saying goodbye too early, somebody forgets to tighten something."

That got a brief sound out of Spike, not quite a laugh. He looked down at the dead cigarette in his hand like it had surprised him by staying unlit.

Jet rubbed the heel of his hand against the scar at his cheek. There were things he could have said then. About Daichi. About the reports on the wall. About the fact that he had spent half the night reading his own handwriting next to a dead man's file until the words stopped being language and turned back into ink. He could have said he was sorry, though he did not know what weight that word had left in it, used this late. He could have offered money they did not have, help she had not asked for, the ship's frequency, a real invitation north to Mars and the spot behind Mama Yun's and bowls of noodles nobody could afford properly.

What came out instead was, "You topped off coolant?"

"Yeah." Ren hooked the lunch tin into a cargo net by the hatch. "And your heat exchanger is running hot."

"That's because this ship is old."

"No," she said. "It's because the exchanger is running hot."

Faye laughed once through her nose. Jet let it go because she was right and because Ren was right and because he was not going to spend the morning defending a heat exchanger to a twenty-two-year-old mechanic who had already listened to his ship breathe.

Ren put one boot on the ladder rung and paused. "Mama Yun's refrigerator is short-cycling."

Jet blinked. "What?"

"The compressor kicks out too fast and then starts again." She nodded toward the north, toward Mars, toward the lot behind the noodle shop like it was just outside the bay instead of half a day's flight away. "You can hear it through the wall if you're trying to sleep."

"You never slept there."

"No," Ren said. "My mother did, once or twice. In the ship."

That shut the air down for a beat. Ayane, in that lot. Ayane close enough to hear the refrigerator behind the noodle shop. Ayane under Faye's name, needing medicine, parking where Jet's ship would later come home as if the universe had been quietly laying cable behind the walls the whole time.

Faye looked away first. She did it by turning toward the open desert, as if there were something out there worth seeing that had not been there thirty seconds ago.

Ren went up the ladder and disappeared into the cockpit. The canopy came down. Systems whined to life under the hull in ascending layers, one high and thin, one lower and rougher, then the steady vibration of power coming online all the way through the frame. Dust skittered across the bay floor in little red snakes.

Ed slid off the crate and came closer, one hand on Ein's head to keep him from deciding this was the moment to investigate rocket exhaust. Jet stepped back with the others until they were in a line he would have denied was a line if anybody had said so.

The ship's running lights came up amber against the dull morning. Ren did not wave from the cockpit. Jet had not expected her to. She had spent years arranging an encounter none of them had known enough to ask for. A cheery hand out the canopy would have been the wrong instrument.

The ship lifted on a controlled burst that hammered the dust flat, then kicked it back up in a wall. Jet turned his face into his shoulder. His cup went over and rolled, spilling the last brown swallow into the grit. The hull cleared the threshold by a meter, two, rose into the washed-out Martian light, and hung there a moment while the engines adjusted pitch.

Ed had to raise her voice over it. "Where is Ren-person going?"

Nobody answered right away. They all looked up as if direction might be written on the underside of the ship.

Spike spoke without taking his eyes off the canopy. "She didn't ask us to do anything."

The ship banked out over the basin, a clean turn, not hurried.

Spike flicked the dead cigarette into the dust and added, "That's the worst part."

Jet felt that settle in him harder than anything from the night before. If Ren had asked for money, he could have counted what they did not have. If she had asked for names, he could have found records. If she had wanted the corrupt bastard who sold out her father, he could have told her the man was already dead. A demand gave shape to a day. It gave you something to put your hands on.

Ren had handed them a map of what had already happened and left them to carry it.

The ship dwindled fast in the thin air. For a second the sunlight caught on the patched seam by the port cowling, a sharp white flash, and then it was just another moving point against the haze. Another ship going somewhere on Mars. Another life peeling away from theirs at an angle.

Ed lifted one arm and wagged it anyway, long after the ship was too far off to see a person behind the canopy. Ein barked once because Ed was doing it. Faye shoved both hands into her jacket pockets. Jet bent, picked up the empty cup, and brushed dust from the rim with his thumb though there was no point in it.

"All right," he said. His voice came out rougher than he'd meant. He cleared it. "Let's get this tub in the air before the afternoon crosswind starts getting ideas."

No one argued. That was its own kind of wrongness too.

They moved because there was nothing else to do. Jet was halfway to the Bebop when he looked back once more at the open sky over Dry Well. The air was already empty where Ren had been.

***

The Bebop lifted out of Dry Well heavy and complaining, the way it always did when the tanks were less full than Jet wanted and the ship had decided the morning was not to its taste. He rode the complaint with both hands on the controls, one flesh, one metal, feeling the old trawler's shudder smooth out as the engines found the climb.

Mars opened beneath them in layers of red stone and bad memory. Dry Well became a scratch in the basin, then less than that. The southern country spread away under a haze the color of old bruises, canyon lines cut hard as knife marks, stretches of failed terraforming hardware rusting into the land where somebody had once spent real money trying to make weather happen on purpose.

Jet kept the nose steady and let the autopilot take the finer corrections only after he'd bullied the Bebop into the altitude he trusted. He never liked handing full control over too early. Machines lied less than people but more often than they admitted.

Behind him the ship settled into its own sounds. The life-support hum thickened when they leveled out. Something in the galley tapped twice with each mild vibration until Faye got up, found it, and shoved a towel under it without saying anything. The hull pinged here and there as the temperature changed around the frame. Ein's nails clicked across deck plates, followed by Ed's bare slap-slap pace as she chased him and then gave up on the chase in favor of collapsing crosswise on the common-room floor.

Spike went to sleep.

He skipped the fake half-doze he put on when he wanted out of a conversation, skipped the sprawled one-eye-open thing that said he could be vertical in two seconds if trouble came through the hatch. He lay down on the couch with one arm crooked over his face and was gone so fast Jet almost looked around for the trick in it. His legs were too long for the couch, boots hanging off one end. The unlit cigarette from the bay sat behind his ear like he'd tucked it there without noticing.

Jet turned his attention back to the forward glass before the sight of it could mean more than it had to. Spike slept like that when something had run all the way to the end and left nothing but quiet behind it. There was a difference between that and peace.

Faye had dug out the same cheap magazine she'd been carrying around the ship for at least a week. The cover featured a smiling woman in a silver dress standing beside a casino cruiser nobody on the Bebop would ever afford to board, much less lose money on. The spine had cracked so many times it no longer knew what page order meant. Faye kept flipping back to an article halfway through and reading it again as if the words might have improved from the last pass.

Jet glanced over his shoulder. "You know how that story ends."

"Yeah," Faye said without looking up. "With a yacht and a divorce."

"Then why are you reading it again?"

"Because it's here."

That was good enough. It was more honest than most reasons people gave for anything.

Ed had both the game console and the main auxiliary screen alive at the same time. On the console, a blue frog in a tiny cape kept dying at an acid river. On the auxiliary screen, file trees bloomed and collapsed under Ed's hands so fast Jet could only track them by color and the occasional familiar word.

"Are you playing," he asked, "or are you working?"

"Both both both." Ed did not look up. "Professor Frog has very poor timing but excellent moral fiber. Also packet refinement is at sixty-two percent. Maybe sixty-three. Depends if checksum goblins count."

"Checksum goblins don't count," Faye said, turning a page she had already turned twice.

"This is anti-goblin prejudice."

Jet let the corner of his mouth move. "Packet for who?"

Ed finally looked up, goggles shoved into her hair, face serious in the middle of all the rest of her. "For Ren-person. Anonymous present. No sender, no snack crumbs, no metadata tails, very clean. Ed fixed the crooked timestamps and put all the folders in nice polite rows. Researcher gift etiquette."

Jet took that in and watched the ridge line ahead at the same time. "You strip the origin tags?"

"Obviously. Ed is not a baby criminal."

"Not a baby anything," Faye murmured.

"A mature criminal?" Ed considered. "No. A citizen scientist."

That got a little sound out of Faye, almost not worth calling a laugh, but it changed the air in the room by a degree. Jet heard it because the whole ship had gone so quiet otherwise.

He adjusted course two points east to avoid a bank of dust that would not hurt them but would sand the paint another layer thinner. The Bebop's starboard side already looked like old teeth. The red country moved under them in a slow scroll.

He thought about Ren saying Ayane had heard Mama Yun's refrigerator through the wall and slept in her ship behind the shop. That one detail kept catching in his head because no report would waste a line on a compressor kicking too often in the back of a noodle shop, or on a woman lying awake in a parked ship trying not to think about what medicine she had left.

Jet reached for the autopilot confirmation switch with his mechanical hand. The metal fingertips clicked softly against the housing.

"Send it when we're back on the Tharsis relays," he said.

Ed's fingers hovered. "Understood by Captain Caution."

"I mean it."

"Yes, yes. No packet flung into the raw screaming void. Only respectable routing through boring infrastructure."

Spike spoke from the couch without moving his arm. "Try not to get her arrested with kindness."

Jet looked back. One of Spike's boots had shifted. That was all.

"You were asleep," Faye said.

"Was."

"Helpful."

Spike let the word pass without picking it up. A minute later he was breathing deep again.

Faye kept reading. She licked a thumb, turned another page, then stared at the same paragraph long enough that Jet knew she was not seeing it anymore. He knew because he was not really seeing the instruments either. Their eyes kept doing the work, but the mind behind it lagged, catching on the same few scraps and worrying them.

He knew this part. The return flight. The part after the thing when the body had not caught up to the fact that there was nowhere else to be.

He remembered other return flights. Bad busts. Empty holds. Blood on a sleeve not necessarily his. The old ISSP shuttle smell of gun oil and cold coffee. Those rides had always been louder than the action before them because everybody on board wanted to prove, by talking or cursing or making plans for the paperwork, that they were still in motion.

This was not that. Nobody on the Bebop had anything to prove. They were just wearing the same ship around themselves again and letting it settle where it always settled.

Ed's hands slowed. The game music on the handheld chirped in tinny defeat as Professor Frog died once more.

"There." Ed sat up straighter and hit three keys on the auxiliary board with ceremonial precision. "Gone gone gone. Wrapped in seven layers of boring. Anonymous to everyone except people who deserve to know that the indexing is too elegant to be governmental."

"That's not anonymous," Faye said.

"It's spiritually anonymous."

"That doesn't mean anything."

"It means if Ren-person sees the folder architecture, Ren-person will know it's from a genius and maybe a frog."

Jet shook his head but did not tell Ed to take it back. The packet was already gone, a thin set of numbers slipping onto the relay ladder and out across Mars after a ship with a patched seam and a mechanic who had spent years building her own archive from pieces the rest of them had been too busy or too scared to look for.

Ein, who had no opinion on data etiquette but reacted strongly to tone, climbed onto Ed's lap and turned once before settling. Ed absentmindedly scratched the dog at the base of one ear while watching the confirmation icons blink green.

"Did you put your notes in?" Jet asked.

"Only useful notes. The records office on Europa misspelled Daichi's precinct code in three places. Ed corrected. Also there were dead links. Also there was a nice index by date because chronology is a love language."

Faye lowered the magazine a few inches. "What?"

Ed looked at her over the top of Ein's head. "For researchers."

"You're thirteen."

"And right."

Jet left them to it. The land below shifted from open basin to broken industrial scars, then to the first outer reaches of settled Tharsis, where old pipe farms and freight spurs made straight lines across the dirt. Traffic thickened on the display. Signals started to crowd one another in the upper corner of the glass. Home, if a place you kept failing to leave on time counted.

When the first blocky spread of the settlement district came into view through the haze, Spike pushed himself upright and scrubbed a hand over his face. He looked like he'd slept exactly long enough to stop being interested in the subject.

"We there?" he asked.

"No," Jet said. "I just took us halfway back for the exercise."

Spike squinted forward. "Looks like Mars."

"Good eye."

Faye turned a page. "Try not to sound excited. It'll strain something."

Nobody escalated it. The line landed and then sat there, ordinary as a spoon on a counter. Jet found he was grateful for that too.

He took the controls fully back from the autopilot as the traffic lanes tightened. His shoulders eased a fraction at the familiar work of it. Banking. Descent math. Wind shear along the commercial stacks. The Bebop knew this part. So did he.

Ahead, hidden somewhere under the settlement haze and the exhaust wash and the bad neon, was the lot behind Mama Yun's.

Jet found himself already lining up for it in his head.

***

The spot was empty.

Jet saw it between two freight sheds on final approach, a rectangle of stained concrete and patched utility hookups behind Mama Yun's Noodle House, half-screened by the bent chain-link fence somebody had been meaning to fix for years. Empty, plain, sun on it, a damp mark by the back wall where Mama Yun's refrigerator discharge always leaked when the drainage tube clogged.

His hands tightened on the controls once, then loosened. He brought the Bebop around wide because the old girl deserved the angle, eased her down with the starboard side a hair high to account for the crosswind, and let the landing struts take the weight in the exact sequence he liked: rear, front, settle, hiss. The docking clamps grabbed late, then hard. Through the hull he felt the familiar bite of them and, under that, the stupid private satisfaction he always felt when he got her into that slot without scraping anything expensive.

This time the satisfaction carried one beat too far and left him looking.

The retaining wall still had the scorch mark from two winters back when Spike had insisted he could guide the Swordfish in with one thruster half out and had kissed concrete for his trouble. The fence leaned at the same tired angle. The refrigerator exhaust rattled in its housing. The damp stripe by the back wall was fresh again where the discharge leaked when the tube clogged. Jet sat with his palms on the controls and looked at the slot they'd dropped into a hundred times until the engine note fell away under him.

The feeling came over him the way solder smell filled a room after the iron was unplugged and the workbench went quiet: hot metal, spent current, work already over.

Behind him Ed whooped because they were back in a place with noodles. Faye announced she was starving in the tone of somebody filing a formal complaint. Spike yawned loud enough to count as rudeness. The spell broke because of course it did.

"Move," Jet said, unstrapping. "And wipe your feet before you track half of Hellas through Mama Yun's shop."

"No promises," Faye said.

"Promises are free," Spike said.

"You'd know."

They clattered down the ramp into late afternoon cold. Mars wind drove grit along the alley in shallow sheets that hissed against the Bebop's landing gear. Mama Yun's back door stood propped open with a sack of dried noodles. Broth steam and scallion smell reached them before the light did.

Inside, the noodle shop looked exactly like it had any right to. Counter seats. Fluorescent buzz. Cheap bowls stacked too close to the sink. The counter still had that tacky patch by the soy sauce where no amount of wiping ever seemed to help. Mama Yun stood at the register wearing her reading glasses low on her nose and a pencil stuck through the knot of her hair. Beside the till, face-down so the broth splatter wouldn't hit the pages, lay a serialized romance magazine with a shirtless man on the cover looking offended by weather.

She glanced up as the bell over the door gave its tired metallic complaint.

"You took your time," she said.

"We were working," Jet said.

"You always say that when you come back hungry."

Without looking down, she bent, pulled a chipped saucer from under the counter, and set it on the floor near Ed's stool. She ladled a little broth into it from a side pot and then pointed the spoon at Ein. "One minute only. I'm not running a kennel."

Ein was already trotting toward the saucer.

"Mama Yun has soft business ethics," Ed informed him.

"Mama Yun has a mop," Mama Yun said.

They took their usual places because there was no meeting and no decision about it. Jet at the end where he could see the kitchen window. Spike slouched two stools down. Faye sat between them and immediately claimed the chili oil. Ed climbed up cross-legged and began narrating the broth temperature to Ein, who was not allowed on the stool and objected silently with his whole body.

The girl working the back burners was new to Jet, young and broad-faced, wearing one pink glove on her left hand and no glove on the right. She had a strip of silver tape holding the heel of one shoe together and hummed under her breath every time she dropped noodles into boiling water. When she slid the first bowls up to the pass, Mama Yun said, "Lina worries her landlord's poisoning the mailboxes, so if any of you are going to start a fight today, keep it away from the front windows."

Lina did not look up from the broth. "He's poisoning the mailboxes. I told you, the paint started after he bought the place."

"It's rust," Mama Yun said.

"Rust doesn't smell sweet."

"It does if you're stupid."

Jet accepted his bowl with both hands because it was too hot not to. "How's the fridge?"

"Still cold."

"That isn't what I asked."

Mama Yun gave him a look over the rims of her glasses that would have done damage if Jet were less used to it. "Short-cycling. Since yesterday. I know."

Jet paused with the bowl halfway down. "How do you know I know?"

"Because you have your repair face on." She jerked her chin at the back door. "Eat first. Then tell me which piece costs money."

Faye lifted noodles with her chopsticks, blew on them, and said, "See, this is why you're everyone's favorite and not mine."

"You don't have a favorite," Jet said.

"Sure I do. Me."

"That tracks," Spike said.

Faye pointed the chopsticks at him. "Don't start. You just woke up."

"Exactly," Spike said. "I don't have the energy."

They ate. Ed said the egg had a face in it. Lina said that was the yolk staring back at the future. Faye demanded another half egg and got told no and then got one anyway because the shell had cracked. Spike stole bean sprouts from Jet's bowl until Jet slapped the back of his hand with the chopsticks and Spike said "Worth it" around noodles. In the back, the refrigerator kicked off with a hard click and tried again. Nobody named Dry Well over the sound. Nobody said Ayane or Daichi.

It sat there anyway, in the pause before the bad motor caught again. Faye did not flinch from Jet when he reached past her for the soy sauce. Spike did not leave when the talk thinned. Ed, who normally bounced between stool and floor and door with enough speed to make counting useless, stayed put long enough to finish a bowl and ask Lina whether poisoned mailboxes could infect dogs by proximity. Lina said only if the dog licked the rent checks. Ein looked offended at the suggestion.

When the bowls were empty and Jet could feel warmth where the broth had landed in him, Mama Yun slapped the old tab pad down on the counter by his elbow.

"You paying or lying?"

"Tab," Jet said automatically.

"Then write something I can read."

The pen was chewed near the cap. The top page of the pad was already ghosted through with old pressure marks from previous names, previous nights. Jet held the paper steady with the heel of his cybernetic hand and wrote without thinking, fast because there was nothing in it that needed thought.

Jet Black.

He tore the page free, handed it over, and only then saw what he'd done as the ink caught the light.

Usually he wrote Bebop. Sometimes J. When he couldn't be bothered, a slash. This time the slip said Jet Black, whole and plain, in his own square hand.

Mama Yun took the slip, glanced at it once, and hooked it onto the spindle by the register with half a dozen others. If she noticed, she did not make the mistake of making it a moment. She just shouted at Lina to stop overcooking the next batch and turned a page in her romance serial with her wrist so her fingers stayed dry.

Jet sat there a second longer than he had to, chopsticks laid across the empty bowl, staring at the spindle as if the slip might turn back into something smaller while he watched.

"You planning to propose to the receipt?" Faye asked.

He got up. "I'm planning to fix a refrigerator."

"There he is," Spike said.

The compressor on Mama Yun's refrigerator was in a closet-sized alcove behind the kitchen, warm enough to sweat in despite the weather outside. Jet got the panel off, found the culprit fast, and swore at it with professional familiarity. The relay switch had carboned up just enough to kick out early. He scraped the contacts clean, adjusted the spring tension, and replaced a cracked bit of insulation with tape from his pocket toolkit while Mama Yun stood in the doorway eating fennel seeds from her palm and pretending not to supervise.

"You should charge more," she said.

"You should stop buying junk relays."

"I buy what the market gives me."

"The market gives you trash."

"And yet here you are."

He clicked the panel shut. The compressor kicked on, ran clean, and stayed running. Through the wall he could hear bowls clatter, Ed laugh about something not remotely tied to the job, Spike's lower murmur too quiet to make out, Faye's sharper answer. The ordinary soundtrack of the place. The thing Ayane had heard. The thing Ren had remembered secondhand because some sounds lasted longer in a family than names did.

Jet stood there with his hand still on the warm metal and let the compressor hum settle.

"You were gone longer this time," Mama Yun said behind him, not prying exactly. She was better at the angle than that. "Trouble?"

He thought about saying no. He thought about saying yes. He thought about all the ways those could both be lies.

"Work," he said finally.

Mama Yun spat a fennel hull into the trash. "You always say that."

He went back through the kitchen without answering because there was nothing useful to add.

Outside, the wind had sharpened while he was in the back. The alley behind the shop carried cold differently at dusk, thinner and meaner, sliding around the buildings to find skin. Ed ran ahead with Ein toward the Bebop's ramp, then doubled back because Lina had given the dog a noodle end on the sly and Ed needed to report this crime to the nearest authority, which was nobody.

Jet slowed to lock the back service hatch on the refrigeration unit. By the time he straightened, Spike and Faye were a few paces ahead of him in the alley, side by side without looking like they'd planned it.

Spike had his hands in his coat pockets. Faye had pulled her jacket close with one fist at the collar.

He heard Spike because the alley had gone momentarily still between gusts.

"I kept thinking about it on the way back," Spike said. "I flew over Hellas a lot. More times than I can count. I never saw that place."

Faye kept walking.

After a second, she reached up and tucked a strand of hair behind one ear because the wind kept dragging it forward. Her boots kept the same pace on the grit. She left his sentence hanging where he had put it.

Jet looked away before either of them could catch him listening.

Back on the Bebop, the ship welcomed them in the only way it knew how: with stale warm air, the smell of broth now layered over old grease, and the faint complaint of a vent fan that wanted oil. Ed dropped to the deck in the common room with the game console, immediately losing all urgency except the kind a thirteen-year-old could summon about digital amphibians. Ein circled twice and collapsed against Ed's shin with the total faith of a creature that had never once doubted where dinner came from. Spike took the couch as if the day had been a minor inconvenience constructed for the purpose of earning it.

Faye went toward her room, paused in the doorway, and looked back into the common room for one beat too long to count as an accident. Then she went in anyway and left the door open the width of a hand.

Jet filled the sink and set Mama Yun's repaired relay on the counter to remind himself to find a better one in the bazaar before it failed again. Through the porthole above the galley counter he could see the back lot, the fence, the strip of alley beyond, the shop's service light throwing a hard yellow cone on the concrete. A noodle carton skipped along the fence, caught on a post, and stayed there.

Ed rolled onto her stomach and called toward the auxiliary terminal, "Delivery confirmation received! No reply, as is elegant."

"Good," Jet said.

"Very professional mystery person behavior," Ed said. "Ren-person appreciated the folder architecture in silence."

Spike, from the couch, made a sleepy sound that might have been approval.

Water ran hot over Jet's hands. He rinsed the bowls they'd carried back from the shop because Mama Yun always forgot two and because bringing them back clean kept the tab from becoming an insult. The ship settled around him one noise at a time. Spike shifting on the couch. Ed's game music blipping low. Ein's collar tag ticking once against the deck. The compressor behind Mama Yun's wall starting up and, now that Jet had fixed it, staying up.

Outside, the Bebop sat behind the noodle shop with her struts planted in the same stained sockets on the concrete. The fence leaned. The wall held the last of the day's heat. Behind it, the refrigerator ran with a steady motor note instead of the old stutter.

Jet set the last bowl in the rack and listened. Spike shifted once on the couch and went still again. Ed's game chirped, failed, restarted. Light from Faye's half-open door lay across the floorboards. The hull ticked as it cooled, and the compressor behind Mama Yun's wall kept on running.
