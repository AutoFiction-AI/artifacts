# Chapter 17

The waystation settled after midnight in layers.

First the voices ran out. Then the charger rig Ren had clamped to the Bebop dropped from a hard industrial whine to a low steady hum that lived in the floor. Then the wind took the whole place back. It came along the outer walls in long dry scrapes, lifted dust into the seams around the door, and worried the loose metal skin of the old bay until it ticked and shivered. Dry Well had been built for men who thought a shipment schedule was the same thing as a future. Half the rooms were dead now, sealed with plate and old foam that had gone the color of teeth. The common room still held heat if nobody opened the door too often.

Ren had given them that room with the same economy she'd used for everything else. The cot was yours, the couch was yours, the floor was flat enough if Ed didn't mind it, there were blankets in the locker, the bay light switch stuck halfway and needed a push. Then she'd taken her own bedding, a thin rolled pad and two rough blankets, and carried them back out to her ship without waiting to see whether any of them would object.

Jet didn't.

He ought to have. A young woman turning her home over to a crew she'd spent years tracking across the system should have made him feel something clear and named. Gratitude. Shame. Protective irritation. Instead he felt tired in a way that had weight to it. Tired in the knees, in the base of his skull, in the hand that wasn't metal. Too tired to argue about sleeping arrangements. Too tired to lie to himself about what kind of room this was.

Ed had gone down first, as if the day had simply run out underneath him. He lay in a shallow crescent on the floor with a route map under one cheek and Ein jammed up against his stomach, one arm flung over a stack of copied manifests like he'd claimed them by touch. Spike had made a circuit of the room without seeming to look at anything, found the couch, sat on the arm of it for a while, then stood back up and gone outside with a cigarette tucked behind one ear. Faye had taken the cot, boots still on, one forearm over her eyes. She wasn't asleep. Jet could tell by the way she held still for it, too deliberate by half.

Jet stood at Ren's wall and read.

The wall took up most of the room opposite the rusted heater. It wasn't arranged for drama. There were no strings, no neat little circles around faces, none of the cheap detective nonsense you saw in cop shows made by people who'd never touched a case file. Ren had built it like somebody rebuilding an engine from salvage. Documents overlapped in practical layers. Route maps were pinned first, then photocopied clinic bills, then ISSP forms, then photos, then notes in a hand compact enough to fit between printed lines. Old black magnets held corners flat. Washers had been used where the paper wanted to curl. Dates were written on strips of masking tape. Some pages had grease on the edges where she'd handled them with work hands.

Jet knew the forms before he got close enough to read them. His body knew them. The width of the boxes. The blunt gray type. The cheap paper ISSP bought in bulk because cheap paper made cheap institutions look efficient from a distance. Informant contact logs. Routing memos. Internal requests. Disciplinary notices. Dead paper, most of it. The kind of paperwork men filled out because a building asked for paper and not because anyone thought a human life sat inside it.

He reached for one page with his prosthetic hand, saw the clamp of polished fingers against the flimsy edge, and stopped. The metal could hold it, but it couldn't feel when the paper wanted to tear. He used his other hand instead. Flesh thumb, flesh forefinger, toner dusting the ridges of his skin.

Confidential Informant Handling Review. Mars Precinct Sector 4. Partial. Copy.

The name block had been blacked out on the original and then copied twice, so the redaction showed as a thick charcoal bar. A note had been added below in Ren's hand: DAICHI KOZUKI. He already knew that. He read anyway.

Risk exposure elevated due to suspected leak in internal chain.

Recommended transfer pending supervisor approval.

Pending.

Pending.

Pending, over three separate dates, each one initialed, each one routed onward, each one stamped and moved and never completed. He knew those initials too. Not all of them, but enough. A lieutenant who transferred to Venus. A records clerk who'd once cried in the break room because her ex had cleared out the apartment while she was on shift. His partner's signature on the third page, slashing across the line where the review had been acknowledged. A note in the margin about resource limitations. Somebody had written hold in blue pen and circled it so hard the paper puckered around the mark.

Jet set the page back carefully and reached for the next.

Incident summary. Unofficial copy retained by source.

Male, civilian, deceased.

He read the words too fast at first, skidding over them because he knew where they were going. Alley behind a freight exchange. Multiple wounds. Compromised cover. No arrest at scene. Follow-up deferred pending internal review.

Deferred.

He remembered that phrase. People loved that phrase. It let a department act like time itself was a meaningful response. Defer, reroute, escalate, close. The verbs of a building where death turned into routing decisions before the blood dried.

He went through the next six pages standing straight-backed, like posture might do something. Expense vouchers. A reassignment request that had never been processed. A precinct roster with his own name halfway down the page, BLACK, J., shift code next to it. Then he found the thing that made him stop reading and simply look.

Officer Fitness Review.

He'd filled out a hundred of them over the years, first as the younger man being judged, later as the older man doing the judging. This one was his. His own block letters in the self-assessment field. His own neat irritation in the notes section where he'd written that the range calibration system was drifting and needed replacement. His own signature at the bottom, heavy from the cheap pen they'd issued that quarter. His partner's approval signature beneath it.

Marksmanship: excellent.

Vehicle handling: excellent.

Administrative compliance: satisfactory.

Areas for growth: patience with civilian procedure.

Jet stared at that line until the words lost shape.

He could see the desk where he'd written it. Green laminate chipped at the corner. Coffee ring at the upper left. The fan in records squeaking every fourth turn because no one wanted to fill out the maintenance ticket. His partner leaning in the doorway with mustard on his tie from a vending machine sandwich, joking about somebody in lockup singing all night. Jet annoyed at the paperwork, annoyed at the range note, annoyed in the ordinary petty way a man could be annoyed inside a functional workday.

Same week.

The dates might as well have been touching.

While Daichi Kozuki was trying to get moved before the leak reached him, Jet had been checking boxes about his own performance and complaining about the damn fan.

He lowered himself onto the metal chair by the workbench because his legs had started to feel less than certain. The chair bit cold through his pants. One of Ren's notes had come loose from the wall and was stuck to the sole of his boot. He peeled it off and set it on the bench. It held nothing more explosive than a list of dates cross-checked against route schedules and clinic purchases. Her hand was cramped but clean. No dramatics there either. Just sequence. This happened, then this, then this, and because of that, this.

The heater coughed twice and kept failing to become useful.

Jet rubbed his face. The room smelled like dust, paper, machine oil, and the ghost of broth from some older time when this station had fed men between runs. On the floor, Ed shifted, smacked his lips, and dragged one sheet halfway under his shoulder. Ein's back foot twitched against the map. Faye moved on the cot and muttered something too soft to catch. The room was full of the crew's sleeping noises, or almost-sleeping noises, and behind them all was the wall.

He read until the pages began to blur together: Ayane's clinic charts under Faye Valentine's name, partial manifests from routes Spike had worked, copies of old bounty data, settlement school enrollment records with Ren's name changing handwriting halfway through as if one clerk had cared and the next hadn't. That was almost the worst part. The whole thing had been built out of a thousand small permissions. A clerk who sold frozen patients' data. A department that treated informants as consumable. A syndicate route that carried medicine because it also carried guns. A man who did his job and asked the exact number of questions required to keep doing it.

He found another page with his own handwriting on it and didn't pick it up. He left it where it was pinned and looked at it from three feet away as if distance still meant anything.

The door clicked.

Jet turned. Faye had shifted onto one elbow on the cot, hair in her face, eyes open now.

"Are you planning to read till dawn," she said, voice thick with not-sleep, "or are you going to pick one document and marry it?"

Jet looked back at the wall. "Go to sleep."

"That's not an answer."

"Didn't realize I was taking questions."

Faye watched him for another second. In the heater light her face looked younger and worse for it, stripped of most of the sharpness she used on purpose. She followed his gaze to the fitness review, to the precinct forms, and her mouth tightened.

"Bad one?"

Jet let out a breath through his nose. "No. Ordinary one."

Faye sat up the rest of the way, slowly, then stopped with one hand flat on the cot.

"Right," she said.

"Yeah."

She flopped back down and put the forearm over her eyes again. "If you start apologizing to the wall, I'm leaving."

Jet almost smiled. Almost. "Wouldn't dream of it."

After a minute her breathing leveled out for real, or close enough. Jet stayed in the chair until his back locked. Then he stood, set the fitness review flat against the wall again, and went outside because the room had run out of air.

The cold hit fast.

Dry Well at night looked unfinished. The dust darkened toward brown where the moonlight caught it. The settlement buildings made a crooked black row against the plain, each one with a few stubborn lines of old sealant still pale around the joints. Farther out, the ground fell away into a wash of rock and rust that used to mean convoy exposure and now meant nothing at all. Ren's ship sat in the open bay with its hull light off, just a darker shape under the frame. The charger cable to the Bebop glowed at one connection point where the insulation had split and been taped.

Spike was twenty yards from the door, sitting on an overturned intake housing, one elbow on one knee. Cigarette ember, then dark, then ember again when he drew. The wind kept trying to steal the smoke before it rose.

Jet didn't say anything at first. He came up beside him and looked out at the empty dark because that was what the dark was for.

After a while Spike said, "You found something?"

"Too much of it."

Spike nodded once. He flicked ash. It blew back against his cuff and clung there.

Jet stuffed his hands under his arms against the cold. "You know her face?"

Spike took a second. "No."

"Yeah." Jet looked down at the hard ground by his boots. Dust had piled against the intake housing in little scalloped ridges. "Me neither."

That was all, and it was enough.

They stood in it together for a while. The wind scraped over sheet metal. Somewhere in the bay, a loose chain knocked against a strut, stopped, knocked again. Spike finished the cigarette and lit another off the stub of it, more habit than need.

When he spoke again his voice had dropped into the flatter place it got when he wasn't bothering to make it sound like anything.

"I keep trying to make a person out of a route."

Jet glanced over.

Spike's eyes stayed on the dark. "Cargo code. Drop point. Some delay notice I never—" He stopped, dragged on the cigarette, held it. "That's what I had. Still is, mostly." The smoke went sideways in the wind. "Everyone wants you to have known what it meant."

Jet could have asked who everybody was. He didn't.

Spike rubbed ash from his thumb with the side of it. "I left for my reasons. None of them had a damn thing to do with somebody's meds."

Jet heard the rough edge in that and left it alone for half a breath too long.

"I know."

"Do you?"

The question landed more like a tired correction than a challenge. Jet took it as one anyway because he wasn't built for anything gentler in moments like this.

"No," he said, and then, because he was too old to dodge a fair hit, "No. I don't."

Spike nodded once more. The nod wasn't agreement. It was acceptance of the answer's limits.

Jet looked back toward the common room window. From here the heater light was a dull square behind dirty glass. He could just make out the wall, pale strips of paper, the shape of the chair he'd left crooked.

"I found my performance review in there," he said.

Spike said nothing.

"Same week."

Still nothing for a beat. Then Spike took the cigarette from his mouth and tapped it against the housing.

"That's how it works," he said. He turned the cigarette in his fingers. "Middle of the day. Paperwork. Some guy eating lunch. A shipment going where it always goes."

It was the longest thing he'd said in hours.

"Yeah."

Spike put the cigarette back between his lips. "Doesn't let anybody off."

"No."

The wind changed direction. It carried a smell out of the bay, old coolant and hot metal, and under that the iron smell dust got when night cold tightened it. Jet stayed until his knees hurt and the cold found the seam at the wrist of his sleeve. He could have said more. He could have said something about informants, about partners, about not seeing what sat three desks over if the paperwork that came to you looked ordinary enough. He didn't. Spike wasn't the man to hear it, and Jet wasn't the man to say it cleanly.

When he finally headed back inside, Spike didn't look over.

Jet stopped in the doorway and glanced once more toward Ren's ship. No light showed through the cockpit glass. She might have been asleep. She might have been lying there awake with both hands under her head, listening to the same wind and wondering what the hell she'd expected from any of this after the telling was done. There was no paperwork for that part. No form to route it.

Inside, the room had gone close and stale again. Faye really was asleep now, one boot hanging off the cot. Ed had rolled onto his back and somehow pulled a precinct roster over his chest like a blanket. Jet bent, took the paper from Ed carefully, folded it once along its old crease, and put it back on the bench.

Then he turned the heater up even though it wouldn't help much and sat in the chair by the wall until the dark outside thinned.

***

Faye woke because somebody was tapping metal in a patient little rhythm that refused to become part of her dream.

She lay on the cot and listened to it for a while with both eyes shut. Tap. Turn. Tap. A pause. Then the soft clink of something small set down in order. The room had gone that gray color right before dawn, not light exactly, just less dark. Jet was slumped in the chair by the wall with his chin on his chest. He wasn't sleeping so much as losing a fight with gravity. Ed had not moved except to acquire another page. Spike wasn't in the room.

Tap. Turn. Tap.

Faye sat up, swore under her breath when her neck answered with a crack, and swung her legs off the cot. The floor was cold enough to bite through her socks. She stepped over Ed, over one of Ren's copied maps, and made it to the door without waking anybody.

The bay smelled different from the common room. Colder. Cleaner in spots, dirtier in others. Machine oil, sealant, metal filings, dust that had come in under the big doors and settled on everything the way Mars always did. A work light hung over the open panel on Ren's ship and threw a white hard circle across the floor. Ren stood inside that circle with one boot braced on a crate, sleeves shoved to the elbows, bent over a coolant pump housing spread open on a rag.

She didn't look up right away. She had all the fasteners lined in a row from longest to shortest, each one turned so the heads faced the same way. Every time she lifted a part free she touched the line with the knuckle of her little finger as if counting without wanting to count aloud. Off to one side sat a dented tin with faded peaches on the lid, full of washers and seals. There was grease on her wrist and a pencil tucked behind one ear worn down nearly to the metal band.

Faye leaned against the bay frame. "You always tear into machinery before sunrise, or are we getting special treatment?"

Ren looked up then, blinked once as if she'd had to come back from farther away than the inside of an engine, and straightened a little.

"It was making a noise."

"That's not a normal answer."

"It's the answer."

Faye almost told her that was annoying. She let it go because it was also true.

"What kind of noise?"

"High whine under load. Bearing's going." Ren lifted the housing and tipped it so Faye could see the dark ring of worn metal inside. "I can get another month out of it if I clean it and shim it, but I'd rather not."

Faye came two steps closer despite herself. "You talk about engines like people talk about dogs."

"Dogs are less predictable."

Faye looked at the laid-out parts, the clean order of them. "You sleep at all?"

Ren put the housing down. "A little."

"Liar."

"Moderate liar."

Faye snorted before she meant to.

The bay door was cracked open an inch at the bottom, enough to let a blade of cold air slide through. Outside, morning hadn't quite happened. The sky was still the wrong side of black. Somewhere beyond the ship, a loose panel kept ticking in the wind. Ren picked up a thin brush and started cleaning old grease from the bearing race, careful, even strokes.

Faye watched her work for a few seconds longer than made sense, then said the thing she'd come out here to ask before she could talk herself out of it.

"What was she like?"

Ren didn't ask who. She set the brush down, wiped two fingers on a rag, and considered the pump housing like it had become a harder repair.

"Which version?"

Faye folded her arms. The motion brought her own hand near her collarbone and she moved it away on purpose. "The one that used my name."

"That's not different enough to be a version."

"You know what I mean."

"Yeah." Ren looked at the open panel, not at Faye. "She was careful in public. She knew what people expected when they heard the name, so she gave them enough of it to stop questions."

"What'd they expect?"

Ren lifted one shoulder. "Money, style, some kind of easy confidence. She could do the confidence part."

The pause before style was small but real. Ren didn't seem like somebody who wore words for decoration if she didn't trust them.

Faye shifted her weight. "And the rest."

"Borrowed." Ren reached for the dented peach tin and tipped out a new seal into her palm. "She practiced the signature on receipts. She'd get angry if the F looked too neat. Said rich people didn't write carefully because they were used to getting another sheet of paper."

That got Faye in the stomach somewhere.

"That sounds stupid."

"It was stupid."

"Did it work?"

"Mostly."

Ren fit the seal into place and ran her thumb around the groove. Faye watched the thumbnail, cracked at one edge and black with grease.

"Miriam said blue dress," Faye said.

Ren nodded. "There was one. Synthetic satin. Cheap up close. She bought it used from a woman in Tharsis who sold clothes out of her apartment and cigarettes one at a time. It was too tight through the ribs and she wore it anyway."

Faye looked away toward the open door. The sliver of sky there had begun to gray.

"And the shoulder scar."

Ren's mouth moved, not quite a smile. "Welding burn. She got lazy with a torch when she was sixteen. If she was telling the story to someone she wanted to impress, it became a knife fight. If she was telling it to me, it was the torch."

There it was, sudden and plain, a woman appearing not as theft or medical file or route casualty but as somebody who lied different depending on her audience. Faye hated how much that helped.

"Great," she said. "So she had range."

"Yeah."

Low information. Still conversation.

Ren picked up the bearing and frowned at it. Faye stayed where she was. The light buzzed overhead. Dust moved in the beam every time the wind found the door gap. After a little while Ren said, without looking up, "She couldn't keep cards square when she was tired. That's what I remember from those nights. She'd come back late and empty her pockets on the table and if she was really worn out the deck would go crooked in her hands."

Faye blinked.

"You watched."

"I was there."

"That's not what I asked."

Ren finally looked at her. "No. I watched."

The honesty of it made Faye want to pick a fight. She did what she always did when the ground shifted under her and went for the angle with teeth on it.

"Did she ever feel bad?"

Ren didn't flinch. "About using your name."

"Yeah."

"Yes."

"That's a fast answer."

"It was a fast fact."

Faye pushed off the frame and paced once across the bay, three steps, turn, three steps back. There wasn't room for more than that without hitting a crate. The place was full of old syndicate hardware stripped for parts, lengths of hose, a busted compressor, a bucket of bolts with dust half-filling it. Ren had made herself a life out of leftovers and labor. Faye hated that, too, because it made anger feel expensive.

"You don't have to say that because I'm standing here," Faye said.

"I'm not saying it because you're standing here."

"Then why say it?"

Ren set the bearing down and wiped both hands on the rag slowly, like she wanted them empty before she answered.

"Because it was wrong." Her hands went still. "It kept her alive, and it was still—" She picked up the rag again. "It was somebody else's name. She knew that. I knew it too. I was old enough to know."

Faye laughed once, no humor in it. "Old enough to know and nobody stopped."

"Nobody could afford to stop."

Faye looked at the line of bolts.

The bay held the silence without ceremony. They stayed where they were. The pump housing sat open between them like a third thing in the conversation, something with pieces that had to go back where they belonged whether or not people got sorted the same way.

Faye found an overturned crate and sat.

"I keep trying to figure out what exactly she took," she said after a while. "I don't mean the legal answer. I know that one. Data, account access, medical clearance, whatever. I mean the actual thing." She looked down at her own hands. "I was frozen. I didn't know my own damn name half the time when I woke up. And meanwhile she's out there being Faye Valentine better than I was because I wasn't even there."

Ren listened the way she did everything else, all the way to the end before touching it.

"I don't think she was better at it."

Faye barked another laugh. "You weren't at the table with Miriam."

"No." Ren picked up the brush, put it down again. "But I saw her come home after. That's different."

"Different how?"

Ren looked past Faye for a second, toward nothing in the bay but old shadows.

"Like wearing shoes that fit if you don't stand still too long. She could hold it for an evening, a clinic visit—maybe a week if the paperwork needed to match." Ren wiped her hand on the rag. "Then she'd come home and start taking it off. Jewelry first. She'd stop talking like that, stop standing like that." One shoulder moved. "By the time she changed clothes she was just herself again. That was the hard part, I think."

Faye stared at her. That was too close to understanding. It made her skin itch.

"Don't make her noble," she said.

"I'm not."

"Because she wasn't."

"I know."

"How?"

Ren gave a small impatient breath and reached for the bearing again, more force than the part required.

"Because I lived with her after," she said. "Because I saw what fear does to a person. Because I saw her take your name and I saw her hate that she needed it and I saw her keep using it anyway. You want me to make it cleaner than that, I can't."

Faye looked at the line of bolts, all the heads facing the same way. Something in her chest wanted to crack open and she had spent too many years making sure it didn't know how.

"I don't want clean," she said.

Ren's gaze flicked up. "Okay."

"I want..." Faye stopped, angry now because the sentence had reached a place without floor. She tried again. "I want one version of this that isn't rotten."

Ren didn't answer right away. She turned the bearing over in her hands. "She loved me," she said. "I don't know if that helps. But it's what I've got."

Faye looked down at her own knees. She could hear Jet snore once in the common room and then catch himself. Somewhere outside the wind shifted grit against the bay door with a dry hiss.

"Yeah," she said.

After a moment Ren added, "And she stole your name."

Faye nodded once.

"Yeah."

Ren picked up the housing again. "Can you hold this?"

Her voice stayed flat at the end, all need. Faye stood, stepped into the light, and took the pump housing when Ren handed it over. It was heavier than it looked, cold on one side, still a little warm near the motor where it had been running earlier. Grease smeared onto Faye's fingers.

"Don't drop it," Ren said.

"I know how gravity works."

"Good."

They worked like that for a while. Ren seated the bearing, adjusted the shim, ran a thin line of sealant with steady pressure from the tube. Faye held the housing where she was told and asked the occasional question because that was easier than not talking at all.

"You learn all this from your mother?"

"Some of it."

"Who taught you the rest?"

"An old woman in Hellas who fixed haulers and cheated at dominoes." Ren tightened one fastener, then another. "She had one eye and hated sentimental music. She'd throw a wrench at the radio if a singer dragged a note."

There it was, another life attached to Ren that had nothing to do with the Bebop crew and everything to do with being a person who had mornings before this one. Faye held the housing steady and let that settle too.

"Sounds charming."

"She was mean as hell."

"Better."

The last bolt went in. Ren tested the spin with two fingers and listened, head tilted, that mechanic's stillness people had when their ears did better work than gauges.

"That'll do," she said.

Faye set the housing down on the rag. Her hands were filthy now. She rubbed thumb against forefinger, felt the grease tack there.

"You can wash up inside," Ren said. "Sink on the left sticks. Kick the pipe under it."

Faye didn't move yet. "What did she call you?"

Ren looked over, not understanding for one beat.

"Your mother?"

"Ren."

"No, when she was mad."

The answer came quick enough that it had been living near the surface already.

"My whole name."

Faye nodded. She could work with that. A woman yelling across a room, full name, the oldest trick in the world. The symbol and the ghost dropped away. What stayed was a mother with a daughter and too much fear in the house.

"Right," Faye said.

Ren picked up the rag, folded it once, twice. "Yeah."

Faye went to wash her hands. She kicked the pipe under the sink when it stuck, just like Ren said. The water that came out was brown for half a second and then clear.

Ren had never trusted quiet kitchens.

In settlement houses quiet usually meant the food had run out, or somebody was sick, or the adults were saving the bad news till there were enough bodies in the room to spread it around. A kitchen was supposed to make noise. A pan on heat. A knife on board. Somebody complaining before the first bite had even happened. She woke with that old expectation lodged under the ribs and lay in her bunk for a minute staring up at the low ceiling of her ship until she realized the noise hadn't started because she hadn't started it.

The sky outside had turned from charcoal to a flat rust gray. Morning.

She dressed, tied her hair back, and crossed to the waystation galley with the inventory in her head. Half a sack of flour. One onion. Dried peppers. A jar of preserved tomatoes she'd been saving for no good reason. Chickpea meal. Two cans of cheap fish. Tea bricks. Enough oil. Enough salt if she was careful. Food that wanted apology if handled badly and something closer to breakfast if handled right.

By the time the first pan warmed, the room had begun to smell alive.

Onion hit oil and gave back sweetness. Pepper darkened. Chickpea batter thickened in the bowl under the fork. She cut the preserved tomatoes into the pan with the fish and let them go until the red deepened and the edges started to catch. It was settlement food, straightforward and unforgiving. The kind that depended less on ingredients than on not being afraid of heat.

She was sliding the third flat cake onto a tray when she heard footsteps behind her and knew from the weight of them it was Jet.

He stopped in the galley entrance and just stood there for a second, broad and sleep-creased and already offended on principle.

"What's that smell," he said.

Ren kept working. "Breakfast."

"I can see that."

"Then we're ahead of schedule."

He came in farther, peered into the pan like it might be committing a procedural violation, and folded his arms. His beard had a flattened patch on one side from sleeping in a chair. He looked like a man who'd been insulted in his own church.

"You started the fish too early."

"No, I didn't."

"It's going to dry out."

"It's in tomato."

Jet leaned over the pan and had the decency to wait until he smelled it properly before he looked more annoyed.

"That doesn't make you right."

"No," Ren said. "The smell does."

From behind him Faye's voice came, rough with sleep and not enough patience. "If you're mad because breakfast smells edible, that's a personal problem."

Faye stepped into the galley pulling her jacket on over yesterday's shirt. There was still a dark grease mark on one thumb from the pump housing despite the wash. She saw Ren notice and did not acknowledge it. Spike came in after her with the look of a man who had slept less than anybody and would deny it if asked. Ed arrived last at a low trot with a piece of paper still stuck to one side of his face and Ein pacing hopefully around his ankles.

"Morning biscuits of destiny," Ed announced, then stopped and sniffed harder. "No, wait. Flat circles. Crunch disks. Smells very legal."

"Nothing in this room is legal," Faye said.

"Speak for yourself," Jet muttered.

He was still staring into the pan.

Ren slid the last cake onto the tray and turned the heat off. "Plates are on the shelf."

Jet did not move.

"You know where the plates are," Faye said.

"I know where the plates are."

"Then why are you standing there like she broke into your house?"

"I'm assessing damage."

Spike pulled a plate from the shelf and handed it past Jet without comment. Jet took it automatically and kept staring into the pan like he had not noticed his own hand move.

They assembled themselves around the narrow table and every available flat surface with the clumsy efficiency of people who had done it too many times to think about it. Jet took the seat nearest the stove because of course he did. Spike leaned against the wall till Faye stole that spot too by setting her hip there first, so he ended up on an overturned crate. Ed sat cross-legged on the floor with a plate balanced on one knee. Ein hovered under the table in a state of focused spiritual commitment.

Ren put the food down in the middle and reached for the tea kettle.

"No coffee?" Jet said, suspicious already.

"No coffee."

"So this is punishment."

"It's tea," Ren said.

Faye took a mug, sniffed it, and made a face. "This smells like a ship part."

"Then don't drink it," Jet said.

She drank it anyway.

For thirty full seconds nobody mentioned Daichi. Or Ayane. Or clinics, precincts, syndicates, stolen names, or any of the other things that had been sitting on all of them since yesterday. They ate because the food was there and because hunger was simpler than moral history.

The first bite made Jet's eyebrows move despite him.

Faye saw it and pounced. "Oh, that's beautiful."

"What is?"

"That look."

"I didn't make a look."

"You made exactly a look." She pointed with half a flat cake. "That's the look you make when something annoys you by being good."

Jet chewed, swallowed, and aimed a glare across the table that would have worked on somebody who wasn't Faye.

"Needs more salt."

Spike took another bite before speaking. "No, it doesn't."

Jet turned on him. "You don't know."

"I'm eating it."

"That's not analysis."

"It's enough of it."

Ed had both hands in the food now, one holding a cake, the other scooping tomato and fish with complete disregard for the plate's intended geometry.

"Crunch disk number one," Ed said around a full mouth. "Very successful. Sauce level also successful. Tiny fish army defeated."

Ein put both front paws on Ed's knee. Ed fed him one illicit scrap under the table with the speed of long practice.

"Don't feed the dog from the table," Jet said automatically.

Ed looked around. "This is not a table. This is a multi-surface meal event."

Faye snorted tea through her nose and coughed.

While Jet was distracted by telling Ed to chew before talking, she leaned over and lifted a strip of fish straight off Spike's plate.

Spike looked down at the theft, then at her. "You have your own."

"I had my own. Now I've got yours too."

"You could've asked."

"And missed the fun."

Jet pointed at her with his fork. "Can you not do that for one meal?"

"No."

"At least she's honest," Spike said.

"Oh, shut up."

The argument had no heat in it. It moved on old rails. Yesterday they had sat in this same station with the whole ugly web spread out plain between them. This morning Jet was trying not to admit somebody else had seasoned a pan better than he would've, Faye was stealing off Spike's plate because Spike always let her get away with that amount and no more, Ed was making a private religion out of the crispy bits, and nobody seemed confused by the pattern of any of it.

Ren poured tea into the last mug and realized everybody else was already eating.

Ed looked up first. There was a red line from a folded document pressed into his cheek.

"Ren forgot Ren-food."

"I didn't forget."

"Then why is the plate empty?"

"Because I was pouring tea."

"That's a technical excuse."

Faye nudged the tray toward her with two fingers. "Sit down before he writes you a citation."

Jet, still chewing his resentment and his breakfast together, jerked his chin toward the only open spot, the edge of the bench near the wall. "Yeah. Sit."

It was casual, which might have been what made it harder. Breakfast had slid into invitation without stopping to announce itself.

Ren took a plate and sat on the bench because refusing would have turned it into something larger than breakfast. The wall of documents was at her back. From here she could see all of them at once: Jet guarding the pan even now, Spike folded into himself around the mug, Faye sprawled in defiance of available furniture, Ed all elbows and appetite, Ein making hopeful passes under the table like a practiced thief. The charger hum from outside still lived under the floor. Wind scraped the far wall. Somebody had left a wrench by the sink and a damp towel over the chair back. Morning had come all the way in.

Jet took a second helping when he thought nobody was paying attention.

Faye saw. "Unbelievable."

"What?"

"Nothing." She grinned around her mug. "Just nice to watch growth."

"I'm hungry."

"Sure you are."

He kept looking at the last flat cake on the tray as if looking did not count. Ren picked it up and set it on his plate.

Jet stared down at it. "I didn't ask for that."

"No," Ren said. "You were staring at it."

Faye folded over her mug laughing. Spike looked down at his plate and failed not to be amused. Ed slapped the floor once with an open palm.

"Crunch disk reassigned," Ed announced.

Jet looked around the room, then at Ren, as if trying to decide whether he'd been insulted or fed. "This is getting out of hand."

"Eat it or don't," Faye said.

He ate half of it in one bite.

Ren ate too. The food was good because she'd cooked it hot enough and long enough, and because old poor ingredients stopped being ashamed of themselves if you treated them like they were meant to be dinner. Jet muttered once that the fish would've taken black pepper better than dried chile. Spike said he was wrong. Jet said Spike had the palate of a floor drain. Ed wanted to know what a floor drain tasted like. Faye said probably like tea.

It went on.

It stayed breakfast. Whatever came next could wait. The knowledge stayed in the room, but it stayed the way dust stayed in Mars light, visible when the angle caught it, otherwise just part of breathing.

Ed looked up from his plate with sauce on his chin.

"Ren's breakfast wins," he declared.

Jet straightened. "Hold on."

"Data is clear."

"Your data is usually sticky."

"Still clear."

Faye lifted her mug in a lazy salute toward Ren. "I'm voting with the side that brought breakfast."

Spike shrugged. "Seems fair."

Jet pointed his fork at Ren because now there was nowhere else to point it. "Don't start looking pleased with yourself."

"I'm eating," Ren said.

"That is a look," Faye said.

Jet looked around the room like a man measuring the scale of a betrayal and finding it domestic instead of fatal.

"Unbelievable," he said.

But he took another piece anyway.

Ren looked down at her plate so they would not make a subject out of whatever was on her face; she could step into the meal, but the years that made it easy were still theirs.

She ate while they complained about tea, argued about pepper, and fed the dog when Jet wasn't looking.
