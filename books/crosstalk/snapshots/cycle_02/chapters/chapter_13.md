# Chapter 13

The galley light was still on when Jet opened the refrigerator.

Under the amber spill, the shelves offered up their little insults. Half an onion wrapped in a wet paper towel. One egg with a crack running through the shell like a hair in glass. A jar of mustard with more yellow dried around the lid than left inside. Soy sauce. A bell pepper that had gone soft at the stem. No meat. No coffee. No beer. Cold air rolled over Jet's knuckles and smelled faintly of mildew and old scallions. He shut the door before the motor could waste any more power pretending there was something worth cooling.

The traffic ticket was waiting for him on the galley screen, red and patient.

MARS MUNICIPAL ENFORCEMENT
OUTSTANDING VIOLATION
8,000 WOOLONGS
STATUS: OVERDUE

He hit dismiss. The screen chimed and brought it right back.

The Bebop sounded hungover. Hull ticking as the morning cold eased out of the metal. Life support humming in the walls. Somewhere below, a loose vent grille rattled every few seconds with the stubborn regularity of something Jet had meant to fix three days ago and now resented on principle. The whole ship smelled like engine grease, stale air, and the soup he had made two nights ago from the last of the mushrooms and a hope that had not lasted past dinner.

They had all come back at different hours.

Jet knew because he had heard the airlock cycle three separate times after midnight and because nobody was where they usually were this morning. Spike was stretched on the common-room couch without taking off his jacket, one forearm across his eyes, one foot on the floor like he had sat down for a minute and lost the argument with gravity. He smelled of cold rooftop wind and old smoke. Faye had not shown up until almost dawn. He knew that from the sound of her heels in the corridor and the brief sharp smell of public soap when she passed his door. Ed was already up because Ed was almost always up, cross-legged on the floor in front of the main screen with three terminal windows open and Ein leaning against one bony thigh.

Ed looked over as Jet stood there with the refrigerator's failure still in his hand.

"Sad box says no breakfast," Ed announced. "But bounty box says maybe breakfast. Maybe breakfast and also fine-paying. Fine-unpaying. One of those."

On the main screen, a mugshot rotated slowly under municipal graphics. SAL PERETTI. BAIL JUMP. COUNTERFEITING. FRAUDULENT SEALS, TRANSIT PAPERS, CERTIFIED ID CHIPS. REWARD: 30,000 WOOLONGS.

Peretti in the photo looked like the kind of man who ironed a shirt before he got arrested. Narrow face. Black hair combed down too carefully. A burgundy scarf tucked into the collar of a market jacket that had probably cost more than anything Jet was wearing. His mustache was just a shade too dark for the hair over his ears, which meant vanity or panic, maybe both.

Jet squinted at the details scrolling beneath. Last confirmed sighting: Tharsis bazaar district. Possible local protection. Likely armed. Bond posted by a private guarantor with a long record of regrettable judgment.

"How fresh?" Jet asked.

"Eight hours if the sleepy municipal people are not lying, which they might be, because sleepy people are chaos goblins." Ed tapped a window with one toe. "Peretti buys fake papers, sells fake papers, makes fake papers, likes the bazaar because everybody there already thinks somebody else is cheating. Natural habitat."

Ein thumped his tail once. That was optimism in dog form.

Jet opened a cabinet, found two packets of instant broth, and decided hot salt water counted as leadership under the circumstances. He set the kettle on.

Spike said, from under his arm, "If that's breakfast, put me back to sleep."

Jet did not turn. "You're awake enough to complain. You're awake."

"Tragic."

The corridor door slid open. Faye came in still buttoning her jacket, dark hair damp at the ends, eyes flat from too little sleep or too much thought. Public bath soap clung to her for maybe two seconds before the ship's normal stink took it back. She stopped when she saw the screen.

"Thirty thousand?" she said.

Jet nodded.

Faye's mouth tightened. "On paper."

"Yeah."

"That's funny."

Jet turned from the kettle then, because if he kept looking at the red ticket warning he was going to start talking to it. "What's funny is eight thousand overdue to traffic enforcement, a fuel gauge that might as well be decorative, and whatever this is." He held up the bell pepper. It sagged in his fingers. "This is not food. This is a cry for help."

Faye dropped into a chair at the table. "You don't need to sell me on being broke. I live here."

"Do you?" Spike asked without moving his arm.

She looked at him. "Start with me this early and I'll sell your cigarettes."

Spike lowered the arm enough to look back at her with one sleepy eye. "You'd smoke half of them on the way to the buyer."

"I'd charge myself retail."

Faye's chair legs scraped the deck when she sat back. Spike let his forearm fall over his eyes again. Jet poured hot water into four chipped mugs and set them in their usual places. Steam climbed off them. Faye took hers without looking up.

He carried his mug to the table, thumbed the Peretti file larger, and said, "We're taking him."

Faye blew across the surface of what was basically seasoned steam. "Who's we."

Jet looked at the screen again, then at the ticket notice, then at the refrigerator as if it might develop character and solve something for him. "Unless one of you found eight thousand woolongs under a cushion overnight, it's we."

Ed brightened. "Ed found three buttons, half a playing card, and a noodle fossil under the couch. No woolongs. Tragedy."

Spike sat up slowly. The couch gave a tired squeak under him. "Bazaar's a bad place to tail a paranoid guy. Too many doors. Too many cousins."

"I know where the bazaar is," Jet said.

"Do you." Spike rubbed at one eye with the heel of his hand. "Because the last time you took the Bebop into a Tharsis side lane we got a letter from traffic enforcement with a picture."

Jet held up one finger. "We're not doing this."

Faye gave a short laugh that had no humor in it. "That's optimistic."

Jet planted both hands on the table and looked at all of them in turn. He did not want a speech. He especially did not want a family speech, not after last night, not after the week they had been having, not with a mug of hot broth standing in for breakfast like an insult with steam coming off it. What he wanted was simple, practical, mercenary, and therefore maybe possible.

"Listen," he said. "We need the money. That's all this is. We catch Peretti, we pay the ticket, we buy actual groceries, maybe enough fuel not to drift in place like idiots. Nobody has to enjoy it."

Faye stared into her mug. "Good. Because I don't."

She said it to the surface of the broth. When she set the mug down, the thin liquid slapped the chipped sides and kept trembling after her hand was still.

Jet nodded once. "Fine."

Usually that would have been the meeting. He would have said the name, the price, maybe one note about location, and the rest would have arranged itself. Faye took social. Spike took approach. Jet took exits. Ed took comms and the invisible electronic layer under everything else. They had done it a hundred times.

This morning Jet had to say every part out loud.

"Faye," he said, hating how formal it sounded already, "I need you in the stalls. Food row, tea houses, gambling corners if they're open this early. Peretti buys local cover. He does not grow it himself. Talk to the vendors, the runners, the people who notice him because he pays late or complains too much. Find out where he's sleeping and who is watching for him."

Faye looked up. "My cut comes off the top."

"We always split it."

"No, Jet. I mean off the top. Before you decide the ship needs twelve gallons of something and a new valve and a replacement screw nobody can live without." She set the mug down. "I'm doing this because I need money. Don't make it into anything else."

Jet felt the old reflex rise in him, the one that wanted to argue about shares and fairness and the invisible accounting by which he fed them all and kept the ship alive while they treated both facts like weather. He shoved it back down because he did not have room for pride with half an onion in the refrigerator.

"Fine," he said. "You get your quarter."

She narrowed her eyes. "A quarter of thirty?"

"A quarter of what we actually get."

"There you go."

Spike took a sip of broth and made a face like the ship itself had offended him. "Inspiring stuff."

Jet ignored him. "Spike. Foot tail. Not roofline unless you have to. Bazaar awnings are rotten and half the catwalk bolts in spice row are original settlement junk. He gets spooked, he'll run for storage or loading access. I want eyes on him before he knows he's being pushed."

Spike leaned back against the couch cushion and crossed one ankle over the other. He looked awake now, which on Spike mostly meant his expression had flattened into attention. "South service lane's no good for the Bebop."

Jet stopped. "I didn't say south service lane."

"You were about to."

Jet hated that he was right. "South service lane gives me loading access and two exits."

"South service lane is six meters on paper and five if the kebab guys put their extra propane out early."

"Then I'll squeeze."

"Then you'll get stuck and he'll walk past your nose."

Faye looked between them. "Can we save the divorce for after lunch."

"We're not married," Jet said.

"Thank God."

Spike did not bother with the joke. "North access. Park wide by the generator bank. You can cover the lane on foot and swing the ship down only if he breaks for the warehouses."

Jet hated that too, because it was better. "North puts the ship farther from the market center."

"Which is why he won't clock it."

Jet rubbed at the scar on his cheek with the heel of his hand. "And if he bolts south?"

Spike shrugged. "Then I push him south and Faye makes noise on the east side."

Faye pointed at him with two fingers. "Make noise means what, exactly."

"Exactly what you're good at."

"You really know how to ask for help."

"I'm not asking."

"Then fuck you."

"Kids," Jet said, louder than he meant to. The word hit the room and lay there. Faye's face shut a little more. Spike's expression did not change at all, which was worse. Jet exhaled through his nose and amended, "We don't have time."

Ed had been listening with bright, shameless interest, chin propped on both hands. "Ed has watcher map," Ed said into the silence. "Two confirmed maybe three. One is red-cap boy on dried-fruit row. He has a school tie in his pocket and keeps touching it when he thinks nobody sees, so probably borrowed cousin labor. One is tea auntie with the gold tooth, lane three. She knits tiny socks between customers and taps her ear when something moves that shouldn't. Third watcher maybe maybe not. Old man at the prayer bead stall with one bad knee and a bad poker face."

Jet took a breath. "Thank you, Ed."

"Ed is very useful."

"Yeah."

The kettle clicked as its base cooled. Somewhere in the corridor, a pipe knocked once and settled.

Jet sat down finally and pulled the bazaar map up from memory more than need, two fingers spreading the grid. "Ed, you stay on comms and camera pulls. I want market feeds, traffic feeds, private security if you can get it, stall cams if you're feeling illegal."

Ed looked offended. "Ed is always feeling illegal."

That got a sound out of Spike that was almost a laugh.

Jet went on before the room could remember how to breathe. "I'll take the Bebop to north access, keep wide, stay out of sight. If he breaks warehouse side, I come down and block. If he runs east, I close east. If he gets near transit, Faye, you call it early. We don't let him get on a train, a tram, a cargo lift, a food cart, whatever the hell is moving in that district today."

Faye rested one elbow on the table. "And if I get to him first?"

"You don't bring him in alone."

"Because you don't trust me?"

Jet met her eyes. There was no good answer. The bad ones all lived on his tongue.

"Because Peretti hires backup," he said. "Because he's got at least two watchers. Because the last thing I need is you taking a stun round from some tea-seller's nephew because we were all too proud to say the obvious part out loud."

She held his gaze a second longer, then looked away first. "Fine."

Spike set his empty mug down on the floor by the couch instead of on the table like a civilized person. "If he sees me, the chase goes bad fast."

"I know."

"Then don't talk in my ear unless you have something."

Jet almost said since when has that stopped you. He let it go. "Fine."

Ed raised a hand. "Peretti has a Thursday candy habit."

Jet's hand stalled on the back of his chair. Faye blinked at Ed. Spike looked over without lifting anything but his eyes.

Ed blinked. "Fennel seeds from the striped cart on lane six. Sweet breath for a liar's mouth. It's not Thursday, but habits bend around panic. Also he hates garlic sauce. Left two complaints under fake names last month. Ed found reviews."

Faye snorted despite herself. "You hacked kebab reviews."

"Public data is lonely if no one looks at it."

The room eased half an inch. Not much. Enough to notice.

Jet pushed away from the table. "Gear up."

The mug in Faye's hand clicked once against the table. Spike rolled forward off the couch and stood, stretching as he went. Faye finished the broth in one annoyed swallow and headed for the corridor to get her sidearm and a jacket with deeper pockets. Ed swung around toward the console, fingers already flying. Ein sneezed once and trotted after the sound of movement, nails clicking over deck plate.

Jet stayed where he was long enough to look at the refrigerator one more time.

Half an onion. One bad egg. A soft pepper. There were people who said money was abstract. Those people had never had to open a door and count the shape of lunch.

He grabbed his coat, killed the ticket alert again, and followed the others out.

* * *

The Tharsis bazaar always felt one loud noise away from collapse.

Canvas roofs sagged over metal frames older than half the people selling under them. Extension cords looped from generator to generator in black knots that would have made any fire inspector cry if Mars had funded enough fire inspectors to cry properly. Charcoal smoke hung low over kebab row. Dust moved underfoot in thin red films, pushed around by boots and cart wheels and the drag of too many coats. Somebody was shouting over the price of dried apricots. Somebody else was shouting because shouting worked even when the price didn't change. Somewhere deeper in the market, a child laughed like a thrown bolt hitting steel.

Faye moved through it in sunglasses and a pale jacket that made her look richer than she was if nobody got close enough to check the hem. Close enough happened all the time. She used that too.

"Lane six, striped cart," Jet said in her ear. "You heard Ed."

"I heard Ed. Try not to narrate my legs while I'm using them."

Jet muttered something that got lost under market noise. Good.

Faye cut between a stall selling bootleg cartridges and a counter stacked with oranges that had been polished harder than the vendor's teeth. She smelled lamb fat, old fryer oil, coffee so burnt it had gone sweet again, cumin, coriander, hot metal, human breath. The bazaar was tight in the shoulders. You did not walk through it so much as let it push you in the direction you had decided to pretend was yours.

The striped cart sat where Ed said it would, white-and-blue awning patched with clear tape. The man behind it was turning skewers with one hand and patting his chest with the other like he had just remembered he owned lungs. He was in his fifties maybe, beard gone mostly gray, a silver thumbnail on his left hand where some kid had painted it and he had never bothered to scrub it off. An inhaler hung from a cord around his neck and bounced against his apron when he moved.

Faye stopped at the counter and gave him the smile she used on men who wanted to feel chosen for something.

"You selling food or smoke," she asked, "because I only have time for one disappointment."

He looked up, took her in, and grinned despite the coughing edge in his chest. "Depends what you order."

"Dealer's choice."

"That is how people get fed organs they did not ask for."

"You always talk like this before noon?"

"Only when I can tell it's being appreciated." He turned a skewer and winced as smoke came back at him. "What do you want?"

Faye leaned on the counter, close enough to suggest interest, not close enough to promise any. "Man in a burgundy scarf. Too pretty to let grease near him. Complains for sport."

The vendor barked out a laugh. "Peretti."

"So you've met."

"Everybody's met. He eats here and then tells me I don't understand restraint." The man touched his inhaler, frowned at it as if it had failed him personally, and lowered his voice. "No garlic sauce, extra napkins, asks if I have filtered water like we're in a hotel."

"Where is he."

The vendor's eyes slid left, then right, not because he was scared exactly. Because fear had been in the bazaar so long it had learned to dress like habit. "Why."

"Because his face annoys me."

"That is not a shortage."

Faye took a folded bill from her pocket and set it beneath the edge of a metal tray. Not much. Enough to say this was not charity and not friendship either.

He covered the bill with his hand and sighed through his nose. "He went through tea lane ten minutes ago. Headed toward spice row. Had red-cap boy drifting behind and old Litsa at the samovar watching his back. He's jumpy today. Kept wiping his fingers after he touched the counter. Ink man who hates his own hands dirty. Imagine."

"I don't have to."

"If you find him," the vendor said, then hesitated.

Faye waited.

He shrugged. "Tell him he still owes me for Tuesday. Eight woolongs."

He tucked her bill under the tray with two smoke-stained fingers and turned the skewer before it burned.

Faye smiled without warmth this time. "What's your name."

"Rafi."

"If I see him, I'll mention it."

She pushed off the counter and kept moving before the moment could think it meant anything. "Tea lane. Spice row. You were right."

"I know," Ed sang back at her through static and market noise. "Red-cap boy shifted. Tea auntie also shifted. Peretti is noodle-wriggling."

Spike's voice came in low and flat. "I see red cap."

Jet said, "Where."

"Above dried fruit. Three stalls east of tea lane."

"That's not where Faye is."

"I can count, Jet."

Faye turned at the sound of a tea glass shattering somewhere behind her and caught the movement three bodies ahead. Burgundy scarf. Market jacket. One hand at the collar, the other brushing at his cuff with a folded square of white cloth that came away faintly blue at one corner. Sal Peretti moved like a man trying to look casual in a place that punished effort. He stepped around a puddle, gave a basket of eels a disgusted glance, and paused by the tea lane samovar only long enough for the old woman behind it to tap one finger against her ear.

Litsa, apparently. Sixty if a day. Thick wrists. Gold tooth. Knitting two tiny yellow socks between pours with the indifference of someone who had seen police, gangsters, tax collectors, and drunks do their little dances in front of her for twenty years and found none of them worth dropping a stitch for.

Faye kept walking. If she stopped, the pattern stopped. If the pattern stopped, Peretti's head would turn.

"I've got him," she said.

Jet came back too fast. "Confirm."

She bit down on the first answer that rose in her throat. "Burgundy scarf. Dark mustache dye job. White handkerchief with ink on it. He looks like he filed a complaint against gravity. That enough for you?"

Spike said, "It's him."

The old familiarity of that irritated her more than if he had disagreed.

Peretti took the mouth of spice row without looking behind him. He had the confidence of a man who trusted other people's eyes more than his own. Red-cap boy moved off the dried-fruit stall roof and melted into the crowd. Litsa kept knitting.

Jet said, "I'm moving north access."

There was a burst of engine noise in the distance as the Bebop shifted from idle to offended motion.

Faye shadowed Peretti past sacks of lentils and hanging strips of cured meat. The air changed as spice row took over: warmer, drier, sharper. Paprika. Turmeric. Ground coriander. Cardamom. Cumin thick enough to taste on the back of the tongue before she was close to the warehouse doors.

Peretti stopped at the striped candy cart on lane six.

Ed made a triumphant noise in her ear. "Habit!"

Peretti bought a paper twist of sugared fennel, tipped a few seeds into his mouth, and checked the reflection in a polished samovar side as he chewed. He was not handsome. He was a man with handsome ambitions and the budget to support them badly. His scarf was real silk or wanted to be. The handkerchief was monogrammed S.P. in the corner. He cleaned ink off his fingers with the devotion other men gave religion.

Faye slowed near a spice merchant pretending to argue over fenugreek and watched him without watching him. He lifted his head, suddenly still. Not because he had seen her. Because something in the market changed behind him. Red-cap boy had reached Litsa. Litsa had looked up from the baby sock. A pulse went through the row.

"He knows," Faye said.

"Push him south," Jet said.

"I'm not a broom."

"Faye."

But she was already moving.

She cut left and grabbed the elbow of a man carrying a tray of tea glasses. "Sorry, sweetheart," she said, and sent the tray tilting just enough for hot tea to slosh across the lane in front of Peretti's shoes.

He recoiled with a hiss, more offended than alarmed. "Watch it!"

He looked up. Saw her. Saw nothing, maybe. A woman in sunglasses swearing at a tea seller. Market noise. Bad luck. He turned right instead of left.

Right was south.

"He's moving," Spike said.

Faye did not look for him. She knew where he would be because she knew how he hunted. Somewhere above eye level, somewhere lazy-looking and exact.

Red-cap boy tried to angle after Peretti and Faye stepped into his path hard enough to make him bounce back a pace. Up close he was younger than Ed's guess, maybe sixteen, acne under the brim and a school tie stuffed in one pocket of his jacket exactly where Ed said it would be.

"Sorry," Faye said with perfect insincerity.

"Hey-"

"Go home."

He blinked at her, startled enough to miss Peretti's next turn. Then he remembered his job and shoved past. Faye caught his sleeve, twisted, and sent him stumbling shoulder-first into a stack of flattened boxes. Not graceful. Effective.

"Watcher down," she said.

"Temporarily," the kid yelled behind her.

"Good for you."

Spike had Peretti now. He dropped from a low service ladder onto the top of a refrigeration unit, crossed it in three silent steps, and watched the man slide through spice row faster than his shoes wanted to go. Peretti was not built for running. He was built for desks and printers and whispered transactions over good tea. But fear made sprinters out of people whose lungs objected, and now he was moving with his scarf half out and his precious cuffs forgotten.

He glanced back once. That was enough.

His eyes caught Spike above the lane and widened, not in recognition but in the ancient animal understanding that someone faster had entered the frame.

Peretti bolted.

"Warehouse side," Spike said.

"I'm cutting him off," Jet answered.

Through gaps in awnings, Spike heard the Bebop's engines rising. Not full burn. Just enough to make every loose sign and hanging light in the lane start trembling. A few heads turned upward with the resigned fury of market people seeing a ship where ships had no business being.

Spike dropped to ground level and went after the scarf.

Peretti hit a stack of burlap sacks with one hip, rebounded, cursed, and kept going. He ran with both hands out from his body like he couldn't bear them brushing the dirty walls. Fennel seeds flew from the paper twist still clenched in one fist.

"Left," Ed said in Spike's ear. "No, his left. Your other left. Good job."

Spike cut through a narrow lane between cardamom bins and came out parallel to Peretti just as the man burst into loading access.

The Bebop's shadow crossed the lane first. Then the ship itself nosed down into the opening like an insult too large for the room. Jet brought her in crooked, ugly, perfect enough. Market vendors scattered with crates in their arms, shouting. A drone rose up from somewhere under the eaves and began issuing legal objections in a flat machine voice.

"Unlawful docking posture. Unlawful docking posture."

"Get in line," Jet snapped at it over comms.

Peretti saw the ship, swore, and veered again, this time through the half-open sliding door of a spice warehouse.

Faye reached the loading lane in time to see him disappear inside. Her breath burned. Her hair had come loose under the jacket collar. Somewhere behind her, Litsa was still shouting in a language that probably predated half the laws being broken in front of her.

"Back door?" Jet said.

Faye was already there, circling the warehouse wall. "On it."

Inside, the air went thick at once.

Spice warehouses never looked like much from outside. Inside they were caves built out of smell. Sacks piled shoulder-high. Wooden pallets. Narrow aisles. Light coming in dusty through vents near the ceiling. Cumin, coriander, chili, turmeric, cinnamon, dried pepper skins. Enough scent to coat the mouth and sit there.

Peretti slipped on loose powder at the first turn and caught himself with both hands against a sack. "Shit."

Spike saw him recover and knew exactly where the man's next three choices were going to be because panic made people simple.

Right aisle blocked by stacked bins. Left too narrow. Straight through the center lane, toward the back door Faye was already taking.

Spike ran.

Peretti glanced back again and made the mistake of trying to dodge before he had room. He cut sideways around a pallet of cumin sacks, one foot skidding on spilled seed, shoulder clipping the stack. Spike hit him low and hard a breath later.

They went into the sacks together.

One split with a muffled pop. Then another. A brown-yellow cloud burst up around them, hot and bitter and dry enough to scrape the wet out of Spike's nose. Cumin filled his mouth. His eyes. The back of his throat. It got under his collar and into the sweat at his neck. Peretti screamed, not from the tackle itself but from seeing both hands disappear wrist-deep into spice.

"Not the hands," he yelled, coughing. "Jesus Christ, not the hands."

Spike pinned his right arm, sneezed twice into his shoulder, and got a knee between Peretti's shoulder blades. The floor was slick with powder. Everything smelled like somebody had ground up a whole kitchen and fired it out of a cannon.

"Stop moving."

"I'm not moving, I'm dying."

"Not yet."

Peretti twisted anyway. Spice caked the burgundy scarf into a dirty rope. The monogram on his handkerchief was gone under dust. He looked less like a careful man now and more like what he was: a thin, middle-aged crook with pretty habits and bad lungs.

The back door slammed open.

Faye came in with her gun up and stopped dead at the sight of them half-buried in cumin. For one clean second she just stared. Then a laugh escaped her, sharp and involuntary.

"Oh, that's incredible."

"Cuffs," Spike said.

"Ask nicely."

Jet thundered in from the loading side before Spike could answer. His boots threw spice prints. His coat smelled like cold ship metal and engine wash. He took in the scene in one sweep and said, "Of course it's cumin."

The drone outside was still reciting the law to anyone with the patience to be corrected by a machine.

Faye tossed restraints. Jet caught them and knelt, cybernetic fingers snapping one cuff closed around Peretti's wrist before the man could remember he had options.

Peretti jerked. "Wait, wait, I can make you money."

"You're making us money now," Jet said.

"No, more money. Papers. Transit seals. Medical clearance chips. Freight stamps. Whatever you need."

Faye crouched beside him, looked at his spice-packed cuffs, and said, "If you could print groceries, you'd be out of here already."

"Lady, I printed a clinic pass for a bishop last month."

"Good for the bishop."

Jet hauled his arms back and clicked the second cuff. "Save it."

Peretti coughed so hard his whole body folded around the sound. When he spat to clear his throat, the spit came out yellow-brown with cumin dust. "Rafi still owes me change," he muttered.

Faye looked at him. "Rafi says you owe him eight woolongs."

For the first time since the chase started, genuine indignation broke through his fear. "That is a lie. I owed him six."

Spike sat back on his heels, sneezed again, and wiped his nose on a sleeve that was already lost to spice. The whole warehouse had gone hazy. Cumin lay over the floor in drifts. It would be in the folds of his jacket for days. He could feel grains grinding between his teeth.

An old woman in a quilted vest appeared at the end of the aisle holding a ledger pad against one hip. Half-moon glasses hung low on her nose. One hearing aid whistled faintly before she slapped it once and made it behave.

"You break it," she said, looking at the burst sacks, "you bought it."

Jet got to his feet with Peretti in tow. "Put it on his tab."

"His tab is terrible."

"Then put it on municipal justice."

The woman sniffed the air as if testing whether sarcasm had a market price. "That one never pays either."

Peretti twisted to look back at the ruined sacks with genuine sorrow. "This was imported."

Faye stood and brushed cumin off her knees. "You forged transit seals for a living. Consider it cultural exchange."

They got him out into the lane with the drone still complaining and half the market pretending not to stare. Red-cap boy had vanished. Litsa at the tea stand gave Peretti one long look, clicked her knitting needles together, and went back to the tiny yellow sock in her hands as if all this proved something she had been saying for years.

Jet marched Peretti toward the holding transport call point. Spike followed, smelling like a spice grinder. Faye walked on the other side with her jacket open and her gun hidden again, face blank now that the laugh had gotten out of her system.

Cumin shook off Spike's jacket every time he moved. A yellow-brown stripe ran down Jet's coat sleeve where he had hauled Peretti upright. Faye wiped her knee with two fingers and only smeared the spice wider.

* * *

The bounty office smelled like paper dust, overheated plastic, and old disappointment.

Peretti sat on a holding bench with his wrists sealed to a ring in the wall and kept trying to brush cumin out of his sleeves with his fingertips. It was hopeless. Every time he thought he had won, a fresh brown smear bloomed across the fabric. He looked sick with it.

At the counter, the clerk took one look at the capture paperwork, one look at Peretti in the bench area, and one look at the spice all over Jet's coat and said, "Warehouse arrest."

Jet set the forms down. "Yeah."

The clerk was a small woman with compression gloves cut off at the knuckles and a peppermint antacid parked in one cheek hard enough to change the line of her face. A strand of silver tinsel from some forgotten holiday decoration was tied around her wrist like she had found it on the floor and never made time to remove it.

She keyed the intake number, frowned at the return, and clicked her tongue. "Oh, that's ugly."

Faye leaned one shoulder against the counter. "How ugly."

"Bond got reviewed down at eleven forty-seven."

Jet looked at the wall clock. Twelve twenty-three.

"Poster says thirty," he said.

"Poster was true when somebody printed it." The clerk sucked the peppermint to one side of her mouth and clicked again. "Judge knocked him to fifteen-eight on nonviolent and community-ties grounds."

Peretti called from the bench, offended all over again. "My aunt was in the room."

The clerk did not look at him. "Your aunt cries on command. Everybody knows that."

Faye closed her eyes briefly. "Fifteen-eight."

"Afraid so."

Jet put both hands on the counter. "He had armed watchers."

"Did he shoot you."

"No."

"Then the judge called it nonviolent." She printed the receipt with a mechanical cough. "Take it up with democracy."

There were arguments worth having and arguments that only cost breath. Jet took the chit when it came out of the machine and felt the weight of nothing in it. Fifteen thousand eight hundred woolongs. Enough to hurt less. Not enough to fix anything important.

Spike, sitting on the windowsill with one boot heel against the wall, said, "Better than six."

Jet looked at him.

Spike shrugged. Cumin dust lifted off his jacket in a faint cloud. "Rafi's version."

Even Jet laughed at that, once, against his own will.

It went away fast.

They stopped at the enforcement kiosk on the walk out because if Jet carried that ticket another day he was going to start seeing its red warning every time he blinked. The kiosk stood between a payday loan office and a machine that sold hot canned coffee, tucked under a plastic awning stained orange by dust and age.

Jet fed the bounty chit into the slot. The screen flashed the ticket back at him with bureaucratic cheer.

OUTSTANDING VIOLATION
8,000 WOOLONGS
PAY NOW?

"No," Faye said flatly behind him. "We just came here for the scenery."

Jet pressed YES hard enough to make the panel complain.

The machine thought about it for a long time. Then:

PAYMENT ACCEPTED
THANK YOU FOR SUPPORTING SAFE MUNICIPAL AIRSPACE

"Liar," Jet said.

"Language," Ed said in his ear from the ship, pleased to be included from afar. "Ed is monitoring the illegal parking count. We're at two warnings, one citation threat, and one very stern drone poem."

"Can it wait."

"Everything waits if you ignore it long enough."

"Not helping."

"Helping spiritually."

They went to the market with what was left and the mood of people doing math they already knew would end badly.

Jet carried the number in his head the whole time. Fifteen-eight minus eight. Seven-eight. Docking fee later. Fuel, not today. Repairs, not today. Coffee, probably not. Food, if they kept their heads. Food and enough left over to let the ship keep insulting them tomorrow.

The grocery stalls nearest the paid docks sold in layers: what you wanted at eye level, what you could afford lower down, what even the broke people avoided in the bins by the ground. Jet worked the shelves from bottom up.

Rice. Dried noodles. Eggs, the cheaper tray with two already cracked and discounted for honesty. Bell peppers because he could still turn those into something that tasted like dinner even when the protein situation was an argument with reality. Green onions. Cabbage. A squat bottle of cooking oil. Cheap protein strips made from soy and lies. Dog food that smelled so bad through the sealed bag he could feel Ein judging him from half a ship away.

He stopped in front of the beef case and did not bother pretending. Thin shaved strips, red under the lights, more expensive by the gram than common sense. He stood there a second too long anyway.

Spike came up beside him, hands in pockets. He still smelled like cumin. So did Jet. So did Faye. The whole day had settled into their clothes.

"Move along," Spike said. "It only encourages them."

Jet snorted and took a pack of tofu instead.

Faye was two stalls over with a basket on one hip and an expression like she was offended by every price tag on principle. She picked up a can of peaches in syrup, turned it to read the cost, and set it back with careful contempt. Then she grabbed three onions and a packet of cigarettes, held both a second, and put the cigarettes back too.

Jet saw. He did not say anything.

In their line of work, dignity about money usually lasted until the cashier named the total. After that you put things back and kept your face still.

At the register, the total came up, they all did the same silent subtraction, and Jet pulled two things off the pile before the cashier could say the number again: the better oil and one tray of eggs. The cashier, a girl with freckles across her nose and a bandage on one knuckle, did not give them pity. Jet appreciated that more than kindness.

"You still want the scallions?" she asked.

Jet looked at the pile.

Faye said, "Keep the scallions."

He glanced at her.

She was looking at the register, not him. "What, you want to eat gray food."

Jet left the scallions on the counter.

The paid dock was two blocks away. By the time they turned onto the second one, the afternoon had gone colder. Mars did that without ceremony. One minute the air was merely thin and mean. The next it slipped under your collar and started collecting rent.

Jet took the heavier bags because of course he did. One loop cut into the fingers of his right hand. The left, with the prosthetic, barely registered weight. Spike carried rice and dog food with the bored posture of a man pretending not to carry anything. Faye had a bag of vegetables and the oil. Ed kept talking in their ears from the ship about docking threats, a salvage forum argument, and the fact that Ein had tried to eat a receipt.

Mostly, though, they walked in silence.

Not dramatic silence. Not movie silence. Just the flat worn-out silence of people who had been up too long, gotten what they went for, and still felt cheated by arithmetic. Their boots scraped dust. Plastic bag handles whispered and stretched. A transit tram groaned somewhere overhead.

Spike said, after a block, "Rafi really said eight?"

Faye kept her eyes on the pavement. "With feeling."

"He's getting soft."

"He has asthma."

"That's not what I meant."

"I know."

A tram rattled over the cross street. Spike hitched the rice higher against his hip. Faye kept her eyes on the pavement, but one corner of her mouth moved before it went flat again. Their steps hit the dust in the same beat for the rest of the block.

Jet shifted the groceries in his hand. One of the cheap bags gave a small plastic complaint. He looked down. The corner of the rice package had started sawing through the seam.

He should have doubled it at the stall. He had been doing the numbers in his head instead.

The Bebop's paid berth came into view between two cargo fences, old hull paint dulled by dust, one docking light flickering in a way that had definitely moved from tomorrow's problem to tonight's. Jet watched it blink and felt the day lining itself up into the next day already: unload, cook, patch the light, see what else broke while they were gone, decide whether fifteen hundred woolongs counted as hope or insult.

He heard himself speak before he had decided to.

"Same time tomorrow, or are we still not talking?"

Spike made a small sound that could have been a laugh or a cough dragged backward through his nose. He did not answer.

Faye didn't either.

She stepped closer, took the splitting bag out of Jet's right hand, and twisted the plastic around the rice until the seam stopped whining. The handle left a red line across her palm.

Jet let go.

The bags whispered against their coats after that. They kept walking toward the ship with the groceries between them and cumin still caught in the cuffs of their coats.
