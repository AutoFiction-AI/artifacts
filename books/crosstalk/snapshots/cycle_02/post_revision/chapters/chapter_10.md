# Chapter 10

Jet hated the hat before he even put it on.

It sat in the middle of the Bebop's galley table like an accusation: cheap brown felt, pinched brim, one side going soft where somebody had once sweated through it and then left it in a locker with mothballs and a leaking bottle of engine degreaser. Faye had found it in storage and announced, with the confidence of a woman who had never had to wear a disguise that made her look like an off-brand cardsharp, that it was perfect.

"It says failed youth with ideas above his station," she said.

Jet stared at it. "That's not helpful."

"It isn't wrong."

"It's the hat," Spike said from the couch. "Maybe the hat is the criminal."

Ed, upside down on top of the backrest with a tablet hanging from one hand, peered at the hat as if it might produce useful data if insulted long enough. "Takara hat. Hosuke hat. Mystery hat with bad decisions in the sweatband."

Jet picked it up between two fingers. The felt left dust on his skin. The thing smelled like damp cardboard and the back end of a pawn shop. "The point," he said, "is not the hat."

"Then we're already in trouble," Faye said.

Outside the common room windows Mars burned under a thin amber haze. They had set the Bebop down in a wash half a kilometer from the settlement, where the hull blended well enough with rusted boulders and old mining scrap that nobody passing overhead would pay it much mind. The settlement itself sprawled along the rim beyond them, sheet-metal roofs and patched domes and a market strip wrapped around a refueling tower like barnacles around a pipe. Every gust of wind sent red dust sliding over the viewport in a dry hiss.

They were close enough to smell other people's generators when the air system cycled.

Jet set the hat down and braced both hands on the table. His cybernetic fingers clicked softly against the laminate. On the screen behind him Ed had pulled up a settlement map and painted routes across it in colors that meant something to Ed and only partly to the adults. Yellow for the market. Blue for sight lines. Green for gossip, which in Ed's system was apparently a measurable resource.

"One more time," Jet said.

"One more time," Ed echoed happily, then tapped the screen. A cluster of blinking symbols bloomed around the market square. "Public registry kiosk here. Old access node here. Gambling room here. Bar here. Roof roof roof, nice roof. Mister Spike gets roof. Faye-Faye gets card people and bar people and lying people. Jet-person gets hat and face and old criminal vibes. Ed gets the skies and the wires and all the boops. Ein gets moral support."

Ein barked once from under the table and resumed worrying at the edge of a frayed seat cushion.

"The pilot has been following old lines," Jet said. He kept his voice practical because that was the only way to say any of this without tasting the grit of it. "Places that matter to one of us. She used Daichi Kozuki's access codes to wake up an old bounty. She knows how to trip wires in systems nobody's touched in years. So we put one in front of her. We make it look like Hosuke Takara surfaced here, in a settlement where he used to move freight and scam inventory tallies, and if she's watching the same channels, she comes to take a look."

Spike's gaze stayed on the ceiling. "And if she already knows what you look like now?"

"Then she already knows what I look like now."

"Strong plan."

Jet ignored him. He was better at ignoring Spike when Spike was horizontal. "I don't need her to fall in love with the disguise. I need her to know the name is in play and come close enough to make a move."

Faye folded her arms. "And my part in this elegant operation is what, exactly? Because if you say 'be charming,' I'm leaving."

"Your part is to do what you always do when money might be in the room," Jet said. "Work the social layer. Bars, card tables, gossip. Find out who's new, who's paying cash, who's asking the wrong questions."

"So be charming."

"Be useful."

"I hate the way you say that like they're different."

"They're different," Spike said.

Faye turned on the couch hard enough to make the springs complain. "You know, if you want to help, you can keep helping from all the way over there."

Spike lifted one shoulder. "That's the roof's job."

Jet pointed at him with the hat. "You're on overwatch. West roofline above the market. If she comes in on foot, you see her. If she comes in with settlement security on her tail, you see that too. And if you get bored and wander off, I'm feeding your share to the dog."

"You keep threatening me with a thing the dog is going to do anyway."

Ed zoomed the map until the market became a jumble of rectangles and lane markers. "Pilot-person likes old routes and secondary exits and places where people can vanish into noodle steam. Ed likes all those things too. Ed has covered the sky and the old frequencies and the boring frequencies and the frequencies that pretend to be boring. If the ship says hello, Ed will hear it."

"Good," Jet said.

Nobody said what everyone in the room knew: they had tried speed already. They had tried surprise and formation flying and Spike's reflexes and Faye's nerve and Ed's eyes. The girl in the sleek ship had run them through a canyon, let them chase a freight drone for twenty minutes, and sent them back with an eight-thousand-woolong traffic summons they could not afford. Now they were trying theater.

It irritated Jet that theater suited this mess as well as it did.

He took the hat and pulled it on. Faye put a hand over her mouth like she was trying, unsuccessfully, not to laugh.

"No," she said. "Absolutely not."

"What?"

"You look like you're about to sell counterfeit heater filters out of a backpack."

"That was sort of the idea," Spike said.

"No, the idea was younger. Petty. This is middle-aged fraud."

Jet reached for the old jacket folded over the chair. It was brown too, because apparently his teenage self had once believed in blending into the woodwork by looking like the woodwork. The shoulders didn't sit right anymore. At seventeen he had been broad already, but not like this. Not with the prosthetic arm. Not with years on him. He looked at the mirror over the sink and saw something worse than a disguise that did not work.

He saw continuity.

Hosuke Takara had not gone away. He had put on a badge, later a bounty license, later a cook's apron in a ship galley, and none of it had erased the kid who knew which loading clerks could be bribed and which security doors latched badly in cold weather. Jet had spent a long time telling himself those versions had come one after the other, clean as train cars. The last few weeks had made a mess of that.

He tugged the hat lower. "We're burning daylight."

Faye grabbed a long coat off a hook by the hatch and shrugged into it over her yellow top. The coat was cheap settlement synth-fur, cream once, now something closer to nicotine. She made it look deliberate anyway. "If I get stabbed because of this plan, I'm haunting you personally."

"If you get stabbed, I'm charging you for the shirt," Jet said.

"Good. Everybody is warmed up," Ed said. "Comedy pressure at ninety-three percent."

"That's not a real number," Spike said.

"Now it's real."

They went down into the cold.

The settlement's outer lane was packed hard by boots, cart treads, and years of dust ground into a red-brown crust. Plastic prayer strips snapped from one awning. Somebody had painted a smiling fish on a fuel drum and then patched the smile with welding tape. The whole place smelled like hot wiring, frying oil, and the mineral dry of Mars itself. Even in the heated corridors the cold waited in the walls.

Jet moved through it in the hat and jacket, shoulders slightly hunched, hands in his pockets, the way he'd once moved when he expected trouble but needed to look like he was too stupid to spot it. The old gait came back more easily than he liked. He knew which faces belonged to stall owners and which belonged to people watching stall owners. He knew the difference between a man leaning against a heater because he was cold and a man leaning because he wanted to own the angle.

That bothered him too.

Faye peeled off toward the gambling room without looking back. Spike disappeared between two service ladders carrying a case that looked like survey gear if you had never seen a rifle broken down into polite components. Ed's voice settled into Jet's ear through the comm bead, tinny and alert.

"Takara-bait is moving through lane three. Card shark has entered the den of bad math. Rooftop grump is ascending with all his tragic coolness. Ed approves."

"Keep your eyes on the frequencies," Jet muttered.

"Ed has many eyes. Too many, maybe."

Jet took up position at a tea stand opposite the registry kiosk. The kiosk itself was old public infrastructure gone a little feral: cracked touch screen, gum fused to the lower panel, two notices taped beside it about livestock permits and a missing heater coil. The tea stand owner had a knitted sleeve on his kettle spout to keep from burning himself and the defeated expression of a man who had been listening to other people's lies for thirty years. Jet ordered the cheapest thing on the menu and got a paper cup of something bitter enough to strip paint.

He made himself be visible.

He checked the kiosk once, slowly, in full view of anyone interested. He let the name sit there on the screen with the old fake credentials Ed had stitched together from public scrap and whatever legal gray magic Ed operated in. HOSUKE TAKARA flashed for half a second before the kiosk froze and had to be slapped back to life.

"Subtle," Spike said in his ear.

"Shut up."

People passed. A woman hauling insulated crates. Two kids arguing over a punctured ball. A mechanic with sealant on both thumbs and a lunch tin painted with moons. Nobody stopped. Nobody gave him a second look.

Jet took another swallow of the bitter tea and decided the stuff was made from boiled screws.

He almost missed the first shout because it came from behind him and because nobody had called him that name out loud in close to twenty years.

"Hosuke?"

The name hit Jet between the shoulders. His grip locked on the paper cup hard enough to crease it before he turned.

The man coming down the lane was thick through the chest and belly now, bundled in a quilted vest the color of old mustard. A bright red knee brace showed below his left trouser leg, knitted by hand and ugly enough that somebody had loved him while making it. Pinned crooked to the vest was a blinking plastic rocket ship with one dead LED; it buzzed faintly every time he laughed, which was often and loudly. He carried a paper sack of sesame twists under one arm and already looked delighted in advance.

"No damn way," the man said. "Hosuke Takara, you ugly bastard."

Jet closed his eyes for half a second.

In his ear Ed said, in a fascinated whisper, "Old friend acquired."

The man clapped a hand on Jet's shoulder hard enough to splash tea over his fingers. Up close Jet knew him. Of course he did. Grosz. Freight-yard thief, amateur card cheat, decent hand with a plasma cutter, once convinced he could teach a stolen warehouse robot to play jazz drums. Back then he had been narrow and hungry and always five seconds ahead of a bad idea. Now his beard had gone iron gray in streaks and his breath smelled like anise and bad dental work.

"Grosz," Jet said before he could stop himself.

"Ha." Grosz slapped the tea stand counter like Jet had just admitted to a prank. "I knew it. I said to myself, nobody else wears a hat that stupid with that kind of confidence. Look at you. Big as a damn loader now."

"Wrong guy."

"Don't start. I've still got the scar from that lift gate in Tharsis and you were laughing too hard to help."

"You got that scar because you were drunk."

"I was seventeen. That's what seventeen was for."

Grosz was grinning so hard the skin around his eyes folded into deep fan lines. He set down the paper sack and pulled out a twist, then hesitated. "Hell, can't eat these anyway. Dentist took the back molar yesterday. My daughter says I've got to learn to enjoy soup now. Soup. At my age." He shoved the bag at Jet. "Take one. Celebrate."

Jet did not take one. Across the lane two men had slowed down, not because they knew anything but because people slowed for volume. Markets listened when someone laughed that hard.

"I'm working," Jet said.

"At what, nostalgia?"

In his ear Faye said, "You know people can hear you from inside the card room, right?"

"Shut up," Jet muttered.

Grosz's grin widened. "You talking to the hat now? That's age, Hosuke. That catches up."

"Name's not Hosuke."

"Sure, sure. Whatever we're calling you these days. You still doing cargo games? You always had the face for harmless paperwork. Here, sit. No, hell, stand, if your knees are bad now too. Everybody's got knees. Mine whistles when the cold comes in. Listen." He bent theatrically, knocked the brace with his knuckles, and made a faint piping sound with his mouth. The blinking rocket on his vest gave one sickly pulse.

Jet wanted to kill him a little.

"Grosz," he said, trying for a level that might pass as fond and land as warning. "I need you to lower your voice."

"For what? Creditors?"

"For life."

"You always were dramatic." Grosz leaned his elbows on the counter and yelled at the tea stand owner. "Maro, you got anything softer than these twists? My old friend here looks like he can only chew regret."

The tea stand owner, Maro apparently, rolled his eyes without heat. A ring of burn scar tissue gleamed around one wrist where a kettle must once have let go on him. "You paying this time, Grosz, or are we doing your tragic history for store credit again?"

"Put it on my tab."

"Then no."

By now Jet could feel the plan fraying in real time. Faye would be hearing every word. Spike, from the roof, would have line of sight on a crowd beginning to notice the noise. Anyone watching the market for Hosuke Takara would have a name, an old association, and a loud witness by the time Grosz finished his tea.

Anyone smart enough to dig Hosuke Takara out of dead systems would hear thirty seconds of Grosz and know exactly what this was. Not a ghost surfacing. A trap with a bad hat on it.

"Jet-person's pulse is very educational," Ed murmured.

Jet cut his eyes to the registry kiosk. Nothing. No alert. No girl in mechanic's coveralls materializing from the dust. Just Grosz, blithe as weather.

He had known, somewhere in the back of his head, that the plan depended on a fantasy: that the old life would come out when called and behave itself. That he could put on a hat, wake a dead alias, and control who answered. The reality was standing two feet away wearing a granddaughter's rocket pin and broadcasting his teenage name down a market lane.

"Listen," Jet said, low now. "Good seeing you. Really. But I need to be somewhere else."

"With who? You hiding a woman? Damn, Hosuke, don't tell me you finally learned something." Grosz squinted. Recognition flared again, not of the face this time but of the refusal. "Wait. This is a job, isn't it? Ah, hell."

For one hopeful second Jet thought shame might finally shut him up.

Instead Grosz looked delighted.

"You're bait," he said. "That's beautiful. That's pathetic, but beautiful."

In the comm Faye started laughing outright.

"Jet," Spike said, dry as dust, "your pillar appears compromised."

"I know that."

"I can hear that."

Grosz slapped the counter again. "Who are we waiting for? Do I know them? If this is Sella from Dock Nine, tell her she still owes me for the winch chain. No, wait, don't, she bit me last time." He lowered his voice by maybe ten percent and leaned in, smelling of anise and antiseptic mouth rinse. "You in trouble?"

The question landed unexpectedly human between all the shouting.

Jet looked at him.

Grosz, for all the volume and the stupidity, was not stupid. He had lived long enough in Mars settlements to tell when a man was holding his back too straight because somebody might be sighting down it.

Jet answered the smaller version because it was the only version available. "I'm busy."

Grosz nodded once, quick. Then he ruined it.

"Fine," he boomed. "I'll stay right here and look unthreatening."

"That's the opposite of what you're doing."

"I've got a friendly face."

"You've got a loud one."

"Friendly and loud can coexist."

Jet rubbed his forehead under the hat. Across the lane a heater kicked on with a cough and a blast of hot metal stink. Somebody in the market started arguing about radishes. Life kept moving, which was the problem. The settlement did not need to stop and formally acknowledge that his trap had gone to hell. It just absorbed the failure and spread it through ordinary noise.

Ed said, "Card shark is collecting woolongs and suspicious eyeballs. Rooftop grump is in position. No ship signal yet. No pilot eyeballs yet. But comedy pressure is now at... wow. New numbers."

Jet watched Grosz unwrap a lozenge with the reverence of a man in recent dental pain and knew, with sick clarity, that they were already reacting instead of acting.

"If she was going to come quietly," Spike said, "I think you scared her off."

"I didn't scare anybody," Jet snapped.

Grosz, chewing carefully on the good side of his mouth, said, "Who is 'she'?"

Jet put the paper cup down before he crushed it.

***

Faye had meant to lose the second hand.

It would have been smarter. The room in back of the card house was small enough that every mistake sweated. A strip heater clicked along one wall. Condensation ran down the inside of the window nearest the market, blurring everybody outside into moving stains of red and gray. The felt on the table had gone bald in the center from years of shuffling, slapping, and bad luck. Seven players crowded it anyway because people in settlements would gamble on a crate lid if there was money on it.

The dealer had cut his nicotine patch into four neat squares to make it last longer; one of them clung to the inside of his wrist like a postage stamp while he dealt. Across from Faye sat a woman with chrome polish peeling off her thumb in one clean crescent. Beside her a freight clerk with frost cracks in both front teeth kept touching the stack of chips in front of him as though heat might grow there if he wanted it enough.

They were not good players.

That would have been fine if Faye had not been Faye.

She took the first hand by reading the freight clerk's shoulders. She took the second because the woman with the bad polish kept overvaluing face cards and getting sentimental about pairs. By the third the table had decided she was either lucky or rude. By the fourth they were leaning in every time she touched her chips.

"You from Tharsis?" the freight clerk asked. His name, according to the stitched patch on his coat, was Yaro. Somebody had mended the coat at both elbows with fabric printed in tiny yellow ducks, which did not match anything else he owned and somehow made his narrow, worried face look younger. "You don't play like local."

"You don't lose like local either," Faye said, and took his money.

The dealer's mouth twitched. "That wasn't an answer."

"It wasn't a useful question."

In her ear Jet's voice had been a low mutter for the last ten minutes, always one breath away from swearing. She had caught enough to piece it together: tea stand, kiosk, somebody loud. Then Ed, delighted: old friend acquired.

Faye laid down a straight. Yaro stared at it like it had offended him personally.

"You know," she said to nobody, "I gave him twelve minutes. That was me being generous."

The woman with the peeling polish blinked. "What?"

"Nothing. Deal."

She was supposed to be watching the door, the corridor, the reflection in the fogged window, the little currents of settlement life that told you when an outsider had entered a room and was pretending not to be one. She could do that. She was doing that. She was also taking these people apart for pocket change because they kept handing it to her with both palms open.

Faye had lived too much of her life with no money not to feel the pull of visible money. Even this, even a few hundred woolongs in tired settlement chips, lit the old wires in her head. Debt. Fuel. Food. The pleasure of not being the mark for once.

The problem with pleasure was that people noticed.

A woman in stripped settlement colors had been by the heater since the third hand, pretending to watch the dealer while counting Faye's stack in the reflection off the window. Faye had clocked the glitter barrettes first, then the lozenge, then the way the dealer never quite raised his eyes in that direction.

By the time the enforcer pushed off the wall and crossed to the table, the whole room already knew what she was there for. Conversation did not stop. It just thinned.

Faye looked up.

The woman by the heater wore settlement security colors stripped of official patches. Local muscle, then. Private or communal, which on Mars often came out the same. Her hair had been buzzed close except for one dark strip on the left side, where three glitter barrettes held it back from her temple in a line: silver star, green flower, tiny plastic cherry. They looked like a little girl's work, and the woman had left them there without embarrassment. She sucked on a clove lozenge that stained the tip of her tongue brown-black when she spoke.

"You're running hot for a tourist," she said.

Nobody at the table looked at Faye. That told Faye all she needed to know. This woman did not have formal power, but she had enough of the room's respect or fear that nobody wanted to be seen measuring the angle.

Faye leaned back in her chair. "Do I look like a tourist?"

The woman's gaze slid over the coat, the boots, the face. "You look expensive in spots. Name's Tamsin."

"Good for you."

"Who are you kicking up to?"

Yaro suddenly found the cards in his hands intensely interesting. The dealer started stacking the muck from the last round too carefully, eyes down.

Faye understood the shape now. Outsider appears. Outsider wins too fast. Either she is a hustler on somebody else's turf or she is foolish enough to be treated like one. Tamsin had probably spent half her day breaking up small fights and the other half making sure nobody without roots extracted cash from people who did.

Fair enough. Annoying, but fair enough.

"I came to play cards," Faye said.

"Everybody says that."

"That's because it's a card room."

Tamsin moved closer. Her jacket zipper had been repaired with a brass key ring and a strip of blue wire. The lozenge clicked against her teeth. "You can keep being funny if you want. Or you can tell me if somebody put you here."

In Faye's ear Jet hissed, "Problem."

She kept her face still. "You know, when you sound that panicked, it's hard for me not to enjoy it."

"Not now."

"It's always now."

Tamsin's eyes narrowed. "You talking to me or yourself?"

"Depends who's listening."

That earned the smallest huff from the dealer. Brave man.

Faye slid two chips back toward the center of the table. "Here. Let's all relax. I got lucky."

"No," Tamsin said. "You got watched."

The room listened harder.

Faye felt her own irritation sharpen into the meaner edge she wore when she did not have time for anyone else's power games. This was what pressure did to her. It made kindness feel stupid and subtlety feel slow. Somewhere overhead Spike was pretending to be architecture. Outside Jet was being reunited with his worst years at full volume. Ed was probably triangulating all of it like it was music. And here she was, getting shaken down by a woman wearing children's barrettes as if Faye had shown up to cheat a church raffle.

"Fine," Faye said. "Let's do this cleanly. You think I'm skimming your locals. I'm not. You think somebody put me here. They didn't. You think I owe you a percentage because I sat down and outplayed Yaro and his duck elbows. I don't."

Yaro looked briefly offended on behalf of his jacket.

Tamsin rolled the lozenge to the other cheek. "Nobody comes in from nowhere and starts peeling my room unless they're working an angle."

"Everybody works an angle."

"Not in my chair."

"Then maybe your chair should play better cards."

The dealer made a choked sound he turned into a cough. Tamsin looked at him and he immediately found a new level of respect for the deck.

Faye was already cataloging exits. Back corridor to the privy. Service door to the alley. Window if she wanted broken glass in her thighs. Tamsin's stance said she had friends within shouting distance, but not right here. If Faye had to put the woman through the card table and bolt, she could. It would end the cover permanently, but at this point the cover was already smoking.

Then movement blurred in the window.

Not a shape, exactly. A rhythm.

Faye's eyes snapped past Tamsin's shoulder to the fogged pane. Someone was crossing outside through the one strip of lane the window still showed cleanly, a paper sack tucked under one arm and another hand wrapped around what looked like a ring of gaskets or seals. Dark hair tied back. Work coveralls. Medium height. No hurry. The walk did it more than the face. Most people in the market moved with the little swerves and checks of settlement life, adjusting around bodies, heaters, carts, gripes. This woman moved as if the lanes had already agreed to her.

Faye knew before she knew.

It was not reason. It was the same ugly, private certainty that had grabbed her in diners and records offices and in the mirror when Miriam said collarbone. Some part of her that had been rubbed raw by the last few weeks recognized the outline of relevance.

In her ear Ed said, very fast now, "Faye-Faye, market lane east, dark coveralls, same height window as chase glimpse, same gait, ninety-one percent probably and rising."

There it was.

Faye stood up.

Tamsin's chair legs scraped back. The glitter cherry barrette winked under the strip light. "Sit down."

Faye looked from Tamsin to the window to the lane beyond.

The woman in coveralls did not turn her head. She kept walking.

Faye shoved her winnings across the table. Chips skidded, clacked, toppled into Yaro's lap and the dealer's hands. "Split it," she said.

Yaro blinked. "What?"

"Among yourselves. Buy soup. Buy lessons. I don't care."

Tamsin came around the table a beat too late, clipped Yaro's chair with her knee, and swore. "You're not leaving."

Faye smiled at her, the mean small smile, the one that promised nobody in the exchange would enjoy what came next. "Then stop me."

She hooked two fingers under the corridor curtain, turned before Tamsin cleared the table, and was back in the market cold by the time anyone behind her had finished swearing.

The air outside hit like dry paper in the lungs. Market noise slammed back in all at once: vendors calling produce, a generator kicking, a child crying over something sticky and urgent. Ahead of her, dark coveralls turning the corner.

Faye went after her.

Spike had chosen the roof because it was ugly enough to be ignored.

Nobody admired the maintenance block above the west market lane. Half the solar tiles on it were cracked. The access ladder rattled in its brackets. Somebody had tagged the water tank with a cartoon saint smoking a cigarette, and the paint had run in the cold so it looked like the saint was crying down both cheeks. From up there Spike had the registry kiosk, the tea stand, most of the market lanes, and a direct view through the card room's front strip if the condensation stayed thin.

He also had wind. Mars wind in a settlement did not come clean. It came with grit and old metal and whatever everyone below had cooked badly that morning. It worked under the collar of his coat and into the joints of the rifle case while he unpacked the scope.

It also had one live motion eye by the hatch, a squat gray box Ed had tagged on the map and Spike had ignored because most settlement hardware looked half-dead until it fined somebody. Paw prints stitched the dust around it anyway.

Still better than conversation.

"Talkative situation developing around Jet," Ed said in his ear.

Spike settled prone behind the low lip of the roof and focused the scope. Jet, in the ridiculous hat, stood at the tea stand with a man Spike had never seen and instantly disliked. Even through the glass the man's whole body looked loud. He kept touching Jet's shoulder. Jet looked like he was pricing homicide.

"I see it," Spike said.

"Card shark is up twelve percent and accumulating local interest."

"That sounds about right."

He scanned the market. Mechanics. Vendors. Kids. Two settlement security officers by the water point arguing over a paper receipt. No sign of the pilot. No sign of the sleek ship either, but he had not expected that. The girl knew better than to bring a ship into the center of a trap she had probably seen coming before Jet finished putting on the hat.

That was the part none of them had said out loud. The operation was built on the hope that superior knowledge could be baited into carelessness. Maybe that happened with amateurs. Maybe it even happened with professionals, once, on bad days. What they knew about this girl suggested patience, not sloppiness. Years of patient research. Years of following dead routes and live people.

Spike adjusted the scope a fraction and caught the reflection of Faye through the card room window, all angles and false ease. A mark at the table was already leaning too far toward her. Somebody else stood behind her chair.

"Jet's made," he said.

"Jet's in a reunion," Ed corrected. "Different emotional tone, same tactical disaster."

Jet's voice crackled into the comm. "I heard that."

Spike smirked despite himself, then let it die. The roof felt too open for humor. He kept watching angles, exits, faces. Old habits nested inside newer ones. You held still. You watched the doors nobody else watched. You measured every body in terms of whether it would duck, draw, or run.

A soft weight touched his boot.

Spike flinched, not much, but enough to hate himself for it. He looked down.

The cat was narrow as wire and roughly the color of the roof dust, which was probably how it had made it up there without him noticing. One ear had been split and healed badly, leaving a notch like somebody had bitten a coin out of it. A strip of reflective orange tape clung to the fur near its tail, maybe from a child, maybe from getting under some mechanic's bench and leaving with evidence. Its eyes were pale gold and wholly unimpressed.

"No," Spike said quietly.

The cat rubbed harder against his boot.

"Absolutely not."

It stepped over the rifle case as if Spike had laid it out for feline inspection, tail up, and headed straight for the squat gray box bolted near the door hatch.

Spike recognized the box half a second before the cat did what cats did to all objects in all universes when told not to.

It leaped onto the motion sensor.

The box chirped.

Not loudly. It did not need to. On a settlement roof, official noises carried authority by memory alone.

Spike closed his eyes once.

"What was that?" Jet asked.

"Nothing," Spike said.

The alarm answered for him with a second, more certain chirp from somewhere below.

Faye, in the comm, laughed so hard she had to breathe twice before words came out. "Please tell me the cat got you."

"It was a sensor."

"Touched by a cat."

"Same result."

The cat sat beside the box and licked one paw, unconcerned. From the lane below came the clatter of a door and the flat electronic bark of an access lock disengaging.

"Security response," Ed sang.

"I know."

Spike moved fast, because unlike Jet he did not suffer from the fantasy that charm might still save a bad situation. Scope back in case. Rifle broken. Cap on the lens. But there was only so fast you could repack equipment on a roof while a cat threaded through your hands like it had a personal objection to tactical discipline.

The hatch banged open.

"Hold it right there."

The voice belonged to a woman in a thermal security cape with a row of tiny green fish stitched along the hem in bright thread. Handmade. Her cheeks were raw from the cold, and every third breath she suppressed a dry cough with visible irritation, as if offended by her own lungs. She had a sidearm, a scanner, and the posture of a person who had found exactly the kind of nuisance she expected when the alarm went off.

Two steps behind her came a younger officer carrying a citation pad and a half-eaten jam bun.

Spike straightened slowly, case in hand. "Roof survey."

The woman's gaze moved from his face to the case to the cat, which had now installed itself on top of the hatch frame like a supervisor. "At dusk. Without checking in."

"Best light for panel line."

"That's not how light works."

"Close enough for this roof."

She looked around at the broken tiles. "You from utilities?"

"Subcontractor."

"For who?"

Spike gave her a company name he had seen on a tanker in Io six years ago and doubted existed anymore.

The younger officer licked jam off his thumb and frowned at the pad. "Don't think we're contracted with them."

The woman ignored him. She stepped closer until Spike could smell menthol rub under the cold and metal smell of her cape. "You carrying optics in there or something with a kick?"

Spike considered several answers, ranked them, and chose the least interesting. "Optics."

"Open it."

The cat chose that moment to step onto Spike's shoulder.

For a second nobody moved.

Then the younger officer made a small involuntary sound that was either a laugh or fear. The woman pinched the bridge of her nose under her visor.

"Moth again," she muttered to the cat. "You little bastard."

So the cat had a name. Good. Bureaucracies named their problems when they lost hope of eliminating them.

"You know it?" Spike said.

"Everybody up here knows it. It steals lunch and triggers alarms." She looked at Spike with open suspicion. "You didn't answer the roof permit question."

"I don't have one."

"No kidding."

In his ear Jet said, "Tell me you're still on the scope."

Spike watched the woman extend her hand for the case. The fish on her hem flashed green when she moved. "No."

"What do you mean, no?" Jet said.

"I mean no."

Faye was breathing hard now, moving fast. "Later. I've got movement."

That cut through everything.

Spike's attention snapped toward the market lane, but the woman had already taken the case and the angle was wrong. All he got was a partial view of Faye shouldering through the crowd toward the east corridor, coat open, face pulled narrow and mean in the way she got when the world had stepped on something personal.

"Who?" Jet demanded.

"Probably pilot-person," Ed said, voice gone sharp under the play. "Faye-Faye is in pursuit."

Spike shifted to move and the woman's hand found the front of his coat with brisk competence. "Not until we're done."

He looked down at her grip, then back at her face.

Any other day he might have smiled. Not charm. Just enough to make trouble feel optional. Today the day had been rotten from the first sight of Jet in that hat, and all he had for her was the truth's ugliest edge.

"Lady," he said, "I really don't have time for this."

She coughed once, hard enough to grimace, and tightened her grip anyway. "Funny. Neither do I."

Below them the market kept moving, and Faye vanished into it.

***

She never broke into a run.

That was the first thing that made the pursuit feel wrong. Anybody who had been spotted in a settlement and wanted to lose a tail fast would've cut hard, changed pace, vanished through private doors or service corridors. She did none of that. She kept the same unhurried clip, paper sack under one arm, shoulders loose, and chose turns that let Faye keep seeing her while wrecking every longer sight line above and behind them.

Half invitation. Half insult.

Faye hated both.

She slipped through the east lane with the coat open and one hand near the small pistol at her back, not because she thought she would need to shoot but because the weight of it helped her think in straight lines. People got in her way. A man carrying hot broth in open containers swore when she clipped his elbow. A child with three scarves wrapped over her mouth stared openly as Faye cut across a stack of feed bags. Somewhere behind, maybe two lanes over, Tamsin yelled at somebody to block a door and was ignored because markets only obeyed so much at once.

Ahead, the coveralls turned beneath a string of hanging lanterns made from old engine housings.

Faye followed.

The market changed under her feet. Produce lanes gave way to repair stalls. The smell shifted from frying oil to hot metal, sealant, old coolant. A man at a bench was carving replacement teeth for a gear housing out of dense green plastic, little curls piling around his boots. Two women argued over a crate of cracked ceramic insulators. A queue waited outside a clinic window where the paint had peeled around the words RESPIRATORY and GENERAL. An old man in line held his number ticket like it might dissolve if he pinched too hard.

Ahead of her, the coveralls passed all of it without hurry.

She knew where every gutter dipped. She knew which vendor would swing a basket into the lane and when. Once she put her free hand lightly on a freighter's flank to slide past him and he shifted without even looking, some local muscle memory recognizing her before thought did. Another time she slowed just enough at a parts stall to take a wrapped bundle from the owner, a square little woman with weld-dark glasses hanging at her throat and one gloved finger taped because she'd split the nail. No money changed hands. The owner only jerked her chin and said, "Tell Neri the fan still rattles."

"I told him twice already," she said, and kept moving.

Her voice hit Faye a second after the words did. It was younger than she'd expected, settled, with no performance in it.

Faye's skin prickled under the coat.

She could have stopped then. She knew that. She could have hit the comm, given Jet the route, let Spike rejoin once he untangled himself from whatever ridiculous bureaucratic trap he'd fallen into. She could have turned this back into crew work.

She did not.

It was too personal now, though "personal" was not the word Faye would have used if anybody asked. Too close. Too fucking strange. She had spent days letting other people tell her about the woman who had worn her name: a waitress with tired eyes, a records clerk who cared more about stamp alignment than moral damage, scraps of paper and dates and a scar Faye did not have. Now the daughter of that woman was twenty yards ahead of her, buying parts and greeting mechanics and walking as if this settlement had built her one practical inch at a time.

In her ear Jet was saying something. Faye turned the volume lower until he became weather.

Laundry brushed Faye's shoulders when the route kinked left into a lane so narrow it felt like the settlement was trying to shove her back out. Children's socks. A work shirt stiff with patch glue. A pair of thermal leggings with cartoon moons at the knees. The lane opened again near the settlement edge, where the buildings thinned and the cold pressed harder through the seams. Beyond a pressure fence the land dropped away in a slope of red rock and scrap metal half-buried in dust. Mars open country. Thin sky the color of bruised apricots. Instead of going for the fence, she cut right into a courtyard between two storage blocks.

Faye saw the dead end three steps in. One wall blind. One old cargo container welded shut. One drainpipe climbing to a roofline too high to reach cleanly unless you already knew where the footholds were.

She'd picked it.

Good, Faye thought, sudden and hot. Good.

She slowed, but did not stop.

The courtyard held the leftovers of useful life: a stripped refrigeration unit, a stack of cracked plastic pallets, an engine casing full of windblown wrappers and one dead flower stem. Somebody had chalked a hopscotch grid on the concrete and lost patience halfway through the numbers. Dust had settled red in every corner.

By the time Faye reached the courtyard, the woman had set down the paper sack and the wrapped bundle from the parts stall. Then she turned.

Up close there was no mistaking the mechanic in her. Her hands were nicked and dark in the creases, cuticles rough, one thumbnail split and grown back crooked. Her face was sharp, thinner than Faye's, with the same general architecture in some unsettling distant way around the mouth and eyes if you were the kind of person cursed to notice. Dark hair pulled back with a strip of blue electrical tape. Not glamorous. Not trying to be. The sort of face records departments would call ordinary right before it rearranged someone's life.

She looked at Faye for a long time.

There was nothing rude or shy in it. She was looking at something she had only known from photographs and paperwork and now had to reconcile with weight, height, breath.

Faye was used to being looked at. She was not used to this kind of looking.

"Who are you?" she said.

The woman's expression shifted by less than a blink. "Ren Kozuki."

The name landed without help. Kozuki. Of course.

Faye heard her own laugh come out hard and wrong. "You could've opened with that."

"You would've believed me less."

There was that settled voice again. Direct, almost plain. No relish. No fear either.

Faye took another step into the courtyard. "You've been jerking us around across half the damn planet."

"Not half."

"Don't do that."

"Do what."

"Answer the smallest possible version of what I said."

For the first time something flickered in Ren's face that might have been surprise. Or recognition. It was gone before Faye could pin it.

Behind Faye the market noise thinned to a muffled wash. Somebody dropped metal two lanes over. The sound came into the courtyard flat and late.

Faye folded her arms because otherwise her hands might have done something premature. "What do you want?"

Ren looked at her collarbone. The glance was brief, involuntary enough to be honest. Then she looked at Faye's face again.

"When you're mad, your mouth goes tight on one side," Ren said. "Hers did that too."

Faye stood there with her arms crossed and her chin up and the coat hanging open and felt the whole last stretch of her life rearrange itself one quiet click at a time around the pronoun.

She.

Not a clerk. Not a file. Not an abstract thief in a cryogenic ledger. A woman. This woman's mother. A woman who had stood in rooms, bought medicine, tipped waitresses, touched her own collarbone, maybe looked in mirrors and seen a name that was not hers printed cleanly under a face close enough to pass.

Faye heard herself say, flat as sheet metal, "She stole my name."

Ren's jaw tightened once. "Yeah."

"And that's all you've got?"

"No." Ren bent and reached into the pocket of her coveralls. "It's what I've got for now."

Faye's hand was on the pistol by then, not drawn, just there.

Ren saw it and did not flinch. "If you were going to shoot me, you would've done it in the lane."

"Don't tell me what I would've done."

"All right."

She held out a data chip between two fingers. Small black wafer, scuffed at one corner, a thin e-ink strip inset along one edge. Faye did not take it.

"You're looking at this backwards," Ren said.

The strip on the chip woke and displayed the same words in pale gray text.

Faye stared at it, then back up. "Backwards from what?"

Ren's gaze shifted, not away, exactly. Past. To something Faye could not see because she had not lived it. "From the fake name and the badge file. Everybody starts there." She brought her eyes back to Faye. "That's late. Start earlier."

That was more than Faye had expected.

"With your mother," Faye said.

Ren's jaw worked once. "With her. With—" She stopped. "With the records. What the name actually cost to keep running."

Her face changed in a way the straight facts hadn't. A tightening at the mouth.

Faye took a step forward. "You don't get to drop riddles in my lap and walk away."

"I know." Ren gave the tiniest nod toward the chip. "That's why I brought documents."

It was such a mechanic's answer that Faye almost hated her for it.

"You knew this was me," Faye said.

"Yes."

"Why talk to me first?"

Ren looked at her for one more unguarded second. Younger, then. Tired in a way twenty-two should not have to know. "Because—" She almost said something, started over. "My mother was the one in clinic lines under your name. Everybody keeps starting with the other parts, and those parts are real, but she was—" A breath. "She was first. That's all."

Faye didn't answer. Her nails pressed through the coat into her arms.

Somewhere in Faye's ear Jet barked her name, distant now only because she had chosen distance. She could have answered. She did not.

Instead she said, "If she was sick enough to need all this, why use my name for eleven years? Why not get another one? A better one."

Ren opened her mouth, stopped, and corrected. "Because it got her through the first clinic door. Then the next one. A clerk sold it cheap, the records matched, and after that every refill and shipment sat under the same name. Changing it would've blown up everything attached to it. The chip has the paperwork. Anything past that, I'd be guessing." Her hand flexed once at her side, fingers curling like she wanted a wrench.

There. Another seam.

Faye did not know what to do with the honesty when it arrived this plain. Anger was easier. Anger had edges she trusted. This had paperwork and grief and a girl in coveralls telling the truth in narrow cuts.

"You could've just sent the chip," she said.

"No," Ren said. "I couldn't."

"Why not."

Ren glanced at Faye's shoulder, left side this time, brief but definite. The absence there. The scar that was not.

"I wanted to see you," she said.

Then she moved.

Not away. Straight in, close enough that Faye caught the smell of machine oil worked into the cuffs of her coveralls. Before Faye could decide whether to grab her or hit her or ask the one question that had actually mattered, Ren caught her hand, pressed the chip into her palm, and folded Faye's fingers over it quick as a dealer slipping a card.

"Read it," she said.

Then she slipped past Faye's shoulder and back into the lane. Faye lunged and caught only the cuff of coveralls for a quarter second, enough to smear grease over her fingers before the fabric slid free.

"Hey!"

She spun after her. By the time she hit the mouth of the courtyard Ren was already going sideways through the crowd, dark coveralls cutting between bodies and hanging strips of plastic awning. She looked back once. Calm. Almost polite. Then the market closed over her.

"Faye." Jet again, closer now. "Talk."

She looked down at her fist.

The chip sat warm in her palm where Ren had shoved it.

By the time Jet and Spike reached the mouth of the courtyard, the lane beyond was already all other people's backs, shopping sacks, and heater exhaust. Spike came in first from the market side, breathing a little hard, coat front open where the security woman must have had hold of it. Red dust streaked one sleeve. Jet was half a step behind, hat gone, face dark with a fury too tired to rise all the way.

"Where is she?" Jet said.

Faye jerked her chin at the market.

Spike looked once at the crowd knitting shut beyond the lane and said, "Right."

"Don't," Faye said.

"I wasn't going to say anything."

"You were."

"Later," Faye said.

That made both of them look at her properly.

Jet's anger shifted first. Not gone. Re-routed. "You saw her."

Faye opened her hand enough for them to see the chip.

Spike's eyes went to it and stayed there. "She gave you that."

"Two things that mattered," Faye said.

Jet took one step closer. "What was the other one."

Faye could have given him the clean version. She did not feel clean enough for clean versions.

"She said when I get mad my mouth goes tight on one side," she said. "Her mother had that too."

Jet stopped short enough to kick up dust. Even Spike came off the wall properly for once. Jet's face did something complicated and brief under the beard, something halfway between confusion and the first shadow of understanding. Neither one asked the next obvious question because the answer was already standing there inside the name Kozuki and the word mother and the chip in Faye's hand.

Ed broke the silence in all their ears at once. "Ed wants to see the tiny doom rectangle."

Faye opened her hand. The e-ink strip still glowed faintly.

Jet leaned in and read it aloud because sometimes reading a thing aloud was how he made it more offensive. "'You're looking at this backwards.'"

Spike exhaled through his nose. "She's enjoying herself."

"No," Faye said.

They looked at her again.

She rubbed the grease from Ren's cuff between finger and thumb. It smelled like machine oil and dry dust and the human skin underneath it, ordinary and infuriatingly real.

"No," she said again. "She's not."

Over the wall the market kept humming. Someone yelled about oranges. A pressure pump kicked on. Somewhere else in the settlement a child laughed at full volume and was shushed too late.

They had come here to make the pilot answer to their plan. Instead the plan had shown its seams in public, and Ren had used them. She picked Faye, walked her through lanes that knew her better than they knew the crew, and by the time Spike got clear of roof security the whole operation fit in Faye's fist.

Jet looked at the chip in Faye's hand. Spike looked at the crowd beyond the lane. Faye looked at the opening where Ren had passed close enough to touch her and then turned into other people's motion.

Nobody said much on the walk back to the Bebop. Faye closed her hand around the chip again and kept it there until the corners bit.

For the first time since the dark ship stole their docking spot behind Mama Yun's, none of them looked like hunters anymore.
