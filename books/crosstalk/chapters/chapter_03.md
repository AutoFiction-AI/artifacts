# Chapter 3

The name on the common-room screen had been dead for nineteen years. Jet had kept it that way long enough to start believing it had finally done the decent thing and stayed dead.

The night before, Ed had thrown it there in bright bounty-office lettering, cheerful as a game show. `HOSUKE TAKARA`. Dormant warrant. Reactivated. The face attached to it had been a cheap age progression built from buried data, but it had still hit hard. Same brow. Same nose, set a little wrong from when he was sixteen and took a pipe to the face over a crate of stolen filters. Same thick neck he had hated as a kid because it made him look older until he needed to look older.

He had been holding the coffee pot when Ed found it. He remembered that part more clearly than anything anybody said. The steel handle hot in his right hand. Burnt grounds on the plate. The hum of the Bebop under his boots. Faye's one short laugh because the picture looked enough like him to be funny for half a second before it stopped being funny at all.

By morning the Mars cold had gotten into the hull. The ship smelled like stale oil, old coffee, dog, and the peppers Jet had fried the night before. Ed and Ein were asleep under the table in a pile of cables and blankets that looked accidental and never was. Spike was somewhere awake or pretending not to be. Jet pulled on his coat, took the tram into Tharsis, and didn't tell anybody where he was going.

Hosuke Takara had never been an arrest record. That was what made the bounty wrong in a way simple bad luck wasn't. There had been no booking, no prints under that name, no mug shot hanging in some dusty juvenile database. At seventeen he had been one more Mars settlement idiot in a fake work jacket, moving freight that wasn't his for men who preferred not to be seen touching it. When somebody on a loading dock asked his name, he gave them one. When a manifest needed a hand that didn't point back where it should, he gave them that too. Then he got older, got tired, got meaner in a more organized way, and years later sat in a fluorescent ISSP interview room and disclosed enough of it to get ahead of the background check. The only honest place Hosuke Takara still existed was in the sealed vetting file Jet had signed with his real name at the bottom.

If that file had been opened, this wasn't random. It wasn't Ed stumbling over some lost warrant in the deep end of the net. Somebody had gone looking through drawers only ISSP was supposed to have keys for.

The tram let him out two blocks from the Mars district office. Tharsis was halfway between waking up and giving up on it. Shop grates rattled open. A produce vendor hosed red dust off crates of bruised greens grown under lights somewhere farther north. A pair of sanitation drones whined past on their rails, brushing yesterday's wrappers into neat gutter piles. The sky above the settlement dome had that washed-out rust color Mars wore when the weather was cold and the dust stayed low. Jet walked with his collar up and his hands in his pockets, shoulder turned against the early wind blowing down the avenue.

The ISSP building looked exactly the way underfunded authority always looked: too much concrete, not enough glass, and a front seal that had been polished recently enough to count as morale. The security arch at the entrance still chirped half a note sharp. He remembered that because the sound had annoyed him every day for years and no one had ever fixed it. The desk cop at the front glanced up, saw who it was, and did the little face people did when an old coworker showed up after leaving ugly: mouth flattening, shoulders tight, eyes already measuring distance. Cautious, like grief you had to keep passing in the hallway.

Jet signed in with `J. Black`. The stylus dragged on the pad. For one stupid second he had the urge to write the other name just to see what the machine would do.

He took the stairs instead of the lift. Habit. Also the lift smelled like wet wool and vending-machine soup. On the second floor an admin clerk he half recognized came out of records carrying three binders and stopped dead when she saw him. Her hair was cut blunt at the jaw now; it used to have been down past her shoulders. Jet couldn't remember her name. She couldn't decide if she wanted to nod. In the end they both kept moving like adults in a small institution.

Donovan had been kicked sideways out of field work years ago and landed exactly where men like Donovan always landed if they didn't quit first: behind a desk with more access than glamour. He sat at the outer records station in shirtsleeves, uniform jacket hanging off the back of his chair, reading glasses low on his nose. A half-finished roll of chalky antacid tablets sat beside the terminal, and he kept smoothing the foil backing flat with his thumb until it shone. Jet remembered Donovan as a broad man with a wrestler's neck and a smoker's laugh. He was still broad. The laugh had gone somewhere else.

Donovan looked up, took in the coat, the beard, the fact of Jet in his office at eight in the morning, and leaned back hard enough to make the chair complain.

"No," he said.

Jet pulled the spare chair out anyway and sat. "I didn't ask yet."

"You didn't need to. If you're here this early, it's either money or trouble, and you don't come to me for money."

"I need a quiet pull."

Donovan pinched the bridge of his nose, then took off his glasses and polished them on the hem of his sleeve. It looked less like cleaning and more like buying time. "Quiet pulls are the loudest kind."

"You owe me."

"I knew you were going to say that." Donovan put the glasses back on. "Every time I see you, I remember that one thing and not the eight times I covered your ass for free."

"Then call it balance."

Donovan gave him a long look. "What am I pulling?"

Jet kept his voice level. "Dormant bounty. Name is Hosuke Takara."

The foil stopped moving under Donovan's thumb.

That was enough. Jet felt it low and ugly, the old cop instinct that measured a room by what went still in it.

Donovan didn't answer right away. He swiveled back to the terminal, typed with two fingers, waited, typed again. The records light washed his face a sick blue. Jet could hear the office around them waking up in layers: printers stuttering, phones starting, somebody down the hall laughing too hard at something not funny.

"Who gave you that name?" Donovan asked.

"It found me."

"That's not an answer."

"It's the one you get."

Donovan exhaled through his nose. "You always did know how to make a favor feel like paperwork."

"Can you pull it or not?"

"I can pull anything. Question is whether I want the audit trail." He kept typing. More windows opened. Jet caught glimpses of archive flags, access trees, old agency codes he hadn't seen since he turned in his badge. Donovan's mouth tightened. "Well. Hell."

"What?"

"You weren't kidding."

"I don't joke before coffee."

"That's one of the reasons people liked you so much." Donovan turned the screen a few degrees. Not enough for the room. Enough for Jet.

The bounty shell sat open in a nested archive window, ugly and thin. There shouldn't have been a shell at all. That was the first wrongness. The second was the source path hanging off it in a list of attached material: sealed vetting summary, intake physical markers, juvenile association note, cross-reference restrictions. It wasn't all the file, but it was enough. Enough to build a body out of. Enough to tell a bounty board this ghost had weight.

Jet felt the back of the chair through his coat like a shove.

"You see it?" Donovan said quietly. "This wasn't some public scrape. Somebody reached down deep."

Jet read the access stamp again, hoping on some dumb level that his eyes were giving him trouble. They weren't. "Who authorized it?"

Donovan clicked into the sighting report. "That's where it gets strange."

"Strange's been here since yesterday. Keep up."

"Three weeks ago the system got a live sighting submission on Takara. Standard format, enough fields filled to wake the shell. The submission came through on a protected source block." He clicked once more, jaw hardening. "Credentials attached to the report belong to a confidential informant."

Jet watched the next line populate.

`KOZUKI, DAICHI`

The name landed flat. No face came with it. No voice, no alley, no old debt.

"Who the hell is that?" Jet asked.

Donovan glanced at him, saw the blank on his face, and seemed almost disappointed by it. "Dead informant. Mars side. Been dead a long time."

"Then the credentials are cloned."

"Maybe. Maybe not." Donovan leaned back and reached for the antacid roll without looking at it. He dry-swallowed two tablets, grimaced, and kept going. "The codes authenticated deep enough to touch sealed material. That's the part I don't like. Spoofing a source block is one thing. Riding it into internal vetting is another. Somebody either had help from inside or somebody on the inside left a hell of a door unlocked."

Jet kept his eyes on the screen. His own younger life sat there in pieces he had once handed over under fluorescent light because honesty had seemed cheaper than getting caught in the lie. He remembered that interview room now with an almost physical clarity. The cracked vinyl chair. The bad coffee. The lieutenant from Personnel who'd let him talk and talk until he started helping the questions along. Prior aliases. Prior uncharged activity. Prior associations of note. Jet had turned freight theft into "informal transport work." He had turned using false manifests into "document irregularities." He had cleaned it up because he wanted the badge. ISSP had taken the cleaned-up version, put it in a sealed file, and called him suitable.

Suitable. Christ.

"How are Kozuki's codes still live?" he asked.

Donovan snorted once. "Because this place never kills anything all the way. Because records don't get budget and dead informants don't get advocates. Because somebody paid to keep a line breathing. Pick your flavor."

"Which one are you leaning toward?"

"The one that keeps me from sleeping?" Donovan folded the antacid foil in half, then in half again. "Deliberate. Accidental neglect doesn't usually thread a needle this fine."

Jet looked at the timestamp on the report. Three weeks. Before they ever saw the ship behind Mama Yun's. Before Ed tripped over the bounty. This had been waiting.

"Can you open Kozuki's file?"

Donovan didn't even pretend to consider it. "Not for you."

"I asked, didn't I."

"And I'm answering." Donovan tapped the screen with one blunt finger. "I can tell you the man existed. I can tell you his access should've been burned when his status closed out. I can tell you somebody used that access to reactivate Takara and pull sealed background material connected to the identity. Anything beyond that, I stop doing you a favor and start donating my pension."

Jet said nothing.

Donovan studied him a moment. Under the office lights the man looked older than fifty, not in the face so much as in the posture. Records work had given him a permanent forward lean, like the files were always trying to pull him in. "You really don't know the name."

"No."

"Then that's worse."

"How do you figure?"

"Because if this was simple blackmail, they'd use a name you knew." Donovan nudged the turned screen with two fingers until it faced himself again. "This looks like somebody building a trail. For you, maybe. For somebody else watching, maybe. I don't like it."

"Join the club."

Donovan's mouth twitched, not quite a smile. "You want my advice?"

"Not especially."

"Too bad." He hooked his glasses off again and rubbed the bridge of his nose with thumb and forefinger until the skin went pale. "Keep this small. If you start asking for dead-source files by name, somebody upstairs notices. If somebody upstairs already expects you to ask, then maybe that's the point."

"You're saying let it sit."

"I'm saying don't swing a hammer at a wall if you haven't checked what's wired behind it."

Jet pushed back from the chair. "You got me the timestamp and the name. That's enough."

Donovan looked offended on principle. "No, it isn't."

"It's what I have."

Jet stood. Donovan stood too, slower, as if his knees had opinions. For a second they were two ex-cops in a room full of computers pretending the years between then and now had been orderly.

"Jet," Donovan said.

He stopped.

Donovan looked past him to the open office, then back. His voice dropped. "If this touches your vetting file, somebody knew where to look. That means they knew the gap between your real name and the other one. That's not broad-net stuff. That's specific."

"Yeah."

"You going to tell the people on your ship?"

Jet thought of Ed's wide excited face, Faye's fast sharp mouth, Spike leaning in the doorway with one shoulder and that expression that said he was paying attention no matter how bored he looked. Thought of the name sitting on the screen. Thought of his own past written up in institutional language, polite as a funeral notice.

"Not yet," he said.

Donovan didn't approve. He didn't disapprove either. He just looked tired. "You always did believe in timing more than honesty."

"That's why you let me buy the first round and not the second."

"Get out of here." Donovan's hand closed over the antacid roll again. "And if anybody asks, you came by to complain about bounty payouts like every other washed-up cowboy."

"Wouldn't be a lie."

"No," Donovan said. "That's why it'll work."

Jet left by the side stairwell this time. The front felt too exposed. Halfway down he stopped with one hand on the rail and looked at his reflection in the narrow wired-glass window cut into the landing door. Broad shoulders. Scar on the cheek. Beard gone gray at the edges whether he liked it or not. No trace of Hosuke unless you knew where to pry. Maybe that had been the mistake, all these years. Thinking a thing wasn't visible unless it was obvious.

Outside, the wind had picked up. Dust skimmed low along the curb in red sheets. Jet put his head down and walked without deciding where.

***

He should have gone back to the Bebop.

Instead he kept walking.

Tharsis had changed the way working districts always changed when money touched them wrong. One block got cleaned up enough to charge triple rent for noodles and old wood furniture shipped in from nowhere anybody sensible had ever lived. The next block still had patched pressure doors and busted pavement plates that clanged when trucks rolled over them. New signage glowed over old concrete. Cheap prayer strips flapped above mechanic bays. Someone had painted a mural of a blue sky on the side of a warehouse and the dust had already chewed through it in brown streaks.

Jet knew the district by slope as much as by sight. He knew where the street dipped toward the drainage grates after a recycling flush. He knew which alley mouths funneled wind hard enough to sting your ears in winter. He knew where security cameras used to have blind spots, though half of those corners now held coffee stands or vending walls or somebody's expensive idea of neighborhood revival. His body kept choosing routes before he thought about them. Left at the shuttered freight office. Across the narrow footbridge over the service trench. Down the block where the old machine shop had become three tiny storefronts selling handmade soap, repair charms, and imported tea in tins more expensive than a meal.

Hosuke Takara had learned these streets fast because learning them had paid. Not big money. Never that. Money enough to get a room with a door that shut and food that wasn't from a vending rack every night of the week. Money enough to pretend he wasn't one missed job from being back on a floor somewhere with his boots on because getting robbed in your sleep happened if people thought you had anything worth taking.

He had not been a glamorous criminal. That was a story other men told about their youth after enough drinks. At seventeen he moved whatever fit in his arms or on the back of a borrowed cart. Broken seal replacement strips. Unlicensed filters. Cases of cheap ampoules with the labels scraped halfway off. Bootleg chips. Once, a crate of handguns so badly packed he could hear metal clacking every time the cart hit a seam in the pavement, and he had spent the whole route waiting for the thing to tip and dump pistols into the street. He never carried what bled warm and he never carried live cargo. That had been his line then, stupid and self-serving and very important to him. A kid's line. The kind that let you keep thinking there was a cleaner version of the same work just around the corner.

There usually wasn't.

He passed a corner where there had once been a card room above a salvage shop. The salvage shop was still there, smaller and meaner. The card room had been turned into family housing; tiny shirts hung behind the safety mesh on a second-floor balcony that used to be full of men smoking over bad hands. A little girl in a school sweater stood on a stool behind the mesh watering a plant that had no business surviving Mars air even under a dome. She frowned at the plant with the grave concentration of someone taking orders from an unseen adult. Jet found himself slowing to watch her, then moved on before the habit could turn into sentiment.

He had spent years telling himself there had been stages. First the kid with the alias and the cargo scams. Then the cop. Then everything after. He liked the shape of that story because it gave him lines to point at. Here, this ended. Here, this started. Here, I got better. Here, I lost the right to say that. It had the clean feel of something filed properly.

Walking those streets, he could feel the lie in it through the soles of his boots.

The cop version of Jet had used everything Hosuke learned. Every damned thing. He had known which foreman lied with his shoulders and which one lied by getting too offended. He had known how stolen goods moved when nobody wanted them seen. He had known which teenagers on bikes were lookouts and which were just killing time because he had been the first kind and pretended to be the second. ISSP had called that street knowledge, practical experience, instincts. They had put a badge over it and called the whole package an asset.

He turned onto Mercer Row without thinking about it and then stopped because Mercer Row shouldn't have existed anymore under that name. The old sign had been stripped, but the bend in the street was the same, a narrow dogleg around a building that had once leaned so hard everybody swore it would come down in the first dust storm and then never did. Jet looked at the front of that building now and had to stand there a second to make his eyes accept it.

Blue plastic paneling.

Big square windows.

Warm white light.

A laundromat.

Mars had taken his old stash hole and filled it with washers. That figured.

The place had been a dead freight office when he knew it, half-abandoned except for the back room and the upstairs closet where hot cargo could sit a night or two while whoever owned it got their story straight. He had slept in that closet once, coat rolled under his head, one hand on a crate of stolen processors because the man paying him said if the goods walked the debt was his. He remembered the smell of mildew and old insulation. He remembered rats in the wall. He remembered thinking he was doing what adults did.

Now the front window was full of washers turning white sheets in calm perfect circles.

He let out a breath that almost became a laugh and didn't. The wind cut around the corner and shoved dust at his shins. After a second he opened the door and went in.

Warm wet air hit him first. Soap. Hot metal. Damp cotton. The dryers along the far wall thumped in staggered rhythm, a rough backbeat under the softer churn of the washers. A machine buzzed to signal the end of a cycle and nobody rushed it. Jet stood just inside the door longer than he needed to, letting the warmth work into his coat.

A woman at the folding counter glanced up from a mystery novel laid spine-broken beside a basket of towels. She looked around sixty, maybe older, with one bright green hair roller still clipped over her bangs like she'd forgotten it halfway through getting ready and decided life could deal with it. Her slippers had rabbit faces on the toes, long ago walked flat. She took one look at Jet's empty hands and said, "If you're waiting on somebody, the blue chair by the change machine's cracked."

"Not waiting," Jet said.

She shrugged like that wasn't her problem either way and went back to her book.

He moved farther in. The bones of the place were still there if you knew where to look. The support column near the middle of the room. The shallow rise in the floor where old conduit ran under cheap tile. The back wall, set a little off square because the original build had been done fast and bad. They had painted over everything, skinned it, scrubbed it, lined it with white machines and plastic bins. Still the space pressed the same way. Still narrow where it mattered. Still made a big man feel one size too large for it.

Jet sat in a molded chair by the window. Not the blue one. The chair flexed under his weight and gave back just enough to be annoying. Across from him a father in work coveralls folded children's clothes with surprising delicacy, smoothing each tiny shirt flat before stacking it. His left thumbnail was painted silver, sloppily, the way kids did it when they were practicing on a patient adult. He caught Jet looking, grinned once without embarrassment, and kept folding. Somewhere in the back, behind the service door, a radio played old jazz through static bad enough to turn brass into sand.

Jet leaned forward with his elbows on his knees and looked at the back wall.

The closet had been upstairs. Gone now, probably knocked through when they renovated. The stash cavity had been behind a false panel near what was now the detergent vending rack. He could still place it. Four feet up from the floor, six inches left of the column. He had hidden crates there, envelopes there, once a pistol wrapped in shop rags for a man who came back for it two days later smelling like blood and antiseptic. He had never asked what that had been about. Not because he was brave. Because he wanted to get paid and go.

Daichi Kozuki.

The name sat in his head with no hooks in it.

If Jet had known him then, he couldn't find the memory. There were men from that time he still remembered by the shape of their boots or the stink of their cologne, but the name did not ring against anything. That bothered him more than it should have. A dead stranger's codes had reached into ISSP and dragged Hosuke up by the collar. A man Jet had never heard of had more access to that old life than anyone on the Bebop did.

The woman at the folding counter turned a page. The dryers kept their beat. Outside the window people moved through the cold with their shoulders up, plastic bags bumping their legs, breath faint in the dome air.

He thought about the vetting interview again, and about the thing he had never put in it. Not the alias. Not the freight jobs. Those he'd admitted in softer words. He had left out winter on Mercer Row, third year in ISSP, when he caught a dock runner named Nilo with two unlicensed filters and a knife too cheap to open smooth. Jet walked him into the freight-office stairwell, shut the fire door, and told him possession could become conspiracy if the report was written the right way. Then he told him he knew the family was already two notices behind on rent and that inspectors liked making examples out of women who worked night shift and missed forms. Nilo bit through his lip hard enough to bleed on his own cuff. Before the heat pipe quit banging, he gave up the back room over the salvage shop.

The raid pulled three handguns, a ledger, and one man slow enough to still be upstairs when the door came off. Jet wrote `COOPERATIVE SOURCE` on the paperwork and watched the language sand the edges off what had happened in the stairwell. He took the clearance rate, the nod from his lieutenant, the groceries it paid for. He never went back to tell Nilo what happened to the room after.

If Daichi Kozuki meant anything, Jet wasn't going to find it by handing the name to Ed and lighting up the net for whoever had laid the trail. Men from that life stuck to places. To corners, welded doors, back stairs, bars that changed owners and never smell. Daylight first. Questions later.

The father with the silver thumbnail finished his folding, called toward the machines, "Mina, your socks," and a girl of about five trotted over in blinking light-up shoes that flashed blue with every step. She held up one damp red sock like a flag of surrender. The father took it, laughed under his breath, and tucked it into the stack. Simple. Ordinary. The kind of moment Jet used to think happened in another country entirely.

The blue chair by the change machine gave a small protesting crack as somebody ignored the warning and sat in it. The sound pulled a short bark of laughter out of the woman with the hair roller. She looked over the top of her book at Jet as if to say she'd told them. Jet almost smiled back.

He stayed through one full washer cycle. When the end buzzer went off, no revelation came with it, just the flat electronic chirp and the father hauling his clean clothes toward the door. That was enough loitering for one day.

When he finally stood, his knees complained. He rolled one shoulder, then the other, and went to the door.

"You leaving already?" the woman at the counter asked, marking her place in the book with a grocery receipt.

"Yeah."

"Mm. You sat here a whole cycle without putting anything in." She looked at him over the book, not unfriendly. "I thought maybe you were waiting on a dryer."

"Just thinking."

"That'll do it." She reopened the book. "Don't use the blue chair if you come back."

Outside, the cold hit harder for the warmth he'd just left. Jet stood a second on the sidewalk with the laundromat light behind him and the district in front of him, old street under new signs, and let the name turn over once more.

Daichi Kozuki. Still nothing he could hold.

He turned toward the paid dock and started back, already sorting the old Mars map in his head by what daylight might still shake loose.

***

By the time he reached the Bebop, the settlement lamps around the lot had come on. The paid dock always felt temporary, no matter how many times they used it. Too exposed. Too narrow. No Mama Yun in the back door yelling about broth. No smell of stock and peppers drifting out into the cold. Just a fenced strip of concrete, a grudging electrical hook-up, and a kiosk that printed fee receipts like insults.

Jet had stopped at a market on the way and bought what they could afford without hating themselves more than usual: a head of cabbage, two onions, a bag of noodles, and a packet of protein scraps honest enough to call themselves scraps on the label. He climbed the ramp with the paper sack tucked under one arm. The ship answered him the way it always did: a hollow clank through the gangway, a faint shift in balance, life support humming under everything.

The common room lights were on low. Ed was upside down on the couch with a terminal braced against bony knees, hair hanging toward the floor, Ein curled in the angle of the cushions like a loaf of bread with ears. Somebody had tied a strip of red repair tape around one of Ed's big toes for no reason Jet could see. Faye sat at the table in one of Jet's old thermal shirts under her yellow jacket, using the edge of a playing card to scrape black nail polish off one thumbnail. Spike leaned against the galley hatch with a cigarette tucked behind one ear and a bowl in his hand he had apparently been too lazy to return to the sink.

Everybody looked up.

Faye was first. Of course she was. "Nice of you to come home before we had to sell the ship."

Jet set the grocery sack on the counter. "You'd get a bad price."

"Probably. It's filthy."

Ed flopped the terminal closed with both feet and righted in one loose movement. "Jet-person brings cabbage. Cabbage means either dinner or emotional avoidance."

"Could be both," Spike said.

His tone was flat enough to count as neutral with anyone else. With Spike it meant he had noticed the dust on Jet's coat, the set of his shoulders, the fact that he had been gone all day and come back with groceries because ordinary actions covered irregular ones better than explanations did.

Jet unpacked the sack and spread the groceries under the counter light: cabbage, onions, noodles, and the packet of protein scraps he'd talked himself into buying on the way back. Even the vegetables looked worn out. "Anybody eat yet?"

"No," Faye said. "I was waiting for our fearless captain to tell us whether he found out why his face is on a bounty under some sad little made-up name."

Jet reached for the knife. "Then you're still waiting."

Faye snorted. "Oh, that's useful."

Ed swung off the couch and landed barefoot on the deck plates. "Was it a clerical ghost? Ed likes clerical ghosts. They have such good secret hallways."

"It was ISSP," Jet said. He split the cabbage, flattened one half, and started cutting. The knife hit the board in steady dull knocks. "Somebody got into sealed background material and hung a bounty shell on it with dead credentials."

That got the room a little quieter.

Spike shifted his weight off one shoulder. "Dead whose?"

Jet kept his eyes on the cabbage. "Old source out of Mars."

Faye stared at the back of his head. He could feel it. He kept cutting.

Ed leaned both hands on the counter and craned to watch the knife work. "Can Ed see the credential trail? Ed can make friends with dead things in the database."

"No."

"Why not?"

"Because I said no."

Ed rocked back, considering that. "Strong legal argument."

Faye tossed the scraped nail polish flake toward the trash and missed by a foot. "If somebody in ISSP is messing with you, that's crew business, whether you like it or not."

Jet chopped onion now, harder than he needed to. The smell opened sharp in the room. "Right now it's a sealed file somebody cracked."

"Which is still crew business."

Jet turned, knife still in hand but pointed at the board. "And that's why nobody goes near Mars records from this ship tonight. No public terminals, no source files, no asking around the dock. Whoever did this knew their way around internal records better than I do from the outside. So we eat, we sleep, and tomorrow we decide what gets touched."

There was a beat.

He heard how hard his own voice had gone and hated it, not because it was loud but because it sounded like a man trying too hard not to sound rattled.

Faye heard it too. Her eyes narrowed. One hand went, quick and unconscious, to the notch at her collarbone before she caught it and folded both arms instead. "Wow. Fine. Keep your secrets. Everybody else is having a great time with theirs too."

Jet set the knife down. "I didn't say that."

"No, you just said shut up and eat cabbage."

"That's not what I said."

Spike finally stepped away from the hatch. "Leave it."

Faye looked at him. "Oh, now you care?"

"Not really." He put the bowl in the sink at last. "But he's right about one thing. If ISSP still has live dead-source access floating around, stomping on it tonight is stupid."

Jet looked at him then. Spike's face gave him nothing useful, only that familiar nearly-bored stillness. The way he backed off carried recognition in it, like Spike saw the shape of a man holding something back because he had his own hands full of the same posture.

Ed, who hated static tension mostly because it interrupted whatever else Ed wanted to do, tilted the head sideways and asked, "So dinner first?"

"Yes," Jet said.

"Then Ed votes noodles."

"Ed votes noodles every night."

"Because noodles are good."

Ein barked once in agreement or opportunism.

That broke it enough. Faye rolled her eyes so hard it almost counted as labor and sat back down. "If this dinner has any surprises in it, I'm suing."

"You can't afford a lawyer."

"I can't afford whatever's in that packet either."

"Then chew carefully."

They were back on the old track now, laying cheap jokes over the sharper thing underneath because nobody wanted to pry it up yet. Jet got a pan hot. Oil hissed. Onion hit metal. The smell went sweet in a hurry. Ed climbed back onto the couch, terminal open again, mumbling to Ein about zombie passwords and delicious encryption. Faye resumed murdering her thumbnail with the card edge. Spike drifted to the porthole and stood there looking out toward the settlement lights, one hand in his pocket, the cigarette still unlit behind his ear.

Jet dumped in the cabbage, then the protein scraps. They browned at the edges if you were generous with the term. The knife work steadied him. So did the ordinary sting of cheap onion in the eyes, the heat off the pan, the hard little problem of making dinner from not enough. He could do that. He had always been able to do that.

"So," Faye said after a while, less sharp now, almost casual. "This Hosuke guy. Somebody you knew?"

Jet scraped the pan. "Old dirt."

Faye opened her mouth, then shut it. Maybe she was tired. Maybe her own day had left bruises in places she didn't want pushed. She flicked the playing card across the table instead and watched it spin to a stop.

Spike didn't turn from the porthole when he spoke. "You want me to ask around?"

The offer was quiet enough that Faye and Ed both missed what sat under it. Jet didn't. Spike never volunteered curiosity without a reason.

"No," Jet said.

"All right."

That was all Spike gave him: a flat all right and the space after it.

Jet had spent enough years reading reluctant statements to know when one carried its own omissions. Spike let it drop with professional courtesy, the kind men used when they were standing on different sides of the same fog bank.

The noodles went in. Steam climbed up and fogged the cabinet for a second. Jet stirred, tasted, added pepper, decided salt would only remind them how cheap the protein was. Behind him Ed laughed at something on-screen. Faye asked if there was any beer. Jet said no. She asked if there was hidden beer. Jet said especially no. Ein hopped down and stationed himself beside the stove with the patient moral certainty of a dog who had never cooked once in his life and still believed the meal depended on him.

When Jet set the bowls out, no one said thank you. They almost never did. Faye blew across hers and burned her tongue anyway. Ed slurped with total concentration. Spike ate leaning on the counter, looking like a man too tired to sit down all the way.

From the corridor it might have passed for normal: four people with bowls in their hands, making cheap remarks over dinner because that was easier than saying what had them by the throat.

Up close the whole room was off.

Faye kept losing the room for half a second at a time, eyes going somewhere else and coming back meaner. Spike's attention kept hanging up on the dark outside the porthole. Ed was halfway through the bowl when the terminal on the couch chimed and bloomed blue against the cushions. A Mars cache handshake spun up in one corner of the screen, quick as a reflex.

Jet crossed the room before he'd decided to move. He planted one wet hand on the lid and snapped the terminal shut. Plastic cracked loud in the common room. Broth slopped over Faye's fingers. Ein stood up so fast his bowl skidded.

"Jet," Faye said.

"I said not tonight."

Ed stared at the closed terminal, then up at him. The kid's face went abruptly still. "I hadn't opened Mars yet."

Jet kept his hand on the lid a second too long. "I know. Leave it closed."

Nobody picked a bowl back up right away. Spike watched Jet over the rim of his own, unreadable. Faye wiped broth off her knuckles onto a napkin and didn't make the joke sitting right there for her. Ed slid the terminal under the couch without another word. That silence sat in the room heavier than all the noise had.

Faye set her empty bowl aside first. "This would've been better with mushrooms."

"Everything's better with mushrooms."

"That's the smartest thing anybody's said for five minutes."

Jet gathered bowls for the sink. Water knocked in the pipes when he turned the tap. No one reopened the terminal. Behind him, Ed scratched one finger behind Ein's ear and said nothing at all.

Spike finally lit the cigarette and cracked the vent. Cold air threaded in around the smoke, carrying dock-lot dust and the faint metallic smell of evening. "Tomorrow then," he said.

Jet looked over his shoulder once. Spike was watching the smoke get pulled toward the vent, face unreadable. Faye had her chin in her hand again. Ed was still crouched by the couch, not hacking, not talking, one palm on Ein's back.

The Bebop ticked in the cold, and nobody went near Mars that night.
