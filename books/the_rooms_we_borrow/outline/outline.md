# The Rooms We Borrow

## Narrative Engine
- Primary engine: literary fiction fused with family character study and coming-of-age, under survival-drama pressure.
- Secondary engine: a restrained love story between Tiana and Malik that alters trust, self-perception, and the imagined routes out of poverty.
- Consequence logic: chapters turn on rent, paperwork, missed deadlines, injuries, humiliation, sex, secrecy, and revised understanding. The book should advance through domestic and emotional aftershocks rather than crime-plot escalation.

## Novel Frame
- Setting: Little River, Miami, centered on a converted garage apartment behind a decaying stucco house; pressure points extend to Coral Gables, Virginia Key, bus lines, a church fellowship hall, and an airport-area apartment.
- Span: June to October 2019.
- POV design: close third rotating mainly through Tiana, Marcia, Delroy, and Micah.
- Family fault lines:
  - Marcia believes survival comes from planning, shame swallowed whole, and never letting the room see panic.
  - Delroy believes survival comes from improvisation, charm, and refusing to admit when he has already lost ground.
  - Tiana wants college to mean self-invention, not just upward mobility.
  - Micah, Miami-born, wants not to be read before he has spoken.
- Texture map: public ceremony, Sunday domesticity, job-search humiliation, institutional discipline, bureaucratic dread, communal financial collapse, erotic escape, beach-day aftermath, bodily injury, cramped cohabitation, police encounter, information-turn discovery, marital rupture, blackout intimacy, flood displacement, search chapter, quiet hard-earned resolution.

## Chapter Plan

### Chapter 1: The Red Paper
- POV: Tiana
- Texture: 2 scenes; public celebration followed by compressed domestic dread.
- Objective: Protect the possibility of leaving for college without humiliating the family.
- Conflict: Graduation-night pride collides with a red code-enforcement notice on the garage apartment door.
- Consequence: A 90-day housing deadline is set, and Tiana hides her August dorm-deposit deadline because the family cannot survive both truths at once.
- Must land beats:
  - Tiana's award night should establish intelligence, ambition, and the class shame of being the student whose family arrives loud, late, and underdressed.
  - The notice must feel bureaucratic and lethal rather than melodramatic.
  - Marcia begins calculating immediately.
  - Delroy minimizes the threat because he cannot afford the alternative.
  - Micah uses humor as self-defense.

### Chapter 2: Sunday Pot
- POV: Marcia
- Texture: 3 scenes; church, school fallout, and a meal that tries and fails to stay ordinary.
- Objective: Keep the family moving through a normal Sunday while preserving the partner hand as their rescue plan.
- Conflict: Micah's school fight, the utility shortfall, and Delroy's refusal to treat the housing notice like an emergency all compete for the same small pool of cash and attention.
- Consequence: Marcia puts the utility money into the partner anyway, deepening the risk because the payout is the only future she can still imagine.
- Must land beats:
  - Church must show community warmth and surveillance in the same gestures.
  - Micah's fight should be rooted in racial and immigrant taunts, not generic teenage temper.
  - Tiana hides her college paperwork in plain sight.
  - Dinner establishes the family at baseline: funny, sharp, affectionate, and dangerous to one another in the same room.

### Chapter 3: Day Labor
- POV: Delroy
- Texture: 2 scenes; external humiliation followed by transactional camaraderie.
- Objective: Find steady work before the housing notice becomes eviction in fact.
- Conflict: Contractors read Delroy as old, hurt, too Jamaican, and too expensive to bother with.
- Consequence: He borrows $400 from Claude and promises himself to a future demolition job, beginning a debt he hides from Marcia.
- Must land beats:
  - Delroy's pride should be legible without making him noble by default.
  - The labor yard must show Miami's racial hierarchy through who gets chosen first and who must wait.
  - Claude should register as useful, funny, and dangerous because he reduces friendship to terms the moment cash appears.

### Chapter 4: The Boy in the Office
- POV: Micah
- Texture: 4 scenes; institutional pressure, bus-ride aftermath, and private drift.
- Objective: Get through the discipline hearing without being remade as the kind of boy adults have already decided he is.
- Conflict: The dean and school cop treat Micah as a pattern before they treat him as a person, and Micah's pride makes him answer pressure with more pressure.
- Consequence: He is suspended for ten days and begins valuing cash work over school legitimacy.
- Must land beats:
  - Micah's point of view should separate public bravado from private shame.
  - Marcia's silence in the office must hurt him more than if she shouted.
  - Dre's offer of dock work should feel seductive because money arrives faster than dignity ever has.
  - Micah deciding to earn instead of explain marks the first serious inward turn away from school.

### Chapter 5: Gables
- POV: Marcia
- Texture: 2 scenes; slow-burn caregiving followed by bus-ride arithmetic.
- Objective: Secure extra hours or an advance from Celia's household before the money gap widens.
- Conflict: Celia is failing, Elena is already managing the ending, and Marcia hates asking rich people for mercy they can pretend is professionalism.
- Consequence: Marcia leaves with no extra money and commits even harder to the partner payout due in July.
- Must land beats:
  - Celia should be more than an employer: difficult, vain, funny, and capable of seeing Marcia too clearly.
  - Elena must be brisk rather than cartoon-cruel.
  - The bus ride home should convert social humiliation into practical arithmetic.

### Chapter 6: Orientation
- POV: Tiana
- Texture: 3 scenes; anticipatory brightness, bureaucratic stoppage, and a held-breath return.
- Objective: Turn college from fantasy into fact by finishing orientation and aid paperwork.
- Conflict: The aid office flags her expired resident card and missing parent paperwork; everything Delroy said he handled is unfinished.
- Consequence: Her scholarship cannot be finalized, and Tiana goes home ready for war.
- Must land beats:
  - Other families' easy confidence should sharpen the class gap without caricature.
  - Malik enters as a witness with his own life, not a device.
  - Tiana's panic must be specific to documents, deadlines, and the terror of being made administratively invisible.

### Chapter 7: The Hand Breaks
- POV: Marcia
- Texture: 2 scenes; community collapse and kitchen aftermath.
- Objective: Collect the partner payout and finally convert months of sacrifice into movement.
- Conflict: Yvette does not appear, and then her message reveals she used the hand money after her husband's stroke and mortgage crisis.
- Consequence: Marcia loses the $5,800 she had built the summer around and must tell the family there is no rescue cash.
- Must land beats:
  - The partner should feel culturally specific and financially rational, not quaint.
  - The women gathered for payout should fracture between rage and pity.
  - Yvette's betrayal must also read as desperation.
  - Marcia's humiliation is public first, private second.

### Chapter 8: Night Bus
- POV: Tiana
- Texture: 4 scenes; domestic explosion, city drift, sex, and dawn recoil.
- Objective: Force Delroy to explain the document lie and get a plan strong enough to hold.
- Conflict: Delroy admits he used the renewal money to cover rent months ago; Marcia learns this in the same fight; nobody has a fix.
- Consequence: Tiana leaves for the night with Malik, and their first sex deepens attachment while exposing how badly she wants a body she can treat as air.
- Must land beats:
  - Delroy's confession should come from shame, not villainy.
  - Marcia's fury is partly about the lie and partly about being cut out of the household math.
  - Tiana and Malik's intimacy must be a narrative event with changed leverage afterward.
  - The dawn ride home should carry guilt, relief, and no false romantic resolution.

### Chapter 9: Virginia Key
- POV: Delroy
- Texture: 3 scenes; attempted leisure, information-turn grief, and quiet self-endangerment.
- Objective: Manufacture one ordinary family day before the house turns into nothing but problem-solving.
- Conflict: Nobody trusts Delroy enough to relax around the day he has staged, and then Marcia gets the call that Celia has died.
- Consequence: Marcia loses her steadiest job, and Delroy quietly commits to Claude's demolition job.
- Must land beats:
  - The beach should feel like real Miami relief, not symbolic scenery.
  - Micah and Tiana briefly remember how to be siblings instead of combatants.
  - Marcia's grief for Celia must be mixed with labor history and class awareness.
  - Delroy's decision to take the demolition job should read as both love and self-harm.

### Chapter 10: Concrete Dust
- POV: Delroy
- Texture: 2 scenes; dawn pickup and bodily consequence.
- Objective: Replace the stolen document money and prove he remains useful.
- Conflict: The demolition site is unsafe, his shoulder is already damaged, and everyone there knows injured men are easiest to discard.
- Consequence: Delroy tears the shoulder badly and comes home nearly unpaid, narrowing the family's options further.
- Must land beats:
  - The job should feel materially specific: dust, heat, fatigue, unsafe shortcuts.
  - Claude is implicated in the system without becoming its mastermind.
  - Delroy's injury must cost future labor, not just present pain.

### Chapter 11: Taking In
- POV: Marcia
- Texture: 4 scenes; negotiation, funeral-adjacent job search, arrival, and spatial humiliation.
- Objective: Keep the family housed after losing Celia's income and Delroy's work capacity.
- Conflict: The landlord refuses extension without cash, and the only immediate solution is to take in cousin Kerene, which means giving away Tiana's space.
- Consequence: Kerene arrives with a folding cot, Tiana's room ceases to be hers, and privacy becomes another bill the family cannot pay.
- Must land beats:
  - The landlord should weaponize procedure rather than shouting.
  - Elena's limited help must feel real but insufficient.
  - Kerene arrives as a person with wit, appetite, and her own migration shock, not a plot burden.
  - Tiana must feel replaced even while knowing Kerene needs the room.

### Chapter 12: Matching Description
- POV: Micah
- Texture: 2 scenes; school-edge dread and the stop itself.
- Objective: Pick up school paperwork and get home without worsening anything.
- Conflict: Police stop Micah and Dre after a nearby theft because they match the description before either boy has opened his mouth.
- Consequence: Micah is released but bloodied and publicly seen against the cruiser, hardening his distrust and costing Marcia clinic money she does not have.
- Must land beats:
  - The stop should show procedure as theater and violence as routine.
  - Dre's fear must register under the jokes.
  - Micah's humiliation matters because neighbors see it.

### Chapter 13: The Trunk
- POV: Tiana
- Texture: 3 scenes; bureaucratic instruction, information-turn discovery, and restrained aftermath.
- Objective: Renew her documents herself and salvage some version of college.
- Conflict: The paperwork requires old records Marcia keeps buried, and the search through the cedar trunk exposes Delroy's remittance receipts to Devon and Marcia's abandoned nursing-school acceptance letter.
- Consequence: Tiana's anger stops being simple; she sees both parents as interrupted people rather than only obstacles.
- Must land beats:
  - The trunk should work as family archive, not magic solution.
  - Kerene can help practically while revealing how recent and raw her own arrival still is.
  - Tiana choosing not to weaponize what she learns must cost her something.

### Chapter 14: What Was Sent
- POV: Marcia
- Texture: 2 scenes; collection call and marital rupture.
- Objective: Keep Claude from humiliating Delroy in front of the children and salvage some working version of marriage.
- Conflict: Claude's visit exposes both the debt and Delroy's secret remittances to Devon in Jamaica; Delroy defends obligation where Marcia hears theft.
- Consequence: Delroy leaves after the fight, and the family can no longer pretend its economy was honest.
- Must land beats:
  - Delroy's hidden support for Devon should complicate him rather than excuse him.
  - Marcia's rage must include class fury, maternal fury, and the old wound of being the last person told.
  - Claude should register embarrassment at what his arrival sets off.

### Chapter 15: Lights Out
- POV: Micah
- Texture: 4 scenes; preparation, blackout meal, practical labor, and communal waiting.
- Objective: Get through the tropical-storm watch without looking scared or useless.
- Conflict: The apartment is overfull, the power fails, Delroy returns to board windows, and everyone is trapped in the same dark while still angry.
- Consequence: The family reaches a temporary truce through food, stories, and practical work just before the storm makes all plans provisional.
- Must land beats:
  - This chapter must contain genuine humor and bodily ordinary life.
  - Kerene should demonstrate competence and appetite for living, not just gratitude.
  - Delroy and Micah find a brief rhythm together that matters later.
  - The chapter's turn comes from weather and waiting, not confrontation.

### Chapter 16: Water Line
- POV: Marcia
- Texture: 2 scenes; flooding and institutional displacement.
- Objective: Save the things the family cannot replace once the storm breaches the unit.
- Conflict: Floodwater enters the garage apartment, the roof fails, and the landlord uses damage and the code notice to clear the property instead of repairing it.
- Consequence: The family is displaced to a church fellowship hall; papers, books, and the partner notebook are water-damaged, and the original housing plan dies.
- Must land beats:
  - Marcia's triage must reveal character through what she grabs first.
  - Church help should feel generous and exposing at once.
  - The water damage must create future continuity stakes around papers and books.

### Chapter 17: Missing
- POV: Tiana
- Texture: 3 scenes; shelter pressure, search, and deadline loss.
- Objective: Decide what to do about school before the last viable deadline closes.
- Conflict: A humiliating shelter photo of Micah circulates, he disappears, Delroy wants a family search, and Malik pushes Tiana not to disappear into duty again.
- Consequence: Tiana misses the original UF move-in window while helping find Micah; that version of escape closes for this year.
- Must land beats:
  - The shelter must feel noisy, intimate, and impossible for private decision-making.
  - Delroy participates in the search instead of posturing from a distance.
  - Malik's love has limits; he should not read as infinitely available refuge.
  - The lost deadline should hurt as a real foreclosure, not a fake setback.

### Chapter 18: Borrowed Rooms
- POV: Tiana
- Texture: 2 scenes; practical choice followed by quiet reassembly.
- Objective: Step back into motion without pretending the loss never happened.
- Conflict: Miami Dade Honors and a later transfer path feel both practical and like defeat; the family is moving into a smaller airport-area apartment with Delroy only partly restored to the household.
- Consequence: Tiana chooses the local start, Marcia takes a hospital transport job, Delroy begins overnight maintenance work, and the family enters a future still precarious but no longer built on lies they refuse to name.
- Must land beats:
  - The local-college decision must feel chosen under constraint, not framed as easy uplift.
  - Kerene exits toward her own shared room and own future, not as disposable scaffolding.
  - Micah returns chastened but still funny.
  - The ending should feel earned, unsentimental, and open-ended rather than triumphant.
