# Chapter 2

The light-bill money rode under Marcia's left bra strap all through service, the folded cash warming against her skin until the paper edges softened and curled. By the second hymn it felt less like money than like a hot leaf pasted to her body, something fragile that would tear if she breathed wrong. She kept one hand flat on her pocketbook anyway. The pocketbook held the tithe envelope, two safety pins, a pack of peppermint she had meant to ration for Micah, and the red copy of the code notice she had slept beside, folded so small it could almost pass for something manageable.

Church was full because people liked to look at a child who had done well and then look at the people who had made her. Tiana sat straight in the pew with her graduation hair wrapped down into a neat puff, the gold clip at one side catching the weak light from the high windows. The girl had changed out of the dress from Friday night, but the room was still treating her as if she were carrying a crown in her lap. Women leaned in to squeeze her hand. Men nodded at Delroy with that public pride fathers got to wear cheaply. Micah sat on the aisle in a shirt that had belonged to a cousin first and then somebody from church before it reached him. He kept two fingers hooked under the collar and tugged when he thought Marcia was not looking.

She was looking at all of them. Church made that seem holy. Everybody else was looking too.

The sermon was from Matthew, the bit about wise men building on rock and foolish men on sand. Pastor Green had a gift for making old warnings sound as if God had typed them that morning and slipped them under your own door. Every time he said house, Marcia saw the warped side wall of the garage apartment and the thin crack above the bathroom window that Delroy kept calling cosmetic. Every time he said storm, she thought of mildew under the kitchenette sink and the way rain already needled in under the back threshold if the wind leaned hard enough from the east. She kept her face arranged. Beside her, Delroy said amen in his big public voice, warm enough to fill three seats.

His hand rested on Micah's shoulder for part of the sermon, loose, fatherly, easy. Anybody watching would have seen a family steadied by faith and a daughter's good news. Marcia had dressed them for exactly that: her own blue church suit let out half an inch at the hip but still hanging clean, Delroy in the tan shirt that made him look less worn than he was, Tiana pressed and composed, Micah scrubbed hard enough behind the ears to count as obedient. There was no place in the arrangement for the city's red warning or for the electric bill due Tuesday or for the fact that the money for one of those things was now warming in her bra because she had not yet decided whether she was going to save the room they lived in or the plan that might get them out of it.

At offering time, Sister Paulette slipped down the aisle with the basket balanced on one palm. She always wore too-bright coral lipstick and always carried a funeral-home fan in her Bible, no matter whose face was printed on it. Today the fan was tucked into the top of her blouse, the dead man peeking over lace. Her left heel clicked late because the rubber cap had worn off again. When she paused at Marcia's row, she smiled at Tiana first.

"Scholar girl," she whispered. "You look nice, eh. Gainesville soon make you too fine for we."

Tiana gave the church smile she had learned young, soft enough to read as humble and careful enough not to promise anything. "Morning, Sister Paulette."

"Morning? It nearly afternoon." Paulette laughed and pinched Tiana's forearm, affectionate and measuring in the same touch. Her eyes flicked once over Marcia's suit, Delroy's collar, Micah's shoes. "Marcia, you must feel proud."

"I do," Marcia said. "By God's grace."

That answer was smooth enough to satisfy church. It did not tell Paulette that pride felt expensive in Marcia's body, that it scraped against fear until both things turned raw.

The basket came. Marcia put in the tithe envelope. The other money stayed where it was, damp and private.

By the time service ended, her slip was sticking to her back. The sanctuary had old air-conditioning units mounted high on the wall, but they mostly circulated the perfume, starch, and body heat. Outside, the church steps flashed white under noon sun. Women opened umbrellas against weather that was not rain. Men loosened ties. Children kicked off church shoes and got rebuked before the shoes could scuff too far under parked cars.

The congratulations came in a line.

Brother Henry, who smelled permanently of hair tonic and gasoline, shook Delroy's hand and said, "You have to start charging the neighborhood now. Two smart pickney in one house, that is luxury."

Delroy laughed. "Then I need luxury money, boss. You know where them keeping that?"

Everybody laughed because Delroy was good at turning lack into performance. He could say broke and make it sound like a temporary misunderstanding.

Sister Paulette came back with a square of bun wrapped in napkin for Tiana and one for Micah. "For the scholar and the one coming after," she said.

Micah took his and muttered, "Yes, ma'am."

"You hear how nice him can sound?" Paulette said to Marcia. "At church only."

"That's already a miracle," Marcia said, and the women around her made pleased church noises, because a mother joking lightly at her son was the sort of thing everybody knew how to hold.

Then Paulette lowered her voice half an inch, which in church was the same as stepping closer. "And Delroy finding enough work?"

There it was. Never naked, never rude. Always wrapped in concern the way cheap cake got wrapped in extra icing.

Marcia kept her smile. "He's managing."

Paulette nodded as if she had been given a number she could use later. "God makes way."

God, Marcia thought, and women who remembered everybody else's business better than their own blood pressure medicine schedule.

She had just taken a paper cup of warm fruit punch when she saw Yvette under the almond tree beside the fellowship hall. Yvette was still in her usher blouse, white enough to show the shadow of her bra through the back. She had kicked off her low heels and stood in nude stockings on the packed dirt, one foot flexed up on its toes to ease a cramp. Her patent Bible case lay open on the folding chair beside her. Inside it, tucked under a worn New Testament and a packet of receipts, was the black-and-white composition notebook that held the hand. Yvette's thumbnail was split down the center from work and had a strip of clear tape over it to keep it from snagging on fabric. She was writing with the slow, careful script of a woman who knew numbers had to stay legible longer than feelings did.

Marcia felt the money against her skin as if it had suddenly turned sharp.

Yvette looked up and gave her the small nod women used when money had to pass between them without announcing itself to every cousin and prayer partner within earshot. "You have a minute?"

Marcia handed her fruit punch to Micah. "Hold this."

"If you come back with no punch, I'm drinking all of it," Micah said.

He took the cup with his left hand. His right stayed knotted inside the bun napkin, and when he answered he kept the right corner of his mouth behind the rim as if the punch needed guarding already. Marcia caught a puffiness there, some small darkening she could not name from three feet away. He wiped at it fast and gave her a look that said not here.

"Try it."

He smiled out of the unhurt side of his face, half challenge, half plea. It made him look younger than the trouble waiting for him.

Marcia crossed the yard. From the church steps she could hear Delroy still talking, still charming. Brother Henry said something too low to catch. Delroy answered louder, "That city thing? Man, city always sends red print first. They trust a stamp more than a person. Monday I go there and settle it."

Marcia kept walking. If she stopped now she would have had to choose between humiliating him in public and swallowing him again.

Under the almond tree, Yvette fanned herself with a supermarket circular. The circular made a tired slapping sound in the air. Close up, Marcia could see the salt line dried at Yvette's hairline where her wig had shifted in the heat. There were flour smudges on one cuff. Yvette had probably baked before dawn again, sweet bread for the church table, one more thing turned into money or obligation.

"Rough morning?" Yvette asked.

"Morning is morning."

"That's not an answer."

"It's the answer I have."

Yvette's mouth twitched. She was one of the few women in church who could hear a dry line and not take offense. "You good for today?"

Marcia could have said no. She could have said the light bill was due, the city had stapled a countdown to her door, Micah had grown a restlessness in his face she did not trust, Delroy was talking like a man with two plans when he had not yet shown her one. She could have said her chest felt packed with crumpled demands, every one of them asking for cash. But saying no to the hand did not only mean saying no to one Sunday. It meant asking to be carried by the circle instead of carrying her place in it. It meant watching the July draw move away from her. It meant admitting, to Yvette and then to everybody else soon enough, that the one rescue she could still picture had started slipping.

So she hooked two fingers inside the top of her blouse, reached under the bra strap, and pulled the folded money free. The cash came out damp from her skin. That shamed her more than the amount.

For one stupid second she thought of the refrigerator light. She thought of the fan on the milk crate beside their bed. She thought of chicken in the freezer softening into pink water if Florida Power decided Tuesday meant Tuesday. Then she put the bills in Yvette's hand anyway.

Yvette did not count in front of her. Poor women knew better than to total up a sister's dignity in public. She slipped the money into an envelope already marked with Marcia's name, licked her thumb without thinking, then remembered the taped nail and winced.

"You're still good for July," Yvette said.

"I need to be."

"Everybody needs to be. That's why the hand exists."

The notebook opened on Yvette's knee. Marcia watched her own name receive its small mark of continuation. The scratch of pen on cheap paper gave her a mean kind of relief. Nothing had improved. The apartment was still on the city's clock. The electric bill was still unpaid. But the line beside her name was not blank, and blankness was how disaster announced itself first.

Yvette tore off Marcia's receipt from the envelope flap and handed it over. "Keep this. And tell Tiana from me, don't let them big college people bleach all the sense out of her."

"I'll tell her."

Yvette looked past Marcia toward the steps where Tiana stood surrounded by women. Her face softened. "That girl looked good Friday. Real good. I cried and it wasn't even my child. My husband laughed at me."

A whole life sat inside that sentence, one Marcia had no room to ask about. Most of them lived like that, skimming the top of one another's days and hoping the weight underneath held.

"You all right?" Yvette asked then, quieter.

The church yard shimmered. Delroy was still making people laugh. Micah had taken a bite of bun and kept the napkin near his mouth as if he were guarding crumbs, pretending not to eavesdrop on adult trouble. Tiana stood with her shoulders polite and tired while three women advised her about dormitories they had never seen.

Marcia tucked the receipt into her purse. "I'll be all right when July comes."

Yvette gave a short laugh that was almost a cough. "Then let July hurry."

Marcia turned back toward her family. She could feel the absence of the light-bill money now more clearly than she had felt its presence. It had left a damp patch cooling against her skin.

On the steps, Delroy reached for her elbow as if nothing in the world were fraying. "You ready, baby? Brother Henry saying his cousin know somebody down by code enforcement. All these people love to make thing sound worse than it's."

Marcia looked at his face, still open from performance. Men liked the feeling of help before help had happened.

"Then let your somebody call before they tack another threat to my door," she said.

His smile held, but it tightened at the edges. "I said I'm dealing with it."

"You said."

Micah made a little sound into his cup, a swallowed laugh or warning, and Tiana looked down at her shoes. Marcia adjusted her hat, turned her church face back on, and walked toward the car. If she started with Delroy there, on church steps with half the congregation breathing close, the whole yard would eat it before she got to the point. She had already spent the electric money. She did not have extra strength to spend too.

The garage apartment hit them with trapped heat when she opened the door. Church sweetness dropped off all four of them at once. Shoes went one way, pocketbooks another. Delroy unbuttoned his collar before the door even latched. Micah headed for the bathroom with his phone speaker leaking a thin mechanical beat. Tiana set down the foil pan of leftover bun Sister Paulette had pressed on them and lifted the edge of the clear vinyl table cover to slide something underneath.

Marcia saw the flash of a heavy white page, UNIVERSITY OF FLORIDA in blue across the top, then it disappeared under old takeout menus, a church anniversary flyer, and a faded photograph of Micah at eight missing his two front teeth.

"What are you hiding there?" Delroy asked lightly.

Tiana did not look up. "Nothing. Just school stuff."

"More scholarship thing?"

"Mostly deadlines."

"Deadlines love you bad."

"Apparently."

Delroy laughed and went to wash his face. He did not hear the way her answer stopped short. Marcia did. She heard more than she had strength to sort. She also meant to stop Micah when he came back out and ask what had happened to the side of his mouth. The phone got there first.

Her phone buzzed from inside the purse. She almost let it keep buzzing. Then she looked at the screen and saw the local school number with a little triangle beside it, voicemail already waiting.

The timestamp read Friday, 4:52 p.m.

While Tiana had been walking across a stage, while Delroy had been waving like the whole county was there to see his daughter, and while Marcia had been trying to hold her family inside one good public room, the school had already been reaching for her other child.

She pressed play.

A woman's voice filled the kitchenette, nasal and tired, the kind of voice that sounded as if it had spent years being patient with other people's children and had finally gone thin from the labor of it. "Mrs. Campbell, this is Dean Robles calling regarding an altercation involving your son, Micah Campbell, on school grounds this afternoon. At this time he is not to return to campus until a parent conference has been held. We have scheduled that meeting for Monday morning at 8:15 with administration and the school resource officer. Please contact me if that time creates a hardship."

The phrase sat there: creates a hardship.

As if hardship were something a person penciled in when convenient.

Micah came out of the bathroom in one of his old school polos, wiping his face on the hem. The right side of his lower lip had gone thick, and the first two knuckles on his right hand were skinned raw where the water had taken off the dried blood but not the sting. One button was missing from the shirt. He saw the phone in Marcia's hand and stopped. Tiana's eyes lifted once from the table.

"Micah," Marcia said. "Come here."

"Ma, I was going to tell you."

"No. You were going to let Monday arrive and then watch me get surprised in clean clothes."

He leaned against the fridge instead of coming closer. He had grown into that habit lately, making furniture carry the disrespect so he could pretend his own body was innocent. "It wasn't even like that."

"Then tell me what it was like."

Delroy came back from the sink rubbing water through his hair. "What happen?"

Marcia held up the phone. "Your son is not to return to school till conference."

Delroy's face shifted, not yet alarm, still looking for the joke that might reduce the thing. "For what?"

"That's what he's about to explain."

Micah rolled his eyes toward the ceiling. "It was a fight."

"I gathered that much from the word altercation. Use your mouth properly now."

He pushed off the fridge and paced two steps in the narrow space between table and stove. "It wasn't no random fight. Them boys kept on."

"Which boys?"

"Brandon and that next one with him, Luis."

"Kept on with what?" Marcia asked.

Micah shrugged, too hard. "Same stupidness."

"Do not give me same stupidness. Give me the thing itself."

He glanced toward Tiana, then away. That embarrassed look, quick and hot, frightened her more than the anger. Micah liked anger. He knew how to wear it. Shame moved on him like clothes borrowed from somebody bigger.

"They were doing Daddy's voice," he said finally. "Since Friday morning. The whole long vowel thing. 'Miiiicah, come ya, bwoy.' That foolishness."

Delroy snorted once. "So because they copy accent, you mash up the place?"

Micah swung toward him. "It wasn't just that."

"Then say the rest," Marcia said.

He dragged a hand over his face. "At lunch Brandon asked if I had goat in the container. I told him suck himself. He kept on. Said Tiana only got all that clap because white people like island sob stories. Then he asked if Mummy still cleaned up after everybody at school same way she clean old people house."

Dean Robles's voicemail kept leaking tinny out of the phone in Marcia's palm. The refrigerator motor kicked hard under it, rattling the salt shaker by the stove. Delroy stopped with his fork halfway up. Tiana looked down so fast her lashes hit her cheek. The bathroom tap kept ticking because Delroy never shut anything fully. Marcia felt the insult arrive there in her own kitchen, laid down between the plates where her children could watch it sit.

"And then?" she said.

Micah's mouth flattened. "Then he did the accent again. Said, 'Go back a yard with all that black ass island shit.' Loud. In front of everybody."

Tiana sucked air through her teeth before she could stop herself.

Micah heard it and got meaner to cover what was in him. "So, yeah. I hit him. First one caught his mouth. Second one landed wrong and split my hand when Coach Everett yanked me off him."

"With your hand?" Marcia asked.

"What else I was supposed to use, a hymn book?" He held up the fist like evidence. The knuckles were puffing around the cuts already, shiny and ugly. "Then Dean Robles marched me to the office, took my backpack, sat me under that freezing vent with a melting ice pack, and wrote emergency removal on a yellow slip like I brought a damn gun to school."

Delroy laughed once, quick and wrong.

Marcia turned on him. "Do not laugh."

"I'm not laughing at you."

"Then do not laugh at all."

Micah crossed his arms. "He deserved it."

"That's not the same thing as it helping you."

"So I should have just stood there?"

"I didn't say that."

"You're about to."

Marcia put the phone down very carefully on the counter. If she slammed it she would only add one more broken thing to the room. "Listen to me good. Tomorrow morning that school does not care how clever Brandon was with his mouth. They care whose fist they can write down. They care whose name fits the box they've already built. You already know whose name that will be."

Micah's face twitched. She had him there. The trouble with boys his age was that truth reached them and pride reached them faster.

"I'm not saying sorry like I started it," he said.

"I didn't say say sorry. I said use sense."

"Same thing."

"No," Tiana said quietly from the table. "It's not."

He rounded on her at once because sisters were safer to fight than systems. "Nobody asked you."

"Then stop talking like you want to get suspended forever."

"I said nobody asked you."

Marcia saw Tiana's fingers flatten the vinyl until her knuckles went pale. The girl's face stayed cool. Lately Marcia kept clocking how much silence Tiana could turn into a weapon.

Delroy leaned against the sink. "If the boy call him out his name like that, he's not wrong to defend himself."

"And when the school stamps ten days on him, you defending that too?" Marcia asked.

"You don't know it's ten days."

"I know schools. I know black boys. I know what an office can do."

Micah laughed without humor. "Yeah. An office. A stamp. Everybody in this house get to lose their mind over whatever desk wants next."

Nobody answered that because the room was already crowded with deadlines.

Marcia picked up the phone again and replayed the message, this time with the volume lower, as if smaller sound might make the thing less public. The words administration and resource officer landed harder on second hearing. Resource officer meant the cop with the tucked-in stomach and the gun on campus like children were a border to patrol.

She would have to miss part of Monday morning with Celia. She would have to call Elena and ask, which meant Elena's pause, Elena's little courteous silence while class considered inconvenience. The school had already reached into tomorrow's wages and squeezed.

"You'll come with me at 8:15," she said to Micah. "You won't go in there smiling. You won't go in there slouching. You'll answer what they ask and you'll leave your little smart mouth outside the office door."

"What if they ask if he called me that?"

"Then you tell them."

"And if they say I should have told a teacher?"

"Then you tell them he had time to speak because the teachers let him."

"And if they still side with him?"

Marcia looked at him. She wanted to lie. Mothers earned that temptation every day. But Micah had already reached the age where a child could tell when comfort was being served in place of fact.

"Then we deal with what they do," she said. "We don't hand them extra."

He dropped into the chair by the door with a thud that shook the table hard enough to rattle the forks. Tiana steadied her water glass without looking up.

She said nothing. One emergency at a time was not wisdom. It was simply all poor rooms could hold.

She untied her head scarf, folded it once, and went to the sink. "Move. I've got to season chicken."

Nobody moved fast enough, which was another Sunday truth.

She made the Sunday pot because not making it would have meant the bad news had won before evening. The chicken was not the nice kind, only backs and a few thick neck pieces from the freezer, but if you browned them hard with enough onion, enough thyme, enough scallion crushed under the knife handle, they could smell like care from the front door. She washed the pieces in lime and salt till the water ran cloudy, then clear, then cloudy again because cheap meat always carried some hidden extra with it. She rubbed in browning, allspice, garlic, black pepper, and the last spoon of Grace jerk paste scraped from the jar with her finger.

Heat climbed the kitchen walls. The apartment always seemed to shrink when a proper meal was underway, as if the steam took up square footage. Tiana shredded cabbage at the counter, long controlled strokes, not wasting movement. Micah peeled potatoes with his left hand because the right one had swollen across the knuckles, and every few strokes he flexed it like he could bully the pain smaller. He still took meat out with the skin. Marcia slapped his wrist once and made him start smaller. Delroy sat at the table with the newspaper sports section spread before him, though she had never in her life seen him read the sports page straight through without stopping to talk at somebody.

"Read that paper one more time and maybe money will fall out," she said.

He looked up, wounded and amused together. "I'm staying out your way."

"You're in my way while sitting down."

"That's marriage. You agreed."

Micah barked a laugh. Tiana tried not to.

For a few minutes the room found its old rhythm. Oil hissed. Onion went sweet. Delroy told a story about one deacon nodding off during sermon and waking only to shout amen three seconds late, which made Micah nearly drop the potato peeler. Tiana said Pastor Green had probably noticed and upgraded the man's salvation points accordingly. Even Marcia laughed once when Micah imitated Sister Paulette's click-heel walk and said her rebukes arrived with built-in percussion.

The laughter hurt and helped. A room could bruise you and keep you alive in the same hour.

She got the rice and peas going in the big pot, thin coconut milk stretched with water because the can had to last. The red peas rolled, starchy and softening. She sliced a sweet pepper fine enough that every spoonful might catch some color even if no one got much meat. On a good Sunday, she would have fried plantain too. Today there were two left on the counter, both going black at the edges. She cut them lengthwise so each person could have a few slices and no one would have to say out loud that there was not enough for greed.

Delroy folded the paper and tapped the clear table cover with one finger. "What is this under here now?"

Marcia did not turn. She knew the answer before Tiana spoke.

"Just my stuff," Tiana said.

"What stuff?"

"School things. Thank-you letters. End-of-year stuff."

"You're telling me school still isn't done with you after graduation?"

Tiana gave a little shrug. "Apparently they like deadlines as much as you do stories."

"That's rude," Delroy said, not sounding offended.

"It's accurate," Micah muttered.

Delroy grinned at him. "See there. Brotherly support."

Marcia set the spoon down harder than she meant to. "Support would look good tomorrow morning too."

The spoon struck enamel with a bright hard click. Tiana's knife stalled in the cabbage. Micah dropped the peeler into the sink hard enough to ring the basin. "I said I'm going."

"You also said you should have been allowed to punch the boy."

"Because I should have."

"This again," Tiana said.

"You hush," Micah shot back. "You're over there with your own problem and want to manage me."

Tiana's head lifted slowly. "I'm not managing you. I'm saying maybe don't make things worse on purpose."

"For who?"

"For yourself, maybe."

He laughed at that, a fifteen-year-old laugh sharpened on humiliation. "That's cute. Everybody wants me to act right so the room stays pretty."

"The room was never pretty," Marcia said. "It still has to function."

Delroy held both hands up, palms out. "All right. Enough. The boy is heated."

"He's not soup. Stop talking like temperature is the problem."

That earned her a look from Delroy, half annoyance, half admiration. He liked when she was sharp until the sharpness pointed directly at him.

She plated the food. She laid down rice first, then spread the gravy wider than the meat, then added cabbage, then two thin strips of plantain each. She watched all three children, yes, even Tiana though the girl would have hated the word child applied to her now. Micah went for the biggest neck piece on instinct. Tiana's hand paused over the ladle and withdrew, letting him take it. Delroy said grace too quickly, as if God might accept speed for sincerity.

For two minutes there was only eating. The first sounds were always practical: spoon on plate, a little hiss when somebody took in food too hot, Micah sucking gravy from his teeth, Delroy asking for pepper sauce before he had swallowed. Marcia loved them most at tables and least at tables. Hunger made everybody honest. So did the distribution of portions.

"This is good," Tiana said after a while.

"It's food," Marcia said.

"It's good food."

"She wants extra plantain," Micah said.

"I want you to mind your plate."

"That means yes," Delroy said.

"That means if any of you keep talking, next Sunday all I make is boiled green banana and attitude."

"You cook attitude excellent already," Delroy said.

Micah laughed with his mouth full. Even Tiana smiled into her plate. Marcia let the line stand because she had meant it and because the room needed one more minute of being itself.

Then Delroy wiped his fingers on the napkin and said, "Tomorrow, after the school thing, I'll go by code. Brother Henry's cousin says the man who actually signs off doesn't settle in till ten. If I rush down there at eight, all I get is some bored clerk telling me come back when the right desk opens."

Marcia looked up. "After?"

"Yes, after. I told you."

"You told me on church step. That's not the same as doing it."

"I can't be in two place one time."

"I've got to lose Monday morning to that school already, come back to work, and Tuesday's light bill still wants paying. Don't hand me after like it's some clean piece of day."

Micah looked from one parent to the other the way people watched tennis in waiting rooms. Tiana kept her eyes on her rice, but Marcia saw her listening so hard the muscles in her hand had gone careful around the fork.

Delroy sat back. "I'm not telling you fairy tale. I'm telling you how those offices run. One woman stamps it, the next man sends you upstairs, then somebody tells you the case man's on lunch. If Henry's cousin can point me straight, I skip the foolish laps. That's me trying to help, not dodge."

Marcia laughed then, one ugly short sound. "While you explain the office, I'm here stretching gravy wider than meat. The red slip is still in my purse. Micah still has conference at eight-fifteen. I need the thing done before they tack another warning to my door."

"You hear red slip and start talking like the bulldozer's already backing down the alley."

"Because ninety days gets small fast in one room."

"Half the city gets tagged like that. You go in, you talk, you fix what they want."

"Then let half the city come split that room with us and see how automatic it feels."

Micah looked down fast. The apartment had only one room to begin with. Everybody in it knew exactly what room she meant: the garage, the thin walls, the air mattress wheezing at dawn, Tiana's corner with the milk crate desk and the school certificates clipped to the string lights. Ordinary did not need explaining in houses like theirs. Threat did not either.

Delroy pushed his plate a little away. His shame always showed up like this, not in silence but in extra argument. "You talk like I enjoy this. You know how many times a man can go downtown, sweat through his shirt, smile at people who don't even look up, and still come home with the same damn runaround? I'm trying not to waste a run. I'm trying not to let them make me look stupid."

"Then don't waste it. But don't feed me charm while I slice plantain into slivers and pretend that counts as enough."

Micah sucked his teeth. "Can we not do this over chicken?"

"You can get up from the table if you don't like dinner talk," Marcia said.

"And go where?" he asked before he could stop himself.

Nobody answered because there was nowhere in the apartment for a dramatic exit. The bathroom was too small to slam impressively, and outside the back door the mosquitoes would have made mockery of any teenager's suffering in under a minute.

Tiana reached for her water glass and nudged the table with her wrist. Under the clear plastic, the corner of one white page showed again. This time Marcia could read the top line. It said UF Housing Contract.

The words sat under old menus and a church flyer advertising Youth Explosion Night, hidden so plainly they almost dared somebody to notice. Tiana saw Marcia looking and put her glass down over the line, casual as a card trick.

Delroy did not see any of it. He was still looking at Marcia as if the argument between them had begun in that moment and not years ago in smaller, cheaper versions.

"So what you want tonight, then?" he asked. "You want me to ride down there, find the building dark, and argue with one security guard and a mop bucket? Tomorrow's when the office opens. That's the plain truth."

"What I want is for you to talk like Tuesday is already in this room, because it is. That light bill's still unpaid. That city warning's still in my purse. I don't have room left for tomorrow-talk."

"And I want every stamped thing in this house to stop walking in here bigger than me. School calling, city threat, light bill, now whatever Ti's hiding under the table. A man gets tired of ink running his life."

Micah snorted. "That ship sail, Daddy."

"You're very funny for somebody with conference in the morning," Marcia said.

"I'm serious."

"That's the problem," Tiana said softly.

Everybody heard her. Her voice had slipped out before she dressed it up. Delroy turned to her.

"What problem?" he asked.

Tiana took one second too long to answer. Marcia could nearly see the girl sorting which truth she could afford. "That we keep calling it tomorrow like that changes the date. No, that's not even what I mean. I mean the words change, but it's always later. After the meeting. After somebody calls somebody. After the money shows up. Then the real deadline gets here and everybody acts like it came early."

Delroy blinked at her, then reached for a joke and missed. "Well. University words now."

"It's not university words."

"Then what is it?"

She glanced down at the glass over the contract and then back at her plate. "Nothing. Eat your food."

Marcia felt the sentence like a small hard seed dropped into her palm. The word was as false as Micah's same stupidness earlier, only polished smoother.

Micah shoved back from the table. "I'm done."

"Bring your plate to the sink," Marcia said automatically.

He did, though the plate hit the stainless steel too hard and sent gravy freckles up the backsplash. "If that dean asks me what happened, I'm saying exactly what he said. Every word."

"Good," Marcia said.

He looked surprised.

"Say it. Let them hear their school through your mouth. But after that, stop performing. Do you understand me?"

He rolled his jaw once. "Yeah."

"And if they ask whether you hit him?"

"I'm not lying."

"Good again."

That confused him more than anger would have. He picked up the dish towel and wiped his fingers on it despite knowing better. Marcia slapped at his wrist with the serving spoon. He yelped, laughed, and finally carried himself to the bathroom, muttering about tyranny.

The bathroom door knocked shut behind him. Tiana set the cups inside one another with more care than they needed. Delroy lowered his head and cleaned his plate in three big bites, either proving a point or hiding inside appetite. "Food nice," he said, standing.

"It was nice before the speech too," Marcia said.

"Everything with you is speech when I open my mouth."

"Then try opening your hands."

He looked at her long enough that Tiana busied herself stacking cups. In another room, in a bigger life, that look might have held flirtation or memory. Here it mostly held history worn down to friction.

"I'm going to smoke outside," he said.

"Take the red slip with you and smoke that too."

His nostrils flared. Then, because Tiana was there and because even now he preferred exit to humiliation, he gave a little shrug and went out the back door.

The screen door gave a soft slap. The mosquitoes got first right of him.

Tiana stayed at the table. She gathered the cups, then stopped with one hand under the clear vinyl. Marcia pretended to rinse plates while watching in the dark shine of the kitchen window. Tiana slid the housing contract deeper beneath the plastic, tucked now between the church flyer and an old Chinese takeout menu from the year before, the corner aligned so neatly it nearly disappeared.

"You know I saw it," Marcia said.

Tiana's shoulders went up, then down. "It's the housing contract."

"I know what it's called. What does it want?"

Water dripped from the plate rack onto the counter in slow fat clicks. Tiana kept her hand under the vinyl another second, as if the contract might still hide if she pressed hard enough. "Signed and back with the deposit by August first," she said. "If they don't get both, they release the room. Somebody else gets the bed and the move-in slot."

Marcia turned off the tap. "And you were going to leave that under a takeout menu till August walked in?"

Tiana looked at her then, not cool anymore, just tired and cornered. "I was going to wait till there was money attached to the sentence."

Marcia dried her hands on the dish towel slowly. July rose in her head at once, the partner payout she had already spent Tuesday's current to protect. She kept that thought in her own mouth. "Don't decide by yourself what I can carry."

"I'm not protecting you," Tiana said. "I'm trying not to watch Daddy promise one more fix with both hands empty."

For one quick second Marcia saw the child still in the girl, the one who used to bring permission slips three days early and stand by the sink until they were signed. Then the look closed. "I'll wash the pot later," Tiana said.

"No. Go rest your head."

"I'm not tired."

"You look tired."

Tiana almost smiled. "That's rude."

"Everything true sounds rude these days."

The girl took that and went to the bed area, carrying the cups. Marcia heard the rustle of sheets, the click of Tiana's phone charger, Micah already back there arguing with somebody through a headset in a voice softer than the one he used on family. Delroy's lighter scratched outside, then caught.

Marcia opened the top cupboard and took down her own partner notebook, the small one where she kept the envelope stubs and dates because trust was easier to survive when duplicated. Yvette's torn receipt went inside. The June line was marked paid. The handwriting looked thin and stubborn on the page.

Then she opened the drawer by the stove and took out the light bill.

The red band across the top shouted in a font that was almost cheerful. She laid it beside the notebook on the table. Under the clear plastic, the edge of Tiana's housing contract still showed if a person knew where to look. There were three claims there now: Yvette's thin promise of July, the light company's red threat of Tuesday, and August first waiting under Tiana's name. They all wanted the same hand, the same wallet, the same woman to answer first.

Outside, Delroy laughed at something on his phone, some man joke floating up warm through mosquito hum. From the bed area, Micah said, "Nah, he wish," and Tiana told him to shut up if he was going to keep lying that loudly. Their voices tangled, sharp and ordinary. It was family noise. It was life noise. It made the room sound held together when it was mostly held up.

Marcia stacked the notebook on top of the bill, then stood there with both palms resting on the covers as if she could press the whole stack into mercy. The electric money was already in Yvette's hand. The city's red slip still sat folded in her purse. Micah's meeting was at 8:15. Tiana had hidden an August first deposit line under the table where people usually stored coupons and church flyers.

Sunday had not stayed ordinary. It had only dressed itself that way long enough to get through the door.

She slid the notebook back into the cupboard and left the light bill flat in the middle of the table where Delroy would have to step around it if he slipped out before dawn. July had to come now. She had paid for that future with Tuesday's current, and there was no getting either one back by wishing.
