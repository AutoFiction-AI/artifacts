# Chapter 5

By nine-thirty the next morning, Marcia had changed Celia Alvarez twice, once because the old woman had not made it to the bedside commode in time and once because she had, technically, made it there but not all the way down. The second mess took longer. Celia's knees had given with a soft folding that still startled Marcia, as if the body had stopped consulting the woman inside it. Marcia got one arm under her back, the other under the meatless thighs, and lowered her to the tile before the fall could become a crack and a hospital and all the other expensive humiliations rich families called complications.

The bathroom was colder than church silver. Elena kept the air so low in that house that Marcia's sweat never dried properly; it just cooled on her spine and sat there. Celia cursed the cold every morning and then refused the blanket because blankets made her look sick. This morning she had one heel hooked on the bath mat, her nightgown twisted over one breast, the skin on her legs stippled purple where blood had started giving up on distance.

"Don't make that face," Celia said from the floor. Her lipstick from yesterday had flaked into the lines around her mouth, a faded coral ghost. "If I wanted pity, I would've married better."

Marcia braced her and got her up in stages. "Hold the rail, Miss Celia."

"I'm holding the rail. The rail's not holding me."

The old woman still had the cracked hand mirror on the sink ledge, the same one with a spiderweb split through the corner. She reached for it before she reached for water. Marcia had to admire that, though admiration was not the word she would ever use out loud. Celia checked her eyebrows with the seriousness of a surgeon reviewing bad work.

"Left one is too thin," she said.

"Nobody's inspecting your eyebrows in here."

"That's because you don't know these people. They inspect everything. That one is still too thin."

Marcia took the brow pencil from the tray. She steadied Celia's forehead with one thumb and filled the arch in small strokes while the old woman breathed through her mouth, sour with medicine and the little sweetness that had started living under her tongue these last weeks. Dying had a smell once it committed itself. It was not rot exactly. It was more like fruit gone soft in a bowl no one had the heart to throw out.

Celia watched her in the mirror. Her hair had been set at the beauty shop on Monday, and even flattened from sleep it still carried a stubborn curve at the ends. She'd rather have gone to the salon herself. She'd said so every day since she stopped being able to.

"Use the good lipstick," Celia said. "Not the dead-lady one your Elena keeps bringing."

"You're not dead."

"Then don't paint me like I'm dead."

Marcia capped the pencil, found the coral tube in the little silver cosmetics case, and ran color over Celia's dry mouth. Celia pressed her lips together, then frowned.

"Again. The top lip looks frightened."

There were women who became easier once their bodies turned against them. They got grateful. They got soft. Celia had gone the other way. Each new indignity sharpened her. She complained about powder texture, soup temperature, the priest's breath, the size of the pills, the angle of light on her bedspread. She also made Marcia laugh at least once a shift, usually against Marcia's will. It was an irritating gift.

When Marcia got the brief changed and the fresh gown over Celia's shoulders, she helped her back to bed. The room looked out over a clipped lawn and a bougainvillea hedge that never seemed to collect dust. Even the sunlight in Coral Gables came filtered, respectable. There were no roosters, no bass crawling through drywall, and no neighbor calling across a yard about who had eaten whose mangoes. There was just the soft hum of central air and, farther down the hall, Elena's voice giving instructions in the kitchen like every object in the house had to justify the space it occupied.

Celia settled against the pillows with a little hiss of pain she tried to hide behind annoyance. The effort cost her more now. Her wrists were narrow enough that Marcia could circle one with thumb and forefinger. The first week Marcia worked there, Celia had slapped those fingers away from a button she preferred to fasten herself. That had been almost three years ago. Now Celia waited for help and then resented the waiting.

"You're quiet," Celia said.

Marcia smoothed the sheet where it had wrinkled under Celia's hip. "I'm working."

"You're thinking. The working part is with your hands."

There it was. Celia had a way of seeing the exact place Marcia wanted to keep covered and tapping it once, lightly, just to prove it existed.

"Everybody thinks while they work," Marcia said.

"Not like that. That's counting." Celia shifted her eyes toward Marcia's handbag on the chair. "Something is short."

Marcia almost laughed, because what in her life was not short. Cash was short. Time was short. Tiana's patience with the house was running short. Micah's summer idleness would soon turn into some other kind of trouble. The red code notice on the apartment door had cut the whole season down to a bad joke. But she only said, "You want tea before your pills?"

"I want my daughter to stop arranging my death like centerpieces for a luncheon."

From the hall Elena called, "I heard that."

"Good," Celia called back, not raising her voice much. She did not need to. The house carried her the way some people carried perfume. "Bring me the pink cardigan. The blue one makes me look like a hospital wall."

Elena appeared in the doorway with the cardigan over one arm and a yellow legal pad tucked against her side. She had changed out of whatever she had slept in and into white linen pants and a sleeveless navy blouse that cost more than Marcia let herself guess, but she'd left her hair in the rough knot from the night before. A black pen held the knot in place. Each time it started loosening, she pushed the pen deeper with two fingers and kept talking. The skin around one thumbnail was torn raw. She worried it when she thought nobody was looking.

"Morning, Marcia," she said. "Did she take any water?"

"Some. Not enough."

"Nobody drinks enough in this family except the dead." Celia held out a hand for the cardigan. "And don't put me in that freezer after lunch. If I die cold, I'm haunting this house."

Elena put the cardigan on her mother with the same brisk care she used on pill bottles and doctor phone calls. No wasted motion, no softness for show. Marcia respected that more than she liked it. Women who had money were allowed to call efficiency realism. Women like Marcia had to make it look like devotion or people started using words like attitude.

"Dr. Feldman is coming at one," Elena said to Marcia. "And the hospice coordinator at two-thirty. I'd like to keep the afternoon quiet."

The word hospice entered the room and sat there. Celia rolled her eyes hard enough to make herself cough.

"See?" she said when the cough passed. "Centerpieces."

Marcia reached for the water cup. Celia waved it off, then took it anyway because her pride had not yet found a way to live without thirst. As she drank, thin and careful, Marcia watched Elena flip to a clean page on the legal pad. She had lists for medications, lists for linens, lists for cousins coming by with casseroles nobody ate. Last week she'd started labeling drawers in the kitchen. Spoons. Towels. Batteries. As if the house had begun slipping away and could be pinned down with handwriting.

Marcia had been waiting all morning for an opening that would not sound like begging. She had rehearsed three versions on the first bus and rejected all of them. One sounded desperate. One sounded like a lie. One sounded too much like she believed she had earned mercy. She had not. In houses like this, mercy arrived gift-wrapped as policy exceptions and made the poor woman thank you twice.

Elena glanced at the clock on the mantel. "Can you change the bed again after her pills? Then maybe just settle her? I don't think we'll need you through the full afternoon."

There it was, and it was not even cruel enough to deserve anger.

"All right," Marcia said. The word came out smooth. Years of practice. "Miss Elena, I wondered if there might be any extra time later this week. Or if there was any way to run part of Friday's check ahead. Just a little. I can make it up over the weekend."

Elena did not pretend not to understand. Marcia gave her that much credit. The younger woman's face changed in one quick, almost invisible way, the way people looked when they had already begun arranging their refusal into something they could live with.

"I'm sorry," Elena said. "No on the advance. Payroll is through the agency and they've already closed this week. And I don't have extra hours right now. I'm actually pulling hours back while we're figuring out what the doctor wants."

"Only for this week?" Marcia asked. She hated the smallness in the question as soon as it left her mouth.

"For now." Elena pressed the pen flat against the pad, then lifted it again. "Maybe next week is different. Maybe not. I don't know yet."

Celia made a sound that was almost a laugh and almost a spit.

"Ay, Dios," she said. "Listen to all that clean language. The woman asked for money, Elena. You don't have to dress the no in a blazer."

"Mami."

"What? I'm dying, not deaf."

Heat moved under Marcia's skin, mean and useless. She kept her face arranged. That was the trick. It was not honesty but arrangement.

"It's fine," she said. "I shouldn't have asked in front of you."

"You should've asked sooner," Celia said. "Need doesn't get more elegant with waiting."

Elena blew out a breath through her nose. Up close the fatigue showed in the gray half-moons under her eyes. She had been sleeping in this house for days. Her phone kept lighting on the bedside table with brothers, nurses, priests, and a son in Tallahassee who wanted updates but not duties. She was not a villain. She was a daughter with a calendar and a mother leaking out of it.

"Marcia, if I hear of anything once this is over, I'll ask around," Elena said. "You know I'd tell you if there was room here. But right now we're paying for the overnight nurse consult, the equipment, all of it. It's not that I don't want to help."

The sentence was meant kindly enough. It still landed like a hand on the chest. Equipment, consult, help: clean nouns for the same old truth that money moved easiest toward the people who already had enough of it to name things properly.

Celia held out her palm. "My purse."

"No," Elena said immediately.

"What do you mean no? It's my purse."

"And you're tired, and tired is when you start solving everybody with cash."

"I'm not confused. I'm generous."

"Same damage, different name."

"Marcia," Celia said. "Bring it here."

Marcia's eyes went to the chair before she could stop them. Black leather, gold clasp rubbed pale where a thumb had worked it for years. She knew that purse better than she wanted to. She had carried it into cardiology, podiatry, the salon, Sunday Mass. She knew the middle pocket held the green wallet, and the green wallet held cash because Celia considered leaving the house without bills in her bag a kind of public nakedness.

"Leave it," Elena said.

"It's my money," Celia said. "Bring me my damn purse."

Marcia crossed the room anyway. Obeying the patient, she told herself. Not reaching. The strap bit into her palm, heavier than it had any right to be. When she set the purse on the blanket, Celia's fingers fumbled at the clasp and failed.

"Open it," Celia said.

"Marcia," Elena said, and now the softness was gone. "Put that back."

The clasp snapped under Marcia's thumb. Inside sat the green wallet, a coral lipstick, pharmacy receipts, and the flat bright edge of cash. Her hand hovered over it. One twenty showed, then another behind it, enough to turn tonight into something less vicious.

"Take out fifty," Celia said. "If I have to listen to poverty in my own bedroom, somebody should at least leave paid."

Elena stepped to the bedside. "No."

The word landed too late. Marcia had already touched the wallet. Heat went up her neck, slow and filthy. She shut the purse hard enough for the clasp to click.

Elena did not thank her. What she gave Marcia instead was a flat, measuring look that made the room feel newly divided.

"No, Miss Celia," she said. She lifted the purse off the bed and put it back on the chair, farther than before. "Take your pills."

"Bossy girl."

"Take them."

"You hear that, Elena? She likes me best when I obey."

Marcia almost smiled despite herself. Celia saw it and gave a little triumphant nod, lipstick bright against all that failing skin.

After the pills, Celia drifted for a while. It was not sleep exactly. She lay with one hand on top of the blanket, fingers moving now and then as if still sorting cards in some old game Marcia had not been invited to learn. Marcia changed the bed again because the pad had leaked at the edge and because work was cleaner than standing in the kitchen feeling the no settle inside her. She rubbed lotion into Celia's calves, powder under the breasts where the skin stayed damp, and tucked the cardigan straight across the shoulders.

When she bent to lift the laundry bag, Celia opened one eye.

"Your husband working?" she asked.

The question was not casual. Celia did not waste curiosity on casual.

"Some," Marcia said.

"Some is a bad amount."

"It's the amount I've got."

Celia's mouth twitched. "You still have your pride too. I can see that from here. Terrible burden. Heavy thing to carry on the bus."

Marcia looked up. The old woman's pupils were cloudy but fixed on her with embarrassing accuracy.

"Rest," Marcia said.

"Mm. You too, if they ever let you."

By eleven-thirty Elena had already set out forms for the doctor and moved two framed family photos off the side table to make room for a machine that had not arrived yet. She was staging the end, just like the outline in Marcia's head had feared. A blue oxygen tank stood by the wall in its carton, unopened, as if the future could be tolerated in packaging but not in use.

Elena walked Marcia to the kitchen, speaking in the low practical tone people used for contractors and clergy. "I'll text you tonight about tomorrow. Morning should stay the same. Afternoon, I'm not sure."

"All right."

"And Marcia?" Elena waited until Marcia looked at her. "I meant what I said. If somebody asks me for a caregiver once this is over, I'll pass your name first."

Marcia nodded because to do anything else would have required a speech and speeches were expensive. On the island counter sat a bowl of nectarines ripening untouched, a stack of hospice pamphlets facedown, and three kinds of imported crackers nobody in that house had the appetite for. The refrigerator gave off its steady expensive hum. Marcia could have stood there naming the cost of the room: granite, a gas range, an ice dispenser that worked every time. She did not. Arithmetic done in anger still had to come out correct.

At the front door Elena touched her arm. "I'm sorry."

There were apologies rich people used the way some churches used fans: to move the air without changing the heat.

"I know," Marcia said.

Behind them Celia called, thin but sharp, "Bring the coral tomorrow. If I end up meeting Saint Peter, I'm not doing it pale."

Elena shut her eyes again, just briefly. Marcia let herself smile that time. It cost nothing, which was good, because she did not have another kind to spare.

Outside, the heat hit her full in the face, raw and wet and honest. The walk from the Alvarez house to the bus stop was only seven minutes if she moved hard, but Coral Gables had a way of stretching distance with prettiness. The sidewalks were broad and empty. Hibiscus hedges held themselves like women with old money. Sprinklers clicked over lawns so green they looked artificial. A man in white shorts jogged past with a dog wearing a bandanna cleaner than Micah's school uniform on picture day.

Marcia kept her work bag high under her arm and did not rush. Rush looked wrong in neighborhoods like this. Rush announced you belonged to the part of the city that sweated for bus schedules. She passed a driveway where two landscapers were loading clipped branches into a trailer and caught one of them looking at her shoes, the sensible black pair she only wore for work because the bus dust ate everything cheaper. His glance held no insult. Just recognition. Service shoes knew one another.

At the stop she sat under a buttonwood tree trimmed so neatly it gave almost no shade. Her blouse stuck to the small of her back. She took out her transit card and checked the balance on the little screen by the shelter: $4.25. It was enough to get home if the transfer behaved, but not enough for another round trip after tomorrow unless she reloaded it.

The bus came heaving cold air and diesel. She climbed on, nodded to the driver, and took a seat halfway back where the vent blasted too hard. Her damp skin went chilled at once. Across from her a nanny in pink scrubs was texting with both thumbs while a toddler slept against her chest, mouth open, one fat hand knotted in the drawstring of her top. Two seats up, a man in paint-spattered whites dozed with his head knocking the window every few blocks and never waking enough to fix it.

Marcia set her bag on her lap and opened the zipper to the small inside pocket. Inside were her wallet, lip balm, two receipts from Presidente, a church envelope, and the partner notebook. She touched the notebook and then took her hand away, as if other people on the bus could tell what it was by temperature alone.

She started with the fixed numbers because panic liked fog and she preferred columns.

Rent was eleven-fifty.

Light was ninety-three and some change if she paid before the red print started.

Phone was seventy-eight because Micah swore he needed data for school even in summer and Tiana needed a line that did not cut out when colleges called. Delroy always said they could lower it next month. Next month was a liar.

Groceries, if she cooked proper and stretched meat with lentils and enough rice to insult nobody, maybe one-forty for the week. Less if she let them complain. More if Micah kept eating like a boy trying to outrun his own height.

Then came bus fare, gas for Delroy when he got a day, laundry, the school money Tiana pretended not to need yet, and the money for moving that no one was saying out loud in front of the children, though the red notice on the apartment door said it well enough on its own.

Income came softer, always in maybes: her Friday check if Elena kept tomorrow's full shift, Delroy's promise of something from a patch job in Hialeah unless the man paid him in excuses again, and Tiana's little check, which Marcia hated touching because every dollar the girl earned already had one foot pointed north, toward paperwork, bus fare, and whatever waited past Miami.

Her thumb worried the edge of the partner notebook while live oaks striped the window with shade and then gave her back to harder sun. Yvette's handwriting filled the first pages, dates and names and small tidy figures. Marcia's draw came in July. It had lived in her mind so long it had started feeling less like money than weather, not guaranteed, not controllable, but coming if the season held.

That was only if the season held.

She took out the church envelope. On the front she had written in pencil two weeks ago: `Yvette - July`. It felt too light. She had already robbed it for bus fare, then for cooking oil, then for bus fare again, each theft from herself small enough to forgive while she was doing it. Any sane advice said stop. Pay the bill that had teeth this week. Keep cash where she could touch it. But if she shorted Yvette now, Yvette would not curse her. She would do something worse: understand, slide Marcia's draw back, and let some other woman with a cleaner week take July in her place. July was the only pile of money near enough to matter: rent, a deposit if they had to run, the first school money for Tiana. That option was gone. The hand had to be made whole.

More bodies shouldered in at the next stop with shopping bags and trapped heat. A woman got on carrying a foil pan wrapped in two dish towels. Garlic and baked cheese spread through the aisle, rich enough to hurt. Marcia's stomach answered with a flat little turn. She had eaten two crackers at Celia's with the morning pills because the house always had crackers and because fainting in rich people's bathrooms was bad for employment. Now she wished she had stolen a nectarine, not because of hunger, but because the bowl had been full enough not to notice one missing. Keeping July whole meant somebody else in her house would have to miss instead. The light bill could go one cycle late if she called before the shutoff notice printed. She could buy the cheaper oil and let them taste the difference. She could tell Micah no more cold drinks from the store and tell Delroy that if he wanted cigarettes he could smoke ambition. She could stretch one chicken over two nights and walk one way to church if the transit card ran out. She had done uglier things for less.

Humiliation from Elena's kitchen had already hardened into assignments. Tomorrow she would give Yvette the full amount due before Delroy could start talking around it. Tonight she would strip the borrowed bus money back out of the grocery money and call the light company for one more week. If Elena cut tomorrow short, fine. If Delroy brought in cash this week, part of it would go straight into the next envelope before rice, before phone, before any bill that could be delayed without a man appearing at the door.

She would not tell the children how close the math sat to breaking yet. Fear made young people stupid in different directions. Tiana got colder. Micah got louder. Neither one of them needed to see how much of the summer was balanced on women passing envelopes after church and pretending that trust was stronger than misfortune.

Little River announced itself before the turn: salt, old fryer grease, damp cotton, somebody's cheap coconut lotion giving up under the heat. The cold air had gone thin and useless. The partner notebook stayed in her hand for the rest of the ride. She did not grip it. She held it. She ran her thumb over the cardboard cover until the edge softened with sweat. She slipped the notebook back into her bag as carefully as if it were breakable and gave the rest of the summer its rule. July was not a promise. It was a door, thin wood with a cheap lock, maybe swollen from rain already. Until it failed, every other bill in the house would have to wait its turn outside it.
