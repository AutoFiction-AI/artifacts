# Chapter 8

The side gate clicked under Tiana's hand. Inside, the apartment already felt used up.

The back light over the stucco house had burned out again, so the path to the garage ran through the damp dark between the bins and the fence, past the smell of wet dirt and old cooking oil. She could hear her family before she opened the door. They were not shouting. That was worse. Marcia's voice was low and exact, the way it got when she was still trying to keep the room from seeing her break apart.

Inside, the place looked smaller than it had that morning. The standing fan shoved hot air from one end of the room to the other. Marcia's partner notebook sat open on the table beside three empty envelopes and a church handkerchief gone gray around the edges with makeup and sweat. Her good blouse, the pale blue one she wore when she needed to look like a woman who had never missed a payment in her life, was dark under the arms. Delroy stood by the sink with a mug in his hand like he had been meaning to make tea for twenty minutes and had gotten no further than boiling the water. Micah was on the bed with one sneaker on and one off, phone face-down beside him, trying to look bored so hard it had become its own kind of fear.

Nobody asked Tiana how orientation had gone. Nobody asked where she had been after work. Marcia looked up once, then back down at the notebook as if her eyes could still find a number in there that would turn into mercy.

"Yvette use the money," she said.

She did not say hello. She did not say sit down. Her finger tapped the page where names and weeks marched in careful columns. "Not some of it. All of it. She use it for the mortgage and hospital and whatever else the Lord send through her front door, and now everybody supposed to stand there and clap for hardship."

Delroy shifted the mug from one hand to the other. "Her man catch stroke, Marcia. The woman drown too."

Marcia lifted her face. "Did I say she wasn't drowning? Did I say that?" Her voice was still controlled, but the control had gone thin. "I said she took five thousand eight hundred dollars that was not hers alone. I said she let us dress ourselves and come stand up in that hot hall like idiots."

Micah let out a breath through his teeth. "Five eight?"

"Yes, five eight," Marcia said. "You hearing numbers now? Good. Hear that one."

Tiana set her bag by the door and kept her hand on the strap a second longer than she needed to. Five thousand eight hundred dollars had been the bridge in the house, the invisible one everybody kept walking on because not looking down was the only way to cross. Marcia had talked about it without talking about it for weeks. When the rent got late, when the light bill got slid two days past safe, when Tiana said UF wanted this form or that fee, Marcia's mouth had tightened with the confidence of somebody protecting a date on a calendar. Mid-July was supposed to be the hand, the turn. After that they would breathe.

Now the notebook lay open like a dead thing with neat handwriting.

Tiana felt the old heat from the financial aid office return, the fluorescent buzz, the girl at the desk saying hold and verification and incomplete file as if Tiana had misplaced herself on purpose. She thought of the plastic chairs, the fathers in golf shirts, the mothers signing without reading because they had no reason to be afraid of forms. Delroy had told her months ago he handled the renewal. He had told her to stop worrying him. He had told her papers took time.

She looked at him. He would not meet her eyes.

"Where's my receipt?" she asked.

Marcia blinked. "What receipt?"

Delroy finally looked up, and Tiana saw it then, the small recoil in his face, the animal knowledge of a trap he had built with his own hands. "Ti, not now."

"No. Now." Her voice came out flatter than she expected. "The aid office put a hold on my file because my card expired and the parent paperwork wasn't there. You said you sent the renewal months ago. So where's the receipt?"

Micah's head turned from one parent to the other, quick and hungry, like he hated the scene already and still could not stop listening.

Marcia straightened in her chair. "What renewal?"

Tiana kept looking at Delroy. "My resident card. He told me it was being processed."

Delroy set the mug down. "These people take forever."

"Then show me the receipt."

"I said it takes time."

"I didn't ask what it takes. I asked what office you sent it to."

Silence hit the room so hard the fan blades seemed louder. Outside, somebody in the main house laughed at a television. A dog started up down the alley and then gave up.

Marcia's hand flattened on the notebook page. "Delroy."

He rubbed the heel of his palm over his mouth. "Tiana, I was going to fix it."

Something in her went very still, practical and ugly, a drawer sliding shut. "So there is no receipt."

Delroy tried a smile, a weak piece of old equipment. "Baby girl, don't make it sound like I lose your whole life."

"Did you send it?"

He looked at the sink, the hot plate, the warped cabinet door over the microwave, anywhere but the document folder she had left on the table two days ago after orientation and had not had the nerve to open since. "Rent was short."

Marcia said, "What?"

Delroy's head came up fast, as if he had forgotten she was in the room with him. Tiana watched the confession move through him in stages. First came the wish that maybe he could still talk around it. Then came the useless charm. Then came the shame, stripped bare and irritated by the light.

"Back in May," he said. "Landlord was at the gate every damn morning. You was doing the hand. Light was due. Rent was short. I use the money and I said I'd put it back before anybody needed to know."

Marcia's chair scraped the floor. "You use which money?"

"The envelope for the renewal."

"How much?"

"Enough."

"How much, Delroy."

He swallowed. "All of it."

Marcia stood. The table was so close to the bed that her thigh hit the frame. Micah snatched his foot out of the way. Tiana could smell the sweat dried into Marcia's blouse, the stale powder under it, the fried onion from somebody else's dinner creeping in through the window unit.

"You took the money for her papers," Marcia said slowly, "and you decide this in your head by yourself."

"I decide we needed a roof."

"No. Do not start with me like that. Do not make this into you protecting us from weather." The notebook snapped shut under her hand. "You made household math without me. You made promise with her future and never bring it to my face."

Delroy spread his hands. "What you wanted me do, let the man throw we out in the yard in May? Tell Tiana, sorry about college, but at least your forms is on their way while your mattress sit under the mango tree?"

"I wanted you to tell me."

"And with what money you would answer me?"

The question landed filthy in the room. Micah looked at the floor. Tiana felt the insult the way she felt the bus seat when it hit a pothole, not clean shock but impact traveling upward through everything under her.

Marcia laughed once, short and disbelieving. "So that's it. You borrow my hand before it turn. You spend her paper money before the emergency come in full. You make whole plans with money I didn't even know was already gone, then you stand up in here and ask me what money I would answer with."

Delroy's shoulders lifted, dropped. "I said I was going to put it back."

"With what?"

"Work."

"Which work?"

He opened his mouth and closed it again.

Tiana heard herself say, "So you lied because you had no plan."

"I had a plan."

"No, Daddy. You had a wish."

He flinched harder at that than at Marcia's voice. For a second she hated that she noticed. She hated that some part of her still knew the way his hurt folded inward.

Micah sat up. "So what now? Like, what actually now?"

Nobody answered him. Marcia moved one of the empty envelopes aside as if she could not bear to look at it anymore. Tiana stared at Delroy's hands. They were thick-fingered and clean-nailed, with a crescent of white paint still trapped near one knuckle from some small job that had not turned into enough money to matter. Those were the hands that had held her bike upright in the apartment lot, the hands that had tucked a twenty into her book bag junior year when she said she did not need lunch money and both of them knew she did. Those same hands had opened her envelope and spent her paperwork into rent.

"The aid office said they can't finalize anything without current documents," she said. "Not housing. Not the scholarship package. Nothing. I stood there looking stupid because I thought the problem was timing."

Delroy's voice went soft, almost clean. He did that sometimes when he was nearest begging. "Ti, listen to me. You can defer a semester if you have to. Plenty people do that. School will still be there."

She thought of UF and the dorm room she had pictured with its cheap desk and cinderblock wall, and of the ugly little freedom of being somewhere nobody remembered her at twelve or thirteen or last week. He said it like a missed bus you could catch tomorrow.

"Don't tell me school will still be there." She had to stop and start again because her voice had gone sharp enough to cut the words in half. "Don't say that to me like I'm being dramatic about a piece of paper. That piece of paper is the difference between me existing where I need to exist and me staying right here while everybody says next time."

Marcia turned to her, and the rage in her face made room for something worse, a mother's recognition of a harm she had not even known to defend against. "He told you it was sent?"

"Yes."

"For how long?"

"Since spring."

Marcia shut her eyes. When she opened them again, the church handkerchief was in her fist. "You make me stand up in front all them women today with my face washed and my pocket empty, and all this time you had another hole cut in the boat."

Delroy took a step toward her. "Marcia, don't."

"Do not come touch me."

He stopped.

"You think this is just the money?" she said. Her voice had started to rise now, finally losing its manners. "Money is bad enough. Money is blood in this house. But you decide I don't get to know what danger we're in. You decide I must budget blind. Every time there is shame, you push me outside it and call that protection."

"I was trying to hold it till July."

"July come and look what July bring."

Micah muttered, "Damn," under his breath.

Marcia rounded on him. "And you. Wipe that look off your face. This is not free show."

He lifted both hands. "I didn't say nothing."

"You never have to say much. Your face run ahead of you."

Tiana stepped around the chair and took her document folder off the table before Marcia's hand or Delroy's hand or the whole house came down on it by accident. The cardboard was soft at the corners from being carried everywhere. Her name, written in black marker months ago, looked childish now.

"What is the plan?" she asked.

Delroy seized on the question. "Tomorrow I go out early. I can pick up something. I know a man in Hialeah got drywall work coming. I can call around."

"That's not a plan," Marcia said. "That's morning talking."

"What you want from me tonight then? Blood?"

"No. I want the truth in full sentences."

He stared at her. For one ugly second Tiana thought there might be more, another envelope, another hidden hole, but he only said, "Rent was due. I panic. I thought the hand was solid. I thought if I keep the room over our head and put the money back before her card come due, nobody would feel the gap. I was wrong."

There it was, smaller than the lie had been. It was neither noble nor monstrous, just a man kneeling in the cheap church of his own bad judgment.

It did not help.

Marcia pressed the handkerchief to her mouth. Tiana knew that gesture. It was the one before crying or vomiting, whichever came first. Instead Marcia lowered the cloth and said, very clear, "I can't talk to you right now without saying things that'll crack this house wider."

Delroy nodded once, as if he had been offered mercy. Tiana despised him for taking it that way.

"I can fix it," he said, but weaker.

"You can't even name it," Tiana said.

She was at the door before anybody told her not to go. Maybe they knew better. Maybe the room had already used up all the authority it had for one night. The air outside hit her wet and warm. Behind her, Marcia said Delroy's name again, the whole name this time, flat with warning, and Micah said, "Mummy," in a voice he usually saved for ambulances.

Tiana kept walking until the side gate clicked shut behind her and the house stopped being a room and turned back into weather.

***

She made it to Biscayne before she decided not to turn around.

The bus shelter sat in front of a shuttered insurance office with bars over the glass and a plastic palm tree drooping in the dark lobby. Mosquitoes worried her ankles. The bench was tacky with old heat. She could still hear Marcia's voice in her ear, not the words anymore but the force of them, that high hard place anger reached when it had to drag humiliation behind it.

Tiana took out her phone. Her thumb hovered over Malik's name longer than pride liked. Then she typed, `You awake?`

He answered before she could regret it.

`Just got off. You good?`

She stared at the question. If she said no, it would become a story. If she said yes, it would become a lie. She went with neither.

`Need to get out for a while.`

The dots came, disappeared, and came back.

`Stay where you're at.`

The northbound bus hissed up first, then pulled away. The southbound one took seventeen more minutes. Tiana counted them by the changing color of the beer sign in the window of the store across the street as it went red, then dark, then red again. A man with two mops slung across one shoulder smoked beside the pole and never once looked at her. A girl in scrubs fell asleep standing up and woke furious at herself every time her chin dropped.

When Malik finally stepped down from the bus, he had his work shoes looped by the laces over one shoulder and a deli cup tucked under his arm. He wore black kitchen pants and a white T-shirt with a damp collar. His left thumbnail was split down the middle, and he worried it with his teeth while he searched the stop for her, a habit she had teased him about once and which he had not even tried to defend. When he saw her, he stopped chewing the nail and came over without rushing.

"You eat?" he asked.

She almost laughed. "Hi to you too."

"That wasn't a joke."

He held out the deli cup. It held mango, cut in thick rough pieces and dusted with salt and chili. The lid had fogged from the heat. Malik always wanted something sharp to eat after work, something that could push the grease and bleach taste off his tongue. It was such a private, unnecessary preference that it made her want to sit down on the sidewalk and cry.

"No," she said.

"Then eat."

She took one piece because arguing about fruit would have been ridiculous. The salt hit her split lower lip and made her realize how hard she had been biting it.

The bus came. They got on through the front and moved to the back where the air-conditioning worked too well and the windows rattled in their frames. Tiana sat by the glass. Malik dropped into the seat beside her and set the deli cup between them.

He did not ask what happened right away. He let the bus pull out into traffic. Little River slid by in pieces: the dark bulk of a tire shop, a botanica window crowded with white candles, men under a carport arguing over dominoes so hard their hands looked violent even in silhouette. The city at night always seemed to Tiana like it was telling the truth and lying at the same time. Every storefront said closed. Every person outside said the night was still collecting.

Malik leaned his forearms on his knees. "You trying to be somewhere specific?"

"No."

"You trying to not be somewhere specific?"

She turned her face toward the window. "You always do that?"

"Do what."

"Ask the one question that makes lying look stupid."

"Only when somebody asks me to come get them and then starts acting like they don't know why."

His tone landed flat and edged, more anchor than accusation, a refusal to let her disappear into fog. Under pressure, Malik's voice cleaned itself down to the blade.

"My father lied to me," she said.

"About what."

"Paperwork. Money. Same thing here." She scraped chili salt off her thumb with her nail. "He told me he handled my card renewal. He didn't. He spent the money on rent and kept talking like the papers were just moving slow."

Malik nodded once. "And you found out tonight."

"My mother found out too."

"That sounds bad."

She laughed then, once, ugly and brief. "Thank you. Very clinical."

"You don't need pretty words."

The bus lurched around a turn. Her shoulder knocked his. He did not move away. A bright ad panel over the opposite seats promised accelerated training for medical billing and coding, smiling women in scrub tops with the kind of confidence that came from never having had a document hold placed on your whole life.

"I just needed out," she said.

"Out of the apartment?"

"Out of my own head for ten minutes."

He looked at her now. "That all this is?"

There it was, the part of him that kept him from becoming a mattress with opinions. Tiana felt irritation rise because he had every right to ask and she had not wanted him to.

"I didn't say that."

"No, you didn't say much at all."

She sat back against the seat. The cold from the vent touched the sweat between her shoulder blades and made her shiver. "What do you want me to say?"

"Say it straight. You called me because you wanted me, or because I was the first door that wasn't yours?"

The deli cup rolled a little on the vinyl seat between them. He steadied it with two fingers and waited.

Tiana had spent most of her life getting rewarded for composure. Teachers liked it. Clerks liked it. Even white girls with easy mothers liked it, because a careful Black girl gave them something flattering to say at banquets. Composure did not help here. Malik would hear it for what it was, another polished object held up between them so he did not have to touch anything warm.

"Both," she said.

He breathed out through his nose. "Alright."

"You asked."

"I did."

"And I came anyway." Her voice dropped. "That's got to count for some honesty."

He rested his head back against the seat and watched the reflection of the bus lights slide over the window. "It counts. I'm just not trying to be the place you crash because home gets loud and then tomorrow we act like none of this happened."

She looked at his throat, the place where sweat had dried in a pale line. She thought of the aid office again, of the click of computer keys deciding if she existed in the right category. She thought of her father saying school will still be there. Then she thought of Malik at orientation, the way he had stood outside with her afterward and not told her to calm down, not once, just handed her his water bottle and waited until she finished cursing out the federal government in a parking lot full of nice cars.

"I've been thinking about you anyway," she said.

He turned.

She hated how young the sentence made her feel, how close it was to a confession she would have mocked in somebody else. So she made it uglier, truer. "Not in some clean way either. I keep thinking about your mouth when I'm supposed to be reading instructions. I keep thinking if I could get out of my house and into a room with you, I might breathe different."

The bus noise filled the silence after it. Metal shaking. Someone coughing three seats up. The driver muttering at a car that cut too close.

Malik's eyes stayed on her face. "You hear how that sounds."

"Yes."

"Like you want me to fix your lungs."

"I know."

"I can't do that."

"I know."

She was tired of saying it, tired of knowing things that did not help. She looked down at her hands. Mango salt had dried in the lines of her palm.

"I still want you," she said. "That part isn't fake."

His expression changed. The flatness in it gave way to something warmer and more dangerous. "Alright," he said again. "That part I can work with."

They rode another ten minutes without much talking. At a red light, a man in a reflective vest ran across six lanes carrying a Styrofoam container like it held his real name inside. The bus lights made everybody look already tired from tomorrow. Malik took one piece of mango and chewed slowly.

"You can come over," he said at last. "Just don't come over to blank out in my room. If an hour's all you got, say so. If you stay, stay for real."

She nodded.

"And if you want me to take you home after, I'll take you home. I'm not putting a contract on this."

That almost made her smile. "Thank you for your flexible terms and conditions."

"See. Now you're irritating again. Better."

He bumped her knee with his. The bus kept moving south, then west, carrying them through blocks she only half knew, where the storefront churches gave way to dark duplexes and chain-link fences with bicycles tipped against them. She rested her head against the window and let the city jolt through the glass into her skull until the apartment behind her loosened its grip by degrees, not anything so generous as mercy, just distance.

***

Malik rented a room at the back of a low cinderblock fourplex where the paint had given up around the window frames and the hallway always smelled faintly of Pine-Sol and old frying oil. The woman in the front unit kept two hibiscus plants in buckets by the stairs and had tied grocery bags around them against the sun. At this hour the bags whispered in the dark.

Inside his room, the window unit clattered like a drawer full of silverware. A twin bed took up most of one wall. On the floor beside it sat a pair of steel-toe boots, a foam roller split at one end with duct tape, and a stack of folded black work shirts strapped with a rubber band. A legal pad on the milk crate he used as a nightstand held columns of numbers, not poetry, not sketches, just hours, rent, gas, and a list titled `Things my right shoulder is complaining about`, which made her bite the inside of her cheek to keep from smiling. There was a cheap speaker on the sill, a half-empty jar of peanut butter, and a photograph tucked into the frame of the mirror of Malik at about twelve in a soccer uniform too big through the chest, already looking skeptical of the camera.

"Nice place," she said.

"Don't lie in my room. It will start something."

He shut the door and set the lock. The sound tightened the air. The room drew in around them, narrower now, every wall one step closer.

He handed her a bottle of water from the little fridge under the window. She drank half of it too fast. The room was cool enough to raise gooseflesh on her arms after the street, but the cool carried the smell of male detergent, clean cotton, and the ghost of whatever he had cooked around all evening. Onion. Oil. Lemon cleaner.

"You want to talk first?" he asked.

She put the bottle down on the crate. "Not really."

"You want me to shut up then?"

"Maybe for a minute."

He nodded, and for a minute they stood there in the thin mechanical cold with the window unit rattling and the neighbor's television leaking game-show applause through the wall. Tiana should have felt foolish. Instead she felt overfull. The room was small enough that all the wanting in it could not spread out and pretend to be atmosphere.

Malik stepped closer. He touched her wrist first, almost formal, giving her time to flinch if she wanted. She did not. His thumb slid once over the inside of her wrist where the pulse beat under the skin, and then he put his hand at the back of her neck and kissed her.

They had kissed before, but this was the first one with a locked door behind it and nowhere respectable for the feeling to go. Tiana grabbed the front of his shirt and kissed him harder than she meant to. The taste of mango and salt and a long shift came off his mouth. He made a low sound, surprised into honesty, and she felt a mean little spark of satisfaction. She wanted to affect something. She wanted proof.

He pulled back first. "You sure?"

"Don't do that in a voice like a guidance counselor."

"I'm serious."

She looked at him. "Yes."

"Yes because you want this, or yes because your house is on fire and I'm close."

The question should have killed the mood. Instead it stripped something off it that needed stripping.

"Both were true on the bus," she said. "Right now I want you."

He searched her face a second longer. Then he nodded, once, and kissed her again.

They got out of their clothes gracelessly in a room built too small for elegance. Her sandal caught in the hem of her dress and nearly pitched her sideways into the milk crate. He laughed and caught her by the waist before the water bottle went over.

"Damn," he said. "Slow down, college girl."

"Don't call me that right now."

He heard the edge in it and stopped smiling. "Alright."

She took the dress off herself and let it land over the chair with his work shoes. Standing there in her bra and underwear under his blunt little lamp, she expected shame to arrive with a sermon. Exposure came instead, hot and total. Malik looked at her like he had no reason to fake indifference. That steadiness did something to her more dangerous than compliments.

He put both hands on her waist. His palms were rough, warmer than the room. "You beautiful," he said, plain as report.

She nearly ruined it by answering with a joke. Instead she kissed the corner of his mouth and then his throat. His body felt worked on, not sculpted for anybody's gaze but used daily, shoulders tight from labor, stomach flat, a small old scar under his ribs she found with her thumb before she meant to. He sucked in a breath.

"What happened there?"

"Bike. Twelve."

"You were clumsy?"

"You ask too many questions in intimate settings."

"That's rich coming from you."

He laughed against her neck. The sound eased something. She reached behind herself, unhooked her bra, and tossed it toward the chair. He went still for half a beat, just enough for her to see the effect, then bent his head and took one nipple in his mouth. The sensation hit her so suddenly she had to grab his shoulder. It cracked the whole day open in a register anger had never reached.

The bed protested when he sat them down on it. The sheet was cool from the window unit and smelled like detergent dried too long indoors. Tiana pushed him back and climbed over him before she could start thinking about the fact that she was eighteen and in a back room with a man she had wanted half out of curiosity and half out of terror. His hands slid up her thighs, under the fabric. When he found how wet she already was, he looked at her with an expression so sharp it almost embarrassed her into lying.

"Don't," she said.

"Don't what."

"Don't look proud of yourself."

"I look accurate."

She slapped his shoulder lightly. He caught her wrist, kissed the inside of it, then moved his hand between her legs again, slower now, giving her somewhere to put the heat besides panic. She dropped her forehead against his and let the room narrow to touch: the rasp of his thumb, the stitched edge of his pillowcase against her knee, the machine breath of the air-conditioner. When she came, the surprise was in its shape. Relief was mental. This was bodily, stupid, immediate. Her mouth opened on his name before she had decided to say it.

After, she rolled off him and laughed once into the crook of her elbow because the sound coming out of herself had startled her. Malik lay there grinning at the ceiling, breathing hard.

"You still with me?" he asked.

"Unfortunately."

"Good."

He sat up, opened the milk crate drawer he had rigged under the bed frame, and pulled out a condom. He checked the wrapper by habit, date first, then tore it with his teeth because his hands were shaky now. Something about that, the ordinary caution of it, the refusal of fantasy, made Tiana want him more. The moment kept its hard plainness: a thin square of latex in a hot little room where rent was due somewhere for everybody.

When he eased back over her, he kissed her first, slow enough to ask again without speaking. She wrapped a leg around his hip and answered that way. He pushed into her carefully. The first stretch hurt, then changed. She shut her eyes and then opened them again because she did not want this to turn into some soft blur she could later pretend had happened to another girl.

"Look at me," he said quietly.

She did.

The second thrust went easier. By the fourth she was breathing against his shoulder in broken little sounds she would have denied making if anybody else told her about them. Malik kept his face close to hers, jaw tight with concentration, and it was that concentration that undid her, the fact that he was not performing ease. He was there with her, in the work of it.

The headboard tapped the wall. He stopped, glanced toward the neighbor's television, then shifted and put one hand under her thigh to change the angle. The new angle made her bite him, not hard, just enough to leave a white half-moon that colored back in after.

"Shit," he said, and laughed under it.

"Sorry."

"No, you're not."

She wasn't. She pulled him back down. The room gathered heat despite the air-conditioner. Sweat ran from his temple into her hairline. Her calf cramped and uncramped. He kissed her mouth when she got too loud, not to silence her completely, just to take some of the sound and make it theirs instead of the building's. When she came again, it felt almost angry, an aftershock following the day's first disaster. Malik followed with his forehead pressed into the pillow beside her ear, his whole body going rigid for a second before it loosened all at once.

For a while neither of them said anything. The window unit rattled. Somewhere down the hall a toilet flushed. A car alarm started three blocks away and then cut off like somebody had strangled it.

Malik rolled off carefully and tied the condom, practical even now. Tiana watched him, the clean unromantic motions, and liked him better for them. He tossed it in the little trash can lined with a grocery bag and came back to the bed. When he lay down again, they did not touch right away.

That was when the fear arrived.

The fear rose from what she had just made true. His room still smelled like sweat and detergent and the lemon cleaner somebody used on the hallway tile. Her dress was on his chair. Her underwear hung off the bedpost by one strap. The world kept the same face, and something under it had shifted.

"You alright?" he asked.

"I don't know yet."

"Fair."

He turned his head on the pillow to look at her. "You about to disappear on me?"

The question should have irritated her. Instead it hit the sore place clean. She let out a breath and stared up at the water stain near the corner of his ceiling.

"I keep wanting to make things mean less than they do," she said. "It feels safer."

"Yeah."

"So maybe."

"Don't hit me with maybe." His tone stayed calm. "If this is one night because your house blew up and you needed somewhere else to put your body, say it ugly and be done. Don't go quiet and leave me looking stupid."

She turned onto her side to face him. He came to her plain: a man in a hot room asking not to be reduced.

"I came here because I wanted you too," she said.

"Say it like that."

"I know." She touched the scar under his ribs again, lightly. "I wanted you before tonight. Tonight just made me stop acting respectable about it."

His mouth shifted. "There it is."

"It's also humiliating."

"Maybe. But I can work with humiliating."

She laughed then, actually laughed, and the sound turned soft by itself. He moved closer and put his hand on her hip, not urgent now, just there. The touch settled her more than it should have. That frightened her too.

"You can stay till morning," he said. "I gotta be up by ten, but that's not morning."

"I should probably go."

"Probably isn't a decision either."

She looked at the room again. The list on the legal pad. The duct-taped roller. The soccer photograph. Malik had a life here that did not arrange itself around waiting for her crisis to come knocking. The fact of that made the bed feel safer than if he had belonged to nobody.

"I'll stay a little," she said.

"Alright."

He reached toward the crate, set a timer on his phone without looking at it, and slid back under the sheet. She did not ask what the timer was for until it went off nearly an hour later and he said, half asleep, "Need to check if the parking lot tow people are out. They got my neighbor twice this month."

Only Malik would interrupt a near-tender night to look out the blinds for a tow truck and curse softly about predatory fees. She watched him do it in his boxers, one hand rubbing sleep off his face, and felt the attachment deepen in a way that had nothing to do with sex and everything to do with seeing the shape of another person's practical worry.

When he came back to bed, she made room for him without thinking. That was worse than anything.

***

At five-thirty the room had gone from night to a colorless gray that made every cheap object look more honest.

Tiana dressed quietly. Her body felt both heavier and more available to her, as if somebody had taken a locked window and forced it open in the middle of the house. Between her thighs there was a dull ache that was less pain than proof. She washed up in Malik's narrow bathroom with its cracked mirror and a bar of soap balanced on a jar lid. When she came back, he was sitting on the bed pulling on his socks.

"I can walk you to the stop," he said.

"You don't have to."

"I know."

He did anyway. Outside, the hibiscus bags clicked in the breeze. The sky over the low roofs had started to bleach, but the heat was already gathering in the concrete. Workers were awake all over the block. A woman in scrubs was scraping frost off the windshield of a car that would be hot in thirty minutes. Somebody's radio played gospel too low to make out the words.

At the bus stop, Malik handed her the last of the mango from the deli cup and a bottle of water he had shoved into his backpack. "Text me when you get in."

"I might be walking into a crime scene."

"Text me if there are bodies too."

She almost told him not to make this sweeter than it was. She almost told him he had no claim on her morning. Both impulses were defensive and both were tired. Instead she said, "Okay."

The bus came with its windows filmed in dawn grime. She climbed on and found a seat halfway back. Malik stood on the sidewalk until the bus pulled away, one hand lifting once and dropping again, more acknowledgement than wave.

As the blocks unspooled toward Little River, guilt moved back into her a piece at a time. She refused the easy good-girl version, the one where sleeping with Malik turned instantly into regret. The guilt came from leaving Marcia in that room with Delroy's confession still sour in the air and Micah trying to joke the walls wider. It came from the fact that while Marcia sat up over a legal pad, scratching due dates into a future that would not stretch, Tiana had been on somebody else's sheets learning the breath she made when pleasure got ahead of caution.

She rested her forehead against the glass. The bus smelled like cold metal, old perfume, and someone's breakfast empanada. Men with lunch coolers got on in twos. A teenage girl in nursing-school scrubs reviewed flashcards under her breath. Biscayne at dawn looked scrubbed and exposed, the pawn shops and churches and closed barber shops all waiting for their signs to mean something again. The city never really slept. It just shifted from one set of laboring bodies to another.

Her phone vibrated once.

`You good?`

She typed `On the way` and sent it before she could turn the answer into something cleaner.

When she reached the side gate, the yard looked indecently ordinary. A pair of damp towels hung over the line behind the main house. Somebody had put out trash. The broken back light was still broken. Tiana stood there a moment with her hand on the latch, the whole night pressed into her skin under yesterday's dress.

She lifted the gate with both hands and eased it shut behind her until the latch answered softly.

Inside, the apartment smelled like coffee boiled too long, sweat gone stale, and the first heat coming back. Marcia sat at the table in one of her house dresses with a scarf tied back from her face. The partner notebook was gone. In its place sat a legal pad covered in due dates and crossed-out numbers. Micah was asleep under the fan with his phone loose in his hand and one foot kicked clear of the sheet. Delroy lay on the bed against the wall under the sheet, eyes shut with the worked stillness of somebody pretending sleep could count as absence. His boots were on the floor beside him. He had not gone anywhere.

Marcia looked up. Her gaze moved over Tiana's face, her hair, the dress she had worn yesterday and again this morning. Tired recognition settled there, along with a question she did not spend.

Nothing had been solved. The card was still expired. UF was still on hold. Rent would still come due whether people had lied or fucked or prayed or not. The house had only taken on one more secret, and this one was still on her skin.

Tiana set her bag down without a word.
