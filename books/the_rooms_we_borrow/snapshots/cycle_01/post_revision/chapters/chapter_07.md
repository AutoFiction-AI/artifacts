# Chapter 7

Earlier that Saturday, while Tiana was still out, Marcia had spent the money so many times in her head it already felt warm from handling. First and security on a place with a real front door, not a converted garage somebody else could decide to call illegal whenever it suited them. The college money she had not named to Tiana because hope got loud in that apartment, and loud hope turned into appetite. She would clear the light bill and the little late fees that bred in the dark when you let them. The numbers changed depending on which office wanted blood first, but the total never moved: five thousand eight hundred dollars, enough to turn July from a cliff into chores.

Inside her handbag she carried three white envelopes with her block printing in the corners: RENT, SCHOOL, LIGHT. She had folded them flat before leaving, the same way she folded medication schedules for Celia Alvarez, crisp enough that even bad news had to stand up straight. Writing the labels ahead of time felt half like discipline and half like temptation. She had done it anyway. A woman could not wait on miracle and then pretend she had no use for it once it came.

People with checking accounts liked to shelve partner among quaint old-country habits, beside rum cake and airport barrels. Marcia never corrected them. Let them keep their little smile. A hand was not folklore. A hand was what people built when the bank charged thirty-five dollars because a paycheck landed one day wrong, when credit asked for paper the poor could not spare, when cash disappeared too easily inside a marriage or a family or your own hungry head. Twenty-nine names, a hundred dollars every week, and Marcia had carried two numbers because one number was grocery hope. Two numbers could move a family.

Her mother had bought a refrigerator in Spanish Town on partner money, paid the rest of the cycle under a zinc roof that leaked over the sink, and never once called it luck. In Miami, Marcia had used a Christmas hand years ago to buy Tiana's crib without putting one more thing on a store card. Yvette had understood what it meant when crib money and grocery money had to come out of the same sore pocket. Yvette kept the book in blue ink, no pencil, no uncertainty, and called late people by Wednesday before their excuse had time to grow pretty. She ironed even the house dresses she wore to collect money, and one of her eyebrows always sat a little higher than the other; she did not quite believe anybody who said, Next week, sis, for sure. That was the face Marcia had trusted with two numbers.

The fellowship hall behind New Testament Revival had one wall unit that spat cold in short mean bursts and then gave up. The room smelled of Pine-Sol, old paper, and the curry goat somebody had served after Bible study the night before. Not all twenty-nine women were there, but enough had come to make the air thick. Miss Pauline sat with her airport parking visor clipped into church hair, still dressed for a Miami sun that was nowhere near this cinderblock room. Her ankles ballooned above white support hose, and she kept spearmints in a little cough-drop tin she snapped shut like a judge. Nadine had come straight from a night shift in bee-print scrub pants and white Crocs, a crescent dent still pressed into the bridge of her nose from a face mask. Claudette, who hemmed hotel uniforms for cash, kept passing her thumb over a loose stitch at her sleeve, worrying it harder each time somebody checked the door.

Marcia chose a folding chair near the table where Yvette usually spread the notebook, calculator, and stack of rubber-banded envelopes. The table was bare except for a Styrofoam cup sweated half empty and a church bulletin advertising youth choir rehearsal. She checked the clock over the serving hatch. It was ten after two.

"Traffic must've catch her," Nadine said, though her voice already carried a doubt she had not earned yet.

Miss Pauline made a small sound through her nose. "Yvette late is like funeral chicken left over. It happens, but it means something went wrong."

Nobody laughed. Even Claudette, who laughed at almost anything with a shape, only rubbed harder at the loose stitch.

Marcia took out her own notebook, the small black one she kept in the zipped side pocket of her bag. Every page carried dates, amounts, and Yvette's initials beside each payment. She had recorded each hundred the minute she got home, sometimes before she changed out of work clothes, while the apartment still smelled of bleach and frying oil and whatever bass line the neighbors had decided belonged to everybody. There were weeks that second hundred had come from ugly places: utility money, bus money, one of Tiana's paychecks shifted quietly from one future into another. The notebook did not blush. It only held the figures.

At quarter past, Nadine called Yvette and got voicemail. Claudette called next and got the same dead answer. Pauline called and left no message at all, just listened until the beep and hung up with a thumb hard enough to make the screen pop.

"Hospital, maybe," Claudette said. "Franklin still doing therapy, no?"

"Then you send word," Pauline said. She shook out one spearmint and bit it in half. "If money is waiting in a room, you send word before the room goes bad."

Marcia said nothing. She had seen Franklin once after the stroke, in church, one side of his face gone slack and his left hand resting in his lap like it belonged to somebody he had not yet met. Yvette had sat beside him in a yellow blouse, jaw set, answering for both of them when people asked how he was doing. Marcia had gone home that day with pity for the woman and relief that pity was not yet necessary in her own kitchen. The relief came back now to shame her.

At two twenty-three, Pauline's phone pinged. The sound snapped through the room so sharply that every chair leg stopped moving.

"From her?" Nadine asked.

Pauline looked at the screen and went still. "Voice note."

The women shifted in, chairs scraping, bodies drawing close with the intimate greed of people who knew bad news always arrived with details and wanted the details first. Pauline hit play and held the phone above the table.

Yvette's voice came out small but clear. There was hospital noise behind it, the rattle of a cart and a television turned too low to understand.

"Pauline, if everybody's there, play this loud. I can't take twenty-nine calls and say the same thing twenty-nine times. I took money from the hand. I did. Franklin's stroke hit, the mortgage was three months down, and I kept telling myself I'd hold from it for one week and put it back before Marcia's draw. Then rehab wanted cash and the bank pinned another notice on the door and I used more. I know what that is. I know what it makes me. I'm not asking anybody to call me good. I'm saying I wasn't going to let them take my house while my husband couldn't lift his own left hand. I will pay every cent back. I don't know how fast. Please don't come by the house and frighten the girls."

The room held for one stunned beat after the message ended. Then it broke all at once.

"Jesus Christ," Nadine said.

Claudette covered her mouth with both hands. "The girls are there by themselves?"

Miss Pauline slapped the phone down on the table so hard the Styrofoam cup jumped. "A stroke doesn't baptize a thief."

Nobody corrected the language. Nobody corrected anything.

Nadine started talking at the same time Claudette did. One wanted the police. One wanted the pastor. Pauline wanted Yvette's sister. A woman by the door, Sonia from the usher board, said she had seen foreclosure take her cousin's house in Lauderdale and it was enough to make any person mad. Pauline wheeled on her fast enough that Sonia stepped back into the hymn-number board.

"Mad at who?" Pauline demanded. "The bank? Good. Be mad at the bank. Why am I supposed to pay for Wells Fargo in the name of Christian sympathy?"

"Because the man had a stroke," Claudette said, and her fingers had moved from the loose stitch to the cheap silver chain at her throat. "Because she must've been losing her mind."

"Everybody in this room joins partner because something is already wrong," Marcia said.

The sentence came out level. It surprised her a little. She had expected fury to arrive hot. Instead it came the way it did when she read dosage instructions to a patient who thought she could charm her way around medicine: level, stripped of comfort, impossible to sweet-talk.

The room quieted just enough for her to keep going.

"Nobody brings easy money into a hand. Nobody here had spare cash making them foolish. That was school money, rent money, childcare money. If emergency gave you license to raid the box, partner wouldn't exist."

Nadine nodded hard. "Exactly."

But Claudette's face crumpled anyway. "Marcia, I know. I know. I'm only saying I can see the table where she did it. That's all I'm saying."

Marcia could see it too, and that was part of what made her want to break something. She could see Yvette's glass-top dining table under the slow fan, Franklin's pill organizer open beside the fruit bowl, one bank letter after another with language sharpened by people who never had to choose between public dishonor and private foreclosure. Cash bands would have sat on the table, not because Yvette was rich enough to stack money but because cash looked different once it stopped being theory and turned into one more month before the sheriff. Marcia could picture Yvette's hand hovering over the pile, telling herself borrow first, replace later, because every poor person she knew had once or twice survived by renaming theft temporary.

Understanding the decision did not soften it. It only made it uglier.

Pauline hit redial and got sent straight to voicemail. She cursed, low and inventive, then jabbed a finger at the blank screen, aiming the gesture straight at Yvette. "You don't send me apology in MP3 format. You answer."

Sonia had already started crying. She was near the end of the hand and had been planning to use her turn for school uniforms in August. Nadine stood over her with one hand on her shoulder, rubbing circles so fast they looked impatient. Someone near the back asked whether any of the money was left. Nobody knew. Somebody else said maybe Yvette had only borrowed from later turns, not today's. Pauline barked out, "Did you not hear the woman say before Marcia's draw?"

Heads turned, though not all at once. Worse than that, they turned one by one, with that careful human slowness people used when they had just remembered where the sharpest pain in the room lived.

Marcia had known they all understood it was her turn. She had not understood how different knowledge felt from attention. The pity landed first on her blouse, then on her face, then in the softening of bodies around her. Claudette pulled a chair closer. Nadine touched her forearm. Even Pauline's anger tried, for a second, to change shape.

"Marcia," Claudette said quietly. "Lord."

Marcia moved her arm off the table before Nadine's hand could settle there. "Don't."

Nobody mistook her meaning.

She was not proud enough to hate sympathy in general. She had taken casseroles, hand-me-down uniforms, advice she did not ask for. She knew the uses of community. But pity in public was a different thing. Pity in public asked a woman to stand still while other people took note of just how badly she had needed something. It made need communal before relief had any chance to become private.

Nadine swallowed. "All right."

"Play it again," Pauline said.

They did. This time the room listened for proof instead of surprise, and each woman leaned in on a different wound in the message: the mortgage, the stroke, Marcia's draw, the promise to pay every cent back, the girls at the house. Nobody heard mercy in the same place.

By the time the voice note ended a second time, the fracture had settled cleanly through the room. One side wanted to march to Yvette's house and pull every curtain open until shame climbed out. The other side wanted to call the pastor first, or Yvette's sister, or the hospital social worker, hoping a better chain of adults could reorganize catastrophe into something reputable. Nadine wanted the police until Pauline reminded her there was no bank statement, no formal contract, only notebooks and cash and witnesses who would all sound ridiculous the minute they sat under fluorescent law. That shut Nadine up for almost thirty seconds.

"So what then?" she said. "We just let her eat us?"

"Nobody said that," Pauline snapped.

"What did you say then?"

"I said police not bringing the money back."

"Neither is church."

"Church can ruin her reputation."

"Reputation doesn't pay FPL."

The argument skidded around the room, hot and useless. Marcia heard only pieces. She kept staring at the table where Yvette's ledger should have been. The church bulletin had soaked up the sweat from the Styrofoam cup until the ink bled through the youth-choir photo. She thought of the three envelopes in her bag, their neat labels waiting for content, and wanted to laugh so badly the feeling nearly made her choke.

Claudette crouched beside her chair. "You all right?"

The question was kind. That made it worse.

"No," Marcia said. "But I'm sitting here."

Claudette gave a helpless little nod. She smelled faintly of camphor and fabric softener, the smell of clothes that had been mended instead of replaced. "If she pays it back piece by piece..."

"Piece by piece is not a draw," Marcia said.

That shut Claudette's mouth for a while.

Pauline started taking names of who had paid what and when, copying from notebooks onto the back of old church programs because outrage loved administration when administration was all it had left. Marcia opened her own notebook only because it gave her hands somewhere to go. She read the dates she already knew: March 8, March 15, March 22. Those were whole weeks of sweat, bus fare counted twice, one pair of shoes reheeled instead of replaced, one light bill delayed, one Saturday lunch skipped at work because hunger was cheaper than pride. The numbers sat there in blue ink, loyal and useless.

Nadine leaned over the table. "You had two shares, right?"

Marcia looked up.

Nadine's face changed the minute she heard how the question sounded. Too late.

"I mean," Nadine said quickly, "I remember Yvette saying you carried two numbers, that's all."

"Yes," Marcia said.

Nadine flinched. Across the room Pauline closed her eyes for half a second. Even Sonia stopped crying long enough to stare.

There it was again, her need passing from rumor into inventory.

Five thousand eight hundred dollars sat in her head like a verdict. It was enough to relocate, enough to put Tiana's future back on rails, enough to buy Delroy time he had not earned. It was enough that the loss itself now made her look foolish, greedy, naive, wrapped in all the little words people offered women after fraud, words that made trust sound like appetite and loss like greed.

She stood before anybody could try kindness a second time.

"Call whoever you want," she said. "Pastor, sister, hospital, all of them. Tell me if money appears. Until then, I need to go home."

"Marcia," Pauline said, and for once she sounded older than sharp. "Let somebody drop you."

"I know the bus route."

"That isn't what I mean."

Marcia pulled her bag closed. "I know."

She left while the room was still deciding whether pity or fury would run the rest of the afternoon. In the hallway the church's back door stuck at the frame and made her shoulder work for freedom. Outside, the heat slapped harder than it had any right to after air-conditioning that weak. Somebody's child was practicing scales in the sanctuary, the keyboard notes slipping through cinderblock one embarrassed at a time.

At the bus stop she took the three envelopes out and looked at them. RENT. SCHOOL. LIGHT. Her own handwriting, calm as a nurse. She held them until the sweat from her fingers softened the paper corners, then slid them back into her bag before anybody waiting beside her could read them and know one more thing.

The ride home was full. A man with paint on his jeans slept with his mouth open. Two girls in crop tops argued about a wig install in voices bright enough to cut the stale air. At every stop Marcia expected the shape of the afternoon to change, for the phone to ring, for Pauline to say they had found some of the money, for Yvette to call sobbing, for God to stop clowning and put something countable back in her hands. Nothing happened. The purse on her lap stayed light.

By the time she pushed through the side gate to the stucco house in Little River, the sun had dropped low enough that the landlord's place stretched across the yard in shadow. The gravel popped under her shoes. Somebody up front was frying fish. Bass thudded through a neighbor's wall, then cut, then started again with more conviction.

Inside the apartment, the fan Delroy had taken apart sat in pieces on the counter, screws lined in a saucer like tiny silver teeth. He was bent over the motor housing in an old Dolphins T-shirt, reading it the way he worked on people, all patience, bluff, and the expectation that a sweet mouth could loosen anything. Micah was at the sink peeling the label off a bottle of ginger beer in one long strip, his headphones around his neck instead of on his ears. Tiana was not back yet. Her work shoes were not by the door, and the room still carried the thin confidence of a place that thought next week existed.

Marcia set down her bag and the small sack of rice she had bought out of habit on the walk from the stop.

Delroy glanced up first. "You get through?"

Marcia went to the sink and washed her hands because she could still smell the fellowship hall on them, Pine-Sol and other people's sympathy. The water came out warm. She rubbed longer than necessary.

When she turned back, both of them were watching.

"The partner broke," she said.

Micah frowned. "What does that mean?"

"It means Yvette used the hand."

Delroy straightened too fast and the fan housing knocked against the counter. "Used it how?"

"She sent a voice note from the hospital. Franklin's stroke, the mortgage, rehab bills, all of it. She said she meant to put the money back before my draw and didn't."

Micah stared at her. "So she just told everybody that and done?"

"That was the shape of it."

No one spoke for a moment. Outside, a car alarm chirped twice and shut up. The apartment's little fridge coughed into its cycle.

Delroy put the fan screwdriver down carefully, which always meant he was trying not to sound as alarmed as he was. "How much are we talking?"

Marcia reached into her bag and laid the three empty envelopes on the table one after another. RENT. SCHOOL. LIGHT.

"Five thousand eight hundred dollars."

Micah's bottle slipped and knocked the sink. "What?"

"That was my turn," Marcia said. "Two shares."

Delroy stared at her. Micah laughed once, one hard unbelieving burst that died before it fully left him.

"You had fifty-eight hundred dollars in that thing?" he said.

"I had two shares in that thing because one share wasn't enough to move a family."

Micah's eyes dropped to the envelope marked SCHOOL. "School like Ti's deposit?"

"It was for her deposit, the housing mess, and the fees that breed the minute a child tries to leave poor. It was for this roof and for the light bill sitting under the sugar bowl. That money already had work."

Delroy dragged a hand over his jaw. "All right. Fine. Fine. Then we go find Yvette."

Marcia almost smiled at the stupidity of it. "Find her where?"

"We go to the hospital first and the house after. If we have to, we drag the pastor in too. She can't send a voice note and disappear."

"Disappear?" Marcia said. "She left a confession with a forwarding address. That doesn't turn into money because you raise your voice in a parking lot."

"So we do nothing?"

"I didn't say that. I said there's no cash tonight. Not from Yvette, not from church, not from anybody with a title and a phone tree."

Micah had stopped peeling the label. He held the damp strip between thumb and finger like a little dead thing. "Damn."

Delroy looked at the envelopes, then at her. "You should've told me it had reached that size."

She turned so slowly he had time to regret the line before she spoke.

"Told you so you could do what with it?"

He opened his mouth and closed it.

"You had another plan?" she asked. "You had hidden money? A landlord who likes us? A bank man waiting on your smile? Tell me now. I'm very ready to hear miracle."

Micah looked away.

Delroy's face tightened. Embarrassment always reached him before anger, just enough to show if you were married to him. "I'm saying a thing that large should've been household business."

"It became household business the minute it died."

"That isn't fair."

"Neither was today."

Micah cleared his throat. "I can call Dre, see if there's anything at the docks."

Marcia did not look at him. "No."

"I'm just saying, I could make a little."

"I'm not filling one hole by digging another under you."

Micah's mouth tightened, but he did not argue again. He dropped onto the bed by the wall and tugged one sneaker loose with the heel of the other, not taking his eyes off the floor.

Delroy pulled a mug from the cabinet and held it without doing anything else. "Give me till Monday. I'll sort something."

The sentence sat in the middle of the room unanswered.

Marcia knew that voice. Delroy used it when he wanted possibility to sound already half done. He could make bad news hold still for a few minutes by talking warm enough around it. Usually that worked best on people who loved him and did not have a bill in their hand.

"Don't promise into the air before the girl even comes home," Marcia said.

Delroy frowned. "What you want me to say then?"

"The truth. When Tiana walks in here, nobody tells her church is cooking up mercy. Nobody spends tomorrow before it gets here."

Micah finally looked up. "You calling her?"

"No. I'm not telling her on a sidewalk or in somebody else's break room."

Finally Micah said, very low, "So we really don't have no backup."

Marcia pulled the church handkerchief from her bag, then the notebook, and set them beside the envelopes. Sweat and powder had turned one corner of the cloth gray.

"No," she said.

Delroy stayed by the sink with the empty mug in his hand. Micah sat on the bed with one sneaker on and one off, his phone face-down beside him. Marcia opened the notebook to the page with her name and listened for the side gate. No one said Tiana's name again. In the room, nobody moved. They all listened for it.
