# Chapter 16

The water won before dawn. What woke Marcia was not thunder. Thunder had been arguing with the roof since midnight. What woke her was the toilet talking back.

It gave one deep animal gulp in the bathroom and then another, a wet choke from the pipes. She sat up before she was fully awake. The emergency flashlight on the milk crate had gone dim to a coin-sized halo, enough to show the room in pieces: Tiana on the mattress with one arm over her face, Micah sprawled on a blanket near the boarded window, Kerene folded on the cot in a satin bonnet the color of mango skin, Delroy still in the chair by the door with his neck bent wrong and his bad shoulder hugged up high even in sleep. Rain slapped the plywood outside. The room smelled of damp socks, cold frying oil, and the sweet-rotten mildew the garage always breathed when weather sat too long on it.

Marcia swung her feet down and stepped into water.

It was already over her ankles, warm at first touch and then colder where the current moved around her shins. She sucked air through her teeth. Not rainwater alone. There was drain in it, dirt in it, the faint bleach sting from yesterday's mop bucket gone dirty in the night.

"Up," she said, too low for the size of the problem. Then louder: "Everybody up. Now. Water's in."

Micah jerked like he had been caught in class. Tiana came awake angry, already frowning. Kerene pushed herself upright and grabbed for the cot frame as it slid a little on the wet floor. Delroy was on his feet last because his shoulder slowed him half a second, and half a second suddenly mattered.

"From where?" Tiana asked.

"From every damn place." Marcia was already moving.

The back door rattled under the rain. Water was pushing under it in a steady flat sheet, not dramatic, not one big wave, just relentless and sure of itself. The bathroom drain had begun to burp brown around the edges. The boarded window by Micah's blanket dripped from the sill in a hard little stream. Overhead the ceiling above the kitchenette had turned the color of old bruises.

Marcia did not look at the mattress first, or the little television on the crate, or the fan that had died with the power hours ago. She went to the top of the fridge and snatched down Tiana's blue document folder. From the cabinet above the sink she took the mug full of bottles and blister packs: Delroy's shoulder tablets, ibuprofen, Micah's antibiotic cream from the clinic after the police split his cheek, the cheap allergy pills that kept Kerene from rubbing her eyes raw. From the flour tin under the counter she pulled the partner notebook and the envelopes tied with a rubber band that had already gone gummy in the humidity. Only after those did she reach for clothes, one armful from the clean pile she had not yet gotten to fold, because clean or not, they were dry for now.

"Garbage bags," she said. "Not the thin white ones. The black."

"We used most of them," Tiana said, already knee-deep in the cabinet under the sink.

"Then use what's left."

Micah had his speaker in his hands.

Marcia cut him a look so sharp he put it down without argument, though his mouth twisted around one anyway. "Get your school bag. Get the clippers too if you can find them. Not that speaker."

"The speaker cost more than those clippers."

"And the clippers can earn. Move."

He splashed off, muttering, "Man, this is some bullshit," but he moved. That was enough.

Kerene, still half asleep and all bone under an oversized T-shirt, had one hand on the cot and one on the little zip case where she kept tweezers, lashes, edge control, and the tiny sharp scissors she wrapped each night in a washcloth so they would not poke through her bag. "Tell me what to lift," she said. "Don't just cuss me out with your eyes."

"Your passport first," Marcia said. "Then your work things. Not the cot."

"America's trying to wash me right back before I even get steady," Kerene said, frightened enough to sound bright.

"Don't turn it into a joke. Lift."

Delroy had gone straight to the cedar trunk. Even before he bent, Marcia saw the stupidness of it. The trunk was heavy dry. Wet, with the bottom already wicking up water, it might as well have been a piano. But he put both hands on the brass handles and tried anyway, jaw tightening, shoulder jumping under his shirt.

"Leave it," Marcia said.

"I'm not leaving this."

"You can't lift it."

"Then help me."

His voice still had the old public warmth in it, as if persuasion might make flesh stronger. It almost angered her more than panic would have. She shoved the clothes bundle into Tiana's arms and went to the trunk.

The water swirled darker around its feet. When she knelt, her knees disappeared. The metal latch was slippery under her thumb. Inside, paper smell rose up at once, the dry dusty smell of saved years turned nervous by damp. She did not have time to honor any of it. She took what she knew by feel. The clear sleeve with birth certificates and old immunization cards. The brown envelope of remittance receipts she had once wanted to throw in Delroy's face and then had not. The nursing-school letter with the fold gone white at the center from being opened and put away and opened again over too many years. A packet of church programs with funeral dates written on the back in her own hand. She shoved them all into a pillowcase.

"What about the rest?" Tiana said from above her, voice already thinning.

"If the rest goes, the rest goes. Hold this open."

Tiana held the black bag wide. Her hair scarf had slipped and one side of her face was wet, whether from rain or sweat Marcia could not tell. "My books are under the desk."

"Take the folder first."

"I've got the folder."

"Then take the books you need, not all the books in the world."

That stung Tiana enough to shut her mouth. Good. There was no room in the storm for wounded feelings unless they could float.

Over by the window, Micah found his backpack and his clippers and then, because he was still fifteen and still himself, tried to hook the speaker strap over his shoulder too. Delroy saw it and barked, "Boy. Leave that foolishness."

"Everybody else get to save their life dream."

"Your life dream better fit in one bag then."

It was the kind of line Delroy would once have landed with a grin, turning command into banter so no one had to call it obedience. This time there was no grin under it. Micah stared at him one second too long and then dropped the speaker on the table hard enough to make the flashlight jump.

Rain struck the roof in a sudden new way, heavy and close. Marcia looked up. A dark blister had swollen in the ceiling over the kitchenette, pulsing with collected water. She opened her mouth to warn them, but the ceiling moved first. A square of plaster gave way with a crack like a plate snapped in two. Then everything above it came down at once: gray mush, dirty rain, pink insulation, one strip of wire hanging like a wet vein. The collapse hit the counter, bounced off the hot plate, and exploded across the floor.

Kerene screamed. Micah swore. Delroy lunged without thinking and caught Micah's shoulder with his good arm just as the boy started toward the noise. The movement yanked something ugly through Delroy's bad shoulder. Marcia heard the sound he made before he swallowed it.

Now the room had no dry side. Water pushed under the door and from the broken ceiling in two different temperatures, two different colors. The floor rug lifted and folded against her calf like something alive. Rice from the split bag under the counter floated out in pale soft worms. A spoon rack clattered loose and vanished under the bed.

"Outside," Marcia said. "We're not fighting this in here."

"The trunk," Delroy said again, stupid with pain and stubbornness.

"The papers out, not the trunk. You hear me? Not the trunk."

This time he heard the tone under the words. He nodded once, breathing through his mouth.

They worked ugly and fast. Tiana knelt at the desk and dragged out three books swollen already at the corners: a composition notebook, the binder where she'd kept scholarship printouts and bus schedules, and the paperback she'd been rereading so often the spine had become a white seam. She left the rest when water licked over the bottoms and blurred the ink on a page before her eyes. Micah dug under the mattress for his sneakers, cursed when one came up trailing black water, then stuffed the clippers, a phone charger, and two shirts into his bag with the quick angry efficiency of a boy who did not want anybody seeing what mattered to him. Kerene took her passport from the plastic sleeve inside her tote, then doubled back for the denim jacket she wore to salon calls because it made her feel dressed even over cheap clothes. Delroy grabbed the toolbox and then dropped it for the medicine bag when Marcia glared at him. That, more than anything, told her how bad the room had become. Delroy believed in tools the way other men believed in prayer.

Marcia stood in the center long enough to lose more than she could count. The little stack of plates from Woolworth's her mother had sent with her when she first rented in Miami. The fan. The folded towels. The church shoes in the clear bin under the cot. The clean sheets she had changed just yesterday because she wanted one decent thing while the storm passed. She watched the hem of one sheet drink brown and knew, with the flat accuracy pressure gave her, that laundry soap cost less than a mind full of grief. If they got the bodies, the papers, the medicine, and enough clothes to look like people in public, the rest could drown.

She hated how quickly she learned that.

"Micah, take the flashlight."

"It barely works."

"Then hold it close."

"Tiana, zip that bag. Zip it all the way. If water gets in after all this I'll box you myself."

"I'm doing it."

"Kerene, where's your shoes?"

"On my feet."

"Good."

The door stuck from the swell of water in the frame. Delroy set his shoulder against it out of habit and nearly went down. Micah, seeing it at the same moment Marcia did, wedged in without a word. Together they forced it open. Rain hit them sideways, cold enough to reset the skin. The narrow strip of yard between the garage and the house had become a shallow brown runnel carrying leaves, cigarette butts, and somebody's escaped flip-flop toward the alley. Plywood scraps from last night's boarding leaned drunkenly against the chain-link fence. The air smelled of wet dirt ripped open.

They passed the bags out one at a time. Marcia went last because she always went last. She took one final look inside and wished she had not. The mattress had gone dark and lumpy. The cot had shifted crooked. Water from the broken ceiling hammered the counter where she'd stood rolling dough a week ago as if the room had been reduced to that one correction. On the doorjamb, the red code notice had bled at the edges, the county seal running into a pink wound.

Outside, they crowded under the mean shelter of the house eave, six inches of dry at best. Tiana clutched the document folder under her shirt. Micah held the flashlight in his mouth while tying his shoes with wet fingers, then gave up and shoved his feet into them loose. Kerene's bonnet was collapsing on one side. She ripped it off, wrung it once, and tucked it into her bag with a face so disgusted Marcia would have laughed another day. Delroy stood in the rain rather than the shelter, as if being wet by choice might count as usefulness.

"Phone the landlord," Tiana said.

"Already trying."

Marcia had left three messages during the night when the leaks first got bad. No answer. She called again now and got the same calm machine voice asking her to leave details. Details. As if he needed narration. As if the water would wait while she arranged it into language.

She tried church next because there were only so many numbers a poor woman got to have for trouble. Sister Paulette answered on the fourth ring sounding wide awake, because women like that slept with one ear still in the world.

"Marcia?"

"We're taking water."

Past tense would have been a lie. The water was still at work, still taking the room from them as she stood there.

"How bad?"

Marcia looked back through the open door at the trunk filling from the bottom up. "Bad enough."

Paulette did not say pray first. She said, "Stay where you can be seen. I'll call around."

"Don't call around too wide."

Paulette paused just long enough for the truth to sting. "I hear you. Let me do what I can."

Marcia thanked her because she had been raised right. Then she put the phone down and watched the rain walk in and out of the room that had been theirs.

By dawn the storm had not ended so much as lost its temper. Rain still fell, but in strips instead of sheets. The worst wind had moved on. What it left behind was uglier because it could be counted. There was mud rucked up along the threshold. There were wet clothes tied in garbage bags like bodies waiting their turn. There was Delroy shivering in a shirt he had insisted on keeping for work because the better ones had been nearer the broken ceiling. There was Tiana trying to smooth the edge of her folder and failing because paper, once buckled, told the truth forever. There was Micah staring at the open doorway with a look Marcia knew from his baby years, when fever had taken away all his jokes and left only insult. There was Kerene redoing her hair under the eave with two fingers and a broken compact mirror because she could not bear to meet calamity looking entirely defeated.

Marcia could count all of that. Counting made a thing real. Counting also kept her from crying until it would do nobody any good.

When the landlord finally came, he arrived dry from the knees up.

He wore grocery bags tied over his loafers and carried a narrow umbrella that covered his head and almost nothing else. His guayabera was clean enough to resent. He was a small man with a careful mustache and the habit of pinching his lower lip before saying no, as if refusal required concentration. Behind him came a younger man in a city poncho with a clipboard held inside his shirt.

Marcia stepped out from the eave before Delroy could.

"Morning," the landlord said, and let the word do the dirty work of pretending the day had begun normally.

"You got my messages."

"Service was in and out."

"You got them now."

He looked past her to the garage, to the broken ceiling, to the red notice softening on the frame. His eyes did not stay on the family long. Men like him liked damage better when it could be called structure.

"The unit is not habitable," he said. "Given the storm intrusion and the existing county issue, nobody can reoccupy this space at this time."

"At this time?" Delroy said. "So when the rain lets up, we go back in, clean it, and keep moving."

The landlord pinched his lip. "No, sir. Not until I have an inspection and know what liability I'm dealing with."

"Liability," Micah said, not quietly.

"Micah," Marcia said.

The younger man with the clipboard had a Dolphins tattoo washing blue at the edges on his forearm. He kept glancing at the collapsed section of roof with the alert boredom of somebody who had already seen a hundred poor people's emergencies and filed them all under inevitable.

Marcia kept her voice level because once she lost it, she would lose other things too. "We need to get the rest of our things out before they mildew through. The trunk. The bedding. The books under the desk."

"I can't authorize entry until I know the electrical situation."

"The power's been out since last night."

"That doesn't make it safe."

"Safe?" Delroy laughed once, raw. "Safe would've been fixing the leak when she called you the first time."

The landlord turned to him with that small, pained patience landlords wore when they wanted to sound civilized while stepping on your throat. "Sir, we had already discussed that the accessory structure had compliance issues."

"Compliance issues," Tiana repeated. She was hugging the folder so tightly the cardboard bowed. "You mean the code notice you used to keep taking rent anyway."

That got the younger man to look at her properly, with interest instead of pity. Young Black girl, articulate, soaked, furious. A whole category of trouble in one glance.

"Miss," the landlord said, "nobody is trying to make this harder than it is."

"It looks like exactly that," Tiana said.

Marcia felt the answer rise in herself too hot and too satisfying. She killed it because they still needed something from him, even if it was only the shape of the no. "What are you offering us?" she asked. "I can see what you're withholding. Tell me what you're actually offering."

The question made him blink. Good. Let him work for his phrasing.

"I can let you know when the county or insurance says the structure can be entered for salvage. If there is any prorated balance after damages, we'll have accounting review it."

"Accounting," Micah said. "Man, shut the-"

"Enough," Marcia snapped, and this time he stopped.

Delroy stepped forward into the rain. "You can come watch me pull out a few bags now. I don't need your money. I need ten minutes."

The landlord's eyes flicked to Delroy's shoulder, the wet shirt, the desperation he was trying to stand over. "I cannot let you in there and assume that exposure."

There it was. The roof was only the excuse. The real barricade was paperwork, liability, the point at which poor people's bodies started mattering as evidence.

Kerene, who had stayed quiet too long to be calm, said in a bright voice that had a blade under it, "So what you're saying is, our passports and drawers can mold up in there, long as the paperwork on your desk stays dry."

For the first time the landlord looked embarrassed, and the feeling skimmed off him almost at once. He adjusted the umbrella and said, "There's a fellowship site open at New Testament. A lot of displaced residents are being routed there."

"Routed," Micah said.

"Church people are already assisting," the younger man offered, as if that sweetened anything.

Residents. The word turned them from family into a problem somebody could sort by cot and clipboard. Marcia let it stand because county mouths fed categories before they fed people, and right now a category might be the only thing between them and another day in the rain.

"When can I know about the rest of our things?" she asked.

"I'll contact you."

"Today."

He pinched his lip again. "As soon as possible."

She had heard enough deadlines in that tone to know a disappearing one when she met it.

The church van came before the landlord left, which made the whole thing feel staged by God and bureaucracy together. It was an old white passenger van with NEW TESTAMENT BAPTIST peeled in blue vinyl across the side. Sister Eudora climbed down first. She wore white church sneakers under a navy skirt because her bunions had long ago overruled elegance, and she carried a plastic grocery sack full of hotel soaps that she gave away like communion. Behind her came Deacon Lewis, a broad man with rain beading on his eyelashes and a cinnamon toothpick tucked in one cheek even this early, because he had been trying to quit smoking since before Marcia met him and had never chosen one substitute when seven would do.

"Lord, Marcia," Eudora said, taking in the bags, the children, the doorway, the whole public wreck of it. Her hand went straight to Marcia's forearm, dry and warm, steady rather than pitying. "Come. We have space for now."

For now. Honest woman.

"We're not the only ones?" Marcia asked.

Eudora gave a small snort. "Baby, after last night? Do you think Miami only flooded your address?"

Generous and exposing in one sentence. That was church too.

Deacon Lewis had already taken the heaviest bag from Tiana and was arguing with Delroy over the cedar trunk.

"Leave it till they clear entry," the deacon said.

"That trunk has our papers."

"The papers you could reach are here. The trunk itself is not going to grow legs and walk to Hialeah."

"You don't know what's in there."

"I know your shoulder is nonsense. Give me the toolbox instead."

Delroy almost refused on instinct. Then he handed over the toolbox. Marcia saw the humiliation land and stay there. No joke came after it.

As they loaded into the van, the neighborhood collected itself enough to watch. A woman from two blocks over stood under her patio awning in a shower cap and called, "Y'all all right?" in the tone people used when they already knew the answer and wanted the right to repeat it later. Two boys on bikes slowed just to see who had been carried out. Micah ducked his head and climbed in so fast he smacked his knee on the van seat. Tiana got in upright, like a girl boarding a campus shuttle and not a shelter van, and the effort of the performance made Marcia love her hard enough to hurt.

The fellowship hall had the smell of lemon cleaner spread over wet fabric and too many bodies. Fluorescent lights flattened everybody the same. Cots ran in rows between the stackable banquet chairs and the rolled-up choir risers. At the far wall, women from the kitchen ministry were lining up foil pans of stew and rice under handwritten signs that said TAKE ONE and PLEASE SHARE. Somebody had already claimed an outlet strip near the stage and turned it into a little power economy of phones, chargers, and bargaining. Children ran until they were hissed at. A baby cried with the offended stamina of a baby who did not understand why sleep had been rearranged around adults' failures.

Marcia stopped just inside and felt the room read them.

The room did not read them cruelly. Cruelty would have been easier. It read them with recognition, which was worse. Wet family. Little River people. The garage apartment behind the cracked stucco house. By evening there would be three versions of how bad it was and two versions of whether Delroy had come back before or after the storm. By tomorrow someone would remember seeing Tiana at awards night and make the comparison with a tenderness that humiliated all by itself.

At a folding table near the entrance sat a volunteer Marcia did not know, a thin woman in a church polo with red cat-eye glasses and a handwriting grip so tight it whitened her knuckles. A little silver thimble hung on a chain at her neck like jewelry. She asked names, ages, and any medical needs in the same brisk voice she might have used for a bake sale sign-up.

"Head of household?"

The phrase landed like another bill. One name for the form, one woman to pin the whole wet wreck to. Marcia flattened the damp edge of the intake sheet with her palm and said, "Marcia Campbell."

"Adults?"

Marcia listed them. When she got to Tiana, the woman looked up. "Eighteen?"

"Yes."

"Adult then."

Tiana bent the softened folder edge deeper under her thumb. Adult then. The form had no box for daughter on her way to college, no line for a girl almost out the door. In here she was just another adult on a cot under fluorescent lights because deadlines and weather and men and money had all found the same weak seam.

They were assigned four cots and one folding church mattress in a corner near the choir robes closet, close enough to the bathrooms to hear every flush and every child dragged there half asleep. Privacy had not followed them. It had only changed uniforms.

Sister Eudora brought towels still warm from a dryer somewhere in the building and distributed them like verdicts. "You dry your head first," she told Kerene. "Your hair catches cold before your feet do." Then, lower so the men could half hear and half pretend not to, she pressed a bundle wrapped in a grocery bag into Marcia's hand. Sanitary pads, two clean pairs of underwear from donations, a travel deodorant, one soft bra in a size that might or might not be right. Careful mercy. Mortifying mercy.

"Thank you," Marcia said, because gratitude cost less than refusing.

Kerene took her towel and, for one unguarded second, pressed it to her face instead of her hair. When she lowered it, she was smiling again. "If anybody has a decent edge brush to donate, send them to cot four," she said.

Eudora barked a laugh. "Girl, do you think catastrophe means we stop being vain?"

"If it does, I don't want that salvation."

Even Marcia smiled at that, a brief easing that kept the room from turning entirely against them.

Delroy sat on the cot nearest the wall and leaned forward with his forearms on his thighs, shoulder hanging. He had gone very quiet, which on him was louder than most men shouting. Deacon Lewis brought him a Styrofoam cup of sweet coffee without comment and stood there long enough to make taking it seem ordinary. A kindness done right left no debt you had to perform in public.

Micah refused food at first. Then he saw the size of everybody else's spoonfuls and went back with his plate. "I'm just saying," he muttered to Tiana as they came from the line, "if one little church lady posts me in these donation shorts, I'm deleting my whole face."

"Then don't act like an idiot in donation shorts," Tiana said.

"That's hate."

"It's strategy."

The fact that she answered at all meant she was not gone under. Marcia filed that away with the other survivals.

There was nowhere to put the wet papers except out where everybody could see them, so Marcia claimed the end of a folding table under a standing fan. She spread the contents of the pillowcase and the bags across it with the care of a woman laying out an injured body. Tiana brought her folder and opened it. The outer cardboard had gone soft at the corners. Inside, the forms were still readable, but the ink on one photocopied page had feathered, and the plastic sleeves had trapped beads of dirty water against the names and dates. She slid the UF housing printout under a borrowed Bible at the far end of the table and kept glancing at the strip of paper left showing. Micah emptied his backpack and discovered one math notebook pasted shut from the bottom six pages up. He swore once, under his breath this time, and set his clippers on the dry end of the table like a tool laid beside a wound.

The partner notebook was worst.

The cover had held long enough to betray them. Inside, several pages had taken the water straight through the spine. Amounts Marcia had written in her careful hand were now blue ghosts swimming into one another. Dates she had kept exact as rent were bleeding at the edges. Yvette's phone number ran down the margin like something crying. The envelopes were soft as bread.

Marcia touched one page and felt it try to come apart under her fingertip.

That book had once been a plan, because she had never been foolish enough to call it a dream. A plan was dates, amounts, names, the order in which a life could be lifted one inch and then another. The partner hand, then the catch-up rent, then Tiana's deposit, then maybe a place with a legal lease and a front door that did not answer to county shame. Then Yvette's husband had his stroke, then Yvette robbed the hand to save her own house, then Delroy's secrets split the marriage, then the storm came and put brown water through what was left of the arithmetic.

Now the notebook lay under a church fan with the ink running, and Marcia knew with a steadiness that frightened her how completely the original housing plan was dead. It had gone under for good. You could not take a room already under notice, flood it from the floor and roof both, get cleared out to a fellowship hall, and still speak as if next month would be a continuation of this month. The line had broken. All the dates on the old side of it belonged to another woman.

Tiana was looking at the nursing-school letter where it had slid from the pile. She did not touch it. She had seen it before in the trunk, but here under fluorescent lights it looked smaller, meaner, just one old white paper curling at the edges while stew smell and baby cries moved through the room around it.

"You should put that back in the sleeve," Tiana said quietly.

"I know."

"I'm just saying the fan's hitting this side harder."

Marcia nodded. The practical correction was her daughter's love language when things got too large to name. She accepted it in the spirit it was given.

Across the hall, a man argued into his phone about insurance deductibles while his mother snored open-mouthed on a cot. A little girl in a sequined church dress from some previous Sunday colored on the back of a funeral program with fierce concentration. Somebody's pot of curry goat opened in the kitchen and sent a hunger through the room so strong even full people turned their heads. Relief did not soften humiliation. It simply fed it and laid a blanket over it and asked if you were warm enough.

Marcia went to the line for food at last because not eating in church looked like pride, and pride ate worse than hunger. A young usher she recognized only by face, a boy with barber-fresh lines in his hair and one front tooth silvered from childhood, spooned rice onto her plate and looked carefully at her shoulder instead of at her eyes.

"You need anything else, Sister Campbell, they got toiletries by the office," he said.

"We have enough for tonight."

"All right."

He meant it kindly. She heard the care in the formality. She hated him a little anyway for being able to offer while she stood there receiving.

When she brought the plate back, Delroy had fallen asleep sitting up, the coffee cup empty between his feet. His head had dropped back against the cinderblock wall, mouth open, face older than she had wanted to admit even in anger. Micah was on his phone under his towel, one leg bouncing. Kerene had somehow made a donated oversized T-shirt look intentional and was helping an elderly woman on the next cot untangle a chain from her hair scarf while talking low and fast enough to keep the woman laughing. Tiana was still at the table with the papers, reading every line as if force of attention alone could flatten them dry.

Marcia set her plate down untouched for a moment and pressed the partner notebook open with both hands.

The fan lifted the corners of the wet pages. They kept trying to curl back into themselves. She flattened them again. If she let go, the pages would buckle. If she pressed too hard, they would tear. It was delicate, annoying work, the kind no disaster movie respected because there was nothing grand in it. Still it was the work the day had left her.

Above the murmur of the hall, Sister Eudora was somewhere in the kitchen scolding someone for putting wet towels on a polished pew. Deacon Lewis laughed at something too tired to be funny. A child asked for their mother in that lost, casual way children did when they assumed the answer would come. Marcia kept one hand on the notebook and one on Tiana's folder so the fan would not carry either off the table.

By night the fellowship hall had become its own weather. Snoring, muttering, phone light on faces, the hiss of the industrial fan, toilets flushing without shame, coughs stitched from one end of the room to the other. Marcia sat awake in the plastic chair by the papers because she could not trust sleep with so much of their life laid out drying in public.

The ink kept drying lighter than it had been written. The pages kept holding the waterline.

So did she.
