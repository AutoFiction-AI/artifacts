# Chapter 7

Marcia had spent the money so many times in her head that by Saturday afternoon it already felt warm from handling. First and security on a place with a real front door, not a converted garage somebody else could decide to call illegal whenever it suited them. The college money she had not named to Tiana because hope got loud in that apartment, and loud hope turned into appetite. The light bill. The little late fees that bred in the dark when you let them. The numbers changed depending on which office wanted blood first, but the total never moved. Five thousand eight hundred dollars. Enough to turn July from a cliff into chores.

Inside her handbag she carried three white envelopes with her block printing in the corners: RENT, SCHOOL, LIGHT. She had folded them flat before leaving, the same way she folded medication schedules for Celia Alvarez, crisp enough that even bad news had to stand up straight. Writing the labels ahead of time felt half like discipline and half like temptation. She had done it anyway. A woman could not wait on miracle and then pretend she had no use for it once it came.

People with checking accounts liked to talk about partner as if it were a quaint old-country habit, something to mention beside rum cake and airport barrels. Marcia never corrected them. Let them keep their little smile. A hand was not folklore. A hand was what people built when the bank charged thirty-five dollars because a paycheck landed one day wrong, when credit asked for paper the poor could not spare, when cash disappeared too easily inside a marriage or a family or your own hungry head. Twenty-nine names, a hundred dollars every week, and Marcia had carried two numbers because one number was grocery hope. Two numbers could move a family.

Her mother had bought a refrigerator in Spanish Town on partner money, paid the rest of the cycle under a zinc roof that leaked over the sink, and never once called it luck. In Miami, Marcia had used a Christmas hand years ago to buy Tiana's crib without putting one more thing on a store card. Yvette had understood that exact math. Yvette kept the book in blue ink, no pencil, no uncertainty, and called late people by Wednesday before their excuse had time to grow pretty. She ironed even the house dresses she wore to collect money, and one of her eyebrows always sat a little higher than the other as if she did not quite believe anybody who said, Next week, sis, for sure. That was the face Marcia had trusted with two numbers.

The fellowship hall behind New Testament Revival had one wall unit that spat cold in short mean bursts and then gave up. The room smelled of Pine-Sol, old paper, and the curry goat somebody had served after Bible study the night before. Not all twenty-nine women were there, but enough had come to make the air thick. Miss Pauline sat with her airport parking visor clipped into church hair, as if Miami sun might break through the cinderblock and get ambitious. Her ankles ballooned above white support hose, and she kept spearmints in a little cough-drop tin she snapped shut like a judge. Nadine had come straight from a night shift in bee-print scrub pants and white Crocs, a crescent dent still pressed into the bridge of her nose from a face mask. Claudette, who hemmed hotel uniforms for cash, kept passing her thumb over a loose stitch at her sleeve as if the cloth had personally offended her.

Marcia chose a folding chair near the table where Yvette usually spread the notebook, calculator, and stack of rubber-banded envelopes. The table was bare except for a Styrofoam cup sweated half empty and a church bulletin advertising youth choir rehearsal. She checked the clock over the serving hatch. Ten after two.

"Traffic must've catch her," Nadine said, though her voice already carried a doubt she had not earned yet.

Miss Pauline made a small sound through her nose. "Yvette late is like funeral chicken left over. It happens, but it means something went wrong."

Nobody laughed. Even Claudette, who laughed at almost anything with a shape, only rubbed harder at the loose stitch.

Marcia took out her own notebook, the small black one she kept in the zipped side pocket of her bag. Dates, amounts, Yvette's initials beside every payment. She had recorded each hundred the minute she got home, sometimes before she changed out of work clothes, while the apartment still smelled of bleach and frying oil and whatever bass line the neighbors had decided belonged to everybody. There were weeks that second hundred had come from ugly places: utility money, bus money, one of Tiana's paychecks shifted quietly from one future into another. The notebook did not blush. It only held the figures.

At quarter past, Nadine called Yvette. Straight to voicemail. Claudette called next. The same. Pauline called and left no message at all, just listened until the beep and hung up with a thumb hard enough to make the screen pop.

"Hospital, maybe," Claudette said. "Franklin still doing therapy, no?"

"Then you send word," Pauline said. She shook out one spearmint and bit it in half. "If money is waiting in a room, you send word before the room goes bad."

Marcia said nothing. She had seen Franklin once after the stroke, in church, one side of his face gone slack and his left hand resting in his lap like it belonged to somebody he had not yet met. Yvette had sat beside him in a yellow blouse, jaw set, answering for both of them when people asked how he was doing. Marcia had gone home that day with pity for the woman and relief that pity was not yet necessary in her own kitchen. The relief came back now to shame her.

At two twenty-three, Pauline's phone pinged. The sound snapped through the room so sharply that every chair leg stopped moving.

"From her?" Nadine asked.

Pauline looked at the screen and went still. "Voice note."

The women shifted in, chairs scraping, bodies drawing close with the intimate greed of people who knew bad news always arrived with details and wanted the details first. Pauline hit play and held the phone above the table.

Yvette's voice came out small but clear. There was hospital noise behind it, the rattle of a cart and a television turned too low to understand.

"Pauline, if everybody's there, play this loud. I can't take twenty-nine calls and say the same thing twenty-nine times. I took money from the hand. I did. Franklin's stroke hit, the mortgage was three months down, and I kept telling myself I'd hold from it for one week and put it back before Marcia's draw. Then rehab wanted cash and the bank pinned another notice on the door and I used more. I know what that is. I know what it makes me. I'm not asking anybody to call me good. I'm saying I wasn't going to let them take my house while my husband couldn't lift his own left hand. I will pay every cent back. I don't know how fast. Please don't come by the house and frighten the girls."

The room held for one stunned beat after the message ended. Then it broke all at once.

"Jesus Christ," Nadine said.

Claudette covered her mouth with both hands. "The girls are there by themselves?"

Miss Pauline slapped the phone down on the table so hard the Styrofoam cup jumped. "A stroke doesn't baptize a thief."

Nobody corrected the language. Nobody corrected anything.

Nadine started talking at the same time Claudette did. One wanted the police. One wanted the pastor. Pauline wanted Yvette's sister. A woman by the door, Sonia from the usher board, said she had seen foreclosure take her cousin's house in Lauderdale and it was enough to make any person mad. Pauline wheeled on her fast enough that Sonia stepped back into the hymn-number board.

"Mad at who?" Pauline demanded. "The bank? Good. Be mad at the bank. Why am I supposed to pay for Wells Fargo in the name of Christian sympathy?"

"Because the man had a stroke," Claudette said, and her fingers had moved from the loose stitch to the cheap silver chain at her throat. "Because she must've been losing her mind."

"Everybody in this room joins partner because something is already wrong," Marcia said.

The sentence came out level. It surprised her a little. She had expected fury to arrive hot. Instead it came cold and exact, the way she sounded when reading dosage instructions to a patient who thought she could charm her way around medicine.

The room quieted just enough for her to keep going.

"Nobody brings easy money into a hand. Nobody here had spare cash making them foolish. That was school money. Rent money. Childcare money. If emergency gave you license to raid the box, partner wouldn't exist."

Nadine nodded hard. "Exactly."

But Claudette's face crumpled anyway. "Marcia, I know. I know. I'm only saying I can see the table where she did it. That's all I'm saying."

Marcia could see it too, and that was part of what made her want to break something. Yvette's glass-top dining table under the slow fan. Franklin's pill organizer open beside the fruit bowl. One bank letter, then another, then a third with language sharpened by people who never had to choose between public dishonor and private foreclosure. Cash bands on the table, not because she was rich enough to stack money but because cash looked different once it stopped being theory and turned into the exact thing that might keep the sheriff away for one more month. Marcia could picture Yvette's hand hovering over the pile, telling herself borrow first, replace later, because every poor person she knew had once or twice survived by turning theft into temporary vocabulary.

Understanding the decision did not soften it. It only made it uglier.

Pauline hit redial. Straight to voicemail. She cursed, low and inventive, then jabbed a finger at the phone as if Yvette could still see it. "You don't send me apology in MP3 format. You answer."

Sonia had already started crying. She was near the end of the hand and had been planning to use her turn for school uniforms in August. Nadine stood over her with one hand on her shoulder, rubbing circles so fast they looked impatient. Someone near the back asked whether any of the money was left. Nobody knew. Somebody else said maybe Yvette had only borrowed from later turns, not today's. Pauline barked out, "Did you not hear the woman say before Marcia's draw?"

Heads turned.

Not all at once. Worse than that. One by one, with that careful human slowness people used when they had just remembered where the sharpest pain in the room lived.

Marcia had known they all understood it was her turn. She had not understood how different knowledge felt from attention. The pity landed first on her blouse, then on her face, then in the softening of bodies around her. Claudette pulled a chair closer. Nadine touched her forearm. Even Pauline's anger tried, for a second, to change shape.

"Marcia," Claudette said quietly. "Lord."

Marcia moved her arm off the table before Nadine's hand could settle there. "Don't."

Nobody mistook her meaning.

She was not proud enough to hate sympathy in general. She had taken casseroles, hand-me-down uniforms, advice she did not ask for. She knew the uses of community. But pity in public was a different thing. Pity in public asked a woman to stand still while other people took note of exactly how badly she had needed something. It made need communal before relief had any chance to become private.

Nadine swallowed. "All right."

"Play it again," Pauline said.

They did. This time the room listened for proof instead of surprise, each woman leaning in on different words. Mortgage. Stroke. Marcia's draw. Pay every cent back. Girls. Nobody heard mercy in the same place.

By the time the voice note ended a second time, the fracture had settled cleanly through the room. One side wanted to march to Yvette's house and pull every curtain open until shame climbed out. The other side wanted to call the pastor first, or Yvette's sister, or the hospital social worker, as if a better chain of adults might reorganize catastrophe into something reputable. Nadine wanted the police until Pauline reminded her there was no bank statement, no formal contract, only notebooks and cash and witnesses who would all sound ridiculous the minute they sat under fluorescent law. That shut Nadine up for almost thirty seconds.

"So what then?" she said. "We just let her eat us?"

"Nobody said that," Pauline snapped.

"What did you say then?"

"I said police not bringing the money back."

"Neither is church."

"Church can ruin her reputation."

"Reputation doesn't pay FPL."

The argument skidded around the room, hot and useless. Marcia heard only pieces. She kept staring at the table where Yvette's ledger should have been. The church bulletin had soaked up the sweat from the Styrofoam cup until the ink bled through the youth-choir photo. She thought of the three envelopes in her bag, their neat labels waiting for content, and wanted to laugh so badly the feeling nearly made her choke.

Claudette crouched beside her chair. "You all right?"

The question was kind. That made it worse.

"No," Marcia said. "But I'm sitting here."

Claudette gave a helpless little nod. She smelled faintly of camphor and fabric softener, the smell of clothes that had been mended instead of replaced. "If she pays it back piece by piece..."

"Piece by piece is not a draw," Marcia said.

That shut Claudette's mouth for a while.

Pauline started taking names of who had paid what and when, copying from notebooks onto the back of old church programs because outrage loved administration when administration was all it had left. Marcia opened her own notebook only because it gave her hands somewhere to go. She read the dates she already knew. March 8. March 15. March 22. Whole weeks of sweat, bus fare counted twice, one pair of shoes reheeled instead of replaced, one light bill delayed, one Saturday lunch skipped at work because hunger was cheaper than pride. The numbers sat there in blue ink, loyal and useless.

Nadine leaned over the table. "You had two shares, right?"

Marcia looked up.

Nadine's face changed the minute she heard how the question sounded. Too late.

"I mean," Nadine said quickly, "I remember Yvette saying you carried two numbers, that's all."

"Yes," Marcia said.

Nadine winced as if struck. Across the room Pauline closed her eyes for half a second. Even Sonia stopped crying long enough to stare.

There it was again, her need passing from rumor into inventory.

Five thousand eight hundred dollars. Enough to relocate. Enough to put Tiana's future back on rails. Enough to buy Delroy time he had not earned. Enough that the loss itself now made her look foolish, greedy, naive, all the little words people offered women after fraud as if trusting the wrong person were an appetite problem.

She stood before anybody could try kindness a second time.

"Call whoever you want," she said. "Pastor, sister, hospital, all of them. Tell me if money appears. Until then, I need to go home."

"Marcia," Pauline said, and for once she sounded older than sharp. "Let somebody drop you."

"I know the bus route."

"That isn't what I mean."

Marcia pulled her bag closed. "I know."

She left while the room was still deciding whether pity or fury would run the rest of the afternoon. In the hallway the church's back door stuck at the frame and made her shoulder work for freedom. Outside, the heat slapped harder than it had any right to after air-conditioning that weak. Somebody's child was practicing scales in the sanctuary, the keyboard notes slipping through cinderblock one embarrassed at a time.

At the bus stop she took the three envelopes out and looked at them. RENT. SCHOOL. LIGHT. Her own handwriting, calm as a nurse. She held them until the sweat from her fingers softened the paper corners, then slid them back into her bag before anybody waiting beside her could read them and know one more thing.

The ride home was full. A man with paint on his jeans slept with his mouth open. Two girls in crop tops argued about a wig install in voices bright enough to cut the stale air. At every stop Marcia expected the shape of the afternoon to change, for the phone to ring, for Pauline to say they had found some of the money, for Yvette to call sobbing, for God to stop showing off and behave like arithmetic for once. Nothing happened. The purse on her lap stayed light.

By the time she pushed through the side gate to the stucco house in Little River, the sun had dropped low enough to throw the landlord's place across the yard like another threat. The gravel popped under her shoes. Somebody up front was frying fish. Bass thudded through a neighbor's wall, then cut, then started again with more conviction.

Inside the apartment, the fan Delroy had taken apart sat in pieces on the counter, screws lined in a saucer like tiny silver teeth. He was bent over the motor housing in an old Dolphins T-shirt, reading it as if charm could be applied to machines the same way he applied it to people. Tiana sat at the table with her document folder open, yellow highlighter cap between her teeth, one stack of forms weighted by the sugar bowl. Micah was at the sink peeling the label off a bottle of ginger beer in one long strip, his headphones around his neck instead of on his ears. The room looked exactly like the kind of room that still believed in next week.

Marcia set down her bag and the small sack of rice she had bought out of habit on the walk from the stop.

Tiana looked up first. "What happened?"

Marcia went to the sink and washed her hands because she could still smell the fellowship hall on them, Pine-Sol and other people's sympathy. The water came out warm. She rubbed longer than necessary.

When she turned back, all three of them were watching.

"The partner broke," she said.

Micah frowned. "What does that mean, exactly?"

"It means Yvette took money from the hand."

Delroy straightened too fast and the fan housing knocked against the counter. "Took it where?"

Marcia gave him a look.

"No, I mean," he said, already revising, "borrowed? Delayed? What are you saying?"

"I'm saying she used it. For her husband, for her mortgage, for whatever story still sounds prettier in church tomorrow. She sent a voice note. She said she meant to put it back before my draw and didn't."

Tiana took the highlighter cap out of her mouth. "Your draw was today?"

"Yes."

No one spoke for a moment. Outside, a car alarm chirped twice and shut up. The apartment's little fridge coughed into its cycle.

Delroy put the fan screwdriver down carefully, which always meant he was trying not to sound as alarmed as he was. "How much are we talking?"

Marcia reached for the rice because hands needed a task. She poured the grains into the red plastic bowl, ran water over them, and watched the cloud rise from the bottom. White starch turned the bowl blind.

"Marcia," Tiana said, sharper now. "How much?"

She let the water run a second longer, then shut it off.

"Five thousand eight hundred dollars."

Micah's bottle slipped and knocked the sink. "What?"

Marcia tipped the bowl and let the cloudy water pour away. The rice shifted against itself with a dry soft hiss, like paper being handled.

"Five thousand eight hundred dollars," she said again. "That was my turn."

Tiana stared at her. Delroy stared harder. Micah laughed once, one hard unbelieving burst that died before it fully left him.

"You had fifty-eight hundred dollars in that thing?" he said.

"I had two shares in that thing," Marcia said. "Because one share wasn't enough to move us anywhere."

Tiana sat back slowly. "Move us."

Marcia reached into her bag and laid the three empty envelopes on the table, one after another. RENT. SCHOOL. LIGHT.

The room went still enough to hear the fan chain tapping against the counter.

"That's what it was for," Marcia said. "Rent. School. Light. Some breathing room. Some chance to get ahead of the notice before the notice got ahead of us."

Tiana looked at the envelope marked SCHOOL as if it had been addressed directly to her face. "You were going to use that for my deposit?"

"For your deposit, for whatever fee came next, for the housing problem, for all of it. Don't make me divide children in this kitchen."

The words came out sharper than she intended. Tiana's mouth tightened. Marcia hated the little hurt she saw there and hated more that she did not have energy to soften it.

Delroy dragged a hand over his jaw. "All right. Fine. Fine. Then we go find Yvette."

Marcia almost smiled at the stupidity of it. "Find her where?"

"Hospital first. House after. Pastor if we have to. She can't just send voice note and disappear."

"Disappear?" Marcia said. "She left a confession with a forwarding address. That doesn't turn into money because you raise your voice in a parking lot."

"So we do nothing?"

"I didn't say that."

"Then what are you saying?"

Marcia set the bowl down harder than she meant to. A few grains jumped the rim and stuck to the wet counter. "I'm saying there's no cash tonight. Not from Yvette, not from church, not from anybody with a title and a phone tree. I'm saying if you go over there hoping outrage can print money, you'll come home with exactly what I brought back, which is nothing."

Micah had stopped peeling the label. He held the damp strip between thumb and finger like a little dead thing. "Damn."

Tiana pressed her palm flat over the SCHOOL envelope. "I need numbers, not just disaster. If that money is gone, I need to know whether I should call housing and tell them not to hold anything. I need to know if there is any version where school is still real, or if we're pretending."

That was Tiana at her most frightened, Marcia knew. Not loud. Precise.

"I don't know yet," Marcia said.

"The aid hold is still there," Tiana said. "The aid office already said they can't finalize without the resident card and the other paperwork. If there was money coming, that was one thing. If there isn't, then I need to stop speaking like there's a plan."

Delroy shifted, and the screw saucer rattled under his wrist. "We're not giving up school because one woman went bad."

Tiana looked at him. "Then what exactly are we doing?"

"I'll sort something."

The sentence landed in the middle of the room and failed to grow legs.

Marcia knew that voice. Delroy used it when he wanted possibility to sound already half done. He could make bad news sit politely for a little while by talking at it warm enough. Usually that worked best on people who loved him and did not have a calculator in reach.

Tiana's expression changed by one degree only, but Marcia saw it. Less fear. More disbelief.

"By when?" Tiana asked.

"Soon."

"That's not a time."

"Tiana," Marcia said.

But the girl's eyes had stayed on Delroy.

"My deadline isn't emotional," Tiana said. "It isn't church, and it isn't one of Daddy's stories. It's a date on paper."

Micah looked from one to the other and muttered, "Here we go."

Delroy drew himself up, performing calm because anger would make him lose leverage. "Watch how you talk to me."

"Then say something I can use."

Marcia reached for the chicken pack in the fridge because if she let the conversation own her hands the whole room would tip. The cold plastic stuck to her palm. She slit it open over the sink, and pink water slid down toward the drain in a string that looked too thin to be called blood and too ugly to be anything else. The smell rose up immediately, raw and metallic. She seasoned hard and fast, salt, black pepper, garlic, scallion, thyme stripped off the stems with more force than tenderness.

"Enough," she said.

No one answered. The sound of seasoning hitting meat took over for a moment, small wet slaps against enamel.

Delroy tried again, softer now because softness was the next tool in his kit. "Marcia, you should've told me it had reached that size."

She turned so slowly he had time to regret the line before she spoke.

"Told you so you could do what with it?"

He opened his mouth and closed it.

"You had another plan?" she asked. "You had hidden money? A landlord who likes us? A bank manager waiting on your smile? Tell me now. Please. I'm very ready to hear miracle."

Micah let out a breath through his teeth and looked away. Tiana did not move at all.

Delroy's face tightened. Embarrassment always reached him before anger, just enough to show if you were married to him. "I'm saying a thing that large should've been household business."

"It became household business the minute it died."

"That isn't what I mean."

"I know exactly what you mean."

Marcia dumped the chicken into the pot. It hissed up in hot oil, loud enough to make Micah step back from the sink. The kitchen filled with scallion and black pepper and the first edge of browning meat. She stood over it with the wooden spoon in her hand and thought, with a clarity so sharp it felt almost peaceful, that she did not have room tonight for Delroy's injured dignity on top of her own.

Micah cleared his throat. "I can call Dre, see if there's anything at the docks."

Marcia did not turn. "No."

"I'm just saying."

"I heard you. No."

"I could make a little."

"And school?"

Micah picked at the wet label strip. "School ain't making me five grand by Monday."

The line hurt because it was true and because it came out of his mouth like a joke he had been practicing in private. Marcia stirred the pot until onions started to soften and disappear.

"Nobody asked you for five grand," she said.

"Feels like life did."

That was Micah too, the quick funny turn toward mean once fear got its hand in his shirt. He looked almost ashamed the second the words were out, but he kept his chin set. Marcia could not deal with that either, not yet.

Tiana drew the SCHOOL envelope toward her, then stopped herself and pushed it back where it had been. "Did Yvette say any of it is left?"

"No."

"Did anybody go to her house?"

"Probably by now."

"And?"

"And I left before the room decided whether shame was a collection strategy."

Tiana let out a breath and pinched the bridge of her nose, the exact public gesture she used when refusing to cry in front of people who might misunderstand the tears. "Okay. Then I need the actual truth. Are we talking about school being delayed, or school being dead?"

Marcia looked at her daughter and saw the whole ugly stack at once: the forms under the sugar bowl, the expired card issue she still did not fully understand, the summer job money Tiana had given over without being asked, the room the girl had been measuring in her head for months without ever saying goodbye to it aloud. She wanted to answer with comfort. Comfort required inventory she did not possess.

"I don't know," she said again, quieter this time. "Tonight I know the money is gone. That is what I know."

Delroy seized the opening because uncertainty was where he did his best work. "Then tomorrow we start making calls."

Marcia gave a short laugh that had no pleasure in it. "To who?"

"Pastor. Claude knows people. There are always people."

The name slipped out before he could catch it.

Marcia looked up fast. "Claude?"

Delroy's mouth changed shape. Not much. Enough.

"Claude from where?" Tiana asked.

"Just a man," Delroy said too quickly. "A work contact."

Marcia held his face for a second, filing it away because she did not have the room to open that drawer tonight. Not with chicken browning, rice waiting, Tiana white around the mouth, Micah already reaching for some bad version of manhood to patch the gap. One catastrophe at a time, if the room would permit it.

She turned back to the stove before the silence could ask more.

The rice water had gone starchy again in the red bowl. She poured it off and freshened it once more. Beside her, the pot needed turning. Behind her, papers shifted on the table, envelopes rasped under nervous fingers, the screws in Delroy's saucer clicked when he gathered them and set them down again. The whole apartment sounded like a handful of people trying not to say the sentence that would make the loss finish becoming real.

Finally Micah said, very low, "So we really don't have no backup."

Marcia put the rice on to boil and watched the first bubbles push up at the edges.

"No," she said.

Tiana folded her hands together so tightly the knuckles went pale. Delroy stared at the fan motor as if it had wronged him personally. Outside, somebody laughed in the yard, full and careless, a sound from a world where Saturday still meant something loose.

Marcia carried the chicken spoon back through the gravy, scraping browned spice off the bottom because flavor, unlike money, still answered labor. The steam hit her face. Sweat slid down her spine under the blouse she had put on for respectability three hours earlier. In the fellowship hall women had looked at her and seen a poor fool. In this kitchen they looked at her and saw, maybe for the first time, how much of the summer she had been trying to hold in one pair of hands.

The rice boiled over. Foam ran down the side of the pot and hissed on the burner. Marcia snatched the dish towel, wrapped the handle, and pulled it half off the flame. Starch slicked across the stove in thick white trails, ugly and immediate. She wiped once, then again, though the mess spread before it cleared.

"Mom," Tiana said, her voice gone soft with something that might have been apology or fear.

Marcia kept wiping.

"Leave it," Delroy said.

"No."

She cleaned until the towel came away gummy, until the burner showed black again, until there was at least one thing in the room that had obeyed her hand. Then she set the towel down and faced them all.

"Listen to me now," she said. "There's no rescue cash. Not in church. Not in Yvette's voicemail. Not in any man saying give him till Monday. What was coming is gone. So if anybody in this room is still waiting on that money to save us, stop waiting."

No one answered. The SCHOOL envelope lay beside Tiana's folder. RENT sat near the sugar bowl. LIGHT had drifted closest to Delroy's elbow, as if even paper knew where accusation liked to settle. The chicken simmered. The fan remained in pieces.

For the first time that summer, Marcia looked at the table and saw nothing there she could divide into a future.
