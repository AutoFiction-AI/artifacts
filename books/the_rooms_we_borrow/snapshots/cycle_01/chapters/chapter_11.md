# Chapter 11

Marcia ironed the blue blouse with the steam turned off because the iron spat brown water when she trusted it. The apartment had already gone hot. Delroy slept flat on his back with his bad arm propped on a folded towel, mouth open, the sharp stink of liniment and sweat spreading off him as steadily as heat off pavement. Tiana's document folder stood upright on the milk crate beside her mattress, squared and orderly, as if papers could shame the world into behaving.

Marcia slid the photocopy of the red code notice, the last rent receipt, and the landlord's text into her handbag. She put in a pen that still worked and the envelope where she kept whatever cash could survive not being touched. The envelope felt weightless. That was the first bad sign of the day, though the day had started with Delroy trying and failing to get his T-shirt over his head without waking himself up from the pain.

He opened one eye when she snapped her bag shut.

"You going now?"

"Before office open proper. Maybe he'll be in a better mood if nobody else reach him first."

Delroy pushed himself up too fast and sucked air through his teeth. Even that sound annoyed her because it carried so much need in it. He looked tall even folded by pain, shoulder wrapped in the drugstore sling Tiana had improvised from an old scarf because the clinic wanted money before better equipment. His hair had flattened on one side and stood up wild on the other. Last week she might have loved him for how ridiculous that looked. This week it just looked expensive.

"I can come," he said. "Man to man sometimes works better."

"Your shoulder can't even manage shirt to man right now."

"Still have my mouth."

"That's exactly what I don't need in there."

He gave her the little smile he used when he wanted to turn rebuke into flirting. The smile had won him more mercy in life than sense ever had. Today it only made the room feel smaller.

"You don't have to make me useless before breakfast, Marcia."

"I don't have to make anything. The workman already do that."

The words came out harder than she meant them to. Micah shifted on the pallet by the kitchenette but did not wake. Through the curtain, Tiana's breathing changed, which meant she was awake and pretending otherwise. Marcia hated performing family life through cloth. She hated the room hearing what belonged in a marriage. She hated that hatred itself cost energy she needed elsewhere.

Delroy looked down at the sling and used his good hand to smooth the frayed edge. "If he starts talking about code and all that foolishness, tell him I can finish the repairs myself once this settle."

"You're not finishing your own shoelace this morning."

He went quiet. Shame shortened him faster than pain did. Marcia saw it happen and did not stop herself. That was the thing she had learned over the last month: pity was another luxury item, lovely to look at, badly priced.

She took the house key from the nail, slipped on the shoes she saved for church and offices, and stepped outside before anybody else in the room could need something from her.

Little River was only half awake, but the noise had already started. A bus sighed at the corner. Somebody's rooster sounded insulted by the light. Bass trembled through a parked Honda two houses down, low enough to rattle the chain-link gate. The stucco main house in front wore its cracks in the same places as always. The converted garage behind it, where the Campbells lived, looked as temporary as bad news.

The landlord's office sat in the front room of a one-story building off Biscayne that pretended to be respectable with fake wood blinds and a plastic ficus by the door. The wall unit blew cold air that smelled faintly of mildew. Marcia sat beneath a sign about late fees and watched the receptionist peel the backing off a hard candy with the concentration of a jeweler. The receptionist had one glitter nail broken down to the quick and kept touching it with her tongue.

When the landlord came out, he was already holding Marcia's file. He wore a stiff pale polo tucked into trousers that had been ironed with military feeling. Reading glasses hung from a blue cord on his chest. He had the kind of face that never needed to raise its voice because the paperwork did the bruising for him.

"Ms. Campbell." He did not offer his hand. "Come in."

His office held a desk, two metal chairs, and three framed certificates that said nothing Marcia could use. On the corner of the desk sat a photo of a girl in soccer socks grinning with a mouthful of braces. Beside it lay a stack of notices face down, as if bad news became less offensive when it showed its back.

The landlord sat. Marcia stayed standing a beat too long, making him look up at her. Then she took the chair because standing had not paid rent yet.

"I won't waste your time," she said. "I need a little extension. A week. Ten days at the outside. My husband's hurt, and I just lost the care job I had. The lady passed."

He nodded once, not unkindly. "I'm sorry to hear that."

The sympathy landed and slid off. He turned the file toward himself, thumb on the paper clip.

"The problem," he said, "is not whether your situation is sympathetic. The problem is the balance on the unit and the city's compliance deadline. Once code enforcement has a file open, I can't keep doing informal accommodations. Everything has to be documented properly."

"Document it, then. Write that I asked. Write that I'm telling you money is coming."

"Money coming isn't payment."

"No, but it's closer to payment than me standing in here talking."

He gave her a tight little smile for that, the kind men gave when they wanted to reward spirit without surrendering anything material.

"Your current rent is past due, there are late charges attached, and because of the occupancy issue with that structure, any extension past Friday would require an additional security payment." He spoke with careful hands, flattening each page after he touched it. "That is the cleanest way for me to protect myself."

Marcia stared at him. "Protect yourself from what."

"Liability. The city. A tenant claiming I made promises I didn't make. You understand."

He said you understand the way church ladies said bless your heart before gossip. It came dressed as inclusion and meant obedience.

"I understand you want extra money I don't have."

"I want the account brought current. For any formal extension beyond that, yes, I'd need added security. Given the circumstances."

"What circumstances. That your garage was making you money till the city remember law?"

His expression changed then, not to anger exactly. More to the patient alertness of a man who had found the line and meant to stand on the safe side of it.

"Ms. Campbell, I am trying to keep this professional."

"Professional is a lovely word when somebody else is the one getting pushed out."

The wall unit kicked louder. Outside the office door a phone rang twice and stopped. Marcia could hear the receptionist laughing softly at something on her screen. The laughter traveled under the door and made the room feel staged, as if one side of life kept happening no matter what the other side owed.

The landlord opened a drawer and took out a printed notice. He did not slide it to her yet. He kept one finger resting on the top edge.

"What I can do," he said, "is hold off on filing until Friday afternoon. If you bring the balance close enough to current by then, we talk again. If you can't, I move to the next step. I don't want to surprise you."

He said surprise as if that were generosity.

"And if I bring half."

"Half keeps you half behind."

"You have people looking already?"

The question slipped out before she could stop it. He did not answer. He only set the paper down now, neatly, within reach but not touching her hand.

"I need to manage the property responsibly."

There it was. Not no. Worse than no. Replaceable.

Marcia looked at the notice without lifting it. Her own name printed cleanly near the top. The unit described as if it were something she had borrowed wrong.

"My children sleep there."

"I know that."

"Do you."

He took off his glasses and polished them with the hem of his polo, quick precise circles, the gesture of a man buying himself ten seconds of not having to feel another person's life press against his own. When he put the glasses back on, his face had closed again.

"Bring me money by Friday, Ms. Campbell."

That was the whole sermon.

She picked up the notice. The paper was cool from his office and went damp almost immediately in her hand. On the way out the receptionist offered her a peppermint from the stuck-together bowl. Marcia nearly laughed. She would have preferred a knife.

Outside, the heat struck her full in the face. Across the lot a man in a yellow safety shirt was pressure-washing the walkway of an empty unit. Water ran gray into the gutter. Every pass of the hose made the concrete look new for about thirty seconds before the stains came back through.

Marcia stood with the notice and thought, not for the first time, that poverty made language dirty. Extension, accommodation, current, security. Words that sounded neutral until they sat on your chest.

By the time the bus came, the paper had gone soft around the folds where her fingers kept finding the same place.

***

Coral Gables looked scrubbed in a way that felt personal. The trees were trimmed before they could get wild. The houses kept their grief behind hedges. Even the garbage bins looked expensive.

Celia's house smelled wrong without Celia in it. Not empty. Wrong. Somebody had taken away the hospital bed from the den, and the rectangle it had covered showed pale on the rug, a ghost cleaner than the rest of the floor. White lilies crowded the dining table and turned the air sweet enough to sicken a person. In the kitchen, aluminum trays from neighbors and cousins filled the counters: croquetas, baked ziti, pastelitos going hard at the edges because sorrow made rich people overfeed one another.

Elena stood at the island in black linen pants and a white blouse that had already wilted at the waist. One heel lay abandoned near the trash can. She was barefoot on one side and still wearing a nude stocking on the other, the foot flexing now and then against the cool tile as if she had a blister she refused to respect. Her phone sat faceup beside a stack of funeral-home papers. Every few seconds she straightened them even though they were already straight.

She looked up when Marcia came in through the side door.

"You didn't have to come today."

"I was nearby."

It was not a good lie. Elena glanced at the handbag, the office shoes, the face Marcia knew looked arranged for asking.

"Sit if you want," Elena said.

"If I sit down in this house right now, I might not get back up."

Elena gave the briefest huff of almost-laughter. It vanished quickly, embarrassed by itself.

"I've got your final check," she said. "And I wanted to talk to you before things got more chaotic."

More chaotic. Marcia nearly looked around for evidence. The woman had died; how much more honest could a house get.

Elena took an envelope from beside the papers and handed it over. Marcia did not open it immediately. She could feel the check through the paper, one thin rectangle pretending to be a bridge.

"Thank you," she said.

"There's also a number in there. A coordinator at an agency my friend uses for her father sometimes. They take private-pay cases all over the county. No promises." Elena's voice tightened on the last words, as if she knew promises were expensive things to say to women who cleaned up bodies for a living. "I texted her already. She said to call today because weekend shifts open fast."

Marcia slid the check out halfway. The business card tucked behind it was for an agency in Kendall whose logo tried too hard to look gentle. She put both back in the envelope.

"Weekend shifts open fast," she repeated.

"Sometimes nights. Sometimes live-in for three days. It depends on who cancels and who's dying."

The word sat there between them, stripped of manners. Elena pressed her lips together after she said it, but Marcia preferred that to soft language. Celia had died. Nobody needed the sentence dressed for church.

For one ugly second Marcia resented the dead woman. Not for being hard, not for talking to waiters as if the world owed her volume, not even for paying just enough to make quitting impossible. She resented her for dying exactly when Marcia had needed her alive another month. The thought came and stayed long enough to be real before shame pushed at it. Shame did not erase it.

"Does the agency pay regular?" Marcia asked.

"My friend's mother likes the girl they send, so I assume she gets paid eventually."

"Eventually doesn't interest my landlord."

Elena leaned both palms on the counter. Her nails were cut short and bare. Marcia had only ever seen them painted pale pink before. Grief had reached even that far.

"I know," Elena said. "I don't have a clean solution for you."

"Didn't ask for clean."

They stood there with that. On the counter, a funeral brochure showed a silver cross and cream paper thick enough to survive rain. Marcia thought of the red city notice softening in her bag and almost laughed again.

Elena picked up the phone. "What's the best number for you right now?"

"Same one."

Elena typed, frowned, then hit send. "I told her you're reliable, you know how to transfer patients, you can handle meds, and you don't panic when old people get mean."

"That last one should go first."

This time Elena did laugh, a short unwilling sound that made her look younger and more tired. "My mother would've said the same."

Marcia looked past her into the den, at the dent in the rug where the bed had been. She saw herself there at two in the morning changing Celia's damp gown while the old woman complained that dying had ruined her skin tone. She saw the suction canister, the crushed ice chips, the tube of coral lipstick Celia insisted on before visitors. Labor stayed in a room after the body left. Rich people called that haunting when poor women called it work.

"If you hear of anything direct, not agency," Marcia said, "call me first. I can do bathing, lifting, wound care, dementia, all of it. I can cook if the people don't mind seasoning."

"I know what you can do."

The answer was simple. That almost made it kind.

Elena opened the refrigerator. Cold rolled out over the kitchen. "Take some food."

"No."

"Marcia."

Elena did not say it like charity. She said it like a woman with too much chicken and no stomach for one more casserole. Marcia hated that this made the offer harder to refuse. She looked at the trays and thought of Micah after school, eating like anger needed fuel. She thought of Delroy trying to swallow pain on a stomach lined with plain tea.

"Something small," she said.

Elena packed a foil container with arroz con pollo and plantains browned almost black at the edges, the way Celia liked them and Elena never had. She snapped the lid on, wiped condensation from the outside with a dish towel, and set it in Marcia's hands with the same brisk care she had once used passing over a bag of medication.

"Call the agency before five," she said. "Ask for Lina. If she doesn't pick up, call again. She's bad with voicemail."

"All right."

"And if you need me to confirm employment, text first. I'm going to be buried in family for the next few days."

Buried. Another word that refused to behave.

"I won't waste your time," Marcia said.

Elena's mouth changed at that, barely. "Do what you need to do."

On the bus stop bench outside, Marcia balanced the foil tray beside her and finally opened the envelope. The check amount was enough to embarrass a person. Enough to matter. Not enough to alter the story the landlord had already printed.

She looked at the numbers anyway. Rent. Late fee. Light bill. Delroy's pills. Tiana's bus card. Rice, oil, bleach. Every line item stepped forward at once, none of them ashamed.

She folded the check back into the envelope and took out her phone.

The first call to the agency went to a recording that sounded like it had been left by a woman wearing pearls. Marcia hung up before the instructions finished. She would call again. But first she went to the contact she had been trying not to count.

Kerene answered on the third ring, breathless, with a dryer roaring somewhere behind her.

"If you're selling me salvation, talk louder," she said.

"It's me."

"Cousin. Hold on."

The dryer noise muffled, then shifted. Marcia pictured her stepping out the back of some beauty shop into the wet heat, hand pressed to one ear, bright voice intact because bright voices got you treated better in new countries until they didn't.

"All right," Kerene said. "Tell me something good."

Marcia looked at the foil tray, the envelope, the bus route sign bolted crooked into the concrete.

"I don't have good today. I've got a question."

Kerene was quiet just long enough to prove she was not stupid.

"That kind of question."

"Yes."

"Say it plain."

Marcia rubbed the soft fold in the city notice with her thumb. "You still looking somewhere to stay."

"I'm staying somewhere now. I wouldn't call it looking if I had a better word. More like bracing."

"How bad."

"The lady from church has been kind enough for the story she tells on Sundays. But her sister arrived from Montego Bay last night with two children and an air mattress attitude. I can read a room, Marcia. My welcome is getting thin."

Despite herself, Marcia smiled. Then she stopped because smiling made this feel easier than it was.

"I've got space," she said.

The lie sat between them.

"You have a corner," Kerene corrected, not unkindly. "And if you're the one offering it, that means the rent is breathing down your neck."

"It already put its hand on my throat."

"How much can I help."

The question hit Marcia low. Not because of the money. Because of the quickness. Because Kerene had skipped gratitude and pride and gone straight to terms. That was respect disguised as roughness.

"If you come, you have to put in. I can't dress it up nicer than that."

"Don't. I didn't fly here to be anybody's delicate flower. I can put in every week if the salon girls stop acting like they invented braid spray."

Marcia heard the strain under the joke then, a raggedness around the edges like a dress hem beginning to fray. New arrival. No proper room. No stable address. America already charging interest.

"It's one room," Marcia said. "Bathroom, kitchenette, us. Tiana has the little back corner with the curtain. That would have to stop being just hers."

"Ah."

Just that. Not complaint. Not surprise. A woman measuring the cost before she agreed to owe it.

"I don't want the girl hating me from day one," Kerene said.

"She might anyway."

"Fair enough."

Traffic hissed through a puddle at the curb though it had not rained. Marcia watched a boy in a school polo kick at the bus-bench leg with the side of his shoe, over and over, as if irritation were something he could polish into shine.

"I've got one suitcase," Kerene said. "One big bag of hair and brushes. And the folding cot Miss Althea says must leave with me today because she's tired of looking at it in her dining room. I can come tonight if you mean tonight."

"I mean tonight."

"Then tell me the truth before I show up. Is this help, or is this emergency."

Marcia looked at the agency card with its soft blue logo and the word compassionate in curling script. She thought of the landlord's finger resting on the printed notice.

"Both."

Kerene let out a breath that could have been a laugh if it had more joy in it. "Good. I hate when people lie first and explain later."

"You sure."

"No," Kerene said. "But sure isn't what I've got to work with."

That honesty felt better than comfort would have.

"Bring whatever can fit."

"That's what everybody in immigration and housing been saying to me since I land."

"Kerene."

"I'm joking. A little." The bright tone flickered back on. "Text me the gate. And don't apologize when I reach there. It wastes time."

After they hung up, Marcia sat another minute with the phone in her hand. She should have called Tiana first. She knew that. She knew it so clearly that the failure had already happened. But Tiana would ask what exactly was changing and where exactly her things would go, and Marcia did not yet have answers that sounded like a mother rather than a clerk.

She typed, `Come straight home after work. We need to move some things.` Tiana answered almost immediately.

`What things`

Marcia stared at the screen until the bus came.

She put the phone away without replying.

***

By the time she got back, the apartment smelled of pain cream, old fan dust, and the chicken she had set in the oven to warm through. Delroy sat in the plastic chair near the door in basketball shorts and no shirt, a fresh strip of white tape crossing his shoulder where Tiana had rewrapped him before leaving for her shift. The tape did nothing for the swelling. His right arm hung in the sling like a sentence being served.

He looked up from the dominoes box he had been pretending to reorganize one-handed.

"You talked to him."

"I talked. He heard paperwork."

"How bad."

"Friday bad."

Micah, sprawled on his stomach with his phone in front of him, let out a low whistle without lifting his head. "That's the kind that comes fast."

"You have homework?" Marcia asked.

"School don't even want me this much. Let's not lie to each other."

"Then find something quiet to do."

He rolled over and sat up. "You got food, though?"

Marcia uncovered the foil tray on the counter. Micah made a sound of reverence loud enough to be rude. Delroy laughed despite himself and then winced, pressing his good hand to the taped shoulder.

"Don't make me laugh," he said. "This house is hostile."

Marcia set plates out because feeding people was easier than speaking to them. When she finally said Kerene's name, she did it with her back to the room.

"Kerene's coming tonight."

Micah straightened. "Which Kerene. The cousin with the good wigs or the one who sells perfume from her trunk."

"Good wigs," Marcia said.

"All right then." He grinned. "House upgrading."

Delroy's face changed more slowly. "Coming to visit."

"Coming to stay."

He looked at the curtain that marked off Tiana's narrow corner. He did not have to ask the next part. The room said it for him.

"Marcia."

"Don't start."

"I'm not starting. I'm asking where."

"In the same square footage God already gave us."

"That's not an answer."

She turned then. "The answer is rent. The answer is Friday. The answer is your shoulder can't earn and my dead lady can't hire. Pick one if you want poetry."

Micah's grin disappeared. Delroy looked as if she had slapped him in public. For a second she wanted to take it back, not because it was false but because truth got ugly fast in a hot room.

He swallowed. "How much she putting in."

"Enough that I had to make the call."

That shut him down. Money reached him where pride usually blocked the road. He looked at the dominoes box again, lids and tiles all mixed together because his left hand could not sort neatly.

Micah cleared his throat. "I can crash by the kitchenette more regular. I don't care."

Marcia looked at him. He shrugged one shoulder, pretending generosity cost nothing.

"You care," she said.

"Not enough to be outside."

That was his better self, rough and quick and embarrassed to be seen working.

Tiana came in twenty minutes later with her work shirt damp down the spine and her hair pulled back into the tight puff she wore when she did not have energy left for vanity. The minute she saw the shifted milk crates and the curtain rod taken halfway down, her face altered.

"Why is my room open."

Marcia dried her hands on the dish towel. "Come inside first."

"I am inside."

Moderate contraction in her voice even angry, Marcia noticed absurdly, because she had raised that girl to sound employable. Tiana set her tote by the door without taking her eyes off the back corner.

"What's happening."

"Kerene needs somewhere to stay. She'll be here soon."

Tiana looked from Marcia to Delroy, then to the half-lowered curtain and the milk crate that held her extra notebooks, skin products, and the cheap twin XL sheet set she had bought on sale in June because she had once believed in August.

"Soon meaning what."

"Soon meaning tonight."

"You might have told me before you started dismantling my room."

"If I'd waited till you got home, the room wouldn't have grown another wall."

"That's not what I said."

"It's what the day said."

Tiana laughed once with no humor in it. "Of course. The day said it, so nobody had to ask me."

Micah stood up. "You want me to move the crate or not, because I feel like this about to become an argument where nobody eats."

"Move it," Marcia said.

Tiana bent and grabbed the other side before Micah could. "I can lift my own things."

The crate scraped the floor. Bottles clicked inside. Marcia watched her daughter carry her life two feet to the left and set it down beside the kitchenette trash can. It was not the distance that hurt. It was the casualness forced on the motion, as if futures could be tidied into new corners whenever rent demanded.

"Where am I sleeping," Tiana asked.

"Here still. You and Kerene will share the back."

"Share." Tiana said the word carefully, as if testing whether it dissolved in the mouth. "Right."

Delroy tried for softness. "Temporary thing, baby girl."

She turned on him with a precision that made Marcia want to look away. "Temporary has been the family religion all summer."

Nobody had anything better than that.

The knock came just then: two quick raps, then a third after a pause, as if the hand at the door had thought better of seeming eager. Micah got there first.

Kerene came in sideways because of the cot. It was folded flat and tied with pink extension cord, one metal leg working loose and clanging against the doorframe. A hard-shell suitcase rolled behind her with green yarn knotted around the handle to identify it. She wore jeans, a white ribbed tank, and a mustard headwrap that had slipped enough to show the clean black braid lines at her scalp. The heat had beaten the brightness from her makeup, but her mouth was still painted a brave reddish brown. One sandal strap had rubbed the skin above her heel raw and she was pretending not to limp.

"Look at this," she said, letting the cot thump against the wall. "I leave one dining room and arrive with furniture. I'm becoming American too fast."

Micah barked a laugh. Even Tiana's mouth twitched before she stopped it.

"Come in," Marcia said.

Kerene stepped fully over the threshold and looked around once, fast and complete. Not judging. Calculating. The room, the bodies in it, the heat, the tension. Marcia respected her for not pretending she saw nothing.

"Delroy," Kerene said, giving him a little chin lift. "You look half handsome, half lawsuit."

"Only half handsome?" he said.

"The tape ruined the ratio."

That got the laugh from him too. It also bought her ten seconds of air, which Marcia saw and appreciated. Charm was work. Some people did it so prettily you forgot it had wages.

Kerene bent to unzip the suitcase, then hissed and straightened again, rubbing the back of her calf. "If anybody asks, this limp is fashion. Miami buses are wicked."

She pulled out a loaf of hard dough bread wrapped in a grocery bag and held it up. "I didn't come empty-handed. That would've made my grandmother rise straight out the grave and slap me."

"You can set that by the stove," Marcia said.

Kerene's gaze found Tiana then, and the room sharpened around the two of them.

"You're Tiana." Kerene's voice lost some performance. "You've grown right past all the photos."

"So have you," Tiana said.

It was not rude. It was not warm either. Marcia could live with that. Warmth asked too much too soon.

"This is your space I'm trespassing in," Kerene said. "I know that already."

Tiana folded her arms. "You're not trespassing if my mother invited you."

"Fine. Then I'm being legally inconvenient."

Micah snorted. Delroy put his head back against the chair as if the laugh cost less than the silence would have.

Marcia took the bread and set it on the counter. When she turned back, Kerene had already dug a folded wad of twenties from the side pocket of her suitcase. The bills were smoothed but not flat, beauty-shop money, edges soft from being tucked and untucked all day.

"Take this first," she said quietly, holding it out to Marcia. "Before I sit down and start acting like a guest."

Marcia did not move.

"Kerene."

"Take it. I got more work lined up this week if the girl in North Miami stops pretending she can braid faster than me. This is what I've got in hand."

The room watched. Even Micah understood enough to become suddenly interested in the loose metal leg of the cot.

Marcia took the money. It was not enough to fix Friday. It was enough to feel. Worse, it was enough to count.

"All right," she said.

Kerene nodded once, transaction done, and immediately reached for the cot as if movement could wipe away the intimacy of cash. "Show me where this glamorous bed going."

The back corner had once been a garage storage niche. Delroy had hung a tension rod years ago and Marcia had sewn a curtain from two different cream sheets, neither one white anymore. Tiana's mattress lay against the wall under the small window. Above it, thumbtacks held up a campus map of Gainesville, a pharmacy receipt with dates circled, and a glossy postcard from UF that showed laughing students carrying books nobody in Marcia's house could yet afford. Beside the mattress sat the plastic bin where Tiana had been storing bought-a-little-at-a-time escape: twin XL sheets, a set of navy towels, shower caddy, cheap flip-flops for communal bathrooms she still hoped to meet.

Kerene saw all of that in one glance. Her face did not change, but the charm slipped quiet for the first time.

"You sure," she asked Marcia, so low only the nearest people could hear, "this is where you want to put me."

"No," Marcia said. "It's what I've got."

That honesty seemed to steady them both.

They moved fast because slowness would have made the indignity ceremonial. Micah hauled the bin to the top of the wardrobe. Tiana took the campus map down herself and slid it between two textbooks without folding it. Delroy tried to stand and help with the cot, failed halfway up, and sat again hard, jaw tight with humiliation. Kerene pretended not to notice him failing; that was another kindness. She and Marcia unfolded the cot together. The metal frame snapped open with a report like a bad idea becoming official.

"Jesus," Micah said. "That thing sound like jail."

"Then sleep polite and it won't arrest you," Kerene said.

The cot fit only if Tiana's milk-crate desk moved out and the plastic drawer tower shifted nearer the window, blocking half the breeze when any breeze came. Once they wedged it in, the back corner no longer belonged to one girl dreaming north. It belonged to need. Need took up room differently. Need had elbows.

Tiana stood with her toiletry bag hanging from two fingers and stared at the arrangement.

"So that's it."

Marcia wiped her forehead with the inside of her wrist. "For now."

"You keep saying temporary like the word itself builds a door."

"And you keep saying things I already know."

Tiana looked at her then, full on, not as daughter to mother but as one woman taking the measure of another under pressure.

"No," she said. "You know rent. You don't know what it feels like to come home and watch the place where you kept yourself get turned into overflow."

The sentence hit with surgical cleanliness. Marcia wanted to answer with age, with sacrifice, with every meal she had gone light on so the children could eat heavy. She wanted to say You think I don't know displacement because I am the one holding the tape. She wanted to remind Tiana who had measured those twin XL sheets against sale circulars and hidden them from Micah so he would not use them to wipe his sneakers. She wanted to say a hundred true things.

Instead she said the truest ugly one.

"If I had another room, you'd have it. I've got this one."

Tiana blinked. That hurt her more than anger would have. Anger she could fight. Facts only sat there.

Kerene picked up the toiletry bag gently from Tiana's hand because Tiana had loosened her grip without noticing.

"Listen," she said, not bright now, not performing. "If this feels bad, it should. It's bad. I'm still grateful and it's still bad. Both can stay in the room."

Tiana let out a breath. "I know you're not the problem."

"No," Kerene said. "But I'm definitely the furniture."

This time the laugh that moved through the room was small enough not to betray anybody. Sometimes that was the best a family could do.

Marcia turned back to the stove because if she kept looking at Tiana's face she would either apologize or harden, and neither one would feed anybody. She sliced the hard dough bread, warmed the chicken, stretched the plantains by cutting them thinner than honesty. From behind her she could hear Micah asking Kerene if the salon had any customers who tipped stupid. She could hear Kerene answering that American women paid forty dollars to look effortless and then acted surprised when effort cost money. She could hear Delroy trying his charm again, slower now, careful not to sound like a man too injured to offer anything but stories.

At the table, which was really a card table with one leg shorter than the others, Kerene ate like a person who had been careful all day and had run out of patience for caution. She closed her eyes on the first bite of chicken and said, "Thank God for dead rich people's casseroles."

Marcia nearly choked. Micah laughed so hard he slapped the table. Even Tiana covered her mouth.

"You really don't waste time," Delroy said.

"I came from a house where people were grieving with ham," Kerene said. "I'm not romantic about class. Pass the bread."

The room loosened by one notch. Not much. Enough to breathe differently.

Later, after Micah had dragged his pallet closer to the kitchenette and Delroy had swallowed the last pain pill with tap water he pretended was rum, the apartment went through the awkward business of becoming night in its new shape. The curtain no longer closed clean because the cot nudged the rod at an angle. A triangle of space stayed open near the floor no matter how Marcia tucked the cloth. Through it she could see Tiana's bare feet near the mattress and Kerene's suitcase standing upright like a witness.

Marcia washed the foil pan from Elena's house because she could not stand waking to rich-people aluminum in her sink. The water ran warm almost immediately. Behind the curtain, voices dropped low.

"You can take the cot and I'll stay on the floor tonight," Kerene said.

"No. Then my mother will come in here feeling guilty every ten minutes."

"She already feels guilty every ten minutes."

"I know."

Silence. Then the squeak of the cot as Kerene sat on it, testing.

"Miami really does charge for everything," Kerene said quietly. "Bus, room, food, dignity. Breathing feels next."

"You noticed that fast."

"I'm from Jamaica, not the moon."

That got a breath of laughter from Tiana. Marcia stood very still with her hands in the dishwater.

"You were really going to Gainesville?" Kerene asked after a moment.

"I still might."

"Good."

"Maybe."

"Also good. Maybe is still alive."

Marcia dried the pan slowly. The words were ordinary. The ache under them was not. She thought of the plastic bin on top of the wardrobe, of the twin XL sheets bought one payday at a time, of Tiana's folder leaning now against the refrigerator because there was nowhere else flat enough to keep it safe. She thought of taking money from Kerene's hand and feeling relieved before ashamed. She thought of Delroy in the next room, breathing through pain he had bought trying to become useful again.

When she switched off the main light, the apartment rearranged itself into shadows and sound. Delroy's breath came rough and drugged from the mattress near the wall. Micah's headphones leaked a tinny snare pattern until Marcia told him once to turn it down and, miracle of miracles, he did. A plane moved somewhere far off, low enough to make the window tremble. From behind the curtain came the rustle of two women who were kin and strangers at once, each trying not to take up more than her share of an impossible square of air.

Marcia lay down beside Delroy without touching him. The money from Kerene sat folded inside the rent envelope under her pillow, close enough to count by feel, too little to save them and too much to dismiss. Friday waited only a handful of sleeps away. The room held six different breaths now and not one place where a person could keep a future entirely to herself.
