# Chapter 3

By five-thirty the lot had already started smelling like diesel, bitter coffee in Styrofoam, and the minty grease men rubbed into knees they swore were fine. Pickup headlights cut across the chain-link fence and moved on. The men left standing in the wash of those lights all made the same small adjustment when a truck slowed: shoulders back, stomachs in, faces arranged into something between readiness and indifference. Nobody wanted to look desperate. Desperation scared off contractors almost as fast as age.

Delroy stood near the front without crowding it. He had put on the faded gray T-shirt from an old Coral Gables remodel because a shirt with a company name on it still suggested invoices, regular jobs, a version of himself who had keys to houses with alarm systems and women who handed him cold water in real glasses. The logo had cracked into pale islands across his chest, but it was better than showing up in a plain white tee like he had come only to carry other men's shit out to a truck.

His canvas tool bag sat by his sneaker, not too full. If it was heavy enough to advertise skill, it would pull on the bad shoulder and betray him before anybody asked a question. He had rubbed blue gel into the joint in the dark kitchen before leaving the house. The smell still rose off him each time he shifted. It wasn't pain yet, not exactly. It was the warning before pain, the hinge talking.

He had stepped around the light bill Marcia left flat on the table and gone out while she and the children were still asleep, though Marcia never really slept once worry took hold of a room. She lay still and listened to what money was not doing. The red notice on the garage door had changed the air inside the place. It had made the bathroom seem smaller, the hot plate meaner, the patched cracks in the wall less like poor people's decoration and more like evidence. Three months sounded like time if you said it fast. It sounded like already too late if you stood in the lot before sunrise with fifty men and watched how easily a city decided which backs were still worth renting.

A thin Honduran kid in a Dolphins cap kept calling home between trucks, checking on a daughter with fever. Each time voicemail picked up, he kissed his teeth and tucked the phone back into the waistband of his jeans. Two Haitian men stood beside Delroy in paint-splashed boots, one of them broad as a refrigerator, the other narrow and elegant, with a funeral-home tie folded neatly inside his back pocket so it would not catch dust before church that night. A Black American boy, maybe twenty, maybe younger, had his barber cape still looped from his backpack zipper and was trying to laugh his way through the wait, making jokes about the mosquitoes getting first pick today. Men answered when they felt like it. Mostly they watched the road.

A pool-service van nosed to the curb and stayed idling. The passenger leaned out, looked over the crowd once, and said, "Need two Spanish. Fast-fast." The broad Haitian stepped forward anyway. The passenger shook his head and crooked two fingers at the Honduran kid and another narrow boy behind him. "No, no. Not that Spanish. Lady hears island, she makes that blank face. I need house Spanish. Easy." When Delroy moved, the man cut a palm at him. "Not you either, bossman. Too much gray. She'll start hollering insurance." The van rolled off with both boys hanging off the back doors, already hired and already cut down to fit the job.

Nobody in the lot ever posted the rules. "Need two Spanish" meant the Honduran kid and not the Haitian. "Homeowner sees gray" meant Delroy and every other man the city had already started pricing out.

The first truck rolled in at quarter to six, a white F-150 with ladders clattering in the rack. The driver, a heavyset man with dried white caulk trapped in the hair on his forearms, leaned out the window and shouted in Spanish for six roofers. He scanned the yard as he spoke, already counting bodies before his words finished. Men moved toward him in a burst. Delroy stepped up too. Roof work was not his best thing, but he had done worse for cash.

"Boss, I can do it," he said. "You need somebody who knows how not to mash up a whole house."

The man gave him half a look. His eyes flicked to Delroy's face, then lower, to the tool bag, then to the shoulder Delroy had tried not to hold too carefully. "Need roof legs," he said. "Young. Spanish. No lessons."

"Spanish can learn from me in five minutes."

That got a snort from one of the boys climbing into the truck bed, but the driver had already pointed past Delroy. "You. You. Blue shirt. Move."

The ladder rack rattled off with six younger men and the Honduran kid hanging from the side rail, still dialing home one-handed as the truck pulled away. Delroy kept smiling half a second too long after it was gone. Then he bent, lifted his tool bag, and set it back down in a different spot as if location had been the problem.

By six-fifteen the lot grew louder. Men who had not been chosen yet started talking more because silence was beginning to feel superstitious. A man selling coffee from a cooler walked the fence line and shouted, "One dollar, black as your landlord heart." Delroy bought none. He had eight dollars in his wallet and needed those eight to remain whole until they had company.

Another contractor skimmed by in a red pickup calling landscaping at eighty cash. Delroy gave him half a step anyway. The driver laughed through the open window. "Nah, boss. Need yard dogs. Bent all day, no bitching." Delroy let that one go. Eighty for full sun and somebody else's bougainvillea thorns was insult money, and he was not yet ready to admit insult money had become real money. Three men climbed in anyway, their gloves already on. The truck rolled off with sod trembling in the bed, and Delroy hated how fast his pride started looking like a luxury item.

He shifted his weight and the shoulder answered now, a hot wire from collarbone to elbow. He rolled it once. Beside him, the elegant Haitian man with the folded tie said, not looking over, "Don't do that. They see."

Delroy glanced at him. Up close the man had a crescent scar in one eyebrow and smelled faintly of the clove gum he kept tucked in his cheek. "What, Miami's hiring by x-ray now?"

"Miami's hiring by any excuse."

The man got chosen ten minutes later by a stucco crew that needed two laborers and one man who could keep the younger fools from falling off a scaffold. He climbed in without gloating, only touched two fingers to the tie in his pocket like he was checking it was still there. Delroy hated him a little for leaving clean.

By seven the sun had burned the softness off the morning. Heat pooled in the blacktop and came back up through shoe soles. A box truck stopped. The man in it had a lizard tattoo crawling out of his collar and a cigarette burned almost to the filter between two fingers stained yellow. "House cleanout," he said. "Need strong back. No talking. Seventy-five and lunch if the owner don't act funny."

"How heavy?" somebody called.

"Heavy enough."

Delroy stepped forward before he had decided to. "Strong back right here."

The tattooed man looked at him, then at the kid with the barber cape, then back again. "You serious with that shoulder, old head?"

For one stupid second Delroy nearly asked how the man knew. Then he remembered himself rubbing it. He dropped the arm. "Shoulder's fine."

"Then quit petting it. I need a man who'll drag a fridge and shut up after."

The kid with the cape laughed so hard he had to slap his own thigh. Not cruel, exactly. Young. The worst kind of witness. Delroy felt the humiliation like heat under his shirt.

"Because I don't waste motion," he said.

"I need waste motion today." The driver jerked his chin at the younger boy. "You. And you. And the little joker too. Come."

The kid hopped into the truck bed and, to his credit, did not meet Delroy's eyes again. That almost made it worse. Delroy had become something polite people avoided looking at directly.

He kept his mouth in its half-smile and let the morning keep insulting him. Jobs like his were supposed to come later, by phone, through men who knew his work and did not need a cattle call to find it. That had been true in other years. The lot kept correcting him. Worse, the shoulder had stopped being private. When the next truck slowed, two men nearer the curb glanced at his arm before they looked at his face.

There was a time he would not have stood here at all. There had been kitchens in Kendall where he laid tile straight as sheet music. There had been bathrooms in the Gables where women twice Marcia's age called him Mr. Campbell and asked his opinion on marble, as if he had not learned most of what he knew on jobs where nobody wore a permit on their belt. He had spent good years making rich people believe their houses reflected taste instead of money. That work had thinned. Shoulder first, then age, then the little humiliations that multiplied until a man found himself in a lot with his jokes growing stale in his own mouth.

A dark blue van pulled in close to the curb. The passenger window came down. A pale Cuban woman in mirrored sunglasses leaned across the driver and spoke rapid Spanish. Delroy caught enough to understand they wanted two men to move appliances and one to patch drywall after. Then she switched to English for the numbers. "Condo job. Pay goes Friday when management clears it. Need one drywall man who can wait."

"Drywall I can do," he said. "Texture too."

She looked straight at him this time. "Can you float till Friday, yes or no?"

The five dollars left in his wallet suddenly felt as loud as coins in a collection plate. "Today is Monday."

"Then you're not my drywall man," she said. "Management pays when management pays. I need somebody who can absorb that."

The driver barked a laugh. The woman did not. She only pointed to a younger man in a clean polo who said yes before the last word left her mouth. The van eased off with him and two movers inside. Delroy watched his own reflection shrink in the mirrored lens of her sunglasses and then disappear.

Even the work that fit his hands needed a man who could afford to wait to be paid.

By eight there were only leftovers in the lot now, the men nobody had matched themselves to yet, or who had once been matched and no longer were. An old Bahamian with swollen ankles. A wiry Jamaican who smelled of Wray and had started cursing every departing truck in detail. A Venezuelan father with a Hello Kitty lunchbox that had belonged, judging by the glitter sticker half-peeled off the side, to a child who had once cared very much what color a thing was. Delroy knew some of them by face, some by first name, some only by the shape of their defeats. They drifted apart as the morning advanced, ashamed of being seen with the last of the stock.

The Wray-smelling Jamaican came over and squinted at Delroy's shirt. "Did you ever do that Gables condo on Alhambra? Gray tile? Lady had a dog that looked richer than me?"

"Maybe," Delroy said.

"I thought I knew your face. Claude asked for you a couple weeks back."

Delroy kept his expression loose. "Claude asks for everybody when he's short a body."

"True enough." The man scratched under his hard hat, which he wore though nobody had hired him. "He said demo's coming."

"Demo's always coming."

The man gave a shrug that said suit yourself and moved on. Delroy kept his face easy, but the name tightened his throat. Last year he had watched Claude stop a plasterer outside the Chevron on Sixty-Second and ask, soft as prayer, whether Friday still meant Friday. Three laborers by the ice machine had heard every word while pretending to study lottery tickets. By Monday everybody in the lot knew the plasterer had taken advance money and gone missing for a weekend. Claude never had to shout if shame could travel for him. Delroy had managed, so far, not to become one of Claude's examples.

Nine o'clock came. The coffee seller was packing his empty cooler. The shade beside the fence had shrunk to a mean strip. Delroy stood a little longer because leaving too early felt like confessing the day was over, and he was not ready to tell the truth to himself before he told it nowhere else. Finally he bent for the tool bag again. This time the shoulder caught hard enough to blur one edge of his vision.

He straightened slowly and pretended he had only meant to retie his lace.

The coffee seller snapped his cooler shut. A truck on the avenue grumbled past without turning in. The canvas handle sweated in Delroy's palm while he stood there pretending for nobody.

He slung the bag across his left side and started walking toward the strip mall on the corner, where a Jamaican bakery sat between a tax office and a store that sold used phone cases nobody trusted. His stomach had begun to gnaw. He had left the house on one cup of sweet coffee and a lie to himself about how the day would go.

The bakery's air-conditioner had lost the war years ago, but stepping inside still felt like being let slightly off the hook. The place smelled of patties, frying oil, old flour, and the bleach somebody had sloshed across the tile after opening. A radio behind the counter muttered morning news over static. Delroy ordered one beef patty and a small cola, then did the math as the girl at the register rang him up. Three dollars gone. Five left.

He carried the plate outside and found Claude Brown sitting at one of the metal tables under the awning, legs stretched, reading glasses hanging on a cord against his chest though he was not reading anything. Claude drank from a pink insulated water bottle printed with fading cartoon mermaids. He had once announced to a whole domino table that cold water did not turn a man soft because the cup was girlish, and the phrase had been stupid enough to keep surviving in Delroy's memory. Claude looked up from his phone and grinned as if Delroy had arrived for an appointment.

"There he is," Claude said. "King of the no-call, no-show himself."

Delroy did not break stride until he reached the table. "If I was a no-show, why are you seeing me?"

"Because Miami humiliates a man in public if you wait long enough."

Claude laughed at his own line, a dry smoker's bark that ended in a wheeze he never treated with the inhaler clipped to his shirt pocket. He wore a lime-green fishing shirt too clean for the lot and jeans dusted red at the cuffs from some site he had visited without working. Claude liked staying one degree removed from grime. It let him talk like management without anybody forgetting he came from the same bottom shelf.

Delroy sat because standing would have made the encounter look accidental. "You came here to feed me or just admire the view?"

"Both, maybe. I saw you over there trying to sell luxury labor to men shopping clearance."

Delroy bit into the patty before answering. Pepper and hot grease hit his tongue. It almost helped. "What I've got, I've got. I can't turn twenty-five because a fool with a pickup wants cheap knees."

"No," Claude said. "But you can stop advertising that you know what a fair price looks like. That's what scares them."

The line landed too close to the truth. Delroy washed the bite down with warm cola. "You're always this cheerful before noon?"

"Only when the sermon already writes itself."

Claude tilted his phone so Delroy could see a list of names in the notes app, some checked off, some not. Delroy saw his own first name halfway down, unmarked. Claude locked the screen again before Delroy could ask why looking at that list felt so much like standing on a hook.

"What do you want?" Delroy said.

"See, that's why I like you. Other men round the thing with pride until hunger starts speaking plain. You at least come to the gate faster."

"I'm sitting at the table because the shade is free."

"Not free. Nothing here is free. Not even shade. Shade belongs to the bakery owner and the city permit and the man who poured this concrete." Claude leaned back and sipped from the mermaid bottle. "But all right. Since we're telling stories, let me tell one. I got a motel gut coming later in the summer, maybe end of July if the paperwork finally coughs itself into action. Airport side. Interior demo only. Drywall, cabinets, old bathroom tile, ugly carpet, all the sins. Two long days if the inspector doesn't start acting holy."

Delroy kept eating, though slower now. "And?"

"And I need bodies I don't have to chase across three neighborhoods once the dumpster lands."

"So hire bodies."

"I'm hiring bodies. I'm talking to one."

Delroy let the silence work a second. Claude watched him with amusement so patient it was almost affectionate. He said it in the old-work voice, the one that belonged to domino tables and half-finished roofs after rain. Delroy could almost smell white rum and wet plywood, and that made the hook worse.

"Demo pays trash money," Delroy said.

"This one pays better."

"Better meaning what?"

"Better meaning enough that you're still listening."

Delroy wiped a thumb across the flaky crust on his plate. His first instinct was to bargain, his second to joke, his third to pretend he had other options. Even now he could feel the old sequence lining up in him, smile first, price talk after, hand never still. "If the work is so nice, why are you scouting from a bakery table like a vulture with data?"

"Because good crews stay booked. Because my regular drywall boys don't want demo dust in their lungs. Because half the men in that lot can't show up two mornings in a row unless their mother threatens them. Pick the reason you like." Claude smiled. "And because I know you need money before your face starts telling the truth."

Delroy looked out toward the road. A bus sighed at the stop and opened its doors onto nobody. "Everybody needs money."

"Not like you."

There it was. Claude did not say it loud, but he never needed volume to make a thing public. The bakery napkin stuck to Delroy's damp thumb while the room at home came at him in flashes: Marcia at the sink before work, rinsing out the same two good food containers because buying new ones would be stupidity; Tiana crossing the room with her folder tucked under her arm like paper could force doors; the red notice nailed bright on the garage door, Micah joking about framing it so at least the government message would look like decor. None of that belonged at this table. Claude could smell household trouble the way sharks got described in bad documentaries. Delroy hated giving him anything to feed on.

"Say what you came to say, nuh," Delroy said.

Claude reached into his shirt pocket and brought out a narrow receipt book, the kind mechanics used. That more than anything made Delroy want to leave. A man ready with paper had already decided the conversation. Claude flipped to a blank slip, clicked his pen, and started writing before the terms were fully spoken.

"I can front you four hundred now," he said. "Advance against the demo. Not gift. Not friend-friend softness. Advance."

Delroy gave a short laugh. "Four hundred? You think I look that bad?"

"Worse. Four hundred is me being respectful."

Claude kept writing. He spoke as he moved the pen, each line a small nail. "When the motel job confirms, you answer on first ring. You show up wherever I say, when I say. First four hundred of your cut comes back to me before you buy a beer, before you buy a screw, before you carry home one pretty grocery bag to act like Providence visited the house. If you disappear, I don't go searching dramatic. I just remember. I let other men remember too."

He tore the pink copy free and slid the white original toward Delroy. On it he had written:

`Advance: $400`

`Worker: Delroy Campbell`

`Job: airport motel interior demo`

`Repay from first payout`

`On call when notified`

Under that was Claude's signature, wide and theatrical, and a space left blank.

Delroy stared at the paper. The itemization was almost funny. Pink carbon had smudged Claude's thumbprint near the margin. The slip looked neat enough for brake pads or an oil change, not four hundred dollars tied around a man's neck. Claude squared it to the table before nudging it closer, like straight edges could keep the stink off.

"You're carrying a receipt book now?" Delroy said. "What next, office downtown?"

"Don't mock efficiency. Efficiency is why I can help when help gets expensive."

"Help."

"Call it what soothes you. I don't care. Sign if you want the four."

Delroy did not touch the pen. "And if the job delays?"

"Then the debt delays. The debt don't vanish."

"Suppose I get something else before then."

Claude's grin widened, not kindly. "Then you better get something good enough that four hundred doesn't matter, because once my money's in your pocket, your calendar and my phone become cousins."

It should have made Delroy stand up. It should have pushed him back into the sun with his remaining five dollars and his injured dignity, sent him somewhere else. Instead he sat there with patty grease drying on his thumb while, across the street, a man dragged a split mattress to the curb and let it flop beside a busted fan. The four hundred on Claude's side of the table already had weight; Delroy could feel it in his chest before it ever touched his pocket.

Marcia's face arrived anyway, not shouting, never first. It would hold still too long. Then she would get exact. What lots did you hit. Who was hiring. Did you call Mr. Lewis from the Kendall backsplash. Is the shoulder worse. Her questions were never only questions. They came trimmed neat as bill stubs.

The red paper would still be on the door when he got home. Rent would still sit on the household like a heavy man refusing to move his feet. Micah still needed school shoes soon. Tiana would still be carrying that folder around like paperwork might get tired of humiliating her and finally behave.

His thumb pressed harder into the paper plate. Under that pressure the four hundred split itself clean: light bill first, then groceries, then one night where Marcia opened the cupboard and found something heavier than a can of tea and two dry onions.

"If I sign," Delroy said, "you don't bring my name to my front gate. Whatever's between me and you stays between me and you."

Claude leaned back and looked almost offended. "You think I like domestic theater? I hate people's children staring at me like I came to steal Christmas dinner. I come to the front gate only when a man starts acting forgetful."

"I'm not forgetful."

"You're charming. Sometimes that's the same disease."

Delroy nearly smiled. Claude saw it and pressed.

"Listen to me proper. I'm not doing you dirty. Dirty is leaving you in that lot till noon and then letting some cousin tell me later you're short on rent and too proud to say it straight. Dirty is pretending friendship means I should front cash and then beg for it back like a church sister collecting pledge. This stays clean. Terms now, peace later."

"Peace later for who?"

"For the man who shows up on demo day."

Claude tapped the pen against the receipt book. Not impatient. Certain. Delroy hated certainty in other men when his own had gone thin.

He took the pen.

The signature looked older than he felt until he saw it there. Delroy Campbell, the D tall, the y tail dragging. Claude tore off the second copy and folded the original into the receipt book. Then he reached into the cargo pocket of his jeans and pulled out four crisp hundred-dollar bills wrapped with a bank band.

That startled Delroy more than the paper had. Cash that clean meant Claude had expected this outcome before Delroy sat down.

"You're walking around with four hundred waiting on me?"

"Not waiting on you only. Waiting on whichever man lost the morning and still had enough sense left to know it."

Claude set the bills on the table but kept two fingers on them. "One more thing."

Of course there was one more thing. Delroy looked at the money and said nothing.

"You don't come to the motel trying to be foreman," Claude said. "You do the work in front of you. If I tell you to strip bathrooms, you strip bathrooms. If I tell you to load the dumpster, you load the dumpster. No speeches about how you used to lay tile for ladies with poodles. No shoulder drama unless the arm actually falls off."

"You're selling money or comedy?"

"Both. Since you enjoy comedy for free."

Claude released the bills.

Delroy put his hand over them. The paper felt cool despite the heat, smooth and almost refrigerated. The bank band pressed into his palm. Forty could go under the light bill. Rice could hit the table with a decent thump. Micah could paw through the breadbox and find more than heels. Tiana could open the cupboard and find something with weight in it. The question waiting behind all that was the same one. There was no clean story attached to these bills, and he was tired enough to take them anyway.

"All right," he said.

"All right what?"

Claude loved a witness, even when there were only flies and traffic nearby. Delroy pocketed the money before answering, forcing Claude to take the concession without ceremony. "All right, when the call comes, I answer."

"On first ring."

"If I'm in the bathroom, Claude, the second ring will still save civilization."

"You see? This is why receipts are needed."

Claude laughed again, coughed, took a hard pull from the ridiculous pink bottle, and then rubbed the center of his chest as if amused air had gone down wrong. For a second he looked his age, or older. Then the expression shut. He was back to being the man with names in his phone and work traveling toward him like weather.

"Spend it smart," he said. "Not flashy. Flashy attracts questions."

Delroy stood. "You're coaching me in deception now?"

"No. I'm respecting your marriage."

That one made Delroy look at him sharp. Claude raised both hands, innocent as a preacher in a tax scandal.

"I didn't say nothing," Claude said. "I know married men. Money appears in a house, women ask what tree bears it."

"My wife asks because she has sense."

"Exactly. So take some of that money home in pieces. Groceries. Light bill. Maybe one little thing for the boy so the story smells nicer." Claude shrugged. "Or tell the truth. Honesty is always available. Just not popular."

Delroy should have walked away then. Instead he said, "You ever tried minding your own blasted business?"

Claude grinned. "This is my business."

Outside the awning, the late morning had thickened into the kind of heat that made parked cars seem angry. Delroy slid the four bills into the side pocket of the tool bag under a tape measure and a set of dull drill bits. The bank band disappeared under steel and dust. He crossed to the family-dollar store two doors down and bought rice, a pack of toilet paper, and the cheap dish soap Marcia hated because it went watery halfway through the bottle. The white rice sack bulged against the thin plastic bag. The toilet paper made the whole load look domestic, harmless. He could set that on the table first and let the tool bag stay behind his leg.

By the time he cut down the alley behind the house, sweat had glued his shirt between his shoulder blades. The red notice still burned on the garage door. Water ran inside. Delroy hitched the tool bag farther behind his leg, balanced the rice where it would show first, and went in.

Marcia stood at the sink in work pants and a faded house T-shirt, rinsing out two plastic food containers until the lids squeaked under her thumbs. The apartment smelled of bleach, onion, and the wet concrete coolness the place kept in its floor even at noon. She looked at the groceries first, then at him.

"You found something."

"Little bit," Delroy said. He set the rice on the table like proof. "Patch job. Quick one."

"Where?"

"Hialeah."

Her eyes dropped to the tool bag. He tucked it against his calf. Marcia turned off the tap. "What kind of patch job pays for rice, toilet paper, and that nasty soap all before lunch?"

"The kind I said yes to."

He forced a laugh and pulled two twenties from his wallet, laying them beside the light bill on the table. "Put that toward FPL."

Marcia did not touch the money. "Who paid you, Delroy?"

He should have kept it simple. Instead he heard himself furnishing the lie. "Man from that Kendall backsplash finally called me. Wall crack by an exhaust fan. Ten-minute thing once I got there."

"Mr. Lewis."

Delroy hated how fast she found the name. "Yeah."

She looked at the untouched twenties, then at the bag still hidden behind his leg. "Mr. Lewis finally paid up on a Monday morning, in cash, and all you had time to buy was the soap I hate?"

"It was on the way."

"Mm."

The one syllable landed heavier than if she had flung the plate. She picked up the two twenties at last and slid them under the red-banded light bill, not because she believed him but because FPL didn't care what story money carried. Marcia going quiet was never mercy. It only meant whatever was owed would come due later. Delroy kept his hand on the tool bag and felt the other three hundred and sixty dollars sitting under the drill bits between them, already inside the house, already spoiling the room.
