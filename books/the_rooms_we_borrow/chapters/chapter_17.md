# Chapter 17

By morning the overhead lights over Tiana's cot were back at full buzz. The fellowship hall had already used up its night.

They had never fully gone dark, only dimmed enough to turn everybody's skin the color of old paper. Children were crying in three different corners with three different pitches. Somebody's phone kept spitting the same gospel hook from under a blanket because the owner was still asleep. The industrial fans church men had rolled in after the storm pushed warm air from one end of the room to the other and spread every smell instead of solving any of them: bleach, wet sneakers, mildew lifting out of donated towels, coffee gone bitter on the burner, the sweet rot of floodwater that had dried into clothes and would not quite leave.

Tiana sat on the edge of her cot with her knees braced around her document folder. The plastic sleeve had buckled at the corners where the water had gotten in. Her immunization record looked as if it had been steamed. The housing printout from UF had gone soft, dried, and then stiffened again with a faint tide line across the middle. She had flattened it overnight under a borrowed Bible and it still curled upward at the edge like it wanted to escape her hands.

`LATE CHECK-IN APPROVED THROUGH MONDAY 5:00 PM,` the email said. `Failure to arrive within this window may result in release of your housing assignment for Fall 2019.`

The phrase may result was the kind of language institutions used when they had already decided what they were going to do and wanted the sentence to look less like a blade.

Malik had texted at six-thirteen.

If we're doing it, be ready by ten. I got the car till tonight.

She had not answered. She had stared at the message until the little gray bubble beneath it stopped feeling like language and became only time. Ten o'clock meant pack what was left dry, get dressed in something that did not smell like church floor and ruined drywall, get to the parking lot, point the car north, and hold the day steady with both hands until Gainesville. What sickened her was not impossibility but the opposite. The trip did not ask for anything noble, only nerve and a borrowed car. It hurt because possible things could still be lost.

Across from her, Kerene was sitting cross-legged on the folding cot, tying a green scarf around her head with the care of somebody dressing for a better room than the one she had. She had cut the scarf from the lining of an old skirt and the satin still caught the light. Her mouth was bare of lipstick for once, which made her look younger and meaner.

"Miss Doreen say if we don't move these bags from under the cots she's going to start throwing things in black garbage sacks," she said, not looking up. "That woman loves a rule the way some people love a man."

"Then move them," Tiana said.

"I'm moving them. I'm also cursing her privately. Both things can happen."

Kerene bent to drag her duffel closer with her foot. Even half asleep she made room like a person who had been temporary in too many places to trust anything left unattended.

Near the coffee urns, Miss Doreen was indeed moving down the aisle with her clipboard. She wore white church shoes with thick orthopedic soles and a cloud of White Diamonds that reached people before she did. A peppermint clicked from one side of her mouth to the other while she checked names and cot numbers and reminded everybody, again, that the side classroom was for mothers with babies and not for "private visiting." The shelter had been open only three nights and already the rules had multiplied like damp.

Tiana was looking for a moment to call housing without the whole hall hearing the shape of her leaving when Micah's phone buzzed hard enough to rattle against the metal chair beside his cot.

He had his hood up though the room was hot. The bruise from the stop two weeks ago had gone from plum to a faint ugly yellow under his cheekbone, but it was still there if you looked. He dragged the phone into his hand, squinted, and then laughed in a way that wasn't attached to anything funny.

"What?" Kerene asked.

"Nothing." He was already typing. "Y'all internet-famous now. Congratulations."

Tiana looked up. Micah held the phone too low and too close to his body, which was how she knew he wanted somebody to ask.

"Micah."

"It's stupid."

He said it too fast. She stood and crossed the narrow strip between the cots before he could tuck the screen away.

The photo was a screenshot from the church's own Facebook Live. She recognized the fellowship hall banner in the back and the metal serving table in front of it. Somebody had been streaming donations the night before, narrating over boxes of canned goods and diaper packs and bottled water, and in the background Micah was caught in a moment that had looked ordinary until it became evidence. He was bent over the table, reaching for a pack of socks, his T-shirt rucked up at the back, one knee showing through his basketball shorts, his face tired and half turned so the bruise caught the light. On the original church stream he would probably have been nobody in particular, just another body in need. On the screenshot somebody had circled him in red and written `LIL MIKE AT HURRICANE HILTON` across the top. The comments below were already filling.

Damn that's him fr

Boy in line for church drawers

Tell Dre come get his mans

Tiana felt the top of her scalp go cold. The room got smaller around the phone.

"Who sent that?"

"Everybody," Micah said. His mouth twisted. "Whole city doing journalism now."

He tried to take the phone back and she let him. Making him wrestle for it would only give the shame more shape.

Delroy looked over from the coffee line. He had slept in yesterday's jeans and a white undershirt gone gray at the collar, and he was shaving off three days of beard with a disposable razor and a paper cup of water because he refused to let the shelter see him looking like it had won. "What happened?"

"Nothing happened," Micah said.

"That don't sound like nothing."

Micah gave him the bright, ugly grin he used when he wanted to get cruel before somebody else could. "You want me to airdrop it to you, Daddy? Probably your church friend stream it."

Delroy crossed the room then, coffee forgotten. Tiana turned the screen toward him before Micah could stop her. Delroy's eyes took in the image, the caption, the comments. She saw the exact second his face stopped being offended on principle and became something more private. Embarrassment came to him before anger. It always did.

"Which fool post this?"

"I don't know." Micah shoved the phone into his pocket. "And I don't care."

"You care."

"Man, everybody got phones. What you want me to do, fight Facebook?"

Miss Doreen glanced over at the rise in their voices. Nearby cots had already gone a little quieter. The shelter had a way of turning every family argument into community theater without anybody admitting they were watching.

Tiana said, low, "Take it down if it's on your story."

"It ain't my story."

"Then report it."

He laughed again, meaner this time. "Yeah. Instagram going to save me. Good idea."

It would have been almost manageable if he had cursed or shouted. What scared her was how light he sounded, as if the humiliation had filed him to a meaner point instead of sinking in. That lightness was his worst look. It meant he was already half gone.

Marcia came back from the women's bathroom carrying a plastic caddy with soap, a comb, and the edge of her patience showing plain. "Why everybody making noise so early?"

"Nothing," Micah said.

"A picture online," Kerene said at the same time.

Marcia looked from one face to the next and understood enough without seeing the screen. Her mouth hardened. "Who took it?"

"Somebody from church Live," Tiana said.

"Jesus." Marcia set the caddy down so hard the comb jumped. "Blessing people for an audience."

Micah gave another little shrug, the performed kind. "I said it's fine."

"It isn't fine," Tiana said.

"Then don't look at it."

He snatched his hoodie from the cot and pulled it on. Marcia said his name in warning. He had already started walking.

"Where you going?" she asked.

"Outside."

"For what?"

"Air."

"You get ten minutes," Miss Doreen called from the aisle, hearing only the exit and not the reason for it. "Minors don't just roam. We already talked about that."

Micah saluted her without turning around. "Yes, ma'am."

The whole hall heard the insolence. A few people laughed. He kept moving.

Tiana watched the back of his neck disappear through the fellowship-hall doors. She almost followed him then, but instead she looked down at the email still open on her phone. Ten o'clock meant Malik and the car; five p.m. meant the late check-in window closing. She had the sudden ugly thought that came before she could stop it: of course it had happened today. Of course the family emergency had found the one clean square of time with her name on it and put its heel there.

She hated herself a little for thinking it and did not have time to hate herself properly.

Marcia picked up the caddy again. "Let him cool off."

Delroy was still looking at the doorway. "No."

Marcia turned to him. "No what?"

"No let him cool off. That boy shame-hot right now. Shame make people stupid."

"He's going to the parking lot."

"Maybe." Delroy rubbed at the bad shoulder without seeming to notice he had done it. "Maybe not."

Tiana checked the time. It was seven twenty-six. Her phone buzzed once in her hand with another message from Malik.

Need me there early?

She looked at the words and then at the door Micah had just gone through. The shelter's air pressed warm and used against her face. Somebody's toddler had started wailing because the pancake mix had run out. Miss Doreen was scolding a man for trying to charge two phones at once. Kerene was cursing softly while wrestling a zipper that had rusted with floodwater. The whole hall breathed against itself. There was no corner in it where a girl could decide her future without an audience.

Twenty minutes later Micah still had not come back.

Delroy did not posture about it. He did not announce himself head of anything. He put on his work boots, asked what Micah had on besides the hoodie, checked the pockets of the chair for the boy's charger, looked once at the cot, and said, "Come."

Marcia frowned. "Where?"

"Out."

"Tiana has to make calls."

"I know what she has." He looked at Tiana then, directly, and the look hurt because it meant he had not forgotten. "I also know if we wait till dark and have to bring police in it gets worse."

Marcia's jaw moved. She hated him most when he was right in a way that cost her.

"I'll stay here," she said. "If he comes back, I'll call."

Tiana's folder was still on the cot. The plastic sleeve caught the fluorescent light like a wink. Malik's message sat unanswered. If she walked out now, the hours would start bleeding. If she stayed, Micah would be out there with everybody in Miami laughing at his need and no one from his own house looking for him yet.

She picked up the folder, shoved it into her tote, and followed her father into the morning.

Outside, the air hit with the wet heaviness of water that had left but not forgiven. The church parking lot was crowded with donated pallets, black contractor bags, and the cars of people who had come to help without having to stay. The storm debris on the curb looked almost festive from a distance until she got close enough to see ruined mattresses, swollen particleboard, and a child's pink rain boot lying by itself in a gutter full of brown leaves.

Delroy headed east first, toward the bus stop and the strip of stores Micah haunted when he wanted to be visible without being home. He walked faster than Tiana expected for a man carrying that shoulder, but not so fast that it looked heroic. The pace only proved he had not come along to supervise.

"He has cash on him?" he asked.

"Maybe twenty. Maybe less."

"Phone?"

"Yeah."

"He answer you?"

She called. It rang until voicemail. The voicemail greeting was one of Micah's old joke ones, done in a fake radio voice: You reached a very important person. He can't come to the phone because capitalism is a disease. Leave something worth hearing.

Delroy almost smiled at that, then didn't.

At the bus stop a man in paint-splattered Crocs said he had seen a boy in a dark hoodie headed south half an hour ago, maybe. At the corner store the cashier said Micah had come in, asked for the Wi-Fi password, and left without buying anything. Delroy thanked everybody. He had a way of pitching his voice half an inch lower with strangers, not submissive exactly, but careful. Tiana saw the effort it took him and hated that she saw it.

By eight-thirty they had checked the basketball court behind the apartment complex on Sixty-Second, the alley where Micah sometimes got haircuts for cash, and Dre's building. Dre himself answered the door in basketball shorts and one sock, his hair flattened on one side. He had a school lanyard still wrapped around his wrist from some habit he never broke.

"He ain't here," he said before Tiana asked. "I sent him the screenshot 'cause I figured somebody was gonna anyway. I didn't post that shit."

"Who did?" Delroy asked.

Dre shrugged. "Some church lady streamed supplies and then people screen-recorded it. Couple boys from school got it. Probably gone by lunch once somebody else get embarrassed."

"That's supposed to help?" Tiana said.

He looked properly ashamed then, which made him younger. "I was just letting him know."

Delroy said, "If he call you, you tell him home. Not tomorrow. Home."

Dre nodded. "Yes, sir."

They kept moving.

The city had that washed-out brightness storms left behind, everything too clean on top and rotting underneath. Mangos split open in the gutters. Tree limbs leaned against chain-link fences like broken furniture. At an intersection a city truck was feeding branches into a chipper that screamed loud enough to empty thought out of Tiana's head for one full block. Her phone buzzed in her pocket twice, three times, and she let it. She knew the pattern by feel now: Malik, then an unfamiliar Gainesville number, then Malik again.

At nine-fifteen Delroy said, "Answer the boy."

"I know it's him."

"Then answer."

"I can't talk right now."

"You can talk and walk."

She stopped under the shadow of a shuttered botanica and looked at him. "Please don't do this like you know how to manage my schedule."

His mouth tightened. He took the rebuke without pretending otherwise. "Fair."

Then, after a second: "Still answer him."

She did, mostly to stop hearing her father say his name like he belonged in the conversation.

"Where are you?" Malik asked without greeting. The line was full of traffic noise and whatever engine he was sitting near.

"Little River."

"That narrows it down about zero."

"We're looking for Micah."

There was a beat. His voice flattened, which was how it did concern. "He left the shelter?"

"After some bullshit picture went around."

"You find him?"

"Not yet."

"What time do you need to leave if we're still going?"

She looked at the street, at a puddle holding the church roof upside down, at her father pretending not to listen while listening to every word. "I don't know."

"Ti."

"I said I don't know."

He let out air through his nose. "I'm by my aunt's job right now. I can hold the car till maybe eleven before it turns into me owing people."

"Okay."

"That isn't an answer."

"My brother's missing."

"I heard that. I'm not saying leave him on the sidewalk. I'm asking what you want me to do."

She opened her mouth to say, Keep the car till five. Give me one impossible afternoon where Micah comes back and Gainesville does not close. The ask died there. She could hear too clearly what it would cost him: a borrowed favor, more gas, more waiting, his whole day parked beside her family's latest disaster.

"I'll call you," she said, and hung up before he could make her more exact.

Delroy started walking again. "He sound like a decent youth."

"Please."

"Just saying."

She almost laughed at the absurdity of being furious and embarrassed in the same breath. He had that effect on her. "You don't get to be casual with him like you're not the reason half of this exists."

"Half?" He gave her a crooked glance. "You are generous today."

It was the kind of answer he would once have turned into a whole performance. This time he left it there. They crossed under the Metrorail overpass where the shade smelled like urine and wet concrete and old cigarettes. A woman with silver lipstick was selling bags of boiled peanuts from a cooler and fanning herself with a church bulletin. Somewhere nearby a radio was playing dancehall through blown speakers.

By ten-thirty the church had called twice. Micah was still not back. Marcia's first voicemail was all control: Call me the minute you hear anything. The second had something thinner in it. Tiana did not play that one a second time.

They checked the canal path, the field behind Micah's school, the dock road where Dre had once taken him for day cash. The places gave them pieces and not the boy. Somebody had seen him on a bench. Somebody had seen a hoodie on a bus. Somebody said a kid fitting that description had gone behind the old strip mall with his head down. The search turned into weather: everywhere around them, nowhere solid enough to hold.

At eleven-oh-two the Gainesville number called again. She answered because not answering had started to feel childish, and because Delroy was no longer pretending not to notice.

"This is Jasmine with UF Housing," said a woman whose voice had the bright sanded-down roughness of somebody talking through a lunch break. Tiana could hear keyboards behind her, another phone ringing, somebody laughing too close to the receiver. "I'm following up on your approved late check-in. We still need confirmation that you'll arrive by five today in order to hold the assignment."

Tiana had to step away from her father to hear. She turned toward a chain-link fence with bougainvillea strangling through it in magenta knots.

"I'm trying to make arrangements," she said.

"Okay. I do need to be very clear that if you do not physically check in by five p.m. today, we will release the space."

The word release was another soft one for something already dead.

"If that happens," Tiana said, hating the shake in her own voice, "what happens to my enrollment?"

"That question would go through admissions and financial aid," Jasmine said. The answer was neither cruel nor kind; it had the office-clean efficiency of a limit she had probably repeated all day. "I can note the file that you were affected by storm displacement, but I can't guarantee housing after today and I can't activate your room without arrival."

Tiana pressed her thumbnail into the soft plastic edge of the folder through her tote until it bent. "Okay."

"Would you like me to hold while you confirm transportation?"

The question was so ordinary it almost made her laugh.

"No," Tiana said. "Thank you."

When she hung up, Delroy was watching her from the curb.

"Five o'clock?" he said.

She nodded.

He looked up the street as if he might find extra hours hanging from a power line. "We keep moving."

He did not offer a lecture or some sudden burst of paternal permission. He only said it, and that usefulness made her angrier than either speech would have.

She opened the map one more time. If she called Malik that minute, if the traffic gave her one undeserved break, she could maybe still scrape into Gainesville by five. She locked the phone instead and stepped off the curb beside her father. If Micah was still out here, she was not leaving him to be found by strangers.

Around noon they bought two cold waters from a gas station and drank them standing up. Tiana could feel the day being eaten. Each time she opened the map north to Gainesville the blue route was longer than the last time. First the map said five hours. Then it said five hours eleven minutes. Then it said five hours twenty-three because of standing water near Ocala or construction or some other indifferent obstacle the phone explained in clean colors.

At twelve-forty Malik texted:

Need a real answer.

Ten minutes later he sent another text:

If you want the ride, say that. If not, say that too.

She put the phone back in her pocket. The thing she wanted was not a ride and not exactly school either. It was a life that did not demand an oath every time she tried to touch it.

By one-thirty Delroy had started moving with the stubborn limp he got when pain was chewing at him and he wanted nobody to see teeth marks. They were back near the old neighborhood almost without planning to be, passing the stucco house with the barred windows, the mechanic yard, and the corner where the church van used to pick up old people on Sundays. Tiana realized before he said it where they were going next.

"He wouldn't go back there," she said.

Delroy kept his eyes on the sidewalk. "If his shame bad enough? He might."

"Why?"

"Because there nobody can reveal him to himself. Place already know."

He had not come closer all day to speaking the real thing.

The main house looked almost cheerful in the sharp afternoon sun, the same cracked stucco painted a pale color that passed for clean from the street. The garage apartment behind it was another matter. The flood had left a water line halfway up the outside wall, a brown necklace around the building. Someone had nailed plywood over the side window. The landlord had chained the side gate but not well. Delroy worked the chain loose with his good hand and the practiced shamelessness of a man who had already paid for the right to trespass on what used to be his own life.

The yard hit Tiana low in the body. Every warped bucket and busted board still knew the outline of their old days, and seeing it again made Gainesville feel indecently far away, like a country she had only rented in her head.

"Micah," Tiana called as soon as they stepped into the side yard.

No one answered. The place smelled like wet insulation and sour mud baked by sun.

Then she heard music, low and tinny, somewhere behind the ruined washing machine that had been dragged near the fence after the storm. It wasn't even a full song, only the bass bleeding through a phone speaker turned down too far.

Relief flashed through her first, hot enough to make her dizzy. Anger crowded in right behind it. He had let them walk the neighborhood for hours while he sat close enough to answer.

Micah was sitting on an overturned bucket behind the machine with his hood up and his knees spread, elbows on thighs, staring at his deadened reflection in a strip of black water caught in a broken plastic bin. He had taken off one shoe and was scraping mud from the sole with a house key. Beside him lay a garbage bag with three things in it: a pair of sneakers he must have dug out, a damp PS4 controller, and the blue towel he had slept with as a baby and still refused to admit he had kept.

The towel caved something in her chest. He had dragged that thing by one corner when he was little, terry cloth dark at the mouth from sleep and spit. Seeing it in the bag beside the controller and the mud-stiff shoes made her relief feel mean.

Tiana stopped.

So did Delroy.

Micah didn't look surprised. He only looked caught. "Damn," he said. "Y'all got GPS on me now?"

"You want a joke," Tiana said. "Make one later."

He went back to scraping at the shoe. "I wasn't lost."

"Then answer your phone."

"Didn't feel like hearing everybody panic."

"Everybody panic because you disappeared."

"I went outside."

"For six hours?"

He threw the key harder than he meant to. It clinked off the washing machine and fell into the mud. "Why you yelling like I got kidnapped?"

Delroy moved first, not toward Micah's face but toward the fallen key. He bent badly, jaw tight, and picked it up with his left hand. The sight made Tiana's throat tighten. His shoulder had been bad for weeks, and now he was paying for Micah's shame with a body that barely had spare parts left. Then he lowered himself onto the cracked concrete block beside the bucket with a care that made her aware of every year in him and every minute still burning down in her pocket. He handed Micah the key back.

"You done?" Delroy asked.

Micah stared ahead. "Done what."

"Done hiding."

"I wasn't hiding."

"All right. Done sitting in stink water then."

Micah's mouth twitched despite himself. Tiana hated that it worked and loved it too.

"That picture got around school?" Delroy asked.

Micah didn't answer.

"School and everybody else," Tiana said.

Micah shot her a look sharp enough to skin.

Delroy nodded once, like he had expected no better from the world. "Church people don't know how to help quiet."

"Don't start," Micah said.

"I wasn't starting."

"Then don't make it a speech."

Delroy spread his hands. "Who making speech? I ask a question."

Micah rubbed both palms over his face, hard, as if he could scrape off the morning. When he spoke again the swagger had thinned. "I didn't want to be there with everybody looking at me like some before-and-after picture. Like if they drop enough canned beans on a table they get to own my face."

The admission came out bare and ugly, no joke wrapped around it, which was probably why she believed it at once.

Tiana felt something inside her shift against something meaner. She could still see the comments. She could also see the blue route to Gainesville shrinking toward impossible in her mind like a muscle going into spasm.

"So you came here?" she said.

Micah looked around the wrecked yard. "Yeah."

"Why."

He shrugged. "This place already knows we're broke."

The sentence was exact enough to make any softer version a lie.

Delroy's eyes closed for a second. When he opened them he did not try to tell Micah not to care. He did not tell him to man up or laugh it off or remember other people had it worse. For once he did not charm the truth smaller.

"I hear you," he said. "Still, you can't run everybody ragged because some little bastards got phones."

"I didn't ask y'all to come."

"No," Delroy said. "You made us."

Micah's face folded then straightened before it could become anything else. Tiana saw the child in him only in flashes now. Fifteen was mostly angles and performance with the boy buried inside like contraband.

Her phone buzzed.

One glance was enough. `UF Housing. Final reminder: approved late check-in expires at 5:00 PM today.`

The time at the top of the screen read 3:57.

If Malik left the church that minute, if traffic broke in their favor, if nothing else happened, she could maybe get to Gainesville after nine. The thought did not even feel close enough to ache. It felt mathematical, like a sum with too much missing from one side.

Micah saw her look at the phone. "That's school?"

She said nothing.

He looked away first. "Damn."

Delroy pushed himself up from the block and winced only after he was already standing. "Come. Both of you."

Micah stayed where he was another second, looking at the garbage bag. Delroy picked it up before the boy could decide he was too old for help. He hefted it with his good hand and motioned toward the gate.

"Bring the shoes if you want. Leave the mold."

It was such a fatherly sentence, so ordinary and unheroic, that Tiana felt tears sting behind her eyes out of sheer bad timing. She swallowed them. The yard was too ugly for crying. Everything would have smelled worse.

They walked back toward the church together. Micah dragged his feet at first, then not at all. Delroy carried the bag. Tiana called Malik and got straight to voicemail.

"It's me," she said. "We found him."

She stopped there, because the next sentence refused to pick a shape.

By the time they got back to the fellowship hall, Miss Doreen had switched from authority to sympathy and was pressing sweet tea into Marcia's hand. Marcia set the cup down untouched when she saw Micah. She did not run to him. She held herself rigid for one second, which was worse, and then gripped the back of his neck hard enough to make the point before pulling him against her.

"You think I don't have enough to bury me?" she said into his hair.

"Sorry," he muttered.

"Sorry can't be the only thing you know how to say after."

Kerene, relief showing as irritation, took the garbage bag from Delroy and pulled out the blue baby towel without comment. She shook it once, smelled it, made a face, and set it aside to dry near the fan. No one mentioned that Micah had gone back for it.

Tiana had just enough time to wash the dirt from her hands in the church bathroom before Malik texted that he was outside.

The parking lot was all late light and broken asphalt and generator noise. Malik leaned against a gray Altima with his arms folded, one ankle hooked over the other. He had on a faded black T-shirt and jeans, and the first thing she noticed, absurdly, was that he had a clean duffel in the back seat and a case of bottled water on the floorboard like he had packed for both rescue and logistics. There was a crescent-shaped grease stain high on one thigh where he must have brushed against somebody's engine or some machine earlier in the day. He worried the edge of a folded gas receipt between thumb and forefinger while he waited, rolling it into a tight pale cord and flattening it again.

"You found him," he said.

"Yeah."

"He okay?"

"Embarrassed. Which for Micah is basically a medical event."

That got the smallest breath of a laugh out of him, then nothing. He looked tired in the plain way people did after a day that had actually cost them something.

"What time is it?" she asked, though she knew.

"Five twenty-two."

The time lodged under her ribs, heavy and useless as a brick.

He opened the passenger door anyway. Cool air spilled out, dry and unreal after the fellowship hall and the streets. "Get in for a second."

She did. The seat was cleaner than anything she had touched in days. She almost wanted to sleep there on principle.

Malik shut the door and came around to the driver's side but didn't start the car. The engine ticked softly under the hood. From inside the church parking lot the fellowship hall looked almost peaceful. You could not see the cots from here. Only the brick wall, the side door, the women from the kitchen carrying foil trays in and out.

"I called you three times," he said.

"I know."

"You could've texted one sentence."

"I know."

He nodded, looking through the windshield. "All right."

That all right was worse than anger. Anger at least made a scene you could answer.

She pulled out her phone. The email had come in at 5:03.

`Because you did not complete check-in by the approved deadline, your Fall housing assignment has been cancelled. Please contact Admissions and Financial Aid regarding your enrollment options.`

The cancellation email had stopped hiding behind soft verbs and maybes. The knife was clean now.

She read it once, then again. The words did not improve under attention.

"It's gone," she said.

Malik waited.

"The room. The move-in. The whole..." She gave up before the sentence made her sound stupid. "It's gone."

He looked over then, really looked, and the tenderness in his face made everything worse because it was not pity. Pity she could have fought.

"Let me see."

She handed him the phone. He read the email and gave it back.

"I'm sorry."

She laughed once under her breath, harsh and airless. "I hate that phrase."

"I know."

"Everybody keeps being sorry and nothing un-happens."

"Also true."

The car filled with silence. Outside, somebody wheeled a trash can across the lot, the loose lid slamming against the side in dull metal beats.

Malik said, "What do you want to do now?"

She turned on him too fast. "Now?"

"Yeah. Now."

"Nothing. I want this morning to not be this morning."

"Can't do that."

"Clearly."

He let the sharpness pass through instead of catching it and throwing it back. It was one of the things she loved about him, and one of the things that sometimes made her cruel.

"Ti," he said, still quiet, "I need you to hear me before you get mad."

"That sentence always means get mad."

"Probably."

She folded her arms over the warped folder in her lap. The plastic edge dug into her wrist.

"I would've taken you," he said. "You know that."

"I know."

"I borrowed my aunt's car, told my job I was coming in late, packed half my room into that trunk because I didn't know what you still had dry. I would've done it."

She looked at the back seat then and saw more clearly what had been there all along: the duffel, a blanket, a little plastic bag with snacks from the gas station, a pack of dryer sheets probably meant to fight the shelter smell out of her clothes. It was effort, laid out plain and unspectacular.

"I know," she said again, softer this time.

"But I can't keep doing days like this with you."

She stared at him.

He tapped the steering wheel once, then smoothed the crumpled gas receipt flat against the dash. He did not dress the sentence up. He never did when it mattered. "I love you. That's why I'm saying it straight. I packed the trunk, burned the gas, and kept the day open for you. I can't keep being the part of your life that idles outside when home catches fire."

"So what, I was supposed to leave while Micah was gone?"

"No. I'm saying if stay was the answer, you had to say stay. Not leave me out here guessing how much of myself to spend."

The sentence fit too neatly against the thought she had been padding over in her own head, and she hated him for a second, not because he was wrong but because he had touched it first.

She put her head back against the seat. The air-conditioning hummed against her damp scalp. For one ugly second she wanted to accuse him of not understanding because he did not sleep in that hall, because he could still go home tonight to one room that belonged to him. But that would have been cheap. He was not rich. He was not free. He was just less trapped in her particular architecture.

"I hate you a little right now," she said.

"All right."

"You make everything sound simple."

"Nothing about this is simple."

She looked down at the folder in her lap. The edge of the UF printout had frayed into soft white fibers where the storm had gotten at it. She thought of the room she had never seen except in the online portal: the narrow bed, the desk, the cinderblock painted hopeful beige, the little rectangle of institutional air-conditioning, the possibility of being anonymous for one whole week before anybody's disaster found her there. She had wanted that anonymity almost more than classes. Wanted to be somewhere no one looked at her and saw the whole Campbell ledger behind her eyes.

Now some girl in Gainesville she would never meet might take the room, or maybe the bed would just sit empty under institutional air-conditioning. The room did not actually care. Procedure did the job fine.

"So what happens now?" Malik asked.

The question was practical. She loved him for that too and resented it too.

"Now?" She swallowed. "Now I call admissions tomorrow and hear them say defer or reapply or local start or whatever clean administrative word they have for not this."

"Okay."

"And tonight I sleep on a church cot again."

"Probably."

"And Micah's still humiliated and Mummy's still exhausted and Daddy's shoulder is hanging on by prayer and bullshit."

"Also probably."

He did not touch her while she stared at the windshield. He gave her the dignity of not rushing to comfort what he had just made more explicit. After a while he said, "If you want me here tonight, I can stay another hour. I can't stay till morning. I really do have to take this car back."

Again she ran into the thing she trusted most about him: his love had edges, set by gas money and borrowed time, and that was why it felt solid.

"No," she said. "Go do your life."

"That's not what I asked."

She looked at him. The generator noise outside rose and fell. Somewhere inside the church somebody started singing under her breath while setting out dinner pans.

"Stay fifteen minutes," Tiana said.

"All right."

They sat there with the air on low. Because the day had already taken her privacy and she was too tired to protect what was left of it, she told him exactly what the picture had looked like: the red circle around Micah's body, the caption, the comments. Malik's jaw tightened once and then leveled back out.

"People are foul," he said.

"Yeah."

"Your brother's going to act like this is about the internet. It isn't. It's about being seen in need by people who think need is funny."

"I know."

"I figured you did."

She told him about finding Micah at the old apartment with the baby towel in the garbage bag, and this time when she laughed the sound came out real for a second, ugly and wet-edged but real. Malik smiled without widening it too much.

"That's his little ass all over," he said.

"Don't ever call my brother little-ass to my face."

"You just did."

"I can. That's blood law."

He shook his head. For fifteen minutes they sat in the only private air she had been in for days, and when he finally left she leaned across the console and kissed him hard enough to bruise the moment into memory. She was not trying to make it romantic or save it for later. She wanted proof, right then, that something in her life still had a body.

When she got out of the car the heat took her back immediately.

The fellowship hall was louder at dinner than it had been at breakfast. Relief did not make anybody quiet. It made them territorial. The metal lids on the foil trays sweated grease. Miss Doreen was back on the microphone asking families with children to line up first and pretending the adults without children were not also hungry. Micah sat at the end of their cot in a clean donated T-shirt, eating too fast and not looking at anyone much. Delroy was near the side wall with a paper plate and his shoulder held slightly wrong. Marcia was folding dry clothes from the fan line with the calm violence of a woman who had run out of time for scenes.

Tiana slid her folder under the cot before sitting down. There was no safe place for it, only less bad ones.

Micah glanced at her. "You going somewhere?"

He meant the parking lot, Malik, maybe more than that.

"No," she said.

He looked at the folder, then at her face. His own changed by a degree. Shame recognized shame and did not say the word.

"Oh," he said.

He left it there and turned back to his plate.

Across the aisle a little boy used a donation pencil to draw a house on the back of a church bulletin, every line too dark, pressing hard enough to tear the paper at the corners. Tiana watched him do it until the outline gave way and the page ripped through the roof.

At lights-down, which was not darkness but a lower wattage of exposure, she lay on the cot with the email open once more because she did not yet know how to stop looking at the body of it. The canceled room, the canceled arrival, and the sentence that turned a life into an option set sat on the screen while the hall settled by layers: coughing, whispering, a baby refusing sleep as a matter of principle, somebody praying softly in patois near the bathrooms, somebody else snoring with complete faith in exhaustion.

She thought of the road north she had not taken, not as some movie of what might have been. The truth was smaller and more embarrassing than that. She thought of small things: the first blast of dorm air-conditioning against her face after unloading a car; the sound of her own footsteps in a hallway where nobody knew her mother or her father's debts or her brother's mouth or the exact way floodwater smelled once it got into paper; the possibility of letting silence mean privacy instead of danger. What had closed was not education in the noble abstract. It was a room, a key, a first clean exit.

Beside her, Micah shifted on his cot and muttered once in his sleep. Across the hall, Delroy coughed until the metal springs under him answered with a thin complaint, then dragged the blanket higher and let the next breath rattle out slow. Tiana put the phone facedown on her chest and felt its heat through the thin church blanket.

Tomorrow would come with forms, calls, alternatives, the local path, the practical path, and whatever roof Mummy could wrestle out of the city. They were all names for what was left after the original thing had already been cut away.

Tonight the loss was simpler than that and meaner.

She had stayed.
