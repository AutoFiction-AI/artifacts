# Chapter 16

The old funeral ferry was too broad for this water now. It shoved at the broken reed islands like an ox trying to shoulder through a market lane, and the lane kept splitting under it. Mats of root and mud the size of rooms peeled apart with wet tearing sounds. Prayer bowls, dead fish, somebody's roof thatch, a drowned goat with its belly opened white by the current, all of it slid through the gaps and disappeared toward the sea.

Riva kept both hands on the pole because if she looked at Daro for more than a heartbeat she would jam them all sideways into the reeds. He was ahead of them in a knot of stolen skiffs and pilgrim punts, the brass ferrule of Sereya's pole flashing whenever the rain thinned enough to let metal answer light. Half Reedmouth trailed him in frightened clots instead of a single rush: widows, shrine boys, two fishermen from Lantern Quay, three women from Halcote with their skirts tucked between their knees and holy string wound around their wrists, a man in a grain-clerk's coat holding a lacquered prayer bowl to his chest as if he could keep his dead daughter inside it by force.

He had done in one afternoon what Riva had failed to do in two weeks of labor. He had made panic look simple.

The ferry's hull hit something buried and jumped under her feet. Nami grabbed the gunwale. Lale, crouched near the stern with one hand over Paska's fresh tide marks as if she could keep them from washing off her own skin, looked up without speaking. Iven swore around a mouthful of wet rope and hauled the spare line clear of a snag before it could foul the pole. Bato stood at the bow with his bad shoulder low and his nearly white hair plastered to his skull. He kept turning his better ear toward the water instead of toward any of them, as if the channel itself was the only voice worth hearing.

"Left of that mat," he said.

Riva dug the pole hard and felt the ferry answer late, swollen wood heavy as debt. "You said he couldn't open it alone."

"I said he couldn't open it right."

"That isn't a comfort."

"It wasn't meant as one."

Ahead, Daro's followers sang back the end of a line he had thrown them. His voice came over the water rounded and rich even in rain, built to make a crowd lean toward it. Riva could not hear the words. She did not need to. She knew the shape of them: one way through, one body, one answer. It was the same lie their mother had sold with better hips and a stronger back.

The key Tovin had given her knocked once against her breastbone beneath her shirt as the ferry lurched. Cold iron on a cord, one more weight she had forgotten to remove. Tovin had shoved it at her with her burned wrists bare and eel smoke in her clothes and said, Keep it where your body will remember it, because your mind is too proud to do the job alone. Riva had taken it because arguing with Tovin cost time. Since then it had been no more than an old habit of weight at her chest, something forged to fit another mouth of the river.

Now the water ahead made a noise like a man breathing through broken teeth.

Lale heard it too. "The channels are crossing."

"They've been crossing since dawn," Riva said.

"No. Listen."

Riva listened because Lale did not waste that tone. Under the slap of rain and the grind of reeds there was a second sound, a hollow inward draw with surf's drag and current's pull twisted together. A throat teaching itself to swallow.

Bato spat over the bow. "Storm Mouth is taking a breath."

Nami lifted her head as if someone had touched the back of her neck. For the past hour she had been too quiet, and quiet in Nami never meant calm. Her eyes had kept fixing on the spaces between things: the slit where two reed mats rubbed together, the black water under a half-sunk corpse-ferry, the place where Daro's voice vanished after it struck rain. She had color again after the fever, but the fever had left a listening habit in her. Sometimes she seemed to hear ordinary words one half-second after anyone else spoke them, as if the river got first say.

"There's a child there," she said.

Riva did not turn. "There isn't."

"There is. Under the split root. He keeps putting his hand up."

Iven looked despite himself. "Nami."

"Don't say my name like that."

Riva glanced then, against judgment. Between two peeled reed islands a small body rolled once in the chop, face down, shirt caught on a root, the water having shaved it narrow enough to pass for a child at a glance. One pale hand knocked up and down with the push of the current.

"Sit down," Riva said.

Nami was already moving with the slow certainty that made her harder to stop. She set one bare foot on the inside rail with the dreamy precision of somebody stepping toward a bed in the dark. Rain had slicked her hair against her cheeks. Her mouth was open a little. Some inward track had taken the ferry's place under her feet.

Riva dropped the pole.

The ferry immediately slewed broadside. Lale caught at a crate. Iven barked, "Riva," but Riva was already across the wet boards. Nami had both hands on the rail now, leaning toward the corpse caught between roots. The dead hand kept lifting with the water, asking and asking with no mind behind it at all.

Riva caught Nami by the back of the skirt and nothing else. Cloth tore. Nami pitched outward anyway, ribs over the rail, fingers reaching. Riva got her other arm around one thigh and dragged with both heels braced against the deck. Nami screamed then, the sound raw with thwarted recognition, as if Riva had dragged her back from the end of a sentence.

"Let me go," she said. "He knows where Jun is."

The name went through Riva like a hook through wet rope. She hauled harder. Nami came back over the rail ugly, all elbows and wet limbs, striking the deck hard enough to knock breath from both of them. She fought for three blind seconds, twisting toward the side again. Riva slapped her once, open-handed and full force, because there was no time for mercy that could be mistaken for permission.

The shock of it brought Nami into her body. She stared, rain and tears mixed to one thing on her face. A red mark rose on her cheek.

"He was dead before you knew the shape of your own hands," Riva said. "He's not waiting under a reed mat to guide you anywhere."

Nami sucked air as if it hurt. "You don't know that."

"I know enough. Stay in the fucking boat."

That last line came out louder than the water. Even Daro's skiffs ahead turned their heads for a moment. The grain-clerk in the wet coat looked back over his shoulder, prayer bowl against his chest, and Riva saw the pure bright accusation in his face: delay. Catch him then, if you're right. Prove it.

The ferry had drifted half into the broken reeds while she wrestled Nami back. Stems scraped along the hull with fingernail sounds. One thick root had jammed under the stern and was levering them toward a mud shelf hidden just under the flood.

Iven was already at the pole. "You done murdering family for the hour?"

"Push."

He grinned at her without humor and drove the pole down. The muscles in his forearms stood out slick and roped. Lale was up too, skirt hitched, shoving at the trapped root with a spare oar. Nami stayed where Riva had thrown her, one hand over her burning cheek, but her eyes kept cutting back to the gap in the reeds until the corpse finally tore loose and went under.

Bato did not look at any of them. He was staring ahead, where the rain had thinned enough to show the sea-edge sky as a sheet of livid tin.

"If he plants that pole before the ferry's set," he said, "the backwash won't stop at his own throat."

Riva reclaimed the pole and heaved. The ferry came free in a sucking rush that almost threw her backward. Mud, root fiber, and a pocket of trapped rot burst up around the stern. The smell was sweet enough to gag on. She got them straight again and drove the pole deep, each stroke a command she did not have to say aloud: move, hold, move, hold.

To port, one of the reed islands split end to end with a crack like green timber in a fire. The whole mat folded under itself and released three tied corpse-barges that had been wedged behind it. They slid out nose first, shrouds dark and tight with rain, the dead under them bumping against one another with the patience of freight. One face had come uncovered. Seed leaves had rooted in both corners of the mouth and lay flat against the cheeks like green tongues.

Nami made a low sound. Lale put a steady hand around the back of her neck.

"Look at me," Lale said.

Nami did.

"At me."

The ferry shot through the gap where the reed island had broken. Ahead, the ruined Salt Room chapel showed itself at last in pieces: one wall crusted white with old salt, the roof half gone, the landing posts listing like rotten teeth. Daro's boats were already clustering there, too many hulls trying to become one congregation. His voice struck the stone and came back bigger.

Riva could hear the words now.

"You came for a passage," he called. "Then take it whole. Shoulder to shoulder. Before the cautious finish talking themselves brave. Come close and be carried."

Pilgrims answered him with the desperate obedience only floodtime ever got from people.

Bato closed his eyes once, briefly, as if pain had shape and he recognized this one. "He's using her cadences."

"He learned from the source."

"So did I," Bato said. "That's how I know what comes after."

Riva did not ask him what. She had seen enough channels fail to know there were answers that only stole time.

The ferry rounded the last broken spit of reed and the sea wind struck them full in the face, colder than inland rain and cleaner only in the cruelest sense. Salt Room opened below the chapel in a black converging reach where river water and tide met head-on and never quite agreed which one was master. The current there had teeth. You could hear them.

Daro was standing on the chapel landing above the boats, Sereya's pole in both hands, his wet shirt plastered to the fine bones of his chest. For one mad instant he looked exactly like the stories had always wanted him to look: solitary, lit from nowhere, built for a crowd's need.

Riva put the ferry at him.

***

By the time the landing came close enough to hit, Iven's shoulders were already burned hollow.

The old ferry did not behave like the Bitter Thread. The barge had answered him like a bad dog that still respected the boot. This thing answered like an elder aunt with a knife in her apron: late, stubborn, offended by all speed. Every correction took too long and then took more than you asked for. The deck shifted under his feet with the thick rolling drag of funeral cargo, because that was what it had been built for. The dead never complained if you came in slow. The living complained plenty.

"Starboard line," Riva snapped.

"I know which side starboard is."

"Then prove it."

It almost made him laugh, but fear took the room back before it could. The line was already in his hands, heavy with rain and old salt. He had coiled it twice and it still wanted to kink, the hemp swollen fat as wrists. Ahead, the chapel landing came and went behind the spin of smaller boats. Pilgrims were shoving each other for nearness to Daro, for the right to be closest to the promise. One woman with a red wedding ribbon still braided into her gray hair kept trying to make her punt touch the chapel steps with her bare hands, as if the stone itself might bless her if wood did not.

Daro saw them coming. Of course he did. He did not look away from his crowd for long, but he gave the ferry one glance and smiled like a man indulging weather.

"He thinks we're part of the spectacle," Iven said.

Lale, bent over the side watching the pull of the water against the tide marks on her own arm, said, "At the moment we're."

Bato pointed with two fingers. The knuckles on that hand were thick and ugly from old breaks. "Capstan. There."

Iven saw it then: a rusted iron drum bolted to what remained of the landing, half buried in salt and bird shit. A mooring capstan from when Salt Room had still expected traffic more respectable than panic. One post beside it was gone. The other leaned seaward at an angle that made the whole structure look drunk.

Riva needed the ferry's bow swung into the suck of the channel so they could come alongside the landing instead of being turned broadside and fed to it. There was no room to pole cleanly with all those little boats swarming. A line around the capstan would warp them in if it held, and old iron had no obligation to hold.

Iven stripped off the coil with his right hand. "Get me close enough that I can make a stupid choice."

"That has never been difficult for you."

"Good. I'd hate to lose a talent."

His mouth was doing the old work for him, throwing noise in front of fear before fear could get a clean grip. But the clean grip was there anyway, under his ribs, cold and exact. He knew capstans. He knew what rope under flood load could do. Enver had taught him before either of them admitted why Enver kept teaching him anything. Never wrap your hand in what wants to leave you. Never stand where the tail can kiss you. Never trust old iron to fail in the direction you deserve.

He caught himself thinking of Enver at the worst possible time and spat into the water as if that would get his father's voice out of his head.

The ferry lunged. Riva had found a seam in the current and was driving it with both feet planted and her whole back in the pole. She looked enormous like this, not grand, just physically inarguable. Rain ran down the scar on her chin. Nami was kneeling by the gunwale with both hands clamped over it, watching Daro as if watching somebody set fire to a room she still wanted to live in. Lale had the spare oar braced, ready to fend off the first idiot who tried to climb them.

Iven put the line between his teeth and jumped.

The landing hit him harder than he expected because the wood was slicker than fish skin and because his knees had forgotten, for one useful instant, that he was no longer twenty. He went down on one shin, felt the bone ring, kept moving. The line snaked after him across the planks. One of Daro's shrine boys shouted something useless. Iven kicked the loose tail clear of the edge and ran it around the capstan drum in a rough first turn.

The ferry kept coming. Good; Riva had read him right.

"Take it in!" she shouted.

"With what, my charm?"

He set his right shoulder against the line and hauled. The first wrap bit the iron. The ferry's bow began, grudgingly, to answer. Water boiled under the hull. The small boats nearest the landing scattered, too late and badly. One struck another and both spun away cursing. Daro's voice went over them, richer now, building. He had started the rite already, the bastard, not waiting even for the crowd to settle. Sereya's pole hit stone once in time with his line, brass on rock, bright as a bell.

Iven took a second wrap around the capstan.

The iron ratchet at the base was frozen half up, half down. Old salt had made a sculpture of it. He kicked it. It dropped with a crack. That was good enough. He leaned back and gave the line more body.

The ferry swung another foot. Riva drove the stern with the pole. They were almost there, almost far enough through the swing.

Then Storm Mouth took its first true pull.

You could feel it before you saw it. The water under the landing sank, not by much, but in a way no river water should: a drop straight down, as if a lid had been lifted somewhere beneath them. The line in Iven's hands snapped tight so fast the hemp burned through his wet palms. The ferry's bow jerked toward the channel and then away again, fighting two directions at once.

"Hold it," Riva yelled.

He laughed once because there was nothing else to do with a command that stupid. Of course hold it. Let it go and the ferry spun broadside into the little boats and chopped them all together. Hold it and maybe you bought her three heartbeats to finish the swing.

He got a third wrap on. The capstan began to turn under load, slow and murderous. The ratchet caught, skipped, caught again. The loose tail kinked around the drum exactly where it should not. If he let the kink ride, the whole line would jump the wrap. If he stopped to clear it with care, the ferry was gone.

So he used the hand he should not have used.

He shoved his left hand between rope and iron to flatten the kink and keep the turn seated for one more bite.

The capstan took him at once.

There was no clean sequence. No first pain, then understanding. There was pressure, obscene and intimate, as if the machine had put his whole hand in its mouth and chosen not to bite through yet. Then skin went. Then sound came back and he realized the raw animal noise in the rain was his own.

The rope dragged his palm open. Two fingers folded wrong. Something in the heel of the hand burst hot across his wrist. He saw one white blink of bone before the next turn crushed that too out of sight. The capstan kept eating, patient as debt.

He yanked back on instinct and got nowhere. The line had him. The only useful thing left in the world was the ferry's bow, and he could see with absurd clarity that it was swinging exactly where Riva needed it. One more heartbeat. Maybe two.

"Iven!"

That was Nami, not Riva. Riva was still doing the work.

He jammed his right heel against the capstan base, grabbed the line above the drum with his right hand, and wrenched himself backward with everything below the neck. Flesh tore loose before the machine let him go. His left hand came free as a shape he did not recognize and would not recognize again.

He fell off the landing into knee-deep black water and slammed the bad hand against the post on the way down. That pain was clean enough to see stars through rain.

The ferry completed the swing.

Its bow drove in between the landing and the first of Daro's follower boats so hard the hull-to-hull crack sounded like a tree splitting. One shrine boy went overboard with a scream. Lale caught him by the back of the shirt with the oar and flung him onto the ferry deck like a hooked fish. Riva jammed the pole down and held the ferry there, chest heaving, eyes on Daro.

Iven tried to stand. The world narrowed to a ring with the capstan in the center of it.

His left hand hung at the end of his arm inside a skin of blood and rain. The little finger still looked like a finger. The rest looked worked over, flattened, half-unmade. He could not close it. He could barely tell where the palm ended. Salt water found everything open and made sure he knew about it.

Bato was suddenly over him, low and spare and stronger than his lowered shoulder promised. He took one look and said, "Don't look again."

"Wasn't planning to court it."

Bato pulled his belt knife and cut the sleeve open to the elbow. Efficient, no pity. His nearly white hair was whipping loose from its cord. Up close he smelled not like shrine smoke or rot, but like clean brine and the bitter leaf sailors chewed to keep from puking in heavy sea.

"Wrap above the wrist," Iven said, because if somebody did not say the obvious thing he was going to start shouting.

"I know."

"Good. I'd hate for my education to die unused."

Bato's mouth moved once, not quite a smile. "Still talking."

"Means I'm alive."

"No," Bato said, pulling the belt tight with a force that made blackness rush in from the edges. "It means you're you."

Above them, Daro's voice rose another step. The water around the landing began to spin.

***

Riva heard Iven scream. She did not turn until the ferry was fixed where it needed to be.

It was one of the ugliest things she had ever done, and she knew that while she did it. The work itself was necessary; that was not what made it ugly. It was ugly because she knew whose scream it was, and because necessity did not cancel the fact that she let the scream stand there unanswered for the length of three pole strokes while she drove the ferry home.

Later, if there was a later, she could wear that.

Now she let the pole bite bottom one last time and shoved until her shoulders burned raw. The ferry came hard against the landing and held. Lale dropped the spare oar and ran for Iven. Nami, white as net float bone, half rose as if to follow and then froze again when Daro's voice changed.

It changed because he had crossed out of persuasion and into rite.

He stood on the chapel step above the landing, barefoot on black stone furred with salt, one hand high on Sereya's pole and the other out to the crowd as if he were blessing them and taking payment in the same motion. Rain had washed his hair flat to his skull. Blood ran from somewhere at his temple where a boom or branch must have clipped him in the chase, making one side of his face look painted for war. The pilgrims below him did not care. If anything, the blood helped.

"You wanted the road cleared," he called, and even now he pitched his voice not to her, but to everyone. "Then don't drag me backward with the fear that made this flood. Come close. Witness it. The mouth knows its own blood."

"Daro," Riva shouted. "Stop."

He looked at her then, and the look was full, neither dismissive nor theatrical, but hurt and furious and exalted, all the worst things at once.

"You had your chance to make them believe in labor," he said. "You taught them weight. You never taught them hope."

One of the Halcote women below him turned enough for Riva to see the patch on her sleeve where she had repaired the same tear three different times in three different thread colors. Cheap household work done careful because it had to last. She held a boy of maybe ten by the shoulder. The boy had an infected mosquito welt at his jaw that he kept scratching raw with one dirty nail. People were bringing their whole ridiculous ordinary lives to this place and handing them to Daro because he had found the right voice for hunger.

Riva climbed off the ferry and onto the landing. Her boots slipped once in bird filth and salt. The key under her shirt struck her chest again, harder this time, because the whole structure was vibrating.

Bato had hauled Iven up far enough that he was sitting against the leaning post with his bad hand wrapped in a belt and half a torn shirt. His face had gone the color of old wax, but he was still upright. Nami crouched beside him with both her hands at his shoulder as if touch alone could stop blood. Lale had stripped off her overskirt and was winding the cloth tighter above the wrist, her mouth a hard narrow line. When she saw Riva glance at the hand she said, "If you're going to be sick, do it elsewhere."

Riva was not going to be sick. She had been through enough wrecks to know nausea by all its names. A cold place opened behind her ribs and stayed there.

The water below the landing drew downward another inch. Little boats bumped and corrected and bumped again. The crowd felt it now. Murmurs broke. Daro raised his voice over them and the murmurs bent back toward him like grass in new wind.

Bato said, "He's found the bite."

"Then unfind it," Riva said.

"I can't. Not from here."

"You kept this place."

"I survived this place. That isn't the same thing."

Daro drove the brass ferrule of the pole against a scored stone slot in the chapel step.

Riva saw the slot because the strike knocked salt loose from around it. Under the crust was iron, old and blackened and toothed, sunk into the stone in the same pattern as Reed Gate's release housing. The pattern was kin to Reed Gate's, only bigger and meaner. She had spent a day at Tovin's side weeks ago with both hands numb from cold mud while the old woman cursed over that first sluice's teeth. She knew the shape of a lock when the river wore one.

The key under her shirt suddenly had weight enough to bruise.

She yanked it free on its cord. Rain washed the rust smell up from it at once.

Bato sucked salt off his upper lip and took one involuntary step toward her, his good hand half raised before he stopped it. Recognition had stripped the weathered blankness off his face.

"Who gave you that?"

"Tovin."

"Of course she did."

"What does that mean?"

But he was no longer looking at her. He was staring at the iron scored into the chapel step, at the way Daro's pole had seated in one channel but not all of them. Now that Riva had seen the first housing, she saw the rest: one to the left under a lip of broken stone, another half buried near the landing post, two more up on the chapel platform where flood slime and salt had hidden them from any eye that had come looking for revelation instead of mechanism. There were five iron bites in all.

Daro struck the pole again and began the old opening line.

The water answered.

It answered like drainage. The whole black reach before Salt Room dropped at the center and started turning. One of the corpse-barges spun nose-first into the forming suck and vanished so quickly the shrouded body aboard only had time to show the shape of knees under cloth before it was gone. A pilgrim shrieked. Another laughed in pure terror because terror and ecstasy were first cousins in a flood crowd.

"Back your boats off," Riva yelled.

No one listened. Or too many listened too late. Oars crossed. Hulls struck. The grain-clerk in the wet coat who had watched her slap Nami tried to stand and lost his footing. The lacquered bowl went from his hands and spun on the water once like something performing before the pull took it.

The boy with the torn jaw welt went after it because children still believed dropped things could be retrieved by desire. His mother caught his shirt and missed. Riva saw the exact instant when the boy understood that the water was not a floor and would not act like one. His mouth opened. The pull took him sideways under another boat.

Nami made a sound like a knife on a plate and started forward. Lale caught the back of her dress.

"Don't."

"He's alive."

"So stay that way."

Daro was still singing. His face had changed now too. It had tightened into concentration so intense it made him cruel by accident. He felt the mouth answer him and mistook answer for obedience. Riva knew the look. She had worn it over engines, over floodgates, over anything she thought hard labor could bully into function.

"Daro!" she roared. "Look at the water."

He did not.

The first living pilgrim came up in the spin close enough to touch. A woman from Lantern Quay with fish-scale scars on both thumbs clawed at the ferry hull. Riva knew those scars because all the women from that quay took them the same way, gutting too fast in cold months. This one had a green eel-hook tied into her braid for luck. She hit the hull twice, mouth opening and closing in the turning black water, and then the spin tore her loose.

Iven, half conscious against the post, said through his teeth, "This isn't a blessing."

Bato ignored the drowning woman because there were already too many bodies to count and because some kinds of mercy had to choose structure over reach. He grabbed Riva's wrist hard enough to bruise and shoved the key toward the first exposed housing in the stone.

"There," he said. "Now."

Riva jammed the key in.

It fit, though not neatly. Tovin had never made neat tools. But the cut of the iron took it, old metal recognizing old metal. Riva turned with both hands. For a terrible instant nothing happened. Then somewhere under the stone a bar clanged back and one section of the chapel platform dropped an inch with a gout of black water forced up around it.

The suction changed shape. It did not stop.

One bite released. Four bites held.

Riva stared. The answer was physically there, stupid as a hammer laid on a table, and still she needed the next second to accept it.

Bato said it first.

"She lied about the last mouth the same way she lied about sainthood."

"Plain speech, Bato."

"It was never one mouth."

Another boat tipped. The grain-clerk surfaced once without the bowl, spectacles gone, both hands spread uselessly. Daro broke his line at last and looked down.

Riva turned the key harder, but the housing was already fully set. Iron would not invent a second notch because she wanted one.

"What is it?" Lale said.

Her voice was steady. Only the hand on Nami's dress showed strain. The tide marks on her forearm were black with rain, each line standing out like fresh cuts.

Bato pointed past the drowning crowd to the five housings hidden in the stone around Daro's feet.

"A lock," he said. "Five bites. Five stations. Sereya tried to make a throat out of it."

Riva looked at Daro, at the pole seated in one slot, at her own key in another, at the remaining housings slick with storm water and neglect. The current wanted sequence. Witness meant nothing to it.

Below them, the water took another pilgrim under.

Daro saw him go. His singing faltered. For the first time that day shame got clean space on his face.

Innocence was gone. Work still had a chance.

***

Pain came as occupation. It moved in and set up house. People with smaller hurts called it waves because they could still imagine it leaving.

It put its wet boots on the table and looked at him as if to say, We live here now.

Iven kept his eyes off the left side of his own body and concentrated on tasks small enough to survive. Breathe before the black ring closes. Don't vomit, because vomiting would mean bending and bending would mean seeing. Keep the bad hand above the heart, or anyway above whatever part of him still took orders. Listen when somebody says a useful thing.

The useful thing, for the moment, was that Bato had been right.

Iven heard it in the iron before he understood it in words. The first housing had opened under Riva's key with the same clanging complaint he had heard at Tovin's gate weeks ago. It was mechanical, a stubborn metal answer to force applied in the right place. And when it opened, the water at Storm Mouth had shifted from one ugly pattern to another instead of clearing clean. Any dock thief with half a season on salvage could read the rest. One release in a five-point rig only changed where the strain went and made the remaining anchors meaner.

He laughed once and it came out sounding like a cough dragged across gravel.

Lale glanced at him from where she was bracing Nami with one knee and keeping pressure on his arm with both hands. Her careful bookkeeper face had gone fierce and blunt. "What?"

"Your saint mother," he said. "She built herself a fucking lock."

"I noticed."

"No, I mean a real one. Iron, load, sequence. Look."

He nodded toward the chapel platform. Daro still had the pole jammed in one housing, staring down at the spin of boats and bodies as if the water had insulted him personally. Riva had Tovin's key in another slot and one boot planted so hard against the stone her leg shook. Three iron housings remained unopened, each hidden in a different piece of the platform and landing. Lale followed his gaze. Her eyes changed as the map of it laid itself over whatever Paska had written in her arm.

"Five points," she said.

"Like a ferry swing," Iven said. "Like any rotten job that kills you if one line takes the whole load."

Bato heard that and gave him one short nod, the sort Enver only ever used when acknowledging a mistake he was too proud to call instruction. "Now you're listening."

"Would've been easier if the lesson cost less hand."

Nami was staring at the whirl below the landing with that same dangerous stillness she had worn before trying to climb overboard. But now her gaze moved around its edges, tracking the places where the black spin touched the reeds, the chapel stones, the ferry hull, Daro's bare feet. She kept one hand on Iven's good shoulder without seeming to know she had put it there.

"It wants all the touched places," she said.

Iven would have rolled his eyes if the world were not already rolling enough. Still, he listened, because Nami's strangest lines had stopped being the easiest to dismiss about nine disasters ago.

"Touched by what?" Lale asked.

Nami blinked at the question as if it were much too small. "By them. By the route. By what each one kept."

Bato turned his better ear toward her this time.

Riva shouted from the platform, "We don't have time for prophecy."

"It's rigging," Iven said, louder than he meant to. The effort sent a hot wash through his arm and almost dropped him into the dark, but he held on.

Riva looked back.

He loved her a little in that second, not cleanly and not in any way he would have named while whole. What he loved was that if you shouted a mechanism at Riva, she would give it more respect than any sermon.

"You open one bite," he said. "The others take the strain. That's why the pull changed without stopping. It's a gate job. A five-line haul."

Bato added, because plain nouns were all he ever trusted once urgency entered the room, "Five children. Five keeps. Five stations."

Daro said, "No."

He had gone hoarse around the edges. The word still carried, but not with the easy fullness from before. Down in the spin, the grain-clerk vanished for good. The prayer bowl struck the chapel wall once, cracked, and disappeared.

"No," Daro said again, now to Bato, to Riva, to the water, to the whole idea of being one piece in a structure instead of its center. "She marked me."

"She used you," Bato said.

There it was, blunt enough to bruise the air. Bato had not raised his voice. He almost never did. He stood crooked under the rain with his bad shoulder low and his salt-scarred face empty of theatre, and still the line landed harder than Daro's whole sermon had.

"She used all of you different," he went on. "That doesn't make any one of you the mouth."

Below, another pilgrim grabbed the landing chain and held. A man this time, thick-necked, beard braided with copper wire probably by a wife who liked her work visible. He kicked and found nothing under him. Riva moved before thought and went flat to the stone, reaching down one arm toward him. The key stayed jammed in its housing under her other hand.

He was too far down.

Iven lurched up. Lale swore and tried to hold him back. He shrugged her off with the shoulder Nami wasn't clinging to. The landing slanted under him. He nearly went down in the same black water that had just taken three other people, but righting himself on the post hurt less than letting Riva reach alone.

"Line," he said.

Nami already had one, because of course she did. She shoved the wet coil at his right hand. He made a loop one-handed badly and hated the clumsiness more than the pain. Then he threw.

The loop hit the pilgrim in the face. Good enough. The man got an arm through it by accident. Riva braced and hauled while Iven leaned back on the free end. His left arm exploded with each shift of balance. The landing edge was slick as gut. He could smell his own blood under the rain now, iron-rich and hot. The pilgrim came up far enough for Bato to seize his collar and drag him onto the stone in a coughing fold of limbs.

The man spat black water and a sprig of reed. Copper wire hung from one braid over one eye. He was maybe forty, one front tooth missing, wedding ring tattooed in blue around a swollen knuckle because the real ring had gone to debt years before. When he got breath enough to speak he said, to nobody and everybody, "My wife said he'd make it plain."

Daro flinched as if struck.

That mattered more than the words themselves because the crowd had begun, finally, to look at the water instead of only at him.

Boats still spun. The suck still took what came too near. But panic had cracked the old obedience. Fear was available to be taught a new shape if they moved fast enough.

Lale stood. Rain ran off the point of her chin. "Riva."

Riva looked up from the rescued man, key still locked under her palm.

Lale raised her marked forearm. "The channels are distributed. Paska said it and I heard it and then I filed it away because I did not want my mother to be right about anything. The final lock is distributed too."

"I know that now," Riva said.

"Knowing isn't sequence."

She was right, infuriatingly and carefully right. Iven would have laughed again if his teeth had not started chattering from shock.

Nami spoke next, very quiet, and everyone heard because the water had reached that terrible point of loudness where a soft voice could cut through it like a knife through tendon.

"It's got all of us already," she said. "Riva's got Tovin's iron. Daro's got the pole. Lale's got the map on her skin. I've got the same salt-fig seed I pocketed when the first drowned girl came back."

Lale turned. "You what?"

Nami's eyes did not leave the whirl. "I kept it in my pocket. I wanted something that said the river had picked me."

There it was: the same hidden proof of chosenness she had been warming in her pocket for weeks like a live coal. They could fight over it later. Right now it was useful.

Bato looked from one sibling to the next and, for the first time since Iven had met him, showed something naked as grief.

"Jun was the sixth mouth because Sereya tried to pay with a child and a lover instead of with the work divided right," he said. "Don't do her trick again."

The sentence went through every one of them. You could see it land. In Riva as a tightening of stance. In Lale as fury made useful. In Nami as horror stripped of glamour. In Daro as a kind of collapsing refusal. In Iven as the final cheap angle leaving the room.

The full ruined set of them was all the ritual would get.

The rescued pilgrim rolled to his knees, coughing, and stared at Daro with river water pouring out of his beard. "If you can't do it alone," he said, missing tooth bright in the dark of his mouth, "say it before the rest of us drown proving you can't."

Daro shut his eyes.

When he opened them again, he did not pull the pole free yet. Pride could still kill half a district between one heartbeat and the next. But he looked at the four unopened housings, at Riva's key, at Lale's marked arm, at Nami with the guilty shape of the long-hidden salt-fig seed under the wet cloth at her pocket, at Iven half upright with his left hand turned into butchered rope-meat because the ferry had needed to swing, and something in his face stopped resembling inheritance.

Riva said, "All five. Now we know."

Iven leaned his shoulder against the post to keep the world from tipping out under him. The capstan sat three feet away, innocent as a stool, blood in its teeth where his hand had been. He stared at it because it was easier than staring at anybody who loved him in whatever broken family way they still knew how.

"Then move," he said. "Before the mouth takes more names."

He saw Daro drag the pole free at last and turn for the chapel, not like a prophet now but like a man late to a job. Riva tore her key out after him, and then Bato and Lale were hauling Iven by the good side because there was no room left for anybody to wait.
