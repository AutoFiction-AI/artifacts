# Chapter 12

The pilgrim hut let the rain in faster than it let the ropehouse fire out. Even on the second night after it, the room smelled like wet ashes, boiled rice, old lamp grease, and the sweet rot climbing out of Nami's hand.

The roof leaked in three places. Iven had shoved cracked prayer bowls under two of them, and the third drip kept landing on the heel of Riva's boot as she sat on the floor with Nami propped against her chest. Every time the drip struck leather it made the same patient tick, as if the hut itself were keeping count of how long the fever had held.

"Let it up," Riva said.

Nami tried to say she had nothing left to bring up, but the broth lurched sour in her throat before the words could get there. Riva got the basin under her in time. Thin rice, yellow bile, a strip of chewed ginger Lale had bullied between her teeth an hour earlier: it all splashed the chipped enamel and came back smelling worse in the warm room. Riva's rope-rough hand stayed at the back of Nami's neck until the spasm was done. She did not flinch when spit ran over Nami's chin and down her own wrist.

"Better out," Lale said from the stove corner. "If it sits in you, it turns mean."

Her indigo-stained nails were darker tonight, almost black in the lampglow. She had torn one of her cleanest lengths of undyed shroud cloth into strips and stacked them on an overturned crate with the neat misery of somebody cutting money into ribbons. The old needle scar in her thumb flashed each time she folded a strip. Nami watched her hands because they were easier to watch than her own.

Iven crouched by the door with a knife, shaving the char off a ferry peg he'd dug out of the mud at dawn. Half his left eyebrow was gone from the fire, and when he forgot himself he rubbed at the bald patch with the back of his wrist, irritated by a face that no longer knew its own shape. Rain had blown his hair into points. Soot still sat in the lines around his gold tooth.

"Soup's wasted if she keeps spitting it into a bowl," he said.

"Then make less bad soup," Riva said.

"With what? Rain and righteousness?"

"With salt, at least."

He snorted but got up and pinched another two fingers from the paper twist. For three drips of roof water, the hut passed for peaceful.

Daro sat at Nami's feet on the reed mat, long legs folded under him, one hand resting lightly over her ankle scars as if he could keep her inside herself by touch alone. He had found an old mourning cord in the hut wall niche and had been unknotting and rebraiding it for the better part of an hour, his instrument-callused fingers moving with blind patience. When Nami started shivering after the vomiting, he did not sing. He only said, low and close enough for her alone, "Stay with this room, little tide. Hear the drip. Hear the stove spit. Don't go where it's colder."

That nearly undid her. She wanted the fever to be clean ordeal, something bright enough to prove she mattered. Instead it had turned her into a damp bundle of rags that needed her sister to hold the basin and her brother to name the sounds of the room back to her.

The cut lay across the webbing of her right hand where the reed glass had opened her days ago. At first it had only burned. By tonight the skin around it had swelled shiny and thick, as if another hand were trying to grow under the first. The edges were white where water had sat in them too long; the center leaked yellow. When Lale unwrapped the old bandage, the smell that came loose was fruit-sweet and foul enough to make Iven swear and push the door wider.

"Fuck."

"Go outside if you're delicate," Lale said.

"I'm not delicate. Your work just stinks."

"My work usually involves the dead washed first."

Riva shifted Nami higher against her chest. "Do it."

Lale dipped a cloth in the kettle, wrung it hard, then poured a thread of lamp spirit over the cut. The spirit ran into every split place at once. Nami jackknifed so violently that the basin clanged from her lap and rolled across the floorboards.

"No, no, no, no-"

"Bite this." Riva shoved a folded rag between her teeth and braced her from behind, broad forearm a bar under her collarbones. "Don't snatch your hand away. If she leaves that rot in, you'll lose the grip."

Grip. As if Nami's hand were a boat part and not her hand. But that was how Riva loved when she was afraid: by naming the damage as a repair problem and throwing her whole body at it.

Lale worked with her mouth flattened shut. She pressed, cleaned, pressed again. A plug of pale pus slid out. Nami made a sound through the rag that did not seem human enough to belong to her. Daro's hand tightened over her ankle. Iven, outside now, muttered to the rain and kicked mud off the threshold with the savage concentration he usually saved for bad cards.

"There's glass still in it," Lale said.

"Can you get it?" Riva asked.

"I can get some of it."

"Get the worst."

"That's what I'm doing, unless you'd like to borrow my eyes."

Riva did not answer. Nami could feel the restraint in her, thick and ugly as a knot in wet rope. The hut gave her nowhere to spend it. There was no wall she could fix, no winch she could set right, no fire left to fight. Only a fevered sister slick with sweat and a hand trying to spoil from the inside out.

Lale took the tip of Iven's knife, now cleaned in the spirit, and slid it under the cut. Nami felt the metal as brightness rather than pain at first, a white stripe from wrist to teeth. Then the pain arrived like a person running late. She bit down so hard on the rag she tasted dust and old soap.

"Nearly there," Daro said.

"Don't lie to her," Lale said.

"I'm praying over the definition."

"Pray faster."

The sliver of reed glass came out at last, no bigger than a fish scale, clear except for the clot of her blood stuck to one edge. Lale held it up between finger and thumb. In the lamplight it looked innocent enough to thread into a child's charm.

"That?" Nami managed when the rag came free.

"Bodies don't care about size," Lale said. "Bodies care what's in them."

Lale said it with the dry finality she used for folding shroud cloth. Absurdly, Nami barked out a laugh. It came out broken and wet and turned into coughing before it could become a real thing. Still, all three of them looked at her with the same startled relief, as if the sound itself had done a little work.

Iven came back inside with his shoulders shining from rain. "If she's laughing, she's probably not dying this minute."

"Excellent medical practice," Lale said.

"I've kept myself alive twenty-nine years."

"Against all visible intention."

He almost smiled. It dragged one corner of his mouth up and showed the gold tooth, but shame blunted it before it became full Iven insolence. Since Coil Reach he had moved as if every object in the room might burst into fire if he touched it wrong. Even now he handled the kettle lid with two careful fingers, then glanced at Riva after, quick as a thief checking whether he'd been seen.

Riva had seen. She saw everything. Trouble grew fast under that kind of light.

Lale packed the wound with a fresh fold of cloth, bound the hand, then slid her palm briefly under Nami's wrist to feel the heat there. "Still burning."

"I know," Riva said.

"Not about you."

"I know it's not."

The fever had gone through Nami's body in layers. First the cold, which made the reed mat scratch like ice under her calves. Then the heat, which turned the blanket into wet bark and the air into soup. Then the dreams. The dreams were not grand, not river-prophecy clean. They were mean little things. Jun's drowned infant face in the knot-hole of the wall. Sereya's ferry pole tapping at the underside of the hut floor. The salt-fig seed hidden in the hem of Nami's spare skirt, clicking against her hipbone like a tooth whenever somebody rolled her from one side to the other.

She had not told them about the seed. She wanted one fact that belonged only to her. Even now, fever-wet and stinking and half carried by other people's arms, she kept her good hand pressed now and then against the bundle of clothes under the pallet just to feel the hard bead sewn into the seam. It had been warmer than it should have been all day. She told herself that was only because everything in the hut was warm.

When the cramping hit lower in her belly, it came with enough warning for shame and not enough for dignity.

"Riva," she whispered.

Riva looked down once and understood at once. She always had been quickest at the bad practical thing. "Out," she said to the others.

"I've seen piss before," Iven said.

"Then go admire it from outside."

Lale was already gathering the clean cloths. Daro rose without a word and touched Nami's hair once as he passed. Iven hesitated just long enough to set the kettle closer to the stove so the room would not cool, then ducked after them into the rain.

"I'm sorry," Nami said, because the wet was spreading under her hips and there was no point pretending otherwise.

"Don't start." Riva slid an arm behind her knees and lifted her as if she weighed no more than sodden laundry. Her breath left her on the effort anyway. She was exhausted enough that the tendon in her neck stood out like cord. "You're sick. The bed's sick now too. That's all."

It was not all. The mattress stank at once. Nami could feel heat climbing into her face hotter than the fever itself. But Riva only laid her on the dry side of the pallet while Lale stripped off the wet blanket and the reed stuffing underneath it with the clean fury of a woman who wanted the world to be solvable by folding.

Between them they got Nami into a dry shift. Lale wiped her thighs with water gone just short of hot. Riva wrung out the old cloths and shoved them in a bucket by the door for morning. They handled her the way they handled wet bedding: brisk, competent, no shrine hush around it. The plainness kept her shame from swelling bigger than the fever.

By the time the others came back in, the room smelled sharper from the piss and the spirit and the boiled rice skin thickening in the pot. Iven opened his mouth, took the smell in, and shut it again without making a joke. He crouched by the threshold and started scraping mud from his boot treads with the knife tip, eyes on the work. Daro sat where he had been before, close enough that his knee touched the pallet now and then when the hut shifted in the wind.

Night deepened. Rain dragged itself across the roof in long wet hands. Someone fed the stove. Someone turned the bandage when it soaked through with sweat. Someone got broth into Nami a spoonful at a time. The chapter of the world with routes and drowned and burned rope and the impossible sea cut narrowed down to the size of a mouth taking water, a cloth cooling on the back of the neck, a body held through the shaking.

It was almost enough to make her believe they had once belonged to each other in ordinary ways.

Near midnight Iven lay down across the door with his rolled coat under his head, as if even asleep he meant to bar trouble with his body first. The burned patch above his eye shone red in the lamplight. Daro went outside twice and came back wetter each time, carrying armloads of reeds for kindling and once a small handful of dock leaves he insisted might pull heat if laid along the inside of Nami's wrist. Lale muttered that if river leaves cured blood poisoning every ferryman in Reedmouth would live forever, but she laid them there anyway.

Riva did not sleep at all.

Nami knew because every time she came up out of the fever she found Riva in nearly the same place, back against the wall post, boots planted, one hand on the blanket over Nami's shin as if she were keeping count through touch. Once Lale made her drink from the cup. Once Daro forced half a heel of bread on her and she chewed it with the flat patience of a mule. Once she went outside and came back with rain in the scar under her chin.

Toward the hour when the lamp went gummy and low, Lale moved to the stove to warm another cloth. She spoke without looking at Riva.

"She would've died if the glass had stayed in."

"I know."

"That's getting thin."

Riva rubbed a thumb over the edge of the blanket. "Then say the thicker thing."

Lale stood very still. The kettle began to tick before boiling. "The thicker thing is that I left her here to be handled by whatever story anybody wanted."

Riva's hand stopped.

Iven slept. Or pretended to. Daro had gone outside again. Nami lay with her eyes closed because opening them would spoil the terrible gift of hearing them before they had arranged their faces.

Lale went on, voice low and level in that way she used when feeling was close enough to bite. "I could've taken her inland. Not the first year, maybe. But after that, yes. I had a room. I had work. I had enough money for another plate if I'd stopped buying myself the lie that I was finished with this family."

Riva said nothing.

"I told myself she had Daro, Tovin, the house, the ferry women. What she had was every old pilgrim in Knot Bend sniffing around her for a sign. She had women asking what she dreamed and men looking at her like holiness turned edible if they waited long enough. I knew that. I went back to Halcote anyway because I liked being the An who could pass for clean."

The kettle had started a soft lid-rattle. Lale did not reach for it. That alone told Nami how deep the cut had gone.

Riva spoke at last. "You think I don't know what I did?"

"I think you know and call it weather."

"No. I call weather weather." Riva's voice stayed blunt, but the bluntness had gone ragged at the edges. "I left because I couldn't stand another room where every board had her hands on it. I left because work kept asking for the next thing and didn't care whose daughter I was. I left because the Bitter Thread was mine in a way nothing at Knot Bend had ever been mine. And I left Nami because once I was gone, coming back meant admitting I'd gone."

Lale finally took the kettle off the stove. Steam wrapped her wrist. She did not seem to feel it. "You could've taken her with you."

"On a salvage barge full of hired men and debt paper? I couldn't keep myself fed some weeks."

"You could've asked her."

That landed. Nami felt it land in Riva's silence like a pole striking bottom.

"Yes," Riva said after a while. "I could've asked."

Rain tapped harder. Outside, something in the reeds gave a long sucking shift, mud letting go of wood.

Lale set the warm cloth on the crate and leaned both palms on the stove lid. "I visited once, three years in. She'd grown again. Ankles all scarred from rash and no shoes that fit. One of the shrine women had her saying she'd heard Sereya laugh in the water trough for the price of sweet buns. I saw it and still got back on the cart that afternoon. That's mine too."

Nami had forgotten the buns. Not the taste of them, sticky with cheap date syrup, but the fact of having once thought them worth lying for. The memory came back so hard she could smell the syrup under the piss and lamp spirit.

"If I said I dreamed her," she said into the dark, eyes still closed, "they brought the sugar ones. If I said she touched my hair, I got two."

The room went still enough that even Iven's false sleep gave itself away; his breathing changed.

Nami opened her eyes. Lale had turned toward her with the cloth in one hand. Riva's face looked older than it had that morning, as if the fire had burned a year into it by the hour.

"I got good at it," Nami said. Fever made honesty easier and meaner. "Not because I believed it every time. Because I was hungry."

Riva bent forward, forearms on her knees, and stared at the floorboards between her boots. "You were seventeen."

"I was hungry," Nami said again.

Lale crossed the room and sat on the pallet's edge. The careful posture she wore like inland tailoring had gone to pieces hours ago; her shoulders rounded now, her damp hair stuck to her neck, and there was soot on one cheekbone she had missed when washing. "You shouldn't have had to make yourself useful that way."

Nami looked at her bandaged hand, thick and stupid in her lap. "Everybody did."

It was the plainest thing in the room. Sereya had made use of love, holiness, beauty, voice, labor. After she died, everybody else had gone on doing the same with whatever scraps remained. Even Nami had done it. Especially Nami.

Riva reached over and pulled the blanket higher over Nami's shins, tucking it under her calves so the draft could not find them. She offered no speech, no promise arriving late and dressed as rescue.

Lale set the warm cloth at the back of Nami's neck and left her hand there longer than needed. On the other side of the hut, Iven rolled onto his back with a curse too soft to count, exposing the fact that he had been awake. No one called him on it. The night had stripped the room past embarrassment.

When Daro came in again, bringing wet reeds and the smell of black water, he looked from one face to the next and understood something had been opened in his absence. He did not ask what. He only fed the stove, wrung rain from his sleeve, and sat down close enough that the five of them formed a rough ring around the pallet, as if that shape alone might hold till dawn.

Nami slept then, not because the fever had relented, but because for the first time since Coil Reach she believed they might still be there when she woke.

***

Morning came gray and close, rain thinned to a sift through the reeds. The fever had not broken, but it had changed weight. Nami could sit without the world tipping outright. That counted as improvement in the hut's hard mathematics.

Riva had wedged the door open with a mooring block. From the pallet Nami could see the backwater below the hut and, tied against the mud bank, Sereya's old funeral ferry.

It looked smaller in daylight than it had in memory, or maybe the Bitter Thread had spoiled scale for all of them. The ferry was long and blunt-nosed, flat-bottomed, black paint worn silver at the gunwales by years of hands and stretchers. Brass rings lined the inner rail where bier ropes had once been tied. One plank near the stern was split and cinched with a temporary splice. Ash smeared the bow where fire had kissed it without taking hold.

Iven stood calf-deep in mud beside it, shirt off, driving a peg home with the butt of a hatchet. His chest and arms were striped with soot where the rain had not fully washed him clean. When he concentrated he tucked his tongue into the corner of his mouth like a child threading a needle; Nami had forgotten that about him. A line of scorched ferry rope hung from one shoulder. Every few blows he stopped to test the plank with both palms, listening through his hands.

Lale sat on the threshold with her forearm bare, studying Paska's fresh tide marks in the gray light and cross-checking them against the reed ledger laid open on her knees beneath a scrap of waxed cloth. The tattoo lines had gone darker in the damp, blue-black against her skin, like new routes surfacing under the old one. Daro leaned against the outer post, wet hair tied back with the rebraided mourning cord, face too composed for the hour. He looked as if he had been speaking to the water and had not liked what it told him.

Riva came up from the bank carrying one of the long ferry poles on her shoulder. She had tested it in the channel mud; the end was black and slick. "Bottom's there," she said, dropping the pole against the wall. "Current's ugly but not blind."

"High praise," Iven said.

"Don't flirt with the boat till you know if she'll float."

"She floats." He slapped the patched plank. "Ugly little thing'll float in spit."

Riva ignored the line, though Nami saw the corner of her mouth move. "Floating isn't the only problem."

It was there now, the morning's real work. Not the patching. Not the bandage change. The question crouched behind every sentence: no dredge, no heavy gear, one sick sister, one final cut still waiting somewhere downriver with the sea on its teeth. Riva had the look she got when she was trying to build a wall out of facts before feeling could get in.

Lale closed the ledger on a thumb and said, "The old bier lane still holds from here to the Halcote branch. Paska marked the west reeds, and the flood hasn't had time to erase all of it."

"That lane was built for this," Daro said, nodding toward the ferry.

Riva looked at him once, flat. "I didn't ask the river choir."

"You never do unless it agrees with you."

"I asked the mud. The mud said maybe."

Iven hauled himself into the ferry and knelt to inspect the bow seam. "Mud says maybe to every bad idea. That's why it's mud."

Nami pushed the blanket off and swung her legs over the pallet edge. The floor came at her sideways, but she got one foot down. Riva was inside at once.

"Sit back."

"I can stand."

"You can almost stand. That's not the same thing."

Nami hated how quickly tears came when she was weak. They made everything seem childish. "If you're deciding whether to leave me, don't do it like I'm furniture."

That stopped the room cold.

Riva's face hardened, not at Nami but at the sentence itself, at how well it fit. Lale set the ledger carefully aside. Outside, a fish jumped in the backwater and fell with a slap flat enough to sound like mockery.

"I'm deciding whether we stop," Riva said.

"Same question," Nami said.

Riva did not deny it. She scrubbed a hand over her mouth, then paced once to the door and back because the hut was too small for the kind of anger that needed walking. "Here's the count. The Bitter Thread's gone. The winch is slag. We saved two poles, one kettle, half a line locker, and that ferry because Iven cut her loose before the barge coughed sparks into the reeds. Nami's hand's bad. Daro hears things I can't build a chart from. The last cut is storm water even in kind weather, which this isn't. So yes. I'm looking for one rational reason to stop before this turns from hard into stupid."

Iven glanced up from the seam. "I cut her loose because she was tied where I could reach, not because I was thinking like a saint."

"Didn't say you were."

"Just keeping the history neat."

Lale's eyes stayed on Riva. "Stopping isn't clean either."

"I know that."

"Do you? Because one version of stopping is leaving Nami here till the fever breaks and pretending that's mercy."

Riva's jaw moved once. "Enver's yard is half a mile back. He has dry sheds and people."

"People we know?" Nami said.

"People who wouldn't let you die."

Nami almost laughed, which hurt too much to finish. "That standard's getting lower every day."

Riva took a breath through her nose. "You think I don't hear that?"

"I think you hear it and keep counting poles."

There it was again, her oldest sister's method laid bare. Task. Weight. Mechanism. If she could make a thing material enough, maybe guilt would stop being a living animal and become something she could lash down.

Lale stood up from the threshold. "No."

Riva turned toward her. "No what?"

"No leaving her in a shed while we go finish our dead mother's work. We already ran that experiment for seven years."

"This isn't that."

"It rhymes too closely."

"Lale-"

"No." Her voice did not rise. That made it sharper. "You wanted a rational reason? Here is one. The flood pulse is already pushing inland. If we stop, Halcote takes the sleepwalkers harder before noon tomorrow. If we leave her, we repeat the exact injury we spent last night naming. And if we take the ferry, we move slower, yes, but we move on a hull built for shallow grief instead of a dredge built to bully mud."

Riva frowned. "Shallow grief?"

Lale looked irritated with herself. "You know what I mean."

"Say it cleaner."

Lale did. "Flat bottom. Light draft. No keel to catch on reed shelves. Small enough to drag where we have to."

"That's cleaner."

Iven grinned despite himself. "She was listening when Enver talked boats. There's hope."

"You listen when anyone is insulting you," Lale said.

"True."

The grin went away as fast as it had come. He tested the seam again, then looked at Riva without the usual slant. "She's sound enough for four and a sick one if we strip her bare and don't get proud. One lantern. No dead weight. We pole, we drag, we bail. That's the job."

Riva folded her arms. "And when the channel turns and the poles stop finding bottom?"

"Then we pray," Iven said.

"Useful."

"You want the useful part? This boat was made to go where people didn't want to go slow. That's the design."

Daro laughed once under his breath, not from amusement. "That's the whole family design."

Riva rounded on him. "If you've got something besides sound, bring it."

He looked at her for a long second. Nami felt the turn before he spoke, the way a current changed pressure under a hull before the water showed it on top.

"Fine," Daro said quietly. "The drowned have been talking to me since I was a child."

Nobody moved. Even Iven's hands went still on the seam.

Riva's expression did not change much. That made it worse. "Now?"

"Not now. Always."

"Daro-" Lale started.

"No, let it come ugly." He pushed off the post and stood in the doorway, not grand, not singing, just wet and tired and past whatever vanity had kept the lie in place. "When I was ten I used to sleep under this ferry because the house was full of mourners and Sereya's lovers and I wanted dark more than comfort. I heard them through the hull first. Knockings. Breath. Then words. A drowned cooper asking for the copper ring he'd swallowed. An old woman furious that her daughter had buried her in the wrong shawl. Childish things. Precise things. Sereya checked twice, maybe to shut me up, maybe because she already knew. Both times I was right."

Iven let out a breath with a curse folded inside it.

"Why didn't you say?" Nami asked. Her voice came out smaller than she meant.

Daro looked at her first, not Riva. "Because I liked it. Because it made me feel less throwaway. Because once you know the dead lean nearer to you than to the next person, it's very hard not to build a throne out of that, even if the throne is rotten wood and river slime."

The scrape in Daro's throat carried his oldest trick, shame arriving dressed half as confession and half as sermon. But the shame was real. Nami could hear it rasping under the words.

Lale's face had gone pale under the soot. "Did she tell you it meant something?"

"Of course she did." His mouth twisted. "She told me it meant I could be useful if I learned not to waste it. She was right about the useful part."

"And you've just been carrying this?" Iven said. "All these years?"

"You've carried worse and called it charm."

"That's not an answer."

"No," Daro said. "It isn't."

Rain whispered through the reeds. Somewhere beyond the backwater a channel gave one of those long under-breath sounds Nami hated, water dragging past a buried obstruction. She felt for the hidden seed through the blanket by pure instinct, fingers pressing against the lump in the bundled skirt under her thigh. It was still there. Hard. Warm. Ridiculous suddenly, and not at all ridiculous.

Riva spoke at last. "You want me to trust the last cut to voices you've hidden since childhood."

"No." Daro's answer came fast enough to surprise all of them. "I want you to stop pretending silence is safer than what we're already carrying. The voices don't make the boat float. The draft does. Lale's marks do. Iven's patch does. Your hands do. The voices are only one more filthy fact in the pile."

That landed harder than if he had tried to make himself oracle.

Riva stared at him, then at the ferry, then at Nami on the pallet with the blanket slipped to her waist and the bandaged hand lying thick in her lap like something borrowed. When she looked back up, the fight had not left her face. It had changed tools.

"And the rational reason not to stop?" she said.

Daro spread his hands. "There isn't one that keeps other people dry."

Lale picked up the ledger again and tapped its edge against her thigh. "Halcote first. The walkers will go where the ground's higher and the families are slower to admit what's happening. We can take the bier lane north, split labor when we have to, and still keep moving toward Storm Mouth."

The name tightened the room at once. Halcote meant walkers reaching dry boards before noon and families stubborn enough to call the damage weather till it had both hands on them.

Iven climbed out of the ferry and wiped his hands on his trousers. "I'll strip the extra bench and the bier hooks. Less weight."

"Leave the rings," Daro said.

"Why?"

"Because the dead aren't done with us."

"That's cheery."

"It's true."

Riva bent and picked up the pole she had tested in the mud. She weighed it, shifted her grip, then set the butt on the floorboards with a knock that made everyone look at her.

"Fine," she said.

Nobody spoke.

"Fine," she said again, because the first had clearly not been believed. "We take the ferry. No cargo we can't eat, drink, bandage with, or strike somebody awake using. One lantern. Two poles on deck, one spare. Lale, you ride the map. Iven, you make sure the bow seam doesn't open and drown us all out of spite. Daro, if the drowned start talking, you give me words I can use, not performance. Nami-"

She stopped.

Nami met her eyes. The fear was there at last, naked enough to trust. Not fear of the cut. Fear of saying the wrong thing to the sister she'd already once left in other people's hands.

"I'm coming," Nami said.

Riva nodded once. "I know. I'm not leaving you in this hut."

It should not have mattered how plain she said it. It mattered anyway.

Lale crossed the room first. She gathered the folded cloths, the jar of salt, the spoon, the ginger heel, every small domestic weapon they had built against the night. Iven went back to the ferry and started undoing bolts with his knife and bad temper. Daro rolled the wet blanket and slung it over his shoulder, then paused by the pallet long enough to touch Nami's bandaged wrist with two fingers, careful of the sore places.

"Still here?" he asked.

Nami thought of the voices in the channels. Of the women who had fed her sweets for invented dreams. Of the seed warming in the hem under the blanket. Of Riva's hand on the basin, Lale's hands in the wound, Iven opening his mouth and closing it on the joke that would have made her smaller, Daro refusing this once to make his secret into a crown.

"Yeah," she said. "Still here."

Getting her to the ferry took all of them except Iven, who steadied the hull from the mud. Riva lifted under her knees and shoulders. Lale kept the bandaged hand from knocking the doorframe. Daro held the blanket around her so the morning cold wouldn't get in at the neck. It was awkward, unceremonious, and so careful it hurt.

The ferry rocked when Riva stepped in. Water slapped the flat hull. Up close, the brass rings smelled green and old. Somebody, years ago, had scratched a little fish into the inside rail with a nail tip; Nami ran her thumb over it before she sat. She did not remember doing that as a child, but the kind of person who scratched a fish into a funeral ferry felt like one of them.

Iven shoved them off the mud with both hands and jumped in after, landing light despite the exhaustion. Riva took the stern pole. Lale spread the ledger and her tattooed arm side by side under the waxed cloth. Daro stood at the bow listening, head tipped, not to the dead alone but to the ordinary trouble of current against reeds.

The hut receded at once, small and warped and smelling, a place built for wayfarers nobody expected to stay. The ferry moved slower than the Bitter Thread had ever moved and closer to danger, so low to the water that Nami could see each swirl take shape before it touched them. Nothing about it felt triumphant. They had traded engine for muscle, gear for hands, illusion for a blunt count of what each body could still do.

No one said they would manage. No one said they were ready.

Riva planted the pole, leaned her weight into it, and the ferry slid into the reed lane toward Halcote.

This time, nobody stayed behind.
