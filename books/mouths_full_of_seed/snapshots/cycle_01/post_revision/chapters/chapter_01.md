# Chapter 1

Rain had thickened the Ravel into something chewed and half-digested by the time Riva brought the Bitter Thread up Knot Bend. Reed hair dragged in the dredge chain. Funeral weed slapped the hull. The paddlewheel bit twice, coughed, and started whining on a submerged branch that should have stayed buried until low water. She leaned her shoulder into the tiller, felt the barge answer a fraction late, and saw the ferry house lift out of the rain ahead of her.

The House of Oaths still stood on its old pilings like a dare that had outlived the woman who first made it. The lower stairs were gone to flood. Prayer rags had wrapped themselves around the corner posts and rotted there into gray knots. Somebody had left fresh lamps burning under the eaves anyway, five small flames fighting the wet. That bothered her more than the water. Tovin Reed did not light lamps for gossip.

Riva nosed the Bitter Thread alongside the lee side of the house and threw a rope around a shrine post where she and Daro had once tied crab traps as children. The post listed under the weight. She made the knot tighter out of spite. Oren Pike's figures ran in the back of her head while she worked: four hundred eighty silver marks, plus repair interest, plus whatever he'd decide a late monsoon was worth when he came to squeeze her. She should not have brought a mortgaged dredge into saint-country for a rumor. She had spent the whole trip telling herself that much.

Then she looked up through the rain and saw her family watching from the open doorway.

Daro stood in front, reed-thin even in a soaked shirt, one hand at his throat as if the rain itself had found a way to roughen his voice. He still rubbed clove oil there when he was worried; she could smell it over wet wood and river mud. Lale was behind him with her inland coat buttoned all the way to the neck though the hem was black with floodwater already, one thumb working the old needle scar in the other hand. Indigo had settled in the cuticles around her nails so deeply it looked bred there. Iven lounged against the jamb with one boot unlaced, a broken playing card flexing between his fingers until the paste board gave and softened in the damp. He smiled when he saw her, and the single gold tooth flashed like a coin somebody had spent unwisely. Nami crouched on the floor just inside the door, bare calves slick with rain, her hair tied up in a cannery rag printed with tiny blue fish. She was not looking at Riva. She was looking down.

And Tovin Reed stood over all of them with her eel-smoke coat dripping at the cuffs, arthritic hands planted on her hips as if pain and weather were both things she could shame into better behavior. She had burn scars up both forearms from old gatework. She always rubbed rendered eel fat into the knuckles of her right hand before rain, and the slick shine was still there now.

"You took your time," Tovin said.

"I had a barge to get through half a drowned county."

"You brought it. Good."

Riva climbed the wet rope and stepped through the doorway. The house hit her with old smells before any memory had time to pick one: lamp oil, rotting reeds, shrine ash, fish skin, wet timber swollen so long it had forgotten being dry. It was Sereya's house, not a shrine no matter what the town had made of it after the burial. The room was too workworn for sainthood. Hooks and oars still hung on the wall, a cracked bait crock sat under the bench, and the floorboards shone with years of boots and brine.

The child lay on the old ferry table.

For one second Riva only saw size: a narrow body in a burial shift gone transparent at the knees, feet small enough that the tied ankles could have been carried in one hand. Then the rest of it came into focus: the wax marks at the ears where someone had sealed them for burial, the reed braid around both wrists, the copper memorial coins set over the eyes and washed sideways by the journey back. The girl's hair had been parted carefully before death and rebraided in a hurry after, with one side tighter than the other. Somebody who loved her had redone it badly because they could not bear to send her out ugly.

Nami had a fishbone comb in her hand. She had been working river silt out of the dead girl's braid with the same care she might have used on her own hair before a feast.

"How long?" Riva asked.

Tovin jerked her chin at Daro. "Ask the singer."

Daro did not take his hand off his throat. "Buried yesterday morning, if the braid's fresh and the wax isn't a village trick. She was laid out right."

"Somebody could've dragged her from a grave."

Lale's mouth moved like she had bitten linen thread. "Across three flooded parishes and into this house?"

Iven folded the ruined card and tucked it behind his ear. "If that's the version you're buying, I know two men in Halcote who'll sell you saints by the basket."

Riva ignored him. She stepped up to the table. The child's face had gone that peculiar river tautness that made skin look stretched over a thought. Salt had dried at the corners of the mouth in a white crust. It was not sea salt but the bitter, dirty kind that came up from backwater marsh when a current went wrong. Riva put two fingers under the jaw and lifted the head enough to look at the gums.

Something black clicked against her thumbnail.

"Riva," Tovin said, too late.

The seed slipped free of the girl's mouth and struck the table with a hard wet sound. It bounced once, rolled to the edge, dropped to the floorboards, and kept rolling through a line of rainwater toward the hearth. The thing was not shaped like any market fig pit she knew. It was bigger, almost polished, the black skin striated with gray like muscle ash. A dark trail shone behind it.

Nami inhaled sharply and reached.

Tovin smacked the back of her wrist with two thick fingers. "Don't."

The seed tipped into a crack between boards and vanished.

The house went quiet except for rain on the roof and the low thump of floodwater under the pilings.

Riva crouched and pressed her thumb against the seam where it had gone. The board edges were wet and soft. Something colder than the rain seemed to breathe up through the crack against her skin. She wiped her hand on her trousers and looked back at the girl on the table.

No one in the room was putting a performance on for her. That was the first hard fact. Iven had stopped smiling. Lale's careful posture had given up its straight line and gone rigid. Daro looked greedy and sick at once, which was how he looked whenever the river gave him the kind of thing he'd once prayed for and then hated himself for wanting. Nami kept rubbing the reddening place on her wrist where Tovin had hit her, but her eyes never left the floor.

Riva rose. "Whose child?"

Tovin said, "Haven't got a name yet. Drift brought her in after dusk. She knew the house, though."

"Corpses don't know houses."

"Then the water does. That's worse."

The answer landed in the room without flourish. Tovin had never cared for omen talk. If she said worse, she meant structure, not poetry.

Riva looked once around the house she had not slept in for seven years. Above the bench, where Sereya had once kept ferry ropes and iron hooks, somebody had tied dozens of prayer knots in fresh red thread. Pilgrims still came. They still fed the lie that this place wanted worship more than maintenance. Her mother would have liked that. Her mother had liked any arrangement where hunger and devotion could not be told cleanly apart.

"Why am I here, Tovin?" she asked. "Really."

Tovin's flattened nose wrinkled. "Because one child would let you call us fools and head back inland by dawn. I thought I'd save time and send for all of you."

Riva turned and finally let herself look at the others as a set instead of separate offenses. All five children of Sereya An were in one room again, and that felt less believable than the seed.

Lale broke the stare first. "I came because her runner said Reed Gate had failed."

"Your runner told me there were bodies in the wells," Iven said.

"Mine said the house called my name in the rain," Daro murmured.

Riva said, "That one sounds like it was written for you."

Daro smiled without warmth. "You still know my weaknesses."

Nami pushed to her feet. She was the only one who still moved toward Riva instead of away. There were river rash scars around her ankles where old salt had eaten the skin and healed badly, and she smelled of smoke, brine, and crab fat from the cannery. She had been seventeen the last time Riva saw her sleeping in this house with a damp braid down her back. Now she stood close enough that Riva could see a tiny hook-shaped nick in one eyebrow.

"I came because Tovin said you'd come if she sent hard enough," Nami said. "I wanted to see if that part was true."

That hit cleaner than accusation would have. Riva looked past her.

"Get the child off the table," Tovin said. "If she opens in the warm, we'll all regret it."

Riva frowned. "Opens how."

"You'll see soon enough if you keep arguing."

They moved because Tovin made motion feel like the only dignified option. Riva took the shoulders. Daro took the feet after a brief flinch he hoped no one had seen. Nami slid a board under the girl's back with both hands as careful as prayer. The body had already loosened in the joints. When they lifted, muddy water leaked from the hem of the burial shift onto Riva's forearm and ran warm for a second before the room air cooled it.

They carried the child to the side porch where the night breeze could keep the flesh from swelling faster. Nami tucked the braid across the dead girl's shoulder before stepping back. It was such a small useless tenderness that Riva had to turn away from it.

***

When they came back in, Tovin bent with a grunt, dragged the ferry table closer to the lamp, and wiped the surface with the heel of her hand. Wet soot and fish grease smeared into a gray film on the boards.

"Don't stand there like offerings," she said. "Lale, more light. Iven, get the hand-ferry hooks off the wall. Nami, find sailcloth that isn't already rotting through. Daro, if your ears are worth anything, use them. Tonight you need the order."

Lale snatched up another lamp from the shelf. Iven stripped two hooks from the wall pegs and tested their weight. Nami went to the bench where old ferry cloths sat in a damp heap that smelled of mildew and shrine ash.

Tovin marked five cuts with her finger, quick and ugly, each line pressed hard enough that her nail squealed over the wood.

"Reed Gate first. Mine."

Riva moved closer despite herself. She knew the places as weight and travel rather than shrine story: Tovin's eel yards, where iron teeth had to be greased with goose fat and prayers if you wanted them not to crack; Ilyan's mudbank, where bell sound carried stupidly far over fog; Paska's reach, where ink and fish blood shared drains; Enver's ropewalk with tar stink in the wood; Storm Mouth, where the delta stopped pretending it was a river and admitted it wanted the sea more than anybody living.

Tovin jabbed the first soot line. "The rain hit the old flood notch at dusk. It tore two eel racks loose, drove a pilgrim skiff sideways into the front teeth, and packed the choke with half the bank behind it. I sent poles in. The current took one and snapped the other. By full dark the gate was sealed."

Lale set the fresh lamp down hard enough to rattle the glass. "You had a new jam."

"I had a new jam on top of seven years of nobody keeping the lower cuts honest. That's why the road's breaking tonight instead of next week." Tovin flattened her palm over the soot map. "Water couldn't carry the drowned seaward, so it turned them wherever it could find an opening. Under houses, into ditches, up wells, back through burial cuts. We've got two in one night already if you count the one still trying to come in through the town well at Back Ladder."

Lale's head came up. "What do you mean, trying?"

"Bucket came up with hair in it and two fingers hooked over the rim. They cut the rope and left the well shut."

That shut Iven up better than the earlier slap from Lale had. He scratched the side of his neck where a tattoo had blurred into green fog over the years and looked at the floor as if it might decide to open under him next.

Riva said, "Wells aren't channels."

"No. They're where the pressure goes when the channels fail."

Daro had gone very still, listening with his whole throat. "Why now?"

Tovin looked at him as if he had asked why rain fell down instead of up. "Because tonight's the hardest rain we've had since Sereya's burial, and Reed Gate just took the hit the rest of the route was already too rotten to bleed off. If I clear my choke and leave Hymn Cut shut, the pressure backs somewhere else. If you idiots run to Ilyan first because song sounds holier than shovels, the backwash drowns Back Ladder by noon. That's why it starts tonight and keeps starting until we do this right."

Tovin stabbed the second soot line. "Hymn Cut for Ilyan. Daro, that means your throat, but not before mine."

Daro leaned one hip against the wall and let his voice go soft in the way that made strangers listen harder. "If I can move Hymn Cut faster than you can move iron, why not start there?"

"Because I like not drowning half the delta," Tovin said. "Inkwater comes after Hymn Cut settles. Lale, you keep count of the order because I don't trust grief or piety with sequence."

Lale looked at the five gouged marks. "You trust bookkeeping in a flood?"

"I trust you to notice when someone's about to do something stupid."

Tovin drove her finger down the third and fourth lines. "Inkwater for Paska. Coil Reach for Enver. Iven, once Reed Gate moves we need rope and capstan grease out of Coil Reach or the Bitter Thread starts eating itself before we make the turn."

Iven gave a little huff through his nose. "That leaves me almost no hobbies."

"Storm Mouth for Bato last," Tovin said.

Nami stood clutching a damp length of sailcloth against her stomach. "If the sea's where they're supposed to go, why can't we open Storm Mouth first?"

"Because brute force is how you drown a parish," Tovin said. "Later cuts opened too early give you backwash, not release."

Nami said, "Why us?"

It was the right question. Riva had been refusing to ask it because she suspected the answer would come smelling of fate, and she hated fate most when it tried to wear her mother's face.

Tovin surprised her by answering plainly.

"Because the lovers kept their pieces, but Sereya braided all of you through the route whether you asked for it or not. She hauled you to work sites. She slept you in the ferry. She made witness out of children because she thought witness was a kind of rope. The cuts remember hands and kin and the people who got hurt building them. That's you."

Nami swallowed.

Lale said, "That isn't law."

"It's law here."

Daro said, "So the river kept account. That's what you're saying."

Tovin snorted. "I'm saying your mother used every body she had."

That hit Daro harder than the mystic version would have. Good, Riva thought, and hated that she thought it.

Iven looked toward the porch where the child lay in the rain-chilled dark. "There's five lovers in the route and five of us in the room. You can hear the cheap story from here."

"Then don't tell it cheap," Lale said.

For a moment Riva almost laughed. It would have been the wrong kind of laughter, too sharp, but it almost came.

Tovin rubbed her eel-fat shine into one aching knuckle and went on. "I didn't drag you here for legend. I dragged you because the town's already choosing between panic and stupidity, and both choices drown people. I'm poling to Reed Gate before first light to see how bad the front teeth look from water level. Riva, you bring the Bitter Thread after gray, when you can see the posts well enough not to gut it. Your barge can clear the front choke if it doesn't snap an axle in the flood."

Riva's attention caught on that like a hook.

"If it snaps an axle," she said, "I lose the barge. If I lose the barge, I lose the debt behind it. If I lose that, Oren Pike strips the claim off my Halcote berth and I start over with a pole skiff and a begging face."

Iven's brows rose. "Only one of those is new."

She looked at him until he stopped grinning.

Lale said, "You brought the Bitter Thread anyway."

"I brought it to look."

"You brought it because you knew Tovin wasn't inventing machinery failure in the middle of monsoon."

Riva disliked that because it was true. Tovin did not sentimentalize a clogged gate. Tovin cursed it, greased it, and hit it with tools until it behaved or broke honestly. That was why Riva had come, not for mother or saint business but for the one old woman in Reedmouth who would rather be right than impressive.

***

Outside, something bumped the pilings under the house.

They all heard it. The whole room tilted toward the sound without moving.

Tovin said, "Driftwood."

No one believed her.

The bump came again, heavier this time, with a drag afterward that spoke of weight catching and not passing through. Water gurgled under the floorboards. One of the house lamps thinned and flared as the draft changed.

Nami whispered, "That isn't wood."

Riva felt the old practical part of herself take hold because the alternative was standing in her dead mother's house waiting for fear to decide the shape of the night. She took a boat hook from the wall and headed for the back stairs.

"Riva," Daro said.

"If you want a hymn, sing one after I know what I'm levering loose."

She climbed down into rain that hit hard enough to sting the backs of her hands. The underside of the house was all black water and cross-bracing, the pilings slick with weed. Her lamp threw a yellow wedge across the flood and came back blunted by the rain. She crouched on the lowest safe tread and reached the hook under the floor.

The thing below shifted.

It was not driftwood or a log. Hair rose in the water first, spreading and catching on a beam like drowned moss. Then a shoulder turned pale under the surface. The body had wedged itself sideways between two pilings, one arm trapped back behind it by the push of current. It was a man, broad in the back, with one boot gone and a shell ring on the right hand, the cheap pink kind sold to newly married men who wanted to look richer than they were. Burial cloth floated from his belt in unraveling strips.

Riva jammed the hook through the cloth and hauled. The corpse rolled just enough for its face to break water.

The mouth was tied shut with funeral braid.

That detail did more than the face did. Somebody had buried him. Somebody had sung him out, wrapped him, set him on the proper road, and the proper road had spat him back to Sereya's door like a complaint.

Tovin was beside Riva before she heard the older woman come down the stairs. Tovin held the lamp lower, her broad wrist steady despite the rain.

"Well," Tovin said. "Now you've got your second."

Riva kept the hook in place. The man's cheek had rasped against the piling, taking skin off in a pale strip. A dark line ran from the corner of his tied mouth into the flood.

"Cut him loose?" Tovin asked.

Riva looked up at the black bulk of the house over them. "Not till I know where the first seed went."

Tovin met her eyes then, and something like approval passed through the contempt there. It was very small, and it annoyed Riva more than insult would have.

They went back up together dripping and mean.

Inside, the others had not held still. Lale had found a pry bar somewhere in the house and was kneeling by the hearth seam. Her careful inland sleeves were shoved past the elbows. Iven held a lamp low for her and tried to make his uselessness look like assistance. Daro stood in the middle of the room listening toward the floor with his head tipped, all theater except for the fact that the theater came from somewhere real and he hated that anyone could tell. Nami had both palms flat on the boards a foot away from the crack, eyes wide and unblinking.

"Don't put your hands there," Riva said.

Nami looked up slowly. "It's warm."

The word made the room change.

Riva crossed to the hearth in two strides and crouched. A hairline split had opened between the planks where the seed had vanished. The wood around it looked swollen from underneath, as if something damp and patient were levering itself toward air.

Lale slid the pry bar under the seam. "The board won't lift. It's rooted to the beam."

"You don't know that."

"I know resistance when I feel it."

Iven said, "That sounded filthier than you meant it."

No one granted him even half a glance.

The split widened while they watched.

It did not happen fast enough to be dramatic. That was worse. The boards simply kept easing apart with small wet ticks, the nails creaking under pressure, until a pale nub showed between them. It was white first, thick as a finger joint, slick and translucent with black veining inside it. The smell that came up was not fruit in any market sense. It was sugar turned in mud, sweetness with rot already married into it.

Nami made a sound in her throat that might have been wonder.

Riva took her by the shoulder and hauled her back. The girl's weight startled her; Nami had grown solid working crab pots and cannery crates. She was not the child Riva had left. The realization came late and mean as a bill.

The nub pushed higher. Skin split at the tip with a tiny seam and showed a dark folded leaf inside.

"If that roots through the boards, you've got two days before people start walking," Tovin said.

Lale stood up too quickly, pry bar hanging at her side. "Walking where?"

"Water. Wells. Cuts. Any place the river sounds louder than home."

Iven stared at the sprout. "You've seen that."

"I've seen the start of it. Once. That was enough."

Daro finally spoke, and his voice had gone hush-soft, nearly intimate. "Mother used to say the river never asks with a clean mouth."

Riva rounded on him. "Don't start."

"You think I want to make this prettier than it needs to be?" His eyes flashed. "I was the one who knew what that braid on the man's mouth meant before you did."

"I knew exactly what it meant."

"Then stop acting like proof owes you courtesy."

That shut her up because it was too close to the shape of her own thought. She had come waiting for evidence to arrive in a form she could reject cleanly if needed: a failed bearing, a gate tooth she could replace, a town panic she could curse and leave behind. Instead she had a buried child on the porch, a buried man under the house, and a black seed splitting her mother's floor open from the inside.

The Bitter Thread rode against the pilings outside with a low iron complaint. She thought of the cracked sprocket tooth in the dredge chain, the repairs she had put off because interest already bit deeper than the machine. She thought of Halcote and its dry brick streets and how long she had spent convincing herself that Reedmouth was something she had escaped instead of something that had stayed inside her hands. She thought of Nami asking whether she'd come if sent hard enough.

Tovin said, "Decide."

The sprout opened.

The first leaf unfurled no wider than a thumbnail, black-green and slick as fish skin, but the underside flashed silver when the lamp caught it. The room smelled suddenly crowded, as if a whole orchard had been left to drown under the floor.

Riva looked at each of them in turn. Daro stood frightened into stillness, the clove oil at his throat sharp in the damp. Lale's indigo nails were dirty now, her coat ruined, and anger held her upright better than rest ever had. Iven had lost all swagger for once; the foolish gold tooth was suddenly only a piece of metal in a bad mouth. Nami stared at the leaf as if it might finally tell her something she'd been waiting years to hear. Tovin looked old and ugly and honest enough to be trusted with terrible work.

Reluctance was still there. So was resentment. So was the very clear fact that Riva did not want to give her barge, her debt, and what remained of her inland life to a route her mother had built through sex and coercion and local superstition refined into custom. None of that had changed.

But the floorboards kept parting around the sprout, and under the house the second corpse thudded once against the piling like a fist from another room.

"All right," Riva said.

No one spoke.

She heard herself continue in the voice she used when deckhands got stupid around chains. "Reed Gate first. Tovin goes ahead before light and reads the choke from water level. When she signals, I take the Bitter Thread in. Lale, find every lantern in this house and fill them. Iven, get the hand ferry clear and strip any shrine rubbish off the deck. Daro, you and Nami get the girl into the side room and keep that thing," she said, pointing at the sprout, "in sight. If it throws seed, you yell before you think."

Iven blinked. "You giving orders already?"

"I'm giving you work. Try to deserve the difference."

His mouth twitched. That was as close to obedience as he offered anybody on a good day.

Lale said, "And if Reed Gate doesn't move."

Riva looked at the wet soot map on the table, then at the black leaf lifting itself from the floor of her mother's house.

"Then we find out exactly how many dead Reedmouth can hold before it starts giving the living back wrong."

The line hung there, and nobody reached for speech or comfort. The roof boomed under monsoon rain, and floodwater dragged low and endless beneath the pilings.

Daro broke first. "Fine," he said, not loudly. "I'll go."

Nami nodded almost before he finished. "Me too."

Lale exhaled through her nose and picked the pry bar back up as if acceptance could be disguised as annoyance. "I wasn't planning to stay here and inventory rot."

Iven tugged the ruined playing card from behind his ear, looked at it, and flicked it into the lamp flame. The paste curled black at once.

"If we're reopening Mother's lovers one by one," he said, "this had better kill the joke before the joke kills us."

Tovin slapped the table once with her broad hand. "Good. Move."

She turned straight for the stove, shoving the blackened kettle onto the iron ring before she went to gather her pole and eel grease for the dawn run.

They did.

Riva took the back stairs two at a time and jumped to the Bitter Thread's deck. Rain struck the dredge bucket, the wheel housing, the patched tarpaulin over the winch, every piece of borrowed metal she had staked her next years on. The barge smelled like oil, wet rust, and the stubborn little hope she had never dressed up as hope because that made it easier to lose. She laid one hand on the tiller post and looked back at the lit house on its pilings.

For seven years she had treated Reedmouth as a place that only asked after her through other people: a debt runner, a funeral notice, a market rumor. Tonight it had put a child on the table and split a seed under the floorboards and dragged the rest of Sereya's children back under the same roof to watch.

That was not sainthood. That was machinery under pressure.

It was also, worse luck, real.

Before dawn Tovin would pole ahead to Reed Gate, and once the light was strong enough to show the drowned posts, Riva would take the Bitter Thread after her and see whether sequence, labor, and the old damage in their blood still counted for anything the river could use. If they did not, the dead would keep climbing inland by whatever openings the flood gave them: wells, pilings, grave cuts, house floors.

Riva checked the mooring knot once more, hard enough to bite her palm with wet rope, then turned toward the house where her brother's shadow crossed the porch carrying the drowned girl toward the side room and her youngest sister followed with the least-rotted sailcloth over one arm and a lamp held stubbornly high.

The life she had built on staying gone was over. The water had seen to that.
