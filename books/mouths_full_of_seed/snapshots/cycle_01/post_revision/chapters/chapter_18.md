# Chapter 18

Three weeks after Storm Mouth opened the sea road and took the dead with it, low water showed what was left to pay for.

The House of Oaths stood above Knot Bend on stripped pilings, half the old floor hacked out, the east wall leaning like a bad tooth. Mud had dried in a thick black cuff around every post. A goat skeleton hung under the landing where floodweed had wrapped it in place and then gone slack. The room that had once held Sereya's relic shelves still stank of soaked reed mat, lamp grease, and the sweet rot of timber that had taken water too long and never meant to survive it.

Riva stood on the slant of the landing roof with a pry bar under a length of warped plank and leaned until the nails screamed. When the board came loose, it let go all at once. She caught the far end against her thigh before it could slide and stove in the ladder below. The wood bent under her palm like wet bread. There was no patching that. It might make firewood, if it dried enough not to poison the smoke.

Below her, Daro gave two low beats on the frame drum he had taken to carrying under one arm. The sound came up through the stripped house and the open water under it, a round dull pulse that told the men on the mudbank to ease the replacement beam higher. He could still speak if he had to, but his voice had never come back from Storm Mouth as anything a crowd could stand under. When he forced it, the sound came out scraped raw, as if somebody had lined his throat with fishbone. So he kept time instead. Reedmouth had learned the beats fast. Two beats meant lift. Three meant hold. One hard strike meant somebody was about to lose a hand.

It made sense. Riva trusted a beat more than prayer.

She tossed the rotten plank down onto the salvage pile. Tovin Reed looked over from the sawhorses and snorted.

"That one was gone before the flood," Tovin said. She had pinned an eel hook to the neck of her sweat-dark shirt because she never trusted pockets, and every time she bent to sort nails the hook flashed like a bad little moon. "Sereya just painted over the rot and called it devotion."

"That sounds like her," Riva said.

Tovin spat into the mud, missed the nail bucket by an inch, and kept counting. Her arthritic fingers were black with tar where she had been sealing the bolts that could still be used. Beside her, Lale had turned two doors laid across barrels into a worktable. The restored reed ledger sat open under a wrapped brick to keep the breeze from taking the pages. Next to it she had three separate columns going in trade script: timber owed, labor promised, and debts nobody could wish out of existence. Her indigo-stained thumbnail moved down the page in dry little jerks while she listened to three people at once and believed none of them fully.

The town had come back to itself the way broken bone came back wrong: usable, swollen, and full of weather. Reedmouth's mud lanes had split open in the dry-down, showing old trash, drowned fence stakes, and clay jars the flood had shoved out of people's floors. Half the eel racks leaned. Three houses downstream had lost their corners entirely and stood open to the bend, beds and pots and baby shirts visible from the water as if privacy had gone out with the crest. The living were not sleepwalking anymore. That counted. So did the fact that no fresh corpse had pushed upstream under anybody's landing since the sea road opened. But the flood had taken payment in timber, skin, lungs, wages, and any illusion that survival came free.

Riva felt Oren Pike's latest demand as a damp square against her ribs where she had tucked it into her shirt that morning. Four hundred eighty silver marks, plus repair interest on a barge that was now ash and bent iron at the bottom of Coil Reach. Pike had written the numbers with a clerk's smug neatness, as if neat figures turned fire back into property. She had not shown the letter to anyone except Lale. The amount lived in her body anyway. It changed the weight of every new board she lifted.

Another beat sounded from below. Riva crouched, hooked her fingers under the next plank, and looked toward the path.

People were coming up from the bend carrying things that were not tools.

First came a grain-hall woman from Halcote with the red line of old flour burns along one wrist and a brass bowl hugged to her chest. Behind her, a novice from Hymn Cut kept hitching the slipping strap of a packet of candles under his shoulder as he walked. Two Reedmouth women followed with white prayer cloth bundled over their arms, good cloth too, the kind you used for burial wraps if you had the money. None of them looked like fools. That was the problem. Hunger for a shrine never arrived looking ridiculous at first. It arrived tired and grateful and wanting somewhere to put the feeling.

Riva climbed down before they could step onto the landing.

"If that's for burning," she said, nodding at the candles, "take it to somebody with a whole roof."

The novice stopped. "It's for the house."

"The house wants bolts."

The Halcote woman shifted the bowl in her hands. Up close, Riva could see a chip out of one front tooth and the skin peeling at the base of both thumbs, grain-hall damage. "People have been asking where to leave thanks," she said. "My sister's boy came out of that trance alive. He'd have walked into quarry water if your lot hadn't dragged him back. A place should mark it."

Riva looked at the brass bowl, the white cloth, the boy with candles, and then past them to the ferry house with its new boards stacked in a wet-smelling line and its open ribs showing. She understood the temptation so quickly it made her angry. A shrine would pull coins. A shrine would bring pilgrims who paid in candles, eels, promises, labor if they felt devout enough. A shrine might even put a dent in Pike's numbers. And once they started that, Reedmouth would wake up five years from now on its knees again, asking one dead woman or one damaged son to carry what should have sat on everybody's back from the start.

"No," she said.

One of the Reedmouth women drew herself up. She had her late husband's pipe stuck through her hair like a pin and the bowl of her shoulder looked permanently wrong under her dress, as if it had healed broken years ago and never forgiven the arm attached to it. "You don't get to say no by yourself."

"Watch me."

Lale's chair scraped. Tovin did not look up from her nail sorting, but Riva heard the old woman start counting under her breath, which meant she was angry enough to need numbers.

The Halcote woman did not move. "We're not asking for a saint circus," she said. "We're asking for somewhere to bring what happened."

Daro struck the drum once, not hard, just enough to stop the sentence from settling cleanly.

Everybody turned. He was standing on the lower platform in rolled trousers, his reed-thin chest still a little hollow under the work shirt, one hand resting on the drum rim. The skin at the base of his throat showed a pale rough seam now, healed badly where the voice had torn itself. When he swallowed, Riva could see the effort of it.

He rasped, "Not here."

The words came thin and ugly and made the novice lean forward to catch them.

"Daro," the Reedmouth woman said softly, as if softness made it less dangerous. "People need a room to kneel in."

His mouth twitched. Once he would have filled the whole landing before answering. Now he drew breath with care and spent it like coin.

"Then build one," he said. "This one crosses."

Silence held for a beat. Then Tovin barked a laugh with no amusement in it.

"There," she said. "The singer has more sense now that the river chewed his throat. This platform needs six new pilings, two braces, and a rope guide, not candle wax dripping into fresh tar."

The novice flushed. "Offerings brought the old house half its upkeep."

Enver Coil came up the path carrying two coils of hemp over his good shoulder and a sack of iron dogs banging against his leg. He limped worse now that the water had gone down and the old damp-weather hurt had nowhere to float. His beard still held a permanent smoke smell, and he had a habit of squinting one eye before he said something cruel, as if sight itself might improve the line.

"Offerings brought the old house rot," he said. "People who kneel don't notice when a sill goes soft. People hauling grain do."

Iven was behind him with a shorter coil tucked under one arm and his left hand strapped flat into a leather brace that ran from forearm to palm. The hand looked half borrowed now. The last two fingers would never uncurl right. Scar tissue shone pale against the faded river tattoos on the back. He still carried it like he could bluff it into usefulness.

"And people who kneel don't pay interest," he said. "Trust me. I've met them."

Riva expected the old shame-spark between Enver and Iven, but Enver only grunted and dropped the coils by the sawhorses.

Lale stood and put both hands on the ledger so the wind would not take the pages. "If you want the numbers," she said, "here they are. The east side of the house needs enough new timber to make a barge owner curse. The hauling teams need feeding. Riva still owes Halcote money on a dead dredge. Iven's market debt did not wash away because the river behaved itself for once. If this becomes a shrine, it becomes one family begging, again, to stand between Reedmouth and its own appetite."

She tapped the ledger with one ink-dark nail.

"If it becomes a crossing," she went on, "we can charge freight, burial passage, and ferry maintenance in plain books. Halcote grain moves here. Reedmouth fish move out. Families get their dead over the bend without waiting for some blessed body to put a hand on the prow. Those are the choices."

The Halcote woman looked past Riva into the stripped room. "And where does gratitude go?"

Riva opened her mouth, but Daro beat her to it with three soft taps, hold, and then a whisper that frayed on the last word.

"Into the work."

The boy with the candles shifted the packet under his arm. A little wax had already gone soft in the heat and spotted his sleeve. Riva watched him look from Daro's ruined throat to the open house to the tar pot smoking beside Tovin's bench.

No one spoke. The bend made its low sucking noise under the pilings. Somewhere downstream somebody was cursing at a stuck skiff. Reed flies worried the black line of mud on the posts.

The Halcote woman let out a breath through her nose. Then, without another word, she set the brass bowl on Lale's table, pulled one of the white cloths from the bundle, and folded it thick. "Fine," she said. "Use this on a shoulder, then. Better than bleeding on your new timber."

Tovin nodded once, which from her counted as tenderness.

The novice stared at the candles, then at the tar pot. "Can rendered wax help the seal?"

Enver looked at him as if measuring how much stupidity could be removed with labor. "A little," he said. "If you skim the dirt off first."

"Then skim it," Riva said.

Things moved after that because there was nothing left to hide behind. The Reedmouth woman with the broken shoulder tied her dead husband's pipe tighter through her hair, tucked the remaining cloth under one arm, and took a brace line without waiting to be asked. The novice knelt by the pot and peeled wax into Tovin's tin with his thumbnail. Enver set Iven to showing him how not to foul the hemp. Lale turned the brass bowl upside down and started using it to sort the larger bolts from the small.

Riva shouldered the new sill beam with the cloth pad under her collarbone. The weight drove her heels into the mud and made her feel the old towing-hook scar in her chin like a live wire. Across from her the Halcote woman lifted too, jaw clenched, forearms trembling. Daro gave them the beat: two to raise, hold, two again. No hymn rose over it. Only timing, breath, and timber coming level because enough bodies had put themselves under it.

When the sill settled into place, the whole stripped house gave a low wooden groan and then sat differently on its legs, less like a relic and more like something that meant to carry weight again.

Riva wiped the inside of her wrist across her mouth and tasted salt, tar, and old flood mud.

"All right," she said. "If anybody's still here to kneel, they can kneel tomorrow while they scrape barnacles off the lower posts."

That got a short ugly laugh, which was as close to agreement as Reedmouth ever needed.

By late afternoon the prayer cloths were gray with pitch, the brass bowl held bent nails, and nobody had tried to light a candle.

When Riva finally stepped inside the gutted front room, she pulled Tovin's iron gate key from her pocket where it had lived since Reed Gate and looked at the blackened beam above the doorway. She drove a nail into the fresh wood with three hard strikes and hung the key there in the open, next to Lale's first maintenance ledger. Anybody walking in could see it. Anybody could reach it.

Tovin saw and grunted.

"About time," she said.

Riva looked at the key swinging slightly over the stripped threshold and knew what had gone with it: the mean comfort of being the last hand on the lock, the one who decided when the place opened and whose orders counted once bodies crowded the door. Since the Bitter Thread burned, she had carried that iron like a spare piece of hull, hard against her hip, proof that she still owned one thing that answered only to her. Now her palm kept closing on the empty pocket seam. The house did not need one hand closed around it anymore.

She went back outside before she could make too much of that thought. There was still daylight. There was still work.

***

Nine days later the first loaded cart crossed the rebuilt landing without the house complaining.

Riva heard the weight before she saw it: wheel rims hitting the new plank joins, the slow rope groan of the guide line taking strain, Daro's drum giving the departure pattern from the west side. The crossing was not pretty. The roof over the waiting room was patched in three kinds of wood, and the new rail had already taken a gouge from somebody's pig crate. The fresh tar on the lower braces still smelled sharp in the heat. But the piles stood straight, the ferry rope tracked true through Enver's guide rings, and the house no longer listed toward the bend as if trying to remember drowning.

She kept one boot braced on the edge beam and watched the cart roll off onto the far mudbank. The load held sacks of grain, two chicken cages, and one old woman holding a covered pot in both hands like it carried religion. She climbed down with the pot still steady. Two coppers clicked into Lale's box for freight, one notation went into the ledger, and the next crossing was already being hauled into place for dusk.

The low-water light made everything meaner and clearer. It showed the cracked tide line on the surviving houses. It showed where storm salt had killed whole strips of kitchen garden downstream. It showed children back to stealing mulberries instead of sleepwalking toward the river, which felt like a miracle only if you had forgotten how ugly the word got in Reedmouth. Riva preferred the look of the freight box with actual coin in it.

Below the west wall, Nami was kneeling beside the nursery beds she had boxed out with old shrine shelves and broken bench planks. She had washed the flood silt three times through reed baskets and mixed it with high-bank dirt carted in from Halcote, because the first handful of mud by the house had tasted too brackish on her tongue. She trusted her mouth with soil the way other people trusted scales. Her hair was wrapped up in a rag that had once been part of one of Sereya's altar cloths, now stained green at the edges from crushed leaves. In the nearest bed, lines of bean and gourd had already pricked the surface in small hard hooks of green. Nothing monstrous. Nothing singing under the dirt.

Riva climbed down and stood over her. "How's it look?"

Nami pressed two fingers into the earth and lifted them. Damp, dark, and alive with a few grains of ash. "Clean," she said. "Mostly. The lower bed went wrong. Too much salt still. Smelled sweet before it turned. I threw it out."

"You tasted it?"

Nami shrugged without embarrassment. "How else would I know?"

She pointed with her chin at a shallow bowl where a handful of pale seeds soaked in water gone faintly cloudy. "These are bottle gourd. Those there are yard beans. The little tray by the wall is mustard. I kept waiting for one of them to split open with teeth in it just to be rude."

Riva huffed a laugh. "And?"

"They're rude the ordinary way." Nami brushed dirt off her knees and looked at the first bean hooks with an expression Riva still had trouble reading because it held disappointment and relief in the same mouth. "I don't hate that."

Inside, Lale was at the long table beneath the hanging gate key with Paska's tide marks copied out in ink and charcoal across three sheets of salvaged paper. She had pinned one edge down with a ferry cleat and the other with the freight box. Her careful posture had gone a little crooked over the past week because she had stopped pretending the work was temporary. Fresh copies of the reopened channels lay stacked by her elbow, each one corrected for what the flood had shifted. Reed banks had moved. One side creek near Marrow Market had cut a new shallow mouth. Two prayer markers were gone entirely. The map had to remember what the water would not.

Lale did not look up when Riva came in. "The south approach needs another marker post before dark," she said. "A burial boat nearly took the wrong side channel an hour ago."

"Then put it on tomorrow's list."

"It's on today's."

Riva leaned over the table. The ledger page beside the map held neat marks for freight coin, timber shares, lamp oil, and a new column headed PIKE. Smaller than the others, because Lale believed in humiliating debt by making it fit on a line. Under the heading sat the draft of the settlement she had been waiting on from Halcote.

Riva touched the paper once and then took her hand back. The seal wax was still soft from being opened.

Lale finally glanced up. "He'll take the berth share, the room, and what's left of your yard tackle in exchange for freezing the worst of the interest till winter fair."

"Freezing isn't forgiving."

"I know how verbs work."

Riva read the page again although she had already memorized the shape of the loss. Her room in Halcote above the salvage sheds. The berth she had paid into board by board until it almost felt like hers. The spare winch Pike had stored after the Bitter Thread burned, as if a man could inventory aftermath into fairness. None of it covered the whole debt. It just changed who got to squeeze later and how hard.

Lale capped her ink and waited. The waiting itself was pressure. She had a way of making silence feel clerical, as if you were delaying paperwork that would outlast you.

"If you're going back inland," she said, "say it before I write the winter maintenance shifts. I'm not redrawing the crossing around one more disappearance."

Riva looked past her through the patched doorway to the bend, to Nami over the seed beds, to Daro on the west platform with the drum tucked against his hip. A child from one of the eel yards had learned the hold pattern and was tapping it on the rail with a spoon. Daro let him do it, correcting him with the lightest two knocks on the rim. Outside on the mudbank, Enver had Iven in front of the rope guide demonstrating how to feed line through with the heel of the bad hand braced against the timber instead of trying to grip with fingers that were never going to obey again.

Iven swore, dropped the line, recovered it with his right hand, and looked up as if he could feel being watched. Even from across the yard Riva could see the sweat dark under his shirt and the awkward care in the way he held the left wrist away from his body. Enver rapped the side of Iven's brace with two knuckles, not gentle, not cruel either.

"Stop courting it," Enver said. "It's a hand, not a widow."

"Easy for you to say. Yours still closes."

"One of them does."

Iven flashed his gold tooth despite himself. "You rehearse those before breakfast?"

"No. They improve on the walk."

Riva watched Enver take the line from Iven, show him how to lay the rope into the guide with the damaged palm acting as a block instead of a clamp, and then give it back without any speech about fathers or second chances or what pain was supposed to make of him. That technical contempt was the shape his care took. Iven, to his credit, was learning the language.

Daro struck the departure beat from outside. The child on the rail copied him a fraction late. Daro grinned, the expression still beautiful and now cut through with damage, and answered with a softer pattern until the boy found it.

Riva looked back at the settlement.

"Write me in," she said.

Lale held still.

"Riva," she said, and for once the dry precision dropped out of her voice entirely. "I mean it."

"So do I."

Riva put her thumb on the page where Pike's clerk had left the line for assent, then took up the pen because she had no reason left to hide behind illiteracy games with Lale. Her name looked ugly in the borrowed ink. That was good. Let the man in Halcote read it ugly.

"Send him the berth papers," she said. "Send the room key too. If he wants the yard tackle, he can drag it out of storage himself."

Lale watched the last of the ink sink into the fibers. "You'll have nothing inland."

Riva set the pen down. "That was true when the barge burned. I'm just done pretending otherwise."

For a moment Lale's face threatened something like grief, then flattened back into its working shape. She sanded the signature, blew the grit away, and slid the settlement under the brick.

"Fine," she said. "You're on dawn rope check, third-day pile inspection, and market tally every sixteenth freight crossing."

"That sounds romantic."

"I try not to embarrass myself."

When Riva stepped back outside, the light had gone amber on the mudflats. The crossing would make two more runs before full dark. After that they would eat because everybody was too tired not to.

The meal gathered without announcement, which was how Riva knew it was real.

Tovin came first with a dented pot of eel and rice so over-peppered it made the air sting before the lid came off. She set it down on the front table and immediately started cursing at anyone reaching for bowls before the spoons were out. Her eel hook still rode at her collar. Somebody had tied a scrap of blue thread around it, maybe to keep the children from taking an eye on the stairs. She pretended not to notice.

The pepper caught at the back of Riva's nose and made her eyes water. For one stupid beat she was back under monsoon canvas eating boiled rice off a shovel head because every bowl had been busy elsewhere. This smelled like a meal somebody had meant to make.

Enver arrived with flatbread wrapped in sailcloth and a jar of pickled green chilies because he believed most cooking failed from cowardice. He took one look at Tovin's pot and said, "You seasoned this like you were trying to kill a creditor."

"Sit down and be grateful I missed."

Paska came in barefoot from Marrow Market with her hem wet to the knee and a packet of dried river herbs tucked into the band of her skirt. She had blue-black ink under every nail and a habit of touching the grain of new wood with the backs of her fingers first, as if surfaces told the truth better when skin did not press too hard. She smelled the pot, dropped the herbs in without asking, and said, "It wants bitterness or it's just heat bragging about itself."

"You're late," Lale said.

"I was correcting your western bend marker because it lied."

"It did not lie. It anticipated."

"That's a charming way to say wrong."

Their voices crossed over the table with the old dry bite they used before the river turned every argument into doctrine. Riva felt herself keeping count anyway: roof, rope, freight box, exits. Even hearing them bicker, some part of her was still standing watch.

Iven came in last because Enver made him finish coiling the guide line one-handed before food. He used the crook of his bad wrist to pin his bowl against the table edge and almost managed the ladle cleanly before the fingers failed him. Broth slopped across the back of the brace and down onto the boards.

"Fuck's sake," he said.

Without comment Enver took the bowl, turned it half sideways, and wedged it into the angle between Iven's wrist brace and forearm. "There," he said. "Stop fighting the shape you've got."

Iven looked at him, mouth opening on some bright stupid answer. None came. He swallowed and sat.

The sight landed in Riva low and mean. She hated how fast her eyes went to the ruined hand, hated the answering pull in her own fingers, but the relief was there too: Enver was teaching him the new mechanics of his life instead of feeding him pity and calling it kindness.

Nami brought in a tray of sliced cucumbers from somebody else's surviving garden, still gritty because she had rinsed them in haste. She kept turning her head toward the nursery beds through the open wall whenever the conversation lulled, as if ordinary seeds might try something sly the moment she stopped watching. Daro followed with the drum under his arm and no bowl yet. People shifted to make room for him without ceremony. Someone at the far end, one of the ropewalk boys, started to ask if he would mark the meal with a song and then cut himself off when he saw Daro's throat move.

Riva had not known until that moment how badly she wanted the room to leave him be. The drum under his arm was enough. It let him come to the table as damaged kin and working rhythm, not as the evening's answer to anybody's fear.

Daro spared him and tapped four light beats on the table edge instead, enough to call silence and then release it.

"Eat," he whispered.

So they did.

Nobody arranged themselves into a picture. That saved the evening. Children from the mudbank sat where they could fit and stole chilies they regretted a second later. Tovin accused Enver of bringing bread too dry for honest teeth. Paska kept reaching across Lale's map for the pickles until Lale moved the whole map under her own elbow with a look of exhausted murder. Nami licked salt and ash from her thumb before she remembered what was on it and laughed at herself. Iven got rice on the table, on his brace, and once on his own lap, then learned to use the bad hand the way Enver had shown him and looked offended by the success.

Riva ate standing for a while because habit said she should keep watch over the rope, the freight box, the patched west brace, the doorway. Then she noticed the house holding all that without her constant eye and sat down on the bench under the hanging gate key.

The key swung once when somebody shouldered past the post. Beside it, Lale's map curled at one corner in the damp. Below both hung the shift slate for the coming week, names chalked in plain sequence with no single one set apart.

Riva spooned eel and rice into her mouth and tasted too much pepper, a little river grit, the fat from the fish, the smoke from a pot that had scorched at the bottom because Tovin had been busy swearing at the roof crew instead of stirring. It hit the hollow under her ribs fast and hot, the kind of food that existed to put backs under one more day.

Outside, the new ferry chain knocked once against the post in the evening current. The sound came through the patched floor and up the bench legs into her spine: chain on post, iron answering timber, tomorrow's first load already tugging at the rope.

Daro answered it with his palm on the drum skin, one quiet beat to keep the crossing's rhythm in the room even while they ate. Nami glanced once toward the seed beds and then let them be. Lale reached for the freight box to count the day's coin and stopped when Paska slapped her wrist away from work for the length of one mouthful. Iven, red-faced and stubborn, got the bowl balanced right on his own the next time.

Riva looked down the table and let herself count what remained instead of what had been lost: five siblings still breathing, though one voice had gone and one hand had changed and all of them carried the flood in ways that would outlast the season; a house with its key hanging in the open and the shift slate chalked full beneath it; a crossing that could take freight, mourners, children, and the plain dumb weight of tomorrow.

They ate until the pot showed metal, and when the ferry chain knocked the new post again outside, nobody broke the meal for it. The house took the sound and held.
