# Chapter 2

By morning the House of Oaths smelled less like a shrine than a wet animal pulled apart for parts. Rain had needled through the shutters all night and left the boards swollen, the mats furred with mildew, the offering shelf beaded in greasy water. Tovin had shoved off before first light with eel grease still shining on her arthritic knuckles, muttering that Reed Gate would wait for daylight but not for foolishness. That left the five of them in their mother's house with floodwater licking the pilings and a drowned girl in the side room wrapped under a sailcloth that did not hide the shape of her feet.

Nami stood barefoot in the middle room and watched her brother and sisters turn, one by one, back into the children the house had trained them to be.

Riva took command before anyone had finished swallowing the bitter tea Tovin had left in a blackened kettle. "We need route notes, tools, keys, oilcloth, anything with marks on it. If it's rotted through, throw it in the burn pile. If it's just holy, stack it by the wall."

Daro, still in last night's damp shirt, raised his cup as if blessing her. "Your tenderness always arrives dressed as inventory."

"Your usefulness can arrive any time it likes," Riva said.

Lale had already tied her hair back with a strip torn from an old flour sack. Her nails were still stained indigo from inland dye work, too clean a blue for Reedmouth. She looked at the room as if she could bring it under control by sorting its shame into smaller heaps. "Not the wall under the west shutter," she said. "Everything mildews faster there."

Iven, sprawled on the edge of a broken chest with one boot still unlaced, grinned without much energy. The gold tooth flashed and vanished. "Listen to us. Two breaths in this place and we're running mother's house again."

"We never ran it," Lale said.

"No," Iven said. "That was the problem."

Nami asked, "What counts as holy?"

Riva did not even look up from the shelf she was stripping. "Most of it."

That was how it began. No vote. No agreement. Riva named jobs and the room obeyed her because it always had, because even seven years away had not taught the beams another center. Daro drifted where there might be an audience, lifting things one-handed and making a show of the dust on his fingers. Lale built stacks so straight they looked punishing. Iven kept picking up objects he had no intention of carrying and talking around whatever nobody wanted named. Nami moved among them all and touched more than Riva liked, because the house still felt crowded with meaning and touch was the fastest way to hear it.

Sereya had made the house do two kinds of work. The front room had been for ferries, route business, offerings, bodies brought in from the cuts. The back rooms had been where she ate, slept, took lovers, scolded children, soaked her feet, trimmed lamp wicks with her teeth when her hands were busy. Over the years the two labors had bled into each other until nobody could stand in the place without feeling slightly watched. There were prayer bowls stacked in the same cupboard that still held cracked soup spoons. Votive ribbons hung from the bedposts. The shelf above the stove held old grease lamps, a jar of ferry nails, and three tiny clay mouths pilgrims had pressed in thanks after safe crossings, each with a slit where teeth should have been.

Nami lifted one of the clay mouths and rubbed the slit with her thumb. Grease and dust blackened her skin. She remembered being nine and watching people kiss those things before they kissed Sereya's hand. She remembered hating them for touching what was hers, then hating herself harder because sometimes Sereya smiled at pilgrims with a softness she never spent at home.

"Leave the offerings alone," Riva said.

Nami put it down. "I was only looking."

"Look later."

Iven snorted. "That was always the rule, wasn't it? Feel later. Look later. Eat later. Everybody queue up behind duty."

Riva turned on him with a fistful of damp prayer cloth. "You want to joke, joke while your hands move."

"They're moving."

"Toward trouble doesn't count."

Daro had found Sereya's ferry harness, old leather dark with oil and salt. He held it against himself, the way children tried on adult coats when they thought nobody saw. "You know what I hate most about this place?" he said. "Nothing in it remembers her honestly. The whole house lies in her voice."

Lale did not glance up from the trunk she was emptying. "The house is wood. The lying came from people."

"Same result."

He let the harness drop. The brass buckles hit the floorboards with a little funeral music. Nami jumped. The drowned man under the pilings, the one who had lodged there in the night, answered with a soft knock from below, wood to bone or wood to pole, she could not tell which.

No one spoke for a moment after that. Rain ticked at the shutters. Somewhere in the side room a fly worried at the sailcloth over the girl.

Riva said, quieter now, "Find the ledger if there is one. Mother didn't run five cuts by memory alone."

They spread wider after that. Nami went to the sleeping room because it still carried the strongest thread of Sereya: hair oil gone rancid, lamp smoke sunk into curtains, old river mud dried in the seam of the floor where Sereya had once kicked off her boots and forgotten them there. The bedstead had been stripped years ago of anything soft. Pilgrims had taken the quilt after the burial. Someone had taken the mirror. But a shallow reed chest still sat pushed under the frame, half hidden by a hanging shawl stiff with salt.

When Nami dragged it out, the floor beneath it gave up a pale dry stripe like skin that had never seen sun.

"Here," she called.

Riva came first, wiping her hands on her trousers. Lale followed with the small care she gave to anything closed. Iven lingered in the doorway. Daro leaned one shoulder to the post and made a chapel of the moment.

The lid stuck. Riva braced the chest between her knees and ripped it open hard enough to break one of the old reed hinges. The smell that came out was close and layered: wet reeds, old incense, mouse droppings, fishskin glue. Inside lay a bundle of route markers wrapped in oilcloth, a string of little brass bells, two folded ferry licenses gone green at the corners, and under them a notebook woven from split reeds, bound with fishskin cord.

Lale made a sound Nami had not heard from her since they were girls. Not surprise. Not grief either. Something more private and meaner, like a drawer sticking where it used to slide smooth.

"That's it," Riva said.

Nami reached first, but Riva slapped her hand away, not hard, just enough. "Let Lale open it."

"Why her?" Daro asked.

"Because she can read all of it."

"I can read enough."

"Then you can hear enough."

Lale took the ledger with both hands. She always handled books as if they might retaliate. The reeds had warped but held. Sereya's writing ran in slanted dark strokes between guide lines, neat where the practical notes began, looser in the margins where temper or memory had crowded in. Lale turned pages carefully, lifting each strip free from the damp.

The names came first.

Tovin Reed. Reed Gate.

Ilyan Moor. Hymn Cut.

Paska Dey. Inkwater.

Enver Coil. Coil Reach.

Then a gap. Not the end of the ledger. A gap where one page had gone missing, the fishskin thong passing through empty air before catching the next leaf.

After that came Bato Serin. Storm Mouth.

Riva swore once, under her breath, with the flat force of metal striking metal. Iven leaned in so far his hair brushed Lale's shoulder.

"Read," Nami said.

Lale cleared her throat and did.

"Reed Gate," she read. "First water only. Iron answers warmed, not forced. If Tovin says wait, wait."

Iven laughed softly. "Mother knew her audience."

Lale turned a page. "Hymn Cut. Do not let Ilyan begin in a sulk. Bells first. Then song. He likes witnesses more than he likes truth."

Daro's mouth changed around that, not quite a smile. "Even dead she couldn't resist taking the measure of people she fucked."

Riva ignored him. "Keep going."

"Inkwater. Paska hides the clean map under the dirty one. Ask for her lower drawer, not the wall rack."

Lale's voice flattened a little more with each entry, as if the fact of Sereya's handwriting made her want to wring style out of herself before it could look like feeling.

"Coil Reach. Enver will pretend coin matters first. Bring rope grease and he will stop pretending."

That made Iven bark a laugh, then stop too quickly, as if the sound had cut him. Nami looked at him. His eyes were on the page, but his thumb had found the chipped edge of his gold tooth and was worrying it, back and forth.

Lale lifted the next leaf and found nothing to hold. Her fingers stayed in the air an extra second before they lowered.

The missing page had not torn jagged. Nami saw that much even from where she crouched. The stubs at the binding were pale and thin, old damage gone smooth with handling.

"What was there?" Nami asked.

"If we knew that, it wouldn't be missing," Riva said.

Daro held out his hand. "Let me see."

Lale did not move.

"Lale."

She gave him the ledger because Riva told her to, not because she wanted to. Daro took it with more reverence than he had shown anything else that morning. He read the pages without speaking, lips moving a little, singer's habit. Then he looked up at the room instead of the ledger.

"She wrote them like lovers," he said.

Iven spread his hands. "They were lovers."

"Not just that. Listen to it. She wrote what pleased them. What soured them. Where they could be handled." Daro tapped the page with one long finger. "She could not write a route without writing a body."

Riva said, "Good. Bodies are what we've got."

"That's not what I meant."

"I know exactly what you meant. It still doesn't row a boat."

Nami took the ledger when Daro let it go slack in his grip. The reeds felt colder than the room. She could not read the hand fast enough herself, but she traced the names. Tovin. Ilyan. Paska. Enver. Gap. Bato. The missing page made the sequence feel wrong in her palm, like a mouth with a back tooth pulled and the hole not yet scarred over.

"Maybe it names one of us," she said before she had decided to.

The room tightened.

Lale said, "What?"

Nami kept looking at the gap. "Maybe the missing page says whose child belongs to which cut. Maybe that's why it is gone."

Riva closed her eyes for half a beat, the way she did when a knot went bad under her hands and she had to choose whether to cut or keep working. "No."

"You don't know that."

"I know we're not wasting daylight playing guess-your-father in this room."

Iven gave a short ugly laugh. "Why not? Reedmouth's played it for years."

Nobody answered him. That was answer enough.

Nami looked up from the ledger and found all of them facing not each other but slightly aside, as if every one of them had their own invisible witness just over somebody else's shoulder.

Daro said, too lightly, "It would be efficient, wouldn't it? Convenient. One lover for each cut, one child for each lover, mother turning flesh into a tidy route."

"She never did anything tidy," Lale said.

"No," Iven said. "Just memorable."

Riva took the ledger back from Nami and held it against her thigh. "It doesn't matter who begot who. It matters who can get us through those cuts without more bodies floating under this house."

Daro's face sharpened. "Easy thing to say when you've already picked your saint."

Riva turned. "What does that mean?"

"Tovin taught you to work. You trust work because it's the only language anybody ever gave you clean."

"And what did you get, Daro? A song through the shutters and the idea it made you special?"

He smiled then, because smiling was cheaper than bleeding in front of Riva. "At least mine was a language."

Iven whistled low. "There she is. Our family liturgy."

Lale snapped, "Don't start."

"Who's starting?" Iven said. "She fucked half the delta and left us a scavenger list. We're already started."

The words sat in the room among the clay mouths and prayer ribbons. Nami could almost feel them settling on the shrine shelf, filthy and true.

Riva said, "Watch your mouth."

"Why? She didn't."

That one landed. Even Iven seemed sorry of it half a breath later, though he hid the flinch by kicking shut the broken chest.

Nami said, because she could not stop herself once the room had torn open, "Do you know who's mine?"

Riva rounded on her. "Nami."

"I asked a question."

"And I'm telling you to leave it."

"Everybody else gets to talk around it. I'm asking it plain."

Lale had gone very still. Her stillness was never calm. It was the stillness of fabric stretched too tight on a frame. "No one knows plain," she said. "People know rumors, appetites, dates that don't line up, who stayed longest that season, who the face favors when the light is kind. That isn't the same thing."

Daro said, "Some people know more than they're saying."

"About your own reflection, perhaps," Lale said.

He laughed once, without amusement. "You think I haven't heard the guesses? Blind singer's mouth, blind singer's hands, blind singer's throat. Reedmouth has been assigning me a father since I was twelve."

"Reedmouth assigns everybody everything," Iven said. "It's our local industry."

Nami looked at him. "What do they assign you?"

His grin came back too fast. "Debt. Charm. Excellent hair."

"They say Enver when they're being cruel," Daro said.

This time Iven's grin stayed, but it had no softness left in it. "And they say Tovin when they look at Riva from the side. Do you want the whole basket emptied?"

Riva crossed the room in two steps. For a moment Nami thought she was going to strike him. Instead she snatched a coil of old ferry line off the table and threw it at his chest. He caught it by reflex.

"If you need your hands busy, scrape that clean," she said.

"That's not an answer."

"No. It's work."

Nami hated how the room bent around that. Around Riva's refusal. Around everybody else's talent for living in refusals. She wanted one clean thing, one name, one fact that did not arrive wrapped in somebody's old embarrassment. Instead she had Sereya's handwriting describing lovers the way butchers described knives, and a missing page nobody would claim.

She said, softer now, "I got the stories. That's what I got."

They all looked at her then.

"When she died," Nami said, "the women from the prayer boats kept touching my hair. They said little tide, little saint, poor sweet mouth, poor last one. They said I'd hear her if I slept near the water long enough. They said not to marry inland because the river had first right." She wiped her palm on her skirt. Clay grit smeared dark. "If nobody knows anything, why'd they talk like that to me and not to any of you?"

No one answered right away.

It was Lale, not Riva, who spoke first. "Because you were young enough to use."

That hurt in a clean way. Nami almost thanked her for it.

Daro's gaze had gone to the offering shelf. "Mother turned everything into worship if she needed it badly enough."

"Including us," Iven said.

Riva's voice dropped. Lower was always worse with her. "Enough."

But the room had already moved beyond obeying. Daro reached up and lifted Sereya's brass-ferruled ferry pole from its wall hooks. The wood was dark with old hand oil, the brass dull except where years of touching had kept a narrow oval bright. Nami had not seen it this close since the burial. Pilgrims had filed past it then like mourners and thieves together, kissing the metal, pressing foreheads to the shaft, rubbing their mouths on the same place until the polish turned cloudy with spit.

Daro balanced it upright beside him. The pole made him look priestly against his own will.

"Put that down," Riva said.

"Why? Are relics only safe if nobody feels anything near them?"

"Because if you drop it in this rot-floor I'll break your wrist."

Iven said, "That's the most sisterly thing you've said all morning."

Lale stood abruptly. The ledger slid a little in Riva's grip. "Stop using the house to perform yourselves at each other. We've got the route order. We know one page is missing. That's the useful fact."

Nami watched her say it. Watched the too-careful line of her mouth, the way her left thumb pressed at the base of the reed binding without seeming to know it was there. Useful fact, her face said. Not wound. Not recognition.

Riva took the pole from Daro and laid it across the rafters where nobody could grab it without making a scene. Then she wrapped the ledger in oilcloth and knotted it shut.

"We're not traveling night water," she said. "The posts are all under and the current is running backward half the time. We leave at first light tomorrow for Reed Gate. Today we strip the house for what matters and burn what can draw pilgrims in."

"Burn?" Nami said.

"Not the dead girl's room," Riva said. "The ribbons. The little clay mouths. Anything that'll turn this place back into a shrine by dusk if somebody sees it lit."

Daro stared at her. "You think ash will solve mother."

"No," Riva said. "But it keeps strangers from helping."

The argument did not end so much as go stale. That was the family method too. Iven took the old ferry line to the doorway and sat scraping mildew off with a fish knife, each drag of the blade a little act of contempt. Daro returned to the harness and handled it as if it might start speaking. Lale made three separate stacks from what had already been sorted, because order was the one prayer she still trusted enough to perform in public. Nami moved between rooms carrying things Riva pointed at and feeling the missing page follow her through the house like a cold draft.

By dusk the middle room held four piles: work, burn, dead use, keep. The shrine wall looked skinned. The place where the pole had hung showed pale wood under the grime, a body outline after lifting a corpse. Riva tied the ledger to her belt before she banked the lamp. No one mentioned the gap again. That made it larger.

Under the floor, the drowned man knocked once against the pilings after dark, patient as somebody waiting to be let in.

* * *

The house changed shape at night.

In daylight it was salvage. At night it remembered its old trade. The stripped wall where the offerings had hung became a row of darker rectangles like shut eyes. The piles Riva had made threw crouched shadows against the floorboards. Rain eased after midnight, and in the quieter air every sound from under the house came clear: water fingering the posts, reeds rubbing the side steps, the slow bump of the lodged corpse turning with the tide and failing to go anywhere.

They slept in the middle room because nobody wanted the side room with the girl and nobody would say that aloud.

Riva lay nearest the door with a boathook within reach and the ledger under her folded coat. Even asleep she looked tasked, one hand hooked in her belt as if keeping herself tied to the world by hardware. Lale had arranged her blanket square to the floorboards and placed her boots side by side at its edge, absurdly neat in a house that smelled of river rot and candle grease. Iven had rolled himself in an old net weight sack and kept one knee crooked out of it, his unlaced boot still on, his hand closed around nothing. Daro was on his back under the rafters, eyes open whenever lightning thinned the dark, lips moving against whatever private scale he would not share.

Nami could not sleep. The house pressed too close. The air tasted of wet reeds and the sweet-sour edge of bodies that had spent too long in the water. Her own skin still held the day: dust from the prayer shelf, smoke from the burn barrel, brass from Sereya's pole when she had helped Riva move it. Every time she shut her eyes she saw the gap in the ledger and the way everyone had looked aside from it as though a missing page might turn its face and look back.

The first call came through the piling nearest the door.

Not loud. That was what made it obscene. A man's drowned throat, full of river water and gravel, forcing itself into the clipped hard shape of a work order.

"Riva."

Riva sat up so fast the boathook cracked against the floor.

Nobody else moved at first. The sound had been too exact. Nami knew it had been a man's voice because of the depth of it, the drag in it, but it had carried the pressure of some older labor shout all the same, as if one ruined mouth had learned just enough of the right command to make Riva answer to it.

"Who's there?" Riva said.

The dark under the floor gave a soft glug of water between posts.

Then the next call came from farther in, under Daro's side of the room. Same drowned throat. Different shape. This time it rose almost musically, rounded on the second syllable as if setting a pitch.

"Daro."

He answered before anybody could stop him. "I hear you."

Riva hissed, "Don't."

Too late. The house had already learned it had them.

The third call slid up the central post where the roof leaked. Dry somehow despite the water. Precise. Each consonant placed like a bead on a thread.

"Lale."

Lale did not speak. She only put both palms flat on the floorboards beside her hips and stared at the black space between two warped planks as though the answer might be written there in trade script.

Iven was still half in shadow when his came. The voice took on a low amused curl, not kind, not quite cruel either, the register of somebody who knew every cheap trick in a room and had stayed anyway to see what it might buy him.

"Iven."

His breath caught. Nami heard it because the room had gone so empty around the names. He rolled to one elbow and laughed once, too soft and too sharp. "Fuck off," he told the floor.

Then the last call came for her.

It did not come from the door or the post or the roof leak. It came from directly under her ribs, straight through the boards where her blanket had gone damp from seep and old weather. The drowned throat gentled, if such a thing could gentle. The name reached her with the intimacy of breath under a bed curtain.

"Nami."

Her whole body answered by leaning toward it. She caught herself with one hand on the floor. The plank there was slick and cold.

Riva struck tinder so hard the spark died. She swore, struck again, and the lamp came up in a wick-small flare that shoved the shadows back only enough to make them angry. All five of them looked worse in the light: hair stuck to faces, blankets twisted, eyes wide and mean with broken sleep.

"No one goes outside," Riva said.

Daro was already on his knees. "It called us."

"I know what it did."

"You heard your own."

Riva's mouth went flat. "I heard a dead man in the tide finding a way to mock what we brought into this house."

"Mock?" Daro said. "That wasn't mockery."

Lale's voice came thin with effort, so controlled it almost broke. "Pressure does strange things through flooded wood. Voices carry. Brains pattern them."

Iven barked a laugh. "Pressure doesn't know how to say my name like it owes me money."

"Everything owes you money," Riva said.

"Not the point."

Nami said, "What did it sound like to you?"

Nobody answered.

That silence frightened her more than the voice had. She had expected argument. Denial. Daro sermonizing and Riva shutting him down. But the room filled instead with each person's refusal to give up what they had heard to anybody else's mouth. Riva kept her eyes on the door. Daro held himself very still, like a singer waiting for the return note. Lale wiped her fingertips on her blanket again and again, as though the sound had left residue there. Iven put his tongue to his gold tooth and would not look at anyone at all.

The voice did not call again immediately. It only moved below them, or seemed to. A knock under one corner, then another. Water lifting a shoulder against the pilings. A body failing to remember which direction was sea.

Daro said, more softly, "It isn't random."

Riva rounded on him. "You don't know what it is."

"Neither do you."

"I know better than to kneel for it."

"Maybe that's why it has to keep knocking."

Riva rose to her feet in the lamplight, blanket dropping from her shoulders. Her hair hung wet and ugly around her face. "If you turn one dead voice into a sermon before dawn, I will drag you to Reed Gate by the back of your shirt and let Tovin work the nonsense out of you by hand."

Daro smiled with all the wrong parts of his mouth. "There. That's the voice it borrowed for you."

Riva's grip tightened on the boathook until the tendons in her forearm stood out like wet cord.

Lale cut in before either of them could tip fully over. "Stop it. Both of you. Whatever is under us wants attention. We don't have to give it shape."

Iven said, "It already took one."

Nami looked at him. "What did it say?"

He finally met her eyes then, and his were clear in a way that meant he was lying or about to. "It said what dead men always say. Come closer."

"That's not all."

"No," he said. "It isn't."

Riva lifted the lamp and went to the side room door first, checking the girl under sailcloth as if the voice might have climbed into her while they argued. The cloth had slipped enough to show one waxy ankle and the river rash around it, red-brown and peeling. She was still dead. Of course she was. Riva came back and set the lamp in the middle of the room.

"We take turns on watch till dawn," she said. "Nobody answers if it speaks again."

Daro said, "You already answered by listening."

"You'd answer a knife if it called you chosen."

That hit, and Nami saw it hit. Daro's face hollowed in the lamplight, not with anger first but with hunger exposed too suddenly.

Lale said, "Riva."

Riva looked away before she had to see what she had done with the words.

That was the end of open talk. They settled badly after, not back to sleep but to a kind of armed waiting. Riva took first watch and stood by the door with the lamp turned low. Daro sat cross-legged under the rafters and listened with his head bent, as if he might hear the current writing music under the boards. Lale pulled the ledger from under Riva's coat when Riva passed it over and held it in her lap without opening it, fingers resting near the missing page as though absence had a temperature. Iven lay down again but kept his eyes open, one hand over his mouth, laughing once in a while without sound.

Nami lasted perhaps ten minutes before the not-knowing became worse than fear.

She waited until Riva's back turned toward the shutters and then rose from her blanket. The boards were cold as fish bellies under her feet. She moved past the piles, past the stripped wall, past the place where Sereya's pole had left its pale silhouette. At the threshold she paused. Riva heard the floor creak and said without turning, "Piss in the bucket."

"I'm checking the steps."

"No, you're not."

Nami went anyway.

Riva grabbed for her and missed by the wet edge of her sleeve. By the time Riva swore, Nami was already on the narrow outer landing with both hands on the rail.

Night had thinned to a dirty gray. The rain was only dripping now, fat drops from the roof edge. Below, floodwater moved under the house with the oily patience of something that knew it had time. The drowned man lay wedged between two pilings where the side steps hit the current. He was turned partly on his face, one shoulder lifted by trapped reeds, one hand drifting free. His shirt had once been a good one, striped faintly enough to pass for plain from a distance, and someone had repaired the cuff with neat yellow triangles that were now dark with river filth. He wore a copper stud in one ear. Nami had the wild useless thought that whoever mended his cuff had expected him home.

"Back inside," Riva said behind her.

Nami crouched instead.

The corpse rocked with the water. His cheek had sloughed open against the piling, showing pale meat under the skin. When the current rolled his face far enough, she saw the mouth. One side of the lips was caught back on the teeth. Something black sat between them, slick as a beetle shell.

The body knocked wood again.

And from below, quiet as hands untying a knot in the dark, the voice said her name once more.

"Nami."

It did not sound like a command now. It sounded like recognition. That was worse.

Riva was two steps away when the current lifted the corpse's jaw and the black seed worked loose. It clicked against the piling, bounced once in the wash, and would have gone under the house if Nami had not reached.

The water was colder than pain. It swallowed her arm to the elbow and packed silt under her nails. Her fingers closed around something hard and polished and alive only in the way a tooth was alive once it had left the mouth.

Riva hauled her back by the collar so hard the seam bit Nami's throat. "Are you out of your fucking mind?"

Nami coughed river water and did not answer. The seed sat in her fist, black and slick and a little ridged, bigger than a plum pit, smaller than an egg. When Riva seized her wrist, Nami flattened her hand against her own belly and rolled the seed into the waistband of her skirt with a movement small enough to pass for panic.

Riva was still cursing. "You don't put your hands in flood mouths. You don't touch what comes back from the cuts. Did you hear me?"

"I heard you," Nami said.

That much was true.

Riva dragged her inside and checked her arm from wrist to elbow for cuts, turning it under the lamp. Nami forced herself to let the inspection happen. Her skin was goosefleshed and silver with river water. No blood. No reed glass. Riva shoved a rag at her and went back to the doorway muttering about stupidity and infected water and children who wanted to die picturesque.

Children. Nami almost laughed.

Daro was watching her too closely. "What did it want?" he asked in a near whisper.

Nami dried her arm. "Maybe it wanted naming."

"From you?"

Lale looked up from the closed ledger. "Don't feed this."

Iven said from his blanket, "Too late. It's already eating."

Nami sat back down on her mat and wrapped the rag around her forearm though she was not cut. The seed pressed against the warm hollow above her hip, hidden in her waistband. Its hard shape made the whole side of her body feel privately altered.

Nobody slept after that. Dawn came slow and colorless through the shutters, turning each face into something used and stubborn. They packed in silence mostly. Riva did not mention the voice again, which meant she was carrying it like weight. Daro moved with a terrible care, as if preserving pitch. Lale kept the ledger close and would not let anyone else touch the binding. Iven joked twice and both jokes died where they landed.

No one asked Nami what she had heard on the landing. No one offered what the voice had become in their own heads. They did not need to. The room had changed in the night. Reed Gate was no longer only a job waiting inland with Tovin and her iron teeth. The route had found the soft places in all of them and put its fingers there.

When Riva opened the door to the morning flood, the house gave one last settling groan under their feet, wood adjusting around water, old vows, and the dead below. Nami pressed her palm to her skirt and felt the seed answer with its cold, patient weight.
