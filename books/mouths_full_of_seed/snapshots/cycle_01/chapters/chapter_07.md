# Chapter 7

By noon Marrow Market had been scrubbed hard enough to raise splinters, and it still smelled like opened mouths. Women on the lower walkways sloshed brine through the gaps between the planks. Boys with reed brooms kept worrying at strings of river weed caught around the posts. Someone had burned camphor in three cracked bowls and only managed to make the rot smell expensive. Lale kept her eyes on the boards as she crossed behind Riva. If she looked outward she would have to look at the places where corpses had wedged among melon crates and prayer boats because Iven had wanted one bright easy profit before Hymn Cut had settled.

Ink showed longer than blood on these boards. She knew that from work. Fat, blood, lampblack, indigo, rust, each stain took its own persuasion. What had happened here would not lift before the next rain. It might not lift before the monsoon broke. A season could keep a joke alive if enough people had watched it curdle.

Riva did not slow. She moved as if shame were weather and there was no use cursing it when the boat still needed pushing. Daro walked with his mouth set in that handsome injured line he wore when the world failed to arrange itself around his significance. Nami kept looking over the railings at the water below the market, as if the black eddies might answer back if she watched long enough. Iven hung a little to the rear, quieter than he had any right to be, his wet boots leaving half-moons on the planks.

Paska's house sat above the fish lane on a web of old pilings, the doorway curtained with stitched ray skin that clicked softly in the draft. Dried herbs hung under the eaves beside lengths of faded prayer cloth and narrow strips of cured hide marked with tide lines. Inside, the room was cooler than the market, all limewashed boards and open shutters, with ink bowls weighted into a long bench so the flood sway would not send them sliding. A one-eyed market cat slept on folded sailcloth by the brazier, and Paska, seated cross-legged on a stool, flicked a fish spine toward it without looking up from the needles she was sorting.

She had gone leaner with age instead of softer. Her forearms were all scars and dark hair. One temple was shaved to gray prickles. Her nails looked permanently dipped in ink. At her elbow sat a chipped saucer of clove stems, and she bit one in half as she finally raised her head to look at them.

"You brought the whole damaged household," she said. Her voice arrived amused and rough-edged, as if she had been laughing at somebody less clever than herself for years. "I was only expecting the ledger girl."

Lale hated the old name on sight. She had not heard it in years, not since Halcote had taught her what clean sleeves and a careful account hand could buy. "We need the Inkwater sequence."

Paska's eyes slid to the book under Lale's arm and then lower, to Lale's bare hands and buttoned cuffs despite the heat. "Of course you do. Sit down."

"I didn't come for a tattoo."

"No," Paska said. "You came for a route that lives in skin and current and thinks paper is a useful lie. Sit down."

Riva planted both palms on the bench. "If this is your price, say it plain."

"I just did."

Daro gave the room a slow look, taking in the hanging hide strips, the jars, the cat, the open shutters with the whole market noise lifting through them. "Why her?"

Paska chose another clove stem, worrying it with her teeth before answering. "Because your brother's body already reads like a wall behind a gambling den, and none of it points where I need. Because you're half in love with hearing yourself named. Because Riva would take the mark and try to use it like a pry bar. Because the little one would follow any line I put in her and never ask where it ended. Because this one carried the book away from her body and called that safety."

Nami moved closer to Lale without seeming to mean to. "Will it hurt?"

"Yes," Paska said, still looking at Lale. "That's part of why it works."

Iven leaned one shoulder against the shutter frame. "She's charging in moral language now. That's always where the real bill hides."

Paska glanced toward him at last. "Kes says you already know that."

The joke landed and rotted where it fell. Iven looked away first.

Lale kept the ledger braced against her ribs. "I can read a map."

"You can read symbols somebody else flattened for you." Paska stood. Up close she smelled of clove, old smoke, and the sharp mineral tang of wet ink. "Inkwater is not a respectable road from one neat column to the next. It drifts. It doubles back. It remembers the wrong banks on certain tides and opens under the right hand on others. If you want me to fix the route in a way your body won't lose when the water turns ugly, you sit."

"Sleeves exist," Lale said. "I don't need the market reading my arm every time I buy salt."

Paska's mouth twitched. "That was quick. There you go."

Riva looked from one to the other, impatience hammering under her stillness. "Lale."

Lale heard what sat under it. We do not have time for your vanity. We do not have time for your inland life. We do not have time for any of the selves we built to survive our mother. She hated Riva a little for being right in such a blunt workmanlike way.

She set the ledger on the bench. "Fine."

Paska did not smile, which made the victory mean more. She cleared a low chair near the shutters and patted the plank arm. "Left arm."

Lale unbuttoned her cuff with fingers that had steadied dead weight, dye vats, creditors, and inland widowers who thought grief excused sloppy arithmetic. It was absurd that a strip of skin could shake her more than any of that. She rolled the sleeve to the elbow. Her forearm looked pale where the sun did not take, all cords and a faint old shine from the needle scar at the base of her thumb.

Paska touched that scar with the back of one finger. Not intimate, not kind, simply exact. "You still brace wrong when you're frightened."

Lale pulled her hand back. "Get on with it."

Paska set water to heat in a blackened kettle and laid her tools out in a line precise enough to soothe and irritate in equal measure. Bone handles. Fine needles. Ink stones. A rag already stained the blue-black of old tides. The market sounded through the shutters the whole time: sellers calling the names of fish, a child crying because somebody had taken his bowl, hammer taps from a man resetting a warped post below. The ordinary noise of trade, with the morning's disgrace moving under it like a second current.

When the kettle hissed, Paska tipped hot water over a bundle of needles, then added a measure of arrack from a stoppered bottle and another spoon of something black and gummy from a jar. The smell rose bitter and marine. Lale knew dye rooms. She knew what happened when heat coaxed color out of matter and fixed it somewhere it did not belong. This smelled less domesticated than indigo, more like the underside of a dock.

"Take the ledger off the bench," Paska said.

Riva reached for it first. Lale moved faster and caught it. She had not meant to. The book was wet-swollen, ugly, already maimed, yet she held it with a reflex so quick the room went briefly still.

Paska saw. Of course she saw.

"Put it by the cat if you need to keep one eye on it," she said. "I won't steal your dead mother's bookkeeping."

Lale carried the ledger across the room and set it on the folded sailcloth near the sleeping cat. The cat opened its one good eye, considered the intrusion, and went back to sleep with a tiny snore. Something about that irritated her almost beyond reason.

When she returned, Paska had rolled a small leather brace under her wrist. "Palm up."

The first wipe of arrack across her inner forearm felt cold enough to erase her. Then the sting arrived. Lale flinched, and Paska made a low unimpressed sound.

"You dye burial cloth for a living and flinch at spirits?"

"Cloth doesn't come with nerves."

"Neither do ledgers. That's been your trouble."

Lale might have answered if the first puncture had not gone in just then. It was finer than she expected and worse. Not a cut, not quite. More like a seam ripper slid under skin and dragged only enough to remind the body how quickly it could be opened. Paska worked with one hand stretching the flesh and the other tapping the needle in steady little strikes. The sound was almost domestic, like seeds thrown against a tray.

Nami took another step closer. Riva caught her shoulder and kept her back without looking. Daro leaned on the wall with his arms folded, his expression carefully detached. Iven watched from the shutter, all his quickness banked down to a wary stillness Lale distrusted more than any joke.

Paska did not hurry. She laid the first line from just above the wrist toward the center of the forearm, black and fine as pen work, then crossed it with a shorter bar of thinned blue that she rubbed in with the heel of her thumb. Blood pearled where the needle lifted. Paska blotted it away with the rag.

"What am I looking at?" Riva asked.

"Not you," Paska said.

Lale almost laughed. The sound would have come out ugly, so she kept it in.

The second line curved toward the vein. Paska followed it as if uncovering something already there. Heat began to gather under Lale's skin, not broad or dramatic, just a persistent focused warmth that made the forearm cease belonging to the rest of her body. The market sounds dulled. The room narrowed to touch, needle, rag, the drag of air through the shutters, clove on Paska's breath whenever she leaned close.

"Hold still," Paska murmured.

"I'm holding still."

"No. You're holding away."

The correction slipped under Lale's ribs more neatly than the needle had gone under skin. She tried to answer and found the words snagged. Paska wiped the arm again and started a fan of shorter marks below the elbow, each one angled a little differently. A map, apparently. A tally. A sequence. Something she ought to have been able to reduce into clean understanding. Instead the lines refused to stay flat. They pressed inward.

Seven years fell through her with no warning at all.

Not memory in any generous orderly sense. Sensation first. A wet hem slapping the backs of her calves as she crossed the ferry house. The smell of oil bowls burning too long. Her mother's body on the wash table behind a hanging sheet, water still dripping from the ends of her hair into the catch pan because nobody had yet decided what counted as reverence and what was only labor delayed. The soft thick resistance of swollen reed paper under Lale's thumb.

She jerked hard enough to drag the needle.

Paska's hand closed around her wrist. "There. That's it."

Riva pushed off the bench. "What happened?"

"Memory," Paska said. She did not look up. "Sit down, all of you. If you want the route, stop stomping around like startled geese."

Lale stared at the opposite wall. Hooks. Nets. A row of tiny brass bells gone green with salt. Her own breath sounded too loud in the room. Paska dabbed the smeared place, muttered once under her breath, and went back in to correct the line.

"You could've mentioned that part," Lale said.

"If I'd mentioned it, you'd have armored against it."

"I was already armored."

"Yes," Paska said. "Exactly."

The room gave a strained little laugh around that, nobody claiming it. Even Daro let one breath out through his nose. The needle resumed its dry patient tapping. Lale fixed her gaze on the open shutter and watched a strip of floodwater move black between the pilings below the market. Every few strokes another shard came loose. Rain on the roof. Nami, younger and all ankles, asleep with her cheek stuck to a cushion by heat. Iven rummaging offering bowls for coin as if theft could still count as not caring. Lale sitting on an upturned crate because the chair by the table was taken by condolence dishes, turning ledger leaves while her mother's body cooled three steps away.

She swallowed. Ink and arrack and old lamp smoke thickened at the back of her tongue.

Paska finished the upper lines, wiped the arm clean, and sat back just enough to study the work. The marks ran from wrist to elbow in a grammar Lale did not yet know: black seams, blue turning bars, a cluster of dots near the inside of the arm where the pulse moved, then a hooked line that doubled on itself as if correcting a lie.

"Good," Paska said. "Now we can talk."

***

Riva spread her hands. "Then talk."

Paska set fresh ink on the bench and took another clove stem from the saucer. The cat had risen and stretched, spine lifting like a drawn bow, then settled against the ledger as if the book were its property now. "Inkwater is not a gate. It is a braid under the market's south pilings and the black reeds beyond. On dry charts it looks like three channels. In flood it wants to become six, and two of those are grave mouths."

"Of course they're grave mouths," Iven muttered.

"You find it funny until one takes your pole and your brother with it."

"I didn't say funny."

"You don't need to. Your face is overeducated."

That earned a real sound from Nami, half laugh, half startlement. Paska pointed at Lale's forearm with the clove stem. "These marks are the sequence. When the falling tide reaches that first bar, you enter from the fish lane, not the open water. When it darkens to that hook, you cut east under the reed shelf. If you miss the second turn and stay on the obvious line, you'll ground the ferry in sucking mud and the backwash will stack the dead against the pilings again."

Riva moved closer. "How much labor?"

"Enough. You clear the old marker stakes by hand. You keep the boat light. You don't try to force speed through a channel that hates being hurried. Tovin gave you the first truth. Ilyan gave you the second. Mine is simple. Inkwater only settles if the body carrying the map stays in the work."

Daro straightened from the wall. "So that's why you wanted a mark."

"That's one reason." Paska's mouth curved, though there was no softness in it. "The other is that your sister's memory has been rotting under clean cloth for seven years."

Lale turned her head. "You don't get to say that like you own it."

Paska met her eyes. "I don't own it. I watched you nail the lid down."

Silence moved through the room. Not shock, exactly. More the soundless rearrangement that happened whenever a sentence found its place too neatly.

Riva said, "What memory?"

"Not yours yet," Paska said. "Don't grab."

If anyone else in Reedmouth had spoken to Riva like that, the room would have gone hotter. Riva only flexed her jaw once and let it go, which told Lale how badly she needed what sat in Paska's hands.

Daro looked from Paska to Lale and back again. "You talk like Mother planned all this, lovers parceled out like shrine holdings and the children left to fetch and carry."

"She did plan it." Paska bent over the ink bowl and stirred with the blunt end of the needle. "Not all of it. Nobody plans flood. Nobody plans grief. But the route, yes."

"Why not leave it to us?" Nami asked. Her voice was open, direct, almost tender in its confusion. "We were hers."

Paska's expression changed then, very slightly. The amusement did not vanish, but it thinned enough for another feeling to show through. Regret, maybe. Or only age. "That's exactly why."

Nami frowned. "I don't know what that means."

"It means blood calls anything it survives an inheritance. Lovers know better." Paska tapped ash from the end of the clove stem into an empty shell dish. "Heirs turn service into rule. They start counting what the river owes them because their mother bled first. Lovers know the body at dawn. Lovers know how foolish devotion looks once the sheets are sour and the stew is burned and somebody still has to pole the dead through rain. We had less room for fantasy."

"That's convenient," Daro said. His voice had gone soft in the dangerous way, velvet stretched over a blade. "To hear one of Mother's lovers tell it, she was wise enough to trust desire but not her own children."

"She trusted desire less than anybody," Paska said. "That's why she spread the route where desire had already done its damage and couldn't pretend to be pure."

Iven barked a short humorless laugh. "There we go."

Riva asked, "And if she'd left it to one heir?"

Paska's gaze slid across them, one by one, without mercy. "Then you'd have a throne made of flood rot. One child claiming the river as title. Four more fighting to either kneel or cut that child down. Pilgrims love one body. One mouth. One answer. It's easier than maintenance."

Nobody looked at Daro, which meant they were all thinking of him.

He smiled anyway, slow and offended. "You make us sound vulgar."

"You are vulgar," Paska said. "So was she. Sanctity never kept a room from smelling of sweat. That was half Sereya's gift and most of the trouble."

The line hit Lale harder than it hit the others, because it was truer in a register she hated. Her mother had been impossible to reduce even when dead. Women had come to the ferry house with cakes and copper and stories. Men had stood outside in rain looking as if they had missed the only boat that mattered. Even the washed body on the table had not looked finished. It had looked interrupted.

Riva pointed to the marks on Lale's arm. "The cut settles if she goes in with us. Fine. What else?"

Paska answered in the practical tones Riva trusted, which only made the chapter of theory before it sink deeper. "You want the tide on the turn after dusk, not full dark. Two poles up front. No lantern higher than your waist or the glare will flatten the water and hide the seams. Keep the left side of the ferry scraping reed if you think you're too close. The route there lies by looking open. It tells the truth by drag and sound."

Nami said, "What does it sound like?"

"Like somebody breathing into a bowl." Paska met her eyes for the first time with something close to gentleness. "And if you hear singing under it, back off. That's the dead caught where they shouldn't be."

Nami nodded as if receiving ordinary shopping advice.

Paska leaned in over Lale's arm again and resumed the work, speaking while the needle moved. "One more thing. Do not leave Inkwater half-open overnight. The third cut sits close enough to town that if the current wakes and doesn't get what it's owed, it starts borrowing from the living."

Riva's head came up. "Borrowing how?"

"You don't need the whole lesson in advance." Paska wiped a new bead of blood away. "Open it clean and you won't have to find out."

That put a hush on the room far more effectively than any sermon Daro could have given. Even he did not dress the warning up. Lale sat in the middle of them with her arm hot and exposed, and had the strangest brief conviction that Paska had chosen the chair not only for light but because it left no corner for anybody to retreat into. The whole family had been arranged around one working body and told to look.

Paska finished the final cluster of dots near Lale's pulse and pressed a folded clean rag hard to the arm. Pain spread bright and disciplined. Lale felt every beat of blood under it.

"Done for now," Paska said.

"For now?" Lale asked.

"Routes need checking, same as nets. If you live through the season, come back and I'll see which lines held."

It was such an indecently practical blessing that Lale nearly thanked her.

Instead she said, "I need to wash."

"You need to sit until the room stops tipping."

"The room isn't tipping."

"Then you won't mind sitting."

Riva, who had already begun recalculating labor, supplies, light, tide, and time, looked toward the doorway. "We need poles cut shorter before dusk. Iven, come on. Nami, with me. Daro."

Daro lingered. "You haven't answered the real question."

Paska wrapped the rag tighter with a strip of linen. "Ask a real one, then."

He set a hand over the spot at the base of his throat where song seemed to live even when he was silent. "If no single heir was ever meant to carry this, why does the river answer one voice at all?"

Paska tied the linen with her teeth and sat back. "Because people do."

That was cruel enough to be useful. Daro flinched in the smallest possible way, then laughed as if he'd chosen it. "You're very pleased with yourself."

"No," she said. "I'm fifty, my knees hate the damp, and half this market thinks grief exempts them from paying. I haven't had time to be pleased with myself in years."

For the first time, Lale almost liked her.

***

The others went out. Their footfalls crossed the outer walkway in a fading stagger: Riva's hard practical tread, Iven's lighter drag, Nami's quick double taps, Daro unhurried because he always wanted departure to look chosen. Market noise rose to fill the space they left. Paska rinsed her tools in a bowl gone pink and then black.

Lale waited until the room had emptied of family before she said, "You knew."

"Enough."

"About what I forgot."

Paska lifted one shoulder. "I knew you'd cut a page from the ledger and then looked at the room for years after like paper might bleed if anybody named the right word."

Lale stared. "You saw me do it?"

"No. If I had, I'd have taken it off you." Paska set the needles in a cloth to dry. "But I saw you the week after. You went tidy in a way grief doesn't make people tidy. Grief makes a mess and calls it weather. You were making drawers inside yourself."

The words should have offended her. They did. They also fit too well to refuse.

Paska poured a little dark oil into her palm and motioned with her chin. "Look."

She held out a hand mirror, the glass clouded at one edge. Lale took it awkwardly in her right hand and turned her left arm toward the light. The marks were angrier now, black and blue rising out of her skin in fine swollen ridges. They should have looked decorative. They looked functional and intimate, like the visible part of some apparatus usually hidden under floorboards. Her own body had been drafted without permission.

"It suits you," Paska said.

"That's not a comfort."

"I wasn't trying to comfort you."

The market below them was settling toward evening trade. Fish knives struck wood. Somebody laughed too loud from the palm liquor stall and was answered by a woman's dry abuse. Rain tapped once at the shutters and withdrew. Lale looked at the marks until the lines blurred and reassembled into the wrong room, the wrong night, the wrong air.

She was back in the ferry house before she understood she had gone.

The sheet hung across the wash corner because Riva had insisted the neighbors did not need to see every stage of the labor. It had not hidden much. The lamp behind it threw her mother's shape onto the cloth in flattened gold: shoulder, hip, one hanging hand. Water ticked from the edge of the table into the pan below. Outside, the pilings knocked under the flood as if something wanted in and was too polite to force the door.

Lale had been twenty-five and already exhausted by everybody else's way of grieving. Daro wanted song. Riva wanted tasks. Iven wanted absence and kept failing to achieve it. Nami, seventeen and brittle with shock, had cried until she slept and then slept as if dropped. The house was thick with condolence food, wet shawls, votive smoke, strangers who claimed Sereya had ferried their dead, their sins, their luck, their own foolish hearts. Every story made the room less livable. Lale had gone looking for an object that could still be counted. She had found the ledger under the shelf by the east wall where Sereya kept lamp oil and receipts no one else ever saw.

The pages were swollen from damp, the reed binding soft under her hand. She took it to the corner by the grain chest because there was almost room to sit there, then began turning leaves while the house breathed around her. Lover names. Tide notations. Offerings owed. A page on Hymn Cut with grease on the edge from somebody's thumb. A page on Inkwater with three lines crossed out and rewritten. Her mother's hand everywhere, impatient and lovely and infuriating, as if even recordkeeping had to flirt with whoever opened it.

Then one leaf stuck.

She remembered the exact irritation of it. Not dread first. Irritation. Wet paper that would not separate cleanly, fibers lifting in her nail. She had licked thumb and forefinger to get purchase, because the room had taught her not to care where her own body ended. The pages came apart all at once. A corner tore. The lamp guttered. And there, half blurred by water, written harder than the pages before it, were the words that had split the arithmetic of the family open.

Sixth mouth.

Nothing else arrived complete. Not then. Not even now. A line above it, maybe. A note below. The shape of another word beginning with J and bled through beyond recovery. But the phrase itself stood there whole, obscene in its neatness. Not symbol. Not omen. Account language. A counted thing. A fact entered somewhere her mother had thought to keep it.

Lale heard, through the sheet, one of the neighbor women calling Sereya saint as she wrung river water from the dead woman's braid. Saint. Mother. Ferryman. Beloved. All the names that had already used up the room. And under them, on the page in Lale's hand, something worse for being written plainly. Sixth mouth. A mouth before Nami? After Nami? Living? Dead? Hidden? Claimed by somebody the town had never been told to count?

The panic had not felt like panic. It had felt like housekeeping pushed suddenly beyond capacity.

Five children were already too many stories for one body to have made honestly. Five children had already left them scattered across debt, shame, bad fathers, and jobs nobody took pride in unless they had to. Five children had already been enough to split every condolence into factions. A sixth made the whole ledger a lie beyond sorting. A sixth made her mother's body larger in death than the house could hold.

So she tore.

Not carefully. Not strategically. She tore with both hands, ripping the leaf free so fast the binding gave a damp little scream. The sound had seemed enormous. No one heard it over the rain and the prayers and the work of washing the dead. She folded the page once, then again, crammed it into the pocket of her apron, and sat there breathing paper dust and lamp smoke while water knocked under the house.

Lale closed her eyes in Paska's room and saw her younger hand pressed flat over the pocket as if the paper might wriggle out.

"Fuck," she said softly.

Paska did not ask what she had seen. She only waited.

Lale opened her eyes. The room around her had the same geometry and none of the same mercy. "I tore it out."

"Yes."

"I saw the words and tore it out."

"Which words?"

Lale looked down at her wrapped arm. The linen had already taken one small oval of blood. "Sixth mouth."

Paska's face did not change, but something in her shoulders settled, as if a line under tension had finally been given its proper knot. "There."

"There what?"

"Now it's in your mouth instead of only your hand."

Lale laughed once, raw and unbelieving. "I don't know what it means."

"No," Paska said. "But you know it was real enough to make you rip paper in a mourning house."

Lale pressed the heel of her right hand against her brow. The skin there smelled faintly of arrack from where she had touched her arm. "I thought if I could get rid of it, the room would stay countable."

"How'd that work out?"

It was not kind. Kindness would have made the answer smaller than it was. Lale looked at the ledger by the cat, at the thick gap in its pages, at the whole family stalled half by flood and half by whatever their mother had buried in lovers and notes and bodies. "Poorly."

Paska took the mirror from her and set it face down on the bench. "You weren't wrong about one thing."

Lale almost smiled despite herself. "Only one?"

"Only one that's useful today. The room wasn't countable. It still isn't. That's not a personal failure. That's family."

Footsteps thudded outside on the walkway. Riva's. Too hard to be anybody else. Lale straightened on reflex, then stopped. The motion pulled at the fresh marks and sent a clean bright sting up to her elbow. She welcomed it. The pain kept the memory from smoothing back into abstraction.

Riva came through the doorway carrying two cut poles on one shoulder. Iven followed with a coil of rope and a face shut flatter than usual. Nami had a packet of lamp wicks in one hand and river mint stuck behind one ear, which meant somebody in the market had fussed over her without winning an argument. Daro entered last, damp at the collar from a rain burst that had not lasted.

"Turn's in less than an hour," Riva said. "Can you stand?"

Lale rose. Her knees were steady enough. "Yes."

Riva looked at the bandage, then at her face. Riva had never needed words to recognize damage in a room. "What happened?"

There were a dozen ways to dodge. Ask about the poles. Repeat Paska's route. Say later. Say nothing. Lale felt each option like folded cloth stacked in front of her, neat and useless.

She said, "I took the page out of the ledger."

No one moved.

Even the market below seemed, for one impossible beat, to lower its voice.

Riva shifted the poles off her shoulder slowly. "What page?"

Lale heard how careful she had gone, and hated it more than if Riva had barked. "The missing one. Years ago. After Mother died."

Iven's rope slipped a little in his hands. Just enough to notice if you knew him. Lale noticed. She had been noticing him all her life.

Daro said, "Why?"

Because she had wanted one fact in the house to remain manageable. Because grief had made the walls sweat. Because she had not been brave and had not even had the decency to call cowardice by its name. Because the phrase on that page had made the whole family feel suddenly unfinished in a way she could not survive.

"It said something I couldn't bear," she said.

Nami asked, very quiet, "What?"

Lale looked at none of them and all of them. "Sixth mouth."

The words changed the air. That was the worst part. Not thunder, not revelation, only the subtle crowded wrongness of a room acquiring one more body than its chairs allowed.

Daro was the first to breathe. "What does that mean?"

"I don't know," Lale said. "Not enough. Just those words. The page was wet. I tore it before I read more."

Paska spoke from behind her, casual as a knife laid on a table. "Which means the catastrophe isn't some market rumor or shrine trick. It's in your family record, and one of you decided years ago not to let it live on paper."

Riva's eyes shut once. Opened. She did not waste time on blame she could not spend yet. "We get the cut open first."

"We do," Daro said, but he was no longer in the room the same way. The phrase had him now. Lale could see him turning it already, looking for rhythm, omen, hierarchy, any shape that might place him in relation to it.

Iven said nothing at all.

That worried her more than Daro.

Paska handed Lale a stoppered vial of dark oil. "Rub that in after the first wash. Not before. If the lines scab thick, you've been babying them."

Lale took it. "Thank you."

Paska looked faintly insulted. "Bring the arm back if the hook line blurs. And don't let anybody grab you by the wrist when the current turns."

Nami touched the river mint behind her ear as if reminding herself it was there. "Will the marks really tell her when to turn?"

"If she pays attention." Paska bent to pick the ledger up from beside the cat and held it out to Lale. "Skin's honest longer than paper. That's the lesson. Try not to waste it."

Lale took the book with her unmarked hand. The gap in the binding felt uglier now that it had a cause she could not blame on anybody else. She tucked the ledger under her arm, careful of the fresh bandage, and followed the others out onto the walkway.

The market had resumed itself below them because markets always did. A man was gutting silver fish under a patched awning. A girl with scarlet glass bangles was haggling over lamp oil as if flood and corpse and saint family had nothing to do with the price of ordinary life. Rain began again in a fine slant, not enough to drive anyone indoors. The water under the pilings looked blacker than ink.

Riva went ahead toward the mooring lane. Daro lingered half a step behind Lale, thinking. Iven dropped to the rear. Nami moved wherever there was room, all alertness and wet hair and dangerous openness. The family kept its old shape even now, each person falling where habit and injury placed them.

Lale felt the wrapped marks pull when she closed her hand around the rail. The sting was immediate and exact. Good. Let the body keep the account for once. Let it refuse neatness. Let the phrase sit where she could not lock it in a drawer.

Sixth mouth.

It had been ink once. Then paper. Then absence. Now it moved warm under her own skin as she stepped toward Inkwater and the waiting turn of the tide.
