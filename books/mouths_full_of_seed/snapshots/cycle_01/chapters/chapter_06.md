# Chapter 6

Marrow Market always looked to Iven like a thought the delta had after too much rain: bright, profitable, and one hard shove from collapse.

By the time the `Bitter Thread` nosed around the reed spit and into the market lanes, the place had already hung its guts out for trade. Eel racks dripped over the water. Prayer bowls flashed tin-bright under awnings patched with sailcloth. Shrouds dyed for better deaths swung beside baskets of crab, strings of peppers, jars of ink, and oil lamps turned low against the morning glare. The whole market stood on thin pilings and old nerve. Floodwater licked the undersides of the boards. Every plank smelled of wet wood, fish blood, lamp fat, and the sweet rot that rose when the river had been eating inland too long.

Behind the barge, two narrow prayer skiffs and one low corpse scow knocked and yawed in the wake. After Hymn Cut answered Daro's voice, people had begun shoving their dead and their hopes at the family as if the river had opened for regular business. Riva had agreed to tow only the ones already waiting at the shrine. She had repeated that agreement three times before they cast off. She was repeating it now from the tiller, jaw hard, hands black with wet rope.

"We cut through, we don't stop, and nobody adds a damned thing," she said.

Iven stood on the side deck with one hand on the rail and watched heads turning along the catwalks. News had outrun them again. Market women lifted knives from fish bellies to stare. Boys ran ahead across the planks, yelling that the saint's children were bringing the road through. A pair of old men in burial white leaned so far over a railing they nearly tipped in. Curiosity moved faster than current in Reedmouth. So did grief when it smelled an advantage.

"We're already dragging three extra things," Lale said from the cargo hatch. She had her ledger board braced against her thigh even here, because if she stopped counting, the world might become exactly as stupid as it looked. "No one else comes aboard."

"They're not coming aboard," Iven said.

Riva cut him a look over her shoulder. "That tone means I ought to tie you to the mast."

"You don't have a mast."

"Then I'll nail you to something flatter."

Nami laughed before she could stop herself. The sound came out thin with leftover shrine-tiredness. She was crouched by the towing cleats, trailing her fingers through spray until Lale slapped her wrist away from the line. Her hair still smelled faintly of the bell smoke from Ilyan's place. Daro sat on the stern locker with his boots planted wide, damp curls stuck back from his face, letting the market get a look at him as if attention were rain and he had always known how to turn his skin upward. Since Hymn Cut, he had been carrying his own throat like a lit lamp.

That did it.

No one in Marrow Market respected a miracle until somebody found the price of standing near it.

Iven leaned over the rail and called, "If you've got dead waiting inland, get your names ready. First tide through Inkwater won't be free."

Riva said, very level, "Iven."

He kept smiling at the market. "If you've got offerings to send, same thing. Priority knots. One mark reserves your place, two if you're ugly about it."

A laugh went up from the catwalk. Not a scandalized laugh. An interested one.

Lale shut her eyes the way some people shut a door. "You can't be serious."

"I'm never serious," he said. "That's why this works."

The first coins came anyway. A woman with a basket of votive candles held two silver bits between finger and thumb and shouted, "My brother's in Halcote with water in him. Do I get him through before rot opens the face?"

"If your mark's good, he gets in line before people who haggle," Iven said.

"That's not a line that exists," Lale snapped.

"It exists now."

He dug into the box of spare cord by the rail, found a coil of waxed blue line gone fuzzy with old damp, and began cutting arm-length pieces with his knife. One knot for a body. Two for a family boat. He tied them around wrists, candle handles, offering baskets, whatever got near enough. The work was quick, intimate, ridiculous. Fingers shoved at him from every angle, cold coins pressed into his palm, names tumbled over each other.

"Mera Pell, two sons and a husband."

"Temple ward from East Slip."

"My mother's on ice, don't let them tell me ice counts."

"You write?" someone asked.

"I remember better than writing," Iven said, which was a lie so small it hardly needed a conscience.

Riva swung the barge broadside enough to clip the first market post and swore. "I said we weren't stopping."

"We're drifting with salesmanship," Iven called back.

"We're drifting with death tied to us, you moron."

He gave her half a shrug and kept working. The blue cord was almost gone already. The market loved anything that looked halfway official, and official in Reedmouth often meant only that someone had the nerve to say a thing twice in public. If he could turn one morning of gawking into marks, that was not theft. That was provisioning. Oren Pike still wanted his four hundred and eighty silver plus the interest gnawing at it. The `Bitter Thread` ate pitch and repair money like a spoiled animal. Nami smelled of cannery salt because she had been underpaid her whole life. Daro's funeral singing could not keep five siblings in rice, and Lale's nice dry bookkeeping had never once floated a hull. Somebody had to be vulgar enough to turn luck into groceries.

Paska Dey was sitting in the doorway of the ink house with a stevedore's calf in her lap, her own bare foot pinning the man's ankle steady while she worked. She always kept fennel seed in one cheek while she tattooed because she said blood smelled less lonely that way. Rain had darkened the shaved side of her head to black velvet. She looked up once, needle still moving.

"You're selling wake-water by the cup again," she said.

"By the knot," Iven told her. "Much cleaner unit."

"Nothing at this market stays clean."

He grinned. "Then I'm in the right neighborhood."

She flicked a thread of wiped ink into the flood and went back to the man's skin, which was almost insultingly close to blessing.

Daro rose at last, either to stop the nonsense or enjoy it from a better angle. Iven knew his brother well enough to bet on the second. The crowd parted just enough to make a lane for his face. A few people bowed without deciding to. Daro lifted one hand, modest as a liar, and the lane widened.

"See?" Iven said softly to Riva. "They're already here."

Riva's answer came without turning. "They're here because floodwater puts idiots on the same road."

"And mourners," Daro said. His voice had that soft shrine-rub to it now, velvet over splinters. "Don't mock need just because it arrives loud."

"I'm not mocking need," Riva said. "I'm mocking my brother."

"Both can be true," Lale muttered.

The lane beside the barge filled with prayer boats nosing close for a better look. Men on catwalks began shouting names of relatives from inland parishes. An old woman flung a wrapped packet of shrine ash onto the deck and demanded they carry it free because Saint Sereya had once shared a bed with her sister's aunt. Nami caught the packet before it slid into the bilge and stared at the careful way the old woman had sewn the corners closed.

"She used wedding thread," Nami said to nobody.

"Charge her extra for sentiment," Iven said.

Nami looked up, startled and hurt for a second, because she still treated jokes like fishhooks and never knew which way to pull. Then the old woman spat toward him and the market laughed again, and the hurt passed. That was the trick of this place. It roughened everything fast enough that no feeling got to stay clean.

He took more coins. Copper, mostly. A bent half-mark. One good silver piece warm from a widow's bra. He passed the money to Lale, who refused it on principle until he dropped it into the pocket of her apron and forced principle to do bookkeeping.

"You're building a riot ledger," she said.

"Then keep the columns neat."

Riva tried to ease the `Bitter Thread` forward at last, but there were too many hulls near them now. The barge moved a boat-length, then another quarter of one. More hands reached in. Somebody started chanting Daro's name. Daro did not stop them. He stood with one palm on the wet rail and an expression so carefully unwilling it made Iven want to laugh and slap him in equal measure.

This, Iven thought, was the real family inheritance. Not holiness. Not labor. Not even the route. The appetite in a crowd when it saw one of them and wondered whether to kneel, fuck, pay, or accuse.

The water under the hull changed its mouth.

At first it was only a sound. Not louder. Wronger. The chop and market slap gave way to a long sucking drag under the boards, as if somebody below had put lips to the pilings and drawn breath through all of them at once.

Riva heard it the same moment he did. Her head snapped toward the stern. "Lines ready."

Lale was already moving.

Nami stood up too fast and nearly lost her footing.

Iven looked back at the tow. The corpse scow had shifted off center. The rope between it and the second prayer skiff went bar-tight, then quivered in a way ropes did when the force on them had decided to come from somewhere no one wanted.

"Hymn Cut's pushing back," Riva said.

For the first time that morning, Daro's face emptied of performance.

The market, still laughing, leaned closer.

Then the dead arrived.

***

The first body came up under a string of smoked peppers and wore them around its neck like festival flowers.

People laughed. They could not help it. The thing was too sudden and too filthy and too dressed by accident. The corpse bobbed up with its face turned politely toward the spice stall, one cheek eaten down to yellow rope, hair slicked over its skull. The peppers bumped against its jaw. Somebody on the catwalk said, "Christ, he's shopping," and the whole lane barked with relieved laughter.

Then the smell hit.

Not ordinary dead. Not one body found in heat and washed for burial. This was the river's kept rot, old sweet and opened all at once, mixed with reed mud, swallowed salt, and the black tannic stink of whatever the current had been steeping them in downstream. The market recoiled as if one hand had shoved every chest backward.

Another corpse surfaced sideways and struck the hull with a hollow thud. Then another. One had both hands tangled in prayer ribbons. One was still wrapped from the waist down in shroud cloth that had come loose and streamed behind like torn sail. A child's body spun once in the eddy under the catwalk, braid intact, eyes gone. The laughter cut off so hard it felt like a blade laid flat across the lane.

"Push them off!" Riva shouted. "Get those boats apart. Iven, hook!"

He lunged for the boathook and nearly took a candle seller in the teeth because three of the witnesses he'd let onto the side deck were trying to climb over him at once. One woman slipped on fish scales and sat down hard in a pile of coiled line, her basket of votives exploding across the planks. Hot wax rolled underfoot. Somebody screamed that the saint's children were dragging the drowned through market trade. Somebody else screamed a name from the catwalk and leaned so far down Iven thought he meant to kiss the dead.

The backwash came through the lane in a single ugly shove.

It rammed the prayer skiffs into the `Bitter Thread`, drove the corpse scow sideways, and spat more bodies out of the reed cut between the ink house and the fish sheds. Half-floating dead jammed against moored boats, stall pilings, and each other, turning the whole lane into a clog of hull wood and flesh. A prayer boat snapped loose and slewed broadside. The pepper-garland corpse rolled under it and vanished. The child with the braid caught at the prow of the second skiff and stayed there, turning slowly.

Iven jammed the boathook under a dead man's shoulder and shoved. The flesh gave too easily. He felt it through the pole, a sliding collapse, and nearly lost his grip. River water fountained black from the body's mouth. Something hard clicked across the deck and struck his boot. A salt-fig seed, black as a polished tooth.

Nami saw it. He knew she saw it because her whole body went still, the way animals went still when sound got inside them. She was looking at the seed, not the bodies, not the boats, not Riva screaming at all of them. Just that one wet black seed turning in the scupper with bits of grave-silt on it.

"Nami." He barked it before he knew he'd chosen her over the line.

She looked up.

The deck heaved. The candle seller slid again and this time went over the side with both hands windmilling. Iven dropped the hook and caught her shawl as she went. The cloth cut into his palm. Below, the water was full of dead faces and broken prayer bowls. He got one knee against the rail, hauled, and felt the woman's whole weight drag through his shoulder. She came back aboard choking and swearing, one shoe gone, her fresh blue promise knot cutting into the wrist he'd grabbed.

"You said we'd ride clean!" she screamed in his face.

"I say all kinds of things," he shot back, because if he did not say something ugly the shame would get in first.

Riva heard him and looked over with murder plain as daylight. "Cut the witness fares loose. All of them. Now."

"They're not fares," Daro said, and then louder, to the catwalks and boats and everyone stupid enough to be listening, "Keep your feet. Keep the hulls apart. Don't crowd the lane."

People listened to him.

That was the worst part. Not all of them. Not enough. But more than listened to Riva.

His voice moved over the water like oil poured carefully, and for one breath the panic took its head up and paused to admire itself. Men on the catwalk stopped shoving. Two women in a prayer skiff got their poles set right. One boy frozen at the edge of a fish stand backed away instead of jumping to the deck. Daro had found the pitch of command that sounded like mercy.

Riva saw it happen. So did Iven.

She hated it and used it anyway.

"Then sing them backward," she snapped. "I don't care if they want to marry you. Clear my lane."

Daro's mouth hardened. He lifted his voice again, not quite song, not yet speech. "Pole with the push. Not against it. Left side, let the dead slide. You there, untie that stern or lose the whole boat."

The market crews might have obeyed either sibling if the dead had stayed abstract. They did not. A bloated woman in pilgrim gray turned with the current and struck the fish counter under the awning so hard the stall legs hopped. Her jaw came open on impact. Two more salt-fig seeds dropped from her mouth and rolled between split mackerel and a butcher's heel. The butcher shrieked and jumped back. One seed vanished through a crack in the boards. The other lodged against a fish head and sat there shining.

Paska Dey did not scream. She handed her tattoo needle to the half-finished stevedore as calmly as if she were passing salt, then stepped into the lane with ink-dark fingers and a boat pole nearly as tall as she was. Her customer, one leg still wet with fresh tide marks, limped after her because some people would rather bleed than look cowardly where everyone could see.

"Move the live ones first," she called. "You can curse him after."

"The hell I can," shouted the candle seller, still sprawled on the deck and trying to wedge her lost dignity back into place.

The current shoved again. A market boy on a catwalk lost hold of his basket and sent river crabs showering into the lane. For one absurd second the crabs climbed the dead like a new kind of armor, all those red legs finding purchase on swollen cheeks and cloth knots and rope-burned hands. Someone laughed once, a cracked reflex, and then choked on it when the child with the braid rolled over and showed half a face they knew.

"That's Fenn's girl," a man on the catwalk said.

Nobody laughed after that.

The market changed under the sound of the name. Fear got personal. Curiosity curdled into blame so fast Iven could almost hear it thicken.

"Get them out of here."

"You brought this."

"I paid him."

"I don't care what you paid him. Get her out."

The lane narrowed as people backed away from the water and crowded each other on the planks. Boats scraped. Pilings groaned. The corpse scow behind the barge struck a post hard enough to split one of its side slats, and two wrapped bodies inside thumped against the opening like sacks dropped by impatient gods.

Lale had stripped off her overskirt and tied it around one wrist so the hem would not foul the lines. Indigo bled down her forearm. She looked less like a bookkeeper now than a woman who had been forced to put both hands into the ruined account. "If that scow opens," she said to nobody and everybody, "we all lose the day."

"Useful wording," Iven said, because talking kept his stomach from turning over.

"Shut up and haul."

He hauled.

He hooked one prayer skiff clear enough for a market rigger to catch the line from above. He kicked a floating crate of oil lamps away before it could smash under the paddle wheel. He saw Nami leaning over the rail again, not with curiosity now but with that unnerving listening look, and grabbed the back of her shirt so hard he nearly tore the seam.

"Stay where your feet are," he said.

She stared at the braid caught on the prow. "She's been in the dark a long time."

"So have I. Stay put."

Her eyes flashed at him, wounded and furious, but she stayed.

Riva drove the barge with both hands and the whole weight of her body, forcing the nose a half-turn into the shove so the stern could angle free. She shouted practical verbs the way other people prayed. "Push. Cut. Hold. Drop it. No, not that line, the red one. Lale, brace. Daro, shut them up or use them. Iven, if you sold another ticket I will bury you myself."

There was nothing saintly about her, which was maybe why Iven trusted her more than any of them. She always sounded like consequence arriving on time.

The market did not care.

When Daro raised his voice again, even just to order the prayer boats wider, faces turned toward him. When Riva shouted, they obeyed only if her command matched the direction they were already frightened of going. Iven watched it happen in real time and hated everybody involved, including himself most of all.

Because he had done this. Not the backwash. Not the dead. The crowd. The slow. The extra bodies leaned too close. The idiot hope tied in blue knots around wrists. He had turned a passage problem into an audience and then the audience into ballast.

The corpse scow struck the piling again.

This time the side plank blew.

The wrapped bodies inside sagged through the gap and hit the lane water with a sound like wet laundry dropped from height. One bundle split at the shoulder. A gray hand came free, wedding thread still looped at the wrist. The woman from the candle basket made a noise Iven would hear later in his sleep and crawled backward on all fours, leaving wax smears under her palms.

Paska planted her pole, shoved one corpse clear of the ink-house steps, and spat her fennel seed into the flood. "You want to keep selling, pretty boy?" she said without looking at him. "There's your market."

He could not answer.

Not because she had cut him. Because she had not cut deep enough to matter beside what the lane looked like now.

Bodies jammed against prayer boats. Prayer boats jammed against stalls. Goods spilled into water already slick with opened rot. A lantern stand toppled. Lit oil spread in one trembling skin over the surface, then broke apart around a dead child's hair. Somebody above started vomiting between the planks. Somebody else was weeping and trying to pole at the same time. The butcher from the fish shed had a hook in one hand and tears running off his chin because the woman on the counter had been his cousin.

It took the rest of the morning and part of the day to get the lane moving again.

By the end of it the dead had been dragged into a rough holding crescent under the lee of the fish sheds, the witness riders had either fled or turned themselves into accusers, and Marrow Market smelled like a stomach split open and prayed over too late.

Iven's palm still burned where the candle seller's shawl had cut him. His pockets were full of wet coin and wetter shame.

***

Nobody in Marrow Market offered easy help after the lane cleared.

They stood around offering difficult opinions for free.

The market riggers had hauled the `Bitter Thread`'s iron drag-frame up onto a loading platform and sat on it with their pike poles across their knees, waiting to see who would pay for the broken stall legs, the fouled lane, the lost trade, and the corpse scow plank they'd had to replace with market lumber. Two other crews had taken the good tarred hawser and the deck block off the barge as "temporary surety," which in Reedmouth meant theft with witnesses. Without the frame, line, and block, Riva could still move the barge. She could not do the work waiting downstream.

She stared at the men on the platform as if she were working out how many teeth she could afford to knock loose before the day became unrecoverable.

"Give me one reason not to take it back by hand," she said.

The nearest rigger shrugged. "Take it up with your pretty brother. He sold half the market a front-row seat."

"I sold them hope," Iven said.

The rigger spit into the water. "Then you owe extra for the smell."

Lale dumped the contents of Iven's pockets onto an overturned bait crate and began sorting by denomination with murderous calm. Wet silver clicked. Copper left green shadows on the wood. The blue promise cords lay among the coins like drowned worms.

"Twenty-six paid knots," she said. "Four more he tied for people who promised to find him later, which is not money, just infection. Seventeen silver marks, eight half-marks, eleven coppers, three temple chips, and one brass button nobody here will admit to spending."

"The button had conviction," Iven said.

"You have a disease," Lale said.

Nami sat on the edge of the deck with her feet tucked up, scraping wax off her palms with a thumbnail. She had barely spoken since the lane clogged. Every so often she looked toward the fish sheds as if she expected the dead to start talking over the gutting tables. Daro stood by the stern ladder with river water dried dark on his trouser legs and let the crowd lingering on the catwalks study his profile.

Riva saw that too. "Don't you start again."

Daro turned. "Start what."

"Whatever this is where you let people kneel without earning it."

His mouth twitched. "I kept them from piling into the lane."

"I kept the barge from splitting on the posts."

"Both things can be true," Lale said without looking up.

"Stop saying that," Riva and Iven said together.

For the first time all day, Nami smiled. It vanished fast.

Daro stepped closer, voice lowering into the hush he used when he wanted intimacy to do the work of force. "They listened."

"To the wrong thing," Riva said.

"To the thing they needed."

"What they needed was room to move, not a fucking revelation."

Heads turned along the catwalk. Even now. Even after the stink, the screaming, the dead hauled under tarps. The family could not fight privately in this town. Sereya had spent too many years making even their ugliness public property.

Iven looked from one sibling to the other and felt the old pleasure in being able to slip sideways while the bigger battle took the room. He opened his mouth to make that slip, some cheap line about revelation smelling worse up close, and found he had nothing left light enough to throw.

Riva jabbed a finger toward the seized gear. "We need that frame, that block, and the hawser back before dark."

"They want stall payment," Lale said. "And crew fee. And corpse handling."

"I'll work it off," Riva said.

The nearest rigger laughed. "Not with a flood lane open and half the market blaming your name. We don't need your labor. We need silver and the right to tell the story with ourselves on the clever side."

Daro looked at the men on the platform, then at the crowd beyond them. Something bright and poisonous moved behind his eyes. "I could ask."

Riva turned so hard the wet boards barked under her boot. "You ask for one more thing in front of them and I'll throw you in after the dead."

He took a step toward her. Not a big step. The kind family took when it meant more than a shove. "You don't get to talk as if you're the only one carrying this."

"No," she said. "Apparently I get to carry it while the rest of you decorate it."

That landed. On Daro. On Iven. On all of them.

Iven laughed because the alternative was to stand there with his mouth open like the dead. "That's unfair."

Riva rounded on him. "Is it."

He gave her the shrug he used when he wanted contempt to look cheaper than pain. "The backwash would've come whether I sold knots or sang hymns or fucked the whole market one stall at a time."

Lale's head came up. "Don't be grotesque because you lack a defense."

"I had a defense. It fell in the water."

Riva stepped close enough for him to smell pitch and river off her skin. "You slowed us. You loaded gawkers onto my deck. You took money for a queue we can't honor. Now those men have our gear and every fool in this market thinks we brought the dead here to turn a profit."

He nearly said, We did turn a profit. Seventeen marks, eight halves, and a brass button. Instead he looked past her at the catwalk where the candle seller was pointing him out to fresh listeners, her one stocking leg still muddy to the knee. Shame came in him not as heat but as cold water down the spine, thin and relentless.

So he lied small first.

"I can smooth them."

"With what?" Lale asked. "Charm has already failed numerically."

"With money."

Riva laughed once. It was a worse sound than any shout she'd made all day. "Do you have some hidden under your gold tooth?"

No. But he knew where money sat in Marrow Market when grief and work started biting each other. It never sat with the pious. It sat with men who set odds on how long piety could hold out.

Kes Varn kept his board under a roof of patched sailcloth two walkways over from the lamp sellers, close enough to the loading slips to hear trouble coming and far enough from shrine traffic that nobody respectable had to admit knowing him. He was in his usual chair when Iven found him, one boot off and his left sock rolled to the knee because wet cuffs irritated him more than debtors did. He kept a strip of orange peel tucked in his cheek to fight the old sweet smell of tooth rot. Before every serious number, he spat the peel into the water and put in a fresh piece.

He watched Iven come without rising.

"I had you at three to one for causing a public nuisance before midmonth," Kes said. "Should've set it lower."

Iven leaned one shoulder to the post because sitting would have made this look like leisure. "You're welcome for the business."

"Business is when both parties pretend they had choices."

The betting board behind him still held morning chalk: storm depth at Knot Bend, eel yield at Tovin's yards, whether Saint Sereya's children would open a third cut before someone important drowned. Someone had already added a fresh line beneath in hurried hand. `MARROW LANE CLEARS BEFORE SUNSET`.

"You taking that action too?" Iven asked.

"Already did. Market says no. I say panic gets boring by supper."

"You should revise upward. We've got a family talent for extending disgrace."

Kes smiled around the orange peel. It made the right side of his mouth look kind and the left look diseased. "How much."

Iven hated how fast the answer came. He'd been doing the count in his head since the riggers sat on the drag-frame. Stall compensation, crew fee, borrowed lumber, one replacement plank, release on the line and block. Sixty-two would stop the argument from breeding. Less would only teach it to limp.

"Sixty-two marks."

Kes whistled softly. "That's not smoothing. That's repentance."

"Don't cheapen me. I'm here for logistics."

"And the favors?"

There it was. Money in Reedmouth never came alone. It brought future through the door with it and sat both of you down.

"One," Iven said.

Kes put in a fresh strip of peel. "Two."

"One and a half."

"You want half a corpse moved or half a boat burned?"

Iven rubbed at the cut in his palm. Salt from the lane had dried there and made the skin tight. "Make them clean."

"Nothing I ask of you will be clean."

That, at least, was honest.

The market kept breathing around them. Hammers knocking reset boards into place. Someone cursing over spoiled fish. Somebody farther off telling the corpse-jam story already, getting the details wrong in profitable directions. Iven could go back empty and watch Riva try force, Daro try charisma, Lale try arithmetic, and all of them fail one after another in full public view. Or he could buy motion and let the bill arrive when it liked.

He said, "Fine. Two."

Kes did not offer a hand. He pulled a slate toward himself, wrote `62 + 2` beside Iven's name, and tapped the chalk once to settle the number. Then he opened the strongbox under his chair and counted out the silver with the tenderness some men kept for infants. Thirty. Forty. Fifty. Sixty-two. Wet light ran over the stacks. Iven held out his palm and Kes dropped the money into it as if feeding an animal he did not trust close to the fingers.

"If Enver asks," Kes said, "I never met you."

That made Iven look up.

Kes shrugged. "Everybody knows whose face you borrowed. Doesn't mean I need the ropehouse spitting in my door."

Shame got an address then. Not just family. Not just Riva's look. Enver. Coil Reach waiting downstream like a bill with legs.

"Go on," Kes said. "Before your sister decides dignity is cheaper than equipment."

When Iven came back to the barge with the marks, the riggers rose before Riva could speak. Money had more authority than blood in Marrow Market, and a great deal more than sainthood after a bad morning. The drag-frame came off the platform. The tarred hawser changed hands. Two hired crews with pike poles and narrow shoulders began arguing over where to set the frame for the next lane without so much as glancing at Riva for instruction.

She saw the silver, then his face, and understood all of it at once.

"From who," she said.

"Kes."

Lale shut her eyes again. This time there was no door in it, only arithmetic.

"How much," Riva said.

He could have lied small again. He did not have the room left for it.

"Sixty-two and two favors."

The hired crews went a little quieter. Catwalk listeners leaned in. Even Daro looked amused for one ugly second before the number finished landing and took the amusement out of him.

Riva's voice came low and flat. "You don't sign future pieces of us without asking."

Iven put the released block on the deck harder than he meant to. Metal rang. "Then next time ask the market to wait while we hold a family vote."

"There won't be a next time."

"There was this time."

That should have sounded clever. It came out tired and almost mean enough to be true.

Daro, because he could never leave a fracture alone if it might widen toward him, said, "At least he got us moving."

Riva turned on him, disbelief so pure it was almost clean. "You want to back this?"

Daro spread his hands, damp and graceful. "I want us not stranded in a market full of bodies while the river keeps speaking."

"The river wasn't speaking," Riva said. "It was choking."

Nami looked up from the wax she'd been peeling off her palm. "It can be both."

Nobody answered her. That was its own kind of answer.

The hired crews hefted the drag-frame and asked Iven where he wanted it. Not Riva. Him. Because he was the one who had paid. Because Marrow Market understood debt faster than competence. Because authority was not a crown you kept dry in a box. It was whatever got men lifting at the moment lifting had to happen.

Riva heard the question as clearly as he did.

For one beat the whole deck held still around it. The fish-shed tarps snapping. The catwalk listeners pretending not to listen. Daro watching like a man who thought every split in the family might yet be read as fate. Lale with both hands flat on the bait crate, knuckles indigo and white. Nami smelling of wax and river and something newly frightened.

Iven should have handed the answer straight to Riva.

He did, eventually. But not quickly enough.

"Starboard lane first," he told the crews. "Then free the stern."

Only after the words were out did he look at Riva and add, "That's what she wants."

The men nodded to him and moved.

That was the part he hated most. Not the debt. Not even the sixty-two marks already gone warm in other hands. It was the way the crews obeyed him first and only then glanced to Riva for correction, as if her authority were now another thing to negotiate on the deck beside the ropes and the dead.
