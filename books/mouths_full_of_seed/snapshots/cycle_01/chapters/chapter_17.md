# Chapter 17

Salt had furred the chapel walls white, but the night was still hot enough to sweat under it. The sea breach kept drawing breath through the ruined nave, a deep wet drag that pulled lantern flame sideways and made the drowned knock softly against one another in the black water below. On the broken altar step Daro braced Sereya's brass-ferruled pole under his arm and sang into the opening she had once tried to own. Every note made the pull harder.

Riva felt it in the iron key knocking her thigh, in the seam of the stone under her bare soles, in the muscles of her back that had already spent themselves once tonight and now had to answer again. The chapel had lost half its roof in the last surge. Moonlight came through in ripped silver panes. Beyond the broken wall the Gray Sea kept shouldering in, then dragging out with corpses and prayer bowls and loose reed that should have been miles upriver. Salt-fig shoots had rooted in the cracks of the forecourt stones. Their leaves were glossy and obscene in the dark.

Daro's voice filled the breach too cleanly. That was what made her hate it. He was hoarse already from the chase through the channels, but the hoarseness only roughened the edges of the sound; it did not weaken it. It made him sound worked on by weather and therefore worth trusting. Pilgrims clung to the chapel posts and stared at him with the stupid exhausted hope she had seen in men watching a towline hold over floodwater. Under the step, the dead gathered and turned. Their mouths kept working. Seeds clicked against teeth.

Iven was tied to the west post by Enver's tarred mooring line so the pull could not take him. The bandage around his left hand had gone black to the wrist. He kept his bad arm braced to his chest and worked the line with his right, his teeth, his shoulder, anything still serviceable. Every so often a laugh tried to get out of him and died halfway, like his body had forgotten what joke it meant to make.

Nami had crawled too close to the lip. Salt had dried in a white crescent on her mouth. She was staring into the breach with both hands flat on the stone as if she could feel some smaller current under the obvious one. Bato had a grip on the back of her skirt, two fingers pinched in the cloth. He kept turning his good ear toward the water and his bad one away from the shouting, as though noise had become another weather he had to survive.

Tovin Reed stood at the chapel's side wheel housing, one boot unlaced because her ankle had swollen big inside it, counting under her breath and working a cracked pair of spectacles up her nose every time the spray knocked them down. She only wore them when she had to look at iron close. The fact that she had bothered now told Riva how little room for error was left.

"He's wrong," Tovin said.

Riva did not answer. She already knew it. She had known it when Daro planted the pole and the water answered him like a dog coming to heel. She had known it when the first corpse rose upright in the turning black and faced him with a seed swelling its dead gums apart. She had known it when Nami leaned forward like a child hearing her name in the next room.

Daro spread his free hand as he sang, palm out, like blessing or command. Blood had started at one corner of his mouth. He didn't seem to notice. He gave the breach another line of song and the current cinched tighter around the altar foundation. Two bodies in prayer cloth smacked shoulder to shoulder and vanished under.

"Daro." Riva pitched her voice to carry through his. "Get off it."

He sang over her.

"You hear me? Let go of the pole."

His eyes found hers at last. Even at this distance she could see the fever in them, the awful tender certainty. He let the line of song taper just enough to speak without losing the pitch.

"It opened for me."

"It isn't open."

"Listen to it."

As if she were deaf. As if all her life had not been spent listening for what wood did under strain, what current did under hull, what men sounded like just before a cable snapped and took a hand with it. The breach was not opening. It was tightening around him. The dead below were not moving seaward. They were circling his voice.

Bato said, without raising his own, "He's making himself the hinge."

Daro heard that and smiled at him with pure contempt. "You hid in a salt hole for seventeen years, and now you're an expert on hinges?"

"I'm an expert on what drowns."

There it was then, the child's cough. Not in the air. In the stone throat under the altar. A small wet catch and fail, like somebody trying to breathe through flood mud. Tovin went rigid at the wheel housing. Nami made a sound so soft it barely counted as voice.

Jun, Riva thought, and hated herself at once for thinking it, because she did not believe in ghosts as clean separations from memory. The river used whatever the living gave it. The cough could have been current. It could have been a reed stem in a crack. It could have been the name they had all finally learned too late. It was enough. It was too much.

Daro lifted his chin and took another breath for song.

Riva moved.

She crossed the salt-slick stone in three strides, used the broken pew as a step, and hit him low in the ribs before the note fully left him. The pole jumped under his arm. Brass rang on stone. Daro's body was all bone and no ballast; he went sideways with a choked curse, his mouth smashing against her shoulder. The note tore in half.

The breach answered like something stabbed.

Water came up through the altar grating in a black sheet. The wheel housing screamed. Tovin swore and threw herself onto the crank. Iven lost the line and caught it again with his right arm wrapped twice around it, the rope flaying skin as it ran. Nami slid on both knees toward the lip until Bato hauled her back by the skirt hard enough to tear stitches. One of the chapel posts cracked. Pilgrims shouted prayers and obscenities into each other's faces.

Riva got both hands on the pole before it could skitter into the breach. The brass ferrule burned cold through her palms. Daro clawed for it, coughing, salt water and blood stringing from his mouth.

"You stupid bitch," he rasped.

"Stand up if you're still alive."

He tried to hit her and only managed to brace against her shoulder, shaking. His throat worked convulsively. Whatever he had been drawing through it had left raw furrows in the sound already. Good, some mean part of her thought, and then she hated that too.

Tovin hauled the crank three clicks and the water dropped from a sheet to a hammering churn. It was not calm and it was never going to be calm, but it was no longer the instant upward grab that would have taken the whole altar with them.

Pilgrims stared as if Riva had strangled a saint in public.

"You broke it," Daro said. His voice came thin, then swelled, then cracked again, searching for its old velvet and not finding the full piece. "You had one useful thing in front of you and you broke it."

Riva shoved the pole against his chest. "Hold that if you want. You're not singing alone."

"You don't get to decide that."

"I just did."

He laughed then, ugly and unbelieving, and blood spotted his lower lip. "You'd rather wreck the only answer than let me be right."

Bato came up the step with the torn piece of Nami's skirt still knotted in one fist. Close, he smelled of brine, old fish oil, and the medicinal mint somebody had rubbed into his shoulder earlier that evening. He looked at Daro the way a diver looked at a bad knot: not frightened, not impressed, only unwilling to trust it.

"No," Bato said. "She'd rather not watch you do what Sereya did."

That landed. It landed harder than the tackle had. Daro's face altered around it, not toward shame but toward naked fury.

"You don't get to use her name like a cudgel after you let her drown."

Bato's jaw moved once. "I didn't let her. I failed to stop her. Same as I'm trying not to fail now."

Tovin climbed the last two steps with the crank handle still in one hand. Her spectacles were fogged solid. She yanked them off and hung them by their cord so they banged wetly against her chest.

"I watched the first attempt from the east rocks," she said. "I watched her plant that pole like a flag and sing as if the whole cut could fit through one throat. Jun went under first because he was strapped nearest. She went after him. Bato went after both. Storm Mouth doesn't love a chosen body. It loves a body it can take for the price of pretending."

The child's cough came again from the grating.

Riva felt Nami flinch behind her.

Daro gripped the pole so hard the tendons stood in his wrists. "Then tell me what you came all this way to tell us, old woman. Go on. If the mouth wants ugliness, it has plenty."

Tovin's eyes cut to Riva. "The key."

Riva pulled it from her belt. Rust had reddened the ring from years of handling. Tovin had forged the thing for Reed Gate and given it over in chapter three with the kind of reluctance that was nearly affection. It looked stupidly small against the altar machinery.

"Not purity," Tovin said. "Not holiness. Sequence. Labor. Five hands doing what one saint tried to steal."

Bato nodded once. "And no lies. The cut's full of them already."

"What sequence?" Lale asked from the chapel floor below. She had come up silent while the shouting held everyone else. Salt spray had dampened the neat braid at her neck and turned loose strands black against her cheek. Her indigo-stained thumb was pressed to the old needle scar in her other hand, a habit Riva only ever saw when Lale was forcing herself not to shake. "Tell me exactly."

Tovin pointed with the crank handle to the altar, the floor, the rigging points, the breach beyond. "Key to wake the teeth. Marks to place the bodies. Rope to spread the strain. Weight to pull the road outward. Song last, once the path exists. Reverse of her vanity."

"And the truths?" Lale asked.

"Fed with each step," Bato said. "What she left in you. Not the pretty hurts. The rot."

Iven barked a laugh from the post and winced at the movement. "That's cheerful."

Riva looked at Daro. "Can you do song last?"

He stared back. His face was bloodless except where the salt had abraded it raw. For one moment she thought he would refuse and force her to choose whether to knock him senseless and hope the breach would accept someone else. Then his gaze slid past her to Nami, who was still kneeling at the lip with Bato's torn fistful of cloth at her side and her own hands clenched empty on the stone.

"You want confession in a storm," Daro said softly. "You always did prefer work to grace."

"There's no grace here."

"That's where you're wrong."

"Maybe," Bato said. "But you'll still do it this way or you can drown alone."

The words took something out of the room. Even the pilgrims stopped muttering. They had wanted a spectacle and perhaps a martyr. What Bato offered was labor with witnesses. The difference insulted everyone present.

Lale exhaled through her nose once. "Fine. Then stop staring at one another and bring me what belongs where."

That got them moving because it sounded like an instruction instead of a revelation. Riva understood the usefulness of that at once. She shoved Sereya's pole at Daro hard enough that he had to catch it or lose it, then jumped down from the altar before anyone could call her brave or cruel. Neither interested her.

"Iven," she said. "You can still tie?"

"I've got one hand and excellent character."

"Then use the hand."

"See," he told the post beside him. "No one appreciates range."

Riva turned to Nami. "Stay off the lip."

Nami looked up. Her eyes were too wide and too dark. "Something's under there."

"I know."

"It sounds little."

Riva could not tell her not to say that. She could not bear to say yes. She settled for the only honest thing she had.

"Then we don't feed it another child."

Nami held her gaze a moment longer, then nodded. It was not obedience. It was worse. It was understanding.

Riva took the key and stepped to the wheel housing while the breach breathed under all of them, hungry and listening.

***

Lale had spent half her life believing that if she could name every object in a room and put each into its proper relation to the next, panic would have to pass around her like floodwater around a tied post. It had not worked at Sereya's burial. It had not worked when she tore Jun's page from the ledger and folded the lie flat enough to hide. It was not working now. But counting still gave the fear edges, and edges could be worked with.

She laid the tokens on the cracked altar slab in the order the new sequence required. Tovin's iron key first, wet and orange with fresh rust. Paska's corked vial next, tide ink thick as old blood inside it, with the copper marking needle wrapped in oilcloth. Enver's rope eye came third, a thumb-thick splice stiff with tar and salt and use. Bato set down the fourth himself: a lead diving weight flattened on one side from years of striking rock, a groove worn into it by his thumb. Last came the thing Daro did not want to relinquish, though he had not yet been forced to: a small bone pitch reed on a faded red hymn cord, polished by years at Ilyan Moor's mouth before it ever touched Daro's hand. He kept it looped around his wrist as if it were vein, not object.

The whole chapter of their mother's lovers reduced to items on stone. Lale might have admired the discipline of that, in another life.

Tovin braced herself at the wheel housing and counted the old mechanism's breath. She had found a strip of eel skin in her pocket and was chewing it slowly for the ache in some cracked back tooth. Every time she bit down, the muscle in her jaw ticked. Bato crouched by the breach, sorting loose iron pins and wedges into a neat line by size without seeming to know he was doing it. Years alone had made him orderly in strange places.

Outside, the sea struck the rocks and came back in through the grating below the altar, blackening the undersides of the stones. The dead had stopped circling quite so tightly now that Daro's solo had been broken, but they had not yet found direction. They knocked and turned and occasionally lifted pale faces through the bars as if listening through a floor.

Lale uncorked Paska's vial. The smell of oil, soot, and clove came up at once, carrying Marrow Market with it: hot planks, gossip, skin under needle. Her forearm tide marks answered with a low sting, black lines on her skin rising almost warm. Paska had laughed when she packed the vial in Lale's bag after the tattooing. For touch-up, she'd said. As if skin obeyed bookkeeping.

"Marks first after the key turns," Lale said.

"That's what was said," Daro answered from the chapel wall. He had planted Sereya's pole beside him and was sitting with his back to the salt-crusted stone, conserving himself in the most theatrical way available. "You don't need to repeat it like minutes from a council table."

"No," she said, "I need to repeat it because you think sequence is something that happens to other people."

Iven barked a soft laugh. He was on his knees by the west post with Enver's rope eye between his teeth, trying to work the splice onto the cut ring-bolt with one hand. Sweat stood on his upper lip. The bandage around his left hand had loosened enough for Lale to see the true shape beneath: two fingers bound crooked and flat, the whole hand swollen to a misshapen red-purple thing that looked less injured than half-finished. She made herself keep looking until pity lost its appetite.

"If you're both done flirting," he said around the rope, "this knot would appreciate a witness."

Riva took the key to the wheel housing and waited. She stood as she always did under pressure, feet set as if on deck, anger flattened into use. Salt had dried silver on the hairs of her forearms. The old towing scar under her chin was open again from where spray and grit had rubbed it raw.

"Tell me where to stop," she said.

Tovin spat the eel skin over the side and listened to the machinery. "First catch. Then hold. If you overshoot, the teeth bite wrong and we all get intimate with failure."

Riva nodded and set the key.

Lale stepped down from the altar with the ink vial in one hand and the copper needle in the other. The floor of the ruined chapel was half stone, half rot-soft boards where old pilgrims had slept under Sereya's relic banners. Salt had climbed the walls in fanned white veins. She used the needle to scratch five short guide lines into the crust on the floor, running from the altar step toward the breach, then inked over them with her thumb. The marks darkened greedily. The five lines stayed separate at the heel, converged at the mouth, and split outward toward sea. They let no single body lead and no single name govern a crossing made from multiple strains held in ugly relation.

"Stand on the black when it's your turn," she said without looking up. "If you step off, start again."

"Who made you treasurer of damnation?" Iven asked.

"Practice."

That would have been enough to get a smaller laugh in a less murderous room. Here it only sharpened attention. That suited her. She wanted attention sharp.

Tovin lifted two fingers. "Now."

Riva turned the key. The first movement did nothing. The second forced a complaint out of the buried teeth. On the third, the whole chapel seemed to take the effort into itself. Stone trembled. Below, the dead jerked as one loose wire of current opened under them.

"Hold," Tovin snapped.

Riva held.

"Truth with it," Bato said.

No one moved. Lale could feel the room looking for some other sacrifice, something more picturesque than admission.

Riva's jaw set. She kept both hands on the key while she spoke, shoulders hunched into the turn.

"She taught me a child could wait," she said. The words came flat at first, as if she meant to get through them like a repair list. Then the flatness broke on one hard edge. "If the work mattered enough, if the water was bad enough, if the thing in front of me was breaking bad enough, the child could wait. I used that. I left Nami with whoever'd feed her. I left her with people who liked the saint story better than they liked her. I called that earning. I called it duty because duty sounds cleaner than neglect."

Nami made a tiny inward sound. Riva did not turn toward it.

"There," Riva said. "Turn or don't."

The mechanism answered with a heavy drop. Somewhere under the altar, one of the sea teeth released. Black water sucked through the new gap with a throatier pull, no longer only upward.

Tovin nodded once. "Marks."

Lale stepped onto her own ink line and pressed her tide-marked forearm to the stone. The skin there burned. Paska had once told her that maps were only scars people agreed to read. Lale dragged the wet heel of her hand across the converging lines and made them darker, thicker, indisputable.

"She taught me smallness could be a tool," Lale said. Her voice came precise enough to cut. "If you look careful and useful and not worth fearing, people hand you ledgers. They let you stand in the room while truth is being made and forget to watch your hands. I liked that. I liked being the child nobody thought dangerous. It let me tear Jun from the record. It let me pretend erasure was tidying."

The last word almost failed her. She made it cross anyway.

The ink bit into the stone as if the chapel were thirsty for soot. The five lines on the floor shone black. Outside, a salt-fig sprout at the threshold bent low and stayed there, leaves trembling.

Lale stepped back.

Iven hauled himself upright with Enver's rope eye over one shoulder. He gave the chapel a grin so cracked and unwilling it barely counted as mischief.

"All right," he said. "If we're emptying pockets."

He set his right foot on the third ink line and shoved the rope eye through the cut ring-bolt beside the wheel housing. Doing it one-handed took brute ugliness. His bad hand flapped uselessly when he forgot and reached with it. The movement tore a sound out of him that was not a laugh and not quite a groan.

"Need help?" Riva asked.

"Need a better body. Work with what you've got."

He got the splice through at last and leaned his forehead against the wet post a moment before he spoke again. When he lifted it, the gold tooth flashed once in the lanternlight. Lale had not realized how much that flash had become part of the family's expectation of him until pain stripped everything else away and the tooth still made the room prepare for a joke.

"She taught me wanting can be worked like debt," he said. He kept his voice light for the first clause, then abandoned the attempt. "Smile right. Lean in. Let somebody think they're the one getting over on you. I've slept with people for passage, for coin, for shelter, for the pleasure of leaving before morning and calling that freedom. Half my charm is just theft with warm hands on it."

He pulled the rope through the ring until tar squealed.

"And I liked it," he added, glancing toward no one and everyone. "That's the rotten part. I liked being the kind of man who could turn hunger into credit."

The rope came taut. The ring-bolt shuddered. Below, something in the breach struck outward rather than in. One of the dead turned fully toward sea, head cocked as if hearing a call from farther off than Daro's reach.

"Weight next," Tovin said.

"Song first," Daro said at once.

All heads turned toward him. He had come off the wall without anyone noticing. The pole was in his hand again. The bone pitch reed flashed red against his wrist.

"The path needs naming now," he said. His voice had lost more of its lower body. When he forced it down, the sound frayed at the bottom. "If you wait too long, the mouth closes around the work and makes the labor pointless."

"That's one theory," Iven said. Sweat ran from his chin. "Any others, saint?"

Daro ignored him. He looked only at Lale, because he knew she had the sequence in her head and wanted to move it by force of attention.

"You always did mistake order for truth," he said.

"And you always mistook attention for instruction."

His mouth twitched. "You practice those in a mirror?"

She had one plain line waiting. She gave it to him clean.

"Jun already died for your solo."

The chapel went silent around that. Even the sea seemed to pause long enough to listen to the damage settle where it had landed.

Daro looked as if she had struck him open-palmed. Then the look hardened into something meaner because shame in him always preferred costume to nakedness.

"Fine," he said. "Do your weight."

Nami had gone still at the threshold. She was staring into the breach with one hand buried in the pocket of her skirt. Bato turned his head toward her, not toward Daro. He saw it too, whatever she was holding. Lale felt the room slide subtly toward the youngest, toward the dangerous old Reedmouth wish that the child nearest wonder might also be the child nearest sacrifice.

Lale corked Paska's vial with her teeth and wiped ink across the backs of her knuckles.

"Nami," she said. "Here."

Nami did not move for three beats. Then she stepped onto the fourth line, Bato's lead weight in both hands, and the whole chapel breathed in with her.

***

The lead weight was colder than the water had any right to be. Nami held it against her stomach and felt the old dents in it through her wet dress. One edge had been worn smooth by years of thumb and current. Another still had a sharp lip where rock had bitten it. Bato had pressed it into her hands without a speech.

"You don't throw yourself," he'd said. "You throw this."

That should have been obvious. It had not been.

The breach below the altar bars was black in layers. Sea on top, river under it, silt moving through both like smoke in lampglass. The dead turned in those layers slowly now, not trapped but not released either, the way fish might hold under a pier waiting for scraps. One woman's hair spread around her face like wet reeds. A man in pilgrim cloth had lost part of one cheek and the seed in his mouth gleamed pale as gristle. Every time the current shifted, the little cough came up through the stone.

Jun, the room wanted it to be. Jun, her own blood wanted. It made her ache in stupid ancient places.

She kept her right hand in her skirt pocket because the seed she had hidden there all these chapters seemed suddenly alive enough to bite. She had pocketed it on the first night at the ferry house, small and slick with drowned spit, because she had wanted proof that the river had its eye on her in particular. She had wanted something that belonged only to her, not to the louder siblings, not to the dead saint, not to the flood. She had wanted the frightening part. That was the truth underneath the nicer versions. She had wanted to be touched by danger if danger meant being chosen out of the blur.

The seed had dried over time and gone hard. She had carried it anyway, wrapped in a square of old apron cloth, warm from her body. More than once she had taken it out to look at it in private and felt ashamed by the relief it gave her. The others had jobs, skills, records, debts. She had omens and a pocket secret. It had felt like a form of wealth.

Now it felt childish and lethal and still not easy to relinquish.

The pilgrims behind her were whispering. She could hear the shape of the question without words: who goes in. Reedmouth had been trained too long to expect a body at the hinge, some beloved ruin offered up to make meaning legible. Even after Bato said no, and even after Riva said no, people wanted one life to stand where their own fear would otherwise have to go.

Nami knew she had wanted that too. Not in those words, but in the softer words that hid the wound. She wanted to matter. She wanted to be answered. She wanted to stop feeling like the extra child added after the fact, the river's chosen little tide. It was ridiculous, and it was not false.

Riva came to stand at her left shoulder. Salt had bleached the ends of Riva's lashes white.

"You don't have to look at them," Riva said quietly.

"I know."

"Do you?"

Nami almost laughed. That was such a Riva question. Sharp enough to be concern, concern flattened until it could pass as instruction. She leaned into the weight instead of into her sister.

"They keep looking back," she said.

Riva did not deny it. "Then look harder."

Bato moved to Nami's other side. Up close, she saw that the cuff of his shirt had been mended with blue sail thread not matching anything else on him, a careful ugly repair. He nodded once toward her pocket.

"That's the other thing, isn't it," he said.

She took her hand out slowly. The little cloth packet sat in her palm dark with damp. Everyone in the chapel saw it at once because secrets made their own light.

Iven muttered, "Well, fuck."

Lale shut her eyes one brief second, not from surprise but from the pain of being proved right about the family having one more piece of rot still tucked away.

Daro's stare hit her like a hand between the shoulder blades. It was not accusatory. It was worse than that. It was hungry. He had always recognized in her the wish that nearly matched his own.

Nami unwrapped the cloth. The seed lay in the middle of it, hard and black and shaped a little like a heart if a heart were an item dug from mud by someone who didn't believe in romance. The sight of it made her throat feel lined with reeds.

"I kept it," she said.

The words shook, so she started again. "I kept it from the first night. I told myself I wanted proof, or insurance, or something to show me later that all this had happened for real. That's not why." She looked at the dead below because she could not say the next part to living eyes first. "I kept it because I wanted the water to want me back."

No one interrupted, and that was good. Let them hear the ugly clean.

"I wanted to be the one it picked," she said. "Even after Bato told me there wasn't any chosen body. Even after I saw what the seeds do. I wanted it anyway because being chosen felt easier than being one more pair of hands. If the river had called and the calling meant I drowned, some part of me still thought that would count as meaning."

Her fingers tightened around the seed until its ridges bit.

"That's what she left in me," Nami said. "The wish to be chosen even if the choosing kills me."

The cough came again under the stone, louder now, or maybe she had simply lined herself up to hear it fully. It did not sound like invitation anymore. It sounded like the moment before drowning, small and panicked and unsacred. Something in her lurched away from it.

Bato opened his hand. "Tie it on."

There was a short length of waxed twine wound around the lead weight's neck, old and stiff. Nami threaded the cloth square through it with fingers that would not stay steady. She bound the hidden seed to Bato's diving weight until the knot sat ugly and firm.

"Good," Bato said, and that simple word nearly broke her because there was no wonder in it at all, only approval for a task done correctly.

Tovin called from the wheel, "Set the line."

Nami stepped onto the fourth ink mark and lowered the weight through the altar bars on the rope Bato fed her. The pull took at once. Not enough to wrench her in, because Riva had one hand locked around her belt and Bato the other around the line above hers, but enough to show its appetite. The lead struck stone below with a sound she felt in her teeth.

"Now say the rest," Bato told her.

She had thought she already had. But there was another truth under the first, meaner for being ordinary.

"I liked thinking I'd be missed differently than the others," she said. Tears came then, hot and humiliating in the salt air. She let them. "I liked thinking if I vanished, people would finally understand I wasn't the little spare one. I wanted my death to prove my value. That's foul. That's still true enough to shame me."

The sea road answered.

The weight dropped another handspan though she had not given rope. Somewhere under the altar the current found the line and began to draw outward. Three of the dead turned seaward at once. The woman with the reed-hair bumped the grating, opened her mouth wider, and the seed lodged there shifted, loosened, and fell into the black with a tiny pale flick.

At the threshold the rooted salt-fig sprout shivered hard and went slack against the stone.

The pilgrims made a sound of collective disgust and relief, as if the chapter had just informed them that no one would die beautifully for them and they would have to stand there anyway.

Daro pushed off the wall.

Nami looked at him and saw, with a clarity that hurt, how much he had built of himself around rooms leaning forward to hear what he would say next. She loved him. She wanted to slap him. She wanted to drag him into the black and wash the vanity out. None of that mattered. His turn had arrived.

He came to the altar carrying the pole in one hand and the pitch reed in the other. His face had changed again since Riva tackled him. The fury had not gone. It had gone deeper, where it had to sit beside knowledge.

"If I do this," he said to no one person, "it won't be mine after."

Riva's grip tightened once on Nami's belt, then released. "That was the idea."

Daro gave a short, raw laugh. "Of course it was."

He stepped onto the last black mark, and the whole chapel leaned with him.

***

The first thing Daro noticed was not the pain in his throat. It was the loss of audience as a simple thing.

The room was still looking at him. He was not invisible. But the stare had changed species. Nobody wanted him to rescue them now in the clean old way, with one beautiful mouth and everybody else absolved into listening. They wanted him to join the machinery. They wanted his gift broken into labor and spent there. Some part of him recoiled from that with such purity it almost made him laugh.

There it was: the rot. It was not holy hunger or the river's mark. It was the ordinary ugly need to be singular in a family built from fracture and rumor.

He rolled Ilyan's pitch reed between finger and thumb. The bone had been smoothed by two lives of saliva and breath before it ever reached his hand. Ilyan had pressed it on him after Hymn Cut in that dark little shrine room full of bells and damp reed mats, saying, Keep your pitch when the room wants to own it. Daro had heard a father giving inheritance and had nearly wept from the greed of it.

Now he could feel where his own teeth had begun to notch the edge over the past weeks.

The altar smelled of rust, clove ink, tar, old prayer wax, salt, and the faint sweet-sour rot of the dead below. The combination was obscene enough to be honest. Riva stood at the wheel with Tovin's key in both hands, forearms knotted. Lale held her marked arm over the ink lines as if keeping a ledger open by force. Iven had Enver's rope wrapped across his back and good shoulder, right hand white on the strain. His left hung bandaged and swollen against his ribs, fingers made abstract by damage. Nami and Bato managed the weight line together. Tovin counted under her breath. Bato watched the water and never once Daro's face.

No one kneels, Daro thought, and was startled to find relief in it.

He lifted the pitch reed.

"You want the rot," he said. His voice frayed at the edges, still trying to gather itself into silk and failing. Good, he thought. Let it fail. "Fine. She taught me to hear a room lean in and call that blessing. She taught me to mistake attention for weather and weather for fate. I wanted one body, one answer, one heir, and I wanted it to be me so badly I'd almost made a grave of the rest of you."

The words came easier once the first cut was made.

"I told myself I was protecting the route from stupidity. I told myself the river had use for a true singer and not for all this tugging and cursing and accounting. That was vanity dressed for shrine work. That's what she left in me. Hunger for exception. Hunger so ugly I'd make doctrine out of my own need if you let me."

He looked at Riva then because she had broken his song and made this possible and he hated that gratitude lived anywhere in him at all. "You were right to stop me."

Riva's face did not soften. But something in her shoulders did. "Then finish it right."

He nodded once. That was enough sentiment for a flood crest.

Daro looped the hymn cord off his wrist and threaded the pitch reed through the iron ring beside Enver's rope eye, where it knocked once against metal. Then, before he could change his mind, he brought the bone down under his heel and cracked it. The sound was small. It still went through him like a snapped rib.

Tovin looked up sharply. "Sing on the count. Not before."

"I know when to come in," Daro said automatically.

"Apparently you don't."

That almost got a real laugh from Iven.

The chapel waited. The breach breathed in and out below them, deeper now, directional but not yet clean. The dead gathered at the mouth of the road they had been denied for weeks. Some seemed to strain toward it. Others still turned stupidly in place. A child-sized bundle brushed the outer grating and vanished before Daro could decide whether he had truly seen it or only wanted punishment shaped that way.

Tovin counted louder. "On six. Riva turns. Iven takes strain. Lale holds the marks. Nami, you give line when Bato says. Daro, you don't climb over any of them. You ride what they've built."

"What a romantic instruction," Iven muttered.

"Shut up and lean."

Daro set his palm on Sereya's brass ferrule where the pole crossed the altar rail. The metal was slick with every one of their hands by now. That pleased him strangely. Let her relic lose its private polish.

He knew exactly how to open his throat for the old kind of song, the shrine kind, the honeyed summoning that made listeners straighten and feel selected by proximity. He did not do that. Instead he pulled air low into the bruised bottom of himself and gave the first note in the plain workman's ferry-call Ilyan had once told him never to waste in a shrine because it lacked grandeur. It was a carrying note and a hauling note, the kind used to keep bodies in time across dangerous water.

It scraped coming out. The scrape was right.

Tovin shouted, "One."

Riva turned the key. Iron ground under stone.

Daro held the note and fed it not upward but outward, across the ink lines, along the rope, down the weight line, through the opened tooth below. He could feel when the others caught it because the sound stopped belonging wholly to his own skull. Iven grunted on the strain at the same beat. Lale's breath came in measured cuts. Nami answered the rhythm with the rope slipping over wet stone one span at a time. Even Riva's turn had a pulse.

"Two."

The key moved. The rope tightened. Water struck the underside of the altar and went seaward instead of up.

Daro changed pitch, lower and rougher, following what Bato's body showed instead of what his own vanity preferred. Bato had leaned half an inch toward the outer current. So that was where the road wanted naming. Daro bent the note there.

Pain ripped bright along his throat. He ignored it.

"Three."

One of the dead hit the outward flow and was taken. The body was not dragged under. It was taken. The body straightened, cloth flattening to its shape, and slid through the black gap as if some clerk of the sea had finally stamped the correct paper. Two more followed. Their mouths opened on nothing. Pale seeds clicked loose against stone.

The pilgrims cried out. Someone started praying to Sereya and Tovin snarled, "Not tonight," without even looking back.

"Four."

Daro had to climb for the next pitch. The climb tore him. He tasted blood at once, fresh and salt-thin. Still he kept the line simple, no ornament, no shrine turns, no coaxing the room to adore the sound for its own sake. A call for work. A call for passage. He hated how much discipline that took. He hated how much he had mistaken difficulty for sanctity all these years.

Nami gave another length of line. Bato braced and dropped the weight deeper. Outside, more of the rooted salt-fig shoots collapsed against the forecourt stones, their leaves wrinkling in on themselves as if heat had finally reached them.

"Five."

The current struck full. Iven nearly lost his footing. His bad hand hit the post and he made a raw animal sound that sliced straight through Daro's note. Daro did not try to smooth around it. He opened the song wide enough to take the sound in. Let pain be part of the structure. Let ugliness hold weight.

Riva looked at him then, quick and startled, because she heard what he was doing.

Good, he thought savagely. Let her hear it.

He shifted the call again, not into beauty but into room for reply, and without planning it the others answered. They did not answer with words or any neat ritual phrase. Riva gave a grunt on the turn. Lale gave counted breath. Iven gave a curse. Nami gave a sharp "there." Even Tovin gave a barked "hold." Human sounds, timed. Labor noises becoming line. The breach took those more willingly than it had ever taken his polished hymn.

"Six."

Daro went for the top note because the road beyond the mouth had finally opened enough to require distance named, and because there was no safe way through this last part. He knew it as clearly as he knew the shape of his own tongue. To reach the sea lane he had to put more force through the ruined throat than it could survive.

He did it anyway.

The note came out bright enough to hurt the air and then immediately tore. This was not metaphor. It was a tear, a sudden inner rip like wet cloth yanked on a nail. Heat flooded his neck. The sound did not vanish. It split into two ruined edges, breath and shred and blood, and he forced both halves forward with everything left in his chest.

Salt water surged through the opened teeth.

The dead began to move in earnest.

They did not move one by one. They moved in a line. That was the mercy and the horror of it. Men and women and children in prayer cloth, fisher trousers, market shirts, naked burial wraps, hair fanned loose, limbs swollen or gnawed or neat with recent death, all taking the sea road at last. The outward current caught them and made order where there had only been return. Seeds fell from their mouths into black water and lost whatever pressure had made them monstrous. They knocked harmlessly against stone and rode out.

Daro kept singing long past the point at which sense told him to stop because stopping too early had nearly killed them once already tonight. His throat flooded warm. Blood ran down between his collarbones. The pole shook under all their hands. He could not tell whether the wet on his face was spray or tears or only the ordinary waste of a body under strain.

Then, through the roar and the work-call and the outward drag, he heard the small cough one final time.

The sound was not trapped. It was leaving.

The sound slid past the opened teeth and was gone into sea noise.

Daro broke.

The last of the note came out as a mangled bark. Air followed, then nothing shaped enough to count as voice. He tried once more on instinct and got only a scraped whisper that hurt worse for its emptiness. His knees buckled. The pole would have taken him down into the altar rail if Riva had not let go of the key with one hand and caught the back of his neck hard enough to bruise.

The chapel did not collapse. Storm Mouth did not seize another body in payment. The mechanism settled into its new relation with a long descending groan. Beyond the rocks, the black lane to sea held.

More dead passed through it, steady now, no longer turning back.

Iven slumped against the post laughing and crying without enough breath to manage either properly. Lale put both ink-black hands over her face for one beat, then dragged them down and looked out as if she meant to memorize every departing body for the ledger she had once mangled. Nami sagged back against Bato, rope burns livid in her palms, and stared at the little dead road opening wider and wider as if ordinary passage were the strangest miracle she had ever seen. Tovin, because she was Tovin, reset the key a quarter turn and checked the bite of the teeth before allowing herself any expression at all.

Riva kept her grip on Daro's neck until she was sure he would not pitch forward.

"Breathe," she said.

He tried to answer and produced only a wet rasp.

Riva's face changed, not by much but enough.

"All right," she said. "Don't."

He put a hand to his throat. The skin there felt swollen and wrong, a stranger's damage sitting where his livelihood had lived. Panic flashed up pure and white. Funeral singer, shrine heir, mouth in rooms: all the versions of himself that began in sound were not gone exactly, but they were broken beyond the shape he had known. The knowledge was so large it could not yet be grieved cleanly.

Bato stepped up beside them. His mended blue cuff was dark with spray. He watched the sea road, not Daro, when he spoke.

"You kept your life," he said.

Daro almost laughed at the blunt poverty of consolation. He could not. Air hissed painfully between his teeth instead.

Below, on the threshold stones, the last standing salt-fig shoots folded in on themselves and lay slack. Seeds that had lodged in cracks all along the forecourt had gone dull. One split under the heel of Tovin's boot with the soft stupid sound of an ordinary rotten fig.

Pilgrims were weeping now, but not in unison. Some cried from relief, some from the loss of a doctrine they had preferred, some because the dead were finally leaving and grief needed movement after weeks of backing up. Nobody looked at Daro like a saint. They looked at the opened road. They looked at the work. They looked at one another.

It should have wounded him more than it did.

Maybe the wound was simply elsewhere.

The sea pulled. The dead went with it. The chapel stank of rust, blood, salt, and used effort. Daro stood in the middle of his siblings' ruin and felt the clean ordinary fact of shared burden settle where chosenness had been pretending to live.

When he tried his voice again, he got only breath and pain.

The road held anyway.
