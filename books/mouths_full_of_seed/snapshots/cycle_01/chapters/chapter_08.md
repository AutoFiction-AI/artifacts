# Chapter 8

By late afternoon Inkwater had the sound of a blocked throat. The third cut had opened enough to take some of the dead, not enough to trust. Water ran seaward under the black roots and then shuddered back against the Bitter Thread's hull, as if the channel kept changing its mind. Every plank under Riva's boots answered with a different complaint: winch chain clicking, wet timber knocking, the low suction moan from the half-cleared mouth ahead.

Lale stood on the forward deck with her left sleeve rolled high, Paska's new tide marks slick on her forearm. The fresh ink made her look wounded from wrist to elbow. She kept turning that arm toward the light and then toward the water, matching the lines to the spin and drag below them as though she hated the fact that skin could know anything before a chart did.

"Two feet right," she said. "No, not there. There's a fold in it."

Riva shoved her pole down and felt for bottom. The river gave her soft mud first, then nothing, then the hard strike of buried timber. "Iven, take the stern."

"I've got the stern," Iven called back. He had his shirt off and his hair tied with somebody's old prayer ribbon, already filthy with black spray. "What I don't have is a polite current."

"You never do."

Nami was knee-deep at the bow beside a heap of uprooted reed and market trash, working a hook under the snag that still kept the cut from settling. She should have been on deck. She had been on deck two breaths earlier. Riva had turned to check the stern line and turned back to find her in the water again, skinny shoulders set, face bright with the kind of usefulness that always looked one insult away from tears.

"Hook, not fingers," Riva said.

"I'm using the hook."

"Then use more of it."

Nami gave her a look that belonged to a much younger girl and a much older one at once. She planted her bare feet deeper in the mud and leaned in. The snag shifted. A slick raft of reed, eelweed, and a cracked chicken crate rolled over. Beneath it, something pale flashed under the black water.

Nami hissed between her teeth.

Riva saw the blood before she saw the thing that cut her: a rib of reed shaved thin and hard as bottle glass, sawing up out of the bundle. It had laid Nami's palm open from the heel of the thumb nearly to the little finger. The flood took the first bright spill at once and spun it into pink ribbons around her wrist.

"Out," Riva said.

"It's not bad."

"Out."

Riva caught her by the elbow and hauled her onto the bow. Nami came up cursing, water streaming off her skirt. The cut gaped clean and white for a second before the blood welled again. Lale was beside them at once with a strip torn from the lining of her own overskirt. She wrapped tight, neat, unsentimental.

"You found reed glass," Lale said. "Congratulations."

"I said it wasn't bad."

"That's because the blood is still in you."

Riva took the hook from Nami and went back into the water herself. The reed glass scraped her boot sole. She worked the snag loose in three hard jerks and dragged it toward the deck while Iven swore at the stern and Daro, farther up the bank, listened with that tilted-head stillness he got when the river was doing something only he believed was language.

He turned before he spoke. "Riva."

She knew that tone. It was not his shrine voice or his room-holding voice. It was the close one, the one that made even bad news sound as if he was leaning to tell it only to her.

"What."

Daro was staring past her toward the market path. Rain had stopped an hour earlier, but everything still dripped. The stilt houses beyond the reeds looked washed and flimsy, laundry sagging between them, little cooking fires making threads of gray smoke. A man came running down the path in one boot and one kitchen sock, still carrying the long spoon he had been using to stir something. He was shouting before Riva could make out what had put that stripped-animal terror in his face.

"Solla. Solla, wake up. Solla, don't go in there."

The woman ahead of him never looked back.

Riva knew her by sight if not by name: the net-mender with three brass thimbles on a cord at her throat, always chewing fennel seeds when she worked. She stepped down the bank in her house apron as if she were walking to wash a bowl. Her eyes were open a sliver. Her mouth hung slack. Black water took her ankles, then her shins. She kept going.

The man splashed after her and nearly fell.

"Line," Riva snapped.

Iven had already flung one. She caught it, looped it once around a piling, and went in. Inkwater hit hotter than it should have, rotten-sweet from the seeds that had rooted after the corpse jam at Marrow Market. Riva felt pods, weed, and the burst skins of salt figs underfoot. Solla walked another step and the water took her to the knees, then the thighs. She never woke.

"Solla hates mud," the man yelled, as if that fact alone should have broken whatever had her. "She won't even wash eels in brown water. Solla."

Riva reached her just as the first reverse pull caught. The half-open cut wanted bodies. It was no cleaner about the living than the dead. Solla's weight changed under Riva's hands the instant the current found her. She leaned forward with dreamy stubbornness, trying to keep walking toward the black notch under the roots.

Riva hooked an arm across her chest and dragged. Solla fought without waking, not violently, just with the blind forward labor of someone moving a full basket uphill. The man came stumbling in to help and nearly got both of them rolled.

"Back," Riva barked. "You want her, stay on the rope."

He obeyed because fear had beaten pride out of him. He braced on the line with both hands, spoon still tucked into his belt like he had forgotten it was there. Between them they got Solla turned. When her face came out of the water she coughed once, hard, and the smell off her mouth was wrong. It was not river rot. It was sweet and overripe, like fruit left split in a shrine bowl.

Riva dragged her to the bank and slapped her, not hard enough to punish, hard enough to call.

Solla's eyes flew wide. She gagged and clutched at Riva's sleeve. "I was in bed."

"No," said her husband. He dropped to his knees beside her, one sock soaking black. "No, you were shelling beans, and then you stood up, and then you came out here like you couldn't hear me."

Solla looked past him to the water, already bewildered, already ashamed. That look chilled Riva more than the current had. Dead bodies were a route problem. You pulled them, poled them, cut them loose, sent them where they were supposed to go. The living came with mouths and kin and rent and promises waiting back in the houses.

Another figure was moving down the path.

Then another followed.

An old man with his suspenders unhooked. A barefoot girl carrying a doll by one leg. A fish seller Riva remembered for the green scarf he wore even in heat, walking straight toward the cut as if summoned to shift a load no one else could manage.

The man with the spoon saw them too. He made a sound that had no words in it.

Riva looked at the black shoots sprouting around the pilings and understood exactly what their delay had bought the town. The rooted seeds had had days to feed under the market houses. All that backward water from the half-open cut, all those corpses jammed in the stalls and prayer boats because they had not settled the route fast enough, and now the damage had climbed up into breathing people.

The stretch of shame had ended. This was where the river started collecting the living.

* * *

By full dark the market path had turned into a bad dream that kept sending up more bodies with warm skin.

Riva tied the Bitter Thread broadside against a row of storm-scarred pilings because there was nowhere better to keep her. If she let the dredge swing free, the half-open pull would take the hull sideways into the root mouth and crack her apart. If she kept all hands on the boat, the walkers would keep stepping past the pilings and into black water nobody could read. She had spent most of her life choosing which piece of a bad situation got to break first. This was worse. Rope and timber did not have mothers screaming their names on the bank.

Lanterns were coming out all along Marrow Market. Families who had laughed at shrine talk yesterday were tying ropes around waists tonight. The banks were noise now: crying children, wet slaps of feet on planks, Daro's half-heard muttering as he listened for whatever note kept drawing the walkers down the paths.

Lale had turned Paska's forearm map into orders. "Not that side," she called from the forward rail. "The water folds under there. Bring them toward the dye posts. Iven, if you lose that skiff I will kill you after the flood."

"Get in line," Iven said, shoving the little market skiff off again with a pole. He had two boys in it already, both half asleep and weeping because they had been hauled out by the backs of their shirts before they could reach the water. "You people get very managerial when I'm doing well."

Riva was hauling a cooper out of the reeds when Nami splashed up on the other side of him, bandage already dark through. She had ignored three direct orders in as many minutes and now had both arms around the man's middle, trying to help lift him while the current sucked at his legs.

"Get back on deck," Riva said.

"He was going under."

"So will you."

"Then move faster."

That was the wrong thing to say to Riva in the middle of work. It was also exactly the thing Sereya would have said, bright-eyed and ugly with certainty. Riva shoved the cooper into Iven's skiff, saw Nami flinch as the cut in her hand pulled open again, and had to bite down on the first answer that came to her.

More walkers were coming. They were not in a rush. They came in a line.

They stepped between people grabbing for them. They climbed over stacked baskets. One woman came still holding the eel knife she had been cleaning for supper, her thumb laid careful along the flat of the blade so she would not cut herself. A boy of perhaps ten walked barefoot through a spill of broken crockery and never once looked at his bleeding soles. Every face wore the same emptied patience.

Daro climbed onto the capstan housing and shut his eyes.

"What are they hearing?" Riva called.

He did not answer at once. Rain ran off his chin. His mouth moved a little, as if he were testing some note against his teeth. "Not a voice," he said. "A pull. A promise. It's low."

"Can you break it?"

He opened his eyes. "What did you just ask me?"

Riva pointed at the bank, at the line of sleepers edging toward the cut. "Can you break it?"

He laughed once. It had no humor in it at all. "You make it sound like prying rust off a hinge."

"If it saves them, then that's what it becomes."

"So that's me to you." Daro put one hand to his throat. "A hinge. A bell. A thing you hit when you need people to move."

Another splash cut through whatever else he meant to say. The boy with the broken soles had walked off the edge of a loading plank and vanished up to the neck.

Riva did not let herself look at Daro again. "Get on the bow and sing them sideways."

For a second she thought he might refuse. His face went flat in a way that meant the hurt had made it past language and into something meaner. Then he came off the capstan in one fluid step, landed barefoot on the wet deck, and shoved past her.

"If I do this," he said, so close that only she could hear it, "you don't get to pretend it's ropework."

"Fine. Do it anyway."

He went to the bow.

Riva had heard Daro sing in shrines, in taverns, over bodies zipped into reed mats, over bodies too poor for mats at all. He always sounded as if the air wanted him in it. This was different. He did not sing out to be admired. He leaned forward and gave the cut a low, steady line that sat just above speaking, just below chant, a sound made to catch in the chest rather than the ear.

Come dry, it said without saying those words. Come this way. Come to boards. Come to hands.

The first walkers turned their heads.

Riva saw it travel through them like a change in weather. Shoulders shifted. Feet dragged away from the mouth of the cut and toward the dredge instead. The boy in the water stopped pushing forward and reached, not for the black roots now, but for the sound. Iven got him by the collar and he came up choking.

There was nothing holy about the next quarter hour. There was only labor made possible by Daro's throat. Riva and Iven ferried bodies from path to deck and deck to bank. Lale shouted safe footing from the rail and wrapped rope around waist after waist. Market wives slapped husbands awake. A grandmother with rice flour still white on her forearms bit her own son's shoulder hard enough to make him cry because it was the only thing that turned him. Somebody vomited into the bilge. Somebody else pissed themselves in terror and apologised while Riva dragged them by both wrists onto the planks.

All the while Daro stood on the bow and fed the sleepers that narrow line of music.

He changed it as the pull changed. He made it softer when a child came near and rougher when the cut sucked hard. Once he broke it into something almost playful to coax a girl of fifteen off a piling she had climbed like a bird to get closer to the water. Riva could feel the cost of it in him even before his voice frayed. Each shift took more. Sweat pasted his shirt to the notch of his throat. His hands trembled between phrases. He looked furious and beautiful and used.

Nami kept working until she nearly dropped.

Riva caught it only because Nami went suddenly slow, the way a lantern goes dim before the oil is gone. She was on the deck beside a rescued woman, trying to retie the bandage around her own hand with her teeth because the first wrap had bled through again. When she rose, the deck tipped under her in a way that had nothing to do with water.

Riva was across to her in two strides. She took the hand and unwrapped enough cloth to see the wound.

The clean cut had turned ugly fast. Black grit sat in it. The flesh around it had gone puffed and pale from soaking, but the center ran raw and red. There were tiny reed splinters still buried under the skin like clear fish bones.

"Damn it, Nami."

Nami pulled once, offended by the grip, too weak to get free. "I said I was fine."

"You said a stupid thing with your whole mouth."

Nami tried to answer, but her eyes slipped past Riva's shoulder, unfocused. She was shivering in hot dark air. When Riva put the back of her knuckles to Nami's cheek, the heat there felt obscene, like touching iron left too near a forge.

Daro's line broke.

Three walkers at once swerved toward the cut.

"Riva," Lale snapped.

She had to choose again. The market path. The boat. Nami. The half-open channel grinding at the hull. Behind her Daro sucked in breath to start the song again, and the sound of it made Riva feel something low and sour in her gut because it worked and because she had asked for it like a woman asking for a hammer.

She shoved Nami down onto a coil of wet line. "Stay there."

"Don't order me like a sack."

"Then stop falling over like one."

Nami's face crumpled with rage and humiliation. Riva had no room to tend either. She turned back to the rail, back to the rescue, back to the work Daro's voice made possible, while the skin between Nami's eyebrows shone with fever and the river kept pulling at all of them like it had finally found the softest seam in the family.

When the last of the visible walkers had been dragged back to houses and tied to pilings, shutters, table legs, whatever their kin could hold onto, Daro let the song die. Silence hit the deck hard. It left everyone's breathing sounding crude.

He came off the bow unsteady. "There," he said, his voice roughened down to bare grain. "Was that enough captaincy for you."

Riva wiped river filth off her forearm with the heel of her hand. "It kept them alive."

"That wasn't what I asked."

The answer in her wanted to come out sharp. She saw Nami on the coil, face white except for the fever, and forced the answer into something useful instead. "You want me to thank you or argue with you. I don't have time for either."

Daro smiled with only one side of his mouth. "That's the prayer, isn't it. If you can't love us properly, at least put us to work."

Lale looked up from rewinding a line, eyes tired and hard. "If either of you turns this into a private grievance right now, I will throw you both in."

Iven snorted once, then saw Nami and stopped.

The whole deck smelled of river mud, fear sweat, and the sugary rot of rooted seeds warming in the pilings. The cut was still not settled. The living had started walking. Nami was burning up.

And they had not yet moved an inch farther downstream.

* * *

Nami told them three times she could still stand, which was how they all knew she could not.

They had pushed the Bitter Thread loose from Marrow Market as soon as the families there had enough rope and bodies to hold their own kin through the night. Riva would not say they were leaving. She said they were taking the barge where it could do more good, which meant the same thing and worse. The market lanterns had thinned behind them to a smudge of amber in the wet dark. Ahead there was only the narrow run toward Lantern Quay and the black shine of flooded reeds.

Lale cleaned the wound with boiled spirits from the medicine tin and a strip of old sailcloth. Nami endured it because she had to. The spirits bit so hard her teeth rang. The reed splinters came out one by one under Lale's needle, each one a clear sliver no bigger than fish bones. Nami watched them land in the saucer and wanted absurdly to laugh. They were such tiny things, and they made such a mess.

"Don't make that face," Lale said.

"What face."

"The one where you're about to call pain interesting."

Nami did laugh then, just enough to prove she was not drifting. "It isn't interesting. It stinks."

"Good. Stay with that."

Riva had given her a blanket even though the night was hot. The blanket smelled of diesel and old rain. Daro sat not far off with his throat wrapped in a strip of dry cloth, humming once in a while under his breath as if he was checking whether the voice was still there. Iven poled at the stern. Every time the hull kissed a hidden post he swore at it with weary affection.

Nami rested her bandaged hand on her belly and felt the secret seed in her skirt pocket press against her hip.

She had forgotten it for whole stretches of the journey. Then it would return to her all at once, its hard little weight, and she would remember why she had taken it from the ferry house floor that first night. She had not taken it to hurt anyone or even to keep it, only to know whether the river had spoken to her specifically or whether she had been one more fool standing under a weather sign and calling it fate.

Tonight the seed felt warm.

She did not know if that was fever or the thing itself.

The banks had gone strange in the dark. Trees stooped lower over the water here, roots exposed by flood and twisted together in thick black knots. The lantern Riva had hung by the bow cast a yellow skin over them and then lost nerve. Everything beyond that skim of light looked half grown, half drowned. Nami kept smelling things that were not there: milk turned in a bowl, fennel crushed under a heel, the wet scalp smell of a baby lifted out of sleep.

She put her good hand against the deck to steady herself. The planks pulsed under her with the work of the hull. Somewhere ahead a night bird made a sound like a hinge needing oil. Somewhere under that came another sound, so small she thought at first it was the fever in her ear.

It was a thin cry.

Nami raised her head.

"Did you hear that?"

Iven did not turn. "If it's the boat complaining, get in line."

"No." She licked cracked lips. "A baby."

No one answered. The lantern swung. Light moved across the roots and pulled back again.

The cry came once more, not louder, only nearer. Nami pushed the blanket off and got to her knees. The deck tipped under her, but the sound kept tugging. It was not coming from the houses. There were no houses here. It was coming from the bank itself, from the tangle where the flood had bitten the earth open and left the roots naked.

"Nami," Daro said softly.

She ignored him. The world had narrowed to one patch of black water slipping under a cage of roots. Something pale rocked there. For a moment it was only moon glare. Then the lantern came round again and the thing became a child.

He was curled on his side in the root cradle as if laid there carefully. He was infant small, not yet walking age. Black hair plastered over the skull in wet ribbons. A rash of pale blisters marbled the chest. One foot, absurdly sturdy for such a little body, was wedged against the root wall as though he had tried to push away and never finished. The roots held him under the water but did not let him drift. They had grown around him or he had grown into them. Nami could not tell which.

His mouth was open.

At first she thought it was full of mud. Then the lantern slid across and she saw the dark oval lodged there, snug as a tongue, the seed swollen and split, pale rootlets pressing the gums apart.

Sixth mouth, something in her mind said, not in her own voice but in the voice memory used when it dragged up words heard once and never understood.

The roots around the baby's head looked like lips. They flexed with the water. When the current breathed in, the whole bank opened.

Nami was on her feet before she knew she had moved.

"Wait," Daro said.

"He's there."

She stepped toward the rail. The deck lurched. The fever made the distance between boat and bank look easy, as if one more stride would put her shin-deep in ordinary yard water. The child turned then, not much but enough for Nami to see the blind white of one eye under the water and the cheek flattened by roots.

He was not crying with his mouth. The cry was coming through the wood under her feet.

"Nami."

This time the voice was Riva's, sharp as a line snapping. Fingers closed in the back of Nami's skirt and yanked. She slammed backward into a chest and smelled rope, river, iron, the bitter skin smell that was always Riva after a day of hauling.

"Let me go," Nami said. Or thought she said. Her mouth was thick and clumsy. "There's a baby in there."

Riva's grip hardened. "There's roots."

"No. No, he has black hair."

Daro had come close enough now that his knee pressed the deck beside her. He looked where she was pointing and his face changed with a speed that frightened her more than if he had laughed. He did not say there was nothing there. He did not say she was wrong either.

"Don't answer it," he said, almost whispering. "Whatever it is, don't answer."

"He's cold."

Nami heard how stupid that sounded even as she said it. Of course he was cold. He was under water. He had probably been under water longer than she had been alive. The thought should have made the vision break. It did not. The root cradle still opened and closed with the black current. The child's mouth still held that seed like a second dark heart.

Lale came from the lantern with her careful posture wrecked by urgency. "What happened?"

Nami pointed again. The bank had already gone back to roots and glare and moving dark. If the child was still there, the water would not let her pick him out.

"There," she said. "The little mouth. He was there."

Lale went still, which for her was nearly as loud as screaming.

Riva lowered Nami to the deck without gentleness and without cruelty either. It was the kind of handling you gave a crate with glass in it when you could not afford to break the contents or waste time apologising to them.

The blanket came over her again. Somebody's hand, maybe Daro's, maybe Lale's, pushed damp hair off her forehead. The fever had risen while she was looking. Now the whole night ran at the edges like cheap dye in rain.

She shut her eyes and the child was there again, clearer now that she did not have to fight the lantern. He was curled small, with his mouth forced open and the roots around him working like gums. He was waiting not to be saved, exactly, but to be remembered by somebody who had not even known he existed.

Nami put her bandaged hand over her pocket. The seed inside felt hot enough to scorch cloth.

"Sixth mouth," she whispered.

No one told her she had said it wrong. No one told her she was only fevering.

That silence was worse than contradiction. It made the child in the roots feel less like a vision than a fact that had finally found the shape of a body.

* * *

They tied up once before Lantern Quay, but only because Lale threatened mutiny if she could not look at the hand in a steadier light.

It was a bad little landing, just three half-submerged posts and a strip of higher mud under a stand of wax trees. The flood had eaten the bank into shelves. Frogs worked themselves frantic in the grass. Riva got the bow line around the tallest post and jumped down before the hull settled, boots sinking to the ankle.

Nami was shivering now despite the heat. When Riva lifted her from the deck the body under the blanket felt too light and too hot, like carrying kindling that had already caught at the center. Nami's head lolled once against Riva's shoulder and then jerked upright with stubbornness.

"I can walk."

"You can barely swear."

"I can swear fine."

"That was weak."

Nami would have answered if the effort had not tipped her into coughing. Riva set her on a dry crate under the awning while Lale opened the medicine tin again. Daro got down beside them without being asked. Iven stayed at the stern, pole across his shoulders, scanning the dark water and the darker banks as if trouble might try to board from either side.

Lale unwrapped the hand.

Riva had seen infected cuts before. Salvage work made a trade in them. Iron rust, eel bites, nails gone under dirty skin. This one still hit her low. The wound had gone shiny at the edges and deeper at the center, as though the flesh had softened and then been gnawed. A thread of redness ran along the underside of Nami's wrist toward the forearm. The smell coming off it was not just rot. It had that same sweet note the walkers' breath had carried, fig left too long in heat.

Lale did not waste anybody's time by saying it looked good. "She needs a bed, boiled water, and a fire big enough to keep the damp out of her bones."

"She needs to stay awake," Daro said.

Nami rolled her eyes, a poor effort. "You're all very cheerful."

Iven came closer, ducking under the awning. "Then let's tie up for the night and get cheerful inland. There's got to be a house on this bank with a stove and somebody mean enough to help."

Riva looked back at the channel. The dark water between the reeds was full of small motions now. They were not fish, or not only fish. Lanterns showed now and then behind distant house slats where families watched over sleepers. Farther off, too far to see plainly, somebody shouted once and then again, the same name, louder the second time.

That was the real shape of it. It was not just Nami's fever or one bad market lane. The pressure had spread. Every hour the third cut stayed unsettled, more seeds rooted, more living bodies leaned toward water in their sleep. If they stopped here in pity, the river would use the night.

She hated how quickly the calculation arrived. She hated even more that it was probably right.

Daro saw the answer in her before she spoke. "No."

Riva kept her eyes on the water. "We push to Quay."

"No," he said again, harsher now. "She can't take another stretch on the boat."

"She can't take the bank either if the whole delta starts walking tonight."

"That's not an answer. That's a weight you throw on every scale until your side wins."

Riva turned then. Mud pulled at her boots. Rainwater dripped from the awning edge between them. Daro had one hand braced on the crate by Nami's knee, protective and furious both. His voice was still scraped raw from the rescue. It made the anger sound intimate.

"You think I don't know what she needs," Riva said.

"I think you know and keep choosing against it."

That landed where it was aimed. Riva felt it hit old damage on the way in. Leaving Reedmouth. Leaving Nami in other people's stories after Sereya died. Choosing the barge. Choosing work. Choosing anything that had bolts and fuel over a girl who still believed specialness might make neglect make sense.

Lale snapped the medicine tin shut. "Fight later. Decide now."

"I did decide," Riva said.

"Of course you did," Daro said. "Everything in your mouth turns into cargo. A voice. A hand. A sister."

Iven rubbed one palm over his face, smearing river grit across his cheek. "As much as I enjoy this family service, I would like to mention that if we stay put and more sleepers come down the bank, we're catching them blind."

Nobody answered him, which was unfair because he was right.

Lale rewound a clean strip of cloth around Nami's hand, tighter this time. "If we move," she said, not looking up, "we move for a place with fire, water, and people enough to keep watch. Not because the boat matters more than she does."

"The boat is how we get there," Riva said.

"That is not the same sentence."

Nami made a small irritated sound. "Can you all stop talking over my corpse like I'm already one of the dead."

Riva crouched in front of her. Fever had put a shine on Nami's lips and a strange glass brightness in her eyes. She looked twenty-four and ten and not old enough for any of this. The blanket had slipped off one shoulder. River rash scars showed pale at her ankles above the mud.

"Listen to me," Riva said.

"You only say that when I'm about to hate you."

"Probably." Riva put the blanket back around her shoulders, rougher than tenderness and meant as such. "We're going to Lantern Quay. They have houses above flood line and more hands than this patch of mud. Lale can boil water there. You can curse me there. If we stop here, we help you and lose whoever walks tonight because we didn't settle the route fast enough."

Nami stared at her for a long moment. Riva could not tell how much sense was still getting through the fever. At last Nami said, very quietly, "There was really a baby."

Riva's first instinct was to tell her to save strength and stop talking nonsense. The image of Daro's face on the deck stopped her. So did Lale's silence.

"I know what you saw," Riva said, which was not true in the way truth was usually measured. "We'll deal with that when we can look at it standing up."

Nami gave a short laugh that broke in the middle. "That's a terrible promise."

"It's the one I've got."

Daro rose so abruptly he hit his head on the awning beam. He swore under his breath, then louder when the pain caught up. "Fine," he said. "Fine. We go. But don't pretend this is duty purified. It's the same sickness as ever. Use the willing body. Keep moving."

Riva stood too. "If you want to hate me, do it on the pole."

His mouth twisted. "There's the captain."

There it was again, that ugly thing between them, worsened now because he had spent his throat and she had spent Nami. Riva did not have any tool for mending it that night.

They shoved off.

Iven took the stern first while Riva worked the bow free of mud. Lale sat Nami under the awning and forced hot spirit-water between her lips in sips. Daro stayed beside her, not singing now, only keeping one hand braced on the crate whenever the hull kicked sideways, as if he could hold her in the world by pressure alone.

The run to Lantern Quay felt longer than the whole route from Reed Gate.

Night thickened. The banks answered with little violences. Once a woman cried out and then was muffled, as if hands had closed over her mouth before she could walk farther. Once Riva saw two men waist-deep at another landing dragging a third back by the suspenders while he leaned toward the water with patient, animal strength. Once a lantern went spinning into the flood and hissed out.

Every sight made the same point. Delay was not embarrassment anymore. It was bodies lost by the hour.

Riva poled until the heel of her palms burned and the cut of old rope in her shoulder opened again under the wet shirt. The work kept her from looking back too often at Nami under the awning. When she did look, she saw the blanket rising with fast breath, Lale's bent head, Daro's knuckles white on the crate edge. The family had become a bundle of bad choices tied together with line.

Then at last the dark ahead loosened.

Lantern Quay showed itself first as pinpricks low over the water, too many and too steady to be stars. Then the smell reached them: fish oil, cookfire, wet nets, rice thickening in pots. Then the houses, built on higher pilings and linked by narrow walks with prayer bowls hung underneath like small moons. People were already waiting on the nearest landing, lanterns lifted, faces tense and sleepless.

Riva did not waste breath calling for help until the hull struck wood.

Behind them, somewhere back upriver where the market houses sat over rooted seeds and families held each other down through the night, somebody shouted a girl's name into the dark. The sound carried farther than it should have.

Riva stepped onto the Quay boards and reached back for Nami.
