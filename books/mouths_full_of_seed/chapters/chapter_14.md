# Chapter 14

Salt Room did not look built so much as stranded there. The ruined chapel crouched on a black tooth of stone where the last cut met the Gray Sea, its roof punched inward, its walls furred white with salt. Every few breaths the tide shoved through whatever hollowness lived under it and the whole place exhaled. Even from the ferry Nami tasted iron, kelp, old shells crushed to chalk, and the cold sour breath of sea caves. She tried not to take the taste as an answer.

In the stitched pocket at her hip, the salt-fig seed she had kept all this time clicked softly against her bone.

She had told herself she carried it for proof. Then she had told herself she carried it because throwing it away felt unlucky. Then because she had gone so long with it that getting rid of it would make the first nights real again: the drowned girl on the ferry-house boards, the seed shining wet as an eye. Here, with the sea opening its rotten mouth under the chapel, Nami stopped lying to herself about it. She had carried it because some ugly hot part of her wanted one thing in this whole flood to belong to her alone.

Riva drove the ferry with a borrowed Halcote pole and the old scar on her chin gone pale with strain. She had been sleeping in bites for days. Every command came out flat now, stripped of anything but use.

"Iven, get the bow line ready. Lale, watch the left wall. Nami, stay where the boat knows your weight."

"The boat doesn't know anybody's weight," Iven said, but he was already coiling line with his quick hands. He had cut one sleeve off at the shoulder two days ago because the wet cloth kept slapping his skin raw, and now his upper arm showed under the rain, faded tattoos swimming blue-green when the lantern shifted.

Lale knelt by the gunwale with her marked forearm lifted into the weather, reading the fresh tide lines Paska had needled into her skin as if they still itched instructions into her blood. "The wall's undercut," she said. "If we come in too hard, we lose a plank."

Daro said nothing. He stood amidships beside the sailcloth-wrapped length of Sereya's brass-ferruled pole, one hand at his throat. He had taken to touching the notch above his collarbone as though he could feel the old answer from Hymn Cut there, nested and warm. His mouth looked bloodless in the lantern light. Nami did not have to look straight at him to feel him straining toward the chapel the way dogs strained toward thunder.

The ferry nosed between broken pilings. Old votive ribbons had hardened on them into strips of salt leather. A gull flew out through the chapel roof and screamed once, furious and human enough to make Nami's heart jump. Under the stone, the water breathed again.

There, she thought, and did not know whether she meant the cut or Jun or the thing inside herself that still wanted a hand to come down from somewhere and point.

They made fast badly because everything at the sea edge was trying to move at once. The line came back slick with weed. The wall shed salt flakes into their hair. A burst of backward current shoved the ferry broadside and Iven nearly went to his knees. Riva swore, low and sharp as metal dropped on stone, and jammed the pole down until the boat stopped arguing.

"Off," she said. "Take only what we can carry back if the tide turns mean."

"That's all of it, then," Iven muttered.

They went ashore into a skin of white crust that cracked underfoot. The chapel doors were gone. Their hinges remained in the jamb like black teeth. Inside, saint faces on the wall had been eaten into blurs. The old floor tiles sweated brine. The smell was stranger than outside: incense once, fish for years after, then mold, then the clean punishing reek of too much salt. Nami reached out and touched a wall. Wet roughness came away on her fingertips like ground shell.

The nave opened to a broken altar. Behind it, where she had expected solid wall, a dark slit ran back into the rock.

"There's a chamber," Lale said.

Riva lifted the lantern. "There'd be one. Sereya never saw a holy place she couldn't stuff with storage."

"Or with witnesses," Daro said softly.

The slit in the rock breathed colder air. Nami heard a drip somewhere deep in it. Then something else: a scrape, followed by the faint bright knock of metal on crockery.

Everyone heard that. Even Iven lost the joke gathering at the side of his mouth.

Riva took the boat hook off her shoulder. "Who's there?"

Nobody answered.

The sound came again, close now. It was not a ghost sound, not moaning or chain-clatter or the wet throat-noise of the returned drowned. It was something ordinary: a cup set down, a heel dragged over stone.

Nami stepped before she knew she had moved. Riva's hand caught the back of her shirt and held.

"Don't," Riva said.

"If it's the drowned, they don't use cups," Iven said.

"You don't know what the drowned use," Daro said.

"I know what I heard."

Another scrape. Then a man's voice out of the dark slit, rough with disuse and annoyance more than threat.

"Shut the outer plank if you're leaving it open. Wind gets in and salts the rice."

For one second Nami thought the chapel itself had spoken. Then the man stepped out from behind the split altar with a lamp in one hand and a skinning knife in the other, and she saw he was made of ordinary damage. Salt-scarred face. Nearly white hair pulled back with a bit of black string. One shoulder hanging lower than the other as if a wave had laid permanent claim to it. He wore a fisherman's sweater with the collar cut clean off, the raw edge curling against a deep chest furred with gray. His bare feet were broad, weather-thick, and planted slightly on their outer edges, the way Nami's were when she was tired.

He squinted into the lantern glare and turned his head so his good ear faced them.

"If you're going to point that light around," he said, "point it lower. It blinds for nothing."

No one moved.

Nami's first thought was stupid and childlike. He was not alive in the way dead men returned; he was too alive for that. Ghosts did not complain about rice and lighting.

Iven found his voice first. "Fuck me."

The man looked at him without interest. "That's your own work."

Riva tightened on the boat hook. "Bato?"

He shifted the knife down by his thigh, not sheathing it, not threatening with it either. "That's still the name."

Daro made a sound Nami had never heard from him before. It was not a note. It was what came before a note when breath hit the body and found there was too much in it. He took one step forward.

"They buried you," he said.

Bato shrugged with the good shoulder. "Poorly."

Salt cracked under Nami's heel. For a beat the split altar doubled in her sight. For years Bato Serin had been a drowned fact, a caution folded into other caution, the last lover at the sea edge, the one Jun went down with. Dead men could not rot into surprise. Dead men could not stand in cut-off sweaters and smell like lamp smoke and dried fish.

Her mouth worked before she could stop it. "I thought you'd be bones."

He looked at her then, fully, and the look landed with such plain force that she forgot the others were there. His eyes were sea-light, neither soft nor hard. Just tired of weather and still open in it.

"Nearly was," he said.

He lowered the knife. "Come in or go out. The tide changes harder after dark and I'm not shouting over it if you've got questions."

He turned and went back through the slit as if this were his house and not the last room in the book of their mother's damage.

For a breath nobody followed. The chapel breathed. Water thudded somewhere under stone.

Then Riva said, "Move," and they all moved at once.

The chamber behind the altar had been a keeper's room once. Bato had made it into a life. A cot of sailcloth and reeds stood against one wall. Nets dried from a line overhead. The floor had been scraped clean enough that the stone showed through the salt. On a crate sat a chipped porcelain cup painted with a tiny red apricot, absurd and delicate in all that ruin. He had weighted tide charts to the table with polished dogwhelk shells. In the far corner three rice sacks stood under waxed canvas, every seam tucked tight against damp. A row of candle stubs had been melted together into one thick useful lump.

Bato set his lamp beside an iron pan and, before he did anything else, went to check the rice sacks with his fingertips as if the whole family had arrived only to threaten the one thing he refused to lose.

"Close that plank," he said over his shoulder. "Not the door. The floor plank by the slit. It rattles."

Nami saw it then: a warped board over a gutter in the stone, jumping faintly with each pulse of water under the room. She crouched and pressed it flat. Cold came up through the crack and needled her wrist.

Bato noticed the motion. "Good. Thank you." It was the most ordinary gratitude in the world. It made him harder to look at than if he had turned spectral again.

Riva did not sit. "Start with Jun."

Bato looked at the lamp wick and pinched a black curl from it with his thumbnail. "You start where you like. It won't change where it ends."

Nami stayed crouched by the plank because standing felt risky. The stone under her feet gave a little with each sea shove, as if the room floated but had forgotten to mention it.

Outside, the chapel breathed again.

***

Bato put water on to heat, because apparently revelations still had to happen around a kettle.

That made Nami angrier than the sight of him had, not because it was wrong but because it was so offensively right. He had lived here long enough to have a kettle blackened to one side, long enough to have opinions about wind in the rice, long enough to keep apricots painted on a cup from being ground to powder, and long enough to stay dead for them on purpose.

Iven must have felt some version of it too, because he laughed once with no humor in it. "You had all this time to send word and instead you became furniture."

Bato did not look up. "I sent none because I wanted none back."

"That's a line," Lale said. "Not an answer."

He glanced at her. "Then here's another one. Shame's lazy. It can sit still for years and call itself grief."

That shut even Iven up for a moment.

The kettle began to mutter. Rain ticked through the broken roof outside. Nami smelled rust, lamp oil, old salt, and some herb Bato had tied up to dry over the pan. It was pennyroyal maybe, or something like it, sharp enough to cut the fish stink. On the shelf above the cot, he had lined up six sinkers in a row from smallest to largest with the care of a man who needed one thing in the world to stay sorted.

Riva said, "You can hate yourself later. We didn't pole all the way out here to watch you boil water."

Bato set the kettle aside. "Fair enough."

He sat on the only stool because it was clearly his and because he had gotten used to not asking permission from anyone. The low shoulder pulled his whole body off true. When he folded his hands, Nami saw one thumb bent wrong from an old break.

"Storm Mouth was never a saint's door," he said. "That was the first lie. Not the worst one. Just the first that got big enough to feed the others."

Daro had not moved from the wall. He was still standing beside the sailcloth-wrapped pole. "You don't get to call everything lie because you hid from it."

Bato turned his good ear toward him. "I didn't call everything lie. I called that lie."

"Hymn Cut answered me."

"It answered song. That's what that cut does."

"The bells rang on their own."

"Metal rings when the room is right. That doesn't make you king."

The words landed so plainly that Nami felt them like a slap she had not expected on herself. Daro's chin lifted by a hair, and his face arranged itself around injury with the dangerous care of a man deciding to turn hurt into theater. He smiled with only one side of his mouth.

"You came up from seventeen years under stone just to be a spiteful old bastard?"

Bato's face changed by almost nothing. "No. I was already one."

Iven barked a laugh, the sound torn in half by nerves. Riva did not laugh. Lale looked at Daro instead of Bato, as if she had already started counting which way the pressure would break.

"Jun," Riva said again. "Speak plain."

Bato rubbed his thumb once over the side of the apricot cup before pouring. His hand steadied on the porcelain the way some men's hands steadied on rope.

"Plain, then. Your mother got tired of hauling labor out of people one meal at a time. Reed Gate, Hymn, Inkwater, Coil. Every season the same fight. Clear silt. Reset markers. Beg for crews. Take promises from men who'd rather kneel to a story than carry mud. If she stood on a ferry prow wet to the waist and called the river by pet names, people came running. If she said the route belonged to all of Reedmouth, half of them found reasons their backs hurt. If she said the river had touched her special, they brought grain, timber, girls for the shrines, boys for the poles, whatever she asked."

Nami could see it too easily. Sereya on a prow, black hair pasted down her spine, mouth making duty sound like heat. Nami had built her mother from other people's faces for years. Every telling added shine. Bato's version did not remove the shine. It showed the grease under it.

Bato went on. "At first maybe she only used the story because it worked. Later she liked how it worked. Later than that she started hearing it herself. That's how these things go."

"You loved her anyway," Nami said before she meant to speak.

He looked at her. "Of course I did. Loving somebody isn't evidence in their favor."

The kettle hissed softly behind him. Rainwater ran down the chapel wall in a slow bright seam.

Lale said, "The ledger page called Jun the sixth mouth."

Bato nodded once. "Because she decided the road would hold if blood sat where labor should've sat. Five children already scattered through the route, one new one born at the sea edge. She thought that made a pattern. Thought if she brought her own body, mine, and the baby to Storm Mouth and fixed it open in front of witnesses, the whole delta would believe the line had an heir. Then they'd keep feeding it because blood would be in it. Because a family story is cheaper to carry than a public duty."

Nami's palm flattened harder over the rattling plank until salt bit into her skin. The board knocked against her hand with each sea shove, and the kettle hissed harder behind Bato's shoulder.

"She brought him out here?" Iven said. His voice had gone thin.

"In a sling against her front," Bato said.

The image came whole and cruel: Sereya broad-hipped and strong at the pole, an infant strapped to her chest under oilcloth, the sea breaking around her calves. Nami saw Jun as she had in fever, not all the way a baby and not all the way a vision, black hair pasted to a tiny skull, storm rash bright on his chest.

"I told her no," Bato said. "That part matters less than I wanted it to later, but it's still true. I told her no when she said she'd bring him. I told her no when she called it proof. I told her no when she said the town needed to see flesh they could follow. Then I came anyway, because I was in love with her and because I liked thinking I could keep the ugly parts from landing if I stayed close enough."

Riva leaned both hands on the table. "What happened?"

"Weather," Bato said.

It was such a bare word that Nami almost hated him for it.

He must have seen that on her face, because he gave more.

"Weather and pride and bad timing. The tide turned mean halfway through. I was in the cut setting the drag chain. She had the pole braced in the throat of the breach, standing where nobody should stand if they want to keep breathing. People were watching from the outer wall. They'd rowed out to see the saint fix the sea. She wanted them looking. She wanted them carrying the story home before dark."

His bent thumb rubbed once, hard, over the apricot on the cup until the red fruit disappeared under his skin.

"A wave hit from the side. Not big. Just wrong. It spun her half around. The pole went. She caught it. The sling did not. I heard him before I saw him. That's the part I still hear."

The rush of blood in Nami's ears drowned the kettle. She saw it happen against her will. Oilcloth gone loose. A baby's mouth filling not with river mud but salt water cold enough to stop thought. Bato coming up through black churn. Sereya screaming not saintly, not beautiful, just screaming.

Nobody spoke.

Bato did not spare them the rest. "I got him back to stone. He was already gone. She still wanted to go back into the cut. Not for him. For the pole. For the witnesses. For the chance to finish what we'd started before the crowd ran home with a different story. That's when I knew the lie had eaten too far in."

Riva swore under her breath.

Lale shut her eyes. Iven turned away and dragged a hand over his face as if he could wipe out the picture and only managed to smear rain and salt.

Daro said, careful as a blade laid flat on a table, "Or that's when grief made her stupid for an hour."

Bato looked at him for a long time. "You need it to be prettier than it was."

"I need you not to crawl out of a crypt and turn her into a cheap fraud."

"She wasn't cheap. She was expensive as hell. Ask the people who paid in backs and children."

Nami heard herself say, "Then why did the river keep answering her?"

The question had lived in her a long time. Longer than the seed. Longer than any single night. If Sereya had lied, then why had the cuts opened, why had voices come through pilings, why had Jun found her in fever roots, why had Daro sung bells into motion, why did this whole flooded world still feel as though it leaned toward certain bodies harder than others?

Bato answered her, not gently and not cruelly either.

"Because the river answers pressure. Put enough grief in one place, it'll echo. Put hunger in it, it swells. Put five thousand people wanting one body to kneel to, it'll throw that wanting back loud enough to sound holy. The signs are real. The stories we hang on them are where the rot gets in."

Daro laughed then, low and bright and wrong. "Listen to yourself. You turn miracle into weather and weather into bookkeeping."

"No," Bato said. "I turn it back into work."

That hit Riva square in the chest, and Nami saw it in the way Riva did not soften. Her whole body simply took the word and held it there.

"Then tell us the work," Riva said.

Bato tipped his chin toward the rattling plank under Nami's hand. "Storm Mouth opens and resets. It doesn't stay obedient. Never did. That's the second lie. There's no forever-open cut. There's only timing, sequence, weight, and enough living hands to keep the road made. One body can't hold it because one body sleeps, bleeds, gets vain, gets old, dies. Your mother wanted a permanent answer because permanent answers draw pilgrims better than maintenance rosters."

Nami's hand had gone numb on the cold plank. She did not lift it.

"No heir?" she said.

Bato's face went very still. "No heir."

Something desperate clawed up through her before she could stop it. She reached into the stitched pocket at her hip and dragged out the salt-fig seed. It had worn smooth with handling. Even now it looked obscene and promising, black with a dull bloom on it, no bigger than a thumb knuckle and heavy as if it had learned from being carried how to pretend at significance.

"Then what's this?" she asked. "Why did it call me? Why did Jun come to me? Why didn't it root in my pocket or rot through me if it wasn't for something?"

Everyone turned. Riva's head came up first in disbelief, then anger.

"Nami."

But Nami was already committed. "I kept it. Since the first night. I kept it and I heard things and I saw him and if none of it meant anything then what the hell was it for?"

Her voice cracked open on the last word. There it was at last, ugly and plain as a fish gutted on a table: not fear of drowning, not even grief, but the need to have been singled out, because singled out was cleaner than one more mouth at the cannery, one more pair of hands that smelled of crab and smoke, one more youngest child left wherever older people dropped her when the story got too heavy to carry.

Bato held out his hand.

"Give it here."

She almost refused. Then she put it in his palm because some part of her still wanted him to say yes, this one is different, this one chose you.

He brought it under the lamp, sniffed it once, and pressed his thumbnail into the seam until black syrup beaded out. The smell that came from it was sweet and rotten at once, fig flesh gone warm in a locked room.

He worried the seam just enough to show the pale threads cramped inside, alive and blind, then pinched it shut again.

"It wanted dark and blood heat," he said. "A pocket qualified. That isn't a crown."

He folded the nicked seed back into her palm. "It would've rooted in any fool's coat if they'd carried it long enough."

Nami stared at the black bead glistening along the seam. It felt indecent that something no bigger than her thumb knuckle could stay so hungry. She wiped her hand on her skirt and shoved the seed back into her pocket.

Then the nothing inside her lurched and reached for shape.

"Use me anyway," she said.

The rattling plank kept ticking under Nami's feet, but nobody else made a sound.

She heard herself, hated the sound, kept going because once she had said it she wanted the humiliation finished in one clean pull.

"If it needs a body at the last cut, use mine. I'm the youngest. I hear him. I can go down where he went if that's what's needed."

Riva moved so fast the stool legs scraped. "Shut your mouth."

But Nami was no longer talking to Riva. She was looking at Bato, because he was the one who had taken the dream apart and she needed him to either finish killing it or give one piece back.

"You said one body can't hold it," she said. "Fine. Then let mine wedge where it needs wedging. Let it count for something clean."

Bato was on her before she understood he had crossed the room. He caught the front of her jacket in his fist and hauled her away from the rattling plank so hard her heel skidded in salt. The lowered shoulder made the movement ugly. The strength in it made it absolute.

"No," he said.

He did not say it loudly. The word landed worse than a shout.

"I'm not feeding another child to water because grown people want a prettier story."

He let go, but only after he was sure her balance belonged to her again. His hand left a wet salt print over her breastbone.

Nami could not answer. The place where he had gripped her burned through the cloth.

He looked down at her feet then, at the way she had braced without knowing it, weight on the outer edges. Something moved across his face and shut again.

"You've got my bad stance," he said. "That's all blood gives you. Bad ankles and weather sense if you're lucky. It doesn't give doctrine."

Riva's knuckles stayed white on the table edge. Even Iven let the silence stand.

He did not say father. She did not ask. The omission sat there bigger than any name.

Outside, a wave hit the chapel wall hard enough to make grit rain from the ceiling.

Riva cleared her throat once, roughly. "Then we do it together."

Bato turned from Nami as if he had not just cut her open with something almost tender. "Yes."

"Tell us how."

"At first light. Before the tide climbs. All five of you on the stones where she tried to stand alone. One in the throat with the chain, one at the key line, two on the drag poles. The fifth watches the sequence with Tovin and keeps anybody from turning it into theatre. The town will need to hold the outer wall. If people start kneeling instead of hauling, you're finished."

Daro pushed off the wall.

"You really believe that," he said. "You really think all the hunger in this place is a civic scheduling problem."

Bato faced him. "I think hunger loves a costume."

Something in Daro's expression went blank with hurt so deep it almost looked like serenity.

"And I think," Bato said, quieter now, "that you're about to prove me right."

***

They should have stayed in the chamber and made a plan. Lale started trying. She asked about the old chain. Riva asked where the stone lip held best. Iven, still pale under his jokes, wanted to know how many bodies the outer wall could safely take if the tide swung. Bato answered in short pieces and marked positions on the table with a fish spine. He had done this too long alone. Every practical detail lived at the ends of his fingers.

Nami heard almost none of it.

She stood in the broken nave with the taste of split fig rot still in her nose and stared out through the chapel's collapsed west side at the sea darkening beyond the cut. Twilight flattened the water into lead. Farther out, the reed islands had come apart into black mats drifting where they pleased. Lanterns moved on two distant boats, then vanished behind spray. The whole edge of the world looked half built and half swallowed.

She pressed her thumb against the place over her chest where Bato had gripped her. Salt had dried there stiff as starch.

No heir.

The words should have made her feel freed. Instead they emptied something load-bearing. Chosenness had been filthy and embarrassing and private, but it had also been clean in one brutal way. If the river picked you, then the rest of the book of your life could be read from that line. If the river picked nobody, then all that remained was the long stupid work of staying, hauling, counting, feeding, watching, not being forgotten, not running, doing it again next season with more gray in your hair and no witness to call it holy.

Shared duty sounded virtuous in older mouths. In hers it sounded like the cannery bell before dawn, like knives needing scraping, like being one more girl whose name was remembered only when the tubs were too heavy.

Behind her, Daro's voice rose once and stopped, laughing softly with no shape to it instead of singing.

She turned.

He was no longer in the keeper's chamber.

For a second she thought nothing of it. Then she saw the empty place by the wall where Sereya's pole had lain wrapped in sailcloth.

Nami moved before fear found language. She crossed the nave and nearly slipped on salt. By the time she reached the outer steps Daro was already on the chapel platform above the mooring wall, the long pole bare in his hands. He had stripped the sailcloth away. The brass ferrule at its foot caught the last of the light and gave it back dirty gold.

He looked right holding it, and Nami's stomach turned. The pole set his shoulders back and steadied his tired mouth into something people could mistake for certainty. Wind pressed his shirt flat to the fine bones of his chest. Down below, on the lee side of the wall, three small fishing skiffs had tucked in from the weather. Tarred cloth stirred. The crying child muffled against somebody's shoulder went quiet. Heads tilted up toward the dirty gold at the ferrule. Nami had not noticed them arrive. Or maybe they had been there all along, just outside the range of her selfishness.

Daro lifted the pole and struck the ferrule once against the chapel stone.

The sound jumped through the ruin like a bell hit underwater. It ran through Nami's teeth. In the skiffs below, heads came up.

"Daro," she said.

He looked back at her, then past her to the room where the others were still talking themselves toward the plain hard truth Bato had laid down.

"Do you feel better?" he asked her quietly. "Now that we've all been made ordinary?"

She opened her mouth and nothing useful came.

He smiled at that, heartbreakingly gentle. "Exactly."

Riva came out behind Nami. One look at the bare pole and her whole body set like a knot pulled tight.

"Put it down."

"No."

"Daro."

"You want them on a roster," he said. "You want floodtime turned into a work gang and a timetable. Good luck with that when the dead are at the doors and the living are walking into wells. People need a body to follow."

Riva took one step forward. "People need to stay alive."

"And what do you think gets them moving before they drown?"

The voices from the skiffs had gone quiet. Even the muffled child had stopped crying. Nami could feel attention climbing the chapel wall toward them like water.

Lale appeared at the doorway next, then Iven behind her, then Bato last of all, the lamp turning his face into scar and shadow. He did not rush. That frightened Nami worse than if he had.

"Don't do this," Nami said.

Daro's gaze flicked to her pocket, as if he could feel the small weight back in it, then came back to her face. "You wanted it too."

The shame of that landed so cleanly she could not deny it.

"I wanted it because I was tired," she said.

"So am I."

He turned then, not to her but to the skiffs below and the chapel dark beyond them, the places where listeners might be. When he spoke again his voice changed. It went warm, carrying, nearly tender, the room voice and the shrine voice, the voice that could make grief feel chosen if you wanted it badly enough.

"You've all been told the water doesn't mean anything," he said. "You've been told all this fear comes down to ledgers and lines and bodies enough to make a wall. Maybe that's true for people who still believe the world is fair to committees."

Riva swore. "Don't you dare."

He did not even glance at her.

"But you know what you've seen," he said to the dark. "You know what answered. You know the flood doesn't wait for everybody to become virtuous. It needs a hand that will take the weight."

The figures in the skiffs were only shadows, but shadows leaned. One woman rose halfway under her tarp. Nami saw the flash of her nose ring and the way she kept one hand on a bundle at her feet as if even terror could not make her forget where she had put her child.

Bato said, not loudly, "There. Quick as weather."

Daro heard him. His mouth hardened.

"You hid," he said, turning back. "You let them rot around a hole because you were ashamed. Now you want to drag the rest of us down into your smallness and call that wisdom."

Bato stood with the lamp low. "I hid because I was broken. You're performing because you'd rather be important than useful."

That got through. For a beat Daro looked exactly what he was: hurt, vain, hungry, and scared enough to sanctify any appetite that kept him from having to name it. Then the hurt sealed over with brightness.

"Useful?" he said. "Useful is what they call bodies right before they spend them."

His hand slid up the pole. The brass ferrule rang once against stone again.

"This was hers," he said. "This route knew her weight. If she lied, then let me lie better. If she made a body for them to follow because people are weak, then fine. People are weak. The flood is here anyway."

Iven took a half step as if to rush him. Riva threw an arm across his chest without looking.

"Don't make me knock you into the cut," she said to Daro.

"You'd miss."

"Try me."

The old sibling heat in it would have made Nami roll her eyes any other year. Here it felt like flint at a gas leak. The skiffs below shifted. Someone began to cry again, very quietly this time, the way people cried when they were trying not to lose an audience.

Nami's tongue stuck to the roof of her mouth. The woman with the nose ring had both feet under her now, bundle lashed to one hip, staring up as if Daro had already told her where to put her knees. Two men edged closer along the gunwale without seeming to notice they were moving. His hurt shone clean on his face, beautiful enough to pass for an answer, and she loved him while wanting to slap his mouth bloody.

"If you go out there with that pole," she said, "you won't be carrying her. You'll be carrying the part that killed Jun."

For one second she thought she had him. His face did not soften; it opened just enough for pain to show through.

Then he gave her the saddest smile she had ever seen on a living person.

"Little tide," he said, and the nickname in his mouth made it feel like a blessing and a theft at once, "that's the only part anybody ever follows."

Before Riva could move, he jumped down from the platform to the outer wall, using the pole to take his weight. The brass ferrule bit stone, slid, caught. One of the skiff men reached up by instinct to steady him. Daro let him. He did not thank him. He did not have to. The gesture itself had already become the first tithe.

Riva lunged too late. Her hand closed on wet air.

"Daro!"

He did not turn back.

He walked the wall toward the moorings with Sereya's pole upright in his hand, lantern light striking the brass at its heel. The people in the skiffs folded back to clear him space before he asked. The man who had steadied him kept a hand hovering at Daro's elbow. Someone passed the crying child farther aft so both hands could go free. Nami felt her knees unlock with the urge to follow, quick and mindless as tide tugging at her ankles. By the time she caught herself, the bodies below had already made him a path into the lead skiff.

Bato came to Nami's shoulder and stopped. The lamp shook once in his hand, either from the wind or from anger finally getting into him.

"Tomorrow gets worse," he said.

Below, Daro's voice had already changed again, smoothing itself for strangers.

By morning the story would beat him to Knot Bend, stripped for speed and swollen with need.

Nami watched the shadows gather after him and felt the ripped-open place inside her answer before she could put words to it. Chosenness had always promised cleanliness: one hand, one body, one line to follow through dark water. Shared duty looked uglier every second, more crowded, more humiliating, and more true.

She tasted split fig and salt and old grief.

Then she went back inside to help plan how to stop her brother.
