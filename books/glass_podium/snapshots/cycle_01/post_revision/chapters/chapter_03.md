# Chapter 3

The calibration bay ran at a constant seventeen degrees Celsius, two below facility standard. It wasn't for the athletes. It was for the equipment. Spectral analyzers drifted at higher temperatures, and Yusuf had spent three months arguing with Building Services for dedicated climate control. He'd won. The bay stayed cold, the analyzers stayed stable, and from October to April he wore a fleece under his lab coat.

He arrived at six forty-five, same time every morning. The processing stacks were already cycling, active calibration to standby analysis and back again, and their hum had become the sound of his working life. It ran at sixty hertz, low and steady. Most people stopped noticing it after a few minutes. Yusuf had stopped hearing it in his first week at Kirchberg, three years ago. Now he noticed it only when it changed.

He set his coffee on the workstation ledge, the same chipped mug with the Imperial College crest half gone, and opened the schedule. Six athletes before noon. Four after lunch. Then came data review and reporting. That was his official reporting. The other kind happened in the gaps.

The first athlete at seven was Anya Petrov, a javelin specialist from the Russian program. It was a standard monthly recalibration. Her right shoulder graft had been stable for two seasons, but the altitude altered the synthetic muscle's oxygen transfer and it needed periodic adjustment. She came in already stretching, one earbud still in, then pulled it free and dropped it in her lap as she settled into the rig.

The rig looked like a dentist's chair designed by people who thought dentists weren't invasive enough. It had a padded recline, articulated arm supports, and a curved overhead array of sensor plates that read thermal output, electromagnetic fluctuation, and nerve firing through the skin.

"Morning, Anya. How's the shoulder?"

"Good. A little tight after yesterday's throws."

"Scale of one to ten."

"Three. Maybe four when I reach overhead."

Yusuf pulled up her integration profile. The graft's performance envelope ran across the display in green and amber bands.

"Right, I'm going to run a tension sweep first. You'll feel pressure cycling through the deltoid, like someone's kneading it. About ninety seconds. Try to stay relaxed."

He initiated the sweep. Through the sensor array, the graft answered with a soft rise in signal, synthetic fibers adjusting their resting tension as the calibration passed through them. The work always felt intimate in a way he'd never got used to. He was inside somebody's body, reading signals they couldn't feel, making changes that would alter how they moved for the next month. His hands stayed steady on the interface. They always did.

Anya's shoulder responded normally. He adjusted the oxygen transfer parameters, logged the calibration values, and released the rig.

"Feels good," she said, rolling the shoulder once. "Looser."

"Should hold four weeks. If the tightness comes back before then, flag it."

She slid the earbud back in and headed out. Yusuf opened the unsynced window and copied the full raw stream before the buffer cleared. On the official tab, Kirchberg kept the calibration values and adjustment parameters. In the raw export, extra channels stacked down the side of the screen: nerve firing, local blood flow, hormone traces at the graft site, tissue response around the synthetic muscle. Thirty seconds in the chair gave him more physiology than most ward admissions.

He saved the file, minimised the window, and called in the next athlete. The copy bar flashed green without asking who had signed off on it. If he sent the same file anywhere anyone might read it, compliance would want names.

Tomás Reyes arrived at seven thirty, twenty-one, Ecuadorian, first Apex cycle. He sat in the rig and looked up at the sensor array the way newer athletes still did, like the machinery was worth seeing.

"How's the integration feeling?"

"Good. I think." Tomás flexed his right hand, opened and closed it. "My grip's been weird in the mornings. Like the hand's awake before I am."

Yusuf pulled up his profile. Right forearm graft, bilateral calf integration, spinal bridge. Standard decathlon build.

"How long's that been happening?"

"Maybe a week. I wasn't sure if it was normal or if I was being an idiot."

"You're not being an idiot. If your system feels off, you say so."

Some of the tension went out of Tomás's shoulders at that. Yusuf ran the forearm scan. Synthetic fibers lit across the display in dense cross-section, fine as suture thread where they anchored to tendon and bone. The resting tension was slightly elevated.

"Could be altitude," Yusuf said. "I'm going to drop the forearm's resting tension about two percent. Your hand might feel weak for an hour while it recalibrates."

"That's fine."

"If it isn't, you come back."

Tomás settled back without another question, forearm on the pad, throat open under the sensor plates. He didn't ask what else the array could see. Most of them didn't. Yusuf made the adjustment, watched the tension trace sink into range, and logged the approved notes.

"Thanks, doc."

"Not a doctor."

"Still. Thanks."

After he left, Yusuf reopened the hidden window. The forearm elevation was there, but in the lower panes the calf grafts were lifting too, a shallow autonomous rise the standard pass rounded away. Sleep chemistry from the night before sat beside it: cortisol, hydration, inflammatory markers at each interface seam. Tomás had come in for his hand and left half his night on Yusuf's screen.

By the Norwegian skier, Yusuf was already dragging traces on top of each other. The hip graft showed the expected winter drift, and under it ran the same faint lift. The diver's visual enhancement carried it too, tucked beside a depth-perception wobble. The French sprinter looked clean until Yusuf opened the raw stream and watched the autonomous channel rise in the same place.

By lunch four files sat tiled across the monitor. They came from different sports and different hardware histories. The same network field sat in each header, and the same upward creep ran through the autonomous channel inside forty-eight hours.

He ate a cheese sandwich at the workstation, brushed crumbs away from the keyboard, and opened Kai Brennan's overnight file all the way out: motor nerve latency across both legs, spinal bridge throughput, visual load, the micro-corrections that kicked in before Brennan meant to move. On the motion plot, the proprioception gap kept spreading after calibration.

Four files didn't prove anything. It was barely enough to make him swear under his breath. Still, the shape of it was wrong. Autonomous activity wasn't supposed to climb in different architectures on the same timetable.

He wrote the official reports. Brennan: proprioception drift noted, continued monitoring recommended, within acceptable parameters. The Norwegian: hip adjustment completed, seasonal norms. Reyes: grip tension corrected, altitude probable. The Brazilian hurdler with the earlier latency spike wasn't his patient today, but Yusuf pulled the filed report anyway and saw the same phrase he'd been seeing for weeks: momentary processing artifact.

On the Kirchberg side, the day looked clean: filed, timestamped, technically accurate.

He saved the correlation analysis to the hidden directory and closed the second window.

---

The calibration bay emptied by six. The last athlete, a German middle-distance runner in for standard monthly maintenance, left at five forty, and Yusuf spent the next twenty minutes on shutdown protocols. It wasn't a full shutdown. The processing stacks never really stopped. But the active hardware had to go dark, the sensor arrays had to be cleaned, and the rigs had to be returned to standby.

He did evening close himself. Sara and Luc handled mornings. Yusuf preferred nights, when the bay was empty and the only sounds were the low hum of the stacks and the click of the cooling system cycling. It felt like a room still working, but not watched.

When the rigs were down, he took his personal drive from his bag. It was matte black, smaller than his thumb, and encrypted with a key that existed only in his memory and one physical backup in the drawer of his room. He'd bought it in Zurich two years earlier from a shop that sold to researchers and paranoiacs in about equal numbers.

He plugged it into the workstation's auxiliary port. The hidden window opened with Brennan's proprioception stream, Reyes's forearm and calf data, the Norwegian's hip values, the rest of the day's pulls, and the correlation analysis. He transferred the files. The transfer took eleven seconds. Biological signals compressed well.

When the bar finished, he disconnected the drive and dropped it into his trouser pocket. The workstation showed only the Kirchberg interface: official logs, filed reports, the clean record of a careful engineer's day.

He turned off the monitor and left.

The connecting corridor had glass walls on the mountain side, and at this hour in late January the mountains were only shape and weight, a dark mass against a slightly less dark sky. The temperature display by the junction read minus twelve. Inside, the facility held steady at twenty-one degrees, and the step from the bay's chill into the corridor's warmth was the only weather Yusuf felt most days.

His quarters were on the third floor of the residential wing. It was a single room, larger than the athletes' rooms, with a bed, a desk, and a bathroom. A wall-mounted diagnostic display still showed facility status data he'd stopped reading months ago. There was a kettle, a jar of loose-leaf Yorkshire Tea his mother posted once a month, and a stack of books on the desk that he was getting through at about a chapter a week. Farah had given him a history of the Birmingham canal network for his birthday three months earlier. He was still in the first third.

He made tea, dropped the drive into the desk drawer beside its twin, then opened his laptop and called Farah.

She picked up on the third ring. Her face filled the screen, dark hair pulled back, the living room of the flat in Moseley behind her, one corner of the bookshelf he'd helped assemble two Christmases ago just visible over her shoulder. She was smiling.

"Yu. Hang on, let me." She adjusted something off-screen. The camera settled. "There. Hi."

"Hey. How was your day?"

"Fine. Normal. Went to Asda, made a lasagne. It was terrible but I ate all of it, so." She shrugged. She looked animated and present. She looked good on the screen. She always did. Compression hid the smaller things.

"Did you use Mum's recipe or the one from that website?"

"The website. Mum's has too many steps. I can't be arsed with bechamel from scratch when there's a perfectly good jar."

"She'd disown you."

"She'd disown me and then bring round a proper one. Win-win." Farah grinned. The pause after it ran a touch long. "What about you? Still in the lab at seven at night?"

"Just finished. Long day."

"They're all long days with you. You need to get out more. Go look at a mountain or something. You're in the Alps and you spend all your time in a room with no windows."

"There's a window in the corridor."

"Yu."

"I'm fine. I like the work."

He took a sip of tea and watched the places where it showed first: the beat before she answered, the slight catch when a noun ought to have come easy, the extra fraction of a second between the shape of a word and the sound of it. She was bright tonight, properly bright, but each answer started a little later than it had last month.

"How's the physio going?" he asked.

"Oh, fine. Same. She's dead enthusiastic. Lots of squeezing things." Farah held up both hands and mimed it. "I told her I don't need more grip strength, I need to be able to open the..."

She stopped. Her smile held. A word-sized space opened and stayed open for a beat too long.

"Jars," she said, recovering it. "I need to be able to open jars. The Tesco pasta sauce one especially. Whoever decided to make those lids that tight is my actual enemy."

"I'll send you one of those rubber grip things."

"I've got three. They don't work on the Tesco ones. I think they're sealed at molecular level."

He remembered her holding the kettle up to the camera months ago and calling it the thing for tea until the word came back. That was how Voss-Kessler announced itself at first: cheap nouns, kitchen objects, a strip of blank air where the sentence should have kept moving.

"Mum called today," Farah said. "She's worried about you. Says you're not eating properly."

"How would she know?"

"She has sources. I think she's paying one of the cafeteria staff."

"She's not paying anyone. She doesn't even know where the cafeteria is."

"Well, she's worried. Eat a vegetable, Yu. One vegetable. For her peace of mind."

"I had a sandwich."

"What was in it?"

"Cheese."

"That's not a vegetable."

"There was lettuce."

"Iceberg doesn't count. It's basically water in leaf form."

He laughed, and hers followed half a beat later, still warm, just slower getting there. He heard the extra breath before speech. He'd trained himself to hear it.

"You still there?" Farah asked.

"Yeah. Sorry. Thinking."

"About work?"

"About what I'm having for dinner."

"Cheese sandwich?"

"Probably."

"Hopeless." She yawned, and the yawn went on a touch too long, her jaw moving with a looseness the camera did not quite hide. "I've got that thing with Priya tomorrow. Early."

"What thing?"

"We're going to the..." She stopped again. Her eyes shifted a fraction to the left, the way they did when a word had dropped out of reach. "Cadbury World," she said at last, smiling over the gap she'd just shown him. "She's never been. I've been four times and it's still good. They give you free chocolate at the end."

"Tell Priya I said hi."

"Will do. Eat a vegetable."

"Night, Farah."

"Night, Yu."

The screen went dark. Yusuf sat with the mug cooling in his hands and counted backward through the call: jars, Cadbury World, the half-beat before hopeless. Two words had gone missing this month. Last month there had been one.

There was no treatment. There were only papers full of might and could. The one he kept going back to, fourteen months old now, had a sentence highlighted in yellow: high-resolution neural maps from enhanced systems might trace the degenerative pathway closely enough to target it. Meridian owned most of those maps. Their reply to his access request still sat starred in his inbox with a licensing quote on page two that would've gutted a small lab.

He reopened the laptop. Today's correlation file waited on the desktop. The incident portal loaded its usual boxes: anomaly class, affected athlete, source dataset, retention authorization. He could fill the first two. The third led straight to the hidden directory. The fourth asked why he'd been keeping other people's raw physiology in the drawer beside his tea.

He opened the encrypted drive, dragged the correlation file into the archive, and watched it vanish into the dated folder. Then he closed the incident form blank, ejected the drive, put it back in the drawer, and turned off the light.
