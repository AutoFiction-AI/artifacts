# Chapter 5

Kai's left ankle clicked when he crouched into the starting blocks, the natural kind, a tendon snapping over bone. His right ankle adjusted a fraction later. Synthetic muscle tightened to set the joint before he'd finished settling into the pedals. He'd mostly stopped noticing the difference. Today it registered faintly, like spotting the same misspelled word on a sign he passed every morning.

Lane three. The indoor track had its own atmosphere: the rubber smell of the surface, the dry high-altitude air that sat in the back of his throat, the overhead lights throwing a flat white glare across the oval. The ceilings were high and packed with acoustic dampening, which took the sharp edge off footfalls without killing the echo the room always kept. Two weeks at Kirchberg had been enough for his lungs to stop complaining, but each morning's first deep breath still carried a small sting at the bottom of the inhale.

Six other sprinters were warming up. Novak on the far lane, doing strides with a gait so bilaterally perfect it looked unreal. Priya Chandrasekaran near the curve, shaking out her hamstrings, her right leg extending and retracting with the evenness of a fresh calibration. Two athletes Kai knew by face but not by name were jogging easy laps. Behind the glass partition at the hundred-meter mark, two coaches sat with tablets on their knees, watching telemetry.

The start tone sounded. He drove out of the blocks.

The first fifty meters were the part that still felt like running. His feet hit rubber, knees drove forward, and the initial acceleration built in his quads with the old familiar burn, lactic acid and effort and muscle doing exactly what he told it to do. His arms pumped. His breathing locked into sprint rhythm, short exhales timed to footstrikes. The grafts were still in the background then, waiting for the point where his body crossed its natural limit. For those first seconds, the effort belonged to him.

Then the assist came in and the work smoothed out. There was a seam somewhere around sixty meters, impossible to pin down, where his own power handed off to the helped version. His stride lengthened by a centimeter he hadn't chosen. His arm swing adjusted. Elbows tucked. Hands loosened. The burn in his quads changed texture and spread out, managed now, load redistributed before fatigue could bunch in one place. He was still running. The running had started doing itself.

Eighty meters. Ninety. The visual enhancement sharpened. Lane markings snapped into hard clean lines. The far wall came forward with too much detail while the edges of the track fell away into blur. Everything outside the lane ahead lost importance.

A hundred meters. The curve. His body leaned into it at an angle his natural balance had never found on its own, banking with a precision he'd never had before. The wall on his left slid by, grey foam over concrete block, close enough to touch if he'd reached out. The padding was meant for bodies already slowing down. It wasn't meant for a sprinter still driving.

A hundred and twenty. A hundred and thirty. His time was good. He could feel that without looking at a clock, the way a driver knows speed from engine pitch.

A hundred and forty.

His left foot struck the track and the track dropped out of him.

It wasn't darkness or silence. It was just gone. Sight vanished so completely even black never arrived. Sound went with it. The map of his own body, shoulders and jaw and knees and the tilt of his head, got ripped out in the same instant. One stride he had a body. The next he was only the thinking left behind.

*I was running.*

*I can't feel my legs.*

*I can't feel anything.*

The thoughts came clear and useless. They had nowhere to land. Nothing grounded him. No breath filled his lungs. No weight sat in his chest. He tried to open his hands and found no hands. He tried to stop running and there was no running from where he'd been dropped. Only the hard certainty that the body he couldn't reach had kept going.

His body was still sprinting.

He knew it without feeling any of it. His legs were still cycling. Arms pumping. Lungs hauling air. The bridge had locked the sprint into his muscles, and the stride kept its shape after he was gone.

He tried to stop. He threw the order as hard as he could inside his own head. *Stop. Slow down. Stop.* It went nowhere. There was nothing between the thought and the legs now. He had no grip on them, no line down to them.

Time had no size in there. No pulse to count, no breath, no footfalls. Only math. The curve. The wall on the curve. Padding built for cooldown collisions, not this speed. He didn't know if the wall was half a second away or already at his shoulder.

He pictured the crash response anyway. Chin down. Shoulder turned. Arms in. The picture stayed in his head. Nothing in his neck or ribs or hands heard it.

Then the world hit him.

Sound came back as a thud inside his skull. Then his right shoulder slammed into the wall. Foam flattened. Concrete took the rest. The joint jumped out of place with a wet grind, shoved forward and down until his arm no longer fit his own socket.

Then the track. He spun off the wall and his face scraped across the rubber. The surface was fine grit built for traction. It peeled skin off his cheek and the skin above his left eye, then packed black crumbs into the raw streaks. Blood came fast, hot at first, then cold in the dry air.

His left hand shot out. Reflex or the last of the stride still burning through him, he didn't know. The wrist folded under him and broke with a clean crack just above the joint. White pain ran up his forearm and stopped under the elbow.

He ended on his side and then his front, twisted between the two. The overhead lights glared. Blood ran across his left eye and turned the fluorescent panels into red-smeared halos. His right shoulder sat wrong under the skin, too far forward, every small shift sending a grinding pulse through his chest. His left wrist was already swelling around the break.

His legs were still twitching.

They weren't running anymore. They were firing in scraps. Quadriceps bunched and let go. Calves jumped. His spikes scratched the rubber in short useless kicks. Whatever had kicked the stride loose was still sputtering through the bridge.

He watched his own thighs jump and couldn't do anything about it. Lying on the track in blood, shoulder out, wrist broken, he watched his feet scrape and kick as if they were trying to finish the rep. The legs were his. The command wasn't.

People were already running toward him. Novak reached him first. Kai saw blue and white shoes stop two feet from his face. Someone shouted for medical. The coaches behind the glass had stood up and abandoned their tablets. Through the blood in his eye he could see Priya at the edge of the far lane, hands hanging at her sides, watching with the stillness of someone looking at a body and thinking of her own.

"Don't move him," a voice said somewhere above him. "Wait, don't move him."

He wasn't trying. He lay there and watched his left leg kick once more, the spike leaving a grey scuff on the track, and then stop.

The medical team arrived with a backboard and a cervical collar. A woman from Petra Engel's staff dropped beside him, broad-shouldered, cold hands, the calm efficiency of someone trained for exactly this and still surprised to need it at a morning sprint session.

"Can you hear me?"

"Yeah."

"Tell me what hurts."

"Shoulder's out. Right one. Left wrist is broken." His own voice came back flat. The joke reflex wasn't there.

"Can you feel your feet?"

The twitching had stopped. What remained was his own body reporting in: toes, ankles, the deep used ache in muscles that had done work without permission. "Yeah. I feel them."

She gave the smallest nod, as if filing the answer.

They rolled him onto the backboard. His shoulder shifted and he made a short closed-mouth sound, choosing it over the scream that wanted out. Five other athletes were standing in a loose semicircle now. Novak had folded his arms. Priya still hadn't moved from the far lane. The track was quiet except for the medical staff, the dampening overhead swallowing the noise and turning the whole scene soft and wrong.

Blood dripped from his face onto the board in small red beads. His cheek burned with ground-in rubber. The cut above his eye kept leaking into his lashes and tinting the edge of the world.

They carried him down the corridor between the track and the medical wing. Three minutes of fluorescent light and ventilation hum, ceiling panels sliding past at other people's walking speed. Kai stared up and kept trying to find the edge of the gap. There wasn't one. One stride he was running, the next he was nowhere, then his shoulder hit concrete and the whole world slammed back in.

The thirty-one seconds in between hadn't been dark. Dark was still a thing. This had been less than that.

---

The medical bay smelled like isopropyl and cold metal trays. They reduced his shoulder first. Two techs held him while a third worked the arm, patient and mechanical, until the joint slipped back where it belonged. The pop was sickening. It hurt in a new way once it was home, as if the shoulder remembered being out and resented the trip back.

"Shit," Kai said, and that was all.

They braced the wrist next. The bones were out of line, and when the technician pressed along the break the pain told him exactly where it had failed. They locked the joint in a rigid splint and left his fingers free. It would need imaging. The grafts would make the bone knit fast, which didn't mean it would knit right.

A doctor Kai hadn't met cleaned the grit out of the abrasions with saline that stung clean and deep. Then he stitched the cut above Kai's left eye, five sutures, neat and quick.

"You'll have a small scar here," the doctor said.

"Sure."

Kai lay back and let them finish. The fluorescent panels overhead were the same as the corridor, the same as every clinical room in the building, flat light that showed everything and warmed nothing. His shoulder throbbed in deep slow pulses. His wrist sat under a constant pressure with sharp flares whenever his fingers moved. Air touched the raw skin on his cheek and it burned.

The door opened and Yusuf came in with his tablet and a data cable. His face had the look Kai knew from calibration sessions, concentration pulled tight, eyes narrowed behind his glasses, an engineer already arguing with the data in his head.

"How bad?" Yusuf asked, dragging a chair to the bedside.

"Shoulder's back in. Wrist is broken. Face is rough."

"Yeah, I can see the face. Can I plug in?"

Calibration shorthand. He wanted system logs, bridge data, graft telemetry, visual records, everything the hardware had kept track of while Kai had been locked out of himself.

"Go ahead."

Yusuf leaned over him, found the port at the base of his skull, and clicked the cable in. His hands were steady the way they always were. Then he sat back and started reading.

Kai watched him read. He couldn't see the screen itself, only its glow in Yusuf's lenses. Timestamps. Metrics. Scrolling blocks of system output. Yusuf's expression tightened around the eyes and then went still.

"What happened at one-forty," Kai said.

"That's what I'm looking at." Professional voice, clean and measured. He scrolled, stopped, scrolled back. "Your visual processing dropped to zero at the one-forty mark. Full shutdown. It wasn't a fade."

"It wasn't just visual."

"No, I can see that. Body mapping went at the same timestamp. Auditory input—" He checked the screen. "Forty-two milliseconds later. So everything, basically. All at once." He scrolled down. "But the motor output didn't stop."

"My legs didn't stop."

"No." Yusuf was still looking at the screen. "The bridge was running a stored sprint routine off the autonomous layer. Your grafts just kept going." He glanced up from the tablet. "Didn't need you there for that."

The equipment in the room hummed softly.

"How long was I out?"

"Thirty-one seconds." Yusuf pulled up another display. "Sensory return at one-seventy. Impact at one-seventy-three."

"I know where the wall is."

"I know you do." His finger moved again, faster now. He opened another window, frowned, and then stopped completely. "Hold on."

"What?"

Yusuf stood, still tethered to Kai by the cable, and moved to the wall terminal. The larger screen split into panes of logs and sync records. From the table Kai could make out athlete names down the left column and the same block of amber digits repeating beside them.

"That's not just your box," Yusuf said. He was half talking to Kai, half to the screen. "This is the central calibration server. Shared handshake logs. Last forty-eight hours." He tapped the first name. "Your session." Then the next. "Novak, eighteen-oh-three last night." Another. "Hoffman, twenty-oh-nine." Another. "Priya, six-eleven this morning." He looked over his shoulder. "Same corrupted package hash on every sync." He turned back to the screen. "Same fault signature in the sensory layer. And the motor offset—" He pulled up another pane. "Yeah, that too."

Kai pushed himself an inch off the pillow before his shoulder shut that down. "What does that mean?"

"It's spreading." Yusuf zoomed into a column of red and amber timestamps. "Your blackout is real, the corruption's real, and every time someone syncs with this network the hash copies itself across." He exhaled through his nose. "I don't know yet if everyone ends up where you did or if some of them just stay at drift. Timing noise." He pulled up one more pane. "But it's in all of them already."

"That's a virus."

Yusuf didn't answer right away. He stood with his hands braced on the terminal. His glasses had slid down his nose and he hadn't pushed them back up.

"It behaves like one," he said. "Self-replicating firmware exploit, that's—" He stopped. Started again. "I'd need a proper diagnostic dump before I put that in writing. But it copies itself, it propagates through the network, it—" He took his hands off the terminal. "It's already everywhere."

The room went very quiet.

Kai looked past Yusuf at the names on the screen. Novak. Priya. People he'd been on the track with an hour ago. People whose bodies were running on the same infrastructure that had just cut him out and kept going.

"Can you fix it?"

"I don't know yet." Professional smoothness was gone now. "We need to—look, nobody trains. Nobody calibrates. Nobody plugs into this network again until I've mapped the scope. Every handshake is another chance for it to copy."

"Every athlete."

"Everyone who's been calibrated here. Which is everyone." He finally pushed his glasses back into place. His fingers weren't steady. "I need Petra and the facility director. We lock the place down now or somebody else hits a wall."

Kai closed his eyes. The darkness behind them was ordinary, chosen. He could feel his eyelids, the stitch pull above his left eye, the dried blood on his lashes, the table under his skull, the weight of his own legs beneath the sheet. He opened his eyes again.

Yusuf unclipped the cable from the port at Kai's neck. The click was small and familiar.

"Get some rest," he said.

He took the tablet and went out. The door shut behind him.

Kai looked down the length of the exam table. The sheet over his legs rose in two low ridges. Both feet pointed where they were supposed to point. Still.

He pressed the call button with his good hand.

When the speaker answered, he said, "Could someone stay in here a bit?"

He kept his eyes on his feet and waited for the door.
