# Chapter 8

The Meridian delegation arrived on a Tuesday morning, three black SUVs grinding up the mountain road through overnight snow. Kai watched them from the cafeteria windows while he ate breakfast one-handed, fork in his right, left wrist braced and too swollen to grip anything smaller than a coffee mug. The vehicles pulled into the loading bay with coordinated timing, one after another, as if they'd practiced the spacing. Three people climbed out of the first: two men in dark coats and a woman in a grey wool suit who crossed the icy concrete without slipping, a leather portfolio held flat against her chest.

"That's Jin-seo Park," Tomas said across the table, leaning forward to follow Kai's gaze. "Athlete liaison. She was at the Seoul orientation last year." He paused with his spoon over a bowl of porridge. "She's all right. She actually listens."

Kai set his fork down. His wrist throbbed under the brace, a deep pulse matched to his heartbeat. The bone kept knitting on a schedule the grafts had set without asking him. He could feel the repair happening, which was its own specific wrongness: a low grind inside the bone, something being filed into shape from within. The stitched cut across his left cheekbone had stopped hurting days ago but still tugged when he chewed.

Outside, the sky was the flat grey of late February at 2,100 meters, snow stacked along the ridgelines in wind-carved drifts. The second and third SUVs emptied out behind the first: technicians, lawyers, or whatever Meridian sent when it wanted to remind people who ran the network inside their nerves.

Tomas watched with a different expression, attentive and measuring, the look of someone keeping track of people who could turn a season on or off. "They don't usually send this many," he said.

"No."

"You think it's about the virus?"

Kai finished his coffee. "Yeah, Tomas. I think it's about the virus."

---

An hour later, thirty-four athletes and most of the Kirchberg senior staff filled the second-floor conference room. The space had been rearranged overnight. Training boards were stacked against the wall. The usual tables were gone, replaced by rows of chairs facing a long table at the front. A projection screen glowed with Meridian's logo. Printed binders waited at every seat. So did bottled water stamped with the Meridian mark, which had not appeared anywhere in the facility before that morning. Someone had carried those bottles up the mountain on purpose.

The fluorescent lights buzzed at a frequency Kai had stopped hearing weeks ago. He sat near the right wall, three rows back, his chair angled so he could see the door and the front table at once, a habit left over from the days after the crash. Lena was two rows ahead, hair still damp from the morning swim, the faint chlorine smell that never fully left her. Margot stood against the far wall with her arms crossed, watching the room with flat coaching attention. Yusuf sat in the front row beside Dr. Engel. His hands were folded. Petra's back was straight, mouth thin, the posture of someone who had been briefed and hated what she heard.

Jin-seo Park stood behind the table. She was smaller than the job made you expect: compact, dark hair cut at the jaw, no jewelry except a slim watch she touched once before she started. She waited for the room to settle without asking for silence. When it did, she opened with Kai.

"I know some of you," she said. "Several from Seoul. Others from your files, which isn't the same thing." She let that sit for a beat. "Twelve days ago, one of you hit a wall at sprint speed because his body kept running after his senses cut out. I've read the medical report. I've seen the telemetry. What I'm about to show you predates Mr. Brennan's crash by eight months. Meridian has been tracking this vulnerability for eight months. The disclosure moved because of what happened to Mr. Brennan."

She glanced at Kai. The room followed her glance. He felt attention settle on the stitched cheekbone, the brace, the shoulder he still kept a fraction too high. A Hungarian weightlifter two seats ahead turned all the way around to look at him, then faced front again.

"Eight months," the weightlifter said. "You sat on it for eight months."

"The initial detection was eight months ago," Jin-seo said. "Characterizing the virus took the rest of that time. The timeline was bad. Here's what we have now."

She tapped the screen. A schematic filled the wall: the calibration network that linked every enhanced athlete in the building, clean blue lines and white nodes, dressed up to look reassuring. Kai had seen rougher versions on Yusuf's whiteboards, coffee-ringed and half erased. This one looked built for an investor call.

"The virus propagates through the shared calibration network," Jin-seo said. "Routine calibrations, firmware syncs, diagnostics. Every time the network opens a transmission window, the corruption has a path. It targets the firmware layer that governs sensory processing. When it triggers, you lose everything. Sight. Hearing. Proprioception. Spatial orientation. Total blackout. The autonomous subsystems in your enhancements do not stop because you stop perceiving them. Your legs keep driving. Your arms keep stroking. Whatever output the system was running, it keeps running."

No one spoke. The silence had pressure in it. Thirty-four people keeping questions under their tongues. Someone shifted in a chair and the creak sounded too loud.

"We've developed a firmware patch that neutralizes the virus's replication mechanism and restores stable sensory processing. Installation takes about four hours per athlete in the calibration bay. Dr. Qadir's team would handle the procedure." She nodded at Yusuf. He did not nod back. "In controlled simulation, the patch eliminated the blackout trigger in every scenario Meridian tested."

"What's the price?" Margot asked from the wall.

Jin-seo turned to her. "The patch requires full read-write access to the firmware layer of your enhancement system. Permanent access. The installation rewrites the base architecture of sensory processing, and Meridian would retain that access in order to maintain compatibility with future updates."

"Access to what, exactly?"

Jin-seo picked up one of the binders and turned to a marked page. "I'll read it directly. Better that than a cleaned-up version."

She looked down at the text.

"'In consideration of the Installation and ongoing maintenance of the Meridian Apex Enhancement Stability Protocol, the Athlete hereby grants to Meridian BioSystems, its successors and assigns, an irrevocable, transferable license to all biometric, genetic, neural, and physiological data generated by or through the Enhancement System, including but not limited to real-time performance metrics, neurological activity patterns, genetic expression data, and baseline physiological markers. This license shall survive termination of any sponsorship agreement, retirement from competitive athletics, or deactivation of the Enhancement System.'"

She set the binder down and kept her eyes on the room.

The first voice came from the second row. A Swedish cyclist, narrow face gone red at the ears. "Transferable means what? You can sell it?"

"It means Meridian can sublicense data to research partners, medical institutions, and other authorized entities under the standard data-sharing framework."

"So you can sell it."

"The framework includes restrictions on who can access it," Jin-seo said, and another athlete cut across her.

"Can you or can't you?" another athlete said.

"Answer him," somebody else snapped.

Jin-seo took one breath. "Yes. Meridian can sell or sublicense the data."

The room moved without getting louder. Heads turned. A chair leg scraped. Kai watched the back of Lena's head. She had not moved. Her hands lay flat on her thighs, still in that full-body stillness she went to when she was forcing herself to keep processing.

Aïssatou Diop spoke from near the front. She did not raise a hand. "If I don't sign and I go to Doha, what am I doing? Getting into the blocks and hoping I stay able to see lane three?"

"The Apex Games will let you compete without the patch," Jin-seo said.

Aïssatou gave a short, ugly laugh. "That's not what I asked."

Jin-seo nodded once. "If you compete unpatched, the blackout risk remains. In sprint conditions, on apparatus, in the pool, on the bike, the outcome could be fatal."

Dr. Engel leaned forward. "The medical assessment supports that," she said. Her voice stayed careful, each word weighed. "The blackout risk under maximal exertion is real. I have reviewed the clinical data. I cannot speak to the contract. I can speak to the danger."

That was enough for the first wave. A German javelin thrower with a pale integration scar at his collarbone opened his binder, turned to the signature page, and signed as fast as if he were approving a hotel bill. He shut the binder and shoved it to the edge of his seat.

Two more followed. Then four. Then three together in the back, as if they had decided sometime between fatal and irrevocable and saw no point stretching it out. The scratch of pens was a small sound, but it carried. By the time the room spoke again, twelve binders had been pushed forward.

---

The remaining athletes sat with binders on their laps or open in their hands. The room had split cleanly enough that Kai could see it from his chair. One side had signed and was trying not to look at the paper anymore. The other still kept touching pages, turning back, checking clauses, as if there might be a version in there that was less final.

Adewale, a middle-distance runner from the Nigerian team, lifted his binder off his knees and held it upright like evidence. "So when I'm forty-five and coaching kids on a cracked track somewhere, Meridian still has whatever came out of my head at twenty-seven?"

"The license survives retirement," Jin-seo said. "Yes."

He let the binder fall back to his lap. "Christ."

A Brazilian gymnast two rows over spoke before Jin-seo could continue. Chalk still clung to the cuticles of one hand. "Baseline physiological markers. That's not bars. That's me before bars. That's my blood."

"The license covers all data generated by or through the Enhancement System," Jin-seo said. "Because the system interfaces with baseline biology, the streams stay tangled in practice."

"Those are separate requirements," Yusuf said.

It snapped every head in the room toward him. He stayed seated. His hands were still folded, but now the knuckles were white.

Jin-seo looked at him. "Dr. Qadir."

"Emergency firmware access is one requirement," Yusuf said. His voice was too level, which meant he was working hard at it. "Permanent rights to baseline biological data are another. Those are different technical problems."

One of the Meridian men at the wall shifted his weight. Jin-seo lifted a hand without looking at him and he stopped.

"On a clean system design, maybe," she said. "On deployed Apex hardware across multiple facilities, with legacy builds and live support obligations, they stay tangled on this timeline."

Petra spoke before Yusuf could answer. "Dr. Qadir is correct that they are different categories," she said. "This contract is broader than the technical requirement. The blackout risk will still be here while people negotiate a narrower one."

Several people who had already signed pulled their binders back into their laps. Somebody in the back said, "Then why is it written like this?" Another voice came over it: "Can I take mine back?" A third said, "Don't touch them yet."

No one signed. No one even uncapped a pen.

Jin-seo put her fingertips against the edge of the portfolio. For a second she looked like she was choosing between three versions of herself and not trusting any of them.

"In 2034," she said, "my brother was in the Asia-Pacific qualifiers. Triathlon. Meridian system. Same hardware generation some of you are still on." She looked at the room, not the table. "During the cycling stage his firmware went into cascade failure. One leg fired full extension and the other locked. Forty kilometers an hour on wet pavement."

The room went still enough that Kai could hear the projector fan.

"He went over the bars and hit shoulder first, then head, then the bike came down on him. Broke his neck. Three ribs. Pelvis." Her voice stayed even. The corporate polish was gone. "He stayed alive for nine hours after the crash. The cascade was still firing in his legs when they got him to the trauma unit. They strapped him down because the muscles kept kicking against the bed. No one there could reach the base layer to shut it off. Meridian kept the authorization code on a corporate channel. The nearest technician with access was in Singapore."

She drank from her water bottle and set it back down with care.

"Jun-ho died in Busan at two in the morning while an engineer was still in the air over the South China Sea." She looked at Yusuf when she said the next part, then at the room again. "So yes. Emergency access and data ownership are different things. Meridian ties them together because it wants to keep everything. I brought these binders up the mountain anyway because the patch is real and the blackout is real and I watched what no access looks like in a hospital room."

Aïssatou's fingers stopped drumming on her binder. The Swedish cyclist looked down at his own signature as if he might still be able to smudge it. No one said anything.

Margot had not changed position at the wall, but her jaw had locked hard enough to show in profile. Yusuf stared at the schematic on the screen as if he could peel the system open by looking at it. Petra lowered her eyes to the table and pressed her thumb once against the seam of her notebook.

Then Kai heard paper slide against paper. A pen cap clicked.

He knew from the drag of paper and the click of the cap that it was Tomas. He looked anyway.

Tomas turned past the signature line once, then back to it. His thumb rested for a second on the small-print cross-reference at the bottom of the page, the part that tied this consent to the rest of Meridian's sponsorship machinery. He signed in careful block letters, printed the date, and shut the binder.

Kai knew that logic. Meridian never left a decision in one document if it could wire it into five. Refusal would not stay inside the calibration bay. It would move into travel approval, housing, the stipend schedule, every checkbox buried in the contracts athletes stopped reading after the first month. Kai had savings, shoe-company money, old deals under other logos. Tomas had a first Games berth and the same blue M on every page that mattered.

Tomas set the binder on the empty seat beside him and folded his hands in his lap. He looked straight ahead. No accusation. No plea. Just the face of someone who had found the narrowest place the trap would let him stand.

The signing started again, slower at first. A swimmer in the front row. The Swedish cyclist who had demanded a yes or no. Two athletes near the back who had been whispering through the whole meeting. Then more once the sound of pens became ordinary again. The Meridian men did not move. They waited.

Kai watched Lena. She still had not opened her binder. Adewale had his shut under one palm. Aïssatou was staring at the signature page as if it were something physical she might be able to outrun. The Brazilian gymnast kept reading the paragraph about baseline markers, lips moving over the same three lines.

By the time Jin-seo checked her watch, only six binders were still closed.

"I'll be here through Thursday," she said. Her thumb touched the face of the watch, the same small check from the start. "If you need time, take it. If you want to ask questions privately, do that. No one is being marched to the calibration bay this morning."

She gathered her portfolio. The two men from Meridian rose and began collecting the signed binders with practiced efficiency. One wore thin surgical gloves. The latex flashed pale each time he bent to pick one up, and Kai had to look away.

His own binder lay open on his lap. At some point he had opened it without noticing. The signature line was blank. Below it, in smaller type, were the cross-references Tomas had touched: sponsorship compliance, support obligations, data-use addenda. Kai Brennan waited there in print, neat and harmless-looking.

His left wrist gave a deep, grinding throb inside the brace. Bone knitting too fast. Tissue pulling itself together under instructions he had never agreed to and could not revise. He closed the binder and pressed his good hand flat over the cover until the pulse in his wrist and the pulse in his palm stopped feeling like separate things.

Outside the conference room windows, the Alps stood white under a sky that was starting to clear. Wind peeled snow off the high ridges in thin sheets, something immense and indifferent moving just beyond the glass.
