# Chapter 11

The first time was in his room, because her room had a window that faced the floodlit outdoor track and neither of them wanted to see it.

His diagnostic display sat on standby, a dim green pulse on the wall, the numbers reduced to a single status bar. Heart rate, bridge latency, graft integration. All normal. He'd stopped checking it every hour about a week ago. That probably counted as progress.

Lena sat at the head of the bed with her knees drawn up and her phone facedown on the mattress. She'd come over after the pool. Her hair was still damp, the chlorine smell so constant on her that he only noticed it now when she was close. They'd been talking about nothing, a movie someone had put on in the common room, whether the cafeteria coffee was getting worse or they were all just adjusting to the altitude. The conversation had run down the way conversations do when neither person wants to leave but neither has a reason to stay that they're ready to name.

He was sitting at the other end of the bed. His left wrist was on his knee, the ache doing its low-grade thing, the bone reminding him it had been broken and reassembled on a schedule the rest of his body hadn't agreed to. The scar tissue on his cheek had smoothed to a pink shine. Six weeks ago he'd been bleeding on the track with his legs still twitching. Now he was sitting on a narrow institutional bed with a woman who smelled like chlorine, and the distance between those two states had no name he could find.

"You're staring at your wrist," Lena said.

"I'm not staring."

"You're looking at it the way you look at the display." She tilted her head toward the wall-mounted screen. "Checking."

He flexed his fingers, felt the ache shift from the wrist into the base of his thumb. "It does this thing where it almost feels normal, and then it reminds me it isn't."

"Almost normal," she said. "I know that one."

He looked at her. She was watching him with the kind of attention she usually gave to things she was trying to measure: the distance to a wall, the temperature of the water. Clinical, but not cold. Interested. He liked being looked at by her, which was a simple thing to feel and he let himself feel it.

"Come here," she said. Plain, certain, simply what she wanted.

He moved up the bed. The mattress was narrow, built for a single athlete sleeping alone, not for two people trying to figure out how much space to leave between them. They ended up close enough that he could feel the heat off her shoulder. She reached over and picked up his left hand, turned it over, ran her thumb along the inside of his wrist where the line from the brace had faded to a thin white mark. The touch registered twice. First her thumb, his skin, the pressure and warmth of it. Then the system's interpretation a fraction behind: temperature mapped to two decimal places, pressure distributed across his wrist's surface area, her pulse detectable through her fingertip. He was used to the echo. Three years. But he noticed it now, here, because the second signal carried information about another person's body that he had not asked for and did not want.

He kissed her. That was simple too. She tasted like the peppermint tea from the cafeteria and her lips were slightly chapped from the pool chemicals, and those were details that belonged to him, things he noticed, not things the system measured and reported back. She kissed him back, and for a few minutes nothing was complicated.

Then it got complicated, though only a little at first. He pulled her shirt over her head and she lifted her arms to help and it caught on her left ear and she said "ow" and laughed, and he laughed, and that was human enough. Her body was a swimmer's body, shoulders wider than her hips, muscle layered across her back in the specific architecture built by years of pull. He'd known this about her in the abstract. Touching it was different. His hand moved along her ribs and he could feel the individual muscles between the bones, the way her breathing expanded her ribcage more sideways than forward. The system processed this too, feeding him biomechanical data about her thoracic expansion pattern that he did not need and could not stop receiving. He needed to know that she was breathing and that his hand was on her body and that both of those things were good. The rest was noise.

She unbuttoned his shirt with the deliberate precision she brought to most tasks, one button, then the next, her fingers working efficiently, which made him smile because most people either fumbled with buttons or turned them into performance and Lena just undid them like they were a problem she intended to solve correctly. When the shirt was open she put her palm flat against his sternum and held it there. He didn't know what she was checking. Maybe just that he was warm.

"Your heart rate is elevated," she said, almost clinically.

"Yeah," he said. "That's happening."

She smiled. One of her small smiles, the kind that arrived and left without making a production of itself. He kissed the corner of it and felt her hand slide up to his shoulder, the right one, the one that had been dislocated, and when she pressed the joint he winced before he could stop himself.

"Still sore?"

"Little bit."

She lightened her grip but didn't move her hand. They were on their sides now, facing each other on the narrow mattress, and the logistics of the bed meant they were either touching everywhere or falling off. They touched everywhere.

He kissed the side of her neck and his mouth moved down to where her hair fell damp against her shoulder, and he pushed the hair aside. His thumb found the neural link port at the base of her skull, a small hard circle, body-warm, the engineered rim flush with the surrounding skin. She didn't flinch. It was as much a part of her body as the vertebrae below it. He traced the edge of it with his fingertip and the system told him the port's diameter and surface temperature and material composition, and he stopped listening to the system and kissed the skin beside the port instead, and she made a sound that was quiet and human and not available for diagnostic interpretation.

What happened next was not graceful because first times are not graceful, and the bed was too narrow, and at one point Kai's elbow hit the wall-mounted diagnostic display and it blinked from standby to active, throwing his vitals across the screen in green text: heart rate 118, neural bridge latency nominal, right shoulder ROM 94%. Lena reached over his arm and switched it off without looking at it, which was the most thoughtful thing anyone had done for him in months. Then it was just the dim light from the hallway under the door and the faint mountain dark through the window and their bodies figuring out the logistics of a bed that had never been designed for this.

She was on top of him because his shoulder couldn't take his weight for long, and her knees were on either side of his hips, and his hands were on her waist, and he couldn't pretend it was only data anymore.

The dual-track sensation was constant. Every point of contact arrived twice: the raw physical signal, then the system's cleaned-up version a fraction behind, like a translator who couldn't stop correcting the original. Her thighs against his hips, first the warmth and pressure and friction of skin, then the system's readout of contact area, force distribution, her muscle tension patterns mapped with clinical precision. He tried to stay with the first signal, the raw one, the one that belonged to him. It wasn't easy. The system didn't turn off because he wanted it to.

He'd never had sex with the echo before. Three years with the neural bridge and he had felt its doubling in every handshake, every training drill, every time someone bumped his shoulder in the cafeteria line. But here the doubling was different because the stakes of the raw signal were different. When she leaned down and kissed him, her hair fell against his face, wet at the tips, and the system reported the water's chlorine concentration and her exhaled CO2 level while his mouth was on hers. The clinical track ran underneath everything like closed captioning he couldn't disable, technically accurate, describing the same event and not the same event at all.

His hands were on her hips and she was moving and he was moving with her and his right hand, the hand that worked fine, that had never been broken, connected to the same spinal neural bridge that governed everything below his neck, shifted. He didn't tell it to. His fingers spread slightly, redistributed across her hip, the grip adjusting from loose to firm with a smoothness that recalibrated the contact for stability. The adjustment took less than a second. It was ergonomically perfect. His hand found the exact angle that held her weight evenly, that supported her movement without restricting it, and it did this the way the system did everything when it got ahead of him: efficiently, without effort, without him.

He kept moving because she was moving and his body had gotten very good at continuing to perform acts he had stopped commanding. But the system's signal was suddenly louder than the raw one, and the hand on her hip felt less like his action and more like a motion he was watching from inside his own arm.

His hand had touched another person's body with a competence he did not generate.

It lasted a second. Maybe two. His fingers on her hip, holding her with a precision that was not his, while the rest of him tried to locate the boundary between the grip he'd intended and the grip the system had decided on. He couldn't find it. The muscle was his muscle. The nerve was his nerve. The bridge connecting them just happened to be faster and better at this than he was.

He moved his hand deliberately, loosened the grip, put his palm flat against her side, pressed it there with conscious force. That was him. He felt the decision travel from his brain down his arm and arrive in his fingers with the slight delay that meant a human being had made it.

Lena's breathing changed. She slowed. He looked up at her face and found her studying him now, taking stock, as if she'd registered the adjustment and was cataloguing it.

"I'm OK," he said. It came out flatter than he meant it to.

She nodded and kissed him and they kept going, and for a while it was fine, or close to fine, or close enough that they could both decide to treat it that way.

Then it wasn't.

It happened in the middle of an ordinary moment, with nothing in it that warned him. She was beneath him, they'd shifted slowly around his shoulder, and her hands were on his back and her eyes were open and she was present, fully, looking at him with an attention that was specific and warm and hers. And then she wasn't.

There was no sound, no seizure, no visible malfunction. She went still. Her hands stayed on his back but the pressure behind them went flat, like someone had stopped squeezing a held object. Her eyes didn't close. They stayed open, stayed pointed at him, but the person behind them, the sharpness and precision and faintly clinical interest and the warmth under it, went away. What was left looked like her. It had her face, her body, her hands on his back. But the presence was gone. The eyes looking at him were looking without seeing, the way a window looks at a street.

Kai stopped. He was still above her, weight on his left arm, right shoulder aching, and the woman under him was physically unchanged and completely absent.

"Lena?"

Nothing. Her eyes were open. She was breathing. Her body was warm. No one was home.

Two seconds. Three. He didn't know what to do. Pull away, touch her face, call someone. The options lined up in his mind and stayed there, equally available, equally useless. He stayed where he was. His arms held his weight. His right shoulder sent a slow ache up into his neck. The staying was his. The holding was his. The hurting was his. He held onto that because those were decisions he had made.

Then she came back. He saw it happen. The eyes first, the quality of light behind them changing. Then her hands, which pressed into his back with renewed intention. Then her face, which registered where she was and what was happening and arranged itself into an expression that was not quite confusion and not quite fear, more like someone finding herself in a place she didn't remember entering.

"Hey," he said. His voice came out rough. "Hey. You went somewhere."

She blinked. Looked at him. Looked at her own hands on his back, as though confirming they were hers.

"How long?" she said.

"A few seconds."

She was quiet. He rolled off her, carefully, and lay beside her on the narrow bed. They weren't touching except at the shoulder, the mattress too small for distance but too narrow for pretense.

"A few seconds," she repeated, as if fixing the number in place.

"Yeah."

She stared at the ceiling. He stared at the ceiling.

She took a slow breath and released it. Then another. The rhythm of someone re-establishing inventory, checking that the body answered to the right name. He recognized it because he'd done the same thing in the medical bay after the blackout, the same deliberate breathing, the same careful audit of what was still there.

"I'm here," she said. To herself as much as to him.

"I know."

The hallway light under the door made a thin gold bar on the floor. Outside, wind moved against the window with the flat, constant pressure of altitude.

---

Neither of them slept. They put their clothes back on, not all of them, enough, and lay in the dark. The diagnostic display stayed off. The wind outside was steady and without opinion. Somewhere down the hall a door closed softly. The calibration bay's low hum was there if you listened for it, always there, the base frequency of a building designed around machines that tuned human bodies.

"Are you cold?" he said.

"A little."

He pulled the blanket higher over both of them. The fabric was the same thin institutional weave as everything else in the athletes' quarters, designed for function, not comfort.

Kai flexed his left hand under the blanket, opening and closing his fingers. The wrist ache was there. It was always there. It sat in the bone like a tenant who had paid up and wasn't leaving.

"I should tell you what it's like," Lena said. She was on her back with her hands folded on her stomach, her hair drying now, the chlorine smell thinning enough that he could catch the warmer smell of her skin. She said it the way she said most things, as a decision she'd made and was now carrying out.

"You don't have to."

"I know I don't have to. I want to tell you."

He waited. She was quiet for long enough that he thought she might have changed her mind, but she was composing, not retreating. Lena composed her sentences before she delivered them. He'd learned this.

"I don't pass out," she said. "Everyone asks that. They want it to be fainting. It isn't. When people faint, the body quits for a minute. Mine keeps going. It gets cleaner."

She turned her head to look at him. The dim light from the hallway caught the edge of her jaw.

"I'm there and then the part that chooses goes missing. The stroke keeps its shape. My hands do the correct thing. If someone speaks to me, my mouth answers. Fast. No hesitation. If the situation changes, the body changes with it. From outside it looks efficient. People think I'm concentrated. Then I'm back and a few seconds have been cut out."

Kai didn't say anything. His hand had stopped flexing.

"The worst part isn't being gone," she said, and her voice was steady in a way that cost her. "I don't feel that. The worst part is coming back and finding out my body was out in the world without me, and nobody could tell."

The wind gusted against the window. Something metallic ticked in the heating vent.

"Nobody," Kai said.

"No. If I'd come back and kept going tonight, you wouldn't have known. If my body had stayed on the same line, you wouldn't have known."

He wanted to say yes. He had felt the moment she left. But that wasn't the question. The question was whether he would have noticed once her body started moving again, or whether he would have stayed over her, touching a version of Lena that hit every mark and wasn't her.

"I don't know," he said.

She nodded. She didn't look hurt by that. She looked like she'd expected it, which was worse.

"What does it — what does that stretch feel like after?" he said.

"I don't feel it. That's the point. I only know it was there afterward. There's a cut. Usually it's only seconds, but time isn't what goes missing. Continuity does. One moment I'm in the pool and the next I'm at the wall and two laps are behind me. I remember them the way I remember race video. Accurate, but external."

"Margot said it's within acceptable parameters," Lena said. Her voice changed when she said Margot's name, going careful.

"You believe that?"

"No." Simple. Definite. A conclusion she'd measured against her own evidence.

They lay there. The bed was narrow and their shoulders were touching and the contact was nothing like what it had been half an hour ago. It was just contact. Two people in a small bed because neither one had left.

The heating vent clicked again. His stomach growled, which was stupid and perfectly timed and they both heard it.

"Sorry," he said.

"You didn't eat?"

"Half a protein bar. I got distracted."

She almost smiled. He could hear it in her breathing even if he couldn't see it in the dark. The moment was small and ordinary and he was grateful for it, proof that they were two people in a room and not two case files lying side by side.

"Can I tell you one thing?" Kai said. He hadn't planned to. She had given him the ugliest part of it, and he couldn't just lie beside her with that.

"Yes."

"About the blackout. Everyone knows about the crash. The shoulder, the wrist, the face." He touched the scar on his cheek without thinking about it. "That's just injury. I've been hurt before. The part I haven't said out loud is the rest."

He looked at the ceiling. It was flat and white and institutional and he focused on the seam where it met the wall because he needed the most boring line in the room while he said this.

"The blackout lasted thirty seconds. I was running the 200 and at the 140 mark everything dropped out. It wasn't darkness. In the dark your eyes are still trying. This was no eyes, no ears, no skin, no sense of which way was up. Just gone."

Lena said nothing. He could feel her attention beside him. He had watched that attention leave her face and come back.

"And then I came back. Floor. Blood in my eyes. Shoulder wrong. Wrist broken. And my legs—" He paused. "My legs were still going. The synthetic muscle. Still firing, still trying to run the sprint pattern. I was lying on the track with my face in my own blood and my legs were still in the race."

He heard her breathing change beside him. Shorter. More controlled.

"Yusuf showed me the telemetry afterward. That last stretch before the crash, the split was good. Steady. Clean. My body kept the lane all the way into concrete."

"And that was terrifying," Lena said. She said it as fact.

"Yeah." He could hear his own breathing. A little fast for someone lying still.

"Most days I tell myself the system is like good spikes. Help. That's all. Then tonight happens and it reaches first. Or the blackout happens and Yusuf shows me a clean split after. I look at that and all I can think is it kept going fine without me there."

He'd never put it that plainly before. Saying it made his chest go tight.

"Tonight," she said after a silence. "Your hand."

He let out a breath. "You felt it."

"Your grip changed on my hip. It got exact. The pressure landed in exactly the right place and stayed there."

"I didn't tell it to."

"No."

"It just found the angle and stayed there. I was still catching up."

She was quiet for a long time. Their shoulders were still touching. The wind kept pressing at the glass.

"That's not what happens to me," she said. "I go missing. You don't. You feel it while it's happening."

"Yeah."

"I thought that would make tonight impossible."

"Did it?"

She took her time. He let her. The ceiling was still boring and his wrist was still aching and the woman beside him was present, right now, in a way he could feel.

"No," she said. "But I felt it."

He reached for her hand and found it on the blanket between them. His fingers closed around hers. The touch registered in the usual way, the warmth and pressure first, then the system's precise measurement of her hand's temperature and the force he was exerting and the distribution of contact across both their palms. He held on to the first signal. The rough one. The imprecise one. The one that was his.

"It's doing it again," she said.

He felt it. His fingers had redistributed around hers, grip micro-correcting to equalize pressure. He hadn't told them to.

"Yeah."

He started to loosen his hold. Lena turned her hand over and laced their fingers together before he could pull away. His grip kept making small, unwanted corrections inside hers. She didn't comment on them again. The diagnostic display stayed dark. The hallway light held its thin gold bar across the floor. Outside, wind moved against the window with the constancy of altitude, and the mountains were behind the glass in the dark.

They lay there awake, feeling each correction as it came.
