# Chapter 7

The brace was off. Petra had cleared him to leave it off for short stretches, and the graft-driven repair had run six weeks too fast, leaving an itch deep in the bone where new tissue met old. The spot held a busy, needling pressure, his body hurrying through a timetable he hadn't agreed to.

Kai sat on the edge of his bed and put his left hand flat on the mattress.

He'd started three nights ago, after his hand went early for a glass of water and he caught the quick flutter in the rebuilt forearm. Since then he'd been trying to catch that flutter before the motion finished.

Index finger. He told himself to lift it, then waited. There was a gathering low in the synthetic muscle, too small for anyone else to see. He pressed the finger flat to the mattress and held. One beat. Two. The pressure stalled against him.

The finger lifted when he let it.

A thread of signal. Each night it got easier to feel, a faint touch gathering before the motion. He could find the place where the rebuilt muscle wanted to go ahead of him and meet it there.

His shoulder clicked as he reached for the wrist brace. The joint had started settling into place with engineered precision. The cuts on his cheek and brow were already down to thin pink lines. He was healing on graft time, being rebuilt around the damage.

He put the brace back on and went to dinner.

---

The cafeteria was half-empty at seven-thirty. Most of the athletes ate at six, their days partitioned into training blocks and recovery windows that left no room for eating late. The stragglers were support staff finishing shifts, a pair of physiotherapists arguing about ice bath protocols at a corner table, and Kai.

He took a tray through the line. Chicken breast, rice, steamed broccoli that had been in the warmer long enough to go soft. A cup of coffee that tasted like it had been brewed hours ago and reheated against its will. The windows along the far wall showed the mountains as dark cutouts against a sky holding its last blue, that deep alpine color February evenings produced when dusk seemed to start before anyone was ready for it.

Lena was sitting alone at one of the long tables near the windows. She had a plate of pasta half-finished and a book open beside her tray, though she wasn't reading it. She was looking at the dark shapes of the mountains through the glass.

Kai set his tray down across from her. "Hey."

"Hey." She shifted the book closer to her plate, making room. She didn't close it.

"What's that?"

She glanced at the cover as though she'd forgotten what she'd brought. "A novel. In German. I keep starting it."

"Good?"

"I don't know yet. I keep reading the same pages."

He didn't ask why. He could guess: the same blankness that had been eating her time since before his crash, the slips that swallowed minutes without leaving a mark. She wouldn't want him naming it here, under the cafeteria lights. He unwrapped his fork and started on the chicken.

They ate without talking for a while. The room gave them enough noise to hide inside, trays being cleared somewhere in the kitchen, the physiotherapists still going on about ice baths, the heating system working against the alpine cold on the other side of the glass. Kai's shoulder clicked when he raised the fork. He'd mostly stopped noticing it except when the room was quiet enough for the sound to travel.

"How's the wrist?" Lena asked.

"Getting there." He held it up, the brace bulky and medical-looking under the lights. "Petra says I can start grip exercises next week. Which is her way of telling me to stop doing them now."

"You were already doing them."

"I was messing with it. Probably doesn't count as exercises." He set the hand back on the table. "It's weird. The bone's basically done. Solid. It still feels unfinished, like somebody hit fast-forward and skipped the part where your body catches up."

Lena nodded. She pushed a piece of penne around her plate without eating it. "My mother broke her arm when I was twelve. Skiing. She wore the cast for eight weeks and complained about it every day. I remember thinking that sounded endless."

"It is endless when you're twelve."

"I know. I didn't understand that then."

Kai drank his coffee. It was worse than he'd expected, which was impressive given how low he'd already set the bar. "Where was that? The skiing."

"Engelberg. We went every February when I was young." She paused, then corrected herself. "My parents went. I went to training camp."

"Every February?"

"Every school holiday. The program ran blocks all year. Summer was Tenero for the outdoor pool. Winter was altitude work here or in Austria. Spring was a facility outside Munich for strength testing." She said it in sequence, like stops read off a board. "I was home ten days at Christmas and two weeks in August."

"Home being where?"

"Zug. My parents' apartment. But I'd been living at training sites since I was eight, so." She made a small closing motion with her hand. "The word catches when I say home."

He looked at her across the table. The overhead light flattened everyone a little, trays and faces and steam pressed into the same pale plane. At the base of her skull, where her hair was pulled up, the port caught his eye the way a scar did: part of her body, impossible to stop seeing once you'd noticed it. "Did you like it? The program?"

She looked down at her plate. "I liked swimming. The program..." She shook her head once. "No, that's too neat. My parents signed the Meridian developmental contract before I was born. Embryo screening, the link at four, calibration every six months. By the time I had opinions about any of it, all the choices had already happened."

"It was there from the start."

"Maybe. If so, she was only an idea."

Kai turned his coffee cup a quarter turn. "I had a before. Twenty-seven years of it. So when the system pulls first now, I know what it's cutting in front of." He glanced up. "If your link does that, maybe it started in October. Maybe it started when you were four."

Lena looked at him for a beat. "Yes. Close, anyway." She set her fork down. "When I was little, five or six, they had me in the rig twice a week. The technician would say, can you feel this, can you feel that. I always said yes. Maybe I felt it. Maybe I was just good at giving the efficient answer. I can't separate those now."

"Jesus."

She gave one shoulder the smallest lift. "It's what I have."

Kai split a broccoli stalk with the side of his fork and ate it.

"Where's your Lafayette?" he asked after a minute.

She frowned.

"Lafayette, Indiana. Sorry. My version of home." He leaned back a little. "Fields behind my parents' house. Soybeans forever. Summer storms you could watch coming for an hour before they hit. And the high school track where I first ran, cracked rubber and floor wax and water stains in the ceiling. That's the picture I get."

"I don't think I have that."

"Nothing?"

She took a moment. "There was an old pool in Zug before they renovated. Windows on the east wall. In the morning the light went through the water and made bands on the tiles. I liked that." She glanced at him, then away. "I remember one room. Homesickness feels bigger than that."

"Maybe that's close."

"No. Not really." Her voice stayed gentle. "You miss weather. Your school. Probably the road out to it. I miss one room because the light was good in the morning. That's different."

Kai finished his rice. The cafeteria had thinned to almost no one, a janitor starting to mop the far end of the floor.

"My mom sends me old race clips sometimes," he said. "From college, from before the rebuild. Last week she paused one on my second step out of the blocks and said, there, right there."

"Right there what?"

"That's the problem. She never quite says." He smiled without much force. "And, fair, my push-off angle is different now. About three degrees. The grafts changed that. But she's not really doing film breakdown. She's looking for the version of me she still recognizes."

"What does she think you are?"

He shrugged. The shoulder clicked. "Fixed. That's her word. 'After they fixed you.' Which, sure, I was broken. Spine compressed, both knees shot. I couldn't make it to the mailbox without my legs folding. But fixed sounds like there should've been a last appointment. There never was one. The system still tweaks itself. I still feel it."

Lena took a sip of coffee and made no face at all. "My parents use the word 'complete.' They say the program made me complete."

"Yeah," Kai said. "That's worse."

Someone at a nearby table was having a loud conversation about lactate thresholds, the numbers floating across the room without landing on anything. Kai finished his chicken. Lena closed her book and stacked it on her tray.

"Do you want to walk?" she said.

"Outside? It's freezing."

"I know."

"Yeah. OK."

---

The cold hit them like a wall. They'd grabbed jackets from the row of hooks by the cafeteria's side door, the heavy insulated ones the facility kept for anyone moving between buildings in winter, and even through the padding the air found every gap. Minus twelve, maybe colder. Lena felt it in her sinuses first, a sharp chemical cleanliness that the building's recirculated air never achieved.

The path from the cafeteria to the residential wing was cleared and salted, but beyond the buildings the snow was deep, banked into drifts that the maintenance crew had pushed to the margins with their plows. The sky was black and enormous. The stars came through in a density that the facility's light pollution couldn't entirely wash out, though the sodium glow from the training halls yellowed the nearest snow and faded to nothing within a hundred meters.

They walked past the residential wing and onto the access road that led down toward the valley. The road was plowed but icy in stretches, and Lena watched her footing with the automatic caution of someone who'd been trained since childhood to protect her body from unnecessary injury. The mountains were shapes at this hour rather than scenery, massive dark presences that blocked the stars where they met the horizon, their ridgelines rough and irregular against the sky.

Kai walked with his hands shoved into his jacket pockets, the wrist brace making his left side bulge. His breath made small clouds that the cold tore apart before they traveled a meter.

"I always forget how many stars there are up here," he said.

"You can see the Milky Way in summer. When it's clear."

"I haven't been here in summer."

"It's different. The fields around the facility go green and there are wildflowers on the lower slopes. The snow goes except on the peaks." She heard herself doing it again, describing the landscape by training season and facility use. The mountains had been here before the buildings. They'd be here after.

They reached a point where the road curved and the view opened: the valley below, dark peaks across the gap, the town of Meiringen visible as a scatter of warm lights against the black floor of it. The air smelled like snow and pine resin and cold stone.

"Can I ask you something?" Kai said.

"Yes."

"When did you first notice the desync?"

She'd answered that before, for Margot, for the diagnostics, for herself in the inventory she ran each night in bed. But Kai wasn't asking for data.

"In October. Maybe late October." She tucked her hands deeper into her pockets. "I came off a wall and hit a clean gap where the turn should've been. I had the breath after it, I had the next stroke, but the turn itself had dropped out."

"Was that scary?"

"The fear came later." She drew the cold air in through her nose and let it out slowly. "After. When I looked at the telemetry."

"But you knew it happened."

"Because the data said it did." She folded her arms tighter. "And the bad part is the data liked it. The lengths I barely remember are cleaner. Straighter line. Better tempo. When I'm less there, I swim better."

Kai let out a breath that might have been a laugh in warmer air. "Fuck."

"Yes."

He opened his mouth, closed it again, and stamped one foot once on the ice before looking back down into the valley. Headlights moved along the river road below, tracing the bend and disappearing behind a stand of trees.

"My mother sent me a care package last week," Lena said. "Chocolate from the shop in Zug, and socks, and a letter. The letter said she was proud of how I'm handling things. I don't know what she thinks I'm handling. She doesn't know about the desync. She just knows there was a virus and the facility went to restricted operations for a while."

"Parents fill in the blanks."

"My mother fills them in with optimism. She's very Swiss about problems. Everything will be organized and then it will be resolved."

Kai laughed, brief and real, cut short by the cold. "My mom fills them in with worst-case scenarios and prayer. She lit a candle at St. Mary's when I called about the crash. I said, Mom, the wrist is already healing. She said, 'The candle isn't for the wrist, Kai.'"

"What was it for?"

"She didn't say. I figured she was praying that whatever comes out the other side of all this still feels like her son."

The wind picked up, coming off the ridge to the north. It carried a fine grit of ice crystals that stung where they hit exposed skin. Lena turned her face from it and Kai did the same, which put them both facing the facility, the cluster of buildings lit up against the mountainside, warm and institutional and sealed against everything outside. From this distance, in the dark, the whole complex looked small. Concrete and glass stuck to the side of a mountain that had no use for it.

They stood for a while without talking. The stars were thick overhead, the Milky Way a faint smear across the center of the sky, barely visible through the facility's residual glow. The air was sharp enough to hurt when she breathed deeply, and the silence had a solidity to it that the building's constant mechanical hum never allowed. Out here, the quiet belonged to the mountain.

Kai pulled his left hand from his pocket and flexed it. The brace creaked in the cold. Lena watched him open and close his fingers, slow and deliberate, waiting for the early jump.

"Your hand," she said.

He glanced over. "I keep doing that."

"What are you checking?"

He took a second. "The synthetic muscle from the reconstruction jumps early sometimes. Not much. Just enough that I can feel it if I pay attention." He flexed again, slower this time. "I've been trying to catch that little kick and hold it off."

"Can you?"

"A little. Or I think I can. Depends whether slowing it down counts."

There was nothing to see in the starlight but the brace and the stiff fingers. Still, the intent in the movement was familiar. She brought one hand up and rested it against the scarf at the base of her neck, where the port sat underneath.

"I wonder if mine has that too," she said. "Before the blank. If there's a lead-in and I keep missing it."

"Maybe."

"It's a different system."

"Yeah. But it still gets there ahead of you."

She kept her hand there another second, thinking of turns, of wall tiles, of the half-second before the world went missing. "I'll try it tomorrow."

The wind came again, harder. The cold had passed through bracing into plain pain, the kind that went through insulated fabric and settled in the joints. Lena's fingers ached inside her pockets and the skin across her cheekbones was numb and drawn tight.

"Now we should go in," she said.

"Yeah."

They turned and walked back along the access road, their boots crunching on the salted ice. The facility grew as they approached, its windows bright squares of warm light, the sound of the heating system becoming audible as they closed the distance, that low mechanical hum that only registered once you'd been out in real silence. At the side door, Kai pulled the handle and warm air poured out around them, thick and almost sweet after the thin cold.

Lena stepped inside. The corridor was empty and fluorescent-lit. Linoleum floor, the smell of cleaning solution and residual cafeteria grease. She pulled off the heavy jacket and hung it on its hook. Her hands were red and stiff, slow to close.

"Thanks for the walk," she said.

"Thanks for freezing with me."

At the point where the residential wing branched, they both stopped. The wind pressed once against the outer door behind them.

"Good night, Kai."

"Night."

She went left. At her room door her fingers slipped once on the handle, then caught. When she got it open and glanced back, he was still standing where she'd left him, brace hand half-raised like he'd thought of one more thing and lost it. Then he went right.
