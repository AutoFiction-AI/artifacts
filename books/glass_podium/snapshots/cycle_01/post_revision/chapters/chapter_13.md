# Chapter 13

The calibration rig's contact surface had gone from cold to warm to tacky with Lena's sweat in the first two hours. By the fourth, the dampness against her back had stopped mattering. Overhead, the fluorescent panels hummed at a frequency the system would normally have filtered out, a constant sixty-hertz buzz. Yusuf had disabled her automatic sensory filtering before the procedure began, and now every ambient sound arrived untouched: the equipment drone, the click of his keyboard, the thin whistle of ventilation through the ceiling vents.

"Stage four," Yusuf said. He took off his glasses and cleaned them on the hem of his shirt. He always did that at transitions, as if each new stage deserved a cleared view of the monitors. "This one remaps the motor pattern library. It's the longest stage. Ninety minutes, maybe more."

"What will it feel like?"

"Honestly, I don't know exactly. The simulation modeled pressure behind the eyes and at the interface." He touched the base of his own skull, mirroring her port. "The link's going to run hot again. Same as before, just sustained. I'll watch thermal output the whole time."

"And if it gets too hot?"

"Then I pull the stage and we're left with a half-rewritten motor library." He glanced at the screen. "Let's not do that."

"Okay."

"Ready?"

"Yes."

Heat bloomed at the port, the familiar two-centimeter circle of metal and ceramic at the base of her skull. It followed the neural pathways like liquid finding channels. That was the closest description she had. Signal routes were not supposed to have temperature, but she could feel the warmth moving where signal had always moved. It reached her temples, pressed behind her eyes, and settled there as a dull force that was neither pain nor comfort.

Past the cranial pathways it kept going. Down through the cervical interface, into the motor networks, into the fine wiring that governed hands, shoulders, core, every chain of muscle firing through her freestyle stroke. By filling it, the heat made the architecture legible.

"How's that?" Yusuf asked.

"Six. Seven. Still seven." His one-to-ten scale had become a shorthand during the earlier stages. Seven meant manageable, uncomfortable, not yet close to losing stillness.

"Good. Stay with me."

Above her, ceiling tiles held ninety-four perforations each. She had counted the first row in stage one. The water stain in the far corner looked, if she felt generous, like Lake Zurich. Generosity had nothing to do with it. After four hours her mind wanted the stain to be anything it could recognize. Counting holes and enduring the rewrite divided her attention cleanly enough that the split became almost meditative. One part of her tracked the ceiling. Another felt the firmware touch muscles she used every day and had never felt separately: the small bands between her ribs, the flexors of her toes, the deep cuff muscles that held her shoulders steady through the catch.

In the smaller muscles the heat changed character. It felt intimate, almost invasive, as if each piece of her were being lifted and checked on its own.

Twenty minutes into stage four, her vision split.

The ceiling stayed where it was, but a second layer rose through it. Numbers. The distance from her eyes to the tile surface: 271.4 centimeters. The luminosity of each fluorescent panel as a value she could read but not name the unit of. The air in the bay: 21.3 degrees Celsius. Every figure refreshed once a second.

It was the system's raw sensory buffer. The link had been collecting and interpreting data her whole life, feeding conclusions into her perception without ever exposing the source. During the rewrite both layers were visible at once: the room she had always lived in and the quantified room the system had always seen.

"Lena." Yusuf's voice reached her as sound and also as readout: decibel level, frequency spread, a minute difference in distance to the right ear and left. "Your heart rate jumped. What's happening?"

"I can see what the system sees." Speech took effort. The motor pattern for talking was being remapped too, and every syllable came with a trace of numbness. "Numbers. Distances. Everything measured."

His typing stopped, then resumed faster. "That's raw-buffer bleed-through. I expected some. Not full overlay. Can you still see the room? The normal room?"

"Both. I can see both."

"Right." Silence for a beat. "It should resolve when stage four completes. Seventy more minutes. Maybe eighty."

Closing her eyes did not help. The data layer remained, floating against the dark inside of her lids: the rig surface temperature, the distance to the nearest wall, Yusuf's thermal signature as a hand-shaped concentration to her right. Open eyes were better. At least then the numbers had a room to sit on.

Time stopped behaving like units and turned into endurance. For the first half hour Yusuf kept talking in the professional register he used during calibrations. Then fatigue started to roughen him.

"Your motor library is massive. I've calibrated dozens of athletes and I've never seen this density of stored patterns. Since you were four, Lena. It's kept everything." He checked another stream of data. "Training sets. Competitions. Drills from the developmental program. I have to rewrite around all of it."

"Is that a problem?"

"It's a lot of data. This is going to run longer than ninety minutes." His accent thickened as he got tired, vowels rounder, t's softer. "Maybe another hour. I'll know more once we're through the competition patterns."

"I can wait."

"Yeah. I know you can." He looked away, then back to the monitor. "I've got tea somewhere if you need it. It'll be cold."

"I'm fine."

The dual view kept feeding her details she had never known the system stored: the rig's vibration frequency moving through her spine, Yusuf's breathing rate a little above baseline, the wavelength spread of the fluorescent light. As numbers, none of it mattered. As knowledge of how long the system had been studying her, it did.

A memory surfaced from the old Meridian facility in Zug: pediatric rigs sized for smaller bodies, a mural of fish on the ceiling, Latin species names she had learned to sound out before she could read German properly. She had spent childhood under painted fish while the link was tuned and expanded and made invisible. Now the heat was giving it edges.

In the fifth hour the temperature spiked.

Her hands clenched the armrests before she could stop them. The port felt like metal pressed to a heating element. Warmth shot into her jaw and teeth.

"Don't move." Yusuf's voice tightened. "I see it. Forty-two point seven. I'm cutting write speed."

Lake Zurich returned in the ceiling stain. The eastern shore. School trips from Zug, when the world outside the program had looked like a place other children belonged to.

"Coming down," he said. "Ten seconds."

Under stress, German numbers came first. *Eins, zwei, drei.* At eight the heat started to break. At twelve it was back to the hard, tolerable burn that had held her all night.

"Sorry," Yusuf said. "Competition patterns are written denser. More data per node, more processing heat. The simulation missed it."

One finger at a time, Lena uncurled her grip from the armrests. Her palms had dented the padding.

"We can stop for a bit," he said.

"No."

"Lena, if we pause, we can reset the curve."

"If we pause, the thermal residue drops and you have to build back to write temperature." The answer arrived whole, either from years of sitting in rigs or from whatever the system was exposing on its way out. "That's twenty more minutes of this before you can do any work. Keep going."

He did.

Stage five remapped the sensory integration layer. For twenty minutes the ordinary room vanished and only the system's version stayed: coordinates, values, measurements, no color or depth, just flattened numerical space. She narrated it to Yusuf in short precise sentences because precision was the only thing that held.

Normal vision came back in a rush that felt wrong at first. Colors oversaturated. Edges too sharp. The fluorescent panels bit at her eyes. After a minute the room settled and became a room again.

Stage six rewrote the autonomous routines. No heat this time. No pressure. Functions simply went absent one by one. First the left hand relaxed against the armrest, then the right. The small muscles in her lower back released an arch she had not known the system was holding for her. Weight dropped through her body into the padding. She had spent her life in low, managed tension and was only noticing it now because it was gone.

"That's restricted mode on the autonomous layer," Yusuf said. "It'll still keep you from falling. Injury prevention stays active. But it won't initiate motor patterns anymore." He paused. "It won't be swimming for you."

"Good," Lena said.

Outside the bay windows the sky had shifted from black to dark grey. Along the eastern ridge a green-white band had formed where dawn was working its way onto the peaks. The valley below stayed dark. She had watched morning arrive at this facility more times than she could count and had never seen the green in it because the system had always corrected the color temperature first.

At 6:43 he disconnected the cables from her port with the small exact movements she trusted: loop, tug-test, withdrawal. He had always been the best calibration engineer she worked with. After nine hours inside the architecture of her body, that fact felt closer to trust than to assessment.

Sitting up tipped the room sideways. Her vestibular system, left to run without its smoothing layer, gave her a blunt report about space and angle. One hand caught the edge of the rig until the tilt passed.

"Easy," Yusuf said, already moving toward the water station.

Room-temperature water hit her tongue and stayed small. She swallowed and the taste was already over.

***

An hour of diagnostics followed. Reaction panels, sensory acuity sequences, motor coordination tests, cognitive load checks. Lena pressed buttons, tracked lights, touched her nose, answered arithmetic. The tests were familiar. The numbers were not.

Reaction time had slowed from 173 milliseconds to 192.

Visual resolution had dropped into what Yusuf called normal high. The small print on the pharmaceutical labels across the bay was no longer readable.

Auditory spatial processing was blunt now. A sound on her left arrived as *left*, not as a location with angle and distance attached.

Proprioception had changed most. The system's continuous body-position model was gone. What remained felt coarse, like running fingertips across fabric that used to have a tighter weave.

"I feel like I'm wearing thick socks," she said. "All over my body."

Yusuf wrote it down, then set the clipboard aside. "The desync markers are gone. Completely. No residual corruption, no anomalous pattern initiation, no autonomous motor firing. Your system isn't going to do things without you anymore." He let the silence sit. "That's what we were trying to fix. It's fixed."

Cold from the tile reached her through the thin facility slippers and sat there, a square chill under each foot.

Only then did Lena notice the quiet.

The bay kept making its noises: equipment drone, ventilation hiss, Yusuf turning a page. They arrived one at a time now, bare and evenly spaced, without the hidden sorting she had always mistaken for thinking.

"It's quiet," she said.

Yusuf looked up. "The bay?"

"My head." Her fingers touched her temple. "It's quiet in my head. I didn't know it was loud."

Fatigue had wrecked him. His shirt was wrinkled, his face slack, stubble dark along his jaw. He pushed his glasses higher on his nose.

"Yeah," he said. "I modeled that. Ambient processing uses bandwidth. When it goes offline, you feel the difference. I just didn't know how it would land."

"It feels like a vent shut off in a room you've slept in for years," she said. "You only hear it after it stops."

He made a note and did not try to improve the metaphor.

The corridor to the pool wing was the same corridor it had always been: concrete floor, overhead fluorescents, chlorine gathering strength as she got closer to the water. Walking through it felt newly plain. Each footfall gave back cold, hardness, a little texture, then finished. Halfway down the corridor she stopped just to stand inside the simplicity of that.

***

The pool sat empty, her training slot left blank on the board. After a quick change in the locker room, she crossed to lane three and went in.

Underwater, the entry boom spread through her skull as one blunt rush. The wall, the lane rope, her own kick all lived inside the same noise until her eyes sorted them back apart.

Freestyle at easy pace still required work she had never had to name before. Every stroke asked for an instruction. Reach. Catch. Pull. Roll. Breathe. Nothing stepped in to adjust hand angle or shoulder rotation or the timing of the kick. Her body did only what she told it to do and did it with the level of skill she actually possessed.

Her palm kept waiting for the fine pressure changes that used to steer the catch. Nothing answered. Chlorine, warmth, and resistance pressed over her hand in one broad sheet, and she had to choose the angle herself.

Four laps later she held the wall and took a breath she had actively chosen. The old respiratory management was gone. Air came when she decided to lift for it, not a fraction early.

Another eight laps were enough. By the last turn the wall kept arriving a fraction later than her body expected, and the fractions kept stacking. A touch less reach, a touch less force, the recovery dragging on the way through. Over four hundred meters that accumulation became seconds. Seconds put you in another part of the sheet.

At the wall she folded her arms over the lane rope. The deck windows were still dark. Silence filled her head and kept filling it. She still couldn't tell whether to count it as damage.

The pool door opened.

Dara came in already suited, tightening her braid as she crossed the deck. The red elastic at her wrist had started to fray white at the edges. She set her bottle on the bench behind lane four, laid her towel square, and looked over.

"You're in early."

"I wanted to swim before anyone else got here."

"Did it work?"

"The desync is gone."

Dara nodded once. "And the rest?"

"I'm slower."

"How much?"

"I don't know yet."

Dara looked at the pace clock, then back at Lena. "Come on. One hundred."

"I just did twelve."

"Do one more."

There was nothing cruel in it. Dara's eyes had already gone to the pace clock; if there was an answer here, she wanted it on the clock face. Lena pushed off when Dara did.

For the first ten meters they held even. Then Dara's stroke length started buying distance. At the far wall Dara flipped while Lena still had three strokes left. By the time Lena turned, white feet were already leaving the other lane. Dara touched at the near wall, lifted her goggles, and waited while Lena took the last five strokes in alone.

"Five seconds," Dara said when Lena reached the wall. "Easy pace."

Water ran off Dara's chin. She did not wipe it away.

"Why did you do it?" she asked. "Not the patch. This part."

Lena kept one hand on the wall. "The desync was taking time I was alive for. I was in the pool and then I wasn't, and I couldn't tell you where the missing part went."

"But the body kept swimming."

"Yes."

"And it was —" She glanced at the lane between them, at the water Lena had taken longer to cross. "Your times were there. When it was doing that."

"It wasn't me."

"Five seconds, though." Dara looked at the pace clock, then back. "At easy."

"I know."

Dara pulled at the frayed elastic on her wrist.

"Okay," she said at last. "I hope it holds."

Then she pulled her goggles back down and left on her interval, first stroke in place before Lena had let go of the wall.

Lena stayed at the wall until the red hand reached the top of the pace clock again. Dara was already halfway down the pool. When Lena pushed off, the lane beside her was empty. Midway through the length the wall came a stroke later than she had judged. She took the extra stroke, turned, and kept going.
