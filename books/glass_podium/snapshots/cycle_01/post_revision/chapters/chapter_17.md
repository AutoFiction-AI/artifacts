# Chapter 17

The ready room was underground. Lena could feel the venue above her in the weight of the concrete, the closed-in cooled air, the forty thousand seats waiting over her head. She sat with her towel across her knees and her goggles in one hand, and the quiet in her skull, familiar now, made everything else sound overdescribed.

Seven other swimmers waited with her. She knew most of them by shoulders, kick timing, and the habits that survived from meet to meet. Adelaïde Moreau stretched in the corner with the saint medallion she always tucked into her suit before the walk out. Kaede Watanabe had her eyes closed and her lips moving through some private rehearsal. Nina Okoro paced the far wall with three fingers taped bright green. The room held that smooth patched readiness Lena could read before anyone touched the water.

"Morozova. Lane five."

The corridor to the pool deck was fluorescent and cold. By the time she stepped into the open, the lights had already found her. They came down hard enough to glaze the water and bleach the color out of people's faces. The noise of the crowd hit her in the sternum before it made sense in her ears. Lane five waited in the middle of a rectangle of blue-green water that looked almost fixed in place under the broadcast rig.

She climbed onto the block. Moreau stood in lane four. Watanabe was in three. Lena bent, settled her feet, and let the small useless wish pass through her that the dive would arrive already solved. Then the tone sounded and she went.

The water hit her all at once, blunt and grainy instead of opening into the old second language of pressure and signal. She found her catch anyway, high elbow, shoulder engaged, and drove through the first length before the field had time to sort itself. Her turn at fifty was clean. She came off the wall even with Moreau and a fraction behind Watanabe, her kick still sharp, her breathing held where she wanted it.

By the second hundred the race had started telling the truth. Moreau's feet stayed just ahead and then began to slide away. Lin, out in six, came up hard on the outside. Lena kept them close through the first half of the pool, but every wall took a little more out of her legs and every pull asked more of her shoulders than the last one had.

At a hundred and fifty she still felt in it. By two hundred the burn had settled into her deltoids and stayed there. Air came fast when she turned to breathe. She did not get any lane map or pacing correction, only the sight of bubbles ahead of her and the drag of water on tired hands. She flipped, pushed, and felt the push come back shorter than the one before it.

The third hundred broke the field open. Moreau and Lin were gone into their own race. Watanabe came back toward her the way Watanabe always did, and Lena went by on stubbornness and a cleaner line off the wall, but the pass cost her. Her elbow started to drop. Water leaked past her palm. On the next turn her feet hit a shade too high and she had to kick once to settle herself before she could pull again.

Nothing in the lane softened. Her shoulders filled with acid. The crowd was a thick muffled weather above the water, loud for somebody, not for lane five. She kept swimming because there was nothing else to do with the last hundred meters except take them, one pull and then the next, until the finish came under her hand.

She touched and came up hard, mouth open, chlorine running off her face.

The board across the pool gave her 4:08.71. Moreau sat first at 3:49.03. Lin took second at 3:50.88. Lena's name dropped in under them in third. No gold, but no missing stretch of water either, and that was the shape of it.

A volunteer met her at the edge with a towel. Another one, older and brisker, touched her elbow after the medal ceremony and pointed her toward the mixed zone.

The bronze hung cold and heavy at first, then warm where it rested against her sternum. Cameras waited behind the barrier. Before she had reached the taped mark on the floor, a reporter leaned in with a microphone and asked, "Did refusing Meridian's update cost you the gold tonight?"

The question arrived in English and then again from her left in French. Lena looked past the microphones to the monitor overhead, where the replay was already running her through the middle two hundred with Moreau cleanly opening the gap.

"It cost me time," she said.

"Do you regret it?"

"No."

The handler moved her along, but the monitor above the barrier was already replaying her face while the subtitle track laid her answer under it in two languages.

---

Kai had been in the call room for forty minutes, thumb pressed against the sore ridge along the outside of his left wrist.

The other sprinters used the room the way patched men always did, all that economical readiness that made preparation look like the body deciding things early. Dekker worked through ankle mobility on the floor. Okafor stood with his eyes closed and his hands loose at his sides, running whatever pre-race sequence his system preferred. Kai watched them and kept still.

He wasn't running any rehearsal. Nothing stood between the original install and the virus except whatever he'd taught himself since Kirchberg.

Two days earlier Yusuf had sat on the edge of the hotel bed with his glasses pushed up on his forehead and tried again. "You know the probability model. At this propagation level, the chance of a triggered event in a two-hundred-meter race is significant."

"I know."

"Kai."

"I know what it is." That was as far as he'd gotten. The rebuilt body was the only one he had. Another layer between him and it was still another layer.

The marshal at the door called them up. Kai rose, rolled his shoulders once, and followed the line through the tunnel.

The stadium was sealed against the Doha heat. Cool air moved across the track while the temperature outside pressed at the glass above. Broadcast light poured down in clean white sheets that made skin shine and erased most of the shadows from the red surface. Forty thousand people filled the tiers, close enough in sound to feel physical.

He had lane seven on the outside. The track gave under his spikes with that familiar engineered compromise between grip and return. His blocks were set from warm-up. He crouched, placed his feet, and felt the synthetic muscle load before the gun, power gathering in his legs through graft and flesh together.

His body was ready. That had never been reassuring.

"On your marks."

He set his hands on the line. The crowd drew breath all at once. The bridge at the base of his spine hummed low and steady, running its preparation scripts, and for the moment it was still on his side.

When the starter called set, his hips came up, his weight tipped forward, and the grafts held themselves on the edge of release. The gun broke the air and he drove.

The first thirty meters belonged to acceleration. He stayed low, pushed hard, and let the curve come to him. By fifty he was in the pack. At eighty he was still there, leaning into the bend with the outer leg working harder, feeling the familiar seam in each stride, his intent landing a fraction late behind execution and catching up before the next footstrike.

The straight opened. Okafor had the lead in four. Kai was fourth, maybe fifth, and each arm pump lit the seam in his left wrist, a small deep wrong that reminded him he could still break. He kept driving. At one twenty his scars were tight in the dry stadium air. At one forty his legs were still doing exactly what he told them to do.

At one sixty his vision broke apart.

This blackout did not empty the world. It shredded it. The red track split into mismatched pieces that hovered in front of him without joining, lane line separated from lane line, scoreboard from wall, his own arm floating a beat away from the shoulder that should have placed it. Then the sound thinned into one high interior tone that swallowed the crowd, the spikes, his breathing.

His legs kept sprinting.

He found them through the same gap he had spent months learning to feel. Not normal proprioception; that was tearing loose with everything else. This was the boundary itself, the place where intent should have become motion and didn't, where the bridge took over and kept the pattern running. Quadriceps firing on commands he had not sent. Calves loading and releasing through a sprint cycle that no longer required him.

He had trained for this in the smallest ugliest way possible, sitting on the edge of the bed in Kirchberg with his eyes closed, feeling tiny autonomous movements start in his healing hand and trying to stop them with nothing but his own body. Most nights he failed. Some nights he caught the motion early enough to hold it.

He caught it now.

His right quadricep was already firing when he drove a different signal into the same muscle. Stop. The contradictory commands met in the tissue and the leg seized mid-stride. Momentum threw him sideways. He got the left foot down before he went over, felt the calf try to launch again, and shut that down too. Earlier than last time. Harder. The muscle shuddered under him, biological and synthetic signals grinding against each other inside the same leg, but it held.

He staggered twice and stayed upright. The track was still coming back together in broken patches. The tone was still screaming in his skull. None of that mattered as much as the next step. He lifted the left foot because he wanted it lifted. He set it down. He did the same with the right. The bridge kept trying to turn the movement back into a sprint, and he kept meeting each new contraction before it could complete.

By the time the finish straight made sense again, the race had ended without him. The others were through. One camera operator in a press vest jogged the edge of the track to stay with him while he walked the last thirty meters in front of forty thousand baffled people.

He crossed in 23.41. His name fell to the bottom of the board, eight of eight.

A race official and a medic were already coming toward him when the replay lit the stadium screen. From the speakers above the track, the English commentary reached him a beat after the pictures. "Possible technical failure for Brennan in lane seven."

Kai bent over with his hands on his knees and watched himself on the screen lurch out of the sprint, fight his own body, and drag it home. The crowd made the sound people make for equipment trouble, confused and a little disappointed.

"Mr. Brennan, we need you at medical," the official said.

Kai straightened. His legs were shaking hard now that he had stopped forcing them into ordinary human pace. The bridge had gone quiet at the base of his spine. On the screen he looked like a man malfunctioning. He knew exactly what had happened.

"It wasn't a failure," he said.

The official either didn't hear him or didn't understand. "Sir."

"I'm fine," Kai said. His voice came out hoarse. "I'm good."

He walked off the track under the replay of himself still stumbling in lane seven. Behind him the stadium was already turning to the next event.
