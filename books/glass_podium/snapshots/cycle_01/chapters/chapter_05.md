# Chapter 5

Kai's left ankle clicked when he crouched into the starting blocks — the natural kind, a tendon snapping over bone. His right ankle adjusted a fraction of a second later, the synthetic muscle recalibrating the joint angle before he'd finished settling into the pedals. He'd mostly stopped noticing the difference. Today it registered, faintly, like seeing a word misspelled on a sign you drove past every morning.

Lane three. The indoor track had its own atmosphere: the rubbery chemical smell of the surface, the dry altitude-compensated air that sat in the back of his throat, the overhead lights throwing a flat, temperatureless brightness across the oval. High ceilings with acoustic dampening that turned footfalls into soft, muted thuds — nothing like the outdoor crack of spikes on a real track. Two weeks at Kirchberg and the thin air had stopped being a problem, but each morning's first deep breath still carried a small sharpness at the bottom of the inhale, a reminder that the oxygen up here was rationed by geography.

Six other sprinters were warming up. Novak on the far lane, doing strides with a gait so bilaterally perfect it looked rendered. Priya Chandrasekaran shaking out her hamstrings near the curve, her right leg extending and retracting with a mechanical evenness that said her grafts were freshly calibrated. A couple of athletes Kai knew by face but not by name jogging easy laps. Through the glass partition at the 100m mark, two coaches sat with tablets on their knees, watching telemetry feeds.

The start tone sounded. He drove out of the blocks.

The first fifty meters were the part that still felt like running. His feet hit the rubber, knees driving forward, the initial acceleration building in his quads with a burn that was unmistakably organic — lactic acid, effort, the specific heat of muscle doing work it was being asked to do. His arms pumped. His breathing locked into the sprint cadence, short exhales timed to footstrikes. The synthetic grafts operated on a latency gradient, ramping up output as his body exceeded its natural threshold. For those first seconds, the effort belonged to him.

Then the system engaged, and the effort smoothed. There was a seam — somewhere around the sixty-meter mark, impossible to pin down — where his own power generation handed off to the assisted version. His stride lengthened by a centimeter he hadn't chosen. His arm swing adjusted, elbows tightening, hands loosening. The burn in his quads didn't vanish but it changed character, became a managed thing, the system distributing load across natural and synthetic fibers in a pattern that prioritized efficiency. He was still running. The running had stopped needing him.

Eighty meters. Ninety. The visual processing enhancement sharpened — lane markings gaining a crystalline clarity, the far wall of the track becoming almost hyperreal while the periphery softened to blur. Enhancement tunnel vision. Everything not directly ahead dimmed to irrelevance.

A hundred meters. The curve approaching. His body leaned into it at the angle the system calculated, banking against the centrifugal force with a precision his own balance had never achieved. The wall on his left — padded concrete, grey foam over grey block — slid past, close enough to touch if he'd reached out. The padding was standard: rated for someone decelerating, someone who'd already started pulling up. Not for someone at full speed.

A hundred and twenty. A hundred and thirty. His time was good. He could feel that without checking the clock, the same way a driver reads speed from the pitch of the engine.

A hundred and forty.

His left foot struck the track and the track was gone.

Not gone. He was gone. Every channel that connected his mind to his body shut off simultaneously — not one after the other, not a sequence, but a single clean severance. Vision didn't go dark. Darkness would have been something he was seeing. This was the removal of the ability to see. Sound didn't go silent. Silence is an auditory experience. This was the end of hearing as a faculty. Proprioception — the sense of his own body's position, the knowledge of where his limbs were — didn't go numb. It ceased to be a category his mind could access.

He was thinking. He knew that. The thoughts arrived clearly, one after another, in a mind that had been untethered from everything it was supposed to be housed in.

*I was running.*

*I can't feel my legs.*

*I can't feel anything.*

The thoughts were precise and useless. They had nowhere to connect. He was aware in the way that a person dreaming is aware — present, conscious, processing — but without a single piece of sensory data to process. No body. No ground. No air in the lungs that he could feel being his lungs. He tried to open his hands and there were no hands. He tried to stop running and there was no running, not from where he sat inside his own skull. Just the knowledge, arriving from nowhere, that somewhere below the thinking, the machine was still going.

His body was still sprinting.

He knew this without evidence. The synthetic grafts, the spinal neural bridge — they were receiving system commands through channels that didn't pass through his conscious mind. His legs were driving through their stride cycle at thirty-seven kilometers per hour. His arms were pumping. His lungs were cycling air. The body was executing the sprint it had been optimized to execute, and the person whose name was on the entry form had been disconnected like an unplugged peripheral.

He tried to stop. He tried with everything that trying meant in a place with no muscles to engage. The command formed in his mind — *stop, slow down, stop* — and went nowhere. There was nothing between the thought and the legs. The channel was cut.

Time passed, or didn't. He had no way to measure it. No heartbeat he could feel, no breathing rhythm, no external reference. The thoughts kept coming: *The curve. The wall is on the curve. The wall is padded. The padding isn't for this.* He was doing math he didn't want to do. Thirty-seven kilometers per hour. The curve was coming. Or had passed. Or was now. He didn't know.

His consciousness sat in the void and waited for something to happen to a body it could not reach.

He thought about bracing. The thought was detailed and specific — tuck the chin, drop the shoulder, turn sideways to distribute the impact — and it went to exactly the same place as the command to stop. Nowhere. He could choreograph the perfect crash response in his mind and his body would not receive it. He was composing instructions for a machine that had changed the locks. The wall was coming at the speed his legs were carrying it, and he could plan and strategize and problem-solve inside his skull for as long as the thirty-seven kilometers per hour allowed, and none of it would reach his arms, his legs, his neck, the muscles that would need to absorb the impact.

The crash brought his senses back.

Sound came first — a deep, concussive thud that he felt through his skull before he heard it, the vibration traveling through bone. His right shoulder hitting the padded concrete at sprint speed, the foam compressing to nothing and the block wall behind it absorbing the remainder with a force that shoved the humeral head out of its socket. The joint separated with a wet, structural wrongness, like a hinge coming off a door — not a clean pop but a grinding dislocation, the ball of the shoulder sliding forward and down to a place where it didn't belong.

Then the ground. His body spun off the wall and his face met the rubber track surface, his left cheek dragging across it with a friction that peeled skin. The rubber was textured — fine grit, designed for traction — and it abraded his cheek and the skin above his left eye with the efficiency of sandpaper. He felt the track surface embedding in the wounds, tiny particles of rubber compound grinding into the raw tissue. Blood came immediately, warm and then cold in the facility's dry air.

His left hand went out. Reflex or system command, he'd never know. The wrist took his weight wrong and something inside it broke — a sharp, specific failure, the radius snapping at the distal end. He felt the bone give, felt the architecture of the wrist lose its structural logic. The pain arrived in a bright, narrowing flare that traveled up his forearm and stopped behind his elbow.

He was on the track. On his side, then on his front, then something between the two. The overhead lights glared. Blood ran from his cheek across his left eye, turning the fluorescent panels into smeared halos. His right shoulder was hot, displaced, the joint sitting inches from where it was supposed to be, every small movement sending a sick, grinding pulse through his torso. His left wrist was broken and he could feel the bones misaligned inside the brace of his own skin, a wrongness he could map with his mind's eye: the clean bone, the break, the offset.

His legs were still twitching.

Not running. Not anymore. But firing — quadriceps clenching and releasing in a stuttering half-rhythm, calves contracting, the synthetic grafts executing fragments of the sprint stride that had nowhere left to go. Short, jerky kicks against the track surface, his spikes scraping rubber. Residual system commands feeding through the neural bridge, the body's optimization software still trying to run a program the body had already crashed out of.

He watched his own legs do it. Lying on the track in blood, his shoulder wrecked, his wrist broken, he watched the muscles in his thighs fire without his permission, watched his feet scrabble at the rubber like something pinned and struggling, and he could not make them stop. The commands were not his. The legs were his. The commands were not.

People were already running toward him. Novak arrived first — Kai saw his shoes, blue and white, stopping two feet from his face. Someone shouted for medical. The coaches behind the glass partition had stood up, their tablets forgotten. Through the blood in his left eye, Kai could see Priya standing at the edge of the far lane with her hands hanging at her sides, not coming closer, watching with the particular stillness of a person looking at something happening to a body and thinking about her own.

"Don't move him." A voice from somewhere above and behind. "Wait — don't move him."

He wasn't trying to move. He was lying on a track he'd run a hundred times in two weeks and watching his left leg kick once more, the spike leaving a grey scuff mark on the rubber, and then stop.

The medical team arrived. Two technicians and a woman from Petra Engel's staff — Erika, he thought, or maybe Ingrid, a broad-shouldered German woman with cold hands and the steady efficiency of someone who'd trained for exactly this and never expected to use it on a morning sprint session. They had a backboard and a cervical collar and a calmness that was either professional or pharmaceutical.

"Can you hear me?" She was stabilizing his cervical spine, her fingers cold against the sides of his neck.

"Yeah."

"Tell me what hurts."

"Shoulder's out. Right shoulder. Wrist is broken, the left one." He heard his own voice — flat, emptied of the humor he'd normally use to pad a situation like this. The humor was gone. Not suppressed, not held back. Gone.

"Can you feel your feet?"

He could. The twitching had stopped and what remained was his own body, reporting in: toes, ankles, the deep ache of muscles that had been doing work he hadn't authorized. "Yeah. I feel them."

She didn't look relieved. She looked like she was filing the answer.

They rolled him onto the backboard. The shoulder shifted and he grunted — a closed-mouth sound that he chose over the scream that was the honest alternative. Five other athletes were standing in a loose semicircle, watching. Novak had his arms crossed. Priya hadn't moved from the far lane. The indoor track was very quiet, the acoustic dampening doing what it was designed to do — absorbing the small sounds of the medical team working, turning everything into a muted, padded version of itself. The same dampening that had swallowed the sound of his crash, reduced it to something the athletes at the far end of the track probably heard as a thump and nothing more.

Blood dripped from his face onto the white surface of the backboard. Small red drops, pooling. His cheek was raw — he could feel the rubber burns, the gritty texture of abraded skin. The split above his left eye was bleeding freely, the blood running into his eye and making the world pulse red at the edges.

They carried him down the corridor between the track and the medical wing. Three minutes of fluorescent light and ventilation hum, the ceiling panels sliding past at the speed of other people's walking. Kai stared at the ceiling and tried to locate the boundary between the moment he'd lost his senses and the moment the wall had given them back. The boundary was clean. No fade. No static. No transition. One stride he was running, the next he was nowhere, and then his shoulder hit a wall and the world poured back in all at once, too loud, too bright, too much.

The thirty seconds in between were not dark. They were not anything. He kept coming back to that. Darkness was something. This had been nothing.

And the legs. His legs had kept running through the nothing as if the nothing were normal. As if the legs didn't need him. As if he were the peripheral, and the legs were the system.

---

The medical bay smelled like isopropyl alcohol and the cold metal of instrument trays. They reduced his shoulder first — two techs bracing him while a third manipulated the joint, working the humeral head back into the socket with a practiced, mechanical patience. The moment it slid home was a nauseating pop, the joint finding its seat with a wrongness that was somehow worse than the dislocation, as if the shoulder remembered where it used to be and was angry about the detour.

"Shit," Kai said, and that was all he said about it.

They braced the wrist. The bones were misaligned, and when the technician palpated the break site, the pain was specific and informative — it told him exactly where the radius had failed and in which direction. They immobilized it in a rigid splint that held his hand in a position of mild extension, the fingers free, the wrist locked. It would need imaging. The grafts would accelerate the bone repair, which was its own kind of problem — the bones would knit faster than they should, and whether they'd knit correctly was a question the accelerated healing didn't ask.

The facial work was done by a doctor Kai hadn't met — a quiet man with a Swiss accent and the practiced steadiness of someone who'd sutured hundreds of faces. He cleaned the abrasions first, flushing the rubber grit from the raw tissue on Kai's cheek with saline that stung in a clean, medical way. Then the sutures above the left eye — five of them, the needle and thread moving with a rhythm Kai tracked because there was nothing else to look at except the ceiling.

"You'll have a small scar here," the doctor said, tying off the last stitch.

"Sure."

He lay on the exam table and let them finish. The fluorescent panels overhead were the same as in the corridor, the same as in every room in this facility — flat, revealing, the kind of light that showed you everything and warmed you with nothing. His shoulder throbbed in slow, deep pulses. His wrist was a constant low-grade pressure punctuated by sharp spikes whenever his fingers moved. The abrasions on his cheek burned when the air touched them.

The door opened and Yusuf came in carrying his tablet and a data cable. His face was set in the expression Kai had learned to read over two weeks of calibration sessions — concentration pulled tight, his eyes slightly narrowed behind his glasses, an engineer looking at data he didn't trust.

"How bad?" Yusuf asked, pulling a chair to the bedside.

"Shoulder's back in. Wrist is broken. Face is—" Kai gestured vaguely with his unbraced hand.

"Yeah, I can see the face. Can I plug in?"

Calibration shorthand. Yusuf wanted access to Kai's system logs — the neural bridge data, the graft telemetry, the visual processing records. Everything the system had recorded while Kai had been absent from his own body. The data that said what the body had been doing when the person inside it wasn't there.

"Go ahead."

Yusuf leaned over him and tilted his head gently, finding the port at the base of Kai's skull. His hands were steady, the way they always were — an engineer's hands, precise and unhesitating. The cable clicked into the port with a small, definitive sound. Yusuf sat back and started scrolling.

Kai watched him read. The tablet's screen was angled away, but the glow reflected in Yusuf's glasses — data streaming past, timestamps and system metrics. Yusuf's expression shifted. A tightening around the eyes first. Then a stillness that was worse.

"What happened at one-forty," Kai said.

"That's what I'm looking at." Professional voice — clean, measured, the Birmingham scraped away. He scrolled. Stopped. Scrolled back. "Your visual processing dropped to zero at the one-forty mark. Full shutdown, not a degradation. Simultaneous."

"It wasn't just visual."

"No, I can see that. Proprioception at the same timestamp. Auditory input—" He checked the screen. "Forty-two milliseconds later. Total sensory dropout." He paused on something. "The spinal bridge maintained motor output throughout."

"My legs didn't stop."

"No." Yusuf still hadn't looked up from the tablet. "The grafts were receiving movement commands from the bridge's autonomous subsystem. Stored sprint pattern. It didn't need your conscious input."

The medical bay's equipment hummed. The same hum as the calibration bay, the same hum as every room in the building — infrastructure processing, running its own patterns, doing things without being asked.

"How long was I out?"

"Thirty-one seconds." Yusuf pulled something up on the screen. "Sensory channels came back at the one-seventy mark. Wall's at one-seventy-three." He said this without emphasis, as a measurement, but his jaw was tight.

"I know where the wall is."

"I know you do." Something cracked in his professional voice — a small fissure. He tapped through several more screens, opened something Kai couldn't see. Then his finger stopped moving.

"What?"

"Hold on." Yusuf was scrolling fast now, his fingertip dragging through the data with the urgency of someone chasing a pattern through noise. He stood up, still tethered to Kai by the cable, and moved to the wall-mounted terminal. The larger screen showed what the tablet had been showing — dense columns of system code, error logs, timestamps flagged in amber and red.

"Your calibration firmware's corrupted," Yusuf said. He was talking to the screen as much as to Kai — the monologue mode, the engineer thinking aloud. "Not a mechanical fault. Corruption. Errors propagating through the sensory processing layer into the motor architecture." He pulled up another data set. "And it's not—" He stopped. Pulled up another. Stood very still.

"Yusuf."

"Three other athletes," Yusuf said. "Novak. Hoffman. Priya. Past forty-eight hours. Same calibration subsystems, same error signatures. Low-level anomalies — micro-disruptions in sensory timing, offset in motor coordination. Nothing that'd cause a full blackout. Yet." He turned from the screen. His glasses still reflected the error logs, amber and red scrolling across the lenses. "But the pattern's the same. The errors are replicating through the shared calibration network."

"Replicating." Kai tried to sit up. His shoulder objected with a grinding flare, and he settled back against the exam table.

"Every athlete who's been calibrated on this network in the past month is connected to it," Yusuf said. His professional register was thinning, the careful clinical vocabulary giving way to something faster, less controlled. "The corruption spreads through the calibration handshake. One system syncs with the network, the errors copy across."

"That's a virus."

Yusuf didn't answer immediately. He stood at the wall terminal with his hands at his sides. His glasses had slid down his nose and he hadn't pushed them up, which was, in Kai's two weeks of watching Yusuf work, something that didn't happen. Yusuf always pushed up his glasses. It was the first thing his hands did when they weren't holding instruments.

"It behaves like one," Yusuf said. "Replication mechanism, propagation vector, cascading error structure. It's consistent with a self-replicating firmware exploit." He paused. "Everyone on this network is carrying it. Every enhanced athlete in this facility."

The hum of the equipment filled the silence. Kai lay on the exam table with his shoulder put back and his wrist braced and blood drying on his face in stiff, crackling patches, and he thought about Novak's perfect stride, and Priya shaking out her hamstrings at the curve, and every other athlete in this building whose body was connected to the same network that had just switched him off and kept running.

"Can you fix it?"

"I don't know yet." Professional voice gone entirely. Just Yusuf — a man from Birmingham who was looking at something in the infrastructure that he hadn't expected to find. "I need to quarantine the network. Full diagnostic. That means grounding every enhanced athlete from training until I can map the scope."

"Every athlete."

"Everyone who's been calibrated here. Which is everyone." He reached up and pushed his glasses into place. The gesture was automatic, but Kai saw his fingers weren't quite steady. "I'll talk to Petra and the facility director. We need to shut down all calibration access until I can isolate the propagation mechanism."

Kai closed his eyes. The darkness behind his eyelids was familiar, ordinary — the comfortable dark of a person choosing not to see. He could feel his eyelids. He could feel the pull of the stitches above his left eye, the crust of drying blood on his lashes, the faint pressure of the exam table against the back of his skull. He could feel all of it, and the feeling was so ordinary, so unremarkable, that it made him want to keep his eyes closed for a very long time.

The thirty-one seconds hadn't been dark. They hadn't been anything. And through all thirty-one of them, his legs had been running at thirty-seven kilometers per hour toward a wall, and the legs had not needed him to be there.

Yusuf unplugged the cable. The click of it disconnecting was small and precise, a sound Kai had heard at the end of every calibration session for two weeks. Yusuf stood, collected his tablet, and paused at the door.

"Get some rest," he said, and the words had the shape of professional advice but the weight of something else — an engineer who had just learned that every system he maintained was carrying something he didn't put there, talking to a body that had just demonstrated what that something could do.

The door closed. Kai lay in the antiseptic light and listened to his own breathing and felt his legs resting on the exam table — still, quiet, entirely under his control. He flexed his right foot. The synthetic muscle responded instantly, the motion smooth, obedient. He flexed the left. Same. His legs did what he told them to do, and the ordinariness of it was the most frightening thing that had happened to him all morning, because thirty minutes ago his legs had done exactly what they wanted, and they had done it well, and he had not been there for any of it.
