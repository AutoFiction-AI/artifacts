# Chapter 16

The light changed over four weeks. Snow retreated up the ridges until only the highest peaks still held it, and the runoff cut new channels down the rock faces visible through the cafeteria windows. The terraces opened. Athletes who'd spent months under fluorescent light stood in actual alpine sun and squinted at the green that had replaced the grey.

The facility emptied in stages. Those who'd signed Meridian's contract left first — patched, data-licensed, their departures arranged through corporate travel. A sprinter from the Czech team. The pair of gymnasts from South Korea. The relay group, all four of them, boarding the shuttle to Zurich on the same Tuesday morning with matching duffel bags and matching firmware. Each departure thinned the cafeteria by a face, the morning track sessions by a body. By late June the residential wing was half-dark, most of the room displays glowing their real-time diagnostics to nobody.

The holdouts were a smaller group than the refusals in February. Time and Tomás had done their work. Most athletes who'd initially refused had come around — watched the footage of the crash, thought about their own bodies mid-stride or mid-tumble or mid-rotation, and signed. The remaining holdouts numbered maybe eight, spread across the Kirchberg campus and the satellite facility in Davos. Some ran Yusuf's partial patch. Some ran nothing and accepted the risk with a clarity that looked either brave or reckless depending on who was judging.

Kai ran nothing. He trained on the indoor track alone most mornings, running two hundreds in the facility's thin air, the rubber smell and the echo of his footfalls the same as they'd been in January except the light through the high windows was different now — brighter, longer, actual daylight instead of the winter's grey approximation. His wrist ached on cold mornings, the bone where the break had knitted too fast — a shallow wrongness, like the memory of an injury rather than the injury itself. He'd stopped wearing the brace weeks ago but the ache stayed. He ran with it. He ran paying attention to something most people couldn't feel: the synthetic muscle's autonomous subsystems, the tiny pre-movements in his quadriceps and calves that fired fractionally ahead of his conscious intent. He'd been training himself to notice the gap between what he told his legs to do and what they did first. He did not call this practice anything. He just ran, and paid attention, and ran again.

Lena swam. The pool was less crowded now, which was its own kind of strange — she'd trained in packed lanes her entire life, and the empty lanes beside her made the water feel wider and flatter, as though something had been subtracted from it. Her times were slower than her optimization peak by margins that would have been unacceptable six months ago. She swam them anyway. The partial patch held. The silence in her head where the system's ambient processing had been was becoming, if not comfortable, then at least recognizable — a space she could orient herself inside of, the way your eyes adjusted to a room after someone turned the light off.

Yusuf worked. He gave the partial patch to two more athletes in the third week — a hurdler from Jamaica and a pole vaulter from New Zealand, both holdouts who'd decided the risk of competing unpatched outweighed the risk of an untested firmware rewrite. The installations went smoothly. Both athletes reported the same things Lena had: blunted sensory acuity, slower reaction times, and a new quiet in their heads that they described in different words but that Yusuf recognized as the same phenomenon. He filed reports. He calibrated the remaining athletes on their adjusted schedules and answered technical questions from the Davos satellite team over encrypted channels. He called Farah on Thursdays and listened to her talk about the card game she was winning, her voice the same warm voice it had always been, her speech just slightly slower than it used to be in a way that he measured against last week and the week before that the way he measured everything — in increments, in data, in the precise language of decline.

Margot walked the corridors. She had nowhere to be and nowhere else to go. Her coaching suspension was total — no feed access, no training supervision, no athlete contact in any professional capacity. She'd been offered a transfer to Meridian's administrative center in Zurich, which she understood correctly as a polite suggestion that she leave, and she'd declined it with three words. She still had her quarters. She still ate in the cafeteria. She still walked the corridors of the facility in the mornings and evenings, her body moving through spaces that had been defined by her work and were now just hallways. She was sleeping badly. She'd taken to running the outdoor loop around the facility in the late afternoons — three kilometers of gravel path that switchbacked down to the treeline and back up, the altitude making her lungs burn in a way she didn't mind because it was at least a sensation she'd chosen. She was not drinking, which was something she noticed herself noticing, because it would have been easy and because the facility bar in the common room stayed open until eleven and nobody was watching.

The night before the team bus left for the Zurich airport, Yusuf made biryani.

---

His mother had sent a care package in March — a padded envelope that arrived battered from Birmingham with two bags of basmati, a jar of her own garam masala, saffron threads in a twist of cling film, a bag of fried onions gone slightly soft at the edges, and a note in Urdu that said *Eat properly.* He'd been saving it. The biryani was a production. He'd negotiated use of the cafeteria kitchen from the night cook, a woman named Ursula who watched him toast whole spices in a dry pan with visible skepticism and then left him to it. The rice soaked in salted water. The chicken — thighs, because his mother had opinions about breast meat that she had passed on to him genetically, or at least through repetition — had been marinating since the afternoon in yogurt and her masala and a quantity of green chilis that would clear out anyone who wasn't ready.

Kai was the first to arrive. He came in through the side entrance from the track, hair still damp from the shower, and stopped when the smell hit him.

"That's not cafeteria food."

"Nah." Yusuf was standing at the kitchen pass-through, stirring something with a wooden spoon that belonged to Ursula. "My mum's biryani. Or my attempt at it. She'd tell me I've done something wrong with the rice."

"Your mom sent you rice?"

"Rice, spices, guilt. The usual care package." Yusuf wiped his hands on a towel he'd tucked into his belt. "Grab plates, will you? The stack by the serving line."

Kai got plates. The cafeteria was mostly empty — two athletes from the French sprint relay eating protein bowls at the far end, a physiotherapist reading on her tablet near the window. The windows themselves were golden with late-evening light, the sun sitting low above the western ridge and making the snowfields there look like they were lit from underneath. Past eight and still fully bright. The days had gotten very long.

Lena came in from the pool side, her hair wet and carrying the faint chlorine trace it always did. She sat down across from Kai without ceremony, looked at the plates, and said, "Is that Yusuf's?"

"His mom's recipe. Allegedly."

"Good." She pulled the plate closer. "The cafeteria chicken's been the same chicken for three days."

"It's the same chicken every day," Kai said. "They just move it to different trays."

"That isn't true."

"I know. But it feels true."

Margot arrived last. She stood in the entrance for a moment — not hesitating, but registering the scene before joining it. She was wearing a grey sweatshirt with nothing on it, the kind of thing you put on when you'd stopped thinking about what you were wearing. She sat down next to Lena, which put her across from where Yusuf's plate was waiting.

"Biryani," Margot said, looking at the serving dish Yusuf was carrying to the table with both hands.

"Biryani," Yusuf confirmed.

"Your mum's?"

"My mum's spices, my execution. Don't hold her responsible."

"I never do." Margot took the serving spoon and started filling her plate. The rice was golden-brown in layers, the chicken underneath, the fried onions on top. The smell was cumin and saffron and the particular warmth of cardamom that wasn't sweet exactly but made you think of sweetness.

Yusuf sat down. "It needs raita and I haven't got any yogurt, so." He gestured at the table as though the absence of yogurt was a physical object they could all see.

"It's fine," Lena said. She was already eating. "It's really good."

"Don't sound surprised."

"I'm not surprised. I said it's good."

For a while they just ate. The biryani was good — the rice had that texture where each grain held its shape but gave under your teeth, the chicken was tender and spiced in layers that arrived at different points in the bite, and the chilis were enough to make Kai's eyes water on the second mouthful but not enough to stop him going back for a third.

"Christ," Kai said, after a particularly loaded bite. He reached for the water.

"The chilis?" Yusuf didn't look up from his plate.

"Yeah."

"My mum goes heavier. This is the mild version."

"This is mild?"

"For Birmingham, yeah."

Margot ate through the heat without comment, which tracked with everything Kai knew about Margot. Yusuf ate steadily, the way he did everything — focused, economical, not especially social about it. Lena ate with the same precision she brought to most physical activities, small bites, thorough. Margot ate like she was hungry, which she probably was. Kai had noticed she'd been skipping meals earlier in the month. He hadn't said anything because it wasn't his business and because Margot would have told him to fuck off.

The French relay athletes finished and left. The physiotherapist left. The cafeteria was theirs.

"So," Margot said. She set her fork down. "Big day tomorrow."

"Yeah," Kai said.

"Plane to Zurich. Zurich to Doha. Doha to forty degrees and whatever Meridian's built out there."

"You've been reading the briefing packet," Yusuf said.

"I've had time to read." Margot picked up her fork again, pointed it at nothing in particular. "I've had a lot of time to read."

The joke was in her delivery more than the words — dry, aimed at herself, the timing landing somewhere between funny and not. Yusuf exhaled a short laugh. Kai smiled. Lena looked at Margot with an expression that held several things at once and then she smiled too, and the four of them sat in it for a moment, the fact that it was funny and that nothing about the reason for it was funny, and the laughter was brief and real and didn't try to become anything larger than itself.

"What are you going to do?" Lena asked.

"While you're all in Doha?" Margot shrugged. "Walk the halls. Eat the chicken. Maybe finally figure out the night cook's name."

"Ursula," Yusuf said.

"See, you've ruined my one project."

Kai finished his plate and served himself more. The light outside was starting to deepen, the gold thinning toward amber as the sun dropped behind the ridge. The snowfields on the western peaks had gone from luminous to just white.

"My mom keeps sending me clips," he said. "Of the old races. The natural-category ones."

Lena looked at him. "Before the rebuild?"

"Yeah. She's got them all saved somewhere — her phone or some cloud thing. She sends them with no caption. Just the video."

"Does she want you to watch them?" Yusuf asked.

"I think she wants me to know she's watching them." Kai looked at the rim of rice on his plate. "She says she can tell the difference. Between those and the enhanced footage."

"Can she?" Margot asked.

"I don't know. Maybe. The old ones — I look different in them. Looser. I used to do this thing with my arms coming out of the blocks, this kind of — " He made a motion with his elbows that looked like nothing specific. "She says she can see the moment where I decide to kick, and in the new footage I don't decide, I just go."

Nobody said anything for a moment. Lena was watching him with that particular attention she gave to descriptions of bodies doing things their owners hadn't commanded — the stillness of someone hearing their own condition described in different terms.

"She's probably right," Kai said. "She's been watching me run since I was nine. She'd know."

Yusuf got up and brought back water for the table — four glasses from the stack by the serving line. He sat down and drank half of his.

"Farah's been on a streak," he said.

"Your sister?" Lena asked.

"Yeah. She plays this — I don't even know what it is. Some card game her flatmate taught her, like rummy but with rules I can't follow. She wins constantly. She rang me Thursday and spent twenty minutes explaining a hand she'd played. I understood none of it."

"Is she good at cards generally?" Kai asked.

"She's good at everything she decides to be good at. It's annoying. It's always been like that." Yusuf's voice was easy when he talked about Farah — not the careful tone he used when her condition came up in professional contexts, just a brother complaining about his sister with visible pride underneath. "She's got this thing where she pretends she doesn't know the rules so people underestimate her, and then she cleans them out. She did it to my cousin at Eid and he didn't speak to her for a week."

"Good for her," Margot said.

"It's proper evil, is what it is."

"I said good for her."

Yusuf smiled at his plate. The biryani was mostly gone. Remnants of rice clung to the serving dish in a golden-brown crust, and Yusuf scraped at it absently with the edge of the spoon — a gesture that said something about how dishes were done in his household, every grain accounted for.

Lena had been quiet for a few minutes. She was looking out the window at the mountains and the last of the light. Her hand went to the back of her neck, where the neural link port sat under her hair. She touched it and brought her hand back down, and it was unclear whether the gesture was deliberate or the unconscious check Kai had seen her make dozens of times.

"It's getting easier," she said. "The quiet."

Nobody asked what she meant.

"The first week after the patch, I kept — checking. Like when you think you've left the stove on, except there isn't a stove. There never was a stove." She turned her water glass in her hand. "Something used to be running in the background and now it isn't, and my brain keeps reaching for it. I think for the first week I confused my own thoughts with symptoms. I'd be thinking something, and then I'd stop and think, was that me? Because I'd never had to ask that before. Everything was just — everything. The system and me were the same signal."

She set the glass down.

"Now I can hear the difference. Most of the time. My thoughts are slower, and they have edges — like, I can feel where one stops and another one starts. The system used to smooth that out. Everything flowed. And now it doesn't flow, and I keep thinking something's broken, and then I remember that's just how thinking works if you're doing it yourself."

"Is it better?" Kai asked.

Lena considered this for a long moment, her thumb against the side of the water glass.

"It's mine," she said. "I don't know if that's the same thing."

Outside, the light was going. The sun had dropped fully behind the ridge and the sky above it was streaked orange and pale rose in bands that faded upward into a blue so deep it was nearly the color it would be in twenty minutes when the stars came out. The mountains held the last of the day on their snow caps — bright against the darkening sky, almost phosphorescent — and then that faded too, and the cafeteria windows became mirrors, reflecting the four of them back at themselves — four people sitting at a table, plates pushed aside, the fluorescent strip above the serving line casting the only light in the room.

"Right," Yusuf said eventually. "Dishes."

"I'll do them," Margot said.

"You don't have to — "

"I know I don't have to. Let me do the dishes."

She stood and started stacking plates. Yusuf let her. Kai cleared the serving dish and the glasses, carrying them to the kitchen pass-through where Margot ran water into the industrial sink. The sound of it — water hitting steel, the clank of ceramic being set down — was ordinary. That was all it was.

Lena stayed at the table. She was looking at the dark windows where the mountains had been. Her hand went to the back of her neck again and she left it there this time, her fingers resting against the small circle of the port.

Yusuf dried as Margot washed. They didn't talk much — a few words about where things went, Margot asking which shelf the serving dish belonged on, Yusuf reaching past her to put it back because she couldn't quite get to it. The efficiency of two people in a kitchen who didn't need to discuss what they were doing.

When the dishes were done, Margot dried her hands on the towel Yusuf had been wearing in his belt. She folded it and laid it on the counter.

"Fly safe," she said. To the room, not to anyone in particular.

"Yeah," Kai said.

"Compete — " She stopped. Whatever she'd been about to say, she thought better of it, or she couldn't find the version of it that didn't sound like coaching. "Just. Yeah."

"Yeah," Yusuf said.

Margot left through the side entrance that led to the residential wing. The door swung shut behind her with the hydraulic hiss that all the facility doors made, a sound they'd heard ten thousand times and that Kai realized he wouldn't hear again for weeks. Then it was three of them.

Yusuf stretched his back and his spine cracked audibly. "I should pack. I haven't packed a thing. I've been making biryani instead of packing."

"Good trade," Kai said.

"Tell that to my bag at four in the morning." He pushed his chair back. "Night, both of you."

"Night."

"Goodnight, Yusuf. And thank you. For the food."

"Thank my mum. I'll tell her someone appreciated it." He raised a hand without turning around and walked out, and the door hissed shut again, and it was Kai and Lena in the empty cafeteria.

The fluorescent strip hummed. The windows were fully dark now. The smell of cumin and saffron and his mother's garam masala still hung in the air, fainter, thinning toward nothing the way smells did when the source was gone and all that was left was what the room had absorbed.

"You all right?" Kai asked.

"Yes." Lena brought her hand down from the back of her neck. "I was thinking about how many meals I've eaten in this room."

"A lot?"

"Since I was sixteen. So." She looked at the table, the surface wiped clean, the chairs pushed back to where they'd been before any of this. "It won't feel like this again."

She wasn't being dramatic. She was describing something she could see clearly — that whatever happened in Doha, the race, the result, whatever public reckoning followed everything that had gone wrong at this facility, the four of them would not sit at this table again in the same configuration. Margot would be somewhere else by the time they came back, if they came back. The investigation Yusuf's data might trigger would change what this place was, or close it.

"No," Kai said. "Probably not."

Lena stood and came around the table to where he was sitting. She stood next to his chair and he reached for her hand and she gave it to him — his left hand, the one with the wrist that ached, her right hand, cool from the water glass. They stayed like that for a while in the empty cafeteria, the fluorescent strip humming above them and the smell of someone's mother's cooking still faintly in the air.

Then they turned off the light and went to pack.
