# Chapter 10

The pool was empty except for Lena.

Margot settled into the coaching chair on the observation deck and pulled up the session display. Six forty-five a.m. The facility was still quiet — the sprinters didn't start until eight, the calibration bays were dark. Below the glass wall, the pool's surface was flat under the overhead lights, ten lanes of still water catching the fluorescent glare and throwing it back in long blue rectangles. Lena was on the far deck, pulling her cap on, tucking the last strands of hair behind her ears. The gesture exposed the neural link port at the base of her skull — a small circle, skin-colored, that disappeared again when she let her hair fall.

Margot strapped the feed interface to her left wrist. The connection pads found her pulse and warmed. She opened the session file on the console, logged Lena's athlete ID, and set the pacing target at 1:04 per hundred meters. Standard aerobic. Nothing that would flag.

She pressed connect.

The feed opened like a door into someone else's room.

First came the temperature — the air on Lena's skin, the deck concrete cool under her bare feet. Then proprioception flooded in: Lena's shoulders rotating in a warm-up stretch, the specific tension in her right latissimus dorsi from yesterday's session, the faint ache behind her eyes that was either fatigue or altitude. Margot knew it was altitude. She also knew it had been six weeks since Lena arrived, so altitude was a generous explanation.

Lena dove. The water hit Margot's borrowed sensorium as a wall of pressure and thermal shift — twenty-seven degrees, a degree warmer than the air, closing over Lena's body like a glove. Then the stroke began and Margot forgot everything else.

Lena's freestyle was not like other swimmers'. Margot had been on feeds with a dozen aquatic athletes over three years and none of them moved through water the way Lena did. The neural link processed hydrodynamic resistance as spatial information — the water's density rendered as a three-dimensional field that Lena's body navigated the way a bird navigates updrafts, adjusting angle and force and timing in continuous micro-corrections that no unenhanced swimmer could execute. Through the feed, Margot could feel each correction as it happened: the left hand entering the water two degrees steeper to catch a denser column, the hip rotating a fraction earlier to lengthen the pull, the kick adjusting its amplitude to match the stroke rate with the mechanical precision of matched gears.

It was beautiful. It was always beautiful.

Lena swam the first hundred meters in 1:03.8. Clean. The second hundred: 1:04.1. The telemetry on Margot's console showed a steady heart rate of 162, stroke rate of 68 per minute, neural link operating within normal parameters. Lena's flip turn at the two-hundred wall was textbook — wall contact, tuck, push, streamline, breakout at four meters.

The third hundred meters began and something changed.

Margot felt it before the data confirmed it. The feed carried a qualitative shift that was not in the numbers, not in the stroke rate or the heart rate or the lap time. It was in the texture of being inside Lena's body. The specific character of Lena's consciousness — the way her attention rested on the water as a medium to be read, the slight formality of her proprioceptive awareness, the deliberateness with which she initiated each stroke — began to thin.

Margot had felt flickers of this in earlier sessions. A moment of flatness, like a television losing signal for a frame. She'd noted it in the logs as minor telemetry drift and told Lena it was within acceptable parameters. It had been easy to believe because the flickers were brief and the swimming continued and the data looked clean.

This was not a flicker.

Lena's personality was leaving. Not fading — evacuating, the way air leaves a room when you open both doors. Margot could feel it happening through the feed in real time: the interior architecture of a specific person collapsing inward, the textures that made Lena *Lena* — her precision, her attentiveness, her awareness of the water as something she negotiated with rather than moved through — smoothing out into something featureless and fast. What took Lena's place was optimization. The neural link running the body the way a program runs a machine. Perfect stroke mechanics executing without hesitation, without doubt, without the wasteful friction of a person deciding to move.

The stroke rate climbed. Seventy per minute. Seventy-two. Lena's body was accelerating without anyone telling it to. The system had found its own pacing, independent of Margot's target, independent of Lena's intent, because there was no intent to be independent of. The system was alone in there.

Through the feed, the experience was — Margot couldn't have named it under oath. She'd spent three years chasing intensity through borrowed bodies. Tomás's hurdle clearance, the hamstrings loading and the explosive drive over the bar. The gymnast's spatial awareness during a release move, the body rotating in air with a certainty that felt like faith. The sprinters' anaerobic rush, the final twenty meters when the lungs are done and the legs are running on chemical afterburn. All of those were athletes pushing their enhanced bodies to the limit, and the feed translated the effort into borrowed sensation that Margot lived off the way a leech lives off blood.

This was nothing like effort. This was the boundary between person and system dissolving in real time, and Margot was at the edge of it, feeling both sides simultaneously. On one side: the ghost of Lena's presence, thinning to a residue. On the other: the system's optimization, seamless, efficient, devoid of any quality that could be called human. The water moved past Lena's body like a negotiated surrender. Every stroke was perfect because nothing was in the way.

Margot pushed the pacing target to 1:00.

She did this with her right hand on the console touchscreen, her left arm extended on the armrest with the feed interface pulsing warm against her wrist. The target update would reach Lena's neural link and adjust the system's pacing demands. Whether Lena would process the adjustment as a conscious decision to swim faster was — well. Lena wasn't processing anything.

Heart rate on the console: 178. 182.

Through the glass wall, Margot could see Lena's body cutting through the water. The stroke looked the same from above — long, clean, the catch and pull and recovery that Margot had coached for six weeks. From the observation deck, you'd think the swimmer was fine. Just fast. Just in the zone.

Through the feed, Margot knew the swimmer was empty.

185. The console flagged it. A yellow border around the heart rate number, the system's protocol marking sustained elevation above the aerobic threshold. The flag would show in the session log.

Margot's own breathing had quickened. She was sitting in the coaching chair on a dry deck above a pool, watching through glass, and her chest was moving with borrowed exertion. The feed translated Lena's cardiac output as a thick rhythmic pressure in Margot's sternum — not her heart, Lena's heart, the muscular contraction arriving through the neural interface as a felt event in Margot's chest. It was fast and strong and running without anyone at the controls.

She pushed the target to 0:58.

The system complied. Lena's stroke rate hit seventy-six. The flip turn at the three-hundred wall was explosive — the push-off drove Lena to breakout in under two seconds, the legs firing with a force that Margot felt as compression and heat in her own phantom quads. Through the feed, Lena's body was a single coordinated event: arms pulling, core rotating, legs driving, lungs cycling air with metronomic regularity. All of it synchronized, all of it purposeful, all of it unmanned.

188. 190.

Margot watched the heart rate number turn red. The system upgraded the flag to a protocol alert — sustained heart rate above 190 during a programmed aerobic session, no interval justification in the session plan. The alert would generate a notification to the medical team unless it was closed within sixty seconds.

She closed it.

The override authority was built into the feed interface — coaches could dismiss false-positive alerts during interval sessions, sprints, and high-intensity blocks. Margot used the override the way she always used it: as permission she'd granted herself.

193.

One more lap. Just one more. The feed was immaculate. The system's optimization had reached a plateau of efficiency that felt like floating in warm current — Lena's body moving through water without effort, without choice, without self, and Margot riding the sensation like a wave she would never generate on her own. Her own lungs hadn't worked at this capacity in nine years. Her own legs hadn't driven through water at competition intensity since the 2028 Olympics, where she'd touched the wall fifth and walked out of the natatorium and never gone back. Through the feed, she was in a body that could do what hers couldn't, and the body wasn't even occupied.

195.

Margot pulled the target back to 1:02.

She did this because at 195 the risk was cardiac, and even through the feed, even in the middle of the best session she'd ever had, Margot was still someone who had trained as an athlete and knew what happened to a heart above 195. It threw extra beats. It stuttered. It could stop.

The system adjusted. Stroke rate dropped. Heart rate began the slow plateau — 193, 191. Still red. Margot closed the second alert.

Lena reached the four-hundred wall and stopped.

She didn't touch the wall and turn. She reached it and hung there, one hand on the gutter, the other on the lane rope. Her shoulders heaved. Her cap had shifted, hair escaping along one side. Her mouth was open, pulling air in rough gasps that Margot could hear through the glass.

Through the feed, the reentry was sudden and ugly. Like watching a room refill with air — the flatness cracking open and something rushing back in, jagged and disorganized. Lena's consciousness returning to her own body, and the return was not smooth. It was confusion first, then a swell of something that the feed translated as cold despite the warm water, then the physical awareness of pain: her chest, her arms, her legs all registering an exertion that she had not been present to experience.

Margot disconnected.

The feed interface went dark. The warmth on her wrist died. Lena's body — the water, the muscle load, the heart's hammering pressure — all of it dropped away in a single neural cliff, and what remained was Margot in a chair on a dry deck, breathing her own air with her own lungs, weighing her own weight on her own bones.

Her hands were shaking. She pulled the interface off her wrist and set it on the console. The skin underneath was flushed and slightly raised where the connection pads had made contact. She pressed her thumb into the mark and held it there, the way she used to press a bruise after hard training — testing whether it still hurt, confirming she could still feel it.

Below the glass, Lena hadn't moved. She was hanging on the lane rope with both arms, her face half in the water, her back rising and falling. A full minute passed. Then Lena's head turned, slowly, scanning the deck as if she didn't recognize it. The pool was empty. Dara's session wasn't until nine. There was no one to ask.

"What lap is this?"

Lena said it out loud. To no one. To the water. Her voice came through the observation deck's audio pickup, thin and flattened by the pool's tile acoustics.

Margot sat still and watched.

After another thirty seconds, Lena pulled herself to the wall and climbed out. Her legs were unsteady on the deck. She sat on the pool edge with her feet in the water, her back curving forward, her elbows on her knees. She looked like someone who'd been woken from anesthesia and was trying to reconstruct the room.

Margot saved the session file. Heart rate peaked at 195 — the override closures were logged but required no further action unless someone audited the session manually. The telemetry showed a training swim with a moderate elevation in the middle split, attributable to intensity. The desync signatures were buried in the neural link data, visible if someone pulled the raw stream and ran a pattern analysis, invisible in the summary dashboard. Nobody would run the pattern analysis because nobody knew to look for it. Margot had been the one running the desync check in earlier sessions. She'd been the one who told Lena the drift was within parameters.

She closed the console, put the interface in her bag, and stood up. Her legs felt heavy and disconnected — the post-feed drop, the body remembering that it belonged to a thirty-five-year-old woman who hadn't competed in almost a decade. The observation deck smelled of chlorine and recycled air. The glass wall in front of her showed the pool, bright and still, and Lena sitting on the edge with her hand on the back of her neck, fingers pressing the neural link port the way she always pressed it — unconsciously, the way someone touches a tooth that aches.

Margot knew what she should do. Walk down to the deck. Tell Lena the session showed a sustained desync event — not drift, not parameters, but a genuine vacancy of consciousness lasting multiple laps. Flag it for Petra. Send the raw neural data to Yusuf, who was in the calibration bay building a patch that was supposed to fix exactly this and who needed to know how bad it had gotten if the patch was going to work. Make a call that protected the athlete, because that was what the coaching position existed to do.

She walked out of the observation deck and down the corridor toward her quarters.

The walk took four minutes. Linoleum floor, fluorescent ceiling panels, the smell of the facility: antiseptic, recycled air, faint coffee from the cafeteria two corridors over. She passed a sprinter stretching in the hallway, a woman she didn't know, young, both calves showing the faint surgical seams of graft integration. The woman nodded. Margot nodded back.

In her room, she sat on the bed and took the feed interface out of her bag. The device was the size of a thick watch — brushed steel housing, the connection pads on the underside still warm from her skin. She'd bought it four years ago when she transitioned from competition to coaching. The vendor had called it a NeuroSync Performance Monitor. Margot had known what it was. A way to feel a body that performed. A way back inside.

She turned the device over in her hands. The connection pads were faintly discolored from years of use — her wrist oils, her pulse, her heat. She put it in the nightstand drawer and closed the drawer.

She lay back on the bed and stared at the ceiling. The diagnostic display on the wall showed her vitals: heart rate 84, slowly settling. Respiration normal. Enhancement status: none. That line was always blank. Margot had never been enhanced. She'd competed in the last years of the Traditional Games, the 2028 Olympics in Brisbane, and she'd swum the 200 butterfly in a body that was entirely hers and that body had placed fifth.

The ceiling was white. The room was quiet. The feed session was twenty minutes behind her and the absence of it was already spreading through her like cold water filling a warm pool.

She knew Lena's desync was dangerous. She knew the two-lap vacancy was not drift, not parameters, not an artifact of altitude adjustment. She knew that a twenty-two-year-old swimmer who lost consciousness while her body continued performing at a heart rate of 195 was in medical danger that was not theoretical, not eventual, but immediate. She knew that the Kirchberg desync protocol required flagging a Class III event within the hour and grounding the athlete until cleared.

She also knew that flagging it meant Petra would pull the session log. The log would show the feed connection — duration, depth, the full seven minutes Margot had stayed in when the protocol called for disconnection at the first sign of Class II drift. It would show the pacing target increases. It would show the override closures on the heart rate alerts. It would show that Margot Voss, neural performance coach, had pushed a desyncing swimmer's heart rate to 195 while that swimmer was not present in her own body to consent, to resist, or to be afraid.

The drawer stayed closed. Her hands had stopped shaking. The flatness was settling in — the familiar post-feed emptiness that she'd stopped trying to name and started just enduring, the hours between sessions when her body was only hers and that wasn't enough.

She closed her eyes.

---

Lena spent the afternoon trying to find the two laps.

She sat at the desk in her quarters with the session data on the wall display — the telemetry Margot had filed after the morning swim. Stroke rate, split times, heart rate, neural link activity. The graphs were continuous. The heart rate showed a moderate elevation in the second half of the set, flagged by the system and attributed to training intensity. No anomalies in the neural link summary. No desync markers.

The data said she had swum four hundred meters without interruption.

Lena remembered two hundred and fifty.

The warm-up. The first hundred meters — smooth, the catch feeling clean, the water receiving her. The second hundred, the turn at the wall, the push-off, the breakout. The beginning of the third hundred. The flip turn at 250.

Then she was on the lane rope. Chest heaving so hard she could feel her pulse in her ears, in her wrists, in the spaces between her ribs. The far wall of the pool impossibly bright under the lights, and she didn't know where she was in the set. Not hazy. Not confused. Absent. She'd been lifted out of the session and set back down at a different point, and the distance between those two points did not exist as experience.

She had said something out loud. *What lap is this.* To the pool, because there was no one else, and the question was not rhetorical.

The telemetry said her body had swum those laps. Her arms had pulled, her legs had kicked, the flip turns had executed, the wall contacts had registered on the timing system. Her heart had been beating at 190. Her lungs had been cycling air. All of it had happened. She had not been there for any of it.

She'd felt something like this before — the evening in her quarters, weeks ago, when twenty minutes had passed while she stared at a paragraph without reading it. She'd come back to herself without any sense of having left. And before that, the training session where her flip turn had executed a rotation pattern from last season's firmware — a motor decision her neural link had made without her. Those had been small. A flicker. A moment. Margot had looked at the data and said *within acceptable parameters,* and Lena had accepted that because Margot had the data and the authority and Lena trusted both.

This was not a flicker. Two laps. At least three minutes, probably longer. Her body in the water, performing at competition intensity, while Lena was nowhere.

She touched the neural link port at the base of her skull. The implant's ceramic edge was smooth under her fingertip, skin-temperature, seamless with the bone beneath. She'd had it since she was four years old. It was as familiar as her own vertebrae. She pressed her thumb hard against the port and held it there, feeling the slight depression where the surface met the bone, as if pressure could tell her whether the thing was still hers.

She pulled up the Kirchberg desync protocol on the display. She'd read it before — everyone had, after the virus scare. Moderate desynchronization, Class II: intermittent divergence between neural link motor commands and conscious intent, duration under five seconds. Report to medical staff within 24 hours. Severe desynchronization, Class III: sustained divergence exceeding ten seconds, loss of voluntary motor control, or loss of conscious awareness during link-directed activity. Report to medical staff immediately. Ground the athlete. Cease all linked activity until cleared.

Three minutes was not a Class II event.

She closed the protocol and stood up. The room was small — bed, desk, bathroom, the wall display glowing softly with her numbers. Heart rate 72. Respiration normal. Neural link status: nominal. All the numbers said she was fine.

She found Margot in the corridor outside the coaching suite. Margot was coming from the cafeteria with a paper cup of coffee, her coaching bag over one shoulder, her hair down. She looked up when Lena came around the corner and her face did the coach scan — the quick assessment, automatic, reading the athlete for damage or fatigue or hesitation. It took less than a second.

"Hey," Margot said.

"Can I ask about this morning?"

"Sure."

They were standing in the corridor. Fluorescent light, linoleum floor, the low hum of the pool ventilation system audible through the walls. A calibration technician passed them pushing a cart of equipment, and they stepped aside to let it through. The cart's wheels squeaked. Margot sipped her coffee and waited.

"Something happened in the water," Lena said. "During the set. I can't remember the last part of the session."

She watched Margot's face. The scan was over. Margot's expression had settled into a careful neutral that could have been concern or patience or nothing.

"Can't remember how?" Margot said.

"I don't mean unclear. I mean it's not there. I remember the flip turn at two-fifty, and then I'm on the lane rope and the set is done. There's nothing between."

"Your telemetry looked clean," Margot said. The coffee cup was at her mouth, not quite drinking. "Heart rate was a little high in the middle split, but that's intensity. Your stroke was solid."

"I've been at this altitude for six weeks."

"Altitude's cumulative. Your body adjusts and readjusts — it doesn't stabilize linearly. Everyone's baselines shift."

"You were on the feed." Lena said it as a fact. "You'd have felt what was happening."

"I felt your stroke and your pacing. That's what the feed monitors. Your stroke was clean, Lena. Honestly, you looked strong."

"It doesn't feel like altitude."

"What does it feel like?"

The question was open and unhurried. The kind of question a good coach asks — giving the athlete room, not leading, not dismissing. Margot was good at this part. She'd always been good at this part.

"Like I wasn't there," Lena said. She heard the words and they were wrong — too simple for what had happened. "Like something removed me and put me back."

She didn't have language for it that was more precise. The absence was total. Not unconsciousness, which she'd experienced once during a childhood procedure and which had a quality of darkness and return. This had no quality at all. No darkness. No blur. No sense of duration. Just a cut in the film of her own experience, and on either side of the cut her body had been swimming and she had not.

"The desync markers in your session data don't show anything past the drift we've been tracking," Margot said. "Same range as last month. Yusuf's got the numbers. If it was spiking outside parameters, the system would've flagged it and it didn't."

"This isn't the same as the drift."

"It can feel bigger than the data shows. Especially right now, with everyone on edge about the virus. A sprinter came to me yesterday convinced her visual tracking was failing. Turned out she was dehydrated."

"I'm not dehydrated."

"I'm not saying you imagined it. I'm saying the session data doesn't match a Class III event. If it did, I'd pull you right now. You know that."

Lena did know that. She knew that Margot was direct and that she didn't waste language on reassurance she couldn't back up. Margot had been the one who flagged the desync in the first place, weeks ago — the telemetry divergence in the post-session review, the slight decoupling between Lena's conscious motor commands and the neural link's execution. She'd identified it, documented it, and told Lena it was within the parameters that Kirchberg and Meridian both considered acceptable for her level of enhancement integration. Lena had believed her because Margot had the data, and Lena had been raised to trust data. The Meridian developmental program had given her that: a faith in numbers that ran deeper than faith in sensation.

"Do you want me to flag it?" Margot said. "I can add a note to the session file and send it to Petra. She'll probably want a full diagnostic — you'd be off the water for two days minimum while they image the link. Maybe longer. Your call."

She offered it plainly. No inflection pushing Lena toward or away from the choice. Just the option, laid out with the consequences attached.

Lena thought about two days out of the water. The diagnostic — lying in the calibration rig while Yusuf's equipment mapped her neural link, the stillness of being read by machines. The results that would either confirm what her missing memory already told her or produce a clean report that contradicted her experience. She thought about what it meant if the diagnostic found something — Class III, grounded, her training block interrupted with the Apex Games four months away. She thought about what it meant if the diagnostic found nothing. If the data said she was fine and her memory said she wasn't, and she had to decide which to believe.

She had always believed the data. The data was what the Meridian program had taught her to trust — the numbers, the metrics, the objective record that existed outside the unreliability of feeling. When she was fourteen and thought she was swimming badly, the coaches had shown her the splits and the splits had been good. When she was nineteen and thought her stroke was changing, the biomechanical analysis had confirmed that the neural link's micro-corrections were within tolerance. The data had always been right and her feelings had sometimes been wrong, and she had learned the lesson the program intended: the system knows your body better than you do.

"I'll think about it," Lena said.

"OK." Margot took a sip of the coffee. The movement was small and ordinary and it closed the conversation — the cup coming down, Margot's posture shifting from attentive stillness back to the loose readiness of someone between tasks. "Let me know."

"Yeah."

"I'll be around."

Margot walked past her down the corridor. Lena heard her footsteps on the linoleum — even, unhurried, the steps of someone who had given an honest answer and moved on.

Lena stood in the corridor for a moment after Margot turned the corner. The ventilation hum was louder here, closer to the pool. Through the wall, she could hear the faint slap of water — someone had started a session in the far lanes. Dara, probably. Nine o'clock.

She walked back to her quarters. The corridor was long, the overhead lights steady, the floor reflecting a dull sheen. The mountains were visible through the window at the end of the hall — late-afternoon light on the ridge, the lower slopes dark with snowmelt and exposed rock. The days were getting longer. She could see it in the angle of the shadows on the peaks.

In her room she sat on the bed and looked at the wall display. Heart rate 68. Neural link status: nominal. The numbers were clean and steady and they told her she was fine.

She put her hand on the back of her neck. The port was there. Smooth, warm, hers since before she could remember. The link was how she processed the water. How she felt her stroke, calibrated her turns, understood the pool as a space her body could negotiate. It was the infrastructure of everything she had ever done as an athlete. It had been in her skull for eighteen years, longer than most of her memories, and she had never once had reason to think of it as something separate from herself.

Two laps. Her body in the water, her arms pulling, the walls passing, the turns executing. All of it happening. None of it hers.

She didn't flag the session.

She told herself she'd decide in the morning, that she'd sleep on it, that the data would still be there. She told herself that Margot had seen the telemetry and the telemetry was clean and Margot would know if something was wrong. She told herself these things the way the program had taught her — by trusting the system, by believing the numbers, by accepting that the infrastructure she'd been built around was more reliable than the feeling that something was missing.

Outside, the last of the afternoon light left the mountains. The snow on the upper peaks turned blue, then grey. Lena sat on her bed with her hand on the port and watched the light go.
