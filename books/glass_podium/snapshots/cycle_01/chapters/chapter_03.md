# Chapter 3

The calibration bay ran at a constant seventeen degrees Celsius, two degrees below facility standard. Not for the athletes' benefit — for the equipment. The spectral analyzers drifted at higher temperatures, and Yusuf had fought Building Services for three months to get dedicated climate control installed. He'd won. The bay was cold, the analyzers were stable, and he wore a fleece under his lab coat from October to April.

He arrived at six forty-five, same time every morning. The processing stacks were already running — they never stopped, just cycled between active calibration and standby analysis — and their hum was the sound his working life was built on. A low, constant sixty hertz, just below the threshold where most people consciously registered it. He'd stopped hearing it during his first week at the Kirchberg Institute, three years ago. Now it was the sound of the room being awake.

He set his coffee on the workstation ledge — the same chipped mug, Imperial College crest half-gone — and opened the day's schedule. Six athletes before noon. Four after lunch, then a block for data review and reporting. His official reporting. The other kind happened in the gaps.

The first athlete at seven was Anya Petrov, a javelin specialist from the Russian program. Standard monthly recalibration — her right shoulder graft had been stable for two seasons, but the altitude affected the synthetic muscle's oxygen transfer rates and needed periodic adjustment. She came in already stretching, one earbud still in, pulled it out and dropped it in her lap as she settled into the calibration rig. The rig looked like a dentist's chair designed by people who found dentists insufficiently invasive: padded recline, articulated arm supports, a curved overhead array of sensor plates that read thermal output, electromagnetic fluctuation, and nerve-firing patterns through the skin.

"Morning, Anya. How's the shoulder?"

"Good. A little tight after yesterday's throws."

"Scale of one to ten."

"Three. Maybe four when I reach overhead."

Yusuf pulled up her integration profile on the workstation. The graft's performance envelope scrolled across the display in green and amber bands. "Right, I'm going to run a tension sweep first. You'll feel pressure cycling through the deltoid area, like someone kneading it. About ninety seconds. Try to stay relaxed."

He initiated the sweep. Through the sensor array, the graft responded — synthetic muscle fibers adjusting their resting tension as the calibration signal passed through them. The process was intimate in a way he'd never quite gotten used to. He was inside someone's body, reading signals that the person couldn't feel, making adjustments that would change how their shoulder moved for the next month. His hands were steady on the interface — they always were, the one part of him that never registered what was happening as strange. The athletes lay still and trusted him with this. They had to. There was no other way.

Anya's shoulder responded normally. He adjusted the oxygen transfer parameters for altitude, logged the calibration values, and she was done by seven twenty.

"Feels good," she said, rolling the shoulder experimentally. "Looser."

"Should hold for four weeks. If the tightness comes back before then, flag it."

She left, earbud already back in. Yusuf returned to the workstation and opened a second window — one that didn't connect to the Kirchberg reporting server — and copied the full raw data stream from Anya's shoulder scan. The official log recorded the calibration values and the adjustment parameters. The raw stream contained everything the hardware had seen: nerve-firing patterns, microvascular flow, hormone concentration at the graft site, the complete biological signature of the tissue surrounding the synthetic muscle. A thirty-second calibration produced more biological data than a hospital stay.

He saved the raw stream, minimized the second window, and pulled up the next appointment.

Tomás Reyes arrived at seven thirty. Twenty-one, Ecuadorian, first Apex cycle — a decathlete whose enhancement package was only three years integrated. He sat in the rig and looked up at the overhead sensor array the way most athletes had stopped doing years ago: with genuine curiosity, as if the equipment were something worth seeing rather than something to tolerate.

"How's the integration feeling?"

"Good. Yeah. I think." Reyes flexed his right hand, opened and closed it. "My grip strength's been weird in the mornings. Like the hand's already awake before I am."

Yusuf pulled up his profile. Right forearm graft, bilateral calf integration, spinal bridge — standard decathlon build. The grip issue could be residual calibration drift from altitude adjustment, or it could be the hand's autonomous subsystem running its morning diagnostic cycle at a threshold Reyes could feel. "How long has that been happening?"

"Maybe a week? I wasn't sure if it was, like, normal."

"Could be altitude. Let me take a look." Yusuf initiated the hand's integration scan. Through the display, the graft's synthetic fibers lit up in cross-section — a dense weave of artificial muscle overlaying the natural forearm musculature, filaments fine as suture thread anchored to tendons and the radial bone. The resting tension was slightly elevated. Not alarming. Probably altitude. "I'm going to drop your forearm's resting tension about two percent. You'll feel the hand relax a bit. Might feel weak for an hour while it recalibrates."

"Okay." Reyes settled back. Trusting, in the specific way young athletes trusted their support teams — not from evaluation of evidence, but because the alternative was unmanageable. You didn't question the person adjusting your nervous system while your nervous system was in their hands.

Yusuf made the adjustment. The tension scan normalized. He logged the calibration, noted the grip complaint and the altitude-probable cause in the official file, and Reyes was done by seven fifty-five.

"Thanks, doc."

"Not a doctor. But you're welcome."

Reyes left. Yusuf pulled up his raw data — not the calibration summary he'd logged, but the full integration stream. The resting tension elevation wasn't just the forearm. The calf grafts showed a similar pattern: a low-grade increase in autonomous activity that the standard calibration smoothed away without comment. He copied it to the second window.

The morning continued. A Norwegian cross-country skier with a hip graft that needed seasonal adjustment. A Chinese diver whose visual processing enhancement was producing a fractional depth-perception offset — a known altitude artifact, easily corrected. A French sprinter with stable readings across the board, in and out in twelve minutes. Between each session Yusuf returned to the second window. The raw data accumulated. Not the calibrated, cleaned summaries that went to the Kirchberg servers, but the unprocessed biological output — heart-rate variability, nerve-firing latency, hormone concentrations, the full spectrum of information that the calibration hardware recorded and the official reports reduced to a handful of approved metrics.

He was not doing anything that the system prevented. The raw data existed on the facility's servers and his workstation access was unrestricted. The prohibition was institutional rather than technical: his role was to calibrate, report calibration values, and flag anomalies for the medical team. He was not authorized to retain raw biometric data for analysis outside his calibration duties. The difference between what the system allowed and what the institution permitted was the gap he worked in. He'd been working in it for eleven months.

After lunch — a cheese sandwich eaten at the workstation, crumbs on the keyboard he pushed into the gap between the keys — he pulled up Kai Brennan's full dataset from the previous evening's calibration. Not just the proprioception drift. The complete integration profile: motor nerve latency across both legs, spinal bridge throughput, visual processing load, and the overnight drift data that had flagged that morning. The proprioception gap — the system initiating micro-corrections ahead of Brennan's conscious intent — hadn't settled after calibration. It had continued, a slow creep in the predictive-to-conscious timing, as if the system were learning to stay ahead of him rather than falling back into alignment.

Yusuf cross-referenced it with anomalies he'd been tracking for the past three weeks. Reyes's forearm and calf elevation. The Norwegian skier's hip graft, which showed the same pattern of slightly elevated autonomous activity he'd attributed to seasonal adjustment. A Brazilian hurdler whose sensory integration had produced a single two-second latency spike during a training session six days ago — flagged by the overnight logs, reviewed, attributed to a momentary processing load peak, filed.

Four athletes. Four different enhancement packages, different sports, different integration histories. But all on the same calibration network. And the pattern, when he laid the raw data side by side, was legible: a correlated increase in autonomous system activity, simultaneous within a forty-eight-hour window, across hardware that had no functional reason to synchronize.

He pushed his glasses up his nose — they'd slid, as they did every afternoon when the fleece warmed and the bridge of his nose got damp — and looked at the display. The fluorescent light above the workstation buzzed at a frequency slightly off from the processing stacks' hum, producing a thin interference pattern he'd long ago stopped noticing.

The correlation wasn't conclusive. Four data points wasn't a trend; it was a suggestion. The shared calibration network was designed to propagate optimized parameters across connected systems — that was its purpose. Correlated behavior wasn't inherently anomalous. He'd need a wider sample, a longer time window, and a controlled comparison with athletes on different networks to determine whether the drift was a genuine systemic issue or a seasonal artifact that altitude and temperature were exaggerating.

But it looked wrong. Not in a way he could articulate in an official report — not yet, not with four points and a sensation in his chest that wouldn't pass for data — but wrong in the way an engineer recognized before the numbers confirmed it. The pattern was too clean. Autonomous activity didn't synchronize across different hardware architectures unless something was coordinating it.

He wrote the afternoon's official reports. Brennan: proprioception drift noted, continued monitoring recommended, within acceptable parameters. The Norwegian: hip adjustment completed, seasonal norms. Reyes: grip tension corrected, altitude probable. The Brazilian wasn't his patient today, but he reviewed the filed report from last week and confirmed the latency spike had been logged as a momentary processing artifact.

Everything in order. Everything filed. Everything technically accurate and institutionally complete.

He saved the cross-referenced correlation analysis to the second window and closed it.

---

The calibration bay emptied by six in the evening. The last athlete — a German middle-distance runner, standard monthly — left at five forty, and Yusuf spent twenty minutes on shutdown protocols. Not a full shutdown; the processing stacks ran continuously. But the active hardware needed to be powered down, the sensor arrays cleaned and returned to standby. He did this himself. His two junior engineers, Sara and Luc, handled morning setup. Yusuf handled evening close. He preferred the bay empty. He preferred the quiet that wasn't quiet — the sixty-hertz hum, the faint click of cooling systems cycling, the sound of a room that was working but not watched.

When the rigs were down, he retrieved his personal device from his bag. A data drive smaller than his thumb, matte black, encrypted with a key that existed only in his memory and one physical backup in a drawer in his quarters. He'd bought it in Zurich two years ago, from a specialist electronics shop in the Niederdorf that served researchers and paranoiacs in equal measure. The encryption was standard academic-grade — nothing exotic, nothing that would draw attention if the device were found, but enough to make the data inaccessible without the key.

He plugged it into the workstation's auxiliary port. The second window opened. The day's collection: Brennan's proprioception stream, Reyes's grip and calf data, the Norwegian's hip integration values, the full afternoon's raw biometric pulls, and the cross-referenced correlation analysis. He transferred the files. The progress bar moved quickly — biological signals compressed well. Eleven seconds. He disconnected the drive, dropped it into his trouser pocket, and closed the second window.

The workstation's display showed only the Kirchberg reporting interface. The official logs. The filed reports. The clean and complete records of a thorough engineer's working day.

He turned off the monitor and left.

The connecting corridor had glass walls on the mountain side, and at this hour, in late January, the mountains were a dark mass against a slightly less dark sky. No moon. The exterior temperature display by the corridor junction read minus twelve. Inside, the facility's heating system held a steady twenty-one degrees, and the transition from the calibration bay's seventeen to the corridor's warmth was the only temperature change Yusuf experienced most days. He didn't go outside often. The cold didn't bother him but the mountains did, in a way he'd never examined — too large, too indifferent, a landscape that was the opposite of Birmingham in every dimension and reminded him of it by sheer contrast.

His quarters were on the third floor of the residential wing. A single room, larger than the athletes' rooms — a staff privilege he'd never requested and didn't refuse. Bed, desk, bathroom. The wall-mounted diagnostic display that glowed faintly with facility status data he'd stopped reading months ago. A kettle, a jar of loose-leaf Yorkshire Tea his mother posted monthly, and a stack of books on the desk that he was getting through at roughly a chapter a week, which meant he was three months behind on a history of the Birmingham canal network that Farah had given him for his birthday.

He made tea. Dropped the data drive into the desk drawer next to its twin — the backup, loaded weekly, same encryption, same matte black casing. Then he sat down and opened his laptop and dialed Farah.

She picked up on the third ring. Her face filled the screen — dark hair pulled back, the living room of the flat in Moseley behind her, the corner of the bookshelf he'd helped her assemble two Christmases ago visible over her left shoulder. She was smiling.

"Yu! Hang on, let me —" She adjusted something off-screen. The camera settled. "There. Hi."

"Hey. How's your day been?"

"Fine. Normal. Went to Asda, made a lasagne. It was terrible but I ate all of it, so." She shrugged. Her face was animated, present. She looked good. She always looked good on the screen — the compression hid the small things.

"Did you use Mum's recipe or the one from that website?"

"The website. Mum's has too many steps. I can't be arsed with bechamel from scratch when there's a perfectly good jar."

"She'd disown you."

"She'd disown me and then bring round a proper one. Win-win." Farah grinned. There was a pause — half a beat longer than the grin warranted — before she continued. "So what are you up to? Still in the lab at —" She checked something, probably the time. "Seven at night?"

"Just finished up. Long day."

"They're all long days with you. You need to get out more. Go look at a mountain or something. You're in the Alps and you spend all your time in a room with no windows."

"There's a window in the corridor."

"Yu."

"I'm fine. I like the work." He took a sip of tea. The screen showed Farah's expression — fond irritation, a familiar arrangement. He watched for what he always watched for: the speed of her responses, the fluidity of her transitions, the gap between thought and word. She was good tonight. Animated. But there was something in the rhythm. A fractional delay, not in the connection — he'd long ago learned the difference between network lag and the other kind — but in her. The sentences arrived at the same brightness, the same pace, but each one took slightly longer to begin. Like a stride that looked the same to a spectator but whose foot hit the ground later.

"How's the physio going?" he asked.

"Oh, fine. Same. She's dead enthusiastic. Lots of squeezing things." Farah held up her hands and mimed squeezing something invisible. "I told her I don't really need more grip strength, I need to be able to open the —" She stopped. Her expression didn't change — the smile held — but there was a gap where a word should have been. "Jars," she said, and the smile widened slightly, performing the recovery. "I need to be able to open jars. The pasta sauce specifically. The Tesco one. Whoever decided to make those lids that tight is my actual enemy."

"I'll send you one of those rubber grip things."

"I've got three. They don't work on the Tesco ones. I've tried. I think they're sealed at a molecular level."

She was past it, the lost word already behind her. But Yusuf had heard the gap. A fraction of a second where "jars" — a word she'd used ten thousand times, a word with no complexity, no retrieval difficulty — had not been there when she reached for it.

Voss-Kessler worked like that. Not the dramatic losses first — not memory, not cognition, not the things that would be immediately alarming. The deterioration began with the small retrieval tasks, the frictionless ones that a healthy brain performed without conscious effort. Word-finding in casual speech. The timing of a sentence. Fine motor adjustments that fell below the threshold of awareness. By the time the losses were visible to anyone who wasn't specifically looking, the progression had been at work for months.

"Mum called today," Farah said. "She's worried about you. Says you're not eating properly."

"How would she know?"

"She has sources. I think she's paying one of the cafeteria staff."

"She's not paying anyone. She doesn't even know where the cafeteria is."

"Well, she's worried. Eat a vegetable, Yu. One vegetable. For her peace of mind."

"I had a sandwich today."

"What was in it?"

"Cheese."

"That's not a vegetable."

"There was lettuce."

"Iceberg doesn't count. It's basically water in leaf form."

He laughed. She laughed. The sound was the same as it had always been — bright, a little too loud for the flat's thin walls. But underneath it, or alongside it, in a register that only someone calibrated to hear it would catch, there was the delay. The half-second gap before each response that hadn't been there six months ago. The word that vanished mid-sentence and was recovered so smoothly that anyone else would have marked it as a normal fumble. The slightly slower execution of the joke's delivery — not the wit, which was intact, possibly sharpening in compensation, but the motor production of the words themselves.

He could track it. He could not fix it.

"You still there?" Farah asked.

"Yeah. Sorry. Thinking."

"About work?"

"About what I'm having for dinner."

"Cheese sandwich?"

"Probably."

"Hopeless." She yawned, and the yawn lasted longer than it should have — her jaw moving with a looseness that would have been invisible on a lower-resolution call but was legible on his screen, to his eyes. "I should go. I've got that thing with Priya tomorrow, early."

"What thing?"

"We're going to the —" She paused. Not the brief pause of thinking. The pause of reaching for something that should have been within arm's length and finding the shelf empty. "Cadbury World." She found it. The path to finding it had been visible. "She's never been. I've been four times and it's still good. They give you free chocolate at the end."

"Tell Priya I say hi."

"Will do. Eat a vegetable."

"Night, Farah."

"Night, Yu."

The screen went dark. Yusuf sat at his desk with the tea cooling in his hands. The wall display cast its faint blue glow across the room. The heating system droned through the walls, the building breathing.

Farah was eight months past diagnosis. Her neurologist in Birmingham, Dr. Adeyemi, had described the typical progression: speech processing and fine motor first, then gross motor, then cognitive. Farah had stopped her there. She didn't want the full timeline. She wanted to know what she could do now, what she'd need help with, and when. Practical questions. She had always been practical.

Voss-Kessler syndrome had no treatment. The condition was rare — perhaps six hundred people worldwide — which meant it attracted neither pharmaceutical investment nor the large-scale clinical attention that produced breakthroughs. The most promising research, published fourteen months ago in a neurology journal Yusuf now read cover to cover, suggested that high-resolution neural mapping data from enhanced-human systems could illuminate the degenerative pathway well enough to design targeted interventions. The authors had proposed a collaborative data-sharing initiative. Meridian BioSystems, which controlled the vast majority of enhancement-generated biological data worldwide, had not responded.

The data existed. It was generated every day in the room Yusuf worked in, by every athlete who lay in his calibration rig and trusted him with their systems. Neural mapping at a resolution no hospital scanner could match, because the enhancement hardware was already inside the body, already reading the signals at the source. Months of it. An archive growing daily in the drawer of his desk, in a resolution that Farah's neurologist could only dream of accessing.

It was not available through any channel he'd tried. He had written to Meridian's research liaison office — a careful, professional inquiry about data-sharing protocols for academic research partnerships. The response, eleven weeks later, had been a two-paragraph form letter directing him to Meridian's commercial licensing division, where the minimum data access agreement started at figures that assumed you were a pharmaceutical company, not an engineer with a sister.

So Yusuf took it.

He set the mug down. Opened the laptop and navigated to his analysis folder — not the raw biometric archive on the drives, but the work he did with it. The cross-referenced correlation from today sat at the top: four athletes, simultaneous autonomous drift, no functional explanation. He could report it. Flag the pattern, submit it to Kirchberg's data review board, let the institutional process evaluate it. That was the correct procedure. That was what his contract, his professional obligations, and his training all required as the appropriate response to an unexplained pattern in safety-critical biological systems.

He would need to explain the cross-referencing. He would need to explain why he'd retained raw data outside his reporting scope. He would need to explain the second window and the eleven months of files in his desk drawer and the encrypted drives that held the biological lives of eighty-three athletes in more detail than those athletes knew about themselves.

He closed the laptop. The blue glow from the wall display settled on the ceiling. The building hummed around him, and outside the window the mountains were nothing, just a darkness where the sky stopped, and he sat there for a long time before he turned off the light.
