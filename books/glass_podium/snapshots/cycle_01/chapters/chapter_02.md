# Chapter 2

Lena Morozova liked the pool at five-thirty in the morning, before the overhead lights came all the way up. The facility ran them on a schedule — dim at five, half-brightness by five-twenty, full fluorescent by six — and in that first stretch the water looked darker than it was, the lane lines softer, the ceiling lost above in shadow. She'd been swimming in pools like this since she was four years old. This one in Kirchberg was newer, cleaner, the temperature regulation tighter than the one in Zug where she'd grown up, but the fundamentals were the same: fifty meters of chlorinated water, the smell of it in the back of her throat before she even got her feet wet, lane ropes, a pace clock, the sound of her own breathing magnified under the surface into something that filled the world.

She changed in the women's room. The facility had individual changing cubicles with sensors that logged her weight, hydration level, and baseline heart rate as she stepped onto the scale, data that flowed directly to the coaching system. She didn't think about this. It had been part of her mornings for eighteen years.

The pool deck was cool under her bare feet. She walked to lane four — her lane, per the rotation schedule — and set her water bottle and pull buoy on the edge. Dara Lund was already at the wall of lane five, adjusting her goggles with small, precise movements. Three adjustments, always three: the left strap, the nose bridge, the right strap. Then she pulled her cap down over her ears and rolled her right shoulder twice, working something loose that had been recalibrated over the winter break. Dara had sent a card from Bergen at Christmas — not to Lena specifically, but to the floor's shared mailbox, a group card with a photo of her mother's garden in the snow. It was still pinned to the communal bulletin board in the hallway. No one had taken it down.

"Morning," Dara said.

"Morning."

They got in the water.

Lena's warm-up was four hundred meters freestyle at moderate pace, building through the second half. She pushed off the wall and the familiar compression closed around her: water temperature against her skin, the pressure of the surface above, the lane rope on her left and the pool floor below, her body settling into the stroke with the efficiency the neural link had been shaping since childhood. Shoulder rotation, catch, pull, hip-driven kick. The coaching files described it as "mechanically optimal." She'd read her own files since she was fifteen, the way some people read medical charts or school reports — clinical documents about someone who happened to be her. The relationship between what the files described and what she felt in the water was usually close enough that the distinction didn't matter.

The main set was eight two-hundreds at race pace, forty seconds rest. She pushed off for the first one and the link settled into the work: managing the pacing, the stroke architecture, the sequence of muscle recruitment that powered each pull. She was in there, somewhere inside the optimization, making the decisions she was aware of making — push harder here, hold the streamline tighter off the wall, breathe every three strokes instead of two because the altitude at Kirchberg thinned her oxygen uptake and the link compensated by extending the breath cycle, but she preferred the rhythm of three. The link handled the rest. What the rest was, exactly, she had never been able to articulate, because there had never been a version of her swimming without it.

Her times came in tight. 2:08.3, 2:08.1, 2:07.9, 2:08.0. The fourth was a slight fade, normal at this point in the training block, nothing that concerned her. She hit the wall, checked the clock, breathed, and pushed off for the fifth.

The first hundred meters were fine. Pull, kick, breathe, turn. Her legs felt heavy from the accumulated lactate but the heaviness was a known quantity, something the link factored into the pacing model. She had fifty meters to the wall and then the turn.

She came in. Tucked. Rotated.

The rotation was wrong.

Not wrong like a mistake — wrong like a different answer to the same question. Her body executed a tighter tuck with a half-degree axis shift that sent her deeper off the wall, her streamline longer, her dolphin kicks firing in a four-beat pattern instead of the three she'd been using since January. The push-off angle was steeper. The depth was greater. She came off the wall a full body-length deeper than she'd intended and her kicks drove her forward in a rhythm she recognized.

November. The firmware update they'd rolled back after three weeks because the deeper push-off cost too much oxygen on the back half of the eight-hundred. She'd trained with that variant and then the coaching team had removed it — or she'd thought they'd removed it. Her link had stored it. And now, without any conscious input from Lena Morozova, her link had selected it.

She broke the surface and stopped. Just stopped. Her legs went still. Her arms hung at her sides. She floated in the center of the lane at the halfway point, the set unfinished, the clock still counting on the wall display. The water moved around her the way water does when the body inside it gives up — small currents from Dara's wake in the next lane, the pool's recirculation pushing against her calves, the lane rope swaying with displaced turbulence.

She treaded water. Her heart rate was elevated from the set, not from the stop. The stop had been quiet. She had simply ceased to swim, the way you'd take your hands off a steering wheel. The sound was different now — her own breathing echoing in the high-ceilinged space, the slap of small waves against the gutter, the muffled churn of Dara's stroke two lanes over. Under the water, during the set, all of that compressed into rhythm. Above it, still, the pool was just a room with too much echo and the taste of chlorine on the back of her tongue.

Dara surfaced at the far wall, touched, and looked down the lane. She had her goggles up on her forehead, water streaming down her face. She waited for Lena to start again, and when Lena didn't, she pushed off and swam back.

"You okay?"

"Yes," Lena said. "Cramp."

"Calf or foot?"

"Calf. Left side. It's already loosening."

Dara nodded. She hung on the lane rope with one arm, her body still in the water, the cap pulled low and dark against her pale skin. "I had one two weeks ago during threshold work. Left calf, same thing. Yusuf said it was a hydration flag — the system sent me a fluid reminder every forty minutes for the rest of the day." She said this evenly, with genuine attention, the information delivered as information. Nothing in her face suggested that it was strange for a body's hydration schedule to be managed by a system that sent reminders like a phone calendar.

"Do you want me to get someone?"

"No, it's fine. Thank you."

"I'm going to do the last three," Dara said. She pulled the goggles back down, adjusted the left strap one more time with her thumb, and pushed off the wall.

Lena watched her go. Dara's stroke was long and clean — the kind of efficient that looked effortless because the effort had been engineered out of it years ago. The Bergen program was different from Meridian's, different calibration philosophy, but the result was similar: a body moving through water as though the water agreed to it.

Lena swam to the wall and got out. Her legs were fine. There was no cramp and there had never been a cramp. There was a flip turn that her body had executed from a pattern her link had chosen without her, and a second and a half of swimming in which the person inside Lena Morozova had not been the one swimming.

She picked up her water bottle, her pull buoy, and walked to the cool-down lane at the far end of the deck, past the observation window where the coaching staff sometimes watched morning sessions. The glass was dark this early — Margot wouldn't be in until eight. Lena got in and swam four hundred meters easy, and the four hundred meters were hers, every stroke a decision she could feel herself making, which was not something she usually noticed because it had never, until this morning, been something she needed to check. The water felt the same as it always did. That was the problem. The water during the flip turn had felt the same too.

---

The neural coaching suite was next to the pool — a small, windowless room with two reclining stations, monitoring displays along the far wall, and the faint residual chlorine that clung to everyone who walked in from the deck. Full fluorescent lighting. A clock. A coat rack with nothing on it. The low hum of processing hardware behind the display panels.

Margot Voss was already seated at the coaching station, scrolling through morning telemetry on the main screen. She had a paper coffee cup balanced on the arm of her chair and her feed interface on her right wrist — the flat silver band she used to connect to athletes during live coaching sessions. It was dark now. Reviews were recorded data, not live feed. Margot had one shoe off under the station, her stockinged foot hooked on the chair's lower rung, the posture of someone who spent most of her working mornings in this room.

"Morning," Margot said without turning around. "Set feel good?"

"First four, yes. I stopped the fifth."

"I saw. The cramp." She pulled up the session log. "Sit down."

Lena sat in the athlete's station. The chair was shaped for reclining, calibrated for a body that would lie still while the coaching system read its output. She didn't plug in for reviews — the data came from the link's recorded log, not a live connection.

"Show me the fifth repeat," Lena said.

Margot brought it up. Two data streams ran in parallel across the display: Lena's conscious motor commands on the left panel, the neural link's execution log on the right. For the first hundred meters, the streams tracked closely — deviations in the hundredths, the kind of noise that showed up in any integrated system. Margot's hand moved across the display, scrubbing through the data the way a sound engineer scrubs through a waveform, looking for the moment it went wrong.

At the flip turn, the streams diverged.

The left panel showed Lena's intended rotation: the standard tuck-and-push, thirty-eight-degree axis, shallow push-off, early breakout. The right panel showed the variant. Tighter tuck, deeper angle, four-beat dolphin kick. The execution log had fired a stored pattern from the November firmware — a rotation model Lena's coaching team had tested and discarded weeks ago.

The divergence window lasted 1.3 seconds.

"There," Lena said.

"Yeah, I see it." Margot highlighted the window. The gap between the two streams was visible on the display like two pencil lines that should have been one, splitting apart at the wall and not rejoining until after the breakout. "Your link pulled a stored optimization variant. It happens — the engine runs background comparisons during sets and sometimes it tests a model before you've selected it. The link found the November rotation scored highest in its current optimization cycle and defaulted to it."

"I didn't select it."

"I know. That's what I'm saying — the link ran the comparison and made the call. It's within acceptable drift parameters." Margot took a sip of her coffee. "This is what the optimization engine does. It's looking for the fastest version of every movement you make, all the time, and occasionally it gets ahead of your conscious command cycle."

"It felt different," Lena said.

"Different how?"

Lena considered. She wanted to be precise. "Like I was watching from slightly behind. Like the turn had already started by the time I was there to do it."

"That's consistent with the timing gap. The link executed one-point-three seconds ahead of your conscious motor command, so you experienced the turn as already in progress when your awareness caught up. The feeling of watching from behind is just the latency. It's not — you weren't displaced. You were there the whole time, just running a beat after the link's execution."

Margot said this the way she said most technical things: plainly, without cushioning, with a certainty that came from years of reading feed data and telemetry charts. She set the coffee down and pulled up a six-week comparison of Lena's drift parameters. Lines on a graph, data points, a range window shaded in pale green.

"Look. Your deviation window's been between point-eight and one-point-five for the entire training block. Today you hit one-point-three. That's mid-range. If it starts pushing past two seconds consistently, we'd flag it for recalibration. Right now this is your system doing what it's designed to do, and you noticed it more than usual." She glanced over. "Some days you notice more than others. That's normal."

Lena looked at the chart. The numbers were right. The range was stable. The explanation was technically complete. Margot was a good coach. She read data clearly and didn't condescend, and when she told you something was within parameters, she meant it.

But the flip turn had not felt like a timing gap. The turn had felt like her body already moving in a direction she had not chosen, the tuck completed before she arrived to do it, her legs pushing off a wall she hadn't told them to push off from. The water had been carrying her in a shape she recognized but hadn't selected — like hearing a song she knew playing from a room she hadn't entered.

She didn't say this. It was not the kind of thing that would show up on the telemetry display.

"Anything else feel off this morning?" Margot asked, closing the chart.

"No."

"Good. Times are solid for the four-hundred. Pace structure's where we want it. I'd hold set volume this week, maybe add a threshold set Friday." Margot paused. "If you want, I'll flag the turn for Yusuf. He can look at the optimization engine during your next calibration."

"Yes, that's fine. Flag it."

"Done." Margot made a note on the display. "Get some food. You're running low on the protein numbers."

Lena stood. Her hair was still damp from the pool, chlorine faint in it the way it always was — in her hair, in the creases of her hands, between her fingers. She'd been swimming in chlorinated water for eighteen years. The smell was not something she noticed anymore, except in moments like this, leaving a room, when it came off her skin and she caught it in the air like a trace of somewhere she'd just been.

---

Her quarters were on the third floor of the residential wing: single bed, desk, bathroom, a window that faced the mountain ridge. The wall-mounted diagnostic display showed her enhancement status in soft green light — neural link connectivity, system load, calibration schedule, a ticker of biomarkers that scrolled slowly and meant nothing to her unless Yusuf pointed at a specific value and said this one's running hot, we'll adjust it Tuesday.

She showered. The water was different from the pool — hotter, softer, no chlorine bite — and she stood under it longer than she needed to because the heat loosened her shoulders. She dressed. She went down to the cafeteria and ate dinner: pasta with chicken, a salad she moved around the plate without finishing, a glass of water. Other athletes were there — a table of sprinters comparing session notes, some of the gymnasts, two of the decathletes whose names she knew from briefing sheets but not from conversation. She ate alone, at a two-person table near the windows. Not by choice exactly and not by design. She simply hadn't sat down next to anyone, and no one had sat down next to her, and eighteen years of institutional dining had taught her that this was ordinary, this was how evenings went sometimes — you ate and you went back to your room.

She was in her quarters by seven. Outside the window, the mountains were dark shapes against a sky that still held a trace of light at the western ridgeline. February in the Bernese Alps. The sun dropped behind the peaks at four-thirty and didn't come back for fourteen hours. The facility's exterior floods lit the snow on the nearest slope in flat white, and beyond that was darkness and the suggestion of geography too large to see.

She sat on the bed with a book. She'd been reading it for two weeks — a novel in German, a recommendation from a girl named Anne she'd gone to school with in Zug. The school attached to the Meridian developmental facility, where the students were program athletes and the academic calendar was built around training blocks. Anne had stopped competing at sixteen and gone to university in Bern. They traded messages every few months. The book was about a woman who inherits a house in the Jura mountains and finds it full of her dead aunt's correspondence — letters, postcards, train tickets, decades of someone else's filed-away life. Lena had been reading it slowly, not because the German was difficult but because the story moved at the pace of someone sorting through other people's things, and that pace required a kind of patience she had to work at.

She opened to page 147. The chapter where the woman discovers letters from a stranger. The first paragraph described the woman sitting at her dead aunt's desk, sorting through envelopes, afternoon light coming through the window at an angle that made the dust visible in the air. Schreibtisch. Umschläge. Licht. Staub.

Lena read the paragraph.

She read it again.

The words were there — German words she understood, her eyes tracking left to right along the lines. Schreibtisch. Desk. Umschläge. Envelopes. Licht. Light. The words sat in her vision without attaching to anything behind them. She could see the shapes of them, could identify each one individually, but they wouldn't assemble. She read the paragraph a third time and could not have said what it contained.

She was not distracted. She was not replaying the flip turn, or the telemetry, or Margot's voice saying acceptable parameters. She was not anxious. She was not thinking about anything at all. There was a place where her attention should have been and the place was empty — not dark, not uncomfortable, not present in any way she could describe. A room with no one in it. A sentence without a subject.

She sat on the bed with the book open on her lap, page 147, the diagnostic display glowing green on the wall, the dark window, the dark mountains. The radiator under the window ticked once and then was quiet. Her hands rested on the edges of the book, thumbs on the page margins, holding it open as though she were about to turn to the next page. She didn't turn the page. She sat there.

Twenty minutes passed.

She knew this because the display clock read 19:23 when she'd opened the book and 19:44 when she looked at it again. Twenty-one minutes. She had been sitting in this room for twenty-one minutes and could not account for any of them.

It was not like falling asleep. She hadn't slept — her body was in the same position, the book open to the same page, her eyes open, the room unchanged around her. The radiator hadn't ticked again, or it had and she hadn't heard it. It was not like daydreaming, where the mind drifts and you can usually reconstruct the chain of association that carried you away. There was no chain. There was nothing. Twenty-one minutes of her life had passed and she had not been present for them, and her body had been here the whole time, sitting on the bed, being a body, while whoever usually inhabited it was somewhere else — or nowhere.

She put the book down on the mattress, face open, the spine cracking softly. She reached behind her head and touched the neural link port at the base of her skull. A small smooth disc, two centimeters, warm from the skin around it. It was always warm. She pressed her fingertip against it gently, the way you'd test a bruise. The port had been installed in the Zug facility when she was four, one medical appointment among a hundred that year, and it had been part of her skull longer than most of the memories she carried inside it.

She went to the bathroom. Ran cold water and splashed her face. In the mirror: herself. The same face she'd always had — brown eyes, the faint mark on her left temple from a fall on the pool deck when she was twelve, her jaw set in the expression she wore when she was deciding whether something was a problem or not. She looked fine. She looked like a twenty-two-year-old swimmer at the end of a training day, tired in the ordinary way.

She went back to the bed. Picked up the book. Found her place. The woman at the desk, the envelopes, the light, the dust.

The words connected this time. The woman found a letter from a man named Aurel, and the letter was about train schedules — the 7:14 from Delémont to Basel, a delay at Laufen, an apology for arriving late to something that wasn't specified. The woman didn't understand why her dead aunt had kept it. Neither did Lena, but the not-understanding was pleasant and ordinary, the kind of small question a novel sets up so you carry it forward to the next page.

She read three more pages. Her comprehension was normal. She was in the room, in the bed, in the book, present the way presence usually worked — so automatically you didn't notice it happening.

She marked her page and turned off the bedside lamp. The diagnostic display shifted to night mode: dimmer, the ticker slowing to a pulse, the green light throwing a faint shifting pattern on the ceiling as the data refreshed. She lay in the dark and looked at it and tried to account for the twenty-one minutes.

Not what she'd been thinking. She hadn't been thinking.

Not what she'd felt. She hadn't felt.

The flip turn was data. A 1.3-second deviation window. A number on Margot's chart. A thing with an explanation and a range and a green shaded zone that meant acceptable. She could tell Yusuf about it, and Yusuf would look at the optimization engine, and the number would stay where it was or it wouldn't, and either way there would be a chart.

The twenty-one minutes were not data. There was no telemetry for an absence. There was no screen she could point to and say here, this is where I wasn't. She'd told Dara it was a cramp. She'd told Margot nothing, because there was nothing on the display to show her.

The wind moved against the building. The display pulsed green on the ceiling, refreshing, refreshing, a system that was always awake even when she wasn't watching.

Sleep came in the usual way — the gradual loosening, her thoughts thinning, her body settling into the mattress. She wouldn't have been able to say exactly when it arrived, which was how sleep was supposed to work: a transition you didn't notice because you weren't there for it.

She'd already had one of those today, and she'd been awake.
