# Chapter 15

Margot's legs were fine.

She sat on the edge of her bed in the grey light before the sun cleared the eastern ridge, feet flat on the cold tile, and her legs were fine. Both of them. She could feel the tile's chill through the soles of her feet, could feel the mattress edge pressing into the backs of her thighs, could feel the small muscles in her calves adjusting to keep her upright. Everything worked. Everything had always worked.

Underneath that — underneath the tile and the mattress and the ordinary fact of a body sitting on a bed at six in the morning — was the other thing. The phantom. Tomas's phantom. Through the feed she had felt his neural bridge break. Not as data, not as a line dropping on a screen, but as sensation transmitted at full resolution through the bidirectional pathway that connected her nervous system to his. The compression in his lower spine. The signals traveling down through his legs and then the signals stopping. A wall of nothing that started at his waist and went all the way down, and she'd felt it from inside — the legs going dead, the nerve pathways going quiet, the specific silence of a body that can no longer feel half of itself.

She stood up. The phantom hit on her third step — a skip in the signal, a gap, as if for one fraction of a second her left leg's proprioception went blank. She grabbed the desk. The gap closed. Her leg was fine. She took another step. Fine. Another. Fine. And then on the seventh step, the right leg this time, the same skip — a microsecond of borrowed deadness that made her weight shift wrong and her hand shoot out for the wall.

She leaned against the wall and waited for it to pass and it passed and she was fine and Tomas was in the medical bay paralyzed from the waist down and she was fine.

The feed interface sat on her nightstand. She'd dropped it there sometime after she'd gotten back to her quarters — she didn't remember when exactly, the hours between disconnecting from Tomas's feed and arriving in this room were compressed and dark and not entirely present in her memory. The device was small. About the weight of a watch. The sensor pad on the underside that read her neural activity while the cable connected to the athlete's system port. A scratch on the face from a doorframe in Stuttgart, three years ago. The indicator light was dark.

She went to the bathroom and turned on the shower. The water took thirty seconds to get hot — the facility's plumbing ran through the building's central spine and the coaches' wing was at the end of the line. She stood under the cold water first, then the warming water, then the hot water, and she felt all of it. Her skin. Her temperature. Her lungs expanding with steam. This was the ritual — the post-feed reset, the thing she'd done after every session for three years. Her own body's sensory recalibration. Usually it marked the end of the feed's presence in her system, the return to the flatness of her own unplugged existence. Today the water was just water. The ritual didn't land.

She turned it off and toweled dry and dressed. Grey pants, a pullover, socks. She looked at herself in the mirror — the bathroom mirror was small and the lighting was fluorescent, designed to help you check for visible injuries, not to be kind. A thirty-five-year-old woman with a former swimmer's shoulders and hips that had widened past her competition frame. The body that had placed fifth at the Olympics and then stopped mattering. The body she'd spent three years escaping through the feed.

She picked up the interface from the nightstand and turned it over in her hands. The sensor pad was smooth, slightly concave, designed to cup the wrist. Her right wrist had a faint indentation from wearing it — three years of daily use, hours at a time, the device pressed against her skin while she lived inside someone else's effort. She set it down again. Her pulse didn't change. That was how she knew the thing between her and the device was already over. Not because she'd decided to stop. Because something structural had shifted, the way a bone breaks — not gradually but all at once, and the sound it made was silence.

She sat on the bed and looked at the window. The spring light was different from what she'd gotten used to — brighter, the sun's angle higher, cutting across her desk at a slant that reached the far wall for the first time since she'd moved in. Outside, the snow had retreated above the tree line on the south-facing slopes. Brown earth below, mud and scree, the skeleton of the landscape visible after months of burial. The drainage channels along the building's foundation carried snowmelt in a continuous murmur, day and night.

Twelve beats per minute. That was the number. She'd checked the telemetry from yesterday's session — it was still cached on the interface, the last session it would ever record — and the number was precise. Tomas's heart rate had been running twelve beats per minute above his target zone when the blackout hit. She'd put it there. She'd pushed his pacing parameters because the feed resolution sharpened above the safety margin, because his body's exertion at that intensity made the borrowed sensation so clear that her own body disappeared, because that disappearance was the thing she needed.

Twelve beats per minute. An elevated cardiovascular load during a virus-triggered blackout. The blood pressure higher. The tissue under more mechanical stress. The impact on his spine arriving in a body that was already running hotter than it should have been. She didn't know if those twelve beats made the difference between a recoverable injury and a permanent one. A biomechanics team could model it and they'd give her a probability distribution, a confidence interval, a range of outcomes with varying likelihoods. Not an answer. The answer was locked inside a single event in a single body at a single moment, and that moment was gone and the result was a twenty-one-year-old kid from Quito who couldn't feel his legs.

She didn't cry. She wasn't going to cry. Crying would have been a reaction to her own distress, and her distress wasn't the thing that mattered. What mattered was in the medical bay, behind a closed door she could see from the corridor window if she walked to the end of the hall, and it would never walk out of that room on its own legs.

The phantom skipped again, her right leg this time, and she sat still until it passed.

---

Petra sent for her at nine. The notification appeared on the wall-mounted display in her quarters — a blue rectangle with Dr. Engel's name and a room number and a time that was thirty-two minutes from now. The display also showed Margot's own biometrics — resting heart rate, sleep quality index, enhancement interface status. The interface status read INACTIVE. It had read ACTIVE every morning for three years.

She walked to the medical wing. Four minutes through the connecting corridor, past the windows that faced the south slope where the snowmelt was turning the access road to mud. Two athletes passed her going the other direction — a middle-distance runner whose name she knew and a gymnast whose name she didn't — and neither of them looked at her. Either they hadn't heard yet or they had heard and were choosing not to look. Both options sat in her stomach the same way.

The phantom hit at the junction between the residential wing and the medical wing — the left leg, the proprioceptive skip, the fraction-of-a-second gap. She caught the wall and kept walking. A maintenance worker at the far end of the corridor glanced at her and looked away.

Petra's office was glass-walled, adjacent to the medical bay proper. Through the glass, if you looked past Petra's desk and through the interior window on the far wall, you could see the corridor that led to the recovery rooms. Tomas's door was closed. It had been closed since they brought him in yesterday.

Petra was standing when Margot entered. That was wrong. Petra was a deliberate sitter — she positioned herself at eye level, a clinical habit that communicated equality even when the power was entirely hers. Standing meant she hadn't settled. Or hadn't been able to.

"Close the door."

Margot closed it.

Two screens on Petra's desk showed data Margot recognized instantly. Feed session logs. The format her interface generated automatically every time she connected to an athlete: heart-rate tracking, respiratory rate, neural feedback markers, session duration, connection quality. Weeks of sessions, displayed as time series. Colored lines.

"Sit down," Petra said.

Margot sat. The chair was the standard medical-office type — padded, adjustable, designed for patients receiving news. She'd sat across from Petra in this chair twice before, both times for routine coaching certifications. Those meetings had lasted fifteen minutes. This one would be different.

Petra turned the left screen so Margot could see it clearly. "Your feed session logs with Tomas Reyes. February eighth through yesterday. Thirty-one sessions."

The data was clean, organized by date, each session a separate track. The target heart-rate zone — the zone Margot had filed in her session plans — was marked in green. The actual heart rate was marked in red. The red lines were above the green lines. In every session. Every one.

"Average deviation of twelve beats per minute above the prescribed training zone," Petra said. She spoke with the precision of someone reading findings, not accusations. Clinical. But her eyes were not clinical. "The pattern is consistent. Not sporadic, not variable. Consistent across the full six-week period."

"Yes," Margot said.

Petra waited. When Margot didn't add anything, she tapped the screen and scrolled to a second dataset. Different athlete. Same pattern: target below, actual above, the gap steady and systematic.

"Lena Morozova. Twenty-three sessions. Average deviation of eight beats per minute." Another scroll. "David Kim. Fourteen sessions. Average deviation of fifteen beats." Another. "Annika Brandt. Nine sessions. Eleven beats."

Four athletes. Weeks of sessions. Systematic elevation. No documented medical justification for any of it.

"I'm not going to ask you why," Petra said. "The telemetry is clear enough."

Margot looked at the screen. The colored lines, the gap between what she'd promised and what she'd done, rendered in clean data that anyone with access could read. It had always been findable. The logs were generated automatically. She'd never deleted them. She'd never even considered deleting them. That fact said something about her that she didn't particularly want to examine — that she'd been so certain no one would look, or so indifferent to the possibility, that she hadn't bothered to cover anything.

"I've also reviewed the network pathway data with Yusuf," Petra said. Her voice shifted — less formal, harder. "The neural coaching feed creates a bidirectional data channel. When you connect to an athlete's system, the pathway opens in both directions."

Margot's hands were in her lap. She watched them not move.

"Every feed session you ran opened an additional propagation route for the virus through the shared calibration network." Petra was not hedging now. The qualifiers she used in most professional contexts — *likely*, *consistent with*, *it's possible that* — were gone. "Yusuf has confirmed that the feed pathway is a viable transmission vector. The virus could propagate through a coaching connection the same way it propagates through calibration."

"I know," Margot said.

"When did you know."

"February. After the facility went to restricted operations."

"After Kai Brennan's crash."

"Yes."

"You identified the feed as a potential virus vector in February. And you continued running sessions through yesterday."

"Yes."

The silence that followed was the silence of a doctor who has heard the answer she expected and is now recalculating the scope of the damage. Petra sat down — slowly, as if the act required more deliberation than usual. Her chair creaked. She took off her glasses and cleaned them on the hem of her sweater, a gesture Margot had seen before, a thing Petra did with her hands when the rest of her needed to hold still.

"Thirty-one sessions with Tomas. Twenty-three with Lena. Fourteen with Kim. Nine with Brandt. Seventy-seven potential virus exposures beyond the shared calibration network." She put her glasses back on. "Seventy-seven sessions where you knew you might be increasing the viral load on athletes whose systems were already compromised."

Margot didn't answer because there was no answer. The number was the answer.

"Your coaching privileges are suspended effective immediately," Petra said. "Your feed interface authorization is revoked at the network level. IT will deactivate your connection credentials by end of day. You're not to connect to any athlete's neural system for any purpose. Can you confirm that verbally."

"I understand. I won't connect."

"You'll retain access to your quarters and common areas. You're not to enter the coaching suite, the calibration bay, or any training environment without my explicit authorization."

"OK."

Petra looked at the closed door of Tomas's recovery room, visible through the layered glass walls. Margot followed her gaze. The door was the same institutional white as every other door in the medical wing, but it was the only one that was closed in the middle of a weekday morning.

"The heart-rate elevation during yesterday's session," Petra said. Her voice was quieter now. More careful. "His cardiovascular load was elevated when the blackout triggered. I've asked the biomechanics team to model whether the elevation contributed to the severity of the spinal outcome."

"It might've," Margot said.

"It might have. Yes." Petra's face did something then — a contraction around the eyes, a brief crumpling that she corrected quickly enough that it almost wasn't visible. But Margot saw it. The face of a woman who had spent years in institutional medicine and still couldn't look at preventable damage without flinching.

"I flagged a rejection risk at the Stuttgart facility four years ago," Petra said. "An enhancement incompatibility in a young gymnast. I wrote the report, filed it through proper channels. Meridian's medical oversight board reviewed my findings and downgraded the risk classification. She died eight months later from the condition I'd identified."

Margot said nothing.

"I'm not comparing what you did to what they did. The mechanisms are different. But the pattern —" She stopped. Started the sentence on a different track. "I've spent four years knowing that I should've made more noise. Should've refused to let the institution's process overrule my clinical judgment. That's what I'm doing now."

"I know what you're doing," Margot said.

Petra nodded once. She looked old in the fluorescent light — the grey streaks in her hair, the lines around her eyes, the particular fatigue of someone who had been tired for years and knew she would be tired for years more.

"You should go," Petra said.

Margot stood. The phantom skipped in her left leg and she caught the edge of Petra's desk with her fingertips. Petra's eyes went to the catch, the recovery, the hand on the desk.

"What was that?"

"Phantom. From the feed. From yesterday. It'll pass."

"When did it start?"

"When I disconnected from Tomas."

Petra's expression went still. "You're carrying phantom sensation from his injury?"

"His legs going dead. It comes in waves. My nervous system stored it."

Petra opened her mouth, closed it, opened it again. "I can order a neural scan. If the phantom is persisting—"

"It'll fade," Margot said. She didn't know if this was true. She said it because the alternative — sitting in a scanner while someone mapped the ghost of Tomas's paralysis in her own neural pathways — was a kind of medical attention she hadn't earned.

She left Petra's office and walked back down the corridor and the phantom hit once more, in the right leg this time, near the junction with the residential wing, and she stopped and stood there with her hand on the wall until it passed and then she kept walking.

---

Kai found her outside.

The facility had a service entrance on the north side — a loading dock that the maintenance crew used for deliveries, a concrete apron bordered by a rusted safety rail that overlooked the valley road and the lower slopes where the snow had retreated. From the dock you could see the road switching back through the tree line all the way down to where it leveled off toward Meiringen. It was the one spot at the facility where you could stand outside and not see any part of the building. Margot was sitting on an overturned equipment crate near the rail, not wearing a jacket, her breath condensing in the spring air.

He'd been with Yusuf most of the morning. The calibration bay, the screens, the data. The structural signatures in the virus code that matched Meridian's proprietary development frameworks — the correlation that was clear enough to an engineer and useless in any context that required proof. Suggestive, Yusuf kept saying. The word engineers used when they were certain but couldn't demonstrate it in language that would survive a legal challenge or a press conference. Five weeks to the opening ceremony and they had a conspiracy they could think but not speak, a paralyzed kid in the medical bay, and a corporate entity that had sold the cure for a disease it had probably created.

And then Lena had told him about Margot's suspension. Flat, factual, the way Lena delivered information when she was trying not to feel it yet. Petra pulled her feed logs. The heart-rate data. The sessions with Tomas, with Lena, with others. The suspension.

With Lena. The words sat in Kai's chest like something swallowed wrong. Margot had been plugged into Lena in the pool. Margot had watched Lena's personality thin to nothing through the feed and had pushed her heart rate higher because the dissolution was its own kind of rush, and then Lena had surfaced unable to remember two laps of her own life, and Margot had looked at her and lied.

He'd asked Lena how she was and she'd said fine and he'd known she wasn't fine and he'd also known that pushing her on it right now would be the wrong thing. So he'd left the question and gone to find Margot.

His left wrist ached. It always ached now — the bone healed too fast by the synthetic grafts, a wrongness in the joint that flared with barometric shifts. Spring in the Alps was all pressure changes. He flexed his hand open and closed as he walked across the loading dock toward Margot and the ache was a useful thing to notice, a physical problem with a physical location, not the other kind.

She looked up when she heard him. Her face was flat. Not composed. Flat. The humor that she usually carried like a blade — the sharp, self-aware darkness that made people uncomfortable when they realized she wasn't entirely joking — was gone. What was left was the face of someone who had stopped constructing anything. She didn't look guilty. She didn't look sorry. She looked like what she was.

"Lena," he said.

Her eyes didn't change. "Yeah."

"You were in the feed when she desync'd in the pool. You saw how bad it was."

"I saw."

"And you told her it was within acceptable parameters."

"That's what I told her."

He stood there. He wasn't going to do this from above — standing over her, looking down. But he also wasn't going to sit next to her, because sitting next to her would be a kind of comfort and comfort wasn't what he'd come with. So he stood at the edge of the dock, three feet from her crate, close enough to talk and far enough to not be sharing anything.

"She lost two laps. She was in the water and she wasn't there and you were inside her system watching it happen and you pushed her heart rate up."

"I pushed it up."

"Because the desync made the feed better."

Margot looked at him and her face didn't flinch and that was the worst part — the absence of flinch, the fact that she could hear this said back to her and not react, not because she was hard but because she'd already said it to herself so many times that hearing it from someone else was just a repetition. "The desync sharpened the resolution. When her personality thinned, the sensory data got clearer. More intense. I — yeah. It made the feed better."

"And you knew her desync was worse than anyone had reported."

"I knew it was bad. Worse than her charts showed. Worse than what I'd told her."

"And you didn't say anything."

"No."

"Because saying something meant losing the feed."

"Because saying something meant admitting how deep I was in it. Which meant losing the feed. Which meant losing —" She stopped. Not a dramatic stop. A recalibration. She was stripping the sentence of anything that might sound like a reason instead of a fact. "Which meant being done. And I wasn't ready to be done."

A truck engine murmured somewhere below, on the valley road. The sound carried in the thin air, distant and mechanical, and then faded as the truck rounded a switchback. The mountains across the valley were still mostly white but the snow had pulled back from the rock faces and the dark stone underneath was wet and glinting in the midday sun.

"Tell me what it is," Kai said. Not a question. Not exactly a demand. The tone was flat in the way his voice went flat when the humor was completely gone, when there was nothing between the words and the feeling underneath them. "The feed. What it is for you. I want to hear you say it."

Margot looked at her hands. The right wrist, bare now, the indentation where the interface had been.

"You plug in," she said. "You connect to their system. Their sensory data comes through. Not a simulation — the actual signals, translated. When they run, you feel the legs. When they swim, you feel the water."

She paused to breathe. The air was cold. She'd been outside long enough that her lips were dry and her fingers had gone pink.

"When the heart rate goes up, the resolution sharpens. Everything gets more present. Their body feels more real than yours does. At a certain point, and it's different for each athlete, your own body disappears. You forget you're in a room somewhere with a device on your wrist. There's just their body, their effort. The feeling of competing at that level. The muscles, the lungs, the focus."

"And that's what you wanted."

"Every time. For three years."

"Three years."

"I started in Stuttgart. Before I came here. First time was an accident — I set a calibration wrong and the athlete's heart rate spiked and the feed got sharp and I — I noticed. What it did. How it felt." She wasn't looking at him. She was looking at the valley, the road, the distant line of the tree cover. "I did it again. Figured out the numbers. Got specific about it. Every athlete's got a threshold where the feed peaks. I learned each one."

"Tomas's was twelve."

"Twelve above target zone. Yeah."

Kai's wrist ached and he pressed it against the cold safety rail and the metal bit into the bone and the sharpness was clarifying.

"When I stopped competing," Margot said, and her voice was the flattest thing he'd ever heard from her — no humor, no edge, no self-awareness performed for anyone's benefit, just a person reciting the shape of a problem she hadn't solved, "I didn't stop wanting it. That's not how it goes. You just stop being allowed. Your body isn't fast enough or your ranking drops or you're thirty-five and seven kilos past your race weight and your best stroke time is a joke. But the wanting doesn't know any of that. The wanting still thinks you're in the water."

She looked at the wrist where the interface had been.

"The feed was the closest thing. Someone else's body doing what mine used to do. The intensity. The physical fact of competing at that level. Through the feed it was real." She said this without inflection. "Through the feed I was still in it."

He sat down on the edge of the dock. Not a decision — his body needed to be lower. The concrete was cold through his pants and his wrist was cold from the rail and the cold was fine. He could feel the cold.

"And the virus," he said. "You knew the feed was a vector."

"I figured it out in February. The bidirectional pathway. Every time I connected, there was a propagation risk."

"And you kept connecting."

"I kept connecting."

"Seventy-seven sessions. That's what Petra said."

"That sounds right."

He looked at her. She looked back. Her face was red from the cold and her eyes were dry and she was giving him the plainest version of herself she had, which was the truth without anything around it. No defense. No performance of remorse that would give him something to accept or reject. Just the facts of what she'd done, laid out in simple sentences by a person who'd run out of more complicated ways to say them.

"I'm not going to tell you I didn't know what I was doing," she said. "I knew. I'm not going to tell you I couldn't stop. I could've. I didn't because stopping meant being just me, in my own body, with my own senses, and that wasn't enough." She paused. "That's the whole thing. There's no deeper version."

He didn't forgive her. The word didn't enter his mind. Forgiveness would have been a transaction, something exchanged, and what Margot had done to Lena was not a thing with a price in forgiveness. She had been inside Lena's system. She had watched Lena disappear inside herself. She had used that disappearance for her own gratification and then lied about it. And Lena — Lena, who checked whether every sentence she spoke was one she'd chosen to say, who touched the neural port at the base of her skull the way you'd touch a scar, whose trust in the people calibrating her body was the only trust she had because the body was the only thing she'd ever known — Lena had believed her. Had accepted the lie because the lie came from a coach, and coaches were the system, and the system was what Lena was.

"I'm sorry," Margot said. The word was plain. Not weighted, not performed, not asking for anything in return. An acknowledgment of a specific damage from the person who'd caused it. "About Lena. About what I did to her."

Kai stood up. The concrete had left a cold strip across the backs of his thighs and his wrist throbbed with the pressure change and the mountains were there, patient and indifferent.

"Yeah," he said.

He went back inside. Behind him, Margot stayed on the crate in the cold. He didn't look back to see whether she was still sitting there or whether the phantom had caught her leg again. He walked through the loading dock entrance and back into the facility's heated corridors and the warmth on his face felt like returning to a building where everything was wrong and would stay wrong and the opening ceremony was five weeks away and Tomas's door was still closed.
