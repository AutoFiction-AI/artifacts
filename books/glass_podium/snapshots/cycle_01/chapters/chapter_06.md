# Chapter 6

Yusuf hadn't left the calibration bay since the crash. Fourteen hours now, maybe fifteen — he'd stopped checking the clock around midnight when the log analysis consumed whatever part of his attention had been tracking time. The mug of coffee on the workstation ledge had gone cold hours ago. He moved it aside every time he reached for the secondary display and moved it back when he was done, a small mechanical rhythm that kept his hands occupied while the data on the screen got steadily worse.

The bay's constant sixty-hertz hum — the processing equipment, the cooling systems, the background drone of a building full of machinery keeping bodies tuned — had shifted in his perception sometime around four in the morning. It no longer sounded like infrastructure. It sounded like a pulse belonging to something sick.

He scrolled through another block of propagation logs. Outside the bay's single window, snow fell in the predawn dark. The facility was under restricted-operations protocol: no training sessions, no calibrations, no scheduled maintenance. Everything offline until his team could map the extent of the corruption. Forty-three athletes were in their quarters or the common areas or the cafeteria, waiting for someone to tell them whether their bodies were safe. Yusuf was supposed to be that someone.

The pattern was clear. He'd been hoping it wouldn't be.

Every athlete who had connected to the calibration network in the past thirty-two days showed micro-level firmware irregularities. Not all were catastrophic — Kai's blackout had been the worst case, a full cascade that wiped sensory processing in under 0.8 seconds. But the corruption was present in varying degrees across every system that had handshaked with the shared network since early January. It was dormant in some systems, active in others. There was no way to predict which would cascade next, or when.

"Right," he said to the empty bay. "So that's everyone, then."

His phone showed seven missed calls from the facility director. He hadn't answered because he'd wanted to be certain before he spoke. Now he was certain, and he still didn't pick up the phone.

The bay door opened. Petra Engel came in holding a tablet and looking like she'd slept about as long as he had — which was to say she hadn't. The grey streaks in her hair seemed more pronounced under the fluorescent lights, and the clinical precision of her posture had a new rigidity to it, as if holding herself upright required active effort.

"Two more," she said.

She set the tablet on his workstation. Two medical intake forms. He read them while she stood next to him, not sitting, not leaning, just present.

The first: Marcus Ndlovu, twenty-six, South African, sprint relay. He'd lost all hearing for approximately three seconds during a start drill the previous morning — the same session Kai had been running on the indoor track, on the other side of the facility. Three seconds of total silence while his body stayed locked in the starting blocks. The hearing had returned on its own. Marcus had finished the drill, told no one, and come to the medical bay at ten o'clock that night because he couldn't sleep and his left ear was still ringing. The intake nurse noted that he'd tried to laugh it off when he arrived. His hands had been shaking.

The second: Sonja Petrić, twenty-three, Croatian, artistic gymnastics. Her left hand had gone numb on the uneven bars during a release skill. Complete loss of sensation from the wrist down, lasting approximately four seconds. The synthetic grafts in her forearms had maintained her grip on instinct — engineered muscle holding a bar her nerves couldn't feel. She'd dismounted cleanly and gone straight to medical. The intake notes recorded that she'd spent the entire wait opening and closing her left fist, over and over, checking that the fingers still answered.

Yusuf took off his glasses and rubbed the bridge of his nose. Put them back on.

"Have you told them what it is?" he asked.

"I've told them the anomalies are under investigation. I haven't given them a mechanism." Petra's voice was measured, each word placed. The register she used when she was building a wall between herself and what she was about to learn. "I need to understand the scope before I say anything definitive."

"The scope is everyone."

A silence. Petra pulled a stool from under the secondary workstation and sat down. She set the tablet on her knees and folded her hands over it.

"Define everyone."

"Every enhanced athlete who's connected to the shared calibration network since the first of January." He pulled up the session log and turned the monitor so she could see. "Forty-three athletes. That's everyone at the facility except the two traditional-category trainers who don't interface with our systems."

"You're certain of the propagation mechanism?"

"I can show you the path." He brought up the propagation map — nodes and connections, each node an athlete, each connection a calibration session where the handshake protocol had carried corrupted firmware. The map looked like a disease network, which was exactly what it was. "The virus — and it is a virus, I'm not being loose with that word — distributes through the calibration handshake. When an athlete's system connects for a tuning session, the network pushes firmware updates as standard procedure. The virus embedded itself in the update payload. Every system that accepted a firmware push in the past month has some degree of corruption."

Petra studied the map. Forty-three nodes, all radiating from the central hub of his calibration bay.

"The severity varies," Yusuf continued. The professional polish in his voice was cracking, more Birmingham coming through. He was talking faster now. "Some systems got a partial load. Enough to cause intermittent disruption — what Marcus and Sonja experienced. Others caught the full payload. Kai got the full payload."

"How do we determine who has partial and who has full?"

"Individual diagnostics on every system. Which means bringing athletes back into the calibration bay and connecting them to the same network that infected them in the first place." He caught himself, exhaled. "I'm working on an isolated diagnostic protocol — air-gapped from the main network, standalone firmware analysis. But it's going to take time."

"How much time?"

"Days. Three at least. Maybe five."

Petra stood. She touched the edge of her tablet, aligning it precisely with the workstation's edge — a small, careful gesture that looked like clinical practice and was probably just the need to impose order on something.

"I'll inform the director," she said. "And then the athletes. All of them. They deserve to know."

"Yeah. They do."

She left. The bay door closed behind her with its usual pneumatic sigh.

Yusuf sat alone with the propagation map. Forty-three nodes, all connected through his calibration bay, his protocols, his firmware update schedule. He had built this network. He had managed every handshake, approved every firmware push, maintained the infrastructure that the virus had used to move from body to body. And he hadn't seen it until a man ran face-first into a concrete wall at sprint speed.

He took his glasses off again and pressed his thumb and forefinger against his closed eyes until he saw phosphene static. When he opened them, the propagation map was still there. His hands were steady. He noticed this and couldn't tell whether it was competence or whether something in him had just gone numb.

***

Lena went to the medical bay at half past three in the afternoon because she had run out of reasons not to.

The restricted-operations protocol had emptied the facility's usual schedule. No pool time, no dryland training, no calibration sessions. The pool was locked. The indoor track where Kai had crashed was sealed behind yellow maintenance barriers taped across the corridor entrance. She'd eaten lunch in the cafeteria with Dara and two other swimmers, the four of them working through high-protein trays without talking about the one thing they were all thinking about. Dara had asked if Lena wanted to watch something in the common room afterward. Lena had said maybe later, and then gone back to her quarters and sat on the edge of her bed for forty minutes doing nothing.

She'd heard from another swimmer that Kai was conscious. She didn't know him — they'd nodded at each other in hallways, shared a cafeteria table once with five or six other people, exchanged maybe twenty words total since she'd arrived at Kirchberg. He was the sprinter who won everything and never looked like winning meant what it was supposed to. She was the swimmer whose neural link had started making decisions without asking. They'd had no particular reason to speak before yesterday.

The medical bay smelled of antiseptic and the faint mineral trace of surgical-grade synthetic polymer — the smell of graft work, maintenance fluid, the slightly sweet chemical note that was supposed to be odorless and never quite was. She knew the smell from childhood. The Meridian developmental facility in Zug had smelled like this during every calibration appointment from age four onward. It was one of her earliest sense memories, and she did not find it comforting.

Kai was in the second recovery room, propped up at a low angle on the adjustable bed. His left wrist was encased in a rigid brace — white polymer extending from his knuckles to mid-forearm. His right shoulder sat in an immobilization sling. The left side of his face showed six butterfly closures tracking the lacerations along his cheekbone and brow, and the surrounding skin had swollen into a mottled yellow-purple that looked worse than yesterday, the bruising still settling into the tissue. He was watching something on the wall-mounted display and turned it off when she appeared in the doorway.

"Hey," he said.

"Hello." She stood in the doorframe, uncertain how to enter a room she had no clear reason to be in. "I don't have to stay. If you'd rather—"

"No, come in. It's fine." He waved with his braced hand, a stiff half-gesture that visibly cost him something. "I've been not doing anything for about sixteen hours straight. I'm genuinely talented at it now."

She came in and sat in the hard plastic visitor's chair. It was the kind of chair designed to discourage long stays — the seat angled slightly forward, no armrests, a surface that made your back stiff within ten minutes.

"How is the wrist?" she asked.

"Weird." He held it up, the brace catching the fluorescent light. "The grafts are knitting the bone faster than it should go. I can feel it happening in there. Like — you know when your foot falls asleep and the blood starts coming back? That pins-and-needles feeling? It's like that, but inside the bone. Constant. This low buzzing, like the bone itself is humming."

She nodded. She did not know the specific sensation but she understood the category: a body performing a repair function that should have been invisible, the repair itself becoming a new wrongness layered over the injury it was trying to fix.

"The shoulder?"

"Back in the socket. Sling for a week. Petra says full range of motion in ten days, which—" He shifted in the bed, adjusting the sling strap with a grimace. "That's insane. Should be six weeks, minimum. But the system doesn't care about should."

Through the wall that separated the medical bay from the calibration bay, the equipment hum was faintly audible — a low vibration at the threshold of hearing. Lena had been aware of it since she'd entered the corridor. She was always aware of it.

"I heard that two other athletes had problems," she said. "Marcus Ndlovu and Sonja Petrić. During training."

"Yeah. Yusuf was here about an hour ago." Kai looked at the ceiling. "He says it's in the network. Everyone who's been calibrated in the past month. So, everyone."

A quiet. The word sat between them. The weight of it — not just one person's crash but forty-three people carrying corrupted firmware in their bodies, all of them walking around the facility waiting to find out if they were next.

Lena touched the back of her neck. The neural link port — two centimeters of surgical polymer, flush with the skin, usually hidden beneath her hair — was warm under her fingertips. She touched it without deciding to, the way you confirm a thing is still where you left it.

"Mine has been doing things," she said.

She hadn't planned to say it. She wasn't sure why she was saying it now, to this person she barely knew, in this fluorescent room with the smell of graft polymer in the air. But he was looking at her with an expression that wasn't curiosity or pity or professional interest. It was recognition.

"During training?"

"During everything. Training, reading, meals." She paused, choosing her words with the care of someone constructing a sentence in a language that was fluent but not first. "I will be present, and then I'm not, and then I come back and I don't know how long I was gone. Yesterday I was reading in my quarters. Twenty minutes passed. I didn't fall asleep. I just wasn't there."

Kai was quiet. The humor she'd seen him deploy in public — the self-deprecating deflection, the media-trained warmth that kept everything at a comfortable distance — wasn't there. Just attention, and a face too swollen and bruised to perform much of anything.

"Your body keeps going when you're out?"

"Yes. In the pool, my stroke continues. The neural link maintains the pattern it was running. From the outside it looks — I'm told it looks normal." She stopped. "From the inside it's not."

"No," he said. "It's not."

Another silence. Outside the medical bay's window, the afternoon light was already failing — winter in the Bernese Alps, the sun dropping below the western ridgeline before four o'clock. Snow had been falling most of the day. A precise line of accumulation sat along the outer window ledge, growing by fractions.

"When the blackout happened," Kai said, and then didn't continue for several seconds. He was looking at his braced wrist, not at her. "I couldn't see or hear or feel anything. Total nothing. But my legs kept going. The grafts were still getting system commands and they were running me at full speed. Perfect form, probably. And I was just... gone." He exhaled through his nose, a flat sound with no humor in it. "It didn't need me there to run. My body did the job fine without anyone home."

Lena sat with what he'd said. She gave it the space it required rather than filling the silence with something inadequate.

"That's what's frightening," she said. "Not the malfunction. The competence."

He looked at her then. Something in his face shifted — not toward warmth, exactly, but toward a kind of openness, as if she had found a word he'd been circling without reaching.

"Yeah," he said. "Exactly that."

They sat for a while without speaking. The medical equipment produced its small mechanical sounds — the IV pump's intermittent click, the diagnostic monitor's low tonal beep every thirty seconds. Snow accumulated on the window ledge. Kai's wrist buzzed its constant pins-and-needles signal from inside the bone, the synthetic grafts knitting fracture faster than any body should heal, and Lena sat with her hand resting against the back of her neck, fingertips on the neural link port that had been part of her since she was four years old and that she had never once considered foreign until this month.

A nurse came in to check Kai's vitals — blood pressure, graft integration readings, the standard post-trauma sequence. They made small talk while the nurse worked. What the cafeteria was serving for dinner. Whether anyone had fixed the projector in the common room. The kind of flat, functional conversation people produce when a third person is present and the real conversation has to wait. Kai asked the nurse about the painkillers wearing off and the nurse said she'd check with Dr. Engel. Lena asked if there was decaf in the hallway machine and the nurse said it had been broken since Tuesday. The nurse left.

The quiet that returned was different from the quiet before the nurse came in. Something had been established between them that had no name yet. Not friendship, not romance, not solidarity in any organized sense. Just two people whose bodies were doing things without permission, and the particular relief of not having to explain why that was terrifying to someone who already knew.

"I should go," Lena said, after a while.

"Yeah. OK."

She stood. At the doorway she turned back.

"The pool will still be closed tomorrow?"

"Yusuf said restricted ops for at least three more days. Maybe five."

"Then I'll probably come back. If that's all right." She started a sentence, stopped it, started a different one. "There isn't much to do."

"Door's open." A trace of humor, deflated at the end, making the invitation deliberately smaller than it was. "Not like I'm going anywhere." He tapped the sling with his braced fingers and the tap made him wince.

She nodded and walked out. In the corridor, the fluorescent lights buzzed at their constant pitch. The calibration bay hum was louder here, the shared wall between medical and engineering transmitting it into the hallway like a low-grade headache. She walked back toward the residential wing with her hand pressed to the back of her neck, fingertips resting on the port, and she did not take her hand away until she was inside her room with the door shut behind her.

***

Margot sat on the edge of her bed in the dark and thought about vectors — not the mathematical kind, the epidemiological kind. She'd done a semester of public health coursework during her coaching certification, enough to know the basics: transmission required a pathway, a medium, and a susceptible host.

Yusuf had confirmed the mechanism by mid-afternoon. Word had traveled through the facility's informal networks faster than any official announcement — the athletes texting each other, coaching staff comparing notes in the corridor, everyone assembling the same picture from different fragments. The virus propagated through the shared calibration protocols. Firmware updates carrying corrupted code. Forty-three athletes on the same infected infrastructure.

But the calibration network wasn't the only way into an athlete's system.

She hadn't turned on the overhead light. Her room was filling with the particular grey of a winter afternoon dying in the mountains, the snow-reflected light from outside fading degree by degree. The wall-mounted monitoring display — the stripped-down coaching-staff version, not the full diagnostic suite the athletes had — glowed faintly with her baseline metrics. Heart rate, neural baseline, integration status, all showing green. The display didn't know what she was thinking about.

The neural coaching feed. Her wrist-mounted interface, the device she'd carried since her first coaching season, personal equipment she'd bought and maintained herself. When she plugged into an athlete, the device established a bidirectional data connection between her system and the athlete's enhancement architecture. She received their sensory input — that was the job, that was what the technology was designed for. A coach experiencing what the athlete experienced, reading the body's signals in real time to guide pacing and technique.

But the connection protocol opened both lanes. It had to. Data flowed from athlete to coach and from coach to athlete — the return pathway carried the pacing adjustments, the heart-rate targets, the micro-corrections that justified neural coaching as a profession rather than passive observation. The feed was a two-way street running directly into the deepest layers of an athlete's neural system.

If the virus could move through the calibration network's handshake protocol — a relatively constrained data exchange compared to a full feed session — then the feed's deep-access connection was a wider road, a more direct line into the exact firmware the virus was targeting.

She ran the list in her head. In the past thirty-two days she had plugged into Tomas Reyes — three sessions, then two more after the hurdle fall. Lena Morozova, two sessions. A distance runner named Amara whose 10K pace needed adjustment, one session. Hendrik, a pole vaulter, one session. Nine feed sessions in a month, four different athletes, and in every single one the bidirectional pathway had been wide open, her interface handshaking with their enhancement systems at a depth that made a calibration tune-up look like a handshake through a closed door.

She stood up and went to the bathroom. The fluorescent tube above the mirror flickered once before catching, and her face in its light looked washed out — bags under her eyes, the softness along her jaw that bothered her some days and didn't bother her other days and right now didn't register at all. She turned the tap on and off without washing anything. Stood there with her hands on the edge of the sink. Turned the light off and went back to the bed.

She should tell Yusuf. The action was simple. Yusuf was in the calibration bay mapping the virus's propagation paths, and his map was incomplete — the feed sessions didn't route through his network, they ran on her personal equipment, a separate data channel that his system analysis couldn't see. If the feed was a transmission vector, he was working with a partial picture. He needed this information. Marcus Ndlovu and Sonja Petrić and the rest of them needed this information.

She picked up her phone. The screen lit her face in the dark room. She opened her contacts, scrolled to Yusuf's name.

She put the phone down on the mattress.

Because telling Yusuf about the vector meant telling him about the sessions. Not just the count — the depth. How far into the athletes' systems she went. How long she stayed connected. The pacing adjustments she made that pushed past coaching protocol boundaries. The heart-rate elevations that any sports medicine doctor would flag as reckless if they ever saw the numbers. The session with Tomas three weeks ago where she'd pushed him past the safety margin and he'd fallen on the track, and she'd stood in the debrief and blamed altitude fatigue. The session with Lena where she'd watched the desync data spike and called it acceptable drift because calling it anything else would mean someone restricting her access to the feed.

Telling Yusuf meant opening the logs. Her device recorded everything — connection duration, data throughput, which system layers she accessed, every parameter she adjusted during a session. The records would show that Margot Voss, neural performance coach, spent twice as long in the feed as her coaching duties required, accessed architecture deeper than any coaching protocol authorized, and pushed athletes' cardiovascular parameters to levels that would end her career the moment a medical director saw them.

She picked the phone back up. She could call Petra instead. Petra was medical, not engineering — she might understand the clinical urgency without immediately digging into the session details. But Petra was thorough, and Petra was already suspicious of everyone right now, and Petra would want the logs. Petra would absolutely want the logs.

She thought about Kai in the medical bay with butterfly closures holding his face together. About Marcus Ndlovu trying to laugh and his hands shaking. About Sonja Petrić's fingers opening and closing around nothing.

She thought about Tomas's hamstrings loading before a hurdle, felt from the inside. The sharpness of Lena's stroke, the chain of muscle from fingertip to hip engaging in sequence, felt from the inside. The way it was to be inside a body that performed — not her body, not her performance, but the rush real and total and hers for as long as the connection held.

Margot put the phone in the bedside drawer and closed it.

The diagnostic display on the wall pulsed its quiet green. Outside, the snow had stopped, and the particular silence that follows heavy snowfall in the mountains filled the room — a dense, padded quiet, the kind that made even your own breathing sound conspicuous. The mountains absorbed sound. They absorbed everything, if you let them.

She lay back on the bed, still in her clothes, and stared at the ceiling she couldn't see and did not call anyone.
