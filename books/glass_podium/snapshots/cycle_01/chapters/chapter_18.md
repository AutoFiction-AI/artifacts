# Chapter 18

Kai sat at an outside table at a café on the Rue du Grand-Pont, his coffee half-finished and the afternoon showing no interest in moving. Lausanne in August: the lake below the old town catching light between buildings, tourists on the steep sidewalks with shopping bags and good shoes, a woman at the next table reading something on a tablet with the absolute attention of someone who did not want to be disturbed. The cathedral's tower was visible above the rooftops, grey stone against a sky that had been blue all day with the settled confidence of a sky that would stay blue until evening.

His wrist ached. It ached every afternoon around this time, a low persistent wrongness deep in the bone where the break from February had healed too quickly. The synthetic grafts had knitted the fracture in nine days. Normal bone would have taken six weeks. The result was a seam inside the bone itself — not a pain, exactly, but a tightness, a place where the architecture of his left wrist did not quite agree with itself. He rotated it. The motion was smooth. The ache held.

The email from SportsVision was still open on his phone, sitting face-down on the table next to the coffee. Third time they'd reached out. They wanted him for the commentary team for the next Apex Games cycle — Kai Brennan, who had finished dead last in the 200 meters in Doha, explaining the sport to an audience who understood the spectacle but not the seam between the athlete and the hardware. He'd be a body-autonomy expert now. The kind of athlete who became a talking head because the story of his failure was more useful than the fact of his performance.

He hadn't said no. He hadn't said yes. The not-deciding felt fine, which was new. For most of his career, indecision had been a condition to get rid of — another thing in the way of the training schedule, the race calendar, the next qualifying heat. Now the not-deciding was just a space he was sitting in, the same way he was sitting in this chair at this café watching Lausanne go by.

A waiter came. Kai ordered another coffee in English and the waiter answered in French and they understood each other fine. The waiter was maybe twenty, with the specific posture of someone who had been on their feet for eight hours and had three more to go.

His right hand picked up the phone. He hadn't decided to pick it up. The motion was the system's — the micro-anticipation of intent that his synthetic grafts ran ahead of his conscious nervous system, the proprioceptive drift that Yusuf had first flagged in a calibration session that felt like it had happened to someone else and had happened seven months ago. His hand held the phone. He looked at it. He put it down again, and that part — the putting down — was entirely his.

This was the daily version. Not a blackout, not the void at the 160-meter mark, not the moment in Doha when his vision fractured and his legs tried to keep running and he stopped them. Just a hand picking up a phone a quarter-second before the brain behind it had caught up. A shoulder that rolled in its socket with a mechanical smoothness — the dislocation repaired, the rotator cuff reattached, the grafts having done their work with the same indifferent efficiency that had knitted the wrist. His body working slightly ahead of him, all day, every day, in small ways that no longer frightened him and that he did not think he would ever stop noticing.

Someone had recognized him on the street yesterday. A man in running shoes, maybe forty, who'd said "tough break" with the sympathetic confidence of a person who had watched the race on a screen and understood the scoreboard. Kai had said "yeah" and the man had meant the race and Kai had meant everything else.

He thought about calling Lena. She was in Montreux, thirty minutes by train, swimming at a municipal center that charged her forty francs a month for a lane and did not ask about the hardware port at the base of her skull. They saw each other two or three times a week. It wasn't dramatic. They cooked, or she cooked and he stood in her kitchen and she put him to work chopping vegetables because she had no patience for someone standing around being useless. They talked, or didn't. When they were in bed the strangeness was still there — his hand adjusting with reflexes that weren't his, the occasional moment where her eyes changed and he waited — but it had become their strangeness, familiar and navigable, and the familiarity was better than the pretending they had both done with everyone else.

He didn't call. The afternoon was good and the coffee was coming and there was no reason to do anything about anything right now.

The waiter set down the cup. Kai thanked him. The cup was warm against his palms — both of them, the left where the bone disagreed with itself and the right where the grafts interfaced with his forearm at a seam he could feel if he pressed. The coffee was good. He drank it and his hand set the cup down on the saucer with a steadiness that was the system's, and his eyes tracked a boat on the lake far below through the gap between buildings, and the afternoon sat there, unhurried, the commentator offer on the table being undecided and the wrist aching and the body doing the small continuous work of being a body, some of it his and some of it not.

A pigeon landed on the back of the empty chair across from him and examined the table with the calculated patience of an animal that had learned where food came from.

"Nothing for you," Kai said.

The pigeon stayed. Kai drank his coffee. The afternoon didn't move.

The pool at the Centre Sportif was twenty-five meters, four lanes, and the ceiling was low enough that the humidity collected in visible patches on the concrete. Chlorine and the specific echo of water sounds in an enclosed space — splashing, the slap of a flip turn, the muffled turbulence of air bubbles on the exhale. Not the Kirchberg Institute's fifty-meter Olympic facility with its glass observation deck and temperature-controlled lanes and silent filtration. This was a municipal pool where the lane lines were faded yellow rope and the other swimmers were a retired man doing breaststroke with his head carefully above water and two teenagers who were not training so much as trying to splash each other while maintaining deniability.

Lena swam. Her stroke was clean but not optimized. The catch — her hand entering the water at the beginning of each pull — had a fraction of slippage that her pre-patch neural link would have corrected automatically, adjusting finger spread and wrist angle to maximize hydrodynamic efficiency. Now her hand entered the water the way her hand entered the water, with the minor imperfections of a body executing a motion it had practiced rather than a motion it had been programmed to execute. The difference was two seconds per hundred meters. She knew this because she timed herself, still, every session.

She was on her sixth two-hundred. Breathing every three strokes, which she chose, which she could feel herself choosing — the turn of the head, the intake of air, the return to the streamline, each element a decision she was making in sequence. The silence in her head, where Meridian's neural link had once run constant ambient processing — optimizing her sensory input, pre-filtering sound and sight and touch into the streamlined data feed she had lived inside for twenty-two years — was not silence anymore. She had stopped calling it that sometime in July. It was just the way her mind sounded when it was doing its own work. Messy. Associative. Occasionally blank for a half-second where a thought should have been. The blankness used to frighten her. Now she recognized it as the ordinary lag of a brain that was not outsourcing.

Halfway through the fifth fifty, her left arm smoothed. A micro-adjustment to her catch angle — the same optimization variant from last season's firmware that had surfaced during a training swim at Kirchberg eight months ago and started everything. Not a blackout. Not a desync episode. Just the system's residual pattern, the neural link running a corrective subroutine that Yusuf's patch had not entirely eliminated. It lasted less than a second. She felt it — the specific smoothness of a motion she had not selected, like a word appearing in a sentence she was writing that she hadn't chosen — and stopped at the wall.

She held the gutter and waited. Her breathing was elevated from the swim. The water lapped against the wall tiles in the uneven rhythm of a small pool with multiple swimmers disturbing the surface. She waited for the smoothness to come back. It didn't.

This was what the patch had given her: not a cure, but a margin. She could feel the difference now between a stroke she had chosen and a stroke the system chose for her. The difference was small — a texture in the motion, a quality of volition that she could not have described to anyone who didn't share her condition. But she could feel it, and when she felt the system assert itself she could stop. She could wait. She could push off the wall and swim the next length under her own power, with her own imperfect catch angle and her own two seconds per hundred meters of inefficiency that belonged to her.

She finished the set. The coach at the Montreux center — a woman named Isabelle who had coached junior national swimmers in the nineties and now ran the pool's public training program — was not watching closely. Isabelle didn't micromanage. She gave Lena a set at the beginning of each session and then sat in the bleachers reading a book about Mediterranean cooking and looked up when Lena touched the wall at the end of a rep to mark her time on a clipboard. Isabelle had never asked about the port at the base of Lena's skull. Lena suspected she thought it was cosmetic.

"Good set," Isabelle said, not looking up from the book. "One-oh-nine on the last one."

"Thanks."

"You want another?"

"No, I'm done."

Isabelle made a note on her clipboard. Lena climbed out and stood on the deck, water running off her suit onto the concrete. The retired breaststroke man was still going, his head held above the surface with the determination of someone who believed the pool was trying to get his hair wet. The teenagers had given up swimming entirely and were sitting on the edge of the far lane arguing about something on one of their phones.

In the changing room, Lena sat on the bench in her suit, dripping, and read the article on her phone.

Yusuf had published the analysis three days ago. Not in a journal — on ResearchGate, the open platform where engineers posted pre-prints and data sets, where the audience was anyone with the technical background to evaluate the work. The title was long and flat in the way of academic engineering: "Structural Analysis of Firmware Corruption Patterns in Shared Bio-Integrative Calibration Networks: Correspondence with Proprietary Development Tool Signatures." Under it, the raw data. All of it. The virus code analysis, the Meridian development tool signatures, the correlation metrics. His name and credentials and the Kirchberg Institute listed as his former affiliation.

The article had been live for three days and Meridian's stock had dropped eleven percent. Lena read this in the business section of the NZZ, which she'd pulled up after the ResearchGate link. Meridian had issued a statement denying the findings and announcing an internal review. The Swiss Federal Office of Sport had opened a preliminary inquiry. The European Athletics Enhancement Authority had requested the raw data, which was already public, which was the point.

It was not proof. Yusuf had written this in the article's conclusion with the precise hedging of an engineer who knew the difference between suggestive correspondence and definitive attribution. But the data was there, published, attached, downloadable. Anyone could run the analysis. Anyone could check. The information existed in the world now, and the corporate license that would have locked it behind Meridian's proprietary access — the license embedded in the data surrender contract that most athletes had signed — was no longer the only gate.

She texted Yusuf. Just: *Saw it.*

His reply came four minutes later: *Yeah. Farah says hi by the way.*

Lena sat with the phone in her hand. The changing room smelled like chlorine and the wood of the old benches and the lavender soap in the dispensers by the showers. Farah. Yusuf's sister in Birmingham, whose progressive neural deterioration had driven everything — the unauthorized data collection, the personal archive, the risk, the broken trust with Kai, the alternative patch that was running in Lena's head right now. The data Yusuf had published was the virus analysis, not the treatment data. But the treatment data — the biological information he'd collected from athletes, the information that might eventually contribute to Voss-Kessler research — that was accessible now too, or the path to it was. The investigation would open Meridian's archives. Researchers who weren't Meridian could reach the data. The gate wasn't gone but it wasn't locked.

She didn't know if it would help Farah. She didn't know enough about Voss-Kessler to know whether biological data from enhanced athletes had any therapeutic application. But Yusuf's text said *Farah says hi*, and the *by the way* carried everything he wouldn't say directly: that his sister was still there, still cheerful, still deflecting questions about her worsening speech with the stubbornness he'd described in the Kirchberg cafeteria the night before they left for Doha. That the data was free and the research could continue and this was the thing he had risked his career for and it was enough. Not a victory. Not proof. Enough.

Lena toweled off. The towel caught on the port at the base of her skull — the small circle of titanium, warm from the pool water, the interface through which her neural link connected to the outside world and through which Yusuf's patch had been installed during a night she remembered in full because she'd been conscious for every second of it, the heat along the interface, the pressure behind her eyes, the hours. She touched it with two fingers, the way she always did, the absent check of a thing that was part of her body but was not her body.

She got dressed and walked out into August in Montreux. The lake was enormous and flat and the sun was on it and the air smelled like warm stone and the jasmine that grew along the promenade railing near the station. She had a train to catch to Lausanne. Her hair was still damp from the pool and the chlorine smell followed her the way it always had, since she was four years old in a calibration pool in Zug, since before she knew that the system in her head was a system and not herself. The smell was the same. What she was inside the smell was different, or starting to be different, or might be different eventually, and she walked toward the station with wet hair and the silence in her head and the two seconds per hundred meters that were hers.

The treatment facility was in Schwabing, in a converted Jugendstil house on a street lined with linden trees that smelled like August. The house had high ceilings and decorative plaster molding and a garden out back where patients were encouraged to sit in good weather. The art on the walls was the kind chosen by someone who understood that institutional spaces needed art but not what good art looked like — watercolors of lakes, a photograph of mountains that could have been any mountains, a print of a Klee that had been hung slightly crooked and that nobody had straightened in the six weeks Margot had been here.

Six weeks without the feed. Forty-two days. She didn't count them the way people in recovery programs were supposed to count them — with accumulating pride, each day a brick in the wall between you and the substance. She counted them the way she'd counted laps: because the number was information, and information was what she had instead of certainty.

The program was called NeuroBalance, and it was one of three facilities in Germany that specialized in neural-interface addiction. Most of the other patients were feed-dependent — people who'd developed compulsive relationships with entertainment-grade sensory interfaces, the immersive sports simulations and full-body relaxation feeds that were marketed as wellness tools and worked as digital opiates. The feeds they described were crude by comparison. They had plugged into recordings. Margot had plugged into people.

She tried to explain this to Helga during their Thursday session. Helga was forty-three, Bavarian, precise in her English the way Germans often were — grammatically careful, slightly over-enunciated. Good therapist. Did not understand what Margot was telling her.

"The entertainment feeds are recorded," Margot said. She was sitting in the chair across from Helga's desk, which had a small succulent on it and a framed photo of a dog. "Recorded and processed and cleaned up. Like a movie of a body. What I did was live. I was inside someone's actual nervous system while they were using it."

"Yes, you've said this." Helga's pen rested on her notepad. "What I'm interested in today is the distinction between the experience itself and the need to repeat it."

"There's no distinction."

"Can you say more?"

Margot looked at the succulent. It was the kind that didn't need much water, which was probably the point of keeping it on a therapist's desk. "The experience is the need. They're the same thing. It's not like I had the experience and then afterward I wanted more. The wanting is the experience. You plug in and you're already wanting to plug in again. The first second of someone else's body is the craving and the fix at the same time."

Helga wrote something. The pen moved across the notepad with steady pressure.

"And the memory of the experience," Helga said. "Is the memory also—"

"Intact," Margot said. "I can build the whole thing from memory. Right now, if I wanted to. The way it felt to be inside Tomás's body when he cleared a hurdle — the hamstring load, the visual lock on the bar, the proprioceptive sense of being twenty-one and built for exactly this. I can feel it now. Sitting here. Not as strong as the real thing. Strong enough."

"And the crash?"

"That too."

"You can reconstruct that sensation?"

"Yeah." Margot held her gaze. "The compression. The silence when the signals stopped. His legs going dead. All of it. I thought that would fix it. For about a week after it happened I thought, okay, this is the thing — you can't want something that did this to someone. But you can. You can know exactly what the feed cost a twenty-one-year-old kid and still want it. These two things just sit next to each other in my head without canceling out."

Helga was quiet. She did this after Margot said things that didn't fit the therapeutic framework — the pause that was supposed to look like reflection but was actually recalculation.

"The wanting," Helga said. "Has it changed in quality over the six weeks? Not necessarily in intensity. In quality."

"No."

"Not at all?"

"It's the same. It was this size on day one and it's this size on day forty-two. Doesn't get bigger. Doesn't get smaller. It's just there. Like furniture."

"We'll continue Thursday," Helga said, and wrote something else, and Margot stood up because the session was over and because there was nothing more to say about it that she hadn't already said.

After group — which was four people in a circle talking about triggers and coping strategies, during which Margot said reasonable things about mindfulness techniques she didn't believe in and made eye contact with a twenty-six-year-old named Stefan who was trembling through withdrawal from an entertainment feed he'd used eighteen hours a day for two years — Margot walked.

August in Munich. Warm, not hot. The lindens on Leopoldstraße in full leaf, the air carrying that late-summer smell of heated pavement and green and the distant mineral note of the Isar. She walked south through Schwabing, past the university, past cafés where students sat outside with laptops and beer in the late afternoon, past the Siegestor with its stone horses catching the low light. She was wearing jeans and a T-shirt and running shoes and she looked like anyone. That was the thing about it. Nobody on this street could see that the woman walking past the monument was carrying the precise sensory memory of another person's spinal cord going silent.

She ended up at the Eisbach. She hadn't meant to come here today, but she kept ending up here. The Eisbach was a channel of the Isar that ran through the Englischer Garten, and at a certain point the channel narrowed and the current formed a standing wave that surfers had been riding for decades. She'd found it her second week and kept coming back.

Four surfers today. Young, most of them. One woman in a wetsuit despite the warm air, her board shorter than standard, shaped for the wave's specific geometry. The wave was maybe a meter high, clean, glassy in the late light. The surfers dropped in one at a time from the bank — a sideways leap, the board angling to catch the current, then the rapid adjustment of weight and balance on a wave that never moved forward. Stationary surfing. The wave held and the surfer held and the river kept moving underneath them.

The woman in the wetsuit was good. She cut back and forth across the wave face with the efficient fluidity of someone whose body was doing what it had learned to do — weight drops, ankle adjustments, the continuous proprioceptive reading of a moving surface. Not enhanced. Not linked. Just muscle and board and river and the accumulated hours that made the motion look easy. Her weight sank into a bottom turn and her arm swept out for balance and her torso rotated and for a moment the line of her body was a clean functional expression of physics and intention and practice.

Margot could see the biomechanics. The quadricep engagement, the dorsiflexion, the counter-rotation of the hips. She could see it because she'd spent fifteen years learning to read bodies the way other people read faces. She could almost feel it — dimly, the way you heard music from another room — because the neural pathways the feed had built were still there, still listening for input that wasn't coming.

She sat on the bank and watched. The surfers rode the wave and climbed out and rode it again. The woman in the wetsuit finally lost her line and splashed into the current and hauled herself out laughing, water streaming off her suit, her board tucked under one arm. She shook her hair and said something to the guy waiting to drop in and he laughed too and the sound carried across the water in the warm air.

Margot watched until the light changed and the surfers climbed out one by one and walked away with their boards under their arms and the standing wave kept going, the water folding over itself the way it did whether anyone was riding it or not.

She stood up and walked back to the facility because it was almost dinner. The evening was warm. The lindens smelled like summer. Her legs carried her through the park and along the Leopoldstraße and up the steps of the old house, and the wanting was there, the same size it had been that morning, which was the same size it had been the morning before, which was the same size it was every morning, ordinary and entire, and she walked.
