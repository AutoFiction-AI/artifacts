# Chapter 8

The Meridian delegation arrived on a Tuesday morning, three black SUVs grinding up the mountain road through overnight snow. Kai watched them from the cafeteria windows while eating breakfast one-handed — fork in his right, the left wrist braced and too swollen to grip anything smaller than a coffee mug. The vehicles pulled into the loading bay with coordinated timing, one after the other, as if they'd rehearsed the spacing. Three people climbed out of the first: two men in dark coats and a woman in a grey wool suit who crossed the icy concrete without slipping, a leather portfolio held flat against her chest.

"That's Jin-seo Park," Tomas said across the table, leaning forward to follow Kai's gaze. "Athlete liaison. She was at the Seoul orientation last year." He paused with his spoon over a bowl of porridge. "She's all right. She actually listens."

Kai set his fork down. His wrist throbbed under the brace — a deep tidal pulse synchronized to his heartbeat, the bone reknitting on a schedule the grafts had set without consulting him. He could feel the repair happening, which was its own specific wrongness: a low grinding deep inside the bone, something being filed into shape from within. The stitched laceration across his left cheekbone had stopped hurting days ago but still pulled when he chewed.

Outside the cafeteria windows, the sky was the flat grey of late February at twenty-one hundred meters, snow stacked along the ridgelines in wind-sculpted drifts. The second and third SUVs discharged their passengers: technicians or lawyers or whatever Meridian sent when they wanted to remind you who owned the calibration network your nervous system ran on.

Tomas was watching too, but with a different expression — attentive, assessing, the look of someone keeping track of the people who controlled his livelihood. He was twenty-one and already fluent in reading institutional arrivals. "They don't usually send this many," he said.

"No."

"You think it's about the virus?"

Kai finished his coffee. "Yeah, Tomas. I think it's about the virus."

---

An hour later, thirty-four athletes and most of the Kirchberg senior staff filled the second-floor conference room. The space had been rearranged overnight — training-schedule whiteboards stacked against the wall, the usual scatter of tables replaced by rows of chairs facing a long table at the front. A projection display glowed with Meridian's logo on the wall behind it. Printed binders at every seat. Bottled water stamped with the Meridian mark, which had not previously appeared anywhere in the facility's supply chain. Someone from the delegation had carried those up the mountain.

The fluorescent lighting buzzed at a frequency Kai had stopped noticing weeks ago. He sat near the right wall, three rows back, his chair angled so he could see the door and the front table at once — a habit left over from the first days after the crash, when he'd started tracking his exits. Lena was two rows ahead of him, her hair still damp from the morning swim, the faint chlorine trace that followed her everywhere. Margot stood against the far wall with her arms crossed, watching the room with that flat coaching attention she brought to everything she observed but did not participate in. Yusuf was in the front row next to Dr. Engel. Yusuf had his hands folded, still. Engel sat with her back straight and her mouth a thin line, the posture of someone who'd been briefed and hadn't liked any of it.

Jin-seo Park stood behind the front table. She was smaller than the role implied — compact, dark hair cut at the jaw, no jewelry except a slim watch she touched once, unconsciously, before beginning. She waited for the room to settle without asking for quiet. When the last murmured conversation died she did not begin with the virus. She began with something Kai hadn't expected.

"I know some of you," she said. "Several of you from Seoul. Others from your files, which isn't the same thing." A brief pause. "Twelve days ago, one of you hit a wall at sprint speed because his body kept running after his senses cut out. I've read the medical report. I've seen the telemetry. I want you to know that what I'm about to present isn't something Meridian assembled in the past twelve days. We've been tracking this vulnerability for eight months. The disclosure timeline was moved forward because of what happened to Mr. Brennan."

She glanced at Kai. The room followed her glance. He felt the attention settle on the stitched cheekbone, the brace, the shoulder he was holding a degree too carefully. The Hungarian weightlifter two seats ahead turned all the way around to look at him, then turned back.

"Eight months," the weightlifter said. "You knew for eight months."

"The initial detection was eight months ago. Characterizing the virus — what it does, how it propagates, what stops it — took the full interval. I'm not going to stand here and tell you that timeline was perfect. I'm going to tell you what we have now."

She tapped the projection screen. A schematic filled the wall: the calibration network architecture that connected every enhanced athlete at the facility, rendered in clean blue lines and white nodes. Kai had seen rougher versions of this on Yusuf's whiteboards, hand-drawn, coffee-ringed. This version looked like it had been built for an investor presentation.

"The virus propagates through the shared calibration network," Jin-seo said. "Every routine calibration, every firmware sync — there's a transmission window. The corruption targets the firmware layer that governs sensory processing. When it triggers, you lose everything. Sight, hearing, proprioception, spatial orientation. Total blackout. The autonomous subsystems in your enhancements don't pause during a blackout. Your legs keep driving. Your arms keep stroking. Whatever intensity the system was running, it keeps running."

She let that sit. The room was quiet in a way that had texture to it — not the absence of sound but the active suppression of it, thirty-four people holding their breath or their questions or both. Someone shifted in their chair and the creak was enormous.

"We've developed a firmware-level patch that neutralizes the virus's replication mechanism and restores stable sensory processing. Installation takes approximately four hours per athlete under calibration-bay conditions. Dr. Qadir's team would handle the technical procedure." She nodded at Yusuf. He didn't nod back. His hands stayed folded. "In controlled simulation, the patch eliminated the blackout trigger in every scenario we tested."

"What's the catch?" Margot said from the wall. Not loudly, but her voice carried the way a coach's voice carries — flat, projected, indifferent to whether the room wanted to hear it.

Jin-seo turned to her. "The patch requires full read-write access to your enhancement system's firmware layer. Not temporary access. The installation rewrites the base-level architecture of your sensory processing, and to maintain that patch — to ensure compatibility with future firmware updates — Meridian needs to retain that access. Permanently."

"And what does permanent access include, exactly?"

Jin-seo picked up one of the printed binders from the table and turned to a marked page. "I'm going to read the relevant provision directly, because the language matters and I'd rather you heard it precisely than got a paraphrase." She looked down at the text. The room went still.

"'In consideration of the Installation and ongoing maintenance of the Meridian Apex Enhancement Stability Protocol, the Athlete hereby grants to Meridian BioSystems, its successors and assigns, an irrevocable, transferable license to all biometric, genetic, neural, and physiological data generated by or through the Enhancement System, including but not limited to real-time performance metrics, neurological activity patterns, genetic expression data, and baseline physiological markers. This license shall survive termination of any sponsorship agreement, retirement from competitive athletics, or deactivation of the Enhancement System.'"

She set the binder down on the table. She did not look away from the room.

Silence. Then a Swedish cyclist in the second row said, "What does 'transferable' mean? They can sell it?"

"It means Meridian can sublicense the data to research partners, medical institutions, or other authorized entities under the standard data-sharing framework."

"But they could sell it."

"The framework includes —"

"Yes or no."

"Yes."

A ripple moved through the room. Not gasps — something quieter and more calculating, thirty-four people adjusting the same equation at different speeds. Kai watched the back of Lena's head. She hadn't moved. Her hands lay flat on her thighs, still, in that way she held herself when she was processing with her entire body.

"What happens if we don't sign?" Aïssatou Diop said from near the front. The 400m sprinter from the Côte d'Ivoire team. She sat with her hands at her sides, one resting on the armrest, the other on the binder she hadn't opened.

"Then Meridian can't install the patch. Without the patch, you remain vulnerable to the virus. The probability of a sensory blackout during high-exertion performance increases with each calibration-network exposure. I can't give you a specific number, but the risk is non-trivial and it's escalating."

"Can we still compete?"

"The Apex Games don't require the patch. You can compete unpatched." She stopped. Not for dramatic effect — she stopped because the sentence she had prepared reorganized itself behind her eyes into something she wasn't willing to deliver as a talking point. "But a blackout during the hundred meters, or on the apparatus, or in the pool — it could be fatal. That's not a negotiating position. That's what the telemetry data shows."

Dr. Engel leaned forward in the front row. "The medical assessment supports that characterization," she said carefully. "The blackout risk during high-exertion states is real. I've reviewed the clinical data." She paused, and the pause had the quality of someone choosing each word as if placing it on a scale. "I'm not in a position to speak to the contractual terms. But the physiological danger is well-documented."

She sat back. It was the kind of clinical confirmation that was helpful and insufficient at the same time — a doctor telling you the surgery was necessary without saying anything about the bill.

That was enough for the first wave. A German javelin thrower — tall, blond, an enhancement scar visible at his collarbone where the muscle-graft integration hadn't quite healed flush — picked up his binder, turned to the signature page, and signed. He did it the way you sign a credit card receipt: quickly, without rereading, because the decision had been made somewhere between "irrevocable" and "fatal." He closed the binder and pushed it to the edge of his seat.

Two more within thirty seconds. Then four. Then three together near the back row, who seemed to have agreed on something without speaking. The scratch of pens on paper was small and specific — a sound that carried in the fluorescent silence. Twelve binders pushed to the edges of seats before the room spoke again.

---

Twenty-two athletes had not signed. They sat with closed binders on their laps or open binders turned face-down, and the room had reorganized itself without anyone moving — a visible fault line between those who had decided and those who hadn't. The air-handling system cycled, pushing warm air through overhead vents, and the draft lifted the corner of a binder page on an empty seat.

A middle-distance runner from the Nigerian team — Adewale, Kai was fairly sure — raised his hand. He held it up like they were in a lecture hall, which in a way they were. "Is there a sunset clause? Any provision for the license to expire?"

"No," Jin-seo said. "The license is irrevocable."

"And after retirement?"

"The license survives retirement."

"So when I'm forty-five and coaching kids somewhere and haven't competed in ten years, Meridian still holds a license to my neurological data."

"The data generated by and through the Enhancement System, yes. In perpetuity."

Adewale closed his binder. He set it on the floor between his feet with a care that made the action feel deliberate.

A gymnast from the Brazilian team — Kai didn't know her name, but he'd seen her on the uneven bars, fearless at four meters — asked without raising her hand: "The contract says 'baseline physiological markers.' That's not enhancement data. That's my body before any of this. My genetics. Things that have nothing to do with athletics."

"The license covers all data generated by or through the Enhancement System," Jin-seo said. "Because the system interfaces with your baseline biology, the data streams are technically inseparable."

"Technically."

"Yes."

The gymnast looked at her binder as though it had changed shape.

Lena raised her hand. She waited for Jin-seo to acknowledge her, then spoke in the precise, slightly formal cadence Kai was learning to recognize — sentences assembled in her second language with a structural care that native speakers rarely bothered with. "If I accept the patch and stop competing, and Meridian determines that my neurological patterns have research value I don't know about — can I withdraw the license?"

"The license doesn't include a withdrawal provision."

"So no."

"That's correct."

Lena nodded once. She didn't close her binder. She didn't open it either. Her hands returned to her thighs, flat and still.

Someone farther back, a voice Kai couldn't place: "You're asking us to sell our bodies to stay alive."

The room's atmosphere changed. Not because the statement was new — everyone present had arrived at some version of it in the past twenty minutes — but because someone had said it out loud, and saying it out loud drew a line across the carpet between the front table and the rows of chairs, between the people presenting and the people being presented to.

Jin-seo's hand went to the edge of the portfolio on the table. She touched it the way someone touches a railing — steadying, not gripping. She started to say something in her professional register and stopped. Her mouth opened and closed. When she spoke again her voice was lower and the corporate cadence was gone.

"In 2034, my brother was competing in the Asia-Pacific qualifiers. Triathlon. He was enhanced — Meridian system, same generation hardware as some of yours. During the cycling stage, his system entered a cascade failure." She looked at the room. "That's when the autonomous subsystems stop coordinating and start issuing contradictory commands. One leg was getting a fire signal on the quadriceps while the other was locking the hamstring. At forty kilometers an hour, on a road bike."

She wasn't reading notes. She wasn't performing this.

"He went over the handlebars. He broke his neck, three ribs, his pelvis. He was alive for nine hours after the crash. The cascade was still firing in his legs when they got him to the hospital. They couldn't stop it because they didn't have access to his firmware. Meridian's protocols at the time required a corporate authorization code to reach the base layer, and the nearest authorized technician was in Singapore. Jun-ho died at two in the morning in a hospital in Busan while a Meridian engineer was on a plane."

She picked up her water bottle, unscrewed it, and drank. Her hand was steady. She set the bottle down.

"They changed the access protocols after that. Obviously. The provisions in this contract are partly a consequence of his death — they ensure that in a medical emergency, the system can be reached immediately, without waiting for authorization from a technician on the other side of the Pacific." She looked at the room. "I know what you hear when I read 'irrevocable, transferable license.' I hear it too. But I also hear the nine hours my brother spent dying because nobody could get into his system to shut it down."

The room was quiet for a long time. The kind of quiet where you could hear the hum of the projection equipment and the wind against the windows two floors up. Kai looked at Margot against the far wall. Her arms were still crossed but her jaw had set, and she was looking at the floor — not at Jin-seo, not at the binders, at the floor — with an expression that Kai couldn't read and wasn't sure she wanted anyone to.

Yusuf, in the front row, had his head tilted at the angle Kai recognized from calibration sessions — the angle that meant he was thinking about a system, taking it apart in his head, looking for the piece that didn't fit. He was staring at the network schematic still glowing on the projection wall, and whatever he was thinking, he wasn't sharing it.

Then Kai heard, to his left and one row ahead, the sound of a binder opening. A pen cap popping.

He knew before he looked that it was Tomas.

Tomas signed with the practiced, economical motion of someone who had filled out official documents before — sponsorship contracts, visa applications, the orientation forms from Seoul, the Meridian compliance paperwork that came in quarterly. He wrote his name in block letters next to the signature line, printed the date, and closed the binder. His face was composed. Not resigned. Composed. The face of someone who had done arithmetic and arrived at a number he could live with.

Kai knew parts of that arithmetic. Tomas's sponsorship from Meridian covered training, equipment, travel, medical support, and a monthly stipend wired to an account in Quito that his mother drew from for rent and his siblings' school fees. Two younger siblings, both still in school. The sponsorship contract had provisions about compliance with Meridian's technical requirements — standard language, the kind of clause nobody read until it mattered. Refusing the patch and its terms wouldn't just mean competing with a virus in his system. It would mean breach of contract. It would mean the stipend stopping. It would mean his mother opening the banking app on her phone and finding the deposit that came on the fifteenth of every month simply absent.

Tomas set the binder on the empty seat beside him and folded his hands in his lap. He stared straight ahead at Jin-seo with an expression that held no accusation, no acceptance, no visible feeling at all. It was the expression of someone who had been offered a choice that wasn't one.

He caught Kai looking at him. Something passed between them — not understanding, not solidarity, something smaller and harder to name. Tomas was twenty-one and his family ate because of what his body could do. Kai was thirty and had savings and endorsement income and a wrist that was repairing itself without his permission. They were in the same room but not in the same conversation. They were in the same building having different emergencies.

A few more athletes signed after Tomas. His signing seemed to work as permission or resignation or simply evidence that it could be done — that you could write your name on the line and then sit back and still be yourself, at least for now. Four more binders closed and pushed to the edges of seats in the next two minutes.

Then the signing stopped. The fault line held. Eighteen athletes sat with binders closed or untouched.

Jin-seo checked her watch — the same unconscious touch from the start, her thumb on the face, a gesture that looked less like checking the time than confirming something was still there. "I'll be at the facility through Thursday. The contract can be signed at any point before the team departs for Doha. If you have questions, I'm available. One-on-one, if that's easier."

She gathered her portfolio. The two men from the delegation, who had sat silently through the entire session, stood and began collecting signed binders with the practiced efficiency of people whose job was collecting signed documents. One of them wore thin surgical gloves, which struck Kai as either hygienic or symbolic and either way made his stomach turn.

He looked down at the binder on his own lap. It was open to the signature page — he didn't remember opening it. The line was blank. His name was already printed beneath it in small type, waiting for a pen to make it binding. Kai Brennan. The same line length as the provisions above it, the ones that said irrevocable and transferable and survive termination. Just a line. Just a name. Just everything your body had ever generated or would generate, in perpetuity, for a patch that might keep you alive.

His left wrist throbbed. The bone grinding itself back together inside the brace, the grafts reknitting tissue on their schedule, the body remaking itself according to instructions he hadn't written and couldn't override. He closed the binder and pressed his right palm flat against the cover, holding it shut.

Outside the conference-room windows, the Alps were white and enormous under a sky that was trying to clear. Snow blew off the high ridges in thin veils, carried by wind that had nothing to do with anyone in this room.
