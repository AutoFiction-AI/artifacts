# Chapter 11

The first time was in his room, because her room had a window that faced the floodlit outdoor track and neither of them wanted to see it.

His diagnostic display was on standby — a dim green pulse on the wall, the numbers minimized to a single status bar. Heart rate, bridge latency, graft integration. All normal. He'd stopped checking it every hour about a week ago, which probably counted as progress.

Lena sat at the head of the bed with her knees drawn up and her phone facedown on the mattress. She'd come over after the pool. Her hair was still damp, the chlorine smell so constant on her that he'd stopped noticing it except when she was close. They'd been talking about nothing — a movie someone had put on in the common room, whether the cafeteria coffee was getting worse or they were all just adjusting to the altitude — and the conversation had run down the way conversations do when neither person wants to leave but neither has a reason to stay that they're ready to name.

He was sitting at the other end of the bed. His left wrist was on his knee, the ache doing its low-grade thing, the bone reminding him it had been broken and reassembled on a schedule the rest of his body hadn't agreed to. The scar tissue on his cheek had smoothed to a pink shine. Six weeks ago he'd been bleeding on the track with his legs still twitching. Now he was sitting on a narrow institutional bed with a woman who smelled like chlorine, and the distance between those two states had no name he could find.

"You're staring at your wrist," Lena said.

"I'm not staring."

"You're looking at it the way you look at the display." She tilted her head toward the wall-mounted screen. "Checking."

He flexed his fingers, felt the ache shift from the wrist into the base of his thumb. "It does this thing where it almost feels normal, and then it reminds me it isn't."

"Almost normal," she said. "I know that one."

He looked at her. She was watching him with the kind of attention she usually gave to things she was trying to measure — the distance to a wall, the temperature of the water. Clinical, but not cold. Interested. He liked being looked at by her, which was a simple thing to feel and he let himself feel it.

"Come here," she said. Not a command. Information about what she wanted.

He moved up the bed. The mattress was narrow — built for a single athlete sleeping alone, not for two people trying to figure out how much space to leave between them. They ended up close enough that he could feel the heat off her shoulder. She reached over and picked up his left hand, turned it over, ran her thumb along the inside of his wrist where the line from the brace had faded to a thin white mark. The touch registered twice. The first was the raw signal — her thumb, his skin, the pressure and warmth of it. The second came a fraction behind, the system's interpretation: temperature mapped to two decimal places, pressure distributed across his wrist's surface area, her pulse detectable through her fingertip. He was used to the echo. Three years. But he noticed it now, here, because the second signal carried information about another person's body that he had not asked for and did not want.

He kissed her. That was also simple. She tasted like the peppermint tea from the cafeteria and her lips were slightly chapped from the pool chemicals, and those were details that belonged to him — things he noticed, not things the system measured and reported back. She kissed him back, and for a few minutes nothing was complicated.

Then it was. Not badly, not yet. He pulled her shirt over her head and she lifted her arms to help and it caught on her left ear and she said "ow" and laughed, and he laughed, and that was human enough. Her body was a swimmer's body — shoulders wider than her hips, muscle layered across her back in the specific architecture of lats and traps built by years of pull. He'd known this about her in the abstract. Touching it was different. His hand moved along her ribs and he could feel the individual muscles between the bones, the way her breathing expanded her ribcage laterally more than frontally. The system processed this too, feeding him biomechanical data about her thoracic expansion pattern that he did not need and could not stop receiving. He needed to know that she was breathing and that his hand was on her body and that both of those things were good. The rest was noise.

She unbuttoned his shirt with the deliberate precision she brought to most tasks — one button, then the next, her fingers working efficiently, which made him smile because most people either fumbled with buttons or turned them into performance and Lena just undid them like they were a problem she intended to solve correctly. When the shirt was open she put her palm flat against his sternum and held it there. He didn't know what she was checking. Maybe just that he was warm.

"Your heart rate is elevated," she said. Almost clinically.

"Yeah," he said. "That's happening."

She smiled. One of her small smiles, the kind that arrived and left without making a production of itself. He kissed the corner of it and felt her hand slide up to his shoulder — the right one, the one that had been dislocated — and when she pressed the joint he winced before he could stop himself.

"Still sore?"

"Little bit."

She lightened her grip but didn't move her hand. They were on their sides now, facing each other on the narrow mattress, and the logistics of the bed meant they were either touching everywhere or falling off. They touched everywhere.

He kissed the side of her neck and his mouth moved down to where her hair fell damp against her shoulder, and he pushed the hair aside. His thumb found the neural link port at the base of her skull — a small hard circle, body-warm, the engineered rim flush with the surrounding skin. She didn't flinch. It was as much a part of her body as the vertebrae below it. He traced the edge of it with his fingertip and the system told him the port's diameter and surface temperature and material composition, and he stopped listening to the system and kissed the skin beside the port instead, and she made a sound that was quiet and human and not available for diagnostic interpretation.

What happened next was not graceful because first times are not graceful, and the bed was too narrow, and at one point Kai's elbow hit the wall-mounted diagnostic display and it blinked from standby to active, throwing his vitals across the screen in green text — heart rate 118, neural bridge latency nominal, right shoulder ROM 94% — until Lena reached over his arm and switched it off without looking at it, which was the most thoughtful thing anyone had done for him in months. Then it was just the dim light from the hallway under the door and the faint mountain dark through the window and their bodies figuring out the logistics of a bed that had never been designed for this.

She was on top of him because his shoulder couldn't take his weight for long, and her knees were on either side of his hips, and his hands were on her waist, and this was the part where the body-autonomy question stopped being a calibration report.

The dual-track sensation was constant. Every point of contact delivered twice — the raw physical signal, then the system's cleaned-up version a fraction behind, like a translator who couldn't stop correcting the original. Her thighs against his hips: first the warmth and pressure and friction of skin, then the system's readout of contact area, force distribution, her muscle tension patterns mapped with clinical precision. He tried to stay with the first signal, the raw one, the one that belonged to him. It wasn't easy. The system didn't turn off because you wanted it to.

He'd never had sex with the echo before. Three years with the neural bridge and he had felt its doubling in every handshake, every training drill, every time someone bumped his shoulder in the cafeteria line. But here the doubling was different because the stakes of the raw signal were different. When she leaned down and kissed him, her hair fell against his face, wet at the tips, and the system reported the water's chlorine concentration and her exhaled CO2 level while his mouth was on hers. The clinical track ran underneath everything like closed captioning he couldn't disable — technically accurate, describing the same event, and not the same event at all.

His hands were on her hips and she was moving and he was moving with her and his right hand — the hand that worked fine, that had never been broken, connected to the same spinal neural bridge that governed everything below his neck — shifted. He didn't tell it to. His fingers spread slightly, redistributed across her hip, the grip adjusting from loose to firm with a mechanical smoothness that recalibrated the contact for optimal stability. The adjustment took less than a second. It was ergonomically perfect. His hand found the exact angle that held her weight evenly, that supported her movement without restricting it, and it did this the way a system does things: efficiently, without effort, without him.

He kept moving because she was moving and his body had gotten very good at continuing to perform things he had stopped commanding. But the system's signal was suddenly louder than the raw one, and the hand on her hip felt less like something he was doing and more like something he was watching from inside his own arm.

His hand had touched another person's body with a competence he did not generate.

It lasted a second. Maybe two. His fingers on her hip, holding her with a precision that was not his, while the rest of him tried to locate the boundary between the grip he'd intended and the grip the system had decided on. He couldn't find it. The muscle was his muscle. The nerve was his nerve. The bridge connecting them just happened to be faster and better at this than he was.

He moved his hand deliberately — loosened the grip, put his palm flat against her side, pressed it there with conscious force. That was him. He felt the decision travel from his brain down his arm and arrive in his fingers with the slight delay that meant a human being had made it.

Lena's breathing changed. She slowed. He looked up at her face and she was looking at him, and the quality of her attention had shifted. Not suspicion. Something closer to inquiry. Like she had registered the adjustment and was cataloguing it.

"I'm OK," he said. It came out flatter than he meant it to.

She nodded and kissed him and they kept going, and for a while it was fine, or close to fine, or close enough that they could both decide to treat it that way.

Then it wasn't fine.

It happened at an ordinary moment. Not a peak, not a crisis. She was beneath him — they'd shifted, slowly, carefully around his shoulder — and her hands were on his back and her eyes were open and she was present, fully, looking at him with an attention that was specific and warm and hers. And then she wasn't.

There was no sound, no seizure, no visible malfunction. She went still. Her hands stayed on his back but the pressure behind them went flat, like someone had stopped squeezing a held object. Her eyes didn't close. They stayed open, stayed pointed at him, but the person behind them — the particular quality of attention that was Lena, the sharpness and precision and faintly clinical interest and the warmth underneath all of it — went away. What was left looked like her. It had her face, her body, her hands on his back. But the presence was gone. The eyes looking at him were looking without seeing, the way a window looks at a street.

Kai stopped. He was still above her, weight on his left arm, right shoulder aching, and the woman underneath him was physically unchanged and completely absent.

"Lena?"

Nothing. Her eyes were open. She was breathing. Her body was warm. No one was home.

Two seconds. Three. He didn't know what to do. Pull away, touch her face, call someone. The options lined up in his mind and stayed there, equally available, equally useless. He stayed where he was. His arms held his weight. His right shoulder sent a slow ache up into his neck. These were his — the staying, the holding, the hurting — and he held onto them because they were decisions he had made.

Then she came back. He saw it happen. The eyes first — the quality of light behind them changed, like a lamp coming on in a room that had gone dark. Then her hands, which pressed into his back with renewed intention. Then her face, which registered where she was and what was happening and arranged itself into something that was not quite confusion and not quite fear but had the texture of someone finding themselves in a place they didn't remember entering.

"Hey," he said. His voice came out rough. "Hey. You went somewhere."

She blinked. Looked at him. Looked at her own hands on his back, as though confirming they were hers.

"How long?" she said.

"Few seconds."

She was quiet. He rolled off her, carefully, and lay beside her on the narrow bed. They weren't touching except at the shoulder, the mattress too small for distance but too narrow for pretense.

"Few seconds," she repeated. Not a question.

"Yeah."

She stared at the ceiling. He stared at the ceiling.

She took a slow breath and released it. Then another. The rhythm of someone re-establishing inventory — checking that the body answered to the right name. He recognized it because he'd done the same thing in the medical bay after the blackout, the same deliberate breathing, the same careful audit of what was still there.

"I'm here," she said. To herself as much as to him.

"I know."

The hallway light under the door made a thin gold bar on the floor. Outside, wind moved against the window with the flat, constant pressure of altitude.

---

Neither of them slept. They put their clothes back on — not all of them, enough — and lay in the dark. The diagnostic display stayed off. The wind outside was steady and without opinion. Somewhere down the hall a door closed softly. The calibration bay's low hum was there if you listened for it — always there, the base frequency of a building designed around machines that tuned human bodies.

"Are you cold?" he said.

"A little."

He pulled the blanket higher over both of them. The fabric was the same thin institutional weave as everything else in the athletes' quarters — designed for function, not comfort.

Kai flexed his left hand under the blanket, opening and closing his fingers. The wrist ache was there. It was always there. It sat in the bone like a tenant who had paid up and wasn't leaving.

"I should tell you what it's like," Lena said. She was on her back with her hands folded on her stomach, her hair drying now, the chlorine smell fading into something warmer and more her. She said it the way she said most things — a decision she'd made and was now carrying out.

"You don't have to."

"I know I don't have to. I want to tell you."

He waited. She was quiet for long enough that he thought she might have changed her mind, but she was composing, not retreating. Lena composed her sentences before she delivered them. He'd learned this.

"I don't pass out," she said. "Everyone asks that. They want to know if it's like fainting. It isn't. When you faint, your body stops functioning. Mine doesn't stop. It functions better."

She turned her head to look at him. The dim light from the hallway caught the edge of her jaw.

"It's like being set aside. Very gently. Something eases me out of the way — not forcefully, nothing hurts — and fills the space where I was. And whatever fills it is more efficient than I am. It doesn't hesitate. It doesn't second-guess. It doesn't wonder whether to move my left hand or my right hand. It just operates. And it adapts — if the situation changes, it adjusts. If someone speaks to me, it answers. And the answers are — correct. People don't notice." She paused. "That's what I can't — people don't notice. And then it eases out and I'm there again, and I don't always know how long I was gone or what happened while I wasn't."

Kai didn't say anything. His hand had stopped flexing.

"The worst part," she said, and her voice was steady in a way that sounded like it cost her something, "the worst part isn't the absence. I don't experience the absence. That's the whole point. I'm not there to experience it. The worst part is coming back and knowing that my body was doing things without me and that no one could tell the difference."

The wind gusted against the window. Something metallic ticked in the heating vent.

"No one," Kai said.

"No. If I'd come back and kept going tonight, you wouldn't have known. Would you? If whatever it was had just — continued. You wouldn't have been able to tell."

He wanted to say yes, he would have known, he would have felt the difference. But the question wasn't whether he'd noticed the moment she left — he had noticed that. The question was whether he'd have noticed when she came back, or whether the thing that replaced her was smooth enough that he'd have kept going, touching a body that performed like Lena and was not Lena in any way that counted.

"I don't know," he said.

She nodded. She didn't look hurt by that. She looked like she'd expected it, which was worse.

"What does it — what does the other thing feel like? When it's there instead of you."

"I don't feel it. That's the point. I only know it was there afterward. There's a gap. Not a gap in time — I don't lose hours, usually just seconds. A gap in continuity. Like a cut in a film. One moment I'm in the pool and the next moment I'm at the wall and two laps happened and I sort of remember them but they don't feel like mine. They feel like something I watched someone else do."

She said this with the clinical precision she always brought to descriptions of physical sensation. Her English was clean and slightly formal, each word chosen and placed. He wondered if that precision was a kind of scaffolding — whether describing the desync in structured language kept it at a distance she could manage.

"Margot said it's within acceptable parameters," Lena said. Her voice changed when she said Margot's name. Not angry. Careful.

"You believe that?"

"No." Simple. Definite. Something she'd measured against her own evidence.

They lay there. The bed was narrow and their shoulders were touching and the contact was nothing like what it had been half an hour ago. It was just contact. Two people in a small bed because neither one had left.

The heating vent clicked again. His stomach growled, which was stupid and perfectly timed and they both heard it.

"Sorry," he said.

"You didn't eat?"

"Half a protein bar. I got distracted."

She almost smiled. He could hear it in her breathing even if he couldn't see it in the dark. The moment was small and ordinary and he was grateful for it — proof that they were two people in a room and not two case files lying side by side.

"Can I tell you something?" Kai said. He hadn't planned to say it. The words arrived because she had given him something honest and his body — the part that still made decisions about trust and reciprocity, the part he was fairly sure was him — wanted to give something back.

"Yes."

"About the blackout. Not the crash. Everyone knows about the crash. The shoulder, the wrist, the face." He touched the scar on his cheek without thinking about it. "That's just injury. I've been hurt before. The part I haven't told anyone about is the other part."

He looked at the ceiling. It was flat and white and institutional and he focused on the seam where it met the wall because he needed something boring to look at while he said this.

"The blackout lasted thirty seconds. I was running the 200 and at the 140m mark everything dropped out. Not like going dark. Dark is still a thing — your eyes are doing something, just the wrong something. This was — I wasn't anywhere. I didn't have the equipment to be anywhere. No eyes, no ears, no skin, no sense of which way was up. Just absence. No body to be absent in."

Lena was quiet. He could feel the quality of her attention beside him — the specific way she listened, which was the same attention he'd watched leave her face and come back. Attention was something you could lose. He knew that now.

"And then I came back. Floor. Blood in my eyes. Shoulder wrong. Wrist broken. And my legs —" He paused. "My legs were still going. The synthetic muscle. Still firing, still trying to run a sprint pattern. I was lying on the track with my face in my own blood and my legs were running a race I wasn't in."

He heard her breathing change beside him. Shorter. More controlled.

"But here's the thing I can't stop thinking about. During those thirty seconds, my body ran. It ran well. The system decided sprinting was still the correct action and it drove my legs at competitive speed into a concrete wall. And Yusuf showed me the telemetry afterward. My split for that last stretch, before the crash — consistent. Good form. My body was running a solid 200m sprint and I wasn't in it."

"And that was terrifying," Lena said. Not a question.

"Yeah." He could hear his own breathing. A little fast for someone lying still.

"Three years," he said. "Since the rebuild. And most of the time I can pretend the system is like — I don't know, a really good pair of shoes. It helps me run. It doesn't change who's running. But then something like tonight happens, or the blackout, and the pretending stops working. Because shoes don't grab someone while you're not paying attention."

He let the word sit. "Not the void. The void was nothing — literally nothing, there's nothing to be scared of when there's nothing. The terrifying part was that my body did fine without me. It ran the race. It didn't need me there to run. So what's the Kai part for? I'm not being — I don't mean that as some big philosophical thing. I mean it practically. What does the person in the body contribute that the system can't already do on its own?"

He hadn't said this to anyone. He hadn't fully assembled it as a thought before now. It had been a physical thing — a tightness in his chest at three in the morning, a reluctance to run at full effort — and putting words on it didn't make the feeling go away but it gave the feeling an edge he could hold.

"Tonight," she said, after a silence. "Your hand."

He exhaled. "You noticed."

"Your grip changed. On my hip. It was very precise. The kind of precise that isn't — " She paused, searching for the word.

"Isn't me," he said.

"I was going to say 'isn't voluntary.' I don't know if it's you or not. Maybe it's part of you."

"It's not." He was sure about that much. "I didn't decide to do anything with my hand. I was just — touching you. And the system decided it could do it better. And it did. It adjusted my grip so it was optimized. Like I was holding a piece of equipment and the machine corrected my form."

She was quiet for a long time. Their shoulders were still touching. The wind was doing its flat, patient thing against the glass.

"So we're the same," she said. "Our bodies do things without us. Yours does it better than you would. Mine does it somewhere I can't follow."

"Yeah."

"That should make this worse." She said it as an observation. A datum she was evaluating. "Being with someone whose body has the same problem. It should make everything worse."

"Does it?"

She took a long time. He let her. The ceiling was still boring and his wrist was still aching and the woman beside him was present, right now, in a way he could feel, and he wanted her to take as long as she needed.

"No," she said. "It doesn't."

He reached for her hand and found it on the blanket between them. His fingers closed around hers. The touch registered in the usual way — the warmth and pressure first, then the system's precise measurement of her hand's temperature and the force he was exerting and the distribution of contact across both their palms. He held on to the first signal. The rough one. The imprecise one. The one that was his.

"Your hand's doing it again," she said. "The adjusting."

He felt it. His fingers had redistributed around hers, grip micro-correcting to equalize pressure. He hadn't told them to.

"Yeah."

"Don't be sorry."

"I didn't say sorry."

"You were about to." She squeezed his hand. Her grip was strong — a swimmer's grip, tendon and bone that had been pulling through water since she could walk. "It's your hand. Even the parts that aren't yours. It's still your hand."

He didn't know if that was true. But she was holding it, and she was present, and the room was dark. The diagnostic display stayed off. The hallway light made its thin gold bar across the floor. Outside, wind moved against the window with the constancy of altitude, and the mountains were behind the glass somewhere in the dark — enormous, old, not caring about any of this.

His fingers kept making their small corrections against hers. She kept holding on. They didn't sleep.
