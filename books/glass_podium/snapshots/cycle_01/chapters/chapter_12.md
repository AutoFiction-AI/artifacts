# Chapter 12

Yusuf had pushed the conference table against the far wall and arranged chairs in a rough semicircle. It hadn't helped much. Eight people in a room built for six, and nobody looked comfortable.

Kai sat between Lena and a Japanese sprinter named Yuki Tanaka, whose left hand still went numb without warning — a gift from the virus that Meridian's patch hadn't fixed because Yuki hadn't signed. On Lena's other side, two middle-distance runners Kai knew by event but not by name: a woman with close-cropped red hair who ran the 1500, and a South African in his late twenties who ran the 800. Behind them all, in a chair she'd pulled a full meter back from the group, Margot sat with her legs crossed and her arms folded, as though she'd come to audit rather than participate.

Jin-seo Park had the chair nearest the door. Lena had invited her, which had surprised everyone including Jin-seo, who sat holding a paper cup of coffee she'd stopped drinking twenty minutes ago.

Through the west-facing windows, the Bernese Alps stood in flat late-afternoon light. The lower ridges were patchy where snowmelt had started, grey rock showing through the white in irregular blotches. Water ticked off the eaves in an uneven rhythm that Kai had been trying not to listen to since he sat down.

Yusuf looked tired. He'd been working nights in the calibration bay — it showed in the smudge on his glasses, the shirt untucked on one side, the way his Birmingham accent sat heavier than usual, the professional polish thinned by fatigue. He had his laptop open on a stool.

"Right," he said. "So. The short version."

He turned the laptop toward the group. On the screen, a schematic Kai couldn't parse — firmware architecture rendered in color-coded subsystems, the visual language of a field Kai had never learned to read. He stopped trying after three seconds.

"I can build a patch that targets the virus's propagation mechanism. Just the propagation — it stops the thing spreading through the calibration network and neutralizes the cascade that causes blackouts." Yusuf tapped the screen. "It doesn't require full read-write access to your enhancement systems. Doesn't require the data license Meridian's asking for. It works at the firmware level, compartmentalized."

"Works how?" Yuki asked.

"By isolating the communication pathway the virus exploits. Your systems talk to the calibration network during tuning, yeah? The virus piggybacks on that conversation. My patch builds a wall around the pathway. Your systems still function, still calibrate, but the virus can't propagate through the shared infrastructure anymore."

"And the blackouts?"

"Should stop. The blackout cascade originates from the corrupted firmware — if the corruption can't spread, it can't trigger."

"Should," Margot said from the back of the room.

Yusuf looked at her. "Should. Yeah. That's the honest word."

He pulled up a second screen. Performance metrics — reaction times, force output, sensory acuity scores. Kai recognized the format from his own quarterly assessments.

"Costs," Yusuf said. "Three things you need to know. One: this hasn't been tested on a live human system. I've run it in simulation and on isolated hardware, but that's not the same thing. I can't promise it works. I can't promise there aren't side effects I haven't modeled."

The room was quiet. Water ticked off the eaves.

"Two: even if it works perfectly, you're looking at a performance reduction. Ten to fifteen percent. The patch limits some of the optimization pathways your systems use — reaction time, motor efficiency, sensory processing. You'll be slower. Less precise. Less augmented. Still enhanced, but the ceiling comes down."

"Ten to fifteen percent isn't making a final," the redheaded runner said. "That's not a medal conversation. That's a first-round exit."

"Yeah," Yusuf said. "Could be."

"And three?" Kai asked.

Yusuf took his glasses off and cleaned them on his shirt. He always did this when he was buying time — the gesture that meant the engineer was choosing how to say something. Kai had catalogued it across weeks of calibration sessions without really meaning to.

"The patch permanently severs the neural coaching feed interface."

Margot's chair scraped the tile. A small sound, involuntary — her body reacting before her face could decide not to. She caught it, recrossed her legs, and her expression settled into the flat assessment she used in telemetry reviews and debriefs, the one that said she was processing rather than feeling. But the sound had already happened, and Kai had heard it, and probably Lena had too.

The word *permanently* sat in the room. Margot's left hand had gone to her right wrist — to the feed interface, the device she wore like a second pulse. She moved the hand away after a moment, set it on her knee, and the motion was deliberate in a way that told Kai the first gesture hadn't been.

"The feed uses the same bidirectional data pathway the virus does," Yusuf said. "I can't wall off the virus pathway without closing the feed connection. They're not separate channels — they're the same architecture. So if you take my patch, no more real-time coaching feed. Permanently."

He put his glasses back on and looked at Margot. Everyone looked at Margot.

She didn't move. Her arms stayed folded across her chest and her face stayed flat. Whatever was happening behind that face wasn't available to the room.

"OK," she said. "So it's untested, it costs double digits in performance, and it dismantles the coaching infrastructure these athletes have been training with for years. Before the biggest competition of their lives."

"That's what I just said, yeah."

"And your alternative to Meridian's patch — the one that's been tested, that's backed by a full engineering team, that addresses the virus without gutting the athletes' support systems — is this."

"Meridian's patch comes with a contract that signs over every piece of biological data your body's ever generated. Forever. Transferable. They can sell it."

"I know what the contract says. I've read it." Margot uncrossed her arms and leaned forward. "What I'm saying is that your patch is a worse solution for the athletes. More risk, more cost, for the privilege of keeping data that most of them don't even know how to interpret."

"It's their data."

"It's their bodies. And you're proposing to experiment on them."

Lena hadn't spoken. She sat with her hands in her lap, her back very straight — the posture she adopted when she was organizing her thoughts into a sequence she could trust. The neural link port at the base of her skull was visible where her hair was pulled back, a small circle of dark composite flush with the skin.

"How would the patch interact with existing desync symptoms?" she asked.

Yusuf turned to her. His voice shifted when he spoke to Lena — slower, more careful, the register he used during calibration sessions when an athlete was in the rig and needed to trust the process.

"Honestly, I don't know for certain. It should stabilize the desync by interrupting the feedback loop between your neural link and the corrupted network. But desync is cumulative. The changes your system's already made to your neural patterns — those don't reverse just because you wall off the cause."

"So the damage stays."

"The damage to date stays. The progression stops. That's the theory."

"Or the patch locks in whatever state my system is in right now."

"That's also possible. Yeah."

Lena nodded once. Her hands were still in her lap but she'd laced her fingers together, pressing her thumbs against each other in a small rhythmic motion. Kai had learned to read this gesture over the past weeks. She was checking — confirming that her hands were doing what she'd told them to do.

His own wrist ached. The deep ache, the one that lived where the bone had knitted too fast, synthetic grafts accelerating a repair that should have taken eight weeks into three and leaving something not quite right in the architecture. He pressed his thumb into the spot without thinking. A habit now.

"What's Meridian's performance hit?" he asked.

"None," Yusuf said. "Their patch is designed to maintain full optimization."

"Because they keep full access."

"Because they keep full access. Yeah."

The South African runner rubbed the back of his neck. "So the choice is: give up our data and compete at full capacity, or keep our data and compete at eighty-five percent with a patch nobody's ever tried on a living person."

"That's the choice," Yusuf said.

"That's a shit choice."

"Yeah. It is."

Margot stood. Not to leave — she walked to the window and stood with her back to the room, looking at the mountains. The late light caught the feed interface on her left wrist, the wrist-mounted device she used to connect to athletes' neural systems. Personal equipment, not facility-issued. She wore it the way some people wore watches they'd stopped looking at.

"You built this in the calibration bay," she said, not turning around. "On institute time. Using institute resources. Does Petra know?"

Yusuf's expression tightened slightly. "That's not—"

"Does the facility director know?"

"This isn't about facility authorization."

"It's about whether anyone's checked your work." Margot turned. "You built a firmware patch in your spare time. You want to install it in people's nervous systems. And the only quality control is you telling us it ran clean in simulation."

The room shifted. Kai could feel it — the weight of agreement moving toward Margot, not because anyone liked her position but because her points were real. The patch was untested. Yusuf was one engineer. Nobody in this room had the technical literacy to evaluate his work independently.

"She's not wrong," Yuki said quietly. Her left hand was resting on her thigh, the one that sometimes went dead. She pressed it against the muscle as if confirming something.

The South African runner leaned forward in his chair. "Why'd you build this?"

Yusuf looked at him.

"You're the calibration engineer. You could've recommended Meridian's patch, filed your reports, gone home clean. You didn't have to spend your nights building something nobody asked for." He paused. "So why?"

Yusuf's posture changed. Kai watched it happen — the shoulders settling, the spine straightening, the Birmingham receding from his voice as his professional register engaged. The tell. When Yusuf sounded his most composed, he was furthest from honest.

"Because Meridian's solution is architecturally excessive for the problem it solves. The data access they're requiring isn't necessary for—"

"That's the technical answer," the runner said. "I'm asking the other one."

The room waited. Jin-seo's paper cup made a faint sound as her grip tightened on it.

Yusuf looked at the screen on his laptop. At his hands. At the window where Margot was still standing. Then back at the group.

"My sister has Voss-Kessler syndrome."

Nobody moved.

"It's a progressive neural deterioration condition. Rare. No treatment. She's twenty-nine and her speech is already—" He stopped. Started the sentence in a different direction, his voice flatter, the accent almost gone. "The biological data generated by enhancement athletes — your data, the stuff Meridian wants to lock under their license — some of it has direct application to neurological treatment research. Not theoretical. Specific pathways, specific neural mapping data, that could contribute to understanding how Voss-Kessler degrades motor and speech function."

He took a breath that was slightly too controlled.

"If Meridian locks that data under their contract, the research pipeline closes. Their license is irrevocable and transferable. They're not going to share it with academic researchers working on a condition that affects four hundred people worldwide. It's not profitable enough." He looked around the room. "So yeah. I built an alternative that doesn't require giving Meridian permanent ownership of your biological information. And yeah, part of the reason is I don't want them owning the data that might help my sister."

The silence that followed wasn't the one Yusuf had wanted. Kai could see it on his face — the jaw that didn't quite relax after closing, the slight surprise behind his glasses. He had expected the revelation to land as sympathy, or at least understanding.

What it landed as was arithmetic.

The redheaded runner glanced at the woman next to her. The South African was rubbing his chin, his expression shifting from curiosity to something more guarded. Yuki was looking at the laptop screen as though the schematic had changed meaning.

Because the equation had changed. Yusuf wasn't offering them an alternative out of principle. He was offering them an alternative that happened to protect the data he needed. His patch didn't lock information under Meridian's contract — and now they knew exactly why that mattered to him.

Kai watched Yusuf stand there absorbing it — the room recalculating him in real time, the sympathy he'd expected converting into something colder and more practical. The engineer who'd worked nights to build them an option, who'd just told them about his sick sister, and the room's first response was to wonder what his angle was. It wasn't unfair. That was the worst part. It wasn't unfair at all.

---

Jin-seo spoke first.

"I'm sorry about your sister."

She said it plainly — not performed warmth, not corporate condolence. She'd set the cold coffee on the floor by her chair. Her hands were in her lap now, fingers laced, one thumb pressing the heel of her other hand in a small controlled motion. She was thirty-three and had the posture of someone who'd spent her working life in rooms where she was the youngest person and the least authorized to speak.

"Thank you," Yusuf said.

"And I understand why you built it. I do." She paused. Chose her next words carefully — the care of someone who'd learned that in rooms like this, imprecise language cost people things they couldn't get back. "But what you're asking these athletes to do is accept a firmware modification that's never been installed in a living human system, built by one engineer with a personal stake in the outcome, to solve a problem that Meridian has already solved with a tested, validated patch."

"Meridian's patch comes with—"

"I know what it comes with. I've heard every version of the objection at this facility and at four others." Her voice didn't rise. It stayed level, carefully controlled, and Kai noticed how similar that control was to Lena's precision when Lena was holding herself together. "The data license is real. The autonomy cost is real. I'm not pretending those aren't serious. But your alternative also has costs, and one of them is risk. Untested risk. Applied to human beings."

She looked at Lena, then Kai, then the others.

"My brother died from a cascade failure in 2034. Enhancement system, shared network, propagation event — very similar architecture to what's happening here." Her voice stayed level but the muscles around her mouth changed, just slightly — not a tremor, a tightening, something held in place by practice. "He was twenty-six. He was running the 1500 and his legs stopped working at the 800 mark. The rest of his system kept firing. His heart rate hit 240 before the emergency shutdown engaged." She paused. "It didn't engage fast enough."

The room was still. Through the glass, the shadows of the mountains had lengthened across the snowmelt.

"So when someone tells me they've built a fix that hasn't been tested on a live system, and they want to install it in athletes about to compete at the highest level, and their primary motivation turns out to be personal — I can't support that. I won't pretend the risk is acceptable."

"Meridian's patch has its own risks," Yusuf said. His accent was thicker now, the professional register breaking under the pressure of being opposed by someone whose argument was better than his.

"Meridian's patch has been through full clinical validation."

"Clinical validation funded by the company that—" He stopped himself. Whatever he'd been about to say, he pulled it back behind his teeth and swallowed it.

Jin-seo caught the shape of the missing words. "That what?"

"Their patch has been validated. You're right."

"Yusuf. That what?"

"It's not relevant to this conversation."

The room registered the gap — something Yusuf knew and wasn't sharing. Kai watched Margot, who was watching Yusuf with the focus of someone filing information for later use. She'd noticed the gap. She didn't push on it. Pushing would have meant joining Yusuf's side of the argument, even temporarily, and Margot was too precise for that.

"I think it's relevant," Jin-seo said, "if you're asking people to refuse a validated patch in favor of yours."

"I'm not asking anyone to do anything. I'm presenting an option."

"You're not neutral and you've just explained why."

"Nobody in this room is neutral." Yusuf's voice dropped, the Birmingham flooding back. "You're not neutral. You work for Meridian."

"I'm here because Lena invited me."

"You're here because getting holdouts to sign is literally your job."

"My job is making sure nobody else dies the way my brother died."

Her voice cracked on *brother*. A hairline fracture in the composure. She didn't smooth it over or correct for it. She let it sit in the room, and the room sat with it.

Margot came back from the window. She didn't sit down. She stood behind her empty chair with her hands on the backrest.

"Can we talk about what actually happens to these athletes?" she said. "Not the data philosophy. Not the principle. The bodies."

Kai looked at her.

"If they take Yusuf's patch and it fails during competition — what happens?"

Yusuf was quiet for a beat too long.

"Same as competing unpatched. Virus-triggered blackout, potential sensory failure, autonomous system engagement."

"Which could kill someone."

"Yes."

"And if it does work but the performance hit is real — they're at the biggest competition of their careers running at eighty-five percent. For some of them that's the difference between a contract renewal and not having one. For some of them that's—" She looked at Yuki. At the runners. "That's money. That's families. That's whether they keep doing this or go home."

"I know that," Yusuf said.

"Do you? Because you're an engineer. You go home the same either way. Your salary doesn't change based on your reaction time. You're not the one walking out there at eighty-five percent trying to explain to your federation why your qualifying splits fell off a cliff."

"Margot—"

"I'm serious. You've got your reason for building this. Fine. But the costs aren't symmetrical. The athletes carry all the risk. You carry none."

Kai's wrist throbbed. He pressed his thumb into the bad spot and thought about what he was going to do — not about the argument, not about who was right, but about the actual physical question of whether he would let Yusuf install firmware in his spine or sign a document that gave a corporation ownership of his body's information or do neither and walk into the Games with a virus in his hardware that could blank his senses at full sprint. None of the options were good. All of them involved someone else's hands inside the part of him he couldn't verify was still his.

Margot was good. Every point she made was legitimate, every objection grounded in real consequence for real people, and underneath all of it was the thing she would not say: the patch severed the feed, and the feed was the only thing keeping her present in any way that mattered to her, and without it she was a thirty-five-year-old former swimmer with a fifth-place finish and nothing on the other side. Kai saw it. He wasn't sure anyone else did.

Lena spoke. The room turned toward her — not because she'd raised her voice but because she'd been so quiet that when she spoke the contrast was sharp.

"I want to understand the desync interaction better."

"OK," Yusuf said.

"If the patch works as designed, my neural link's current desync pattern becomes the permanent state. The damage stops progressing but doesn't reverse."

"Correct."

"And the desync has already changed how I process language. How I experience continuity." She paused, and the pause had a different quality than hesitation — she was checking, making sure the next sentence was one she'd chosen. "There are gaps. Periods where I'm not present and the system operates without me."

"I know."

"So the person who comes out of your patch is different from the person who went in. Not because of the patch. Because of what the desync already did before the patch stops it."

"That's — yeah. That's accurate."

"And Meridian's patch?"

"Meridian's maintains the full integration. It'd stabilize the network without interrupting the link's optimization pathways. So your desync symptoms might improve as the link reoptimizes around the fixed network. But—"

"But the link would still be making decisions I don't initiate."

"Yes."

Lena's thumbs pressed against each other. The small rhythmic check. Her hands were doing what she'd told them to do. For now.

"So one option locks in the damage and makes me less than I was. And the other might fix the damage but keeps running the system that caused it."

"Yeah," Yusuf said. His voice had gone quiet. "That's it."

Nobody offered Lena a solution. There wasn't one to offer. Kai put his hand on her knee, briefly, and she let it rest there for a moment before shifting forward in her chair, the gesture not a rejection but a returning to herself, to the problem she was sitting inside.

The argument didn't end. It scattered.

The South African runner said he'd think about it, which meant he'd sign Meridian's contract within the week. Kai could hear it in the tone — the man had already made his calculation and was being polite about it. The redheaded runner asked Yusuf technical questions about the isolation architecture, questions Yusuf answered with the careful patience of someone who needed allies and was losing them. Yuki sat with her intermittently dead hand and stared at the firmware schematic on the laptop as though she could learn to read it by force of will.

Jin-seo stood, bent to pick up her cold coffee, looked at it, and dropped it in the bin by the door. She paused beside Lena's chair.

"I'm in room 214 if you want to talk."

Lena nodded. Jin-seo left.

Margot left next. No announcement, no exit line. She picked up her jacket from the chair back and walked out. The feed interface on her wrist caught the hallway light, and then the door closed behind her.

The redheaded runner left. The South African runner shook Yusuf's hand and left. Yuki stayed another minute, flexing her left hand open and closed, open and closed, before she stood and went without saying anything.

Yusuf closed his laptop. He stood in the half-empty room and looked at the chairs arranged in their ragged semicircle, and for a moment his face did something unguarded — not disappointment exactly, but the particular tiredness of someone who had bet on honesty and watched it buy him nothing.

"I'm in the calibration bay tonight," he said. To the room, but he was looking at Lena. "If anyone has questions."

He left. The door closed. Through the glass, the mountains stood in the last of the light, the snowmelt still ticking off the eaves in its uneven rhythm.

"You OK?" Kai asked.

Lena didn't answer immediately. She was looking at her hands in her lap. The thumbs had stopped moving.

"Both options are a different person," she said.

Kai didn't have anything to say to that. He sat with her in the emptying room, thumb pressed into the knot in his wrist where the bone sat wrong, while the light went off the mountains and the water kept ticking off the eaves and the argument hung in the air the way arguments do when nobody wins them — not fading, just settling, becoming part of the room's weight.
