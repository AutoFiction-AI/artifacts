# Chapter 4

The coaching suite smelled like adhesive tape and the faint metallic discharge of processing hardware running warm. Margot arrived fifteen minutes before Tomás's session, which was ten minutes early. She told herself this was professionalism — checking the monitoring displays, running a diagnostic on the feed interface, reviewing his training targets for the day's hurdle and four-hundred-meter blocks. All of that was true. She did do those things.

But she also sat in the coaching rig with the wrist mount already docked, waiting for the signal handshake that wouldn't begin until Tomás arrived and connected on his end, and she watched the green ready-light blink its slow patient rhythm, and her mouth was dry.

The suite was a small room adjacent to the indoor track, separated from the training floor by a glass observation window. Three monitors on the wall showed biometric grids waiting for live data. The chair was padded, adjustable, designed for sessions that could last two hours. It was the most comfortable chair in the Kirchberg Institute, which was a detail nobody except Margot would ever notice or care about.

Tomás came through the door at 9:02, already in his training kit, carrying his hurdle spikes in one hand and a water bottle in the other. The spikes were custom-fitted, Meridian-issued, and he carried them the way Margot had seen other first-Games athletes carry expensive equipment — careful, slightly amazed, like a kid with a birthday present he wasn't sure he deserved. He was smiling. Tomás was always smiling.

"Morning, Coach."

"Morning. You eat?"

"Yeah, eggs and that quinoa stuff from the cafeteria. It's not — how do you say — it's not bad, actually. My mom makes better, but." He shrugged. "She makes better than everything, so."

"Sit down, let me check your calibration readings before we start."

He sat in the athlete's station across from her and she plugged the auxiliary cable from her interface into the port on the back of his training vest. The handshake began: his system and her feed device negotiating data access, confirming neural channel permissions, establishing the one-way sensory pipeline that would let her feel what he felt. The monitors populated with his resting biometrics. Heart rate sixty-two. Core temperature thirty-seven point one. Enhancement subsystem status: all green.

"How's the shoulder?" she asked. He'd bruised his left deltoid two days ago in a shot-put session — a training weight that had shifted in his grip at release, the kind of minor thing that happened when you were learning to integrate three-year-old synthetic grafts with a throwing motion the grafts hadn't been optimized for.

"Good. Little sore when I raise it past here." He demonstrated, lifting his arm to about a hundred thirty degrees. "But it doesn't catch. Just, you know. It aches."

"Don't baby it on the hurdles. Your trail arm needs full extension or your hip rotates and you clear the bar like you're climbing over a fence."

He laughed. "Got it."

"I'm serious. I watched your last session tape. Your trail leg is fine but that arm drops three centimeters on the left side."

"Three centimeters? That's nothing."

"It's three centimeters that turns into a tenth of a second that turns into sixth place. You didn't come here for sixth place."

"No." The smile changed — still there but sharper underneath. Tomás was twenty-one and eager and came from a family in Quito that needed him to be good, and sometimes the need showed through the good nature like bone through skin. "I didn't."

"Ready?"

"Ready."

She initiated the feed.

The world doubled. Her own body stayed in the chair — she could feel the padding against her lower back, her feet on the floor, the suite's controlled twenty-one degrees against her forearms — but layered over it, arriving through the neural feed like a second skin pulled on while the first was still wet, was Tomás's body. His hamstrings, loose and warm from the pre-session stretching. His right quadriceps, where the synthetic muscle graft met natural tissue and the integration seam produced a faint thermal gradient she could feel as a line of cooler pressure running from mid-thigh to the lateral side of the knee. His breathing, deep and regular, the rib cage expanding in a rhythm that pushed against her own breathing like a hand on her sternum. His heart, at sixty-two, the contraction arriving in her chest as a ghost beat that didn't match her own.

He stood. She felt the standing — the load shift through his feet, the engagement of his calves, his core stabilizing around the spinal column and the grafted musculature pulling everything into alignment the way a well-tuned suspension settles under weight. The sensation of his body was not the same as feeling her body. It was translated, slightly delayed, a borrowed proprioception that arrived through the feed's sensory mapping rather than through her own nerves. But it was enough. It was more than enough.

He walked to the indoor track and she watched him through the observation window and felt him through the feed at the same time, the two channels of information overlapping like a conversation where both people talked simultaneously and you somehow followed both. He set up the hurdles. Ten hurdles, standard spacing for the 110m, regulation height because Tomás always trained at competition spec. He bent to adjust the third one and she felt the stretch through his lower back, the way his hamstrings resisted for a half-second before the graft-enhanced tissue released, elastic and massive.

"Pacing targets loaded," she said into the headset mic. "First set, seventy percent effort. I want clean technique, don't worry about the clock."

"Got it."

He positioned himself in the blocks. His heart rate climbed to seventy-eight — anticipatory, normal. Through the feed she felt the balls of his feet pressing into the block pads, the coiled readiness in his quads, the way his enhanced visual system sharpened focus on the first hurdle forty-five meters away. The rubber track surface resolved in his vision into individual granules of texture, and she experienced the resolution as a brightening, a crispness in her own visual field that wasn't real but felt real, the feed translating his sensory enhancement into a version her unenhanced brain could parse.

He pushed out of the blocks.

The acceleration was a compression behind her sternum — not her body's, his, but the feed mapped it onto hers, and for the first three strides she was in two places at once, sitting in a chair and driving forward on synthetic-enhanced legs, the track surface hammering up through feet that were not her feet. His hamstrings loaded with each stride, elastic and enormous, and the first hurdle came up fast. His lead leg drove forward — she felt the quadriceps fire, the hip flexor engage, the brief extraordinary moment where both feet left the ground and his body traveled over the bar in a tight controlled arc, the air rushing past his forearms and the hurdle dropping away beneath him. Trail leg pulled through. Landing. The impact absorbed through his knee and ankle and the immediate reacceleration, three strides to the next hurdle, and his heart rate climbed to a hundred thirty-eight and the feed was beautiful.

That was the word she didn't use with anyone. Beautiful. The feed at full intensity — a young athlete's body working near the edge of its engineering — was the most beautiful thing Margot had experienced in years. More beautiful than her own swimming had ever been, and she'd had good days in the pool, days when the stroke was right and the water felt like it was cooperating. But even her best days had been limited by her own body, by the specific disappointments of Margot Voss's musculature and cardiovascular ceiling and the gap between what she wanted and what she could produce. Through the feed, there was no gap. Through the feed, she was a body that could do the thing.

Second hurdle. Third. Fourth. His form was excellent — Tomás had natural talent, three years of enhancement optimization, and the disciplined eagerness of someone who couldn't afford to waste this. His heart rate at a hundred forty-six. The pacing target she'd loaded was set for a hundred fifty peak.

She touched the adjustment on her console. Moved the target up four beats. A hundred fifty-four.

It was such a small number. She'd done this before — not every session, but enough that the pattern lived in the telemetry data if anyone bothered to look for it. A few beats above the prescribed margin during Hana Petrov's sprint conditioning last week. Same adjustment for Marcus Adler's gymnastics session the Tuesday before that. The coaching interface logged pacing changes in a sub-table that the standard medical summary reports didn't pull from. You had to query the raw session data directly. You had to go looking.

Nobody went looking. Margot knew this because she'd checked.

Fifth hurdle. His visual field sharpened as the bar approached, the enhanced optics pre-calculating clearance trajectory, and through the feed she experienced the calculation as a spatial compression, as though the air between his chest and the hurdle thickened and thinned in a rhythmic pulse timed to his stride. His heart rate hit a hundred fifty-one. Climbing. She felt the cardiovascular load through the feed as a warmth spreading from the center of her chest outward — not her warmth, his, but the feed didn't distinguish, it just delivered the sensation and let her nervous system mistake it for her own. The warmth was effort. Real effort. The zone past the enhancement system's optimization routines where the athlete's biology was working harder than the engineering had budgeted for, where the body had to produce something the system couldn't supply, and that raw surplus — that was the high. Not the efficiency. The inefficiency. The part that was Tomás, not Meridian's design.

Sixth hurdle. Clean. His breathing came harder and she felt it in her own diaphragm, the secondary respiration riding alongside her own like a harmony she couldn't stop hearing. A hundred fifty-four now. His body meeting the target she'd set, his cardiovascular system pushing past the optimization ceiling, his synthetic quads demanding more oxygen than the efficiency models allocated, and the deficit showed up as a beautiful, stupid, human strain.

She could have pulled the targets back.

Seventh hurdle. His heart rate at a hundred fifty-eight. His lead leg drove forward but the timing was off by a fraction she felt before she saw — the cardiovascular load pulling resources from his motor coordination, the fine-grained muscle timing that the enhancement system managed degrading under the strain she had built. Through the feed, it arrived as a wrongness in the spatial rhythm, the hurdle bar approaching at a slightly different angle than his body expected, and then through the observation window she watched it happen: his trail leg catching the bar at shin height. Not a glancing touch. The bar was at regulation competition height — Tomás liked full spec — and his shin struck it at an angle that torqued his knee as his momentum carried him forward and down.

He went down. Hands first, palms slapping the rubber. Then his knees. Then his right shoulder and the side of his face sliding on the track surface, the friction-burn of rubber against skin arriving through the feed as a hot scraping pressure across Margot's own cheek and shoulder. His heart rate spiked to a hundred sixty-one. His body, horizontal and not understanding why, sent a cascade of proprioceptive signals through the feed — where is the ground, where is up, why has forward stopped — and Margot sat in the coaching rig and felt all of it, the confusion of a borrowed body that she had pushed past its margins and then watched fall.

She disconnected. Her fingers found the release on the wrist mount and the doubled world collapsed to one — her body, in the chair, her own heart rate at ninety-one from the sympathetic feed response, her own palms sweating against the armrests, the observation window showing Tomás getting up off the track and examining his knee.

"Tomás. You good?"

"Yeah." His voice in the headset, breathless. "Yeah, I'm all right. Knee's a little — I think I tweaked it on the way down, but it's not bad. What happened?"

She had the answer ready. "Your pacing fell off on the last three hurdles. Cardiovascular response lagged — that's altitude. You've been here, what, twelve days? The thin air catches up once you push past seventy percent. I should've started you at sixty-five. My fault."

A small, specific, plausible lie. Altitude adjustment was the all-purpose explanation at twenty-one hundred meters, and she deployed it with the fluency of someone who'd used it before.

"Oh." He tested the knee, standing with his weight on it, shifting. Winced, but stayed upright. "So we skip the four hundred today?"

"Yeah, let's call it. I'll have the training team look at that knee. We can reschedule the four hundred for Thursday if it checks out."

"Okay." He sat on the track apron, drank from his water bottle, and pulled off his spikes with the same care he'd carried them in with. "Coach? It's weird, I felt fine until the sixth hurdle and then it was like my legs got heavy. Is that the altitude too?"

"Yeah. That's what it does. Your system's optimizing for sea-level oxygen saturation and the air up here doesn't cooperate. Takes about three weeks to fully adjust."

He nodded. "Thanks for catching it."

Margot looked at him. Twenty-one, sitting on the track apron with a tweaked knee and a rubber burn on his right cheek, thanking her for catching the thing she'd caused. His trust in her was total and unexamined and rested on the simple fact that she was his coach and coaches kept you safe. He had no reason to think otherwise. He wouldn't have a reason for months.

"Go get that looked at," she said. "And eat something. You're burning through calories at this altitude."

"Yeah, I will. See you Thursday?"

"Thursday."

He gathered his spikes and left. The facility trainer came in ten minutes later, reviewed the session summary on the monitor — the one that showed the heart-rate spike but not the manual target adjustment — and noted the altitude explanation in the incident log without comment. Minor knee sprain. Two days off hurdle work. Light conditioning permitted.

Margot sat in the rig after everyone left. The coaching suite was quiet. The monitors had dropped to standby. The green ready-light on her docked interface blinked its slow rhythm again, waiting for the next session, patient and indifferent. Her wrist, where the mount pressed against skin, was warm from the connection. The device held a residual charge for about thirty minutes after disconnection, and she could feel it — a faint pulse against the tendons of her inner wrist, like a second heartbeat winding down.

She filed her session report. Noted the heart-rate anomaly. Attributed it to altitude acclimatization, incomplete cardiovascular adjustment, and recommended a reduced intensity threshold for his next two sessions. The report was thorough, professional, and accurate except for the part that mattered.

---

Her quarters were on the third floor of the residential wing, facing the mountain. At five in the afternoon in late January the view through the window was a black ridge against a sky not much lighter, and the glass radiated cold.

She locked the door. Took off her shoes, her coaching jacket, her track pants. Stood in a t-shirt and underwear in the middle of the room. Her body felt like a house she'd come back to after staying somewhere better.

She stretched. Hamstrings, quads, hip flexors — the old competitive routine, which she'd never stopped doing even though the competition was eight years gone. The body kept its habits after the reason for them left. Her hamstrings were tight in the way they'd been tight for years now, the specific tightness of tissue that remembered what it used to be asked to do and resented the demotion. When she'd been swimming six hours a day they'd been responsive and warm, and stretching had been a conversation between her and the muscles, a negotiation before the real work. Now it was maintenance. She was maintaining a body for no particular purpose.

Thirty minutes ago she'd felt Tomás's hamstrings through the feed. Elastic, dense, engineered for the kind of explosive power her body had never been built for even at its best. Through the feed, his body had been a machine she was permitted to ride. Hers was the parking lot she came back to afterward.

She went into the bathroom. The mirror was full-length — institutional bathrooms at training facilities always had full-length mirrors, because the athletes needed to inspect their bodies the way mechanics inspected engines, and nobody had swapped the mirror for a smaller one in the coaching staff quarters. She looked at herself. Five-eight, a hundred fifty-two pounds, which was fourteen more than her competitive weight. The shoulders were still broad from the swimming years but the definition had softened into a mass that moved differently when she raised her arms. Her stomach was flat enough if she stood straight. Her thighs were thick in a way that had been functional at twenty-seven and was just mass at thirty-five.

She was being cruel to herself and she knew it. There was nothing wrong with her body. It was healthy, it worked, it carried her from room to room and held her upright and processed food and sleep and oxygen without complaint. But she worked in a building full of engineered bodies — Tomás's twenty-one-year-old decathlon build, Kai Brennan's sprinter architecture with its inhuman bilateral symmetry, the swimmers with their optimized shoulder girdles and lung capacity, all of them projects, all of them works in progress toward something their bodies were designed to become — and she was the only person in the building whose body wasn't becoming anything. Hers had arrived at its destination and the destination was this.

She sat on the edge of the bathtub. The porcelain was cold through the cotton of her underwear, and she registered the cold the way she registered everything in her own body now — as fact, without texture. Through the feed, cold had been information. Temperature differentials mapped to nerve response, the enhancement system cataloguing sensation for future optimization. Through her own skin, cold was just cold.

She pressed her thumb against the feed mount on her left wrist. The residual charge had faded to almost nothing, the faint pulse barely detectable, and the skin beneath the mount was slightly indented from the docking pressure. She pressed until the indentation whitened and began to ache.

The comedown was always the same. Not dramatic. No shaking, no sweating, nothing a medical scan would flag. Just a flatness. A subtraction. Through the feed she had been a body at full intensity — a cardiovascular system at its working limit, a visual field sharpened by optics she didn't have, hamstrings that could launch a hundred-eighty-pound body over a meter-high bar at twenty-eight kilometers per hour. And now she was back in her own nervous system, and her own nervous system offered her the cold of porcelain, the tightness of her hamstrings, and the reflection in the mirror that she could see from the bathtub's edge, half her body visible in the glass, looking like exactly what it was.

She wanted to plug in again. Not the next scheduled session — now. Walk down to the track, find whoever was still training, jack into their feed and feel anything other than this. She wanted it the way she'd wanted the wall at the end of the last lap in her swimming years, the specific desire for the race to end so the pain would stop but also for the pain to never stop because the pain was the only thing that confirmed she was in it, she was competing, she was real.

She didn't call it addiction. She called it immersive coaching methodology. She called it the approach that produced measurable results in athlete performance metrics, which it did — the athletes she worked with improved their times, their form, their competitive readiness, and if she nudged their heart rates past the prescribed margins during sessions, that was a training judgment about optimal intensity, and training judgments were her job. She was good at her job. She had the data to prove it.

The diagnostic display on the wall of her quarters showed her biometrics — not enhanced, but all Kirchberg staff wore basic monitoring patches as facility protocol. Heart rate sixty-six. Core temperature normal. Blood pressure normal. Everything normal. Thoroughly, completely, miserably normal.

She showered. The hot water was hot water. Through the feed, the hot water on Tomás's skin would have arrived as a registered thermal event — temperature differential logged, nerve response catalogued, the sensation annotated by the enhancement system for future calibration. Through her own skin, it was just warm and then it was done and she turned the tap off and stood in the steam looking at the tiles.

She got dressed. Sat at her desk. Opened her schedule for the week. Hana Petrov at seven a.m. tomorrow — sprint conditioning. Two afternoon sessions Thursday with the gymnasts. Friday, Kai Brennan had requested a neural coaching evaluation for his proprioception issues, which she'd agreed to because Kai's system was complex and the feed experience during a sprinter's acceleration was the closest thing to what she'd felt during Tomás's hurdles — that same explosive, compressive, total engagement of a body built for speed.

She closed the schedule. She wrote up the incident report for Tomás's fall. Minor sprain, right knee. Contributing factor: altitude adjustment, incomplete cardiovascular acclimatization. Recommendation: two days off hurdle work, light conditioning permitted. She filed it. It joined the other session reports in the coaching database — Hana's sprint sessions, Marcus's conditioning blocks, all of them with their pacing adjustments logged in the sub-table that the summary view didn't surface. The data was there. It sat in the system, timestamped, tagged with her coaching ID, linked to the target changes she'd entered on her console. A clear, legible pattern for anyone who queried the raw feed logs rather than the medical summaries.

Nobody queried the raw feed logs. That was the thing about institutional data — there was always more of it than anyone had time to review, and the systems were designed to surface the important numbers and bury the rest. The important numbers were the athlete outcomes: times, performance metrics, injury rates. The coaching adjustments were infrastructure. Background noise. There unless you knew to look.

Margot knew where the data lived and she knew nobody was looking and she filed her report and closed her laptop.

Through the window, the last light had gone from the ridge. The mountains were a black absence against a sky nearly as dark, and inside the facility the artificial lighting maintained its constant warmth — the same temperature at five a.m. and five p.m. and midnight. She could still feel the seventh hurdle in her forearms. Not the real impact — the feed's translation of it. Tomás's palms hitting the rubber, the jolt traveling up through his wrists to his elbows, the hot scrape of track surface against skin, all of it mapped onto her motor memory and stored as a sensation that didn't belong to her but that her body kept replaying anyway, the way a song stuck in your head wasn't your song but your brain wouldn't let go of it.

In two days the ghost sensation would fade. In two days she'd be plugged into Hana or one of the gymnasts, and a newer borrowed experience would overwrite this one, and the cycle would continue because the cycle was the job, the job was the cycle, and Margot Voss did not have a problem. She had a methodology.

She checked her email. Nothing urgent. She looked at her reflection in the dark window — just an outline, a shape without detail, which was better than the bathroom mirror. She turned off the desk lamp and sat in the near-dark, the only light the faint glow of the biometric display on the wall and the green standby indicators on the monitoring equipment down the hall, visible through the gap beneath her door.

Her wrist mount sat on the desk where she'd placed it after the shower. She picked it up. Turned it over. The docking contacts were still slightly warm. She pressed the mount against her left wrist, not connected to anything, just the familiar pressure of the device against her skin, and she held it there until the warmth transferred from the metal to her pulse point, and then she put it down and went to dinner.
