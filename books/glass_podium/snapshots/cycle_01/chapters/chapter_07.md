# Chapter 7

The brace was off. Petra had cleared him to remove it for short periods, and the bone had knitted -- faster than any break had a right to mend. Not healed, exactly. Knitting implied the body's own patient labor, tissue finding tissue on its own schedule. This was different. The synthetic grafts had driven the bone repair forward on a timeline that had nothing to do with his pain tolerance or his patience, and the wrist had closed itself up six weeks ahead of the curve. The itch was deep inside, below the skin where the brace had rubbed raw, down in the bone itself where new tissue met old -- a wrongness that wasn't pain but speed, his body rebuilding on a schedule he didn't set.

Kai sat on the edge of his bed and put his left hand flat on the mattress.

The experiments had started by accident three days ago. He'd been reaching for a glass of water and caught the movement early -- his hand arriving at the glass a fraction ahead of his decision to reach. Normally he let these moments pass, the way you let your hand find a light switch in a dark room without thinking about how it got there. But this time he'd frozen with the glass in his fingers and tried to feel inside the gap. It was there -- not visible, not painful, just a tiny anticipatory firing in the rebuilt forearm muscle, the synthetic tissue queuing the motion before his conscious intent arrived. The system reading his mind and jumping the queue.

He'd started testing it. Index finger. He told himself to lift it, then waited. Felt for the flutter -- there, a gathering in the synthetic muscle, below the threshold of anything anyone watching would notice. He pressed the finger flat against the mattress. The system pushed. He held. One beat. Two beats. The gathering stalled against his resistance.

The finger lifted when he let it.

It was nothing. A fraction of a second of conscious override, invisible to anyone else, barely perceptible to him. But he'd been doing it every evening since, small repetitions with the same hand, and the guesses were getting less like guesses. He was starting to feel where the system's timing lived in the rebuilt muscle -- not as data, not as numbers on a calibration screen, but as sensation. A shape he could press against.

His shoulder clicked as he reached for the wrist brace. The joint had been tracking smoother since the dislocation healed, clicking into place with a precision that felt engineered rather than organic. His body was repairing itself into something more efficient than the version that had been injured. The scrapes on his cheek and brow had closed into thin pink lines, fading faster than they should have, the skin knitting itself shut with the same accelerated urgency that had mended the bone. He wasn't healing. He was being optimized around the damage.

He put the brace back on and went to dinner.

---

The cafeteria was half-empty at seven-thirty. Most of the athletes ate at six, their days partitioned into training blocks and recovery windows that left no room for eating late. The stragglers were support staff finishing shifts, a pair of physiotherapists arguing about ice bath protocols at a corner table, and Kai.

He took a tray through the line. Chicken breast, rice, steamed broccoli that had been in the warmer long enough to go soft. A cup of coffee that tasted like it had been brewed hours ago and kept alive through institutional persistence. The windows along the far wall showed the mountains as dark cutouts against a sky that was holding its last blue -- that deep alpine color February evenings produced when the days were short enough that dusk seemed to start at three.

Lena was sitting alone at one of the long tables near the windows. She had a plate of pasta half-finished and a book open beside her tray, though she wasn't reading it. She was looking at the dark shapes of the mountains through the glass.

Kai set his tray down across from her. "Hey."

"Hey." She shifted the book closer to her plate, making room. She didn't close it.

"What's that?"

She glanced at the cover as though she'd forgotten what she'd brought. "A novel. In German. I keep starting it."

"Good?"

"I don't know yet. I keep reading the same pages."

He didn't ask why. He could guess -- the same thing that had been eating her time since before his crash, the cognitive blankness that swallowed minutes without leaving any trace of its passage. She wouldn't want him to name it here, under the cafeteria's fluorescent lights. He unwrapped his fork and started on the chicken.

They ate without talking for a while. The cafeteria's background noise was enough to fill the silence comfortably -- trays being cleared somewhere in the kitchen, the physiotherapists still going about ice baths, the steady thrum of the heating system working against the alpine cold on the other side of the glass. Kai's shoulder clicked when he raised the fork. He'd mostly stopped noticing it except when the room was quiet enough for the sound to travel.

"How's the wrist?" Lena asked.

"Getting there." He held it up, the brace bulky and medical-looking under the lights. "Petra says I can start grip exercises next week. Which is her way of telling me to stop doing them now."

"You were already doing them."

"I was doing something. I don't know if exercises is the right word." He set the hand back on the table. "It's weird. The bone's basically done. Structurally sound. But it doesn't feel like my wrist healed. It feels like someone fast-forwarded through the healing and skipped the part where your body catches up to the repair."

Lena nodded. She pushed a piece of penne around her plate without eating it. "My mother broke her arm when I was twelve. Skiing. She wore the cast for eight weeks and complained about it every day. I remember thinking it seemed like such a long time for something to mend."

"It is a long time. That's sort of the point."

"I know. I didn't understand that then."

Kai drank his coffee. It was worse than he'd expected, which was saying something given that his expectations for institutional coffee were already on the floor. "Where was that? The skiing."

"Engelberg. We went every February when I was young." She paused, and then corrected herself. "My parents went. I went to training camp."

"Every February?"

"Every school holiday. The program had training blocks through the year. Summer was Tenero, for the outdoor pool. Winter was altitude work here or in Austria. Spring was a facility outside Munich for strength testing." She listed these the way someone describes a transit route -- factual, organized, without complaint or nostalgia. "I was home for ten days at Christmas and two weeks in August."

"Home being where?"

"Zug. My parents' apartment. But I'd lived at training facilities since I was eight, so." She made a small motion with her hand that seemed to close a door on the rest of the sentence. "It's just how it was."

"Sure." He looked at her across the institutional table. The fluorescent lighting did what fluorescent lighting always did -- washed the color out of everything, made the trays and the food and the faces all look like they existed on the same flat plane. He could see the neural link port at the base of her skull where her hair was pulled up. A small disc, almost but not quite the color of her skin. "Did you like it? The program?"

She considered this. He'd noticed she did that -- took a question and turned it before answering, not from evasion but from a genuine need to get the answer right.

"I liked swimming," she said. "The program was how I swam. So, yes. But the question doesn't quite fit, because you're asking as if there was an alternative."

"There wasn't?"

"My parents signed the developmental contract before I was born. Embryo-stage selection, then the link at four, then calibration every six months. By the time I was old enough to have opinions about it, the program was the structure of my life. My body was the program's body." She said this evenly. It wasn't a confession and it wasn't a complaint. It was a description of how things had worked. "I don't know what I'd be without it, because there isn't a version of me that exists without it."

"That's different from what I had," Kai said.

"I know."

"I had twenty-seven years of knowing what my body felt like before they rebuilt it. I've got a whole baseline sitting there. I know what running felt like when it was just me, because I spent a decade doing it. I can feel the gap." He turned his coffee cup on the table a quarter rotation, no reason. "You don't have that."

"No."

"So when your system does something you didn't choose -- when the link makes a decision before you make it -- you can't even tell if that's new, or if it's been happening your whole life and you're only noticing now."

Lena looked at him. "That's exactly it."

"Sorry. I'm not trying to --"

"No, it's fine. You're right. I don't know what my baseline is. Maybe I've never had one." She set her fork down. "When I was small, five or six, I had calibration sessions twice a week. I'd lie in the rig and the technician would say, can you feel this, can you feel that. And I always said yes. But I don't know now if what I was feeling was the calibration or my own nervous system reacting to the calibration. I can't separate them. There isn't a way to untangle it."

"Jesus."

"It's just how it is." She said it again, that phrase. Kai heard the work in it -- the practiced acceptance that wasn't quite acceptance but was the nearest thing she'd found.

He didn't try to reframe it or find the usable lesson. He ate his broccoli. It was overcooked the way institutional broccoli always was, the stems collapsing under his teeth into a flat green mush.

"Where's your Lafayette?" he asked after a minute. Then, seeing her confusion: "Sorry. I mean -- I'm from Lafayette, Indiana. When I think about home, I've got this specific picture. The fields behind my parents' house. Flat soybean fields that go to the horizon with nothing in between. Summer storms you could watch building for hours. These big thunderheads stacking up out of nothing, and you'd see the wall of rain coming from fifty miles away, this dark curtain dragging across the fields. And the high school track where I first ran. Nothing special. Four hundred meters of cracked rubber behind a school that smelled like floor wax and had water stains on the ceiling tiles. That's my home. That's what I miss when I miss something."

"I don't think I have a Lafayette," Lena said.

"Nothing?"

She thought about it. "The pool in Zug. The old facility, before they renovated. It had windows on the east wall, and in the mornings the light came through the water and made patterns on the tiles at the bottom. I liked that." She paused. "But that isn't homesickness. That's just remembering a pool."

"Maybe that's the same thing."

"It's not. You miss the fields and the storms and the specific smell of your school. You miss a place that existed before you. I miss a room in a facility that was built for my training program." She turned her water glass on the table. "Those aren't the same."

She was right, and Kai didn't have a way to argue her out of it without minimizing what she was telling him. So he didn't. He finished his rice. The cafeteria had emptied further while they'd been talking, the physiotherapists gone, a janitor beginning to mop the far end of the floor.

"My mom still watches my old race footage," he said. "The pre-enhancement stuff. She records herself reacting to it and sends me the videos. It's a lot."

"Does she like the old races better?"

"She says she can tell the difference. She says I used to lean differently out of the blocks. Which is true -- the grafts changed my push-off angle by about three degrees. But I don't think she's analyzing biomechanics. I think she's looking at her son versus whatever she thinks I am now."

"What does she think you are?"

He shrugged. The shoulder clicked. "Fixed. She uses that word. 'After they fixed you.' Like I was broken and then someone repaired me and now I'm finished. Which -- I was broken. Spine compressed, both knees gone. I couldn't make it to the mailbox without my legs going out. But 'fixed' implies there's an end point, and there isn't. The system keeps adjusting. I'm still being fixed."

Lena picked up her coffee and drank it without expression. It was the same bad institutional stuff from the same machine, and she consumed it the way she consumed everything the facility provided -- without complaint, without pleasure, as a fact of the environment.

"My parents use the word 'complete,'" she said. "The program made me complete. That's worse, I think."

"Yeah," Kai said. "That's worse."

Someone at a nearby table was having a loud conversation about lactate thresholds, the numbers floating across the room without landing on anything. Kai finished his chicken. Lena closed her book and stacked it on her tray.

"Do you want to walk?" she said.

"Outside? It's freezing."

"I know."

"Yeah. OK."

---

The cold hit them like a wall. They'd grabbed jackets from the row of hooks by the cafeteria's side door -- the heavy insulated ones the facility kept for anyone moving between buildings in winter -- and even through the padding the air found every gap. Minus twelve, maybe colder. Lena felt it in her sinuses first, a sharp chemical cleanliness that the building's recirculated air never achieved.

The path from the cafeteria to the residential wing was cleared and salted, but beyond the buildings the snow was deep, banked into drifts that the maintenance crew had pushed to the margins with their plows. The sky was black and enormous. The stars came through in a density that the facility's light pollution couldn't entirely wash out, though the sodium glow from the training halls yellowed the nearest snow and faded to nothing within a hundred meters.

They walked past the residential wing and onto the access road that led down toward the valley. The road was plowed but icy in stretches, and Lena watched her footing with the automatic caution of someone who'd been trained since childhood to protect her body from unnecessary injury. The mountains were shapes at this hour rather than scenery -- massive dark presences that blocked the stars where they met the horizon, their ridgelines rough and irregular against the sky.

Kai walked with his hands shoved into his jacket pockets, the wrist brace making his left side bulge. His breath made small clouds that the cold tore apart before they traveled a meter.

"I always forget how many stars there are up here," he said.

"You can see the Milky Way in summer. When it's clear."

"I haven't been here in summer."

"It's different. The fields around the facility go green and there are wildflowers on the lower slopes. The snow goes except on the peaks." She heard herself describing the landscape the way she'd described the pool in Zug -- as a feature of the institute's environment, framed by what training season it accompanied. The mountains had been here before the facility. Before the access road, before anyone decided this altitude was useful for calibrating enhanced athletes. They would be here after.

They reached a point where the road curved and the view opened -- the valley below, dark peaks across the gap, the town of Meiringen visible as a scatter of warm lights against the black valley floor. The air smelled like snow and pine resin and cold stone.

"Can I ask you something?" Kai said.

"Yes."

"When did you first notice the desync?"

She'd answered this before -- for Margot, for the diagnostics, for herself in the quiet inventory she ran each night in bed. But Kai wasn't asking for data.

"In October. Maybe November. I was in the pool and I finished a length and I couldn't remember the turn. Not like forgetting -- it was more like the turn hadn't happened for me. The movement was in the telemetry, in my muscle memory, but it wasn't in my experience."

"Was that scary?"

"I don't know what it was. It was like asking whether it's scary to blink and miss a second. You don't miss it. You arrive at the next moment without the transition."

"But you know it happened."

"After. From the data. There's a gap in what I experienced and the telemetry fills it in." She folded her arms tight against her body. The cold was getting into her hands. "The part that frightens me is that the gaps swim better than I do. The desync sections in my data -- my stroke rate is more consistent, my timing more efficient. When I'm not there, the system performs better."

Kai was quiet. The cold pressed against both of them, and she could hear his breathing -- the slight shallowness the altitude imposed on everyone, even after weeks of acclimatization.

"That's rough," he said.

"Yes."

"I don't have anything useful to say about that. I just -- yeah."

She appreciated that he didn't try to reframe it. Didn't tell her the data might be wrong, or that efficiency wasn't the same as better, or any of the reassurances that would have required her to perform gratitude. He let the fact sit in the frozen air between them, ugly and specific and hers. A car was moving on the valley road below, its headlights tracing the curves along the river.

"My mother sent me a care package last week," Lena said. "Chocolate from the shop in Zug, and socks, and a letter. The letter said she was proud of how I'm handling things. I don't know what she thinks I'm handling. She doesn't know about the desync. She just knows there was a virus and the facility went to restricted operations for a while."

"Parents fill in the blanks."

"My mother fills them in with optimism. She's very Swiss about problems. Everything will be organized and then it will be resolved."

Kai laughed -- a brief, real sound that the cold compressed into something small. "My mom fills them in with worst-case scenarios and prayer. She lit a candle at St. Mary's when I called about the crash. I said, Mom, the wrist is already healing. She said, 'The candle isn't for the wrist, Kai.'"

"What was it for?"

"She didn't say. I think she was praying that whatever comes out the other side of all this is still her son."

The wind picked up, coming off the ridge to the north. It carried a fine grit of ice crystals that stung where they hit exposed skin. Lena turned her face from it and Kai did the same, which put them both facing the facility -- the cluster of buildings lit up against the mountainside, warm and institutional and sealed against everything outside. From this distance, in the dark, the whole complex looked small. Concrete and glass stuck to the side of a mountain that had no use for it.

They stood for a while without talking. The stars were thick overhead, the Milky Way a faint smear across the center of the sky, barely visible through the facility's residual glow. The air was sharp enough to hurt when she breathed deeply, and the silence had a solidity to it that the facility's constant mechanical hum never allowed -- out here, the quiet was the mountain's quiet, the kind made by distance and cold and the absence of machinery.

Kai pulled his left hand from his pocket and flexed it. The brace creaked in the cold. Lena watched him open and close his fingers -- a small, deliberate movement that was neither stretching nor fidgeting. He was doing something specific with the hand, his attention focused on it in a way that was different from the casual gestures of the rest of the evening.

"Your hand," she said.

He looked at her. "Sorry. Just -- it does this thing."

"What thing?"

He was quiet for a moment, and she could see him reaching for the right words -- not the technical version, not the dismissive one. "Some of the muscle in there is synthetic. From the reconstruction. And the synthetic stuff has its own timing -- it fires a little before I tell it to. So I've been trying to catch it. Feel the difference between when it starts and when I start. Hold against it for a beat."

"Can you?"

"A little. Maybe. It's like trying to hear one instrument in an orchestra when you don't know what that instrument sounds like by itself."

Lena looked at his hand. In the starlight, with the brace and the cold stiffening his movements, there was nothing visible -- no difference between a hand gripping under its owner's command and a hand gripping under the system's. The contest was entirely internal, entirely his. But she understood the impulse. She'd spent years lying in calibration rigs trying to feel where she ended and the link began.

"I should try that," she said.

"What?"

"Listening for the system's timing. In the pool. If I could feel when the link is about to take over, before it completes --" She stopped. "I don't know. It's probably different. The link is in my head, not in my hand."

"Probably. But the idea might be the same. There's a gap between you deciding and the system deciding, and the gap has a shape. You can feel the edges of it if you know what to listen for."

She turned this over the way she turned most things -- carefully, from several angles, not committing until she'd checked it against what she already knew. It was a useful way to think about the problem. Not as a malfunction to diagnose but as a boundary to map. She didn't know if it applied to the desync, which worked in her cognition rather than her muscles, in the space between thoughts rather than between movements. But the principle -- that the system's timing was a separate thing from her timing, and that the difference might be something she could learn to perceive -- was something she could hold.

"The gap has a shape," she repeated. "I'll think about that."

"Yeah, well. I've had a lot of time in bed with nothing to do but stare at my hand."

The wind came again, harder. The cold had passed through bracing into something that simply hurt, the kind that went through insulated fabric and settled in the joints. Lena's fingers ached inside her pockets and the skin across her cheekbones was numb and drawn tight.

"Now we should go in," she said.

"Yeah."

They turned and walked back along the access road, their boots crunching on the salted ice. The facility grew as they approached, its windows bright squares of warm light, the sound of the heating system becoming audible as they closed the distance -- that low mechanical hum that was the building's version of breathing, constant and unnoticed until you'd spent twenty minutes in genuine silence. At the side door, Kai pulled the handle and warm air poured out around them, thick and almost sweet after the thin cold.

Lena stepped inside. The corridor was empty and fluorescent-lit. Linoleum floor, the smell of cleaning solution and residual cafeteria grease. She pulled off the heavy jacket and hung it on its hook. Her hands were red and stiff, slow to close.

"Thanks for the walk," she said.

"Thanks for freezing with me."

She smiled. It was small and it was real, and the hallway's flat light made it look ordinary -- the same light that made everything in the facility look ordinary, functional, evenly observed. They stood for a moment at the point where the residential wing branched, her floor to the left, his to the right. The wind was audible on the other side of the door, a muffled pressure against the building's sealed walls. Neither of them moved toward their separate corridors. It was the natural pause of two people who'd been together in the cold and weren't yet ready to go back to being separately warm.

"Good night, Kai."

"Night."

She went left. He went right. The facility hummed around them, sealed and heated and running its systems through the long Alpine dark.
