# Chapter 14

Tomas counted his steps the way his first coach had taught him — *uno, dos, tres* — the rhythm locked in before his body had ever been touched by engineers, back when counting was the only technology available to a thirteen-year-old on a dirt track outside Quito. Three years of neural integration hadn't erased it. The enhancement handled stride frequency to the hundredth of a second now, smoothed his approach pattern into something that felt less like running and more like a machine discovering what running was for, but the counting stayed. His one piece of untouched equipment.

The indoor track smelled like hot rubber and recycled air. Mid-morning. A group session — four athletes spread across the facility's straight and curve. Kai was doing light sprint work on the far lane, his stride still favoring the shoulder that had healed wrong after the blackout crash five weeks ago. Two other athletes Tomas knew only by event — a pole vaulter from the Dutch program, a middle-distance runner whose name he kept forgetting and felt bad about — occupied the curve. The high ceilings swallowed the sound of their footfalls and returned it as a faint percussion, a dozen rubber impacts overlapping into something that was almost rhythm.

Tomas had called his mother last night. She'd wanted to know about the weather, so he'd described the snowmelt off the peaks — how the meltwater ran in channels beside the road down to Meiringen, how the Alps looked different now that the ice was pulling back, exposing gray rock underneath that had been hidden all winter. His sister Valeria had grabbed the phone to announce she'd made the regional soccer team, and he'd promised to video-call her first match. His brother Diego, who was nine and not interested in sports, had shouted something about a math test from the other room. Tomas hadn't caught the words. He'd told his mother the food was fine and the training was going well and he loved her, and she'd said *cuídate, mi amor*, and he'd hung up and taped his lead ankle the way he always did — three wraps, tight, the tape pulling at the fine hair above the joint.

The ankle tape was still there now, under his compression sock, a small pressure he could feel with every stride.

Fourth hurdle. Clean. His lead leg snapped forward and the trail leg swept through with the delayed precision that still surprised him sometimes — the enhancement smoothing his clearance into something aerodynamically ideal, a pattern his body hadn't been able to produce before the grafts, a shape the air seemed to have already agreed to let him through. He loved this part. Not the way the senior athletes loved it, which was not at all, which was resigned tolerance of a body that performed without being asked. Tomas still felt wonder at the intersection of his effort and the machine's. Three years hadn't dulled that. The hurdle behind him rattled faintly on its base from the wind of his passage.

Fifth. Clean. The approach was good, his stride frequency settled, his arms pumping in the tight pattern Margot had drilled into him. He could feel her presence in the feed — a small warmth at the base of his skull, a faint sense of being watched from inside. Margot monitored his cardiovascular output and pacing targets in real time through the coaching interface. He didn't think about it. It was like knowing your heart was beating. True, constant, not worth attention.

Sixth hurdle. Twelve meters from the seventh. His body drove forward and the counting continued underneath everything — *uno, dos, tres* — and his heart rate was climbing, had been climbing for the last three hurdles, pushed past training range into a zone his body reached for because the coaching signal wanted it there. He didn't notice. The push felt like effort. Like desire.

Seven meters from the seventh hurdle, the world stopped.

Not white. Not void. Not a fade or a flicker or a dimming — Kai had described his blackout to the group and Tomas had listened and prepared himself for something gradual, something with a shape he could recognize and fight. This was nothing like that. This was the world present and then the world not present. Everything — sight, sound, proprioception, the rubber under his spikes, the temperature of the air in his lungs, the faint chlorine smell drifting from the pool three corridors away — all of it, at once, ceased. Not replaced by darkness. Not replaced by anything. The inputs simply terminated. Like a wire cut.

His enhancement did not stop.

His legs kept driving. The autonomous motor subsystem, still receiving firmware commands through the corrupted calibration network, maintained the sprint pattern — full speed, maximum power output through the synthetic muscle grafts. His arms kept pumping. His body continued its approach to the seventh hurdle with mechanical precision and no one at the controls.

He did not clear the hurdle.

---

Kai saw it happen from forty meters away.

He was standing at the edge of the curve, toweling sweat off his neck, his left wrist aching the way it always ached now — the bone knitted too fast, the synthetic repair leaving a seam in the marrow that the cold indoor air found and pressed on. He'd been watching Tomas without particular attention, the way you watch someone young do something well — appreciating the form, the economy of effort, the eagerness that hadn't been beaten out of him yet.

Tomas's stride didn't break. That was the first wrong thing. From Kai's distance, the sequence looked almost normal for a fraction of a second — an athlete approaching a hurdle, legs driving — and then the geometry was wrong. Tomas's lead leg drove forward at full sprint power but did not lift. The hurdle caught him just below the knee. At that speed — close to nine meters per second, the enhancement driving him harder than his natural body ever could have — the impact was not a stumble. The hurdle's crossbar bent against his shin and his momentum pitched him forward and over, his upper body rotating around the point of contact with a violence that made the Dutch pole vaulter, twenty meters away, drop her water bottle.

Tomas went over the hurdle and down. He did not put his hands out. His arms were still driving the sprint pattern, pistoning at his sides. He hit the track surface chest-first, and the forward momentum drove his body along the rubber in a scraping slide, and then his torso buckled — his hips, still being driven forward by legs that had not received the instruction to stop, jackknifed over his upper body, and his spine compressed.

The sound. Kai heard the sound from forty meters away. Not a crack — something wetter, a compressed impact that carried through the acoustic dampening of the high ceiling and arrived at Kai's ears as a single dense syllable.

Tomas's legs stopped moving. Not gradually. They just stopped, the way a switched-off machine stops — mid-stride, the left leg extended, the right leg half-cocked in trail position. The rest of his body continued for another half-second, momentum carrying his torso forward in a loose slide, and then he was still.

Kai was running before he understood what he was doing. His own synthetic grafts fired his legs — the enhancement responding to his alarm faster than conscious thought, and for one sick instant his body was doing the same thing that had just destroyed Tomas, moving without permission, and he had to fight the urge to stop his own legs, to refuse the help, to insist on running at the speed of his own fear.

He reached Tomas in six seconds.

Tomas's face was turned to the side, his right cheek pressed into the track surface. His eyes were open. The blackout had ended — his senses had returned, Kai could see it in the way his pupils were tracking, the way his mouth was moving, trying to form words. He was conscious. He was present. The thing that had happened to his body had happened while he was gone, and now he was back and inside a body that had been rearranged.

"Don't move him," Kai said. He didn't know who he was saying it to. The Dutch pole vaulter was there, her hands hovering over Tomas's back, and the middle-distance runner was shouting something toward the medical bay corridor.

Tomas said something in Spanish that Kai didn't catch. Then, in English: "I can't — my legs. I can't feel my legs."

His voice was calm. Not because he was calm, but because the thing that had happened was so far outside any fear he had prepared for that his vocal cords defaulted to the register of minor confusion, of a missing shoe, of a wrong turn. He said it the way you'd say *I think I left my phone in the other room.* Flat. Informational. The terror would come later, when his brain finished processing what his body already knew.

There was blood on the track from his shin where the hurdle had caught him, and a longer smear of abrasion from his chest slide, visible through his torn training top. But the real damage was invisible, buried in his spine where the neural bridge had been.

Petra arrived in under two minutes, moving fast, her gray-streaked hair escaping its pin. She had a portable diagnostic unit in one hand and a spinal stabilization kit under her other arm, and she dropped to her knees beside Tomas with a practiced efficiency that Kai found both reassuring and horrible — this was something she had done before, or trained for, or both.

"Tomas, can you hear me? Don't try to move."

"I can hear you." His voice was still flat. "My legs, Dr. Engel. I can't feel them."

Petra's hands moved along his spine, not touching — hovering — while the diagnostic unit's sensor bar swept the length of his back. Kai watched the readout from where he knelt. He didn't understand the medical data, but he understood the color: the neural bridge status indicator, which should have been green, was gray. Not red for damaged. Gray for absent.

"The bridge is gone," Petra said. She was talking to the medical staff arriving behind her, not to Tomas. But Tomas heard.

"What do you mean gone?"

"The neural bridge hardware has been destroyed. The impact —" She stopped herself. Looked at Tomas's face. His eyes were tracking hers with the focus of someone who understood that the next sentence would determine the rest of his life. She didn't finish the sentence. She said, "We're going to stabilize you and get you to imaging. Don't move."

Tomas lay on the track and looked at the ceiling, which was white acoustic tile under fluorescent strips, sixty feet up. The hum of the ventilation system. The rubber smell of the track surface against his uninjured cheek. His hands were flat on the floor beside his head and he could feel the rubber's texture against his palms and that feeling stopped at his waist as completely as if someone had drawn a line across his body with a marker and said: *below this, nothing.*

He was twenty-one. His legs had been the entire architecture of his life — the thing he was for, the reason Meridian paid for his family's apartment in Quito, the reason his sister could play soccer on a real pitch instead of the street, the reason his brother could fail math tests without it mattering because Tomas was going to the Apex Games and the sponsorship money would cover everything. His legs. His legs, which had carried him over six hurdles thirty seconds ago with a grace that still made him feel like he was being let in on a secret about what a human body could become, were now silent below the waist and would remain silent.

He signed the contract. He'd signed Meridian's contract because he couldn't afford not to. They'd installed the patch. The corporate fix, the one that traded his body's data for his body's safety, was supposed to prevent this. He'd surrendered everything they asked for — every biometric reading, every neural pattern, every genetic marker — and the virus had come through anyway, the blackout seizing him mid-stride and throwing him into a hurdle at nine meters per second, and his legs were gone.

*Cuídate, mi amor.*

He did not cry. He opened his mouth and closed it and opened it again and what came out was a sound that wasn't Spanish or English, that wasn't language at all, that was just the noise a body makes when it understands that the thing it was built for is finished.

---

Margot had felt everything.

She was in the coaching suite, fifteen meters from the track entrance, her feed interface live on her wrist, the neural connection open and streaming. Tomas's sensory world arrived at her nervous system with the half-second delay of the feed translation — enough lag that she couldn't react in real time, not enough that the experience felt secondhand. She was inside his effort. His hamstrings loaded on the approach, and she felt the loading as a phantom tension in her own legs, a ghost of athletic power that her body hadn't produced in years. His heart rate climbed — 168, 172, 176 — and she'd pushed it there, nudging his pacing targets upward through the coaching interface because the intensity was good, the rush was clean, his cardiovascular output at 178 bpm produced a cascade of sensation through the feed that lit her up in ways her own flat, unplugged nervous system could not.

She knew what she was doing. She always knew. The knowing had never been enough to stop.

The sixth hurdle. Tomas's clearance was beautiful through the feed — the explosion at the bar, his body airborne for a fraction of a second, the landing shock traveling up through his legs and into her borrowed proprioception. She pushed the pacing target again. A small nudge. His heart rate hit 182. His cardiovascular system was running hot, blood pressure elevated, the vessels in his spine and legs under more strain than training range called for. She registered this the way an addict registers the third drink: noted, not acted upon, already reaching for the fourth.

Then the feed went blank.

Not static. Not interference. The sensory stream from Tomas's neural system simply stopped, as if the other end of the connection had been unplugged from reality. One moment she was inside his effort — the rubber under his feet, the air in his lungs, the rhythmic pulse of his heart at 182 — and the next she was alone in her own body, her own unstimulated nervous system, the coaching suite's fluorescent light flat on her skin. The feed display on the wall showed all channels zeroed. Heart rate: still transmitting, 184 and climbing. Neural input: nothing. Sensory relay: nothing. Motor pattern: active. Active on all channels, his body doing things at full output with no sensory data to guide it.

She should have disconnected. The protocol for a feed disruption was immediate severance of the coaching link. She knew this. Her hand moved toward the interface on her wrist.

She didn't disconnect.

Because the feed channels were coming back. Not Tomas's senses — those were still dark. But the motor pattern data was streaming, and through the coaching interface's sensory relay she could feel what his body was doing. The legs driving at sprint speed. The approach to the hurdle. The impact.

She felt the impact.

Through the feed, at full sensory resolution, her nervous system received the translation of a human body hitting a hurdle at nine meters per second and going down. The shin strike first — a sharp crack of pain that radiated up through her left leg as if she'd been the one to hit the bar. Then the chest impact, the slide, the scraping abrasion that the feed transmitted as a hot strip of friction across her sternum and ribs. And then the compression.

The spinal compression arrived through the feed as a dense, total sensation that Margot had no analogy for. A closing. A compaction of the nerve pathways that carried information between brain and legs, translated through the neural interface into something she experienced as the feeling of her own lower body being — *folded.* Not in half. Inward. As if the space her legs occupied in her proprioceptive map was being compressed into a line and then into a point and then into nothing.

Then the nothing. The legs going silent. Not painful — that was the worst part, worse than the impact, worse than the compression. The absence of pain. The feed channel that had been streaming Tomas's lower-body sensation went flat, and Margot's nervous system received the flatness as a phantom paralysis that settled over her own legs like a cold sheet.

She yanked the interface off her wrist. The disconnect was brutal — the feed severing mid-stream, the sudden lurch from Tomas's ruined sensory world back into her own. She screamed. The sound came out of her before she decided to make it, a raw sound that did not contain words, that was the vocal equivalent of ripping a plug from a wall.

She was on the floor of the coaching suite. She didn't remember falling. Her legs were fine — she could feel them, she could move them, she pressed her palms against her thighs and felt the muscle and bone underneath, whole, intact, her own. But the phantom was still there. A layer underneath the real sensation, a ghost of paralysis that the feed had written into her nervous system and that her body had not yet overwritten. Her legs worked and they also felt dead, both things true at once, and she sat on the floor with her hands clamped on her thighs and her breath coming in short wet sounds that were not quite sobs.

The interface lay on the floor beside her, the wrist mount's display still showing Tomas's vitals. Heart rate: 171, dropping. Neural bridge status: gray.

She'd had his heart rate at 182 when the blackout hit. Training range for hurdle work was 155 to 165. She'd pushed him twenty beats above threshold because the rush was better at 180, because his cardiovascular effort at maximum output produced the specific flavor of borrowed intensity that she couldn't get any other way, and when the virus struck and his body crashed and his spine compressed, the elevated blood pressure had been there, the stressed vessels had been there, the cardiovascular system running hot and hard had been there underneath the impact, and she didn't know — she couldn't know for certain — whether the outcome would have been different if his heart rate had been at 160, whether the spinal blood flow would have been less catastrophic, whether the neural bridge might have survived a lower-energy impact. She couldn't know for certain.

But she knew enough.

She sat on the floor of the coaching suite and looked at her hands, which were shaking. Her hands, which had adjusted the pacing target. Her hands, which had pushed the slider upward on the interface because the feedback at 182 felt better than the feedback at 165. Her hands, which were still attached to her body, which still worked, which could still feel the cold tile floor underneath her and the sweat drying on her forearms and the faint hum of the ventilation system pushing processed air through the room where she sat with the knowledge that a twenty-one-year-old's legs were gone and she had been inside them when they went.

---

Three hours later. The calibration bay.

Kai went to check his own system logs because the alternative was standing in the medical bay corridor looking at the closed door behind which Petra and her team were imaging Tomas's spine, and he couldn't do that anymore. He'd stood there for ninety minutes already, his wrist aching, his shoulder aching, his body remembering its own crash with a precision that felt less like memory and more like rehearsal. Tomas's blackout was the same virus that had taken Kai's senses at the 140-meter mark five weeks ago. The same corruption in the shared calibration network. The same firmware eating through the infrastructure that kept their bodies from killing them.

Tomas had signed Meridian's contract. Had received their patch. The corporate fix — the one that required permanent data surrender, the one that was supposed to be the solution — had not stopped the virus from reaching him.

The calibration bay hummed. The sound was always there, the low-frequency drone of processing equipment that Kai had learned to tune out in his first week at the facility. Tonight it sounded different. Not louder. More present. As if the machines were doing something they shouldn't be.

He sat down in the calibration rig — the same reclined chair where Yusuf had first shown him his proprioception drift, weeks ago, when the body-self boundary had been a data point rather than a lived catastrophe — and pulled up his system logs on the rig's display. His neural bridge status. His graft performance. His sensory integration metrics. All green. All normal. His body continued to function within parameters that did not predict the future.

He was about to close the interface when he saw the transfer log.

It was buried in the system's administrative sidebar — a chronological record of data flowing to and from his enhancement hardware during calibration sessions. Most of it was routine: diagnostic uploads to the facility server, firmware update confirmations, the small exchanges that happened whenever an athlete's system connected to the calibration network. But there were other entries. Timestamped during his calibration sessions, data packets flowing to a destination address he didn't recognize — not the facility server, not Meridian's cloud, not any system address the rig was supposed to communicate with.

Days of biometric files. His neural bridge telemetry. His proprioception logs. His graft performance data. His genetic baseline readings. All of it, flowing to an external device, during sessions where he'd been lying in this chair with his eyes closed while Yusuf adjusted his systems.

Kai sat in the calibration rig and looked at the transfer log. He scrolled back. The entries went back weeks.

"Yusuf." He said it to the empty room. Then he stood up.

Yusuf was in the back office of the calibration bay, a small glass-walled room that looked out onto the main calibration floor. He was sitting at his desk with his glasses pushed up on his forehead and a cup of tea going cold at his elbow. Three monitors. One showed Tomas's crash diagnostic — the neural bridge failure cascade, rendered in a color-coded timeline that Kai couldn't read. The other two showed something else. Code. Pages of it. Comparisons. Side-by-side columns with sections highlighted in yellow.

"Yusuf." Kai stood in the doorway. "I found the transfer log."

Yusuf looked up. His face did the thing it did when he was deciding whether to perform competence or abandon it — a brief flicker of professional polish crossing his features and then failing, the Birmingham underneath showing through like a bone through skin.

"Right," Yusuf said. "Yeah."

"You've been copying my data. During calibration. All my biometric files, neural logs, graft performance — everything. To some device that isn't in the system."

"Yeah."

"How long?"

"Since February." Yusuf's hands were on the desk. His engineer's hands, steady and precise. They were not steady now. "Since I got here. Since before the virus, Kai. I was doing it before any of this happened."

Kai stepped into the office. The door didn't close behind him because it was a sliding door and the track was stuck, had been stuck since March, and Yusuf had never filed a maintenance request because the open door made the room feel less like a box. Kai didn't sit down. He looked at the monitors. The code on the two right-hand screens meant nothing to him, but the yellow-highlighted sections had a visual pattern — recurring structures, repeated sequences.

"Tell me what I'm looking at," Kai said.

"Sit down."

"I'm fine standing."

Yusuf took his glasses off his forehead and put them on. The gesture was familiar — Kai had watched him do it a hundred times before calibration work, the small adjustment that meant he was shifting into technical mode. But his hands shook when he settled the frames on his nose, and the shift looked less like competence and more like a man putting on a costume that didn't fit anymore.

"My sister Farah has Voss-Kessler syndrome," Yusuf said. "Progressive neural deterioration. There's no treatment. There's barely any research, because the condition's rare enough that nobody funds it. But the biological data from enhanced athletes — the neural integration telemetry, the graft interface metrics, the way your systems interact with your native tissue — some of that data maps onto pathways that are relevant to Voss-Kessler. Not directly. It's adjacent. But it's the most detailed neural-interface data set in the world, and it's being generated right here, every day, in every calibration session."

"You've been stealing our data for your sister."

"I've been copying biometric files to a personal archive, yeah. I'm not going to dress it up. I took the data because if I didn't take it, it was going to end up behind Meridian's corporate license and nobody outside their R&D department was ever going to touch it again."

Kai looked at the cold cup of tea. It had a skin on it, a thin milky film. The office smelled like old tea and cable insulation and the faint metallic ozone of overworked processing units.

"Tomas is paralyzed," Kai said.

"I know."

"He signed their contract. He got the patch. The fix. The thing that was supposed to make it safe."

"I know." Yusuf turned to the right-hand monitors. The code comparisons. The yellow-highlighted sections. "Sit down. Please."

Kai sat. The office chair was a cheap swivel model with a torn armrest, and it creaked under his weight.

"I've been analyzing the virus code since February," Yusuf said. "Since before Meridian arrived with their offer. The virus propagates through the calibration network — you know that. What I've been trying to understand is how it's structured. Where it came from. How something this targeted ended up in a shared infrastructure that Meridian designed, maintains, and controls."

He turned one of the monitors toward Kai. The yellow-highlighted code sections were arranged in parallel columns — left and right, with lines connecting matching patterns between them.

"Left column is the virus's core propagation code. The part that interfaces with the calibration network and spreads between connected systems. Right column is from Meridian's proprietary firmware development framework. This is their standard architecture — the building blocks they use to write all their enhancement software. The calibration tools, the neural bridge firmware, the graft integration protocols. All of it built on this framework."

Kai looked at the columns. He didn't read code. "What am I seeing?"

"Structural signatures. The virus's propagation mechanism was built using Meridian's own development tools. The code architecture, the way functions are nested, the naming conventions, the memory allocation patterns — it's their framework. Not copied from it. Built in it. The way you'd build a product, not a hack."

"You're saying Meridian made the virus."

"I'm saying the virus was built using Meridian's tools by someone who had access to Meridian's development environment. That's not the same thing, but it's — yeah. It's close enough that the gap is mostly legal rather than factual."

The calibration bay hummed around them. The sound that Kai had stopped hearing weeks ago was back, filling the space between Yusuf's words, and it sounded different now. Not mechanical. Institutional.

"Tomas signed their contract because he couldn't afford not to," Kai said. "His family needs the money. He gave them everything they asked for. His data, his genetic baseline, all of it. And their patch didn't stop the virus from destroying his spine."

"No."

"And you think that's because they made the virus. That the whole thing — the blackouts, the contract, the data surrender — was a setup."

"I think the virus was engineered to create conditions where the data surrender became the only rational choice. I think the patch is real — it does suppress the virus's propagation — but I think the fact that it requires full data access is the point, not a side effect. And I think —" Yusuf's voice shifted, the professional register cracking and the Birmingham breaking through underneath, faster, less controlled. "I think the patch didn't save Tomas because the virus is still in the network. The patch suppresses propagation, it doesn't eliminate the code. It's a leash, not a cure. It keeps the virus dormant as long as Meridian's servers are authorizing the suppression. Which means they can pull the leash whenever they want."

"You can't prove this."

"No. I can show structural similarity between the virus architecture and Meridian's development framework. I can show that the patch's suppression mechanism requires ongoing authorization from Meridian's cloud. I can show that the data lockdown would bury every piece of biological information that anyone outside Meridian could use for independent research. But I can't prove intent. I can't prove anyone at Meridian sat in a room and decided to create a virus that would paralyze a twenty-one-year-old kid from Quito."

Kai looked at the monitors. The code comparisons blurred in his vision — he didn't have the technical literacy to evaluate what Yusuf was showing him, and he knew it, and Yusuf knew he knew it. Which meant this conversation was not about evidence. It was about trust. Whether Kai trusted the man who'd been stealing his data for months, who'd lied to his face during every calibration session, who'd built an unauthorized personal archive out of information that belonged to Kai's body — whether that man's analysis of a corporate conspiracy was worth believing.

"You lied to me," Kai said. "Every session. I'd lie in that chair and close my eyes and you'd copy my data to your private archive and you never said a word."

"Yeah."

"You built an alternative patch that severs the feed interface — the thing Margot needs — and you never told anyone that your motivation was your sister's data access, not our safety."

"I told the group about Farah. In the argument."

"After you'd already built the patch. After Lena had already volunteered to test it. You told us when the information couldn't change any decisions we'd already made."

Yusuf took his glasses off. Rubbed his eyes. The gesture was so ordinary and so human that Kai felt something shift in his chest — not forgiveness, not sympathy, just the recognition that the man sitting across from him was not a villain or a whistleblower but a person who'd been making bad decisions for understandable reasons and who was now sitting in the wreckage.

"Tomas's neural bridge is gone," Yusuf said. His voice was quiet. The Birmingham was soft in it, the vowels rounded, the consonants blurred. "I ran the crash diagnostic. The virus triggered a cascading corruption in his sensory firmware, same as it did with you in February. His enhancement's autonomous motor system kept his body in the sprint pattern. He hit the hurdle at full speed. The fall compressed his thoracic spine and the impact destroyed the bridge hardware. The grafts kept his cord from severing — he's alive because of the synthetic reinforcement — but without the bridge, there's no neural pathway to his lower body. The biological cord was already degraded from the graft integration process. The bridge was compensating. Now it's gone and there's nothing to compensate."

"Is it permanent?"

"The bridge hardware has to be surgically replaced. Which means another full integration procedure, months of rehabilitation, and a new neural pathway that has to be grown from scratch. Even then, the probability of full restoration is —" Yusuf paused. Calculated or pretended to. "Low. The tissue damage from the compression complicates regrowth. He'll walk again, maybe. He won't run hurdles."

Kai stood up. The office chair rolled backward on its cheap casters and bumped the wall.

"You should have told me," he said. "From the start. About the archive, about Farah, about the virus code. All of it."

"If I'd told you about the archive, you'd have reported me. The data would have been locked down. My sister's —"

"Don't." Kai's voice was flat. The humor was gone, completely, the way it went when the things he was feeling exceeded the register of irony. "Don't make this about Farah right now. A kid is paralyzed. The corporate patch that was supposed to protect him didn't work. And you've been sitting on virus analysis that suggests Meridian engineered the thing that destroyed his legs. You've been sitting on it."

"I didn't have enough to — "

"Enough for what? Publication? A lawsuit? Enough for Tomas, lying in the medical bay trying to figure out how to tell his mother he can't walk? He might not need your standard of evidence, Yusuf. He might just need to know."

Yusuf didn't answer. He looked at his monitors — the code comparisons, the yellow-highlighted sections, the parallel columns that documented something that might be corporate sabotage or might be coincidence or might be the kind of pattern that an anguished engineer found because he needed it to be there. His hands were on the desk. They were shaking.

"What do you want to do?" Yusuf asked.

Kai looked through the glass wall of the office at the calibration bay beyond — the rigs where athletes lay down and trusted the system to maintain them, the humming machines, the fluorescent lights. Three corridors away, Tomas lay on an imaging table and learned the dimensions of his new life. One floor above, Margot sat with the phantom of his paralysis still alive in her legs. Outside the building, the spring thaw was pulling the last of the winter off the mountains, and the meltwater ran downhill the way it always did, finding the path of least resistance to the valley below.

"I don't know," Kai said. And he meant it. The trust was gone — not just in Yusuf, not just in Meridian, but in the entire apparatus of optimization that had rebuilt his body and called it improvement and could not prevent a virus it may have designed from destroying a young man whose only crime was being too poor to refuse the contract. He didn't know what to do with that. He didn't know what to do with any of it.

He left the office. The sliding door stuck behind him, half-open, the light from Yusuf's monitors spilling across the calibration bay floor in yellow columns that looked, from the right angle, like the bars of something.
