# Chapter 10

The pool was empty except for Lena.

Margot settled into the coaching chair on the observation deck and woke the session display. It was six forty-five a.m. The facility hadn't come online yet. The sprinters would not start until eight. The calibration bays were dark. Below the glass, the pool lay flat under the overheads, ten lanes of hard blue light. Lena stood on the far deck pulling on her cap. When she tucked her hair back, the port at the base of her skull showed for a second: a small round disc, skin-colored, then hidden again.

Margot strapped the feed interface to her right wrist. The pads found her pulse and warmed. She entered the athlete ID. The set was standard aerobic. The pace target was 1:04 per hundred. It was clean enough to live inside.

She connected.

Air came first. The deck was cool on Lena's bare feet. There was tightness through the shoulders from yesterday. There was also a dry ache behind the eyes that still shouldn't have been altitude after six weeks. Then Lena dove and the water closed over Margot like a second skin.

Lena's stroke always hit her hardest at the catch. That first loaded grip on the water ran up Margot's forearm and into her jaw. Her mouth went wet every time. The turn at the wall drove heat into her thighs, into muscles she hadn't trusted in years. This was what she used the feed for. It was not insight. It was not coaching. It was the hit when somebody else's body found the right line and hers answered anyway.

The first hundred came in 1:03.8. The second came in 1:04.1. Her heart rate held at 162. Stroke rate held at 68. Lena's flip turn at the two-hundred wall was exact, shoulder tucked just enough, kick snapping back into the surface on the third beat.

The third hundred started.

Margot felt the slip before the numbers moved. Lena usually met the water with tiny choices. There would be a fraction more pressure on the left palm. She would hold a breath a beat longer. The turn would adjust for what the last stroke had done. Now those little checks were gone. The hand went in. The body pulled. It kicked. It breathed. Everything landed on time and all of it was dead flat.

Earlier sessions had carried a blink of this. Half a second where the stroke lost its texture and came back. Margot had logged drift and kept going.

This wasn't drift.

The breath pattern kept its count, but the usual flinch at the burn in Lena's chest vanished. The wall contacts hit too hard. There was no protective slack through the shoulder and no tiny hesitation before the push. When Margot nudged the pace down to 1:00, Lena's body answered before any trace of assent or resistance came through the feed. It was like grabbing a handrail and finding nobody's hand on it.

Stroke rate hit seventy. Then seventy-two.

From the deck the stroke still looked clean. Through the feed it felt wrong in three separate places at once: lungs pulling air with no panic in them, legs turning over without fatigue arguing back, arms catching exactly the same pressure every cycle as if pain had been cut out of the lane. Lena wasn't in the water with Margot anymore. Her body was.

Margot tightened her grip on the armrest until her knuckles hurt. The clean pull kept landing in her shoulders. Her calves twitched against the floor. She wanted another turn. She pushed the target to 0:58.

Heart rate climbed to 178, then 182.

The yellow warning box lit on the console. Margot barely looked at it. Lena hit the three-hundred wall hard enough that the impact rang through Margot's teeth, then fired off the turn with both legs at once. The push-off burned through her quads. Her own chest started chasing Lena's rhythm, short and fast. There was no drag in the stroke now. There was no doubt in it. There was no person in it. There was only open water and force and the next wall coming.

Her heart rate touched 185.

Margot cleared the warning.

Then it hit 188. Then 190.

The box went red this time. The system threw a protocol alert and started the medical countdown in the corner of the screen. Margot killed that too. Her thumb moved before the timer hit fifty-eight seconds. The feed kept giving. Her mouth tasted like chlorine though she was nowhere near the pool. One more length, she thought. She wanted one more wall.

Then it hit 193.

The fourth hundred was worse and better. Lena's hands found the water without search. Her lungs took what they needed and gave nothing back. The whole body ran hot and blank. Margot rode it with her shoulders locked and her thighs shaking under the chair.

Then it hit 195.

That got through. Margot dragged the target back to 1:02. Above 195 a heart could skip. Above 195 the session stopped being worth it even to her. The stroke rate fell. The red box stayed red. 193. 191. She closed the second alert anyway.

Lena reached the wall and stayed there.

One hand was on the gutter and the other on the lane rope. Her mouth was open. Her shoulders jumped with each breath. Water slid off her cap and down her face. Through the feed the return came in pieces: pain first, hot and immediate through the arms; then cold crawling over the back of the neck; then confusion, jagged and late, as if Lena had been dropped into a race already half done.

Margot disconnected.

The feed cut clean. Warm water, loaded muscle, Lena's heart hammering up under her sternum. It was gone. The observation deck came back thin and dry. Her own pulse felt too small. Saliva soured under her tongue. For a second she thought she might throw up.

The skin under the interface was red and ridged when she tore the strap loose. She pressed her left thumb into the welt until it hurt. Below the glass, Lena hung on the rope and stared at the opposite wall like she'd never seen the room before.

A minute went by. Then Lena turned her head, slow, searching the deck.

"What lap is this?"

Her voice came up through the audio pickup, flat and tinny in the tile room. No one answered.

By the time Lena hauled herself out and sat on the edge with both feet in the water, Margot had the session log open again. Two red alerts sat in the history bar. She tapped each one and changed the auto-label from sustained HR anomaly to coach-authorized build. Then she reclassified the set from aerobic to mixed-intensity and switched the export to summary sync only. Raw feed would stay on the device unless someone asked for it by hand. The dashboard would show a hard middle split and nothing else.

Lena bent over on the pool edge, one hand clamped to the port at the back of her neck.

Margot killed the console, dropped the interface into her bag, and stood. Her knees felt hollow. Sweat cooled under her shirt. The feed was gone and her body kept reaching for it anyway.

The corridor to residential took four minutes. Linoleum, fluorescent panels, antiseptic in the vents, stale coffee from the cafeteria wing. A sprinter she didn't know was stretching against the wall, calves marked with faint graft seams. They nodded at each other and kept moving.

In her room, Margot sat on the bed and took the interface back out. It was brushed steel, with connection pads still warm from her skin. Four years of use had left them dark at the edges with her oils and her pulse. She rubbed a thumb across one pad, then checked the device memory light. It held solid amber. The raw feed was still there.

The order should have been Petra first, then Yusuf. A Class III event meant raw data and a grounded athlete. If Petra pulled the session she would also get the seven-minute feed depth, the pace jumps, both overridden alerts, the set classification change, and the place where Margot had stayed in after the first bad slip because it felt too good to leave.

Margot put the interface in the nightstand drawer and shut it.

She wanted Lena safe. She wanted the feed more. That was the order it sat in her body.

On the wall display, her heart rate had come down to 84. Her respiration was normal. Enhancement status read none. That line stayed blank. It always would. She had gone to Brisbane in 2028 in an unenhanced body and finished fifth in the 200 fly. Now she sat on a narrow bed in Kirchberg with her own lungs working at an ordinary human rate and a sour ache climbing the back of her throat because ordinary wasn't enough.

The room stayed bright. She lay back anyway. Ten minutes after disconnect, her legs still gave a small useless tremor now and then, as if they expected another wall. The empty part had started already. It always started fast.

---

Lena spent the afternoon trying to find the missing laps.

At her desk, she pulled up the session telemetry Margot had filed that morning. The display gave stroke rate, splits, heart rate, and neural link activity. The graphs ran clean from start to finish. There was a moderate rise through the second half of the set. There was no desync marker in the summary field. There was no interruption.

The record said she had swum four hundred meters.

Lena remembered two hundred and fifty.

She remembered the warm-up, the first hundred, the second turn, the beginning of the third, and the flip at 250.

Then the lane rope was under her hand. Her chest tore for air. The pool lights were too bright. Her own question came out of her mouth before she could stop it.

What lap is this.

The telemetry said her body had done the rest. Her arms had pulled. Her legs had kicked. The walls had registered contact. Her heart rate had gone into the 190s. All of it was real. None of it had been experienced.

Smaller slips had happened before. Twenty minutes gone in her room while a page sat open in front of her. A turn pattern last season's firmware chose for her. Margot had called those within acceptable parameters, and Lena had accepted it because Margot had the data and the authority, and because the program had trained her early to trust whatever could be graphed.

This morning was not that.

She touched the port at the base of her skull. The ceramic edge sat smooth under her thumb, skin-warm, built into her like a joint or a tooth. She'd had it since she was four. She pressed harder, as if pressure might give her a clearer answer than the screen did.

The Kirchberg desync protocol sat three menus down. She pulled it up and read the relevant lines again. Class II: intermittent divergence under five seconds. Report within twenty-four hours. Class III: sustained divergence, loss of voluntary motor control, or loss of conscious awareness during link-directed activity. Report immediately. Ground the athlete.

Three minutes was not Class II.

She found Margot outside the coaching suite later that afternoon. Margot had coffee in one hand and her coaching bag over her shoulder. Her hair was down. She looked tired. The quick coach scan happened as soon as Lena rounded the corner, automatic and clinical.

"Hey," Margot said.

"Can I ask about this morning?"

"Sure."

A technician pushed a cart past them and they both stepped aside. Wheels squeaking. Ventilation humming through the wall.

"Something happened in the water," Lena said. "I can't remember the end of the set."

Margot's face went still.

"Can't remember how?"

"I don't mean blurred. I mean absent. I remember the turn at two-fifty. Then I'm on the rope and it's over."

"Your telemetry looked clean," Margot said. She lifted the cup but didn't drink. "Heart rate ran a little high in the middle. That's intensity. Your stroke held."

"I've been here six weeks. This isn't altitude."

"Altitude can still hit weird."

"You were on the feed. You would've felt it."

"I felt your pacing and your stroke." Margot kept her voice even. "Both looked good."

"It didn't feel good."

"What did it feel like?"

Lena waited a beat, checking the sentence before she said it.

"Like I was removed. Then returned."

The words were not exact. There might not be exact words. It had not been darkness or dizziness or a blackout in the ordinary sense. There was no tunnel, no blur, no sense of time passing. One moment she had been in the lane. The next moment her body had already spent itself.

Margot watched her over the coffee cup.

"The desync markers don't show a Class III event," she said. "Same drift range we've been tracking. If it had gone outside that, the system would've flagged it."

"This wasn't the same as the drift."

"It can feel bigger than it is. Everyone's half spooked right now. A sprinter came to me yesterday convinced her visual tracking was failing. She was dehydrated."

"I'm not dehydrated."

"I'm not saying you made it up." Margot lowered the cup. "I'm saying the session data doesn't back a severe event. If it did, I'd pull you. You know that."

Lena did know one thing: she had been taught from childhood that the printout outranked sensation. The rule was splits over feeling, diagnostics over panic. If the display said nominal and the coach said clean, the body was the unreliable instrument.

"Do you want me to flag it?" Margot asked. "I can note the session and send it to Petra. She'll ground you for imaging, at least two days off the water, maybe more. Your call."

Margot said it plainly. There was no push in either direction. It was just consequence.

Lena thought about the calibration rig, Yusuf's equipment mapping her link while she lay still inside it. She thought about the clean report that might come back and tell her the gap in her morning had not counted. She thought about a real report, a grounding order, four months to the Games.

"I'll think about it," she said.

"OK."

Margot took a drink. That small ordinary movement closed the space between them more effectively than anything sharper would have.

"Let me know," Margot said.

"I will."

Margot walked on. Her footsteps stayed even down the corridor, no hurry in them, no sign she'd just lied to someone who trusted her.

The quarters were quiet when Lena got back. Her wall display still glowed green. Her heart rate was 68. Neural link status was nominal.

She sat on the bed. For a while she looked at the word nominal and did nothing else. Then she left the session unflagged.

The program had trained her too well. If the screen said she was fine, that counted more than the hole in her own morning.

Her thumb stayed pressed to the port until the skin went sore. The display did not change.
