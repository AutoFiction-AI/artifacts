# Chapter 4

The coaching suite smelled like adhesive tape and the faint metallic discharge of processing hardware running warm. Margot got there fifteen minutes before Tomás's session, which was ten minutes early. Professionalism, she told herself, meant checking the monitoring displays, running a diagnostic on the feed interface, and reviewing the hurdle and four-hundred targets. All of that was true.

But she also sat in the coaching rig with the wrist mount already docked, waiting for the signal handshake that could not begin until Tomás came in and connected on his end, and watched the green ready-light blink until her mouth went dry.

The suite sat beside the indoor track, cut off from the training floor by a glass observation window. Three monitors on the wall held empty biometric grids. The chair was padded and built for long sessions. It was the most comfortable chair in the building. Margot hated that she knew that.

Tomás came through the door at 9:02 in training kit, hurdle spikes in one hand, water bottle in the other. The spikes were custom-fitted and Meridian-issued. He carried them carefully, still a little amazed by them, like they might stop belonging to him if he gripped too hard. He was smiling. He usually was.

"Morning, Coach."

"Morning. You eat?"

"Yeah, eggs and that quinoa stuff from the cafeteria. It's not — how do you say — it's not bad, actually. My mom makes better, but." He shrugged. "She makes better than everything, so."

"Sit down. Let me check your calibration readings before we start."

He sat across from her. The auxiliary cable clicked into the port on the back of his training vest. The handshake started, system meeting system, negotiating data access and neural permissions, building the one-way sensory pipe that let her feel what he felt. The monitors filled with his resting biometrics. His heart rate was sixty-two. Core temperature was thirty-seven point one. Enhancement status was all green.

"How's the shoulder?" Two days ago he had bruised his left deltoid in shot put, a training weight shifting in his grip at release.

"Good. Little sore when I raise it past here." He lifted his arm to about a hundred thirty degrees. "But it doesn't catch. Just, you know. It aches."

"Don't baby it on the hurdles. Your trail arm needs full extension or your hip rotates and you clear the bar like you're climbing a fence."

He laughed. "Got it."

"I'm serious. I watched your last session tape. Your trail leg is fine, but that arm drops three centimeters on the left."

"Three centimeters? That's nothing."

"It's three centimeters that turns into a tenth and then sixth place. You didn't come here for sixth."

"No." The smile changed, still there but sharper underneath. Tomás was twenty-one and eager and his family in Quito needed him to be good. Sometimes the need showed through the charm. "I didn't."

"Ready?"

"Ready."

She initiated the feed.

The world doubled. Her own body stayed in the chair, with padding at her back, feet on the floor, and twenty-one degrees against her forearms. Over it came Tomás's body, laid across hers through the feed. His hamstrings were loose and warm from the pre-session stretch. His right quadriceps, where synthetic muscle met natural tissue, carried a cooler seam running down toward the knee. His breathing was deep and regular. His heart sat at sixty-two and arrived in her chest as a second beat that did not belong there.

When he stood, the load shift ran through her too. His calves engaged and his core tightened. Grafted musculature pulled him into alignment. It was not the same as feeling her own body. The feed translated it and lagged half a breath behind. It was still enough.

Out on the track, he set ten hurdles at standard spacing and competition height, because Tomás always trained full spec, while she watched through the glass and felt him through the link at the same time. He bent to adjust the third bar and the stretch through his lower back hit her a beat later. His hamstrings were big and the release came easy.

"Pacing targets loaded," she said into the headset mic. "First set, seventy percent effort. Clean technique. Don't worry about the clock."

"Got it."

In the blocks, his heart rate climbed to seventy-eight. It was anticipatory and normal. The balls of his feet pressed into the pads. His quads coiled. His enhanced vision locked on the first hurdle. The rubber surface snapped into individual granules, bright and stupidly clear.

He pushed out of the blocks.

The acceleration hit behind her sternum. It was not her body's, but the feed mapped it onto hers anyway, and for three strides she was in the chair and on the track at once. Synthetic legs drove. The first hurdle came fast. His lead leg rose. The quadriceps fired and the hip flexor caught. Both feet left the ground for that tight clean arc over the bar. Air ran over his forearms. Then came the landing and the reacceleration, and then the next three strides. His heart rate hit a hundred thirty-eight. It was beautiful.

She never said that word out loud. The feed at full intensity, a young body working near the edge of what its engineering could hold, was the best part of her day. Better than swimming had ever been, even on the good days. Through the feed there was no gap between wanting and doing.

He cleared the second hurdle, then the third, then the fourth. His form held. His heart rate was one forty-six. The pacing target she had loaded peaked at one fifty.

Her thumb touched the adjustment tab and bumped the target by four beats, to one fifty-four.

An amber strip flashed across her private pane: MANUAL TARGET EDIT RECORDED. The wall monitors did not change. They stayed on the summary display trainers and medical staff used on the floor: peak rate, incident markers, coach note. The pacing-history tab sat under audit access. Petra could open it. A trainer doing triage could not.

At the fifth hurdle, his visual field tightened on the bar ahead. Distance compressed and opened with his stride, squeezing her chest. His heart rate hit one fifty-one. Warmth spread out from the middle of her chest. It was effort, real effort, past the point where the system cleaned it up and made it efficient. That was the part she wanted.

He cleared the sixth hurdle clean. His breathing turned ragged enough that she started matching it without meaning to. His heart rate hit one fifty-four. His body was past the sweet spot now, asking for more oxygen than the model liked. The strain got ugly. She liked that too.

She could have pulled the target back.

At the seventh hurdle, his timing slipped by a fraction she felt before she saw it. The load had started stealing fine control. His lead leg came through. The trail leg clipped the bar at shin height. It was not a brush. It was a hard catch that twisted his knee as momentum carried him down.

He hit hands first, then knees, then right shoulder and the side of his face, sliding on the rubber. The scrape came through the feed hot across her own cheek and shoulder. Heart rate spiked to one sixty-one. His body went flat and confused and sent up a burst of bad signals. Where is the ground. Why did forward stop. Why can't the leg find the floor. Margot sat in the rig and felt all of it.

Her fingers hit the release on the wrist mount. The doubled world collapsed. She was back in the chair with armrests slick under her palms, her own heart rate at ninety-one, and Tomás on the track getting up slowly and looking at his knee.

"Tomás. You good?"

"Yeah." He was breathing hard. "Yeah, I'm okay. Knee's a little messed up. I think I tweaked it. What happened?"

"Your pacing fell off on the last three hurdles. Same thing from Monday's stair set. That's altitude. You've been here twelve days. It waits until you think you've got your legs and then it takes them away. I should've started you at sixty-five. My fault."

He frowned, tested the knee again, then nodded once. "Like Monday."

"Yeah."

"So we skip the four hundred today?"

"We skip it. Training team can look at the knee and we move the four hundred to Thursday if it settles."

"Okay." He sat on the track apron, drank from his bottle, and started unlacing his spikes. "Coach? It was weird. I felt fine until the sixth hurdle and then my legs got heavy all at once. That's still altitude?"

"Yeah. Your system's tuned for sea-level oxygen. Up here the lag shows late. Give it another week."

"Okay." He rubbed the back of his neck. "Thanks for catching it."

The facility trainer came in before Tomás finished with the spikes. He flexed the knee, cleaned the scrape on Tomás's cheek, and glanced at the tablet in his hand. A red summary card sat open on it: PEAK HR 161. FALL EVENT. COACH NOTE PENDING.

"What did you see?" he asked Margot.

"Late-set altitude drop," she said. "He lost the leg on seven. No blackout."

The trainer turned back to Tomás. "Any head strike hard enough to blank you?"

"No. Just hit and slid."

The trainer tapped through range-of-motion prompts, selected "ALTITUDE / LOAD" from the incident menu, and kept moving. His tablet mirrored the wall summary. It did not show coach-side target edits. Those lived in the pacing history under medical audit, behind a different login and a different level of concern.

"Minor sprain," he said. "Two days off hurdles. Bike work if it doesn't swell. Medical can clear the next block."

Tomás nodded. "Okay."

"Go get ice and eat something," Margot said. "You're burning through calories up here."

"Yeah. See you Thursday?"

"Thursday."

After Tomás and the trainer cleared out, the suite went quiet. The monitors dropped to standby. The green ready-light on her docked interface started blinking again. Her wrist stayed warm where the mount had pressed into the skin. Residual current sat in the tendons of her inner wrist. It would take half an hour to fade.

The trainer's note had already populated the medical summary. Margot only had to complete the coaching field, choose a cause code, and recommend the next threshold. She picked altitude acclimatization, incomplete cardiovascular adjustment, reduced intensity for two sessions. It was thorough and professional and false where it counted.

---

Her quarters were on the third floor of the residential wing, facing the mountain. At five in the afternoon in late January, the window showed a black ridge against a sky not much lighter. The glass threw back cold.

Inside, the door locked with a clean click. She kicked off her shoes, shrugged off the coaching jacket, stepped out of the track pants, and stood in a T-shirt and underwear with the feed mark still pressed into her left wrist.

On the floor mat, she ran through the old stretch sequence: hamstrings, quads, hip flexors. Her left hamstring bunched high under the sit bone and stayed there. The right one gave her nothing. She bounced once, then again, waiting for the muscle to wake up. It didn't. Her molars hurt from how hard she was clenching.

In the bathroom mirror, she took stock the way she used to before weigh-ins: five eight, a hundred fifty-two, fourteen pounds over competition weight, broad shoulders gone soft at the edges, thighs still thick, stomach flat enough if she stood straight. She pinched the skin above her hip, let go, and watched the nail marks fill back with color.

Nothing was wrong with her body. It worked. It just was not enough.

The tub edge was cold through the cotton of her underwear. Her mouth kept going wet and then dry again. The skin under the wrist mount prickled. She pressed her thumb into the dent left by the dock until the mark went white and pain climbed up her forearm. When the ache faded, she did it again.

Her chest kept waiting for the second heartbeat from the feed. Nothing came. Her own pulse kept tapping along, small and regular.

At the desk, the live training board showed two swimmers in the pool, one cyclist in the erg room, the gymnastics hall booked until six. She read the list twice. Anyone still moving would do. She got as far as the door, hand on the handle, before voices passed in the hall and made her let go.

It wasn't addiction. It was work. Athletes improved under her. Times dropped. Form cleaned up. If she had to feel the edge of a session to coach it right, that was still work.

The shower did nothing. Hot water hammered her shoulders until the skin went pink. Then there was soap, steam, tile. That was all.

Dressed again, she opened the week's schedule. Hana Petrov at seven tomorrow morning, sprint conditioning. Two gymnasts Thursday afternoon. Friday, Kai Brennan had requested a neural coaching evaluation for his proprioception issues. She left his name on the screen longer than the others.

Next came Tomás's report. Minor sprain, right knee. Contributing factor: altitude adjustment, incomplete cardiovascular acclimatization. Recommendation: two days off hurdle work, light conditioning permitted. The trainer's summary was already locked into the medical file before she finished reading. Her coaching note dropped underneath it. The target edit would slide into the nightly audit queue with every other threshold tweak and missed biometric warning unless Petra chose to go looking.

Her forearms still remembered the jolt when Tomás hit the track, not the whole scene, just the jolt in her palms, wrists, and elbows, plus the hot scrape along the cheek where his face had gone down. The feed left pieces behind.

By tomorrow, Hana would replace it. One of the gymnasts might, if Hana didn't, and Kai on Friday could do it if the others failed. A newer body would push this one out. That was the plan.

Nothing urgent sat in her email. Outside, the ridge had gone fully black. In the window she was only an outline, which was better.

The wrist mount waited on the desk. She picked it up, turned it over, thumbed the warm contacts, and pressed it to her pulse point without connecting it. The metal sat dead on her skin. She held it there through four breaths, until the fresh pressure mark matched the old one, then set it down and went to dinner.
