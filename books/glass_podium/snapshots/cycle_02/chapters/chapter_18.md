# Chapter 18

Kai sat at an outside table at a cafe on the Rue du Grand-Pont, his coffee half-finished and the afternoon showing no interest in moving. Lausanne in August: the lake below the old town catching light between buildings, tourists on the steep sidewalks with shopping bags and good shoes, a woman at the next table reading something on a tablet with the absolute attention of someone who did not want to be disturbed. The cathedral's tower was visible above the rooftops, grey stone against a sky that had been blue all day and looked ready to stay that way until evening.

His wrist ached, the same deep tight ache that arrived every afternoon where the break had sealed too fast. He rotated it once and the ache stayed.

The email from SportsVision was still open on his phone, sitting face-down next to the coffee. It was the third time they'd reached out. They wanted Kai Brennan, dead last in the 200 meters in Doha, on the commentary team for the next Apex Games cycle, explaining the sport to people who liked the spectacle more than the body inside it. He'd be useful now as the athlete who failed in public and kept talking afterward.

He hadn't said no. He hadn't said yes. The not-deciding felt fine, which was new. For most of his career, indecision had been something to clear out before the next training block, the next race, the next heat. Now it was just part of the afternoon, another thing sitting there with the coffee.

A waiter came. Kai ordered another coffee in English and the waiter answered in French and they understood each other fine. The waiter was maybe twenty, with the posture of someone who had been on his feet for eight hours and had three more to go.

His right hand lifted the phone before he meant it to, and he set it back on the table.

This was the daily version. It wasn't Doha. It wasn't a catastrophe. It was just small corrections arriving early: the phone in his hand, the cup meeting the saucer dead center, his shoulder settling in its socket with a smoothness that still made him aware of the joint. His body moved a little ahead of him and then waited, as if expecting him to catch up.

Someone had recognized him on the street yesterday. A man in running shoes, maybe forty, who'd said "tough break" with the sympathetic confidence of a person who had watched the race on a screen and understood the scoreboard. Kai had said "yeah" and the man had meant the race and Kai had meant everything else.

He thought about calling Lena. Lena was in Montreux, thirty minutes by train, swimming at a municipal center that charged her forty francs a month for a lane and did not ask about the port at the base of her skull. They saw each other two or three times a week. It wasn't dramatic. They cooked, or she cooked and put him to work chopping onions because she had no patience for anyone standing in her kitchen being decorative. They talked, or didn't. Once, in her bed, his hand had slid up the inside of her thigh and then closed too hard, thumb digging in before he knew he'd done it. He had felt the grip lock straight into his forearm. Lena had sucked in air, caught his wrist with both hands, and held it there until the pressure went out. Then she'd turned his palm over, set it flat on her stomach, and pulled him back down. He could still feel that pause now: heat in his face, the delayed release in his fingers, her skin warm under his hand after.

He didn't call. The afternoon was good, the coffee was coming, and he didn't need her to talk him through anything.

The waiter set down the cup. Kai thanked him. Warmth spread through both palms, the left still wrong deep in the bone, the right carrying that familiar seam under the skin. He drank. His hand set the cup back on the saucer with the same unwanted steadiness, and he watched a boat on the lake through the gap between buildings. The offer stayed undecided. The wrist kept aching. The afternoon kept going.

A pigeon landed on the back of the empty chair across from him and examined the table with the calculated patience of an animal that had learned where food came from.

"Nothing for you," Kai said.

The pigeon stayed. Kai drank his coffee. The afternoon didn't move.

The pool at the Centre Sportif was twenty-five meters, four lanes, and low-ceilinged enough that humidity stained the concrete. The air held chlorine, the slap of flip turns, and bubbles on the exhale. It was not Kirchberg's silent fifty-meter pool. A retired man did breaststroke with his head carefully above water while two teenagers in the far lane tried to splash each other without admitting that was what they were doing.

Lena swam. The stroke was clean but not optimized. Her catch slipped a fraction on entry. The cost was two seconds per hundred meters, and she still timed every session.

This was her sixth two-hundred. Every third stroke she turned to breathe and felt herself choosing it: head, air, back to streamline. The quiet in her head was not silence anymore. It was just her own mind, messy and associative, with the occasional half-second blank where a word should have been. By July she'd stopped calling that frightening. It was ordinary now.

Halfway through the fifth fifty, the left arm smoothed. It wasn't a blackout. It wasn't full desync. It was just the old correction surfacing for less than a second, the stroke going cleaner than she'd asked for. Lena felt it and stopped at the wall.

At the gutter she waited, breathing hard, while water slapped tile in the chopped rhythm of the small pool. The smoothness didn't come back.

This was what the patch had given her: margin. She could tell the difference soon enough to stop. Then she could push off again under her own imperfect catch and take the lost two seconds as the price of being there for it.

At the end of the set, the coach at the Montreux center was still in the bleachers with a book about Mediterranean cooking and a clipboard. Isabelle did not micromanage. At the start of each session she gave Lena a set and looked up only to mark times. The port at the base of Lena's skull had never prompted a question. Lena suspected Isabelle thought it was cosmetic.

"Good set," Isabelle said, not looking up from the book. "One-oh-nine on the last one."

"Thanks."

"You want another?"

"No, I'm done."

Isabelle made a note on her clipboard. Lena climbed out and stood on the deck, water running off her suit onto the concrete. The retired breaststroke man was still going, his head held above the surface with the determination of someone who believed the pool was trying to get his hair wet. The teenagers had given up swimming entirely and were sitting on the edge of the far lane arguing over something on one of their phones.

In the changing room, Lena sat on the bench in her suit, dripping, and read the article on her phone.

Yusuf had published the analysis three days ago on ResearchGate. The title was long and flat in academic engineering: "Structural Analysis of Firmware Corruption Patterns in Shared Bio-Integrative Calibration Networks: Correspondence with Proprietary Development Tool Signatures." Under it sat two public repositories: the virus-analysis files, and a de-identified extract from the athlete biometrics in Yusuf's archive. His name was on it. Kirchberg was listed as his former affiliation.

The article had been live for three days and Meridian's stock had dropped eleven percent. Lena had read that in the business section of the NZZ after opening the link. Meridian had issued a statement denying the findings and announcing an internal review. The Swiss Federal Office of Sport had opened a preliminary inquiry. The European Athletics Enhancement Authority had requested the files everyone else could already download.

It was not proof. Anyone technical enough could rerun the public analysis and check the correspondence between the corruption patterns and Meridian's development tools. The biometric archive was public too, which meant the data Yusuf had gathered for Farah could be studied outside Meridian. What stayed shut was Meridian's own archive: internal records, full calibration histories, whatever might turn correspondence into attribution. The inquiry was aimed at that door.

Lena texted Yusuf. Just: *Saw it.*

His reply came four minutes later: *Yeah. Farah says hi.*

Lena sat with the phone in her hand. The changing room smelled like chlorine and old wood and lavender soap. Farah. The reason Yusuf had built the archive in the first place. The public files were not a treatment. Lena knew that. But researchers no longer needed Meridian's permission to start. If the inquiry forced the company open, they might get the rest. If it didn't, the archive was still loose in the world now. Research could continue.

Whether it would help Farah, Lena didn't know. She didn't know enough about Voss-Kessler to pretend otherwise. But Yusuf had written *Farah says hi* as if it were ordinary. His sister was still there, still speaking into the future, and the work he had risked everything for had made it into public hands. It wasn't a victory. It was enough.

Lena toweled off. The towel caught on the small circular port at the base of her skull. She touched it with two fingers, the absent check of a thing that was part of her body but not her.

Dressed, she walked out into August in Montreux. The lake was enormous and flat and the sun was on it and the air smelled like warm stone and the jasmine that grew along the promenade railing near the station. A train to Lausanne left soon. Her hair was still damp from the pool and the chlorine followed her, same as it had when she was four in a calibration pool in Zug, before she knew the system in her head was a system. The smell was the same. The mind inside it had started to sound like hers. She walked toward the station with wet hair and two seconds per hundred meters that were hers.

The treatment facility was in Schwabing, in a converted Jugendstil house on a street lined with linden trees that smelled like August. The house had high ceilings and decorative plaster molding and a garden out back where patients were encouraged to sit in good weather. The art on the walls looked chosen by someone who understood that institutional spaces needed art but not what good art looked like: watercolors of lakes, a photograph of mountains that could have been any mountains, a print of a Klee hung slightly crooked and left that way for all six weeks Margot had been here.

It had been six weeks without the feed. Forty-two days. There was no accumulating pride in it, no sense that each day was another brick in a wall between her and the substance. She counted as she'd counted laps: because number was information, and information was what she had instead of certainty.

The program was called NeuroBalance, and it was one of three facilities in Germany that specialized in neural-interface addiction. Most of the other patients were feed-dependent, people with compulsive relationships to entertainment-grade sensory interfaces, immersive sports simulations and full-body relaxation feeds marketed as wellness tools and functioning as digital opiates. The feeds they described were crude by comparison. They had plugged into recordings. Margot had plugged into people.

On Thursday she tried to explain this to Helga. Helga was forty-three, Bavarian, precise in her English, grammatically careful and slightly over-enunciated. Good therapist or not, Helga did not understand what Margot was telling her.

"The entertainment feeds are recorded," Margot said. Across from Helga's desk, with its small succulent and framed photo of a dog, Margot sat back in the chair and looked at the plant instead of the woman. "Recorded and processed and cleaned up. Like a movie of a body. What I did was live. I was inside someone's actual nervous system while they were using it."

"Yes, you've said this." Helga's pen rested on her notepad. "What I'm interested in today is the distinction between the experience itself and the need to repeat it."

"There's no distinction."

"Can you say more?"

Margot looked at the succulent. It was the kind that didn't need much water, which was probably the point of keeping it on a therapist's desk. "When you plug in, the first thing you feel is their heartbeat. Not yours. It's in your sternum, and your breathing changes to match before you even — your diaphragm just does it. And you're already afraid it's going to end. First second. You're still feeling their heart in your chest and you're already afraid of losing it. There's no 'I had the experience and then wanted more.' You want it while it's happening."

Helga wrote something. The pen moved across the notepad with steady pressure.

"And the memory of the experience," Helga said. "Is the memory also—"

"Intact," Margot said. "I'm sitting here right now and I can feel his hamstrings loading on the approach. The way his eyes locked on the bar — not my eyes, his, but I felt the lock. His whole body was just —" She stopped. "He was twenty-one and everything worked and he could feel the clearance height in his hips before he left the ground. I can feel that right now. My hands are sweating."

"And the crash?"

"That too."

"You can reconstruct that sensation?"

"Yeah." Margot looked at her own hands. "The compression in his spine. The silence when the signals stopped. His legs going dead. I was inside his nervous system when it happened." She was quiet for a second. "For about a week I thought that would fix it. You can't want something that did this to a kid. But if you plugged me in right now I'd say yes. I know what it cost him and I'd still say yes."

Helga was quiet. It was the pause that was supposed to look like reflection but was actually recalculation.

"The wanting," Helga said. "Has it changed in quality over the six weeks? Not necessarily in intensity. In quality."

"No."

"Not at all?"

"It's the same. It was this size on day one and it's this size on day forty-two. Doesn't get bigger. Doesn't get smaller. It's just there. Like furniture."

"We'll continue Thursday," Helga said, and wrote something else, and Margot stood up because the session was over and because there was nothing more to say about it that she hadn't already said.

After group, four people in a circle talking about triggers and coping strategies, during which Margot said reasonable things about mindfulness techniques she didn't believe in and made eye contact with a twenty-six-year-old named Stefan who was trembling through withdrawal from an entertainment feed he'd used eighteen hours a day for two years, Margot walked.

August in Munich was warm, not hot. The lindens on Leopoldstraße were in full leaf, and the air carried that late-summer smell of heated pavement and green and the distant mineral note of the Isar. South through Schwabing she walked, past the university, past cafes where students sat outside with laptops and beer in the late afternoon, past the Siegestor with its stone horses catching the low light. In jeans and a T-shirt and running shoes, she looked like anyone. That was the thing about it. Nobody on this street could see that the woman walking past the monument was carrying the precise sensory memory of another person's spinal cord going silent.

By the time she reached the Englischer Garten, she'd ended up at the Eisbach again, though she hadn't meant to come there today. The Eisbach was a channel of the Isar, and at one narrowing the current formed a standing wave that surfers had been riding for decades. It had become her place by the second week, and she kept coming back.

There were four surfers today. Young, most of them. One woman in a wetsuit despite the warm air, her board shorter than standard, shaped for the wave's geometry. The wave was maybe a meter high, clean, glassy in the late light. The surfers dropped in one at a time from the bank, a sideways leap, the board angling to catch the current, then the rapid adjustment of weight and balance on a wave that never moved forward. The whole thing was stationary surfing. The wave held and the surfer held and the river kept moving underneath them.

The woman in the wetsuit was good. On the wave she cut back and forth with efficient fluidity, weight drops, ankle adjustments, the continuous proprioceptive reading of a moving surface. There was nothing enhanced or linked about her. It was just muscle and board and river and the accumulated hours that made the motion look easy. Her weight sank into a bottom turn and her arm swept out for balance and her torso rotated and for a moment the line of her body was a clean functional expression of physics and intention and practice.

Margot could see the biomechanics plainly: quadricep engagement, dorsiflexion, counter-rotation of the hips. Fifteen years of reading bodies had done that to her. The movement almost reached her, dimly, like music from another room, because the neural pathways the feed had built were still there, still listening for input that wasn't coming.

On the bank, Margot watched. The surfers rode the wave and climbed out and rode it again. The woman in the wetsuit finally lost her line and splashed into the current and hauled herself out laughing, water streaming off her suit, her board tucked under one arm. She shook her hair and said something to the guy waiting to drop in and he laughed too and the sound carried across the water in the warm air.

Margot watched until the light changed and the surfers climbed out one by one and walked away with their boards under their arms and the standing wave kept going, the water folding over itself as it always did whether anyone was riding it or not.

At almost dinner, Margot stood and walked back to the facility. The evening was warm. The lindens smelled like summer. Her legs carried her through the park and along the Leopoldstraße and up the steps of the old house, and the wanting was there, the same size it had been that morning, which was the same size it had been the morning before, which was the same size it was every morning, ordinary and entire, and she walked.
