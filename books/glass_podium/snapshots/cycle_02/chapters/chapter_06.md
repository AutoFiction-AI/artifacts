# Chapter 6

Yusuf hadn't left the calibration bay since the crash. He'd been in there fourteen hours now, maybe fifteen. He'd stopped checking the clock around midnight, when the log analysis ate whatever part of his attention had been keeping time. The mug of coffee on the workstation ledge had gone cold hours ago. He moved it aside every time he reached for the secondary display and moved it back when he was done, a small rhythm that kept his hands occupied while the data on the screen got steadily worse.

The bay's constant sixty-hertz hum, the processors, the cooling system, the background drone of a building full of machinery keeping bodies tuned, had shifted in his perception sometime around four in the morning. It no longer sounded like infrastructure. It sounded like something running a fever.

He scrolled through another block of propagation logs. Outside the bay's single window, snow fell in the predawn dark. The facility was under restricted-operations protocol: no training sessions, no calibrations, no scheduled maintenance. Everything was offline until his team could map the extent of the corruption. Forty-three athletes were in their quarters or the common areas or the cafeteria, waiting for someone to tell them whether their bodies were safe. Yusuf was supposed to be that someone.

The pattern was clear. He'd been hoping it wouldn't be.

Every athlete who had connected to the calibration network in the past thirty-two days showed firmware irregularities. Not all were catastrophic. Kai's blackout was the worst case, a full cascade that wiped sensory processing in under 0.8 seconds. But the corruption was present in varying degrees across every system that had handshaked with the shared network since early January. It lay dormant in some systems, active in others. There was no way to predict which would cascade next, or when.

"Right," he said to the empty bay. "So that's everyone, then."

His phone showed seven missed calls from the facility director. He hadn't answered because he'd wanted to be certain before he spoke. Now he was certain, and he still didn't pick up the phone.

The bay door opened. Petra Engel came in holding a tablet and looking like she'd slept about as long as he had, which was to say she hadn't. The grey in her hair looked harsher under the strip lights. The clinical precision of her posture had a new rigidity to it, as if staying upright required active work.

"Two more," she said.

She set the tablet on his workstation. Two medical intake forms were open on the screen. He read them while she stood beside him, not sitting, not leaning, just present.

The first was Marcus Ndlovu, twenty-six, South African, sprint relay. He'd lost all hearing for approximately three seconds during a start drill the previous morning, the same session Kai had been running on the indoor track on the other side of the facility. For those three seconds his body had stayed locked in the blocks in total silence. The hearing had returned on its own. Marcus had finished the drill, told no one, and come to medical at ten that night because he couldn't sleep and his left ear was still ringing. The intake nurse noted that he'd tried to laugh it off when he arrived. His hands had been shaking.

The second was Sonja Petrić, twenty-three, Croatian, artistic gymnastics. Her left hand had gone numb on the uneven bars during a release skill. For approximately four seconds, sensation from the wrist down had vanished completely. The synthetic grafts in her forearms had kept her grip closed on instinct, engineered muscle holding a bar her nerves couldn't feel. She'd dismounted cleanly and gone straight to medical. The intake notes recorded that she'd spent the entire wait opening and closing her left fist, over and over, checking that the fingers still answered.

Yusuf took off his glasses and rubbed the bridge of his nose, then put them back on.

"Have you told them what it is?" he asked.

"I've told them the anomalies are under investigation. I haven't given them a mechanism." Petra's voice was measured, each word placed. It was the register she used when she was building a wall between herself and what she was about to learn. "I need to understand the scope before I say anything definitive."

"The scope is everyone."

Silence held for a beat. Petra pulled a stool from under the secondary workstation and sat. The tablet rested on her knees, her hands folded over it.

"Define everyone."

"Every enhanced athlete who's connected to the shared calibration network since the first of January." He pulled up the session log and turned the monitor so she could see. "Forty-three athletes. Everyone here except the two traditional-category trainers who don't interface with our systems."

"You're certain about the propagation mechanism?"

"Yeah." He brought up the map: nodes and lines, each node an athlete, each line a calibration session that had carried corrupted firmware. "It rides the handshake. Standard tuning session, standard firmware push, corrupted payload inside it. Every system that accepted a push in the past month has some degree of contamination."

Petra studied the screen. Forty-three nodes radiated from the central hub of his bay.

"The severity varies," Yusuf said. The professional polish in his voice was cracking now. More Birmingham came through when he spoke fast. "Some systems only caught enough to cause intermittent disruption. That's Marcus. That's Sonja. Others got the full payload. Kai got the full payload."

"How do we determine who has partial and who has full?"

"Individual diagnostics on every system. Which means bringing athletes back into the calibration bay and connecting them to the same network that infected them in the first place." He stopped, breathed once, and started again. "I'm building an isolated diagnostic protocol. Air-gapped from the main network. Standalone firmware analysis. It's going to take time."

"How much time?"

"Three days at least. Maybe five."

Petra stood. She touched the edge of her tablet and squared it with the workstation as if alignment itself might count as control.

"I'll inform the director," she said. "Then the athletes. All of them. They deserve to know."

"Yeah. They do."

She left. The bay door closed behind her.

Thirty seconds later the intercom clicked alive. Petra's voice came through the ceiling speaker, thinned by the cheap system but steady enough. All enhanced athletes were to report any sensory or motor anomaly to medical immediately. No one was to connect to shared systems or personal interfaces until engineering cleared them. Restricted operations would remain in effect until further notice.

Yusuf sat very still and listened to the building answer her. Doors opened. Shoes hit concrete. Voices rose in the corridor outside, not loud, not yet, but too many of them at once. Chair legs scraped somewhere down the hall. He could picture the line forming before he stood up to see it. The screen still showed forty-three nodes, and the corridor was filling with forty-three people on the move.

When he looked back at the map, his hands were steady. He noticed this and couldn't tell whether it was competence or whether something in him had just gone numb.

***

Lena went to the medical bay at half past three in the afternoon because she had run out of reasons not to.

Restricted operations had emptied the facility's usual schedule. There was no pool time, no dryland training, no calibration sessions. The pool was locked. The indoor track where Kai had crashed was sealed behind yellow maintenance barriers taped across the corridor entrance. She'd eaten lunch in the cafeteria with Dara and two other swimmers, the four of them working through high-protein trays without talking about the one thing they were all thinking about. Afterward she'd gone back to her quarters and sat on the edge of her bed for forty minutes, doing nothing except listening for the intercom in case Petra said something worse.

By the time she reached the medical wing, Petra's announcement had turned the corridor into an overflow waiting room. Cafeteria chairs lined the wall under the windows. Athletes sat in training gear and winter layers with symptom forms on their knees, too wired to read them and too frightened to put them down. Marcus Ndlovu was there with one finger pressed into his left ear as if he could keep the ringing from getting farther in. Sonja Petrić stood instead of sitting, opening and closing her left hand until the tendons jumped in her wrist. At the far end of the hall, a thrower Lena didn't know was arguing with an orderly in a voice gone thin from trying not to shout.

"Then what am I supposed to do if it happens again?"

"You come back here," the orderly said for what sounded like the fourth time. "You do not connect to anything. You wait."

Another nurse sat behind a folding table that had not been there yesterday. She slid a symptom card toward Lena without looking up.

"Everyone takes one."

"I'm only visiting," Lena said.

The nurse looked up then, taking in Lena's accreditation badge and the set of her face. "Keep it anyway. If anything happens, fill it out before you leave."

Lena folded the card once and put it in her pocket.

The medical bay smelled of antiseptic and the faint mineral trace of surgical polymer, graft work, maintenance fluid, the slightly sweet chemical note that was supposed to be odorless and never quite was. She knew the smell from childhood. The Meridian developmental facility in Zug had smelled like this during every calibration appointment from age four onward. It was one of her earliest sense memories, and she did not find it comforting.

Kai was in the second recovery room, propped up at a low angle on the adjustable bed. His left wrist sat inside a rigid brace that ran from his knuckles to mid-forearm. His right shoulder was held in an immobilization sling. The left side of his face showed six butterfly closures tracking the cuts along his cheekbone and brow, and the surrounding skin had swollen into a mottled yellow-purple that looked worse than yesterday, the bruising still settling into the tissue. He was watching something on the wall display and switched it off when she appeared in the doorway.

"Hey," he said.

"Hello." She stayed in the frame a second longer than necessary. "I don't have to stay. If you'd rather not."

"No, come in. It's fine." He lifted the braced hand in a stiff half-wave that visibly cost him something. "I've been doing nothing for about sixteen hours straight. I'm getting good at it."

She sat in the hard plastic visitor's chair, the kind designed to make your back ache if you stayed longer than ten minutes.

"How's the wrist?" she asked.

"Weird." He held it up. The brace caught the white overhead light. "The grafts are knitting the bone too fast. I can feel it happening in there. Like when your foot falls asleep and the blood comes back. Pins and needles, except inside the bone. Constant."

She nodded. The exact sensation wasn't familiar, but the category was. It belonged to a category she knew too well: a body doing work that should have stayed invisible, the repair itself becoming a new wrongness layered over the injury.

"And the shoulder?"

"Back in the socket. Sling for a week. Petra says full range of motion in ten days." He shifted against the pillow and grimaced as he adjusted the strap. "Which is insane. Should be six weeks. The system doesn't care."

Through the shared wall with engineering, the calibration-bay hum sat at the edge of hearing. Under it came the softer corridor noise from outside: shoes, low voices, a clipboard slapped against a table, someone asking the same question again and getting the same answer.

"I saw Marcus Ndlovu out there," Lena said. "And Sonja Petrić."

"Yeah. Yusuf was here about an hour ago." Kai looked at the ceiling. "He says it's in the network. Everyone who's been calibrated in the past month. So everyone."

The word settled between them and stayed there.

Lena touched the port at her neck.

"Mine has been doing things," she said.

She hadn't planned to say it. The sentence simply arrived, and he was looking at her with an expression that wasn't curiosity or pity or professional concern. It was recognition.

"During training?" he asked.

"During everything. Training, reading, meals." She chose each word with care, as if the order mattered. "I'll be present, and then I won't. When I come back I don't know how long I've been away."

"Your body keeps going when you're out?"

"Yes. In the pool the stroke continues. At meals I keep eating. From the outside it looks normal." She took the folded symptom card from her pocket and flattened it on her knee without meaning to start filling it out. "From the inside it isn't."

"What happens right before?"

Lena looked down at the card. It was all boxes and check marks, with a blank line marked DESCRIBE SYMPTOMS.

"Usually nothing. Sometimes there's pressure here, and then I am not..."

Kai said her name.

The pen was in her hand. She did not remember picking it up. The card lay open on the blanket over his knees, filled in with her compact handwriting.

ABSENT PERIODS.

UNAWARE OF DURATION.

MOTOR TASK CONTINUES.

The letters were pressed hard enough to emboss the paper underneath. Her chair had scraped back from where it had been. Kai's good hand was wrapped around the call button, thumb set against the plastic but not yet pressing.

"How long?" she asked.

"Maybe thirty seconds. Maybe a minute." He kept his voice low, as if louder speech might push her back out again. "You weren't asleep. You just started writing. I asked if you wanted me to get Petra and you said, 'No acute distress. Monitoring is sufficient.'"

Lena stared at the words on the card. They sounded like her and not like her. They were too clean, too detached.

"I didn't decide to say that."

"I know."

Her fingers closed harder around the pen. Only then did she feel the ache where the barrel had cut a groove into the side of her middle finger.

"What did I look like?"

Kai's eyes stayed on her face. "Focused. That's the bad part. You looked like you knew exactly what you were doing."

She folded the card once, badly this time, and set the pen on the tray table.

"When the blackout happened," he said, and stopped. He was looking at his braced wrist. "I couldn't see anything. Or hear anything. And my legs kept going." He shifted against the pillow. "The grafts were still getting commands. They ran me at full speed, probably perfect form, straight into the wall. And I was just—not there." He breathed out through his nose. "The running was fine. My body ran fine. It didn't need me for that part."

Lena looked at the card between them.

"When it happens to me in the pool," she said, "my stroke gets better. More efficient." She touched the back of her neck. "The timing cleans up without me there to make it worse."

"Yeah," he said.

For a while they didn't speak. The room filled with small medical sounds: a monitor tone every thirty seconds, the plastic shift of tubing, distant footsteps in the hall. Snow thickened on the outer window ledge. Kai's wrist kept up its deep internal buzzing while the bone knitted faster than it should have. Lena sat with the folded card in her lap and the groove from the pen still burning in her finger.

A nurse came in to check Kai's vitals. She took his blood pressure and graft integration readings in the ordinary post-trauma sequence. Conversation flattened in the nurse's presence the way it always did when a third person entered a room. Kai asked about the painkillers wearing off. Lena asked whether the queue outside had gotten any shorter. The nurse said no to both and left.

The silence that came back was different from the one before.

"I should go," Lena said after a while.

"Yeah. OK."

At the doorway she stopped.

"The pool will still be closed tomorrow?"

"Yusuf said restricted ops for at least three more days. Maybe five."

"Then I'll probably come back, if that's all right." Her first version of the sentence died halfway out. The second one was smaller. "There isn't much else to do."

"Door's open." A trace of humor came and thinned out before it could become anything bigger. "Not like I'm going anywhere."

That almost made her smile.

The corridor was still full when she stepped back into it. The same nurse behind the folding table was collecting completed symptom cards in a plastic tray under a printed sign that said REPORT EVERY EPISODE. Lena felt the folded paper in her pocket, warm from her hand.

She kept walking.

By the time she reached the residential wing, her hand was at the back of her neck again. It stayed there until she was inside her room with the door shut behind her.

***

Margot sat on the edge of her bed in the dark and thought about vectors. She didn't mean the mathematical kind. She meant transmission.

Petra's announcement had gone through every speaker in the building: no training, no calibrations, no personal interface connections until engineering cleared them. Everyone was to report every sensory lapse and every motor anomaly. By mid-afternoon the medical corridor was jammed with athletes and symptom cards and staff pretending the line meant order instead of fear.

The calibration network wasn't the only way into an athlete's system.

Her room was filling with the grey of a winter afternoon dying in the mountains. The wall display, the stripped-down coaching version rather than the diagnostic suite the athletes got, glowed with baseline metrics: heart rate, neural baseline, integration status, all green. The display didn't know what she was thinking about.

The feed was sold as observation. It never was. Her wrist rig read the athlete and sent corrections back: pacing targets, micro-adjustments, pressure through the return channel. It gave her deeper access than a calibration tune-up and longer contact. If the virus could ride a routine handshake, the feed was a door propped open.

She ran the list in her head. Tomas Reyes had done five sessions in thirty-two days. Lena Morozova had done two. Amara from the 10K squad had done one. Hendrik, pole vault, had done one. That made nine sessions across four athletes. Nine times her equipment had opened a direct lane into someone else's system.

The thought of the rig made her mouth go wet.

She pressed the heel of her hand against the inside of her wrist where the strap usually sat. The skin there prickled anyway. Want moved through her body fast and stupid: saliva, a tightening low in the gut, the small tremor already starting in her fingers.

She should tell Yusuf.

The action itself was easy. Yusuf was in the calibration bay mapping the virus through the systems he could see. His map would be wrong without her. Feed sessions didn't touch his network. They ran through her personal hardware on a separate channel his logs couldn't reach. If the feed was carrying contamination between athletes, Marcus Ndlovu and Sonja Petrić and the rest of them were waiting on information she already had.

Margot picked up her phone. The screen lit her face in the dark. Yusuf's name was two taps away.

Her other hand was already in the bedside drawer.

The feed cuff lay next to a charging cable and two spare batteries. She touched the contact pads with her thumb. The want got worse.

Telling Yusuf meant names, dates, durations. It meant telling him Tomas Reyes had hit two hundred and five beats per minute under her and she'd kept the return channel open because the feed felt best right there, at the edge where the body stopped hiding anything. It meant telling him she had overridden red warnings, muted safety prompts, and written altitude fatigue in a debrief after she'd pushed Tomas past the margin and watched him clip a hurdle.

It meant telling him about Lena too: the sync spikes, the stretches where the stroke stayed clean while something in the data went missing, the note Margot had entered after one session because she wanted another lap tomorrow: acceptable drift.

Petra would want the logs. Yusuf might not ask first, but Petra would. The files would show connection depth, manual overrides, suppressed alerts, every extra minute Margot had stayed in the channel after coaching stopped and appetite took over. They would show the second vector. They would also show exactly what kind of coach she was.

She pictured handing all of that over and felt the stupid animal part of herself recoil, not from guilt, from loss. There would be no feed, no borrowed lungs, no legs loading under her before a jump, no clean hit of someone else's body arriving stronger and sharper than her own.

In the corridor outside, someone's door slammed. Margot didn't move.

The phone was still lit in one hand. The cuff was in the other.

She could call Petra instead. Petra was medical. Petra cared about damage. But Petra was also thorough enough to peel the whole thing open, the kind of doctor who would ask for the logs, read every line, see Tomas at 205, see the caution bands overridden, and see that Margot had known there was another transmission route and had waited anyway.

She saw Marcus with his hand over his ear, Sonja opening and closing her fist, Kai's face held together with butterfly closures. All of them were real. So was the want.

Margot put the cuff back in the drawer.

Then she put the phone beside it and shut both away.

Her hand stayed on the wood a second too long. The tremor in her fingers did not stop. Neither did the wetness in her mouth.

She lay back on the bed fully dressed and stared into the dark, thinking about the first second after connect, and she did not call anyone.
