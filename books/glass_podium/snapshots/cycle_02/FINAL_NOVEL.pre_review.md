# Glass Podium

# Chapter 1

The shuttle from Meiringen climbed the switchbacks in low gear, and Kai sat in the back with his bag between his knees and watched the road fold away behind each turn. Snow packed both shoulders of the road, and the guardrails were crusted white. The driver hadn't spoken since the valley floor, just worked the wheel through the hairpins with the practiced indifference of someone who drove this road four times a day and didn't need conversation to do it. A small mercy. Kai had used up his supply of pleasant talk on the Zurich connection, chatting with a woman who'd recognized him from a Meridian promo spot and wanted to know what it felt like. Running enhanced. Like running, he'd told her. But faster. She'd laughed as if he'd made a joke.

The altitude registered at the second hairpin. Not dramatic, just a tightness behind his sternum, breath becoming labor instead of habit at two thousand and change meters. His lungs would catch up in a few days. The spinal neural bridge had already caught up. If he paid attention, he could feel a faint warmth running along his back, the hardware recalibrating his oxygen distribution before his body had finished registering the deficit. The bridge was always ahead of him. That was the point.

Snow-covered firs lined the road, bowed under weight, thinning as the altitude climbed. The light was going. Late January in the Bernese Alps meant the sun started quitting around four, and the sky above the ridgeline was already the pale, exhausted grey of a winter afternoon giving up early. Kai pressed his forehead against the cold window and watched the tree line drop away.

The Kirchberg Institute appeared around the final curve. A campus of concrete and glass set into the mountainside: training halls, residential blocks, a medical wing with windows catching the last of the daylight. Not beautiful, but functional, the kind of place that assumed you were there to work and had designed every surface accordingly. Floodlights had already come on along the approach road, and the facility's glass facade reflected them back in flat panels of white that made the building look like it was generating its own weather.

The shuttle stopped at the main entrance. Kai stepped out and the cold hit his face, the dry metallic cold of high altitude, thinner than Indiana winter, sharper, with none of the damp heaviness that meant snow was actually coming. This cold was just the air being honest about its temperature. Negative twelve, maybe. His breath came out solid.

Inside, the lobby was warm, carpeted, aggressively neutral. A receptionist scanned his credentials and handed him a room key and an orientation packet in a folder embossed with the Meridian BioSystems logo. The folder was thick enough to suggest someone at corporate believed paperwork was a substitute for conversation.

"Dinner's at seven in the cafeteria. Dr. Qadir will see you for your initial calibration at eight."

"Great. Thanks."

"Welcome to Kirchberg, Mr. Brennan."

He followed corridor signs to the residential wing. The corridor was fluorescent-lit, with doors on both sides, each fitted with a small digital display showing room status. Most were blank, dark rooms, empty, or athletes who'd disabled their readouts. A few showed scrolling biometric data visible through half-open doors. Heart rate, graft integration, neural latency. The casual broadcast of what would have been private medical information anywhere else. Here it was like leaving your shoes outside your door.

He passed a woman stretching in her doorway, legs in a split that ran the full width of the frame with the mechanical precision of a compass opening. She nodded at him without interrupting, her calves showing the same engineered definition his did, bilateral symmetry too clean to be the product of genetics and training alone. Her eyes tracked him with the flat focus of someone whose visual processing was running at a higher refresh rate than his. He nodded back and kept walking.

The corridor cut through a connecting wing between residential and medical. Through a glass partition he could see a corner of what had to be the calibration bay: reclining chairs, monitor banks, the blue standby glow of diagnostic screens in a room that looked unoccupied. The hum was louder here, a bass throb pushing up through the floor so that by the time he heard it, it was already in his sternum and the arches of his feet. Further on, a second glass wall overlooked the pool deck: fifty meters of chlorinated water under flat white light, the surface so still it looked solid. A swimmer in a green cap sat on the far edge with her feet in the water, not moving, not preparing, just sitting there with the patience of someone waiting for something she expected to take a long time.

The room was spartan and adequate. It had a bed, a desk, a bathroom, and a window facing the eastern ridge. The wall-mounted diagnostic display was already active, scrolling data from his enhancement system: heart rate, core temperature, synthetic graft integration, neural bridge latency. All green, everything functioning. He dropped his bag on the bed and stood at the window. Snow fell in the floodlights' periphery. The mountains beyond were shapes now, losing detail to the dark. Warm air kept hissing through the vent over the door, a dry mechanical breath that made the room feel occupied even with nobody else in it.

He'd been to training facilities before, Colorado Springs, the Meridian campus outside Portland, altitude work in Flagstaff. They'd all had the same basic architecture of purpose: track, gym, medical, cafeteria, sleep. But those places had natural athletes mixed in with the enhanced, and the mix created a visible gradient you could use to locate yourself. You knew where you stood. At Kirchberg, there was no gradient. Every body in the building was rebuilt. Enhancement wasn't the variable here. It was the baseline. The woman doing splits in her doorway hadn't been showing off. She'd been stretching before dinner.

Kai sat on the bed and ran his thumb along the inside of his left thigh, pressing until he found the seam. The synthetic muscle graft sat under the skin without a scar to mark it, but the texture changed where engineered fiber met natural tissue. A faint ridge ran from mid-thigh to just above the knee. The right leg had the same seam in the same place. Below the knee, the calf grafts had their own. Bilateral symmetry. His legs looked better than they ever had when they were entirely his, the proportions a little too balanced, the muscle definition a little too even, legs a sculptor would give an athlete who existed only in reference photos.

Three years since the reconstruction. Two ACLs and a spinal compression fracture at twenty-seven, the kind of injury that ended natural careers because natural bodies didn't come back from it, not fully, not to world-record form. Meridian had offered the rebuild six weeks after the surgery. Full reconstruction: synthetic grafts in both legs, spinal neural bridge to replace the compromised nerve pathways, visual processing enhancement for reaction time. He'd signed in a hospital bed in Indianapolis with his mother sitting in the corner not saying anything and his agent on speaker saying everything.

He was faster now than he'd been at twenty-five, when the 200-meter record had been his and the body had been too.

***

The indoor track was a 200-meter oval under high ceilings with acoustic dampening that didn't quite work. Footfalls carried, each one a clean report off the rubber surface followed by a fast, shallow echo. Kai walked in at nine the next morning and the sound of other athletes warming up was like listening to rain on a drum. Four runners were on the infield stretching and jogging short bursts, and a coach stood by the finish with a tablet and a stopwatch. The air was altitude-controlled and warmer than outside, and it smelled of rubber and the particular chemical cleanliness of a facility that was maintained rather than lived in.

He changed in the locker room and worked through his warm-up sequence: hip flexors, hamstrings, calf grafts. The grafts stretched differently than natural tissue. There was compliance but no complaint, a willingness in the synthetic fibers that felt cooperative rather than resistant. His real quads still ached when he pushed too deep. The grafted sections just extended, obliging, as though the muscle had already read the script.

The qualifying heat was a formality: five runners, posted times, a gate officer with a starter pistol and the unhurried manner of someone who would be doing this six more times today. Kai set his blocks at the line and sank into the crouch, hands on the rubber, weight forward on his fingertips. His visual processing enhancement activated without being asked. The lane ahead sharpened, depth of field narrowing, peripheral detail dimming to irrelevance. He hadn't triggered it. The system had read the posture, the setting, the context, and done what it always did at a start line.

The gun fired.

He came out of the blocks and his legs drove him into the curve. The synthetic muscle fired in a sequenced cascade, calf, quad, hip, and the power was clean and immediate and felt like his own effort in the same way that driving a car fast felt like running: the speed was real, the direction was yours, and somewhere in the middle was an engine doing the parts you couldn't. Through the curve, leaning, the centrifugal force was managed by something in his hips and lower back that anticipated the angle before he thought about banking. The rubber passed beneath him in a blur of red-brown. Into the straight now, full speed, and this was where the quiet lived. There was no burn in his legs, no oxygen debt building in his chest, no negotiation between what the lungs could supply and what the muscles demanded. Just his body moving at a speed that should have felt like violence, feet barely touching the surface, arms pumping with a rhythm he wasn't setting, and felt instead like coasting. Like being carried very fast in a direction he'd agreed to.

He crossed the line. His body decelerated through the curve past the finish, and the transition from sprint to jog was seamless: no shock, no buckled stride, no sudden announcement of oxygen debt. The synthetic grafts downshifted smoothly, and his breathing barely changed. He used to finish the 200 and fold over, hands on knees, lungs screaming, vision spotted at the edges. That was the natural version. Now he ran the fastest 200 meters of his life and his body's first response was to inform him, politely, that it could go again.

The clock said 19.02.

Personal best by three hundredths. The other runners were still finishing. The gate officer noted the time on his tablet. A coach on the infield looked up, registered the number, nodded once, and went back to his stopwatch. One of the other runners, a hurdler from the Houston facility whose name Kai knew but couldn't find right now, jogged past and said, "Nice run," and Kai said, "Thanks," and that was the full extent of the event. A man had just run faster than anyone had run 200 meters indoors, and the appropriate institutional response was a nod and a noted time.

Kai stood at the edge of the track with his hands on his hips and tried to locate the race in his body. The usual markers weren't there. No residual burn in the quads, no trembling in the hamstrings, no gratitude from his lungs for the air they were now getting. His heart rate was settling into a rhythm that felt more like waking from a nap than recovering from a flat-out sprint. He'd been in the blocks ten seconds ago. Now he was standing here, breathing normally, and the distance between those two facts felt like someone had edited out the middle of a film and left him watching the credits.

He jogged the cool-down curve and his heart rate was already settling. The spinal bridge managed recovery the way a thermostat managed temperature: smooth, automatic, without consulting him. Sweat cooled on the back of his neck. The rubber under his shoes had a faint chemical bite to it, and he focused on that smell because it was something his senses were doing without optimization, just his nose being a nose, and even that felt thin, the sensation arriving and departing with less weight than it should have carried.

Nineteen-oh-two. Faster than anything he'd run in his natural career. Faster than the version of him that had set world records on legs made of what he'd been born with. The number was correct and the clock was correct and his legs had done the work and his lungs had done the work and his spine had done the work and all of it was functioning as designed and he had been present for the entire ten-point-something seconds of it.

He just couldn't tell which parts had been his idea.

He showered, changed, and ate lunch in the cafeteria. Institutional food: grilled chicken, rice, vegetables steamed past the point of having personality. The windows showed mountains in midday winter light, the sun a flat white presence behind a veil of cloud that hadn't committed to being overcast. The upper ridges held the light for a moment longer than the valleys, white against grey. Athletes ate in clusters. The conversations Kai overheard were ordinary: sleep quality, supplement protocols, someone's ongoing battle with the facility's hot water system. A woman two tables over was arguing quietly on her phone about a missed delivery. A young guy eating alone, twenty-one maybe, with a decathlete's broad-shouldered build and the slightly unfinished proportions of someone whose enhancement was recent enough that his body hadn't fully calibrated around it, was reading something on his tablet with the concentrated focus of a student who'd be tested on it later. He had an energy bar half-eaten beside his plate, forgotten in favor of whatever he was reading.

Kai's phone buzzed. His mother, texting from Lafayette. A photo of snow on the front porch, the specific yellow-grey Indiana snow that clung to everything, covering the porch railing and the mailbox and the edge of the welcome mat she'd had since he was in high school.

*Probably colder here than where you are*

He typed: *Doubt it. 2100 meters up here*

*Is that high? I don't know what that is*

*About 7000 feet. Cold*

*How was the run?*

*Good. New personal best*

*That's wonderful honey. Call me this week ok?*

He set the phone down. His right hand was resting on the table and he noticed, in the distracted, half-attentive way of someone catching a familiar movement out of the corner of their eye, that his fingers were tightening around the water glass. He hadn't reached for it. The urge for water had barely formed when his wrist turned and the glass left the table. The movement was correct. That made it worse.

He drank. The water was cold. He set the glass down and looked at his hand.

It was nothing. A fraction of a second. The kind of thing that happened below the threshold of attention a hundred times a day. He would not have noticed it six months ago, or three months ago. He'd started noticing it in Portland, and then in Flagstaff, and now here, at a table in a cafeteria in the Swiss Alps, the motion arriving a fraction ahead of the wanting.

***

The calibration bay was in the medical wing, a clean room with fluorescent tubes and the permanent low hum of processing equipment, a sound that lived in the walls, below the pitch of conversation but too present to be called silence. Kai had been told you stopped hearing it after a week. He wasn't there yet.

Yusuf Qadir was setting up when Kai arrived at eight. He was short, dark-haired, in real prescription glasses, one of the few people at the facility who'd declined enhancement for himself. He moved around the calibration rig with the comfort of someone who spent most of his waking hours in this room, adjusting monitor angles, tapping commands at a console, a grey diagnostic cable looped over one shoulder that he hadn't found a use for yet.

"Brennan. Come in, sit down." He gestured at the rig, a reclining chair boxed in by screens and connector ports, a setup that could have passed for a dentist's chair if dentists needed six monitors and a firmware interface. "How was the trip in?"

"Long."

"Always is. Zurich connection?"

"Yeah."

"God, yeah, that layover. Right, so." He patted the chair's headrest. "First calibration since Portland, which means I need a full baseline. About forty-five minutes, maybe an hour if your graft firmware's been running updates I don't know about. You good with that?"

"I'm good."

Kai settled into the chair. The surface was medical-grade vinyl, body-temperature, shaped to distribute his weight evenly across his back and legs. Yusuf attached sensor pads to his legs, inner thigh, outer calf, just above both ankles, his hands moving with the unhesitating precision of someone who'd done this ten thousand times and no longer thought about where to put them. Then he pressed a flat contact pad against the small of Kai's back so the rig could sync with the bridge through the skin. The pad caught with a soft click, and Kai felt a brief, clean pressure at the base of his spine, a sensation that would have been uncomfortable if it lasted longer than a second.

"You'll feel a handshake. That's me saying hi to your bridge."

"Felt it."

"Brilliant. Right." Yusuf dropped into his chair and scrolled through the incoming data, his face lit by the console's blue-white glow. He had the focused, slightly rumpled look of an engineer at the end of a long day who still had three things left to do and was prioritizing by interest rather than urgency. "Any changes since Portland? Pain, numbness, anything with range of motion?"

"No. Everything's been fine."

"Good. Graft integration's solid, both legs. Your calf firmware pushed an update last week. Did you catch that?"

"I felt a warmth in my lower legs for about ten minutes. Wednesday, I think."

"Wednesday, yeah, that tracks. Routine optimization pass. Nothing to worry about, it just recalibrated your calf response to some updated baseline data. Happens automatically." He scrolled further. "Bridge latency's within spec. Visual processing nominal. How's the visual been? Any artifacts?"

"Like what?"

"Halos around light sources, depth-of-field lag, colour banding. Some athletes get this thing where the world goes slightly flat for a second. Hard to describe. Like someone compressed the depth out of it."

"No, nothing like that."

"Good." Yusuf tapped a command and the sensor pads on Kai's legs came alive with a low vibration. "I'm going to map your graft response times. This bit's automatic. Just try to stay relaxed."

He stayed still. A calibration pulse moved through his left calf, a contraction he hadn't initiated, the muscle tightening and releasing in a wave that ran from ankle to knee. Then the right calf. Then both quads, one after the other. Then the hamstrings. His body twitched and responded and twitched again, and Kai lay there looking at the ceiling tiles and let it happen. Each pulse was a small theft, his muscle doing something at someone else's instruction, and each time the muscle released, the control returned to him. Each time control returned he noticed the seam of the handover. The system giving him back what was already his.

This was the part that never made the interviews. The lying still. The being adjusted. The specific intimacy of letting another person tune the parts of your body you couldn't reach yourself, while you stared at the ceiling and felt your legs twitch on a schedule you didn't set.

Yusuf worked in focused silence for a while, scrolling data, occasionally leaning over to reposition a sensor pad. He had a habit of muttering to the data under his breath, not words, just small sounds of acknowledgment, the audible equivalent of nodding at something on a screen. When he moved a sensor on Kai's thigh, his fingers found the graft seam by touch, without looking.

"Right, so," Yusuf said after about twenty minutes.

His tone had shifted. Still easy, still conversational, but there was a weight under it now, the way a voice changes when the data says something the person was hoping it wouldn't.

"Your proprioception scores."

"What about them?"

"They're drifting." He angled the console screen toward Kai. Numbers, graphs, a timeline. Two trend lines, one blue, one orange, that had been tracking close together six months ago and were now separating. The gap between them was small but consistent, and it widened at the right edge of the graph.

"Blue line is your conscious motor intent. That's the signal your brain generates when you decide to move. Orange is actual motor execution, when the graft fires." He pointed at the gap. "In a healthy integrated system, these should overlap. Your brain says move, the graft moves, same moment. But over the last three months, the orange has been leading. Not by a lot. Forty, fifty milliseconds."

"So the graft is moving before I tell it to."

"Not exactly. Your system has predictive subroutines. They're supposed to be there. They're a feature. They reduce response latency. What they do is watch your motor patterns, learn your habits, and start prepping the muscle before the full conscious command comes through. You're reaching for a glass, the system recognizes the reach pattern halfway through your intent and gets the muscle ready a few milliseconds early. In a sprint, that's what gives you those start times. It's meant to be invisible."

"And mine isn't invisible anymore."

"The window's widening. At ten milliseconds, nobody feels it. At fifty, some people start to notice. You're at fifty and trending up." He sat back, took off his glasses, rubbed the bridge of his nose with his thumb and forefinger. Put them back on. "Have you noticed anything?"

"Yeah."

"When?"

"Small things. I go for a glass and the motion's halfway done before the decision feels finished. Door handle, phone, stupid stuff like that. It's not wrong. It's just early."

"How long?"

"Couple months. Maybe longer." Kai looked at the graph on the screen, the two lines diverging. "It's hard to say when it started because, I mean, how do you time a decision? You don't. You decide and you move and they feel like the same thing. So when the move starts happening first, you don't notice right away. You just feel like you're very decisive."

Yusuf almost smiled at that. "Yeah. That's the design problem, isn't it. When the system predicts correctly, it just feels like you. It's only wrong predictions you'd catch. And from your data, it's not predicting wrong. It's predicting early."

Kai looked at the two lines on the screen. The blue one was his intent, his decisions, the electrical fact of wanting to move. The orange was his body doing it. And the orange was winning. Not by much. Not by enough to matter during a race or a training session or any other moment that moved too fast for the gap to register. But in the slow moments, the water glass, the door handle, the phone, fifty milliseconds was a lifetime. It meant the motion could be underway while he was still catching himself wanting it.

"Is it getting worse?"

"The trend is up. Consistent over three months. I'm going to log it as within normal drift parameters, because technically it is. Meridian's spec allows up to eighty milliseconds of predictive lead before they flag it. But I'm setting a tracker so we can compare session to session." He began disconnecting the sensor pads, peeling each one off with a small, precise pull. "If the window keeps opening, I'll talk to Meridian's firmware team about dialing the predictive threshold down."

"And if they don't want to dial it down."

"Then we have a different conversation." Yusuf wound the diagnostic cable around his hand in a neat loop and hung it on its hook by the console. "Look. Your system is working. It's working well. You ran a nineteen-oh-two today on an unfamiliar track at altitude after a transatlantic flight. The system is doing what it was built to do."

"I know."

"But it doesn't feel like yours."

"I didn't say that."

"Nah, you didn't." Yusuf tapped something final on the console and the screens went to standby, the blue light dimming to a low pulse. "Same time Thursday for a follow-up. Get some sleep. Altitude takes about three days to stop messing with your deep cycles."

"Thanks."

"Yeah. Night, Brennan."

Kai walked back to his room through the fluorescent corridor. The facility was quiet now, nine-thirty, athletes in their quarters, the common areas emptied, the cafeteria dark. His footsteps were the only sound except the building's ventilation, thinner here than it had been by the calibration bay but steady enough to sand the edges off each step. He listened to his own gait on the hard floor: left foot, right foot, even and smooth, the synthetic grafts managing his stride with the consistency his natural legs had never quite achieved. He'd always been slightly dominant on the left side. Now he wasn't. The grafts had corrected the asymmetry without asking.

His room was dark except for the wall display's glow. Green readouts, all systems nominal, the system reporting on itself in the same quiet room where the person it was installed in was trying to fall asleep. He undressed, brushed his teeth, stood at the window. The mountains were invisible, just darkness and the faint reflection of the diagnostic display behind him, green numbers floating in the glass where the Bernese Alps should have been.

He thought about the graph. Two lines diverging. Forty to fifty milliseconds. A gap he hadn't been able to name until Yusuf drew it on a screen, and now it had a shape and a slope and the slope was up.

He reached for the light switch. The plastic met his fingertips while the decision was still forming, and then the room went dark.


# Chapter 2

At five-thirty the overhead lights were still climbing toward full brightness. The facility brought them up on a schedule: dim at five, half-bright by five-twenty, full fluorescent by six. In that first stretch the water looked darker than it was, lane lines soft in the gloom, ceiling lost above in shadow. Pools had looked like this for as long as Lena Morozova could remember. Kirchberg's was newer than the one in Zug where she'd grown up, cleaner, better regulated, but the fundamentals were the same. Fifty meters of chlorinated water. Lane ropes. Pace clock. The smell already in the back of her throat before her feet touched the deck. Under the surface, her own breathing got loud enough to fill the world.

In the women's room, the cubicle sensors logged weight, hydration level, and baseline heart rate the moment she stepped onto the scale. The data went straight to the coaching system. After eighteen years inside programs like this, the transfer barely registered.

The pool deck felt cool under her bare feet. At lane four, her lane on the rotation schedule, she set down her water bottle and pull buoy. Dara Lund was already at the wall of lane five, adjusting her goggles with small precise movements. Three adjustments, always three: left strap, nose bridge, right strap. Then came the cap pulled over her ears and the two rolls of her right shoulder, working loose whatever had been recalibrated over winter break. At Christmas a card from Bergen had arrived for the whole floor, not for Lena specifically, with a photograph of Dara's mother's garden under snow. It was still pinned to the hallway bulletin board. No one had taken it down.

"Morning," Dara said.

"Morning."

They got in.

Warm-up was four hundred freestyle at moderate pace, building through the second half. Off the wall, the familiar compression closed around Lena: water against skin, pressure from the surface above, lane rope on the left, tiled floor below, body settling into the stroke the neural link had been shaping since childhood. Shoulder rotation, catch, pull, hip-driven kick. The coaching files called it mechanically optimal. Since fifteen she'd read those files the way other people read medical charts or school reports, clinical pages about someone who happened to be her. Usually the gap between what the files described and what she felt in the water was small enough not to matter.

The main set was eight two-hundreds at race pace, forty seconds rest. On the first repeat the link disappeared into usefulness, handling pacing, stroke timing, muscle recruitment, all the quiet labor below decision. Inside that work she could still locate herself: push harder here, hold the streamline longer off the wall, breathe every three instead of two because the altitude at Kirchberg thinned her oxygen uptake and the link kept trying to stretch the breath cycle. She preferred three. The rest happened where it always had, in a place too old and too intimate to name.

The times came in tight. 2:08.3, 2:08.1, 2:07.9, 2:08.0. Slight fade on the fourth, nothing unusual for this point in the block. At the wall she checked the clock, pulled in air, and pushed off for the fifth.

The first hundred meters were fine. Pull, kick, breathe, turn. Lactate sat heavy in her legs, but it was familiar heaviness, something the link factored into pace. Fifty meters remained, then the wall.

At the wall she came in, tucked, rotated.

It was wrong.

Not wrong like a mistake. Wrong like a different answer to the same question. Her body folded tighter than she'd asked it to, came off the wall deeper, lengthened the streamline, drove into a four-beat dolphin instead of the three-beat pattern she'd been using since January. The push-off angle bit steeper. The water colder that far down. By the time she knew what the turn was, the turn had already chosen itself.

November. The firmware update they'd tried for three weeks, then rolled back because the deeper push-off cost too much oxygen late in the eight-hundred. She'd trained that variant and watched the coaches remove it, or thought she had. The link had kept it. Now it had selected it without any conscious input from Lena Morozova.

At the surface, everything stopped. Legs still. Arms hanging. Mid-lane, with the set unfinished and the wall clock still running, she floated while water kept moving around her: Dara's wake in the next lane, recirculation pushing at her calves, lane rope ticking softly against the chop.

Her heart was high from the set, not from the stop. The stop itself had been quiet. One moment she was swimming; the next she had taken her hands off the wheel and let the body drift. Above the surface the pool turned back into a room: breath echoing, gutter slapping, Dara's stroke muffled two lanes over, chlorine bitter at the back of her tongue.

From the far wall Dara surfaced, pushed her goggles to her forehead, and looked down the lane. When Lena didn't start again, she swam back.

"You okay?"

"Yes," Lena said. "Cramp."

"Calf or foot?"

"Calf. Left side. It's already loosening."

Dara nodded and hung on the lane rope with one arm. "I had one two weeks ago during threshold work. Left calf. Yusuf said it was a hydration flag. The system sent me a fluid reminder every forty minutes for the rest of the day."

Nothing in her face suggested that this was strange.

"Do you want me to get someone?"

"No, it's fine. Thank you."

"I'm going to do the last three."

Goggles down. One last adjustment to the left strap. Dara pushed off.

Her stroke was long and clean, the kind of efficient that looked effortless because the effort had been engineered out of sight. Bergen used a different calibration philosophy from Meridian, but the result in the water was close enough.

There was no cramp. Her legs were fine. There had been only a flip turn executed from a pattern the link chose without her, and about a second of swimming in which the person inside Lena Morozova had not been the one swimming.

Water bottle in one hand, pull buoy under the other arm, she went to the cool-down lane at the far end of the deck. The observation window stayed dark this early; Margot wouldn't be in until eight. Four hundred easy followed. Every stroke belonged to her. Usually that ownership wasn't something she had to verify. This morning it was. The water felt normal now. That was the problem. It had felt normal during the turn too.

---

Next to the pool sat the neural coaching suite, small and windowless, with two reclining stations, a wall of displays, and the faint chlorine residue everyone carried in from the deck. Full fluorescent lighting. A clock hung on one wall. The coat rack stood empty. Processing hardware hummed behind the panels.

Margot Voss was already at the main station, scrolling through morning telemetry. A paper coffee cup balanced on the arm of her chair. Her feed interface, the flat silver wrist band she used during live sessions, rested dark against her right wrist. Reviews used recorded data, not live feed. One shoe lay off under the station while her stockinged foot hooked the chair rung, the posture of someone who spent most mornings in this room.

"Morning," Margot said without turning. "Set feel good?"

"First four, yes. I stopped the fifth."

"I saw. The cramp." She pulled up the session log. "Sit."

The athlete's station reclined for bodies that needed to lie still while the system read them. For a review Lena stayed unplugged; the data was already in the log.

"Show me the fifth repeat."

Margot brought it up. At the wall the two traces split: Lena's intended turn on the left, the November variant on the right. Standard tuck and shallow push-off against the deeper angle, tighter fold, four-beat dolphin. A small marker sat over the breach.

1.3 seconds.

"There."

"Yeah." Margot zoomed in. "Stored variant. November rotation. The link thought it was the faster answer and took it."

"I didn't."

"I know. I'm telling you what it did." Margot tapped the marker. "One-point-three puts you inside drift. Acceptable range."

"It made the call before I did."

"Yes." She said it without emphasis, as if confirming a split time. "The engine keeps comparing patterns in the background. Sometimes it reaches for one. This one was old, but it still scored high."

"It felt different."

Margot looked over. "Different how?"

Lena took a moment. Precision mattered. "Like I was behind it. The turn had already started."

"That's the gap." Margot pulled up six weeks of drift data. Today's mark sat in the middle of a pale green band. "You've been between point-eight and one-point-five all block. Today was one-point-three. You felt it more sharply, that's all."

"That's all."

"Feeling it isn't the same as being out of spec." Coffee. Another tap on the screen. "If this starts sitting above two seconds, Yusuf gets it for recalibration. Right now the chart says your system is doing what it's built to do."

"Done." Margot made the note. "Get some food. Your protein numbers are low."

Chlorine still clung to Lena's hair and the creases of her hands when she left the room. Eighteen years in the water had made the smell invisible most of the time. In moments like this it returned all at once, lifting off skin as though she were carrying the pool out with her.

It wasn't sleep. Her eyes had stayed open. The room hadn't changed. It wasn't daydreaming either; daydreaming at least left a trail, some chain of association to walk back. This left nothing. In the mirror there was only herself: brown eyes, the faint mark on her left temple from the fall on the pool deck when she was twelve, jaw set in the expression she wore when deciding whether something counted as a problem. She closed the menu.

Margot sat on the edge of her bed in the dark and thought about vectors. She didn't mean the mathematical kind. She meant transmission.

Petra's announcement had gone through every speaker in the building: no training, no calibrations, no personal interface connections until engineering cleared them. The calibration network wasn't the only way into an athlete's system.

Her room was filling with the grey of a winter afternoon dying in the mountains. The wall display, the stripped-down coaching version rather than the diagnostic suite the athletes got, glowed with baseline metrics: heart rate, neural baseline, integration status, all green. The feed was sold as observation. It never was. Her wrist rig read the athlete and sent corrections back: pacing targets, micro-adjustments, pressure through the return channel. The thought of the rig made her mouth go wet.

Her other hand was already in the bedside drawer.

Petra would want the logs. Yusuf might not ask first, but Petra would. In the corridor outside, someone's door slammed. Margot didn't move.

The phone was still lit in one hand. The cuff was in the other.

She could call Petra instead. Petra was medical. Petra cared about damage. She saw Marcus with his hand over his ear, Sonja opening and closing her fist, Kai's face held together with butterfly closures. All of them were real. So was the want.

Margot put the cuff back in the drawer.

Then she put the phone beside it and shut both away.

Her hand stayed on the wood a second too long. The tremor in her fingers did not stop. Neither did the wetness in her mouth.

She lay back on the bed fully dressed and stared into the dark, thinking about the first second after connect, and she did not call anyone.


# Chapter 7

The brace was off. Petra had cleared him to leave it off for short stretches, and the graft-driven repair had run six weeks too fast, leaving an itch deep in the bone where new tissue met old. The spot held a busy, needling pressure, his body hurrying through a timetable he hadn't agreed to.

The pigeon stayed. Kai drank his coffee. The afternoon didn't move.

The pool at the Centre Sportif was twenty-five meters, four lanes, and low-ceilinged enough that humidity stained the concrete. The air held chlorine, the slap of flip turns, and bubbles on the exhale. It was not Kirchberg's silent fifty-meter pool. A retired man did breaststroke with his head carefully above water while two teenagers in the far lane tried to splash each other without admitting that was what they were doing.

Lena swam. The stroke was clean but not optimized. Her catch slipped a fraction on entry. The cost was two seconds per hundred meters, and she still timed every session.

This was her sixth two-hundred. Every third stroke she turned to breathe and felt herself choosing it: head, air, back to streamline. The quiet in her head was not silence anymore. It was just her own mind, messy and associative, with the occasional half-second blank where a word should have been. By July she'd stopped calling that frightening. It was ordinary now.

Halfway through the fifth fifty, the left arm smoothed. It wasn't a blackout. It wasn't full desync. It was just the old correction surfacing for less than a second, the stroke going cleaner than she'd asked for. Lena felt it and stopped at the wall.

At the gutter she waited, breathing hard, while water slapped tile in the chopped rhythm of the small pool. The smoothness didn't come back.

This was what the patch had given her: margin. She could tell the difference soon enough to stop. Then she could push off again under her own imperfect catch and take the lost two seconds as the price of being there for it.

At the end of the set, the coach at the Montreux center was still in the bleachers with a book about Mediterranean cooking and a clipboard. Isabelle did not micromanage. At the start of each session she gave Lena a set and looked up only to mark times. The port at the base of Lena's skull had never prompted a question. Lena suspected Isabelle thought it was cosmetic.

"Good set," Isabelle said, not looking up from the book. "One-oh-nine on the last one."

"Thanks."

"You want another?"

"No, I'm done."

Isabelle made a note on her clipboard. Lena climbed out and stood on the deck, water running off her suit onto the concrete. The retired breaststroke man was still going, his head held above the surface with the determination of someone who believed the pool was trying to get his hair wet. The teenagers had given up swimming entirely and were sitting on the edge of the far lane arguing over something on one of their phones.

In the changing room, Lena sat on the bench in her suit, dripping, and read the article on her phone.

Yusuf had published the analysis three days ago on ResearchGate. The title was long and flat in academic engineering: "Structural Analysis of Firmware Corruption Patterns in Shared Bio-Integrative Calibration Networks: Correspondence with Proprietary Development Tool Signatures." Under it sat two public repositories: the virus-analysis files, and a de-identified extract from the athlete biometrics in Yusuf's archive. His name was on it. Kirchberg was listed as his former affiliation.

The article had been live for three days and Meridian's stock had dropped eleven percent. Lena had read that in the business section of the NZZ after opening the link. Meridian had issued a statement denying the findings and announcing an internal review. The Swiss Federal Office of Sport had opened a preliminary inquiry. The European Athletics Enhancement Authority had requested the files everyone else could already download.

It was not proof. Anyone technical enough could rerun the public analysis and check the correspondence between the corruption patterns and Meridian's development tools. The biometric archive was public too, which meant the data Yusuf had gathered for Farah could be studied outside Meridian. What stayed shut was Meridian's own archive: internal records, full calibration histories, whatever might turn correspondence into attribution. The inquiry was aimed at that door.

Lena texted Yusuf. Just: *Saw it.*

His reply came four minutes later: *Yeah. Farah says hi.*

Lena sat with the phone in her hand. The changing room smelled like chlorine and old wood and lavender soap. Farah. The reason Yusuf had built the archive in the first place. The public files were not a treatment. Lena knew that. But researchers no longer needed Meridian's permission to start. If the inquiry forced the company open, they might get the rest. If it didn't, the archive was still loose in the world now. Research could continue.

Whether it would help Farah, Lena didn't know. She didn't know enough about Voss-Kessler to pretend otherwise. But Yusuf had written *Farah says hi* as if it were ordinary. His sister was still there, still speaking into the future, and the work he had risked everything for had made it into public hands. It wasn't a victory. It was enough.

Lena toweled off. The towel caught on the small circular port at the base of her skull. She touched it with two fingers, the absent check of a thing that was part of her body but not her.

Dressed, she walked out into August in Montreux. The lake was enormous and flat and the sun was on it and the air smelled like warm stone and the jasmine that grew along the promenade railing near the station. A train to Lausanne left soon. Her hair was still damp from the pool and the chlorine followed her, same as it had when she was four in a calibration pool in Zug, before she knew the system in her head was a system. The smell was the same. The mind inside it had started to sound like hers. She walked toward the station with wet hair and two seconds per hundred meters that were hers.

The treatment facility was in Schwabing, in a converted Jugendstil house on a street lined with linden trees that smelled like August. The house had high ceilings and decorative plaster molding and a garden out back where patients were encouraged to sit in good weather. The art on the walls looked chosen by someone who understood that institutional spaces needed art but not what good art looked like: watercolors of lakes, a photograph of mountains that could have been any mountains, a print of a Klee hung slightly crooked and left that way for all six weeks Margot had been here.

It had been six weeks without the feed. Forty-two days. There was no accumulating pride in it, no sense that each day was another brick in a wall between her and the substance. She counted as she'd counted laps: because number was information, and information was what she had instead of certainty.

The program was called NeuroBalance, and it was one of three facilities in Germany that specialized in neural-interface addiction. Most of the other patients were feed-dependent, people with compulsive relationships to entertainment-grade sensory interfaces, immersive sports simulations and full-body relaxation feeds marketed as wellness tools and functioning as digital opiates. The feeds they described were crude by comparison. They had plugged into recordings. Margot had plugged into people.

On Thursday she tried to explain this to Helga. Helga was forty-three, Bavarian, precise in her English, grammatically careful and slightly over-enunciated. Good therapist or not, Helga did not understand what Margot was telling her.

"The entertainment feeds are recorded," Margot said. Across from Helga's desk, with its small succulent and framed photo of a dog, Margot sat back in the chair and looked at the plant instead of the woman. "Recorded and processed and cleaned up. Like a movie of a body. What I did was live. I was inside someone's actual nervous system while they were using it."

"Yes, you've said this." Helga's pen rested on her notepad. "What I'm interested in today is the distinction between the experience itself and the need to repeat it."

"There's no distinction."

"Can you say more?"

Margot looked at the succulent. It was the kind that didn't need much water, which was probably the point of keeping it on a therapist's desk. "When you plug in, the first thing you feel is their heartbeat. Not yours. It's in your sternum, and your breathing changes to match before you even — your diaphragm just does it. And you're already afraid it's going to end. First second. You're still feeling their heart in your chest and you're already afraid of losing it. There's no 'I had the experience and then wanted more.' You want it while it's happening."

Helga wrote something. The pen moved across the notepad with steady pressure.

"And the memory of the experience," Helga said. "Is the memory also—"

"Intact," Margot said. "I'm sitting here right now and I can feel his hamstrings loading on the approach. The way his eyes locked on the bar — not my eyes, his, but I felt the lock. His whole body was just —" She stopped. "He was twenty-one and everything worked and he could feel the clearance height in his hips before he left the ground. I can feel that right now. My hands are sweating."

"And the crash?"

"That too."

"You can reconstruct that sensation?"

"Yeah." Margot looked at her own hands. "The compression in his spine. The silence when the signals stopped. His legs going dead. I was inside his nervous system when it happened." She was quiet for a second. "For about a week I thought that would fix it. You can't want something that did this to a kid. But if you plugged me in right now I'd say yes. I know what it cost him and I'd still say yes."

Helga was quiet. It was the pause that was supposed to look like reflection but was actually recalculation.

"The wanting," Helga said. "Has it changed in quality over the six weeks? Not necessarily in intensity. In quality."

"No."

"Not at all?"

"It's the same. It was this size on day one and it's this size on day forty-two. Doesn't get bigger. Doesn't get smaller. It's just there. Like furniture."

"We'll continue Thursday," Helga said, and wrote something else, and Margot stood up because the session was over and because there was nothing more to say about it that she hadn't already said.

After group, four people in a circle talking about triggers and coping strategies, during which Margot said reasonable things about mindfulness techniques she didn't believe in and made eye contact with a twenty-six-year-old named Stefan who was trembling through withdrawal from an entertainment feed he'd used eighteen hours a day for two years, Margot walked.

August in Munich was warm, not hot. The lindens on Leopoldstraße were in full leaf, and the air carried that late-summer smell of heated pavement and green and the distant mineral note of the Isar. South through Schwabing she walked, past the university, past cafes where students sat outside with laptops and beer in the late afternoon, past the Siegestor with its stone horses catching the low light. In jeans and a T-shirt and running shoes, she looked like anyone. That was the thing about it. Nobody on this street could see that the woman walking past the monument was carrying the precise sensory memory of another person's spinal cord going silent.

By the time she reached the Englischer Garten, she'd ended up at the Eisbach again, though she hadn't meant to come there today. The Eisbach was a channel of the Isar, and at one narrowing the current formed a standing wave that surfers had been riding for decades. It had become her place by the second week, and she kept coming back.

There were four surfers today. Young, most of them. One woman in a wetsuit despite the warm air, her board shorter than standard, shaped for the wave's geometry. The wave was maybe a meter high, clean, glassy in the late light. The surfers dropped in one at a time from the bank, a sideways leap, the board angling to catch the current, then the rapid adjustment of weight and balance on a wave that never moved forward. The whole thing was stationary surfing. The wave held and the surfer held and the river kept moving underneath them.

The woman in the wetsuit was good. On the wave she cut back and forth with efficient fluidity, weight drops, ankle adjustments, the continuous proprioceptive reading of a moving surface. There was nothing enhanced or linked about her. It was just muscle and board and river and the accumulated hours that made the motion look easy. Her weight sank into a bottom turn and her arm swept out for balance and her torso rotated and for a moment the line of her body was a clean functional expression of physics and intention and practice.

Margot could see the biomechanics plainly: quadricep engagement, dorsiflexion, counter-rotation of the hips. Fifteen years of reading bodies had done that to her. The movement almost reached her, dimly, like music from another room, because the neural pathways the feed had built were still there, still listening for input that wasn't coming.

On the bank, Margot watched. The surfers rode the wave and climbed out and rode it again. The woman in the wetsuit finally lost her line and splashed into the current and hauled herself out laughing, water streaming off her suit, her board tucked under one arm. She shook her hair and said something to the guy waiting to drop in and he laughed too and the sound carried across the water in the warm air.

Margot watched until the light changed and the surfers climbed out one by one and walked away with their boards under their arms and the standing wave kept going, the water folding over itself as it always did whether anyone was riding it or not.

At almost dinner, Margot stood and walked back to the facility. The evening was warm. The lindens smelled like summer. Her legs carried her through the park and along the Leopoldstraße and up the steps of the old house, and the wanting was there, the same size it had been that morning, which was the same size it had been the morning before, which was the same size it was every morning, ordinary and entire, and she walked.
