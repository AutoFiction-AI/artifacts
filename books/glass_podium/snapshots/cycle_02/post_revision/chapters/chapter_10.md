# Chapter 10

The pool was empty except for Lena.

Margot settled into the coaching chair on the observation deck and woke the session display. It was six forty-five a.m. The facility hadn't come online yet. The sprinters would not start until eight. The calibration bays were dark. Below the glass, the pool lay flat under the overheads, ten lanes of hard blue light. Lena stood on the far deck pulling on her cap. When she tucked her hair back, the port at the base of her skull showed for a second: a small round disc, skin-colored, then hidden again.

Before eight, the deck console ran in local mode. It talked to the lane hardware, the athlete's link, and whatever coaching unit was physically paired in the room. Anything medical serious enough to finish its countdown would queue itself for central routing and fire the moment the campus network woke. Until then there was only the room, the water, and whoever had a thumb on the controls.

Margot strapped the feed interface to her right wrist. The pads found her pulse and warmed. Her unit was personal, not institute-issued. It mirrored raw sensory packets by default because coaches were supposed to review them after hard sets, annotate them, dock the unit later, and let the central system keep only what the export profile allowed. She entered the athlete ID. The set was standard aerobic. Pace target 1:04 per hundred. Easy enough on paper. Clean enough to live inside.

She connected.

Air came first. The deck was cool on Lena's bare feet. There was tightness through the shoulders from yesterday. There was also a dry ache behind the eyes that still shouldn't have been altitude after six weeks. Then Lena dove and the water closed over Margot like a second skin.

Lena's stroke always hit her hardest at the catch. That first loaded grip on the water ran up Margot's forearm and into her jaw. Her mouth went wet every time. The body line settled and lengthened. The kick took over in precise narrow beats. At the wall the turn drove heat into Margot's thighs, into muscles she hadn't trusted in years. This was what she used the feed for. It was not insight. It was not coaching. It was the hit when somebody else's body found the right line and hers answered anyway.

The first hundred came in 1:03.8. The second came in 1:04.1. Heart rate held at 162. Stroke rate held at 68. Lena's flip turn at the two-hundred wall was exact, shoulder tucked just enough, kick snapping back into the surface on the third beat.

The third hundred started.

Margot felt the slip before the numbers moved. Lena usually met the water with tiny choices. There would be a fraction more pressure on the left palm. She would hold a breath a beat longer. The turn would adjust for what the last stroke had done. Now those little checks were gone. The hand went in. The body pulled. It kicked. It breathed. Everything landed on time and all of it was dead flat.

Earlier sessions had carried a blink of this. Half a second where the stroke lost its texture and came back. Margot had logged drift and kept going.

This wasn't drift.

The breath pattern kept its count, but the usual flinch at the burn in Lena's chest vanished. The wall contacts hit too hard. The shoulder never gave its protective slack, never offered that tiny hesitation before the push. When Margot nudged the pace down to 1:00, Lena's body answered before any trace of assent or resistance came through the feed. It was like grabbing a handrail and finding nobody's hand on it.

Stroke rate hit seventy. Then seventy-two.

From the deck the stroke still looked clean. Through the feed it felt wrong in three separate places at once: lungs pulling air with no panic in them, legs turning over without fatigue arguing back, arms catching exactly the same pressure every cycle as if pain had been cut out of the lane. Lena was not in the water with Margot anymore. Her body was. The absence had a shape. Not sleep. Not fainting. Not confusion. Something had stepped aside and left the work of performance running.

Margot tightened her grip on the armrest until her knuckles hurt. The clean pull kept landing in her shoulders. Her calves twitched against the floor. She wanted another turn. She pushed the target to 0:58.

Heart rate climbed to 178, then 182.

The yellow warning box lit on the console. Margot barely looked at it. Lena hit the three-hundred wall hard enough that the impact rang through Margot's teeth, then fired off the turn with both legs at once. The push-off burned through her quads. Her own chest started chasing Lena's rhythm, short and fast. The stroke had gone frictionless, certain, empty of Lena. Everything narrowed to open water and force and the next wall coming.

Her heart rate touched 185.

Margot cleared the warning.

Then it hit 188. Then 190.

The box went red this time. Local mode armed a Class III medical page and started a sixty-second countdown in the corner of the screen. If the timer reached zero, the console would lock the session, stamp the event into the morning medical queue, and route it to Petra as soon as the central network came up. Margot killed it at fifty-eight seconds. Her thumb moved before she finished reading. The feed kept giving. Her mouth tasted like chlorine though she was nowhere near the pool. One more length, she thought. She wanted one more wall.

Then it hit 193.

The fourth hundred was worse and better. Lena's hands found the water without search. Her lungs took what they needed and gave nothing back. The whole body ran hot and blank. Margot rode it with her shoulders locked and her thighs shaking under the chair. She knew, distantly, that the local history bar was catching every command she sent: the pace changes, the dismissed countdown, the red alert still open in the stack. It did not stop her.

Then it hit 195.

That got through. Margot dragged the target back to 1:02. Above 195 a heart could skip. Above 195 the session stopped being worth it even to her. The stroke rate fell. The red box stayed red. 193. 191. A second alert opened because the first override had not cleared the threshold breach, only the page. She closed that one too.

Lena reached the wall and stayed there.

One hand was on the gutter and the other on the lane rope. Her mouth was open. Her shoulders jumped with each breath. Water slid off her cap and down her face. Through the feed the return came in pieces: pain first, hot and immediate through the arms; then cold crawling over the back of the neck; then confusion, jagged and late, as if Lena had been dropped into a race already half done.

Margot disconnected.

The feed cut clean. Warm water, loaded muscle, Lena's heart hammering up under her sternum. It was gone. The observation deck came back thin and dry. Her own pulse felt too small. Saliva soured under her tongue. For a second she thought she might throw up.

The skin under the interface was red and ridged when she tore the strap loose. She pressed her left thumb into the welt until it hurt. Below the glass, Lena hung on the rope and stared at the opposite wall like she had never seen the room before.

A minute went by. Then Lena turned her head, slow, searching the deck.

"What lap is this?"

Her voice came up through the audio pickup, flat and tinny in the tile room. No one answered. Margot stayed in the chair and watched Lena look at the water clock, then at the lane line, then back at the wall as if some missing piece might still be hanging there in the air above it.

By the time Lena hauled herself out and sat on the edge with both feet in the water, Margot had the session log open again. Two red alerts sat in the history bar, each one stamped with her own wrist-unit override. The paired device had mirrored the whole morning by default: raw feed depth, heart-rate stream, pace commands, alert dismissals, local timestamps. She tapped each alert and changed the auto-label from sustained HR anomaly to coach-authorized build. Then she reclassified the set from aerobic to mixed-intensity. She added a note field: post-set disorientation after middle-build effort, resolved poolside. Then she switched the export to summary sync only.

Summary sync kept the official record thin. Splits, heart-rate curve, final set classification, coach note. It stripped out the raw feed, the countdown history, and the command log that showed exactly when she had dismissed medical escalation. Anyone looking at the dashboard later would see a hard middle split and a messy recovery. Anyone wanting the ugly version would have to dock her wrist unit and pull the local cache by hand.

Lena bent over on the pool edge, one hand clamped to the port at the back of her neck.

Margot killed the console, dropped the interface into her bag, and stood. Her knees felt hollow. Sweat cooled under her shirt. The feed was gone and her body kept reaching for it anyway.

The corridor to residential took four minutes. Linoleum, fluorescent panels, antiseptic in the vents, stale coffee from the cafeteria wing. A sprinter she did not know was stretching against the wall, calves marked with faint graft seams. They nodded at each other and kept moving.

In her room, Margot sat on the bed and took the interface back out. It was brushed steel, with connection pads still warm from her skin. Four years of use had left them dark at the edges with her oils and her pulse. She rubbed a thumb across one pad, then checked the device memory light. It held solid amber. Unsynced local cache.

She woke the screen.

Session buffer: 07:12.

Raw sensory packets retained.

Local action history retained.

Export profile: summary sync pending dock.

There was a purge option in the lower corner. Two taps, thumbprint confirm, and the raw feed would be gone before anyone in medical knew there had ever been more to ask for. The summary would still post when she docked the unit. The dashboard would stay clean. Petra would have nothing but a hard set and a coach note.

Margot stared at purge and did not touch it.

Instead she opened the buffer.

The replay came back at her with no mercy. The first length of the missing stretch hit through Lena's shoulders and ribs, but the thing inside it was not effort. It was vacancy. The body took the water in complete confidence and offered nothing personal back. No adjustment. No dread. No argument. Margot felt the clean blankness arrive again in her own mouth, her own quads, her own jaw. The exact point where Lena stopped being present and performance went on without her lit under Margot's skin like a drug finding an old vein.

She cut the replay after three strokes.

Her hands shook so badly she nearly dropped the unit. She sat there breathing through her mouth until the room steadied. The purge icon stayed in the corner, patient, unchanged.

The order should have been Petra first, then Yusuf. A Class III event meant raw data and a grounded athlete. Petra would not stop at the summary if Lena reported missing time. She would ask for the device. She would pull the cache herself or hand it to Yusuf and tell him to do it. Then she would get everything: the seven-minute feed depth, the pace jumps, both canceled countdowns, the relabeled alerts, the summary-only export, the coach note that turned a vanished person into post-set disorientation, and the raw segment where Lena's body kept swimming after Lena was gone.

Margot set the unit on her thigh and looked at it the way other people looked at a bottle they had already opened.

She could still tell herself she was waiting for more information. She could tell herself she did not want Petra yanking Lena out of the pool on the basis of one ugly session during a week when everyone at Kirchberg was already half-convinced the virus lived in every ache and skipped beat. She could tell herself imaging first thing tomorrow would change nothing except panic.

The cleaner sentence sat underneath all of it.

If Petra saw the buffer, Petra would take the unit.

Margot put the interface in the nightstand drawer and shut it.

She wanted Lena safe. She wanted the feed more. That was the order it sat in her body.

On the wall display, her heart rate had come down to 84. Her respiration was normal. Enhancement status read none. That line stayed blank. It always would. She had gone to Brisbane in 2028 in an unenhanced body and finished fifth in the 200 fly. Now she sat on a narrow bed in Kirchberg with her own lungs working at an ordinary human rate and a sour ache climbing the back of her throat because ordinary was not enough.

The room stayed bright. She lay back anyway. Ten minutes after disconnect, her legs still gave a small useless tremor now and then, as if they expected another wall. The empty part had started already. It always started fast.

She lasted six minutes before opening the drawer again.

This time she did not replay the missing length. She scrolled the action history instead. Six fifty-two and thirteen seconds: pace target lowered to 1:00. Six fifty-two and forty-one: pace target lowered to 0:58. Six fifty-three and twenty-two: Class III page armed. Six fifty-three and twenty-four: page dismissed by coach unit. Six fifty-four and nine: second red alert issued. Six fifty-four and eleven: alert reclassified. The device kept the sequence plain and ugly and exact. She could not tell whether reading it made her feel better or worse.

If she docked the unit after breakfast, the official system would swallow only the summary. If she did not dock it, the amber light would keep burning and any manual pull would still have the raw cache intact. Either way the evidence sat in steel and memory two feet from her bed because she had not deleted it.

She closed the log and held the unit in both hands until the warmth from her palms took the chill out of the casing.

There were people she could call. Petra, who would go cold and formal and start protecting Lena in ways that would also ruin the training block. Yusuf, who would ask for the device before he asked how she was. Lena, who would hear the lie in Margot's voice before the first sentence was over. Margot did not call anyone.

By eight the campus network would wake fully and the pool schedule would fill with sanctioned movement. Coaches would have clipboards. Athletes would have breakfast in them. Somebody would make a joke about altitude and somebody else would nod because the building ran on people agreeing to the explanation that cost the least.

Margot slid the unit back into the drawer and forced herself to leave it there.

When she finally shut her eyes, the thing she felt most clearly was not guilt. It was hunger with nowhere to go.

---

Lena spent the afternoon trying to find the missing laps.

She started with her body because the body had done them whether she remembered them or not.

After the shower she sat on the closed toilet lid in her room with a towel around her shoulders and checked herself the way Petra checked injured athletes. Skin first. No split knuckles. No blood in the whites of the eyes. Then muscle. Her lats were blown out the way they got after a race-pace back half, not after an aerobic four hundred. The tendons at the front of both shoulders ached from hard wall contact. There was a raw spot on the inside of her lower lip where she had bitten through the turn and not felt it until later. None of that matched what she remembered doing.

At her desk, she pulled up the session telemetry Margot had filed that morning. The display gave stroke rate, splits, heart rate, and neural link activity. The graphs ran clean from start to finish. The second half showed a moderate rise. The summary field carried no desync marker. The interruption flag read none.

There was a coach note.

Post-set disorientation after middle-build effort, resolved poolside.

Lena read it twice. She did not remember any poolside conversation. She remembered the lane rope under her hand, the chlorine taste too sharp in the back of her throat, her lungs trying to climb out through her mouth, and her own voice asking a question she had not meant to say aloud.

What lap is this.

The record said she had swum four hundred meters.

Lena remembered two hundred and fifty.

She remembered the warm-up, the first hundred, the second turn, the beginning of the third, and the flip at 250.

Then the lane rope was under her hand. Her chest tore for air. The pool lights were too bright. Her own question came out of her mouth before she could stop it.

The telemetry said her body had done the rest. Her arms had pulled. Her legs had kicked. The walls had registered contact. Her heart rate had gone into the 190s. The split graph showed a sudden hard middle build she had never been assigned. All of it was real. None of it had been experienced.

Smaller slips had happened before. Twenty minutes gone in her room while a page sat open in front of her. A turn pattern last season's firmware chose for her. Margot had called those within acceptable parameters, and Lena had accepted it because Margot had the data and the authority, and because the program had trained her early to trust whatever could be graphed.

This morning was not that.

She touched the port at the base of her skull. The ceramic edge sat smooth under her thumb, skin-warm, built into her like a joint or a tooth. She had had it since she was four. She pressed harder, as if pressure might give her a clearer answer than the screen did.

The Kirchberg desync protocol sat three menus down. She pulled it up and read the relevant lines again. Class II: intermittent divergence under five seconds. Report within twenty-four hours. Class III: sustained divergence, loss of voluntary motor control, or loss of conscious awareness during link-directed activity. Report immediately. Ground the athlete.

Three minutes was not Class II.

From the protocol page she could open the athlete report form. The button sat there the way an emergency exit sits there, obvious and ugly and hard to use without changing the rest of the day. Lena looked at it, then closed the menu and opened the telemetry again.

The summary view gave her numbers and a story about the numbers. Heart rate spike. Coach-authorized build. Post-set disorientation resolved. Missing time had nowhere to attach itself on the page.

She checked the splits against the training plan Margot had loaded before dawn. The first two hundreds matched. The back half did not. The set was supposed to sit at 1:04 pace. The graph showed the third hundred tightening, then the last hundred breaking under the minute mark before the recovery at the wall. Margot had changed the target in the water. The entry offered no reason.

Lena stared at that for a long time.

The clean explanation would have been that Margot had seen something and tested it. Coaches did that. They changed sets in real time all the time.

The less clean explanation was that Margot had felt something wrong and pushed harder anyway.

Lena saved the session locally, shut the wall display, turned it back on, and opened it again as if the numbers might have altered in the dark.

They had not.

At twelve thirty she went to the cafeteria and stood with a tray she could not make herself fill. The room smelled of coffee, eggs, and bleach from the morning mop. Two cyclists were arguing softly in French over a lactate chart. Someone dropped cutlery at the far end and nobody looked up. Ordinary noise kept happening around her with the insulting confidence of things that had not lost any time.

She took yogurt, fruit, black bread. She ate half the bread and none of the fruit. Her jaw worked. Her stomach accepted the food without opinion. When she looked down at her own hands on the table, they felt temporarily assigned to her.

By one fifteen she was back in her room with the protocol open again.

The report form asked for the same things it always asked for. Time. Session type. Subjective symptoms. Whether the athlete had retained voluntary motor control. Whether conscious awareness had been continuous. Whether the coach had already filed. Whether medical had been informed.

Lena did not fill it yet. She wanted to hear Margot say the morning out loud first. Not because Margot was more likely to tell the truth if asked directly. Because Lena needed to see how much of the truth Margot was willing to leave out.

She found Margot outside the coaching suite later that afternoon. Margot had coffee in one hand and her coaching bag over her shoulder. Her hair was down. She looked tired. The quick coach scan happened as soon as Lena rounded the corner, automatic and clinical.

"Hey," Margot said.

"Can I ask about this morning?"

"Sure."

A technician pushed a cart past them and they both stepped aside. Wheels squeaking. Ventilation humming through the wall.

"I checked the session summary," Lena said. "It says mixed-intensity build."

"Because your middle pace climbed."

"I didn't choose that."

Margot's face went still.

"What are you asking me?"

"Something happened in the water. I can't remember the end of the set."

"Can't remember how?"

"I don't mean blurred. I remember the turn at two-fifty. Then I'm on the rope and the set is over. There's a hundred and fifty meters the graph says I swam. I don't have them."

"Your telemetry looked clean," Margot said. She lifted the cup but did not drink. "Heart rate ran high in the middle. That's intensity. Your stroke held."

"I have the graph. I saw the note."

"Then you saw what I saw."

"No." Lena kept her voice level by force. "I saw what you filed."

For a second Margot almost smiled. Not because anything was funny. Because Lena had named the thing exactly.

"Fine," Margot said. "You want the plain version? The summary doesn't show a Class III. It shows a pace build, a heart-rate spike, and confusion at the wall."

"I lost conscious awareness."

"You think you did."

"I know I did."

"The record doesn't know that."

Lena felt something small and cold settle under her ribs.

"You were on the feed," she said. "You would've felt it."

"I felt your pacing and your stroke," Margot said. Her voice stayed even. "Both went ugly in the middle. That's not the same as a confirmed severe event."

"What did I say when I got out?"

Margot's eyes shifted once to Lena's shoulder, then back to her face.

"You were disoriented."

"That is not what I asked."

Margot let the beat sit there. When she answered, she answered too flatly for kindness.

"You asked what lap it was."

The corridor noise seemed to move farther away. Lena kept her hands at her sides because if she crossed her arms now it would look defensive and she did not want to give Margot anything she could name as nerves.

"Then why is this not Class III?"

"Because the telemetry the system kept doesn't support a Class III classification."

"The protocol says loss of conscious awareness."

"I know what the protocol says."

"Then why are you arguing with it?"

"I'm telling you what happens next if you put those words into the official file." Margot lowered the coffee cup. "Petra sees an awareness-loss report attached to a clean summary. She puts an automatic hold on your pool clearance before the day is over. Imaging, observation, probably a full link review. That part is medicine. After that it stops being medicine and starts being administration."

Lena did not answer.

"An unexplained awareness-loss entry sits on your clearance file until somebody can prove exactly what happened," Margot said. "If the summary still reads nominal, it goes in as uncorroborated severe-event reporting. Meridian sees that. Selection sees that. Everyone with a login sees that. You don't just lose tomorrow morning. You might lose the whole block while they decide whether the problem is your firmware or your reporting."

"That sounds rehearsed."

"It sounds institutional because it is institutional."

"Did I have a severe event or not?"

Margot looked at her. Really looked at her this time. Lena could see the answer living somewhere behind the eyes and being refused passage.

"I think you had drift under pressure and a hard physiological response," Margot said. "I think the middle build went bad. I think if you file it as Class III off this summary, the system will treat you like the unstable variable."

"Because the system is correct."

"Because the system is what decides whether you train tomorrow."

There it was. Not about whether she was right. About who got to name what had happened.

Lena heard herself breathe in through her nose, slow, controlled.

"If I report loss of awareness," she said, "you think they will believe the graph over me."

"Yes."

Margot did not soften the word. That made it worse.

"And you're comfortable with that."

"I'm not comfortable with any of it."

"But you filed the note anyway."

"I filed what the summary supports."

"That is not the same sentence."

Margot's jaw moved once.

"No," she said. "It isn't."

Lena let the silence go long enough that a lesser person would have tried to fill it.

"Do you want me to flag it?" Margot asked at last. "Say the word and I'll file it. But once it's in, it starts moving and you don't get to choose where it stops. Immediate hold. Medical review. Maybe Meridian. Maybe a week of people explaining your own head to you. If you want that, say it."

Lena thought about the calibration rig, Yusuf's equipment mapping her link while she lay still inside it. She thought about a physician asking her to describe three missing minutes while the official graph on the wall stayed clean. She thought about a selection note carrying the phrase uncorroborated severe-event reporting into a season with four months left before the Games. She thought about the way the program had trained her since childhood to understand that the body could panic, misremember, dramatize, and lie, while a recorded system error was a fact that could be acted on.

"I don't know," she said.

Margot nodded once.

"Then don't do it blind."

"Meaning what?"

"Meaning if it happens again, report it immediately and don't wait until the summary is all anybody has. Meaning let me watch the next session. Meaning don't put a contradiction in the file unless you're ready for them to talk about that instead of what happened."

Lena looked at her.

"You're asking me to wait."

"I'm telling you what waiting buys."

"And what does it buy you?"

Margot's face changed by almost nothing. The change was enough.

"This isn't about me," Margot said.

It was the first line in the conversation Lena knew for certain was false.

"I'll think about it," Lena said.

"OK."

Margot took a drink. That small ordinary movement closed the space between them more effectively than anything sharper would have.

"Let me know," Margot said.

"I will."

Margot walked on. Her footsteps stayed even down the corridor, no hurry in them, no sign she had just lied to someone who trusted her.

The quarters were quiet when Lena got back. Her wall display still glowed green. Heart rate 68. Neural link status nominal.

She sat on the bed and opened the report form.

Time of incident.

Session type.

Symptom description.

Loss of voluntary motor control: yes or no.

Loss of conscious awareness: yes or no.

Supporting telemetry attached automatically from official session record.

She selected yes.

A yellow banner appeared across the top of the form.

Class III submission triggers immediate suspension of training clearance pending physician review.

Below it, the attached summary populated on its own.

Session classification: mixed-intensity.

Coach note: post-set disorientation after middle-build effort, resolved poolside.

Objective severe-event markers: none in attached summary.

The cursor waited in the free-text box.

Lena typed: Missing approximately 150m of conscious experience during link-directed swim set. No memory after 250m turn. Awareness resumed at wall with severe respiratory distress and disorientation.

She read it back. The sentence was accurate. It looked insane sitting beside the clean summary.

She imagined Petra opening the file and doing exactly what a physician should do. Grounding her. Imaging her. Pulling her from the water. Then she imagined the screens after that. Selection staff. Institute admin. Meridian compliance. People she would never meet reading the mismatch between her account and the official data and assigning it whatever language institutions used when a person insisted on a break the graph did not show.

Uncorroborated.

Subjective.

Athlete-reported.

She knew those words. The program had built whole corridors of behavior out of them.

When she was ten, a coach in the developmental program had told her, gently and with a hand on her shoulder, that enhanced athletes survived by learning the difference between a system warning and a frightened feeling. Do not promote sensation into fact, he had said. Wait for the data. She had been small enough then that the hand had felt paternal instead of instructional. The sentence was still sitting in her muscles twelve years later.

Lena looked at the port mirrored dark in the window. Her body on the bed. Her body on the screen. Her body in the lane that morning doing work she had not lived through. Every version felt hers until the system disagreed. Then the recorded one outranked the lived one.

She moved the cursor back to the box and added a second sentence.

Coach was present on feed.

She stopped there.

If she submitted that line, Petra would pull the raw session if raw session still existed. If it did not, then the official record would stay what Margot had made it and Lena would have filed herself into a hole with no ladder. Either way the form in front of her was no longer just medicine. It was an accusation she could not yet prove.

Margot's pressure sat in the room with her now, no longer abstract. Immediate hold. Clean summary. Contradiction becoming the whole story.

Her thumb found the edge of the port at the base of her skull.

The display pulsed green.

Nominal.

She closed the form without submitting it.

The system asked if she wanted to save as draft.

No.

The form disappeared. The green status bar remained. For a while she looked at the word nominal and did nothing else.

Then she left the session unflagged.

Her thumb stayed pressed to the port until the skin went sore. The display did not change.
