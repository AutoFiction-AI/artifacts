# Chapter 3

The calibration bay ran at a constant seventeen degrees Celsius, two below facility standard. Not for the athletes. For the equipment. Spectral analyzers drifted warm, and Yusuf had spent three months arguing with Building Services until they gave the room its own climate loop. Since October he'd been wearing a fleece under the lab coat and pretending not to notice.

At six forty-five the main station was already awake. Schedule on the left. Queue on the right. Across the top, an orange Meridian banner he'd dismissed twice this week and still couldn't stop reading.

`RETENTION MIGRATION: 31 JAN 23:30. Local raw-channel cache access disabled after switchover. Full-resolution export thereafter requires Compliance ticket and Meridian research authorization.`

Security upgrade, the memo called it. Better stewardship. Cleaner chain of custody. Tomorrow night the machines would keep seeing everything they already saw; Yusuf just wouldn't. The full streams, the hormone traces, the tiny pre-movement corrections before a limb committed, all of it would route to Meridian's Zurich mirror and come back to him only as licensed research material. Farah didn't have licensed-research time.

Coffee went on the workstation ledge. Six athletes before noon. Four after lunch. Then data review and reporting. Official reporting, anyway.

Engineer credentials opened the service pane under the same login he used every day. Nothing illicit about that part. Calibration staff needed access because bad graft days went bad fast, and if an adjustment misfired the only way to roll it back cleanly was to watch the uncompressed maintenance stream while the athlete was still in the chair. The loophole sat inside legitimate access: during an active session the rig staged ninety seconds of raw physiology in local cache before collapsing it into the compliant summary. Compliance cared about what got filed, not what passed through RAM. If a raw stream touched synced storage, or if the service pane remained open after the session token closed, the workstation generated a retention exception for the morning audit. Mistime it once and somebody from Compliance would appear in the doorway with a neutral face and too many questions.

The first athlete at seven was Anya Petrov, a javelin specialist from the Russian program. Monthly recalibration. Right-shoulder graft stable for two seasons, but the altitude altered synthetic-muscle oxygen transfer and the numbers needed nudging every few weeks. She came in already stretching, one earbud still in, then pulled it free and dropped it in her lap as she settled into the rig.

"Morning, Anya. How's the shoulder?"

"Good. A little tight after yesterday's throws."

"Scale of one to ten."

"Three. Maybe four overhead."

Her integration profile opened across the display, the graft's performance envelope running in green and amber bands.

"Right. Tension sweep first. You'll feel the deltoid cycle pressure for about ninety seconds. Try to stay loose."

The sweep passed through the graft. Synthetic fibers adjusted resting tension in soft increments. Work like this always felt more intimate than Yusuf wanted it to. The athletes sat there answering simple questions, and on his screen their bodies opened in layers they couldn't feel themselves: heat drift, local blood flow, nerve firing across the seam where flesh yielded to engineered muscle. His hands stayed steady on the interface. They always did.

Anya's shoulder behaved. Oxygen transfer down a fraction. Resting tension back inside range. Notes filed. Release the restraints.

"Roll it once for me."

She did.

"Feels better," she said. "Looser."

"Should hold four weeks. If the tightness comes back before then, flag it."

Anya slid the earbud back in and headed out. The official chart kept calibration values and approved adjustments. Behind it, while the session token still lived, the service pane held everything else in a temporary block of local memory: raw nerve activity, inflammatory markers at the graft line, stress chemistry, the autonomous correction channel the summary screen rounded into nothing. Yusuf dragged the full stream into the unsynced analysis buffer and watched the progress ring fill. No network write. No ticket number. No audit trail yet. Ninety seconds later the cache would zero itself and leave a clean institutional story behind.

Tomás Reyes arrived at seven thirty, twenty-one, Ecuadorian, first Apex cycle. He sat in the rig and looked up at the sensor plates the way newer athletes still did, as if the equipment might reveal what it thought of them.

"How's the integration feeling?"

"Good. I think." Tomás flexed his right hand, opened and closed it. "My grip's been weird in the mornings."

"Weird how?"

"Like the hand's awake before I am." He gave a quick embarrassed laugh. "That sounds stupid."

"No, it doesn't. Tell me exactly."

Tomás looked at his own hand. "I pick up the toothbrush and it's already squeezing. Harder than I meant. Yesterday I reached for the rail by my bed and my fingers were shut before I thought about holding it."

Yusuf opened the profile. Right forearm graft, bilateral calf integration, spinal bridge. Standard decathlon build.

"How long's that been happening?"

"Maybe a week. Maybe less. I kept thinking altitude, or I slept on it wrong, or I was being an idiot."

"You're not being an idiot. If your system feels off, you say so."

Some of the tension went out of Tomás's shoulders. Yusuf ran the forearm scan. Dense synthetic fibers lit the display in fine cross-section where they anchored to tendon and bone. Resting tension sat slightly high. So did the small readiness spikes that should have stayed flat while Tomás lay still.

"Any trouble in the calves?" Yusuf asked.

"On stairs, a bit. Not pain. Just..." Tomás frowned. "Like my legs start the step before the rest of me."

There it was again.

"Could be altitude," Yusuf said, because that was the answer with the lowest immediate blast radius. "Could be overcorrection in the forearm calibration. I'm dropping resting tension about two percent. Your hand may feel weak for an hour while it settles."

"That's fine."

"If it isn't, you come back."

Tomás settled back without another question, forearm on the pad, throat exposed under the sensor bar. Most of them never asked how much else the system could see. Yusuf made the adjustment, watched the tension trace sink into range, and logged the approved note.

"Thanks, doc."

"Not a doctor."

"Still. Thanks."

After he left, the hidden buffer showed what the official pass had no reason to mention. The forearm elevation was there, but both calf grafts were lifting as well, shallow autonomous pulses rising before any voluntary movement. Sleep chemistry from the night before sat beside it: cortisol, hydration, inflammation around each interface seam. Tomás had come in for his hand and left half his night on Yusuf's screen.

By eight fifteen the Norwegian skier had described the first step of every staircase as arriving too early, her left hip catching before she was fully over it. At nine the diver with visual enhancement said the board had felt a little closer on her last two approaches, not wrong enough to miss, wrong enough to make her stomach tighten. The French sprinter reported nothing at all and still carried the same shallow lift when Yusuf opened the raw stream and looked where the summary screen didn't.

Lunch found four files tiled across the monitor. Different sports. Different enhancement histories. Different complaint language because bodies announced the same problem in sport-specific ways. The common field sat in every header, and inside forty-eight hours of calibration the same channel had begun to creep upward.

Autonomous activity wasn't an abstract engineer's squiggle. That layer tightened a calf before landing, steadied a wrist when a bar slipped, corrected a line of movement faster than conscious intent could manage. In tiny bursts it kept enhanced bodies from tearing themselves apart. Rising at rest meant the system was rehearsing movements nobody had asked for yet. If it kept climbing, Tomás's hand would close before he reached, the skier's hip would fire before the stair was under her, and the diver would start getting depth corrections from a body that had decided distance on its own. None of that was theory. The traces supported it. What remained hypothesis was cause. A bad altitude patch could do it. So could local stack noise. So could something moving through the shared calibration network. Four files made an ugly pattern, not proof.

He ate a cheese sandwich at the workstation and brushed crumbs away from the keyboard with the side of one hand. Kai Brennan's overnight file opened all the way out: bilateral motor latency, spinal-bridge throughput, visual load, the micro-corrections that kicked in before Brennan meant to move. On the motion plot, the proprioception gap had widened across three sessions. Fourteen milliseconds. Twenty-two. Thirty-one.

Thirty-one milliseconds meant nothing to most people. At sprint pace it meant foot strike arriving before the body finished registering the strike, correction entering the system after the knee had already committed to a line. Another jump and Brennan wouldn't just feel strange; he'd start getting the lane a fraction late, as if the report from his own legs had to travel back to him from somewhere else.

The Brazilian hurdler with the earlier latency spike wasn't scheduled today, but Yusuf pulled the filed report anyway and saw the same phrase he'd been seeing for weeks: momentary processing artifact. Clean wording. Nothing to frighten anyone. Nothing false, exactly. Just a polite institutional version of not yet.

An incident report sat two clicks away. Open one and Compliance would freeze the raw files to the case, preserve chain of custody, and route the full-resolution material straight into Meridian retention the moment tomorrow night's migration hit. The athletes might get monitored faster. Farah would get nothing. Yusuf wrote the official reports instead.

Brennan: proprioception drift noted, continued monitoring recommended, within acceptable parameters.

Norwegian program athlete: hip adjustment completed, seasonal norms.

Reyes: grip tension corrected, altitude probable.

Technically accurate. Morally not tidy enough to call honest.

Afternoon sessions did nothing to improve the picture. A German middle-distance runner showed a minute tremor in the quadriceps channel before standing from the rig. An Italian cyclist came back clean on first pass, then betrayed the same shallow correction lift when Yusuf reopened the maintenance stream and compared it to last month. By four thirty the pattern had spread from interesting to sickening. Not epidemic. Not yet. Just broad enough that coincidence started looking like cowardice.

A second Meridian notice dropped into the corner of the screen at five twelve while he was closing out the last chart.

`REMINDER: 31 JAN 18:00 endpoint inventory rehearsal for retention migration. Active external devices on calibration stations will be logged.`

He clicked it away and kept typing, more slowly than before.

On the Kirchberg side, the day still looked immaculate: filed, timestamped, technically defensible. Nobody reading the official system would see a body beginning to get ahead of its owner.

The calibration bay emptied by six. The last athlete left at five forty, and Yusuf spent the next fifteen minutes on shutdown protocols. Not a full shutdown. The processing stacks never really stopped. Active hardware went dark. Sensor plates got cleaned. The rigs returned to standby. Sara and Luc handled mornings. Yusuf preferred evenings, when nobody wanted one more explanation and the room gave him back his own thoughts.

When the rigs were down, he took the personal drive from his bag. Matte black. Smaller than his thumb. Encrypted with a key that existed in his memory and one paper backup in the drawer of his room.

The hidden directory on the workstation was only a staging ground. It wasn't invisible, only local and unsynced, tolerated because engineers sometimes needed a place to compare two live traces side by side before deciding whether a calibration had genuinely failed. Weekly maintenance images wiped it. By tomorrow night Meridian's switchover would cut off fresh raw pulls entirely. Everything he meant to keep had to leave the machine before then.

The endpoint rehearsal mattered because the auxiliary port was meant for firmware dongles and vendor diagnostics. Under normal operating rules, device inventory happened after the bay locked for the night and nobody was watching live. Tonight Meridian wanted a rehearsal at six. If his drive was still mounted when the poll ran, its serial would land in the audit bundle with no service ticket attached. That was the trace. Not proof by itself, but enough for someone diligent to start matching times and asking why Yusuf Qadir kept plugging personal hardware into calibration stations that were never supposed to hold patient-level raw physiology.

The system clock in the corner read 17:57.

Bollocks.

He plugged the drive into the auxiliary port. Brennan's widened gap. Reyes's forearm and calf data. The Norwegian skier's hip values. Diver, sprinter, hurdler, cyclist. The cross-athlete comparison file. Last week's baseline pulls that would be useless without today's anomaly laid beside them. He selected everything that still had explanatory value and started the transfer.

Progress bar. Eleven seconds.

At second four he thought he'd chosen too much. At second seven the port light flashed amber and his stomach clenched hard enough to hurt. At second nine the indicator went green. Transfer complete.

Eject. Pocket. Service pane closed. Staging directory scrubbed. By the time the workstation returned to the clean Kirchberg interface, the official day had become the only day the machine was prepared to acknowledge.

He turned off the monitor and left.

The connecting corridor had glass walls on the mountain side, and at this hour in late January the mountains were only shape and weight, a dark mass against a slightly less dark sky. The temperature display by the junction read minus twelve. Beyond the glass, snow had gone blue with evening. Inside, the facility held steady at twenty-one degrees, and the step from the bay's cold into corridor warmth was the only weather Yusuf felt most days.

His quarters sat on the third floor of the residential wing. Single room, larger than the athletes' rooms, with a bed, a desk, and a bathroom. A wall-mounted diagnostic display still showed facility status data he'd stopped reading months ago. There was a kettle, a jar of loose-leaf Yorkshire Tea his mother posted once a month, and a stack of books on the desk he was getting through at about a chapter a week. Farah had given him a history of the Birmingham canal network for his birthday three months earlier. He was still in the first third.

Tea first. Drive into the drawer beside its twin. Laptop open.

Farah picked up on the third ring. Her face filled the screen, dark hair pulled back, the living room of the flat in Moseley behind her, one corner of the bookshelf he'd helped assemble two Christmases ago visible over her shoulder. She was smiling.

"Yu. Hang on, let me."

She reached off-screen, adjusted something, came back into frame. The camera settled.

"There. Hi."

"Hey. How was your day?"

"Fine. Normal. Went to Asda, made a lasagne. It was terrible but I ate all of it, so." She shrugged. Animated. Present. Good colour in her face. Compression hid the smaller things until it didn't.

"Did you use Mum's recipe or that website one?"

"The website. Mum's has too many steps. I can't be arsed with bechamel from scratch when there's a perfectly good jar."

"She'd disown you."

"She'd disown me and then bring round a proper one. Win-win." Farah grinned. The pause after it ran a touch long. "What about you? Still in the lab at seven at night?"

"Just finished. Long day."

"They're all long days with you. You need to get out more. Go look at a mountain or something. You're in the Alps and you spend all your time in rooms that are trying to be refrigerators."

"One room, singular. There's a window in the corridor."

"Yu."

"I'm fine. I like the work."

He took a sip of tea and watched the places where it showed first: the beat before she answered, the extra half-second while a noun worked its way up, the slight miss when she reached for her mug and had to adjust her fingers on the handle. Small things. Easy to forgive if you loved somebody and wanted to stay stupid.

"How's the physio going?" he asked.

"Oh, fine. Same. She's dead enthusiastic. Lots of squeezing things." Farah held up both hands and mimed it. "I told her I don't need more grip strength, I need to be able to open the..."

She stopped. Her smile held in place while the sentence didn't.

"Jars," she said, recovering it. "I need to be able to open jars. The Tesco pasta sauce ones especially. Whoever designed those lids is my actual enemy."

"I'll send you one of those rubber grip things."

"I've got three. They don't work on the Tesco ones. I think they're sealed at molecular level."

Months earlier she'd held the kettle up to the camera and called it the thing for tea until the right word came back. Cheap nouns first. Kitchen objects. Handles. Then longer gaps if the day had tired her out. Voss-Kessler never arrived all at once. It took little, stupid pieces.

"Mum called today," Farah said. "She's worried about you. Says you're not eating properly."

"How would she know?"

"She has sources. I think she's paying one of the cafeteria staff."

"She's not paying anyone. She doesn't even know where the cafeteria is."

"Well, she's worried. Eat a vegetable, Yu. One vegetable. For her peace of mind."

"I had a sandwich."

"What was in it?"

"Cheese."

"That's not a vegetable."

"There was lettuce."

"Iceberg doesn't count. It's basically wet paper."

He laughed, and hers followed half a beat later, still warm, just slower getting there. The delay had become measurable enough that he caught himself counting without deciding to count.

"You still there?" Farah asked.

"Yeah. Sorry. Thinking."

"About work?"

"About what I'm having for dinner."

"Cheese sandwich?"

"Probably."

"Hopeless." She yawned, and the yawn went on a little too long, her jaw moving with a looseness the camera did not quite hide. "I've got that thing with Priya tomorrow. Early."

"What thing?"

"We're going to the..." Her eyes shifted left, the way they did when a word had moved just out of reach. "Cadbury World," she said at last, smiling over the gap she'd just shown him. "She's never been. I've been four times and it's still good. They give you free chocolate at the end."

"Tell Priya I said hi."

"Will do. Eat a vegetable."

"Night, Farah."

"Night, Yu."

The screen went dark. Yusuf sat with the mug cooling in his hands and counted backward through the call: jars, Cadbury World, the pause before hopeless, the mug handle slipping once before she caught it properly. Two obvious word losses this month. Maybe three, if he counted the one she covered well enough that somebody else might have missed it.

There was no treatment. There were papers full of might and could and further study required. The one he kept coming back to, fourteen months old now, had a sentence highlighted in yellow: high-resolution neural maps from enhanced systems might trace the degenerative pathway closely enough to target it. Meridian owned most of those maps already. Tomorrow night, after the migration, every new one at Kirchberg would become Meridian property before he could even pretend otherwise. The licensing quote on his old access request sat starred in his inbox, page two still carrying a number that would've gutted a small lab.

Their reply had been polite enough to make him hate it more. Institutional affiliation required. Ethics board approval required. Indemnity coverage required. Archive access billed separately from live-stream access, and live-stream access excluded from the standard license once the new retention model went active. Every line had been written to sound reasonable. Taken together, they meant the same thing as a slammed door.

The incident portal loaded its usual boxes: anomaly class, affected athlete, source dataset, retention authorization. He could fill the first one. Maybe the second, if he picked a name at random and lied by being premature. The third led straight to the files he'd just stolen out from under tomorrow night's switchover. The fourth asked why he'd been keeping other people's raw physiology in a drawer beside his tea.

Formal reporting would make the problem real in exactly the wrong way. Compliance would lock the datasets, Meridian would inherit them, and Yusuf would get whatever filtered summary the company decided a facility engineer was entitled to see. He understood what that made him. The understanding did nothing useful.

The encrypted drive mounted on the laptop. He dragged the day's comparison set into the archive beside older pulls and watched the copy complete. Folders by date. Backups nested inside backups. A private catalogue of bodies that did not belong to him, kept because his sister's nerves were dying and the institutions with the relevant data charged by the gate.

Then he closed the blank incident form, ejected the drive, returned it to the drawer, and turned off the light.
