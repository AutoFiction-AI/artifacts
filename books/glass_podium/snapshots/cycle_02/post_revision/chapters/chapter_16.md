# Chapter 16

By late June the shuttle to Zurich left twice a week. On the Tuesday the relay group went, Kai came in off the indoor track with his shirt stuck to his back and stopped by the front windows while Ursula stacked breakfast cups behind him. A Meridian coordinator in a navy jacket checked names against a tablet. Four relay runners climbed aboard with matching duffel bags and the same new corporate luggage tag looped through each handle. Then the Czech sprinter went, then the two South Korean gymnasts. The shuttle door folded shut. Somebody laughed on the other side of the glass. Then it pulled down the switchback road and was gone.

He cut through the residential wing on his way to the showers. Half the room displays were dark now. The ones still on floated pulse traces and battery diagnostics over empty beds. Most of the institute had made its bargain. By then the holdouts were a handful spread between Kirchberg and Davos: Kai still running nothing, Lena and two others on Yusuf's patch, everyone else signed or already gone. Choices had stopped sounding like arguments. They were just departures.

That morning he'd passed two of the patched holdouts coming back from final diagnostics: a weightlifter from Sarajevo and the Bergen cyclist whose name he always forgot. Both had fresh adhesive shadows at the base of the skull. Both were moving with the careful fraction of delay he'd started to recognize in Lena, not weakness exactly, just people doing their own timing by hand again. Each carried a printed travel packet because their systems no longer synced cleanly with the institute calendar. From ten metres away you could tell who had taken whose version of safety.

The patched ones looked less sponsored now and more like patients carrying instructions.

That afternoon he found Margot at the bottle filler by the side entrance, bent over from the gravel loop below the treeline with one hand braced on the wall. Sweat had soaked the back of her grey sweatshirt. Dust had dried pale on her calves above her socks. Margot drank until the sensor cut off, hit it again, drank more. When she straightened, two fingers went to the pale strap mark the feed interface had left on her wrist. She pressed there once, hard, then seemed to feel herself doing it and shifted her grip to the steel edge of the filler instead, knuckles whitening against the metal.

"You all right?" he asked.

"Not especially." Her voice stayed flat. "Running helps."

"With what?"

Margot looked past him, out through the glass to the path she'd just come up. "Everything else feels like cardboard." The hand on the steel flexed once. "The lungs at least still know how to complain."

Kai nodded.

Margot looked at him properly then, bloodshot-eyed from the climb and something else. "If anything feels wrong in Doha, stop. Don't do the brave thing for the cameras."

He frowned. "You telling me not to race?"

"I'm telling you they clap for stupid." She let go of the metal. "That's different."

Then she pushed off the wall and went back inside.

The night before the team bus left for the Zurich airport, Yusuf made biryani.

---

His mother had sent a care package in March, a padded envelope battered from Birmingham: two bags of basmati, a jar of her own garam masala, saffron threads in a twist of cling film, a bag of fried onions gone slightly soft at the edges, and a note in Urdu that said *Eat properly.* He'd been saving it. The biryani was a production. He'd negotiated use of the cafeteria kitchen from the night cook, a woman named Ursula, after she'd finished labeling boxed breakfasts for the airport bus with room numbers and diet codes. She watched him toast whole spices in a dry pan with visible skepticism and then left him to it. The rice soaked in salted water. The chicken, thighs because his mother had opinions about breast meat and had passed them on through repetition if not genetics, had been marinating since the afternoon in yogurt and her masala and enough green chilis to clear out anyone who wasn't ready.

Kai was the first to arrive. He came in through the side entrance from the track, hair still damp from the shower, and stopped when the smell hit him.

"That's not cafeteria food."

"Nah." Yusuf was standing at the kitchen pass-through, stirring something with a wooden spoon that belonged to Ursula. "My mum's biryani. Or my attempt at it. She'd tell me I've done something wrong with the rice."

"Your mom sent you rice?"

"Rice, spices, guilt. The usual care package." Yusuf wiped his hands on a towel he'd tucked into his belt. "Grab plates, will you? The stack by the serving line."

The extra politeness sat there between them. Yusuf used to just say plates.

Kai got plates. The cafeteria was mostly empty. Two athletes from the French sprint relay were eating protein bowls at the far end beside half-zipped team duffels, and a physiotherapist was reading on her tablet under the noticeboard where tomorrow's bus call, flight codes, and accreditation windows had been taped up in Meridian blue. Late light hit the windows hard enough to bleach the paper. Past eight and still fully bright. The days had gotten very long.

Lena came in from the pool side, her hair wet and carrying the faint chlorine trace it always did. Without ceremony Lena sat down across from Kai, looked at the plates, and said, "Is that Yusuf's?"

"His mom's recipe. Allegedly."

"Good." Then she pulled the plate closer. "The cafeteria chicken's been the same chicken for three days."

"It's the same chicken every day," Kai said. "They just move it to different trays."

"That isn't true."

"I know. But it feels true."

Margot arrived last. At the entrance she paused for a moment, taking the room in before she crossed it. Then she sat down next to Lena. Lena moved her water glass half an inch to make room without looking up.

"Biryani," Margot said, looking at the serving dish Yusuf was carrying to the table with both hands.

"Biryani," Yusuf confirmed.

"Your mum's?"

"My mum's spices, my execution. Don't hold her responsible."

"I never do." Margot took the serving spoon and started filling her plate. The rice was golden-brown in layers, the chicken underneath, the fried onions on top. The smell was cumin and saffron and the particular warmth of cardamom that wasn't sweet exactly but made you think of sweetness.

Yusuf sat down. "It needs raita and I haven't got any yogurt, so." He gestured at the table as though the absence of yogurt was a physical object they could all see.

"It's fine," Lena said. She was already eating. "It's really good."

"Don't sound surprised."

"I'm not surprised. I said it's good."

For a while they just ate. The biryani was good. The rice had that texture where each grain held its shape but gave under your teeth. The chicken was tender and spiced in layers that arrived at different points in the bite. The chilis were enough to make Kai's eyes water on the second mouthful but not enough to stop him going back for a third.

"Christ," Kai said, after a particularly loaded bite. He reached for the water.

"The chilis?" Yusuf didn't look up from his plate.

"Yeah."

"My mum goes heavier. This is the mild version."

"This is mild?"

"For Birmingham, yeah."

Margot ate through the heat without comment. On the first really punishing bite her eyes watered and she kept going, faster if anything. It was the first time in weeks Kai had seen her pay attention to what was in her mouth. Earlier in the month she'd sat over untouched lunches until they went cold, jaw working on nothing, as if taste had gone thin on her. Yusuf ate steadily, the way he did everything, focused, economical, not especially social about it. Lena ate with the same precision she brought to most physical activities, small bites, thorough. Margot ate like she was hungry, which she probably was. He hadn't said anything because it wasn't his business and because Margot would have told him to fuck off.

The French relay athletes finished and left. The physiotherapist left. The cafeteria was theirs.

"So," Margot said, setting her fork down. "Big day tomorrow."

"Yeah," Kai said.

"Plane to Zurich. Zurich to Doha. Doha to forty degrees and whatever refrigerated cave Meridian built so television doesn't have to look at the desert."

"You've been reading the briefing packet," Yusuf said.

"I've had time to read." Margot pointed her fork first at Lena, then Kai. "Four-thirty for the swimmers. Six for the track. Extra medical declaration if you're still stupid enough to be running unpatched."

Kai looked up. "Unpatched, you mean."

"I know what I mean."

"That's not how the form words it," Yusuf said. The vowels had flattened into his professional voice. "But yes. There's an additional screen on your file."

"Good to know I'm special."

Yusuf kept his eyes on his plate. "If the body starts the set before you mean it, you come up. I don't care if the gun's gone."

Kai set his fork down.

"You don't get to give me instructions like nothing happened," he said.

"I know that."

"Do you?"

Yusuf lifted his head then. "Yeah," he said. "I do."

The silence after that was long enough for somebody in the kitchen to bang a tray into a stack and make them all look up.

"What happens if he reports it?" Lena asked.

"Meridian reruns diagnostics and pulls him if they don't like what they see," Yusuf said.

"Which they won't," Kai said.

"False start's cheaper," Margot said.

Kai let out a breath through his nose. "Everybody's very inspiring tonight."

"That's not what this is," Yusuf said.

"No," Kai said. "It isn't."

Lena put her fork down. "What are you going to do?"

"If they pull him?" Margot asked.

"No. While we're in Doha."

Margot shrugged. "Walk the halls. Eat the chicken. Maybe finally figure out the night cook's name."

"Ursula," Yusuf said.

"See, you've ruined my one project."

Lena's fork stayed in her hand. "And after that?"

Margot rolled a grain of rice flat against the plate with the back of her fork. "Munich, probably. Petra's got a place there that takes feed cases."

"Treatment?" Lena asked.

"That's what the paperwork says."

"And what do you call it?"

Margot's mouth shifted, not quite a smile. "Being somewhere with locked drawers and people who take the hardware before breakfast."

Nobody said anything for a moment.

"When?" Kai asked.

"After you're gone. Maybe the day after." Margot kept looking at the plate. "They're still arguing over paperwork, which feels right somehow."

Kai finished his plate and served himself more. The light outside had thinned enough that the laminated travel sheet by the window started reflecting across the glass: bus call, Zurich transfer, accreditation, extra medical declaration for unpatched athletes. The ridge was still there beyond it, but the glass gave him tomorrow before it gave him the mountains.

"Meridian sent the broadcast reel for approval," he said.

Margot gave a short laugh. "Approval."

"Exactly. They let you catch spelling errors and pretend that counts." Kai scraped rice loose from the side of the dish. "Mine opens on a college final. Then the crash. Then one of those clean surgery renders where they make your spine look like premium plumbing. Then Lausanne. Thirty seconds and apparently that's a life."

"Messy how?" Lena asked.

"Left arm high. Hips late. I spend half the straight fixing it." He looked at the rice crust clinging to the serving dish. "That used to be the job. Now they keep the half after I've sorted it."

"No hospital?" Margot asked.

"No rehab. No six weeks of not being able to turn over in bed without feeling the screws. That bit didn't test well, I guess."

"Recovery is boring on television," Lena said.

"Yeah," Kai said. "It was pretty boring in person too."

"It's a highlight package," Yusuf said, careful again.

"Yeah." Kai shrugged. "That's sort of the problem."

"Did you tell them to put it back in?" Margot asked.

"No." He scraped again at the rice. "I fixed the spelling and sent it back."

Lena was watching him with that particular attention she gave to descriptions of bodies doing things their owners hadn't commanded, the stillness of someone hearing their own condition described in different terms.

Yusuf got up and brought back water for the table, four glasses from the stack by the serving line. He sat down and drank half of his.

"Farah's been on a streak," he said.

"Your sister?" Lena asked.

"Yeah. It's this thing, I don't even know what it is. Some card game her flatmate taught her, like rummy but with rules I can't follow. She wins constantly. Thursday she rang me and spent twenty minutes explaining a hand she'd played. I understood none of it."

"Is she good at cards generally?" Kai asked.

"Farah's good at everything she decides to be good at. It's annoying. It's always been like that." Yusuf's voice was easier now, but not easy. "Farah's got this thing where she pretends she doesn't know the rules so people underestimate her, and then she cleans them out. She did it to my cousin at Eid and he didn't speak to her for a week."

"Good for her," Margot said.

"It's proper evil, is what it is."

"I said good for her."

Yusuf smiled at his plate. The biryani was mostly gone. Remnants of rice clung to the serving dish in a golden-brown crust, and Yusuf scraped at it absently with the edge of the spoon, every grain accounted for.

For a few minutes Lena had been quiet, looking out the window at the mountains and the last of the light. Her water glass turned between her hands.

"It's getting easier," she said. "The quiet."

Nobody asked what she meant.

"The first week after the patch, I kept checking. Like when you think you've left the stove on, except there isn't a stove. There never was a stove." The glass turned in her hand. "Something used to be running in the background and now it isn't, and my brain keeps reaching for it. I think for the first week I confused my own thoughts with symptoms. I'd be thinking something, and then I'd stop and think, was that me? Because I'd never had to ask that before. Everything was just everything. The system and me were the same signal."

Then she set the glass down.

"Now I can hear the difference. Most of the time. My thoughts are slower, and they have edges now. I can feel where one stops and another one starts. The system used to smooth that out. Everything flowed. And now it doesn't, and I keep thinking something's broken, and then I remember that's just how thinking works if you're doing it yourself."

"Is it better?" Kai asked.

Lena considered this for a long moment, her thumb against the side of the water glass.

"It's mine," she said. "I don't know if that's the same thing."

Margot was looking at the table now, not at Lena. "I still don't know how much of the bad weeks you remember."

The glass stopped turning in Lena's hand.

"Enough," she said.

After a moment she added, "Enough that you should've told me."

Margot nodded once. "Yeah."

Outside, the ridge had gone dark enough that the cafeteria windows chose the room over the view and turned reflective. They gave back plates pushed aside, the fluorescent strip above the serving line, the departure notice taped by the door, and the pale band on Margot's wrist when she moved for her glass. The mountains were still out there somewhere, but the room had become the thing you could actually see.

"Right," Yusuf said eventually. "Dishes."

"I'll do them," Margot said.

"You don't have to-"

"I know I don't have to. Let me."

Margot stood and started stacking plates. Yusuf let her. Kai cleared the serving dish and the glasses, carrying them to the kitchen pass-through where Margot ran water into the industrial sink. Water hit steel. Ceramic knocked against ceramic. It was ordinary.

At the pass-through Yusuf took the serving dish from him and kept his voice low. "I meant it about the blocks."

Kai kept his eyes on the sink. "I heard you."

"That's not the same as doing it."

Kai let go of the dish. "If it starts before me, I come up."

Yusuf's hand tightened once on the rim. "Right," he said.

After a beat Kai said, "Send me the start traces from March."

Yusuf looked at him.

"The legal ones," Kai said. "Not the archive."

"All right."

"I want to look once on the flight. That's it."

"All right," Yusuf said again.

Lena stayed at the table, looking at the dark windows where the mountains had been. After a while two fingers brushed the quiet hardware at the base of her skull, a check so brief it barely counted, and dropped again.

Yusuf dried while Margot washed. They didn't talk much. A few words about where things went, Margot asking which shelf the serving dish belonged on, Yusuf reaching past her to put it back because she couldn't quite get to it. The efficiency of two people in a kitchen who knew where everything lived and did not know what to do with each other.

When the dishes were done, Margot dried her hands on the towel Yusuf had been wearing in his belt. Then she folded it and laid it on the counter.

"Fly safe," she said. To the room, not to anyone in particular.

"Yeah," Kai said.

Margot looked at Lena, then at the dish rack. "Swim-" She stopped. "No. Forget it. Goodnight."

"Goodnight," Lena said.

"Night," Yusuf said.

Margot left through the side entrance that led to the residential wing. The door swung shut behind her with the hydraulic hiss that all the facility doors made, a sound they'd heard ten thousand times and that Kai realized he wouldn't hear again for weeks. Then it was three of them.

Yusuf stretched his back and his spine cracked audibly. "I should pack. I haven't packed a thing. I've been making biryani instead of packing."

"Good trade," Kai said.

"Tell that to my bag at four in the morning." He pushed his chair back. "Night, both of you."

"Night."

"Goodnight, Yusuf. And thank you. For the food."

"Thank my mum. I'll tell her someone appreciated it." He raised a hand without turning around and walked out, and the door hissed shut again, and it was Kai and Lena in the empty cafeteria.

The fluorescent strip hummed. The windows were fully black now, holding only the table, the folded towel by the sink, and the white departure packet still clipped to the noticeboard. The smell of cumin and saffron sat on top of the cafeteria's old bleach-and-protein smell and lost ground to it a little more every minute.

"You all right?" Kai asked.

"Yes." Lena looked over the room before answering the rest. "I was thinking about how many meals I've eaten in this room."

"A lot?"

"Since I was sixteen. So." Her eyes moved over the table, the surface wiped clean, the chairs pushed back to where they'd been before any of this. "It won't feel like this again."

Kai looked at Margot's empty chair, then at the folded towel still on the counter by the sink. In the dark window behind Lena there were only the two of them.

"No," Kai said. "Probably not."

Lena did not sit down again. "When Yusuf told you to come up if it starts early, you said yes."

"Eventually."

"Was that real?"

Kai looked at his left hand on the table, the tendons quiet under the skin. "If it goes in the blocks, I come up. If it goes later, I don't know yet." He rubbed the heel of his hand against his jeans. "But I'm not handing it the finish. That's the plan, anyway."

"And the traces?"

"One look," he said. "I don't want the pretty version. I want the part where it's still work."

"You trust him with that?"

Kai shook his head. "Not with me. With the lines."

Lena studied him for a moment. "That is enough for tonight."

Then she came around the table to where he was sitting. At his chair she stopped. He reached for her hand and she gave it to him, his left hand, the one that still complained in the cold, her right hand cool from the water glass. They stayed like that for a while in the empty cafeteria, the fluorescent strip humming above them and the smell of Yusuf's mother's cooking still stubborn against the cafeteria bleach.

Then they turned off the light and went to pack.
