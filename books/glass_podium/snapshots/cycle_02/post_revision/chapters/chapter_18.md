# Chapter 18

Kai sat at an outside table at a cafe on the Rue du Grand-Pont, his coffee half-finished and the afternoon showing no interest in moving. Lausanne in August: the lake below the old town catching light between buildings, tourists on the steep sidewalks with shopping bags and good shoes, a woman at the next table reading something on a tablet with the absolute attention of someone who did not want to be disturbed. The cathedral's tower was visible above the rooftops, grey stone against a sky that had been blue all day and looked ready to stay that way until evening.

His left wrist had been complaining since lunch. He turned it once. The ache stayed.

The email from SportsVision was still open on his phone, face-down beside the coffee. It was the third reach-out in two weeks. They wanted Kai Brennan, dead last in the 200 meters in Doha, on a commentary team for the next Apex Games cycle, explaining sprinting to viewers who liked the spectacle more than the body inside it.

He hadn't said no. He hadn't said yes. The not-deciding felt fine, which was new. For most of his career, indecision had been something to clear out before the next training block, the next race, the next heat. Now it could sit there with the coffee and not spoil the afternoon.

A waiter came. Kai ordered another coffee in English and the waiter answered in French and they understood each other fine. The waiter was maybe twenty, with the posture of someone who had been on his feet for eight hours and had three more to go.

His right hand lifted the phone before he meant it to, thumb already waking the screen, and he set it back on the table.

This was the daily version. It wasn't Doha. It wasn't a catastrophe. It was just small corrections arriving early: the phone in his hand, the cup meeting the saucer dead center, his shoulder settling in its socket with a smoothness that still made him aware of the joint. His body moved a little ahead of him and then waited.

"Mr. Brennan?"

He looked up. The woman standing by the empty chair across from him was maybe mid-thirties, dark hair pulled back, linen shirt, sunglasses hooked into the collar. She held a phone and a notebook with the compact patience of someone used to stopping strangers in public.

"I'm Elise Marchand," she said. "SportsVision."

Kai let out a breath through his nose. "You guys are getting creative."

"You weren't answering."

"I was considering not answering forever."

"Can I sit down for five minutes?"

He looked at the chair. "Sure."

She sat. "I'll be quick."

"That's usually a lie."

"Sometimes." She set the notebook on the table but didn't open it. "You're not interested in coaching, and you clearly hate press. Commentary is a different job. Less pretending anybody cares what your recovery breakfast looks like."

"Strong pitch."

"It's not the actual pitch." She glanced at the phone beside his cup. "Doha is the pitch."

Kai stayed still.

"Most former athletes can talk splits and lane discipline," Elise said. "We've got those already. We don't have many people who'll look at something ugly on a track and not call it bad luck because that's the easy phrase."

"You say that like your producers are brave."

"Some of them aren't." She didn't smile. "I heard the effects mic after your race. You told the official it wasn't a failure."

Kai looked at the lake through the gap between buildings. "Maybe I was trying to be difficult."

"Were you?"

He tapped one finger once against the table. "No."

Elise waited him out. She was good at it.

"Everybody wants a clean version," Kai said at last. "Hardware issue. Athlete guts it out. Crowd applauds. Then everybody goes home feeling normal."

"And your version?"

He gave a short shrug that didn't involve the bad shoulder. "The body got out ahead of me. I was still in it. I just wasn't setting the pace anymore."

Elise watched him. "Would you say that on air?"

"Depends who's paying to hear it."

"SportsVision."

"And Meridian buys ads."

"Meridian buys ads everywhere."

He laughed once, quietly. "Yeah. That's kind of the point."

The waiter returned with the coffee and set it down between them. Kai thanked him. Warmth spread through his palms.

"I'm not asking you to decide right now," Elise said. "If you want, come to Geneva next Thursday and do a screen test. If you hate it, you leave. If you don't hate it, we talk."

"And if I ignore you again?"

"Then I'll assume it's no and stop embarrassing myself in cafes."

"Appreciate that."

She pulled a card from her notebook and slid it across the table. "Tuesday."

Kai didn't touch it.

Elise stood. "Enjoy the afternoon, then."

"That's the plan."

She left him with the card under the sugar jar and the chair still angled a little toward him. A pigeon landed on the back of it a moment later and leaned forward to inspect the table.

"Nothing for you," Kai said.

The pigeon hopped down anyway.

Kai drank half the coffee before he stood to pay. The card was still there. He reached for the cash and his right hand took the card first, folding it into his pocket in one practiced motion he hadn't approved or stopped.

Yusuf had rented the room two blocks from Lausanne station because the internet was fast and the desk was large enough for two laptops and three external drives. Everything else about it was temporary: narrow bed, pale walls, one print of a sailing boat, curtains too thin to keep out the afternoon. The room smelled faintly of dust and hot electronics.

On the desk, the public version of the archive waited in three windows.

He had been through the de-identification pass so many times that the files now felt damaged in his hands. Names first. Then dates. Then event IDs. Then the little pieces a lawyer could use to stitch a body back to a person: lane assignments, exact timestamps, location tags, internal athlete numbers. Every pass made the dataset worse science and better defense. He kept making himself take the loss.

The article form on ResearchGate sat open on the right screen. The title was long and flat and ugly in the way he wanted, the kind of engineering title civilians skipped and the right people still clicked. Under his name, the affiliation line read Former Kirchberg Institute.

He had typed Kirchberg Institute out of habit the first time and stared at it until he deleted the word.

The repositories were ready too. One held the virus-analysis files: the structural correspondences between the corruption patterns and Meridian's internal tool signatures, cleaned into something another engineer could rerun. The other held a de-identified extract from the athlete biometrics in his archive. Not everything. Never everything. Enough to make the point that the biological data did not belong inside Meridian's walls forever.

Every file on the drive had begun in somebody else's body.

He knew that sentence too well by now. It had followed him through the calibration bay, through Doha, through the train back to Switzerland, through three nights of sleeping badly in this room. He knew what Kai would say about it. He knew what he would say back. None of that changed the work on the desk.

Yusuf checked the hashes one last time against the handwritten sheet beside the keyboard. His tea had gone cold. When he swallowed, his mouth still felt dry.

"Right," he said to the empty room. "Fine."

He made the repositories public first. Each one asked if he was sure. Each one made him confirm again. Then he clicked Publish on the article and watched the button grey out under his cursor.

Nothing happened for almost two seconds.

Then the page refreshed with the public URL.

His stomach dropped anyway.

An automated email arrived first. Your article is now live. Another from the code host. Repository visibility changed to public. Then mirror notifications from two other services he had set up the night before in case Meridian tried to bury the links by force or process. His hands were steady, which annoyed him. He would have preferred some visible sign that he'd just set the rest of his professional life on fire.

His phone buzzed on the desk with Farah's name.

He answered on video. Farah was at her kitchen table in Birmingham with a mug in both hands and the article already open on a laptop beside her. Her hair was tied back badly. She had never cared about doing that neatly for family.

"Well," she said. "That title's a hate crime."

Yusuf laughed once. "It's meant to keep the wrong people away."

"It also keeps the right people away if they're tired."

"Then the right people can have a coffee and come back."

Farah looked from the screen to him. Her speech was still clear, but there was that half-beat now before some words, the tiny delay that made him want to climb through the camera and drag time backward with both hands. "Is it up properly?"

"Yeah. Properly public."

"So they can use it."

"They can rerun the signature analysis. They can inspect the archive extract. They can work without asking Meridian for permission first."

"Can they work on mine?"

He looked at the article instead of at her. Technical redirection came to him as easily as breathing. "They can work on Voss-Kessler correlations without Meridian's licensing gate in the middle. They can test across a real comparative set. They can at least start."

Farah let that sit for a second. "That's not the same answer."

Yusuf rubbed the heel of his hand against his mouth. "No."

"Try again."

He looked back at her. "I don't know if it helps you. Not directly. Not now. It might make it possible for somebody else to help later who couldn't even get in the room before."

"So maybe."

"Yeah."

She smiled a little, tired and not fake. "You hate maybe."

"Today I'm charging it rent."

Farah laughed, then coughed into her wrist and took a sip from the mug. When she spoke again her voice was softer. "They'll come after you."

"Yes."

"You didn't put it out anonymously."

"Anonymous would've made it easier to dismiss."

"And easier on you."

"Probably."

She shifted in the chair. For a moment she lost the word she wanted, and he watched her find another route around it the way she always had. "I don't know what to say that isn't either thank you or sorry."

"Then don't do either one."

"All right." She lifted the mug slightly. "Eat something, then. You look awful."

"That's very loving."

"I'm being serious."

"I know."

Another call flashed on the screen: Petra Engel.

Farah saw his eyes move. "Take it."

"I can call her back."

"Take it, Yusuf."

He nodded. "I'll ring you later."

"Do that. And tell Lena I'm not dead just because you went full whistleblower."

"Very reassuring message."

"Use your own wording, then."

He ended the call and answered Petra on audio.

"It's public," she said without greeting.

"That was the intention."

"Meridian legal contacted Kirchberg twelve minutes ago."

"Slow of them."

Petra ignored that. "They are alleging theft of medical data, breach of confidentiality, and sabotage of proprietary systems. The Federal Office of Sport has opened a preliminary inquiry and requested the publication materials. The European Athletics Enhancement Authority wants to know whether additional files exist."

Yusuf sat down again. "Of course they do."

"I told them they could review what is already public and make formal requests for anything else."

He closed his eyes once. "Thank you."

"Do not thank me yet." Papers moved on her end; he could hear them. "Is the de-identification conservative?"

"Yes."

"That is not an emotional question."

"I know. Yes. It's conservative. Names, dates, event IDs, internal numbers, location traces. Anything that could directly relink on a first pass is gone."

"On a first pass."

"If someone with internal Meridian access and enough time wants to play clever games, they can argue probabilities. They'd do that anyway."

Petra was quiet for a beat. "Have you preserved the source chain?"

"Three encrypted mirrors. Two public mirrors. Checksums on paper and offline."

"Good. Do not alter the repositories now unless a mirror fails. If you touch timestamps after the inquiry request, they will use that."

"I know."

"Also, you need counsel."

He laughed without humor. "With what money?"

"I am sending a name. Use it."

The line went quieter for a second. When Petra spoke again, some of the institutional metal had gone out of her voice. "Did you tell your sister?"

"Just now."

"And?"

He looked at the open article, the public URL, the download counter already climbing by single digits. "Same answer as before. Maybe."

"That may be the only honest one." Petra paused. "Stay reachable."

"All right."

He ended the call and sat with the phone in his hand until the screen went dark. Then it lit again with alerts stacked one over another.

*NZZ Breaking: Meridian BioSystems falls 11% after published firmware analysis*

*Swiss Federal Office of Sport opens preliminary inquiry into Meridian-linked calibration corruption*

*Comment on your repository: can you provide verification hashes for dataset B?*

The room stayed exactly as cheap and temporary as it had been ten minutes earlier. The sailing boat on the wall kept sailing nowhere.

His phone buzzed again. This time it was Lena.

*Saw it.*

He looked at the words a second, thumb hovering over the keyboard. Then he typed back.

*Yeah. Farah says hi.*

He sent it before he could smooth it into something less revealing.

The pool at the Centre Sportif was twenty-five meters, four lanes, and low-ceilinged enough that humidity stained the concrete. The air held chlorine, the slap of flip turns, and bubbles on the exhale. It was not Kirchberg's silent fifty-meter pool. A retired man did breaststroke with his head carefully above water while two teenagers in the far lane tried to splash each other without admitting that was what they were doing.

Lena swam. The stroke was clean but not optimized. Her catch slipped a fraction on entry. The cost was two seconds per hundred meters, and she still timed every session.

This was her sixth two-hundred. Every third stroke she turned to breathe and felt herself choosing it: head, air, back to streamline. The quiet in her head was not silence anymore. It was just her own mind, messy and associative, with the occasional half-second blank where a word should have been. By July she'd stopped calling that frightening. It was ordinary now.

Halfway through the fifth fifty, the left arm smoothed. It wasn't a blackout. It wasn't full desync. It was just the old correction surfacing for less than a second, the stroke going cleaner than she'd asked for. Lena felt it and stopped at the wall.

At the gutter she waited, breathing hard, while water slapped tile in the chopped rhythm of the small pool. The smoothness didn't come back.

This was what the patch had given her: margin. She could tell the difference soon enough to stop. Then she could push off again under her own imperfect catch and take the lost two seconds as the price of being there for it.

At the end of the set, the coach at the Montreux center was still in the bleachers with a book about Mediterranean cooking and a clipboard. Isabelle did not micromanage. At the start of each session she gave Lena a set and looked up only to mark times. The port at the base of Lena's skull had never prompted a question. Lena suspected Isabelle thought it was cosmetic.

"Good set," Isabelle said, not looking up from the book. "One-oh-nine on the last one."

"Thanks."

"You want another?"

"No, I'm done."

Isabelle made a note on her clipboard. Lena climbed out and stood on the deck, water running off her suit onto the concrete. The retired breaststroke man was still going, his head held above the surface with the determination of someone who believed the pool was trying to get his hair wet. The teenagers had given up swimming entirely and were sitting on the edge of the far lane arguing over something on one of their phones.

In the changing room, Lena sat on the bench in her suit, dripping, and read the article on her phone.

The title took up half the screen in flat engineering English. Under it sat Yusuf's name, then the former-affiliation line, then the two public repositories. Lena opened the first link and read the phrases she could follow: corruption patterns, structural correspondences, Meridian internal tool signatures. Matched. Resembled. Rerunnable. Nothing on the screen said Meridian had built the thing. The second repository was easier to understand. De-identified archive extract. Names removed. Dates removed. Event IDs removed. Athlete biometrics still there in clean columns. Above the article, the NZZ push alert she had already opened said Meridian was down eleven percent and the Federal Office had started an inquiry.

Not proof, then. But too much for Meridian to bury under a private email and one careful statement to press. Other researchers could download the files now. They could test the signature analysis themselves. They could work from the archive without asking Meridian first.

Lena texted Yusuf. Just: *Saw it.*

His reply came four minutes later: *Yeah. Farah says hi.*

Lena sat with the phone in her hand. The changing room smelled like chlorine and old wood and lavender soap. Farah. The reason Yusuf had built the archive in the first place. The files on the screen were not treatment. They were not even close. They were the part before that: something outside Meridian's lock, something another lab could touch. If the inquiry forced the company open, there might be more later. If it did not, the work no longer had to stop at Meridian's door.

Lena toweled off. The towel caught on the small circular port at the base of her skull. She touched it with two fingers, the absent check of a thing that was part of her body but not her.

Dressed, she walked out into August in Montreux. The lake was enormous and flat and the sun was on it and the air smelled like warm stone and the jasmine that grew along the promenade railing near the station. Her hair was still damp from the pool and the chlorine followed her, same as it had when she was four in a calibration pool in Zug, before she knew the system in her head was a system. The smell was the same. The Lausanne train was already waiting when she reached the platform. Lena climbed in with her bag damp against her hip and took the window seat before the doors shut.

The treatment facility was in Schwabing, in a converted Jugendstil house on a street lined with linden trees that smelled like August. The house had high ceilings and decorative plaster molding and a garden out back where patients were encouraged to sit in good weather. The art on the walls looked chosen by someone who understood that institutional spaces needed art but not what good art looked like: watercolors of lakes, a photograph of mountains that could have been any mountains, a print of a Klee hung slightly crooked and left that way for all six weeks Margot had been here.

It had been six weeks without the feed. Forty-two days. There was no accumulating pride in it, no sense that each day was another brick in a wall between her and the substance. She counted as she'd counted laps: because number was information, and information was what she had instead of certainty.

The program was called NeuroBalance, and it was one of three facilities in Germany that specialized in neural-interface addiction. Most of the other patients were feed-dependent, people with compulsive relationships to entertainment-grade sensory interfaces, immersive sports simulations and full-body relaxation feeds marketed as wellness tools and functioning as digital opiates. The feeds they described were crude by comparison. They had plugged into recordings. Margot had plugged into people.

On Thursday she tried to explain this to Helga. Helga was forty-three, Bavarian, precise in her English, grammatically careful and slightly over-enunciated. Good therapist or not, Helga did not understand what Margot was telling her.

"The entertainment feeds are recorded," Margot said. Across from Helga's desk, with its small succulent and framed photo of a dog, Margot sat back in the chair and looked at the plant instead of the woman. "Recorded and processed and cleaned up. Like a movie of a body. What I did was live. I was inside someone's actual nervous system while they were using it."

"Yes, you've said this." Helga's pen rested on her notepad. "Several of our patients describe a similar intensity with the immersive feeds. The sense of being inside another—"

"It's not similar."

Helga waited.

"Those feeds are recordings. They're designed to feel like a body. What I felt was a body." Margot's jaw worked. "There's no version of this where you compare it to Stefan on his entertainment feed eighteen hours a day and I nod along."

Helga wrote something. Her pen moved across the notepad with steady pressure. "I'm not equating the experiences. What I'm asking about today is the distinction between the experience itself and the need to repeat it."

"There isn't one."

"Help me understand that."

Margot looked at the succulent. It was the kind that didn't need much water, which was probably the point of keeping it on a therapist's desk. "When you plug in, the first thing you feel is their heartbeat. Not yours. It's in your sternum, and your breathing changes to match before you even—" She stopped. "Your diaphragm just does it. First second. You're already afraid it's going to end."

"Afraid."

"Yeah." Margot didn't elaborate.

"Afraid of the disconnection, or afraid of—"

"Of losing it. You're still feeling their heart and you already know it's going to stop." She looked at her hands. "There's no 'I had the experience and then wanted more.' You want it while it's happening. My hands are sweating right now."

Helga was quiet for a beat. "And the memory. Is the memory of the experience also present? Or is it more—"

"I'm sitting here right now and I can feel his hamstrings loading on the approach." Margot cut across the question. "The way his eyes locked on the bar — not my eyes, his. But I felt the lock." She stopped again. "He was twenty-one. Everything worked. He could feel the clearance height in his hips before he left the ground."

"You're describing Tomas."

Margot looked at her. "Yeah. I'm describing Tomas."

"And you also carry the memory of what happened to him."

"You mean the crash."

"Yes."

"The compression in his spine. The silence when the signals stopped. His legs going dead." Margot was quiet for a second. "I was inside his nervous system when it happened. So yeah, I carry it."

Helga set the pen down. "Most of our patients find that the negative consequences of use — the crash, the physical aftermath — gradually degrade the—"

"For about a week I thought that too." Margot's voice flattened. "You can't want something that did this to a kid. Except you can. If you plugged me in right now I'd say yes. I know what it cost him and I'd still say yes."

Helga was quiet. It was the pause that was supposed to look like reflection but was actually recalculation.

"The wanting," Helga said. "Has it changed in quality over the six weeks? Not necessarily in intensity. In quality."

"No."

"Not at all?"

"It was this size on day one and it's this size now. Doesn't get bigger. Doesn't get smaller. It's just there. Like furniture."

Helga looked at her notepad. "That is unusual for this stage of treatment. Most patients at six weeks—"

"I know. I'm not most of your patients."

"We'll continue Thursday," Helga said, and wrote something else, and Margot stood up because the session was over and because there was nothing more to say about it that she hadn't already said.

After group, four people in a circle talking about triggers and coping strategies, during which Margot said reasonable things about mindfulness techniques she didn't believe in and made eye contact with a twenty-six-year-old named Stefan who was trembling through withdrawal from an entertainment feed he'd used eighteen hours a day for two years, Margot walked.

By the time she reached Leopoldstraße, the muscles along her jaw ached from clenching. Stefan's shaking hands had set something off in her. Her own fingers kept closing around the empty air above her left wrist where the feed band had sat for years, ugly little grasping motions she didn't notice until after they happened. Saliva kept flooding her mouth the way it used to in the minute before she plugged in. She spat into the gutter outside a bakery and kept walking.

August in Munich was warm, not hot. The lindens on Leopoldstraße were in full leaf, and the air carried that late-summer smell of heated pavement and green and the distant mineral note of the Isar. South through Schwabing she walked, past the university, past cafes where students sat outside with laptops and beer in the late afternoon, past the Siegestor with its stone horses catching the low light. In jeans and a T-shirt and running shoes, she looked like anyone. That was the thing about it. Nobody on this street could see that the woman walking past the monument was carrying the precise sensory memory of another person's spinal cord going silent, or that she was trying not to run her tongue over her teeth like a dog waiting for a bowl to hit the floor.

By the time she reached the Englischer Garten, she'd ended up at the Eisbach again, though she hadn't meant to come there today. The Eisbach was a channel of the Isar, and at one narrowing the current formed a standing wave that surfers had been riding for decades. It had become her place by the second week, and she kept coming back.

There were four surfers today. Young, most of them. One woman in a wetsuit despite the warm air, her board shorter than standard, shaped for the wave's geometry. The wave was maybe a meter high, clean, glassy in the late light. The surfers dropped in one at a time from the bank, a sideways leap, the board angling to catch the current, then the rapid adjustment of weight and balance on a wave that never moved forward. The whole thing was stationary surfing. The wave held and the surfer held and the river kept moving underneath them.

The woman in the wetsuit was good. On the wave she cut back and forth with efficient fluidity, weight drops, ankle adjustments, the continuous proprioceptive reading of a moving surface. There was nothing enhanced or linked about her. It was just muscle and board and river and the accumulated hours that made the motion look easy. Her weight sank into a bottom turn and her arm swept out for balance and her torso rotated and for a moment the line of her body was a clean functional expression of physics and intention and practice.

Margot could see the biomechanics plainly: quadricep engagement, dorsiflexion, counter-rotation of the hips. Fifteen years of reading bodies had done that to her. The movement almost reached her, dimly, like music from another room, because the neural pathways the feed had built were still there, still listening for input that wasn't coming. Each time the surfer dropped in, Margot's calves tightened in answer and released uselessly. She moved down the rail for a cleaner angle, then another step, as if sightline had anything to do with it.

Her shoe slid on the wet stone at the edge. A man waiting with a board under one arm caught her elbow before she went farther.

"Careful," he said.

"I'm fine," Margot said, too fast.

He let go of her arm. She stepped back onto dry ground and hated the cold little burst of relief in her chest that had come from being touched by someone coming off the wave.

On the bank, Margot watched. The surfers rode the wave and climbed out and rode it again. The woman in the wetsuit finally lost her line and splashed into the current and hauled herself out laughing, water streaming off her suit, her board tucked under one arm. She shook her hair and said something to the guy waiting to drop in and he laughed too and the sound carried across the water in the warm air. The laugh tightened Margot's throat. Not concern. Hunger.

Margot watched until the light changed and the surfers climbed out one by one and walked away with their boards under their arms and the standing wave kept going, the water folding over itself as it always did whether anyone was riding it or not. Twice on the walk back she took her phone out of her pocket and stared at the black screen before shoving it away again.

At almost dinner, Margot went up the steps of the old house and through the front hall to the evening nurse's desk. She set the phone on the wood between them.

"Keep this till morning," she said.

The nurse looked up from her paperwork. "You can keep it."

"I know." Margot pushed it an inch farther across the desk. "Keep it anyway."
