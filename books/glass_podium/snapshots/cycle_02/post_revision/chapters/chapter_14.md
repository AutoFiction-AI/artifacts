# Chapter 14

Tomas counted his steps as his first coach had taught him: *uno, dos, tres*. The rhythm had locked in before engineers ever touched his body, back when counting was the only technology available to a thirteen-year-old on a dirt track outside Quito. Three years of neural integration had not erased it. The enhancement handled stride frequency to the hundredth of a second now. It smoothed his approach pattern into a shape that felt less like running and more like discovering what running was for. But the counting stayed. His one piece of untouched equipment.

Mid-morning group session. Four athletes shared the straight and curve, each occupying the part of the room habit had assigned them. In the far lane Kai was doing light sprint work, his stride still favoring the shoulder that had healed wrong after the blackout crash six weeks ago. Two other athletes Tomas knew only by event, a pole vaulter from the Dutch program and a middle-distance runner whose name he kept forgetting and felt bad about, occupied the curve. Tomas liked group sessions. Somebody always noticed a clean clearance.

Tomas had called his mother last night. She'd wanted to know about the weather, so he'd described the snowmelt off the peaks, how the water ran in channels beside the road down to Meiringen, how the Alps looked different now that the ice was pulling back and exposing gray rock. Then his sister Valeria had grabbed the phone to announce she'd made the regional soccer team, and he'd promised to video-call her first match. From the other room his brother Diego, who was nine and not interested in sports, had shouted about a math test. Tomas hadn't caught the words. Before hanging up, he'd told his mother the food was fine and the training was going well and he loved her, and she'd said *cuídate, mi amor*, and he'd taped his lead ankle as he always did, three tight wraps, the tape pulling at the fine hair above the joint.

The ankle tape was still there now, under his compression sock, a small pressure he could feel with every stride.

The fourth hurdle was clean. The lead leg snapped forward and the trail leg swept through with the delayed precision that still surprised him sometimes, the enhancement smoothing his clearance into an aerodynamically exact pattern his body had not been able to make before the grafts. This was the part he loved. Not with the senior athletes' resigned tolerance of a body that performed without being asked. Tomas still felt wonder at the meeting point between his effort and the machine's. Three years had not dulled that. The hurdle behind him rattled faintly on its base from the wind of his passage.

The fifth was clean too. The approach was good, his stride frequency settled, his arms pumping in the tight pattern Margot had drilled into him. A small warmth at the base of his skull told him she was there in the feed, a faint sense of being watched from inside. Margot monitored his cardiovascular output and pacing targets in real time through the coaching interface. Thinking about it never seemed necessary. It was like knowing his heart was beating. True, constant, not worth attention.

The sixth hurdle went clean. Twelve meters from the seventh, his body drove forward and the counting continued under everything, *uno, dos, tres*, and his heart rate was climbing, pushed past training range into a zone his body reached for because the coaching signal wanted it there. Nothing in it felt wrong to him. The push felt like effort. Like desire.

Seven meters from the seventh hurdle, the world stopped.

Not white. Not void. Not a fade or a flicker or a dimming. Kai had described his blackout to the group and Tomas had prepared himself for a sequence he could recognize and fight. This was not that. This was the world present and then the world not present. Sight, sound, proprioception, the rubber under his spikes, the temperature of the air in his lungs, the faint chlorine smell drifting from the pool three corridors away, all of it ceased at once. Not replaced by darkness. Not replaced by anything. The inputs simply terminated. Like a wire cut.

The enhancement did not stop.

Below the blackout, his legs kept driving. The autonomous motor subsystem, still taking firmware commands through the corrupted calibration network, held the sprint pattern at full speed. At his sides, the arms kept pumping. Momentum carried him toward the seventh hurdle with perfect geometry and no one at the controls.

The seventh hurdle caught him full-on.

---

Kai saw it happen from forty meters away.

At the edge of the curve, Kai stood toweling sweat off his neck, his left wrist aching as the cold indoor air found the marrow and pressed on it. Mostly he'd been watching Tomas without much attention, just watching a young athlete do something well, appreciating the form, the economy of effort, the eagerness that had not been beaten out of him yet.

Tomas's stride did not break. That was the first wrong thing. From Kai's distance, the sequence looked almost normal for a fraction of a second, an athlete approaching a hurdle, legs driving, and then the geometry bent out of shape. Tomas's lead leg drove forward at full sprint power but did not lift. The hurdle caught him just below the knee. At that speed, close to nine meters per second, the impact was not a stumble. The crossbar bent against his shin and his momentum pitched him forward and over, his upper body rotating around the point of contact hard enough that the Dutch pole vaulter, twenty meters away, dropped her water bottle.

Tomas went over the hurdle and down. No hands came out to break the fall. The arms were still driving the sprint pattern, pistoning at his sides. The track caught his chest first. Momentum drove him along the rubber in a scraping slide, and then the torso buckled. Legs that had not received the instruction to stop kept shoving his hips forward until his upper body folded under him. The compression drove into his spine.

The sound reached Kai forty meters away. Not a crack. Wetter than that. A dense impact that made it through the acoustic dampening and landed in his ears as a single blunt syllable.

Tomas's legs stopped moving. Not gradually. They stopped mid-stride, left leg extended, right leg half-cocked in trail position. The rest of him continued for another half-second, momentum carrying his torso forward in a loose slide, and then he was still.

Kai was running before he understood he had started. Kai's own synthetic grafts fired his legs, the enhancement answering alarm faster than conscious thought, and for one sick instant his body was doing the same thing that had just destroyed Tomas, moving without permission. He had to fight the urge to stop his own legs, to refuse the help, to insist on running at the speed of his own fear.

Six seconds later he reached Tomas.

Tomas's face was turned to the side, his right cheek pressed into the track. Tomas's eyes were open but empty. The pupils moved without landing on anything. His mouth worked around breath with no words behind it. Kai clapped a hand against the track by his head and got no flinch, no turn, no sign that Tomas knew he was there. Six seconds was not enough. The blackout still had him.

"Don't move him," Kai said. The words were out before he knew who he was saying them to. The Dutch vaulter was there, her hands hovering over Tomas's back, and the middle-distance runner was shouting toward the medical bay corridor.

Kai counted without meaning to. Seven. Eight. Nine. Ten. Tomas stayed gone. On the twelfth second his pupils finally steadied. The next breath caught on a Spanish word Kai missed. Then Tomas said, in English that had gone small and careful, "I can't. My legs. I can't feel them."

There was blood on the track from his shin where the hurdle had caught him, and a longer smear of abrasion from his chest slide, visible through his torn training top. The real damage was deeper, buried in his spine where the neural bridge had been.

Petra arrived in under two minutes, moving fast enough that the diagnostic unit knocked against her thigh. She had the portable scanner in one hand and a spinal stabilization kit under the other arm. She dropped to her knees beside Tomas with a practiced efficiency that Kai found both reassuring and horrible. This was work she knew how to do.

"Tomas, can you hear me? Don't try to move."

"I can hear you." His voice stayed quiet and exact, his English pared down to the few words he trusted. "My legs, Dr. Engel. I can't feel them."

Petra's hands moved along his spine without touching while the scanner bar swept the length of his back. Kai watched the readout from where he knelt. Most of the medical data meant nothing to him, but he understood the color. The neural bridge status indicator, which should have been green, was gray. Not red for damaged. Gray for absent.

"The bridge is gone," Petra said. She was talking to the medical staff arriving behind her, not to Tomas. Tomas heard anyway.

"What do you mean, gone?"

Petra looked at his face before she answered. "The neural bridge hardware has been destroyed. We're going to stabilize you and get you to imaging. Don't move."

Tomas lay on the track and looked at the ceiling, white acoustic tile under fluorescent strips, sixty feet up. The hum of the ventilation system. The track rasping against his uninjured cheek. Both hands were flat on the floor beside his head, and feeling stopped at his waist as cleanly as if someone had drawn a line across him and said: *below this, nothing.*

At twenty-one, his legs had been the entire architecture of his life, the thing he was for, the reason Meridian paid for his family's apartment in Quito, the reason his sister could play soccer on a real pitch instead of the street, the reason his brother could fail math tests without it mattering because Tomas was going to the Apex Games and the sponsorship money would cover everything. His legs. Thirty seconds ago they had carried him over six hurdles with a grace that still made him feel as if he had been let in on a secret about what a human body could become. Now they were silent below the waist and would stay silent.

Signing had been the only answer he could afford. Meridian had installed the patch after he gave them everything they asked for, every biometric reading, every neural pattern, every genetic marker. The fix had not fixed this. The virus had taken him mid-stride and thrown him into a hurdle at nine meters per second, and his legs were gone.

*Cuídate, mi amor.*

No tears came. He opened his mouth and closed it and opened it again, and what came out was not Spanish or English, not language at all, just the noise a body makes when it understands that the thing it was built for is finished.

---

Margot had felt everything.

She was in the coaching suite, fifteen meters from the track entrance, her feed interface live on her wrist, the neural connection open and streaming. Tomas's sensory world arrived at her nervous system with a half-second delay. It was enough lag that she could not react in real time, not enough that the experience felt secondhand. She was inside his effort. Tomas's hamstrings loaded on the approach, and she felt that loading as phantom tension in her own legs, a ghost of athletic power her body had not produced in years. The numbers climbed, 168, 172, 176, and she'd pushed them there, nudging his pacing targets upward through the coaching interface because the intensity was good, the rush was clean, his cardiovascular output at 178 producing a cascade of sensation through the feed that lit her up in places her own flat, unplugged nervous system could not reach.

She knew what she was doing. She always knew. The knowing had never been enough to stop.

The sixth hurdle looked beautiful through the feed, the explosion at the bar, his body airborne for a fraction of a second, the landing shock traveling up through his legs and into her borrowed proprioception. She pushed the pacing target again. A small nudge. The display hit 182. Cardiovascular strain ran hot through him, blood pressure elevated, the vessels in his spine and legs under more strain than training range called for. She registered it like an addict registers the third drink: noted, not acted on, already reaching for the fourth.

Then the feed went blank.

It cut cleanly, as if someone had lifted a cable. One moment she was inside his effort, the rubber under his feet, the air in his lungs, the rhythmic pulse of his heart at 182, and the next she was alone in her own body, her own unstimulated nervous system, the coaching suite's fluorescent light flat on her skin. The feed display on the wall showed all channels zeroed. Heart rate still transmitting, 184 and climbing. Neural input at zero. Sensory relay at zero. Motor pattern still active. His body was still doing work at full output with no sensory data to guide it.

She should have disconnected. The protocol for feed disruption was immediate severance of the coaching link. She knew this. Her hand moved toward the interface on her wrist.

She did not disconnect.

Because some channels were coming back. Tomas's senses stayed dark. But the motor data was streaming, and through the coaching interface's relay she could feel what his body was doing. The legs driving at sprint speed. The approach to the hurdle. The impact.

She felt the impact.

Through the feed, at full sensory resolution, her nervous system received the translation of a human body hitting a hurdle at nine meters per second and going down. The shin strike came first, a sharp crack of pain radiating up through her left leg as if she had taken the bar herself. Then the chest impact, the slide, the scraping abrasion that came through as a hot strip across her sternum and ribs. Then the compression.

The spinal compression arrived as a dense total sensation that Margot had no analogy for. A closing. A compaction of the nerve pathways that carried information between brain and legs, translated through the interface into the feeling of her own lower body folding inward until the space her legs occupied in her proprioceptive map narrowed into a line and then into a point and then disappeared.

Then the channel flattened. Pain vanished with it. That was the worst part, worse than the impact, worse than the compression: the feed channel that had been streaming Tomas's lower-body sensation went flat, and Margot's nervous system received that flatness as a phantom paralysis settling over her own legs like a cold sheet.

She yanked the interface off her wrist. The disconnect was brutal, the feed severing mid-stream, the sudden lurch from Tomas's ruined sensory world back into her own. She screamed. The sound came out before she decided to make it, a raw noise with no words in it, the vocal equivalent of ripping a plug from a wall.

She was on the floor of the coaching suite. She did not remember falling. Her legs were fine. She could feel them, move them, press her palms against her thighs and feel the muscle and bone underneath, whole, intact, her own. But the phantom was still there. A layer under the real sensation, a ghost of paralysis the feed had written into her nervous system and her body had not yet overwritten. Her legs worked and they also felt dead. Both things were true at once, and she sat on the floor with her hands clamped on her thighs and her breath coming in short wet sounds that were not quite sobs.

The interface lay beside her, the wrist display still showing Tomas's vitals. Heart rate 171, dropping. Neural bridge status, gray.

She had him at 182 when the blackout hit. Training range for hurdle work was 155 to 165. She had pushed him twenty beats above threshold because the rush was better at 180, because his cardiovascular effort at maximum output produced the exact flavor of borrowed intensity she could not get anywhere else. When the virus struck and his body crashed and his spine compressed, the elevated blood pressure had been there under the impact. The stressed vessels had been there. She did not know if 160 would have left him with a bridge. She knew she had made sure it was not 160.

She sat on the floor of the coaching suite and looked at her hands, shaking in her lap. Her hands had adjusted the pacing target. Her hands had pushed the slider upward because the feedback at 182 felt better than the feedback at 165. Her hands were still attached to her body, still working, still capable of feeling the cold tile under her and the sweat drying on her forearms while a twenty-one-year-old's legs were gone and she had been inside them when they went.

---

Three hours later, Kai went to the calibration bay.

The alternative was standing in the medical bay corridor outside imaging, and he could not do that anymore. Ninety minutes in the corridor had been enough, his wrist aching, his shoulder aching, his body remembering February with a precision that felt less like memory and more like rehearsal. Meridian's fix had not saved Tomas.

From the corridor he'd heard Tomas once, not words, just the raw animal sound that had come out of him on the track after Petra named the bridge. It kept arriving in Kai's body a second late, as if the building were still throwing it back at him.

The calibration bay hummed. The sound was always there, the low drone of processing equipment Kai had learned to tune out in his first week at the facility. Tonight it sounded closer. Less like background. More like a room full of machines working on a person who had not agreed.

The rig nearest the wall was still in emergency audit mode. Post-incident protocol had kicked every station that had shared a mesh link with Tomas's session into read-only trace capture and frozen the raw routing until medical signed it off. An orange banner stretched across the screen: `POST-INCIDENT TRAFFIC REVIEW`. In ordinary athlete view Kai would have seen green status tiles and a one-line health summary. Orange mode peeled that away. Every outbound route sat under the session log until someone cleared it.

He dropped into the chair and pulled up his own logs. Neural bridge status. Graft performance. Sensory integration metrics. All green. All normal. His body continued to function inside parameters that predicted nothing.

No one had cleared this one. Petra was still in imaging with Tomas. The junior tech who'd helped carry the stabilization board had followed her with the portable scanner, leaving the bay collecting evidence nobody had time to read. Kai almost left the screen alone. Then he saw his own name in the session list.

He had no business being in an orange screen. Petra would have said that. Yusuf would have said it more gently. But the reader on the rig flashed green under his wristband because the system still recognized him as the owner of the sessions, and once the file tree opened the line between looking and knowing was only a scroll.

With his wrist against the reader, only his traffic opened. At first it looked like crash triage: facility server, Meridian sync, firmware verification, packet sizes too small to care about. One line farther down sat a destination with no server name at all, just a red triangle and a device class: `REMOVABLE STORAGE / NON-FACILITY ASSET`. He thought Petra's team might have docked a scanner there. Then he opened the manifest. Neural bridge telemetry. Proprioception logs. Graft performance data. Genetic baseline readings. Full raw dumps, gigabytes at a time. Not just today's crash, either. The timestamps rolled backward into February, into ordinary calibrations, shoulder-rehab mornings when he'd closed his eyes in this chair and listened to Yusuf tell him to relax his jaw. Every transfer carried the same operator signature: `qadir_y`. Every destination carried the same unregistered local device.

One February entry carried the note `POST-CRASH SHOULDER RANGE / WRIST LOAD TEST`. Kai remembered that session because Yusuf had made him squeeze foam around the healing wrist until he swore at him. The routing showed the pattern in sequence: facility server, Meridian sync, then the same session ID copied again sixteen seconds later to the local device. Not forwarded. Duplicated. That was the moment suspicion stopped being suspicion.

Ordinary athlete reports flattened routing into clean checkmarks and no addresses. Only the forensic trace left the routes intact. Petra's team hadn't already flagged it because the capture was pulling everything that had touched Tomas's network path, and nobody handling an acute spinal injury was paging through Kai's February calibrations while Tomas was still in imaging. Kai looked at the device class, the session dates, the file labels that were obviously his body, and knew it wasn't crash traffic.

He checked one more file because one file could still be a glitch. Same pattern. Same second copy. Same device. The theft was patient, boring, procedural. Not panic after Tomas. Not some opportunistic sweep during the crash. Yusuf had built it into ordinary days.

"Yusuf," Kai said to the empty room. Then he stood up.

Yusuf was in the back office, a small glass-walled room overlooking the main calibration floor. He sat at his desk with his glasses pushed up on his forehead and a cup of tea going cold at his elbow. Three monitors. One showed Tomas's crash diagnostic, the neural bridge failure cascade laid out in color bands Kai could not read. The other two showed code comparisons, side-by-side columns with sections highlighted in yellow.

"Yusuf." Kai stopped in the doorway. "I found the transfer history."

Yusuf looked up. A brief flicker crossed his face: professional polish rising and then failing, Birmingham showing through underneath like bone through skin.

"Right," Yusuf said. "Yeah."

"You've been copying my data. During calibration. February, March, all of it. My biometric files, neural logs, graft performance. Everything. To a device that isn't in the system."

"`POST-CRASH SHOULDER RANGE.` `WRIST LOAD TEST.` Same endpoint every time." Kai heard his own voice flatten. "You were backing me up to something you carried in yourself."

"Yeah."

"How long?"

"Since February." Yusuf's hands were on the desk, his engineer's hands, usually steady and precise. They were not steady now. "Since I got here. Before the virus, Kai. I was doing it before any of this."

The dates kept flashing in Kai harder than the code on Yusuf's monitors. February. March. All those afternoons he'd closed his eyes because calibration went faster when he stopped watching.

Kai stepped into the office. The sliding door stuck half-open behind him as usual. He did not sit. He looked at the monitors. The code on the right meant nothing to him, but the highlighted sections repeated in visible patterns.

"Tell me what I'm looking at."

"Sit down."

"I'm fine standing."

Yusuf took his glasses off his forehead and put them on. Kai had watched him make that same gesture a hundred times before calibration work, the small adjustment that meant technical mode, but now his hands shook against the frames and the whole thing looked like costume.

"Farah's getting worse," Yusuf said. "You know that. The neural data from the athletes, the integration telemetry — it maps onto pathways that are relevant to her condition. Not directly, but it's the most detailed neural-interface data in the world and Meridian was about to lock all of it behind their —"

"So you stole from us."

"Off the rig to my own drive, yeah." The Birmingham was thicker in his voice now, the professional register failing. "Raw sessions. All of them. Meridian was about to seal the archive behind that contract, and Farah hasn't got time for me to ask nicely."

Kai looked at the cold cup of tea. A skin had formed on top, thin and milky. The office smelled like old tea and cable insulation and the metallic ozone of overworked processors.

"Tomas is paralyzed," Kai said.

"I know."

"He signed. He took their fix. He still ended up on Petra's table."

"I know." Yusuf turned one screen farther toward him. "Sit down. Please."

Kai sat. The office chair was a cheap swivel model with one torn armrest, and it creaked under his weight.

"I've been in the virus code since February," Yusuf said. "Trying to work out where it came from. And it's —" He pointed at the left monitor. "Right. That column. That's the propagation core, the bit that interfaces with the calibration network. And the right column is Meridian's firmware framework. Their standard architecture."

Kai looked at the columns. Code meant nothing to him. "Yusuf."

"The nesting logic's the same. The memory handling — it's not just similar, Kai, it's the same. Same naming conventions, same function structure, it's — it came out of their development environment. It's their code."

"You're saying Meridian made the virus."

"I'm saying somebody with access to their —" Yusuf's hand was still pointing at the screen. He lowered it. "Their systems, yeah. I can't tell you who decided to do it. I don't have the meeting, I don't have an email saying go ahead. But this wasn't built outside."

The hum from the bay filled the space between them. Kai had tuned it out weeks ago. Now he could hear every layer of it.

"Tomas signed their contract," Kai said. "He gave them everything they wanted. His family's depending on that money and he still —" He looked at the cold tea. "He's twenty-one."

"The patch suppresses the virus," Yusuf said. "It doesn't eliminate it. The suppression requires Meridian's servers to keep authorizing it, which means —" Now his voice was speeding up, the Birmingham breaking through. "If they pull the authorization, you're back to the virus. And if they change the terms, you take the new terms, because the alternative is your firmware goes back to being compromised. The whole thing only works as long as they keep letting it."

"You can't prove any of that."

"No." Yusuf rubbed his face. "I can show the structural similarity. I can show the dependency. I can't prove someone sat in a room and said do this."

Kai looked at the monitors. The code comparisons blurred in his vision. None of it gave him enough technical footing to evaluate the claim, and Yusuf knew it.

"You lied to me," Kai said. "Every session. I'd lie back, close my eyes, and you'd copy my data to your private drive without saying a word."

"Yeah."

"You built an alternative patch that kills Margot's feed access, and you never told anyone your real stake in it was your sister's data."

"I told the group about Farah."

"After you'd already built it. After Lena had already said yes. You told us when it couldn't change the decision you'd pushed us toward."

Yusuf rubbed his eyes with the heel of his hand. The motion was so ordinary it threw Kai off for a second. Yusuf looked wrung out. Just a man in a glass office with cold tea at his elbow and his hands shaking on the desk.

"Tomas's neural bridge is gone," Yusuf said. The words came quiet now, Birmingham thick in them. "The virus hit his sensory firmware, same as yours. The motor system kept running. He hit the hurdle at full speed and the compression —" He stopped. Put his hand flat on the desk. "The bridge hardware is destroyed. The grafts kept his cord from severing, but without the bridge there's no pathway to his legs. The cord was already degraded from the integration process. The bridge was doing that work. And now it's not."

"Is it permanent?"

"They'd have to replace the bridge. That means a full integration procedure, months of rehab, regrowth from scratch." He looked down at the desk, blinked once, hard. "He might walk. He's not going to run."

Kai stood up. The chair rolled backward and bumped the wall.

"You should've told me," he said. "All of it. From the start."

"If I'd told you about the archive, you'd have reported me. The data would've been locked down and Farah —"

"Don't." The wrist ached. The shoulder too. "Don't make this about Farah right now. A kid is paralyzed and you've been sitting on this." He gestured at the monitors. "All of it."

"I didn't have enough to —"

"Enough for what?" Kai looked at him. "He's down the hall right now finding out he can't walk. What were you waiting for?"

Yusuf did not answer. He kept looking at the monitors, at the yellow blocks lined up beside each other, as if staring hard enough might make them settle into proof. Both hands were on the desk. They were shaking.

"What do you want to do?" Yusuf asked.

Kai looked through the glass wall at the calibration bay beyond, the rigs where athletes lay down and trusted the system to maintain them, the fluorescent lights, the humming processors. Three corridors away, Tomas was on an imaging table learning the dimensions of his new life. One floor above, Margot was sitting with the phantom of his paralysis still alive in her legs. Outside, the spring thaw was pulling the last of the winter off the mountains and the meltwater kept running downhill.

"I don't know," Kai said.

Kai left the office with the sliding door stuck half-open behind him. Yusuf's monitor light fell across the calibration bay floor in yellow bars. The rigs sat under the fluorescents with their straps loose, ready for the next athlete to lie down and hand the room his body. A few weeks ago Kai would have done it without thinking. Now the whole place looked rotten.
