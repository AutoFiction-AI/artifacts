# Chapter 15

Margot's legs were fine.

In the grey light before the sun cleared the eastern ridge, she sat on the edge of her bed with both feet flat on the cold tile, and her legs were fine. She felt the chill through her soles, the mattress edge against the backs of her thighs, the small adjustments in her calves that kept her upright. Everything worked. It had always worked.

Under the tile and the mattress and the ordinary fact of a body sitting on a bed at six in the morning was Tomas's phantom. Through the feed she had felt his neural bridge break. Full-resolution sensation came down the bidirectional path between her nervous system and his: compression in the lower spine, signals running through his legs, then nothing at all. The stop at the waist had been clean and dead.

On the third step the phantom hit, a skip in the signal, a gap as if her left leg's proprioception blanked for one fraction of a second. Her hand shot to the desk. The gap closed. Another step. Fine. Another. Fine. On the seventh step the right leg vanished the same way and her weight pitched sideways until the wall caught her.

She stayed there until the wave passed. Tomas was in the medical bay paralyzed from the waist down, and she was standing in her own room waiting for borrowed deadness to drain out of working legs.

The feed interface sat on the nightstand where she'd dropped it. Her hand closed around it before she'd decided to move. The sensor pad fit her palm. At once the inside of her right wrist went alert, skin remembering the pressure of the clasp. Saliva gathered under her tongue. Her body issued the old sequence: fasten it, wait for the handshake, get out of here. Tomas was in the medical bay unable to feel his legs, and her body still knew the sequence. A scratch crossed the face of the device from a doorframe in Stuttgart, three years ago. The indicator light stayed dark.

In the bathroom she turned on the shower and waited through thirty seconds of cold pipe before the heat reached the coaches' wing. Usually the shower marked the end of a feed. Steam, hot water, the brute fact of her own skin: that was how she burned someone else's body out of hers. Today the place where the strap had sat felt naked under the spray. She kept setting her jaw against the heat.

Under the fluorescent strip above the mirror waited a thirty-five-year-old woman with a former swimmer's shoulders and hips widened past her competition frame. The body that had placed fifth at the Olympics and then stopped mattering. The body she'd spent three years climbing out of through other people's effort.

Back at the bed, the interface ended up in her hands again. Shock had damped her pulse. The wanting stayed. Her thumb kept circling the concave pad. Before she noticed, she'd lifted her right arm halfway, elbow already bent for the fastening motion. She dropped both hands to her lap. The want sat low in her gut, mean and stupid and alive.

She dressed without looking at the wall display. Black training pants. Grey sweater. Socks. The ordinary uniform of a coach whose day was supposed to start in the suite with athlete schedules and performance notes. When she finally glanced up, the room had cycled to the facility bulletin. VISITING RESTRICTIONS: MEDICAL WING - AUTHORIZED STAFF AND DESIGNATED ATHLETE CONTACTS ONLY. Under it, weather. Under that, breakfast hours. The list of food made her stomach turn, not from nausea exactly but from the stupid persistence of appetite in a world where Tomas Reyes had woken up without legs.

She left the room anyway and walked the corridor toward the medical wing before she'd decided what she was doing. The building smelled of cleaning solution, wet concrete, coffee from the cafeteria below. Night staff were crossing with day staff at the junctions, clipped greetings, badges catching the thin light. Nobody stopped her. Nobody had the authority yet.

Tomas's recovery door stood at the far end of the corridor under a privacy strip that turned the glass panel opaque. A portable monitor sat just inside the threshold, green bars moving across it too far away to read. There was no sound except the air system and, once, the short motorized whine of a bed adjusting position.

She stopped two steps short of the door.

Yesterday she had been in his body when the hurdle came wrong, in his spine when the impact telescoped everything below the waist into pressure and then absence. Now there was a sheet of institutional paper over the window and a sealed door and whatever version of waking he was doing on the other side of it. Knock and say what? I wanted the cleanest version of your legs. I kept taking it. I pushed until the signal got sharp and then I kept pushing.

The phantom took her left leg again. She caught the wall with one palm and waited through the blank fraction while the fluorescent lights buzzed over her head. By the time the floor came back, a nurse in navy scrubs had turned into the corridor from imaging with a tablet tucked against her chest. Margot straightened before the nurse could reach her, walked away without looking up, and felt the wanting move under her skin like a second pulse.

She made it as far as the cafeteria after that because bodies still moved on schedule even when everything else had split. Trays knocked against rails. Coffee steamed in thick brown urns. Someone at the back table laughed too hard at something that wasn't funny enough, the forced volume of people trying to make ordinary noise in the wrong morning. Tomas's usual chair by the window sat empty under a stripe of pale light.

Margot took coffee because her hands needed something to do. The paper cup burned her fingers. The first swallow tasted of metal and old filter paper. She looked down the length of the room and found athletes looking away one by one with the quickness of people who had already heard some version of the story and didn't yet know whether pity or disgust was the correct response. A junior cyclist's eyes dropped to the strap mark on her wrist and snapped off it again.

She left the cup half full beside the condiment station. By the time she hit the stairs the wanting had already outrun the caffeine. Her palm kept checking for the weight of the dead interface in her pocket as if she might somehow have lost it in the two minutes she'd spent pretending coffee and fluorescent light still belonged to the same world as breakfast.

Back in her room she opened the cached telemetry from yesterday's session, the last one the interface would ever record if Petra did what Petra should do. Twelve beats per minute. Tomas's heart rate had been running twelve beats above target when the blackout hit. She'd put it there. She'd pushed his pacing parameters because the feed sharpened above the safety margin, because his body's exertion at that intensity made the borrowed sensation so clean her own body dropped out, because that disappearance was the thing she kept coming back for.

She opened older files. Tomas two days earlier. Lena last week. Kim on Tuesday. Annika before that. In every overlay the green prescribed zone sat underneath the red trace by exactly the amount she'd learned for that athlete. Enough to sharpen. Not enough to trip the hard-stop alerts. She had spent three years teaching herself to read that gap as craft. Now it looked like handwriting.

Twelve beats per minute. It meant elevated cardiovascular load during a virus-triggered blackout, higher blood pressure, more stress through the tissue when he hit. She didn't know whether those twelve beats made the difference between a recoverable injury and a permanent one. A biomechanics team could give her a model, a confidence interval, a distribution of outcomes. None of it would answer the only question that mattered. Tomas Reyes was twenty-one years old and couldn't feel his legs.

Spring light cut farther into the room than it had a month ago, reaching across the desk and touching the far wall. On the south-facing slopes outside, snow had retreated above the tree line. Brown earth below, mud and scree, the skeleton of the landscape exposed after months of burial. Runoff moved through the drainage channels along the foundation in a continuous murmur.

No tears came. Crying would have been about her own distress, and her distress was not the thing in the medical bay behind the closed door.

The phantom caught her right leg again. This time she stayed sitting and let it pass.

---

At nine Petra sent for her. A blue rectangle appeared on the wall display in her quarters with Dr. Engel's name, a room number, and a time thirty-two minutes ahead. Beneath it the display listed Margot's own biometrics: resting heart rate, sleep quality index, enhancement interface status. The interface status read INACTIVE. For three years it had read ACTIVE.

Four minutes took her through the connecting corridor and past windows that faced the south slope, where snowmelt had turned the access road to mud. A middle-distance runner and a gymnast came the other way. Neither looked at her. Maybe they hadn't heard yet. Maybe they had and were choosing not to look. Either option settled the same in her stomach.

At the junction between the residential wing and the medical wing, her left leg disappeared for that fraction of a second again. The wall caught her palm. A maintenance worker at the far end of the corridor glanced over and then looked away.

Petra's office was glass-walled and adjacent to the medical bay proper. Past Petra's desk, past the interior window on the far wall, ran the corridor to the recovery rooms. Tomas's door was closed. It had been closed since they brought him in yesterday.

Petra was standing when Margot entered. That was wrong. Petra was a deliberate sitter. She positioned herself at eye level, a clinical habit that implied equality even when the power was entirely hers. Standing meant she hadn't settled, or hadn't been able to.

"Close the door."

Margot closed it.

Two screens on Petra's desk held data Margot recognized at once: feed session logs. The format her interface generated automatically every time she connected to an athlete. Heart-rate tracking, respiratory rate, neural feedback markers, session duration, connection quality. Weeks of sessions, laid out as time series.

"Sit down," Petra said.

The chair was standard medical-office issue, padded and adjustable, built for patients receiving news. Margot had sat across from Petra in it twice before for routine coaching certifications. Those meetings had lasted fifteen minutes. This one would not.

Petra stayed standing long enough to brace one hand on the desk. "Yesterday's spinal injury triggered a catastrophic-incident audit. Tomas lost motor function during a virus event, so I asked IT to open cross-system review. Yusuf needed the full pathway map for the forty-eight hours before the crash. I wanted every network, every session, every route into his system."

Margot said nothing.

"Under routine operations medical doesn't receive raw coach-side telemetry," Petra said. "We get your session plans, any variance forms you submit, and automatic alerts when an athlete crosses a hard safety threshold. The live overlays stay in the performance suite unless a coach asks for medical review or I open an audit."

Petra tapped the left screen. "Your athletes stayed below the hard-stop alarms. Above prescription, yes. Above the ceiling, no. So the pattern sat in another system and read as ordinary coaching data unless someone laid the raw trace over the prescribed zone and looked for chronic drift."

She tapped again, harder. "After Kai Brennan's crash we were tracing calibration corruption and blackout events. We were looking for the virus moving through shared calibration. We were not auditing six weeks of coach load decisions inside a firewalled performance feed. Tomas's paralysis changed the scope. The audit changed the access."

The data was already on the screen when Petra turned it toward her. "Your feed session logs with Tomas Reyes. February eighth through yesterday. Thirty-one sessions."

Each session sat on its own track. The target heart-rate zone Margot had filed in her session plans showed in green. The actual heart rate showed in red. The red lines ran above the green lines in every session.

"Average deviation of twelve beats per minute above the prescribed training zone," Petra said. She sounded like someone reading findings. Her gaze stayed fixed on Margot. "The pattern is consistent. Same gap, session after session, across the full six-week period."

"Yes," Margot said.

Petra waited. When nothing else came, she scrolled to a second dataset. Different athlete. Same pattern. Target below, actual above, the gap steady and systematic.

"Lena Morozova. Twenty-three sessions. Average deviation of eight beats per minute." Another scroll. "David Kim. Fourteen sessions. Average deviation of fifteen beats." Another. "Annika Brandt. Nine sessions. Eleven beats."

Four athletes. Weeks of sessions. Systematic elevation. No documented medical justification for any of it.

"I'm not going to ask you why," Petra said. "The telemetry is clear enough."

On the screen, clean colored lines showed the gap between what Margot had promised and what she'd done. The logs had always been there. They generated automatically. She'd never deleted them. Hadn't even considered deleting them. The real concealment had been procedural: keeping the damage in the strip of space between a target zone and a hard-stop alarm, trusting that nobody outside the coaching suite would bother to open the right overlay until somebody broke badly enough to force them to.

"I've also reviewed the network pathway data with Yusuf," Petra said. Her voice lost some of its clinical remove. "The neural coaching feed creates a bidirectional data channel. When you connect to an athlete's system, the pathway opens in both directions."

Margot watched her own hands stay still in her lap.

"Every feed session you ran opened an additional propagation route for the virus through the shared calibration network." Petra wasn't hedging now. The usual qualifiers were gone. "Yusuf has confirmed that the feed pathway is a viable transmission vector. The virus could propagate through a coaching connection the same way it propagates through calibration."

"I know," Margot said.

Petra was quiet for a beat. "When."

"February. After the facility went to restricted operations. After Kai Brennan's crash."

Petra's hand went to her glasses, adjusted them, returned to the desk. "You identified the feed as a transmission risk six weeks ago. And you continued running sessions through yesterday."

"Every one of them."

The silence that followed belonged to a doctor who had heard the answer she expected and was now recalculating the scope of the damage. Petra sat down slowly, as if the act required thought. Her chair creaked. She took off her glasses and cleaned them on the hem of her sweater, a gesture Margot knew well, something Petra did with her hands when the rest of her needed to hold still.

"Thirty-one sessions with Tomas. Twenty-three with Lena. Fourteen with Kim. Nine with Brandt. Seventy-seven potential virus exposures beyond the shared calibration network." Petra put the glasses back on. "Seventy-seven sessions where you knew you might be increasing viral load on athletes whose systems were already compromised."

There was nothing to say. The number said it.

"Your coaching privileges are suspended effective immediately," Petra said. "Your feed interface authorization is revoked at the network level. IT will deactivate your connection credentials by end of day. You're not to connect to any athlete's neural system for any purpose. Can you confirm that verbally?"

"I understand. I won't connect."

"You'll retain access to your quarters and common areas. You're not to enter the coaching suite, the calibration bay, or any training environment without my explicit authorization."

"Okay."

Petra did not move on. "By seventeen hundred I want a written accounting of every feed session where the chart and the raw telemetry tell different stories. Any anomaly you normalized. Any symptom you minimized. Any athlete you put above prescription and then documented as acceptable."

"All right."

Petra looked through the layered glass at Tomas's closed recovery-room door. Margot looked too. It was the same institutional white as every other door in the wing and the only one shut in the middle of a weekday morning.

"The heart-rate elevation during yesterday's session," Petra said. Her voice was quieter now. "His cardiovascular load was elevated when the blackout triggered. I've asked the biomechanics team to model whether the elevation contributed to the severity of the spinal outcome."

"It might've," Margot said.

"It might have. Yes." For an instant Petra's face contracted around the eyes before control returned. "I flagged a rejection risk at the Stuttgart facility four years ago. Marius Koch. Decathlete. Twenty-four. I wrote the report and filed it through proper channels. Meridian's medical oversight board reviewed my findings and downgraded the risk classification. He died eight months later from the condition I'd identified."

Margot said nothing.

"I'm not comparing what you did to what they did. The mechanisms are different." Petra stopped and started the sentence again on a different track. "I've spent four years knowing I should've made more noise. Should've refused to let the institution's process overrule my clinical judgment. That's what I'm doing now."

"I know what you're doing," Margot said.

Petra nodded once. Shadows sat under her eyes. Blue ink marked the side of one finger. She looked like someone who'd been awake too long and knew she'd be awake longer.

"You should go," Petra said.

When Margot stood, the phantom erased her left leg for half a beat and her fingertips caught Petra's desk.

Petra's eyes went to the catch, the recovery, the hand on the desk. "What was that?"

"Phantom. From the feed. From yesterday. It'll pass."

"When did it start?"

"When I disconnected from Tomas."

Petra went still. "You're carrying phantom sensation from his injury?"

"His legs going dead. It comes in waves. My nervous system stored it."

"I can order a neural scan. If the phantom is still present tonight, you report to medical. That isn't optional."

Margot almost said no on reflex. The word got as far as her teeth and stopped. "Okay."

Back in the corridor, another wave took her right leg near the junction with the residential wing. She stopped with one hand on the wall until it cleared, then kept walking.

---

Kai found her outside.

The facility's service entrance opened on the north side, onto a loading dock with a rusted safety rail and a clear view of the valley road. From there the switchbacks were visible all the way down through the tree line to where the road leveled toward Meiringen. It was the one place on campus where a person could stand outside and not see any part of the building. Margot sat on an overturned equipment crate near the rail without a jacket, her breath still showing faintly in the cold.

He'd spent most of the morning with Yusuf in the calibration bay, in front of the screens and the code comparisons and the highlighted sections that might prove something if proof were a thing any of them actually had. Structural signatures in the virus matched Meridian's proprietary development frameworks well enough to convince an engineer and not well enough to survive a legal challenge or a press conference. Suggestive, Yusuf kept saying. Four months to the opening ceremony, a paralyzed kid in the medical bay, and a corporate entity that had sold the cure for a disease it had probably made.

Lena had found him when he came out of the bay to rinse the coffee taste out of his mouth. Her hair was still damp at the ends from the pool. Chlorine sat sharp on her sweatshirt.

"Petra suspended Margot," she'd said.

"For what?"

"The feed logs." Lena had held his gaze the way she did when precision mattered. "The raw telemetry. Not the summaries. Tomas. Me. Other athletes. My heart rate was above the prescribed zone in every session Petra reviewed."

"How many sessions?"

"Twenty-three with me. Thirty-one with Tomas." She had looked past him then, toward the window over the stairwell where meltwater streaked the glass. "Petra asked if there were incidents I did not report because I trusted the chart more than what I remembered. I said yes."

He had asked how she was before he could decide whether the question was useful.

Lena's mouth had tightened as if she were testing a word for fit. "I don't know yet," she said. "It's still becoming specific."

That was all she gave him. He knew better than to push harder while she was still arranging the facts into something her body could live beside. He left the question where it was and went to find Margot.

When she heard him, she looked up. Her face had gone flat. The humor she usually carried like a blade, the sharp self-aware darkness that made people uncomfortable when they realized she wasn't entirely joking, was gone. What remained was stripped down and ugly.

"Lena," he said.

"Yeah."

He'd had the words organized on the walk over, accusation first, then the questions, then whatever came after. Standing three feet from her crate in the cold, the sequence fell apart.

"Petra pulled the feed logs. The heart-rate data."

"I know what's in them."

He stayed standing. Standing over her would have been wrong, but sitting next to her would have been worse. So he took the space between those two bad options and stopped at the edge of the dock, close enough to talk and far enough not to share anything.

"She lost two laps," he said. "In the pool. She was in the water and she wasn't there."

"I was in the feed when it happened-"

"Don't tell me what it felt like."

Something moved once at the corner of her mouth and died. "Fine."

"Tell me what you did."

She swallowed once, hard enough to shift her whole throat. "I pushed her heart rate up because that's when the feed peaked. The desync made it sharper. I told her it was within acceptable parameters. It wasn't."

"Did you know she was worse than the charts?"

"Yes."

"Since when?"

"By February." Her eyes went past him to the road and came back. "No. Earlier than that I knew something was wrong. February was when I stopped being able to call it noise."

Below them, a truck engine moved along the valley road and vanished around a switchback. Across the valley the mountains were still mostly white, but the snow had pulled off the rock faces and left the stone dark and wet in the midday sun.

"So you kept her running hot anyway."

"Yeah."

"And Tomas."

"Yeah."

He watched her breathing stay shallow in the cold. "Tell me what the feed is. Not the technical version."

"There isn't another version."

"Try."

She licked dry lips once. "You connect and their signals come through. Legs, lungs, water, impact, all of it. When the load goes up, it gets cleaner."

"Cleaner."

"Sharper." Her breath snagged and came out white. "More of them. Less of me."

He said nothing.

"At a certain point I stop noticing my own body." She dug her fingers into the rough crate edge until the wood bit her palms. "The room thins out. My skin goes dead. Their effort doesn't. That's the part I wanted."

"Like a high."

"Yes."

"Like stealing."

"Yes."

"Three years."

"I started in Stuttgart. Before I came here. First time was an accident. I set a calibration wrong, the athlete's heart rate spiked, the feed got-" She cut herself off hard enough to show teeth. "It felt good. I did it again on purpose. Then again. I learned the numbers."

"Everybody has a number."

"Not exact. A range." She looked at him squarely. "Tomas was twelve above target."

He pressed his wrist against the cold safety rail until the metal bit into bone. The pain was clean. It helped.

"When I stopped competing, the wanting didn't stop with me," Margot said. "My body did. The wanting didn't. The feed was the closest thing. Someone else's body doing what mine couldn't. After a session the room came back wrong. Flat. Food tasted dead. Water felt like nothing. I'd spend the day waiting for the next one."

He thought of Lena at the pool wall with water running off her shoulders, asking what had happened to the last two laps. Margot had called it acceptable.

"That's why Lena."

"That's why all of them." She didn't look away. "Lena was worse because the desync sharpened it. Tomas because hurdles gave me impact and load. Kim because sprint sessions burned hot. I knew what I was choosing every time."

"And the virus."

"I figured it out after your crash. After the facility locked down. The bidirectional feed wasn't sealed off from calibration. Every session was another route in."

"You knew and kept going."

"Yes."

"Seventy-seven times."

"Yes."

Wind moved through the loading dock hard enough to rattle the loose chain on the side gate. She didn't flinch.

"Why not stop?" he said, and kept talking before she could answer with something tidy. "Not in general. Why not stop when it was Lena. When you knew what was happening to her."

Margot's jaw worked once. "Because by then I was already building my days around it. Because I could tell myself I had rules and that was bullshit. Because stopping meant being in my own body full time." She looked down at the pale strap mark without touching it. "That wasn't enough for me. That's the answer."

He let that sit between them. There wasn't a cleaner version under it. That was the worst part.

"Petra asked for a statement by five," Margot said after a while. "All the sessions where the chart and the telemetry don't match."

"Put Lena in it."

"I was going to."

"Put the missing laps in it. The days you wrote acceptable when they weren't. Every time you let the chart lie for you."

Her face did something like a wince and flattened again. "Okay."

"And you stay away from her unless she asks for you."

"Okay."

The apology came after that, too late to redirect anything, too plain to be strategy.

"I'm sorry," Margot said. "About Lena. About Tomas. About all of it."

"Don't give that to me," Kai said. His voice had gone quiet enough that he barely recognized it as his. "I'm not the route in."

She nodded once. "All right."

He stood. The backs of his thighs were cold. The mountains stayed where they were, patient and indifferent.

He went back inside. Behind him, Margot stayed on the crate in the cold. He didn't look back to see whether she was still sitting there or whether Tomas's phantom had taken her leg again. The loading-dock door shut behind him, and the warmth on his face felt like re-entering a building where everything was wrong and would stay wrong and the opening ceremony was still four months away and Tomas's door was still closed.
