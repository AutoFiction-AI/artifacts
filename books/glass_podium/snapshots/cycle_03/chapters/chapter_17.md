# Chapter 17

The ready room was underground. The venue sat over Lena like a second pressure system, concrete and cooling ducts and forty thousand seats holding themselves above her head. In Kirchberg the pool had always sounded alive before she entered it: pumps, air handlers, the feed hiss in Margot's wrist unit, all the little system noises braided into one thing. Here the room was cleaner than that, almost ceremonial. Her own skull stayed cleaner still. There were no ambient calculations and no low hum of suggested timing, only silence where there had always been company.

Seven other swimmers waited with her. Lena knew most of them by shoulders, kick timing, and the habits that survived from meet to meet. Adelaïde Moreau stretched in the corner with the saint medallion she always tucked into her suit before the walk out. Kaede Watanabe had her eyes closed and her lips moving through some private rehearsal. Nina Okoro paced the far wall with three fingers taped bright green. The room held that smooth patched readiness Lena could still read before anyone touched the water, bodies already halfway into the race through systems she no longer trusted. Months ago she would have recognized it as ordinary. Tonight it looked slightly indecent, the body being helped in ways nobody in the seats would ever see.

Her towel lay across her knees. The terry cloth kept catching on the dry skin of her fingertips. She pulled the strap on one goggle, then the other, equal pressure, and made herself stop there. The old Lena would have been running invisible checklists without naming them, stroke count windows and turn timing and the fine adjustments a linked system offered so smoothly they had once felt like intuition. This version had to do the rude manual work. She took four controlled breaths, checked that the right shoulder was loose enough and the left calf free of cramp, and gave herself a plain instruction: keep the first hundred clean, no heroics, count to the wall yourself, stay in the water you can actually feel.

One of the volunteers at the door checked the line against a tablet and called, "Morozova. Lane five."

Lena rose. The room changed around her as everyone else rose too, the brief scrape of benches and the soft slap of bare feet on sealed concrete. Moreau touched two fingers to the medallion at her sternum and followed the official. Watanabe opened her eyes and looked not calm but already absent, the way patched athletes sometimes looked just before the body handed certain jobs off. Lena did not want that look anymore. She wanted the ugly human version with all the drag left in it.

The corridor to the pool deck was fluorescent and cold. Chilled air came through the vents hard enough to dry the chlorine on her arms before she reached the light. At the final turn in the corridor the sound of the crowd stopped being abstract vibration and became one enormous moving thing. She walked into it and the lights found her at once. They came down hard enough to glaze the water and bleach the color out of faces. Blue-green pool, black lane ropes, white splash guards, cameras on their tracks above the far side. Lane five waited in the middle as if the whole building had been built around it.

For one second she looked toward the coaches' section out of old habit. It was only an empty reflex. Margot was in Munich. The place where a coach would have been was just another rectangle of dark seating and people in credentials. That was good. Let the race belong to the people actually in it.

The starter introduced them one by one. Lena stepped onto the block when her name came and the crowd made the broad appreciative noise crowds made for finalists they did not yet care about individually. Moreau stood in four, heavy favorite, French flag painted on one cheek by some child in the stands behind her. Watanabe was in three, still and narrow. Lin in six shook out both arms once and then went expressionless. Lena bent, settled her feet against the rough surface, and let the small useless wish pass through her that the dive would arrive already solved.

The tone sounded and she went.

Water hit her blunt and grainy instead of opening into the old second language of pressure and signal. There was no soft guided feeling of the lane receiving her. Depth was just depth. Cold was just cold. The first pull bit high and clean anyway. She kept the shoulder engaged, the elbow up, and the kick hard enough to hold the line. She broke out under the flags with Moreau half a body to her right and Watanabe's white splash low and efficient to her left. Fifty meters disappeared in ordinary human labor.

On the first turn she counted late on purpose because she still did not fully trust the place where instinct had used to live. She took six strokes from the flags, made the last one longer, tucked, planted, and pushed. The wall gave her a good clean return and for a moment the race simplified into pressure on the soles of her feet and the long narrow relief of streamline. When she came off it she was even with Moreau and a fraction behind Watanabe. That was fine. It was more than fine. It meant she had judged the wall herself and not been wrong.

At a hundred the field was still pretending balance. Moreau's turnover remained quiet, economical, almost rude in how little surface she disturbed for the speed she carried. Lin was coming up outside. Lena turned to breathe and got one brief sliced image of the crowd above the lane rope, mouths open, white panels of advertisement, then water again. The patch had taken away a layer of anticipatory ease she had once mistaken for talent. Every correction now arrived after perception, not before it. She had to feel the hand entering wrong, then fix it. Feel the hips rise too far, then press them down. The race did not forgive the delay, but neither did it take her away from herself.

At a hundred and fifty her deltoids began to burn in the steady nonnegotiable way that meant the body had stopped lying. A different race might have frightened her there. This one did not. Fear would have required room for abstraction, and abstraction had no place in lane five. She hit the wall, pushed, took two underwater kicks, and surfaced. Moreau's feet were still just ahead and then they began, inch by inch, to slide away.

Two hundred brought the part of the evening everyone else would later call decisive. The split numbers flashed at the turn, bright and gone before she could read more than shape. Air came fast on the breath to the right. Lin was there now with a harder, more urgent tempo, the outside lane suddenly level with Lena's shoulder and then not level at all. Watanabe held for a little longer, then her line started to fray. Lena flipped, pushed, and felt the push come back shorter than the one before it, not because the wall was different but because nothing inside her was smoothing out the waste.

That was where the old system used to become seductive. In bad races, in dangerous races, in training sets where the body wanted to quit or drift or blur, something behind her own decisions had always started solving small problems early. The hand rotated a degree before the catch could slip. The kick corrected depth before she noticed she had flattened out. The turn arrived as if a quieter smarter swimmer had done it first and she was merely inheriting the work. For years she had called that integration. Tonight all of it was gone. Her right hand came in slightly wide and she had to pull it back into the line herself. Her feet hit the next wall a shade too high and she had to kick once to settle before the first pull. Every error cost time because every error belonged to her.

The third hundred broke the field open. Moreau and Lin went into their own race. Watanabe came back toward Lena the way Watanabe always did, late suffering written into the Japanese swimmer's whole style. Lena went by on stubbornness and a cleaner line off the wall, but the pass cost her. Her elbow dropped. Water leaked around the heel of her palm. Chlorine sat at the back of her throat with the hot metallic taste that came when she was breathing too often and still not getting enough air. By three hundred the board was no longer a set of positions. It was just proof that the thing kept moving whether or not anyone in it approved.

The lane narrowed in the last hundred, not physically but morally. Nothing in it softened. Her shoulders filled with acid so thoroughly the burn became a texture rather than a pain. The crowd above the water was a thick muffled weather, loud for somebody, not specifically for lane five. On the breath she could see Moreau's lead only as bubbles and foamed white wake; on the opposite breath Lin's outside splash entered and exited her vision like weather seen through a train window. There was no lane map, no pacing advice, no synthetic whisper that this wall required six strokes instead of seven. There was only counting and getting it wrong by half a hand and still having to live with the result.

At three hundred and fifty her triceps began to tremble on the recovery. Lena felt the tremor all the way into her wrist, ugly and specific. Good. Let it be ugly. Let it be specific. On the final turn she nearly over-rotated because fatigue had shifted her sense of depth by a breath's width. Her heels found the wall crooked, corrected, pushed. The correction cost a fraction and then the final length was underway and there was nothing left to bargain with. She did not need the race to become beautiful. She needed it to remain continuous.

The last fifty meters took place at a speed that would have felt insulting six months earlier. Each stroke had to be built in public, hand in and catch and pull and breathe and hold the line. There was no missing stretch of water, no blank seam stitched across the memory later, no cold astonishment of surfacing with work done by someone else. Her shoulders were close to failure, palms going numb from overworked tendons, legs soft at the end of each kick, and she was there for every centimeter of it. The finish came under her hand like a blunt fact.

She touched and came up hard, mouth open, a sheet of chlorine running off her face. The first breath tore. The second was worse. She got both forearms onto the touchpad and hung there while the whole building resumed shape around her.

The board across the pool gave her 4:08.71. Moreau sat first at 3:49.03. Lin took second at 3:50.88. Lena's name dropped in under them in third, a fraction of a second before the lane number locked beside it. It was bronze, and it was slow by any standard that had governed her old life, slower than the system would once have permitted. But the distance between dive and wall remained intact in her head. There was no gap and no theft, only the full humiliating complete four hundred meters.

A volunteer met her at the edge with a towel and a hand under one elbow because her legs had not yet decided whether land was a reasonable idea. "Warm-down pool that way," the volunteer said in English practiced for international use. "Then ceremony in twelve minutes."

Lena nodded. Speaking took too much breath.

The recovery pool behind the competition lanes was half shadow and half television spill. Swimmers from earlier finals moved through it in slow exhausted circles, each in their own country jacket or flag or private misery. Lena slid in anyway. The water sat differently on the patch than race water did: flatter, less urgent, not the medium where she had once vanished but only warm chlorinated resistance closing around sore skin. She took the lane rope with one hand and let her body lengthen under the surface. Muscles in both shoulders twitched in delayed revolt. Her heart was still trying to outrun the finish.

She could still count the turns backward: six strokes from the flags, Lin going by outside on the third hundred, the slight crooked plant at three fifty. The lane rope left a red braid across her palm where she held it too hard.

She rolled onto her back and looked at the rafters. In the relay of giant venue screens and mirrored glass above, her own race was already running again. Her right hand leaked water at the catch in exactly the place she remembered it, and at the final turn both triceps gave the same ugly little shake they had given her in lane five.

An official in a navy blazer knelt by the recovery pool and said, "Ms. Morozova, medal ceremony in three minutes."

By the time Lena climbed out, the medal finalists' towels and sandals had already been lined up with small bureaucratic care. Someone handed her a jacket in Swiss team colors though she had not felt Swiss on a pool deck since childhood, maybe never. Chlorine cooled on her skin under the fabric. The walk to the podium took place through a service corridor lined with screens, and every screen had found the same frame from her finish: head up, mouth open, face not triumphant and not devastated either, just brutally alive.

Moreau stood on the center block with the easy upright posture of someone whose body had given her what it usually gave. Lin took silver with both shoulders drawn back as if she were presenting the medal to the cameras rather than receiving it. Lena climbed onto the third step and felt a brief shiver run through both calves as the legs understood they were done. The bronze ribbon settled cold against the back of her neck before the medal itself found her sternum with a small decisive weight. Metal and body heat negotiated. The medal warmed.

The anthem for Moreau went up into the cooled air. Lena did not sing anything, did not mouth anything, did not look at the cameras when they moved in. The giant screen above them cut among the three medalists and then, for no good reason except that this was the story people wanted, cut to the lower graphic from the lane introductions again with a side note about Meridian's voluntary update. Refused. Declined. Unpatched. The exact wording shifted depending on which feed was active. The meaning did not. Bronze had become an argument before she had even dried her hair.

Applause carried them off the podium. Another volunteer touched Lena's elbow and pointed her toward the mixed zone.

The bronze hung cold and heavy at first, then warm where it rested against her sternum. Cameras waited behind the barrier in a long bright row. Before she had reached the taped mark on the floor, a reporter leaned over the rail with a microphone and asked, "Did refusing Meridian's update cost you the gold tonight?"

The question arrived in English and then again from her left in French. Lena looked past the microphones to the monitor overhead, where the replay was already running her through the middle two hundred with Moreau cleanly opening the gap.

"It cost me time," she said.

"Do you regret it?"

"No."

The first reporter tried again, louder because someone behind him was already talking. "You swam almost nineteen seconds off your season best. Is that a result you can build on, or—"

"It is the result I have."

"—or is it a sign that unpatched athletes can't compete at this level?"

Lena shifted the towel higher on one shoulder. Her deltoids had started to stiffen and the motion cost more than she wanted anyone with a camera to see. "I don't know what other unpatched athletes can do. I swam my race."

Another microphone came in from below, branded with a network she did not recognize. "You said you have no regrets. Can you expand on that?"

"No."

The reporter waited. When she didn't continue, he tried from another angle. "Do you mean the patch prevented another desynchronization event?"

"I didn't desynchronize. I was slow."

"Isn't that the same thing?"

"No."

That got a brief shuffle behind the barrier, reporters recalibrating. The handler to Lena's right shifted closer, wanting to move her along, but another reporter had already found the gap.

"Was this a political decision? A statement against Meridian?"

"It wasn't political."

"Then what was it?"

Lena tightened her grip on the towel because the medal kept knocking against her sternum every time she breathed. She could feel them waiting for the sentence that would fit on a screen. "I wanted to swim the race and know I was swimming it," she said. "That's all I can tell you."

"Is third place enough for that?"

"I'm not comparing them."

The young reporter opened his mouth, but the handler put a hand at the small of Lena's back and said, "One more."

A French reporter with a calm face and a notebook instead of a microphone said, "Mademoiselle Morozova, when you say you wanted to know you were swimming — are you saying that before this, you did not know?"

Lena stopped. That was at least a real question.

She answered in French because he had asked in French and because it let her be exact in a slightly different way. "Sometimes I lost sequence," she said. "Not consciousness. Sequence. This was slower. It was also mine."

He nodded once, wrote it down, and did not ask her to improve it.

The handler moved her on. Above the barrier the subtitle track had already found the line in English and was laying it under replay footage of her third turn: THIS WAS SLOWER. IT WAS ALSO MINE.

The corridor out of the mixed zone curved beneath the seating bowl. By the time Lena reached the athletes' passage, venue staff were already discussing the next event on headsets, voices clipped and practical, as if the race she had just swum had been a tray they had set down and cleared away. That, too, felt correct. Public scrutiny was enormous and impersonal at the same time. Forty thousand people had watched her choose herself and none of them could do anything with that except turn it into language that fit on a screen.

She should have gone to the village transport. She should have showered, eaten something bland, answered the federation message that had come in during warm-down. Instead she took the stairs up toward the athletes' seating with the medal tucked under her jacket so it would stop striking her chest every time she walked.

The track infield opened below her all at once, red and white under harsher light than the pool had carried. Field-event officials moved equipment with efficient indifference. A men's relay semifinal was ending on the far straight. On the giant board above the curve a studio panel in sharp clothes was already using her race as a question. One commentator said the bronze proved the danger of refusing validated updates. Another said it proved courage. A third said something about athlete autonomy that turned the whole thing smooth and generic. None of them had seen the exact moment her heels hit the wall crooked at three fifty.

Lena took a seat high enough to see lane seven on the curve when the assignment board came up. Brennan. Seven. The medal lay warm now beneath the jacket, a second pulse that was not a pulse at all.

The warm-up hall under the track smelled of rubber dust, menthol gel, and overworked air-conditioning. Kai was on a stationary bike facing a cinderblock wall when Lena's face came up on the monitor mounted above the stretching lane. No sound reached him from there, only the silent shape of her mouth under captions he could read by lifting his eyes a little: THIS WAS SLOWER. IT WAS ALSO MINE.

He looked back at the wall and kept pedaling.

The left wrist had started complaining an hour earlier and had not stopped. The ache sat along the outside ridge where the break had sealed too fast, not sharp, just insistent, a remembered failure with good manners. Sweat moved cold under his singlet. The legs felt ready in the narrow technical sense, hamstrings loaded, calves responsive, both grafts answering warm-up commands without lag. Readiness had stopped being reassuring months ago. Readiness only meant the machinery was armed.

Yusuf came through the security curtain with a credential bouncing against his chest and a tablet in one hand. The expression on his face was the one Kai had come to distrust most over the last months: not panic, not honesty, but professional composure polished until it hid everything under it. He stopped at the bike and did not waste time pretending he had come for any reason except one more attempt.

"You can still scratch," Yusuf said.

Kai kept pedaling for another three revolutions before he answered. "No."

"They'd call it precaution. Nobody would make a thing of it until later."

"Later is still a thing."

Yusuf glanced once at the silent monitor where Lena's subtitled face had given way to a replay of some pole vaulter's approach. "I'm not talking about reputation."

"I know what you're talking about."

The bike clicked under Kai's feet, smooth little cycles with none of the violence the actual race would require. Yusuf lowered his voice, though there was nobody close enough to hear.

"At this propagation level the probability of a triggered event in a two-hundred-meter race is significant."

"You told me that two days ago."

"It didn't become less true because you were tired of hearing it."

Fair enough. Kai let the bike coast down and stood carefully, because stepping off too fast sometimes brought a short ugly protest from the left calf even when nothing was actually wrong. "You want the useful answer or the real one?"

"Preferably both."

"Useful answer is I know the model." He wiped his hands on the sides of his shorts and immediately regretted it because the fabric was already damp. "Real answer is I don't want one more layer between me and whatever happens out there."

Yusuf's jaw moved once. In another year, in another relationship, he might have argued. He might have gone technical, or angry, or earnest. The damaged version of trust they were carrying now allowed him only a small exhale and a professional nod. "If it goes," he said, "don't try to salvage the race."

"There won't be much to salvage."

"I mean it."

"Yeah."

The engineer's eyes dropped to Kai's legs, not staring, just checking the hardware he knew too well. "You have maybe a second, maybe less, between trigger and full sprint retention if the blackout hits on the straight. Earlier if you feel the lead-in. Don't wait for clarity. There won't be any."

Kai gave him a look. "Very comforting."

"I'm not here for comfort."

"No," Kai said. "That part I definitely know."

For a beat they stood inside the low ugly room with the bike still ticking as the flywheel lost momentum and the monitor above them showing a replay from the pool. Yusuf shifted the tablet into his other hand.

"Lena's through," he said, because of course he had seen it already.

"Bronze."

"Yes."

Kai nodded. He wanted to ask what she had looked like coming off the water, whether she had seemed relieved or wrecked or both, whether the patch had held the whole way without the small legacy corrections Yusuf had warned might still surface under maximum fatigue. None of those questions belonged in this room. He said, "Good."

Yusuf studied him as if deciding whether that word was enough. "She remembers all of it," he said finally. "That's what she told the press."

The words hit Kai in a place he had been keeping deliberately numb all afternoon. He looked up at the soundless monitor again and saw Lena in freeze frame, wet hair slicked back, medal at her throat, mouth half open on some answer the world was already trimming down to size.

"All right," he said.

The marshal called for finalists to report to the call room.

Kai picked up his spikes bag, though there was nothing in it he still needed. The walk from warm-up to call room ran through a service corridor under the stands where chilled air moved hard enough to raise gooseflesh on the backs of his arms. Somewhere overhead the crowd changed register for the next event and the sound came through the structure as a dull moving pressure. His body kept trying to interpret that pressure as cue and countdown and opportunity. He kept refusing the help.

The call room itself was smaller than the pool ready area had been, more crowded with muscle and intent. Sprinters always changed the air in a room. Even standing still, they made stillness look temporary. Dekker was on the floor rolling one calf over a hard ball. Okafor stood with his eyes closed and both hands loose at his sides, running whatever sequence his updated system preferred before a major final. Chen bounced once on the balls of his feet and then became completely motionless. Every one of them looked a fraction ahead of the room they were actually in.

Kai sat on the bench under lane seven and pressed his thumb against the sore ridge of the wrist. He had no rehearsal and no inner film of the race. Nothing stood between the original install and the virus except the crude control he had taught himself in ugly private minutes since Kirchberg: fingers stopping halfway to a glass, a calf cramp strangled before the foot could kick, a hand held open on his own thigh while the graft wanted to close it. Those were tiny embarrassing things, not the sort of training anyone put in a biography. They were still what he had.

An official checked bib numbers, shoes, names. Someone on the far bench laughed at a joke Kai didn't hear. Another athlete spat gum into a tissue and folded the tissue with weird ceremonial care. The room held all that economical readiness patched men carried now, preparation looking like the body deciding things early. Kai watched it and kept very still.

The marshal opened the door. They rose together and followed the line through the tunnel.

Doha heat pressed at the sealed glass above the stadium, visible only in the hard white of the light and the fact that everyone coming in from outside looked briefly assaulted by the cold. The track gave under Kai's spikes with that engineered compromise between grip and return he had known for years and never fully trusted. Lane seven waited on the outside curve. Good lane for disappearing if things went badly. Bad lane for winning if they did not.

He knelt at the blocks and set both hands against the track. Rubber granules pressed into his fingertips. A camera floated past on a remote arm low enough for him to see his own face in its polished housing, smaller and flatter than it felt from inside. The crowd made the big generalized noise it made for finalists, louder for Okafor in four, louder still when the stadium screen cut to Kai's name because everybody loved a comeback story and loved catastrophe even more. The screen did not say any of that. It only gave lane, country, season best, sponsor history, the usual clean facts with all the dirt hidden under them.

On your marks.

He put his hands down, tipped his weight forward, and let his hips load. The bridge at the base of his spine hummed low and steady in the familiar preparatory way, running scripts, aligning force, doing the work enhancement had been sold as doing invisibly and well. For the moment it was still on his side. That had to be enough.

Set.

The gun cracked the air and the whole field drove.

The first thirty meters belonged to acceleration, no room for thought, only angle and force and the blunt old argument between track and body. Kai stayed low and pushed. The blocks were gone, the curve was entering, and the outer leg was taking the load. At forty his chest opened a fraction. At sixty the stagger still hid most of the race from him. He could feel only the quality of his own stride: the slight seam between command and execution that had never entirely disappeared after the first blackout, intent landing a fraction late and being caught by practiced muscle before it became visible.

At eighty he was in it. The stagger began to unwind and other bodies resolved from geometry into people. Okafor was inside him, clean and ferocious. Dekker was moving well in six. Chen held form with that severe compact style that always looked as if he resented the event for existing. Kai ran the curve hard and let his arms do what they had been built to do. The wrist lit up once on the back swing, deep and wrong, reminder rather than injury. That was fine. He stayed with it.

The straight opened.

That was usually the part where the race simplified into violence. He had to lift the torso, keep the jaw loose, keep the shoulders from climbing, and trust the legs because there was no longer time not to. Tonight the straight came with its own private weather. Air-conditioning poured over the lane from somewhere high above and dried the sweat at his temples before it could run. The sound of the crowd flattened into one broad moving sheet. At one twenty his scars were tight in the dry air. At one forty his legs were still doing exactly what he told them to do, which would have felt ordinary to anyone else and miraculous to him.

At one fifty something flickered at the edge of the visual field, not darkness but wrong assembly. A lane line drifted half an inch above where it should have been. The far wall arrived too early and then slid back into place. It was the lead-in. There it was.

Kai did not wait for clarity.

At one sixty the world came apart.

This blackout did not empty anything. It shredded adjacency. The red track split into pieces that no longer belonged to the same place. White lane markings hung at separate depths, one near, one impossibly far, the straight and the infield failing to agree on a common world. His own right arm existed twice, once as weight and once as image, out of sync by a beat that would have meant nothing at a dinner table and everything at sprint speed. Then sound thinned into a high interior tone that swallowed crowd, spikes, breath, history.

The legs kept sprinting.

It was not metaphorical and it was not residual momentum. The bridge took the pattern and ran it. His right quad fired on a command he had not sent. His left calf loaded. Hip extension kept time with a race that no longer required him. Somewhere in the broken pieces of the straight his body was still trying to win.

Months of ugly private practice returned not as memory but as grip. The point had never been to regain some pure natural self. That ship was gone. The point had been smaller and meaner than that: to feel the instant when autonomous movement crossed the line from help into theft, and to get there quickly enough to interfere. In Kirchberg he had done it sitting on the edge of a bed with his eyes closed, catching a healing hand as it reached toward nothing. Later he had done it standing barefoot in the bathroom when the left calf wanted to fire early and he forced the foot flat against tile until the whole leg trembled. They were ridiculous secret exercises, nothing anyone would call training unless they knew why.

He caught the right quad first.

Stop.

The contradictory signals met inside the muscle and the leg seized halfway through the stride. Pain tore up the front of the thigh so suddenly and specifically he almost lost the rest of the body to it. Momentum threw his torso left. The track came at him in broken red panels. One spike scraped, found purchase, skidded, held. The left leg tried to complete the sprint cycle on its own and he killed that one too, not smoothly, not cleanly. A hard wrench through the calf. Teeth clacked together. Something bit bloody on the inside of his cheek.

The body wanted the original instruction back. Sprint. Sprint. Sprint.

Kai gave it something smaller and uglier. Step.

The left foot came down because he told it to, not because the bridge had found the next line in the pattern. Weight transferred badly. Another autonomous contraction jumped in the right hamstring and he met it with a stop so hard the whole back of the leg cramped from glute to knee. Fine. Better a cramp than the wall at full speed with no map of where the wall was. Another step. Then another. The straight kept trying to reassemble around him and failing. The high tone in his head did not let up.

By the time the images started agreeing again, the race had gone on without him. Bodies were through the line, arms were up, deceleration phases were underway, and lane officials were already moving. A camera operator in a press vest jogged the edge of the track to stay with him as he covered the last thirty meters in front of forty thousand baffled people, not walking exactly, not running either, dragging a sprint back into something a human nervous system could inhabit.

He crossed in 23.41. His name fell to the bottom of the board, eight of eight.

The noise from the crowd had changed. The cheer broke apart into scattered clapping, a few drawn-out whistles, and people in the lower rows standing to point at lane seven instead of the board. On the screen above the finish, replay footage had already cut the race into neat angles. From the speakers, the English commentary arrived a beat after the picture. "Possible technical failure for Brennan in lane seven."

Kai bent over with both hands on his knees because standing upright asked too much of the legs just then and because if he tried to explain anything immediately he might vomit on the track. Saliva tasted metallic where he had bitten the inside of his cheek. Both calves were still twitching in ugly little aftershocks, autonomous fragments trying to restart a race that had already ended.

A race official and a medic reached him almost together.

"Mr. Brennan, we need you at medical."

"You able to walk?" the medic asked.

Kai straightened slowly. The bridge had gone eerily quiet at the base of his spine, not healed, not safe, just done spending its violence for the moment. On the screen above him he looked like a man malfunctioning. Under the damp fabric of his shorts, the right quad had balled hard where he'd cut the stride in half, and the left calf kept giving small involuntary jumps.

"It wasn't a failure," he said.

The official either did not hear him or filed the sentence under athlete incoherence. "Sir. This way."

The tunnel under the stands felt colder after the track. Fluorescent light caught on the sweat cooling over Kai's shoulders and made the whole service corridor look clinical before the actual medical room had even begun. Staff kept moving around him with event-day briskness, headsets, clipboards, practiced concern. One of the television monitors mounted near the entrance still had his stumble on loop with a graphic asking whether unpatched athletes were taking unacceptable risk.

Inside medical, paper crackled on the exam table when he sat down. A nurse clipped a pulse sensor to one finger. Another medic wrapped the blood pressure cuff around his arm. Electrodes found his chest with chilly adhesive precision. The whole room smelled of alcohol swabs and synthetic fabric and the thin sour note of athlete sweat that no amount of venue money ever eliminated.

"Did you lose consciousness?" the first medic asked.

"No."

"Visual disturbance?"

"Yeah."

"Auditory?"

"Yeah."

"Motor control?"

Kai looked at him. "Not in the normal direction."

The medic paused over the tablet. "Can you clarify that?"

"My legs kept going."

"After the sensory event."

"During it."

That earned a quick glance between the medic and the official standing by the door. Neither of them looked interested in the sentence as lived experience. They looked interested in how to categorize it. Heat illness, collision, seizure, hardware error, athlete statement pending. The form on the tablet kept demanding the kind of story institutions preferred: one thing happened, then another thing, then liability traveled neatly to the right place.

Yusuf appeared in the doorway before Kai had to say anything else. Credential, tablet, face too composed. The official moved to intercept him, then saw who he was and stepped aside.

"I just need sixty seconds," Yusuf said.

"He's under assessment," the medic replied.

"So assess him. I need sixty seconds."

They let him in because people always let technicians in when the problem might be technical.

Yusuf stopped at the foot of the bed. "How much sensory loss?"

"Enough."

"Specific, please."

Kai laughed once without humor and immediately regretted it because the laugh tightened the cramp in the right hamstring. "Visual fragmentation first. Then most of the rest dropped out."

"Complete blackout?"

"No. Worse, actually. Pieces."

Yusuf nodded as if he had expected exactly that and hated being right. "Override latency?"

"I caught the lead-in."

"So before full pattern retention."

"Barely."

The engineer's gaze flicked to both legs, reading tremor, involuntary twitch, the way Kai was holding himself a fraction too still around the right thigh. "Any tear?"

"Don't think so."

"That's not the same answer."

"Then no." Kai swallowed. "Feels like I strangled both quads with my brain, but no."

The medic had been listening despite pretending not to. "We need a more standard description for the report."

Kai looked at the ceiling tiles, then back at the medic. "There isn't one."

Silence held for a second. Monitor leads ticked faintly against his skin when he breathed.

The official at the door touched his earpiece and said, "Mixed zone still wants him if he's ambulatory."

Of course they did.

The medic checked the monitor one more time, then peeled the cuff loose. "No collapse, no ongoing arrhythmia, pupils reactive. I want him rechecked in an hour. Hydration, no further exertion."

"No chance of that," Kai said.

The joke landed dead, which suited him fine.

By the time he left medical, the worst shaking had concentrated itself into the hands and the right leg. Not enough to stop walking. Enough to notice with every step. The inside of his cheek had swollen where the teeth caught it. He could taste blood every time his tongue found the cut. The track replay was still everywhere. One screen had slowed the moment of his sideways lurch into something dramatic and wrong enough to keep viewers from changing channels.

The mixed zone after a track final was louder and less orderly than the pool's had been, more adrenaline still floating in the air from the event itself. Barriers leaned under the pressure of cameras. Reporters shouted over one another because that was the economy they worked in. Someone called his name before he had even reached the taped mark.

"Kai, was that a technical failure?"

He stopped because there was no version of this that improved through pretending not to hear.

"No," he said.

"Then what happened in lane seven?"

The question came from three directions at once, English and French and something else under them. Kai looked past the microphones to the overhead screen where his own body was still staggering down the straight under a banner about enhancement risk. The image had no interior. It made sense that the people watching it wanted a simpler caption.

"I lost the map for a second," he said.

"The map?"

"Sight, position, where things were. Not all the way out. Just wrong enough." He rubbed his thumb once against the ridge of the bad wrist because pain there stayed ordinary and ordinary helped. "My legs kept sprinting. I made them stop."

Several reporters spoke at once.

"Are you saying your enhancement system overrode you?"

"I'm saying my legs kept sprinting."

"Without conscious input?"

"Yeah."

"And you stopped them consciously."

"Eventually."

That answer produced the wrong kind of attention. Kai could feel it harden around him, the bright narrowing that happened when a messy event suddenly offered a clean strange sentence television could keep. He tried again, less neatly.

"Look, it wasn't a protest and it wasn't me giving up if that's where you're going. I was trying not to hit the finish straight blind at full speed."

"Would Meridian's update have prevented it?"

There it was again, the same question in a different uniform.

Kai let out air through his nose. Behind the barrier one of the camera operators shifted to get a cleaner angle on his face. "Maybe," he said. "That wasn't the choice I made."

"Do you regret that choice after finishing last?"

His first instinct was humor, something dry and disposable. Under actual fear the humor kept missing the train, and tonight fear had too much blood in it to be dressed up. "Ask me later," he said. "My legs are still trying to rerun the race."

A couple of people laughed because they thought he had made a line. He had not. The right calf jumped hard enough at that exact moment to prove it.

"So you would make the same decision again?"

Kai looked back at the replay on the screen. There he was, not winning, not even racing by the end, just forcing a body full of borrowed systems into something recognizably his in front of the whole stadium. He heard Lena's caption in memory from the warm-up hall: THIS WAS SLOWER. IT WAS ALSO MINE.

"Yeah," he said. "I would."

"Even if it costs you the event?"

"It already did."

"Then why?"

He did not want to be absent while something faster wore him to the finish.

The true answer rose complete for once and he distrusted it on sight. Too finished. Too much like a sentence built for strangers. He gave them the rougher one.

"Because if my body wants one thing and I want another, that's still my problem," he said. "I don't know how to explain it better than that."

"Do you consider tonight a success?"

"No." Kai could feel the blood from the inside of his cheek slipping salty and bright toward one molar. "I consider it what happened."

The handler touched his elbow. "Last question."

A woman with a public-broadcast microphone and the patient manner of someone who had covered athletes for years asked, "When you say you made your legs stop, do you mean you trusted yourself more than the system?"

That one deserved an honest answer if he had one.

Kai thought about the bathroom tile in Kirchberg under his bare foot. The healing hand arrested halfway to a glass. Nights on the bed edge with the room dark because he had not wanted to watch his own fingers move early again. He thought about the exact tearing pain through the right thigh when the contradictory commands met. Trust was not the word for any of that.

"No," he said. "I mean I had practice."

That was the line that silenced them, not because it was elegant but because it was not the answer they had been building toward.

The handler moved him on before anyone could recover.

Beyond the final camera position the service corridor widened into a staff thoroughfare with stacks of hurdles against one wall and a row of bottled water on a folding table no one had bothered to make look glamorous. Lena was waiting there in a team jacket with the bronze hidden underneath it badly enough that the shape still showed. Her hair had dried rough from chlorine. The skin around her eyes looked tired in the specific post-race way he knew well now, the face still catching up to what the body had already spent.

She looked first at his legs, then at his face. "Did they understand?"

"No."

"All right," she said.

There was no comfort in it and no surprise either, only agreement with the facts.

Kai put one hand on the back of a plastic chair somebody had abandoned by the wall because the right leg had chosen that moment to twitch again from hip to calf. Lena saw the movement and stepped closer without making a production of it.

"Did you stop it in time?" she asked quietly.

"Yeah."

"Did it hurt?"

He laughed once through his nose. "Yeah."

She nodded as if that, too, was exactly what she had expected. Then she reached for his left hand, careful of the wrist without making the care obvious, and held it for one second, two, in the fluorescent corridor while overhead the stadium erupted for somebody else's start.

The next event's gun cracked through the concrete above them. Staff started moving again around the edges of the hall. On one of the service monitors his own replay ran once more, stripped of pain and sequence and training and choice, a man in lane seven reducing unexpectedly from sprint to stagger. Beside it, in a smaller box, the broadcaster had found Lena's face and pinned her subtitles under it again.

Slower. Mine.

Kai looked at the split screen, then at Lena, then away. The medal under her jacket knocked lightly against his forearm when they started walking. Together they went down the service corridor toward the athletes' exit while the stadium above them kept cheering for what came next.
