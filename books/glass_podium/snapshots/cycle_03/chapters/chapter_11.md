The scar tissue on his cheek had smoothed to a pink shine. Six weeks ago he'd been bleeding on the track with his legs still twitching. Three years. But he noticed it now, here, because the second layer carried information about another person's body that he had not asked for and did not want.

Her mouth was open against his throat, her hands moving over his back in short strokes that were more practical than romantic because the mattress kept shifting under them and both of them were trying to stay on it. Nothing about it was elegant. Nothing about it was optimized. That was exactly why it felt good.

Then it wasn't.

It happened in the middle of an ordinary moment, with nothing in it that warned him. She was beneath him, they'd shifted slowly around his shoulder, and her hands were on his back and her eyes were open and she was present, fully, looking at him with an attention that was specific and warm and hers. And then she wasn't.

There was no sound, no seizure, no visible malfunction. She went still. Her hands stayed on his back but the pressure behind them went flat, like someone had stopped squeezing a held object. Her eyes didn't close. They stayed open, stayed pointed at him, but the person behind them, the sharpness and precision and faintly clinical interest and the warmth under it, went away. What was left looked like her. It had her face, her body, her hands on his back. But the presence was gone. The eyes looking at him were looking without seeing, the way a window looks at a street.

Kai stopped. He was still above her, weight on his left arm, right shoulder aching, and the woman under him was physically unchanged and completely absent.

"Lena?"

Nothing. Her eyes were open. She was breathing. Her body was warm. No one was home.

Two seconds. Three. He didn't know what to do. Pull away, touch her face, call someone. The options lined up in his mind and stayed there, equally available, equally useless. He stayed where he was. His arms held his weight. His right shoulder sent a slow ache up into his neck. The staying was his. The holding was his. The hurting was his. He held onto that because those were decisions he had made.

Then she came back. He saw it happen. The eyes first, the quality of light behind them changing. Then her hands, which pressed into his back with renewed intention. Then her face, which registered where she was and what was happening and arranged itself into an expression that was not quite confusion and not quite fear, more like someone finding herself in a place she didn't remember entering.

"Hey," he said. His voice came out rough. "Hey. You went somewhere."

Blinking, she looked at him, then at her own hands on his back, as though confirming they were hers.

"How long?" she said.

"A few seconds."

After a beat, he rolled off her, carefully, and lay beside her on the narrow bed. They weren't touching except at the shoulder, the mattress too small for distance but too narrow for pretense.

"A few seconds," she repeated, as if fixing the number in place.

"Yeah."

Both of them stared at the ceiling.

A slow breath in, then out. Then another. The rhythm of someone re-establishing inventory, checking that the body answered to the right name. He recognized it because he'd done the same thing in the medical bay after the blackout, the same deliberate breathing, the same careful audit of what was still there.

"I'm here," she said. To herself as much as to him.

"I know."

The hallway light under the door made a thin gold bar on the floor. Outside, wind moved against the window with the flat, constant pressure of altitude.

For a while neither moved. Then Lena sat up and reached for her shirt at the foot of the bed. The fabric had twisted itself into a damp rope. Sleep didn't come. The diagnostic display stayed off. The wind outside was steady and without opinion. Somewhere down the hall a door closed softly. The calibration bay's low hum was there if you listened for it, always there, the base frequency of a building designed around machines that tuned human bodies.

Every few minutes someone passed in the corridor and the bar of light under the door brightened, then thinned again. Lena lay on her back with one knee bent under the blanket. Kai could see the outline of her hand on her stomach each time the light shifted. Nobody stopped at his room.

"Are you cold?" he said.

"A little."

The blanket came higher over both of them when he pulled it up. The fabric was the same thin institutional weave as everything else in the athletes' quarters, designed for function, not comfort.

Kai flexed his left hand under the blanket, opening and closing his fingers. The wrist ache was there. It was always there. It sat in the bone like a tenant who had paid up and wasn't leaving.

"I should tell you what it's like," Lena said. "I know I don't have to. I want to tell you."

Silence held long enough that he thought she might have changed her mind, but she was composing, not retreating. Lena composed her sentences before she delivered them. He'd learned this.

"I don't pass out," she said. "Everyone asks that first. It isn't fainting."

She turned her head to look at him. The dim light from the hallway caught the edge of her jaw.

"The body keeps going. Mine keeps going." A pause. "No. The part that chooses goes away. The stroke keeps its shape. My hands do the correct thing. If someone talks to me, I answer. From outside it looks efficient. Nobody stops anything. Then I'm back and seconds are missing."

Nothing from Kai. His hand had stopped flexing.

"The worst part is coming back," she said, and her voice was steady in a way that cost her. "Not being gone — I don't feel that part. It's finding out my body was doing things without me, and nobody could tell."

The wind gusted against the window. Something metallic ticked in the heating vent.

"No. If I'd come back and kept going tonight, you wouldn't have known. If my body had stayed on the same line, you wouldn't have known."
