# Chapter 13

The calibration rig's contact surface had gone from cold to warm to tacky with Lena's sweat in the first two hours. By the fourth, the dampness against her back had stopped mattering. Overhead, the fluorescent panels hummed at a frequency the system would normally have filtered out, a constant sixty-hertz buzz. Yusuf had disabled her automatic sensory filtering before the procedure began, and now every ambient sound arrived untouched: the equipment drone, the click of his keyboard, the thin whistle of ventilation through the ceiling vents.

"Stage four," Yusuf said. He took off his glasses and cleaned them on the hem of his shirt. He always did that at transitions, as if each new stage deserved a cleared view of the monitors. "This one remaps the motor pattern library. It's the longest stage. Ninety minutes, maybe more."

"What will it feel like?"

"Honestly, I don't know exactly. The simulation modeled pressure behind the eyes and at the interface." He touched the base of his own skull, mirroring her port. "The link's going to run hot again. Same as before, just sustained. I'll watch thermal output the whole time."

"And if it gets too hot?"

"Then I pull the stage and we're left with a half-rewritten motor library." He glanced at the screen. "Let's not do that."

"Okay."

"Ready?"

"Yes."

Heat bloomed at the port, the familiar two-centimeter circle of metal and ceramic at the base of her skull. It followed the neural pathways like liquid finding channels. That was the closest description she had. Signal routes were not supposed to have temperature, but she could feel the warmth moving where signal had always moved. It reached her temples, pressed behind her eyes, and settled there as a dull force that was neither pain nor comfort.

Past the cranial pathways it kept going. Down through the cervical interface, into the motor networks, into the fine wiring that governed hands, shoulders, core, every chain of muscle firing through her freestyle stroke. By filling it, the heat made the architecture legible.

"How's that?" Yusuf asked.

"Six. Seven. Still seven." His one-to-ten scale had become a shorthand during the earlier stages. Seven meant manageable, uncomfortable, not yet close to losing stillness.

"Good. Stay with me."

Above her, ceiling tiles held ninety-four perforations each. She had counted the first row in stage one. The water stain in the far corner looked, if she felt generous, like Lake Zurich. Generosity had nothing to do with it. After four hours her mind wanted the stain to be anything it could recognize. Counting holes and enduring the rewrite divided her attention cleanly enough that the split became almost meditative. One part of her tracked the ceiling. Another felt the firmware touch muscles she used every day and had never felt separately: the small bands between her ribs, the flexors of her toes, the deep cuff muscles that held her shoulders steady through the catch.

In the smaller muscles the heat changed character. It felt intimate, almost invasive, as if each piece of her were being lifted and checked on its own.

Twenty minutes into stage four, her vision split.

The ceiling stayed where it was, but a second layer of numbers rose through it: the distance from her eyes to the tile surface, 271.4 centimeters; the luminosity of each fluorescent panel, a value she could read but not name the unit of; the air in the bay, 21.3 degrees Celsius. Every figure refreshed once a second.

It was the raw sensory buffer, all the stripped calculations underneath the finished room. The rewrite had hauled them up and left them on top of everything else.

"Lena." Yusuf's voice reached her as sound and also as readout: decibel level, frequency spread, a minute difference in distance to the right ear and left. "Your heart rate jumped. What's happening?"

"I can see what the system sees." Speech took effort. The motor pattern for talking was being remapped too, and every syllable came with a trace of numbness. "Numbers. Distances. Everything measured."

His typing stopped, then resumed faster. "That's raw-buffer bleed-through. I expected some. Not full overlay. Can you still see the room? The normal room?"

"Both. I can see both."

"Right." Silence for a beat. "It should resolve when stage four completes. Seventy more minutes. Maybe eighty."

Closing her eyes did not help. The data layer remained, floating against the dark inside of her lids: the rig surface temperature, the distance to the nearest wall, Yusuf's thermal signature as a hand-shaped concentration to her right. Open eyes were better. At least then the numbers had a room to sit on.

Time stopped behaving like units and turned into endurance. For the first half hour Yusuf kept talking in the professional register he used during calibrations. Then fatigue started to roughen him.

"Your motor library is massive. I've calibrated dozens of athletes and I've never seen this density of stored patterns. Since you were four, Lena. It's kept everything." He checked another stream of data. "Training sets. Competitions. Drills from the developmental program. I have to rewrite around all of it."

"Is that a problem?"

"It's a lot of data. This is going to run longer than ninety minutes." His accent thickened as he got tired, vowels rounder, t's softer. "Maybe another hour. I'll know more once we're through the competition patterns."

"I can wait."

"Yeah. I know you can." He looked away, then back to the monitor. "I've got tea somewhere if you need it. It'll be cold."

"I'm fine."

The dual view kept feeding her details she had never known the system stored: the rig's vibration frequency moving through her spine, Yusuf's breathing rate a little above baseline, the wavelength spread of the fluorescent light. As numbers, none of it mattered. As knowledge of how long the system had been studying her, it did.

A memory surfaced from the old Meridian facility in Zug: pediatric rigs sized for smaller bodies, a mural of fish on the ceiling, Latin species names she had learned to sound out before she could read German properly. She had spent childhood under painted fish while the link was tuned and expanded and made invisible. Now the heat was giving it edges.

In the fifth hour the temperature spiked.

Her hands clenched the armrests before she could stop them. The port felt like metal pressed to a heating element. Warmth shot into her jaw and teeth.

"Don't move." Yusuf's voice tightened. "I see it. Forty-two point seven. I'm cutting write speed."

Lake Zurich returned in the ceiling stain, the eastern shore this time. School trips from Zug, when the world outside the program had looked like a place other children belonged to.

"Coming down," he said. "Ten seconds."

Under stress, German numbers came first. *Eins, zwei, drei.* At eight the heat started to break. At twelve it was back to the hard, tolerable burn that had held her all night.

"Sorry," Yusuf said. "Competition patterns are written denser. More data per node, more processing heat. The simulation missed it."

One finger at a time, Lena uncurled her grip from the armrests. Her palms had dented the padding.

"We can stop for a bit," he said.

"No."

"Lena, if we pause, we can reset the curve."

"If we pause, the thermal residue drops and you have to build back to write temperature." The answer arrived whole, either from years of sitting in rigs or from whatever the system was exposing on its way out. "That's twenty more minutes of this before you can do any work. Keep going."

He did.

Stage five remapped the sensory integration layer. Yusuf told her to keep talking through it.

Brown drained out of the water stain in the ceiling corner. The seam where wall met ceiling loosened into angle and brightness. Yusuf leaned over her and his face broke into temperatures and distances, glasses two cool shapes in front of the warmer field of his eyes.

"Tell me what you've got."

"Position. Luminance. Temperature." The words came clipped by a sudden rise in her stomach. "No surfaces."

He lifted a hand into her sightline. She knew it was a hand because five narrow heat signatures moved together.

"Track it."

She tried. The movement made sense as changing numbers. Her body still recoiled too late. Near and far had stopped being instincts and become arithmetic. By the time she understood his hand was close, nausea had already turned hard under her ribs. She twisted her head against the rig and the world flattened further.

"Don't move," Yusuf said.

"I wasn't trying to."

He had an emesis basin under her jaw before the dry heave hit. Nothing came up. The retch still hurt. Her abdominal muscles fired in the wrong order, upper first and lower late, each part joining after the part before it had already failed.

Her eyes watered. The tears entered the visual field as saline values at the edges.

"Again," Yusuf said, quieter now. "Localize me."

"Right side. Thirty-eight centimeters."

"Good. Where am I touching?"

Something brushed her left arm. Pressure arrived first without place. Location dragged after it like a boat coming loose from the dock.

"Forearm," she said.

"Where?"

She guessed wrist. It was elbow. He wrote that down without comment.

For twenty minutes the room would not become a room again. She narrated coordinates and values to Yusuf in short precise sentences because nouns were suddenly expensive. When he moved a penlight across her field, the point of brightness did not cross space. It jumped left to right through a grid and her eyes jerked after it. When he touched a cold metal instrument to the side of her neck, temperature and pressure arrived first. Skin came later, if at all.

Yusuf kept her working: look left, look right, count the lights, tell me which monitor is brighter, tell me if the vibration under your spine changed when he raised the write speed. Every answer had to be dragged up whole. The strain of naming things kept panic from taking the space.

At one point he asked her to reach toward the edge of the rig, not far, just enough to lift two fingers from the padding. She stopped before the command became motion.

"I can't tell where the edge starts."

"You don't have to grab it. Just point."

She pointed too high. Air met her fingertips. Shame came stupidly, like she had failed a child's task in front of a teacher. She brought her hand back to the armrest and pressed until the vinyl seam bit her skin.

"It's fine," Yusuf said. "That's what this stage is."

No, Lena thought. This was what her body was when the hidden work stopped.

The numerical room held and held. A value stream refreshed above her like rain falling in columns. Her own pulse displayed in the corner of her vision as a climbing integer that looked calmer than she felt. The rig's vibration moved through her back in measured frequency. Yusuf's breathing registered from the right, then the left, depending on where he leaned. She began separating the body of him from the data of him by memory alone.

Then color came back wrong. The stain in the ceiling corner turned sickly yellow-brown. Yusuf's shirt went too blue. The red standby light on the monitor struck her eyes like a pin.

She turned toward the water station because the clear bottle on it looked safest, and the movement sent another wave through her stomach.

"Steady," Yusuf said. "Don't chase it. Let the room catch up."

Edges sharpened by degrees. Depth returned as an ache behind the eyes, then settled into something she could use. The basin stayed in Yusuf's hand for another minute before he set it down.

"Color's back," Lena said.

"Normal color?"

"No. Too much."

"That should settle."

It settled only as far as tolerable. The fluorescent panels still bit. The grain in the rig's plastic side rail showed every fleck and seam under the overhead light.

Stage six arrived without heat, pressure, or warning. The stage began as absence.

At first she thought nothing was happening. Then saliva gathered under her tongue and stayed there.

Usually a swallow would have happened somewhere below notice. This one waited. She had to lift the back of her tongue on purpose, close her throat on purpose, hold the next breath back on purpose. By the time she managed it, panic had already flashed once through her chest.

"What changed?" Yusuf asked.

"It didn't swallow."

He looked at the thermal and motion traces, then back at her face. "Right. Keep telling me."

The low brace in her lower back released next. Then her shoulders. Then the tiny tension in her neck that had always held her head one degree off the padding. Weight poured down through her body as if someone had drained a hidden scaffolding out from under the skin.

Her left hand slid off the armrest, not far, just enough that she had to notice it.

"Lift it," Yusuf said.

She sent the command the way she always would have. Nothing visible happened at first. The intention was there, but the hand did not move.

She had to rebuild the instruction smaller: shoulder first, then upper arm, then elbow, then wrist, then fingers. By the time the hand rose, the motion looked clumsy even from inside it.

"Again," Yusuf said.

She lowered it. Raised it. The second attempt came quicker and still wrong.

"Other side."

The right hand obeyed with the same delay, the same ugly sequence. The motion had to travel the whole route before the hand rose: shoulder, elbow, wrist, fingers.

Yusuf adjusted something on the board. "Try a partial sit-up. Not all the way."

Lena drew breath and told her body to rise.

Her head came first. Her stomach answered late. Her hips stayed heavy. She folded in the middle, lost the line of herself, and dropped back hard enough to jar the port.

Pain flashed white at the base of her skull.

"Stop." Yusuf was beside her before she tried again, one hand braced at her shoulder without pinning her down. "Smaller. Think ribs first."

"I did."

"No, you thought up. That's different."

She hated that he was right. Up was not a usable instruction anymore.

She tried again, thinking ribs first, then abdomen, then neck last. This time she made it halfway before the effort started shaking in her arms. Yusuf pressed two fingers against her sternum and guided her back down.

He looked older when he was tired. The careful warmth he used with athletes had thinned into concentration and sympathy, both exhausted.

"That's restricted mode on the autonomous layer," he said. "It'll still keep you from falling. Injury prevention stays active. But it won't initiate motor patterns anymore." He paused, watching her absorb it. "It won't be swimming for you."

"Good," Lena said, and meant it in the narrowest way possible.

He gave her another task: flex the left foot, then the right, rotate the head slowly to the left and stop before the port pulled, squeeze his fingers, release. Each movement was available. None came cheap. She kept running into missing anticipations: the ankle that did not brace before the foot moved, the shoulder that waited to be told about the arm, the jaw that hung half a second too loose between sentences.

Outside the bay windows the sky had shifted from black to dark grey. Along the eastern ridge a green-white band had formed where dawn was working its way onto the peaks. The green startled her. The valley below stayed dark.

At 6:43 he disconnected the cables from her port with the small exact movements she trusted: loop, tug-test, withdrawal. He had always been the best calibration engineer she worked with. After nine hours inside the architecture of her body, that fact felt closer to trust than to assessment.

He helped her swing her legs off the rig but did not lift her. The patch was in. Whatever her body did next needed to be hers.

The floor found her feet too abruptly. The tile was cold. The slipper foam felt thin. The left knee drifted inward before she caught it. She put a hand on the rig rail and waited for the room to stop rocking.

"Easy," Yusuf said, already moving toward the water station.

He held out a paper cup. She took it too hard, crushing the rim. Water jumped over her knuckles.

"Sorry."

"No need."

She tried again with the next sip and overcorrected, holding the cup so lightly it trembled. Room-temperature water hit her tongue and stayed small. She had to tell her lips to seal around the rim. A thread ran down her chin anyway. She swallowed on purpose and the taste was already over.

"Sit," Yusuf said.

"If I sit, I'll stay there."

"You'll still do the diagnostics."

"I know."

She stayed standing. He did not argue. He only moved the stool closer in case she changed her mind.

***

He started the diagnostics before the patch had fully gone quiet in her nerves.

"I need the first pass now," he said. "Before you compensate."

The reaction panel stood on an articulated arm beside the main monitor, a half-circle of touch targets and light squares she had used a hundred times. She sat because sitting was faster than proving she could stand through it. Yusuf reset the program and the first green square flashed.

She saw it. She moved for it. Her finger landed on plastic an inch to the right.

The error tone was soft and still humiliating.

A second flash came. She hit the square and felt the correction she had not made in time. The finger had traveled on the wrong line all the way to the panel. Nothing underneath had rescued it mid-reach.

A third flash came, and she was too slow.

By the fourth sequence, she understood the new rule: if the first line of movement was bad, the movement stayed bad unless she consciously repaired it. Conscious repair cost time, and time added up.

Yusuf ran the panel four times. The first score was ugly. The second was better. The third settled. The fourth matched the third closely enough that he stopped there.

"Again," Lena said.

"Not yet."

"Again."

He met her eyes, then restarted the sequence. Fatigue had already thickened her fingers, and the fifth run came back slower than the fourth. She hated him a little for being unsurprised.

"Reaction time had slowed from 173 milliseconds to 192," he said after logging it. "That's probably close to real baseline for today."

"Probably."

"You're nine hours deep on no sleep."

"So are you."

"I'm not racing anyone."

The next station was a visual acuity screen mounted across the bay. He stepped her through lines of letters and shapes, one eye, then both. She still read farther than unenhanced athletes could, but the smallest lines fuzzed into each other too soon. The pharmaceutical labels on the supply shelf beyond the screen were white strips with dark marks now, not text. She knew the name of the top bottle by habit and could not make herself see it.

"Last line?" Yusuf asked.

She guessed wrong twice and stopped guessing.

"I can't read it."

"I know."

He switched the program to contrast tests. The screen filled with grey bars inside grey fields. The subtle differences took too long. The bars stayed buried until she stared hard enough to drag them out. The effort made her eyes ache.

Auditory spatial processing came next. The bay speakers clicked one at a time from hidden points in the ceiling and walls. One came from the left. One from the right. Another came from somewhere behind, with no shape of room around it.

She pointed high when the sound was low. She pointed to the wrong corner twice in a row. A speaker behind her right shoulder might as well have been in the wall itself.

"It's left," she said after one tone, frustrated enough that the words came sharper than intended. "Why isn't that enough?"

"It might be enough outside this room."

"That's not the question."

No, it wasn't. The question was how much of her speed had lived in those fractions.

He wrote down the misses and moved on.

Motor coordination was worse because there was nowhere to hide. He set a line of colored pegs on the counter and asked her to move them from one tray to another as lights changed overhead. The first peg slipped and rang against the tile. The second she gripped hard enough to drive the narrow edge into the side of her index finger. The third made it cleanly because she watched it like an enemy.

"You're staring at your own hand," Yusuf said.

"Yes."

"You don't have to answer every observation like it's criticism."

"I'm not."

She was, a little.

For the proprioception sequence he cleared a square of floor and brought over the foam balance pad. A camera tracked joint angles from the monitor behind him. He asked her to stand with her feet together, then shoulder-width, then one foot forward, first with open eyes and then with closed ones.

With her eyes open she could borrow the room. Without them, the body went blunt.

The first sway came from the ankles and should have corrected there. It rose to the hips instead. By the time she caught it, her right foot had already stepped off the pad.

"Again," she said.

"You're not a machine."

"Again."

He waited until she had reset her feet.

The second attempt lasted longer. Then the left shoulder drifted back, the pelvis answered late, and she lurched sideways hard enough that the foam pad shot out from under her heel. Yusuf caught her by the forearm before she hit the counter.

His grip was careful. Her pulse wasn't.

"This is the one," she said after a moment.

"Which one?"

"The worst change."

He did not soften the answer. "Probably."

He asked her to close her eyes and touch the tip of her nose with her right index finger. She brushed cheek first, corrected, then made it. Left hand missed by less and felt like a victory so small it barely counted.

"I feel like I'm wearing thick socks," she said. "All over my body."

Yusuf wrote it down, then finally set the clipboard aside. "Proprioception had changed most. The system's continuous body-position model is gone. What remains is yours, plus whatever you rebuild."

He had her walk a taped line on the floor, heel to toe, then turn and walk it back. She could do it while looking directly at her feet. The moment she lifted her eyes to the wall ahead, she drifted two centimeters left. On the return pass the right foot clipped the tape and snagged.

By the time he moved to cognitive load checks, fatigue had thickened the whole morning. Arithmetic came back correct but slower. Naming sequences of shapes while tracking a light made her want to stop speaking altogether. The desync flatness was gone; her mind stayed present. Presence was not the same as ease.

Only after all that did he sit on the stool opposite her and read the page back in a shape that sounded like conclusion.

"The desync markers are gone," he said. "Completely. No residual corruption, no anomalous pattern initiation, no autonomous motor firing. Your system isn't going to do things without you anymore." He let that sit. "That's what we were trying to fix. It's fixed."

She looked at the missed-peg tray on the floor, the damp mark where water had spilled over her hand, the balance pad still skewed from her step off it.

"And the cost."

He glanced down at the page. "Visual resolution has dropped into what I'd call normal high. Auditory spatial processing is blunt now. Reaction's down, which we knew it would be. Motor smoothing is gone, sensory pre-filtering is gone, autonomous pattern initiation is gone." He rubbed at one eye with thumb and forefinger. "Some of it may improve as you relearn. The architecture won't."

"How much slower?"

"In the water? I don't know yet."

"Guess."

He looked unhappy before he answered, which made the answer easier to trust.

"Enough to matter. Not enough to make you ordinary."

Cold from the tile reached her through the thin facility slippers and sat there, a square chill under each foot.

Only then did Lena notice the quiet.

The bay kept making its noises: equipment drone, ventilation hiss, Yusuf turning a page, the tiny crackle of static from the monitor casing when he shifted in the chair. She could hold each one apart without trying. Nothing blurred into the next.

"It's quiet," she said.

Yusuf looked up. "The bay?"

"My head." Her fingers touched her temple. "It's quiet in my head. I didn't know it was loud."

Fatigue had wrecked him. His shirt was wrinkled, his face slack, stubble dark along his jaw. He pushed his glasses higher on his nose.

"Yeah," he said. "I modeled that. Ambient processing uses bandwidth. When it goes offline, you feel the difference. I just didn't know how it would land."

She listened again. Ventilation. A cable settling against metal. Yusuf breathing through his nose. The sounds stood in the air like separate objects.

"It feels like a vent shut off in a room you've slept in for years," she said. "You only hear it after it stops."

He made a note and did not try to improve the metaphor.

When he offered to walk her to medical observation, she said no. When he suggested sleep before the pool, she said no to that too.

"You need rest."

"I need to know what happened in the water."

"It still happened here."

"Not enough."

He started to argue, then gave up. "If you get dizzy in the pool wing, you sit down. If your vision blurs, you stop. If anything starts feeling like the old desync, you come straight back."

"I know."

"And Lena."

She looked up.

"This part, the relearning part, don't confuse it with punishment."

She wanted to tell him that bodies did not care what the right word was. Instead she nodded once and took her towel from the shelf by the door.

The corridor to the pool wing gave her concrete floor, overhead fluorescents, chlorine gathering strength as she got closer to the water. Each step ended cleanly. Her heel met cold tile. Texture followed. Then nothing until the next step. Halfway down the corridor she stopped beside the handrail just to stand inside the plainness of that.

Her body did not like standing still anymore. Stillness had to be held from several places at once.

She went on.

The women's locker room was empty except for the hum of the dryers and the smell of detergent baked into tile. On the bench she peeled the last adhesive sensor pads from her ribs and shoulders. The skin underneath stayed pink. There was a perfect round mark around the port where the interface collar had sat all night, the edges darkening toward bruise.

She undressed more slowly than she meant to. Her right hand missed the locker latch the first time. The sports bra stuck at one shoulder because she failed to lower the scapula in the right order. Pulling on the swimsuit should have taken three seconds. Instead she had to watch her own fingers flatten the suit under the straps and tug each side level. The motions were simple. They only refused to disappear into habit.

At the sink she bent to splash water on her face and had to brace a hand on the counter because the angle of her body did not report itself until she was already in it. The mirror showed the same face as yesterday and not the same arrangement inside it. Her eyes looked tired, yes, but more than tired, untuned.

She left before the word could settle.

***

The pool sat empty, her training slot left blank on the board. The overhead lights were on, turning the surface into pale moving sheets. She set her towel on the bench, rolled her shoulders once, and listened.

Water lapped the gutter.

Ventilation hummed.

She heard her own breathing.

That was all.

She crossed to lane three and went in.

Underwater, the entry boom spread through her skull as one blunt rush. The wall, the lane rope, her own kick, the slap of displaced water from the dive all lived inside the same noise until her eyes sorted them back apart. The first stroke hit wrong, too flat at the entry and without enough purchase through the forearm. She corrected on the second and overcorrected on the third, hand angled so sharply that her shoulder complained.

Freestyle at easy pace required work she had never had to name before. Every stroke asked for instructions: reach, catch, pull, roll, breathe. Hand angle, shoulder rotation, the timing of the kick all stayed where she put them, good or bad. Her body did only what she told it to do and did it with the level of skill she actually possessed.

At twelve meters her head had drifted low enough that water rode over the back of her cap and down the line of her neck. She fixed it. Two strokes later the kick lost rhythm because the left ankle had gone slack at the end of the beat. She fixed that too.

Her palm kept waiting for the catch to announce itself cleanly. Nothing answered. Chlorine, warmth, and resistance pressed over her hand in one broad sheet, and she had to choose the angle herself. The choice came a fraction late each time, not disastrously, but late enough to count.

At the first turn she misjudged the wall by half a stroke. Her fingers skimmed tile instead of planting square. She had to shorten, tuck, and flip off a compromised line. Water punched up her nose on the push-off. She broke out angry and swallowed some of it with the next breath.

At the wall after one hundred meters she coughed pool water into her hand and looked at the red arm of the pace clock sweeping upward.

Nothing arrived early. Air came only when her lungs demanded it. Entry angle, catch, roll, kick: each had to be chosen and corrected in real time. The stroke was hers all the way through, messier than before and quieter than she had expected.

She pushed off again.

The second hundred went cleaner. She gave herself fewer tasks at once: hand first, then breath timing, then kick. When she tried to control everything, the stroke broke apart. When she picked one fault and let the others ride for a length, the body started learning the edges of the new rule.

At one wall she reached for air too late and had to take a hurried half-breath, mouth clipping the surface hard enough to sting her front teeth. At another she set the catch right and felt a whole length go by without obvious error. The pleasure of that was strange and almost small, more relief than pride.

Four laps later she held the wall and took a breath she had chosen cleanly this time.

She turned onto her back and floated for a moment in the middle of the lane. The ceiling panels above the pool looked plain and finite, white rectangles breaking in the surface glare. The water held her with the same blunt generosity it would have offered any other body.

Ordinary, she thought, and the word did not feel like insult.

She rolled back over and kept going.

Another eight laps were enough to show the shape of the loss. By the last turn the wall kept arriving a fraction later than her body expected, and the fractions kept stacking. A touch less reach, a touch less force, the recovery dragging on the way through. Over four hundred meters that accumulation became seconds. Seconds put you in another part of the sheet.

At the wall she folded her arms over the lane rope and let the lane line knock lightly against her forearms. The deck windows were still dark. Silence filled her head and kept filling it. She still couldn't tell whether to count it as damage.

The pool door opened.

Dara came in already suited, tightening her braid as she crossed the deck. The red elastic at her wrist had started to fray white at the edges. She set her bottle on the bench behind lane four, laid her towel square, and looked over.

"You're in early."

"I wanted to swim before anyone else got here."

"Did it work?"

"The desync is gone."

Dara nodded once. "And the rest?"

"I'm slower."

"How much?"

"I don't know yet."

Dara looked at the pace clock, then back at Lena. "Come on. One hundred."

"I just did four hundred."

"Do one more."

There was nothing cruel in it. Dara's eyes had already gone to the pace clock; if there was an answer here, she wanted it on the clock face. Lena pushed off when Dara did.

For the first ten meters they held even. Dara's stroke entered clean and disappeared clean. No wasted splash. No visible correction. Then Dara's length started buying distance. At the far wall Dara flipped while Lena still had three strokes left. Lena shortened, turned, and came off the wall knowing before she surfaced that the line out had been crooked.

By the time she found rhythm again, white feet were already leaving the other lane.

Dara touched at the near wall, lifted her goggles, and waited while Lena took the last five strokes in alone.

"Five seconds," Dara said when Lena reached the wall. "Easy pace."

Water ran off Dara's chin. She did not wipe it away.

"Why did you do it?" she asked. "Not the patch. This part."

Lena kept one hand on the wall because the body still wanted the wall there. "The desync was taking time I was alive for. I was in the pool and then I wasn't, and I couldn't tell you where the missing part went."

"But the body kept swimming."

"Yes."

"And it was faster than this."

"Yes."

Dara looked down the lane, then back at Lena. "I don't know how to think about that."

"You don't have to."

"You do."

Lena let water drip from her mouth before she answered. "I do."

Dara pulled at the frayed elastic on her wrist. "If it kept you on your race pace, if it kept you in line, if it kept you from falling apart in the water, I don't know why you'd cut it out."

"Because it wasn't keeping me in line. It was taking over the line."

"That's not different enough for five seconds."

Lena almost said *to you*. She didn't.

"It is to me."

Dara's face did not change much. That was one of the things about her. Most of the time she looked composed in the way a well-calibrated body looks composed, everything arriving at the visible surface already corrected. Only her hands gave her away, the small pull at the elastic, the thumb rubbing the inside of her wrist where the skin was pale.

"Did it hurt?" Dara asked.

"The patch?"

"The silence after."

Lena looked across the water at the lane markers disappearing under the surface and at her own wake already flattening.

"Yes," she said. Then, because Dara was waiting for the rest and because the rest was harder to find, she added, "It still does."

"And you'd still pick it?"

"Yes."

"Even if this is the version of you that goes to Doha."

The question landed in the water between them, heavy and not theatrical. Dara was not trying to wound her. She wanted the answer the same way she wanted the clock answer, plain and usable.

"I think so," Lena said.

"You think."

"I haven't had breakfast."

That made Dara breathe out something that was almost a laugh and not warm enough to be one.

"All right," she said. "Bad timing for philosophy."

"This isn't philosophy."

"No," Dara said. "I know."

She pushed off the wall to make room for a swimmer entering lane four, though there was no one there yet.

"I hope it holds," she said at last. "The fix."

Then she pulled her goggles back down and left on her interval, first stroke in place before Lena had let go of the wall.

Lena stayed where she was until the red hand reached the top of the pace clock again. Dara was already halfway down the pool, movement neat enough to seem pre-decided.

When Lena pushed off, the lane beside her was empty. Midway through the length the wall came a stroke later than she had judged. She took the extra stroke, turned, and kept going.
