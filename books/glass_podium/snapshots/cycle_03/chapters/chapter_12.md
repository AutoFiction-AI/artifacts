# Chapter 12

Yusuf had pushed the conference table against the far wall and arranged chairs in a rough semicircle. It hadn't helped much. Eight people in a room built for six, and nobody looked comfortable.

Kai sat between Lena and Yuki Tanaka, a Japanese sprinter whose left hand still went numb without warning, a gift from the virus. Meridian's patch hadn't fixed it because Yuki hadn't signed. On Lena's other side sat two middle-distance runners Kai knew by event but not by name, a woman with close-cropped red hair who ran the 1500 and a South African in his late twenties who ran the 800. Behind them all, in a chair she'd pulled a full meter back from the group, Margot sat with her legs crossed and her arms folded, as though she'd come to audit rather than participate.

Jin-seo Park had the chair nearest the door. Lena had invited her, which had surprised everyone, including Jin-seo, who sat holding a paper cup of coffee she'd stopped drinking twenty minutes ago.

Through the west-facing windows, the Bernese Alps stood in late-afternoon light the color of old tin. The lower ridges were patchy where snowmelt had started, grey rock showing through the white in irregular blotches. Water ticked off the eaves in an uneven rhythm that Kai had been trying not to listen to since he sat down.

Yusuf looked tired. He'd been working nights in the calibration bay. It showed in the red threading his eyes, the shirt untucked on one side, the way his Birmingham accent sat heavier than usual. He had his laptop open on a stool.

"Right," he said. "So. Short version."

He turned the laptop toward the group. On the screen was a schematic Kai couldn't parse, firmware architecture in color-coded subsystems, the visual language of a field he had never learned to read. He stopped trying after three seconds.

"I can build a patch that targets the virus's propagation mechanism. Just that part. It walls off the conversation your systems have with the calibration network, so the corruption can't ride it from body to body." Yusuf tapped the screen. "It doesn't require full read-write access to your enhancement systems. Doesn't require the data license Meridian's asking for. It's compartmentalized."

"Works how?" Yuki asked.

"Your systems still calibrate. They still do what they do. But the shared pathway gets isolated, so the virus can't keep moving through the network."

"And the blackouts?"

"Should stop. The cascade starts in corrupted firmware. If the corruption can't spread, it shouldn't trigger."

"Should," Margot said from the back of the room.

Yusuf looked at her. "Should. Yeah. That's the honest word."

He pulled up a second screen. Performance metrics. Reaction times. Force output. Sensory acuity scores. Kai recognized the format from his own quarterly assessments.

"Costs," Yusuf said. "Three things you need to know. One, this hasn't been tested on a live human system. I've run it in simulation and on isolated hardware, but that's not the same thing. I can't promise it works. I can't promise there aren't side effects I haven't modeled."

Water ticked off the eaves. Kai could feel everybody doing the arithmetic and hating the sum.

"Two, even if it works, you're looking at a performance reduction. Ten to fifteen percent. The patch shuts down some optimization pathways. Reaction time. Motor efficiency. Sensory processing. You'll be slower. Less precise. Still enhanced, but the ceiling comes down."

"Ten to fifteen on what?" the redheaded runner asked.

"On the part that cleans up after you," Yusuf said. "Starts. Turns. The last little correction before you even know you needed one. Off the gun you'll feel the gap first. Not a dramatic one. Just enough that the race has started and your calf is still arriving. In the pool the wall stops arriving early. If your hand is short, you feel the drag and fix it yourself."

The South African runner grimaced. "So every mistake gets bigger."

"Not every mistake," Yusuf said. "Just the ones your system's been hiding for years."

Yuki lifted her left hand off her knee. Two fingers lagged before they curled. "And the sensory side?"

"You lose the smoothing layer," Yusuf said. "Things land dull or late and you do more of the sorting yourself."

"Ten to fifteen percent isn't making a final," the redheaded runner said. "That's not a medal conversation. That's a first-round exit."

"Yeah," Yusuf said. "Could be."

"And three?" Kai asked.

Yusuf looked down at the laptop long enough that Kai knew the answer was bad.

"The patch permanently severs the neural coaching feed interface."

Margot's chair scraped the tile. A small involuntary sound. Her body went first. By the time her face caught up it had gone flat again, the same look she wore in telemetry reviews. Her left hand had already snapped to her right wrist. Her thumb jammed under the edge of the interface hard enough to whiten the skin before she made herself let go.

After *permanently*, all Kai could hear was water off the eaves.

"The feed uses the same bidirectional data pathway the virus does," Yusuf said. "I can't wall off the virus without closing that connection too. They're the same architecture. So if you take my patch, no more real-time coaching feed. Permanently."

He looked at Margot. Everyone looked at Margot.

She didn't move. Her arms stayed folded across her chest and her face stayed flat. Kai couldn't get past the flatness.

"OK," she said. "Untested. Double-digit hit. No feed. Right before the Games."

"That's what I just said, yeah."

"And Meridian's is tested."

"Meridian's patch comes with a contract that signs over every piece of biological data your body's ever generated. Forever. Transferable. They can sell it."

"I know what the contract says." Margot leaned forward. "I'm saying your fix is worse for the athletes. More risk. More cost. And you're asking them to take your word for it."

"It's their data."

"It's their bodies. And you're proposing to experiment on them. No feed means nobody feels a drift while it's happening except the athlete inside it."

Lena still hadn't spoken. She sat with her hands in her lap, her back very straight. Her hair was pulled back tightly enough to leave the port bare.

"How would the patch interact with existing desync symptoms?" she asked.

Yusuf turned to her. His voice shifted when he spoke to Lena, slower, more careful, the calibration-bay voice that made bad news sound manageable.

"Honestly, I don't know for certain. It should stabilize the desync by interrupting the corrupted feedback loop. But desync is cumulative. Whatever changes your system's already made to your neural patterns, those don't reverse just because the cause gets cut off. If the link has rewritten a motor path, that rewritten path stays. If it's pushed words out of reach, those don't come back just because I close the door behind them."

"So the damage stays."

"The damage to date stays. The progression stops. That's the theory."

"And the missing time?" Lena asked. "Sometimes I come back with my shoulders burning and no memory of the set that put it there."

Yusuf's mouth tightened. "That part doesn't reverse either."

"Or the patch locks in whatever state my system is in right now."

"That's possible too. Yeah."

Lena nodded once. Her fingers laced tighter, then loosened, then laced again. Kai had learned that pattern over the last few weeks. She was checking that her hands were doing what she'd told them to do.

Kai kept both hands flat on his thighs.

"What's Meridian's performance hit?" he asked.

"None," Yusuf said. "Their patch is designed to maintain full optimization."

"Because they keep full access."

"Because they keep full access. Yeah."

The South African runner rubbed the back of his neck. "So the choice is give up our data and compete at full capacity, or keep our data and compete at eighty-five percent with a patch nobody's ever tried on a living person."

"And if we do neither?" Yuki asked. She pressed the pads of her numb fingers into her thigh hard enough to dimple the fabric. "If we wait."

Yusuf looked at her hand. "Then you keep whatever symptoms you've got and you gamble on how fast they spread. Numbness stays numbness until it doesn't. A dropout at breakfast is one thing. A dropout off the blocks is your teeth on the track before you know you've gone."

The redheaded runner looked at Yuki's hand. "Yesterday my calf fired on the stairs and I hit the rail hard enough to bruise my ribs. I'm not even sure that was the virus."

"That's the choice," Yusuf said.

"That's a shit choice."

"Yeah. It is."

The room held.

Yuki looked at her hand again. "This morning I dropped a bowl because I didn't know I'd stopped holding it," she said. "It shattered in the cafeteria. I stood there with cereal on my shoe and still didn't feel the cut until I saw blood." She held up the pad of her index finger. A narrow strip of plaster crossed it. "That was before training."

The South African runner stared at the plaster. "My federation clause drops if I'm not top eight at trials. Not medal. Top eight. If I lose a tenth on the first lap, they put someone else in my lane and that's that."

The redheaded runner gave a short laugh with no amusement in it. "My sponsor bonus disappears if I don't make the team final. So yes, I'd like the ethical version. I'd also like rent in August."

Kai looked from one face to the next. It wasn't debate anymore. It was accounting in skin, time, and money.

Margot stood. She didn't leave. She walked to the window and stood with her back to the room, looking at the mountains. The late light caught the feed interface band at her pulse point. Her left hand kept straying to the seam of it, worrying the edge once, then again, then going still when she caught herself.

"You built this in the calibration bay," she said. "On institute time. Using institute resources. Does Petra know?"

Yusuf's expression tightened slightly. "That's not—"

"Does the facility director know?"

"This isn't about facility authorization."

"It's about whether anyone checked your work." Margot turned back toward him. "You built a firmware patch in your spare time. You want to install it in people's nervous systems. The only quality control here is you telling us the simulation looked clean."

That landed and stayed there. Yuki looked down at her numb hand and pressed it into her thigh as though confirming it was still there.

"Why'd you build it?" the South African runner asked.

Yusuf looked at him.

"You're the calibration engineer. You could've recommended Meridian's patch, filed your reports, gone home clean. You didn't have to spend your nights building something nobody asked for. So why?"

Yusuf's posture changed. The shoulders settled. The spine straightened. When he got that composed, Kai had learned to watch him harder. It usually meant he had found the version of the truth he could live with saying aloud.

"Because Meridian's solution is architecturally excessive for the problem it solves. The data access they're requiring isn't necessary."

"That's the technical answer," the runner said. "I'm asking the other one."

Jin-seo's paper cup gave a faint crack under her grip. The sound was small, but in the room it landed like a plate breaking.

Yusuf looked at the screen on his laptop. Then at his hands. Then back at the group.

"My sister has Voss-Kessler syndrome."

Kai had expected money, maybe ego, maybe simple hatred of Meridian. Not a sister.

"It's progressive. Neural deterioration. Rare. No treatment. She's twenty-nine and her speech is already—" He stopped there, jaw working once. "Meridian wants a license to data that could actually matter for people like her. Not in some abstract future. Specific mapping. Motor pathways. Speech pathways. The stuff you generate every day."

He took a breath and kept going because stopping would have been worse. Kai could hear the point where this had stopped being an argument and turned back into one body he couldn't save.

"If Meridian locks that data under their contract, the research path closes. Their license is irrevocable and transferable. They're not sharing it with outside researchers working on a condition that affects four hundred people worldwide. There's no money in it." He looked around the room. "So yes. I built an alternative that doesn't require giving Meridian permanent ownership of your biological information. And yes, part of that is because I don't want them owning data that might help my sister."

Yuki spoke before anyone else. "Did you use our calibration data for it?"

Yusuf didn't dodge. "Yes."

"For this specifically?"

"Yes."

Jin-seo exhaled through her nose. "Without asking them."

"It was already collected," Yusuf said. "I stripped identifiers where I could."

"Where you could," Margot said.

The redheaded runner glanced sideways at the man beside her. He rubbed his chin. Yuki looked back at the laptop as though the diagram on the screen had changed meaning. Kai felt the room tip a few degrees. The patch hadn't changed. The motive had.

The South African runner said, "So your reason to protect us and your reason to put this in us are sitting in the same chair."

Yusuf didn't flinch. "Yeah."

Kai felt that hit harder than denial would've. A lie you could push against. *Yeah* just sat there.

Then everyone looked at Yusuf like the patch came with his sister attached.

---

Jin-seo spoke first.

"I'm sorry about your sister."

She said it plainly. Not corporate condolence. Not performance. She'd set the cold coffee on the floor by her chair. Her hands were in her lap now, fingers laced so tightly the knuckles had gone pale. Kai believed her, which made the room worse.

"Thank you," Yusuf said.

"I understand why you built it. I do. But you're asking these athletes to let you install firmware nobody's used on a live system. You built it alone. You need it to work. Meridian already has a patch that's been validated."

"Meridian's patch comes with..."

"I know what it comes with." She cut him off without raising her voice. "I've heard every version of that argument at this facility and four others. The license is real. The autonomy cost is real. I'm not pretending it isn't. I'm saying your version has costs too, and one of them is unknown risk applied to human bodies."

She looked at Lena, then Kai, then the others.

"My brother died from a cascade failure in 2034. Same kind of architecture. Shared network. Propagation. All of it." The muscles around her mouth tightened. "He was twenty-six. He was running the 1500. At eight hundred his legs just stopped. The rest of him didn't. His heart rate hit 240 before the emergency shutdown engaged."

She swallowed once.

"It was late."

Outside, the mountain shadows had lengthened across the snowmelt. Kai could see why nobody rushed to tell her she was wrong.

"So no," she said. "I can't tell people this risk is acceptable because Meridian's contract is ugly. Ugly isn't the same thing as untested."

"Meridian's patch has its own risks," Yusuf said. His accent had thickened again. The professional layer was starting to split.

"Meridian's patch has been through full clinical validation."

"Clinical validation funded by the company that—"

He stopped. Kai watched him do it: the jaw lock, the quick swallow, the decision to leave the worse thing unsaid.

Jin-seo caught the shape of it anyway. "The company that what?"

"Their patch has been validated. You're right."

"Yusuf."

"It's not relevant to this conversation."

The gap stayed in the room after he shut his mouth. Something he knew. Something he wasn't saying. Kai watched Margot watch him with the focus of someone filing information for later.

"I think it is relevant," Jin-seo said, "if you're asking people to refuse a validated patch in favor of yours."

"I'm not asking anyone to do anything. I'm presenting an option."

"You're not neutral and you've just explained why."

"No one's neutral in this room." Yusuf's voice dropped. "You're not neutral either. You work for Meridian."

"I'm here because Lena invited me."

"You're here because getting holdouts to sign is literally your job."

"My job is making sure nobody else dies the way my brother died."

Her voice cracked on *brother*. Just once. She didn't smooth it over.

Margot came back from the window. She didn't sit. She stood behind her empty chair with both hands on the backrest, except her right hand kept slipping off and hooking under the interface band before she put it back.

"Can we talk about what actually happens to these athletes?" she said. "Not the data philosophy. Not the principle. The bodies."

Kai looked at her. She hadn't fought this hard over contracts or consent. Only over the feed.

"What does ten to fifteen feel like?" Margot asked.

Yusuf was quiet for a second. "Starts drag. Fine correction goes. If your foot lands wrong, the mistake runs higher before it gets caught. Ankle, knee, hip. In the pool you're late to the wall and you wear the drag with your whole shoulder."

Kai's own shoulder gave a dull pulse at that, memory arriving before thought: the wall, the wrong angle, the part of the body that kept going after the rest of him had vanished.

"And the feed?"

"Gone."

"No coach inside the body if something starts to slide."

"Yes."

"If they take Yusuf's patch and it fails during competition, what happens?"

Yusuf was quiet for a beat too long. Kai knew that pause too. It was the space where the answer got checked for how ugly it would sound.

"Say it," Margot said.

"Whatever sense drops goes," Yusuf said. "Sight, balance, spatial mapping. The body keeps the last command it had. So if you're in drive phase, it keeps driving. If you're turning in the pool, you keep turning blind. Same as competing unpatched."

"Which can kill someone."

"Yes."

"And if it works, they're still carrying all of that into the Games. Sticky start. Late wall. Raw landings. Maybe slow enough to miss a final. Maybe slow enough to lose a contract. Maybe slow enough that the federation decides somebody else gets the lane next year." She looked at Yuki. At the runners. "That's not abstract. That's rent. That's parents. That's little brothers and sisters and whatever else people are sending money home to."

"I know that," Yusuf said.

"Do you?" Margot asked. She had the strap pinched between two fingers now, stretching it and letting it snap back against her skin. "Because you go home the same either way. Your pay doesn't drop because your reaction time does. You're not the one walking into the Games at eighty-five percent and pretending that's still a choice."

"Margot—"

"I'm serious. You have your reason for building this. Fine. But the costs aren't symmetrical. The athletes carry the risk. You don't."

Kai kept both hands flat on his thighs and ran the options until they all came up bad. He could let Yusuf install firmware in a part of his body he couldn't check for himself. He could sign a document that gave Meridian ownership of every measurement his body had ever produced. Or he could do neither and go to the Games with the virus still sitting in his grafts, waiting to pull the floor out while his legs kept driving. None of it was good.

He kept looking at Margot's right wrist. While she talked about contracts and finals and rent, her hand kept creeping back to the interface band, catching under it, worrying the skin there pink. Every time Yusuf said *patch* or *permanently*, the motion sharpened.

Lena spoke. Yuki lifted her head. The two runners looked over. Even Margot turned from Yusuf.

"I want to understand the desync interaction better."

"OK," Yusuf said.

"If your patch works, this is the state that stays." She looked at her hands as she said it. "The language gaps. The missing time. The soreness after work I don't remember doing. This version of me."

"Yes."

"And Meridian's?"

"Meridian's keeps the full integration. The symptoms might improve once the network stabilizes, but the link keeps its optimization access."

"So it keeps choosing first."

"Yes."

Lena pressed her hands heel to heel. Kai could see how hard she was working to keep both of them answering to the same instruction.

"Then with yours I stop here. Maybe wrong, but here. And with theirs maybe I get cleaner performance, maybe even some continuity back, and I keep the thing that's already deciding ahead of me." She shook her head once. "I don't know how that's supposed to feel like a choice."

"If you wait," Yusuf said, because nobody else was going to, "the corruption keeps writing."

Lena lifted her head. "What does that mean in the pool?"

"It means the gaps can get bigger. It means you come back halfway down a length with water in your nose and no memory of pushing off. It means a turn goes missing. I can't tell you the exact shape. I can tell you it doesn't stop by itself."

Kai pictured her surfacing with chlorine in her sinuses and no memory of the wall. The image sat there, mean and ordinary.

The South African runner stood first. "Ms. Park," he said. "Room 214, yeah?"

Jin-seo looked at him.

"If Meridian's offer is still open, reopen my file tonight." He dragged a hand over the back of his neck. "My sisters eat off my contract. I can't show up slower and call it a principle."

Jin-seo nodded once. "All right."

The redheaded runner stayed where she was. "I'm not signing because Meridian tells me they tested themselves," she said. Her voice had gone flat now, tired more than angry. She looked at Yusuf. "And I'm not letting you put that thing in me tonight either. Lena goes first. If she's still herself tomorrow, you show me the numbers again."

Yusuf gave a short nod. "Fair."

Yuki flexed her left hand open and closed. Two fingers stalled halfway. "I stay one more day," she said. "Not because I trust anybody in this room. Because I want to see what happens to her." She looked at Lena, then at Jin-seo. "After that I choose."

Jin-seo stood, picked up the untouched coffee, looked at it, and dropped it in the bin by the door. She paused beside Lena's chair.

"I'm in room 214 if you want to talk."

Lena nodded.

"Come with me," Jin-seo said to the South African runner.

He went with her.

Margot left next. No announcement. No line on the way out. She picked up her jacket from the chair back and walked through the door with her right wrist tucked against her ribs, guarding the feed interface like bruised skin.

The redheaded runner left after that, not with Jin-seo and not with Yusuf either. At the door she looked back at Lena, opened her mouth as though there might be something useful to say, then gave up and kept going. Yuki stayed seated long enough to touch each fingertip to the chair edge in turn. When she got to the ring finger, nothing happened on her face at all. Then she stood and went.

Yusuf closed his laptop. He looked at the half-empty room, at the chairs in their ragged semicircle, and for a second his face just sagged before he pulled it back into place.

"I'll be in the calibration bay tonight," he said. "If anyone wants to go through the numbers again."

He started toward the door.

"Wait," Lena said.

Yusuf stopped.

"If you start tonight, how long before the patch is in?"

He looked at her. "Installation's a few hours. Calibration goes longer. Why?"

Lena looked down at her hands. One had gone still in her lap. She flexed the fingers open, then closed them again, waiting for the lag to catch up.

"Because if I go back to my room and sleep on it, I'll lose more time and call that thinking." She lifted her eyes to him. "If I'm doing it, I want it tonight."

Yusuf didn't speak for a second.

"Are you sure?"

"No," Lena said. "Set it up anyway."

He held her gaze, then nodded once.

"All right. Forty minutes."

He left with the laptop under his arm.

Kai looked at Lena. "You OK?"

She kept staring at her hands.

"No," she said. "Come with me anyway."

She stood. Kai stood with her.

In the corridor, the fluorescent light flattened everybody. Jin-seo stood with the South African runner under the fire-evacuation map, tablet in hand now, contract text bright against his face. He scrolled too fast to be reading. Farther down, Yuki sat on the bench by the vending machine with her left hand on her knee and her right forefinger moving from fingertip to fingertip, checking what answered. The redheaded runner had stopped at the window at the end of the hall, arms folded, looking out at the darkening ridge as though the mountain might choose for her.

When Lena stepped out, all three looked up.

The South African runner looked back down first.

"Tomorrow," the redheaded runner said. "Tell us what it cost."

Lena nodded once.

Yuki said, "Tell us if you can feel the wall."

In the corridor, Yusuf's steps were already receding toward the calibration bay. Lena followed the sound of his steps.
