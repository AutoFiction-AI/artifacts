# Chapter 8

Kai saw the Meridian delegation before he finished breakfast: three black SUVs grinding up the mountain road through overnight snow. He watched them from the cafeteria windows one-handed, fork in his right, coffee cooling untouched beside him. The vehicles pulled into the loading bay with coordinated timing, one after another, as if they'd practiced the spacing. Three people climbed out of the first: two men in dark coats and a woman in a grey wool suit who crossed the icy concrete without slipping, a leather portfolio held flat against her chest.

"That's Jin-seo Park," Tomas said across the table, leaning forward to follow Kai's gaze. "Athlete liaison. She was at the Seoul orientation last year." He paused with his spoon over a bowl of porridge. "She's all right. She actually listens."

Kai kept watching the woman in grey. Behind her, the rear doors opened on the second SUV and more people stepped out with hard cases, laptop bags, a flat archive box the size of a legal file. Nobody came up the mountain with that much paper to make life easier.

Outside, the sky was the flat grey of late February at 2,100 meters, snow stacked along the ridgelines in wind-carved drifts. The second and third SUVs emptied out behind the first: technicians, lawyers, or whatever Meridian sent when it wanted to remind people who ran the network inside their nerves. The noise in the cafeteria thinned around the windows. Someone two tables over said, very quietly, "Fuck."

Tomas watched with a different expression now, attentive and measuring, the look of someone keeping track of people who could turn a season on or off. "They don't usually send this many," he said.

"No."

"You think it's about the virus?"

Kai finished his coffee. "Yeah, Tomas. I think it's about the virus."

---

An hour later, thirty-four athletes and most of the Kirchberg senior staff filled the second-floor conference room. The space had been rearranged overnight. Training boards were stacked against the wall. The usual tables were gone, replaced by rows of chairs facing a long table at the front. A projection screen glowed with Meridian's logo. Printed binders waited at every seat, each one tabbed in red and marked with an athlete name in block print. So did bottled water stamped with the Meridian mark, which had not appeared anywhere in the facility before that morning. Someone had carried those bottles up the mountain on purpose.

The fluorescent lights buzzed at a frequency Kai had stopped hearing weeks ago. He sat near the right wall, three rows back, his chair angled so he could see the door and the front table at once. Lena was two rows ahead, hair still damp from the morning swim, the faint chlorine smell that never fully left her. Margot stood against the far wall with her arms crossed, watching the room with flat coaching attention. Yusuf sat in the front row beside Dr. Engel. His hands were folded. Petra's back was straight, mouth thin, the posture of someone who had been briefed and hated what she heard.

Jin-seo Park stood behind the table. She was smaller than the job made you expect: compact, dark hair cut at the jaw, no jewelry except a slim watch she touched once before she started. She waited for the room to settle without asking for silence. When it did, she opened with Kai.

"I know some of you," she said. "Several from Seoul. Others from your files, which isn't the same thing." She let that sit for a beat. "Eight days ago, one of you hit a wall at sprint speed because his body kept running after his senses cut out. I've read the medical report. I've seen the telemetry. What I'm about to show you predates Mr. Brennan's crash by eight months. Meridian has been tracking this vulnerability for eight months. The disclosure moved because of what happened to Mr. Brennan."

She glanced at Kai. The room followed her glance. He felt attention turn toward him, quick and hot, then settle. A Hungarian weightlifter two seats ahead turned all the way around to look at him, then faced front again.

"Eight months," the weightlifter said. "You sat on it for eight months."

"The initial detection was eight months ago," Jin-seo said. "Characterizing the virus took the rest of that time. The timeline was bad. Here's what we have now."

She tapped the screen. A schematic filled the wall: the calibration network that linked every enhanced athlete in the building, clean blue lines and white nodes, dressed up to look reassuring. Kai had seen rougher versions on Yusuf's whiteboards, coffee-ringed and half erased. This one looked built for an investor call.

"The virus propagates through the shared calibration network," Jin-seo said. "Routine calibrations, firmware syncs, diagnostics. Every time the network opens a transmission window, the corruption has a path. It targets the firmware layer that governs sensory processing. When it triggers, you lose sight, hearing, proprioception, spatial orientation, all of it. It's a total blackout. The autonomous subsystems in your enhancements don't stop because you stop perceiving them. Your legs keep driving. Your arms keep stroking. Whatever output the system was running, it keeps running."

No one spoke. The silence had pressure in it. Thirty-four people kept questions under their tongues. Someone shifted in a chair and the creak sounded too loud.

"We've developed a firmware patch that cuts the replication routine out of the sensory-routing layer and rewrites the tables it keeps corrupting. Installation takes about four hours per athlete in the calibration bay. The access route is the same one used for a full recalibration. Dr. Qadir's team would handle the procedure." She nodded at Yusuf. He did not nod back. "Meridian has run it against captured infected handshake traffic from three facilities and on retired enhancement stacks under load simulation. In those tests, the blackout trigger did not recur."

Someone near the back said, "That's not a body."

"No," Jin-seo said. "It isn't. What we know in live systems comes from the access route, not long follow-up on this payload. Expect headache, nausea, and bad sensory lag for a day if your handshake settles slowly. No heavy training until the bay clears you."

"What's the price?" Margot asked from the wall.

Jin-seo turned to her. "The patch requires full read-write access to the firmware layer of your enhancement system, and Meridian would retain that access permanently in order to maintain compatibility with future updates."

"Access to what, exactly?"

Jin-seo picked up one of the binders and turned to a marked page. "I'll read it directly. Better that than a cleaned-up version."

She looked down at the text.

"'In consideration of the Installation and ongoing maintenance of the Meridian Apex Enhancement Stability Protocol, the Athlete hereby grants to Meridian BioSystems, its successors and assigns, an irrevocable, transferable license to all biometric, genetic, neural, and physiological data generated by or through the Enhancement System, including but not limited to real-time performance metrics, neurological activity patterns, genetic expression data, and baseline physiological markers. This license shall survive termination of any sponsorship agreement, retirement from competitive athletics, or deactivation of the Enhancement System.'"

She set the binder down and kept her eyes on the room.

The binders opened across the room, first a few, then almost all at once. Paper rasped. Tabs clicked against metal rings. Meridian had color-coded the surrender: blue for installation terms, red for data rights, grey for dispute resolution, a yellow insert for calibration scheduling. Kai flipped three pages with his thumb and found the same sentence coming back in different clothes, each version of it written to outlast sponsorship, retirement, even deactivation. Somebody in the back laughed once under his breath and stopped when no one joined him.

The first voice came from the second row. A Swedish cyclist, narrow face gone red at the ears. "Transferable means what? You can sell it?"

"It means Meridian can sublicense data to research partners, medical institutions, and other authorized entities under the standard data-sharing framework."

"So you can sell it."

"The framework includes restrictions on who can access it," Jin-seo said, and another athlete cut across her.

"Can you or can't you?" another athlete said. Behind him someone was still turning between tabs — "Which section is that even in?"

"Answer him," somebody else snapped.

Jin-seo took one breath. "Yes. Meridian can sell or sublicense the data."

The room moved without getting louder. Heads turned. A chair leg scraped. Kai watched the back of Lena's head. She had not moved. Her hands lay flat on her thighs, still in that full-body stillness she went to when she was forcing herself to keep processing.

Aissatou Diop spoke from near the front. She did not raise a hand. "If I don't sign and I go to Doha, what am I doing? Getting into the blocks and hoping I stay able to see lane three?"

"The Apex Games will let you compete without the patch," Jin-seo said.

Aissatou gave a short, ugly laugh. "That's not what I asked."

Jin-seo nodded once. "If you compete unpatched, the blackout risk remains. In sprint conditions, on apparatus, in the pool, on the bike, the outcome could be fatal."

Dr. Engel leaned forward. "The medical assessment supports that," she said. Her voice stayed careful, each word weighed. "The blackout risk under maximal exertion is real. I have reviewed the clinical data. The install itself uses a route your systems already tolerate for recalibration, so the immediate procedural risk is lower than the room seems to think. What I do not have is follow-up data under competition load. I can't speak to the contract. I can speak to the danger."

That was enough for the first wave. A German javelin thrower with a pale integration scar at his collarbone opened his binder, skipped straight to the signature tab, and signed as fast as if he were approving a hotel bill. He shut the binder and shoved it to the edge of his seat, then wiped his palm once on his thigh.

Across the aisle, the Swedish cyclist who had kept pushing on transferable held his pen above the page for a long second. He looked toward the windows, not really seeing them, jaw working. Then he wrote his name hard enough that Kai heard the nib squeal.

The swimmer in the front row had already found the schedule insert clipped behind the contract. She checked the first available calibration slot, checked it again, and signed with the quick blank face of somebody choosing between bad timing and worse timing.

Aissatou uncapped her pen and set the point to the line. The tendons in the back of her hand stood out. After a few seconds she capped the pen again and slid it under the binder's spine.

At the back, three athletes from smaller federations bent over the page together, not conferring, just moving at the same moment because the room had finally shown them what refusing cost. One signed. One didn't. One kept turning pages like more pages might help. By the time the room spoke again, twelve binders sat at the aisle or in signed hands.

---

The remaining athletes sat with binders on their laps or open in their hands. The room had split cleanly enough that Kai could see it from his chair. One side had signed and was trying not to look at the paper anymore. The other still kept touching pages, turning back, checking clauses, as if there might be a version in there that was less final.

Adewale, a middle-distance runner from the Nigerian team, lifted his binder off his knees. "This part — survives retirement. So when I'm done, when I'm forty-five coaching kids somewhere, they still have all of this?"

"The license survives retirement," Jin-seo said. "Yes."

He let the binder fall back to his lap. "Christ."

Forty-five hit Kai harder than irrevocable had. Forty-five was a real age, not contract language. It was some other life starting, and Meridian was still reaching into it.

A Brazilian gymnast two rows over cut in before Jin-seo could continue, chalked finger pressed to the page. "Baseline physiological markers — that's not even — that's my blood."

"The license covers all data generated by or through the Enhancement System," Jin-seo said. "Because the system interfaces with baseline biology, the streams stay tangled in practice."

The gymnast said blood and Kai felt his wrist answer inside the brace, a slow ugly throb. Meridian already had the crash footage, the scans, the telemetry. Blood made it sound as if even the part of him that predated the system had stopped being his.

"Those are separate requirements," Yusuf said.

It snapped every head in the room toward him. He stayed seated. His hands were still folded, but now the knuckles were white.

Jin-seo looked at him. "Dr. Qadir."

"Emergency firmware access is one requirement," Yusuf said. His voice was too level, which meant he was working hard at it. "Permanent rights to baseline biological data is a completely separate — those are different technical problems. You don't need the second to do the first. I can see from the install manifest where the patch wants to sit and what layer it's supposed to protect. I can't audit sealed Meridian code once it's living inside your system."

Kai had spent weeks letting Yusuf explain what his own body was doing. Hearing him draw the line here, with Meridian three feet away, made the binder in Kai's lap feel heavier. If Yusuf was willing to say this part out loud, the grab was worse than the room had let itself believe.

One of the Meridian men at the wall shifted his weight. Jin-seo lifted a hand without looking at him and he stopped.

"On a clean system design, sure," she said. "On what's actually deployed — you've got legacy builds, live support obligations across multiple facilities. They don't come apart on this timeline."

Petra spoke before Yusuf could answer. "They are different categories, yes," she said. "The install profile reads like an aggressive recalibration: headache, nausea, sensory lag, maybe a day or two of poor timing while the system settles. We can supervise that here. What we cannot supervise yet is weeks of race-load behavior, because that evidence does not exist. The contract as written asks for more than the technical problem requires, and the blackout risk will still be here while people try to negotiate something narrower."

That landed harder than Yusuf's objection. Yusuf could be dismissed as technical, territorial, difficult. Petra almost never stepped outside medical scope unless she had to. Kai looked down at the red tab under his thumb and saw a trap arranged into sections.

Several people who had already signed pulled their binders back into their laps. The German javelin thrower opened his again with two fingers, staring at his own signature as if he no longer remembered making it. Somebody in the back said, "Then why is it written like this?" Another voice came over it: "Can I take mine back?" A third said, "Don't touch them yet."

No one signed. No one even uncapped a pen.

Jin-seo put her fingertips against the edge of the portfolio. For a second she looked like she was choosing between three versions of herself and not trusting any of them.

"In 2034," she said, "my brother was in the Asia-Pacific qualifiers. He was a triathlete on a Meridian system, the same hardware generation some of you are still on." She looked at the room, not the table. "During the cycling stage his firmware went into cascade failure. One leg fired full extension and the other locked. He was doing forty kilometers an hour on wet pavement."

The room went still enough that Kai could hear the projector fan.

Kai pictured the crash too easily. He saw wet pavement, one leg locked, the other still firing, the body obeying a system after the rider had already lost the bargain. His shoulders tightened before he noticed.

"He went over the bars and hit shoulder first, then head, then the bike came down on him. He broke his neck, three ribs, and his pelvis." Her voice stayed even. The corporate polish was gone. "He stayed alive for nine hours after the crash. The cascade was still firing in his legs when they got him to the trauma unit. They strapped him down because the muscles kept kicking against the bed. No one there could reach the base layer to shut it off. Meridian kept the authorization code on a corporate channel. The nearest technician with access was in Singapore."

She drank from her water bottle and set it back down with care.

"Jun-ho died in Busan at two in the morning while an engineer was still in the air over the South China Sea." She looked at Yusuf when she said the next part, then at the room again. "So — yes. They're different things. Emergency access and data rights, those are different problems and Meridian knows it. They bundle them because that's what Meridian does." She touched the edge of the binder in front of her. "I brought these up the mountain anyway because the patch is real and the blackout is real and I was in that room."

Aissatou's fingers stopped drumming on her binder. The Swedish cyclist looked down at his own signature as if he might still be able to smudge it. No one said anything.

Margot had not changed position at the wall, but her jaw had locked hard enough to show in profile. Yusuf stared at the schematic on the screen as if he could peel the system open by looking at it. Petra lowered her eyes to the table and pressed her thumb once against the seam of her notebook.

Then Kai heard paper slide against paper. A pen cap clicked.

He knew from the drag of paper and the click of the cap that it was Tomas. He looked anyway.

Tomas turned past the signature line once, then back to it. His thumb rested for a second on the small-print cross-reference at the bottom of the page, the part that tied this consent to the rest of Meridian's sponsorship machinery. He signed in careful block letters, printed the date, and shut the binder.

Kai knew that logic. Meridian never left a decision in one document if it could wire it into five. Refusal would not stay inside the calibration bay. It would move into travel approval, housing, the stipend schedule, every checkbox buried in the contracts athletes stopped reading after the first month. Kai had savings, shoe-company money, old deals under other logos. Tomas had a first Games berth and the same blue M on every page that mattered.

Tomas set the binder on the empty seat beside him and folded his hands in his lap. He looked straight ahead, accusing no one and asking nothing. It was just the face of someone who had found the narrowest place the trap would let him stand.

It wasn't reverence after that. It was arithmetic.

Near the back, one of the athletes who had been whispering through the whole meeting, a narrow man in federation warm-ups three seasons old, asked without lifting his head, "If I wait, do I lose my place?"

One of the Meridian men answered from the wall. "Patch scheduling will prioritize completed agreements."

Nobody in the room pretended not to hear that.

Two more binders opened almost together. A woman in the second row signed, then turned straight to the yellow schedule insert as if the real decision had moved there. The athlete beside her kept reading for another five seconds and signed anyway.

Kai watched Lena. She had opened her binder now, but only far enough to remove the schedule insert. She read the available slots, folded the paper once, and tucked it back into the pocket without touching the line for her name.

Lena leaving the signature line untouched steadied him more than it should have. The blank box under his own name had started to feel small, like stubbornness dressed up as principle. Seeing hers stay blank made it look like a choice again.

Adewale kept his binder shut under one palm like he was pinning something down. Aissatou was reading the liability waiver now, lips moving once over fatal. The Brazilian gymnast had the paragraph about baseline markers under one chalked fingertip and still had not turned the page.

By the time Jin-seo checked her watch, only six binders were still closed or unsigned.

"I'll be here through Thursday," she said. Her thumb touched the face of the watch, the same small check from the start. "If you need time, take it. If you want to ask questions privately, do that. No one is being marched to the calibration bay this morning."

She gathered her portfolio. The two men from Meridian rose and began collecting the signed binders with practiced efficiency. One wore thin surgical gloves. The latex flashed pale each time he bent to pick one up.

Before the stack was halfway built, a woman from Meridian legal laid out a second clipboard on the front table. The heading at the top read CALIBRATION BAY APPOINTMENTS in blue block letters. The first slots started at thirteen hundred.

The signers did not need to be told what it was. Chairs scraped. The front-row swimmer stood first. So did the Swedish cyclist, binder already gone from his hands. The gloved technician checked names against the signature stack and handed out small white cards with bay numbers and times, each one stamped Meridian red in one corner.

"How bad's the first night?" the cyclist asked.

"Headache. Motion lag. Sometimes your timing goes strange till the handshake settles," the technician said. "That's why the early slots stay under observation. If the system comes back clean, light work tomorrow."

That moved the line faster, not slower. Better to find out on this mountain that the lane lines doubled or your legs came back half a beat late than in Doha, in front of cameras and a clock that didn't care.

Tomas stayed seated long enough that Kai thought he might refuse the next step after all. Then he got up, gave his binder to the gloved man, and stood in the short line with the others, shoulders squared the way athletes stood for accreditation photos. When he came back past Kai's row he had a white appointment card between two fingers and would not quite meet Kai's eyes.

Kai's own binder lay open on his lap. At some point he had opened it without noticing. The signature line was blank. Behind it waited the addenda Tomas had touched: sponsorship compliance, support obligations, data-use schedules, calibration consent. His name sat there in print, neat and harmless-looking, with a box on the next page for preferred installation time.

When the gloved hand reached for another signed binder, his left wrist answered with a deep throb inside the brace. He closed his own and pressed his good hand flat over the cover until the pulse eased.
