# Chapter 4

Margot got to the coaching suite fifteen minutes early and docked the wrist mount before Tomas arrived. The ready-light blinked green against the inside of her right wrist. She watched it until her mouth went dry.

Professionalism, she told herself, meant running the feed diagnostic, checking the hurdle block, and reviewing Tomas's altitude targets before he came in. All of that was true. She also liked the room empty, the adhesive-tape smell, the rubber, the minute before a body entered it.

Tomas's file was already open on her pane. Week-two acclimation model suggested a ceiling of one forty-eight for hurdles, one fifty if he stayed smooth through six barriers. She had loaded one fifty before he walked in. Coaches nudged templates all the time. No one called that an edit. The real line was the one she had learned by repetition: four beats over and the floor still looked clean, six and it started leaving a shape.

Two older amber tags sat collapsed at the edge of the pane, thin as paper cuts. One was Hana from Tuesday. One was beam group from Thursday. Petra hadn't opened either. Tomas's stair session from Monday sat lower in the stack with a smaller bump on it, only two beats, easy to fold into altitude language if nobody read past the first note.

Tomas came through the door at 9:02 in training kit, hurdle spikes in one hand, water bottle in the other. The spikes were custom-fitted and Meridian-issued. He carried them carefully, still a little amazed by them, like they might stop belonging to him if he gripped too hard. He was smiling. He usually was.

"Morning, Coach."

"Morning. You eat?"

"Yeah, eggs and that quinoa thing from the cafeteria. It's not bad, actually." He made a face. "My mom would say I'm lying, but it's not bad."

"Sit down. Let me check your calibration before we start."

He sat across from her. The auxiliary cable clicked into the port on the back of his training vest. His numbers opened for her in the usual order. His heart rate was sixty-two. Core temperature was thirty-seven point one. Enhancement status was all green. Monday's stair set sat in recent history with the small, almost invisible manual bump she had already half-forgotten making.

"How's the shoulder?" Two days ago he had bruised his left deltoid in shot put, a training weight shifting in his grip at release.

"Good. Little sore when I raise it past here." He lifted his arm to about a hundred thirty degrees. "But it doesn't catch. Just, you know. It aches."

"Don't baby it on the hurdles. Your trail arm needs full extension or your hip rotates and you clear the bar like you're climbing a fence."

He laughed. "Got it."

"I'm serious. I watched your last tape. Your trail leg is fine, but that arm drops three centimeters on the left."

"Three centimeters? That's nothing."

"It's a tenth and then sixth place. You didn't come here for sixth."

"No." The smile changed, still there but tighter underneath. His family in Quito needed him to be good. Sometimes the need showed through the charm. "I didn't."

"Ready?"

"Ready."

She initiated the feed.

The world doubled. Her own body stayed in the rig, forearms cool, feet flat on the floor. Over it came Tomas's body, laid across hers through the link: hamstrings loose from the pre-session stretch, right quadriceps carrying the cool seam where synthetic muscle met natural tissue, breathing deep enough to widen her ribs half a beat late. His heart sat at sixty-two and arrived in her chest as a second beat that did not belong there.

When he stood, the load shift ran through her too. Calves engaged. Core tightened. Weight rolled clean across the balls of his feet. He was one of the easier bodies for her to take because his system still let effort through. Some athletes came to her already filtered, strain shaved down before it reached the coach-side feed. Tomas was newer, less polished. His body still told on itself.

He jogged one easy lap first, then did A-skips and two practice clearances. The pace barely counted, but she felt the left deltoid catch at the top of the arm swing and the correction his system made before he noticed it. By the second practice hurdle the shoulder had opened and his stride had gone cleaner than the model expected. Monday had gone like that too. The file said caution. His body said more.

Out on the track, he set ten hurdles at standard spacing and competition height, because Tomas always trained full spec, while she watched through the glass and felt him through the link at the same time. He bent to adjust the third bar and the stretch through his lower back hit her a beat later. His hamstrings were big and the release came easy.

"Pacing targets loaded," she said into the mic. "First set, seventy percent effort. Clean technique. Don't worry about the clock."

"Got it."

In the blocks, his heart rate climbed to seventy-eight. It was anticipatory and normal. The balls of his feet pressed into the pads. His quads coiled. His enhanced vision locked on the first hurdle. The rubber surface snapped into individual granules, bright and stupidly clear.

He pushed out of the blocks.

The first drive hit behind her sternum. Synthetic legs fired. The first hurdle came fast. Lead leg up, trail arm steady, hips high, that tight clean arc over the bar and the hammering landing after. Air touched the damp skin above his lip. The work came into her chest bright and hot and exact. His heart rate hit one thirty-eight.

She never called it beautiful out loud. The feed at full intensity was the best part of her day. Better than swimming had been even on the days she won. Her mouth went wet with it. If she wanted the bar cleared, his body cleared it. If she wanted air, his lungs dragged it in. When she wanted more, she kept taking until something started to shake.

He cleared the second hurdle, then the third, then the fourth. His form held. His heart rate was one forty-six. The pacing target she had loaded peaked at one fifty, already above model and still defensible.

Her thumb touched the adjustment tab and bumped the ceiling by four beats, to one fifty-four.

An amber strip flashed across her pane, telling her what she already knew: manual target edit recorded. Review if incident. If Tomas stayed on his feet, it would live in her coaching log and nowhere that mattered. If he went down, the floor trainer's card would show the new ceiling, the peak rate, and whatever note she wrote. Petra, if the file ever climbed that far, could open the audit trail and watch exactly when Margot had touched the number.

At the fifth hurdle, his visual field narrowed on the bar ahead. Distance compressed and opened with each stride, squeezing her ribs from the inside. His heart rate hit one fifty-one. Warmth spread out from the center of her chest. This was the part she came for. His breath had rough edges now. Each landing hit harder. She could feel the session turning mean and she wanted it to keep going.

He cleared the sixth hurdle clean. His breathing turned ragged enough that she started matching it without meaning to. His heart rate hit one fifty-four. His body was asking for more oxygen than the model liked. The strain got ugly. She liked that too.

She could have pulled it back.

At the seventh hurdle, timing slipped by less than a blink. She felt it before she saw it, the small theft of fine control when load started eating accuracy. His lead leg went up. His trail leg came late. His shin hit the bar hard enough to knock it sideways. His knee twisted inward as momentum kept driving him forward.

He went down ugly. He hit with the left hand first, then the right knee, then the shoulder, then the side of his face. Teeth slammed together and copper burst across her own tongue through the feed. The scrape ripped hot over her cheekbone and forearm. His heart rate jumped to one sixty-one. For half a beat the signal from his right leg fuzzed into blank noise and then came back raw and panicked: knee hot, palm burning, jaw ringing, where is the floor, why is the floor still here.

Her fingers hit the release on the wrist mount. The doubled world collapsed. She was back in the rig with the armrests slick under her palms, her own heart rate at ninety-one, and Tomas on the track rolling to one hip and grabbing his knee.

"Tomas. You good?"

"Yeah." He sucked air through his teeth and tried to stand, then sat back down again. "Yeah. I think so. Knee's a little fucked. Sorry. I caught it."

"What happened?"

"I don't know. I felt fine and then not fine." He pressed his thumb under the kneecap and winced. "Like my legs got full of wet sand."

"Your pacing fell off on the last three hurdles. Same thing from Monday's stair set. That's altitude. You've been here twelve days. It takes your legs right when you think you've got them. I should've started you lower. My fault."

He frowned, tested the knee again. "Monday was different, though. Monday it came on slow. This one just hit. I was fine and then I wasn't."

"That happens at this stage. First week the drop is gradual. Second week your body thinks it's adjusted and then the load hits a wall and everything goes at once. Same thing, just faster."

He looked at her. Not suspicious, just working through it. "But my legs felt fast. Right before. They felt really good."

"Yeah. That's how it gets you up here. The effort's harder than it reads because the oxygen isn't where it should be. Your system covers the gap until it can't."

He sat with that for a second. "Okay." Then: "So we kill the four hundred."

"We kill it. Training team checks the knee. We move the four hundred to Thursday if it settles."

"Okay." He sat on the track apron, drank from his bottle, and started unlacing his spikes. "Coach? That heavy-leg thing. Do I put that in the medical form or just the knee?"

"Just the knee. You put the leg thing in there and they'll order panels and you'll spend Thursday in the medical wing instead of on the bike."

He nodded right away. "Okay."

The facility trainer came in before Tomas finished with the spikes. He flexed the knee, cleaned grit and blood from the scrape on Tomas's cheek, and glanced at the short red incident card on his tablet: peak HR 161, fall event, target ceiling 154, coach note pending.

"What did you see?" he asked Margot.

"Late-set altitude drop," she said. "He lost the leg on seven. No blackout."

"No blackout," Tomas said. "Just hit and slid."

The trainer kept working through range-of-motion prompts. "Pain here?"

"Yeah."

"Here?"

"Not as bad."

He pressed along the joint line, nodded once, then looked back at the tablet. "One sixty-one. You raised him to one fifty-four?"

"At hurdle four. Monday he showed the same late-onset heaviness on the stairs. Same pattern, solid early, then everything dropped at the end. I bumped the ceiling four beats to see if it repeated or if Monday was a one-off."

"Did it."

"At seven. Yeah."

He looked at her, then back at the tablet. "Note both sessions. If the comparison's attached, it goes to Petra's night audit. If it isn't, live medical gets paged now."

"Sure."

Tomas looked from one to the other. "It's still just altitude, yeah?"

"Yeah," Margot said. "Your leg didn't go dead. You got late and ate a hurdle. That's it."

He nodded, and some of the strain went out of his face, like ordinary was all he needed this to be.

"Minor sprain," the trainer said. "Two days off hurdles. Bike work if it doesn't swell. Medical can clear the next block."

Tomas nodded again. "Okay."

"Go get ice and eat something," Margot said. "You're burning through everything up here."

"Yeah. See you Thursday?"

"Thursday."

After Tomas and the trainer cleared out, the room went thin and still. The ready-light on her docked interface kept blinking.

She opened the coaching report.

The ordinary cause-code menu was gone. In its place the screen had thrown up an all-caps warning: incident with manual target change, peak rate outside the acclimation band, attach comparison justification or auto-send to live medical review.

Of course.

The session graph showed his heart climbing in clean blue steps and the small amber nick at hurdle four where she had touched the ceiling. Monday sat beside it because she had said Monday out loud. She had handed the lie a witness.

Coaches kept a bank of phrases for moments like this, words meant to make the system lose interest: transient acclimation delay, hydration variance, load spillover from prior block. She had used them often enough that they were already in her fingers. That was its own problem. It meant practice.

She could have sent the whole thing to Petra instead. Petra would see the nick, the fall, the Monday bump, the older amber cuts still sitting in her queue, and start asking in that flat careful way of hers that never sounded like anger until it was too late.

Margot opened Monday. The system would not take altitude by itself after a manual ceiling change. It wanted a prior session attached or a physician paged.

The stair-session trace was ordinary until rep five, where Tomas's rate had climbed, stayed there, then gone sloppy at the end. She had already nudged that one two beats above model and tagged it conditioning variance. Now she changed the note. Late acclimation lag observed after sustained load. Athlete tolerated elevated ceiling before sudden heaviness. Monitor for recurrence. The amendment stamped her credential and the new time beside the old lie.

She returned to today's report and copied the shape of it over. Late-set altitude loss. Prior similar presentation Monday. Reduced intensity for two sessions. Bike re-entry Thursday if swelling controlled.

The note did what notes like that were built to do. With Monday attached and the cause tagged as recurrence instead of novel event, the system dropped today's fall out of live medical review and into Petra's night stack. The raw numbers did not move. The amber nick stayed in the graph. Monday stayed beside it with the fresh amendment stamp. The two older tags stayed where they were, waiting for Petra or anyone patient enough to stack them.

Margot closed the pane anyway.

---

At five in the afternoon in late January, the window had already gone half-mirror. The glass held cold and returned her outline in pieces.

Inside, the door locked with a clean click. She kicked off her shoes, shrugged out of the coaching jacket, stepped out of the track pants, and stood in a T-shirt and underwear with the feed mark still dented into her right wrist.

On the floor mat, she ran the old stretch sequence: hamstrings, quads, hip flexors. Her left hamstring bunched high under the sit bone and stayed there. The right one gave her nothing. She bounced once, then again, waiting for the muscle to wake up. It didn't. Her molars hurt from how hard she was clenching.

She got down and did fifty push-ups because swimmers counted in fifties. At thirty her triceps started to shake. At forty-two her breath got loud. At fifty she rolled onto her back and waited for her heart to punch up hard enough to matter. Her watch read one twenty-eight, then one thirty-two. Sweat gathered in the hollow of her throat and slid into her bra. There was nothing under it but her own body asking to stop.

She stood, did air squats until her quads burned, then a wall sit until the muscles fluttered and her knees hurt. It was still not even close. Her legs shook because they were tired, not because they were carrying anybody else's speed.

When she bent over the sink to rinse her face, copper rose in her mouth again. For a second she thought she had bitten through her own tongue. It was only the afterimage of his impact, nerves repeating something that had happened to somebody else.

In the bathroom mirror, she took stock the way she used to before weigh-ins: five eight, a hundred fifty-two, fourteen pounds over competition weight, broad shoulders gone soft at the edges, thighs still thick, stomach flat enough if she stood straight. She pinched the skin above her hip, let go, and watched the nail marks fill back with color.

Nothing was wrong with her body. It obeyed. It warned her before it failed. That was the whole problem.

The tub edge was cold through the cotton of her underwear. Her mouth kept going wet and then dry again. The skin under the wrist mount prickled. She pressed her thumb into the dent left by the dock until the mark went white and pain climbed up her forearm. When the ache faded, she did it again.

Her chest kept waiting for the second heartbeat from the feed. Nothing came. Her own pulse kept tapping along, small and regular. She pressed the wrist mark again, harder this time, until the tendon jumped.

At the desk, the live training board showed two swimmers in the pool, one cyclist in the erg room, the gymnastics hall booked until six. She clicked through the names without thinking. Bodies were still in motion. Heart curves were still building. Anyone still working would do. She got as far as the door, hand on the handle, before voices passed in the hall and made her let go.

It wasn't addiction. It was work. She said it once under her breath and felt the lie catch in her dry mouth. Athletes improved under her. Times dropped. She went back to the board and kept looking anyway.

The shower did nothing. Hot water hammered her shoulders until the skin went pink. Then there was soap, steam, tile. That was all.

Dressed again, she reopened the coaching dashboard.

Tomas's fall sat in the queue now as a full amber bar instead of a collapsed slash. Under it sat Monday stairs. Under that sat Hana Tuesday and beam group Thursday. Four times she had leaned on the numbers in nine days. Four notes in her own clipped language about athlete-specific tolerance, late heaviness, controlled overreach. She had told herself each one was separate because each body was separate. In the column they looked like repetitions.

The queue carried tiny thumbnails of the curves beside the notes. She did not need them. She knew the shapes already. Hana's long rise and neat plateau. Beam group's sharp spikes at the dismounts. Monday's stair sawtooth. Today's clean climb and sudden cliff.

The little audit marks beside each entry stayed grey. Petra hadn't opened them yet.

Tomas's message landed while she was still looking at the queue.

`Knee is bigger now but I can walk. Sorry again about the fall.`

He was apologizing to her.

She typed back: `Don't apologize. Ice twenty on twenty off. Compression sleeve tonight. We'll bike Thursday if the swelling drops.`

Then she opened Thursday's re-entry block. Medical had already shifted hurdles to bike work and physician-locked the ceiling at one forty-eight. It was conservative, boring, safe in the way people meant when they did not care what training felt like.

A warning bar slid over the plan when she touched the number: medical cap in force. Coaches could lower it, not raise it, unless they wanted the override stamped into Petra's morning audit. She changed the bike ceiling to one fifty-two anyway and toggled live sensory feed back on. No page tonight. Just her credential on the override and Petra's queue in the morning. She saved it before she could stop.

Thursday might die in medical before warm-up if the knee swelled. If it didn't, the block was there.

Nothing urgent sat in her email. Outside had disappeared into the glass. In the window she was only an outline, which was better.

She went to dinner because not going would mean staying in the room with herself.

The cafeteria was loud in the flat, tired way it always was after training. Trays knocked against tabletops, cutlery clicked, and chairs scraped concrete. Tomas sat three tables over in fresh sweats with a compression sleeve on his right knee and the scrape on his cheek drying pink. He was eating too fast. Two cyclists had him telling the story already.

"Seventh hurdle," he said, laughing a little because everybody else was laughing. "I was good and then altitude just took my legs away. Coach said it happened Monday too."

"Altitude'll do that here," one of the cyclists said. "First week I nearly ate shit on the stairs."

"Yeah. Same thing," Tomas said. Relief loosened his face. He was taking the explanation the way she'd hoped he would, like this was just one more dumb altitude fall.

Margot took soup and bread and sat far enough away that he did not wave her over. She listened to him repeat the lie twice more, each time shorter, cleaner, easier to say. By the second telling he had stopped mentioning the heaviness arriving all at once. By the third it was just altitude and a bad clip on the bar. The room took it from there with a joke, a nod, and somebody saying everybody had one stupid fall up here.

By the time he limped out with his tray balanced against his hip, the story belonged to the room. It would be what he told medical again if they asked. If he kept hearing it back from the room and from her, maybe that would be the version he stuck with.

Back in her room, the wrist mount waited on the desk. She picked it up, turned it over, thumbed the warm contacts, and pressed it to the pulse point on her right wrist without connecting it. It was dead metal on living skin. She held it there through four breaths, until the new pressure mark matched the old one, then set it down beside the dock. The shape stayed after the metal was gone.
