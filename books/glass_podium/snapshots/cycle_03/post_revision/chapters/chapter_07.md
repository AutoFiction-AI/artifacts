# Chapter 7

The brace was off. Petra had said short stretches were fine as long as he did not get stupid with it, which left Kai the problem of deciding what counted as stupid. The graft-driven repair had run six weeks too fast. The bone in his left wrist no longer felt broken. It felt crowded. A busy, needling pressure worked at the join, new tissue packing itself tight around old fracture lines and synthetic anchors, his body hurrying through a timetable he had not agreed to.

Kai sat on the edge of his bed and put his left hand flat on the mattress.

He had started three nights ago, after his hand went early for a glass of water and he caught the quick flutter in the rebuilt forearm. Since then he had been trying to catch that flutter before the motion finished, before the hand got to pretend it had been his idea all along.

Index finger. He told himself to lift it, then waited.

There it was. A low gather in the synthetic muscle, too small for anyone else to see. He pressed the finger harder into the mattress and held. One beat. Two. Three. The pressure thickened, pushed up his forearm, found the welded ache in the wrist. Sweat pricked under his T-shirt even though the room was cold.

When he let go, the finger lifted at once.

Not much. Barely anything. Enough.

He tried again with the middle finger and got nothing but pain. Third try, the same small early kick. He caught it for half a beat, maybe less, and the success sent an ugly little flare of satisfaction through him. Private override training, one finger at a time, in a room that smelled like radiator heat and laundry powder. Petra would have called it premature loading and told him to stop. Yusuf, if he had known, would have had a more technical name for it. Kai only had the feeling of getting one hand on a door that kept trying to close.

His shoulder clicked as he reached for the brace. The joint had started settling into place with engineered neatness. The cuts on his cheek and brow were already down to thin pink lines. He was healing on graft time, being rebuilt around the damage, and none of it felt like recovery exactly. More like being overtaken.

He fastened the brace, flexed once against the strap, and went to dinner.

---

The cafeteria was half-empty at seven-thirty. Most of the athletes ate at six, their days partitioned into training blocks and recovery windows that left no room for late meals unless a session had run long or someone was avoiding company. The stragglers were support staff finishing shifts, a pair of physiotherapists arguing about ice bath protocols at a corner table, and Kai.

He took a tray through the line. Chicken breast, rice, steamed broccoli that had sat in the warmer long enough to give up, and a cup of coffee that tasted like it had been brewed at noon and punished ever since. The windows along the far wall showed the mountains as dark cutouts against a sky holding its last blue, that deep alpine February color that made dusk seem to start before anyone was ready for it.

Lena was sitting alone near the windows. She had a plate of pasta half-finished and a book open beside her tray, though she was not reading it. Her thumb sat between the same pages as if she had been holding a place for a long time.

Kai set his tray down across from her. "Hey."

She looked up like surfacing. "Hey." She moved the book closer to her plate, making room, but she did not close it.

"What are you reading?"

She glanced at the cover as though she had to check. "A novel. In German. I keep starting it."

"That good, huh."

"Apparently not."

He smiled a little and sat. The joke had not cost much. That felt useful.

They ate without talking for a minute. The room gave them enough noise to hide inside: trays being cleared in the kitchen, the physiotherapists still fighting about inflammation protocols, the heating system working against the alpine cold on the other side of the glass. Kai's shoulder clicked when he raised the fork. He had mostly stopped noticing it except when the room was quiet enough for the sound to travel.

"How's the wrist?" Lena asked.

"Getting there." He lifted the hand a little. "Petra says I can start grip exercises next week. Which is doctor language for stop doing them now."

"You were already doing them."

"I was messing with it. That feels different."

"It doesn't sound different."

"That's fair." He set the hand down. "The bone's basically done. Solid. It still feels unfinished, though. Like somebody hit fast-forward and skipped the part where your body catches up."

Lena nodded. She pushed a piece of penne around her plate without eating it. "My mother broke her arm when I was twelve. Skiing. She wore the cast for eight weeks and complained about it every day. I remember thinking eight weeks sounded impossible."

"It's impossible when you're twelve."

"I know. I didn't know it then." She shook her head once. "I thought adults were weak."

Kai drank the coffee and regretted it immediately. "Where was that? The skiing."

"Engelberg. We went every February when I was young." She paused, then corrected herself. "My parents went. I went sometimes. Usually I was in training camp."

"Every February?"

"Every school holiday, mostly. The program ran blocks all year. Summer was Tenero for the outdoor pool. Winter was altitude work here or in Austria. Spring was a facility outside Munich for strength testing." She said it in sequence, like stops read off a board. Then she winced at herself. "That sounded like a brochure."

"I've heard worse brochures."

She almost smiled. "I was home ten days at Christmas and two weeks in August."

"Home being where?"

"Zug. My parents' apartment. But I'd been living at training sites since I was eight, so." She made a small closing motion with her hand. "The word catches when I say home."

He looked at her across the table. The overhead light flattened everyone a little, trays and faces and steam pressed into the same pale plane. At the base of her skull, where her hair was pulled up, the port caught his eye the way a scar did: part of her body, impossible to stop seeing once noticed.

"Did you like it?" he asked. "The program."

She looked down at her plate. "I liked swimming. The program..." She stopped, shook her head once. "No. That's me making it sound balanced. I liked the water. The rest was already decided. My parents signed the Meridian developmental contract before I was born. Embryo screening, the link at four, calibration every six months. By the time I had opinions, the paperwork was older than I was."

"It was there from the start."

"Maybe. Yes. But when people say that, it sounds organic. It was signatures. Appointments. Adults saying excellent." She picked up her fork and put it down again. "When I was five or six they had me in the rig twice a week. The technician would say, can you feel this, can you feel that. I always said yes because yes made the session shorter. Maybe I felt it. Maybe I was just good at being efficient. I can't separate those now."

"Jesus."

She gave one shoulder the smallest lift. "It's what I have."

Kai split a broccoli stalk with the side of his fork and ate it.

"I had a before," he said after a minute. "A long one. Long enough to be annoying about it, anyway." He nudged rice into a neater shape than he needed to. "So now when the graft jumps the line, I can feel that it jumped a line. There's still an old version under it. Or right behind it. Whatever."

Lena looked at him for a beat. "Yes. Close."

He took another mouthful of rice. "Where's your Lafayette?"

She frowned.

"Lafayette, Indiana. Sorry. My version of home." He leaned back a little. "Fields behind my parents' house. Soybeans forever. Summer storms you could watch coming for an hour before they hit. And the high school track where I first ran, cracked rubber and floor wax and water stains in the ceiling. That's the picture I get."

"I don't think I have that."

"Nothing?"

She took her time. "There was an old pool in Zug. Before they renovated it. Windows on the east wall." Her thumb worried at the edge of the napkin. "At six in the morning the light came through the water and striped the tiles. I used to get there before anyone else if I could." She looked past him at the dark window. "Not for the pool exactly. For the ten minutes before it turned into a training place." Her mouth pulled tight for a second. "That is probably it. Not a town. Just one room, empty."

"One room still counts."

"Maybe. I don't know if it counts the same way." Her voice stayed gentle. "You miss weather. Your school. Probably the road out to it. I miss one room for ten minutes of the day. That's different."

Kai finished his rice. The cafeteria had thinned to almost no one, a janitor starting to mop the far end of the floor in broad patient strokes.

"You said your mother broke her arm skiing," he said. "Does she still ski?"

"Yes. With great confidence and moderate talent."

"That sounds like every skier I've ever met."

"It's not untrue." This time the smile came and stayed half a second. Then it went. "She sent me a package last week."

"What kind of package?"

"A mother package. Chocolate from the shop in Zug, wool socks I didn't ask for, and a letter telling me to rest." Lena opened the book and unfolded a strip of waxed paper she had been using as a marker. Inside were two dark squares of chocolate gone slightly soft at the edges from the heat of the room. "I finally opened it this afternoon because even I could see I was being absurd."

"You're hiding chocolate in literature now?"

"It improves both." She broke one square in half and slid a piece across to him on the waxed paper. "Take it."

"You sure?"

"It's not medicine."

He picked it up with his right hand. Orange peel, dark cocoa, the kind of expensive bitterness Midwestern supermarkets sold in a locked case near Christmas and called European. "This is way better than anything in here."

"That's a very low bar."

"Still." He chewed, swallowed, and looked at the other half still on the paper. "Why leave the package unopened?"

She considered that. "Because if I opened it I had to answer it."

"That sounds bleak for socks."

"It's only partly about the socks." She looked at the book instead of him. "My mother thinks I'm dealing with a virus outbreak and a bad schedule. She thinks I'm tired, busy, irritated. All the normal versions." She turned the waxed paper over with one finger. "Not that I'm losing pieces of training. If I answer, I have to decide how much of the real week goes back to Zug."

Kai turned his coffee cup a quarter turn.

"I do that with calls," he said. "Not the time-loss part. Different disaster. My mom asks how rehab's going and I talk about putty exercises and cafeteria coffee and how annoyed Petra gets when I pick things up with the bad hand. I don't mention the hand reaching early sometimes."

"You haven't told Petra."

"Nope."

Lena lifted her eyes. "That seems unwise."

"It does, yeah." He scraped the last of the chicken into one corner of the plate. "I keep thinking if I can get one clean read on it first, one thing I can name, then it becomes real in a useful way instead of just more trouble."

She waited a beat longer than most people would have. "That's also not wise."

"You're having a real banner night for bedside manner."

"I'm trying honesty."

"Terrible habit. Don't start."

The smile came back, smaller this time. He looked down at his tray and pushed a loose grain of rice through the sauce with his fork.

"So," he said. "We're both sending home the cleaned-up version."

"Yes."

"Badly?"

"Probably." She pushed her plate away a few centimeters. "Although my mother prefers optimism to accuracy. It makes the work easier."

"My mom prefers worst-case scenarios and prayer. That's its own kind of work."

Lena folded the napkin beside her tray into a smaller square without looking down. "Does it help her?"

"I think it keeps her busy."

He had not meant to keep going, but the room was almost empty now, the janitor closer, the mop water squeaking faintly across the tiles. The late hour made continuation feel less like disclosure and more like failing to stop in time.

"She asks me things on the phone," he said. "Old stuff. Does your knee still pop going downstairs. Do you still get that cramp in your calf when it rains." He shook his head. "And I keep saying no, that one's gone. No, that got replaced. No, that doesn't hurt like that anymore."

"What does she say?"

"Nothing. She just moves to the next one." He rubbed the side of the coffee cup with his thumb. "I think she's trying to find one complaint that still belongs to the old me. That sounds worse now that I said it."

Lena watched him a moment. "And are you."

He let the question sit between them a moment.

"Sometimes," he said. He looked down at the cup again. "If the wrist aches or the shoulder clicks, there's this stupid half-second where I'm relieved. I know that one. It belongs to me." He rubbed harder at the paper sleeve on the cup. "That sounds bad too."

"No," she said quietly. "I know."

His thumb went still on the paper sleeve.

The janitor parked the mop bucket by the next table and gave them the look people in institutional buildings gave when they wanted a surface empty without having to say so.

Lena closed her book and stacked it on her tray. "Do you want to walk?"

"Outside? It's freezing."

"I know."

"That wasn't a counterargument."

"No."

He glanced at the window, at the dark pressed up against the glass. "Yeah. All right."

---

The cold hit them like a wall. They had grabbed jackets from the row of hooks by the cafeteria's side door, the heavy insulated ones the facility kept for anyone moving between buildings in winter, and even through the padding the air found every gap. Minus twelve, maybe colder. Lena felt it in her sinuses first, a sharp chemical cleanliness the building's recirculated air never achieved. Then in her teeth. Then in the thin strip of skin between glove and sleeve.

The path from the cafeteria to the residential wing was cleared and salted, but beyond the buildings the snow was deep, banked into drifts the maintenance crew had pushed to the margins with their plows. The sky was black and enormous. Stars came through in a density the facility's light pollution could not fully wash out, though the sodium glow from the training halls yellowed the nearest snow and faded to nothing within a hundred meters.

They walked past the residential wing and onto the access road that led down toward the valley. The road was plowed but icy in stretches, and Lena watched her footing with the automatic caution of someone trained since childhood to protect her body from unnecessary injury. The mountains were shapes at this hour rather than scenery, massive dark presences that blocked the stars where they met the horizon, their ridgelines rough against the sky.

Kai walked with his hands shoved into his jacket pockets, shoulders hunched against the cold. His breath made small clouds that the air tore apart before they travelled a meter.

"I always forget how many stars there are up here," he said.

"You can see the Milky Way in summer. When it's clear."

"I haven't been here in summer."

"It's different. The lower fields go green and there are wildflowers on the lower slopes. The snow pulls back to the high ridges." She looked up toward the dark line above them. "No. That's not it. You can see the rock again."

"I knew what you meant."

"That doesn't make it a good sentence."

"It was fine."

They reached the bend where the view opened: the valley below, dark peaks across the gap, the town of Meiringen visible as a scatter of warm lights on the black floor of it. The air smelled like pine resin, cold stone, and the faint metal tang that snow sometimes had before more weather came in.

Kai stopped first. She did too.

"Can I ask you something?" he said.

"Yes."

"When did you first notice the desync?"

She had answered that before, for Margot, for diagnostics, for herself in the inventory she ran each night in bed. But Kai was not asking for data in the way the others did. He had too much of his own.

"In October. Maybe late October." She tucked her hands deeper into her pockets. "I came off a wall and hit a clean gap where the turn should have been. I had the breath after it. I had the next stroke. The turn itself was gone."

"Did you know in the moment?"

"Not exactly. Not there." She breathed in through her nose; the cold pinched high under her eyes. "The fear came after. When I looked at the telemetry. If it had been messy I think I would have felt it. But the bad part is it wasn't messy."

"But you knew it happened."

"Because the data said it did." She folded her arms tighter. "And the data liked it. The lengths I barely remember are cleaner. Straighter line. Better tempo." She stopped, dissatisfied with the neatness of the sentence. "That sounds almost good. It's not. It means whatever keeps swimming when I'm gone may be better at it than I am."

Kai let out a breath that might have been a laugh in warmer air. "Fuck."

"Yes."

He opened his mouth, closed it again, and stamped one foot once on the ice before looking back down into the valley. Headlights moved along the river road below, tracing the bend and disappearing behind a stand of trees.

"My mother keeps asking whether restricted operations means I have more time to rest," Lena said. "As if the problem is admin."

"You going to tell her?"

"Not now." She pressed her chin deeper into the collar of the jacket. "I can't decide if that's protection or cowardice. Probably both."

Kai rubbed his face with his good hand. "Same. Different version."

"You're still not telling Petra about the hand."

"No."

"Will you?"

He gave a small shrug. "Probably when it does anything embarrassing in public."

"That's not a plan."

"It's the plan currently available."

She should have let that go. Instead she said, "I think I do the same thing with everyone here. There's the symptom I report because it fits in a form. Then there is the other part."

"What other part?"

Lena watched her breath leave and vanish. "The minute after. The checking. Looking at a clock and doing the math backward. Looking at people to see if they are behaving like there was a gap I missed."

Kai did not answer at once. His silence was careful, not blank.

"Yeah," he said finally. "The counting after. Seeing if anybody else noticed."

The wind moved across the road again, lighter this time, just enough to set dry crystals skating over the salt. For a while they watched the lights below.

Then Kai pulled his left hand from his pocket and flexed it. The brace creaked in the cold. Lena watched him open and close his fingers, slow and deliberate, waiting for the early jump.

"You're doing it again," she said.

He glanced over. "I know."

"What are you checking?"

He took a second. "The synthetic muscle from the reconstruction jumps early sometimes. Not much. Just enough that I can feel it if I pay attention." He flexed again, slower this time. "I've been trying to catch that little kick and hold it off."

"Can you?"

"A little. Or maybe I just make it later. Hard to tell."

"Show me."

"There's not much to show."

"Not see. Feel."

He hesitated, then pulled his right glove off with his teeth and tucked it in his pocket. Lena did the same with hers. The air hit her bare fingers like a burn. She stepped closer and set two fingertips lightly against the inside of his forearm above the brace, where the repaired muscle sat under skin and synthetic weave.

"All right," he said quietly. "Wait."

He flexed.

Nothing at first, only the cold skin of his arm and the brace pressing into her knuckles. Then a small hard gather jumped under her fingers, precise and unpleasant, like a cable pulling tight in a wall. Half a beat later his hand closed.

Lena looked up at him.

"That was before," she said.

"Yeah."

He flexed again and she felt it a second time, now that she knew where to look for it. The motion itself was almost an afterthought. The first thing was the warning.

She took her hand back and pressed it against the scarf at the base of her neck, where the port sat underneath.

"I wonder if mine does that," she said. "Not the blank. The first second before it."

"Maybe."

"In the pool I only know after. I miss time, then I look at the telemetry and build the missing part backward." She kept her hand at her neck. "Or Margot asks what I remember and I give her the hole. That's all after."

"It's a different system."

"Yeah. But it still gets there ahead of you."

"Maybe. Or maybe I only tell myself that once I see the data." She looked at him. "This would be different. Standing still. One cue. You'd be outside it."

She kept her hand there, thinking of turns, of tiled walls, of the half-second before the world went missing. The cold had sharpened every surface. She could feel the seam of the scarf under her glove, the hard oval of the port below skin and scar tissue, the pulse in two fingers pressed against it.

"No," she said.

Kai looked at her. "No what?"

"Not tomorrow." She pulled off her right glove and tucked it in her pocket. The air bit at once. "Now. If I wait until tomorrow I'll file it under bad ideas and never do it."

"What are you doing?"

"I want to see if there's a lead-in." She slid her fingers under the scarf and touched the skin just below the port. The cold made the metal rim feel deeper than it was. "Not in the water. Here. Standing still." She swallowed. "You say now whenever you want. I turn when I hear it. You watch. I watch the port. If the link gets there first, maybe one of us catches that first movement."

Kai did not move. "On the ice?"

"I'm standing still."

"That's not the same as safe."

"It's close enough." She swallowed. "Please."

The word came out flatter than she intended. He heard the need under it anyway.

"All right," he said. "But if it feels off, we stop."

She nodded once. She fixed her eyes on the facility lights and tried to loosen the back of her neck.

The first time he said it, she turned cleanly. Nothing but cold skin and the ordinary pull of muscles under the jaw.

The second time she thought she felt a pull, but it was only fear arriving early and calling itself data.

The third time a hard, thin tightening drew below the port before she had fully taken in the word.

"Now."

Her chin started left. She caught it halfway. Not a big movement. A twitch. The muscles along the scar locked under her fingers, a wire pulled tight. She tried to hold still and the correction shifted somewhere else.

The valley lights narrowed to one yellow window in the residential wing. Sound flattened. Her right hand slid higher under the scarf without instruction, thumb jamming against the metal rim, nail scraping skin. For a moment it felt as if her body had stepped half a pace ahead and was waiting for the rest of her to catch up.

"Lena," she heard, but from distance.

Then his hand was around her left forearm, hard enough to hurt through the jacket.

"Lena."

The world came back all at once: the sting in her neck, the road under her boots, the cold in her bare fingers, Kai standing too close because he had had to get there fast. She was pitched slightly toward the icy edge of the road.

She took one breath. Then another.

"How long?"

"Three seconds. Maybe four." His grip eased but did not leave her arm. "You went still. Then your hand went for your neck. Before that your chin twitched left, like the turn started and got checked."

She pulled her hand out from under the scarf. Even in the dark she could feel the wetness on the side of her thumb where the nail had cut skin.

"Did I stop it at all?"

He looked at her face as if checking whether she was fully back. "A little. I think. You caught the turn for a second. After that it moved somewhere else."

"So there is a lead-in."

"There was one tonight," he said. "Standing still, with me giving the cue. I know that much. I don't know if that's what happens in the pool every time or if we just tripped a different bad thing."

That made sick sense. She hated that it made sense.

She looked past him at the snowbank, white with sodium light, and saw the clean print of one boot sliding half an inch toward the drop before his hand had caught her.

"Again," she said.

"No."

The refusal was immediate. Not sharp. Not negotiable.

"I need to know."

"You know enough to do the next one somewhere that is not frozen concrete over a hill."

"It's a road."

"It's a mountain road."

He was right. The irritation that rose in her had nowhere useful to go. She closed her bare hand into a fist and felt the sting at her neck answer it.

"Sorry," he said a moment later, because he had read the silence wrong.

"No." She shook her head once. "Don't say sorry. Just say what happened."

"I did."

"More exactly."

Kai let go of her arm. His hand had left a band of warmth through the jacket that the cold was already eating.

"Your eyes changed," he said. "Not dramatic. They just stopped tracking. Like you were looking through the buildings instead of at them. And you were digging at the port hard enough to bleed."

She touched the side of her neck and winced. The skin there felt raw, a small crescent of heat in all the cold.

"Shit," she said, the word awkward in her mouth.

"Yeah."

For a second neither of them moved. The mountains stayed where they were. The lights below kept burning. Her pulse thudded in the cut under her scarf.

"I hate..." She stopped and pressed her thumb against the cut at her neck. "I hate that you know what I look like when it happens."

He nodded once. "I figured."

She waited through one breath, then another. He only stood there in the cold, steam leaving his mouth. Her thumb eased off the cut at her neck.

Then he said, "What I hate is you were already doing this alone. Maybe not out here yet. But somewhere."

Lena looked at him. He had gone very still again.

The cold had worked through the jacket now and settled in her joints. Her fingertips were going numb again, though the scrape at her neck still burned.

"We should go in," Kai said.

"Yes."

He bent and picked up the glove she had dropped without noticing. He had to use his right hand. The left stayed stiff at his side. He held the glove out to her and she took it. Their fingers touched, nothing more than that, but she felt the contact all the way up her forearm.

They walked back along the access road slower than before. Kai stayed on the outside edge, between her and the dark drop to the valley, like he was not making a decision about it and had only happened to drift there. The facility grew as they approached, its windows bright squares of warm light. Before they reached the side door, Lena could hear the heating system through the wind, a low mechanical hum riding over the snow.

At the side door Kai pulled the handle and warm air poured out around them, thick and almost sweet after the thin cold. Lena stepped inside first. Fluorescent light laid everything flat again: linoleum floor, the smell of cleaning solution and residual cafeteria grease, melted snow darkening the entry mat.

She took off the heavy jacket and hung it on its hook. When she unwound the scarf, Kai saw the thin red mark before she could tuck it away. Not much blood. Enough to show.

"You were right," she said, not looking at him. "About the ice."

He pulled a face. "I hate being right in a doctor's voice."

"That wasn't a doctor's voice."

"What was it?"

"Annoying teammate voice."

"I can live with that."

She checked the blood on her thumb, rubbed it away on a paper napkin from the hook shelf, and regretted that immediately when the paper scraped the cut.

At the point where the residential wing branched, they both stopped. The outer door thudded once behind them as the wind leaned against it.

"What does it look like from outside?" she asked. "When it starts."

Kai shifted his weight. He would rather have answered a question about bone density or split times. That was clear.

"Your face goes flat first," he said. "Not empty. Just less like you. Then your eyes fix on one thing and stop doing the little adjustments. That's when I said your name."

She nodded slowly. The description hurt in a way the cut did not.

"All right."

"Kai."

"Yeah?"

"If it happens again, don't wait to be polite."

"I won't."

"Even if we're in public."

"Okay."

The knot in her chest loosened enough for a full breath.

"Good night," she said.

"Night."

She went left. At her room door her fingers slipped once on the handle, then caught. When she glanced back, he was still there, not half-raising a hand this time, just waiting long enough to see the door open before he turned away.
