2038. The Olympics have split: the traditional games and the Apex Games, where bio-integrative enhancement (neural links, synthetic grafting, engineered muscle) is the standard. Six months before the opening ceremony, four people collide at a training camp in the Swiss Alps. A former natural-category world record holder who was rebuilt by a tech sponsor after a career-ending injury and now wins every heat but can't tell which parts of his performance are him. A swimmer optimized since birth whose neural link is desyncing, blurring the line between her personality and her software. The team's lead engineer, who treats athletes as systems to be tuned and has his own reasons for needing their biological data. A failed medalist turned neural coach who plugs into her athletes' sensory feeds during competition and pushes them into dangerous heart-rate zones because it's the only way she can still feel the rush. Then a virus starts circulating through the athletes' hardware, causing violent sensory blackouts mid-training. The corporate fix requires surrendering permanent ownership of their biological data. The alternative is competing unpatched and risking a fatal system crash on the world stage.

Near-future thriller. Self-contained. Four-character ensemble at a contained training facility. The body-autonomy question is the spine: at what point does optimization make you someone else? The ticking clock is the opening ceremony. The physical horror is real: enhancement going wrong, sensory blackouts, the feeling of your body doing things you didn't tell it to. The relationships (athlete/engineer, athlete/coach, athlete/athlete) are where the tension lives. Black Mirror meets Friday Night Lights.
