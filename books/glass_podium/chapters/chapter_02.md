# Chapter 2

The smell was already in the back of Lena Morozova's throat before her feet touched the deck. At five-thirty the overhead lights were still climbing toward full brightness. The facility brought them up on a schedule: dim at five, half-bright by five-twenty, full fluorescent by six. In that first stretch the water looked darker than it was, lane ropes soft in the gloom, pace clock a red blur at the far end, ceiling lost above in shadow. Pools had looked like this for as long as she could remember. Kirchberg's was newer than the one in Zug where she'd grown up, cleaner, better regulated, but the fundamentals were the same. She had a lane assigned on the rotation schedule. Under the surface, her own breathing got loud enough to fill the world.

In the women's room, the cubicle sensors logged weight, hydration level, and baseline heart rate the moment she stepped onto the scale. The data went straight to the coaching system. After eighteen years inside programs like this, the transfer barely registered.

The pool deck felt cool under her bare feet. At lane four, her lane that week, she set down her water bottle and pull buoy. Dara Lund was already at the wall of lane five, adjusting her goggles with small precise movements. She always made three adjustments: left strap, nose bridge, right strap. Then she pulled the cap over her ears and rolled her right shoulder twice, working loose whatever had been recalibrated over winter break. At Christmas a card from Bergen had arrived for the whole floor, not for Lena specifically, with a photograph of Dara's mother's garden under snow. It was still pinned to the hallway bulletin board. No one had taken it down.

"Morning," Dara said.

"Morning."

They got in.

Warm-up was four hundred freestyle at moderate pace, building through the second half. Off the wall, the familiar compression closed around Lena: water against skin, pressure from the surface above, the lane rope on her left, the tiled floor below, her body settling into the stroke the neural link had been shaping since childhood. Her shoulder rotated, her hand caught and pulled, and the kick came cleanly off the hip. The coaching files called it mechanically optimal. Since fifteen she'd read those files the way other people read medical charts or school reports, clinical pages about someone who happened to be her. Usually the gap between what the files described and what she felt in the water was small enough not to matter.

The main set was eight two-hundreds at race pace, forty seconds rest. On the first repeat the link disappeared into usefulness, handling pacing, stroke timing, muscle recruitment, all the quiet labor below decision. Inside that work she could still mark what was hers: push harder here, hold the streamline longer off the wall, breathe every three instead of two because the altitude at Kirchberg thinned her oxygen uptake and the link kept trying to stretch the breath cycle. She preferred three. Everything else still arrived in the sequence she knew: catch, pull, hip turn, and her mouth turning clear on the third stroke.

The times came in tight. 2:08.3, 2:08.1, 2:07.9, 2:08.0. Slight fade on the fourth, nothing unusual for this point in the block. At the wall she checked the clock, pulled in air, and pushed off for the fifth.

The first hundred meters were fine. She pulled, kicked, breathed, and turned. Lactate sat heavy in her legs, but it was familiar heaviness, something the link factored into pace. Fifty meters remained, then the wall.

At the wall she came in, tucked, rotated.

It was wrong.

Not wrong like a mistake. Wrong like a different answer to the same question. Her body folded tighter than she'd asked it to, came off the wall deeper, lengthened the streamline, drove into a four-beat dolphin instead of the three-beat pattern she'd been using since January. The push-off angle was a fraction steeper, and the water was colder that far down. It was the November firmware variant they'd tried for three weeks and then rolled back because the deeper push-off cost too much oxygen late in the eight-hundred. She'd trained that version and watched the coaches remove it, or thought she had. By the time she knew what the turn was, the turn had already chosen itself.

She broke the surface too hard and got pool water up her nose. For two strokes she went nowhere, sculling in place. Immediately she flexed the left ankle, then the right, then kicked once behind her as hard as she could. The calf took the load cleanly. There was no catch and no knot in it, nothing except the remembered pressure of the tighter fold and the colder water she should not have been touching.

Mid-lane, with the set unfinished and the wall clock still running, she floated while water kept moving around her: Dara's wake in the next lane, recirculation pushing at her calves, lane rope ticking softly against the chop. Above the surface the pool turned back into a room. Her breath echoed, the gutter slapped, and Dara's stroke came muffled from two lanes over. Chlorine came bitter at the back of her tongue.

From the far wall Dara surfaced, pushed her goggles to her forehead, and looked down the lane. When Lena didn't start again, she swam back.

"You okay?"

"Yes," Lena said. "Cramp."

"Calf or foot?"

"Calf. Left side. It's already loosening."

Dara nodded and hung on the lane rope with one arm. "I had one two weeks ago during threshold work. Left calf. Yusuf said it was a hydration flag. The system sent me a fluid reminder every forty minutes for the rest of the day."

Nothing in her face suggested that this was strange.

"Do you want me to get someone?"

"No, it's fine. Thank you."

"I'm going to do the last three."

Dara pulled her goggles down, made one last adjustment to the left strap, and pushed off.

Her stroke was long and clean, the kind of efficient that looked effortless because the effort had been engineered out of sight. Bergen used a different calibration philosophy from Meridian, but the result in the water was close enough.

Lena stayed at the rope a moment longer, heel drawn up behind her under the surface, thumb pressed into the left calf until it hurt. The muscle felt warm and ordinary. If there had been a cramp, it had chosen impossible manners.

Water bottle in one hand, pull buoy under the other arm, she went to the cool-down lane at the far end of the deck. The observation window stayed dark. Margot did not come poolside before eight unless there was a live feed session, and Lena still had nearly two hours before the standard review slot. On the first fifty she open-turned at both walls, palms flat to tile, refusing the flip entirely. On the second she flipped at half speed and counted each part. She tucked. She planted. She pushed. She took three kicks and surfaced. On the third she let the wall come a little faster. The body held to the count because she was watching it, or felt like she was. By the time she settled into actual easy swimming, her shoulders were tight from paying attention in places where attention usually wasn't needed.

The pace clock read 6:11 when she climbed out for good.

In the women's room the post-session traffic moved quietly: lockers opening, shower valves squeaking, wet sandals slapping tile. Lena sat on the bench in a towel and pressed both thumbs down the length of her left calf from knee to Achilles. It was sore from work, no more than that. No spasm sat waiting under the skin. She could not even identify the exact place she was supposed to have lied about.

Under the shower she reached for the shampoo dispenser, found her hair already slick with soap, and stood still for a second with her hand on the button. She could not remember pressing it the first time. That was ordinary enough to dismiss. It was one distracted second, steam in her face, too little sleep. She rinsed, counted to ten while the water went over the back of her neck, and did not count it as anything.

Breakfast service had just opened. By the time she carried her tray into the cafeteria the first tables were filling with track athletes coming off lift, coaches with coffee, two gymnasts in matching thermal tops still pink from the cold corridor. She took oatmeal, two boiled eggs, yogurt, tea. The room smelled like toasted bread and wet synthetic fabric and the industrial coffee the staff called coffee because there was no reason not to.

She sat alone near the windows. Outside, the snow beyond the floodlights looked blue at the edges. Inside, the room's glass reflected her back at her more strongly than the mountain did.

Dara came through the line ten minutes later with a tray that looked measured to the gram: eggs, rye bread, cut orange, one bottle of electrolyte mix already open. She stopped beside Lena's table without sitting.

"Calf better?"

"Yes. It was brief."

"Good." Dara peeled the label from her bottle in one clean strip. "Mine last month wasn't even painful. The system kept flagging sodium and I ignored it. Then Yusuf changed the ratio and the alerts stopped."

"You didn't feel it before the alerts?"

Dara considered that as if it were a technical question, not a personal one. "Not exactly. I felt normal. The numbers were wrong."

She said it matter-of-factly, not proudly and not with resentment either. Then she nodded once and went to the Bergen table.

Lena finished the eggs and half the oatmeal. At 6:54 she looked at the wall clock. At 7:07 she looked again and found herself still holding the spoon in midair, oatmeal cold and thickening in the bowl. She put the spoon down, drank the rest of the tea in three swallows, and told herself this counted as waiting, not losing time. She could account for the minutes. She had watched them pass. That was different.

At 7:42 she left the cafeteria and walked the corridor to the coaching suite. The building had warmed fully by then. Ventilation pushed dry heat out through the floor grilles. Somewhere above her, a medicine ball hit rubber in slow regular beats. The neural coaching suite door was shut. A cleaning cart stood against the wall opposite. She sat on the bench outside, damp hair cooling through the back of her sweatshirt, and watched the clock at the end of the corridor advance from 7:48 to 7:59.

Margot arrived at 8:03 with a paper coffee cup in one hand and her tablet tucked under the other arm.

"You beat me here," she said.

"I wanted to see the session log."

Margot looked at her for a second, then pushed the door open with her shoulder. "Fine. Come in."

---

Next to the pool sat the neural coaching suite, small and windowless, with two reclining stations, a wall of displays, and the faint chlorine residue everyone carried in from the deck. The fluorescent lights were fully up. A clock hung on one wall. The coat rack held Margot's dark parka and nobody else's. Processing hardware hummed behind the panels.

Margot Voss set down her coffee, shrugged out of the parka, and woke the main display with a tap. Her feed interface, the flat silver wrist band she used during live sessions, rested dark against her right wrist. Reviews used recorded data, not live feed. One shoe was already off by the time the system finished loading, her stockinged foot hooked around the chair rung in the posture of someone who spent most mornings in this room.

"Sit," she said. "Show me where it felt wrong."

The athlete's station reclined for bodies that needed to lie still while the system read them. For a review Lena stayed unplugged; the data was already in the log.

Margot pulled up the fifth repeat. At the wall the two traces split: Lena's intended turn on the left, the November variant on the right. Standard tuck and shallow push-off against the deeper angle, tighter fold, four-beat dolphin. A small marker sat over the breach and read 1.3 seconds.

"There," Lena said.

"Yeah." Margot zoomed in. "Stored variant. November rotation. The link thought it was the faster answer and took it."

"I didn't."

"I know. I'm telling you what it did."

Margot widened the view. Three smaller markers appeared earlier in the set, pale enough that Lena hadn't noticed them at first.

"What are those?"

"Minor corrections. Hand entry here. Kick timing off the wall here. This one on repeat four." Margot tapped each in turn. "All under a second."

"It made those too."

"Looks like it."

Lena kept looking at the screen. The earlier markers sat there with the same blunt certainty as the big one. Three moments from the morning when the link had altered hand entry or kick timing before she felt it clearly enough to stop.

"Today's one-point-three," Margot said. "You've been between point-eight and one-point-five all block. This isn't a cliff."

"It felt different."

Margot turned in her chair. "Different how?"

Precision mattered. "Like I was late to it. The turn had already started."

Margot held her gaze another second, then looked back at the screen. "That part I believe. Usually you don't feel the small corrections. This one got big enough to register."

"That's not the same as saying it's fine."

"No." Margot took a sip of coffee. "It's not the same as saying it's catastrophic either."

The chart was clean. The explanation held together. Margot was good at this part of the job. She read data clearly, didn't hedge, didn't patronize. None of that made the split traces easier to look at.

"If it happens in a race?" Lena asked.

"Then the system thinks it's helping you in a race." Margot set down the cup. "The link makes tiny calls all the time. Stroke timing, load distribution, angle correction. That's the arrangement. What matters is whether the calls stay inside tolerances and whether they start costing you anything."

"This cost me the rest of the repeat."

"Yes." Margot clicked to the physiological overlay: heart rate, oxygen uptake, turn depth, recovery curve. "But not because it hurt performance. Because you stopped."

There was no good answer to that. If she had kept swimming the numbers might have treated the moment as a success.

Margot leaned back. "You told Dara it was a cramp."

"Yes."

"Why?"

Lena looked at the screen instead of at her. "Because I didn't want to say what it was in the lane."

"Fine." Margot did not push. "Here are your options. I can flag it to Yusuf as an engine issue and he looks at the stored pattern tree. Or I can kick it to Petra and make it medical. If I do that, you'll lose training time while they run a full work-up. With today's numbers she's likely to hand you straight back, but she'll still want the work-up."

The sentence sat between them for a moment. It was the first thing that morning that sounded like a choice and not like a description.

"Yusuf first," Lena said.

"That's what I figured." Margot made the note. "If it happens again, you tell me before you tell the next lane it's a cramp."

"All right."

Margot closed the turn trace but left the broader drift chart open. "Anything else feel off this morning? Not emotionally. In the system."

Lena thought about the shower dispenser under her hand, the second she could not account for. A second was nothing. Everyone forgot ordinary things. Everyone stood in the shower and drifted. If she said it aloud it would become a symptom the way naming weather on a training report made it part of the record.

"No," she said.

"Good. Your pace structure's where we want it. I'd hold volume this week, maybe add threshold Friday if recovery stays clean." Another tap on the screen. "And eat more. Your protein numbers are low."

"I ate breakfast."

"Then eat breakfast harder."

It was flat enough to almost count as a joke. Almost.

Margot sent the note to Yusuf and swivelled the screen far enough for Lena to see the confirmation tag appear.

"Done. If he wants you in the bay, he'll message. Anything else?"

"No."

"Then go recover."

Chlorine still clung to Lena's hair and the creases of her hands when she left the room. Eighteen years in the water had made the smell invisible most of the time. In moments like this it returned all at once, lifting off skin as though she were carrying the pool out with her.

---

By evening the room had shrunk to what she'd left in it: damp towel cooling on the chair back, radiator ticking under the sill, the novel Anne had recommended lying open on the blanket where Lena had left it after dinner, a finger's width of spine giving way under its own weight. The wall display above the desk sat dimmed to a narrow strip, still cycling through neural link connectivity, system load, calibration schedule, the slow ticker of biomarkers that meant nothing unless Yusuf pointed at one and said this is running hot, we'll adjust it Tuesday.

She had checked for Yusuf's name on the display at 16:10, 17:02, and 18:31. Nothing. The flagged turn existed now as a note in somebody else's queue, something that had happened inside her body and then been translated into text waiting for review. No answer should have made the morning smaller. Instead it left the shape of it entirely to her. By dinner she was looking up every time footsteps slowed outside her door. Once, after recovery, she had tried closing her eyes for ten minutes and found she could not tell whether she had slept or only lain there with her jaw clenched against the pillow.

Under the shower the water felt hotter and softer than the pool, with none of the chlorine bite. She stayed there longer than she needed to, then dressed and went down to the cafeteria. Dinner was pasta with chicken, a salad she moved around without finishing, and a glass of water. Around her, a table of sprinters compared session notes, some gymnasts traded complaints, two decathletes from the briefing sheets sat shoulder to shoulder over their trays. A two-person table near the windows stayed empty long enough for her to claim it. No one joined her. Eighteen years of institutional dining had taught her that this too was ordinary.

By seven the room had gone dark around the window. Late January in the Bernese Alps: sun gone behind the peaks by four-thirty, exterior floods flattening the nearest slope into white, the rest of the range reduced to shape and rumor.

For two weeks she'd been reading the German novel Anne had mailed from Bern, the one about a woman sorting through her dead aunt's papers. It moved through letters, postcards, train tickets, whole decades of somebody else's filed-away life. Nothing about it was difficult. The pace just asked more patience than most things in Lena's day.

On page 147 the woman in the book sat at her aunt's desk sorting envelopes while afternoon light made dust visible in the air. The German nouns arrived one by one: Schreibtisch, Umschlaege, Licht, Staub.

Lena read the paragraph.

Then she read it again.

The nouns stayed separate on the page. She could identify desk, envelopes, light, and dust, and still not hold them together long enough to understand the paragraph.

She set the heel of her hand against the page and started from the first line again. The result was the same. Her eyes tracked properly. The words did not blur. She could translate any of them in isolation. By the time she reached the end of the sentence, the first words had already gone.

Book open on her lap, she sat still while the paper warmed and cooled under her thumbs. The radiator under the sill ticked once. Her shoulders held the same angle as if the next motion would be to turn the page.

It didn't happen.

When she looked up, the display clock read 19:44.

It had read 19:23 when she'd opened the book.

The left leg had gone numb under her. The page edge was damp where her thumb had held it. One shoulder had stiffened from keeping the same angle. Twenty-one minutes had gone by with her sitting upright, eyes open, on the same page. She had not fallen asleep. She had not drifted into a chain of thought and come back from it. There was nothing to return from. The clock had moved and her body had been here when it did.

She put the book down on the blanket, face open, the spine cracking softly. On the back of the cafeteria receipt still in her pocket she wrote, in small block letters, 19:23-19:44 AWAKE. Then she looked at the words to make sure she believed them.

Her hand went up behind her head to the neural link port at the base of her skull. The disc was small and smooth, two centimeters across and warm from the skin around it. It was always warm. She pressed her fingertip against it gently, the way you'd test a bruise. The port had been installed in the Zug facility when she was four, one medical appointment among a hundred that year, and it had been part of her skull longer than most of the memories she carried inside it.

In the bathroom, cold water ran over her hands before she splashed her face once, then again. In the mirror there was only herself: brown eyes, the faint mark on her left temple from the fall on the pool deck when she was twelve, jaw set in the expression she wore when deciding whether something counted as a problem. She looked fine. Tired in the ordinary way, not altered.

Back on the bed, the book reopened to page 147. The woman at the desk returned, along with the envelopes, the light, the dust.

This time the words connected. She found a letter from a man named Aurel, train schedules, the 7:14 from Delemont to Basel, a delay at Laufen, and an apology for arriving late to something the letter never bothered to specify. The dead aunt had kept it for reasons the novel hadn't explained yet. Not understanding that felt ordinary again, almost pleasant.

Three more pages went by without trouble. Comprehension returned so completely that it became invisible. Room, blanket, book, page, all of it joined up the way it ordinarily did.

At lights-out the bedside lamp clicked off and the wall display folded itself down to a single muted status line above the desk. Lena rolled onto one elbow and woke the screen again.

A symptom menu opened. Dizziness. Nausea. Muscular pain. Loss of consciousness.

She read the list twice. Nothing on it matched twenty-one minutes upright in bed with her eyes open and no memory attached. If she flagged loss of consciousness, the overnight log would show otherwise. If she left a note, it would be only her saying it had happened.

She opened the note field anyway.

For a few seconds the cursor blinked in the empty box. Then she typed: Awake. Reading. Twenty-one minutes missing.

She looked at the sentence. It seemed flatter than the thing itself, smaller and less reliable. By the time someone read it, Yusuf maybe, or Margot, or a nurse who did not know her, the sentence would already have started sounding like poor sleep or nerves or language fatigue. She deleted it word by word until the box was empty again.

Yusuf was already going to inspect the turn. Margot already had the morning anomaly in the system. She was not going to report this. Not yet. Not without a trace to point at that wasn't only her own account.

She set the receipt on the bedside table, closed the menu, and tapped the brightness back up until the desk chair, the hanging towel, and the book on the blanket sharpened out of the dim room. Then she lay flat on her back and watched the seconds in the top corner turn over one by one, needing to see each change happen.
