# Chapter 1

The shuttle from Meiringen climbed the switchbacks in low gear, and Kai sat in the back with his bag between his knees and watched the road fold away behind each turn. Snow packed both shoulders of the road, and the guardrails were crusted white. The driver hadn't spoken since the valley floor, just worked the wheel through the hairpins with loose shoulders and bored accuracy, each turn arriving in his hands before the shuttle reached it. A small mercy. Kai had used up his supply of pleasant talk on the Zurich connection, chatting with a woman who'd recognized him from a Meridian promo spot and wanted to know what it felt like. Running enhanced. Like running, he'd told her. But faster. She'd laughed as if he'd made a joke.

The altitude registered at the second hairpin. Not dramatic, just a tightness behind his sternum, breath becoming labor instead of habit at two thousand and change meters. His lungs would catch up in a few days. The spinal neural bridge had already caught up. If he paid attention, he could feel a faint warmth running along his back, the hardware recalibrating his oxygen distribution before his body had finished registering the deficit. The bridge was always ahead of him. That was the point.

Snow-covered firs lined the road, bowed under weight, thinning as the altitude climbed. The light was going. Late January in the Bernese Alps meant the sun started quitting around four, and the sky above the ridgeline was already the pale, exhausted grey of a winter afternoon giving up early. Kai pressed his forehead against the cold window and watched the tree line drop away.

The Kirchberg Institute appeared around the final curve. A campus of concrete and glass set into the mountainside: training halls, residential blocks, a medical wing with windows catching the last of the daylight. Not beautiful, but functional, the kind of place that assumed you were there to work and had designed every surface accordingly. Floodlights had already come on along the approach road, and the facility's glass facade reflected them back in flat panels of white that made the building look like it was generating its own weather.

The shuttle stopped at the main entrance. Kai stepped out and the cold hit his face, the dry metallic cold of high altitude, thinner than Indiana winter, sharper, with none of the damp heaviness that meant snow was actually coming. This cold was just the air being honest about its temperature. Negative twelve, maybe. His breath came out solid.

Inside, the lobby was warm, carpeted, aggressively neutral. A receptionist scanned his credentials and handed him a room key and an orientation packet in a folder embossed with the Meridian BioSystems logo. The folder was thick enough to suggest corporate believed paperwork could do the work of conversation.

"Dinner's at seven in the cafeteria. Dr. Qadir will see you for your initial calibration at eight."

"Great. Thanks."

"Welcome to Kirchberg, Mr. Brennan."

Corridor signs led him to the residential wing. The corridor was fluorescent-lit, with doors on both sides, each fitted with a small digital display showing room status. Most were blank, dark rooms, empty, or athletes who'd disabled their readouts. A few showed scrolling biometric data visible through half-open doors. Heart rate, integration scores, neural latency. The casual broadcast of what would have been private medical information anywhere else. Here it was like leaving your shoes outside your door.

In one doorway, a woman was stretching, legs in a split that ran the full width of the frame with the mechanical precision of a compass opening. She nodded at him without interrupting, her calves showing the same engineered definition his did, bilateral symmetry too clean to be the product of genetics and training alone. Her eyes followed him without flicker, too smooth by half. He had the brief, stupid sense that she was seeing him in more frames than he was seeing her. Kai nodded back and kept walking.

The corridor cut through a connecting wing between residential and medical. Through a glass partition he could see a corner of what had to be the calibration bay: reclining chairs, monitor banks, the blue standby glow of diagnostic screens in a room that looked unoccupied. The hum was louder here, a bass throb pushing up through the floor so that by the time he heard it, it was already in his sternum and the arches of his feet. Further on, a second glass wall overlooked the pool deck: fifty meters of chlorinated water under flat white light, the surface so still it looked solid. A swimmer in a green cap sat on the far edge with her feet in the water, not moving, not preparing, just sitting there and letting the delay happen.

The room was spartan and adequate. It had a bed, a desk, a bathroom, and a window facing the eastern ridge. The wall-mounted diagnostic display was already active, scrolling data from his enhancement system: heart rate, core temperature, synthetic muscle integration, neural bridge latency. All green, everything functioning. His bag landed on the bed, and he went to the window. Snow fell in the floodlights' periphery. The mountains beyond were shapes now, losing detail to the dark. Warm air kept hissing through the vent over the door, a dry mechanical breath that made the room feel occupied even with nobody else in it.

He'd been to training facilities before, Colorado Springs, the Meridian campus outside Portland, altitude work in Flagstaff. They'd all had the same basic architecture of purpose: track, gym, medical, cafeteria, sleep. But those places had natural athletes mixed in with the enhanced, and the mix created a visible gradient you could use to locate yourself. You knew where you stood. At Kirchberg, there was no gradient. Every body in the building was rebuilt. Enhancement wasn't the variable here. It was the baseline. The woman doing splits in her doorway hadn't been showing off. She'd been stretching before dinner.

Kai sat on the bed and ran his thumb along the inside of his left thigh, pressing until he found the seam. The synthetic muscle graft sat under the skin without a scar to mark it, but the texture changed where engineered fiber met natural tissue. A faint ridge ran from mid-thigh to just above the knee. The right leg had the same seam in the same place. Below the knee, the calf grafts had their own. Bilateral symmetry. His legs looked better than they ever had when they were entirely his, the proportions a little too balanced, the muscle definition a little too even, legs a sculptor would give an athlete who existed only in reference photos.

Three years since the reconstruction. Two ACLs and a spinal compression fracture at twenty-seven, the kind of injury that ended natural careers because natural bodies didn't come back from it, not fully, not to world-record form. Meridian had offered the rebuild six weeks after the surgery. Full reconstruction: synthetic grafts in both legs, spinal neural bridge to replace the compromised nerve pathways, visual processing enhancement for reaction time. He'd signed in a hospital bed in Indianapolis with his mother sitting in the corner not saying anything and his agent on speaker saying everything.

Now he was faster than he'd been at twenty-five, when the 200-meter record had been his and the body had been too.

***

The indoor track was a 200-meter oval under high ceilings with acoustic dampening that didn't quite work. Footfalls carried, each one a clean report off the rubber surface followed by a fast, shallow echo. Kai walked in at nine the next morning and the sound of other athletes warming up was like listening to rain on a drum. Four runners were on the infield stretching and jogging short bursts, and a coach stood by the finish with a tablet and a stopwatch. The air was altitude-controlled and warmer than outside, and it smelled of rubber and the particular chemical cleanliness of a facility that was maintained rather than lived in.

In the locker room, he changed and worked through his warm-up sequence: hip flexors, hamstrings, calves. The rebuilt sections stretched differently than natural tissue. There was compliance but no complaint, a willingness in the synthetic fibers that felt cooperative rather than resistant. His real quads still ached when he pushed too deep. The engineered sections just extended, obliging, as though the muscle had already read the script.

The qualifying heat was a formality: five runners, posted times, a gate officer with a starter pistol and an unhurried manner that said he'd still be standing there long after Kai was done. Kai set his blocks at the line and sank into the crouch, hands on the rubber, weight forward on his fingertips. His visual processing enhancement activated without being asked. The lane ahead sharpened, depth of field narrowing, peripheral detail dimming to irrelevance. It had triggered before the conscious decision to trigger it. The system had read the posture, the setting, the context, and done what it always did at a start line.

The gun fired.

Out of the blocks, his legs drove him into the curve. The synthetic muscle fired in a sequenced cascade, calf, quad, hip, and the power was clean and immediate and felt like his own effort in the same way that driving a car fast felt like running: the speed was real, the direction was yours, and somewhere in the middle was an engine doing the parts you couldn't. Through the curve, leaning, the centrifugal force was managed by the hardware low in his hips and lower back, anticipating the angle before he thought about banking. The rubber passed beneath him in a blur of red-brown. Into the straight now, full speed, and this was where the quiet lived. There was no burn in his legs, no oxygen debt building in his chest, no negotiation between what the lungs could supply and what the muscles demanded. Just his body moving at a speed that should have felt like violence, feet barely touching the surface, arms pumping with a rhythm he wasn't setting, and felt instead like coasting. Like being carried very fast in a direction he'd agreed to.

A stride later he was through the line. His body decelerated through the curve past the finish, and the transition from sprint to jog was seamless: no shock, no buckled stride, no sudden announcement of oxygen debt. The rebuilt legs downshifted smoothly, and his breathing barely changed. The natural version of him used to finish the 200 and fold over, hands on knees, lungs screaming, vision spotted at the edges. That was the natural version. Now he ran the fastest 200 meters of his life and his body's first response was to inform him, politely, that it could go again.

The clock said 19.02.

Personal best by three hundredths. The number held on the overhead board long enough for him to think he'd read it wrong, then an amber box lit around lane four. REVIEW appeared under the time in German, French, and English. Closed qualifiers at Kirchberg didn't touch any record book; the system kept them internal until it verified the start blocks, lane pickup, and graft telemetry against the athlete's calibration file. Too much Meridian money sat in the walls here for a number like that to pass as just a number.

Now the gate officer looked up properly. So did the coach by the finish.

"Hold there, Brennan," the coach called. "Don't disappear yet."

Kai stopped at the edge of the track.

The gate officer's thumb moved over the tablet. "Start was clean. Block pressure normal. Lane sensors clean."

"Telemetry sync?" the coach asked.

"Pending medical cross-check."

The coach glanced at Kai, then at the board again. It wasn't admiration exactly. More like the sharp attention people gave an expensive machine when it started behaving in a new way.

"Fine," he said. "We'll log it provisional till medical clears the pull. Qadir will get the split. You're bumped into seeded section tomorrow."

"Okay."

One of the runners still coming off the bend gave a short laugh that sounded more startled than amused. The hurdler from Houston jogged past a few seconds later, breathing harder than Kai was, looked up at the board, and said, "Jesus. Nice run."

"Thanks."

The coach was already speaking into his headset by then, walking toward the finish table. Two staff on the infield had turned to their screens. The board kept the amber box around lane four while his breathing sat there, ordinary, giving him nothing.

Kai stood at the edge of the track with his hands on his hips and tried to locate the race in his body. The usual markers weren't there. No residual burn in the quads, no trembling in the hamstrings, no gratitude from his lungs for the air they were now getting. His heart rate was settling into a rhythm that felt more like waking from a nap than recovering from a flat-out sprint. He'd been in the blocks ten seconds ago. Now he was standing here, breathing normally, and the distance between those two facts felt like someone had edited out the middle of a film and left him watching the credits.

On the cool-down curve, his heart rate was already settling. The spinal bridge managed recovery the way a thermostat managed temperature: smooth, automatic, without consulting him. Sweat cooled on the back of his neck. The rubber under his shoes had a faint chemical bite to it, and he focused on that smell because it was just his senses doing their own work, his nose being a nose, and even that felt thin, the sensation arriving and departing with less weight than it should have carried.

Nineteen-oh-two. Faster than anything he'd run in his natural career. Faster than the version of him that had set world records on legs made of what he'd been born with. The number was correct and the clock was correct and his legs had done the work and his lungs had done the work and his spine had done the work and all of it was functioning as designed and he had been present for the entire ten-point-odd seconds of it.

What he couldn't tell was which parts had been his idea.

After a shower and a change of clothes, he ate lunch in the cafeteria. Institutional food: grilled chicken, rice, vegetables steamed past the point of having personality. The windows showed mountains in midday winter light, the sun a flat white presence behind a veil of cloud that hadn't committed to being overcast. The upper ridges held the light for a moment longer than the valleys, white against grey. Athletes ate in clusters. The conversations Kai overheard were ordinary: sleep quality, supplement protocols, the facility's ongoing hot-water problem. A woman two tables over was arguing quietly on her phone about a missed delivery. A young guy eating alone, twenty-one maybe, had a decathlete's broad shoulders and the slightly unfinished proportions of a body still settling around the rebuild. He was reading his tablet with the dutiful concentration of pre-meet homework. An energy bar lay half-eaten beside his plate, forgotten in favor of whatever he was reading.

Kai's phone buzzed. His mother, texting from Lafayette. A photo of snow on the front porch, the specific yellow-grey Indiana snow that clung to everything, covering the porch railing and the mailbox and the edge of the welcome mat she'd had since he was in high school.

*Probably colder here than where you are*

He typed: *Doubt it. 2100 meters up here*

*Is that high? I don't know what that is*

*About 7000 feet. Cold*

*How was the run?*

*Good. New personal best*

*That's wonderful honey. Call me this week ok?*

He set the phone down. His right hand was resting on the table and then it wasn't. His fingers cinched around the water glass while he was still looking at his mother's last text. By the time the wanting caught up, his wrist had already turned. The bottom of the glass dragged a wet crescent through the condensation ring and clipped one of his front teeth on the way up. Cold water spilled across his lower lip. He swallowed too fast, felt it hit the back of his throat wrong, and set the glass down harder than he'd meant to. A few drops jumped onto his knuckles.

He looked at his hand.

Nothing anybody else would have seen. A fraction of a second, maybe less. But his pulse had kicked once, hard enough that he felt it in his throat, and his palm stayed braced on the table as if the hand might try again. Six months ago, or three months ago, the motion would have slipped past him. He'd started noticing it in Portland, and then in Flagstaff, and now here, at a table in a cafeteria in the Swiss Alps, the motion arriving a fraction ahead of the wanting.

***

The calibration bay was in the medical wing, a clean room with fluorescent tubes and the permanent low hum of processing equipment, a sound that lived in the walls, below the pitch of conversation but too present to be called silence. Kai had been told you stopped hearing it after a week. A day in, he still heard every cycle.

Yusuf Qadir was setting up when Kai arrived at eight. Short, dark-haired, in real prescription glasses, he was one of the few people at the facility who'd declined enhancement for himself. Around the calibration rig, Yusuf moved without looking for anything, adjusting monitor angles, tapping commands at a console, a grey diagnostic cable looped over one shoulder that he hadn't found a use for yet.

"Brennan. Come in, sit down." He gestured at the rig, a reclining chair boxed in by screens and connector ports, a setup that could have passed for a dentist's chair if dentists needed six monitors and a firmware interface. "How was the trip in?"

"Long."

"Always is. Zurich connection?"

"Yeah."

"God, yeah, that layover. Right, so." He patted the chair's headrest. "First calibration since Portland, which means I need a full baseline. About forty-five minutes, maybe an hour if your graft firmware's been running updates I don't know about. You good with that?"

"I'm good."

Kai settled into the chair. The surface was medical-grade vinyl, body-temperature, shaped to distribute his weight evenly across his back and legs. Yusuf attached sensor pads to his legs, inner thigh, outer calf, just above both ankles, his hands moving without hesitation, each pad finding its place before Kai had finished registering the last touch. Then he pressed a flat contact pad against the small of Kai's back so the rig could sync with the bridge through the skin. The pad caught with a soft click, and Kai felt a brief, clean pressure at the base of his spine, a sensation that would have been uncomfortable if it lasted longer than a second.

"You'll feel a handshake. That's me saying hi to your bridge."

"Felt it."

"Brilliant. Right." Yusuf dropped into his chair and scrolled through the incoming data, his face lit by the console's blue-white glow. In that light, he had the focused, slightly rumpled look of an engineer at the end of a long day who still had three things left to do and was prioritizing by interest rather than urgency. "Any changes since Portland? Pain, numbness, anything with range of motion?"

"No. Everything's been fine."

"Good. Graft integration's solid, both legs. Your calf firmware pushed an update last week. Did you catch that?"

"I felt a warmth in my lower legs for about ten minutes. Wednesday, I think."

"Wednesday, yeah, that tracks. Routine optimization pass. Nothing to worry about, it just recalibrated your calf response to some updated baseline data. Happens automatically." He scrolled further. "Bridge latency's within spec. Visual processing nominal. How's the visual been? Any artifacts?"

"Like what?"

"Halos around light sources, depth-of-field lag, colour banding. Some athletes get this thing where the world goes slightly flat for a second. Hard to describe. Like someone compressed the depth out of it."

"No, nothing like that."

"Good." Yusuf tapped a command and the sensor pads on Kai's legs came alive with a low vibration. "I'm going to map your graft response times. This bit's automatic. Just try to stay relaxed."

He stayed still. A calibration pulse moved through his left calf, a contraction he hadn't initiated, the muscle tightening and releasing in a wave that ran from ankle to knee. Then the right calf. Then both quads, one after the other. Then the hamstrings. His body twitched and responded and twitched again, and Kai lay there looking at the ceiling tiles and let it happen. Each pulse was a small theft, his muscle obeying someone else's instruction, and each time the muscle released, the control returned to him. Each time control returned he noticed the seam of the handover. The system giving him back what was already his.

This was the part that never made the interviews. The lying still. The being adjusted. The specific intimacy of letting another person tune the parts of your body you couldn't reach yourself, while you stared at the ceiling and felt your legs twitch on a schedule you didn't set.

Yusuf worked in focused silence for a while, scrolling data, occasionally leaning over to reposition a sensor pad. Under his breath he made small sounds of acknowledgment, not words, just the audible equivalent of nodding at the data on a screen. When he moved a sensor on Kai's thigh, his fingers found the seam by touch, without looking.

"Right, so," Yusuf said after about twenty minutes. "One more series. Voluntary this time. Left ankle. Don't rush it. Just do it when you mean to."

Kai kept his eyes on the ceiling. For a second nothing happened. Then the back of his calf went tight and his heel dragged a fraction against the chair before the internal yes had finished arriving. The foot flexed. He let it fall back.

"You feel that?" Yusuf asked.

"Yeah."

"Again."

Kai waited longer, stubborn now, trying to catch the exact start. The muscle loaded under the skin with the same clean eagerness, and his foot was already moving by the time the decision turned into language in his head.

Yusuf angled the console screen toward him. Numbers, graphs, a timeline. Two trend lines, one blue, one orange, that had been tracking close together six months ago and were now separating. The gap between them was small but consistent, and it widened at the right edge of the graph.

His tone had shifted. Still easy, still conversational, but there was a weight under it now, the way a voice changes when the data confirms bad news.

"Your proprioception scores are drifting."

"That's what this is."

"Yeah." He pointed at the gap. "Blue line is conscious motor intent. That's the signal your brain generates when you decide to move. Orange is execution. In a healthy integrated system, they should overlap. Over the last three months, orange has started edging ahead. Forty to fifty milliseconds."

"So my body starts before I do."

"Not exactly. The predictive layer is supposed to lean forward. That's how it strips latency out. It sees the beginning of a pattern and primes the rest. Usually the lead disappears inside the motion. What you just felt in your ankle is that lead getting big enough to notice. In a sprint, that's where your start times come from. Most people never feel it."

"And mine isn't invisible anymore."

"The window's widening. At ten milliseconds, nobody feels it. At fifty, some people start to notice. You're at fifty and trending up." He sat back, took off his glasses, rubbed the bridge of his nose with his thumb and forefinger. Put them back on. "Have you noticed anything?"

"Yeah."

"When?"

"Small transitions. Getting out of a chair. Rolling over in bed. First step when I've only just decided to stand. Nothing dramatic, just enough to make me stop and check whether I actually told myself to do it."

"How long?"

"Couple months. Maybe longer." Kai looked at the graph on the screen, the two lines diverging. "It's hard to say when it started because, I mean, how do you time a decision? You don't. You decide and you move and they feel like the same thing. So when the move starts happening first, you don't notice right away. You just feel like you're very decisive."

Yusuf almost smiled at that. "Yeah. That's the design problem, isn't it. When the system predicts cleanly, it just reads as movement. You only catch it once there's room between the thought and the muscle. And from your data, that room's getting wider."

Kai looked at the two lines on the screen. The blue one was his intent, his decisions, the electrical fact of wanting to move. The orange was his body already on its way. And the orange was winning. On the chair he'd felt fifty milliseconds as a clean tightening in his calf, the foot beginning to lift before the internal yes had fully taken shape. In a race the gap would hide inside speed. Here it sat on a screen in two separate colours.

"Is it getting worse?"

"The trend is up. Consistent over three months. I'm going to log it as within normal drift parameters, because technically it is. Meridian's spec allows up to eighty milliseconds of predictive lead before they flag it. But I'm setting a tracker so we can compare session to session." He began disconnecting the sensor pads, peeling each one off with a small, precise pull. "If the window keeps opening, I'll talk to Meridian's firmware team about dialing the predictive threshold down."

"And if they don't want to dial it down."

"Then we have a different conversation." Yusuf wound the diagnostic cable around his hand in a neat loop and hung it on its hook by the console. "Look. Your system is working. It's working well. You ran a nineteen-oh-two today on an unfamiliar track at altitude after a transatlantic flight. The system is doing what it was built to do."

"I know."

"But it doesn't feel like yours."

"I didn't say that."

"Nah, you didn't." Yusuf tapped a final command on the console and the screens went to standby, the blue light dimming to a low pulse. "Same time Thursday for a follow-up. Get some sleep. Altitude takes about three days to stop messing with your deep cycles."

"Thanks."

"Yeah. Night, Brennan."

Kai walked back to his room through the fluorescent corridor. The facility was quiet now, nine-thirty, athletes in their quarters, the common areas emptied, the cafeteria dark. His footsteps were the only sound except the building's ventilation, thinner here than it had been by the calibration bay but steady enough to sand the edges off each step. His own gait on the hard floor came back at him: left foot, right foot, even and smooth, the rebuilt legs managing his stride with the consistency his natural legs had never quite achieved. He'd always been slightly dominant on the left side. Now he wasn't. The reconstruction had corrected the asymmetry without asking.

His room was dark except for the wall display's glow. Green readouts, all systems nominal, the system reporting on itself in the same quiet room where the person it was installed in was trying to fall asleep. In the darkened room, he undressed, brushed his teeth, stood at the window. The mountains were invisible, just darkness and the faint reflection of the diagnostic display behind him, green numbers floating in the glass where the Bernese Alps should have been.

The graph stayed with him. Two lines diverging. Forty to fifty milliseconds. A gap he hadn't been able to name until Yusuf drew it on a screen, and now it had a shape and a slope and the slope was up.

His hand found the light switch. The plastic met his fingertips while the decision was still forming, and then the room went dark.
