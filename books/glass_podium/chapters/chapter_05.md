# Chapter 5

Kai's left ankle clicked when he crouched into the starting blocks, the natural kind, tendon over bone. A fraction later the right one corrected itself. Synthetic muscle tightened to set the joint before he'd finished settling into the pedals. Most days the lag between the two ankles passed beneath notice. This morning it stayed with him, small and stupid, like a word misspelled on a sign he saw too often.

He was in lane three.

Dry air bit low in his throat on the inhale. Two weeks at Kirchberg had been enough for his lungs to stop complaining outright, but morning sessions still left a sting at the bottom of the breath before his chest opened. Six other sprinters moved through warm-ups around the oval. Novak in the far lane, doing strides with a gait so bilaterally perfect it looked unreal. Priya Chandrasekaran near the curve, shaking out her hamstrings, her right leg extending and retracting with the evenness of a fresh calibration. Two athletes Kai knew by face but not by name were jogging easy laps. Behind the glass partition at the hundred-meter mark, two coaches sat with tablets on their knees, watching telemetry.

The start tone sounded, and he came out of the blocks.

The first fifty meters were the part that still felt like running. Feet hit rubber. Knees drove forward. Burn built in his quads with the old familiar honesty of lactic acid and effort and muscle doing exactly what he told it to do. Arms pumped. Breathing locked to footstrikes, short hard exhales. The grafts stayed in the background then, waiting for the point where his body crossed its natural limit. For those first seconds, the work belonged to him.

Around sixty meters, the assist came in and the effort smoothed out. There was always a seam there, impossible to pin down, where his own power handed off to the helped version. Stride lengthened by a centimeter he hadn't chosen. Arm swing adjusted. Elbows tucked. Hands loosened. The burn in his quads changed texture and spread out, managed now, load redistributed before fatigue could bunch in one place. He was still running. The running had started doing itself.

At eighty meters the visual enhancement sharpened. By ninety, lane markings snapped into hard clean lines while the far wall came forward with too much detail and the edges of the track fell away into blur. Everything outside the lane ahead lost importance. When the curve came up at a hundred meters, natural balance had never found that angle, but his body banked into it anyway, precise and efficient, grey padding sliding by on his left over concrete block close enough to touch. The foam was built for bodies already slowing down, not for this speed.

At a hundred and twenty, and again at a hundred and thirty, the time felt good without a clock, the way a driver knows speed from engine pitch. At a hundred and forty his left foot hit and the track dropped out from under him.

It wasn't darkness. It wasn't silence. It was just gone. Sight vanished so completely even black never arrived. Sound went with it. The map of his own body, shoulders and jaw and knees and the tilt of his head, got ripped out in the same instant. One stride he had a body. The next there was only the thinking left behind: *I was running. I can't feel my legs. I can't feel anything.*

The thoughts came clear and useless. They had nowhere to land. Nothing grounded him. No breath filled his lungs. No weight sat in his chest. He tried to open his hands and found no hands. Tried to stop running and found no running from where he'd been dropped. Only the hard certainty that the body he couldn't reach had kept going. His body was still sprinting. No sensation came up from it, but the fact of it stayed obvious. The legs were still cycling. The arms were still pumping. The lungs were still hauling air. The bridge had locked the sprint into his muscles, and the stride kept its shape after he was gone.

Inside his own head he threw the order as hard as he could. *Stop. Slow down. Stop.* Nothing happened. There was no grip on the legs, no line down to them, no body left to hear.

Time had no size in there. There was no pulse to count, no breath, no footfalls. There was only math: the curve, the wall on the curve, padding meant for cooldown collisions, not this speed. He didn't know if the wall was half a second away or already at his shoulder.

Crash response came to him anyway, pure training. He pictured his chin down, his shoulder turned, his arms in. The picture stayed in his head. Nothing in his neck or ribs or hands heard it.

The world hit him shoulder first. Sound came back as a thud inside his skull when his right shoulder slammed into the wall. Foam flattened. Concrete took the rest. The joint jumped out of place with a wet grind, shoved forward and down until his arm no longer fit his own socket.

Momentum spun him off the wall and flung his face across the track. Fine black grit tore skin off his cheek and the skin above his left eye, then packed itself into the raw streaks. Blood came fast, hot at first, then cold in the dry air.

His left hand shot out. Reflex or the last of the stride still burning through him, he couldn't tell. The wrist folded under him and broke with a clean crack just above the joint. White pain shot up the forearm and stopped under the elbow.

He ended on his side and then his front, twisted between the two. Overhead panels glared. Blood ran across his left eye and turned the lights into red-smeared rectangles. The right shoulder sat wrong under the skin, too far forward, every small shift sending a grinding pulse through his chest. The left forearm was already swelling around the break.

His legs were still twitching.

They weren't running now. They were firing in scraps. Quadriceps bunched and let go. Calves jumped. Spikes scratched the rubber in short useless kicks. Whatever had kicked the stride loose was still sputtering through the bridge.

He watched his own thighs jump and couldn't do anything about it. Lying on the track in blood, shoulder out, left arm broken, he watched his feet scrape and kick as if they were trying to finish the rep. The legs were his. The command wasn't.

People were already running toward him. Novak reached him first. Blue-and-white shoes stopped two feet from Kai's face. Someone shouted for medical. The coaches behind the glass had stood up and abandoned their tablets. Through the blood in his eye he could still see Priya at the edge of the far lane, hands hanging at her sides, watching with the stillness of someone looking at a body and thinking of her own.

"Don't move him," a voice said somewhere above him. "Wait. Don't move him."

He wasn't trying. The left leg kicked once more, spike leaving a grey scuff on the track, and then stopped.

The medical team arrived with a backboard and a cervical collar. A woman from Petra Engel's staff dropped beside him, broad-shouldered, cold hands, calm in the way of someone trained for exactly this and still surprised to need it at a morning sprint session.

"Can you hear me?"

"Yeah."

"Tell me what hurts."

"Shoulder's out. Right one. Left wrist is broken." His own voice came back lower than he expected. The joke reflex wasn't there.

"Can you feel your feet?"

The twitching had stopped. What remained was his own body reporting in: toes, ankles, the deep used ache in muscles that had done work without permission. "Yeah. I feel them."

The woman gave the smallest nod, filing the answer away.

They rolled him onto the backboard. The shoulder shifted and a short closed-mouth sound came out of him, chosen over the scream that wanted out. Five other athletes stood in a loose semicircle now. Novak had folded his arms. Priya still hadn't moved from the far lane. The whole room had gone wrong around the crash. Even the quiet sounded wrong.

Blood dripped from his face onto the board in small red beads. His cheek burned with ground-in grit. The cut above his eye kept leaking into his lashes and tinting the edge of the world.

They carried him down the corridor between the track and the medical wing. Three minutes of fluorescent light and ventilation hum, ceiling panels sliding past at other people's walking speed. Kai stared up and kept trying to find the edge of the gap. There wasn't one. One stride he was running, the next he was nowhere, then his shoulder hit concrete and the whole world slammed back in.

The thirty-one seconds in between had been bare thought, stripped clear of lungs and weight while the sprint kept spending his body.

---

The medical bay smelled like isopropyl and cold metal trays. They reduced his shoulder first. Two techs held him while a third worked the arm, patient and mechanical, until the joint slipped back where it belonged. The pop was sickening. Pain changed shape when it went home, as if the shoulder remembered being out and resented the trip back.

"Shit," Kai said, and that was all.

They braced the wrist next. Bones sat out of line, and when the technician pressed along the break the pain told him exactly where it had failed. She locked the joint in a rigid splint and left his fingers free. It would need imaging. The grafts would make the bone knit fast, which didn't mean it would knit right.

A doctor Kai hadn't met cleaned the grit out of the abrasions with saline that stung clean and deep. Then she stitched the cut above his left eye, five sutures, neat and quick.

"You'll have a small scar here," she said.

"Sure."

Scissors slid under the laces of his spikes. The nurse cutting them off had to work carefully around the splint on his left arm and the raw scrape along his cheek. When she lifted his right heel clear of the mattress, his calf seized hard enough to jerk the whole leg straight.

The mattress frame rattled.

Everyone in the room stopped.

"Sorry," Kai said automatically.

"Did you do that on purpose?" the nurse asked.

"No."

She kept hold of the heel until the muscle let go. Another jump ran through the thigh, smaller this time, then faded. Her eyes went to the chart. "Residual autonomous discharge," she said to no one in particular, and wrote it down.

The phrase made him colder than the saline had.

After that they left a rolled towel under both knees as if he were liable to snap straight again. The towel sat there white and routine, one more small precaution set in place because his legs might jump without warning. That made it worse.

Imaging took longer than the treatment. They wheeled him one room over for X-rays, slid his arm and wrist into positions that kept finding new pain inside the old pain, and rolled him back to the exam room with a temporary sling pulling at his neck. A monitor clipped to his finger tracked pulse and oxygen. Another lead sat on his sternum. No one bothered pretending he was here only for a broken wrist and a dislocated shoulder.

Voices moved in and out beyond the curtain and then beyond the door. Track staff came through. A trainer came through. Somebody asked whether the morning sessions were canceled. Somebody else said not yet, we're waiting on Dr. Engel. A little later the same voices came back tighter. The word blackout reached him once. Then the voices dropped into the careful quiet people use when the injured person might still be listening.

He was still listening.

Between interruptions the room flattened into fluorescent light and machine hum. His shoulder throbbed in deep slow pulses. The break sat under constant pressure with bright flares whenever his fingers moved. Air touched the raw skin on his cheek. The towel stayed under his knees. The memory of the calf locking by itself when someone else picked up his leg stayed with it all.

He tested the toes under the sheet. They moved when he asked them to move. He did it again. The result was the same.

The third time, fear got into the sequence and made it stupid. He flexed and released, then flexed and released again.

The right foot answered a fraction before he finished thinking the second flex.

Maybe it hadn't. Maybe pain had sped the thought up or slowed it down or made him notice the wrong part of it. That would have been the generous explanation. He stared at the sheet until the two ridges over his feet looked steady again and didn't try another test.

The door opened and Yusuf came in with his tablet and a data cable. His face had the look Kai knew from calibration sessions, concentration pulled tight, eyes narrowed behind his glasses, an engineer already arguing with the data in his head.

"How bad?" Yusuf asked, dragging a chair to the bedside.

"Shoulder's back in. Wrist is broken. Face is rough."

"Yeah, I can see the face. Can I plug in?"

Calibration shorthand. He wanted system logs, bridge data, graft telemetry, visual records, everything the hardware had kept track of while Kai had been locked out of himself.

"Go ahead."

Yusuf leaned over him, found the port at the base of his skull, and clicked the cable in. His hands were steady, the same hands that always made the interface feel routine. He sat back and started reading.

Kai watched him read. The screen itself stayed out of sight, only a pale glow reflected in Yusuf's lenses. Timestamps, graphs, and scrolling blocks of system output moved across the display. Tightness gathered around Yusuf's eyes and then held there.

"What happened at one-forty?"

"That's what I'm looking at." His professional voice stayed clean and measured. He scrolled, stopped, scrolled back. "Your visual processing dropped to zero at the one-forty mark. Full shutdown. It wasn't a fade."

"It wasn't just visual."

"No, I can see that. Body mapping went at the same timestamp. Auditory input forty-two milliseconds later. So everything, basically. All at once." Yusuf moved to another pane. "Motor output didn't stop."

"My legs didn't stop."

"No." He was still looking at the tablet. "The bridge kept a stored sprint routine alive on the autonomous layer. Your grafts just carried on." He glanced up briefly. "Didn't need you there for that."

The room hummed softly while Kai tried to fit that answer anywhere useful. "How long was I out?"

"Thirty-one seconds." Another pane. Another pause. "Sensory return at one-seventy. Impact at one-seventy-three."

"I know where the wall is."

"I know you do."

His finger moved again, faster now. He scrolled, stopped, backed up, and zoomed in. The professional tone thinned by half a degree.

"Hold on."

"What?"

"There's a post-install verification fault in your crash dump." Yusuf didn't look up. "From yesterday's calibration, not from the impact. The receipt says the sensory package installed clean. The live module hash doesn't match it."

Kai stared at him. Pain had made his patience short. "English."

Yusuf finally looked up. "Your system says it downloaded one thing and ran another. Same label. Different payload."

"Because I hit a wall?"

"No. That's why I'm looking at it." Yusuf turned the tablet so Kai could see almost none of it and still understand the shape: pale lines, a red one cutting across them, a block of amber text repeated in two different places. "Impact damage mangles data. This doesn't look mangled. It's neat. The sensory fail-safe table was already rewritten before you left the blocks."

"Rewritten to do what?"

"To stay quiet when the sensory layer drops." He tapped the red line. "Normally if sight and body map vanish together, the bridge trips a hard stop. Legs lock or dump you to a protective crawl. Ugly, but not a hundred-and-forty-meter wall strike. Yours never fired. The autonomous layer just kept the sprint going."

The explanation sat there long enough for Kai to say, "So that was in me before the run."

"Yes."

"And you missed it."

Yusuf's mouth tightened. "The dashboard missed it."

"You were the dashboard yesterday."

"No, I was the person trusting a summary report." The words came out sharper than his usual bedside tone. A beat later he corrected by addition, not apology. "It keeps the version label and strips the live hash unless something fails hard enough to dump memory. Your crash preserved the part the normal report flattens."

He was already standing as he said the last sentence, still tethered to Kai by the cable. The wall terminal woke under his hand. The larger screen split into panes of logs and sync records.

From the table Kai could make out athlete names down the left column and the same block of amber digits repeating beside them.

"What are you checking?"

"Shared handshake logs." Yusuf opened another pane. "If the mismatch lived only in your box, maybe you've got a local corruption event. Storage fault, damaged sensory board, one-off nightmare. If the same payload hash shows up in the network receipts, it came through calibration and we're all fucked." The last part came in his own voice, not the professional one.

Kai watched the names climb past. His session came first. Then Novak, eighteen-oh-three last night. Hoffman, twenty-oh-nine. Priya, six-eleven this morning. Two swimmers followed, then a hurdler. More amber digits came after that.

Yusuf went still, then pulled the first record open beside the second and the third. "There."

"Same package label," he said, more to the screen than to Kai. "Same approved version string. Same clean install receipt. Same bad hash sitting underneath it. Christ." Another pane. "And the sensory rollback counter's up in all of them. Low, but it's there."

Kai pushed himself an inch off the pillow before his shoulder shut that down. "What does that mean?"

"It means your blackout isn't some freak one-body malfunction." Yusuf zoomed in until numbers turned to colored blocks. "This hash is propagating through the shared calibration handshakes. Every time somebody syncs, the package copies across and then pretends it's the ordinary sensory update."

"How long?"

"Can't tell yet." He moved faster, calling up older logs. "Forty-eight hours for sure. Maybe more, but the audit summaries roll up after that and dump the full payload comparison unless someone manually preserves it."

There were forty-odd people. Kai pictured the track again, Priya at the curve and Novak folding his arms, every body in the building running on the same invisible thing.

"Same hash means same blackout?"

"No." Yusuf snapped that out and then moderated. "No, not automatically. It proves exposure. It proves the sensory layer is carrying the same corruption. It doesn't prove everyone gets your exact cascade." He pointed at another column. "Expression depends on load, subsystem mix, what the body is asking for when the fault tips over. Some people may get drift. Hand numbness. Audio drop. A bad turn in the pool. You were at full sprint on a curve when it went."

"Lucky me."

Yusuf had no answer to that. He opened one more record, then another, then swore under his breath.

"What?"

"The rollback counter isn't just there after calibration. It's climbing after ordinary telemetry pings." His hands went flat on the terminal. "It's not only copying during full sessions. The network handshake's enough."

Kai felt the cold spread out from that sentence. "That's a virus."

Yusuf didn't answer right away. He braced both hands on the terminal and stared at the logs like staring harder might make them contradict themselves. His glasses had slid down his nose. He still hadn't pushed them back up.

"It behaves like one," he said at last. "Self-replicating firmware exploit, that's the plainest name for it. I need a proper diagnostic dump before I put that in writing. But yes. It copies itself. It uses the shared handshake. And it already moved beyond your system."

The room went very quiet as Kai looked past Yusuf at the names on the screen: Novak, Priya, people he'd been on the track with an hour ago, people whose bodies were running on the same infrastructure that had just cut him out and kept going.

"Can you fix it?"

"I don't know yet." The professional smoothness was gone now. "First thing is containment. Nobody trains. Nobody calibrates. Nobody plugs into this network again until I've mapped the scope."

"Every athlete."

"Everyone who's been calibrated here. Which is everyone."

The door opened before either of them moved. Petra Engel came in still wearing track-side scrubs under her coat, grey hair pulled back too tight, fatigue written across her face in old familiar lines. A brown-red smear marked one cuff where she'd gotten Kai's blood on herself earlier and either hadn't noticed or hadn't had time to care.

"I was told you wanted me now," she said.

Yusuf turned from the screen. "Yes. Right now."

Her eyes went once to Kai, taking inventory: sling, splint, stitched brow, monitor leads, the towel under his knees. Then back to Yusuf. "What do you have?"

"Not a local failure." He pointed at the wall display. "Kai's crash dump shows a live sensory hash that doesn't match yesterday's approved install receipt. Same bad hash is sitting in multiple athlete records over at least forty-eight hours, and the rollback signature is moving through the shared handshakes."

Petra took two steps closer to the screen. "How certain are you?"

"Certain on the repeated hash. Certain on the mismatch between receipt and live payload. Probable on active propagation. I am not yet certain every exposed athlete is at immediate blackout risk."

"But immediate risk exists."

"Yes."

"Why was this not flagged by the system?"

"Because the summary report kept the approved label and stripped the underlying hash unless a session failed hard enough for manual review." Yusuf's voice sped up again. "Kai hitting the wall gave me raw data before the summaries rolled."

Petra looked at him for one beat longer. Then the decision happened in her face before it happened in her body.

"Can you isolate the calibration server without bricking anyone's implants?"

"Yes. Local control stays local. We lose live telemetry and remote diagnostics."

"Good. Lose them."

She crossed to the wall phone and hit the emergency key.

"Engel. Initiate restricted-operations protocol immediately." Her English flattened further when she was angry. "Close the track, pool, weight room, and coaching suites. Suspend all calibrations, remote diagnostics, and elective maintenance. No athlete ports used without medical authorization. Notify operations and unit leads that medical briefing is in ten minutes."

She listened for half a second, then cut across whatever answer came back.

"No, I am not waiting for the facility director to arrive. Start it now. You can wake him with the building already closed."

The line crackled. Petra's jaw tightened.

"All enhanced athletes to quarters or common areas under observation. No one trains. No one connects personal equipment. Security if necessary."

Another pause.

"Yes. Now."

She hung up and turned back to Yusuf.

"You go to the calibration bay. Pull complete handshake archives before the automated rollup erases more detail. Preserve every payload receipt and every live hash you can get. I want a preliminary risk table by noon if that is possible, and if it is not possible I want to know why."

"Right."

"Don't say right. Say clear."

"Clear."

The exchange landed harder than the raised voices would have. Yusuf nodded once and reached for the cable at the base of Kai's skull.

Kai caught his forearm with the good hand before he could unplug it.

"How long has it been in me?"

Yusuf looked down at Kai's hand on him, then back at Kai's face.

"I don't know yet."

"Say a range."

"At least since yesterday's calibration." The answer came clean because Petra was in the room and there was no space left for softening. Then a second sentence followed, smaller, truer. "Possibly longer. I need the older records before I can tell."

Kai let go.

The cable clicked free.

Petra stepped to the bedside while Yusuf gathered tablet and adapters. Her gaze went briefly to the chart at the foot of the bed.

"You will stay here today," she said. "Observation, imaging review, neuro checks. No attempts to stand without staff."

"Wasn't planning sprints."

Her mouth moved as if something almost like a smile had passed near it and kept going. "Good."

The intercom came alive overhead before anyone said more.

"Attention staff and athletes. Restricted-operations protocol is now in effect. All training spaces are closed until further notice. Athletes are to return immediately to quarters or designated common areas. Do not connect to any diagnostic or calibration interface. Await further medical instruction."

The announcement repeated in German a second later. Before the French version finished, the exam-room door banged hard enough to hit the stop.

Priya stood there in warm-up pants and spikes with a residence aide half a step behind her. One hand was jammed against the port under her hair as if holding herself back from touching it. She looked past Petra to Yusuf, not to Kai.

"Mine was this morning," she said. "Tell me if it's the same thing."

The aide beside her said, "She left the track before we could clear the lane."

Down the corridor another athlete shouted, "I'm not sitting in quarters till somebody checks me."

Yusuf had already half-turned with the cable in his hand. Petra took it from him before he got a step.

"No one plugs into anything," she said. "Not him. Not you. Observation wing, now."

Priya didn't move. "I need to know if it's in me."

"And I need that archive intact," Petra said. "If he connects you now, he learns less and risks more. You will wait under observation."

For a second Priya stayed where she was, hand still at the port, looking at Yusuf like she could make him choose with his face. He didn't. The aide touched her elbow. This time she let herself get turned back into the corridor.

The French announcement kept going over the top of it. By the time the last words cleared, the corridor outside had changed shape entirely. Running footsteps sounded in bursts. Doors opened. Someone farther down the hall was crying and trying not to make noise.

Yusuf had gone still with his empty hand half raised, his diagnosis now in other people's bodies whether he named it or not.

Petra heard it too and didn't turn her head. "Go."

That got him moving. He slid the tablet under his arm, missed the pocket for the cable on the first try, tried again, and headed for the door.

"Yusuf."

He stopped.

Kai hadn't planned the sentence before it came out. "If you find out who's next, don't tell them like you told me."

It was a meaner line than he'd intended. Pain did that. Fear did it more.

Yusuf took the hit without arguing. "No," he said. "I won't."

Then he was gone.

Petra stayed long enough to adjust the monitor clip that had gone crooked on Kai's finger and to read the wrist imaging results that had just appeared on the wall screen. The report said distal radius fracture, non-operative for now, follow-up scan tomorrow. She read quickly, lips tightening once, then signed the order set with a thumbprint.

The intercom kept working elsewhere in the building. Through the open door came fragments of the shutdown as it spread: a trainer telling someone to leave the treadmills alone; a swimmer asking if this was about infection control; a metal cart rattling too fast over tile; security shoes, heavier than the rest. Once, for half a second, Priya crossed the doorway again, moving toward the residence wing while the same staff member kept pace beside her. She didn't look in. He was glad of that.

Petra followed his line of sight anyway.

"They saw the crash," Kai said.

"Yes."

"That's going to help."

"No," Petra said. "It will not."

Nothing in her tone pretended otherwise.

She checked his pupils with a penlight, asked his name, the date, where he was. Asked whether sensation in both feet still felt intact. Asked whether he had lost time since arriving in medical.

"No more than usual," he said.

The line came out dry enough to count as a joke in another room. It didn't count in this one.

"If that changes, you tell us immediately," Petra said.

"Yeah."

Another burst of noise went up in the corridor. Someone was crying now, quietly and angrily, the way people cry when they are trying not to give the sound away. Petra listened for two seconds, said nothing about it, and closed the exam-room door most of the way.

She left a hand's width of corridor in the doorway, enough to block the bed from passing eyes while a nurse could still clock him from the station. Institutional compromise sat visible in the strip of light.

"A nurse will stay on this wing," she said. "If your legs begin firing again, do not try to pin them down with the other hand. Use the call button."

"You say that like it happens a lot."

"I say it because I don't know if it happens a lot."

She took the chart and moved to the door.

"Dr. Engel."

She turned back.

"Am I the worst one so far?"

The question put the first hesitation into her body all morning. It wasn't much. A fractional stop before the answer.

"You are the worst one we know about so far," she said.

Then she went out.

The room got quiet again, carrying the rest of the building inside it. Doors were closing across the facility. Training was being cancelled mid-rep. Athletes were walking back to quarters with their bodies still attached to a network they had just been told not to trust. Somewhere down the hall, Yusuf was running toward the calibration bay before the logs disappeared into summary.

Kai looked down the length of the exam table. The sheet over his legs rose in two low ridges. Both feet pointed where they were supposed to point.

They stayed still.

He watched long enough for stillness to stop meaning safety and start meaning only this second, and this second, and this second after that.

The call button sat clipped to the rail by his good hand. It was plastic, off-white, ordinary.

He pressed it.

When the speaker answered, he said, "Could someone stay in here a bit?"

The reply came at once. "Yes. Someone's coming."

Footsteps passed in the corridor. More footsteps followed. A cart rattled by. A door slammed somewhere down the hall. The building had not settled yet and wasn't going to.

Kai kept his eyes on his feet and waited for the next movement to be his.
