# Chapter 10

"What lap is this?"

Her voice came up through the audio pickup, flat and tinny in the tile room. One hand was on the gutter and the other on the lane rope. Water slid off her cap and down her face. Her mouth stayed open between breaths. She stared at the opposite wall like the room might sort itself out if she waited long enough.

Margot had already disconnected. The feed was gone. Warm water, loaded muscle, Lena's heart hammering up under her sternum. Then dry glass, dry air, her own pulse shrinking back to size. The skin under the interface stood up red and ridged when she tore the strap loose. Below the observation deck Lena looked like somebody dropped into the wrong part of the morning.

Before eight, the deck console ran local. Anything serious enough to finish its countdown would queue for central routing and fire the moment the campus network came online. The console in front of Margot still held two red alerts in its history bar, each one stamped with her own wrist-unit override.

By the time Lena hauled herself out and sat on the edge with both feet in the water, Margot had the session log open. The paired device had mirrored the whole set by default: raw feed depth, heart-rate stream, pace commands, alert dismissals, local timestamps. She changed the auto-label on both alerts from sustained HR anomaly to coach-authorized build. She reclassified the set from aerobic to mixed-intensity. She added a note field: post-set disorientation after middle-build effort, resolved poolside. Then she switched the export to summary sync only.

Summary sync kept the official record thin. Splits. Heart-rate curve. Final set classification. Coach note. It stripped out the raw feed, the countdown history, and the command log that showed exactly when she had killed medical escalation. Anyone checking later would see a hard middle split and a messy recovery. Anyone wanting the ugly version would need her wrist unit and the local cache still sitting on it.

"Coach."

Margot looked down.

Lena had one hand clamped to the port at the back of her neck. Her face had not come all the way back yet.

"What happened?"

Margot kept her eyes on the console. "You built the middle too hard and came up spun. Give it a minute."

Lena frowned. "I don't remember the end."

"You finished the set." Margot shut the log. "Shower first. We'll look at it after breakfast."

This time Lena just stared at her. Margot picked up her bag before the stare had time to become a second question.

The corridor to residential took four minutes. Linoleum, fluorescent panels, antiseptic in the vents, stale coffee from the cafeteria wing. A sprinter she did not know was stretching against the wall, calves marked with faint graft seams. They nodded at each other and kept moving.

In her room, Margot sat on the bed and took the interface back out. It was brushed steel, the connection pads still warm from her skin. Four years of use had darkened the edges with oils and pulse sweat. The memory light held solid amber. Unsynced local cache.

She woke the screen.

Session buffer: 07:12.

Raw sensory packets retained.

Local action history retained.

Export profile: summary sync pending dock.

The purge option sat in the lower corner. Two taps, thumbprint confirm, and the raw feed would be gone before anyone in medical knew there had ever been more to ask for. The summary would still post when she docked the unit. Petra would have the clean story and nothing else.

Margot opened the action history instead.

Standard aerobic set loaded. Pace target 1:04. First hundred 1:03.8. Second 1:04.1. Six fifty-two and thirteen seconds: pace target lowered to 1:00. Six fifty-two and forty-one: pace target lowered to 0:58. Six fifty-three and twenty-two: Class III page armed. Six fifty-three and twenty-four: page dismissed by coach unit. Six fifty-four and nine: second red alert issued. Six fifty-four and eleven: alert reclassified.

The device kept the order plain and ugly and exact. She did not need the replay to remember why each line was there.

At the start of the third hundred, the little choices had gone out of Lena. Usually there was a fraction more pressure on the left palm, a breath held a beat too long, a turn adjusting for what the last stroke had done. Then that texture vanished. The hand went in. The body pulled. It kicked. It breathed. Everything landed on time and all of it was dead flat.

Earlier sessions had carried a blink of this. Half a second where the stroke lost its texture and came back. Margot had logged drift and kept going.

This wasn't drift.

She opened the buffer.

The replay came back with no mercy. Through the feed Lena's body felt wrong in three places at once: lungs pulling air with no panic in them, legs turning over without fatigue arguing back, arms catching exactly the same pressure every cycle as if pain had been cut out of the lane. From the deck the stroke had still looked clean. Inside the feed there had been nobody there making the small corrections that made it Lena.

Margot watched herself lower the target to one minute and felt the answer come back before any trace of assent or resistance. She watched herself do it again to fifty-eight. Lena hit the three-hundred wall hard enough that the impact rang through Margot's teeth and fired off it with both legs at once. Heart rate 178. 182. Yellow warning. Cleared. 188. 190. Red box. Sixty-second countdown. Margot killed it at fifty-eight because the feed was still giving and she wanted one more wall.

The missing stretch ran hot and blank. The whole body was working. None of the person was in it. That clean absence hit Margot in the jaw and shoulders like a drug finding an old vein. She cut the replay after three strokes and still had it in her mouth.

Her hands shook hard enough that she nearly dropped the unit. She sat there breathing through her mouth until the room steadied. The purge icon stayed in the corner, patient, unchanged.

The order should have been Petra first, then Yusuf. A Class III-equivalent event meant raw data and a grounded athlete. Petra would not stop at the summary if Lena reported missing time. She would ask for the unit. She would pull the cache herself or hand it to Yusuf and tell him to do it. Then she would get everything: the pace jumps, both canceled countdowns, the relabeled alerts, the summary-only export, the lie at poolside, the raw segment where Lena's body kept swimming after Lena was gone.

Margot set the unit on her thigh and looked at it the way other people looked at a bottle they had already opened.

She could tell herself she was waiting for more information. She could tell herself she did not want Petra yanking Lena out of the water on the basis of one ugly session during a week when everyone at Kirchberg was already half-convinced the virus lived in every ache and skipped beat. She could tell herself imaging first thing tomorrow would change nothing except panic.

The cleaner sentence sat under all of it.

If Petra saw the buffer, Petra would take the unit.

Margot put the interface in the nightstand drawer and shut it.

She wanted Lena safe. She wanted the feed more. That was the order it sat in her body.

On the wall display, her heart rate had come down to 84. Her respiration was normal. Enhancement status read none. That line stayed blank. It always would. She had gone to Brisbane in 2028 in an unenhanced body and finished fifth in the 200 fly. Now she sat on a narrow bed in Kirchberg with her own lungs working at an ordinary human rate and a sour ache climbing the back of her throat because ordinary was not enough.

The room stayed bright. She lay back anyway. Ten minutes after disconnect, her legs still gave a small useless tremor now and then, as if they expected another wall. The empty part had started already. It always started fast.

She lasted six minutes before opening the drawer again.

This time she did not reopen the replay. She went back to the action history and read the pace changes until the numbers stopped looking like numbers and turned back into touch: the clean blank stroke, the hard wall, the countdown she had killed with her thumb. She could not tell whether that made her feel better or worse.

There were people she could call. Petra, who would go cold and formal and start protecting Lena in ways that would also ruin the training block. Yusuf, who would ask for the device before he asked how she was. Lena, who would hear the lie in Margot's voice before the first sentence was over. Margot did not call anyone.

By eight the campus network would wake fully and the pool schedule would fill with sanctioned movement. Coaches would have clipboards. Athletes would have breakfast in them. Somebody would make a joke about altitude and somebody else would nod because the building ran on people agreeing to the explanation that cost the least.

Margot slid the unit back into the drawer and forced herself to leave it there.

When she finally shut her eyes, the thing she felt most clearly was not guilt. It was hunger with nowhere to go.

---

Lena spent the afternoon trying to find the missing laps.

She started with her body because the body had done them whether she remembered them or not.

After the shower she sat on the closed toilet lid in her room with a towel around her shoulders and checked herself the way Petra checked injured athletes. Skin first. No split knuckles. No blood in the whites of the eyes. Then muscle. Her lats were blown out the way they got after a race-pace back half, not after an aerobic four hundred. The tendons at the front of both shoulders ached from hard wall contact. There was a raw spot on the inside of her lower lip where she had bitten through the turn and not felt it until later. None of that matched what she remembered doing.

At her desk, she pulled up the session telemetry Margot had filed that morning. The screen gave stroke rate, splits, heart rate, and neural link activity. The graphs ran clean from start to finish. The second half showed a moderate rise. The summary field carried no desync marker. The interruption flag read none.

There was a coach note.

Post-set disorientation after middle-build effort, resolved poolside.

Lena read it twice. It matched what Margot had told her at the pool. Hard middle. Disoriented at the wall. Nothing else. She remembered the lane rope under her hand, the chlorine taste too sharp in the back of her throat, her lungs trying to climb out through her mouth, and her own voice asking what lap it was because the question had arrived before the rest of her did.

The record said she had swum four hundred meters.

Lena remembered two hundred and fifty.

She remembered the warm-up, the first hundred, the second turn, the beginning of the third, and the flip at 250.

Then the lane rope was under her hand. Her chest tore for air. The pool lights were too bright. The set was over.

The telemetry said her body had done the rest. Her arms had pulled. Her legs had kicked. The walls had registered contact. Her heart rate had gone into the 190s. The split graph showed a sudden hard middle build she had never been assigned. All of it was real. None of it had been experienced.

Smaller slips had happened before. Twenty minutes gone in her room while a page sat open in front of her. A turn pattern last season's firmware chose for her. Margot had called those within acceptable parameters, and Lena had accepted it because Margot had the data and the authority, and because the program had trained her early to trust whatever could be graphed.

This morning was not that.

She touched the port at the base of her skull. The ceramic edge sat smooth under her thumb, skin-warm, built into her like a joint or a tooth. She had had it since she was four. She pressed harder, as if pressure might give her a clearer answer than the screen did.

The Kirchberg desync protocol sat three menus down. She pulled it up and read the relevant lines again. Class II: intermittent divergence under five seconds. Report within twenty-four hours. Class III: sustained divergence, loss of voluntary motor control, or loss of conscious awareness during link-directed activity. Report immediately. Ground the athlete.

Three minutes was not Class II.

From the protocol page she could open the athlete report form. The button sat there the way an emergency exit sits there, obvious and ugly and hard to use without changing the rest of the day. Lena looked at it, then closed the menu and opened the telemetry again.

The summary view gave her numbers and a story about the numbers. Heart-rate spike. Coach-authorized build. Post-set disorientation resolved. Missing time had nowhere to attach itself on the page.

She checked the splits against the training plan Margot had loaded before dawn. The first two hundreds matched. The back half did not. The set was supposed to sit at 1:04 pace. The graph showed the third hundred tightening, then the last hundred breaking under the minute mark before the recovery at the wall. Margot had changed the target in the water. The entry offered no reason.

Lena stared at that for a long time.

The clean explanation would have been that Margot had seen something and tested it. Coaches did that. They changed sets in real time all the time.

The less clean explanation was that Margot had felt something wrong and pushed harder anyway.

Lena saved the session locally, shut the wall screen, turned it back on, and opened it again as if the numbers might have altered in the dark.

They had not.

At twelve thirty she went to the cafeteria and stood with a tray she could not make herself fill. The room smelled of coffee, eggs, and bleach from the morning mop. Two cyclists were arguing softly in French over a lactate chart. Someone dropped cutlery at the far end and nobody looked up. Ordinary noise kept happening around her with the insulting confidence of things that had not lost any time.

She took yogurt, fruit, black bread. She ate half the bread and none of the fruit. Her jaw worked. Her stomach accepted the food without opinion. When she looked down at her own hands on the table, they felt temporarily assigned to her.

By one fifteen she was back in her room with the protocol open again.

The report form asked for the same things it always asked for. Time. Session type. Subjective symptoms. Whether the athlete had retained voluntary motor control. Whether conscious awareness had been continuous. Whether the coach had already filed. Whether medical had been informed.

Lena did not fill it yet. She wanted to hear Margot say the morning out loud first. Not because Margot was more likely to tell the truth if asked directly. Because Lena needed to see what Margot would do when the missing time was no longer a number on a screen but something Lena put in front of her face.

She found Margot outside the coaching suite later that afternoon. Margot had coffee in one hand and her coaching bag over her shoulder. Her hair was down. She looked tired. The quick coach scan happened as soon as Lena rounded the corner, automatic and clinical.

"Hey," Margot said.

"Can I ask about this morning?"

"Sure."

A technician pushed a cart past them and they both stepped aside. Wheels squeaking. Ventilation humming through the wall.

"I checked the session summary," Lena said. "It says mixed-intensity build."

"Because your middle pace climbed."

"I didn't choose that."

"No," Margot said. "You didn't."

Lena waited.

"You changed it," she said.

"I adjusted the set."

"After I stopped remembering."

Margot shifted the coffee cup to her other hand. "After your stroke went flat."

"Flat is not my question."

"It's part of it."

"My question is why you changed the target when something was already wrong."

Margot looked down the hall once, then back at Lena. "I saw drift. I wanted to see whether load would pull you through it."

Lena felt the words arrive in her body a beat before the meaning did.

"You felt me go wrong and pushed harder."

"I changed the pace."

"Until the last hundred went under a minute."

"Yes."

"You were on the feed."

"Yes."

"So you knew I wasn't choosing any of it."

Margot's jaw moved. "I knew you weren't swimming like yourself."

"That is not what I asked."

"It's what I've got."

Lena kept her hands at her sides because if she folded her arms now it would look too much like holding herself together.

"When I got out," she said, "I asked what happened."

"You were disoriented."

"I asked what happened."

Margot started to answer, then didn't.

"You told me I'd built the middle too hard," Lena said. "You said I came up spun."

"I told you what the summary was going to show."

Lena stared at her. That was the first thing Margot had said all afternoon that sounded close enough to truth to hurt.

"Then why isn't it Class III?" Lena asked.

"Because the summary won't carry Class III."

"The protocol says loss of conscious awareness."

"I know."

"Then why did you file against it?"

Margot rubbed her thumb once over the cardboard seam of the cup. "Because if awareness loss goes in against a clean summary, Petra grounds you before dinner. That's automatic."

"She should."

"Maybe."

Lena waited.

"Then it keeps going," Margot said. "Imaging. Link review. Admin. Selection. Meridian if they want the clearance file."

"They don't read it as my coach believed me."

"They read it as the graph stayed clean and the athlete said something else."

"Because you made the graph clean."

"I filed the official summary."

"Stop changing the words."

A door opened somewhere behind them, then shut again.

"Did I have a severe event or not?" Lena asked.

Margot took too long.

"I had missing time," Lena said. "I had no control of the last part of that set. You felt that."

"I felt the stroke go blank. I felt you stop adjusting," Margot said. "Then the heart rate went red."

"And you kept sending pace."

A pause. "Yes."

The corridor seemed narrower after that.

"So this is not about whether it happened," Lena said. "It's about whether I'm allowed to say it happened."

Margot's face gave almost nothing back. "It's about what happens to you when you say it without proof the system accepts."

Lena breathed in through her nose, slow enough that the breath shook anyway.

"And what happens to you."

Margot's eyes came back to her face. "It turns into a whole lot more than you and me."

"You want me to leave it alone."

"I want you not to walk into a hold and a review on one bad morning."

"Bad because of who?"

Margot said nothing.

"Do you want me to flag it now?" Margot asked after a beat. "I'll do it if that's what you want, but once it moves, you don't pull it back."

Lena thought about the calibration rig, Yusuf's equipment mapping her link while she lay still inside it. She thought about a physician asking her to describe three missing minutes while the official graph on the wall stayed clean. She thought about a selection note carrying the mismatch forward into a season with four months left before the Games. She thought about the way the program had trained her since childhood to understand that the body could panic, misremember, dramatize, and lie, while a recorded system error was a fact.

"I don't know," she said.

Margot nodded once.

"Then wait until you know."

"That helps you."

"It might help you too."

Lena could not tell which part of that sentence was the lie.

"I'll think about it," she said.

"OK."

Margot took another drink, adjusted the strap of her bag, and moved past her with no hurry in it.

The quarters were quiet when Lena got back. Her wall display still glowed green. Heart rate 68. Neural link status nominal.

She sat on the bed and opened the report form.

Time of incident.

Session type.

Symptom description.

Loss of voluntary motor control: yes or no.

Loss of conscious awareness: yes or no.

Supporting telemetry attached automatically from official session record.

She selected yes.

A yellow banner appeared across the top of the form.

Class III submission triggers immediate suspension of training clearance pending physician review.

Below it, the attached summary populated on its own.

Session classification: mixed-intensity.

Coach note: post-set disorientation after middle-build effort, resolved poolside.

Objective severe-event markers: none in attached summary.

The cursor waited in the free-text box.

Lena typed: Missing approximately 150m of conscious experience during link-directed swim set. No memory after 250m turn. Awareness resumed at wall with severe respiratory distress and disorientation.

She read it back. The sentence was accurate. It looked insane sitting beside the clean summary.

She imagined Petra opening the file and doing exactly what a physician should do. Grounding her. Imaging her. Pulling her from the water. Then the screens after that. Selection staff. Institute admin. Meridian compliance. People she would never meet reading her sentence beside the attached summary and stamping the mismatch with the words institutions used when a person said something the graph did not.

Uncorroborated.

Subjective.

Athlete-reported.

When she was ten, a developmental coach had put a hand on her shoulder and told her not to promote sensation into fact. Wait for the data. She could still feel the weight of the hand.

She moved the cursor back to the box and added a second sentence.

Coach changed pace target during missing interval.

She stopped there.

If she submitted that line, Petra would ask for the raw session. If the raw session still existed, Margot was finished. If it did not, the yellow warning and the attached summary would be all that remained, and Lena's report would sit under them by itself.

Her thumb found the edge of the port at the base of her skull.

The status bar pulsed green.

She closed the form without submitting it.

The system asked if she wanted to save as draft.

No.

The form disappeared. Nominal stayed lit on the green status bar.

She left the session unflagged.

Her thumb stayed pressed to the port until the skin went sore.
By the time the outdoor track lit up outside her window, the wall display had turned the glass into a mirror. The green bar kept pulsing.
