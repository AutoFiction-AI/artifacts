Eszter let that sit. Then she leaned forward, elbows on knees, the blanket slipping so he could see the marks his fingers had left on her thigh.

"Lili's father wasn't older," she said. "He wasn't glamorous either, before you imagine some idiot with a leather jacket and sad guitar. He stocked shelves in a builders' merchant and knew every football result from three countries. He could make a baby laugh by sneezing on purpose. For a while that seemed enough."

Beni listened. The light outside the window had gone from white to iron grey.

"Then he hurt his back," she said. "Not badly enough for sympathy, but badly enough for anger. He was home more and worked less. Then there was betting, then borrowing, then little lies about what he'd spent. The usual shit." She shrugged. "You can live through a lot of usual shit. Women do it every day."

She picked a loose thread on the blanket and wound it around one finger until the tip blanched.

"The part that made me leave wasn't the first shout," she said. "Not even the first time he took money from the flour tin. It was one evening in winter. I'd hidden cash for Lili's nursery fees inside an old sanitary-pad box because he never looked there. He came home half drunk and went through the kitchen anyway. Flour on the floor, tins open, the whole place turned over. Lili woke and came in crying. He told her to shut up. She wouldn't. He grabbed her by the arm and moved her aside like furniture."

Her voice stayed steady. That steadiness did more than tears would have.

"He didn't beat her," she said. "People start sorting it straight away so they can sleep at night. Bruised, not bruised. Police, no police. Good man, bad man. He left marks for two days. I was still brushing flour off her sleeve and I knew if I stayed he'd have a new reason ready next time. Money. Drink. Shame. Being tired."

Beni looked at the crescent scar by her thumb again and understood it in the company of flour dust and a child crying in a kitchen that should have been safe.

"So I left," she said. "I didn't leave elegantly. My mother took Lili for what was meant to be three weeks. I came to London because a cousin knew a woman who knew a cleaning agency and because I had two hundred pounds and no better miracle. Three weeks became months. Then schools, then rent, then papers, then the stupid fact that once a child settles somewhere you feel like a criminal for moving her again. So now I clean rich people's baths and post hair ties home in other people's boots."

She laughed once, without humor. "That's the romance."

"Do you still talk to him?" Beni asked.

"Only about Lili, and only when I have to." She lifted one shoulder. "He behaves better now. Distance makes some men look almost decent. It doesn't make me stupid."

He reached out and touched the thread she had wound too tight around her finger. She let him unwind it.

"Why are you telling me this?" he asked.

"Because you told me the ugly version and not the souvenir one." She shifted closer. "Because if I'm in your bed and half your history is in the room anyway, I'd rather know which half."

Half your history, she had said. The words landed with a force out of proportion to their volume. They were only half, not all, and the missing part seemed suddenly lit from behind.

He hated how quickly the answer came, smooth from use. "Probably."

She watched him another beat. "You don't have to answer."

"I know."

The question landed with care. She had narrowed it enough to be merciful and left it wide enough to matter.

He should have told her then. The dark window, the shopping still unpacked, the smell of them, the lighter in his hand like evidence from an older case: everything around him leaned toward truth. He thought of Nina's mouth in the garage, Nina's money folded into envelopes, Nina telling him he was safe because he never asked for anything. He thought of the phone bright in his pocket at the grocer, the name turned down against his thigh while Eszter bought wafers for her daughter.

She sliced sausage and spread pot cheese with the blunt knife that bent if you asked it for honesty. Now and then his wrist brushed hers and she did not pull away, but she did not lean into him either. Once she caught herself thumbing one of Lili's pink elastics and slid the card straight back into the bag.

They ate on the bed because there was nowhere else, but not cross-legged and tangled now. Eszter sat with one boot already laced, the other on the floor, talking in shorter pieces about Lili refusing to wear wool tights unless they were turned inside out because she hated the seam on her toes, about a teacher who kept sending notes home about handwriting as if better loops on the page would make the heating work. He told her about the village shop where old men had once made him buy cigarettes one by one because they liked watching boys pretend not to cough. The talk kept the room from going dead. By six she was already in her coat, bag repacked, Monday back in the room before she opened the door. The Ashdowns would want breakfasts and ironed shirts and the house smelling like money again by morning. When she bent to lace the second boot, he watched the muscles in her forearms move under the skin and thought again of how much of her life required lifting other people's weight.

He reached for her. She gave him one quick kiss, warm but already leaving, then kept her palm flat on his chest when he started to follow her into a second.

"Yeah?"

"Don't make me stupid."

She laid it down as terms.

He sat on the edge of the bed with the phone in one hand and the silver lighter in the other. The little table beside him was bare now.
