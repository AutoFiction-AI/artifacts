# Chapter 7

At six forty-two the mews was still more night than morning. The strip of light under the garage door looked grey enough to wipe with a thumb. Beni had the rear door of the Mercedes open and one knee on the concrete, his good trousers darkening at the knee while he worked a towel into the seat seam. The leather had gone cold again. It still gave off stale perfume under the citrus spray from the valet kit, and every time he pressed harder the memory came back through his hands before it reached his head: Nina bent against the far door, one heel scraping the mat, his belt catching on the buckle, the windows turned white with breath so thick he had not been able to see the brick wall on the other side of the garage.

He had gone home for one hour and twelve minutes, which had been long enough to peel off the shirt that smelled of her mouth and the garage dust and long enough to stand under Laci's coughing shower while the pipe knocked in the wall, but not long enough to sleep. His body had the ugly looseness that came after not enough rest and too much adrenaline, a false softness that made him clumsy. He had tied his tie twice and still had not got the knot right. Now the tie hung inside his jacket pocket while he scrubbed at what the first wipe had missed: one cloudy thread of himself dried into the stitch line of the seat, a smear near the seat belt latch, glitter from Nina's face powder caught in the crease like pale grit.

There was a torn foil packet in the side pocket and one of her earrings on the floor mat, a small gold hoop with a pearl hanging from it. He had set the earring on the center console beside a folded receipt from the gallery caterer and a hair tie that was not hers. He did not know why he had kept lining things up instead of throwing them away. It felt like work if the objects stood in a row. It felt like less than what it was.

Bea would be in the back seat in less than two hours if Nina did not change the morning. That thought kept his hand moving when the spray smell made his stomach tilt. He cleaned for the girl as much as for Nina. He cleaned because the child would sling her school bag onto the same leather and complain about traffic and not know she was leaning against the place where her mother had bitten his shoulder through the shirt.

The roller door rattled once, then lifted halfway. Nina ducked under it before it had finished rising, one hand already on the hem of her camel coat so the metal would not brush it. She had a pair of dark glasses in her hair although there was no sun. Her face was washed clean and expensive-looking, pale around the mouth from a night that had not ended the way she had wanted. She had put herself back together so completely that the only proof of anything was the missing earring and the ladder in one stocking, visible where the coat opened over her knee.

She looked first at the console. "Thank God."

Beni stood up too quickly and felt the blood leave his head. He took the earring and held it out. "You dropped it."

She put it into her palm and shut her fingers around it in a quick protective fist. "It was my grandmother's," she said. "Or rather that's the family version. The true version is that my mother bought it at Sotheby's because my grandmother had much worse taste than anyone likes to admit."

It was the kind of thing Nina said when she wanted to sound amused instead of exposed. Beni watched her slide the hoop back through her ear with practiced fingers. She did not ask whether he had slept. She did not look at the inside of the car for more than a second.

"Is it dry?" she asked.

"Nearly."

"It needs to be dry. Bea has school at eight fifteen."

"It will be."

Nina nodded, already elsewhere. She reached into the inside pocket of her coat and took out a cream envelope, unsealed, the flap tucked in. "You've got Bea, then Petersham Cleaners, then the pharmacy on Elizabeth Street. I need the blue dress from Sloane before lunch, the navy one altered if they've finished ruining it, and stay reachable this afternoon because I may need you again."

She held out the envelope. He did not move.

"Take it, Beni."

He dried his hand on the towel before he took it. The paper was warm from her body.

"For the extra hours," she said. Then, because he was still looking at the envelope instead of pocketing it: "And because I hate untidy accounts."

He looked up. She had said it lightly, almost kindly, and that was worse than if she had made it vulgar. The garage smelled of the citrus spray and the petrol seep from somewhere under the wall and her perfume buried beneath both. It was hard to believe the night had contained anything frantic at all.

"Mrs. Ashdown," he said.

She made a face. "Don't do that."

He stopped.

"Not in here," she said. "Not after you've had your hand up my skirt. It turns us both into idiots."

The words were quiet. She was not angry, and it was not flirtation either. It was just an adjustment, as cool as changing the radio station because she did not like the song. She stepped closer and straightened the tie hanging half out of his jacket pocket, not bothering to ask. Her knuckles brushed the sore place on his chest where she had gripped him in the dark.

"Be normal in the house," she said. "Please. I'm not asking for a tragedy in a service corridor."

"I wasn't going to."

"Good." She glanced past him into the car at the damp patch darkening as it dried. "Last night was ugly enough before it improved. We don't need to turn the morning into theater."

He could have said no to the envelope before she touched him. He could not say no after the list of errands, the school run, the easy assumption that of course he would still be there, of course he would fold whatever the night had been into the day because that was what he was good for. He slipped the envelope inside his jacket.

Nina's mouth softened for one second, almost pity, almost triumph. "Thank you," she said. "And Beni. Keep the windows cracked for a bit. You went mad with the spray."

Then she was gone, heels clicking away through the mews toward the house. The roller door stayed open behind her. Cold air rolled in over the concrete and under the car, and Beni stood with the towel in his hand until the smell of her thinned enough for the citrus to win.

He finished the seat, though he no longer trusted it. The leather took the cloth and gave nothing back. By seven ten he had thrown away the foil packet in the outside bin, folded the towel into a black rubbish bag, and driven the Mercedes from the garage round to the front of the townhouse. The house had already begun its daily split. The upstairs windows held yellow lamp light and money. The basement steps were wet from a bucket someone had sloshed too quickly, and the smell rising from below was coffee, onion, bleach, toast. It was a machine that ran on polished quiet above ground and heat and wet cloth below.

He sat behind the wheel with both front windows open a finger's width and finally looked inside the envelope.

There were seven fifty-pound notes inside, new and stiff at the corners.

He counted them twice because his hands did not believe the first count. The total was three hundred and fifty, more than the night's work and the morning errands justified and less than enough to change anything. Laci's face came into his mind with automatic precision, the number the same each time: nine hundred. Three hundred and fifty meant Laci would stop with the muttered comments for maybe two weeks if Beni handed it over untouched. It meant he could buy groceries without calculating to the pound. It meant Nina had put a price on the part that had not been in the job description and had chosen a number large enough that refusing it would feel like vanity.

He folded the notes once, slid them back into the envelope, and tucked it into his inside pocket. It made the jacket sit thick and awkward against his ribs when he bent.

At eight twelve Bea came down the front steps in her navy blazer and grey skirt with her tie hanging loose and one of her loafers unlaced. She carried a cello case bigger than her patience and had black ink on the side of her right thumb, a crescent stain she kept scraping with the nail of her other hand, trying to get it off before the school gate. The case banged the door frame.

"Morning," Beni said.

"Is it?" she said, and got in without waiting for him to open the door because she never did if she was annoyed with the existence of school.

When he pulled away she sniffed once, then leaned forward between the seats.

"Christ. It smells like a dental surgery in here."

"Sorry."

She dropped back. "Not to me. To the car."

That was all. She put one earbud in and left the other dangling, looking out at the wet square with the expression she used for people who had personally arranged her timetable. At the red light on Eaton Terrace she bent to retie the loose shoe and muttered, not really to him, "If Mum buys one more arrangement of white lilies the whole house is going to smell like a funeral home."

It was a teenager's complaint, impatient and exact, and it hit him harder than if she had asked questions. He kept his eyes on the road and took the turn too neatly, putting extra care into the wheel because there was nowhere else to put it.

The morning packed itself with tasks. He dropped Bea at school and circled back west. He waited outside Petersham Cleaners behind a man in a waxed coat arguing about cashmere, picked up Nina's dress bag, long and uselessly heavy in tissue and plastic, and stood in a pharmacy that smelled of eucalyptus and money while the woman at the counter said Mrs. Ashdown's name the way receptionists said the names of women who never waited alone. He took the cream and the little white box the pharmacist added in a paper bag and did not look at the label.

He was good at this part. If the work stayed broken into destinations, tickets, timings, and the pressure of the steering wheel under his palms, he could move through it without letting the pieces touch. There was a route between Belgravia and Sloane Street that avoided the worst delivery lorries after nine. There was a place on Elizabeth Street where the wardens watched less closely if he stopped under the tree with the split trunk. There was a sequence for carrying expensive clothes without letting the plastic catch in the rain. There was even a way to hold the pharmacy bag away from the dress bag so the cream could not leak onto the silk if some idiot had failed to close the lid.

What there was not was a clean route between the woman upstairs, who had told him not to make a theater of it, and the woman in the basement kitchen, who had kissed him on a stairwell landing with onion on her fingers and no witness at all. Driving did not help with that. Every time he stopped at a light his mind tried to put both women in the same house at the same hour and failed like an overloaded fuse.

By a quarter past eleven he was back at the mews entrance with the dress bag on one arm and the pharmacy packet hooked in two fingers. Rain had started properly, not a mist now but a fine mean drizzle that found the back of his neck between collar and hairline. He took the service stairs down first because the kitchen was warmer and because he wanted five minutes before he went upstairs with Nina's things. He told himself he wanted coffee.

Eszter was at the stainless counter with her sleeves pushed up, scraping burnt milk skin out of a saucepan with the back of a spoon. Her hair was tied with a blue elastic that had stretched thin enough to show white lines in it. Someone had left a voice note playing low from her phone near the kettle: a child's voice in Hungarian, bright and indignant about a lost glove, then a cough, then Eszter's mother's slower answer from farther away. Eszter hit pause with the heel of her hand when she heard the door.

"There you are," she said. "The man of the hour. You look like shit."

Her tone was ordinary, almost cheerful, but it landed in him where the morning had not left room. He set the bags on the table more carefully than the objects deserved.

"Thanks," he said.

"You want coffee or you want to stand there growing mould?"

"Coffee."

She pointed with the spoon. "Then take the pot. My hands are disgusting."

He poured from the metal coffee pot into a chipped mug she always gave him because the handle had been glued twice and she did not trust anyone else not to finish the job of breaking it. The kitchen was damp-warm from boiling things, a good heat that got into his face and made the lack of sleep obvious. Eszter looked at him once, properly, and her expression shifted by a fraction.

"Long night?" she asked.

"Yes."

"Gallery people again?"

"And errands this morning."

"Mm."

The sound held more attention than agreement. She rinsed the spoon, wiped the counter with one swipe of a cloth, then moved the bowl of chopped parsley three inches to the left, the kind of needless correction that meant the room was getting on her nerves. Beni knew it. He knew it well enough now that seeing it should have warned him to stop talking like a route sheet.

Instead he lifted the mug and burned his tongue because he had forgotten to wait.

Eszter looked at the dress bag, then the pharmacy packet. "She has you shopping now."

"Today, yes."

"Lucky you."

He might have smiled another day. This morning the shape would not come. He drank again, slower.

The kitchen door swung open behind him. Nina came in without a coat now, only a pale cashmere sweater and dark trousers, barefoot in backless slippers that whispered on the tiles. She had put the missing earring back in. Her face held no trace of the garage except a little dryness at the corner of her mouth where she had bitten the lipstick off in the night. She glanced first at the bag on the table, then at Eszter, then at Beni, counting the room for hazards.

"Did you get the hem fixed?" she asked.

"They said two o'clock."

"Of course they did." She lifted the pharmacy packet and peered in. "And the cream?"

"In there."

"Good."

She did not thank him. She rarely did for ordinary work. Instead she set the paper bag down, reached past him for the dress bag, and in the same movement smoothed two fingers over the front of his jacket where a drop of rain had darkened the wool. The gesture lasted half a second. It was nothing if you had not seen what her hands did when the garage door was down. It was too much if you had.

"Keep your phone on," she said. "I may send you out again after lunch."

"Yes."

"And open the car up before you collect Bea. It still smells appalling."

Eszter's eyes moved, very slightly, from Nina's face to his and back. Nina must have felt it or decided she had said enough. She gave Eszter the kind of smile employers used when they wanted to appear intimate with staff while keeping every rung of the ladder intact.

"Could we have the lilies taken out of the front hall?" she said. "They've turned the place into a chapel."

"I'll do it."

"Thank you."

Nina lifted the pharmacy bag again and left. The slippered whisper faded up the service stairs.

For a few seconds the kitchen held only the kettle ticking on the hob and the slow tap from the leaky pipe above the sink. Eszter took the milk pan, rinsed it once, then set it back down and dried her hands on a tea towel. She did not look at him while she spoke.

"What happened?"

He should have had something ready by then, a human sentence with weather or traffic in it, something that sounded like a person talking to a woman he wanted rather than an employee answering a question in a hallway. What came out of him was the first available shape.

"Nothing happened. It was just late."

Eszter looked up. "That wasn't what I asked."

"The gallery ran late. She was in a mood."

"She's always in a mood."

"I know."

"Then try again."

He put the mug down. He could feel the damp of the envelope through the lining of his jacket where the rain had reached it, a paper softness over the notes. He had forgotten he still had it until her gaze dropped to his chest pocket.

"What is that?" she asked.

"Cash."

"I can see that."

"For the hours."

She laughed once, no amusement in it. "You sound like a payroll form."

The words stung because they were true. He was still answering like he was protecting a client, not speaking to her. He reached into the pocket, took out the envelope because hiding it suddenly looked guiltier than showing it, and laid it on the table. He kept one finger over the flap out of reflex.

"She paid extra," he said.

Eszter wiped the counter again although it was already clean. "For what?"

"For keeping me late."

"Until when?"

"Late."

"Jesus Christ, Beni." She threw the cloth into the sink. "Do you hear yourself?"

He did hear it. He heard the defensive flatness, the way each answer got smaller and dumber the more he trimmed it. The trouble was that the fuller version did not exist in any language he could use here. There was the truth upstairs in Nina's hands and the not-truth he needed downstairs with Eszter, and between them nothing that felt human enough to say aloud.

"She needed driving," he said. "Then errands today. That's all."

Eszter watched him for a moment so long he had to stop himself from filling it. Her eyes were dark and level and not kind in the lazy way some people called kindness when they meant softness. When she was still with him like this it felt worse than shouting.

"No," she said at last. "That isn't all."

"It is."

"Bullshit."

He looked past her shoulder at the row of labeled jars by the wall: flour, rice, lentils, pasta. They were ordinary words, safe ones. Eszter followed his glance and got angrier, not louder.

"Don't do that cupboard-face thing with me," she said. "I'm not one of her chairs."

The pronoun hung in the room between them, more dangerous because she had not named Nina. Beni felt heat go up the back of his neck despite the damp shirt cooling under his jacket.

"I'm tired," he said.

"Yes. I can see that." She took the envelope off the table before he could stop her, weighed it in her hand once, then put it back down. "This isn't tired money."

"You know she pays cash."

"I know she pays cash when she wants a thing done off the books. I also know what your face looks like when you've been driving all night, and I know what it looks like when something is wrong. Those are different faces."

He said nothing.

"Did she shout at you?" Eszter asked.

"No."

"Did she cry?"

"No."

"Did she make you sit there while she talked at you for three hours and forgot you were a person?"

He almost laughed because that would have been easier to explain. "No."

Eszter folded her arms. "Then what?"

He should not have looked at the service-stair door. The glance was tiny, an animal thing, but she saw it. Her mouth tightened.

"Ah," she said. "So it's that kind of what."

"You don't know what kind."

"No. I don't. That's the problem."

Rain clicked against the high basement window above the sink. Somewhere upstairs a vacuum cleaner started, stopped, started again. The house went on being a house. That was what made it hard. If there had been sirens or smashing or some dramatic proof, he could have met that. Instead there was a saucepan drying on the rack, a child paused inside a voice note on a phone by the kettle, and Eszter giving him the look she reserved for dirty things set down beside the bread.

"If you don't want me asking, say that," she said. "Don't stand there feeding me work answers."

"I'm not feeding you anything."

"No? 'For the hours.' 'She kept me late.' 'Errands.' You might as well hand me a bloody invoice."

He rubbed at the wet mark his coffee mug had left on the table. The surface was scarred from years of hot pans, knives, bleach, honest damage. He liked that table. He had liked sitting at it because nothing he said there seemed to matter less than food or more than it should. Now he had brought the wrong language to it and could not get it back out.

"I can't tell you every detail of her business," he said.

The moment the sentence left him he knew it was the wrong one, not because it admitted too much but because it put Nina between them like a fact of nature, something official, something that deserved guarding. Eszter's expression went flat.

"Her business," she repeated.

"You know what I mean."

"I do now."

She turned away and took the paused voice note from the counter, listening long enough to hear the child's cough again before she stopped it. When she spoke next her voice was steadier, which meant she had chosen each word.

"Listen to me. You don't owe me a report. We're not married, and I'm not your mother. But if something is off, don't piss on my shoes and tell me it's weather."

He let out one breath through his nose, nearly a laugh and not one. "I wouldn't say that."

"No. You'd say 'for the hours.'" She opened a drawer, took out a roll of bin bags, tore one off harder than needed. "You think because you lie politely it doesn't smell."

"Eszter."

"What?"

"Nothing."

"Exactly."

The word landed cleanly. She pulled a loaf from the bread tin, sawed off two thick slices, and set one on a plate without looking at him. She spread butter, then lard, then a dusting of paprika. Her movements stayed practical. That was worse than a scene. It meant she was making herself continue the morning even while he irritated every nerve in the room.

She pushed the plate toward him. "Eat. You look as if the radiator coughed you out."

He had not realized he was hungry until the smell hit him. The bread was still a little warm in the middle. He took a bite because refusing would have turned this into something larger and because he needed the steadiness of chewing. She watched him once and then looked back to the sink.

"I was going to ask if you're free Sunday," she said.

He swallowed too fast. "Sunday?"

"Yes, Sunday. It comes after Saturday. We could go to Finsbury Park if the weather isn't disgusting. There's a market, and I need a few things. Lili wants a stupid pink pencil case from the shop near the station, the one with glitter hearts that will fall off in a week." She shrugged one shoulder. "I was going to ask. Past tense now, apparently."

The chapter had been breathing around that possibility without him naming it: train ride, food, the market, an ordinary day that belonged to neither work nor Nina. Hearing it aloud now made the envelope in his pocket feel heavier than the notes deserved.

"I might be working," he said.

Eszter nodded once, clipped and exact. "Maybe."

"I don't know yet."

"Of course you don't."

She took the plate when he finished the first slice and set the second closer, an automatic kindness her face had not agreed to. He wanted to tell her yes to Sunday, yes before Nina filled the afternoon and the evening and whatever came after that. He wanted to say he would make himself free. What stopped him was not guilt in the grand sense. It was the more practical filth of having no idea whether he could keep that promise without inventing another sequence of explanations by tomorrow.

"Eszter," he said, and because he had no truthful continuation ready, the name just sat there.

She gave him a tired look that aged her and softened her at the same time. "That's not a sentence either."

His phone buzzed in his pocket.

They both looked at it. He did not need to check whose message it was. Nina had a way of timing things; the rest of the house bent around her schedule.

Eszter stepped back from the table. "Go on."

"It could be Mercer."

"And I could be the Queen."

He took out the phone. Nina's message sat on the screen in a blue bubble with no greeting and no apology for existing:

Come up when you're done.

He should have put the phone away without showing her, but he had already looked, and the change in his face told her enough. Eszter did not lean in to read. She did not need to.

"Right," she said.

"I've got to take the dress up."

"Obviously."

"She needs the alteration note."

"Beni."

He stopped because the way she said his name was not soft. It was exact.

"I'm not stupid," she said. "Whatever this is, I'm not going to beg it into English for you."

He opened his mouth.

"No," she said. "Save it. If you want to tell me something real later, do that. If you don't, then don't. But don't come down here with that face and that envelope and then make me the rude one for noticing."

She picked up her phone, thumbed the voice note back to the beginning, and turned away from him toward the stove. The child's voice started again in a burst of Hungarian complaint, bright enough to make the kitchen feel briefly full of a life that had nothing to do with him or Nina or the garage. Eszter listened with her head bent, one hand braced on the counter, and he knew from the shape of her shoulders that the conversation was over unless he was ready to break something open properly.

He wasn't ready.

He took the envelope from the table, then the dress bag and the pharmacy packet. At the door he looked back once. Eszter had already set a pot on the hob and was reaching for the onions. She did not tell him to stay. She did not tell him to go to hell either. She simply left him outside the circle of the next task.

Upstairs the house was warmer and quieter, the heat from the radiators trapped in carpets thick enough to swallow footfall. He climbed with Nina's dress bag knocking his leg and the envelope pressing against his ribs, and by the second landing he was already arranging the next version of the day in his head. Nina had kept him late after the gallery. Nina had paid extra because of the hours. Nothing had happened. Something had happened but not anything Eszter could prove. Sunday was uncertain. Sunday was probably work. The car smelled of spray because he had been overcareful.

The lies were not hard one by one. That was the ugly part. Each of them was small enough to carry. The work was in keeping them from touching, in remembering which stair belonged to which life, which woman got the clean answer and which one got the useful one. By the time he reached Nina's door he could already feel the strain in it, not moral, not yet, but mechanical, like driving with the wheels a fraction out of line. The car would still go where you pointed it. It would just keep asking for correction.
