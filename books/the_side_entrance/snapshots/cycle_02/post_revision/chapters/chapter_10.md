# Chapter 10

Back in the Mercer drivers' room at 1:23, it smelled of burnt coffee, wet nylon, and the cold coins drivers emptied from cup holders before handing keys back. Beni had one heel up on the leg of the plastic chair, eating the end of a petrol-station sandwich he did not want, when Nina called his private mobile instead of dispatch.

He looked at her name for one ring too long before he answered.

"Where are you?" she said.

"Depot."

"Good. Come to Hackney Wick. The studio on White Post Lane. Now."

Her voice was too even. It had the scraped-flat sound she used with caterers and women on charity boards when she was furious enough to be elegant about it. Behind her he heard music cut off mid-bar and a metal door slamming somewhere farther inside the building.

"Are you all right?"

There was a pause. Then she said, "No. Get the car."

The line went dead.

Beni folded the sandwich back into its paper and dropped it in the bin. Noor's warning from yesterday still sat in him like a stone under the ribs. Rich people's secrets are never worth a poor man's paperwork. He took the Ashdown Mercedes anyway, because saying no to Nina after one in the morning did not feel like a choice he possessed. He wrote nothing on the movement board. The hook clacked empty behind him when he lifted the key.

Rain had been falling for hours without force, the kind that never turned dramatic from indoors and yet got into every seam. By the time he took the Ashdown Mercedes east past Old Street and the wet arches near Bethnal Green, the windows had furred over at the corners and the cuffs of his shirt felt damp. The wipers made a patient metronome across the glass. London at that hour was not asleep, only stripped down: office floors still lit for nobody, delivery riders pooled under railway arches with their boxes shining in the rain. He kept hearing the flatness in Nina's voice over the wipers.

Hackney Wick arrived in pieces: shuttered breweries, canal water black under security lamps, brick made expensive by being near artists and by being old in the correct way. Jonas's studio sat in a yard behind a metal gate that had failed to latch. Beni nosed the Mercedes in past two white vans and saw Nina before he saw Jonas.

She was standing under the strip light by the entrance, coat hanging open over a dark silk dress, one bare leg wet from the knee down where the rain had blown at her. She had kicked off one shoe and let it dangle useless from two fingers. Her other hand was wrapped in a dish towel that had soaked through at the center to a black-red blot. Jonas stood just inside the doorway in paint-marked work trousers and a charcoal T-shirt, rubbing one foot up the back of his other calf against the bite of the concrete floor. There was gray clay under one thumbnail. He kept twisting the tea towel in his hands even though it was already twisted to rope.

"She cut herself on a glass," he said when Beni got out. "It needs looking at."

Nina turned her head toward him without looking at him. "Do not brief the staff, Jonas."

"For fuck's sake, Nina."

"That isn't even inventive from you."

Beni moved between them before the argument found shape. Up close the towel around Nina's hand smelled of whiskey, iron, and the clean lemon soap people used when they had never had to buy the cheap kind in bulk. Blood had run over the heel of her palm and dried in the fine lines of her wrist. Her mascara had held. Her mouth had not. The inside of her lower lip was rubbed raw from her teeth.

"You should go to hospital," Beni said.

"I absolutely shouldn't go to hospital."

"It might need stitches."

"Then it can remain artistically unfinished."

Jonas shut his eyes for a second. "You see? This is what I mean."

She laughed once. It was a bright little sound, almost girlish, and it made the back of Beni's neck tighten harder than shouting would have. "No, darling. What you mean is what you always mean. You mean I've become inconvenient."

"I mean it's the middle of the night and you've smashed a tumbler because I said I was done."

"You said other things."

"Because we were speaking."

The blood had started fresh under the towel. Beni opened the rear door and said, more firmly than he usually spoke to her, "Sit down, please."

Nina met his eyes then, properly, and for a moment he thought she might slap him or kiss him or laugh in his face. Instead she climbed in with the broken shoe still in her good hand and sat sideways on the leather like someone boarding a train she fully intended to complain about. Jonas took half a step forward.

"Take her somewhere she'll get that cleaned properly."

"I'm not going with you," Nina said, not to Beni but still looking at Jonas. "Let the record show that you have found your boundary at last."

Jonas's mouth tightened. He was handsome in the thin-skinned way of men who had spent enough years being told so that annoyance came to them with a mirror already inside it. "You're drunk and bleeding."

"And you are so brave when a woman is already leaving."

Beni shut the door before Jonas could answer. He did not want the next line, from either of them. He went around to the driver's seat, put the car in drive, and took them out into the rain.

For two streets she said nothing. He could hear only the blower, the tires on the wet road, and the faint dry crackle of the towel when she adjusted her grip on it. In the mirror he saw her lean back, close her eyes, then snap them open again with a small panic crossing her face.

"There's a first-aid kit under your seat," he said.

"Of course there is."

"If you let me stop, I can wrap it."

"Drive first."

"Mrs. Ashdown."

"Don't do that."

He waited.

"Not tonight," she said. "Just Nina, if we're still pretending names matter."

He took the A12 west because at that hour it was the cleanest line out. Rain silvered the barriers. A billboard over the carriageway promised studios from £2,150 pcm in letters big enough to be seen from a speeding car. Beni read the number twice without meaning to. More than half his monthly pay for one room and a view of water cold enough to kill you.

At the lights before Bow Roundabout he pulled the hazard switch on and turned half around.

"Give me your hand."

"We're in traffic."

"We're at red."

She made a face at him like a displeased schoolgirl and then held the injured hand out. The towel peeled away with a wet reluctance. The cut ran diagonally across the base of her palm, not deep enough to see anything white, deep enough to keep opening every time she flexed. Blood welled fresh and fat. Beni took saline from the kit, tore it with his teeth, and washed the grit away while the light changed and the man behind them honked once, offended by a world that had not organized itself around him quickly enough.

Nina sucked air between her teeth. "That stings."

"Good."

She gave him a quick look, almost offended and almost amused. "You've been spending time with Eszter."

His fingers paused for half a second. He was glad the light had gone green and the car behind them had moved off, because the man would have seen him sitting there like an idiot with his body turned toward the back seat, one hand around Nina's wrist as if he had forgotten who was employed by whom.

"Pressure," he said.

He folded gauze thick, set it over the cut, and wrapped her hand with the white bandage from the kit. Her skin was warm from drink and shock. The tendons in her wrist shifted under his thumb. He could smell the whiskey on her breath, the sharper green scent of her perfume gone sour with rain, and under both of those the clean iron smell of blood. His body answered before his mind did. He hated that the answer was so old and so immediate.

"Can you move your fingers?"

She did, slow and obedient for once. "There. A triumph of roadside medicine."

"If it keeps bleeding, we stop."

"If it keeps bleeding, you'll drive faster."

He pulled back into the lane. The city tightened around them as they came through Mile End and Whitechapel and then loosened again into the broad damp shine of roads built for finance men to leave after midnight. When he reached Tower Hill she leaned forward.

"Take the river."

"Where do you want to go?"

"Along it. I don't care. West first."

"Home eventually?"

"Eventually everything. West first."

He brought the Mercedes down onto the Embankment and the river appeared to the left, black and broad and carrying strips of torn light. The city made more sense to him from a driver's seat than it ever did on foot. The lane markings held. The bridges arrived in sequence. Near St Katharine Docks a sales office glowed with a model apartment no one had cooked in and a sign that said from £3.1 million. In the mirror Nina had gone too still, her bandaged hand white against the dark leather.

Nina had stopped pressing the bandage. That worried him more than the blood.

"What happened?" he asked.

She watched his eyes in the mirror. "You mean what was the line? There was a line, yes. There always is with men like Jonas. They think history starts when they've spoken. Before that it's just weather. Atmosphere. The bit they were happy enough to live in."

He said nothing.

"He ended it. Properly this time. No pause for dignity, no absurd promise to call when things were less complicated, no little sermon about timing. Almost admirable, actually. He's been practicing. Or rehearsing. Same thing."

At Temple the road narrowed around roadworks and Beni had to drop speed. A taxi cut across him with no signal. He braked, swore softly in Hungarian, and Nina laughed again, this time with actual amusement.

"There you are."

"What?"

"A personality. I was beginning to think you only had shoulders and hands."

He kept his eyes on the lane. "He told you not to come back?"

"He said he couldn't keep fitting me around the rest of his life. That I'd become all appetite and administration. Imagine that. No, really, imagine it. Administration. Like I was a filing system he'd once found erotic."

She leaned her head back. Streetlight washed her face flat, catching the pale tracks where rain had thinned her foundation. "He said he wanted less consequence. That was the phrase. The actual one. Less consequence. He's forty pounds of damp emotional laundry and a trust fund, and suddenly he wants less consequence."

"How much had you drunk?"

"Enough for him to use it."

"How much?"

"Whiskey. Then his natural wine, which tasted like a horse had died in a plum."

Beni almost smiled. He did not let it happen where she could hear it.

"Did he hit you?" he asked.

She opened her eyes. "No."

"Did you hit him?"

"No. I'm not sixteen." She shifted, then corrected herself. "I threw the tumbler at the wall. It missed the wall in the sense that walls are not made of glass."

He took them over Waterloo Bridge, turned south, then west again because she had not given him any destination he could complete. The river flashed in and out beside them between hotels and office blocks, cold as sheet metal. He kept driving because motion was easier than asking what destination would count as enough for her.

"He said something worse before I threw it," Nina said.

"What?"

She worried the edge of the bandage with one thumbnail. He could see her trying not to pull it loose. It was the first scared thing she had done all night.

"He said he was tired of being cast in someone else's emergency."

Beni's hands stayed steady on the wheel, but inside him something shifted and found purchase. She had called him from the middle of hers anyway.

"Maybe he meant tonight," he said.

"He meant me." She leaned her head back and shut her eyes. Another wash of light found the streaks in her makeup. "Do you know what's vulgar about being forty-one? No, vulgar isn't the word. Cheap. It makes people feel generous. They congratulate you on how little damage it shows. Men my age want peace. No, that's a lie. They want peace and admiration at once. Younger men want novelty with references. Oscar wants optics. Everybody loves appetite while it behaves."

"You have a daughter. A house. A husband."

"Thank you, Beni. Do continue the inventory of my prison."

He felt the rebuke and the perversity of the heat that came with it. He knew that tone now, the one that made contempt sound like private attention. Six weeks ago he would have heard only the attention.

"I mean people see those first," he said.

"Yes. Exactly." She opened her eyes and looked at him in the mirror. "They see arrangement. The frame, I mean. The reasons I should be grateful and contained."

They passed the London Eye half extinguished, its great circle a ghost over the wet road. Near Chelsea the river widened and the terraces across it sat curtained and blank, rich enough to make absence look deliberate.

"Pull over a second," Nina said.

He glanced at her. "Why?"

"I want a cigarette."

"You don't smoke."

"Tonight I very much do."

He found a legal bay on Cheyne Walk where no one was likely to care for ten minutes and stopped under a plane tree dripping old rain. The river wall was slick. Across the water the power station's chimneys stood ghost-pale in the thinning dark. Nina opened her bag one-handed, swore softly, and let it spill into the seat beside her: lipstick, card case, a crushed packet of mints, one small perfume bottle, receipts folded into soft white knots, and finally a cigarette case in black enamel.

"You've had those how long?" Beni asked.

"Since a summer in Antibes when I was nineteen and feral."

"Do you know where Antibes is from Tottenham?" he said before he could stop himself.

That made her laugh properly. She bent over the laugh, forehead briefly against the back of his seat. "There he is again. God, don't become funny now. I've got enough to miss already."

She put the cigarette between her lips and held it there, then realized her injured hand made the lighter awkward. Beni reached automatically into his coat pocket for the cheap black lighter he carried for clients who lied about not smoking. The plastic was warm from his body. For one stupid second he almost put it away again. Then he turned in his seat and lit Nina's cigarette with a flick practiced long before London.

She drew in, the tip flaring orange, and looked at the lighter. "That's not yours by class."

"No."

"Was it a gift?"

He shut it and put it back in his pocket. "Smoke or don't."

She exhaled through the crack in the open window. Rain smell, river smell, cigarette smoke, warm leather, blood. It made the car intimate in a way he did not trust. A red double-decker went by nearly empty, top deck lit like an aquarium for one man asleep against the glass.

"Why'd you call me?" he asked.

"Because you answer."

"You could have called anyone."

"No." She looked out at the river. "That's the whole point. I couldn't."

He waited, because waiting was the work and because some part of him still wanted what might come next if he left enough space for it.

"Oscar would turn this into liability by breakfast," she said. "A doctor would make notes. A friend would want to know whether I'm humiliated enough yet. No, a friend wouldn't. That's the issue. Jonas had finished being noble for the evening. Bea's a child. Worse, she's observant. You..." She tipped ash again, missed the crack in the window, and brushed the gray dust off her dress with the side of her thumb. "You don't make me perform it. You're good in disasters."

"I drive."

"You contain."

The word sat between them.

Beni looked out through the steamed windshield at the river wall, the slick pavement, the basement light burning in a house where someone had already started breakfast for someone else. He could feel the line drawing tighter, the one between being seen and being used. It was always there with Nina. Sometimes sex blurred it. Sometimes money did. Tonight nothing was blurred enough.

"If you want to go home, I'll take you home," he said. "If you want a clinic, I'll take you there. If you want me to wait until morning, I'll wait. But don't talk about me like I'm furniture."

Ash lengthened at the tip of her cigarette until it bent and fell onto the black enamel case in her lap. She brushed at it once, missed, then pinched it into a gray smear with her thumb before she turned toward him fully, one bare foot tucked under her on the seat, the broken shoe hanging from two fingers. Up close she carried both women at once: older than daylight, younger than dinners. He could see both and understood how exhausting that must be.

"You're not furniture," she said quietly. "Furniture is expensive and stupid. You... no, that's cheap of me. You're a relief."

He did not answer.

"Do you know how rare that is? A man who doesn't immediately start bargaining. You don't ask for declarations. Or where this is going. You don't make me soothe you because I've disappeared. You don't turn one bad night into an invoice."

The rain ticked in the branches above the car. Somewhere along the embankment a fox barked, sharp and ridiculous and lonely. Beni felt the old reflex rise in him, the one that stepped aside from pain and called the movement practicality. He knew how to let a sentence pass over him. He had done it in two languages for years.

Nina mistook his silence for permission and went on.

"It's your silence, Beni. Not the driving. Not the sex. That. That's what makes you useful to me. You can sit in the dark and leave a thing alone."

He looked at her in the mirror.

At first he felt only the simple hot sting of it, the way anyone would at being praised for the damage they had spent so long teaching not to show. Then something colder arrived behind the sting. He saw, with a clarity so clean it almost calmed him, that she did not mean he was discreet or kind or loyal. She meant he was built for absence. She meant he could be put where need went when need had no respectable address. She meant the worst thing about him had been convenient to her.

Outside, beyond the river wall, a houseboat window lit and a man in a vest stood brushing his teeth over a sink the size of a shoebox. Even in that lit shoebox he had more room than Beni felt.

"Drive," Nina said, softer now. "Please."

He put the car back in gear.

He crossed Albert Bridge, its lights trembling in the wet, and stayed on the south side after that, taking the long eastbound pull because he was not ready to point the car toward Belgravia while her words were still warm in the air. The city was changing shifts: street cleaners at the curbs, bakery vans nosing toward stations. He kept east until she told him otherwise.

Nina smoked half the cigarette and then could not stand it, crushing it carefully into the water bottle and leaving even her disgust in a neat residue.

"Jonas said he's tired of hiding me," she said after Westminster, looking past him to the dark river glass. "Tired of being hidden by me. He said sneaking me in through the yard made him feel cheap."

Beni kept both hands on the wheel. "Hold the bandage higher."

She talked over him. "Oscar hasn't touched me in months unless there's a photographer about. Tonight he moved my hair off my necklace because the flash was catching on it. That was the most intimate thing anybody did to me all week."

"Drink some water."

"I looked at myself in Jonas's bathroom and thought, there she is. Forty-one, dress half open, begging in somebody else's mirror."

His grip tightened on the wheel until the leather seam bit his palm.

"I could've cut deeper," she said to the window, almost conversationally.

The Mercedes drifted an inch toward the center line before he corrected it. He took his foot off the accelerator and dropped them to forty.

"Don't say that again."

She turned her head. "Why? Because then you have to hear it?"

"Because you're drunk and bleeding and I'm not dragging your body out of this car as well. Hold the bandage up. Now."

For a second she only stared. Then she obeyed, lifting the white pad above her lap with resentful care. At the next red light he took the water bottle from the cup holder, twisted the cap loose one-handed, and passed it back without looking at her.

"Drink," he said.

After that he did not take the car above forty.

By the time they came through Pimlico the rain had thinned to mist. Not light yet, only a little less black in the cloud, but the city felt it. Early buses had begun to gather the first bodies of morning. A row of townhouses stood wrapped in scaffolding printed with a fake image of the facades beneath, wealth covering its repairs with a photograph of itself.

Nina had gone quiet enough that he checked the mirror twice to make sure quiet was all it was. She was awake, watching the river through half-lidded eyes. The fury had drained from her face. What remained was worse. Not sorrow exactly. Exposure.

"Take me home," she said at last.

He turned inland.

Belgravia before first light was indecent to him, a district that managed to have its rubbish removed before daylight could accuse it of being inhabited. When he pulled into the mews behind the Ashdown house, the paving stones reflected the garage lamps in one long expensive ribbon.

He got out first and came around to open her door. She sat a moment without moving, gathering herself with the discipline of someone who had built a whole adulthood out of entering rooms correctly. The bandage on her hand had spotted through at the center, a rust-dark bloom no wider than a coin.

"You'll need that looked at in the morning," he said.

"Yes."

"Actually looked at."

"You're becoming very authoritarian for nineteen."

"I'm serious."

"I know."

She put her good hand on the edge of the door and began to stand. Then she stopped and looked up at him from the seat, her hair roughened by the night's damp, one foot still bare on the black rubber mat. In that moment she came down to ordinary human scale, and none of the danger went out of her.

"If Oscar asks, I broke a glass taking off my earrings," she said. "You picked me up because I didn't want the house calling an ambulance."

Beni held the door and said nothing.

Her mouth changed. Not quite a smile. Not quite defeat.

She stepped out, balancing the broken shoe by its strap, and passed close enough that the fabric of her coat brushed his wrist. The contact lit his skin stupidly, treacherously. She felt it or guessed it; he could not tell. She touched the knot of his tie once with two fingers, straightening nothing.

"Sleep a little," she said. "You look wrecked."

"Go inside, Nina."

Then she went through the side door and vanished into the house that always seemed to swallow evidence by breakfast.

Beni stood in the mews until the automatic light timed out and the garage front went dim. Rain tapped somewhere in the guttering. A plane crossed low and unseen above the city. He got back in the driver's seat because there was nowhere else for him to put what the night had become.

The car smelled of cigarette smoke now, and blood under perfume, and the mechanical warmth of expensive leather being asked to hold too much human mess without showing it. On the rear seat a mint had rolled from Nina's bag and come to rest in the crease by the armrest. He picked it up, looked at the clean silver wrapper, and set it in the cup holder instead of throwing it away.

His private mobile showed 2:24. In a few hours the house would wake. Someone would bring up coffee. Someone would lay out Bea's breakfast. Someone would remove the towel from whatever sink Nina had dropped it in. If she chose, she could make the night into nothing more than a difficult mood and a small domestic accident. Jonas would sleep. Oscar would read numbers. The city would begin charging for itself in daylight rates.

The Mercer phone buzzed in the console pocket: 05:00 sign-in. Terminal 5 after. He looked at the screen until it dimmed. Not enough time to go home, sleep, and come back clean. Enough time to get across town, sign the sheet, drag a clean shirt off the rail, and make his face into something a stranger could trust.

Beni started the engine and keyed the heater higher.

He put the car in reverse, backed carefully out into the wet mews, and drove toward Mercer, where the coffee would be burnt, the fluorescent office too bright, and the Terminal 5 route slip already waiting in a place that did not care what had happened before dawn as long as the car was clean and the driver kept quiet.
