# Chapter 17

By seven the house smelled expensive and overworked. Lilies heated too close to the radiators had gone thick and sweet in the front hall, and under them lived the smells nobody put on invitations: dishwasher steam from the basement, frying oil from the caterers' burners in the mews, bleach, wet wool, the sour metal breath of lids lifted and dropped over chafing dishes. Beni moved between the front step and the garage with a radio clipped under his lapel and rain drying in a cold stripe down his back. Every room upstairs looked effortless because three rooms below it were not.

The gala had eaten the whole townhouse. The library tables were gone under white cloth and auction cards. The morning room had become a champagne station. The drawing room held a small forest of hired trees, pale roses wired into them so tightly they looked pinned there by force. On the easel by the stairs a cream card explained, in a font that managed to seem both charitable and expensive, what the evening would do for women and children in emergency accommodation. One table funded six weeks. One premier bid funded a whole room. Beni had seen Eszter's church-basement bed the night before, the blanket folded with church neatness and the metal chair that left dents in the wall when you scraped it back. He read the number once and did not read it again.

The staff moved with the flat, shut face of people who could not afford a mistake. A coat-check woman from the agency kept blotting at her nose with the inside of her wrist because the lilies had started her hay fever, then smiling at fur-collared guests before her eyes had stopped watering. One of the catering boys, red-haired and too young for the jacket they had put him in, slipped his left foot out of his shoe whenever he thought nobody was looking; the back strap had rubbed his heel raw, and he was working on the ball of that foot like a dancer with no glamour in it. In the kitchen a dishwasher tray jammed and somebody swore in Polish. Upstairs a trustee laughed too loudly at something not funny, and the laugh travelled through the house better than any order.

Oscar Ashdown moved through it all with the stillness of a man who believed nothing ought to touch him directly. He never hurried. He only redirected. A flower arrangement shifted six inches. A donor's place card changed tables because the woman who ran the foundation had finally understood which divorce could not be sat beside which remarriage. Two champagne trays were held back until the photographer was ready. When a waiter crossed the front hall too early with oyster shells stacked like wet fingernails on silver, Oscar did not tell him off. He said, "Not yet," in the same voice other men used for prayer or threat, and the waiter went red all the way to the ears.

Nina was even better at it than Oscar because she could make command look like grace from a distance. She stood beneath the staircase in dark blue silk that looked black until the chandeliers caught it, greeting the board women by name, touching elbows, listening with a tilt of the head that implied warmth while keeping her body out of reach. She had dressed for scrutiny as much as beauty, and Beni knew the labor that had gone into that too: the steamed gown, the pinned hair, the makeup spread over fatigue like lacquer over cracked wood. Once, crossing the hall with a message from the mews, he came close enough to smell her perfume under the lilies, something dry and expensive with a bitter edge like grapefruit peel. She did not look at him. Her mouth moved through a donor smile while she said, low enough that only he heard it, "Stay close after the auction."

He had already decided he was not going to Paris with her. That decision had sat in him all afternoon like an object in a pocket, hard-edged and private and easy to touch without changing anything. Not going was one thing. Speaking was another. All day he had been telling himself that refusal counted as a kind of honesty. He would finish the shift, hand the future back, keep the rest buried where it had already done the damage. That was how he had imagined surviving it.

Near eight the first real crush hit: late donors in damp black cars, women with bare shoulders pulling cashmere over them against the draft, men already pink from cocktails elsewhere. Beni took coats, passed glasses, opened the front door, closed it, crossed the hall, went down the service stairs, came up again. The auction catalogues were printed on paper thicker than anything in Laci's flat except the front door. A woman in diamonds said to her husband, not quietly, that emergency housing had become "terribly difficult politically" and took a second blini she did not finish. A man with a face like suet asked whether the mews entrance was where "all the magic happened." Beni told him yes, sir, because the truthful version would have taken too long and cost the wrong person.

Bea came down just before the call for the family photographs. He knew it was her before he saw her face because nobody else her age in that house moved like she was bracing for school inspection. She wore a black dress cut plain enough to pass for adult taste, but she kept pinching the side seam near her thigh as if the fabric had offended her personally. Her dark hair was pinned back too tightly. Someone had put earrings on her that looked old enough to belong to a grandmother she probably never liked. She paused on the last stair and looked across the front room with the dry, appalled expression she usually saved for her mother's friends.

Nina's hand landed briefly at the middle of Bea's back. Oscar was already near the sponsor wall the foundation had built beside the tree, all white flowers and printed logos and calculated innocence. The foundation chair, a narrow woman with a tennis elbow strap still under her sleeve, smiled with all her teeth and said, "Just one quick family shot before dinner, if we may."

Oscar turned. "Bea. Here, please."

Bea did not move.

Nina kept smiling for the chair. "Darling, one minute."

"No."

The chair laughed as if she had heard a joke and not a seventeen-year-old refusing a public order. Oscar's face altered by almost nothing. "Now isn't the time."

Bea looked at the flowers, then at the sponsor logos, then at her parents. "Use the orchids," she said. "They hold still better."

Two donors within earshot discovered urgent interest in their champagne. The photographer lowered his camera a fraction without lowering it all the way. Nina's smile tightened at one corner.

"Don't be vulgar," Nina said.

"I'm not the vulgar thing here."

Oscar stepped nearer, voice dropping instead of rising. "You'll come here, stand for thirty seconds, and then do whatever performance of outrage you've planned in private."

Bea gave a short laugh that sounded older than her face. "That's the whole point, isn't it? Private. Everything ugly private, and then this." She touched the air between them, the flowers, the logos, the camera. "I'm not doing it."

Nina said, still smiling for the room, "Beatrice."

"No. You can crop me in from some other year."

She turned before either of them could get a hand on her and walked across the hall, not fast, which made it worse. She passed Beni close enough that he saw the skin at her throat blotched red above the dress neckline. She smelled of hair spray and the peppermint gum she always chewed in the car after school. The front door was blocked by guests, so she cut left through the little passage toward the coat room and the service stairs, knowing the house well enough to disappear by its useful routes instead of its pretty ones.

For one suspended second the whole front room held. The foundation chair still had the bright fixed face of someone who would rather be bitten than acknowledge disaster. The photographer pretended to check his settings. A waiter bent to wipe a splash of champagne from the marble with his napkin because the tray in his hand gave him something to do besides stare.

Oscar did not look at Nina. He looked at Beni. "Go after her," he said. "Find out where she's going."

Beni moved before he had answered. The passage smelled colder than the front hall and faintly of polish and old umbrellas. He heard Nina behind him say something to the chair in the soft apologetic murmur rich people used when they expected the room to help them keep lying. Then the swing door cut the sound off and he was in the service corridor with the fluorescent lights and the stacked crates from the florist.

Bea had not gone down to the basement. She had gone out toward the mews, fast enough that one of the side doors was still rocking on its hinge when Beni reached it. Cold rain needled his face. The stone behind the house shone black under the security lights, and at the far end, just beyond the bins and the catering van, Bea was crouched on the lowest step with one heel already off. A black wool coat lay beside her in a heap. She had a canvas tote open between her knees and was dragging out a pair of white trainers with the laces knotted together from being jammed in too fast.

"Bea."

She did not startle. She only looked up once and went back to the laces. "I'm aware."

"You can't go out like that."

"In a coat and shoes? London will cope."

He came down the step into the rain. Up close her hands were not steady. She was fighting the heel buckle with fingers made clumsy by cold and fury, and the tiny clasp kept slipping. Beni crouched without asking and held the shoe still while she yanked the strap free. She let him do it because it was quicker, not because she wanted help.

"Where are you going?" he asked.

"Away from the nativity."

"Be serious."

She shoved one trainer on hard enough to bend the back. "I'm being serious. That's why everybody hates it."

He looked past her toward the mouth of the mews. No car was waiting yet. No friend on the pavement. Just wet brick, bins, the florist's wilted stems in black sacks, and the red pulse of brake lights from the street beyond. "I need to know you're not just walking off."

She straightened up, one trainer tied, the other unlaced, coat still hanging open over the black dress. "Why? So my father can say you recovered the asset?"

"So I know you aren't alone."

That gave her pause. Not softness. Just a different angle. She pulled her phone from the tote, thumb moving fast across the screen. Dark polish chipped at the edges. "I'm not doing yesterday again," she said. "I'm not turning up at your flat with a bag and making my life everybody else's problem. Nessa's mum's coming for me. She knows. She actually asked if I wanted picking up instead of saying I ought to think about optics."

The phone glowed between them. A map pin flashed on it. She had already sent messages to three people. One of the names was Eszter. He looked away from that before she noticed.

"Did you tell them where?" he asked.

"I told them I wasn't dead in a canal, if that's what you mean."

He almost said her mother's name and did not. Bea tucked the phone into her coat pocket and crouched again to knot the second trainer. The wet stone darkened the knees of her tights. The expensive earrings were still in, absurdly formal above the hunched, practical business of tying a shoe in the rain.

"When he doesn't like something," she said, eyes on the laces, "he turns it into somebody else's hours. He'll say the evening was disrupted and suddenly the kitchen has too many staff or the driver should've managed me better or the agency girl in coats wasn't attentive enough. That's how it works here."

Beni said nothing.

She glanced up, sharp enough to see the silence land. "You know that."

"Yes."

"Then don't let him do it for this."

From inside the house came a burst of applause, hollow through the walls. Somebody had started speeches or pretended to. Bea stood and pulled on the black coat. It was plain, warm, and far more like her than the dress. She pushed her hands into the pockets and hunched once against the cold before forcing herself upright again.

Headlights turned into the mews, too quick for a hired car idling for money. A dark Volvo nosed between the catering van and the Ashdowns' garage and stopped. Music thudded once from inside before being cut off. The back door flew open and a girl Beni recognized vaguely from school pickups climbed half out, waving. She wore an oversized velvet blazer over a silver slip dress and had bitten the lipstick off the centre of her mouth. Another girl in the back scooted sideways to make room, one false eyelash lifting at the corner as she blinked. In the front passenger seat sat a woman in navy tracksuit bottoms under a camel coat, hair flattened at the crown as if she had jammed a knit hat over it earlier and not cared what happened after.

"Bea," the woman called, with the resigned competence of a parent already interrupted three times that evening. Then she looked at Beni, took in the house, the suit, the wet mews, and said, "She's with us."

Bea walked to the car but stopped before getting in. "Nessa's mum knows where we're going," she said. "There's an adult. Everybody can unclench."

Nessa's mother snorted. "A debatable adult, but yes."

The line was dry enough that Bea nearly smiled. It passed over her face and was gone. She looked back at Beni. For a second she seemed younger, not because she softened but because the rage sat on top of something thin and frightened and still trying not to show. "Tell him I'm not missing," she said. "Tell him I just wasn't available for display."

Beni nodded.

She held his gaze another beat. "And don't let them put this on Eszter."

"I won't."

"You'd better not."

She got into the car. The girl with the loose eyelash shoved a packet of salt-and-vinegar crisps at her without comment. Nessa's mother waited until Bea had shut the door and fastened the belt, then gave Beni a short glance that managed to say she was not asking questions tonight but could if forced. After that she pulled the Volvo back toward the street. Bea did not look out again. The taillights turned left at the mouth of the mews and were gone toward Sloane Square.

Beni stood in the rain long enough for the cold to get through the shoulders of his jacket. He could still go back upstairs and say only that Bea was safe with friends. He could let the house close over the scene the way it closed over everything: a lowered voice, a shifted schedule, somebody else docked by morning for an inconvenience that had not started with them. Nina would take him aside later, after the auction, maybe after midnight, and talk about Paris again as if a new city changed the arrangement. Oscar would count the evening a success if the pledges held. The driver would drive, the staff would clean, the lies would move to their next container.

He thought of Eszter's church-basement room with the neat blanket and the bag under the bed containing everything that could be packed in four minutes. He thought of Bea on the step in a black dress and trainers, fighting with a heel buckle like it was a hand on her. He thought, with sudden flat clarity, that not going was not enough.

The Mercedes sat in the Ashdowns' garage where he had left it after the last airport pickup, black paint filmed with the damp that crept in even under cover. He opened the driver's door and leaned into the leather hush he knew too well. The route logbook was in the glove compartment under the insurance papers, a black Mercer binder with duplicate pages and October's lies in his own careful handwriting. He took it out. Then he opened the inner pocket of his jacket and pulled out Nina's envelope.

What was left in it came to one hundred and seventy pounds: three fifties and two tens, the notes still flat and almost crisp except for the one he had sweated through last week. It looked smaller outside the house than it had inside it. Smaller than the rent it had helped. Smaller than the hours it had bought. He slid the notes back into the envelope, tucked the binder under his arm, and went in through the service door.

The speeches had started. On the small monitor by the kitchen pass the ballroom showed rows of dinner jackets and bright bare shoulders turned toward a lectern. The foundation chair was talking about dignity, safety, and the importance of private generosity in public times. Freya, the catering girl with the blistered heel, stood by the dumbwaiter with six empty champagne flutes in each hand and rolled her eyes so hard she nearly lost one. Then she saw Beni looking and shrugged as if to say she had not meant anything by it. Her mascara had smudged at one corner from heat.

Beni went upstairs.

Oscar was not in the ballroom. Beni found him in the morning room just off the hall, door mostly closed, phone in hand, listening while someone on the other end spoke too long. A half-finished whisky sat on the side table beside a tray of untouched canapes, the pastry already sweating butter into the napkin. Through the wall came the softened cadence of the speech and polite donor laughter at lines meant to prove the evening had a heart.

Oscar saw him before he finished the call. He lifted one finger for wait, listened another ten seconds, then said, "No, keep the press line outside until after the second course. No exceptions." He ended the call and set the phone facedown. "Well?"

"Bea's safe," Beni said. His voice sounded drier than his mouth felt. "She left with a school friend and the friend's mother. Nessa Doyle."

Oscar nodded once. He knew the name, or pretended to. "Thank you."

Beni did not move.

Oscar's eyes dropped to the binder under his arm, then to the envelope in his hand. Nothing showed in his face, but the room tightened anyway. "What's this."

Beni put the envelope on the side table beside the whisky and set the Mercer book next to it. "I need to give you these."

Oscar did not touch either one. "Explain."

"The money is from Mrs. Ashdown."

"That narrows very little."

"Private cash," Beni said. "For drives outside Mercer. For keeping quiet."

Oscar looked at the envelope then, properly now. The speech next door reached a point where the room beyond them applauded and then settled again. "About what."

Beni felt the vinyl edge of the logbook digging into his palm where he still held it flat to the table. "Routes. Where she went. Who she saw. I changed the book."

Oscar's gaze moved to the binder. "You altered company records."

"Yes."

"When."

"October first. After the gallery. Then later." He swallowed. "Hackney Wick entries. Some riverside nights. Pickup times. Mileage."

"Specifics, Mr. Varga."

Beni opened the binder because it was easier to point at ink than keep talking into Oscar's face. The pages crackled under his fingers. His own writing looked almost offensively neat. "These here. And these. She called late and I wrote earlier returns. If dispatch asked, I kept the route inside Belgravia or Chelsea. Sometimes she was with Mr. Hale. Sometimes we only drove."

Oscar did not sit. He stood over the table reading the pages in quick, efficient flashes, not as a husband first and not even as an employer first, but as a man presented with falsified numbers. "How many times."

"I don't know exactly."

"You must."

"Not exact." The shame of that irritated him, because it was a childish answer and also the truth. "Enough."

Oscar gave him a look cold enough to take the heat out of the room. "Enough for what."

Beni forced himself not to look away. "Enough that Bea knew something was wrong. Enough that Eszter knew I had money that wasn't clean. Enough."

The speech next door ended. Cutlery and conversation swelled up at once, a whole room relieved to be allowed back into appetite. Oscar put one hand on the back of a chair and asked, very quietly, "Was this arrangement sexual."

Beni could have lied smaller even now. He knew that in the second before he answered. He could have said personal. He could have said she leaned on him. He could have said it got mixed up. All the phrases that left some air in a story for everybody else to breathe. Bea had stood in front of the flowers and said no. He heard that no again, flat and young and expensive.

"Yes," he said.

The door moved behind him. Nina had come in without either of them hearing the handle over the cutlery noise. She shut it with more care than the moment deserved. Her face was still the gala face, makeup perfect, mouth painted, but the control in it had thinned. "Oscar," she said, "not now."

Oscar did not turn. "How much did you hear."

"Enough to know this conversation can wait until we're not hosting two hundred people."

"It has been waiting for months by the look of it."

Nina looked at Beni then, and whatever she felt hit him through polish before it hit him through anger: disbelief first, then contempt sharpened by embarrassment. "What are you doing."

He had no good sentence ready. That was the point. "I brought him the book."

"I can see that."

"And the money."

She looked at the envelope with immediate understanding. "Jesus Christ." The curse came out clipped and low, not for effect but because she had forgotten who she was performing for. "This is not confession. This is panic."

"Maybe," Beni said.

"You could have simply left."

"I know."

Her eyes narrowed. "Then why didn't you."

He did not answer because every answer he had was partial and ugly. Because Bea had walked out. Because Eszter had folded her life small enough to fit under a church bed while donors next door bought emergency rooms by raising paddles. Because Nina had offered him Paris like a prettier service entrance. Because he was tired of hearing his own silence praised as if it were character. None of that could be said in a room like this without sounding better than it was.

Oscar picked up the envelope at last and thumbed the notes inside without counting them in front of Beni. "Is this all."

"No."

"How much is missing."

"I used some."

"On what."

Beni heard the humiliation in the question before he answered it. "Rent. Food. Some went to Eszter when her shifts were cut."

Nina shut her eyes once, fast, as if against a bright light. "Of course it did."

Oscar looked at him over the envelope. "You took money from my wife to conceal an affair with my wife, falsified a company's records to support it, and then redistributed some of that money to a member of staff whose employment was already compromised by this household."

"Yes."

"Did Ms. Toth know where it came from."

"Not all of it. Then later, yes."

Oscar nodded once, almost to himself, assembling sequence. "And my daughter."

"She knew enough."

"Evidently."

For a second nobody spoke. The ballroom next door had moved on to the first course. Plates clicked. Somebody laughed hard and false at the wrong end of the room. Somewhere in the hall a radio hissed with kitchen timing, the little static burst weirdly intimate in the silence.

Nina put a hand on the edge of the sideboard. Her nails were pale and perfect and pressed hard enough at the wood that he could see the tendons in the back of her hand. "Oscar, if you would like to conduct a forensic audit of my humiliations, do it tomorrow. Right now what matters is that the evening stays intact."

Oscar turned to her then. He did not raise his voice. "The evening isn't intact."

"It's salvageable."

"For donors, perhaps."

Something like a smile touched her mouth, bitter and gone at once. "Please. As if you care about any other category."

He let that pass without dignity because dignity was cheaper than time just then. He looked back at Beni. "Give me your keys."

Beni stared at him a moment too long before understanding. Then he reached into his pocket and set the Ashdown house key, the mews fob, and the Mercedes key on the table beside the envelope and the whisky. The small metal sounds they made seemed louder than the cutlery through the wall.

"You're finished here," Oscar said.

Nina drew breath to speak, but he kept going.

"You won't drive for this household again. I'll inform Mercer tonight that their records were falsified and that you're no longer to be assigned to any member of this family. Accounts will settle the hours already worked. There'll be no recommendation from me, and if Mercer asks whether you handled routes honestly, the answer will be no."

The disaster arrived in figures before it arrived in feeling. Monday's wage. The Ashdown gratuity that would not come at Christmas. The extra cash nights when Nina wanted the car and paid as if banknotes could keep consequence decorative. The line on a future form where somebody important wrote reliable. Laci's rent notebook on the kitchen table, opened flat under his thick hand. It all came at once, practical and exact, and Beni found himself looking not at Oscar or Nina but at the half-melted canape grease staining the napkin under the untouched pastry.

"All right," he said.

Oscar's expression altered slightly at that, maybe because he had expected pleading, maybe because he had wanted it. "Do you have anything further to say."

Beni thought of a hundred useless things. Sorry. I know. I didn't mean. She used me and I let her because I wanted it. I knew better. I am nineteen and that does not help. None of them would change the ledger now in front of him.

"This was me," he said instead. "Not the kitchen. Not Eszter. Bea walking out wasn't staff failure."

Oscar's eyes sharpened, and for the first time a little heat touched the edges of his control. "Don't tell me how to categorize failure in my own house."

"I'm telling you who did what."

"You're in no position to tell me anything. You've told me what you needed to tell me, and that position is now over."

Nina laughed once under her breath, a sound with no amusement in it. "There. Belated ethics, and it still ends in payroll language."

Oscar ignored her. "Leave by the mews. Tonight. If you contact my daughter directly again, I'll treat it as harassment. If you contact my wife, that's between the solicitors currently earning too much money from us."

That, absurdly, was what made Nina's face go white under the makeup. Not the affair. Not the envelope. The neat public-private line being redrawn in front of her by someone who already had a room full of witnesses to manage.

Beni nodded because there was nothing else to do with his head. He turned for the door.

"Beni." Nina's voice stopped him before his hand reached the handle.

He looked back.

She had dropped the gala smile completely now. What was left was needier and meaner and more honest for it. "You think this makes you honest?"

He considered lying to her out of habit and did not bother. "No."

The answer seemed to offend her more than any defense would have. Her mouth tightened. For a moment he thought she would say something cruel enough to keep him in the room with her. Instead she looked past him to the door, toward the dining room noise, and said, "Then what a waste."

He went out before she could make him stay for the rest.

The hall looked almost unchanged. That was the expertise of houses like this. A rupture could happen ten feet away and the silver still travelled, the guests still lifted forks, the foundation chair still smiled at a donor as if the entire evening were proceeding under divine management. The photographer was back near the tree taking table shots instead of family ones. On the sponsor wall the printed promise of dignity and shelter shone in the candlelight as if nobody had just argued over who would pay for truth.

Beni took the service stairs because he knew them better than any formal route in the house. On the half-landing he passed Freya carrying a crate of dessert spoons against her hip, limping openly now that she thought she was out of sight. She gave him one quick look at his empty lapel where the radio had been and understood enough not to ask. The dumbwaiter rattled. Somebody in the kitchen called for more beurre blanc. Upstairs a donor laughed with his whole throat. The night had not paused to let his life break in it.

In the mews the rain had started again, finer now but steadier, needling the black paint of the cars and drawing silver lines down the catering van doors. Beni stood under the weak yellow light by the bins and listened to the house behind him go on earning its own story. He had one hundred and seventy pounds less than silence used to be worth and no shift in the morning worth counting on. He had no key in his pocket for the side door. No next instruction. No respectable sentence to put between himself and what he had done.

The practical disaster had already started. Laci would still want rent. Mercer would hear by morning. Eszter would not forgive him because unemployment was not virtue and confession did not make her housing safer retroactively. None of that changed because he had finally put objects on a table and said yes when asked a factual question.

Still, when he stepped out of the mews and into the wet Belgravia street, the air felt different in his chest, not cleaner, not lighter, but no longer rented. He turned away from the front door and walked because walking was what remained once the car, the keys, and the house had been taken back.
