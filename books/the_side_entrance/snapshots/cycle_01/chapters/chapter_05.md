# Chapter 5

Beni had forty minutes between getting home and the time Eszter said she would be at the chicken shop on West Green Road. Forty minutes was enough to strip the blanket off the sofa bed, shake the stale sleep out of it at the window, and learn again that damp plaster and old frying oil were stronger than lemon spray from the pound shop. He shoved his Mercer tie into the drawer under the television stand, kicked Laci's spare work boots behind the armchair, and folded yesterday's shirt so its collar stains did not show. The room still looked like what it was: a front room rented as a bedroom because London could always find one more way to charge a man for standing upright indoors.

The buses made the glass shiver every few minutes. Somebody downstairs was frying something sweet with too much cinnamon. Somewhere in the building a child cried in short outraged bursts, stopped, started again. Beni stood in the middle of the room with the blanket in his hands and tried to decide whether he had made it better or only arranged the embarrassment into straighter lines.

At noon, in the Ashdown basement kitchen, Eszter had said it like an instruction.

"I'm finishing at six. There's a place near Seven Sisters that panics before closing and drops the chicken price. I'll get one. You buy potatoes and sour cream. Don't do anything stupid like light candles."

He had looked up from the slice of bread he was eating. "Why would I light candles?"

"Because you're nineteen and frightened."

"I'm not frightened."

"Then don't clean too hard either. I hate entering a room that looks like a murder happened and then somebody got ashamed of it."

Now, with the blanket smelling faintly of spray and the window letting in wet air that was not much cleaner than the room, he understood that she had known exactly what he would do anyway.

He checked his wallet before he left. Twenty-six pounds and some coins: enough for potatoes, sour cream, bread if they needed it, and two lagers if he did not look too long at the better shelf. He put the coins in his right pocket so he would not have to count them in front of the cashier twice.

Eszter was waiting outside the chicken shop with a blue supermarket bag in one hand and a canvas tote in the other, her sleeves pushed up and leaving red elastic marks on her forearms. She had changed out of her work black into jeans and an old green jumper with one cuff gone loose. The hems were dark with rain. A bunch of parsley stuck out of the tote like something trying to escape. When she shifted the bag from one hand to the other, he heard the clink of a tin against a jar.

"You came," she said.

"You told me to."

"That isn't the same thing."

The chicken shop window was fogged with grease. Behind the glass a handwritten sign promised "FAMILY BOX REAL TASTE ENJOYMENT," the last word squeezed in sideways where there had not been room.

Eszter tilted her head at it. "Real taste enjoyment. That's serious English."

"Maybe the fake taste sold out."

She gave him a look that was half surprise, half approval. "Good. You do know how to talk without being interrogated."

The warmth from the bag reached him when she handed it over. The chicken inside was whole, yellow-skinned, still faintly warm from the rotisserie even though she meant to cut it up and drown it in sauce. The tote held three onions, the paprika tin, a small packet of bay leaves, and an apple with a bruise blooming down one side. He noticed the apple because it seemed like Eszter to carry one around all day and then forget to eat it.

"You bought everything," he said.

"No. I bought the important things. You can still contribute potatoes. Unless Tottenham has banned vegetables."

They went into the off-licence next door because it sold more than alcohol if you knew where to look. Potatoes were in a crate by the drinks fridge, dusty and beginning to sprout at the eyes. Sour cream sat beside sandwich meat and energy drinks. Beni chose the cheapest tub that did not expire tomorrow, a kilo of potatoes, and, after standing with the lager shelf open too long, two cans with dented sides because they were forty pence less.

Eszter watched him put the good sour cream back and take the cheaper one.

"Don't buy poison to prove a point," she said.

"It's fine."

"Fine is not a flavor."

She swapped the tub without asking and put the better one in the basket. Eighty-nine pence instead of sixty-five. He opened his mouth.

"If this ruins your financial plan, I'll pay the difference," she said. "Relax."

He paid anyway, coins first, notes after. She did not look away while he counted. That was one of the things about her. She never performed delicacy like it was kindness. If there was something to see, she saw it.

Outside, the rain had thinned to a wet shine on the pavement. Men stood under the bus shelter eating from white clamshell boxes with steam coming up against their faces. Traffic dragged and swore. West Green Road smelled of diesel, wet cardboard, and sweet bread from the Turkish bakery on the corner. Beni took the heavy bag from her, and she let him.

"How bad is it?" she asked as they walked.

"What?"

"Your room. The way you're carrying that chicken like it contains bad news."

He kept his eyes on the pavement where rainwater filled the broken parts in black shapes. "It's not a room. It's the front room."

"I assumed Buckingham Palace was unavailable."

"Laci has the bedroom."

"That makes sense. He's older and probably snores like machinery."

"You haven't met him."

"I know men who work construction. They all sound like they've swallowed loose bolts."

He laughed before he could help it. She heard it and smiled without turning it into a victory.

"It's damp," he said.

"Everywhere is damp."

"There's no privacy."

"Now that is useful information."

He glanced at her. "You can still go home."

"I know. I'm not a hostage."

The reply should have stung. Instead it steadied him a little. She was not pretending she had drifted here accidentally. She was here because she had decided to be.

By the time they reached the building the late light had gone the color of dishwater. The hallway smelled of bleach laid over old cabbage. Three takeaway menus had been shoved through the letter slot and lay dampening on the mat. One had "WE DELIVERS HAPPINESS QUICKLY" in red capitals across the top.

Eszter bent, picked it up, and snorted. "No, they don't."

"Maybe slowly."

"Then they should say that. I'd trust it more."

He unlocked the flat and let her in first because there was no graceful way to block her view. The room was exactly as he had feared: the sofa folded out into his bed, the blanket tucked too tightly in a way that announced recent panic, the radiator crowded with drying socks, the wall by the window bubbled under the paint where damp kept returning. A suitcase sat half under the coffee table. The cheap chest of drawers leaned a little left because one foot was shorter than the others. Laci's spare plastering overalls hung behind the door like a second man waiting.

Beni put the bags down. "It's temporary."

Eszter looked once around the room, not hurrying, not pretending. She put her tote on the table, shrugged out of her coat, and touched the damp wall with the back of one finger.

"Temporary is generous," she said. "This wall is trying to become soup."

Heat rose up Beni's neck.

Then she turned toward him, practical as ever. "Where's the biggest pot?"

He stared. "That's all?"

"What else did you want? An inspection report?"

"No."

"Good. Then get the pot."

He had one decent pot and a frying pan with a handle that wobbled if you forgot and lifted it too fast. She examined both, made a face at the knife, and tied her hair back tighter with the elastic she had been wearing on her wrist.

"This knife has given up on life," she said.

"It still cuts."

"That isn't a recommendation."

She put him to work at once. Potatoes peeled. Onions sliced. Chicken jointed at the counter space beside the sink, which was really only the bit of laminate not yet stolen by the kettle and the dish rack. He had expected awkwardness, some hovering stage where they would both have to invent reasons to touch the same cupboard or laugh at the same thing. Instead the work arrived and took up the space. She salted the chicken hard, rubbing it in with the flat of her hand. He peeled onions and tried not to watch the flex of her wrists, the way she moved as if kitchens made sense in her body even when the kitchen was no more than a bad corner and a sticky hob.

He was used to instructions. Left here. Wait downstairs. Pick up Bea at four fifteen. But in the car instructions turned him into part of the mechanism. With Eszter it was different. "Pass me that," "hold this," "taste it now" kept pulling him back into the room instead of wiping him out of it.

The onions hit the pan and started giving themselves up. She took the pan off the heat before opening the paprika, looked at him over her shoulder, and said, "If you burn this, I will insult your entire bloodline."

"I know how paprika works."

"Do you? Excellent. Then you can marry a Hungarian woman over forty and destroy your life properly."

The words landed oddly in him. Not because she had meant anything by them. Probably she had not. But he felt his hands pause over the potatoes.

She saw it at once. "What?"

"Nothing."

"That answer is getting boring."

He cut another potato just to have something to look at. "I'm not marrying anyone."

"Nobody asked you to. I was explaining the stakes of burnt paprika."

"You make everything sound dangerous."

"Most things are."

She spooned the red powder into the pan. The smell rose up warm and dusty and sweet, instantly deeper than the room had been a second before. She stirred fast, added onion, then water, then the chicken pieces, and the pot began its thick wet muttering. The paprika stained the wooden spoon the color of brick. Steam touched the window and clouded out the estate across the road.

"Taste," she said.

He did. It was not finished, not near, but already it had shape.

"More salt," he said.

"Good. You do have a tongue."

He found himself smiling into the pot.

While it simmered she opened the cupboard above the sink, found tea bags, rejected them, and took one of the takeaway menus from the table instead. She flattened it with her palm and read in a grave voice.

"Chef's Burger Coming With Smile Potato."

"What's smile potato?"

"Chips that know they're trapped."

He laughed again. She kept reading.

"Free chicken pieces for order upper twenty pound." Upper twenty pound. Very grand.

"You have to go above the money."

"Ascend to the chicken."

She folded the leaflet and used it to fan steam away from her face. "These places always sound like they're threatening you with dinner."

"Maybe the food is fine and only the English suffers."

"That is also London."

He leaned against the counter with the knife in his hand and looked at her because she was busy reading the leaflet aloud in a deadpan so dry it made the room seem less wet. He realized after a second that he was simply looking. Not checking whether she was bored, not bracing for mockery, not waiting for an order. Looking because he wanted to.

She caught him at it. "What?"

"Nothing."

"Again? You're wasting the word."

"I didn't know you'd actually come."

That made her stop fanning the steam. She looked at him more directly then, the joke set aside but not dropped.

"Why not?"

He shrugged. The knife handle was slick in his palm from potatoes.

"Because of this place?" she said.

He gave the smallest nod.

"Beni." She set the leaflet down. "I work in a house where people apologize if the tulips open too fast. I know the difference between embarrassment and an actual problem."

He said, before he could shape it better, "You're older."

There was half a beat of silence, then she barked one surprised laugh.

"Jesus Christ. Is that what this is?"

He wished at once he had said anything else. "I didn't mean..."

"No, tell me exactly what you meant."

He put the knife down. There was nowhere good to rest his hands. "You're twenty-seven."

"And?"

"And I'm nineteen."

"I'm aware. I can count faster than you think."

He stared at the pot. The lid rattled once and settled.

"Do you think I walked here by accident," she said, less amused now, "and only just now discovered your date of birth?"

"No."

"Do you think I need protecting from a sofa bed and one rude cousin?"

"No."

"Then stop saying no like you're asking permission. Say the real thing."

The real thing was ugly in his throat. Too young. Too poor. Too easy to place. A boy pretending his wages made him a man because the suit fit well enough if he kept his shoulders back. He could feel the room around him as if it had moved in closer: the radiator damp, the hanging socks, the window that did not shut properly unless you pushed at the top corner.

"I don't want you to come here and realize you made a mistake," he said.

She looked at him for long enough that he had to meet her eyes.

"All right," she said. "That is at least a sentence."

Then she turned the flame lower under the pot and reached past him for the sour cream. "Next time say that first. It saves time."

The tightness in him did not vanish, but it changed shape. Less like panic. More like being held in place.

She mixed flour and sour cream in a mug to keep it from splitting. He sliced bread. She found the apple in her bag, cut the bruise off with the bad knife, and ate the rest standing at the counter.

"You carried an apple around all day and forgot about it," he said.

"I didn't forget. I postponed."

"That's not better."

"It is if you're busy."

Her phone buzzed once on the table. She glanced at it, typed two quick lines with her knuckle because her fingers were wet, and put it face down again. No explanation. He liked that too, the lack of performance. If there was a life outside the room, she did not brandish it or conceal it for effect.

By the time the paprikash was done the room smelled of onion, pepper, chicken fat, and the sour edge of cream turning soft in the heat. It covered the damp almost completely. They ate at the table because there was only one chair and the armchair was full of folded laundry, so he sat on the edge of the bed and she took the chair and turned it sideways, knees nearly touching his. He served her first because the habit was quicker than thought. She noticed, but only by lifting one eyebrow.

"You can feed yourself too," she said.

"I know."

"Good. Then take more. You look like a greyhound."

He did. The first mouthful was too hot and burned the roof of his mouth, but he did not care. The sauce was thick and sweet and sharper at the back from the paprika. The potatoes had gone soft at the edges and held the sauce like they had been built for it. He had been hungry since before lunch, though he had not named it as hunger until the bowl was in his hands.

Eszter watched him eat with an expression he could not sort out at first. Not amusement exactly. Not pity. Appraisal, maybe, but without the cold part. As if appetite told her something worth knowing.

"See?" she said. "You weren't going to make this for yourself."

"I could."

"You were going to eat toast standing up over the sink."

He wanted to deny it. He was still chewing, so honesty got there first. "Maybe."

"Exactly."

He looked down into the bowl because her gaze had become difficult in a new way. Most of the time when someone studied him there was an angle to it. Employers checking punctuality. Clients checking discretion. Men at the depot checking whether he could be trusted with a better route. Even desire, when it had come to him before, had usually arrived with the feeling that he was being chosen for a use he would only understand too late. This was quieter and somehow harder to bear. She looked as if the fact of him eating, sitting there, existing in the bad room he had tried and failed to improve, interested her for its own sake.

The key hit the lock twice before it found the hole.

Beni knew from that alone that Laci had been drinking.

The door pushed inward. Laci came in with one orange earplug still tucked behind his left ear and a plastic sandwich bag of bent screws in his hand, as if he had picked them up from site rather than let them get thrown away. His jacket smelled of damp plaster and lager. He stopped three steps in, took in the table, the steam, Eszter in the chair, the blanket folded almost carefully at the bed's foot.

"Fuck me," he said in Hungarian. "He scrubbed the room."

Beni set his bowl down. "We made food."

"I can smell that. I said the room."

Eszter held out her hand before Beni could decide whether introductions would make this better or worse. "Eszter."

Laci stared half a second, then shifted the screws to his other hand and shook with surprising care, like he knew his palm was rougher than necessary. "Laci."

"Take your boots off," she said, glancing at the wet prints starting on the floorboards. "Unless you want plaster in the dinner."

He barked a laugh and toed the boots off by the door.

"You hear that?" he said to Beni. "I leave you alone one day and a competent person appears."

"Sit or don't," Eszter said. "But stop dripping."

It could have gone bad there. Beni felt the possibility of it as clearly as a pothole under tyres, the split second where you knew the car could go one way or the other. Laci looked at her again, more awake now under the drink. Then he set the bag of screws in the margarine tub on the radiator and rubbed at the plaster dust caught in his brow.

"There's enough?" he asked.

"If you don't complain while eating," she said.

"Then no, probably not."

But he took a bowl when Beni gave him one and sat on the arm of the armchair because there was nowhere else. He ate too fast for the first few bites, as if he had meant to say something uglier and the food interrupted him.

"Good," he said at last.

"Try not to sound shocked," Eszter said.

"I'm shocked he owns paprika."

"I brought it."

"That tracks."

He ate more, blew out a breath, and leaned back. Up close the early lines around his mouth showed under the stubble. His eyes were bloodshot with site dust and tiredness more than drink. He looked older than thirty-two in the flat light. When he shifted his weight, he touched his right knee first with a quick practiced rub, as if it had been aching long enough to need no discussion.

"Foreman docked us for a broken spirit level," he said to nobody in particular. "As if we ate the fucking thing."

"Maybe you did," Eszter said. "You sound hungry enough."

"I was hungry enough till five minutes ago."

Then he looked at Beni, and the room tightened again.

"Friday," he said.

Beni's face went hot. "I know."

"Knowing isn't money."

Eszter did not look away to spare him. She spooned more sauce onto her plate and said, "Then let him eat before you invoice him. Debt sounds worse when the food is good."

Laci's mouth twitched. Not a smile exactly. Something close enough to pass.

"You work in one of those rich houses too?" he asked her.

"Yes."

"Then you've seen worse than this."

"I've seen cleaner," she said. "Worse is a different category."

Laci grunted, accepting that. He finished the bowl, mopped the rest with bread, and stood. "If you come again, bring a better knife. This one is insulting the chicken."

"I already said that."

"Then you're ahead of him."

He picked up one lager from the table, shook it beside his ear to guess whether it had gone warm, and headed for the bathroom in his socks. The flat had thin walls and a thinner sense of dignity. When the bathroom door shut, the pipe in the kitchen knocked twice. Water hammered into the tub with no subtlety at all.

Beni stared at the table.

"You can breathe," Eszter said.

"Sorry."

"No."

He looked up. "What?"

"Don't do that."

"Do what?"

"Keep apologizing every time your life makes a noise."

He picked at a loose bit of label on the lager can. "He shouldn't have said that."

"About Friday? Why not? Friday exists."

"Not like that."

She pushed her empty bowl away. "Beni, I count money too. I know what rent sounds like when people are behind. It isn't contagious."

The shower stopped. Laci coughed hard once, spat into the sink, ran the tap again.

"It's not just the rent," Beni said quietly.

"I know."

"Then what?"

She leaned back in the chair and looked around the room once, taking it all in again: the suitcase, the socks on the radiator, the blanket, the warped drawers.

"Then this is what you have right now," she said. "It's not a trick. It's not a moral test. It's a damp front room in Tottenham with one cousin, one bad knife, and decent paprikash. I can either be here or not be here."

The bathroom door opened. Laci came out toweling his face with a T-shirt, nodding at them as if the room were his and not his and he had stopped caring which. He took the second lager and limped, very slightly, to the bedroom. A minute later Hungarian football commentary started through the wall, turned down low enough to count as a gesture and loud enough to remain a fact.

The noise should have killed whatever the evening had been. Instead it settled into the background with the buses and the pipes and the building's general complaint. Eszter stood, collected the bowls, and took them to the sink.

"Leave it," Beni said.

"No. You cooked too."

"I mostly peeled things."

"Exactly. Shared labor. Modern romance."

He took the bowls from her anyway and washed while she dried because the sink was too small to let them both do anything else. Their elbows touched and separated, touched again. The water ran hot for thirty seconds, then lukewarm. A bit of chicken skin clung to the drain. She shook out the tea towel and slapped it once over his wrist when he tried to set a wet bowl straight onto the table.

"You really do live like a man on probation," she said.

"From what?"

"Adulthood. Pleasure. Basic housekeeping. Choose one."

He laughed despite himself. "You're very encouraging."

"I didn't come to encourage you. I came to eat."

He put the last bowl down to dry. The room felt smaller after the washing up, not because there was less space than before, but because they had used so much of it. Her coat over the chair, his hands smelling of onions, the spoon reddened with paprika on the plate beside the stove. Signs of occupation. Not service. Not waiting. A life, for one evening at least, behaving as if it were allowed indoors.

She picked up another menu from the mat pile and read, "Burger meal with free delightedly."

"What does that mean?"

"It means no one editing this has slept enough."

"That's fair."

She folded the leaflet small and put it under the wobbling chest of drawers foot, improving the room by accident. Then she straightened, looked pleased with herself for half a second, and reached for her coat.

"I should go before your cousin decides football requires singing."

The sentence dropped too quickly into him.

"It's still early," he said.

"It's nearly ten."

"I can walk you."

"I assumed you would. The hallway smells like old soup."

He helped her into the coat because the room was too cramped for elegance. His fingers brushed the back of her neck under the collar, just once, because her hair had caught. She did not move away. He was suddenly aware of everything at once: the warmth still coming off the stove, the football murmur through the wall, the wet cuffs of her jeans not fully dry, the place on his own shirt where steam from the sink had dampened it.

At the door she paused, glanced back into the room, and said, "Buy another sour cream next time. This one barely survived you."

"Next time?"

"Don't be stupid."

They went down the stairs single file because the stairwell was narrow and the banister sticky with old gloss paint. The window on the landing had been painted shut years ago and kept a skin of grime that turned the streetlamp outside into a weak yellow blur. Somewhere below, a television laugh track rose and fell. The building held all its lives badly, with no insulation thick enough to protect anyone from anyone else.

At the first landing Eszter stopped and turned. Beni nearly stepped into her.

"What?" he said.

He had meant it neutrally. It came out careful, almost defensive, as if he were already bracing for some sensible end to the evening. Thank you for dinner. See you tomorrow. Don't worry about the room.

She looked at him for a second with that same directness she had carried all night, the refusal to soften facts into something more flattering. Then she took the front of his shirt in her fist, not hard, just enough to anchor him where he stood, and kissed him.

It was not tentative. It was also not theatrical. Her mouth was warm from the stairwell heat and still tasted faintly of paprika and lager foam. He felt the paint-cold banister at his back through his shirt, the rough knit of her jumper cuff against the side of his wrist, the quick shocked opening in his own chest that had nothing to do with surprise at the kiss itself. Desire did not surprise him. He knew desire. He knew how fast it could turn stupid and how much damage could fit inside a body wanting another body.

What hit him was the looking that had led to it. She had seen the room. The blanket tucked too tight. The damp wall. Laci and Friday and the bad knife and the entire humiliating layout of his life. She had watched him eat as if hunger did not make him small. She had heard him say the worst sentence he had in him and had not mistaken it for a trick. Then she kissed him like all of that had been included, not excused, and not enough to send her elsewhere.

She drew back only far enough to see his face.

"You look robbed," she said softly.

He could not think of a useful answer.

One side of her mouth lifted. "What, you thought I brought paprika for the architecture?"

That got a breath out of him that was almost a laugh and almost not.

"I should go," she said.

"Yes."

"That sounded disappointed."

"Maybe."

"Good."

She kissed him once more, shorter this time, then let go of his shirt and started down the stairs. He stood where he was until she reached the bottom and looked up.

"Tomorrow," she said.

He nodded because speech had deserted him in a way that did not feel like failure.

Then she was out through the building door and into the wet yellow street, gone under the streetlamp shine toward the bus stop with her shoulders set against the damp.

Beni stayed on the landing a moment longer, his hand still half lifted from where it had failed to find anywhere to rest on her. The stairwell smelled of dust, boiled cabbage, and the ghost of her coat after rain. Above him the football commentary lifted in sudden outrage through Laci's wall. A child somewhere in the building laughed so hard it turned into a cough.

When he went back upstairs the room was the same room: damp in the wall corner, radiator ticking, two clean bowls on the drainer, blanket coming loose already at one edge. Nothing had improved except that now the place held evidence against him. Someone had come there on purpose. Someone had eaten at his table, mocked his menus, told his cousin to take off his boots, kissed him on the landing. It made a different life imaginable in one ugly clean stroke.

That was the dangerous part. Not wanting her. Wanting had never been the worst of it. The dangerous part was understanding, all at once, how much he wanted to be seen whole and not immediately put to work by it, and how little protection there was against losing a thing like that once it existed.
