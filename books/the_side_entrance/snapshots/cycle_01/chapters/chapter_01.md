# Chapter 1

Before Nina Ashdown came out, the Mercedes still belonged to Beni.

He had it idling in the service lane behind the gala venue, not on the lit sweep where the donors climbed into photographs and thanked one another for saving the world in black tie. The back lane was for the other traffic: florists shoving empty buckets into vans, caterers smoking beside stacked dessert trays, drivers in dark suits looking at their phones without ever appearing to look up. Rain had dried and started again twice since ten. The stone under the rear tires held a greasy shine. Water kept needling down from the gutter onto the roof in an exact little tap that the insulated cabin turned faint and expensive.

He liked the half minute before a passenger got in. Mirrors, curb, route sheet, climate control, the clean line of the bonnet under the yellow security light. Left palm at eleven o'clock, right hand loose near the gear selector, body settled into the seat so the movement of the engine came through the base of his spine and nowhere else. Roads made claims he could answer. A lane closed, a bus cut wide, a cyclist came up blind on the inside: all of that had shape. Rich people mostly arrived after the shape was gone.

Mercer had sent him at nine with a route card and one line of instruction from dispatch: `ASHDOWN, N. / POST-EVENT / SIDE EXIT`. No return time. No room for extras. If Mrs. Ashdown came out when she was meant to and let herself be driven to Belgravia, he could be back at the depot before one, hand over keys, take the night bus north, and still get four hours before the first Central European builders in Laci's flat began crashing pans and swearing into the kettle. If the run turned stupid, it turned invisible first. Two extra hours on a client who changed her mind in the back seat could disappear from the week without twenty quid ever attaching itself to his name.

Through the rain-blurred glass he watched people leak from the side door in ones and twos. A woman in a sequined cape laughed so hard she bent double and then stood up with mascara on the inside of her wrist. A man in a velvet jacket argued with nobody visible, finger stabbing the wet air, then got into the wrong Range Rover and had to be guided back out by a driver with the patient hands of a nurse. Nobody apologized. The drivers were there for that. They held doors, took handbags, found dropped phones under seats, offered water to women who had been crying five seconds earlier and pretended not to hear men saying the sorts of things they would have denied in daylight. Mercer kept packets of stain wipes and black paper sick bags in every car. Wealth produced the same mess as anything else. It only expected cleaner witnesses.

At half past eleven a girl came out and stopped under the awning as if she had no intention of going farther. Seventeen, maybe. Thin, dark-eyed, wearing a black dress that had been chosen by somebody older and school-black patent shoes she had either refused to change or changed back into out of spite. She peeled the silver label off a bottle of still water in one careful strip and wound it round two fingers until it cut her skin pink. When a photographer at the far corner lifted his camera on instinct, she turned her whole body away from him without even looking. A few minutes later somebody inside called, "Bea," and she went back in with the bottle still uncapped.

Beni had just checked the time again when Nina Ashdown appeared.

She did not stumble. That was the first thing about her. She came through the service door with a charity volunteer behind her, a young woman still clutching a clipboard and worrying the lifted corner of a false eyelash as she spoke at Nina's shoulder, and she kept moving until the volunteer had to stop in the doorway and let the rain have her. Tall, dark blond, the kind of woman who looked arranged even when the arrangement had come loose. Her black dress was simple until you looked at how the silk held the light. One earring was missing. The badge from the gala still sat pinned at her waist as if she had forgotten or refused to take off the evidence. She had her heels in one hand by the straps, and there was blood on the back of one ankle where a blister had opened.

The volunteer said, "Mrs. Ashdown, shall I have someone call your husband?"

Nina didn't turn. "You can have someone do whatever helps you sleep."

Beni was already out of the driver's seat. By the time he reached her she had put one heel back on and was failing to fasten the other one without bending far enough. He took the shoes from her before she had to ask. She let him, which was more intimate than thanks.

"Good evening, Mrs. Ashdown."

She looked at his face for exactly long enough to confirm he existed. "Am I meant to know where my car is?"

"Here."

He had the rear door open. She got in with a controlled collapse, gathering the dress under her knees so the fabric would not catch. The car dipped once on its suspension and held. When he bent to set the shoes on the floor mat, the smell that came off her was champagne, wet wool, expensive perfume, and the hot metallic edge of somebody who had been angry for hours and was only now getting properly drunk.

"Home?" he asked.

She leaned her head back. Her eyes were closed. "For the moment."

That was enough instruction to move. He shut her in, went round the bonnet, and slid behind the wheel. The cabin sealed with the soft pressurized hush rich passengers paid for without ever naming it. He checked the mirror, eased out past a florist van, and followed the line of taillights toward the gate.

In the mirror she had both hands over her face, fingers spread against her forehead as if she were holding the skull together. Her nails were pale and neat except for the thumb, where the polish had a ragged bite mark taken out of one side. On the seat beside her she had dropped a gold clutch, a folded paper program, and the missing earring, which had come loose from somewhere in the folds of the dress and was making slow arcs every time he took a turn.

"Water?" he asked.

"Please."

He passed the chilled bottle back without looking fully round. She drank too fast. When he stopped at the light by the gate she coughed, swore softly, and then laughed at herself in a voice that had not been aimed at him and landed on him anyway.

"That room," she said. "Christ. Another hundred people congratulating themselves for enjoying canapes in aid of girls they've never had to smell."

Beni pulled out onto the road. Rain polished the lane markings into something almost blue. "Would you like the temperature cooler?"

"No. If I get any colder, I'll calcify."

She set the water bottle down. A second later she picked it back up and aligned it carefully along the strip of walnut beside her thigh, label turned inward so the blue logo disappeared. Then she leaned forward between the seats.

"Did Oscar leave already?"

It was the first question of the night and it was impossible to answer correctly. Beni kept his eyes on the traffic ahead, a knot of black cabs under the lights by the park. "I didn't see, Mrs. Ashdown."

"No, of course you didn't." She sat back. "That's not criticism. It's practically a sacrament in this city, not seeing."

He took a line through Knightsbridge that kept them moving. Park Lane was thick with departing event traffic and taxis nose to nose. He preferred long sightlines when somebody drunk was in the back. Stopped cars made people bold. Motion used up their certainty. He could read the spread of brake lights half a mile ahead and feel, before the satnav said anything, where a lane would give him enough room to breathe.

At Sloane Street she said, "Not that way."

He changed lanes cleanly. "Which way would you like?"

"Anywhere but home."

He let the indicator tick twice before canceling it. The muscles between his shoulders went careful under his shirt, the old response to a woman drinking in his back seat and not yet saying where she meant to land. "Would you like me to keep driving for a bit?"

"That's what I just said."

"Yes, Mrs. Ashdown."

She laughed again, but this time the sound had a hook in it. "You're not one of those men who tries to rescue women from a bad decision, are you?"

"No."

"Good. Rescue is theater. Men love it because they get to feel innocent."

She took off the second heel and shoved both shoes under the opposite seat. Bare feet now, one ankle bleeding lightly against the cream mat. Beni saw the red print begin in the mirror and hated it on the car's behalf before he hated himself for that. He kept a box of wipes in the side compartment. That was the job. Clean after.

He drove over Chelsea Bridge because the river road was freer than the center and because bridges gave people the brief illusion that they were going somewhere absolute. London spread out in wet fragments under them: black water, orange works lights, the lit windows of towers too new to have collected any sadness yet. Nina had her phone in one hand. The screen kept brightening, dimming, brightening again. Once she made a noise through her nose that might have been disgust or could have been laughter and stabbed her thumb at the glass hard enough to rock her shoulder.

"If one more donor tells me art is a conversation starter," she said, "I might actually commit a crime."

He said nothing. The rule was not silence itself. The rule was proportion. Give the passenger enough reply that they did not feel ignored, not enough that they had to notice the driver as a man with a mind. Beni had learned that a well-placed yes could be felt as care, contempt, or furniture depending on who was in the seat. Tonight she did not want agreement. She wanted the shape of witness without the complication of another human need leaning back at her.

At the lights by Battersea she leaned forward again. Her hair had begun to come loose at one temple. The skin under her makeup looked tired rather than old, and the difference mattered because she would know it mattered.

"Do you ever get bored," she asked, "hearing rich people break their own hearts in your back seat?"

"I'm driving."

That made her smile properly for the first time. "Very diplomatic."

"It's easier if I watch the road."

"For you, maybe."

She sat back and let the silence stretch. He expected her to fill it. Instead she lowered the window an inch and let damp air cut in. Traffic hissed by. Somewhere outside, a siren moved along the river and away.

By Vauxhall her phone rang. She looked at the screen, turned the sound off, and let it ring itself dead in her hand.

"That'll be Oscar," she said. "Or somebody who works for Oscar and believes in verbs like manage." She put the phone down on the seat and immediately picked it up again. "Actually, no. It isn't him. He notices absence only when it affects a room."

She opened a text thread and stared at it. Beni caught only blocks of light in the mirror, not words. She typed. Deleted. Typed again.

"Do you know Hackney Wick?" she asked.

He did not react quickly enough to be rude, but the pause existed. She saw it.

"You do."

"Yes."

"Good. Go there."

The job tightened around him. Belgravia to Hackney Wick at nearly midnight was not a misunderstanding. It was a change of story. Mercer wanted route changes noted. Mercer wanted if possible to know where the cars were and why. Mercer also wanted clients who tipped at Christmas and asked for the company by name. Those desires sat on the same line until they didn't.

"Would you like me to update dispatch?" he asked.

She made a small sound of impatience. "No. I'd like you to turn right at the next lights and stop speaking like a consent form."

"Yes, Mrs. Ashdown."

"And don't 'Mrs. Ashdown' me quite so hard. I'm drunk, not dead."

He took the turn.

They ran east along the river because the roads were cleaner there and because it gave her windows full of London rather than tunnels of private stucco. The city changed in bands. White terraces and embassies gave way to office glass, then construction hoardings slick with rain, then the late-night spill of people who would never see the inside of a gala but still paid to orbit its edges: kitchen porters in paper hats waiting for night buses, women smoking in heels outside hotels after shifts that had started before breakfast, boys on hire bikes with takeaway bags steaming on their backs. Beni knew this map better than he knew the inside of anybody's feeling. He knew where a bus lane became dangerous after eleven, where cameras caught illegal turns, where the road surface near Blackfriars had a seam that lifted into the wheel if you took it too fast.

He felt the car through his hands and shoulders, the slight drag of standing water, the smooth return of the steering after each turn. With roads he never had to guess what somebody meant by still or careful or enough. The machine told him when he had misjudged it. People let you find out after the damage.

Behind him Nina called someone. The line rang. Rang again.

"Pick up," she said, softly at first. "Come on. Don't be theatrical." Then, after another beat: "Jonas. For fuck's sake."

The name settled into the cabin and stayed there.

She ended the call and made another one immediately. Straight to voicemail this time. She waited through the greeting, then said nothing at all, just breathed once hard enough for him to hear it, and hung up.

"He'll be working," she said after a while, and Beni could not tell if she was speaking to him or fixing the excuse in place for herself. "He likes to announce that he works at night, as if no one else in London has ever been tired before."

The windshield filled briefly with the red wash of brake lights. He eased them down and across, through a lane narrowing by roadworks, then back into open road.

"He's a sculptor?" he asked.

He had not meant to. The question left his mouth because he needed to know what sort of building he was looking for when they got there, and because the name had already made a room in the car.

Nina gave a short, surprised laugh. "There you are. I was beginning to think Mercer had grown you in a cupboard."

"Sorry."

"Don't be. Yes. Sculptor, if we are being generous. He makes large objects that suggest injury to expensive people and then pretends to dislike the people who buy them." She rubbed under one eye with the heel of her hand and looked at the smear on her skin as if it belonged to somebody else. "That's unfair. He's talented. Which is often much worse."

She watched the city go by for a block or two more and then said, "Take the next left. I hate the A-road from here."

He took it. Narrower streets, wet brick, halal shops shuttering for the night, a minicab office still lit. She knew the route well enough to hate parts of it. That told him more than anything she had said about the man himself.

"He doesn't like these things," she said, meaning the gala, the donors, the house, perhaps her whole life. "He says rooms like that turn need into decor. Imagine being so pure you can say that in a room full of auction pledges." Her laugh came sharp now. "And still take the commission when it suits you."

She picked up the program from the seat and flicked through it one-handed. A glossy page tore under her thumb. She kept talking.

"Do you know the worst part? He was probably right. Not about himself. About the room. Every woman there had been blow-dried into the same polished plea. Every man had the same little voice on for talking about mortality and donor cultivation. We are all so touched. We are all so moved. And then they send the girls back to whatever hostel qualifies as a redemption arc and the men compare schools." She looked out at the passing warehouses. "Oscar loves those rooms. He can smell a tax efficiency from fifty feet."

Beni passed a bus stop where three kitchen staff in black trousers were sharing chips out of one paper tray. Steam lifted white into the rain. He thought about saying nothing, kept saying nothing, and felt her notice it as a usable quality.

"You really don't ask questions," she said.

"Not unless I need to."

"And do you ever need to?"

He considered the road. "Usually I know where I'm meant to go."

That made her laugh again, lower this time. "God. That's almost philosophy."

"It's only driving."

"No. It's better than philosophy. It works."

She went quiet after that, which was different from silence. She had spent whatever first burst of rage and performance had kept her upright through the gala. What stayed behind it was uglier because it was smaller. She kept turning her phone faceup, facedown, faceup again. Once she pulled the gala badge from her dress and stabbed the pin back through the cardboard hard enough to bend the point. Once she pressed her thumb to the inside of her wrist as if checking a pulse she did not trust.

Rain thickened as they crossed into the east. He slowed for a cyclist cutting wide without lights, then took a clean line between two vans half parked over the curb. The buildings flattened, widened. Brick, corrugated shutters, bits of canal black between railings. Hackney Wick announced itself by the sudden industrial blankness of the streets and by the kind of bars that wanted to look accidental. Big windows. Concrete planters. Someone's idea of ruin with investment in it.

"White Post Lane," Nina said.

He was already there in his head. "Yes."

She gave him the number before he had to ask.

The studio was in a converted warehouse by the canal, one long building cut into units with metal stairs and external walkways. Half the windows were dark. One top-floor pane glowed a raw white that made everything around it look dirtier. A bicycle hung from the railing below like somebody had forgotten it there on purpose. There was a stack of plasterboard leaning under a tarp, rain soaking through the tears in the plastic. Not Belgravia. Not any honest version of poor either. A place arranged to keep the evidence of work visible.

Beni pulled in where the curb was broken and put the car in park.

For a second Nina didn't move. The engine hummed. Rain stitched down the windshield. In the mirror her face had sharpened with the stillness. Whatever the drive had given her, the stop took it back.

"Would you like me to walk you to the door?" he asked.

"No." She was already fixing herself in the dark glass, fingertips at her mouth, at her hair, at the strap of the dress. "God, no."

She found the missing earring, put it on by touch, took it out because she had the wrong side, then put it in properly on the second try. She wiped under both eyes with the pad of her thumb and then licked the thumb clean without thinking. When she saw the blood on her ankle, she stared at it as if it were an insult somebody else had arranged.

"Tissues are in the door pocket," he said.

She took two, folded one around the back of her heel, and pressed. "Thank you."

It was the first time she had thanked him. It changed nothing.

She leaned to reach for her shoes. One had worked its way under the front passenger seat. Beni got out before she could struggle for it in that dress. Rain hit the back of his neck at once, cold through his collar. He opened her door, crouched, found the heel by touch, and handed both shoes up to her. The sole of one was slick with city water and something gritty. She slipped them on anyway, bracing a hand on his shoulder without asking. Her fingers were cool and ring-heavy and steadier than they had any right to be.

When she stood, she did not let go at once. She looked past him to the warehouse, then back at his face as if he had changed while she was in the car.

"What's your name?" she asked.

He almost said, Driver. Not out loud. Out loud he said, "Beni."

"Benny?"

"Beni. With one n."

She held his gaze for a beat too long, committing not his face but the entry on a form. "Beni."

Then she reached into her clutch for her phone. Her posture altered even before the call connected. The drunkness didn't vanish, but it put on shoes. When Mercer answered, her voice came out polished enough to step over puddles.

"Hello, it's Nina Ashdown. Sorry, I know it's late." She listened, eyes on the warehouse windows. "Yes, thank you, the car was here. I only wanted to make a note for future bookings. The driver tonight was Beni." Another pause. "Yes. Beni. I'd like him again, if possible." She listened once more and smiled without warmth. "Because he doesn't ask questions. Exactly."

The line on Beni's shoulder where her hand had rested seemed to stay cold after she moved it.

She ended the call and slipped the phone back into the clutch. "There. Administrative excellence."

He said, "Would you like me to wait?"

That nearly brought back the smile. "No. If this goes badly, an audience won't improve it."

She shut the door herself. He stood under the rain a second longer than he needed to and watched her cross the broken pavement toward the entry buzzer. She walked carefully because of the heel and because the drink had not left her as cleanly as her voice suggested. At the door she pressed the button once, then again harder. The white-lit window above did not change.

On the third buzz a figure came onto the metal walkway and looked down. Young enough that Beni felt the age gap before he could think about it. Jonas Hale, presumably. Dark hair pushed back with one wrist, grey T-shirt marked with plaster along the ribs, one ankle clipped with a cyclist's reflective strap he had forgotten to remove. He did not come down immediately. He rested his forearms on the rail and stared at Nina in a way that made the rain between them part of the conversation.

Even from the curb Beni could see that Jonas's first instinct was irritation, not concern.

Nina tipped her head back. She said something Beni couldn't hear through the shut car and the rain. Jonas answered. Short. Then he disappeared from the walkway.

By the time Beni had wiped the heel print from the mat with one of Mercer's black cloths, the downstairs door had clicked open. Nina did not look back at the car before going in.

He got behind the wheel and pulled away slowly because the street was tight and because there was no reason now to show speed. In the mirror the warehouse receded into wet brick and white light. His route sheet still said Belgravia. The return leg to the depot would be dead mileage. He had another damp hour between himself and Tottenham. None of that was what sat with him.

He had been noticed. That was all.

She had not asked his name to thank him, or because she had suddenly understood that he was nineteen and far from home and finishing a shift past midnight in a suit that smelled faintly of other people's perfume. She had asked because she had found a use she wanted repeated. The same steady wheel. The same shut mouth. The same seat to pour herself into while somebody else kept the car between the lines.

At the red light by the canal he touched the indicator and waited for it to change. Rain tapped the roof in that exact little place above his head. He looked at the blurred glow behind him where Jonas's studio still stood and understood that she had not picked him out from the dark because she respected him. She had only learned which driver could carry her shame without spilling it.
