# Chapter 4

The east corridor had a draught problem. Thin cold leaked between the stones near the third window, the one with cracked leading older than Draco's father. By late autumn he dressed for that passage without thinking, collar up, warming charm ready, body already braced for the bite.

Granger had been in the Manor for four days, and the draught was gone.

On Tuesday morning, halfway to the main staircase, Draco realized what was missing: no cold on the left side of his neck, no thin pull of air under his collar. Warmth held in the wall instead, deep and stored, summer caught in the stone.

It was November.

His palm went to the wall before he had decided to touch it. Something under the stone answered him, pulse-like and faintly alive where there should have been none. After a long second he took his hand away and finished the walk without brushing the wall again.

The library doors were already open. Both panels stood wide. Usually he had to shoulder one aside and rap the old pattern on the other if the latch had settled badly; that morning the oak waited for him like a welcome he had never once been given.

At the long table, Granger had three texts spread in a neat half-circle, quill in her left hand, wand in her right. Without looking up, she said, "The Alderic folio was on the third shelf yesterday. Red binding. Preservation charms on the spine. It's moved."

"Moved where?"

The tip of her quill indicated the table beside her. "There. I came in and it was waiting."

Across the room, a black rectangle gaped on the shelf where the book had sat for years. Dust still marked its outline. Eighteen inches from her hand, the folio leaned against a stack of astronomical journals.

"The house does that," Draco said. "It rearranges."

"For me." Only then did Hermione glance up. Interest sat on her face more clearly than alarm. "Three other books I meant to pull today are here as well. I wrote the list last night. I didn't mention it to anyone."

Nothing useful came to mind. Agreement would sound absurd. Denial would sound worse. Throughout his childhood the Manor had ferried Narcissa's reading glasses from room to room without comment, but the house had known his mother for twenty-five years. Hermione Granger had been sleeping here since Friday.

Tea gave him something to do with his hands. In the service alcove off the library, the kettle took up its ordinary muttering while he listened past it. Preservation charms ticked in the shelves. Somewhere overhead a gallery board creaked. Under both sounds sat a lower hum that had not existed last week and now seemed to live in his sternum whenever Hermione was nearby.

By the time he returned with two cups, she was at the genealogical shelves with one arm stretched overhead, fingertips grazing a volume she could not quite reach. The wall altered so subtly that it took him a second to register it. Stone, shelf, and distance shifted just enough. The top row lowered into her reach. Hermione took the book down as if nothing in the room had moved.

His tea landed beside her untouched cup. Silence felt safer than comment. Twenty-five years in the Manor had never earned him a shelf lowered to meet his hand.

---

Morning work in his father's study went nowhere. The Mark had been bad overnight and worse at dawn, a hot wired ache threading the old brand under his skin. With his sleeve rolled up, pale forearm bare, he looked down at the skull and serpent and saw that time had done nothing to it. Curse marks did not soften. Neither did memory. Riddle House rose behind his eyes with humiliating fidelity, Lucius's hand digging into his shoulder while a sixteen-year-old boy bit the inside of his mouth bloody and kept still.

Once the sleeve was pulled down again, cool fabric helped a little. The study offered no such relief. Too much of Lucius remained in the room: the chair, the desk, the arrangement of shelves maintained for years out of inertia and cowardice and the practical difficulty of deciding where else anything should go.

A measured knock broke across the quiet. Three even raps announced Granger.

Unlike the library doors, the study made her open it herself. That small resistance cheered him more than it ought to have.

In the doorway she held an old manual open against one palm. "I think this is a diagnostic text on curse marks. It's outdated, but the methodology is solid. Your great-great-grandmother wrote it."

"Cassiopeia."

Hermione looked back down at the page. "There's an inscription. Was she a Healer?"

"Not publicly. She researched privately." Family history wanted saying next and got stopped before it reached his mouth. Curiosity had drawn Hermione's attention off the text and onto the room itself, onto Lucius's desk and Lucius's shelves and Draco in Lucius's chair. "She was good at it."

"She was," Hermione said. "This will help."

The latch gave an ordinary click when she left. Once she had gone, the study felt stubbornly his again, and the comfort in that was thin enough to be embarrassing.

By afternoon the pattern had become impossible to ignore. The west gallery kept its habitual sullen delay with him. Minutes later, crossing the central hall with a sandwich in one hand and a scroll in the other, Hermione drew a bloom of warmth up through the flagstones. Window light softened around her and flattened out again as soon as she was gone. She read while walking and noticed none of it. Draco noticed too much.

Noticing did not improve his temper. For years the Manor had tolerated him with institutional reliability: food in the kitchen, heat enough to prevent pneumonia, doors that opened eventually. With Hermione it was suddenly eager, helpful, attentive. His house, his name, his inheritance, all bending themselves around a woman who had arrived with one case and a stack of Ministry notes.

The thought sent him into the smaller sitting room instead of anywhere useful. Outside, the grounds had gone brown with the last of autumn. Inside, cool grey light pooled on the carpet and left him alone with the obvious conclusion he kept trying not to reach. The Manor wanted her comfortable. It had never cared whether he was comfortable at all.

---

Narcissa arrived at half three by Floo, stepping out of the parlour hearth with the contained irritation of a woman who expected the failing network to work properly out of respect for her inconvenience. She gave the room one glance, brushed soot from her sleeve, and touched the mantel.

"The fire is running too high," she said.

"I hadn't noticed."

"No." Her fingers remained on the stone for another beat. "You would not."

Tea was implied. While the kettle heated, Narcissa made her usual circuit through the adjoining corridor, heels precise against the floor, hand sometimes drifting over stone as though reading a language only she could interpret. By the time he brought the cups back, she was standing in the doorway to the central hall and looking not at him but at the corridor beyond.

"You have a guest."

"I told you about the arrangement."

"You told me about the arrangement. You did not tell me the house had taken such a lively interest."

At her chair in the parlour, the one object the Manor never moved, she accepted the tea and waited until she had decided to answer him. "The route from the guest wing to the library is shorter. So is the route from your study. From this room as well."

"It rearranges."

Narcissa repeated the word as if testing whether it was sufficient and finding it wanting. "It has straightened every path that leads to her work."

Across from her, Draco said nothing. The pattern had been visible already; hearing it aloud made it harder to pretend it was random.

"The library has not seen real use in years," she said.

"I use the library."

"You sit in it." No malice, no softness either. Merely accuracy. "That is not the same occupation."

The correction landed because it was true. To cover the bruise of it, he said, "She's been useful with the Mark."

Something in Narcissa's expression tightened and was put away before it could become legible. "I am glad."

Once tea was over, the real visit began. Narcissa did not come to the Manor for conversation. She came to look. Through the central hall she moved without comment, though her attention skimmed temperature, light, and the resonance underfoot. At the bottom of the main stairs her hand settled on the newel post. A moment later she gave a small, dissatisfied hum and continued.

At the junction of the gallery and the east wing, her palm met the wall and stayed there.

"The draught," she said.

"Gone."

"Since when?"

"A few days."

No need existed to ask what had changed a few days ago.

The door appeared just past the turn toward the library. Dark wood stood where blank stone had been that morning, its brass handle worn at the base, its grain buried so deep in the wall that it looked rooted there. Narcissa stopped so abruptly that Draco nearly walked into her.

Recognition crossed her face before composure sealed over it.

"Mother."

"Leave it."

The words came level, but colour had gone from her fingertips where they pressed the wall.

"That door wasn't there this morning."

"No."

"You've seen this before."

"Not that door." Her gaze stayed on the handle, nowhere else. "This habit. After my wedding, sealed rooms began presenting themselves. Your grandmother called it welcome. I was young enough to find that flattering."

"And now?"

"Now I think the Manor notices where it means to spend its attention. Affection has very little to do with it." Only then did Narcissa turn away from the door. "Do not put your hand on it again."

The single word again stopped him. "You knew I would."

"I know you were raised by a man who mistook force for competence." The sentence stayed cool, but the effort of coolness showed in it. "When this house is pressed, it does not yield. It withdraws something else."

That was more than she had meant to say; the fact was audible in the thin pause afterward. Draco heard it and pushed anyway.

"What does it withdraw?"

"If another door appears, send for me. Do not test it. Do not mention it to Ms. Granger unless it opens to her first." Narcissa resumed walking before he could ask the obvious next question.

Past the library they went, doors shut now, hum still present under the stone. In the guest wing corridor Narcissa slowed near a window alcove where the wall felt warmer than the surrounding stretch, almost smoothed by use.

"Is she comfortable here?"

"She's working."

"That is not what I asked."

Morning offered itself back to him in pieces: Hermione at the long table as if the room had already admitted her, a shelf lowering to meet her reach, the kitchen claimed for sandwiches as though practicality alone could overrule history. Behind those newer impressions sat the older one he kept skirting, the image of her on the drawing room floor with blood on her arm and Bellatrix standing over her while he did nothing useful at all.

"As comfortable as anyone could be," he said, and stalled there.

What remained unsaid was obvious enough. Narcissa heard it without help.

"Yes," she said quietly. "Given."

By the time they returned to the parlour, the house had produced her cloak on the stand by the hearth. Narcissa fastened it with her mother's silver clasp and looked at him directly for the first time since the door.

"The Manor does not act out of spite," she said. "That should not tempt you to trust its intentions."

"I don't."

"See that you continue not to."

She kissed his forehead, brief and familiar enough to be almost embarrassing, and then green fire took her.

Silence settled fast after her departure. The parlour fire dropped to ordinary height. Cool air edged back into the room. Somewhere under the floor the house shifted its weight.

He went to the corridor anyway.

The new door remained where it had been. Varnish and brass met his hand, and a faint dry scent of old cedar lingered around the jamb. Narcissa's warning sat in his head and did nothing useful. After a moment's stillness his hand closed on the handle.

Resistance came at once, not the catch of a lock but the pressure of another hand closing over the handle from the other side.

Draco let go before he could decide whether he was obeying his mother or merely conceding defeat. Under his feet the corridor answered with a soft run of stone against stone, so low he almost convinced himself he had imagined it.

The library proved he had not.

Both oak doors were shut. Since Hermione's second day the room had not closed on her while she worked, not even once. Now the handles refused him completely. There was no give, no rattle, nothing. Through the wood came the scratch of her quill, one scrape of chair legs, and beneath both sounds the low hum threaded through the house like breath.

It had not locked her in. It had locked him out.

For several seconds he stood there with his hand on his own library door and did not knock. Pride accounted for part of it. The rest was worse: if he knocked and the house opened for Hermione before it opened for him, the point would become unbearable by being public.

At last he stepped back. The Manor kept humming. Somewhere beyond the oak, Granger turned a page. In the corridor outside, Draco listened to his home refuse him and understood that his mother had not been warning him about a curiosity. She had been warning him about a rule.
