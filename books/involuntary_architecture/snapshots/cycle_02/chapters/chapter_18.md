# Chapter 18

The seventh day was the worst.

Not because the Mark hurt more, though it did. The magic in it kept reaching for the diagnostic touch that was no longer there. The seventh day was worst because the house had finally stopped trying.

For six days the Manor had managed something. Not the responsive architecture he had grown used to when she was here. No shortcuts, no corridors rearranging themselves, no rooms surfacing like suggestions. There had been small signs instead: a sconce flickering in the east wing, a door that stuck and then released, a contraction in the hallway outside the library like the building clearing its throat. Signs of life, if he was generous. Last autonomic functions, if he was honest.

On the seventh day, nothing. The Manor held its shape. A house that moved was a house with opinions. This was just a house.

At ten in the morning Draco stood at the library entrance, dressed because dressing still divided one hour from the next. Trousers, shirt, shoes, the costume of someone with somewhere to go. The door had been open all week. Beyond it the windows let in what December offered, which was barely anything: flat grey light and an unbroken sheet of cloud. He had lost track of whether it was the second or the third of the month.

The archive wall gave him blank stone. His palm went to it anyway, part habit and part test. Cold met skin. Not the pointed cold of a house choosing to withhold. Just winter in old stone.

Warming charms would have helped. He had not bothered.

What he missed was not only her touch on his arm, though his body kept arranging the week around that absence. It was the sustained fact of her attention. Six weeks of her moving through the library, reorganizing shelves, arguing over translations, falling asleep over a text while he sat across from her inventing reasons not to leave. Six weeks of being looked at closely and not treated as a problem to manage or a relic to avoid. The withdrawal from that had its own humiliating clarity.

In the kitchen the tea cooperated because the kitchen always cooperated. Water boiled. The kettle steamed. A cream-coloured cup waited by the sink, slightly too wide, the handle turned wrong for his grip. Her cup. He had been using it for three days before he noticed. After that he kept using it because changing cups would have been its own confession.

The east wing corridor lay ahead in its full, indifferent length. Every door along it stayed where it had been put. The guest room she had used. The morning room his mother preferred. The narrow passage to the conservatory neither of them had ever bothered with. Halfway down stood the drawing room door, open on its hinges. He did not look in. He had not looked in all week. Still, he felt the room each time he passed it, the way you feel a loose tooth with your tongue even when you mean not to.

Back in the library, a seventeenth-century genealogical record sat open on the table. One of the accessible texts. One of the useless ones. It repeated what the bonding folio had already told him about mechanism and amplification and old Malfoy habits of arranging intimacy into structure. The harder knowledge was not in the book. It was in the fact that Hermione had found the relevant texts first, read them without him, and gone on standing close enough to touch.

For two days anger had made everything clean. She had withheld material information while examining his body. She had decided what he could bear to know. By the third day the neatness had started to rot. Doubt had not made her innocent, but it had made her recognizably human. Of course she had been frightened. Of course she had wanted proof before bringing him the worst possibility either of them had found. The trouble was that understanding her did nothing to reduce the damage.

Cold light thinned toward afternoon. His tea went untouched beside the book.

Footsteps came first. Measured. Unhurried. His mother.

Narcissa paused in the doorway in a dark coat and gloves. One look took in the whole scene: the dead grate, the blanket on the chair arm, the solitary teacup, the translation note in Hermione's hand still lying where he had found it.

"You look dreadful," she said.

That almost made him laugh. "I'm fine."

"You are not fine." She removed her gloves with precise fingers. "Has Mipsy been coming?"

"I told her not to."

A glance moved over the room again. Then she draped her coat over the chair by the door and chose to stay. "Tea?"

He made it the way she preferred, black and unsweetened, in the cup from the set she always used. When he returned, she stood at the window with one hand resting on the frame, head slightly tilted as though listening for an answer from the house. Nothing answered. After a moment she sat across from him in the chair Hermione had used for weeks, the one with worn velvet and the ink stain on the arm.

Silence held for a while. His mother knew how to use silence. So did he.

"I did not come to ask what happened," she said at last. "I know what happened. The house told me before it stopped telling anyone anything."

"The house told you."

"In its way." Her fingers adjusted on the cup. "I have not been fully honest with you, Draco."

The sentence did not sound strategic. It sounded expensive.

He set his tea down. "About the house."

"About the house. About what it does and what it cannot do." Her gaze lowered to the tea, then returned to him. "I told myself I was protecting you from knowledge you were not ready for. That was partly true. It was also convenient."

"Then say it now."

She met his eyes. Same grey. Same habit of watching before speaking.

"The Manor's bonding architecture," she said. "You have read the texts."

"I've read them."

"Then you know the mechanism. What you do not know — what the texts assume anyone in this family would already understand — is the limitation." A thumb traced the cup's rim once. "The house amplifies. It gates. It encourages. All of that is true."

"I know."

"What the house cannot do is fabricate."

The word settled in the room and stayed there.

"It cannot create a connection where none exists." For the first time she had to gather herself before the next sentence. Her thumb went still on the cup. "It can make a thing louder. It cannot put it there."

He stared at her.

"What the house responded to between you and Ms. Granger — the resonance, the examinations, whatever followed." Narcissa's gaze went to the dead grate and came back. "That was already there."

Furniture shifted in his head, heavy and slow. Her hands on his forearm. Midnight in the library, her mouth moving around a translation. The kiss, frightened and clumsy and better than anything he had expected to have again. The house had turned up the volume. The sound had been there first.

"How do I know what belongs to the base level?" he asked. "How do I separate it?"

"You do not."

"That's not an answer."

"It is the only one." Something in her voice altered, not softer exactly, but less defended. "I have lived with that question myself, Draco. There is no bottom to it."

She took a sip of tea. He watched her and realized she was not reciting family lore. She was speaking from a place in herself he had not been invited into before.

"Your father," she said.

Every part of him went still.

"Your father never resonated with the house." She said it plainly. "The bonding architecture activated when I moved in. Mildly. A few rooms reopened. The temperature altered. But the deep resonance, the mutual awareness, the gated rooms. That never came. Not once in twenty-five years."

The room did not have enough air in it.

"The house respected him. He was a Malfoy. It answered the family line even when it did not answer him." Her hand went to her cuff and back again. "But what it has been doing with you and Ms. Granger, it never did that for us."

"Mother."

"This is not a complaint." The composure held. The work of holding it showed at the corners of her mouth. "Your father and I had a real marriage. We chose it. We survived with it. I am not telling you we were lesser. I am telling you the house never answered us the way it answers what it sees between you and her."

Between you and her. She said it as fact.

"It was already there," Narcissa said more quietly. "Whatever you do with that is your affair."

He looked down at his tea and found he could not remember lifting the cup.

"She kept the texts from you. That was her error, and I will not excuse it. But she did not create the thing she was frightened of. She found it already in progress and handled that badly. People do."

At the end of that, she stood. Her skirt smoothed beneath careful palms.

"I did not come to tell you what to do. Your choices are your own." A near-smile touched her mouth and disappeared. "The house will have opinions regardless."

"Why didn't you tell me sooner?"

The pause in the doorway was real. She was deciding how much of her private life to hand him.

"Because explaining why I knew meant admitting that the house gave you something it never gave me," she said. Gloves went back on, one finger at a time. "I have had some time with that now."

By the time her coat was buttoned, the room felt rearranged even though nothing had moved.

"Your arm," she said.

He glanced at the gauze. "It'll be fine."

"It will not." She adjusted the right glove. "But you know that."

Her footsteps receded down the entrance hall. The front door opened and closed. Somewhere upstairs a pipe knocked once, and then the house kept still.

He sat with what she had given him: the grief of a woman telling her son that her marriage, real and chosen and survived, had never moved the house the way this messy, possibly broken thing had. She had carried that knowledge alone for years. Today she had set it in front of him and let him do what he liked with it.

Dusk had thickened at the windows by the time the knock came.

---

It was the side entrance off the garden. Not the front door. She knew which door was closer to the library.

Draco crossed the corridor at a pace that wanted to be a run and arrived trying not to look as if he had. Outside stood Hermione in a heavy coat, scarf wound high at her throat, leather satchel over her shoulder. Work bag first. Work bag before speech. That registered before the rest of her did.

Her gaze moved over his face, the gauze, the general state of him. She declined to comment.

"Hi," she said.

Not Malfoy. Just hi.

"Hi."

Cold air held between them for one beat too long.

"I should've told you," she said. Her eyes were somewhere near his collar. "About the texts. All of it. I should've told you the day I found them."

"You should have."

A flinch crossed her face at the plainness of it. "I told myself I needed to verify. Some of that was true. Most of it was fear." She drew a breath that did not steady anything. "I thought if I said it out loud too soon, you'd pull back and that would be the end of it. So I made the decision for both of us and pretended I was being responsible."

The house offered no warmth, no convenient shift in the corridor. It left them alone with the words.

"My mother was here," he said.

Hermione looked up properly then.

"She told me what the house can do. And what it can't."

She waited. That waiting was familiar from the examinations. Not passive. Attentive. Gathering.

"It can't fabricate." Saying it aloud changed the air in his own mouth. "It can amplify. It can shove and gate and make a nuisance of itself. It can't make something from nothing."

A long second passed.

"All right," she said softly. "That matters."

"Yes."

"It doesn't make what I did less wrong."

"No."

Her hand tightened on the satchel strap. "And it doesn't settle the influence question."

"No. It doesn't."

Silence again. He could have let them sink into that old argument, the one with no bottom. She looked ready to do exactly that, because thinking was where she went when the ground was bad. A week ago he might have let her. Or joined her in it until both of them were buried.

Instead he said, "If you stay, it can't be like before."

That stopped her.

He had not planned the sentence. The rest arrived after it, rough and already inadequate. "No more finding something that changes the terms and deciding alone how much of it the other person can bear. No more private conclusions while we're pretending we're in the same conversation."

Hermione did not interrupt. That alone told him she was listening with her whole body.

"And I don't get to do my version either," he said. "I don't get to go silent for a week and call it principle. If something is wrong, I say it when it's wrong."

She swallowed. "Together, then."

"If the archive opens again, we read whatever it gives us in the same room. If one of us finds something elsewhere that changes what this is, we say it that day." He heard how formal he sounded and hated it, but the precision mattered. "No sparing. No managing."

Her answer took a second because she was checking it for weakness before she let it out. "Agreed. And if one of us wants to stop, or can't do it, we say that out loud too. Not after we've built a whole private case. Not after we've already left."

He looked at her. "Fine."

A breath escaped her. Not relief exactly. Something closer to surviving the first cut.

"Good," she said.

There was still more between them. Damage. Want. Doubt. The corridor held all of it and offered no help.

"I want to show you something," he said.

He turned toward the east wing. Hermione followed without asking where. Stone answered under their shoes. The corridor kept its full length. Good.

The drawing room door stood open where it had stood all week. Just a door now. No stagecraft in it. No invitation. The worst room in the house, left ajar and cold.

He stopped beside it. So did she.

Dim light from the corridor caught the chandelier and the marble floor beyond it. Dust. Cold stone. A crack in one tile near the far wall. No atmospheric performance. December in an unheated room.

"It's been open all week," he said. "I kept walking past it."

Hermione looked in without moving. He knew what she was seeing because he saw it too. The chandelier from the floor. The marble under her back. Bellatrix's arm. His own seventeen-year-old stillness.

"I didn't go in alone." The admission rasped on the way out. "I wasn't going to let the house choose that for me again."

A few breaths passed. Her left hand lifted and adjusted the sleeve over the scar in the small habitual motion he now noticed every time.

"All right," she said.

They entered together.

The room was just a room. That was the obscene part. Smaller than memory. Dustier. One crack in the tile. Plaster smell and cold. He had spent years carrying it as a wound with walls. Standing inside it now, he could see how much ordinary stone had been involved.

Hermione went to the center of the floor and stopped. Draco joined her there. Above them the chandelier hung motionless. Once, during the war, he had stood against the far wall and watched the points of firelight move across the ceiling because watching the ceiling had been easier than watching Bellatrix cut into Hermione on the floor. Fourteen points before it ended. He had counted them because he could not do anything else.

"I used to dream about this floor," she said. Nearly steady. "The cold of it. That was the part that kept turning up."

Nothing useful existed for him to say. In this room, inadequacy was more honest than comfort.

Her gaze moved from chandelier to wall to him. "It's also just a room. The room where she carved me, yes. But still a room. Still stone. It doesn't get to keep doing it unless I hand it the knife."

She was saying it because she needed to hear herself say it here. That was different from performing courage. Different from closure. It was simply true or not true, and she was testing it.

His hand found hers by accident first, then on purpose. Wrapped gauze brushed her wrist. Fingers settled. Cold skin, calluses, steady pressure. She held on.

This time the house answered.

Not warmth. Not the golden, manipulative indulgence it had once produced. Just a shift in pressure and the faintest easing under his skin, enough that the Mark stopped demanding the center of his attention. The room acknowledged them and went still again.

They left without closing the door.

At the threshold Hermione let go to step over the stone lip, then took his hand again in the corridor as if the break had been practical rather than meaningful. Dim sconces along the east wing had lit themselves. Barely. A suggestion, not a performance.

"The archive?" she said.

"Still sealed." He glanced at her. "If it opens, we go in together."

"Good."

"How bad is it outside?"

"The Floo was down eight hours yesterday. St. Mungo's is rationing again."

That was enough. Britain was still failing. Work was still waiting.

The walk back to the library stayed ordinary in the best possible way. No corridor folding to help them along. No room appearing ahead of them. Just two people moving through the full length of the house under bad light.

At the library door Hermione released his hand and looked inside. The reading table. Two chairs. Stacks of books. Dead grate. Her translation note still beside the genealogical record.

"Is that my note?"

"You left it."

"I can see that."

He let the rest stay unsaid. So did she.

Her satchel landed beside the chair. A warming charm sent fire slowly through the grate, wood taking the spell reluctantly before the flames caught for good. Heat spread outward by degrees. Not the Manor being generous. A woman lighting a fire in a cold room.

She sat in her chair. Draco took the other one. For a moment the only sound was wood settling in the grate.

"I'm going to make more tea," he said, pushing to his feet.

Then the next sentence arrived before he could stop it.

"Hermione."

Her name in his mouth felt new enough to alter the room. Four syllables he had kept locked behind Granger for months because the surname was safer and less revealing. Now it was here, spoken, impossible to take back.

Brown eyes lifted to him.

"More tea would be good," she said. Then, after the smallest pause, "Draco."

In the kitchen he filled two cups, cream-coloured and blue-chipped, and carried them back. She had already reopened one of the texts from her satchel. He set her cup beside her elbow, took his own seat, and opened the genealogical record again.

A line in the text snagged his eye. This time he turned the book toward her at once.

Hermione leaned across the space between their chairs, finger resting on the cramped notation. Her mouth moved around the first line. The fire cracked behind them.
