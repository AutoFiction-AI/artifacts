# Chapter 13

The pub was a Muggle place near Charing Cross Road, a narrow room with uncomfortable chairs and middling beer and no associations. She and Ron had started using it after the divorce because neither of them had ever kissed or argued or decided anything in this building, which was exactly why it worked. There was no memory to navigate around. It was just a room.

She'd Apparated into the alley behind the used bookshop three doors down and walked the rest, her coat pulled close against the December cold. The Floo would have been easier, but the Floo was unreliable now. There had been three failures in the last week alone, connections dropping mid-transit, and she didn't fancy rematerializing in someone's kitchen in Birmingham because the network had lost her routing. So she had Apparated, with the familiar squeeze and the ringing in her ears afterward, and then felt ordinary London pavement under her boots. The simple solidity of it, pavement that stayed where it was and walls that didn't rearrange themselves when she wasn't looking, felt strange. It wasn't bad. It was only strange, the way any normal thing feels after weeks inside the abnormal.

Ron was already there. These days he was always early, the way he'd never been when they were married. At the sight of her, he stood, which was new too, some politeness he'd adopted after the separation that she hadn't asked for and didn't correct because correcting it would mean discussing it, and discussing it would mean admitting the shape of their interactions had changed.

"Hey." He kissed her cheek. His face was cold. "You look — you look good."

It came out slightly wrong, the compliment carrying too much weight because he'd had to decide to say it, and the decision was audible. It wasn't worth rescuing.

"Got you a cider. Is that still right?"

"That's perfect."

They sat. The pub was half-empty at two in the afternoon: a few regulars nursing pints, a woman reading a paperback in the corner, a football match on the mounted television with the sound off. Ron glanced at it. Since the divorce he'd become genuinely interested in football, one of the small expansions his life had made without her in it. He'd explained the offside rule to her twice and she still didn't understand it, which he found funny in a way that wasn't mean. The fact that he could find it funny instead of irritating was one of the ways she knew they'd done the right thing.

"How's work?" she asked.

"Good. Weird. George has got me managing the books for the second shop and I'm completely rubbish at it, but it turns out that when I'm rubbish at the books, George does them himself, and then they're actually done right. So it's a system."

Her laugh brought out a grin, pleased with itself for getting it and then slightly embarrassed by the pleasure. A second later his ears went pink and he looked down at his beer, and the familiarity of it pulled at the old, muted place where their marriage used to live. It wasn't loss. It was fondness for the thing they'd been that had become the thing they were now, which was different and less and also, in ways she still couldn't fully articulate, more honest.

"You?"

"Still at the Manor. The research is — it's significant. Material in the collection that nobody's documented in centuries." The professional frame arrived automatically, the one that cost her nothing. "It could be important for the decay."

Ron drank his beer. Across from her he looked broader than she remembered, which happened every time she saw him after a gap. Her mental image of him was still nineteen and lanky; the man in front of her was twenty-five, solid, and slightly sunburned from the running Harry had mentioned. He'd taken it up in the summer and kept going through autumn. Now he ran along the canal near his flat three mornings a week, and the idea of Ron voluntarily running, Ron, who at school would have considered a brisk walk to the Great Hall excessive exertion, was one of the small surprises of their post-marriage life that she found genuinely, uncomplexly good.

"So you're there full-time," he said. It wasn't a question.

"Mostly. I come back for meetings."

"And Malfoy's there."

"It's his house, Ron."

"Yeah, I know. I know how it sounds." He stopped, rubbed the back of his neck, then started again with the visible effort of someone rerouting a sentence away from where it was headed. "I mean the house. How's the house? Like, is it... fine? As a place to be?"

There were several ways she could answer that. The house was extraordinary, terrifying, beautiful, sentient, responsive, and currently adjusting its internal geography to accommodate her preferences in ways she hadn't asked for. The house had shown her the room where she'd been tortured and then, weeks later, started warming her bathwater to the exact temperature she preferred. The house knew things about her that she hadn't told it.

"It's unusual," she said. "But manageable."

Ron gave her the look reserved for obvious evasions, the one that said he'd heard the gap and was still deciding whether to push on it. He'd gotten better at that since the divorce. During the marriage he'd either pushed or he hadn't, with almost no calibration. Now there was a middle range, a pause where he weighed it.

"Hermione."

"What?"

"You said manageable. You didn't say fine."

A swallow of cider gave her something to do. The pub was warm with the ordinary warmth of a radiator on a December afternoon, not the intelligent, adjusting warmth of a house that noticed her. From the corner came the dry sound of a page turning, and from the regulars' end of the bar came the muted commentary of the match. Beneath all of it, like a radio signal from very far away, ran the low hum of the Mark resonance. It was faint in London and thinned further by distance, but it was there. Draco's pain, that restless ache the Mark carried now, magic searching for a channel that no longer existed, transmitted through miles of December air and settled in her forearm like an echo.

Pressure helped, so she pressed her wrist against the edge of the table.

"I don't have a neat answer for you," she said. "The house is complicated. The research is real and it matters. And I'm handling it."

A strip of label was already coming loose beneath Ron's thumb, a nervous habit she'd forgotten about and now remembered with the sharpness of something found at the back of a drawer. The recognition was almost physical. All through their engagement he'd done that in restaurants, peeling labels while they talked about the wedding or didn't talk about the things they should have been talking about instead.

"George and Angelina are getting serious," he said after a while.

"Really?"

"Yeah. She's been at Sunday dinners three weeks running. Mum's broken out the good china. You know what that means."

"Molly Weasley pulling out the good china is practically a betrothal announcement."

"Don't say that to George, he'll bolt." Ron smiled. The smile was genuine and easy and had nothing to do with Hermione, which was the kind of smile she liked best from him now, the ones that were about his life, not about their shared past. "Anyway. She's good for him. She's got this way of just not taking his shit, you know? Like, he'll do the George thing where he deflects with a joke and she just... waits. Doesn't laugh. Waits until he says the real thing."

"Good."

"Yeah."

They were quiet. The football match continued its muted drama. Someone at the bar laughed at something. The woman turned another page. Hermione sat in the middle of those ordinary human sounds and felt the resonance pulse, faint but continuous, a thread that connected her to a house in Wiltshire and the man inside it. Ron, peeling his label across from her, had no idea that she could feel another person's pain in her own arm. He didn't know that a house might be engineering everything she felt, or that the reason she couldn't say fine was stranger than anything he was probably imagining.

"Listen," Ron said. He put the beer bottle down. The label was half off, a ragged strip of wet paper. "I know it's not my — I know I don't get to say this. But I'm going to say it once and then shut up."

"Okay."

"You do this thing." He wasn't looking at her. "Where you find a problem and it becomes — look, you did it with Buckbeak's appeal. Third year. You did it with the Horcruxes. You did it with us, our marriage, when it was ending, you had a whole — you researched the divorce like a project, Hermione, and it was the most organized breakup in history and also the saddest thing I've ever been part of."

"Ron—"

"I'm not — that's not a dig. I don't even know what I'm trying to say exactly." He pressed his thumb into the shredded label. "Just that you do it. And I'm only saying it because I watched it happen with us and didn't say anything until it was too late for it to matter."

She didn't say anything. The cider had gone warm in her glass.

"That house," he said. "That family. The research and the decay and whatever else is happening that you're not telling me. I don't mean Malfoy — or, I don't know, maybe I do, I don't actually care about that bit. I just—" He stopped. Started again, quieter. "I just don't want to ring you in three months and find out you haven't left the building. All right?"

She turned her glass on the table.

"You're not wrong," she said.

He blinked. "That's new. You admitting I'm not wrong."

"Don't get used to it."

He smiled. The rueful, self-knowing one she'd always liked best on him. "Wouldn't dream of it."

By the time they were putting on coats, he was telling her about the flat. He'd finally hung the shelves in the bedroom, badly, and they'd already fallen down once. This weekend he was going to try again with a level because Harry had pointed out that eyeballing it wasn't working. In return she gave him a translation problem she'd been stuck on, kept vague and professional. As ever, he listened without tracking every technical detail and still caught the shape of it, the frustration and the fascination, nodding in the right places.

"Oh," he said, as they headed for the door. "The other thing. Mum went to St. Mungo's for her knee, the old jinx, the Moody one, and they turned her away. Said they're rationing healing magic. Prioritizing critical cases."

"I know about the rationing."

"I figured you would." He wound his scarf around his neck, a Chudley Cannons scarf, orange and faded, that she'd given him three Christmases ago when they were still married. He still wore it. That left her with no idea what to do. "The Floo dropped again this morning. Two hours. And a kid in Diagon Alley, six maybe, set a rubbish bin on fire. Accidental magic, but the kind that shouldn't happen that young, not that strongly."

"I know."

"Right." He looked at her. "If the research matters, the stuff you're doing at the Manor, then I suppose it matters. I just want you to come back from it. That's all."

At the door she hugged him. He was warm and solid and smelled like beer, cold air, damp wool, and the soap he'd used since school. His return hug was careful, his arms finding her the old way and then stopping short, leaving space between her ribs and his as if he were measuring it by feel.

"Take care of yourself," he said.

"You too."

He left. She watched him walk up Charing Cross Road toward the Leaky, hunching into his coat, and felt the ache of watching someone you loved walk away from you in the most ordinary way, not leaving, just going, back to his flat and his badly hung shelves and his Sunday dinners and the life he was building that didn't need her in it. It wasn't loss. Loss had already had its turn. This was the healed place afterward, skin closed over, only a faint pull when something brushed it.

On the pavement she felt the December cold on her face and the Mark resonance in her arm. When she pressed her wrist against the brick wall beside her, the wall was only a wall, cold and rough and utterly indifferent to her touch.

---

Harry's office at the Department of Magical Law Enforcement was the same corner room they'd given him two promotions ago. He'd done nothing with it except accumulate files and add a photograph of Ginny and James that sat on the desk facing outward. James was nine months old in the picture, chewing on a toy broomstick with the focused commitment of someone who'd found his purpose.

"You look tired," Harry said.

"Hello to you too."

He stood and hugged her, a real hug with both arms, the kind of contact that required no negotiation because the friendship had survived a war and everything after. It could take the weight. He smelled like cedar soap. He'd used the same brand since fourth year.

"Tea?"

"Please."

Harry made tea badly. It was too strong, there wasn't enough milk, and the cup had been repaired so many times the handle sat slightly crooked. Hermione drank it anyway because Harry's tea was one of the constants of her life, and she wasn't prepared to lose constants right now.

"Ron said you saw him," Harry said as he settled behind his desk.

"An hour ago."

"How was it?"

"It was fine. Good, mostly. He told me about Molly and St. Mungo's."

Years of absorbing bad news professionally had reorganized the muscles around Harry's eyes. He didn't look haggard. He looked set, alert in a low-frequency way that never quite switched off.

"It's not just the rationing," he said. "The Floo's been intermittent for three weeks. We've had four accidental magic incidents in the last ten days, all kids under seven, all discharging way beyond what their age should produce. One of them blew the windows out of a flat in Camden. It was a Muggle flat. We had to send a team for the glass and the structural damage and the neighbors who'd heard the explosion. And the parents, the magical parents, they were terrified, Hermione. Their child can't control what's happening and neither can they, and no one at the Ministry can explain why a five-year-old is producing magic that should be beyond a third-year."

"The ambient field's destabilizing," she said. "The infrastructure that regulates baseline magical pressure has degraded to the point where young wizards can't discharge safely. They're getting surges because the field isn't absorbing the excess."

"I got the Mysteries briefing. They said roughly that, with more words."

Under other circumstances she might have smiled, but this had flat windows in Camden and parents too frightened to trust their own child's magic. For months the decay had been background noise in her professional life, something she studied and tracked from the clean distance of data. Now it was a public emergency. Families couldn't trust their children's magic. Hospitals were turning away patients because healing spells drew on the same failing infrastructure. Transportation was collapsing. The entire substrate of wizarding life in Britain was coming apart, not in a dramatic catastrophe but in a slow, grinding degradation that was worse because you couldn't point to the moment it broke.

"How's the research going?" Harry asked.

"There's material in the Malfoy library that documents the original magical infrastructure. The anchoring points, the ley-line connections, the deep structure that the ambient field depends on. It was built centuries ago, partly by the Malfoy family."

"And the war damaged it."

"Yes. That's what's failing. The anchoring points were designed to be permanent, but the scale of Dark magic during the war, the curses and the protections and the sheer volume of hostile magic passing through the field, degraded them. It didn't happen all at once. It happened slowly. The degradation has been compounding for seven years."

"Can you fix it?"

"I don't know yet. The texts are in archaic notation that takes both the Malfoy family's inherited knowledge and formal theory to decode. We've made progress, but there's more to do."

After a moment Harry looked at her. "We," he said.

"Malfoy and I. He reads the family notation. I handle the theory. Neither of us can do it alone."

"Right."

Through the office wall she could hear someone arguing with a filing cabinet. The cabinet was making a grinding, petulant sound, the kind of noise an enchanted object makes when its underlying magic is misbehaving, another symptom of the ambient instability, institutional magic fraying at the seams as the small enchantments that kept the Ministry running started to stutter and fail. The wizard outside swore. Something metal fell.

"People are talking," Harry said.

"About the decay?"

"About you. Where you've been." He leaned forward, elbows on the desk, with the posture of someone delivering information he'd rather not deliver. "You've spent most of the last month at the Manor. People notice, Hermione. You're not someone who disappears into a Malfoy property for weeks without generating opinions."

"I'm doing critical research in the only archive that—"

"I know. The Prophet had a paragraph last week. Skeeter's column. 'Ministry researcher spending extended time at the Malfoy estate.' Nothing direct, just the implication. She doesn't have to say it outright."

"I don't read Skeeter."

"Ginny does." He paused, and his hand went to his hair, the gesture so automatic he probably didn't register it. "Ginny said to tell you, and this is Ginny, not me, that if you're going to set up camp at Malfoy Manor, you should have a cover story better than 'the library is very interesting,' because no library is that interesting."

"I'm not setting up—"

"Her words. I told her it's about the research and the decay and the infrastructure. She said she believes you about the research. She also said the research isn't the only reason you're still there, and she's not going to insult your intelligence by pretending she doesn't notice."

Ginny didn't soften things. She'd survived enough to stop performing diplomacy, and the result was a woman who said what she saw without apology. Hermione wanted to be angry about it and couldn't quite get there, because Ginny was right in a way that bypassed argument, not about the details, which Ginny didn't know, but about the shape of it, the outline of a situation that looked from the outside like more than what it claimed to be.

"The research is real," she said. "The decay is getting worse. The archive is the only source."

"I know. I believe you." Harry looked at the photograph of James and the broomstick, then back at her. "I'm not asking you to justify being there. I'm telling you that people are paying attention, and most of them aren't going to assume it's about ley-line theory. It's Malfoy Manor, Hermione. You know what that name does in a headline."

"Since when do I care about headlines?"

"You don't. That's the problem."

The tea tasted terrible. Outside the office, the filing cabinet had apparently won its argument with the wizard, because something heavy slammed and footsteps retreated. The ambient hum of the Ministry felt different than it had six months ago, thinner, less stable, the way a building feels when the power is fluctuating and the lights aren't quite steady.

"How bad is it going to get?" Harry asked, quieter now.

"Bad. If we don't understand the anchoring infrastructure well enough to repair it, or at least stabilize it, it'll keep degrading. The Floo is just the beginning. Healing magic draws on the same field. So do the wards at Hogwarts, the protections at Azkaban, the enchantments on the Ministry itself." She gestured at the room around them. "Everything magical in Britain sits on top of the same foundation, and the foundation is cracked."

Harry absorbed that in silence.

"And the Malfoy texts are the only way to understand it."

"The Malfoys helped build the original infrastructure. Their records are the only documentation of how the anchoring points work. There's nothing in the Ministry archives. Nothing at Hogwarts. Nothing anywhere I've looked. The original builders kept the knowledge in the family."

"Convenient."

"It was designed to be."

He didn't say anything for a moment. Then he met her eyes. "Look. Do what you need to do. I'll handle Skeeter if it comes to that." He paused. "Just — make sure you know why you're there. That's all."

"I know why I'm there."

He looked at her the way he looked at people who were nearly convincing, and she felt a flare of irritation because he was right and she was lying and they both knew it. The lie wasn't that the research was fake. The lie was that the research was enough of an explanation, when the truth included a sentient house and a resonance she could feel in her arm right then, sitting in an office at the Ministry of Magic, the low pulse of Draco Malfoy's pain carried across sixty miles of English winter.

"Okay," Harry said.

He let it go. She could see him letting it go, the way Ron had earlier — the same deliberate step back.

They stood. His second hug was briefer, an arm around her shoulders and a squeeze.

"If you need anything," he said. "You know."

"I know."

The visitors' entrance took her out through the red telephone box and deposited her on a grey London street in December. It was four o'clock. The light was already failing. In mid-December the sun was gone or going by then, and the sky had flattened into a steel colour that would be black within the hour. On the pavement around her London kept moving, commuters and shoppers and a man selling roasted chestnuts from a cart on the corner, smoke rising into the cold air and dispersing.

Without much sense of direction, she walked the way she used to when she needed to think and the flat was too small and the office was too full of people who wanted her to have answers, past the bookshop where she'd bought her first non-magical novel after the war and past the cafe where she and Ron had sat on a Tuesday afternoon and agreed, over lukewarm coffee, that the marriage was done, not in those words and not dramatically, but in the accumulation of small honest sentences that added up to the same conclusion. At the Tube entrance a busker was playing something she almost recognized, his guitar case open on the pavement, and a woman dropped a coin in while he nodded without breaking the melody.

Everything about it was ordinary. The walls stayed where they were. The buildings didn't rearrange themselves to suit their inhabitants' moods. The people brushing past her didn't know that the magic holding their world together, the magic they didn't even know existed, was degrading underneath them like rot in the joists of a house that looked fine from the street.

On Shaftesbury Avenue she passed a shop whose display window should have held a concealed advertisement for Madam Malkin's, one of those charmed billboards that Muggles saw as a shoe advert, and the magic was flickering. The image stuttered between dress robes and brogues, two frames overlaid, and a passing Muggle glanced at it and kept walking, registering the glitch as a faulty display. Hermione saw what it was. The concealment charm was drawing on the same failing ambient field, the same cracked infrastructure, the same damage that was making children's magic spike and Floo connections drop. It was the same damage she'd spent a month studying in a library in Wiltshire, getting closer to understanding, getting closer to the man whose family had built the thing that was now breaking.

From the alley behind the telephone box she Apparated, and the world compressed to a needle-point and expanded again until she was standing on the frosted gravel of the Manor drive with the last grey light dying over Wiltshire and her ears ringing and her breath coming out in clouds.

---

The walk up the drive took four minutes. Once, early on, she'd timed it, when the Manor had still been a professional assignment and she'd still been pretending measurement could make this manageable. She didn't bother anymore. The cold bit at her face. Her boots crunched on frozen gravel. The bare trees along the drive stood like structural drawings of themselves, all line and no softness, black against the darkening sky.

With each step the Mark resonance strengthened. The faint signal she'd been carrying all day in London filled in and gained resolution, the way a radio station clarifies as you drive toward its tower. His pain and wakefulness came through with it, plus a quality she couldn't name but had learned to recognize, something like agitation, something like waiting. The resonance put him two floors up, though she was still outside and the information shouldn't have narrowed that sharply at this distance. Either the bond was learning her or she was learning it.

The front door opened before she reached it.

It didn't open the way doors open when you turn a handle. It opened the way a door opens when the building behind it has decided. Heavy oak swung inward, smooth and unhurried, and the entrance hall beyond was lit with that golden light the Manor made when it seemed almost pleased.

Then she stepped inside.

Warmth met her at once. It wasn't the blunt heat of a radiator or a fire. It seemed to know the cold spots in her coat, the numbness in her fingers, the ribbon of December cold that had slipped down the gap at her collar while she'd stood on a London pavement thinking about things she didn't want to think about. It found all of it and answered with an attention that felt less like a heating charm and more like being noticed by something enormous and careful.

The air smelled of the library, old paper, preservation spells, and the faint mineral tang of the Manor's stone. Beneath that was another quality, not a smell but a settling, the way the house exhaled when she came back, as if the building itself had been holding tension that her presence released.

For a moment she stood in the entrance hall with her coat still on.

Ron's voice, rough with restraint: don't let that place narrow everything down to itself.

Harry's voice: be honest about why you're there.

Ginny's voice, carried by Harry and clean as glass: the research isn't the only reason.

Tonight the corridor to the library was short. When she'd left that morning, the house had taken her past two empty rooms before releasing her at the stairs. Now a single left turn opened straight onto the hall outside the library doors.

Using the hook the Manor had made for her, she took off her coat and hung it up. It was plain iron, fixed at exactly the right height. She'd never asked for it. It had appeared on the third night, and she'd used it without comment because commenting would mean admitting the house was accommodating her, and admitting that would mean admitting she liked it.

The library door was already open. Inside, the room glowed warm and gold, the lamps lit, the shelves steady. Her desk sat where she'd left it, notes stacked in order, a cup of tea beside them that she hadn't made and that was, she knew without touching it, exactly the temperature she preferred. Across from her chair, Draco's stood pulled slightly back from the long table, as if someone had made ready for him too.

Then she sat down. The chair held the last of the day's warmth. When her fingers touched the edge of the desk, the resonance altered. The ache under Draco's Mark eased by a fraction, enough that she felt the release in her own arm before she let herself think what it meant.

A soft click sounded across the table. A second cup had appeared beside Draco's place, steam lifting from it in a straight pale line. He wasn't there. The house was still making room for him.

Hermione looked at the cup for a long moment, then pulled her notes closer and set her quill to the line they hadn't solved that morning. The library door stayed open behind her. And she left it that way.
