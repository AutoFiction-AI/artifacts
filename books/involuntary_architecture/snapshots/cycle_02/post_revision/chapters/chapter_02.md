# Chapter 2

The Mark woke him at four again.

Not with the old burn. Not with the fishhook drag behind his navel that had once meant summons, movement, whatever room the Dark Lord had chosen for that night's audience. That signal died with the man who cast it. What remained was slower and less useful: an arrhythmic ache deep in the bones of his forearm, magic trying to force itself through a route that no longer led anywhere, heating the sealed channel until the whole arm felt inhabited by failed intent.

In the dark, Draco lay still and waited for it to pass. The bedroom was too large for one person. Three years earlier he'd moved into his parents' suite, not out of appetite for grandeur but because the alternative was the room he'd slept in at sixteen, and that room still carried the ghost of the boy who had come back from the Dark Lord with bile on his chin and a strip of blackened skin on his arm. Airing it out had not helped. Redecorating had not helped. Eventually he'd shut the door and left it shut.

Around five the ache eased from unendurable to familiar. He got up because there was no reason not to.

At the far end of the ground floor sat the servants' kitchen, the one he actually used because the formal one still implied staff and a table laid for more than one person. Tea first. Then he ate toast at the counter, with no plate and no chair.

From there his body took the same route it always took to the library: east corridor, back stair, portrait gallery, narrow passage, south entrance. The reception rooms would have been faster. Faster meant the west-front room with its chandelier, the conservatory where Bellatrix had once sat humming in the evenings as if she were visiting relatives, his father's study with Ministry reports spread across the desk like a failed barricade. Thought rarely entered into it now. His feet made the turns that kept him out of certain rooms and let the rest of him catch up later.

The library was the only room that justified the house. By the south window waited his chair, shaped to him by repetition, and on the table beside it lay the treatise he'd been worrying through for a week. Seventeenth-century sympathetic magic, dry as old plaster and no more useful than the rest. For eight months he'd looked for anything that might explain why a dead man's brand had begun to wake in his arm. St. Mungo's had refused to touch it. One private curse-breaker had lifted his sleeve, watched the serpent twitch under probe light, and stepped back far enough to make her refusal feel physical. Another had been steadier. She had asked sensible questions, smelled of dittany and old wool, and spent nearly an hour mapping the curse's surface before admitting the visible work sat on top of something older and denser than her training reached. She'd suggested the Department of Mysteries with a straight face. He had laughed until she left.

So he read. Somewhere in the family's collection there might be a reference, or a precedent, or at least a theory that did not end in institutional helplessness. Morning after morning he pulled books, made notes, followed citations into older shelves, and found nothing but dead men's confidence.

All morning the Mark kept up its low pulse. Pressing his thumb hard against the brand sometimes blunted it for a minute or two. Lately even that had started to fail.

Winter altered the Manor before it did anything stranger. By November the east wing whistled at the windows, far rooms went hard with cold, and corridors sometimes seemed to narrow by inches he could never prove. He did not call the house alive, or listening. That way a shutting door could stay timber, stone, old magic, and the possibility that he was tired. This morning, with the Mark beating in his arm and the windows black, the distinction felt thinner than usual.

His mother had not visited in two weeks. That was deliberate. Narcissa Malfoy did nothing by accident. Three years earlier she had taken a rented house in Knightsbridge, tasteful and proportionate, rooms that stayed where they were put. She'd been graceful about it. *The Manor is yours, darling. It needs someone in residence.* She had never said *I can't live there anymore.* She had only paused on the threshold, set two fingers against the doorframe, and left them there a beat too long before stepping out. Twenty-five years in this house had worn at her in ways she did not discuss. Draco had not asked for details. Asking would mean admitting the house could lay hands on people at all.

By noon the treatise had yielded nothing. Lunch was soup, bread, and an apple he forgot on a side table until the flesh began to brown against the wood. After that came the east lawn, walked because his body needed movement and the grounds were the one place large enough to cross without brushing past doors he preferred closed. At half three, the usual circuit broke.

An owl struck the library window hard enough to rattle the panes and dropped Ministry stationery onto the desk.

Department of Mysteries. Departmental seal bitten deep into the wax. Paper thick enough to feel official before he read a word of it. At the library desk, where all mail got opened, he broke the seal and found Granger's name at the foot of the page.

First reading: content. She requested access to the Malfoy library for work related to the ongoing investigation into systemic magical decay. She listed the categories she needed with the precision of someone who had already exhausted everything easier: pre-Union anchoring theory, estate magic, curse-binding records, old family ledgers, architectural magic that predated the Statute. The Ministry would provide a standard stipend for room and board. She anticipated a stay of several weeks at minimum. She required unrestricted access to all holdings, including restricted sections, and permission to keep working notes in the house rather than transport them through a Ministry whose own wards were starting to misfire.

Second reading: the rest of it. She had not entered this house since Bellatrix Lestrange drove her onto the marble under the chandelier and carved a slur into her left forearm while Draco stood there with a wand in his hand and discovered, in real time, how useless wanting to stop something could be. The last time they had spoken was at his trial seven years earlier, when she sat in a courtroom and described that night with measured accuracy. Not melodrama. Not mercy either. Her voice had stayed level all the way through the bit about the knife. No word he might have offered then would have survived the distance between them. Silence had seemed less insulting than an inadequate apology, so silence was what he'd given her.

Beneath the formal request sat the real fact. Hermione Granger would not have written to him unless every other door had failed to open. He was the last one.

At her name the Mark kicked once under his sleeve, sharp enough to make his hand jerk. His thumb went to it before he'd decided to move.

She needed the library. In return, he needed a witness who could look at the brand on his arm and not recoil from what had put it there.

The Unspeakables could probably have done something with it if he let them. Something classificatory. Something invasive. They would have turned his arm into a case file, his body into Department property, his house into a site visit. The Ministry had already owned enough of him after the war. He was not going to invite a second occupation.

He pulled writing paper closer. Ruined one sheet with *Accepted,* which sounded like a clerk. Ruined another with *You are welcome to the library,* which was both untrue and impossible. On the third he kept to terms. Access granted. Rooms available. Arrival at her convenience. One additional condition, set out in a single sentence as flatly as he could make it: if she intended to remain in the house, she would examine the Dark Mark and provide a full diagnostic assessment.

He nearly struck the sentence before sealing the letter. It was opportunistic. It was also the only version of asking he had.

Her owl returned the next morning with a reply no longer than his own. She would arrive Thursday at eleven. She accepted the additional condition only as a matter for later discussion and reserved the right to decline after preliminary inspection.

Two days later, with the date fixed and no further correspondence likely, Draco stopped pretending this could be managed by posture alone.

---

On the morning she was due, Draco cleaned.

Not the whole Manor. That project belonged to someone with staff or delusions. The library got done, the guest wing got done, the kitchen got done, and so did the corridors between them. Dust lifted from shelves under his wand. Stone floors lost their film of disuse. Fresh candles replaced the stubs in the library sconces. He stripped the old sheets from the east-arm suite, laid out clean linen that smelled faintly of lavender from the preservation chest, and opened the windows long enough for November air to scour the shut-room smell into something merely old.

There was a narrow study adjoining the library, east-facing and mostly abandoned except for household ledgers and a dead inkwell on the sill. His grandfather had once used it for accounts. Draco cleared it as well. The desk came out. The larger oak worktable stayed. He stacked the ledgers in a corner, opened the curtains, and tested the lamps twice. If Granger stayed several weeks, she was going to need somewhere to spread out her notes without taking over every flat surface in the library. He would rather surrender one room deliberately than watch the house do the choosing for him.

In the restricted section he unsealed the north-wall wards far enough to inspect them, then reset them to admit a second wand signature once he keyed hers in. Several shelves held books that objected to casual handling. One volume shrieked when disturbed. Another leaked sleep charms from a cracked spine. He moved both to a locked cabinet rather than risk the first afternoon turning into an avoidable disaster.

By half ten the cleaning had tipped into arrangement. Tea tray in the morning room. Extra towels in the guest bath. A spare quill and fresh ink in the study. He recognized, somewhere between decanting biscuits into a tin and checking whether the guest-room lock still worked, that this was the most attention he'd paid the house in over a year. The recognition annoyed him enough that he kept working harder.

By eleven he was in the entrance hall because that was where guests were received, and whatever else this arrangement was, it would be conducted correctly. The hall was one of the few formal spaces he had kept up. Black-and-white marble. A ceiling built to dwarf arrivals. A staircase wide enough for four people abreast. The family had always understood entrances better than what followed them.

From the drive came the crack of Apparition. She had not used the Floo. That could have meant the network was misbehaving again, or it could have meant she had chosen the method that left a front door between them. He suspected the second.

Before she could knock, he opened it.

On the step stood Hermione Granger in a dark coat with a leather bag over one shoulder and a second case shrunk to pocket size in her left hand. Her hair was pulled back and already escaping. The years had changed her and failed to change her. She was smaller than memory had made her, more contained, less mythic than the Ministry clippings and courtroom recollections he'd allowed to accumulate in her place. What remained most familiar was the expression. She had prepared for this. She knew how the first half minute was supposed to go and had come armed for it. Across the courtroom seven years ago he had watched that same preparation carry her through testimony that spared him nothing and still kept him out of Azkaban.

"Malfoy," she said.

"Granger."

No hand offered itself. The absence stood between them as plainly as any contract.

"Come in."

She crossed the threshold under her own power and it still looked like impact. The leather strap creaked under her grip. Her right hand left the bag and went straight to the button at her left cuff, thumb grinding once over the fabric as if checking that something under it remained covered. A pulse jumped at the base of her throat. Then she was past him, shoulders pulled square by force, and the front door was swinging shut behind her.

Formal hush closed around them in the entrance hall. Her gaze moved over the marble floor, the sweep of the stairs, the height of the ceiling, measuring what had altered and what had not. Then, involuntary enough that he might have missed it in anyone else, her eyes cut toward the west side of the house where the corridor turned out of sight. The last time she'd crossed this floor she'd been half dragged that way, Bellatrix's hand twisted in her hair, blood already wet on her sleeve. Draco did not need the trial transcript to remember the sound she had made in that room. He also didn't need to say any of this out loud for it to take up the whole hall.

"The morning room's this way," he said. "We'll discuss terms before I show you anything else."

"Fine."

He took the east corridor on purpose. She followed half a pace behind, quiet enough that the old house had room to speak between their footfalls. Her bag brushed her coat with each step. Once, at the turn, he heard her exhale through her nose the way people did when trying to stop a body from doing something embarrassing in public.

The morning room was the closest thing the house had to neutral ground. South-facing windows. A desk used for household business by three generations of Malfoys. Two chairs placed opposite each other with enough oak between them to count as civility. Tea already set out, whether from hospitality or habit he could not have said.

Granger took the chair opposite without waiting to be placed. She set her full-sized bag on the floor by her right foot and kept her coat on. Draco sat behind the desk and reached for the duplicate parchment she'd already drawn from the case in her hand.

"Ministry authorization," she said. "Access to the library and residential stipend. Croaker signed it yesterday because the accounts office refused to process anything involving this address without a second signature."

"Sensibly paranoid of them."

"Some of it is paranoia. Some of it is experience."

He skimmed the form. Technical wording, departmental seals, the usual language by which government tried to make a dangerous arrangement look administrative. At the bottom sat her signature, sharp and controlled.

"You've been specific," he said, tapping the list of materials. "Anchoring texts. Estate magic. Curse-binding. You're not wandering in here to browse."

"I've already done the browsing elsewhere. Public archives are incomplete, Hogwarts let me at what it had, and most of the private collections that survived the war are either inaccessible or useless on this point."

"And mine is the exception."

"Your family kept records instead of principles."

The line might once have invited a reflexive sneer. These days it only sounded accurate.

"You'll have full access to the library," he said. "All holdings, including the restricted sections, provided I warn you first which books object to handling."

"Object how?"

"Teeth, in one case. Sleep charms in another. General bad temper in several."

"Useful to know."

"The guest wing's in order. You've got your own rooms and a private bath. Use the kitchen whenever you like. There's a study adjoining the library if you need a separate workspace."

That got her attention in a way the tea had not. "Separate as in a door and four walls, or separate as in a table in a corner?"

"A door. Four walls. East-facing windows."

"Good."

He could hear the relief because she had not bothered to hide it. Not comfort. Not gratitude. Pure professional practicality, and underneath that the smaller, uglier fact that a room with a door she could shut mattered to her in this house.

"If you need to move through the rest of the house for research, do it," he said. "I'd only ask for notice before you enter private rooms."

"Which rooms are private?"

"My suite. My mother's rooms when she visits. A few family studies. I'll tell you as we go."

"Who else is in residence?"

"No one."

That was too quick, too bare to stand alone, so he added, "My mother comes out from Knightsbridge on occasion. She doesn't stay often. No staff beyond maintenance spells."

Her gaze held him a second longer than the question seemed to require, taking the measure of the answer and what it implied. He knew what she would have seen if she chose to see it. One cup. One place at table. The house too large and him thinned down inside it.

"And the Ministry?" he asked. "How often am I meant to expect supervision?"

"If you're worried about officials tramping through the corridors, don't be. Croaker wants results, not spectacle. I'll send reports by owl. If I need outside materials, I arrange them from here."

"So they've installed you and gone away."

"They've installed me where the books are."

Flat, factual, almost rude in how little room it left for self-importance. He respected it more than he meant to.

He set the Ministry parchment down. "My additional condition concerns the Mark."

Across from him, she didn't move. The only visible shift was in her hands. They went still on the arms of the chair, not loose anymore. Her left sleeve was buttoned tight at the wrist. Habit, then, and not an accidental one. Most people would have missed why. He did not. The room seemed briefly crowded with memory anyway: her body twisted on the floor, Bellatrix kneeling over her, Draco's own uselessness taking up as much space as the violence did.

Without ceremony, he pushed up his left sleeve and laid his forearm on the desk.

Even now the body's memory came with the exposure. Sixteen. A table edge against his hip. Lucius's hand locked around his elbow hard enough to bruise. The Dark Lord's wand pressed to skin, the spell driving in deeper than ink, and the smell after, not smoke exactly but burnt protein and fever. The Mark had faded after the war, gone to a dead dark grey. In the last month it had started blackening again, the skull and serpent sharpening at the edges as if old instructions were working their way back toward the surface.

Granger leaned forward. She did not touch him.

"Turn your wrist," she said.

He did.

Her gaze tracked the skull first, then the serpent's tail, then the raised skin at the edges, quick and level and unsentimental. He watched her noticing happen in sequence. Recognition. Professional concentration. Something else she killed before it reached her face.

"It's been waking up," he said. "Not the summoning. That's gone. This is different. Pain without a signal. The magic in it is restless. It keeps trying to move and there's nowhere for it to go."

"When did that start?"

"Eight months ago. At first it was mostly at night. Now it's constant enough to make sleep optional."

"Any precipitating event?"

"Not one I noticed."

"Any old objects taken out of storage. Any blood magic. Any curse exposure outside the obvious one. Illness. Fever. Injury."

"No."

"That's an actual answer or a polite one?"

"Actual."

Another second passed while she kept looking. At this distance the scar on his arm was nearly between them like a third participant in the conversation, and an old one.

"From the surface alone, this isn't simple residual curse activity," she said. "There are at least two layers visible, maybe more. The original brand is one structure. Something else is sitting under it or running through it, but I can't — that's a surface read. If you want an actual map of the curse, I'll need proper light, time, and sustained contact."

Not now hung in the room without her needing to say it twice.

"What are you proposing?" she asked.

"You get the library for as long as you need it. In return, you examine the Mark. Diagnostics, records, whatever you need to tell me what it's doing."

"You want me to study a curse two Healers wouldn't."

"I want you to study it because two Healers already couldn't. Or wouldn't. And I'm not — the Ministry is not an option."

"That's not the whole reason."

No, it wasn't. She knew that. He knew she knew it. The room held for a beat on the edge of the thing neither of them was going to say cleanly.

"You know what this is," he said at last, looking at the Mark rather than at her. "I don't need to explain the object to you. Or the provenance."

"No," she said. Her voice stayed even. "You don't."

That was the war-loaded center of it. Not just her department. Not just her competence. Him asking the woman Bellatrix had pinned to the floor in this house to come closer to the symbol burnt into his own arm by the other side of the same war.

She sat back a fraction. Enough to restore distance. Enough to remind them both the desk was doing work.

"I can assess it," she said. "I'm not promising treatment — I'm not even promising a full diagnostic until I know what the layering is. If it's unstable in a way that risks me or anyone else, I stop. If I need outside consultation, including the Department, I tell you before I seek it, not after."

"Agreed."

"The research comes first. The library is why I'm here. The Mark work gets scheduled around it, not the other way round. No improvised diagnostics because we happen to be in the same room."

"Understood."

"Last names."

"Of course."

"Records stay in my hand unless we agree otherwise. You can read them. You don't edit them."

"I'm wounded by the implication."

"You should recover."

There it was, the first movement that sounded remotely like the old version of them and nothing like it at all. He almost answered with something dry and easy. The moment did not permit easy.

"One more condition," she said. "If the house does anything that affects the work or the route between my rooms and the library, you tell me."

That pulled his attention off the Mark. "Such as?"

"Locks changing. Doors sticking. Corridors deciding to be elsewhere. I work with enough unstable systems already. I won't have the building pretending it's not one."

He might once have dismissed that as nerves. He did not now.

"Fine," he said. "If the house does anything odd, I tell you."

She looked as if she knew there were half a dozen loopholes in that sentence, all of them potentially useful to him. Then she let it stand. Either because she didn't want to spend the first hour here negotiating architecture, or because she had already spent enough effort getting through the front door to prioritize every risk correctly.

"The study," she said. "I want to see it before I unpack."

"You can."

"And for the Mark. Tomorrow morning, if there's decent light."

"There is."

"Good."

Neither of them reached for the tea. Steam climbed between them and thinned. At length he poured anyway, because the sound of liquid in porcelain gave his hands something to do. She accepted the cup and wrapped both palms around it without drinking. It took him a moment to understand why she held it that way: not for warmth, though the room was cool, but because it kept both hands occupied and visible.

"One other question," he said.

Her eyes lifted over the rim of the cup.

"How many places said no before you wrote here?"

She looked at him a long enough second that he thought she might refuse the premise outright.

"Enough," she said. "Public collections first. Then the predictable private ones. Then the people who claimed to have records and didn't. Then the people who had records and preferred not to let a Ministry researcher tell them what those records meant. Your library is one of the last intact family collections with any chance of holding pre-Statute anchoring work."

"And because I'm difficult, you assumed the books might be useful."

"Because your family hoarded old magic, Malfoy."

That, too, was fair.

"I'll show you the library," he said. "Then the study. Then the guest wing."

---

In the library doorway she stopped with one hand still curled around the brass. For one clean second she forgot to guard her face.

His usual route brought them there by the east corridor and the back passage rather than the reception rooms. If she noticed the detour she said nothing. Through the south entrance she went, then stopped just enough for the pause to register: shelves rising to the ceiling, mezzanine circling overhead, centuries of collection and theft and scholarship compressed into one room.

"Your catalogue," she said after a moment.

"Mostly nonexistent. My grandfather kept a ledger. It covers about a third of the holdings and stops in 1971. Everything since then is memory, luck, and family shorthand."

"Show me the ledger."

He went to a side cabinet, pulled out a thick green volume, and set it on the nearest table. She was already moving before it landed, fingertips gliding along shelf spines, head tipped to read titles. A folio came off one shelf, got opened, dismissed. Another replaced it. Then three more landed in a neat stack on the nearest table: French, Latin, English. She was not looking around anymore. She was sorting the room into use.

"Restricted section?" she asked, flipping open the catalogue without glancing at him.

"North wall behind the screen. It's warded, but I'll key you in. Most of it is Dark Arts adjacent rather than outright Dark. Theory, history, magical taxonomy. Some of it is genuinely dangerous. Some of it only thinks it is."

"That's your reassurance?"

"My honesty."

She made a small sound that might have been agreement and might have been the beginning of a headache. At the ledger she worked fast, eyes moving between his grandfather's script and the shelves themselves, already building correspondences. Draco watched her recalibrate the room in real time. To him the library was weight, ancestry, duty, the only part of the house he had not come to resent. To her it had become, within thirty seconds, a problem with components.

"You'll need to show me anything on estate anchors even if it isn't catalogued as such," she said. "Families called these things by different names. Foundations. Seat magic. House-binding. Inheritance wards. Half the time they're talking about the same structural work and pretending otherwise."

"There are several sections on domestic wards and line magic."

"Not line magic. Bigger than that."

"You haven't seen the shelves yet."

"I've seen enough of this country to know what people hide under tidy labels."

The dryness of it made him want, very briefly, to smile. He didn't.

He crossed to the north wall and lifted his wand to the blackwood screen. The ward there recognized him with a low click and a stirring of air cold enough to smell metallic.

"Come here," he said.

She joined him, leaving the cup untouched on the reading table. Up close he could see the fatigue under her glamour of competence. Not the scar itself. That remained hidden. But her eyes had the overbright precision of someone who had not slept enough and intended to make that everybody else's problem rather than her own.

"The ward records owners and entrants," he said. "If you try to remove anything from the restricted shelves without telling me, I will know."

"I wasn't planning to smuggle your books."

"Good. This is the sort of house where theft becomes melodrama quickly."

"Charming."

He keyed in her wand signature. The screen split at the center and drew back into the wall with a soft scrape of hidden mechanisms.

She took one look and swore under her breath. Not theatrically. Not for his benefit. Genuine professional shock.

"What?"

"You have two copies of Vablatsky's field amendments."

"Is that unusual?"

"One complete copy is unusual. Two is obscene."

"My ancestors were obscene in several directions."

She was already inside the ward before the line had fully left his mouth, head turning shelf to shelf. He followed because letting anyone stand alone in the restricted section on the first day felt irresponsible even when the anyone in question was Hermione Granger.

"These are arranged by century, not by subject," she said.

"They were arranged by whichever Malfoy acquired them."

"Of course they were."

She drew out a narrow volume bound in cracked blue leather, checked the title page, replaced it, took down another. Her movements remained efficient, but the strain of the entrance hall had shifted form rather than disappearing. He could see it in the way she kept her left arm tucked closer to her body than her right, in the set of her mouth when she found something useful and did not allow herself the luxury of open excitement.

"How much of this have you already gone through for the Mark?" she asked.

"Enough to learn that old families loved writing about permanence and very little about failure."

"That's because failure embarrasses archives."

"It embarrasses families more."

That made her glance at him. A real glance, not a professional scan. For a moment there was something close to recognition in it. Not forgiveness. Certainly not. Just the faintest acknowledgment that he had said a true thing and not hidden himself behind irony while doing it.

"Any family records on alterations to the house itself?" she asked.

"Ledgers, maintenance books, some correspondence. Not organized."

"Fine. I'll organize them."

"That sounded less like a plan than a threat."

"It's both."

Within minutes she had built three stacks on the nearest library table: anchoring theory, domestic magic, and historical curse structures. A fourth pile started when she found references that looked irrelevant but not safely irrelevant. She claimed the western reading table without consultation, then the smaller side table for notes. He let her. Watching her establish territory had a strange steadiness to it. Less invasion than usefulness. Less threat than motion.

"The study's through there," he said, nodding toward the east door. "If you want a separate workspace."

She followed him out of the library proper and into the adjoining room he'd cleared that morning. Compared with the library it was plain: oak table, two lamps, shelves along one wall, windows facing east over a stand of bare trees.

She set her bag on the worktable and finally took off her coat. Underneath she wore a dark green jumper and a buttoned blouse, sleeves down despite the heat trapped in the room.

"This will do," she said.

"High praise."

"It has a door that closes, stable light, and enough table space for notes. That's all I need."

He almost told her there was a locking charm on the inside if she wanted it. The information felt too intimate in his mouth for a first afternoon and too obvious once given. She would find it herself.

"If you leave papers here, the room generally holds them where you put them," he said instead. "More reliably than the rest of the house."

"Generally."

"I said what I meant."

She walked the perimeter once, testing the floorboards with her weight as if the room itself were under examination. At the window she looked out over the grounds, not because the view mattered, he thought, but because it gave her a moment to stand with her back to him and put her face back in order.

"Tomorrow morning," she said. "I'd like to start the Mark diagnostics here. Better light. Better table height. Less chance of a cursed book shouting while I'm trying to concentrate."

"What time?"

"Nine."

"Fine."

"I'll need uninterrupted access for at least an hour."

"You'll have it."

The arrangement had now acquired a table, an hour, a room, and therefore reality.

From there the ground-floor tour covered only what he meant it to cover. The morning room again. The servants' kitchen with its uneven stone floor and grey basin light. Her gaze flicked once to the single mug on the rack, once to the lone plate drying beside it, then to the saucepan he'd left soaking in the sink.

"You weren't exaggerating about no staff," she said.

"Would that have improved the sales pitch?"

"No."

He opened the pantry to show her the shelves. Tea, bread, tins, fruit, whatever the weekly delivery had brought from the village. Nothing elegant. Enough.

"Use what you like," he said. "If you need anything specific for work or for yourself, put it on the list by the door."

"I can manage my own groceries."

"I'm not implying otherwise. The house is remote. The list is practical."

"Right."

She sounded tired rather than chastened, and he disliked himself a little for how quickly his voice had gone formal. He shut the pantry before the conversation could stiffen further.

Through the back corridor he showed her the gardens, then the passage linking kitchen wing to library. The west side stayed unmentioned. So did the shut doors along the way. She did not ask for names. He did not volunteer them. The quiet between those omissions had texture.

Up the main stair, across the landing, into the east arm: the guest corridor panelled in dark wood, sconces brightening as they passed. At the end waited the suite itself. November light came through the windows thin and unforgiving, laying the room bare in a manner he found unexpectedly reassuring.

"Bedroom there," he said. "Sitting room here. Bath through that door. The hot tap behaves better if you threaten it first."

"I'll keep that in mind."

She moved into the room rather than merely looking from the threshold. The first thing she checked was not the bed or the view. It was the door. She turned, tried the latch, found the lock, tested it twice, then let go as if she had not done anything notable at all.

Draco looked away before his noticing became visible.

"The windows stick when it's very cold," he said. "There's an owl perch outside the smaller one. If the Floo starts failing again, Apparition from the drive is easier than from the front steps."

"Do portraits come in here?"

"No."

"Good."

Her attention moved next to the bath, then the wardrobe, then the writing desk by the far wall. Inventory. Exit points. Working surfaces. Not the movements of a guest admiring accommodation. The movements of someone deciding whether a room could be trusted enough to sleep in.

"It's adequate," she said at last.

"There are preservation charms on the bedding. Fresh towels in the bath. If you need anything adjusted, let me know."

"Thank you, Malfoy."

"Granger."

The correction slipped out on old reflex and landed between them oddly, almost lighter than the rest of the exchange because it was so unnecessary. She looked at him, and for a moment he thought she might say something sharper than the day had yet permitted. Instead she set the shrunk case on the writing desk and straightened.

"Tomorrow at nine," she said again.

"I'll be there."

He left her to settle.

Ordinarily the walk back to the library went east corridor, landing, main stair, back passage, portrait gallery. At the foot of the stairs, where the passage should have bent left, a corridor stood open that had been wall that morning.

It wasn't new. It had returned. As a child he had used it often enough to remember his mother's voice following him through it; sometime around adolescence the house had swallowed it, the way it swallowed doors and routes when it no longer cared to keep them. Now it ran clean from the east stair to the library's north entrance, warm sconces lit, no dust on the floor, already smelling of paper and preservation charms.

Two turns were gone. So was the portrait gallery. The route from the east-arm guest rooms to the library had shortened to something indecently direct. The house had not opened a quicker way to the front door or the drive. It had opened one to the library, to the study where tomorrow morning she meant to put his arm on a table and touch the Mark.

Within an hour of Granger crossing the threshold, the Manor had given back a route it had kept from him for years.

The skin at the back of his neck tightened with the old animal warning of being noticed in the dark, of something larger than himself having just swung its head toward him.

One step took him into the passage. The sconces brightened. His footfall came back at him fuller than it did on the usual route, as though the stone were relearning sound.

Three steps from the reading tables, four from his chair, the corridor delivered him to the north library door.

He did not mention it.

The book on the table opened under his hand. Above him, boards in the guest wing answered her movements once, twice, then fell quiet. Behind his chair the north door stayed ajar, with the corridor beyond it still lit as if the house expected traffic.
