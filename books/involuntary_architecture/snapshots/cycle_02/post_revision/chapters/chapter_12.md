# Chapter 12

On Tuesday Draco counted twenty-three paces from the guest wing to the library. By Thursday the same walk took forty-one.

Counting gave the front of Draco's mind a job while the rest of it worked itself raw. Forty-one paces. Same grey stone. Same amber sconces. Same runner underfoot. Only the distances had altered. Twice that morning he had nearly turned toward the guest wing with some excuse about a reference text or the archive. Twice he had kept walking. If Hermione wanted distance, the least he could do was stop presenting himself at her door and calling it work.

Wednesday morning sent him to the gated archive before she arrived. Testing. Pointless, because the room had always required both of them and he knew it, but he went anyway. The alcove in the library's south wall gave him blank stone. No door. No seam. Cold mineral under his palm. He stood there long enough to build and discard three opening lines: *The archive won't open.* *We need the folios.* *Are you avoiding me on purpose.* By nine he was back at his desk with a book open and his face arranged, as if the wall had told him anything useful.

Hermione came in exactly on time with her satchel crosswise over one shoulder and her coat still on against the morning cold. She gave him the nod one might give an overfamiliar colleague and went to the table she had started using after Monday, the one far enough from his to make every ordinary question require volume. Her notes came out in squared stacks. Quill. Ink. Catalogue slips. She set each thing down with the care of a person removing evidence after herself.

A week earlier there would have been a cardigan over the back of his chair by noon. A book left facedown where she'd been checking a citation. Half a cup of tea cooling beside the wrong pile of papers because she'd reached for one source and found six on the way. Now the library held her neatly. By day's end there was no sign she had been in it at all.

At half eleven he found himself in the servants' kitchen without remembering the walk there. The kettles were already singing. An elf had laid out bread, butter, and the tea she preferred, stronger than his, because the house and its staff had learned her habits faster than he had. He sent the elf away, made the tray himself, and told himself that was all it was. Tea. The same thing they had been doing for days. Weeks. No message in it unless she chose to read one.

She looked up when he set the tray on the near edge of her table. Not startled. Only caught.

"I thought you might want tea."

A pause, brief enough to deny and long enough to feel. "Thank you."

No smile. No softening. Her eyes dropped back to the parchment before his hand had cleared the tray. The dismissal was not rude. That made it worse.

"You've got the Devon copy of the drainage notes," he said, because standing there silent had become impossible. "The translation in the margin's wrong. It's not *bank* there. It's *bound*."

"I know." Her quill kept moving. "I'm checking whether the copyist made the same error elsewhere."

Of course she was.

"Right."

He went back to his own table. An hour later, when he crossed for a reference text he did not need, the cup sat exactly where he had placed it, a pale skin cooling over the surface. She had not touched it.

That ought to have been enough explanation on its own. Instead his mind worked at the problem until it had worn grooves. The drawing room was the obvious answer, though not the argument they had had in it. That wound had scabbed over enough to touch. What came after it might have frightened her more: meals that ran long for no reason, books left on his chair, tea made without asking, the ease that had started to feel less like an accident and more like a habit. Perhaps she had looked at whatever had started growing between them and decided the soil in this house was too poisoned to trust. Or perhaps it was simpler. He was a Malfoy. She was Hermione Granger. Distance had been the truest thing between those facts for years.

So he matched her retreat with one of his own. Matching rooms, tempers, and expectations was one of the few talents his family had bothered to cultivate in him. He hated every second of using it here. Saying *what happened* or *why have you gone* would have required breaking first, and Draco Malfoy had been trained too well for that.

Thursday produced the same blank wall at the archive and a more spectacular failure everywhere else. The house had gone parsimonious. Corridors lengthened. The air in unused rooms sat still and unhelpful no matter how many fires he ordered lit. By afternoon he had stopped asking the elves to lay fires anywhere except the rooms he meant to use and started shutting doors behind himself, as if narrowing the Manor into a smaller maze might make it feel less deserted. It did not.

At luncheon Hermione did not come down. He knew because he heard the small dining room doors stay shut and because the tray he told the elf to bring to the library returned an hour later with the sandwich untouched and the apple gone brown where she'd left the knife in it. The sight of that half-cut fruit bothered him disproportionately. Some domestic insult in miniature. She had not even been hungry enough to finish the evidence.

By dinner he had made the worse adjustment. "One place tonight," he told the elf arranging Narcissa's table, and heard the capitulation in his own voice.

The dining room had once been built to flatter a family larger than the one it got. Even the smaller table managed grandeur when laid properly. Candlelight on silver. Porcelain that remembered better decades. Thick curtains against the frost. One place setting turned it funereal. He sat at the end instead of the side they had drifted into using together, because the side made the empty chair opposite too obvious. The elves served pheasant and potatoes and a bitter winter green Hermione had once said tasted like damp soil in a way she somehow meant as praise. He swallowed what he could, drank more wine than he usually permitted himself on a working night, and kept looking at the unused stretch of polished table as if another plate might appear there if he stared hard enough.

The whisky waited in the sideboard where his grandfather had kept it. Amber in cut glass. Family remedy for every form of silence. He took the stopper out, smelled peat and smoke and old habit, and put it back with more force than necessary. The clack against crystal sounded adolescent in the room's quiet. No one witnessed it. That did not improve matters.

At least the Mark offered a measurable problem. The interval between examinations had stretched too far. Without her hands and her spellwork, the curse had grown restless again. It was not the old summoning burn. That had always been command from elsewhere, fire with a master attached. This was pressure building in the bones of his forearm and keeping on because it had nowhere useful to go. By dusk the pain had settled into the wrist and the meat of his palm, making the grip on his fork subtly wrong. He started noting the hour of each flare in the margin of a catalogue sheet, as if precision could compensate for not asking her to touch it.

Two fourteen. Three fifty-one. Five o'clock sharp, bad enough that the quill blotted.

He told himself the record was for Hermione's diagnostic series and not because writing the times down felt less pathetic than wanting her.

Sleep failed in increments. He rebuilt the fire twice, undressed, lay down, sat up again, and finally gave up on the bed altogether when the Mark sent one hard pulse into his elbow that made the muscles jump. The house around him held its breath in that particular way it had when the winter cold got into the walls. He stood at the window with one palm braced on the glass until the pane stole the heat from his skin. Frost silvered the lawns and flattened the yews into dark blocks. Somewhere in the west wing an old board gave a single crack and then thought better of being a sound.

Past midnight the curse flared again, not with emergency heat but with a long slow burn that spread from forearm to shoulder and stayed there, deep and mean as toothache. He pressed the heel of his hand into the inside of the arm until bright points climbed his vision. It did not help. The only thing that had helped for weeks was Hermione's thumb over his pulse and her wand in the other hand and the concentrated frown she wore when she was trying not to care too visibly. The Mark had learned her touch. It knew the cadence of her spells. Three days without either had left it angry.

At half past one he found himself outside his room and halfway down the corridor before he admitted where he was going.

The guest wing landing lay in a draught the rest of the house did not share. The sconces had burnt low. Her door, halfway down, showed a line of gold at the threshold. She was awake then. Or she had fallen asleep with the lamp on. He stood with one hand on the banister and listened hard enough to hear his own pulse.

Nothing from inside. No turning page. No footstep. No voice.

If he knocked, she would either open the door or she would not. Both possibilities looked intolerable. He stayed where he was long enough to become ridiculous, then went back the way he had come, measuring the corridor in reverse as if mathematics could make retreat look chosen.

Morning came without sleep. Friday found him in the library before her, catalogue sheet folded into his pocket, a book open to a page he had read four times and not absorbed once. She stopped in the doorway with her coat still buttoned, as if she needed an extra layer of outside between herself and the Manor, and for one suspended second the space between them was only space.

"The diagnostic series," she said. "We've let the interval run too long."

She was right. The examinations had found their own rhythm and then lost it.

"I know."

She looked at him properly then, at whatever the night had done to his face. "After lunch. The usual setup."

"Fine."

Some small check passed behind her eyes. Once the coat came off, she crossed to the separate table she had claimed earlier in the week and unpacked her satchel with precise, indifferent efficiency. Quill. Ink. Notes. By the time he looked back down at his text, the conversation had already been dismissed.

---

After lunch they used the worktable by the tall windows because the light there was best, even now, even with the winter afternoon already thinning at the edges. The instruments went down in their familiar order: wand, diagnostic crystals tuned to the Mark, the probe resonator she had built herself out of Ministry salvage and a piece of old Malfoy silver, a clean sheaf of parchment for notes. Hermione unrolled the protective cloth around the crystals and checked each one against the light before setting it down. One had a hairline crack through the base from the last series. She turned it between finger and thumb, frowned, and set it aside for the newer one.

"How many flares since Monday evening?"

He took the folded catalogue sheet from his pocket and passed it across. The margin was inked over with times and one or two useless annotations written when the pain had been bad enough to make his hand clumsy.

Her eyes skimmed. "You wrote these down as they happened."

"That was the idea."

"This one says *hand went numb for maybe a minute*."

"That was also the idea."

A faint movement at one corner of her mouth suggested contempt for his phrasing rather than amusement. He took what he could get.

"When did it start radiating past the forearm?"

"Yesterday afternoon."

"Shoulder too?"

"After midnight."

"Headache? Nausea? Any hearing distortion?"

He looked at her. "You think it's getting ambitious."

"I think dead command channels do strange things when they're trying to reconstitute themselves." She laid the sheet beside the quill. "And you look as if you haven't slept."

"I slept some."

"Don't bother lying while I'm taking notes."

Her sleeves were already half rolled. The right stopped neatly below the elbow. The left she managed differently, leaving the cuff lower and arranging one extra fold with more care than the task required. He had noticed the asymmetry before without understanding it. Now he only registered that he was noticing it again.

"Sleeve," she said.

His own left sleeve went back. The Mark looked vicious today, skull and snake cut sharp under inflamed skin. The skin from wrist to inner elbow had taken on the glossy, stretched look it got before a bad flare. She would see the change at once. She did. Her mouth set. She did not comment.

Cold fingers closed around his wrist. Three days without contact had made even that baseline touch a jolt. He kept his face still and failed at the shoulders.

"Your hands are cold," he said.

"The library's cold."

"It is."

Not an argument. Only data. Her thumb settled over his pulse, feeling for something more useful than his answer. Outside the windows the grounds had gone iron-grey under low cloud, hedge lines black with frost. The daylight already looked tired.

"Standard series first," she said. "Then I want to run the deeper mapping again. I've been through last week's data and the secondary decay pattern isn't stable enough to call noise."

"What is it, then."

"I don't know yet."

That was as much uncertainty as Hermione Granger ever put in her voice on purpose. It sharpened the room.

Blue light netted over the Mark. The first diagnostic always felt thin, almost polite, a surface pass over the curse's visible architecture. The crystals took the charge and returned it as layered colour: cobalt for active curse structure, white-fire at the joints where old compulsion channels still tried to remember themselves, a murkier red along the edges when the skin was inflamed enough to distort the readings. She moved the wand slowly, reading off numbers under her breath while the self-scribing quill kept pace on the parchment.

"Stabilization index down another four points," she murmured. "Surface reactivity high. Channel irritation obvious. That's expected."

"Expected is comforting."

"No, it isn't." She didn't look up. "It only means I hate surprises more than I hate bad news."

The curse surged once against the charm and then went obediently still under her hand. Relief came so fast his fingers uncurled on the tabletop before he could stop them. He had not realised how hard he had been bracing until the absence of bracing became possible.

Her eyes flicked from the parchment to his hand. "The pain's been worse."

"Manageable."

"Your definition of manageable is not medically serious."

"How tragic for medicine."

"Malfoy."

There it was, the version of his name that was mostly warning and partly fatigue. He let the deflection go.

"It started flaring harder after dinner yesterday," he said. "And again after midnight."

She nodded once, already inside the second sequence. This one required the resonator. She set the silver frame over the inside of his forearm, adjusted the crystals into the notches around it, and spoke the activation charm at a lower pitch than usual. The device answered with a live hum against the wood of the table. Pale structure lifted out of the Mark in translucent layers, rotating above his skin like something architectural rather than bodily, a small obscene model of damage.

"Don't move."

"I wasn't planning to."

"I'm serious. The deeper pass is unstable today."

"That's reassuring."

"It isn't meant to be."

One hand held his wrist steady. The other guided the wand through the rotating planes, marking intersections, following old magic down into older damage. The setup pulled her closer than the first series had. Her left forearm rested along the table near his hand while she angled the wand with her right. Lavender preservation soap clung faintly to her skin under the mineral dust of the library. The scent had got into the Manor with her and then into the linens, the towels, the air after she left a room. Now, after three days of distance, the ordinary fact of it felt almost invasive.

"The lower channels are inflamed all the way to the elbow," she said, more to the parchment than to him. "You've got sympathetic response along the old coercion lattice. Hold still."

"I am holding still."

"Your pulse is arguing."

He looked down at the place where her thumb pinned it. "You should have a better patient."

"I have the patient I've got."

The quill scratched faster. Sweat gathered at her temples as she pushed deeper than usual, mapping not only Voldemort's brand but whatever lay under it now: the eight months of reactivation, the weeks of resonance, the house's interference or assistance or whatever name one gave to a sentient building behaving like weather around two people. She said numbers. She said *again*. She said *that wasn't there on Monday*. Each phrase fell flat and technical on the air, but the hand on his arm betrayed her. The pressure kept changing by degrees.

"Was it worse in the guest wing corridor?" she asked suddenly.

He went still for reasons unrelated to the warning she'd just given him. "What."

She lifted the catalogue sheet with her wand hand and tapped the line he had written in the margin near the worst flare. *past guest wing. bad one.*

"I assume this means what it looks like it means."

His mouth almost did the old family thing and turned the truth into something elegant and uninformative. He was too tired for elegance.

"I was walking," he said.

"At half one in the morning."

"Obviously not as a hobby."

Her thumb shifted once on his wrist. Not quite approval. Not quite censure.

"You shouldn't let it build to that point before telling me."

"You didn't seem interested in my company this week."

The instant the words were out, he wanted them back. Not because they were false. Because they were too close to the bone to survive the room intact.

Her wand paused midair. The resonator whined as the charm lost its smooth path and then found it again.

"I'm interested in the Mark not tearing half the channels in your arm open," she said. Each word clean. "Whatever else this week was."

There were a dozen different bad ways to answer that. He took the least bad and said nothing.

The work made a scaffold they could sit inside. Within it they were examiner and subject again, researcher and problem, two capable people occupied by a technical difficulty. Her hand on his arm, his pulse under her thumb, the relief moving through the Mark when she touched it: all of that could pass as apparatus if he kept his thinking narrow enough.

For a few minutes he managed it.

Then she shifted the resonator lower. The motion pulled her left sleeve back a fraction. He saw the cuff catch, not on fabric, but on resistance. A shimmer ran over the skin above her forearm, slight as heat over stone. Hermione swore under her breath and corrected the angle, but the strain had already started to show. The glamour did not fail all at once. It thinned.

At first he thought it was only the light on the crystals. Then the even brown tone of her skin went soft around one section and refused to settle. The surface blurred, re-formed, blurred again. Beneath it something paler pressed up with ugly patience.

"Hermione."

She didn't answer. She was staring at the resonator, forcing the spell through one more pass as if she could outwork the problem before it became visible.

The shimmer travelled. What lay underneath resolved by degrees, like lettering under rubbed ash.

The letters had been carved, not cut. Bellatrix had taken her time. The scar tissue lay raised and silver-white against Hermione's skin, edges ragged where the cursed blade had torn instead of slicing cleanly. The word had been made to last.

MUDBLOOD.

Five letters and five more, spanning the inside of her left forearm. The opening strokes bit deepest. The M still carried the hardest pressure, the blade driven down into skin pinned flat against marble. The U dragged a fraction at the lower curve where Hermione had jerked. The final D ran shallower. By then Bellatrix had been laughing, the arm slick, the knife slipping. He knew that sequence because he had watched it happen from twelve feet away and failed to move.

The drawing room came back with the clarity of punishment. The chandelier throwing too much light onto the marble. Bellatrix's skirt dragging through blood. Hermione on the floor with her hair half out of its tie, one hand trapped awkwardly beneath her because Bellatrix had wanted her still and got it. Lucius at the edge of the room, white and vicious and unwilling to interfere with his own sister-in-law's entertainment. The knife flashing when it turned. The smell, metallic and hot, fighting with polish and old smoke. Hermione making a sound through her teeth because screaming would have given Bellatrix pleasure and she had known it even then. Draco against the wall with every survival instinct in his body pointed inward, pretending stillness was not participation.

Before thought caught up, his hand left the table and landed over the scar.

Warm. Raised. Real.

Hermione flinched hard enough to break every spell she was holding. The mapping collapsed into sparks. The resonator screamed once and went dark. One crystal rolled in a bright blue spin across the tabletop and struck the wooden lip. The quill scored one wild line through the parchment and stopped. Her whole body had drawn inward around the exposure, and her forearm went rigid under his palm.

"Granger."

Her gaze stayed fixed on his hand.

"Granger, I-"

"Don't." The word came flat and over-controlled. "Don't say you're sorry."

His fingers started to lift. She caught his wrist before he could move them far. Her grip was hard enough to bruise.

"I wasn't," he said. It was not clear whether that was true.

"No." Her breathing had shortened, then corrected. "No, you were."

The urge to say *I didn't know* rose uselessly to his mouth. He had already failed that line once. Knowledge was not the point. The letters were under his hand whether he had expected them or not.

He expected her to wrench away. Instead she set the wand down with deliberate care, caught the cuff of her left sleeve in her right hand, and dragged it higher. The fabric snagged against the scar tissue for a second before clearing it. She pulled until the last letters stood fully exposed from wrist nearly to elbow. Then she turned the arm completely upward on the table and left it there between them, pulse hammering under the thinnest part of the skin.

"Don't do it halfway," she said.

There was anger in it. Not loud. Harder than loud. He understood then that seeing it by accident and pretending he hadn't would be its own kind of pity. A quick retreat. A moral reflex disguised as courtesy.

Only then did he move his thumb. Slowly, across the first groove of the M.

The scar tissue had its own map under his skin, dense and ridged, healed only in the technical sense that the wound had stopped bleeding. Some parts were smooth with bad magic's overcorrection. Others held tiny catches where the blade had torn going in or out. He traced the U. The D. Under his thumb the letters felt like a record no spell could revise.

Her breathing changed. It did not quicken. It turned deliberate, one measured inhale after another as if she had decided that control could still be had in this room if she chose the units carefully enough.

He read by touch as the light went down. B. L. O. O. D.

At the final letter he stopped, not because the word was finished but because his hand had started to shake. He had not noticed it until the tremor transferred into her skin.

"You can stop," she said.

He looked up. Her face gave him nothing easy. No permission. No accusation. The same expression she wore over a difficult text, stripped of every social buffer.

"I don't want to," he said.

The truth of it made the room smaller.

Something flashed across her face then and disappeared before he could name it. She did not take her arm back.

Back at the beginning, he traced the M again. This time she made a small sound into her closed mouth, half swallowed before it became anything he could safely interpret. Not pain. Or not only pain. The sound went straight through him and lodged somewhere structural.

"I see it," he said. His voice came out scraped raw. "Every time I close my eyes, that room is there. The floor. The chandelier. Your hair on the marble. The way she kept talking to you like the knife was part of the conversation." He stopped because the memory had turned too exact to move through smoothly. "I see it."

Hermione stared at the table between them. "I know."

"I didn't know it was still there. On you. I thought..." The assumption revealed itself only while he was making it. Seven years of believing a Healer or a spell or Hermione's own brutal competence must have erased it. Must have filed it under war damage already processed. "I thought somebody had fixed it."

That got her eyes back on his face.

"Fixed it."

He heard the failure as soon as it left his mouth. Not because the word was wrong in medicine. Because it implied there had been a version of her afterward that needed restoring to acceptability.

"I mean-"

"I know what you mean."

"No, you don't. I mean I thought there'd been a treatment. A curse reversal. Something."

"Where would it go?"

The question dropped straight through him. It wasn't sharpened for effect. She sounded honestly baffled.

His thumb stalled on the L. The scar kept all its ridges under his skin, the first cuts driven deeper than the last. Hermione had her arm turned upward between them because there was nowhere else for it to have gone.

"Dark magic doesn't heal clean," she said. "You know that."

"I know." His thumb rested on the L. "I just wasn't thinking about yours."

"No." Her gaze dropped to his hand on the scar. "People don't."

He thought of the extra fold she had made in the left sleeve before the examination. He thought of how often she kept that arm angled away from the light, and of his own willingness to take the glamour for the whole answer. She had been hiding it in front of him for days, and he had let her.

"You cover it every day," he said.

"Every day."

"With glamour."

"And sleeves. And not wearing half the things I used to own. And pretending I don't notice when it's July and I'm the only person in the room buttoned to the wrist." She said it evenly. The evenness made the cost plain. "It took me longer than I'd like to admit to work out that the glamour only gets me so far under heavy magic. So I built the rest of my life around the sleeve."

He followed the raised edge of the B with one fingertip. "Because when people see it, they stare."

"They stare first," she said. "Then they correct themselves and try not to, which is worse. Or they ask whether it still hurts, which is idiotic because of course it still hurts sometimes, it's cursed tissue. Or they tell me I was brave." Her mouth twisted. "As if I had a choice worth naming."

The library held the sound of her breathing and the soft settling ticks from the spent resonator. Outside, some wind moved against the windows without enough force to rattle them.

"When I first went back to the Ministry after the war, I covered it badly," she said. The words came as if she had not intended them and now could not justify stopping. "A clerk in Records saw it in the lift. I'd spoken to him every day for a week by then. He'd argued with me about filing procedure and borrowed my pencil and called me difficult. After that he started asking whether I needed someone to walk me home. Every morning. He never argued with me again." She looked at him steadily. "That was the week I learned pity can make a room unusable faster than fear."

He had spent years thinking hatred was the worst way a room could tilt around a person. Hatred at least admitted personhood. Pity reduced.

"You didn't want that from me."

"I didn't want it from anyone." She pulled a breath in through her nose, let it out carefully. "I can work with almost anything except being turned into evidence. Once people know, the scar arrives before I do. It does the talking. I don't."

His hand, still on the word, felt suddenly both intrusive and insufficient. Not because she had asked him to stop. Because she had not.

"What am I doing right now, then."

Her eyes went to his face, assessing as if the answer might change while she watched it. "You're still here."

It was such a small distinction that he nearly missed the scale of it.

"You think that'll last."

"I think you don't know yet." A beat. "Most people don't."

He could not argue. There was a part of him that wanted to separate himself from *most people* on principle. That part sounded adolescent even in silence. Instead he let his fingers rest against the letters and told the truth he had been dragging around the room since the moment the glamour failed.

"I watched," he said. The admission came out heavy and badly shaped. "I stood against the wall and watched her do it. I was sixteen. I was terrified. My father was in the room. None of that is enough. I know it isn't."

"No." Her voice held no mercy and no cruelty either. Only fact. "It isn't."

He nodded once. The answer hurt less than the possibility of absolution would have.

"And don't do the other thing either," she said.

He looked up.

"Don't turn this into your confession because it's easier to hold your guilt than what happened to me." Her thumb tapped once, almost absent-mindedly, against the inside of his wrist where her hand still lay from the examination. "I'm not carrying that for you."

The rebuke landed clean because it was deserved. He had been raised around people who made confession into performance and remorse into a method of keeping the subject themselves. He knew the trick well enough to feel sick at having approached it by instinct.

"I'm not asking you to."

"Good."

The word lay between them a moment. Fire shifted low in the grate. One of the diagnostic crystals, forgotten on its side, slowly bled the last of its stored light into the table.

He looked back down at the scar. "I'm not going to apologize."

Her eyes narrowed a fraction, not because the line was wrong, but because it risked being one more version of male self-importance if he handled it badly.

"That sounds very like something a Malfoy would say."

He deserved that too. "Probably. Let me finish."

She waited.

"If I say sorry, then you have to answer it." His thumb pressed into the groove of the L. "Even if you don't want to, the room changes. You're stuck telling me it wasn't my fault or it was a long time ago or I was sixteen or some other thing that makes this smaller so we can both stand up and pretend we've done the civilized part." The sentence frayed and he let it fray. "And I can't make this—" He stopped. Shook his head once. His thumb stayed on the L.

The hammering in her pulse eased by degrees under his hand. Across the table her other hand still circled his wrist where it had held him for the examination. He only registered it fully when her thumb shifted once against the skin below the Mark, a live warm stroke that might have been accidental if everything else in the room had not been so deliberate.

"Nobody does that," she said. Her voice had dropped out of its usual register. "Everyone always—" She stopped. "Nobody just stays in it."

No register he possessed offered a useful reply. He left the air between them unfilled, and for once the omission did not feel like cowardice.

She moved first again. Her hand turned on his arm and settled flat over the skull and snake. The touch had nothing to do with diagnosis at first. Warm palm. Warm fingers. His Mark quieted under it with humiliating speed.

She felt it happen. Her brows drew together, not in surprise, but in concentration.

"It's hot all the way through," she said, almost to herself. "Much worse than Monday."

"You can stop."

"So can you, apparently. You haven't been telling me half of it."

"I'm telling you now."

"Because I found the margin notes and because you look dreadful."

"Professional victory to you."

"Don't be difficult."

"I wasn't aware I had another texture."

That almost got the corner of her mouth again. Almost. She shifted her hand higher, feeling along the swollen edge of the curse with her fingertips rather than the flat of her palm. The motion was clinical until it wasn't. No spell. No crystals. Just the knowledge in her hands and what his skin did in answer.

"It settled the second I touched it," she said.

"It usually does."

"Not like this."

He made the mistake of meeting her eyes. Whatever she read in his face, she chose not to name.

"The deeper pass isn't finished," she said after a moment. "The readings broke when the glamour did, but the flare pattern is bad enough that I need at least a stabilization sweep before I leave it alone."

"Can you do that."

"Yes." She glanced at her exposed forearm, then back at him. "Unless you're about to tell me the sight of me is too upsetting for the science."

His grip on the scar tightened by a fraction before he made himself ease it. "Don't be obscene."

Her eyes held his for one suspended second. "Good."

She withdrew her hand from the Mark only long enough to retrieve the wand. The loss of contact made the curse stir at once, a small ugly flex under the skin. Both of them felt it. Hermione swore softly, put the wand down again, and set her left hand back over the Mark before the flare could build.

"Not like that," she said. "It's keyed too tightly right now."

The statement carried more than the immediate problem. He heard the rest of it and had no better response than stillness.

"What do you need."

"The flare log. The clean parchment. And don't move unless I tell you."

He passed the catalogue sheet with his free hand while she kept the other pinned under her palm. She drew the unruined pages toward herself, uncapped the ink, and began copying the times from his margin notes in quick precise strokes, all without removing her hand from his arm. The position brought them absurdly close over the table, shoulders nearly touching, her hair fallen forward enough that he could see the loosened curl at the nape of her neck.

"This one," she said, tapping the line from half one. "You wrote *guest wing* and then crossed it out."

"I noticed."

"You crossed it out so badly I had to angle the page toward the light to read it."

He said nothing.

"I am not going to ask what you thought you were doing."

"Thought would be generous."

That got him a brief look. Not soft. Not hard either. Simply present.

"All right," she said. "Then I'm not going to answer it."

The changed mercy of that nearly undid him.

She worked through the notes and the broken data side by side, reconstructing what the self-scribing quill had caught before the spell collapse. Numbers. Channel density. Reaction intervals. The language returned them to safer ground without fully rescuing them from what had happened. Every few lines she paused, eyes half closed, feeling with her hand what the instrument had not finished telling her.

"The secondary decay isn't decay," she said finally.

"What is it."

"I said I don't know yet."

"But you think something."

Her thumb moved once over the inner curve below the skull. "I think the curse is trying to use a pathway it didn't have when it was first cast. That's why the flare profile looks wrong. It's not summoning. It's not simple reactivation." She stopped herself with visible effort. "I need more data before I call it anything."

He had spent enough time beside her work to recognise that tone. Not caution. Withholding. Not necessarily from him. Sometimes Hermione's discipline took the form of refusing language before language could simplify a thing.

"Does it end with my arm still attached."

"I expect so."

"An inspiring bedside manner."

"I'm not here bedside."

No. She was here with her hand on his Mark and Bellatrix's word open to the room between them, which was something far worse and far better than bedside.

When the clean page had more numbers than blank space, she set the quill aside and finally looked down at the contact holding his arm quiet. Her forearm lay fully bared beside his. Scar and Mark close enough that one more inch would have put silver letters against blackened snake. The juxtaposition was so violent it almost looked contrived. It wasn't. It was only history refusing to distribute itself fairly.

He heard the shape of the thought before he said it and knew it would land badly.

"This isn't the same."

Hermione's head lifted sharply. "No."

"I didn't mean-"

"I know what you meant." Her voice had roughened at the edges, exhaustion or strain or the simple fact of sitting here unhidden. "But don't let the sight of them next to each other tempt you into symmetry. Your arm is what they did to you. Mine is what your side did to me in your house. Those things can talk to each other. They are not twins."

The correction did not humiliate him. It steadied the room.

"All right," he said.

That seemed to cost her more than the speech had. Some of the tension went out of her shoulders. "All right."

They sat with that for a while. Fire dropped lower. The colour had gone out of the windows completely, leaving only the reflection of the room in the glass: table, instruments, two bent figures, one bare forearm and another. Somewhere in the Manor a clock sounded six, muffled by distance and too much house between.

Hermione blinked down at the page as if the sound had reminded her there was still a sequence to the afternoon. "I need the tea."

It took him a second to understand the sentence. Not because it was unclear. Because she had asked.

"I'll get it."

Her hand remained on his arm until the last possible second. When she lifted it, the Mark stirred once in complaint and then subsided, as if whatever protest it had planned had been delayed by the certainty of return. He did not comment on that. Neither did she.

The corridor to the servants' kitchen was thirty paces again.

He noticed it, stopped, and kept walking.

The kitchen fires were hotter than the library's, iron kettles steaming and copper pans hanging dull in the light. An elf appeared before he could call and looked up at him with the wary competence of a creature who had learned the household's tempers generations ago.

"Tea for the library," he said. "Strong. And send up something edible."

The elf's ears twitched. "For one place or two, sir?"

The question struck him so hard it might as well have been aimed. "Two."

Bread arrived first, then a plate of cold chicken, pickled onions, hard cheese, and the heel of a loaf still warm in the middle. By the time he carried the tray back himself, the answer had not grown easier in his mouth. Two.

Hermione had not moved far. She was at her own table now with the pages spread around her in a semicircle, making a clean transcript from the ruined notes. The left sleeve stayed shoved above the elbow. She looked up when he came in, glanced to the tray, and then to his face with an unreadable expression that might once have become refusal.

"It's only tea," he said, too quickly. "And bread. You haven't eaten since-"

He stopped. There was no safe measurement to finish that with.

"Since luncheon," she said for him, though they both knew the luncheon tray had gone back mostly untouched. Her eyes dropped to the bread, then to his hands. "All right."

He set the tray on the table between the workspaces, not too close to either of them. An old instinct made him reach for the second cup first. He checked it halfway to the saucer and let himself finish the motion rather than pretending not to have noticed. Hermione watched the correction happen and said nothing.

The first swallow put colour back into her face so incrementally he might have imagined it if he had not been looking for any evidence he had not made things worse by bringing the tray at all.

She held the cup in her left hand.

There was no performance in it. No testing glance to see whether he would flinch now that the scar had become part of an ordinary gesture. She was simply thirsty, tired, and unwilling to waste the effort of hiding from him again tonight. The letters curved white around the handle.

He broke bread because doing nothing with his hands had become impossible. She took half when he offered it. That was all. No ceremony. The simplicity of it hurt more than a declaration would have.

For several minutes the only sounds were cup against saucer, quill against parchment, and the low stubborn hum still trapped inside the damaged resonator. The quiet had changed register. Earlier in the week it had been refusal. Now it was work the room had permission to hold.

Hermione finished the first page of clean notes and slid it aside. "Read me the flare times again."

He did. She wrote them into the margin beside the deeper measurements. When his voice roughened at the second half-one entry, she did not look up. The mercy of that was beginning to feel less like avoidance and more like skill.

"This line," she said, tapping the jagged place where the quill had gone wild at the spell break. "I think this is the channel response, not artifact."

He came around to her side of the table before he had considered whether that was now allowed. She made space without moving her chair back. Their shoulders nearly touched over the parchment.

"Here," he said. "That isn't a three. The tail continues underneath the blot. It's eight."

"It could be either."

"It's eight. You do the same shape when you're rushing."

She turned her head just enough to look at him. "You've started identifying my numerals."

"We've been working together for weeks."

"That wasn't an accusation."

It had sounded like one because he still heard accusation in any sentence that revealed he had been watching her closely. He looked back down at the page.

"It's eight," he said again, more quietly.

Her quill hovered over the line. "Fine. Eight."

The concession should not have mattered. It did.

They worked through the rest of the broken series that way: her reading the instrument's shorthand, him recalling where the line had been interrupted, both of them pretending the arrangement was ordinary enough to survive. At one point she reached past him for the catalogued folio they had been pretending to consult all morning and her forearm brushed the inside of his wrist. The Mark answered with a short, almost grateful pulse. They both felt it. Neither commented.

By the time the last page was in order, the food had thinned to crumbs and the tea had gone from hot to merely warm. Hermione set her quill down and flexed her cramped fingers. Ink had smudged along the side of her hand.

"The inflammation's lower now," she said without looking at him. "The surface heat came down once the contact stabilized."

"Because of you."

"Because of the examination."

He let the correction stand even though they both knew it was only partly true.

Her gaze shifted to the scar, to the letters made ordinary by proximity and impossible by content. She rubbed a thumb once along the inside of her wrist, not over the word exactly, but close enough to count.

"I should recast it," she said.

"Should you."

His two words came out level, but she heard the rest anyway. Recasting it now would return them to the week she had just spent building: one where the skin existed and the room pretended it did not. Perhaps that would be easier tomorrow. It seemed impossible tonight.

She leaned back in the chair and shut her eyes for one brief second. When she opened them again, they were clearer.

"Not tonight," she said.

The answer did something to his breathing he did not trust enough to examine closely.

She gathered the clean pages into order. He watched the care with which she aligned the corners, the competence returning as structure around whatever the afternoon had exposed. It would have been easy to mistake that competence for retreat. He knew better now, or knew enough not to make that mistake casually.

"This week," he said before he could decide whether it was wise, "was it because of this."

Hermione's hands stopped on the pages. Not frozen. Arrested.

"Partly," she said after a moment.

The single word sat there with all the restraint of an entire closed door.

He could have asked the rest. He could have said *and the other part?* He could have tried to prise information out of the space that answer left behind. Instead he looked at the bare forearm still visible between them and at the exhaustion she had not bothered disguising.

"All right."

Her eyes came to him then with something very like surprise.

That was not the end of what she had not told him. He knew it. Some asymmetry still stood in the room with them, technical or personal or both. But whatever had happened this afternoon, he had no intention of forcing it at wandpoint by calling a question honesty. Not tonight.

For the first time in days, the silence that followed did not feel punitive.

Hermione gathered the instruments back into their cloth in slower order than she had set them out. The cracked crystal went into a separate wrap. The resonator, still sulking from the break in the spell, required both hands and a muttered adjustment charm before it would collapse into travel size. Draco took the parchment stack when she handed it over without asking why. He held the pages while she tied the ribbon around them. A small task. Domestic almost. The sort of thing that would once have passed unnoticed. Now his awareness of each transfer of weight felt indecently sharp.

When the last instrument was away, she did not roll the left sleeve back down.

That fact altered the room more than any declaration could have. Not permanently. Not, perhaps, beyond tonight. But enough. Enough that the sight of the scar stopped arriving as revelation and started arriving as presence. Still terrible. Still specific. No longer newly hidden.

He realised after a while that the Mark had gone completely quiet.

Not numb. Not absent. Resting.

Hermione saw him notice.

"Does it hurt now."

"No."

"At all."

He tested the answer honestly. The arm still ached in the deep bruised way any cursed tissue ached after being roused and handled. But the live viciousness had gone out of it. The commandless reach. The blind agitation.

"No," he said again. "Not like before."

She nodded and made a note of that on the cleanest page, as if the relief deserved the dignity of record.

The hour had pushed fully into evening. The fire had dropped to coals. Most of the candles in the library were guttering, wax spilled into pale loops down the iron branches. He should have rung for more light. He did not. The half-dark felt proportionate to the day.

Hermione drew the final page toward her and began the formal transcription the Ministry would eventually see if she ever decided this set of notes was fit for any eyes but her own. Her hand had steadied. The script on the page was the competent, elegant version of her work, not the slanting field notes she allowed herself in the middle of a live problem. She wrote with her left forearm bare above the parchment, the silver letters catching and disappearing as the candlelight moved.

He went back to the other table because remaining at her shoulder while she made the clean copy would have turned intimacy into surveillance. From there he could still hear the scratch of the quill. He opened the book he had failed to read all morning and found, to his surprise, that the words now stayed where they were put.

A little later an elf came silently to clear the tray. Draco almost spoke, then thought better of it. Let the household see what it saw. Let it take the measure of the untouched second cup no longer waiting untouched.

Hermione did not look up until the elf had gone.

"The archive should open tomorrow," she said, eyes still on the page.

He should have asked how she could know that. He should have made some dry remark about the house's opinions or the unreliability of magical architecture with emotional ambitions. Instead he heard only the plain fact under the sentence: *tomorrow.* Not departure. Not another day of shut doors and professional frost. Tomorrow.

"All right," he said.

Her quill moved again. "We'll test it before nine. If it doesn't, I want the surface measurements from the south wall and the threshold stone."

"You assume I'm awake before nine."

"I assume misery has made you punctual."

Against every reasonable instinct, that almost made him laugh. The sound came out small and rough and real enough that her mouth changed in answer, not quite a smile, but the memory of one making a first attempt back into the room.

Then she bent over the parchment again and kept writing.

The left sleeve stayed where she had shoved it. Each pass of the quill carried the silver letters through the dying firelight, and across the room his Mark stayed quiet.
