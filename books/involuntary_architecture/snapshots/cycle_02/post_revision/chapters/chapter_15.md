# Chapter 15

By midnight they still hadn't forced the Levett manuscript to yield.

They'd been at it since dinner, or since what had passed for dinner: a tray sent down at seven and abandoned in stages over the next four hours until the tea went tannic and the bread hardened at the edges. Hermione had eaten most of the cheese without noticing. Malfoy had eaten whatever his hand found first, bread, then the sliced apple she'd forgotten she'd cut, then the heel of a loaf gone dry near the fire. At some point the house-elves had replaced the teapot without interrupting them. At some later point he had nudged her plate back within reach after she let it drift under a stack of parchment. Neither of them had thanked the other for any of it. The careful politeness of the first weeks had thinned enough that these small acts now passed between them unannounced, as if naming them would make them fragile again.

The text itself looked like the work of a man who despised posterity. It was a fourteenth-century construction manual for anchoring infrastructure, written by a Malfoy called Levett only because one branch of the family had briefly used maternal names for younger sons. Marginal annotations ran in three languages and at least two hands. The primary text used runic modifiers neither of them had seen independently, but cross-reference between her theoretical framework and his family knowledge of the symbols had made the whole thing almost legible.

For the past forty minutes, one passage had refused to yield.

"It's not 'binding,'" she said, frowning at the glyph cluster in the center of the page. "The radical is wrong. It's more like threading. Weaving through?"

"Plaiting." He sat close enough that their shoulders nearly met every time they leaned over the text, which was often. Cedar soap clung to him, and beneath it lived the mineral tang of the deep archive, preservation charms layered so thick in the air that everything tasted faintly of chalk. All evening his handwriting had kept shrinking in the margins of their working translation, tighter and denser with each hour. "The secondary radical modifies it. It's a textile metaphor. My great-great-however-many-times grandmother was apparently a weaver before she married into the name."

"How do you know that?"

"There's a tapestry. Third floor. She wove it herself, and my mother made sure I knew, because..." The sentence stopped there. Nearly midnight, and whatever had almost followed was more than he meant to hand over. "Because apparently every dead Malfoy must remain educational."

That made her smile. "Public service through embroidery."

"Textiles," he said, as if accuracy could protect him from the joke. "Apparently."

So plaiting was the right word.

Under that reading, the anchoring magic described threads drawn through substrate, through land and ambient field, then braided into a structure that held. It was not bound or hammered into place. It was woven. At once the decay looked different: not masonry failing but fabric coming apart, thread after thread slipping loose.

"That works," she said. "Look. If you read it as plaiting, then this section follows. The whole anchoring system is textile structure. The ley-line connections aren't channels, they're threads. And war damage would be..."

"Cuts. Tears in the fabric."

"Which means the Ministry's been trying to mend a tapestry by building scaffolding around it. No wonder nothing's held."

He pulled a second text toward him, a later manuscript in Latin with French glosses. The page he wanted had been marked with a strip of parchment in his own hand two days ago. Without thinking about it, she watched him scan the Latin. His reading speed there beat hers, one of the few places pureblood education had outpaced her own. Sometime in the third week, she'd stopped resenting that and started relying on it. "This passage references a repair method," he said. "Rethreading. Actually running new thread through the existing warp instead of patching over damage."

"You'd need access to the original structure. The loom, essentially."

"Which would be the anchoring points themselves."

"Which are..."

"Under the house. Among other places."

Across the desk their eyes met. The implication settled with the same weight so many things had been carrying between them lately. What they found in this library was going to matter beyond the two of them and beyond this room.

"We should map them."

"Not tonight." He pushed his chair back a fraction and stretched until his neck cracked.

"If I stop now I'll lie awake rebuilding the notation in my head."

"You already do that."

"I do not."

He looked down at the manuscript instead of at her. "Last Tuesday you informed an imaginary review committee that it had confused depth with direction."

Heat rose into her face. "I was half asleep."

"You were indignant in your sleep."

"I'm usually indignant awake. There's no reason to waste the talent."

That got the breath of a laugh she had learned to look for and never to press. He scrubbed one hand over his mouth, considering the page and the hour as if the books might rescue him from the fact that he had already given in.

"Twenty minutes," he said.

It took closer to forty just to get the surveyor's case down.

The second gallery ran along the north wall above the reading floor, iron rail cold under Hermione's fingers and the upper shelves sunk in a dustier dark than the lamps below reached comfortably. From up there the library looked smaller and stranger. The reading table where they'd been working made a bright island in the middle of the room, cluttered with cups, parchment, and the loose domestic debris of two people who no longer remembered to stage-manage each other's presence. Their chairs sat too close. Her cardigan hung over the back of his because she'd shed it hours ago and neither of them had moved it. The view of their evening, seen from above, made it look less like research and more like a life.

He had already wedged himself into a narrow gap between folios of property disputes and seventeenth-century drainage schemes by the time she came up beside him. "You weren't meant to follow."

"You said twenty minutes. I wanted to watch you fail efficiently."

"That's ungenerous."

"It's accurate."

The leather case had been shoved behind a row of linen-backed rolls and one heavy atlas of tenant boundaries. He pulled once and nothing moved. The second attempt got the case half free and the atlas sliding toward the rail.

Hermione caught the atlas before it fell. "Do you want help or would that wound your bloodline?"

"The bloodline's been wounded plenty." He shifted to make room, which on the narrow gallery meant her stepping almost into him. "Take the left side."

The case came free with a gritty scrape. Dust shook loose over both of them. He coughed into his sleeve and looked offended, as if ancestral paper ought to have behaved with more decorum.

"What were you doing with surveyor's records at twelve?" she asked while they wrestled the weight of it between them.

"Hiding from a dinner party."

"That part I believed already."

He kept his attention on the case as they maneuvered it toward the stair. "The adults were boring and the children were worse. Theo had discovered he could set the peacocks fighting if he whistled through his teeth. Pansy was helping. Blaise was pretending not to help. I opened the wrong cabinet and found maps."

"And that was preferable?"

"The maps stayed where they'd been put." He waited a beat before adding, "Mostly."

That answer landed lower than the joke warranted. She looked at him, but he was already descending ahead of her with the case, shoulders tight under the weight.

They set it on the reading table with a thud heavy enough to upset the spoons in both teacups. The brass latches stuck for a moment before giving. Inside lay rolled vellum plans, folded linen surveys, depth tables, tenant boundaries, drainage schematics, and a brass divider in a fitted slot lined with green baize gone black at the edges. Tabs in three different hands labeled sections by century. Malfoy lifted one roll with the wary competence of someone who'd been taught archival manners before he was old enough to object to them.

"This is absurd," Hermione said softly.

"That narrows nothing."

"No, I mean this specifically. Your family indexed six hundred years of land management and then wrote the real information in code around the edges."

"If it's any consolation, we also did that to one another."

He said it lightly, but not lightly enough.

They spread the first vellum plan across the table and discovered at once that the table was too small. A second plan followed. Then a third. Within minutes the reading floor had become an improvised worksite. She sat cross-legged on the rug weighting one corner with the Latin manuscript while he knelt opposite with a stack of boundary rolls pinning another. He found the pre-war estate survey at last, linen-backed and brittle at the folds, the Manor rendered in hard ink lines that still assumed walls were honest and corridors meant what they measured.

Hermione laid a sheet of tracing vellum over it and began copying the nodi from Levett's notation while Malfoy translated depth marks from family shorthand into proper measurements. The work moved quickly once the principle clicked. Knot-points sat where plaited lines converged, not at decorative centers but at structural junctions: below the eastern library wall, under the old central stair, beneath a stretch of corridor that fed into the guest wing, and farther out under service passages that no longer existed on the present floor plan. Every point mapped to age, load, and repetition, to places people crossed often and the house had reason to keep live.

"If Levett's right, repair isn't reinforcement," Hermione said, not looking up from the vellum. "It's rethreading load through surviving structure. Which means every Ministry team working with buttresses and brace charms has been addressing symptoms."

"Not causes."

"Yes." Her pencil moved faster. "If the filum has frayed at the nodes, then all the stabilization work they've done is cosmetic. It would hold long enough to generate a report and fail again the moment the pressure returned. If we can prove that here, the rest of the network stops looking impossible."

"Comforting."

"I didn't say it was comforting."

He gave her a look over the map edge that would once have counted as dry and now carried too much tiredness to manage dryness properly. "No. You rarely do."

She found herself smiling again, brief and unwilling. The pencil kept moving. One line from the eastern knot-point ran under the library toward the deeper archive. Another fed north before bending in a way that made no sense on the present plan until she unrolled a second survey and saw the older corridor arrangement beneath later renovations. The guest wing had not been built where it now sat. The rooms had shifted functions over generations, but the lines underneath had remained stubborn about where paired traffic wanted to travel.

"Look at this," she said.

He leaned over the vellum. Their heads nearly knocked. "Guest wing junction."

"It should be farther west."

"It was, on the earlier plan."

"Not just the plan." She reached for the older survey and flattened it with her palm. "Here. The original corridor run is longer, then the later survey shortens it after the east wall alterations. But the knot-point doesn't move with the wall. It stays at the same load point. The architecture changed around it."

His eyes tracked the overlay. "Meaning?"

"Meaning the house may be routing movement toward the loaded points rather than away from them." She heard how that sounded and forced herself back into the language of work. "For maintenance. Or because those paths carry more ambient traffic. Or both."

He was still looking at the map, but the stillness in him changed. "You think it's deliberate."

She almost said, *Of course it's deliberate. I know it is.* Instead she picked up the brass divider and measured the distance between the library threshold and the guest-wing bend on the survey. "I think the plan says one thing and the house keeps doing another. There's a difference."

"There's also a difference," he said, "between a corridor shifting by a few feet and the Manor making policy choices."

"You say that as though the corridor business is ordinary."

"In this house?" He lifted one shoulder. "Less ordinary than creaking pipes, more ordinary than I'd prefer."

That irritated her for reasons too numerous and too hypocritical to sort cleanly. "How long have you known the route changes that much?"

He took too long to answer. "A while."

"And you didn't mention it."

"You were already conducting research in a sentient estate built into Britain's failing magical infrastructure. I wasn't sure 'the corridor's in a mood' would improve your morale."

"You might have let me decide that."

The reply came faster than she expected. "You might have told me if you were worried."

Silence answered that. He had not meant to say it. She had no honest answer to offer.

He looked back down first, rearranging the surveyor's rolls into needless straightness. She could feel the moment wanting to turn into apology or retreat. She did not want either.

"Come on," she said.

His eyes lifted. "Where?"

"To count."

He stared at her as if assessing whether fatigue had finally pushed her into a form of madness he ought to respect. Then he rose without argument, because curiosity had become their least dangerous shared vice.

The corridor outside the library was warm enough to make the cold brass latch sweat against Hermione's hand. Sconces had already brightened ahead of them, throwing light farther down the passage than they had when she'd come up from dinner. She marked the library threshold with the tip of her wand, a thin blue line only they would see, and checked the survey again.

"To the turn by the guest wing door," she said.

"I know where my own corridor turns."

"Good. Then you'll know if it cheats."

He made a quiet noise that might have been amusement. They started walking.

The first count came too quickly. Twenty-six paces took them to the bend where the passage should still have had another stretch of wall before Hermione's rooms began. The sconce nearest the turn lit as they approached, bright and eager. Beyond it, the carpet runner the house had laid down in November continued in the exact blue-grey Narcissa would have chosen if warmth could masquerade as restraint.

Hermione stopped and checked the survey again. The drawn distance and the lived one would not reconcile.

"Again," she said.

"That wasn't enough proof?"

"No."

They turned immediately and walked back toward the library. The return took thirty-two.

At pace twenty-nine she looked up, certain she had lost count. The blue wand-mark glimmered ahead, still three strides away.

Malfoy stopped when she did. He had come close enough that the heat of him touched the air at her back without contact. For a strange second she was more aware of that than of the corridor.

"You noticed," he said.

"That it changed under us? Yes."

"I meant before tonight."

She turned. He was holding the survey rolled under one arm, thumb pressed white against the linen edge. No irony left in his face at all.

"I've noticed the walk shorten," she said carefully. "I didn't know whether to call that architecture, fatigue, or the house trying to be useful."

"Those aren't mutually exclusive."

"No." Her voice came out thinner than she liked. "Apparently not."

The corridor held itself around them with that attentive, almost-polite warmth the Manor had been favoring for weeks. The guest-wing door stood closed at the end of the short run. Behind her, the library waited open. The house had pulled the two spaces too near each other and was acting as though proximity were kindness.

If the knot-point lay here, then the shortening was not merely convenience. It was routing through repetition, a path walked often enough to matter. Paired inhabitants brought over the same threaded span until the house could read them by movement alone.

Her stomach turned. The insight arrived dressed as theory, but it hit lower than theory.

"Granger."

She realized he had said it without the usual dry edge, almost gently.

"What?"

"You're doing that thing again."

She knew precisely which thing he meant: going still, leaving the room while her body stayed in it. "I'm thinking."

"Dangerous habit."

"Shut up."

That landed better than it should have. The corner of his mouth shifted. It wasn't quite a smile, more the memory of one.

Then his gaze dropped to the blue line by the threshold, to the impossible distance between it and where they stood, and the humor left him again. "If the route's changing around the knot-point..."

"Then the architecture isn't just sitting on the infrastructure." She forced the words into the driest shape available. "It's participating in maintenance. Or trying to."

"And that's good?"

"I didn't say that either."

He nodded once. The wand-mark threw a faint light between them.

"We need the estate cross-index," she said. "The survey tells us parcel and depth, but without the ley-line correspondence table we're guessing at which lower runs feed which rooms."

"There may be one in the east-wall records." He glanced back toward the library. "Third unit, if the house hasn't buried it behind a wall of tedious parcel ledgers."

"Tomorrow, then."

"Tomorrow."

They stood another second too long before moving. On the way back the route shortened again. Neither of them counted aloud this time. They didn't need to.

Inside the library, Hermione wrote both pace counts in the margin of her working notes and underlined them twice. Malfoy resettled the surveys with the careful irritation of a man pretending paper required all of his focus. The room had shifted during their absence. Another lamp burned at the side table though neither of them had lit it. A fresh pot waited beside the kettle. Two more cups sat out.

Hermione stared at them. "Did you ask for that?"

"No."

"Convenient."

"That's one word for it."

She wanted to banish the spare cups, then realized the gesture would be theatrical and childish and therefore tempting. Instead she sat again and pulled the Levett manuscript toward her. Malfoy poured tea into the cups the house had provided as if refusing the tea would grant the house a moral victory it had not earned.

The tea was exactly right. It always was now. Milk, brew time, the small bitter strength she preferred late at night. He had never asked. He'd simply observed until the cup he made matched the cup she made herself, and the precision of that care still landed in her body like an injury she had chosen.

They worked for another hour.

The maintenance protocol turned out to be worse than the construction notes, not because it was more obscure but because it assumed a body of family knowledge Levett had seen no reason to explain. Rethreading required access to the old load points, yes, but it also required sequence. Disturb the wrong knot first and tension redistributed badly through the remaining spans. The later Latin glosses helped only enough to make disaster legible. Hermione copied sequence markers while Malfoy hunted corroboration in property notes, his face drawn with the effort of holding archaic shorthand and live inference in the same place.

At intervals they cross-checked the maps. The eastern library node fed downward toward the archive and outward toward the corridor they'd just walked. A second line ran under older family rooms no one used now. A third vanished beneath renovations that had outlived the logic behind them. The house was not a neutral shell around the network. It was one of the knots, old enough and inhabited enough to have learned preferences of its own.

If they could map the Manor cleanly, they might have the first workable repair sequence anyone had found since the crisis became impossible to dismiss. The thought should have steadied her. It usually did. Instead each breakthrough arrived twined with the same private knowledge she still hadn't handed over: the house preferred paired inhabitants, widened awareness, pressed on attachment already present. She knew too much to greet any new proof cleanly.

Midnight passed without announcement except in their bodies. Her neck had gone stiff. The bridge of his nose held the red mark of two fingers pressed there too often. Ink darkened the side of her hand. Dust tracked his sleeve where the surveyor's case had fought him on the gallery shelf. The fire in the grate burned low and neither of them bothered rebuilding it because the Manor had been warm for days now, walls holding heat with the steady generosity of a house that wanted its inhabitants comfortable. Outside, December did what December did in Wiltshire: frost on the grounds, dark since four, wind finding old window joins and making that thin winter sound she'd started associating with these late hours.

Around them the library had settled into what she'd started thinking of as its real self. It was not the sunlit room she'd catalogued in early November, when every shelf had looked like evidence and every chair like a diplomatic problem. At night it drew inward. Lamplight shortened the room. Shelves came closer. Silence populated itself. The windows reflected them back at themselves, bent by age in the glass so that their heads hovered over maps and manuscripts like figures in a painting about scholars too tired to stop.

Across from her, his hand went to the back of his neck. Lamplight caught the edge of the Mark below his rolled sleeve, and she looked away. By touch she knew it better than by sight. Tonight it sat in its quiet register, not pain exactly, only a low awareness under her skin of his body's condition and location.

That was the problem. She knew what she knew.

Weeks ago, in the deeper archive, she'd found the bonding texts that named this sensation with clinical precision: resonance, the growing inability to keep your own physical state cleanly separate from the other person's. The Manor's architecture amplified what already existed between its inhabitants and gated access behind the strength of the bond. She had read the folio twice. She understood more of the mechanism than he did. She had not told him. Since then they had sat like this every evening, close over manuscripts, elbows almost touching, while the knowledge stayed in her like swallowed glass, present always and sharp whenever she moved wrong.

Tonight had made concealment worse. It was one thing to keep silent while the evidence stayed subtle: shortened routes, warmth, surfaced books, the low spill of his pain through the Mark. It was another to stand in a corridor that had altered beneath their feet and go back to the table without saying, *I know why the house cares whether we're side by side. I knew before you did. I let it keep happening anyway.*

"You've gone quiet," he said.

"I'm thinking about loom mechanics."

"You're a terrible liar."

"I'm an excellent liar, actually." The answer was out before she could stop it, and the truth inside it made her grip tighten on the pen until the barrel bit into her fingers. Back to the manuscript. Back to the page. "I'm thinking about sequencing. If you disturb one knot-point too early, you could pull strain through the whole house."

On that subject he did what he almost always did: asked once, accepted the deflection, let the silence stand without worrying it into injury. Harry would have circled back. Ron, once upon a time, would have circled back. Malfoy let unanswered things remain unanswered. It made the space between them more bearable. It made concealment easier. Both facts felt terrible.

Her own marginal notes had deteriorated over the past weeks, faster and smaller and full of abbreviations that only made sense if you'd been following the work from the beginning. His were neater, just as dense, and she had started reading them with the same attention she gave the primary text itself: another mind recording how it moved, trusting her to keep up.

"This section," he said at last, angling the text toward her with one hand. His index finger rested under a line of archaic notation. "I think it's maintenance protocol. The language shifts. Imperative, not descriptive. Instructions rather than theory."

Leaning in should have been nothing. His finger was on the page. The notation was dense, the glyphs cramped, and she tilted her head to catch them properly. Instead his face was there, closer than the text required. Lamplight traced his jaw. Shadow lay under his lashes. The notation blurred because he had gone very still.

He was looking at her.

He was not looking at the page. He was not looking at the text. He was looking at her.

For weeks she had been pretending not to see that expression when it flashed across his face and vanished again: the unguarded thing, neither dry nor careful nor precise, only want.

Any sensible version of herself should have leaned back. A comment about the notation would have done it. So would a remark about the hour, the maps, the cross-index, anything that could have rebuilt the distance they'd been maintaining like a bandage over a wound neither of them wanted to inspect. She knew what the Manor did. She knew what the connection between them had become. A few weeks of concealed knowledge sat behind her eyes telling her that every moment of closeness was suspect, that the warmth might be architecture rather than desire, that the pull between them might be amplification rather than the plain fact of wanting someone.

Still, she didn't lean back.

His mouth was slightly open. A dry place marked his lower lip where he bit it while concentrating. Weeks ago she'd catalogued that detail without asking why. In the quiet she could hear his breathing.

"Granger." His voice came out lower than usual, roughened by restraint rather than speech.

"What?"

"I'm not..." He stopped. Of course he stopped. Self-correction lived in him too, except his always pulled toward less. There was nowhere to retreat now, though. Her face was inches from his. The page lay between them, ignored by both of them. "Never mind."

Her hand went over his on the manuscript.

For a second neither of them breathed. Under her palm his skin felt warm and dry with chalk dust from the preservation charms. Then he turned his hand beneath hers, not away but over, until his palm met hers and his fingers closed around hers. Parchment crumpled under the joined weight of their hands. Neither of them cared.

"I don't know what I'm doing." No research voice in that. No competent, measured version of herself. "I don't, I should..."

He kissed her.

Nothing about it was smooth. His mouth found hers at the wrong angle. Teeth caught her lip. He sucked in a startled breath through his nose. One hand tightened around hers while the other came up and hovered at her jaw, not quite touching, as if he were still waiting for permission or for his body to catch up with what it had already done. A startled sound left her, low and unmanageable. She leaned in. The angle corrected. Then his mouth was on hers properly, clumsy still, honestly clumsy, nose knocking hers, tension hard in his jaw when her free hand rose to him without conscious decision.

She had imagined composure. She had imagined his precision extending here too. It didn't. When his hand finally landed on her jaw it gripped too firmly, fingers pressing into the soft skin below her ear with more need than tenderness, and she liked that immediately, liked the plain honesty of his body speaking before the rest of him could interfere. Her free hand caught in the front of his shirt. Through the cotton his heart hammered.

At the same moment the resonance along the Mark went taut.

It did not get louder. It went total. His heartbeat seemed to strike inside her own chest. Adrenaline moved through him and some part of her answered as if it were hers. Under his sleeve the Mark shifted into a strange register, not pain, not for either of them, but a live vibrating measure that matched her pulse so exactly it made her dizzy. It was the most intimate thing she had ever felt. It was also the one she trusted least.

*Is this me?* The thought split the surface of the moment. *Is this me or is this the house?*

So she kissed him harder.

His hand slid from her jaw to the back of her neck and into her hair, which had come loose from its tie hours ago. Fingers tightened there. Her scalp tugged. By the time her mind caught up, her body had already opened to him. Tea and library dust touched his mouth, and under both was the clean mineral taste that belonged to him.

The desk made the whole thing graceless. A stack of reference texts toppled sideways. One hit the floor with a flat, heavy thud that ought to have broken the moment and didn't. Half out of her chair now, hip jammed against the desk edge, she held his shirt in one hand and his other hand in the other while maintenance protocols for ancient English magic crumpled beneath their fingers.

The house stayed warm around them. The route to her room had shortened under their feet. The Mark was still beating through both of them, too exact, too alive. She couldn't sort any of it cleanly. She kissed him again anyway.

Only an inch separated them. He stayed close, forehead to hers, breathing rough, eyes closed.

"Is this..." he started.

"Don't." She did not know whether she meant the question or the whole conversation waiting behind it. "Don't. Not yet."

This time the kiss slowed. Startle had gone out of it. His mouth moved over hers with a care he denied himself everywhere else. Then the corner of her mouth. Then her jaw. Head tipped back, throat exposed, she felt his breath there and thought, suddenly sick, *I know what the house does and I'm here anyway.*

Against her throat he said, "Granger."

Still her surname. It should have put distance back. It didn't.

Her hand went to his face, thumb along the sharp hinge of his jaw, over the faint scratch of evening stubble. He turned into the touch with his eyes still closed. No dry remark, no carefulness, just him there wanting her and not hiding it fast enough.

They stayed there longer than they should have, desk between them, dying fire at their backs, lamp nearly guttered out. The fallen book lay open on the floor. His hand stayed at the back of her neck, not moving, as if letting go too quickly might make the whole thing look mistaken.

Eventually they broke apart and sat back in their separate chairs. Cool air touched the places his hands had been. Across the desk he looked wrecked by it, open and stunned and a little afraid. She knew from the shake in her own hands that she couldn't have looked much better.

"I should go."

"Right." His hand went through his hair and left it standing up wrong. For a moment he looked younger, or maybe just less assembled.

Automatically she started gathering notes, closing the manuscript, recapping the inkwell, aligning loose pages that did not need aligning. A tremor ran through both hands. He noticed. He did not comment. Even that silence lodged in her throat.

"Tomorrow," she said. "The knot-points. And the cross-index."

"Tomorrow."

They stood. The library answered with a small settling sound from the wood and stone around them, like the house taking a breath.

"Goodnight, Granger."

"Goodnight."

Outside, corridor sconces burned low. Flames dipped as she passed and rose again behind her. Lately the walk to the guest wing had been doing this, compressing itself, making five minutes into three. Tonight the turns arrived almost before she reached them. The house could tell she was tired, apparently, or wanted her back in bed as quickly as possible. The thought should have made her furious. Instead it landed with a sickening trace of gratitude.

No chance of sleep, though. Her lips still felt bruised from him. One hand carried the ghost of his hand inside it. With every step away from the library, the last hour sharpened instead of fading.

---

Her room had been prepared for her.

Fire burned in the grate though she hadn't left it lit. Bedcovers had been turned down. The lamp beside the bed glowed at its lowest setting. Lavender threaded the air, new and unmistakably deliberate. For ten long seconds she stood inside the doorway with her palm flat to the frame, caught between laughter and the urge to scream.

The door locked under her wand. After that came the deadbolt, because a piece of metal sliding into place still felt reassuring even in a house that rearranged its own corridors.

The connection between them had quieted with the distance but not disappeared. One floor above, he was still awake. She could not hear thought through it, never that. Only the uneven pace of his heart and the shock still moving through him hard enough to echo in her and keep sleep away.

She sat on the bed and pulled her jumper over her head. Under the glamour her left forearm still held the scar, letters carved there seven years ago by a dead woman in the room downstairs. Three weeks earlier his thumb had traced that word. Tonight she could still feel the place his mouth had been, his hand at her jaw, the pull in her own body when he leaned in.

Ordinarily the research breakthrough would have kept her upright until dawn. Plaiting changed everything. Repair methodology, anchoring points, the Ministry's entire approach, all of it looked different now. Tonight the notes stayed closed on her bedside table.

Instead she lay back with her fingers pressed to her mouth and replayed the kiss: the wrong angle of his mouth, the shake in his hand before it found her jaw, the way he had turned into her palm once he stopped trying to hold himself apart. The Manor could warm a room and shorten a corridor. It could not make his hand shake.

Tomorrow she could put the folio on the desk between them and tell him the rest. She pictured his face closing before he spoke, the openness from tonight sealing back over. She would not say it.

All around her the Manor held the room at the warmest point it had yet offered her. Lavender hung in the sheets like an argument. The room was too ready for her. She didn't trust a second of it. One floor above, he was still awake. Only one storey of stone separated them. The rest was what she'd read in the folio and still wasn't telling him.

She lay there wanting him and mistrusting every part of the wanting.
