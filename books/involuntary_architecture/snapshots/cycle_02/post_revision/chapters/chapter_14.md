# Chapter 14

The texts were in a script he had never seen his ancestors use.

Draco sat with the folio open across his knees because the desk had stopped pretending to be large enough an hour ago. It was bound in a hide he couldn't name, the pages thick as card and faintly warm where his thumbs rested. The notation ran in cramped vertical columns: runic shorthand braided with drafting marks, measurements dropped straight into the language as if the writer had never believed words and stone were separate things. He could pick out fragments: family names, compass bearings, *Fundament* repeated often enough to matter. Read as a whole, the page refused him.

He was not alone with it.

Across from him, Granger was bent over a second manuscript in the gated archive. The room had opened that morning with suspicious ease. The threshold had not paused them or demanded that they prove anything first. After breakfast the inner door had simply been there, already ajar, as if someone had risen early to prepare for guests. If the house meant welcome, fine. If it meant to make togetherness feel ordinary, that fit too.

She had noticed it too. Her gaze had touched the doorframe, paused there, moved on. She did that more now, storing away whatever the Manor offered instead of naming it aloud. Four days earlier, after the Floo failed twice and finally sent her back from London, he'd told her the archive had not opened once in her absence. That was true, and not the thing he'd meant. Since then they had fallen back into the work with a dangerous ease. Where the room had once snapped shut when one of them crossed the threshold, it now held itself open until both of them left, lighting the bowls before either wand came out and keeping both chairs tucked up to the table as if they belonged there. The house had stopped shoving. It waited them out.

"This column here," Granger said. A quill sat behind her ear. The other half of a biscuit rested with criminal calm on the spine of a fourteenth-century manuscript. Her hair, which had started the day tied back, was now mostly being governed by a pencil. "The notation shifts. It's not the same hand as the first three pages."

Closer made sense. The table was wide, the script tiny, and her finger had already found the line in question. Draco moved his chair until their sleeves brushed.

"Different register," he said. "The earlier pages are formal. This is rougher."

"Rougher how?"

The strokes were thicker. The verticals ran uneven. Abbreviations had been shoved into the margins with no concern for elegance.

"Field notes. Written on site, I'd say. While the work was being done."

Her attention sharpened. "Original construction?"

"Fourteen-thirties. Braccus Malfoy oversaw the anchoring work here, but he didn't do it himself. He designed it. Other families handled the field construction. Lineal artisans, my father called them. Families older than ours in England, older in this kind of magic too." Lucius's voice came back with unpleasant clarity, one more inheritance Draco had not asked to keep. "The script must be theirs, or partly theirs."

"So neither of us can read it alone."

"No."

A small nod, more to herself than to him. "Your family knowledge gives us the names and geography. I can work the structural theory. If this is a trade language, that may be enough."

It was not the first time she'd said *enough* and meant *us*.

Work lowered her guard by inches. Concentration put color in her face. It also made her careless in oddly specific ways. The biscuit balanced on the manuscript. The pencil was losing its war with her hair. She read under her breath and never seemed to notice. He had been watching her for weeks. Today he stopped bothering to pretend otherwise.

The Mark ached low in his left arm with that newer, stranger responsiveness that had followed her sustained examinations. Near her, the ache pulled instead of bit, and he caught himself leaving the marked arm braced on the table's edge instead of folding it away. He had stopped pretending the Mark and his own body were separate systems, and the house kept arranging the work so that arm stayed nearest her.

"Look at this," she said, drawing the folio toward the candle. "These aren't repair instructions. They're readings."

Her finger traced a recurring cluster: the root rune, a simplified property sigil, then two parallel strokes tied together by a short bar.

"Inspection?" he asked.

"Assessment. The year mark changes here, here, and here. So do the figures in the margin." She tapped the paired strokes. "This cluster only shows up beside the stable entries."

A cramped note sat under the third mark, written in the mixed hand they were starting to recognize: family shorthand overlaid on trade notation.

Draco sounded it out. "*Concordia incolarum.* Or near enough."

"Concordance of inhabitants," she said. "Household accord, maybe. That's direct textual evidence, not me speculating. And this hooked sign beside it is a resonance mark. I've seen the same form in older anchoring theory."

"Meaning the readings improve when the people in the house are aligned."

"Meaning the people who kept these records thought inhabitant concordance affected anchor stability." Her quill hovered above the page without touching it. "What I'm inferring is the next part. The folio doesn't tell us whether that concordance means blood, marriage, oath, or any two people the house can turn into a usable relation. It doesn't prove the Manor is doing this to us on purpose. But it does show that the house was designed to route stabilizing force through its inhabitants."

"Connection meaning the bond."

"Connection meaning some magical relation between house and resident. I'm being careful on purpose."

He looked at the paired strokes again. "You're also not saying no."

She didn't answer immediately. She flipped back a page instead, though he could see from the way her eyes moved that she was no longer reading. "This much is solid," she said at last. "The Manor wasn't only built on the ley-lines. It was built into them. The house is one of the anchors."

That settled between them with real weight. Britain's magic was failing. The Floo staggered. Children's accidental surges kept worsening. St. Mungo's rationed. The damage was here too, in the stones around them.

"And the house knows it's failing," he said.

"I think it reacts to strain," she said. "Whether that's knowing in any human sense, I don't know."

He pointed again to the paired strokes, and to the note beneath them, and to the whole nauseatingly neat logic of the page. "And that?"

She set the quill down with more care than the gesture should have required. "That says accord matters. Not romance, not necessarily. Not even affection in how you'd usually mean it. Just a usable alignment between inhabitants."

"Usable by whom."

"The house, presumably."

No joke came this time. The room was too warm for one and the line on the page too ugly. He thought of shortened corridors, doors opening before they touched the handle, tea appearing at the exact temperature she preferred, and the way his Mark stopped needling him when she sat close enough to brush his sleeve.

"So when the lattice weakens," he said, "the house leans harder on whatever local stabilizing mechanism it's got left."

"Yes." Her voice had gone flatter, faster. "Or it rewards the pattern that produces the least local strain. Which amounts to the same problem."

"Meaning all of this." He gestured at the archive, the warmth, the ready light, their chairs already too close together. "Not neutral."

"No." She looked finally at him. "If the house gives us access, comfort, maybe even pain relief because we're easier to route when we're in step, that isn't hospitality. That's leverage. I don't know whether it's intentional leverage. I don't know whether the original builders cared about the distinction. But the distinction matters to me."

It mattered to him too. Mostly because she had said *pain relief* without looking at his arm.

"You're talking about coercion."

"I'm talking about a building that may reward compliance with easier conditions and punish the opposite." She dragged a hand through the air between them, impatient with her own phrasing. "No. That's not precise enough. I'm talking about not knowing when we're choosing something and when the house is arranging the choice."

For a beat neither of them moved.

Then she pushed her chair back.

The scrape of its legs across stone sounded too loud in the room. Her sleeve came away from his. The air changed so quickly he almost thought he'd imagined it, except the folio under his hand cooled at once and the ache in his arm sharpened into something meaner.

"Granger."

"Don't move." She was already standing. Her face had gone intent, stripped of everything but procedure. "If we're going to be disgusted, we may as well be accurate about it."

She took three steps toward the inner threshold.

On the second, the candle nearest the door guttered sideways.

On the third, the brass lattice at the archive entrance gave a small metallic click and began, almost politely, to draw inward.

Draco's hand clamped flat on the page. The line they had just translated didn't vanish, exactly, but it lost its coherence. The two scripts stopped meeting in his head. The Mark bit hard enough to drag a breath out of him.

She turned at once. "What changed?"

He could have lied. He had the reflex for it, still. "The door," he said.

"And?"

The pain had already locked his fingers into the edge of the page, and she already knew too much for false modesty to do either of them any good. "My arm."

Her jaw set once under the skin. "How much?"

"Enough."

She took one more deliberate step backward anyway, watching him instead of the threshold. The brass lattice moved another inch. Somewhere deeper in the shelves a preservation charm gave a strained little whine.

"Come back," he said, more sharply than he meant to.

She did. The door stilled before it shut. The candle righted itself. Warmth returned to the page under his palm, and his fingers uncurled from the parchment as the vicious edge came off the ache in his arm so fast it almost felt obscene.

Neither of them spoke for a moment.

Then Granger pulled a clean sheet of parchment toward her, dipped her quill, and wrote in a hand so neat it was nearly violent: `threshold response / proximity / contact / Mark`.

"Write it down," she said.

"You are."

"Then say it."

He looked at the new column headings, at the page that now felt complicit in his lap, and said, "Distance of roughly six feet. Door began to close. Candle distortion. Mark flare."

Her quill moved. "At the moment the sleeves stopped touching?"

"Before the door. After the chair moved."

"Fine." She wrote that too, then added something smaller underneath that he couldn't make out because she'd angled the page away from him. "We track every environmental change from here on. Door behavior. Room temperature if it shifts enough to matter. Unsolicited objects. Route changes if we leave and come back. Anything your Mark does."

"Anything my Mark does."

She looked up again. "Yes."

He ought to have objected to the clinical tone. It would have been easier than admitting how relieved he was that she had given the ugliness a method instead of recoiling from it. "And what exactly does that get us?"

"A chance not to be stupid." She sat again, though not where she had been. She set her chair beside his with a measured handspan between them, laid her notes flat in the strip of wood between their elbows instead of passing them hand to hand, and adjusted the chair another inch when the house, apparently displeased by moderation, gave a soft settling click in the wall behind the shelves. "If we're going to keep working in here, we do it with the variables named."

"Do you want to stop?"

That got no answer at first. She stared at the words *proximity* and *contact* as if she might revise them into something less naked.

"No," she said finally. "I want to know what stops happening if we refuse the terms. That's different."

"And if the terms are simply us being in the same room."

"Then we document that too." Her fingers tightened once around the quill. "But I'm not going to go on calling it innocent because it comes with decent lighting."

The line was almost funny. Neither of them smiled.

He looked at her new chair placement, at the precise gap she'd left between them, at the notes she now deposited between them as if touch itself had to go through procedure first. "You think it can tell the difference between agreement and surrender."

"I think it can detect reduced local strain." Her voice went clipped with concentration, the way it always did when emotion threatened and she chose precision instead. "Whether it understands the human distinction is a different question."

"That isn't better."

"No."

He watched the threshold for another second, half expecting it to twitch again. It stayed still now that she'd sat back down.

"So we're load-bearing," he said.

This time the sound that escaped her was not a laugh. It was shorter, rougher, something between disbelief and disgust. "Apparently."

"The bleakest available formulation."

"Usually your specialty."

"Not on purpose."

She wasn't looking at him now. She was redrawing the column headings so the ink dried clean. "Let's not let the house mistake sarcasm for concordance either."

"Can it do that?"

"I don't know. I'd rather not train it."

That, at least, was funny enough to register, but only just. The room had changed shape around them. Not physically. The shelves still rose to the same dim height, the candles still burned in the same glass bowls, the old folios still smelled of hide and mineral dust. But the comfort had turned conditional. Every convenience now carried a hook.

They went back to the page more carefully than before.

The difference announced itself at once. They no longer drifted into collaboration. They built it by hand. Her notes stayed on her side until she chose to slide them over. He read aloud only after checking she was following. When the script forced them closer, they moved closer deliberately and Granger marked the moment in the margin of the log with a quick, furious stroke of ink.

For the next hour the work split into two parallel tasks. On one sheet they built the language. On another they recorded the room.

The language came first in fragments: a property sigil he recognized from land grants, a shortened trade mark she identified as a load notation, a year-form he knew from family memorial tablets that turned out, under her eye, to be a maintenance cycle rather than a date. Their key began as a mess of arrows and crossed-out guesses. Then a structure emerged. The mixed hand was not sloppiness. It was mediation. The family script carried names, claims, locations, rights of access. The artisan notation carried procedure, force, stress tolerances, the practical language of how not to let a thing collapse while standing inside it.

"It's not a register," she said, leaning over the central sheet without quite crossing the distance she'd set. "It's an interface."

"For people who didn't trust each other enough to share a proper language?"

"Or trusted each other exactly enough to make one for the job and never write it down anywhere else." Her eyes narrowed over a repeated cluster halfway down the page. "Look. That same mark turns up beside three different family names, but the number form only changes when the household count changes."

"Household count?"

She tapped the margin with the end of the quill. "I think so. Or inhabitant load. Something body-based. Not bloodline. Occupancy."

He read the notation again and didn't like how neatly it fit with the rest. "The house needs people in it."

"The anchor system inside the house may need resident presence to stabilize. That's not the same claim."

"You keep saying that as if cleaner wording will make it nicer."

"It won't." She didn't even pause. "I'm doing it because I don't want to accidentally prove more than we have."

That too fit. Not just the caution. The ferocity underneath it.

She reached past him for the folio, stopped short of contact, and angled the page toward the light. "This line here. If I've got the root right, then this says the west range went unstable in a winter when the inhabited count dropped."

"Dropped how?"

"No idea yet. Deaths, travel, staff moved elsewhere, family away. The notation doesn't moralize. It just records load loss."

He had grown up in rooms where staff appeared and vanished without anyone admitting the mechanics. He had never before considered what the house itself counted when it counted them.

"Read the gloss below it," she said.

It took him two tries. The words were half in family shorthand, half in the trade argot that still snagged his eye at inconvenient angles. "Threshold lag corrected by redistribution to service corridor."

Her gaze sharpened. "Exactly. It compensates. If there aren't enough inhabitants in one part of the house, it reroutes strain somewhere else."

"People as ballast," he said.

"People as part of the mechanism."

"Again, not better."

"Again, no."

She wrote *occupancy-linked redistribution?* on the translation sheet and, after a small visible hesitation, copied *west range instability after resident loss* into the log beside their notes on the threshold reaction. Draco watched the second notation appear and felt the morning harden into something less deniable. This was no longer the vague suspicion that the Manor liked them close. This was architecture.

A book slid halfway out of the shelf to his left.

Neither of them moved.

The volume waited there, protruding from the row at a helpful angle.

Granger turned her head slowly and looked at it as if it were a spider that had chosen literacy as a hunting strategy.

"Write that down," she said.

"You really are committed to making the room feel unwelcome."

"The room has done enough to deserve it."

He rose, because it felt better to stand than to accept the book while sitting obediently in the seat the house preferred for him. The volume was a late-seventeenth-century survey of repaired estate enchantments. He recognized it from the main library, where it had lived two rooms and several privileges away from anything as practical as use.

"It surfaced a secondary reference when we stalled," Granger said as he brought it back. She bent over the log again. "Unrequested."

"Helpful, though."

"That's the issue."

He set the book down between them instead of handing it to her. "You're allowed to be helped by something and dislike the terms."

"I'm aware."

She opened it anyway.

He disliked how much relief the sight gave him, not because the book mattered but because she had stayed.

The surfaced text confirmed two of their guesses and complicated a third. By noon they had enough of the notation to distinguish assessment entries from repair entries and baseline readings from crisis notations. Baseline entries ran cleaner, with full measurements and fewer marginal corrections. Crisis entries crowded themselves sideways, like people forced to share a passage built for one. Stable seasons produced one set of marks. Strain produced another. And threaded through both, with dismal regularity, were the paired strokes for concordance.

They showed up beside marriage entries and inheritance shifts, beside nothing so legible as love, but all over anything that altered who lived in the house and how they related to one another inside it.

"There," Granger said, and shoved a page toward him hard enough that the candle flame jumped. "Read that without editorializing."

He did.

The gloss was maddeningly compact, but the sense of it came through. "Resident accord sufficient despite disputed claim."

She sat back.

"That's bad," he said.

"Yes."

"Because the claim dispute didn't matter so long as the residents behaved in a way the house could use."

"Because the system can apparently tolerate legal or political instability if the relational strain inside the house stays under whatever threshold it considers workable." She pressed her fingertips into the bridge of her nose. "God. That's foul."

It was more than foul. It was intimate in the ugliest possible way, not because it cared about love but because it didn't have to.

"Does it make a difference," he said slowly, "whether the accord is genuine?"

She dropped her hand. "What do you mean?"

"If two people are in step because they agree, or because one of them yields, or because they're frightened, or because the house has learned what arrangement buys the least resistance. Does the system know the difference."

Her silence landed harder than any immediate answer would have.

"I don't know," she said at last. "And I don't think the people who built it were designing for consent."

That could have been a joke about his family if she'd chosen to sharpen it that way. She didn't. She said it like a scientist identifying a contaminant in the sample.

"Neither were most of the families involved, I expect," he said.

"No."

He looked down at the line again. *Resident accord sufficient despite disputed claim.* It could have referred to a boundary inheritance, an oath conflict, a marriage negotiation, a custody matter, a servant dispute. The text didn't care. The house had taken the fact of reduced strain and recorded success.

"If we're the local mechanism," he said, "can we afford to refuse it?"

That got her eyes back on his face immediately.

"Of course we can."

"That's not what I asked."

The room went quiet enough that he could hear a charm ticking faintly in the far shelf.

"No," she said, after too long. "Maybe not without consequences beyond us. But that's different from saying it gets to decide for us."

"Is it?"

"Yes." The answer was instant now, almost angry. "It has to be. Otherwise we're letting a building set the ethical terms because it's old and useful."

"And very well insulated."

That did get a brief, unwilling change in her face. Not a smile. More like the beginning of one she had judged inappropriate and cancelled halfway through.

"Write that down too," she said.

"The insulation?"

"No, Malfoy. The actual point."

He obeyed because she was right. On the log, in the margin beside *threshold response / proximity / contact / Mark*, he wrote in a smaller hand than the rest: *cost of refusal indeterminate; utility not consent*.

She read it over his shoulder and didn't correct the wording.

The first tray appeared at twelve twenty-three.

There was no sound before it. One moment the cleared corner of the desk held nothing but dust and the shadow of stacked reference texts. The next it held a silver tray with tea, two cups, a plate of sliced pear, and a folded linen cloth still warm from whatever kitchen had been instructed without being asked.

Neither of them reached for it.

"Useful," Draco said after a moment.

"I know it's useful."

"I was agreeing with you, not the tray."

She bent over the log and wrote *unrequested refreshment* so hard the nib caught. "The timing is appalling."

"Were you hungry?"

"Yes."

"Then unfortunately the appalling tray has made a fair point."

She gave him a look sharp enough to strip varnish, then picked up the teapot and poured without another word. He waited until she'd taken the first sip before taking his own, less out of suspicion than out of a rank desire not to be first in any new form of submission. The tea was exactly strong enough. Of course it was.

Granger closed her eyes for one swallow, then opened them and said, "I hate that."

"That it's good?"

"That it knows."

He looked at the tray, at the pear slices arranged in a fan precise enough to be either courtesy or surveillance. "It has known for some time."

"I know." She set the cup down. "That's what this morning confirmed."

They ate anyway.

The domesticity of it made the problem worse. They drank tea over a page that had just told them inhabitant concordance was a functional variable. They took fruit from a silver plate while the house watched them consume the precise amount of care required to keep working. Nothing in the scene looked violent. That did not make it benign.

When the tray vanished half an hour later, taking the peels and empty cups with it, Granger did not look up from the log. She only added *cleared without request* and underlined it once.

By early afternoon their translation key had started to hold.

They identified seven recurring categories: property designation, anchoring quadrant, seasonal condition, household load, concordance state, intervention type, and outcome. The language sharpened as they worked. He began to hear where the family shorthand had been laid over the artisan terms rather than translated. She started anticipating which family abbreviations hid actual measurements instead of names. Several times their hands moved toward the same line at once and checked short before contact. Each time the hesitation sat in the air longer than it should have.

At one point she frowned over a repeated glyph and said, "This can't be direction. It doesn't track the geometry."

"It does if it's local rather than compass-based."

"No, because then the ordinal markers wouldn't—"

"You're assuming a stable frame of reference."

"In a structural assessment, yes, I am."

"That seems optimistic for medieval builders."

She glanced at him, offended into full wakefulness. "Not medieval. Fourteenth century. And no, it isn't optimistic. It's basic competence."

"From your side of the blood war, perhaps."

"What does that even mean?"

"It means the family glosses use directional language opportunistically. Look at the variant here."

"That's not a direction marker."

"It is in this hand."

"No, it's seasonal flow. The stroke opens downward, see? If it were directional it would close on the return."

They stared at the page together.

Then, from somewhere behind the shelves, came a soft internal thump. Not loud. Not dramatic. Just enough of a sound to make both of them look up at the same instant.

Granger's expression changed first. She snatched the log toward her and wrote *room response during disagreement?* with quick, furious strokes.

"You heard it," she said.

"Yes."

"Good. I didn't want that to be only me."

He looked from the note to the page they'd been arguing over. "Could be settling."

"Could be." She blew on the wet ink. "Could also be a house that prefers resolved conditions."

"Perhaps it simply admires your competence."

"Don't flatter it."

He went back to the glyph. The page swam for half a second, not enough to count as magic, maybe only enough to count as irritation. Then he saw what she'd seen and grimaced. "Fine. Seasonal flow."

"Thank you."

The sound did not repeat.

They both looked at the shelves anyway.

"I am not," she said, still staring into the room, "going to fake agreement for a building."

"That had been my next suggestion."

"I know."

"And?"

"And if you let me win a point I haven't actually won, the translation key becomes useless." She tapped the margin note she'd just made. "If the house reacts to concordance, I want to know whether it reacts to truth or just to the shape of reduced friction. I'm not contaminating the data because the room likes us compliant."

He should have been able to make a joke out of that. There was one waiting, something about her being catastrophically unromantic at precisely the wrong moment. It died before he said it. Nothing in her face suggested room for deflection.

"Right," he said.

"Right."

She nudged the disputed page toward him again. "And write down that the response stopped after resolution."

"You really don't believe in half measures."

"Not where sentient architecture is concerned, no."

He wrote it down.

The work that followed went slower and better.

It went slower because every convenience had to be assessed before it was used. It went better because naming the danger had made their collaboration sharper rather than blunter. If the house wanted concordance, it was getting a version built out of actual scholarship and mutual irritation, not dreamy compliance. They checked each other harder. They challenged shortcuts. They used the surfaced books, but only after cross-referencing them against older sources she pulled herself from the shelves and volumes he identified from family sigils burned faintly into the leather. When the room warmed, it went in the log. When a shelf lit before either of them reached for a wand, it went in the log. When his Mark eased after ten straight minutes of working shoulder to shoulder over a knot of shorthand too cramped to parse at a distance, that went in the log too, though he resented dictating it and she resented writing it.

By four o'clock the page of observations had become as dense as the translation key.

Some patterns emerged anyway.

Distance alone was not the whole variable. Once, while they remained three feet apart, the room steadied noticeably when she solved a structural ambiguity he had been dragging in the wrong direction and he acknowledged it without argument. Another time their chairs were nearly touching, but when a line about property succession sent them into an ugly twelve-minute dispute over whether a compressed family mark indicated claim or custody, the candles in the west niche burned low and blue until they reached a reading they both accepted. Granger added *response may track reduced interpretive strain, not merely proximity* and underlined *may* twice.

"So it doesn't just want bodies in a room," he said.

"No." She rubbed ink off the side of her hand with the heel of her palm. "It may want coherence. Or what's functionally close enough to coherence."

"Still charming."

"Yes."

He watched her rewrite a translation from scratch because she no longer trusted the earlier version, and understood suddenly why the work felt better with the ugliness named than it had when the room was trying to coax them into comfort. Hidden leverage wanted passivity. Seen leverage had to contend with Granger, which was bad luck for any system stupid enough to think comfort alone would do the job.

The first real breakthrough came over a page neither of them had been reading correctly.

He caught the family hand first. A genealogical marker buried in the margin, almost casual. "Draco Malfoy, sixteenth of his name," he murmured. "Cousin line, not mine. They split after Braccus. Normandy and Wiltshire."

"Site supervisor?" she asked.

"I think so. Look. His notes sit between the artisans' marks and the family script. He's translating both directions."

She shifted closer before catching herself, then came the rest of the way anyway with an expression that made it clear the movement had been chosen, not surrendered. Her shoulder stopped a breath from his. Together they studied the line.

"If he was mediating," she said, "then this whole document isn't a pure family register. And it isn't a pure trade register either. It's a working hybrid."

"A creole."

"Yes." Her eyes stayed on the page, though her voice had gone softer. "A project language. Temporary, practical, and probably uncodified anywhere else."

"So the only way to rebuild it is to do what they did."

"Put both knowledge sets in the same room and see what sticks."

There it was again, the thing the house wanted and the thing he wanted and the fact that those were not, disastrously, mutually exclusive.

He said, because he needed the sentence to land somewhere less dangerous, "Convenient for the Manor."

"Useful for the work," she corrected automatically, then stopped. "No. That's lazy. It's useful for both, probably."

"Which one are we serving."

"Today?" She went quiet for a second, then answered without looking at him. "Both. I don't know in what ratio."

That was honest enough to make his pulse change.

They stayed bent over the line until it yielded the rest of its sense. The cousin-line supervisor had been recording mediation between resident authority and field correction teams. Family claim established access. Artisan procedure established stability. Neither set of knowledge was sufficient without the other. The note itself was dry. The implication wasn't.

"There never was a version of this work that happened alone," Draco said.

"Apparently not."

The room, as if pleased by the conclusion, let a second candelabrum kindle itself in the east niche.

Granger closed her eyes.

"Write that down," she said.

He did.

By twilight the table looked like the scene of a very orderly siege. Three manuscripts lay open at competing angles. Their central key had become a sheet and a half. The log of house responses had spread to a second page. Draco's handwriting, which usually ran clean and economical, had shrunk tighter and tighter beside hers until the difference between their notes looked less like separate authorship and more like a conversation being forced to live on parchment because speech could no longer keep up.

Fresh candles waited on a shelf just inside the threshold. This time the house had not placed them directly on the table, perhaps having understood from the log that naked helpfulness was now being entered into evidence. Draco fetched them himself. By the time he turned back from lighting them, she'd shifted the desk arrangement: his folio beside hers, reference texts arced between the chairs, space cleared in the middle for a shared index of the symbol families they'd identified.

She had done it absentmindedly, probably. It still knocked him off balance.

"What?" she asked when she caught him looking.

"You reorganized me."

"I made room for the other manuscript."

"Very noble of you."

Something on her face loosened for a second, dry and unwilling and much closer to amusement than the rest of her ever admitted in public. Then it was gone. "You were spreading."

"A terrible accusation."

"And true."

He sat again. The chairs were closer now because there was no longer room for them not to be. She glanced once at the log, at the latest notations on distance and response, and then deliberately did not move either chair.

That decision changed the air more than a confession would have.

Evening came down hard outside the windows. Inside, the archive narrowed to candlelight, open texts, chalky air, and the friction of two minds refusing to let each other be lazy. They argued for nineteen minutes over whether a compressed glyph marked seasonal variance or directional load transfer, only to realize the answer was both, dependent on the accompanying property sign. They found a sequence of baseline entries clean enough to prove the southern anchoring records would matter more than the northern ones when they came back tomorrow. They traced one ugly annotation through three different hands before discovering it referred not to repair failure but to a resident oath that had lapsed and been renewed. They did not discuss the implications of that one beyond the strictly necessary, perhaps because the implications were standing between them at the table already.

At some point the house produced a wool throw over the back of the chair Granger wasn't using.

She stared at it for a full five seconds.

"Absolutely not," she said to the empty room.

The throw remained where it was.

Draco looked from the neatly folded fabric to her face. "Do you want me to write down that the house has developed a sense of affront."

"I want you to write down that it is escalating."

"Those are not mutually exclusive."

"Malfoy."

He wrote *unsolicited comfort object* in the margin while she glared at the throw as if daring it to advance. Five minutes later, without a word, she dragged it across her lap with the tip of a quill and kept reading.

He said nothing. So did she.

The intimacy of that silence was different from the one that had lived between them earlier in the book. It was not the brittle quiet of two people guarding territory. It was the quiet of work shared so long and so closely that speech had become selective. He could tell from the way her shoulders angled when she was about to object. She could tell, apparently, from the movement of his hand before he reached for a reference. Twice he went for a book and stopped because she had already slid it toward the center. Once she started to ask for a measurement table and cut herself off because he was already there.

The work was making a language out of them. The house liked that far too much.

So did he.

That ought to have been the disqualifying detail. Instead it sat in him with the kind of clarity that made denial look adolescent.

Later, while she reread a passage they'd finally cracked, the pencil gave up and her curls fell loose around her face. One foot was tucked under her. A hole showed at the toe of her sock. She did not notice him noticing. She almost never noticed the things about herself that made him feel most helpless.

The name for it arrived cleanly: love.

He was not falling. It was not the start of anything. It was a fact, and once he let himself see it, it became the plainest one in the room.

And the morning's discovery did not make it smaller. That was the problem. If anything, the uglier logic of the house exposed the feeling more brutally by proving what it wasn't. The Manor might exploit proximity. It might reward concordance. It might ease his pain when she sat near enough and punish distance with a precision that felt obscene. None of that explained the hole in her sock. None of it explained the way he wanted her notes left in unstable piles on his table, or the exact sound of her voice when she said *no, that's lazy* to a bad inference, or the stupid amount of tenderness called up by seeing her ignore her own exhaustion because a line of fourteenth-century notation had offended her.

He had spent years building a life narrow enough to survive in. He ate alone. He kept corridors empty. He reduced friendships to the few people who could bear his silences. Need had always been the dangerous part. Need was leverage. Need made a person visible.

Now he wanted ridiculous, ordinary things. He wanted tea brought without ceremony and cursed on sight for being too well timed. He wanted her coat on the hook outside the library because she'd stopped pretending she wasn't staying late. He wanted her shoulder against his when the script turned ugly. He wanted the argument that lasted nineteen minutes because neither of them would let the other get away with being half right. He wanted to touch her outside the sanctioned brutality of the Mark examinations. He wanted a hand at her back as she passed, his fingers in her hair, contact that belonged to a life rather than to damage.

And because the morning had happened, he could not tell himself that wanting those things was ethically simple. The house had turned even innocence into a variable. Which meant every choice now had to survive being chosen anyway.

"Malfoy."

He looked up.

The folio sat open in her lap, forgotten for the moment. Attention had replaced concentration on her face, alert and careful both. She had caught him staring.

"You're doing it again," she said.

"I know."

That should have been the point where he looked away and made a joke durable enough to hide behind. It should have been the point where she used the work to step cleanly around whatever this was becoming. Instead neither of them moved.

The air between them went very thin.

Then she said, with the kind of precision that meant she had noticed more than she wanted to admit, "You've got that look you get right before you stop listening to anything I say."

"That seems unfair."

"Is it?"

"Possibly not."

Her gaze flicked once to the log lying open beside them. *proximity / contact / Mark*. Then back to his face.

He said, because anything else felt more dangerous, "You've got ink on your chin."

Her hand went to the wrong side of her face.

Some part of him understood exactly what the room would make of the next movement. That part suggested very firmly that he remain still. Another part, older or newer or simply more tired of cowardice, ignored it.

Before he could reconsider, he reached out and brushed the smudge away with his thumb.

Her skin was warm.

She stayed perfectly still.

Somewhere in the wall behind the eastern shelves, a hidden latch eased with a sound so soft he might have imagined it if her eyes had not changed at the exact same moment. She had heard it too.

Neither of them mentioned it.

"There," he said, quieter than he meant to.

"Thanks." Her voice was careful now, but not retreating. Her eyes did not leave his.

He should have taken his hand back at once. He did, eventually. It felt like taking it out of a flame by stages.

For another beat she looked at him as if deciding whether the next sentence ought to be personal or procedural. Procedure won, but only just.

"Can you check the property markers on the northeast section?" she asked. "I think this entry matches the inspection sequence, but the branch sigil is wrong."

"Yes."

Work resumed.

The change was slight and undeniable. Her chair stayed where it was. When their shoulders touched again, no one shifted away. The log remained open at the edge of the table with its ugly columns and damp ink and all the evidence they'd accumulated against the room. None of it stopped them.

If anything, that was the most compromising thing in it.

The next hour produced what they had actually come here for: not answers, but a way forward. They isolated the notation cluster that marked baseline readings prior to major strain. They identified the southern anchoring sequence as the cleanest surviving run because later damage had hit the northern quadrants harder. They built enough of a working lexicon to tell the difference between failure in the structure and failure in the resident field around it. That distinction alone would save them days.

"Tomorrow," Granger said, tapping the heading they'd finally broken open, "we start here. Southern records first. If the baseline holds, we can compare the postwar variance to the current decay maps."

"And keep the log."

"Obviously."

"You sounded offended that I'd ask."

"I am."

He let that settle, watching her stack the loose pages in order of use rather than chronology, which was how he had always known she was more tired than she admitted. Her fingers had gone ink-stiff. A dark smear crossed the side of her hand. There was another one, faint, at her jaw where he'd missed a spot after all. He wanted absurdly to fix that too.

"Granger."

Her notes paused in her hands.

This was the point where he ought to have said something measured and safe about the research. The key they'd built. The efficiency of the day. The value of tomorrow's work. Instead he heard himself say the truer thing.

"This is the best work I've ever done."

The admission came out rougher than he had planned. Perhaps because he had not planned it at all.

"I wanted you to know that," he added.

For a moment she only looked at him. The room did not dare make a sound.

Then, very quietly, "Yeah. Me too."

He should have let her go with that. He should have let the sentence stand on its own and not reached for more. But the day had already established that restraint and safety were not the same thing, and cowardice had bought him very little for years.

"Leave the southern folio here," he said. "I'll have dinner sent down tomorrow and we'll keep going."

That got a fuller look. Not shocked. Not even wary, exactly. Measured.

He added, because the distinction mattered now, "On purpose this time."

Something in her face shifted at that. Not softness. Recognition, maybe.

"All right," she said.

She gathered her notes, but not all of them. The log and the central key stayed on the table between their chairs, pages weighted with an inkstone as if both of them understood without saying it that tonight's work could not yet be packed away into innocence.

At the threshold she stopped and looked back once, not at him at first but at the room: the candles, the open manuscripts, the chair she'd left too close to his. Then she looked at him.

"If it starts asking for more than we're prepared to give," she said, "we stop."

She offered no qualification and no theory language to soften it.

"Yes," he said.

She nodded once and went.

After the door shut behind her, the archive stayed open a few seconds too long, holding the threshold wide and the candles steady as if it expected him to call her back. That patience no longer felt innocent.

He did not move at first. He listened to her steps recede into the outer library and then into whatever route the house had chosen to offer her tonight. He listened until even the echo of them was gone.

Then he looked down at the page they'd been working on and tried, stupidly, to read the last line alone.

The first two words yielded: a family designation and an outcome mark. The third went dead under his eye. It was not blank. It had not changed. It had simply closed in a way that felt almost bodily, like a door pushed to but not latched. He tried again with the key they'd built together, managed another fragment, and lost the thread on the next line entirely.

The room seemed to notice.

The warmth at his back thinned just enough to raise the hairs along his neck. The brass lattice at the threshold gave the faintest preparatory tick.

His Mark answered before the door did with one sharp pull, deep enough to make his fingers curl against the page.

He sat very still and hated how much the pain felt like information.

So that, too, went into the evidence, this time in the body rather than on parchment. One of them could remain in the room alone for a moment now, perhaps, but it could not hold its center that way. Not yet. The work wanted both knowledge sets. The house wanted whatever alignment let it keep using them. And he, apparently, wanted her enough that those two facts had become impossible to sort cleanly from each other.

He could not tell whether that last part was the room's triumph or his own.

On the table, the log lay open to the final exchange they'd written together. *cost of refusal indeterminate; utility not consent.* Below it, in Granger's hand, from later in the evening: *continue only by deliberate choice*.

He read that twice.

Then he reached over, turned the log face down, and snuffed the candles one by one.

The archive door had begun to draw inward by the third flame. By the fifth it was almost closed. He left both folios spread on the table and did not move her chair back across from his.
