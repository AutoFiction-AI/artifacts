# Chapter 11

After dinner the next day, by the second reading, the pattern was too clean to dismiss.

The base layer at the bottom of the Mark had strengthened again. Hermione wrote the new measurement in the ledger, checked last week's entry, then the week before. The figures did not wobble. They climbed. The lowest stratum, the one that did not belong to Voldemort's original binding and had no business thickening under any known treatment, was answering sustained contact by growing denser, more articulate, harder to separate from the rest of the curse structure.

Every long examination moved it.

That correlation stayed out of the ledger.

She copied down the safe numbers instead: lattice degradation, surface reactivity, residual pain response. No mention of what the buried layer did when her hand stayed on him too long. No mention of the way the whole configuration now altered fastest under her touch. If she wrote that part cleanly, she would have to decide whether the examinations were helping or teaching the Mark a new trick. She was not ready to make the call in ink.

Across the table Draco sat with his sleeve rolled past the elbow and a book open beside his right hand. He had stopped pretending to read three minutes earlier. She could tell by the stillness. Real reading changed his face in small ways. His mouth loosened. His eyes moved at a pace that had nothing to do with caution. Now he was holding himself still and letting her work, which was not the same thing.

"I'm going to push lower," she said.

His gaze dropped to the crystals arranged beside her wand, then to the ledger, then back to her face. "Lower than last time."

"I need the interface under the defensive layer. The readings keep blurring before I get there."

"And if it goes badly?"

"Then I stop."

He looked at her for a moment longer. He did not look suspicious. He looked only tired, and careful with the tiredness. "Go on."

Hermione shifted her chair closer. The padding on the table kept his forearm at the right angle, left wrist turned up, the Mark exposed in the firelight and the colder wash from her diagnostic charm. The brand had changed since the beginning. It was no less ugly, but it was more active now, the black lines raised in places like old scar tissue remembering flame. When she first came to the Manor, direct contact with it had felt like handling a live wire under cloth. Lately it felt worse than that. It felt more intimate. The curse recognized her hand before her magic even settled.

She placed two fingers just above the Mark and let the charm sink.

Magic slid under her skin, through his. Past the degraded lattice of Voldemort's binding. Past the defensive dark matter still feeding on his core. Past the hot, familiar violence of the curse itself. She went lower. Then lower again. There, at the buried layer she had only caught in fragments before, where the spellwork stopped behaving like injury and started behaving like architecture.

The contact gave way.

The room went wrong.

There was the library as she knew it, with fire on the grate, papers at her elbow, her own hand on his wrist, and her breathing held too high.

There was also the library from inside his body.

He could feel the crease in the page beneath his thumb and the weight of his arm on the padding. He could feel the exact pressure of her fingertips, the wool brushing the inside of her wrist where her sleeve had slipped back, and the smear of ink near the base of her left thumb. The fire warmed one side of his face; the sharper heat of her attention warmed the other. He had been aware of her before she touched him, aware in the stupid granular way bodies were aware when minds were trying to work. He noticed the shift of her chair, the quiet click of glass when she moved a crystal, and the fact that she had chewed the inside of her cheek while checking the figures and stopped only when she spoke.

That alone would have been humiliating enough.

Under it sat the rest: old shame, ground deep and mean as rust, wrapped tight around the curse brand on his arm. He was sixteen, in a drawing room that smelled of polish and blood, with his father's hand holding him in place. The thing on his skin was meant to turn him into an instrument and had succeeded often enough. Then her palm flattened the brand into quiet. Relief flooded up at once, crude and absolute, as if his body had learned her and would take ease from her hand whether he deserved it or not.

There was no thought in that relief that could be defended. There was only want, quiet, and the ugly, traitorous gratitude of pain easing because she was there.

Hermione tore her hand away.

The doubled view collapsed. Her fingertips buzzed so hard she nearly dropped her wand. Across from her Draco had already set the book down without care. It lay open and bent, face-down on the table, abandoned.

"What happened."

Even now his voice came out measured. Only the missing contraction gave him away.

"I caught spillover." Hermione put the wand down before her hand could show the shake in it. "The resonance went both ways for a few seconds. Sensory information."

His gaze held on her face. "That's not all."

No point denying the flinch. She closed the ledger, buying herself a breath and a square of wood to look at instead of him. "I had your angle for a moment. I saw the table and the book and felt the pressure from your side. It was disorienting."

"What else?"

What else was not data. What else belonged to the part of him that had not volunteered itself for examination. She looked at the exposed Mark, then at his mouth, gone flat with effort.

"Nothing I can use," she said. "Nothing I need to report back to you."

"That wasn't what I asked."

"I know."

He waited. She could feel him doing it, feel the restraint like a second posture layered over the first. Draco did not usually interrupt. When he wanted an answer and failed to get one, he went quieter, not louder. The quiet now had edges on it.

"If you saw a medical risk," he said, "you tell me."

"If I had, I would."

"And if you saw anything else."

Hermione met his eyes because not meeting them would concede too much. "Then I decide whether it was mine to say."

That landed. She saw it land. His face did not change much, but the line of his shoulders did. A small withdrawal, not offended but wary.

"Right," he said.

It was not agreement. It was what he said when agreement was off the table and he had no intention of begging for it.

She hated the flicker of relief that came with that. Hated more that he had made room for a boundary she had not even explained. The decent response unsettled her harder than anger would have.

"If this is getting less safe," he said after a beat, "we stop."

"It's still useful."

"That wasn't my question either."

"No." Hermione forced her hands flat on the table. "No, it wasn't. But the spillover lasted seconds. The lattice didn't rupture. The curse structure held. I can change the depth and narrow the contact field next pass."

"You're shaking."

Only one hand, and less now than before. She curled it into her skirt under the table. "That happens when I push complex diagnostics."

"Granger."

The warning in it was mild. The fact that he used it at all was not.

"I said what I meant," she said.

Silence settled between them. He almost pushed. She saw the moment he decided not to, and then saw the harder thing after that, which was him turning his arm over on the padding again of his own accord.

"Tell me if it changes," he said.

"I will."

That was true in the narrowest useful sense. She would tell him if the diagnostics changed, if the risk profile changed, if the measurements changed. She would not tell him that the contents of the spillover kept burning against the inside of her skull.

She reset the crystals. This time she kept the contact shallower, tracing the outer edges of the interface rather than driving through it. The Mark answered with the usual resistance and the less usual softening beneath her hand. His pulse slowed under her fingertips within half a minute. She took the readings anyway. Surface reactivity was down. Pain response was down. Base-layer density was up again by a fraction that would have looked negligible if she did not have six weeks of figures proving it wasn't.

Draco watched the ceiling while she worked. Once, when the curse jolted under her touch, his right hand closed hard on the edge of the table. He said nothing. Neither did she. The old arrangement still held on the surface: researcher, subject, diagnostic frame. Only the surface had been getting thinner for weeks, and now she had looked through it from the wrong side.

When she finally released him, he sat up slowly and reached for his sleeve with his right hand. The left was stiff for a moment after long examinations. He hated when she noticed that. She pretended not to, passed him the linen wrap they used for the cooling charm, and turned back to the ledger while he bound his forearm again.

The omission sat on the page in its own shape: safe numbers in neat columns, no mention of the lower-layer climb, no mention of the spillover's actual content. The bookkeeping had started as prudence. She could feel it turning into something else.

"You should eat before you faint onto the equipment," Draco said.

Hermione looked up. "I'm not going to faint."

"You skipped breakfast."

"I had tea."

"Tea is not breakfast."

"It was enough to get through the examination."

He gave her the particular look he reserved for arguments he had no intention of taking seriously. "A low bar."

She should have snapped back. Instead she found herself watching him rebutton his cuff with one hand, awkwardly because the other was not yet fully cooperative. The motion was practiced enough to suggest routine. That was worse. She had built her understanding of the Mark around pain and curse mechanics, not around the daily indignities of one hand slow from the strain of letting her near it for an hour.

Hermione rose too fast, crossed to the sideboard, and busied herself capping the vials. "I'll eat later."

"You'll forget."

"Then remind me in half an hour."

"I am reminding you now."

She shot him a look over her shoulder. "Are you going to stand there until I take a bite of toast."

"Possibly."

There was a trace of dry humour in it, so slight it barely qualified. Under other circumstances she might have answered in kind. This morning the sound of him almost joking only made the spillover hit again in useless fragments. She could feel his awareness snagging on the crease in her sleeve, and the blunt bodily relief that had gone through him when she touched him. She looked away first.

"Fine," she said. "Half an hour."

He nodded once, as if terms had been settled, and bent to collect the copy notes he'd made during the examination. "The east-stack ledger from 1812 has references to corridor compression tolerances. I can read the margins, but the main hand shifts into old anchoring notation halfway through. It's on the side table if you want it."

Hermione's head came up before she could stop it. "Corridor compression."

"Yes. Apparently one of my ancestors considered the question of how much a passage may shorten before inhabitants start walking into walls." He stacked the papers square. "Useful people."

That, more than the joke, got through to her. Corridor compression. She had her own notes on route lengths by then. The walk from the guest wing to the library had taken twenty-three paces that morning, where last Tuesday it had taken thirty-one. She had written the numbers down in irritation, then again the next day when the count shifted back. The Manor kept making spatial decisions and pretending it had not.

"Leave it out," she said. "I want to compare it against the survey maps."

He tucked the loose pages under a weight. "The archive's holding well enough at the moment. I'm going back in before it changes its mind."

He said it as if the house were weather and not a participant.

When he went through the south-wall alcove into the deeper room beyond, her attention tracked him without consent. It brought no thoughts and no language, only the felt fact of his movement in space. She knew when he turned left among the older shelves and when he stopped. She knew it in the same bodily register that knew where a chair stood in the dark if one had stubbed a toe on it enough times. She pressed her thumb into the heel of her other hand until the sensation passed or disguised itself.

Half an hour, he had said.

She sat down to work.

---

The south-wall alcove was still open that afternoon, which meant the archive was holding better than usual.

That alone would once have counted as useful good news. By now it had too many possible causes. Hermione kept her eyes on the folios spread across the main-library table and did not look toward the gap in the wall more than necessary. Past it, in the deeper room, Draco moved among the oldest shelves with his usual economy. Every so often the edge of his concentration brushed hers hard enough to break her own. It was never words. Never a stolen thought. Just the fact of him reading, pausing, finding a line worth copying, moving again.

Work sat in safer territory, or it had until the Manor made work itself suspect.

The table in front of her held a week's worth of dullness: estate surveys, warding logs, repair orders, a household inventory written by a man who appeared to have believed dust could be defeated by force of handwriting alone, and three different catalogue sheets from three different centuries. She had spent the last seven days building a concordance between the current library system and the older Malfoy numbering that predated the postwar reorganisation, because too many references in the theory texts kept pointing to locations that no longer existed on paper. The task had been tedious enough to feel medicinal. It was all numbers, shelf marks, cross-indexes, architecture as fact instead of behaviour.

She had also, without telling Draco, started another notebook.

That one held route lengths, temperature irregularities, doors that opened only when both of them stood there and refused when one did not, which room gave up a blanket before either of them had asked, which fire woke early, and where tea appeared before tea had been mentioned. The notebook had not yet become an argument, only a pile of observations. She was not sure whether that made it more honest or less.

The east-stack ledger he had left her was worse than advertised. The first quarter was in crisp household shorthand, almost clerical in its boredom. Then the hand changed and the lines narrowed, Latin threaded with anchoring notation and the family habit of abbreviating any noun that had lasted across more than two generations. Hermione spent forty minutes on six pages and got only as far as confirming that corridor variance had once been treated as maintenance rather than anomaly: pressure thresholds, occupancy harmonics, domestic adjustment allowances. Terms that sounded absurd until one lived in a house that moved its own walls.

By the time she set that ledger aside, the fire had sunk and the light beyond the west windows had gone from colourless winter afternoon to the flatter grey that warned of evening. She reached for the next folio in the stack, a surveyor's report from 1743 with three later repair hands layered into the margins, and nearly missed the note altogether.

It was hidden under a strip of old paper pasted along the outer edge to reinforce a tear. Someone had written on the reinforcement after it dried. The ink had sunk sideways into the fibres and gone brown with age. From straight on it looked like a smear. Only when she tipped the page toward the fire did the cramped notation separate into legible marks.

It was not a sentence. It was three fragments.

*South run corrected.*

*Threshold answer restored.*

Then, smaller, squeezed into the corner as if added after the rest:

*cf. lig. fol. ar. xiv-c, resonant measure*

Hermione read it twice. Then a third time with her finger over each abbreviation.

*Lig. fol.* could have been *ligature folio*, *lineage folio*, *lignum folio* if the writer had been an idiot, which he likely had not. *Ar. xiv-c* was a catalogue reference, but not in the current system. She knew that at once because the current system did not use Roman numerals for archive subpresses, and because she had spent a week cursing every dead Malfoy who had renumbered the place without respecting the old citations.

Without the concordance she had built, the note would have been nonsense.

With it, the nonsense narrowed.

She pulled her cross-index sheet closer. The older archive codes split by room, then press, then shelf. Fourteen-C was not a public shelf at all but a narrow run inside the gated archive, one of the sections that had been folded into "miscellaneous estate papers" when the catalogue was modernised. No titles carried over. No subject headings either. Just a graveyard of plain bindings and the assumption that if a Malfoy wanted one of them, he probably already knew where it lived.

Hermione sat back and listened.

From beyond the alcove came the faint scrape of Draco's chair leg against stone, then the quieter drag of parchment being moved across a table. Still there. Still inside. Which meant the archive would likely stay visible if she went in. Once or twice in the first weeks, she had watched the alcove flatten into blank stone the moment one of them crossed too far from the other. The room had since grown less theatrical about it. It preferred to behave as if its rules were merely practical.

The archive door stood ten feet away.

She could have called to him first. Asked whether he knew the old 14-C shelf. Asked if he had ever heard of a binding folio tied to resonant measure. She did not. The note itself felt too thin to bother him with yet. One marginal fragment in a surveyor's hand. Better to confirm the book existed before she handed the possibility over.

That was the explanation she used while rising from the chair.

Past the alcove the air turned cooler and drier. The smell of old preservation charms sat heavier there, with iron under it and the faint medicinal sting of parchment powder. Shelves ran higher and closer together than in the main room, the passage between them just wide enough for one person carrying a stack. Another grille, already unlatched because the archive was open for both of them, separated the working stacks from a narrower run of older texts. At the far end Draco stood with his back to her, bent over a crumbling ledger on a narrow desk. He had one hand braced near the spine while the other copied onto loose parchment before the original page gave up under the archive's usual instability. He did not turn. Either he had not heard her or was pretending not to notice.

The newer brass plates were no use in the older run. Half had fallen off decades ago. Hermione used the concordance sheet instead, counting uprights from the corner, then moving back when the numbers failed to align. Fourteen-C in the old scheme did not mean the fourteenth shelf. It meant press fourteen, column C, third run in from the outer grille. She had to work that out twice before it settled.

The shelf itself held exactly what the modern catalogue promised: plain bindings, no titles, no dates on the spines. Four black folios. Two calfskin registers. A packet of loose quires tied with faded green tape. The first book she pulled turned out to be a marriage settlement register with half the names eaten by damp. The second was foundation-seal maintenance written in a hand so clipped it might as well have been a cipher. The third had no title page at all, only a table of household accounts from a year when everyone in Wiltshire appeared to have been buying too much salt.

She was sliding the third volume back when her thumb caught on a shallow impression near the bottom board of the fourth.

It was not decorative tooling. It was a number, pressed so lightly she had to tip the binding toward the nearest lamp to read it.

XIV-C.

The book was heavier than it looked. It was not broad, but dense, with the compact disproportion of a text copied onto better vellum than its plain boards implied. When she drew it free, a fine drift of dry leather dust came off on her sleeve. The fore-edge had darkened where damp had once got at it and then been driven out by heat. It had no title and no family crest, only the half-buried catalogue number and, on the spine, one strip of older repair thread that had gone almost black.

Hermione glanced over her shoulder. Draco was still at the narrow desk, head bent, quill moving. The archive held steady around them.

She carried the folio back to the main table.

The first obstacle was getting it open without damaging it.

The upper board lifted. The first two leaves did not. Damp had once fused them along the outer corner and later preservation work had fixed the damage in place. Hermione set the book on blotting cloth, drew her wand, and used the thinnest releasing charm she trusted on old vellum. Even that took patience. The leaves parted with a papery drag that made her teeth ache.

The title page had taken the worst of the damp. Brown tide lines climbed halfway through the text. The lower margin had warped and stiffened where water had dried in the fibres generations ago. The hand, though, remained legible, if old enough to fight her.

Meaning came slowly. Grammar first. Then the house abbreviations. Then the anchoring terms whose uses had drifted over centuries.

The title yielded in pieces.

*On the Binding Architecture of the House and the Keeping of the Line.*

Hermione read it again to make sure she had not made it uglier in translation than it was in the original. She had not. *Keeping* was generous, if anything. The Latin sat closer to *continuance*, with overtones of maintenance and perpetuation both.

Her hands went very still on the leather.

The opening section did not read like ward theory. It read like instruction.

It was not about protecting a house.

It was about making one answer.

The first clause she could translate cleanly sat near the top of the second page, written in the same dry practical hand used for drainage systems and bloodline trusts:

*Let chambers incline toward habitual company and answer warmth for concord, chill for estrangement, until right measure is restored.*

Hermione stared at the line until the words separated from one another and became history instead of language.

Warmth for concord. Chill for estrangement.

The guest wing had been colder on nights she stayed in her rooms and warmer on the evenings work ran late. The library fire had started banking itself before dusk without instruction. Twice she had woken to hot water already drawn in the washroom after evenings spent in the archive together. She had treated all of it as house intelligence sharpened by occupancy, the sort of advanced environmental responsiveness old magical estates occasionally developed. It had been irritating and useful. She had not thought it personal.

The folio disagreed.

She turned the page too fast and had to flatten it again with her palm.

Further down, under a heading she first misread as *threshold harmonics* and then corrected to *threshold obedience*, another clause made the correction worse.

*Inner presses, sealed rooms, and guarded passages shall answer only where the paired measure is met; let denial instruct where separation will not.*

Hermione's eyes lifted from the page to the alcove in the south wall as if the book had physically pointed there.

She thought at once of the archive. She thought of the drawing-room doors that had opened only when both of them stood there. She thought of the rooms that offered no seam at all to one of them and yielded at once to two. She had treated those as tests, almost playful in their cruelty. The house was setting conditions. The house was valuing proximity. She had not yet put a plainer word under it.

Paired measure.

It was not household occupancy, bloodline permission, or random magical compatibility. It was a pair.

In the outer margin, squeezed between two lines in a later hand, sat a note in clipped property shorthand:

*for restricted access and concordant habitation*

That was the first point at which Draco's absence from this page became legible in practical terms. He could have read the margin. He might even have translated the heading with time. But the body of the text lived in the old anchoring hand his family had used for theory and then abandoned to specialists. The shorthand gloss turned machinery into housekeeping. Restricted access. Concordant habitation. Estate management language for what, on the page proper, was a manual for altering two people's lives by stages so the house could better hold its line.

No wonder he had not found this cleanly before. No wonder the modern catalogue had buried it among uncategorised estate papers after the old shelf marks fell out of use. Without the week she had spent building the concordance, she would not have known where fourteen-C was. Without her work in structural theory, she would have missed what the text was saying once she got there. The concealment had not been a single locked drawer. It had been sediment. Generations of it.

Her pulse beat once, hard and obvious, in the base of her throat.

She kept reading.

Midway through the next gathering the language turned from rooms to bodies.

It did not do so metaphorically.

It did so clinically.

*A light announcement of pulse, burden, and distemper may be made from one body into the other, that increase be reckoned and household concord better guided.*

Hermione's fingers went numb around the page edge.

Pulse. Burden. Distemper.

A light announcement from one body into the other.

The weeks of knowing which floor he was on before she heard him. The low ache in her own arm when the Mark bit him hard enough downstairs. The physical certainty of his location moving through the house. This morning's breach into his point of view, the feel of her touch from inside his own skin. All of it set down in a dead man's practical hand as if the transfer of one body's condition into another were no stranger than checking the weather.

She looked toward the alcove again. Draco was still out of sight from where she sat, hidden by the angle of the deeper shelves. Even without seeing him she could place him precisely. Left side of the archive now. Near the narrower desk by the cracked cabinet. Paused, not moving. Reading or trying to. She knew it at once and without sound.

The page blurred. She blinked hard and forced the lines back into place.

At the bottom of the gathering, where the parchment had taken the worst of the old water damage, the sentence she needed most had half rotted away.

She knew that before she finished translating it. The grammar broke under the missing fibres in exactly the place where the clause's conditions should have lived.

What remained was enough to wound.

*Where a first inclin[ation] lies... the architecture may take purcha[se] and enlarge it toward... concord...*

Below that, a torn gap.

Then a surviving phrase in the next line:

*...whether mutual from the first or answering only in one...*

Hermione bent closer. The damaged section had swallowed the verbs and two key modifiers. The sentence could still be forced into meaning, but not one meaning only.

Where a first inclination lies, the architecture may take purchase. That part was clear enough, or as clear as old vellum permitted. But the missing segment mattered. It might have specified how much inclination was required. It might have limited the mechanism to mutual attachment. It might have allowed one-sided attachment to be enough. It might have meant the house could seize on the smallest bias and deepen it until it felt original. The surviving phrase underneath made the range worse, not better. *Whether mutual from the first or answering only in one...* One what. One party. One line. One body. She could not tell. The water had taken exactly the words that decided the question.

Hermione translated the damaged clause three times on scrap paper.

Version one made the house an amplifier of pre-existing feeling.

Version two made it an opportunist, taking hold where any slight preference already existed, however faint.

Version three, the one she disliked enough to set the quill down between words, suggested that one person's attachment might be sufficient for the machinery to begin, with mutuality arriving later because the architecture rewarded it into being.

She crossed that version out so hard the nib caught.

Then wrote it again beneath the strike-through because crossing it out had not made it less available.

This, then, was the uncertainty. It was not whether the house had been acting. The folio settled that. It was not whether it widened awareness or shifted rooms or trained access against proximity. The pages were explicit. The uncertainty sat in the threshold. She could not tell how much had to be there before the Manor could begin. She could not tell whether the architecture had found a real attachment and leaned on it, or found only the smallest opening and worked the rest loose with time, warmth, access, and repetition.

The chapter's central question had been a fog until then. The damaged page gave it edges sharp enough to cut on.

She turned another leaf with more care.

Later marginal notes appeared in alternating hands, one of them so clipped it might have belonged to Abraxas Malfoy, another older and broader, both treating the same mechanism as line maintenance. *Do not force estranged heirs into the east suite before measure improves.* *Watch access in winter months.* *Fire response often precedes passage response.* The notes did not sound monstrous. That made them more so. Domestic management written around the assumption that a house may supervise attachment between the people inside it and should do so for the good of the line.

Near the back of the folio, after a section on room sequencing she barely skimmed because her pulse had climbed too high for clean comprehension, one sentence stopped her cold enough to bring the whole library into focus at once.

*Most sure efficacy attends the unknowing pair. Once the engine is named, continuance stands at election and cannot be urged in the former manner.*

She read it twice more to make sure *election* was not wishful thinking on her part. It was a legal term in this context: choice, option, a thing standing to be accepted or refused.

The house worked best when the pair did not know.

Once the mechanism was known, continuation changed.

It would not necessarily end. It would not be undone. But it would change.

Draco had to know.

No, that was already too tidy. He at least had to see the page. He had to know what his family had built into the place before she put her hand on the Mark again tomorrow. This was his house, his family's machinery, his life being shaped without his informed consent. If the folio was right, even the timing mattered. Knowledge altered the conditions. Delay was not neutral.

Hermione shut the book so abruptly the closing leaves slapped the table.

For a few seconds she looked only at the fire, not for comfort but for a fixed point. The room had gone intolerably specific: the guest-wing corridor taking twenty-three paces that morning instead of thirty-one, the library chair warming before she sat, doors opening only when both of them reached them at once, the extra cup already on the tray before tea had been requested. He knew, without asking, how much butter she liked on bread because he had watched her long enough to know. He handed her the right reference before she asked. Her body turned toward the south wall before she heard him come through it. Every useful domestic fact of the last weeks now sat under the clauses she had just translated.

Not all of it could belong to the house.

That was worse.

If the Manor had invented the whole thing out of air, the solution would be monstrous but simple: rage, refusal, leaving if needed. What the damaged line offered instead was the nastier possibility that the house had found a real inclination and worked on it until she could no longer tell what part was hers, what part was his, and what part was architecture rewarding the same movements until they felt inevitable.

She opened the folio again and searched the margins for a citation. There, half buried under a later gloss:

*see companion quire xiv-f for first-taking conditions*

The breath left her in irritation so sharp it was almost relief. Good. A second text. A place to confirm the missing clause before she said any of this aloud. She snatched up the concordance sheet, rose, and went back into the archive with the folio open to mark the reference.

Fourteen-F was deeper in the older run, beyond a shift of shelving the modern catalogue no longer acknowledged. Hermione had only reached the second counting post when the room changed around her.

The shift was not dramatic. The archive never performed when a practical inconvenience would do.

The pressure difference came first. The drier air thinned. Then the brass at the inner grille dulled, the way metal did when an active ward withdrew from attention. Somewhere beyond the outer stacks came the scrape of a chair and the faint receding footfall of someone moving toward the front room.

Draco was leaving.

The shelves to her right gave a soft granular sigh. One of the paper labels she had been using as a marker lifted at the corner and then lay flat again under a skin of dust that had not been there a second earlier.

Hermione turned toward the sound. Through two gaps in the shelving she caught a partial view of him at the far end of the archive, gathering his copy pages into a stack. He had likely finished that ledger and was taking the notes out before the room decided to eat them. Another three steps and he would be through the alcove into the main library. Another five and the deeper run might flatten into blank shelving or stone.

If she called his name now, he would come back.

If she called his name now, she would have to tell him why.

She stood between the stacks with the folio open in her hands and did nothing.

Draco crossed the alcove.

The change finished at once. The older run did not vanish, but it lost definition. The labels that had helped her count the shelves blurred into ordinary strips of darkened paper. A seam in the back wall that marked the turn toward the hidden subpresses smoothed itself almost flat. Hermione got as far as laying her fingertips on the place where fourteen-F should have begun before the stone went cold and featureless under her hand.

The archive was still technically open. The front room still held. But the deeper access had gone with him.

There went the plan to verify first.

No, not quite. She could still have called him back. He would have come. That was the part that sat wrong. The shelf closing on her was one thing. Letting it close because explaining would have meant telling him was another.

When she returned to the main table she brought the folio with her, but only long enough to copy the damaged clause and the line about unknowing pairs into her private notebook, not the work ledger but the other one. Then she closed the cover, carried the book back to shelf fourteen-C, and slid it into place between the genealogy register and the foundation-seals volume with more care than she had removed it.

The reshelving felt obscene.

On the walk back to the main table, the route through the library caught at different points and offered memories up under new headings.

*Let chambers incline toward habitual company.*

The guest-wing route shortened whenever she had reason to be in the library anyway. The corridor outside her rooms warmed toward evening on days he worked late. The chair at her usual place was already drawn back from the table.

*Inner presses, sealed rooms, and guarded passages shall answer only where the paired measure is met.*

The archive opened for both of them and blanked itself to one. The drawing-room doors had done the same. The south wall seam had taught them, without explanation, that access itself could be a pressure tactic.

*A light announcement of pulse, burden, and distemper may be made from one body into the other.*

Her own arm ached when the Mark tore at him. She knew his location by floorboard and distance without sound. This morning she had felt his shame, his attention, the ugly grateful quiet of his pain receding under her hand.

And under all of it, the damaged line:

*Where a first inclination lies.*

How much inclination there had to be, whose it was, since when: the questions did not erase the last weeks. They got into them.

By the time Draco came through the alcove into the front room, she had the surveyor's report open again and her own notes spread over the crucial corner of the margin. Her left hand rested there as if the paper needed weighing down. The private notebook sat shut beneath two older maps.

"The archive's fighting me again," he said. "The back wall went soft around four. I had to copy the last pages from memory."

His voice sounded ordinary. She hated how ordinary it sounded.

He crossed to the side table, set down his copy pages, and only then looked properly at her. His eyes moved over the surveyor's report, the spare quill, her hand pinning the papers, then up to her face.

"Have you eaten?"

"No."

"It's past seven."

"I know."

Concern showed plainly enough to be embarrassing. It was not theatrical concern or solicitousness wielded like a claim. It was the practical, habitual kind. He glanced at the cold tea beside her elbow, at the untouched crust of bread from noon, then back at her.

"I'll get food," he said.

"You don't have to."

"I know."

There it was again. Her own phrasing, handed back cleaner than she deserved.

He moved toward the corridor. Hermione heard herself say his name before deciding to.

"Malfoy."

He stopped in the doorway and turned just enough to look at her over one shoulder.

That was the point at which she could still say it plainly. If she spoke now, the next hour would be about the folio, the damaged clause, and the fact that his house had been managing both of them in ways neither had agreed to. He would go still in that brittle way he had under real pressure. He would ask to see the book. The rest of the evening would split open around it.

Instead she reached for the smaller question first, the safer one, and hated herself a little even as she heard it leaving her mouth.

"Do you want to stop tomorrow's examination," she said, "after this morning?"

For a second he only looked at her, clearly hearing the substitution and letting it pass because he thought she was frightened by the spillover.

"Do you?"

Hermione could feel the folio's lines under her skin as if she had swallowed them. Paired measure, unknowing pair, election, choice. Tomorrow she would put her hands on his arm again knowing what the Manor did with touch, access, warmth, routine. She knew at least enough to know it was not neutral. If she said yes, stop, the work would halt before she had to answer the damaged clause. If she said no, they would continue on terms he did not know had altered.

Her mouth moved before her conscience got there.

"No," she said. "The lower readings are shifting too fast. I need another pass."

He held her eyes. "Is that about the diagnostics or about you not wanting to lose a day's data."

Both, and neither in the way he meant. The question was too precise to survive with a neat answer.

"Both," she said finally.

He gave a short nod, unhappy with it and accepting it anyway. "Nine, then."

"Nine."

Tomorrow was in place now. She had done that.

"Did you find anything useful?" he asked.

He was not suspicious. He was just asking. That made it harder to miss what she was doing.

Hermione's fingers pressed harder on the margin she had covered. "Administrative rubbish. Foundation records. Corridor repairs."

He watched her for a beat longer than the answer deserved. "That sounds like your idea of a good evening."

On another day she would have snorted. On another day he might have meant it lightly enough to earn one.

"Apparently," she said.

He did not move at once. The library held the pause between them without helping. She wondered, with a flash of useless fury, whether the house knew what it had made possible and was waiting to see which way she would break.

"All right," Draco said at last. "Soup or bread."

The question was so practical it nearly undid her. "Either."

"Helpful as ever."

The dryness in it was real this time, thin but real. Then he turned and went down the corridor toward the kitchen.

Hermione sat very still until his footsteps passed beyond the range where she could track them by ear alone. The bodily sense of him remained, moving through the house, turning left at the lower stair. The knowledge came with such clean certainty that she almost laughed at the obscenity of it.

Instead she pulled the private notebook back out from under the maps and stared at the lines she had copied:

*Where a first inclination lies...*

*Most sure efficacy attends the unknowing pair...*

Her own handwriting looked ugly, too hard pressed. The ink had feathered where the nib caught on the cheap paper.

Verify first, she told herself again. That was still reasonable. It was. One damaged clause was not enough to drop in his lap. One companion quire she still had not been able to reach. If she told him now and later proved the threshold mistranslated, she would have broken the work, frightened him, maybe broken the house's cooperation as well, all on the strength of a compromised text.

The argument might have held if she had called him back to reopen fourteen-F. Or if she had sent an owl to the Ministry that night for a second reader on the damaged clause. Or if she had copied the folio into the work ledger instead of the hidden notebook.

She did none of that.

When Draco returned he carried a tray with a bowl of leek soup, a heel of bread, and fresh tea in the blue pot the house seemed to produce whenever evenings ran long. He set the tray beside her elbow with the care of someone placing instruments near a live charm array. He was not fussy, only exact.

"It's hot," he said. "Don't ignore it until it isn't."

"I'm not six."

"No. Six-year-olds usually need less supervision."

That got a breath of air out of her that was nearly a laugh and not one. He sat down across from her with his own tea, not food, and opened the book he had abandoned that morning. The spine had not forgiven him. He flattened it with one hand and pretended interest.

Hermione broke bread because not doing so would make the whole lie more obvious than it already was. Butter had been put on the tray without her asking. It was not much. It was exactly what she liked. She looked at it too long before taking a bite.

Draco lowered the book an inch. "Terrible."

"No."

"Then why are you looking at the bread as if it insulted your family."

"I'm tired."

"That, at least, I believe."

He turned a page. The domestic quiet that followed should have been ordinary. They had spent weeks inside versions of it, working at opposite ends of the same table, letting tea cool unnoticed, one of them making a remark every ten minutes that expected no real answer. The intimacy of repeated practical company had never needed a declared name. Tonight the same arrangement felt wired through with hidden apparatus. The bowl warmed her hands. The chair had been pulled to the right distance before she sat. He had taken the familiar route through the kitchen to bring the tray back. She could no longer tell which of those things belonged to him, which to her, and which to the house leaning on both of them.

She made herself swallow the soup. It tasted of leeks, stock, and too much pepper. It was real enough. It was human enough.

"Did the morning pass leave you with anything useful," Draco asked without looking up from the page, "or only more reasons to tell me not to move my arm."

Hermione set the spoon down too carefully. "Useful enough."

"That's not an answer. It's adjacent."

The observation was mild. Accurate, though.

"The outer lattice is still degrading," she said. "The pain response drops under sustained contact. The lower layer's still unclear."

Every word was true. Not one of them was sufficient.

"You can say stop, if you need to," he said after a moment.

She looked up. "What."

He kept his eyes on the book. "Tomorrow. Or after tomorrow. Whenever. If the diagnostic value stops justifying the rest of it."

By that he did not mean the pain only, or the inconvenience. He meant the strain without naming it, which was probably the most direct he could have managed without more prompting than she had any right to ask of him.

Hermione looked at his bent head, the loosened line of his mouth, the book he was not reading. If she told him now, this exact scene would rupture. If she kept silent, she would preserve it under false conditions. There was no version left in which she did not owe for the next sentence.

"I'll tell you if that changes," she said.

He turned the page. "Right."

Again it was not agreement. Again it sounded like him putting a door where there had not been one a minute earlier.

They drank tea in the library while the fire settled lower. Once, near the end of the tray, he asked whether the corridor maps from 1812 matched her route notes. Hermione answered too quickly that they needed more comparison. Once she asked whether the archive had yielded anything legible from the back wall. He said only, "Fragments." The word sat between them with more relevance than he knew.

By the time the clock on the mantel struck eight, the evening had narrowed to practical exits.

"You should sleep," Draco said, closing the ruined book at last.

"I still have notes to sort."

"You say that every night."

"Because it's true every night."

He rose, gathered the empty cup from the tray, then paused with his hand on the handle of the teapot. "Nine."

"Nine," Hermione repeated.

His eyes came to her face one last time, searching for a reason he had not yet been given and perhaps no longer intended to ask for. Whatever he saw there, it was not enough to keep him in the room. He took the tray and went.

Only when his footsteps had gone far enough down the corridor to blur into the Manor's broader quiet did she let herself stop arranging her face.

The library looked unchanged. The surveyor's report lay open. The maps were stacked. Her work ledger sat squared beside the private notebook she should never have started and definitely should not have used tonight. The alcove in the south wall was still visible, though dimmer now that the archive was holding on residual grace rather than active paired presence. It could have been any ordinary working room if she had not known better.

She knew better, and tomorrow she would sit across from him again. She would ask for his arm. She would put her hand on the Mark with the folio's lines still snagging in her head and the damaged clause unresolved. She would take the tea he brought and let the house go on answering them while he still didn't know what the machinery was called.

Verification, she thought, and almost said it aloud just to hear how thin it sounded. If verification had really been the point, she would already have pulled him back to the south wall or been writing to the Ministry for a second reader. Instead Hermione pulled the private notebook toward her, tore out the page with the copied clauses, folded it twice, and slid it into the inner pocket of her cardigan. Then she put the notebook back under the maps, closed the work ledger over the safe figures, and extinguished the brighter of the two desk lamps so the room would not look, at a glance, as though anything had happened there at all.

Nothing had stopped her from telling him. The missing words in the damaged line made the silence easier. So did fourteen-F. So did the fact that tomorrow's data really did matter. But by the time any of that came to hand, she had already heard herself ask the smaller question instead.

By morning she would turn that smaller choice into procedure: the farther table, the brisker tone, distance she could call caution if he noticed and fear if she was being honest.
