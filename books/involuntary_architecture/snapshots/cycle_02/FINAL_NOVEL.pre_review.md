# Involuntary Architecture

# Chapter 1

The filing charm collapsed on a Tuesday evening, and three years of sorted correspondence slid from its shelves and pooled across Hermione's office floor.

Hermione stared. The charm was hers, a standard administrative enchantment she'd anchored to the oak cabinet in her first week in the Department of Mysteries, the kind of spell that should have sat in the wood and worked without attention for years. She'd refreshed it once, maybe twice, in all that time. It was not supposed to fail.

Kneeling, she picked up the nearest scroll, an internal memo from August with the wax seal still intact, and pushed magic back into the cabinet's grain. The spell caught, wavered, and held, but only thinly. The difference registered at once: same wand, same intention, same clean structure, but the enchantment sat in the wood like something balanced there, not grown through it.

So she filed the memo by hand, then the next one, then the next. By the time the last scroll was back in place, it was past eight and her knees ached from the stone floor. After hours the Department of Mysteries did not sound empty so much as stoppered; the soundproofing charms sealed the silence until her own breathing was the loudest thing in the room.

In the corridor, the lights were thinning again. They weren't flickering. They were just dimming so gradually it was impossible to call the moment of failure. For weeks she'd been logging small degradations: the east archive's preservation charms developing a stutter in September, Section G's diagnostic arrays returning readings she was no longer willing to call anomalous, her office temperature holding two degrees low since mid-October. Anyone else could have ignored it under a jumper. Hermione Granger measured things.

In September she'd written the report: detailed, thorough, exhaustive enough to irritate anyone assigned to read it. Croaker had read it once, asked her to keep monitoring, and said nothing further. The Department of Mysteries did not acknowledge crises. It acknowledged data.

The data said Britain's ambient magical field was declining. The decline was not dramatic. No buildings were collapsing. The Prophet was not issuing special editions. There was just accumulation. A charm that should have lasted three years failed after two and a half. Floo connections dropped mid-sentence. A Healer at St. Mungo's had to cast twice where once used to suffice. Each failure was small enough to dismiss; together they formed a pattern she had stopped pretending not to see. By mid-October her private log ran to thirty-seven pages. People who understood the graph got quiet. People who didn't argued with it.

Every archive available to her had already been searched: the Department's restricted collection, Hogwarts under a special dispensation from McGonagall that came with no questions and no deadline, two private collections, one in Devon and one owned by a Ravenclaw academic who owed her a favor, and everything the Ministry had ever published on ambient-field theory. None of it reached far enough back. The decay was older than the Ministry, older than Hogwarts, rooted in infrastructure that predated modern magical governance, and the records that mattered had mostly stayed in family hands.

Most collections had been catalogued after the war.

One had not.

Three weeks of cross-referencing Devon notes against Hogwarts frameworks had gotten her only this: somewhere in the deep structure of British magic sat an anchoring system no surviving public text described adequately. Its outline showed up in margins and footnotes, in references to anchoring lines and foundational nodes and a network underneath the visible spellwork. The primary sources were missing. Every trail ended at old families. Most had died out, cooperated, or yielded nothing new. One was named Malfoy.

Down in the Atrium, a maintenance wizard was trying to fill a cleaning bucket from the fountain. On his third attempt the water vanished again before the mop touched the floor. He swore at the bucket, refilled it, and swore harder when the charm stripped the water away a second time. Hermione watched long enough to confirm it wasn't a one-off, then walked out through the visitor entrance into the October night.

London was cold, damp, and entirely itself. Muggle infrastructure had its own forms of failure, but tonight the streetlights worked, the buses ran, and the pavement held steady under her feet. She passed the Tube station and kept walking. The choice had started in January after a Knight Bus ride ended with her sick on the pavement outside a Tesco and the simple thought, humiliatingly useful, that she could just use her legs. The habit had stayed. Forty minutes through Holborn and Camden, past steamed chip-shop windows, a man swearing into his mobile, a woman hauling a sleeping child out of a minicab. None of it relied on magic. Reliability had become more valuable than convenience, which was not a preference she'd expected to acquire.

---

Her flat in Kentish Town sat above a shuttered dry cleaner's. It was one bedroom on a cheap lease, with cream walls she'd never bothered to cover. Books were everywhere: shelves, table, kitchen counter, the armchair by the window that had become storage after the cushion's warming charm started bleeding out by midnight. A blanket still lay folded over one arm, unused. In the bathroom, a Muggle umbrella occupied the tub because a drying charm had once held long enough to become invisible and then failed, leaving the object dry, purposeless, and somehow permanent. No one had been invited over since the move. The place was for sleeping, reading, eating, and leaving again.

By the door, the sticking charm she used as a coat hook had started to peel from the wall. Hermione pressed it back with her thumb until it caught, barely. In the kitchen the kettle charm was unreliable, so water went on over the gas hob instead. The hob was Muggle. The flame did what fire did every time.

Dinner was toast and the last of the cheddar because the refrigerator's expansion charm had collapsed the week before, shoving everything back to actual size and cracking a jar of chutney against the rear shelf. She'd cleaned the glass, restocked the basics, and filed the whole thing under the same word she used for everything now. Managed was the word. Her flat was managed. So was the divorce.

Two months earlier Ron had called to say he was seeing someone from his department. He'd handled it carefully, with the decency he reached for when he knew he was crossing old ground, and she'd said the right things and meant them. Afterwards she'd stood in the kitchen and waited for hurt, jealousy, anything with edges. Nothing came. Just the flat remainder of a marriage that had subtracted itself so slowly there was nothing left to divide.

At the counter she read a journal article on her tablet while she ate. The subject was ambient-field theory, a fresh defense of a conjecture she'd already dismissed twice. The author called magical fluctuation cyclical, self-correcting, ultimately benign; the data stopped in 2001. Thoroughness made her finish the piece anyway. Then the plate was washed, the tablet set down, and the kitchen went quiet around the specific tiredness of a person who had exhausted every available avenue and still couldn't see the next one.

Her left forearm itched. Automatically, she tugged her sleeve lower. The glamour held. It always held. Every morning she checked it the same way she checked the flat wards: fingertips to skin, the smooth surface of the charm, the brief confirmation that concealment remained intact. Seven years of repetition had made the sequence part of dressing: wake up, shower, charm the forearm, choose a shirt, leave.

The Floo coughed.

Green flame rose to her knees and died. Hermione waited. The network tried again with a wheeze and a brief unsteady flare, then dropped. A voice cut through the fire for a second and vanished.

Kneeling at the grate, she cast a diagnostic. The relay was still there. She could feel the north London node humming under the failure, serving four thousand households and barely holding together. Harry's fireplace at Grimmauld Place was trying to reach her.

Apparition was an option. An owl was an option. Instead she wrote, *Floo dropped again. I'm fine. Talk tomorrow?* and sent the note through the flat's mail charm, which still mostly worked unless it decided to deposit Ministry correspondence in a Muggle postbox three streets over.

Harry would ask if she was sleeping. She'd say yes. He'd make that face where his mouth stayed neutral and his eyes said he'd known her too long for this. Then she'd redirect because the truthful version was harder to explain: sleep was technically happening, food was technically happening, everything was technically managed, and managed had become an entire life.

On the sofa between two journal stacks, she listened to the Floo cough once more. Green fire flickered; Harry's voice made it through this time, just long enough for "Hermione?" before the network failed again.

She left it there.

---

Croaker's memo arrived Thursday morning on pale lavender parchment, interdepartmental seal, and language so carefully imprecise it might as well have had *routine* stamped over it in red. His margin note was smaller and more direct: *Granger. Relevant to your research. Last uncatalogued major collection. Authorize at your discretion.*

The substance was simple. The Malfoy family library in Wiltshire remained the last major private collection in Britain still uncatalogued. It was known to contain pre-Ministry records, foundational magical theory, and archive material unavailable in any public institution. A qualified researcher was requested to assess the collection for texts relevant to current departmental concerns.

Hermione read it once for content, once because *Malfoy family library* landed in her chest like dropped weight, and once more for the omissions. Croaker did not mention magical decay. He did not mention the stabilization indices she'd been tracking for months. He did not put in writing that the books in that house might be the only surviving record of the system now failing across Britain.

So Croaker was being careful. The memo could sit in a file as routine heritage work. The margin note could not. Croaker was telling her, as discreetly as the institution allowed, that every conventional route had run out.

She flattened the parchment on her desk and pressed both palms to the wood. The Ministry-standard oak felt solid under her hands. It did not move.

Seven years had passed since she'd last set foot on the property. Draco Malfoy she had not spoken to since the trial in the spring of 1999, six years ago, when she'd still been nineteen and angry enough to believe survival ought to buy some authority over how the war was remembered. The Wizengamot had been ready to send him to Azkaban. He had been sixteen when he took the Mark and seventeen at the battle. The prosecution called that choice, as if any child in that house had ever possessed the kind of freedom the word implied.

So she'd testified. It wasn't generosity or impulse. It was correctness. The record showed what had happened in the drawing room, and she had no intention of naming the word on her arm aloud for a chamber full of quills. What she gave them instead was the part that mattered for judgment: Draco Malfoy had been a frightened boy in a war, and when the Snatchers dragged them into that room and demanded identification, he had looked at Harry's swollen face and lied. Fear and mercy were not the same thing. Neither did one erase the other.

The verdict was acquittal with conditions and two years of wand restriction. He had stood when it was read, looked at the bench, looked at his mother, looked at the door, and left. From the gallery Hermione had watched with her sleeves pulled down and her glamour freshly cast, waiting for something she would not have admitted to wanting: a glance, a nod, anything. He had given her nothing.

That silence still sat in her like a barb. It wasn't because she needed gratitude. Principle had been enough to make her testify. Principle had not been enough to explain why six years later the absence of thanks could still catch when she turned the memory the wrong way.

The library was still the right lead. For weeks she'd known the gap in her research was historical. The anchoring infrastructure was older than the Ministry and partly built by old families; the Malfoys were one of them, and their archive had stayed private through war, scandal, and every post-war cleanup effort that had pried open the rest. Bathilda Bagshot mentioned the collection in a footnote. Heritage Division whispered about it over lunch. Lucius had shown off selected texts to scholars when it suited him and kept the foundational records locked away. That left Hermione with one remaining option and no clean feelings about it.

She authorized the assignment. Then she drafted the access request to the current head of the Malfoy household, outlining scope, departmental interest, and proposed terms. Professional language came easily. It mentioned neither the trial nor the marble floor nor the room's dead cold pressing through her back.

Her best external hand made the letter look like correspondence between strangers. That, at least, was true. Whatever Draco Malfoy had become at twenty-five, she didn't know him. Whatever she had become, all work and exhaustion and a flat full of half-failed charms, he didn't know her either.

By the time she signed, sealed, and pushed back from the desk, her hands were shaking. For a few seconds she only watched them where they rested on the chair arms. The decision had reached her body later than her handwriting. Eventually the shaking eased. The corridor lights outside her office dimmed, steadied, dimmed again. She sat there until her hands were still enough to send the owl.

---

Harry's note came Friday evening on a Ministry owl that looked nearly as frayed as the network.

The Floo had been down most of the day. North London's relay had failed again and the public explanation in the morning Prophet had used the phrase *scheduled maintenance*. Hermione had read it over tea at her desk and felt heat rise behind her eyes. Lying was infuriating on its own. Being lied to about data she'd gathered herself was worse.

His handwriting remained tight and slightly crooked, the trace of learning to write in a cupboard and never fully losing the angle of it.

*Floo's out again. Are you all right? Ginny says come for dinner Sunday. Don't bring wine, we've still got the six bottles from your birthday that no one drank because you left early.*

*Are you sleeping?*

The note made her smile. Harry had known her since she was eleven, bossy, certain, and unbearable in very specific ways. He had stayed through every version since. He didn't ask *how are you* anymore because that question gave her room to build a lie. *Are you sleeping* demanded numbers.

Back came her answer in a hand only slightly neater than his.

*I'm sleeping. I slept eight hours last night. I've taken up watercolours. I have a robust social calendar and many leisure interests. Tell Ginny yes to dinner. And I left early because your friend Robards trapped me in a thirty-minute story about ward calibration and I could feel parts of my brain shutting down in self-defence.*

The quill hovered after that. She could tell him about the Manor now or wait for the Auror grapevine to do it for her within the week. It would be better from her, probably. It would be better while it could still be framed as what it was: professional necessity, viable lead, nothing else.

She told him nothing. Sunday would be soon enough. Face to face she could manage the conversation. Face to face she could use the flat practical tone that usually got Harry to hear *handled* and stop there.

After the reply went out, the flat seemed quieter than it had before. Somewhere over Wiltshire, her access request was already in transit, Ministry parchment crossing old wards toward the house she had spent seven years not naming as a destination. The thought of her handwriting landing there brought the familiar wrongness to her forearm. It wasn't pain, not quite. It was just the scar announcing itself.

Down came her sleeve again. The glamour was fine. Years ago the Healers had explained cursed wounds, phantom sensation, nerve damage, the body's refusal to forget what had been done to it. She had written the explanation down, filed it, and continued living with the itch. Knowledge and flesh still had not agreed to mean the same thing.

Tea went on over the gas hob. Even when the kettle charm happened to work, trust no longer followed. Mug in hand, she sat on the edge of the bed because the sofa was buried in journals and the chair by the window had gone cold months ago.

Sleep had been failing since summer, before the decay became undeniable. The first bad diagnostic had come back, then the next, and suddenly competence no longer guaranteed an answer. That was the part she had not adjusted to. Problems were supposed to yield if she was prepared enough, rigorous enough, willing to outwork everyone else in the room. This one kept widening. All the surplus effort in the world had not narrowed it. What remained at the end of the day was toast, the walk home through Camden, and a flat maintained just well enough to keep going.

Malfoy Manor presented itself, as most things did in her mind, in ordered layers. The library was the best remaining lead. The collection might contain the only surviving documentation of the infrastructure failing across Britain. She was the most qualified person to examine it. Refusal would have been professionally indefensible.

Beneath those true things sat the other true thing. Bellatrix Lestrange had pinned her to a marble floor in that house and carved a word into her arm with a cursed knife while Hermione screamed hard enough to tear something in her throat. Across the room Draco Malfoy had stood against the wall with his hands at his sides. At one point she had turned her head and found his face in the cold chandelier light, white and fixed and motionless.

It wasn't what he'd done. It was what he hadn't done.

The tea was too hot. She drank it anyway because the burn in her mouth was clean, located, usable.

Refusing the assignment had never been real. It hadn't been real once the corridors at the Department started dimming. It hadn't been real once the archives ran out. For seven years she had simply not gone back, and avoidance had hardened by repetition until it resembled conviction. Now the difference had closed. Professional necessity would win because it always won, because competence was the architecture she'd built around every other fracture, and because British magic was failing while she lay awake in Kentish Town afraid of a house.

The lamp on her nightstand was Muggle, plug-in, bought in January for reasons she'd claimed were aesthetic. Its switch clicked off. Darkness settled in with pipes knocking in the walls, a distant siren, someone's television through plaster, the ordinary electrical hum of a city that did not care about magical infrastructure and would keep moving until magic failed enough people at once to matter.

Nothing in the room shifted. Nothing watched. The walls stayed where walls were meant to stay.

In the dark she tried to picture Draco at twenty-five reading her name on Ministry parchment. The image would not hold. Seventeen kept replacing it: courtroom grey suit, eyes on the door, gone before she could decide whether she wanted him to turn.

He was her age now, twenty-five, and maybe thinner, maybe only more tired. Survival had a recognizable look on people from the war: not heroism, not wisdom, just the worn-down expression of those who still hadn't worked out what the surviving was going to cost.

She rolled onto her side, battered the pillow flatter, closed her eyes, and got the stabilization index first. The decay curve followed, numbers moving in the wrong direction. Then came the drawing room: the chandelier, the marble, the sound she had made there.

Her eyes opened again.

Children were starting to misfire all over the country. She'd read the reports herself: accidental magic spiking in Muggle-born homes, windows blown out, kitchens scorched, parents with no language for what was happening to their children. In the Atrium that same week a maintenance wizard had fought a bucket because the fountain could no longer keep water in it. Britain was fraying in small failures. Hermione knew more about that fraying than anyone else alive.

Fear did not become irrational just because it interfered with useful work. The body kept its own records. Under the glamour the scar tissue still rose from her left forearm in hard ridges, letters carved in that house by that hand. Soon she would walk back into the place that had made them.

Sleep finally took her sometime after three, and even that felt closer to collapse than rest. At six the alarm went off to a cold room: the warming charm had bled out during the night. Then came the old sequence of repair. She showered, faced the mirror, cast the glamour, pulled the sleeve down, buttoned the cuff. By the time she left for work, concealment was smooth again and the scar was hidden where it had always been.


# Chapter 2

The Mark woke him at four again.

Not with the old burn. Not with the fishhook drag behind his navel that had once meant summons, movement, whatever room the Dark Lord had chosen for that night's audience. That signal died with the man who cast it. What remained was slower and less useful: an arrhythmic ache deep in the bones of his forearm, magic trying to force itself through a route that no longer led anywhere, heating the sealed channel until the whole arm felt inhabited by failed intent.

In the dark, Draco lay still and waited for it to pass. The bedroom was too large for one person. Three years ago he'd moved into his parents' suite, not out of appetite for grandeur but because the alternative was the room he'd slept in at sixteen, and that room still carried the ghost of the boy who had knelt on the carpet and dry-heaved after taking the Mark. Airing it out had not helped. Redecorating had not helped. Eventually he'd shut the door and left it shut.

Around five the ache eased. He got up because there was no reason not to.

At the far end of the ground floor sat the servants' kitchen, the one he actually used. The formal kitchen belonged to a vanished household staff and made a single person feel absurd. Tea first. Then toast, eaten standing at the counter with no plate because choosing one chair at the table meant seeing the other three stay empty.

From the kitchen to the library, his body took the same route it always took: east corridor, back stair, portrait gallery, narrow passage, south entrance. The reception rooms would have been faster. He had not used them in years. Thought rarely entered into it now. His feet knew which turns kept him out of certain rooms and made them before the rest of him caught up.

Other rooms stayed closed as well. The drawing room where his father had held meetings. The conservatory where Bellatrix had once sat humming in the evenings. The second-floor study where — no. Before the second cup of tea, he was not going to start making an inventory of absences.

The library justified the house. Two stories of shelves climbed the walls. A mezzanine ran the perimeter, fitted with rolling ladders and iron rails polished by hands long dead. Afternoon light usually flooded the west-facing tables; this early, the room held only a dim grey wash and the smell it always held, old paper, dust, preservation charms worked so deeply into the bindings that the air itself seemed lacquered with them.

By the south window waited his chair, shaped to him by repetition, and on the table beside it lay the treatise he'd been worrying through for a week. Seventeenth-century sympathetic magic, dry as old plaster and no more useful than the rest. For eight months he'd looked for anything that might explain why a dead man's brand had begun to wake in his arm. St. Mungo's had refused to touch it. Two private practitioners had tried. One recoiled outright. The other finished the examination and admitted the layering was beyond her training. She'd suggested the Unspeakables with a straight face. He had laughed.

So he read. Somewhere in the family's collection there might be a reference, or a precedent, or at least a theory that did not end in shrugging helplessness. Morning after morning he pulled books, made notes, followed citations into older shelves, and found nothing.

All morning the Mark kept up its low pulse. Pressing his thumb hard against the brand sometimes blunted it for a minute or two. Lately even that had started to fail.

Winter altered the Manor in plain ways before it did anything stranger. In summer the place loosened. Curtains lifted in draughts with no source. Doors swelled in their frames. Whole wings seemed to exhale. Winter drew the walls close. Corridors narrowed by inches he could never prove. Rooms at the far ends of the house went hard with cold. By November the east wing whistled at the windows and the upper halls held onto silence long after his footsteps had passed. He did not call the house alive. Call it alive and every shutting door became an answer. He did not call it listening. Call it listening and every pause on the stair had to belong to someone. Before breakfast he preferred timber, stone, old magic, and the possibility that he was tired. This morning, with the Mark beating in his arm and the windows black, that preference held less firmly than usual.

His mother had not visited in two weeks. That was deliberate. Narcissa Malfoy did nothing by accident. Three years earlier she had moved into a townhouse in Bath, tasteful and proportionate, rooms that stayed where they were put. She'd been graceful about it. *The Manor is yours, darling. It needs someone in residence.* She had never said *I can't live there anymore*. She had only paused on the threshold, set two fingers against the doorframe, and left them there a beat too long before stepping out. Twenty-five years in this house had worn at her in ways she did not discuss. Draco had not asked for details. Asking would mean admitting the house could lay hands on people at all.

By noon the treatise had yielded nothing. Lunch was soup, bread, and an apple he forgot on a side table until the flesh began to brown against the wood. After that came the east lawn, walked because his body needed movement and the grounds were the one place large enough to cross without brushing past doors he preferred closed. At half three, the usual circuit broke.

An owl struck the library window hard enough to rattle the panes and dropped Ministry stationery onto the desk.

Department of Mysteries. Departmental seal bitten deep into the wax. Paper thick enough to feel official before he read a word of it. At the library desk, where all mail got opened, he broke the seal and found Granger's name at the foot of the page.

First reading: content. She requested access to the Malfoy library for work related to the ongoing investigation into systemic magical decay. The Ministry would provide a standard stipend for room and board. She anticipated a stay of several weeks at minimum. She required unrestricted access to all holdings, including restricted sections.

Second reading: the rest of it. She had not entered this house since the night she was dragged across the floor of a room he no longer used. The last time they had spoken was at his trial seven years earlier, when she sat in a courtroom and described, with measured accuracy, what his family had done and what he had failed to prevent. No word he might have offered then would have survived the distance between them. Silence had seemed less insulting than an inadequate apology, so silence was what he'd given her.

Beneath the formal request sat the real fact. Hermione Granger would not have written to him unless every other door had failed to open. He was the last one.

At Granger's name the Mark kicked once under his sleeve, sharp enough to make his hand jerk. His thumb went to it before he'd decided to move.

She needed the library. In return, he needed a witness who could look at the brand on his arm and not flinch.

That evening he wrote back. He accepted her terms and added one of his own.

---

On the morning she was due, Draco cleaned.

Not the whole Manor. That project belonged to someone with staff or delusions. The library got done, the guest wing got done, the kitchen got done, and so did the corridors between them. Dust lifted from shelves under his wand. Stone floors lost their film of disuse. Fresh candles replaced the stubs in the library sconces. Somewhere in the middle of it he recognized that this was the most attention he'd paid the house in over a year, and the recognition annoyed him enough that he kept working harder.

In the east arm waited the guest rooms: a bedroom, a sitting room, a private bath, all held under preservation charms and the hush of space that had gone too long unoccupied. November air came in through the window he forced open and corrected the stale stillness a little.

By eleven he was in the entrance hall because that was where guests were received, and whatever else this arrangement was, it would be conducted correctly. The hall was one of the few formal spaces he had kept up. Black-and-white marble. A ceiling built to dwarf arrivals. A staircase wide enough for four people abreast. The family had always understood entrances better than what followed them.

From the drive came the crack of Apparition. She had not used the Floo. That could have meant the network was misbehaving again, or it could have meant she had chosen the method that left a front door between them. He suspected the second.

Before she could knock, he opened it.

On the step stood Hermione Granger in a dark coat with a leather bag over one shoulder, hair pulled back and already escaping. The years had changed her and failed to change her. She was smaller than memory had made her, more contained, less mythic than the Ministry clippings and courtroom recollections he'd allowed to accumulate in her place. What remained most familiar was the expression. She had prepared for this. She knew how the first half minute was supposed to go and had come armed for it. Across the courtroom seven years ago he had watched that same preparation carry her through testimony that spared him nothing and still kept him out of Azkaban.

"Malfoy," she said.

"Granger."

No hand offered itself. The absence stood between them as plainly as any contract.

"Come in."

Past him she went, and the moment her body crossed the threshold her breath caught. Her shoulders rose under the coat, then dropped. By the time the door latched she had flattened the flinch almost out of sight. He knew the labor of that. He'd spent years smoothing his own reactions before anyone else could look at them.

Formal hush closed around them in the entrance hall. Her gaze moved over the marble floor, the sweep of the stairs, the height of the ceiling, measuring what had altered and what had not.

"The library's through the east corridor," he said. "I'll show you once we've discussed terms."

"Your letter mentioned additional conditions."

"It did."

To the morning room he led her, the closest thing the house had to neutral ground. South-facing windows. A desk used for household business by three generations of Malfoys. Tea already set out, whether from hospitality or habit he could not have said. Granger took the chair opposite without waiting to be placed. He sat behind the desk because oak between them was easier than open space.

"You'll have full access to the library," he said. "All holdings, including the restricted sections."

"Fine."

"The guest wing's in order. You've got your own rooms and a private bath. Use the kitchen whenever you like. If you need to move through the rest of the house for research, do it. I'd only ask for notice before you enter private rooms."

"That won't be a problem."

So far they were talking about rooms, keys, plumbing. He could do rooms, keys, plumbing.

"My additional condition concerns the Mark."

Across from him, she waited without moving. Her left sleeve was buttoned tight at the wrist. Habit, then. Most people would have missed why. He did not. The room seemed briefly crowded with memory anyway: her on a floor not far from here, his own uselessness standing over it, the fact that she had still come.

Without ceremony, he pushed up his left sleeve and laid his forearm on the desk.

Darkening had made the skull and serpent look newly inked. The surrounding skin was faintly raised. Along the edges, in this light, a shimmer showed where residual magic sat close to the surface.

Granger leaned forward. She did not touch him.

"Turn your wrist a little," she said.

He did.

Her gaze tracked the skull first, then the serpent's tail, then the raised skin at the edges, quick and level and unsentimental.

"It's been waking up," he said. "Not the summoning. That's gone. This is different. Pain without a signal. The magic in it is restless. It keeps trying to move and there's nowhere for it to go. St. Mungo's refused it. Two private practitioners had a look. Neither helped."

"When did it start?"

"Eight months ago. At first it was mostly at night. Now it's constant enough to be tiresome."

Another second passed while she kept looking.

"From the surface alone, I'd say the structure isn't simple residual curse activity," she said. "There are at least two layers visible here, maybe more. But that's only surface reading. If you want an actual map of the curse, I'll need proper light, time, and sustained contact."

Not now hung in the room without her needing to say it twice.

"What are you proposing?" she asked.

"You get the library for as long as you need it. In return, you examine the Mark. Properly. Diagnostic work, records, whatever you need in order to tell me what it's doing and whether it can be managed."

"You want me to study a curse two Healers wouldn't."

"I want you to study it because you're the most qualified person likely to say yes."

That landed harder than he'd intended. The real clause sat underneath it, plain enough without being spoken all the way out.

"We keep it to the work," she said.

"Of course."

"Last names. Clear boundaries around the examination work. I keep records and you see them. If I find a treatment path, I tell you. If I find a risk to you or anyone else in the house, I tell you that directly too."

"Agreed."

"And the research comes first. The library is why I'm here. The Mark work gets scheduled. No improvised diagnostics because we're already in the room together."

"Understood."

Neither of them reached for the tea. Steam climbed between them and thinned. At length he poured anyway, because the sound of liquid in porcelain gave his hands something to do. She accepted the cup and wrapped both palms around it without drinking.

"I'll show you the library first," he said. "Then the guest wing."

---

In the library doorway she stopped with one hand still curled around the brass. For one clean second she forgot to guard her face.

His usual route brought them there by the east corridor and the back passage rather than the reception rooms. If she noticed the detour she said nothing. Through the south entrance she went, then stopped just enough for the pause to register: shelves rising to the ceiling, mezzanine circling overhead, centuries of collection and theft and scholarship compressed into one room.

"Your catalogue," she said after a moment.

For this, she'd overprepared. Of course she had. "I assumed."

"I'm telling you so you're not startled."

"I won't be startled." His gaze stayed on the far wall.

Two stories of shelved walls rose around a mezzanine gallery. Rolling ladders clung to brass tracks. Reading tables sat in long shafts of autumn light. # Chapter 5

On her eleventh day at the Manor, mornings had settled into a rhythm she hadn't asked for and didn't resist.

By the second week the irritation had altered into something else. Not comfort, exactly. Reliable presence.

The argument started over one word.

*Deperdere*, in a 1643 text written in the cramped hand of Abraxas Malfoy's great-great-grandfather. Standard Latin gave them *to lose, to destroy*. Malfoy insisted the family usage had narrowed over time into something more precise, closer to *to disperse irretrievably*, and he thought the distinction mattered for the magical-decay model they were building.

"It's not a synonym for *amittere*," he said, turning the book toward her. One fingertip rested on the line. "My grandfather's journals use the same construction. It's about dissipation, not loss. The magic isn't being taken or destroyed. It's bleeding out."

Her own parallel text, a 1790s theoretical treatise in standard Latin, supported part of his reading and not the rest. "The distinction only holds if the original builders imagined ambient magic as a contained system. These texts don't." She tugged her volume closer, found the place she wanted, and tapped it. "Here. *Fundamentum magiae est fluvialis, non lacustris.* The foundation of magic is riverine, not lacustrine. They're describing flow, not containment. In context, *deperdere* means dissipation from something already in motion, not leakage from a sealed system."

"I'm not arguing it was sealed. I'm arguing there's a difference between natural flow loss and structural hemorrhage, and this is the word they used when the damage was structural."

Late afternoon had gone golden without either of them noticing. Dust crossed the high windows in bright slants. Books, scrolls, her notes, his annotations, and two dead cups of tea had taken over the largest reading table by the east wall.

On a second reading he was right, at least in part. Her framework still held. So did his. The people who built the system had used one vocabulary for attrition and another for failure, and the distinction was not ornamental.

"Fine," she said. "You're right about the word. The riverine model still holds for the broader system."

"Obviously."

"Then we need both frameworks. Macro flow, structural failure mode. The decay we're seeing now isn't the system running down. It's the system breaking."

Across from her, he was already writing. His hand stayed small and even on the parchment, nothing like the erratic family scrawl in the older texts. Watching him work had become its own distraction. When he was thinking, stillness took him over in layers: pen paused, eyes gone slightly unfocused, body quiet without going rigid. Then the pen moved again and whatever problem had been under consideration was already solved.

It had been years since she'd worked beside someone who could keep up like this. Harry was brilliant in ways that had nothing to do with archival research. Ron had always been better at strategy than anyone gave him credit for and had never cared about the puzzle for its own sake. Her colleagues at the Ministry were competent. A few were good.

Malfoy was good. Fast too, and precise, and unusually willing to be wrong. That last quality mattered more than brilliance. Twice this week he'd caught things she'd missed. Three times she'd corrected him. Neither of them pretended the score didn't matter.

"There's a third instance," she said, pulling another volume from the stack near her elbow. "Seventeen twelve. Different author. Same usage."

Without looking up, he held out his hand for the book. The handoff left an inch of air between his skin and hers. A week ago that would have been nothing. Now it wasn't.

They stayed in the library until the light failed. As darkness gathered outside, the lamps brightened so gradually that the room never seemed to change, not until she glanced up and found black glass at the windows and Malfoy still across from her as if neither of them had anywhere else to be.

Which, she supposed, they didn't.

"The Floo in the east parlor has been unreliable," he said. His eyes stayed on the page. "Use the study fireplace if you need the Ministry."

"Thanks."

He nodded, already back in the text.

The translation chain kept lengthening under her hand. Each text opened three more. The pattern emerging from the cross-references felt close enough to touch now, not visible yet but waiting under the surface.

"Are you hungry?" he asked.

Only then did she check. "Yeah. Probably."

"The kitchen puts out supper at seven." He glanced at the wall clock. "It's twenty past."

"I can eat late."

"You could also eat on time."

Nothing in his tone made it an order. That almost made it worse. Hunger had caught up with her. It was past seven. The text would still exist in the morning.

"Fine."

He waited until her book closed before he stood. She noticed that and declined to examine it.

It happened on the walk back to the guest wing.

Tonight the Manor gave her a straight corridor from the kitchen end of the house to her room, portraits lining the walls with the bored silence of people who had given up trying to speak to her. The Tethys Working occupied most of her attention. So did the structural-hemorrhage distinction. Malfoy did not. Even so, halfway down the corridor she became aware that he was on the third floor.

She stopped.

It was not a guess. It was not inference. His position in the house arrived in her mind with the same clean certainty as the weight of her wand in her pocket. Third floor, east side. His rooms.

Under that knowledge sat a second sensation: a dull ache in her left forearm, separate from the scar and wrong in a different way. Scar pain had a hot, scratchy texture, like a burn that had healed badly and never fully finished. This was deeper. Borrowed. A slow pulse that belonged elsewhere and had somehow found its way into her arm.

Her palm flattened over the spot through her sleeve. Pressure changed nothing. The ache wasn't in skin or muscle. It sat inside the diagnostic trace, maybe, in the residue left by three examinations and too much sustained contact with curse magic. She had read about that. Cursed-object examiners sometimes developed temporary trace bonds: sensory artifacts, usually minor, usually brief. Standard recommendation: reduce contact frequency and duration.

A harder press sharpened it for an instant into something recognizably his. Restless magic. The Mark trying to move through a channel that no longer went anywhere.

By the time the sensation fell back again, she was standing in a quiet corridor with her hand clamped around her own forearm and her breathing too fast. One lamp at the far end flickered, recovered, and left the shadows in a slightly different arrangement.

Diagnostic residue. That was the explanation available to her, so it became the explanation she used. Sustained spell contact with an unusually active dark artifact had produced an expected side effect. It would degrade. If it didn't, she could alter the protocol.

Inside her room, the literature argued with the evidence. Belby's monograph and the St. Mungo's examiner protocols described sensory aftereffects as minor, transient, and non-directional. A faint echo of a curse's quality. Hours, sometimes days. What she had felt had a location. It had pointed to him through walls. Either the Mark's reactivation behaved unlike anything in the literature, or the literature had no category for what had just happened.

She chose the first model because the first model let her keep working.

Notes came out. For two more hours, she worked at the desk under the window. At some point the ache returned, faint as a radio signal at the edge of reach. At some point it told her, without mechanism and without permission, that he was still awake.

By eleven the lamp was out. Midnight found her flat on her back in the dark, fingertips resting against the inside of her left forearm, feeling something that was not hers pulse beside the scar that was.

If it persisted, she would tell him. Clinically relevant data belonged in the protocol. Keeping it to herself would compromise the work.

The mattress beneath her was better than the one in London. Lavender and cedar preservation charms clung to the sheets. Tomorrow, she told herself.

That lie lasted less than a minute.

Not telling him was not a decision so much as a fact that had already settled inside her. The information felt private in a way that had nothing to do with science and everything to do with the intimacy of it. She could feel him in his own rooms. She could track the Mark's ache as it thinned toward whatever counted as rest in cursed tissue. Being inside the peripheral weather of his body without his knowledge or consent felt like a trespass. Handing that trespass over out loud felt worse.

It was residue. It would fade.

In the dark, with the ache still there, she let it keep her company and refused to call that wanting.

He was looking at her.

Not the page. Not the text. Her.

For weeks she had been pretending not to see that expression when it flashed across his face and vanished again: the unguarded thing, neither dry nor careful nor precise, only want.

Any sensible version of herself should have leaned back. A comment about the notation would have done it. So would a remark about the hour, the maps, the tea, anything that could have rebuilt the distance they'd been maintaining like a bandage over a wound neither of them wanted to inspect. She knew what the Manor did. She knew what the connection between them had become. A few weeks of concealed knowledge sat behind her eyes telling her that every moment of closeness was suspect, that the warmth might be architecture rather than desire, that the pull between them might be amplification rather than the simple fact of wanting someone.

Still, she didn't lean back.

"What?"

"I'm not—" He stopped. Of course he stopped. Self-correction lived in him too, except his always pulled toward less. There was nowhere to retreat now, though. Her face was inches from his. The page lay between them, ignored by both of them. "Never mind."

For a second neither of them breathed. Under her palm his skin felt warm and dry with chalk dust from the preservation charms. Then his mouth was on hers properly, clumsy still, honestly clumsy, nose knocking hers, tension hard in his jaw when her free hand rose to him without conscious decision.

She had imagined composure. She had imagined his precision extending here too. It didn't. Her free hand caught in the front of his shirt. Through the cotton his heart hammered.

At the same moment the resonance along the Mark went taut.

Not louder. Total. His heartbeat seemed to strike inside her own chest. Adrenaline moved through him and some part of her answered as if it were hers. Under his sleeve the Mark did something strange, not pain, not for either of them, but a live vibrating register that matched her pulse so exactly it made her dizzy. It was the most intimate thing she had ever felt. It was also the thing she trusted least.

*Is this me?* The thought split the surface of the moment. *Is this me or is this the house?*

That wasn't an answer. It was refusal. His hand slid from her jaw to the back of her neck and into her hair, which had come loose from its tie hours ago. Fingers tightened there. Her scalp tugged. The doubt was just as specific: the house was warm around them, the Mark alive between them, and she could not separate what she felt from what the Manor might be intensifying.

"Don't." She did not know whether she meant the question or the whole conversation waiting behind it. "Don't. Not yet."

Not her first name. The surname they had kept between them like the last intact formality. With his mouth at her skin it sounded like no distance at all.

"Right." His hand went through his hair and left it standing up wrong. For a moment he looked younger, or maybe just less assembled.

"Tomorrow."

They stood. The library answered with a small settling sound from the wood and stone around them, like the house taking a breath.

"Goodnight."

Tonight the turns arrived almost before she reached them. The house could tell she was tired, apparently, or wanted her back in bed as quickly as possible. The thought should have made her furious. Instead it landed with a sickening trace of gratitude.

No chance of sleep, though. Her lips still felt bruised from him. One hand carried the ghost of his hand inside it. With every step away from the library, the last hour sharpened instead of fading.

She could say it.

She would not say it.

More selfish than she wanted to admit, she could not make herself be the person who put those walls back up.

One floor up, the boy she'd kissed lay awake in his own room. Between them sat stone, house, secrecy. All three distances measured exactly the same.

She did not sleep for a long time.

The archive had been letting him in for three days.
