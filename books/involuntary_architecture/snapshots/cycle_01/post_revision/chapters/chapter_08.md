# Chapter 8

By half three the frost still clung to the east window, and Granger wanted the examination done before the light failed.

For three days she'd been rebuilding the diagnostic array, checking her notes against the archive texts, talking herself toward a calibration she didn't entirely trust. Surface readings told them how the Mark was misfiring now: damaged channels, cyclical spikes, cursed magic trying to run through routes that no longer connected to anything. What she wanted today was older than that. If the original casting still sat intact under the later damage, it might be holding more than structure.

"Residue from the bind itself," she'd said over tea that morning, eyes on her notes instead of on him. "Not just the spell's shape. If the first layer kept any lived trace, pressure, sequence, sensory carryover, I need to know before I keep putting my hands into it blind."

He'd said fine. He'd been saying fine for weeks.

They used the same small study off the library corridor, its desk already lit by sconces because a December afternoon at the Manor went dark early.

Across from her, Draco rolled his left sleeve to the elbow.

The first weeks had made that motion feel like exposure. Now it was routine: button, fold, forearm on the desk. Routine did not make the skull and serpent less ugly. It only made the sight of it survivable.

Cool fingers bracketed his wrist. Her right palm settled over the Mark.

"I'm going past the surface," she said. "The first layers should feel ordinary. If the older magic wakes up, it may not."

"And you'll know what that feels like?"

"No." A breath. "That's why you're telling me the second it changes."

"My pulse is excellent."

"I've felt your pulse lie to me before."

The first pass was familiar. Magic moved over the Mark in a controlled wash, reading density, contour, the distribution of dark magic through skin and muscle. Weeks ago he'd learned to sit still and let his mind go somewhere dull.

Today dull would not hold. Frost feathered the window in branching white. A cracked place in the frame admitted enough cold to sting the knuckles of his free hand. He concentrated on that.

Under her palm the second layer pressed deeper. The ache sharpened from background throb to local pain. The serpent gave one involuntary twitch under the skin.

A pause, then her voice, low and mostly to herself. "Surface decay where we expected. Lower channels still active. There. There's another layer under the curse matrix. Packed tight."

His fingers tightened on the desk.

"It feels old," she said. "Older than the reactivation. If that's original-casting residue..." The words thinned. "All right. I'm going in carefully."

Careful bought her less than a second.

The change started at the point where her skin touched his and dropped through the Mark like a trapdoor giving way. Pressure drove inward, then kicked back out. It felt like the Mark splitting open along an old seam.

"Granger."

"I know."

The Mark convulsed.

Dark magic rolled through his forearm, shoulder, ribs, and the room tore loose.

---

He was sixteen.

He was all the way down in it. The body came first: too much wrist bone, shoulders not finished broadening, breath trapped high in his chest. Stone under his shoes. A room he knew before he named it. His father's study.

No longer his father's.

Heavy curtains shut out a July evening. The desk was stripped to bare oak, one candle, a folded length of black cloth. Blank frames marked the far wall where portraits used to be. Even the painted Malfoys had abandoned the room.

The air smelled of leather, cold stone, overheated magic, and fear gone sour. His sweat. Lucius's. Too much of it for either of them to deny.

A hand gripped his shoulder.

His father's thumb dug into the muscle above the collarbone hard enough to hurt. The hand shook anyway. Sixteen-year-old Draco felt only the command in it: stand still. The older man trapped inside the memory recognized the terror.

His left arm lay across the desk.

School shirt. White cuff. Sleeve shoved to the elbow because no one had told him he might want different clothes for this. Pale inner forearm bare in the candlelight. Veins showing. A boy's arm. Unmarked.

Across the desk stood the Dark Lord.

The face would never stay whole in memory. White skin. Red eyes. Mouth like a cut in wax. Enough.

"Your father assures me you're ready."

Nothing came out when Draco tried to answer.

"He is, my Lord," Lucius said behind him, steady in the voice, not in the hand.

Then the wand touched the inside of his arm.

The cold bit first, a corpse-chill pressed exactly where the skin was thinnest. Magic crowded the point of contact before the words were finished. He never kept the incantation. The memory had stored the mouth moving, not the sound.

Then burning.

It punched through skin and kept going, down muscle, through bone, into the channels his own magic used, clearing room for foreign magic by force. His body tried to wrench away. Lucius's grip multiplied. One hand at his upper arm, one against his back, pinning him to the desk while the wand carved the Mark into him.

He heard the noise he made: raw, shocked, barely human.

Vision went white. Grey after that. Knees failed. Stone tipped sideways. The last clear sensation was the wand still writing its claim into the arm that had been bare that morning, and then even that went.

---

The study returned in fragments: desk, chair, candlelight, frost at the window. December. Twenty-five. Her hand gone.

Hermione was pressed back in her chair, both palms lifted as if the skin of him might still burn. Color had left her face completely. Her mouth worked once before sound came.

"Oh God."

The resonance between them was no longer a background thrum. It had blown wide. Shock hit him through it before he had his own breathing under control. Her body was still carrying his memory: the hand on his shoulder, the heat in his arm, the age of him.

He yanked his arm off the desk. The Mark looked unchanged, but the skin around it had flushed dark and the veins stood up under the surface.

Three tries for the cuff button. On the fourth he got it through.

"Malfoy," she said.

"Don't."

The word came out flatter than he'd meant.

Silence closed hard around them. Candle flame. Their breathing. Nothing else.

At last her hands dropped to the table. "I need to say this accurately."

A humorless breath left him. "Do you."

"Yes." Red rimmed her eyes, but they were dry. "What came through clearly was the room, the wand, your father's hand, your age, the pain. I know you lost consciousness. I don't know every edge of it. If I say anything beyond that, if I talk about what your father meant or what you meant, that's me inferring from pressure and context. Not the spell."

Jaw tight, he looked at the window instead of at her.

"Sixteen," he said before she could ask. "I'd just turned. It was July."

Her gaze dropped to his covered forearm, then back up. "Your father held you."

"He held me still."

"Malfoy."

"I walked into the room." Breath came too short. "I put my arm out. Whatever else he did, don't turn me into something simpler because you were there for ten minutes."

"I wasn't trying to."

"But you could."

The answer stalled in her mouth. When it came, it was quieter. "You were sixteen."

He had no reply that did not sound thin. Stone scraped under the chair as he stood and went to the window. Frost blurred the lawn into blankness.

Behind him the vocabulary assembled in pieces. Imprint. Residue. Transmission. The words did nothing. A boy had been held down against a desk.

"We stop here," she said.

"Yes."

Neither of them moved for a moment. Then he left the study before either of them could say more and make it worse.

---

Outside his rooms he put his forehead against the corridor wall and stayed there until the stone numbed the bridge of his nose.

Pain could have remained abstract. Plenty of people knew about the pain. What she'd got was worse: the exact age of him, the white school shirt, Lucius's hand shaking at his shoulder, the noise he made when the curse burned through bone and magic alike. No one had ever had that version of him. No one.

Cold water across his face did nothing useful. The towel smelled of lavender because every towel in the Manor smelled of lavender, and the ordinary domesticity of that was almost insulting.

By the time he went back downstairs, the library fire had been built up.

Hermione sat in her usual chair with the Marchetti open on her lap. Shoes off by the hearth. She stared at the same page long enough to admit she wasn't reading.

The chair opposite creaked when he sat down. A book came off the nearest stack, opened in his hands, and stayed unread.

Pages turned. Fire shifted. Neither of them was pretending this was work.

At some point she said, "Tea?"

Ordinary as weather. It nearly undid him.

"Please."

At the side table she set the kettle going with her wand, measured leaves into the pot, and brought his cup first. The cups never matched. Tonight that felt useful.

Warmth reached his hands before the smell did.

"I'm sorry you had to feel that," he said.

Across from him she sat with her own cup braced in both hands. "I'm not. Sorry, I mean. For knowing."

His eyes lifted to hers.

"I'm sorry it happened to you," she said. "I'm not sorry I know. Someone should've."

No answer came. He drank because the tea gave his hands work, because the cup was hot, because speaking felt impossible.

Research stayed open on the table between them, untouched. Instead of leaving, he remained in the chair and let the fire settle lower while she sat across from him with her tea and her unread book and the knowledge that could not be taken back.
