# Chapter 14

The texts were in a script he had never seen his ancestors use.

Draco sat with the folio open across his knees — too large for the desk, leather-bound in something that was not leather, the pages thick as card stock and oddly warm to the touch. The notation ran in cramped vertical columns, a hybrid of runic shorthand and what looked like architectural drafting symbols, measurements embedded in the text itself as though the writer couldn't separate the language from the thing it described. He could pick out fragments. Family names. Compass bearings. The word *fundament* recurring in what might have been Latin or might have been an older cousin of it, something that predated the standardization of magical script by centuries.

He could not read it. Not alone.

Granger sat across from him at the long table in the gated archive, a room that had opened that morning with what he could only describe as willingness. No resistance, no testing. The door had been there when they came down to the library together after breakfast, wide and unhesitating, as if the house had been waiting for them and was glad they'd arrived.

She had noticed. He'd seen her notice — the brief pause, the way her eyes moved to the doorframe and then away, cataloguing without commenting. She did that more now. Registered things about the Manor and kept her observations internal, where before she would have spoken them aloud as data. He didn't know what had changed. He knew only that something had, during those few days she'd been gone, and that the version of her who'd returned was different in ways he couldn't fully map. Present, but guarded. Close, but holding something back.

He'd stopped trying to decode her withdrawal. The effort had nearly wrecked him the first time — three days of assuming he'd done something wrong, defaulting to his oldest conviction that he'd been found wanting in some essential way. When she'd left for London he'd been certain she wasn't coming back, and the Manor had confirmed it, going cold and sullen, the corridors stretching between rooms like the house was pulling away from itself. He'd eaten alone and the dining room had felt like a punishment.

Then she'd returned. The evening of the third day, the Floo flaring green in the entrance hall, and the house had responded before he had — warmth flooding the ground floor, the library doors swinging open, a corridor shortening between the entrance and the wing where she'd been sleeping. He'd come around the corner and found her standing in the hall with her bag over one shoulder and an expression he couldn't read. She'd said, "The Floo's getting worse. It took three tries," and he'd said, "The archive hasn't opened since you left," which was true and which was not the thing he'd meant to say.

That had been four days ago. They'd fallen back into the work with an ease that should have unsettled him but didn't — or did, but in a way he'd grown tired of fighting. The gated archive was behaving differently now. Where before it had required their proximity as a condition, testing them, closing if one stepped out, now it seemed to welcome their sustained presence. The room was stable. The light from the high windows held steady, a thick winter afternoon gold that shouldn't have been possible given how dark it was outside. The Manor's contribution to their working conditions was visible and generous, and Draco was aware of it the way you're aware of someone pouring your drink without being asked — a gesture you couldn't refuse without making it into something.

"This column here," Granger said, not looking up. She had a quill tucked behind her ear and another in her hand and a third lying across the open text she'd been cross-referencing. Her hair was piled on her head in something that had started the morning as a knot and was now a structural event, held together by what appeared to be a pencil and determination. "The notation shifts. It's not the same script as the first three pages."

He moved his chair closer. Not a decision, exactly — the table was wide and the text was small and he needed to see what she was pointing at. But he registered the proximity. The sleeve of her jumper against the sleeve of his shirt. Her hand on the page, index finger resting beneath a line of text, and her nails were short and unvarnished and there was an ink stain on the side of her middle finger that she'd had since breakfast.

"Different hand," he said, looking at the script. "The earlier pages — that's a formal record. Someone trained. This is rougher."

"Rougher how?"

He studied it. The strokes were thicker, less uniform, the vertical alignment wavering in places as if the writer had been working quickly or in poor light. The runic elements were simplified, almost shorthand, and the architectural symbols were mixed in more freely, less separated from the text.

"Field notes," he said. "Someone was out at the site. These aren't library records, they're — this was written while the work was being done."

Granger looked at him. He could feel her attention shift, the way it always did when he said something that opened a path she hadn't seen. Her pupils were slightly dilated from hours of close reading in dim light. The archive had windows but they were narrow and the Manor's golden warmth, generous as it was, did not extend to adequate reading lamps. They'd been working by wand-light and the candles she'd found in a box under the second shelf, and the effect was something closer to a medieval scriptorium than a research facility.

"Field notes from the original construction," she said. "That would be — when was this? Fifteenth century?"

"Fourteen-thirties. The first Malfoy to build on this site was Armand's great-grandson, Braccus. The Manor itself is older in pieces, but the foundational magic — the anchoring work — that was his project."

"And he wrote in this?"

"I don't think so. Braccus was — from what my father told me, he was the architect but not the builder. He designed the anchoring lattice but the actual field construction was done by specialists. Families who knew the land magic, the ley-line work. Some of them predated the Malfoys in England." He paused. He was reaching into knowledge he hadn't accessed in years, things Lucius had told him during long evenings in the study that had been lessons disguised as family history, or family history disguised as lessons. "My father called them the lineal artisans. They're in the family records somewhere but I never — I didn't pay enough attention."

The admission cost him something small. It always did, the moments when his father's teaching surfaced and he had to acknowledge both that Lucius had given him something valuable and that Lucius had given him everything else too.

"So these field notes are from the actual construction team," Granger said, her voice picking up speed the way it did when she was building a framework. "The people who laid the anchoring points. And the notation is different because it's a working language, not a formal one — a technical shorthand shared among practitioners."

"Which is why neither of us can read it alone."

She was already nodding. "Your family knowledge gives us the referents — the names, the geography, the genealogy. I can work the theoretical frame, the magical-structural principles. But this script is a trade language. It would have been passed from master to apprentice. It wouldn't be in any standard reference because it was never standardized."

"We'd have to decode it from context."

"We'd have to decode it from context." She repeated it not because she hadn't heard him but because she was already thinking past what he'd said, the repetition a placeholder while her mind ran. He'd learned this about her in the weeks they'd been working together — the way she processed through speech, using his sentences as handholds while she climbed toward something she could see and he couldn't.

He should have resented it. The old Draco would have. Instead he found himself watching the way her brow creased when she was concentrating, the slight movement of her lips as she read silently, the way she'd push her hair off her face with the back of her wrist because her fingers were always occupied — quill, page, wand, pencil. She was messy when she worked. Not disorganized — the opposite, ferociously systematic — but physically messy, surrounded by stacks and lists and cross-references, ink on her hands and a smudge on her chin she didn't know about. The controlled, precise woman who'd arrived at his door weeks ago in a professional cloak with her hair contained and her sleeves down was not the person sitting across from him. This was someone in a jumper with the sleeves shoved up, her shoes kicked off under the table, a woman who'd eaten half a biscuit and left the other half balanced on the spine of a fourteenth-century manuscript without apparent concern for the archival implications.

He realized he was staring and didn't stop. That was the difference. Not that he was watching her — he'd been watching her for weeks, in the way you watch a fire or a storm, something happening in your peripheral vision that you kept turning toward — but that he didn't make himself stop. He let himself look at the ink stain and the pencil in her hair and the line of her throat where the jumper's neckline had gone crooked, baring a collarbone and the hollow beneath it.

The Mark ached. Low, persistent, the magic in it restless in a way that had nothing to do with Voldemort and everything to do with the woman two feet from him. The ache had changed since her sustained diagnostic contact — it was responsive now, attuned to her presence, and when she was close it didn't exactly hurt less but hurt differently, a pull rather than a burn. He'd stopped trying to separate the Mark's behavior from his own.

"Here," Granger said, pulling a page toward her. "Look at this. These aren't just construction notes. These are maintenance records."

He leaned in. Her index finger traced a column of symbols — repetitive, cyclical, the same cluster recurring at what looked like regular intervals.

"Inspection schedules?" he said.

"Assessment schedules. They were checking the anchoring points. Regularly. Over — I think this is a span of decades. Look, the notation for the year changes here, and here, and the readings change with it." She flipped back two pages, cross-referencing. "The anchoring infrastructure wasn't a one-time construction. It required ongoing maintenance. Sustained attention. And the readings — if I'm interpreting this right — the readings fluctuated based on the strength of the connection between the Manor and the land."

"Connection meaning the bond."

"Connection meaning whatever magical relationship tied this house to the anchoring lattice. The Manor wasn't just built on the ley-lines. It was part of them. The house itself was a node in the network."

The implication settled between them. He could see her working through it, the rapid-fire analytical process she didn't bother to hide from him anymore, and he could feel when the pieces connected because her hand tightened on the page and she went very still.

"The decay," she said. "The magical decay we're seeing across Britain. If the Manor was a node — if the Malfoy estate was part of the original anchoring infrastructure — then the damage isn't just to distant ley-line connections. The damage is here. To this house. To the magic that sustains it."

"And the house knows it."

They looked at each other. The gated archive hummed around them, the light golden and steady, the walls close in a way that felt sheltering rather than confining. Outside, December was doing its worst — the grounds frozen, the sky dark by four, the wind audible even through stone. In here, the air was warm and still and smelled of old paper and candle wax and, faintly, of her — something clean and ordinary, soap or shampoo, the domestic smell of a person who'd been living in your house long enough to have her own scent settled into the furniture.

"That's why the house is doing what it's doing," Granger said, and he could hear the caution in her voice, the careful approach to territory she wasn't willing to name directly. "The Manor's behavior — the rooms opening, the archive, the —" She gestured between them, a small, unfinished motion. "It's not arbitrary. The house is trying to repair its own infrastructure. It needs the bond between its inhabitants to be strong because the bond is part of what sustains the anchoring magic."

"So we're load-bearing."

She laughed. Short, startled, genuine — the kind that escaped before she could decide whether she wanted to. "That's one way to put it."

"The house needs us to — what? Work well together? Be in the same room? Be —" He stopped before the word. He was doing it again, starting a sentence that would arrive somewhere honest and pulling back before it got there. The gap between what he'd almost said and what he actually said was wide enough to walk through, and he suspected she could see it.

"I think it needs the resonance," she said, steadier now. "The sustained magical interaction between people who are — who have a strong enough connection to feed the anchoring network. The gated archive, the way the house responds to our proximity — it's not a test, or not only a test. It's a mechanism. The house is channeling our combined magical output through its own infrastructure."

"Using us as a battery."

"Using our collaboration as a power source." She corrected him reflexively, the precision instinctive, and then caught herself and made a face that was almost an apology. "Sorry, I'm — it matters, the distinction. It's not about raw magical power. It's about the quality of the interaction. The sustained, intentional, collaborative engagement with the material. When we're working well together, the archive is stable. When we're not —"

"It collapses. I know. I was here when you weren't."

The sentence landed harder than he'd intended. He saw it hit — a small flinch, quickly controlled, and then the careful evenness of her expression settling over whatever she'd felt.

"I know you were," she said.

Silence. The candles flickered. The archive held around them, steady, patient, as if it had all the time in the world and was willing to wait while they figured out what they were to each other inside its walls.

Draco looked down at the folio. The field notes from six centuries ago, the handwriting of artisans who had built the magical infrastructure that sustained an entire country, and it was sitting in his lap because his family had built this house and her mind could read what his eyes could only see. They were, between them, the only two people alive who could decode this. The thought was staggering and he didn't let himself stagger at it. He sat with it instead, turning it over, and what he found underneath was not the intellectual satisfaction he'd expected but something closer to vertigo. They were indispensable to each other. The word sat wrong in his mind, too large and too precise simultaneously.

"We should keep going," he said.

"Yeah." She pulled a fresh sheet of parchment toward her, already organizing, already systematic. "If these are maintenance records, there should be a baseline — a healthy reading, before whatever damage occurred. If we can find that baseline, we can start mapping the degradation. Compare it to the current decay patterns I've been documenting."

They worked. The afternoon darkened outside the narrow windows and the candles burned lower and neither of them moved to light new ones, adjusting instead by mutual, unspoken agreement to the dimming light, leaning closer over the texts, their heads nearly touching over a passage that required both of their attention.

The script began to yield. Not quickly — the trade notation was dense, contextual, full of abbreviations that assumed knowledge neither of them had — but in fragments, in pieces that assembled when he could identify a place name and she could connect it to a theoretical principle. A Malfoy ancestor's shorthand for a geographic feature, cross-referenced against her knowledge of ley-line mapping. A rune cluster he recognized from his father's study, matched to a structural spell she'd read about in a text that no longer existed in any other archive. Each breakthrough required both of them, his knowledge and hers, and the process of decoding was slow and absorbing and — he admitted this only to himself, in the deep interior where no one was listening — more satisfying than anything he'd done in years.

"Draco Malfoy, sixteenth of his name," he murmured, tracing a genealogical marker in the margin. "Not my direct ancestor. A cousin line. They split after Braccus — one branch stayed at the original estate in Normandy, the other came here."

"And the cousin was involved in the construction?"

"He was the site supervisor, I think. The person mediating between the lineal artisans and the family. Look — here's his notation mixed with theirs. He's translating between registers."

Granger leaned across the table to see, and her shoulder pressed against his. She didn't move away. He didn't move away. The contact was warm and unremarkable and he was ferociously aware of every point where her body met his — the shoulder, the arm, the way her elbow rested against his forearm when she reached for the page.

"If the sixteenth Draco was mediating," she said, her voice thoughtful, unaware of or indifferent to their proximity, "then the full notation might be a creole. A hybrid of the family's formal script and the artisans' trade language. That would explain why we can partially read both registers but neither is complete on its own."

"A creole."

"A mixed language. It forms when two speech communities work together long enough that their vocabularies merge. It wouldn't be codified anywhere because it only existed in practice, between these specific people, during this specific project."

"So the only way to reconstruct it —"

"Is to do what they did. Put the two knowledge bases together and let the hybrid emerge."

She turned her head and their faces were very close. Her eyes were brown, lighter near the iris, and he could see the fine lines at their corners that hadn't been there when she was eighteen. The candlelight was orange on her skin and he could count the freckles on the bridge of her nose if he wanted to, and he wanted to, and he didn't count them. He held still and she held still and neither of them breathed for a beat too long and then she looked down at the text and said, "Here. This symbol — I've seen it before. Third-century anchoring theory, Ptolemaic. It means *root* or *source*. And this one next to it, can you —?"

"It's a property marker. The Malfoy crest, simplified. Braccus's branch used a variant without the serpent."

"So *root-of-Malfoy*. The anchoring point's identity was tied to the family."

"The family was the anchor."

Another silence. The house breathed around them — he could feel it, the way he'd always been able to feel it, the Manor's awareness like a pressure in the walls. But it was different now. Warmer. The house was not testing them or pushing them together or engineering proximity. It was simply present and content, the way a house feels when its inhabitants are doing the thing they are supposed to be doing, and the contentment was its own kind of seduction because it showed him what this could be. Not the fraught, careful negotiation that had defined their weeks together, but something sustained and natural and his.

He went to get more candles. The archive held while he was gone, which was new — the room had never been stable without both of them present before. He found the candles in the library's supply closet, fat ivory pillars that smelled faintly of beeswax, and when he returned she'd reorganized the desk in his absence, making space for his folio next to hers, arranging the reference texts in a shared arc between their two chairs. A working space designed for two. She'd done it without thinking, probably — the organizational instinct was as natural to her as breathing — but the effect was intimate in a way that knocked something loose in his chest. She'd made room for him.

He set the candles down and lit them with his wand and the archive brightened, the golden light expanding, and she looked up from the text with an expression that was entirely unguarded — slightly dazed from the close work, her eyes adjusting, a question she hadn't finished forming dying on her lips when she saw him looking at her.

"What?" she said.

"Nothing." He sat down. Their chairs were closer now, angled toward the shared workspace she'd created. When he settled into his, their knees were an inch apart under the table.

"You were looking at me."

"You were looking at the fifteenth-century drainage notation. I was looking at the person looking at the drainage notation. These are different activities."

She almost smiled. The almost was the thing — not the smile itself but the effort of suppressing it, the way her mouth tightened against the impulse and her eyes gave it away anyway. He liked making her almost-smile. He liked that she tried not to and failed. He liked the specific quality of the failure — not a surrender but a concession, as if she was allowing herself this one small thing and would reevaluate later whether it had been a mistake.

They worked through the afternoon and into the evening. The folio yielded more as they developed a shared vocabulary for the hybrid notation — not a complete translation but a working one, built from his fragments and hers, tested and refined by consensus. They argued about a symbol that could have been a direction marker or a temporal reference and the argument went on for twenty minutes and was, Draco realized with a clarity that startled him, the most engaged he'd been in anything since — since he couldn't remember. Since school, maybe. Since before the Mark, before the war reduced everything to survival and its aftermath.

She was right about the symbol, ultimately. It was temporal, not directional — a marker for the seasonal cycle that affected ley-line flow. He conceded the point and she accepted the concession without gloating, which was its own form of generosity, and then she said, "But your instinct about directionality wasn't wrong. Look at this passage — the artisans were mapping flow direction as a function of the season. It's both."

"It's both," he repeated, and the sentence had a weight he hadn't put in it.

Evening deepened. The windows were black now, the December dark absolute outside the Manor's walls. Inside, the archive was a pool of warm light and old paper and the specific domestic comfort of two people who'd been working together all day — the empty teacups on the floor beside his chair, the crumbs from the biscuits she'd brought down from the kitchen, the companionable disorder of shared labor. He could feel the Mark settling into something like quiet, the restless ache easing the way it always did when she was near and their interaction was good, the dark magic in his arm responding to her presence as if her proximity were a kind of medicine, which maybe it was. The resonance between them was a low hum, present and undemanding.

He watched her. He let himself watch her.

She was bent over a passage they'd cracked an hour ago, re-reading it now with the benefit of the notational key they'd been building together. She'd taken the pencil out of her hair at some point and the whole structure had collapsed, dark curls falling around her face, and she kept shoving them back with an impatient hand that achieved nothing. She was mouthing the translation as she worked it, her lips forming words in a language that no one had spoken in six hundred years, a language that existed only in the space between his knowledge and hers. Her feet were tucked under her in the chair. One sock had a hole in the toe.

The thought arrived not as a decision but as a fact, the way gravity arrives when you step off a ledge: he was in love with her. Not falling. Not beginning to. Was. The tense was wrong — he'd been in love with her for weeks, probably, the way you're in a river before you notice you're wet, the way weather changes while you're looking at something else. The realization was not a dramatic upheaval. It was more like coming home to a house you'd been living in without knowing the address, and finding that you recognized the furniture.

He was in love with Hermione Granger in his family's archive, surrounded by texts that proved his ancestors had built the magical infrastructure of Britain, while outside the windows his country's magic was dying and inside his left arm the Dark Lord's curse was quieter than it had been in years because she was close enough to touch.

The facts of this were absurd. He could hear Theo's voice in his head, the dry precision: *A Malfoy in love with a Muggle-born in the cursed family library while the world ends. Somehow you've managed to make catastrophe romantic.* And Draco would have said something cutting back, something about Theo's own talent for constructing narratives that kept the actual feeling at arm's length, but the hypothetical Theo was right. It was absurd. And he didn't care, which was the truly dangerous part — not the feeling itself but the collapse of the architecture he'd built to prevent it.

He'd spent years constructing the conditions of not-wanting. After the war, after the trial, after the slow understanding that his life would be lived in the diminished space his name allowed — he'd made himself small. Careful. He'd learned to need nothing from anyone because need was leverage, and he'd given the world enough leverage already. The Manor was too large and he let it be too large. He ate alone and he worked alone and his friendships were limited to the people who could tolerate his precision and his silences, which meant Theo and his mother and, distantly, the occasional colleague who hadn't known him before the Mark.

He'd built this. Deliberately. A life where no one could hurt him because no one was close enough to reach, and the life had worked — not well, not happily, but it had worked, which was a lower bar than happiness but a bar he could meet. The Mark hurt and the house was too quiet and the world looked at him and saw the Mark and that was survivable because he expected nothing else.

And now she was sitting two feet from him with a hole in her sock and ink on her chin and he wanted everything. Not just her — the whole of it. The work, the house alive and warm instead of cold and watchful, the shared meals and the arguments about notation and the sight of her asleep in his library with a book as a pillow. He wanted to bring her tea in the morning without the careful distance that made the gesture safe. He wanted to brush the hair out of her face and feel her lean into his hand instead of away from it. He wanted to know what she looked like when she wasn't careful, when the precision dropped and the competence stopped being armor and she was just a woman in his house who was glad to be there.

He wanted to touch her. Not the clinical contact of the Mark examinations, where her fingers on his forearm were professional and his stillness was a kind of holding his breath. He wanted the ordinary touching of someone who belonged in your space — a hand on her back as she passed, his fingers in her hair, the thoughtless contact of a shoulder or a hip that two people develop when proximity is not a negotiation but a condition.

He wanted this and the wanting was the most dangerous thing he'd done since the war.

"Malfoy."

He looked up. She was watching him. The text was forgotten in her lap, or not forgotten but released — her hands were resting on the folio's edges and she was sitting very still, the way she sat when she was paying attention to something that wasn't the work. Her expression was — he didn't have a word for it. Open, careful, warm, suspicious, all at once, all overlapping.

"You're staring," she said.

"I know."

He didn't look away. That was the thing. The old Draco — the Draco of a week ago, a month ago, the careful precise diminished version who occupied this house and this name and this Mark — would have looked away and said something deflecting, a dry remark about the quality of the light or the aesthetic superiority of archaic drainage notation. He would have taken the moment and made it safe, and she would have let him, and they would have returned to the work and the distance would have held.

He didn't look away. He sat in his chair in his family's archive with the candles burning low and the Manor warm and steady around them and he looked at her and she looked at him and the distance between them was an inch of air and six centuries of notation and the entirety of what they hadn't said.

"You've got ink on your chin," he said.

She touched her chin. Wrong side. He reached out — not quickly, not slowly, at the speed of someone doing something ordinary — and brushed the smudge with his thumb. Her skin was warm. She went still under his hand, not a flinch but an attention, the way someone goes still when they want to be sure of what's happening before they respond to it.

His thumb rested on her chin. He didn't move it. She didn't move away.

"There," he said. His voice was rougher than he'd planned, which was fitting because he hadn't planned any of this.

"Thanks," she said. Her voice was careful.

He let his hand drop. The contact had lasted maybe three seconds and his pulse was doing something he'd have to think about later.

She looked at him for another long moment — assessing, or deciding, or struggling with something he couldn't see — and then she turned back to the folio and said, "I think this passage is the inspection schedule for the northeast anchoring point. Can you check the property markers? I need the geographic referent."

"Yes," he said. He picked up his quill. His hand was steady. He was aware of the steadiness as a separate phenomenon from the state of his interior, which was not steady at all but liquid and rearranged and, for the first time in years, not braced against anything.

They worked. Side by side, closer than the work required, in the warm archive while the Manor held around them like hands cupped around a flame. The house was showing him something — not forcing, not testing, but showing. This is what it looks like. This is what it could be. The light, the warmth, the woman, the work. The life where the corridors don't stretch and the rooms don't punish and someone is here and you are not alone.

He'd spent years learning not to want. He'd been good at it. The discipline of not-wanting had organized his entire post-war existence and he'd mistaken the discipline for the thing itself, confusing the absence of desire with the absence of need. She'd undone this — not deliberately, not through seduction or strategy or anything so dramatic. She'd undone it by being in his library with ink on her hands, by knowing things he didn't know and needing things he could give, by arguing about notation with an intensity that made his blood move, by having a hole in her sock.

The archive was the warmest room in the Manor. The fire they hadn't lit — no fireplace in this room, there never had been — should have left the space cold. Instead the walls themselves seemed to radiate, a gentle, encompassing heat that was the house's contribution to the evening. The beeswax candles gave the light a quality that was almost domestic, almost intimate, the kind of light you'd read by in a bedroom you shared with someone. The manuscripts were still open between them and their work was still the center of the space they occupied, but the work had changed valence. It was no longer only research. It was the thing they did together, the activity that made the house warm and the archive stable and the Mark quiet and the distance between them impossible to maintain.

It was late. He knew it was late because the candles had burned to their last inch and the winter silence outside was the deep, pressurized quiet of a world past midnight. They should stop. They should go to their separate rooms in separate wings and sleep separately and maintain the architecture of distance that had kept them both alive in here.

Granger closed her folio. She stretched — arms overhead, spine arching, the jumper riding up to show a strip of skin at her waist that he saw and filed and would think about later in the dark of his room. She rolled her neck and her hair moved across her shoulders and the pencil that should have fallen out hours ago finally clattered to the floor.

"I think we've got enough of the notation to build a proper key," she said. "Tomorrow we can start on the southern anchoring records. If the pattern holds, the early readings should show us the baseline before the degradation."

"Tomorrow."

"Mm." She stood. Her socked feet were silent on the archive floor. She gathered her notes — a stack of parchment covered in her cramped, precise handwriting, cross-referenced and indexed because she couldn't help herself — and tucked them under her arm. She looked around the archive, at the golden light and the warm walls and the candles dying on the desk, and her expression was the one he'd been learning to read over weeks of proximity: the one where she wanted to say something and was calculating whether saying it was worth the risk.

She didn't say it. She said, "Goodnight, Malfoy," and turned toward the door.

"Granger."

She stopped. Didn't turn. Waited.

He looked at her back — the jumper, the disastrous hair, the rolled parchment under her arm, the socks on the stone floor — and he thought about what he wanted to say and what he was allowed to say and the gap between the two, and then he stopped calculating.

"This is the best work I've ever done," he said. "I wanted you to know that."

She was quiet for a moment. Then she turned her head, not fully, just enough that he could see the line of her jaw and the corner of her mouth. "Yeah," she said. "Me too."

She left. The archive held. The room stayed warm and lit and open, as if it would have waited for them all night, and the Manor around it hummed with a contentment that was not his and not hers and not the house's alone but something shared between the three of them, a resonance that was as much about the work as the want, and Draco sat in the golden light with his family's texts open on his lap and the Dark Mark quiet in his arm and did not pretend, even to himself, that what he was feeling was anything other than what it was.

He was in love with her. The house knew. He suspected she knew. The only person who hadn't known, until today, was him, and that was because knowing required a kind of surrender he'd trained himself against, and the training had finally, mercifully, broken.

He blew out the last candle. The archive dimmed but didn't close. He walked through the library and the corridors were short and warm, the Manor guiding him with the specific gentleness it reserved for the moments when he was most himself, and he went to his room and lay on his bed and stared at the ceiling and felt the Mark settle into its quietest register, the dark magic resting, and felt the resonance telling him she was one floor down, awake, in the room that the house had placed close enough to hear if she called and far enough to make the distance a choice.

He didn't sleep. He lay in the dark and wanted her and didn't do anything about it and the wanting, for the first time, was not something to be survived but something to be inhabited, and the difference between those two was the whole of what had changed.
