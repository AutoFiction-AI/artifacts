# Chapter 12

The corridor to the library was twenty-three paces on Tuesday. By Thursday it was forty-one.

Draco counted because counting was something he could do — a small, precise task for the front of his mind while the rest of it paced through darker territory. Forty-one paces. The walls unchanged, the same grey stone, the same sconces casting their steady amber glow, but the distances between things had stretched, the house breathing out, making room in the way large animals make room: slowly, without explanation, as if the new arrangement had always been there. He did not want room. He wanted the corridors tight and warm the way they had been last week, when she would come around a corner with a translation in her hands and look up and say something about a fourteenth-century suffix and he would pretend he had not been counting the minutes since her last appearance.

The gated archive would not open. He went on Wednesday morning, early, before she arrived at the library — testing, because the room had always required both of them and he knew it, but he went anyway. The alcove in the library's south wall was blank. Stone, seamless, cool under his palm when he pressed it. No door. No seam. No suggestion that a door had ever existed in this wall. He stood there with his hand flat against the stone for longer than made sense, as if his own warmth could coax the house into yielding, and the stone gave nothing back.

He tried once more on Thursday. Same wall, same nothing. The Manor held the research they needed behind a condition he could not meet alone, and the condition was her, and she had gone somewhere he could not follow.

The temperature was the other thing. Not the ordinary cold of early winter in a stone house — he had grown up with that cold, knew its textures, the way it pooled in certain corridors and climbed your legs if you stood still too long. This was different. Specific. The heating charms still worked; fires still burned. But the warmth stopped at the edges of whatever room he occupied, pooling in a tight radius around the grate and fading before it reached the walls. Walking from the library to his rooms was like crossing a series of thermal borders — warm pocket, cold passage, warm pocket — each warm space smaller than it should have been, the house rationing its heat in a way that felt deliberate. Like a body conserving warmth when it thinks it's failing.

He did not mention any of this to her.

She was still in the Manor. She was still working. She arrived at the library at nine each morning and left by five, her notes squared and stacked at the edge of her table, her quill cleaned and laid in its leather case, the chair pushed in with the precise geometry of someone who was finished for the day rather than merely stepping away. The tidiness was new. A week ago she had left books open on the table, quills bleeding ink onto the blotting paper, her cardigan thrown over the chair back. A week ago her presence in the library had been porous, expansive — she took up space, spread outward, left traces. Now she was compact. Every sign of herself gathered and removed when she left, as if she were a guest who knew she might not be invited back.

Draco had gone through every conversation. Three days of them — not very many, which was the problem. The change had happened between one evening and the next morning, no conversation to mark the boundary, no argument, no miscalculated word he could identify and retract. They had been working on a sixteenth-century text about ambient field theory, and she had been close, her shoulder near his, and she had said something about the semantics of a word that meant both *to anchor* and *to bind*, and he had suggested a connection to an older Malfoy notation, and she had looked at him with the expression that was the best thing in his days — the one that meant her mind was moving fast and he was keeping up — and then the next morning she was brisk, professional, present only when the work demanded it and gone the moment it did not.

The obvious explanation was the drawing room. Not the confrontation itself — three weeks past, the rawness scabbed over, the things they had said settling into the silt of what they now knew about each other — but what it had opened up. Maybe the aftermath had been too gentle. Maybe the domestic register of the past week, the shared meals and the books left on his reading chair and the tea he made without asking, had alarmed her in its ease. Maybe she had looked at whatever was growing between them and decided the roots were in poisoned soil. She would not have been wrong. The soil in this house was very old and had held terrible things.

Or maybe it was simpler than that. He was a Malfoy and she was Hermione Granger and the distance between those facts was always going to win.

He matched her withdrawal with his own. He had been matching things his entire life — his father's formality, his mother's composure, the social temperature of every room he entered — and he matched her distance, and hated every word of the performance because it was a performance and because he was good at it and because being good at this particular thing meant he would never be the one to break first and say *what happened, why have you gone.*

He passed her in a corridor on Wednesday afternoon. She was carrying an armful of texts from the second-floor stacks, her chin propped on the top volume to keep it from sliding, and he was going nowhere in particular — just walking, learning the Manor's changed distances, because if the house was going to rearrange itself around him the least he could do was learn the new map. She nodded. He nodded. She said, "I'll be in the library until four," which was information rather than invitation, and he said, "Right," and she passed, and the corridor behind her was warmer than the corridor ahead of him, a trail of residual heat that the house left in her wake. He stood in it for a moment, the warmth thin and fading. Then it was gone, and the stone was just stone again.

The meals were the worst part. They had been eating together — not by arrangement but by the gradual collapse of the pretense that they were merely sharing a kitchen by coincidence. It had become a thing: the small dining room, Narcissa's dining room, the one meant for family rather than display. Sitting across from each other and talking about the research or about nothing. She ate slowly, read while she ate, sometimes looked up to say something about a passage and sometimes forgot to look up at all. He had learned to read the quality of her concentration by the angle of her head and the stillness of her fork, and all of this was gone now. She ate early or alone or somewhere he wasn't, and the dining room was too large for one person, and the silence in it was ordinary silence, and ordinary silence was worse than the other kind because the other kind at least meant the house was paying attention.

On Thursday night he sat at the table and ate what the elves had prepared — warm, adequate, he did not taste it — and the fire was built high and still the warmth failed at the walls, and the chair across from him was empty, and he considered pouring himself a drink and decided against it. Alcohol and self-pity were a Malfoy tradition he was trying to break.

The Mark was worse. That was the concrete thing, the measurable thing, the thing that existed outside his interpretive spiraling. Without the sustained diagnostic contact of their regular examinations, the dark magic was restless again — not the old summoning burn, which had been sharp and purposeful and at least made a kind of sense, but the new ache, the one that felt like magic trying to move through channels that no longer connected to anything. The ache thickened each evening and kept him awake, a slow persistent pressure in the bones of his forearm that radiated to his shoulder if he let it. During the examinations, her spells quieted it — the dark magic going still under her diagnostic contact like a restless animal under a specific hand. Without the examinations, the restlessness built, and built, and he could feel it now at the dinner table, the Mark pulsing its slow idiot pain, and the pain was a reminder that the arrangement they had set — library access for Mark help — required her hands on his arm at regular intervals, and she had pulled away, and the Mark did not care about her reasons.

He lay awake that night in his rooms. The bed was cold — the sheets, the heavy duvet, the stone walls bleeding winter into the room despite the fire he kept going. The Manor's upper floors had always been inconsistent, the warming charms layered over centuries and never quite adequate, but this was worse. The house was withdrawing from him the way she had withdrawn, and he lay in the gap between the two absences and pressed his thumb into the Mark through his sleeve and the pain sharpened briefly, a hot flare, and then settled back into the ache. The Mark remembered her. The dark magic had been given something it wanted — her touch, her spells, the specific frequency of whatever her diagnostic magic did to the curse — and now the magic was being denied, and the denial was pain.

He stared at the ceiling and thought about the last look she'd given him before the mask went up, on Monday evening, the fraction of a second before the professional distance clicked into place. The expression underneath had been — he couldn't name it. It had looked, for a moment, like guilt.

He did not sleep. At some point after midnight the Mark flared — not the sharp emergency of the old summons but a long, slow burn that spread from his forearm into his shoulder and sat there, radiating, the curse reminding him of its residency. He got up and stood at the window in the dark. The grounds were silver with frost, the hedges casting moon-shadows across the lawn, and the Manor was enormous and silent around him, and the silence was the kind that could drive a person to conversation with the walls. He had done that, in the first months after the war, when his mother had moved out and the house was his alone. He had talked to the corridors, to the walls, to the empty rooms. He had asked the library to open doors. He had negotiated with the architecture like a man talking to God — uncertain whether anyone was listening, unable to stop. He had not done it in months. The fact that the impulse was back told him something about what her presence had changed and what her withdrawal was undoing.

He went back to bed and lay in the cold sheets and waited for morning, and the Mark ached, and the house was silent, and the silence was not the kind that meant peace.

Friday he was in the library before her. She stopped in the doorway, her coat still buttoned — she had taken to keeping her coat on longer, a layer of *outside* between herself and the house — and she looked at him and he looked at her and for a moment the distance between them was just distance, crossable, ordinary.

"The diagnostic series," she said. "We've let the interval run too long."

She was right. The examinations had a rhythm — every three to four days — and the rhythm was broken.

"I know," he said.

"After lunch. The usual setup."

"Fine."

She unbuttoned her coat, crossed to her table — the one she had established six feet from his, the gap new as of this week, a measured distance that hadn't existed when they worked shoulder to shoulder — and began unpacking her satchel. Quill, ink, notes. The small precise motions of someone who had already moved past the conversation.

He watched her for a moment. Then he looked down at the text he had been failing to read.

---

They set up after lunch at the worktable by the tall windows. The arrangement was familiar: table cleared, her instruments laid out in a neat row — wand, the set of diagnostic crystals she had calibrated to the Mark's magical frequency, the probe resonator she had built herself and handled with the particular care of someone who had made a thing and knew exactly what it could do, a clean sheet of parchment for notations. She was efficient about it. Professional. He had watched her prepare for examinations a dozen times now and the ritual was steady — the way she squared the parchment, checked the crystal alignment, rolled her sleeves to the elbow with two practiced motions. Right sleeve first, pushed up with her left hand. Then left sleeve — though the left always came down further, the fabric folded one extra turn, positioned with more precision. He had noticed this before without understanding it.

"Sleeve," she said.

He rolled his left sleeve to the elbow and laid his arm on the table, palm up. The Mark was vivid today, the snake and skull sharper-edged than they had been in weeks, the skin around the lines faintly inflamed. The dark magic in it was agitated, the ache visible in the way the lines seemed to pulse, and she would see that. She would register the deterioration without his having to say it.

Her fingers circled his wrist. Cold. The library was cold and her hands carried it, and the jolt of her skin against his after three days of nothing was worse than he had braced for. He kept his face still.

"Your hands are cold."

"The library's cold."

"It is."

She pressed her thumb to the inside of his wrist — the pulse point, her starting baseline. His heartbeat under her thumb before the first spell was even cast. He looked at the window. Outside, the grounds were iron-grey under low cloud, the hedges black with frost, the lawn colourless. The light was already thinning — dark by half three today. They had maybe an hour of daylight before the candles would have to carry the room.

"Standard series first," she said. "Then I want to run the deeper mapping again. The decay rate in the secondary channels — I've been reviewing last week's data and the pattern's not—" She caught herself, trimmed the sentence back. "I want to confirm something."

"All right."

The first diagnostic charm settled over the Mark like a net of pale blue light, fine-grained, the spell catching on the dark lines and illuminating the magical substrate beneath. The dark magic surged once — the habitual resistance, the curse pushing back — and then quieted. It always quieted for her. The restless ache that he'd lived with for three days eased under her spell like a cramp releasing, the pressure in his forearm dropping, and his hand unclenched on the table before he could stop it.

She noticed. She always noticed. "The pain's been worse."

"Manageable."

"Your knuckles were white, Malfoy."

He didn't answer that. She moved to the second spell, a mapping charm that traced the Mark's deeper magical architecture and projected it as a luminous overlay above his skin — a three-dimensional rendering of the curse layers Voldemort had embedded, the channels connecting the Mark to the ambient magical field. She studied this overlay with the same focused intensity she gave everything — her wand moving in slow precise arcs over his forearm, her left hand holding his arm steady at the wrist.

The mapping proceeded. She recorded readings, murmured notations to the self-scribing quill that hovered beside the parchment, adjusted the crystal alignment twice when the probe resonator's numbers drifted. He watched her work because he could. During the examinations her attention was on the Mark and not on him, and the arrangement gave him the one form of proximity the withdrawal had not taken — permission to look while being looked at. He knew her working expressions: the slight furrow between her eyes when a reading came back unexpected. The way she held her lower lip between her teeth when parsing something dense. The half-squint against the spell light when she was too absorbed to shade her eyes. He knew that she mouthed translations to herself and didn't know she was doing it, her lips forming silent words in Latin and sometimes in something older, and he knew the sound her breathing made when she was concentrating hard — slower, deliberate, as if she were rationing her focus.

He made himself look away from her face and watched her hands instead. Her right hand holding the wand, tracing the diagnostic patterns. Her left hand on his wrist, fingers wrapped around his forearm just below the Mark, holding the arm in position. Her left sleeve had been pushed back for the work, the cuff folded neatly above her wrist, and the skin of her forearm was smooth and unblemished in the candlelight — the same forearm it had been in every examination, the careful surface she showed the world.

She pushed deeper today. The diagnostic spells shifted from the Mark's visible surface into the substrate — older magic underneath Voldemort's brand, something that predated the Dark Lord and connected to the Manor's own magical infrastructure. This was the work they had been doing before her retreat: mapping the deep channels that linked the Mark's residual curse to the ambient field's decay. It required sustained output. Her wand traced analytical patterns over his forearm while she simultaneously maintained the mapping charm and fed data to the recording quill — three concurrent spell operations, each demanding its own thread of concentration. He could see the effort in the tendons of her wrist, in the slight tremor in her fingers on his arm, in the sheen building at her temples. She was channelling more magic than she usually did, mining layers they hadn't reached before, and the expenditure was visible.

He lost track of the minutes. The diagnostic work had its own rhythm, the spells cycling through their analytical patterns, her readings murmured in a low steady voice, and inside that rhythm the silence between them was bearable — structured, purposeful, the silence of two people doing something difficult and technical together. It was not the dead silence of the past three days. The work gave them a frame to sit inside, and inside the frame they could be what the arrangement had always asked them to be: examiner and subject, researcher and research, two competent people solving a hard problem. That the problem was on his arm and her hands were the instruments and his pulse was the baseline — those intimacies were incidental. Part of the frame. Nothing more.

He nearly believed it.

The light outside thinned and went. Cloud thickening over Wiltshire, the short day surrendering early. The candles she'd lit at the start of the session were doing most of the work now, their uneven glow falling over the table, over his arm, over her hands.

Her left forearm shimmered.

Not a flicker of light — a shift in the surface. The way heat haze warps the air above a road in summer, a ripple passed over the skin below her rolled sleeve, the smooth even tone wavering. Then the glamour thinned and he saw through it the way you see the bottom of a stream when the current stills — not all at once, but in a slow, irreversible clarification.

The letters were carved deep.

Not cut — carved. The difference was in the furrows, wide and deliberate, the strokes of someone who had taken her time, who had wanted the work to last. The scar tissue was raised and silvery-white, almost luminous against the skin beneath, the edges ragged where the cursed blade had torn rather than sliced cleanly. The word was legible. It had been made to be legible. It had been carved by a woman who believed the word was true and wanted it written where it could be read for the rest of the body's life.

MUDBLOOD.

Five letters and five more. Spanning the width of her forearm from the inside of the wrist to the edge of the rolled fabric. The first downstrokes were the deepest — the M, the opening cuts, the blade biting hard into skin that had been held flat against marble. The letters were not even — they tilted slightly toward the elbow, following the angle of Bellatrix's hand, the writing of a woman who had been kneeling on her subject's chest for leverage. The final D was shallower. By then the arm had been slick and Bellatrix had been laughing — the laugh he could still hear if a room went quiet enough — and the blade had found less purchase. He knew this. He knew the order and the depth and the sound because he had been standing against the wall of the drawing room twelve feet from where it was happening, and he had not moved, and his memory of that night had the merciless fidelity of a memory you have tried to destroy a thousand times and cannot.

His hand was on the scar before he made any decision about it. Not reaching — reaching would have been conscious, something he could have stopped. His hand moved from the table to her forearm and his fingers settled over the letters the way one touches something to verify that it is real, and the scar tissue under his thumb was warm and raised and real.

She flinched.

Not away. Inward. A full-body contraction, the pulling-in of a person hit by a gust of wind — not pain but exposure, the shock of air against skin that has been covered for years. Her arm went rigid under his hand. The diagnostic spell she was maintaining shattered. The mapping overlay above his Mark dissolved into scattered motes. The probe resonator's readings went to noise. The recording quill scratched a wild line across the parchment and stopped. Three concurrent spells collapsed in an instant, and the library was suddenly darker and quieter and full of the absence of magic.

His hand stayed.

He could feel her pulse. Fast — not the measured baseline she took from his wrist at the start of every examination but rapid, hunted, the heartbeat of a caught thing. And beneath his thumb, the ridged letters. The texture of cursed damage that had lived under glamour and fabric for seven years and was now under his hand in the candlelit library of the house where it had been made.

She was looking at his hand on her arm. Not at his face — at the point of contact, his fingers over the word, his pale skin against her skin and the silver-white ridges of the scar between. Her face was very still, the expression on it something he had never seen: not anger, not the sharp fierceness she wore in confrontation, not the guarded professional mask of the past three days. Something older than any of those. The face of a person who has carried a weight alone for a long time and has just had it seen by the one person she could not bear to show.

"Granger."

She didn't move.

"Granger, I—"

"Don't." Her voice was flat. Not angry — load-bearing. Holding up the rest of her. "Don't say you're sorry."

He closed his mouth. He had not been going to say sorry. Though sorry was in the room, had been in the room since a night in 1998 when a woman with wild dark hair and the Dark Lord's favour had held this arm against cold marble and taken a cursed knife to it while a boy in black school shoes stood against the wall and did nothing. Sorry was the size of the house and the weight of the name and the permanent fact of the word under his fingers, and sorry was the smallest possible coin and they both knew it would not buy a thing.

He moved his thumb. Not away — across. Slowly. Tracing the first letter, the M, the deep furrows that Bellatrix's blade had opened in the arm of an eighteen-year-old girl on his drawing room floor. The scar tissue had its own topography, raised and dense, the way cursed wounds heal — not back to what they were but into something new, something that is both healed and not, the body's best effort meeting a magic designed to make repair impossible. He traced the U. The D. Under his thumb, the letters were a braille, a record written in the only material that could not be edited or revised or lost.

Her breath changed. Not faster — deliberate. The breathing of someone deciding, with each inhale, not to bolt.

The candles had burned low. The library was lit mostly by firelight now, the grate across the room still holding a few red logs, and the thin remainder of the November afternoon through the tall windows — a blue-grey wash that would be gone in minutes. In the dimness the scar was harder to see but easier to feel. He traced the letters by touch the way a person reads in the dark.

B. L. O. O. D.

He went back to the beginning and started again, and this time she made a sound — small, closed-mouthed, somewhere between a word she swallowed and a breath she didn't intend to let out.

"I see it," he said. His voice came out wrong — scraped down to something he didn't usually let into the air. "Every time I close my eyes, the room's there. The floor and the chandelier and you on the floor and—" He stopped. The sound she'd made while Bellatrix worked. Some doors should not be opened from this side. "I see it."

"I know."

"I didn't know it was still here. On you. I thought—" He stopped again, and this time the stopping was the sudden understanding of how monstrous the assumption had been. He had assumed it was gone. For seven years he had assumed that someone — a Healer, a spell, the sheer force of Hermione Granger's competence applied to her own body — had fixed it. Erased it. Returned the arm to what it was before. He had assumed this the way people assume that other people's damage heals, because the alternative is to know that every person you pass is carrying something permanent, and that knowledge is not survivable in quantity.

"I didn't know," he said again, and the inadequacy of it lay on the table between them like the diagnostic instruments that were no longer needed.

She looked at him. Not at his hand on her arm but at his face. The expression was clear-eyed and dry and very tired.

"Where would it go?"

Three words. The simplest question. And it opened a hole in the floor of every assumption he had built over seven years. Where would it go. Where does a Dark magic scar go when the Dark witch who made it is dead and the war is over and the world has agreed to move on. Where does the word carved into a woman's arm by a cursed blade in a house of Dark wizards go, once the Ministry has held its trials and the Prophet has printed its retrospectives and everyone has decided that what happened was terrible and is now past tense. The answer was nowhere. The answer was the same arm, every morning, glamoured because the world had moved on and the arm had not.

"Dark magic," she said, in the flat tone of a thing she had explained too many times. "It doesn't heal clean. You know that."

"I know that." His thumb was on the L. He could feel the ridge of it, the way the tissue had knitted around the cut without closing it, the body growing over the wound like a tree growing over a fence wire — incorporating the damage, not erasing it. "I know that. I just didn't — I wasn't thinking about yours."

"No," she said. "People don't."

He heard what was underneath that. People looked at Hermione Granger and saw competence, brilliance, the war heroine, the brightest witch, the divorced researcher who worked too much and cared too deeply about dead languages and dying magic. People did not see the arm. She had made sure of it. The glamour, the long sleeves even in summer, the habitual positioning of her left forearm against her body or below a table — all of it a system, engineered and maintained with the same thoroughness she brought to everything, for the purpose of making the worst thing that had ever happened to her invisible.

"You hide it," he said. "Every day."

"Every day."

"Why."

It wasn't a real question. He knew why. But she answered anyway, and the answer was different from the one he'd expected.

"Because people see it and they do the maths." Her voice had gone tight, the composure under strain. "The scar and my name and the war and the fact that I was eighteen and held down on a floor while a woman carved it into me with a knife, and the number they come up with is pity. Every time. And I can't—" She stopped. Restarted sharper, the way she did when the first attempt wasn't precise enough. "I can't function in a room where the first thing people know about me is the worst thing that was done to me. I can't be that person. I won't."

"So you hide it."

"So I hide it."

He traced the M again. She let him.

"I watched," he said. The words came out heavy, ungraceful, with the weight of things stored too long. "I stood against the wall and I watched and I didn't move. I didn't — I was sixteen and she was Bellatrix and my father was in the room and I was terrified and none of that's enough. I know it isn't."

"No." Not cruelly. Just true. "It isn't."

The silence after that was bad. He could hear the fire, the small ticking of the logs settling, and the wind outside pressing against the windows, and neither of those sounds was loud enough to fill the space where a better man would have known what to say. He was not a better man. He was the boy who had stood in the drawing room and watched, and he was the man who sat in the library and held the record of what he had watched, and the distance between those two people was seven years and a lifetime of learning that watching was its own form of participation.

"I'm not going to apologize."

Her eyes sharpened.

"Not because I don't owe—" He stopped, reorganised. The habit of rerouting away from the honest thing was so deep it was nearly physical, the words being pulled toward safe before he could force them back. "An apology is a transaction. I say sorry, you say something, we agree the account's closer to settled. This isn't — there isn't a word that settles this. I can't give you something that makes it balance. And I won't pretend sorry covers any of it."

She was quiet for a long time. Her pulse under his fingers had slowed, the hammering easing into something steadier, though still faster than rest. His hand was on her scar. Her left hand — the one that had been holding his wrist through the examination — was still there, her fingers loosely circling his forearm below the Mark. He noticed this the way you notice something that has been true for several minutes and only now registers: they were holding each other at the point of damage. His fingers on the word in her arm. Her fingers below the mark on his.

"That's the first honest thing anyone's said to me about it," she said. Her voice was quiet. "In seven years."

He didn't know what to say to that. The correct response didn't exist in any register he'd been trained in — not the pureblood formality, not the careful precision, not the dry deflection that kept the world at its distance. So he said nothing, and the nothing sat between them, and the silence was not the Manor's silence or the withdrawal's silence but one they were making together, and it was enough.

She moved first. Not away — forward. She turned her hand on his arm, the one still resting below the Mark, and placed her palm flat over the skull and snake. Not diagnostic. Not clinical. Her hand on the place where they had branded him at sixteen, her palm warm across the dark lines, her fingers curving over the edges, and the gesture was so simple and so deliberate that something in him that had been locked since that night in the study — his father's hand on his shoulder, the burning that whited out the world — gave way, not dramatically, not with any sound, but the way a bolt gives that has been holding under strain for years and finally doesn't.

He kept his hand on her scar. She kept hers on his Mark. They sat like that in the near-dark, the fire down to embers, the candles guttering in their pools of wax, the November light entirely gone from the windows. The library was enormous around them and the two of them were at the centre of it, at a worktable covered in diagnostic instruments and abandoned parchment, and the house around them was quiet. Not the cold hostile quiet of the past three days. Not the Manor's attentive, testing silence. Something closer to stillness — the hush of a structure recognizing that the people inside it were doing something it could not engineer and, for once, holding itself back.

Outside, frost thickened on the grounds. Inside, they did not relight the candles. Did not add wood to the fire. The cold crept in from the corridors and the warmth held where they were touching, and neither of them moved to fix anything because what had happened was not a thing fixing could reach.

At some point her breathing steadied fully, the deliberate quality gone, and his matched it without his trying. The probe resonator on the table between them had gone dark, its readings abandoned, and the diagnostic crystals sat in their case catching the firelight in small prismatic flickers, and the parchment with its wild terminal line of quill-scratch lay curled at the edge. The detritus of the examination, the clinical apparatus, was all still there, but it belonged to a different evening — the one where this was an examination and nothing more.

Eventually she drew her hand away. Slowly — not reluctant, but careful, the withdrawal itself a gesture, as if she were setting something down rather than letting go. She looked at her own forearm where the scar was still visible, the glamour still gone, the letters legible in the dim orange light from the dying fire.

"I should recast it," she said. Meaning the glamour.

"Should you."

Not a question. Not really. It was the closest he could come to saying what he meant, and the indirectness was his native mode — the real thing said at an angle, offered sideways so the other person could pick it up or leave it. He watched her hear it. The small shift in her face. The recognition that he'd said more than the words contained.

She looked at the scar. She looked at him. She did not recast the glamour.

"The diagnostic readings," she said, after a pause that held its own weight. "Before the spell collapsed, some of the deep-layer data came through. I should transcribe it while it's fresh."

"All right."

She stood. Gathered her wand, her instruments, her notes. The motions were efficient — the professional frame coming back like a coat taken from a hook — but something was different in the way she moved, something less rigid, the competence still there but the armour loosened. She crossed the library to her table, the one six feet from his, and sat and began transcribing, her quill moving in quick sure strokes across the parchment, her head bent over the work.

Her left sleeve was still pushed back. The scar was visible each time her arm moved across the page, the silver-white letters catching the last of the firelight, and she had not pulled the fabric down. He did not know if this was a decision or an oversight and it did not matter. The glamour was off and the scar was present in the room with them, as real and as permanent as the Mark on his own arm, and she was letting it be there.

He sat at his table and did not pretend he wasn't watching. The Mark was quieter than it had been in days — the restless ache gone, the dark magic stilled. Not by the diagnostic spells, which had collapsed twenty minutes ago. By something else. By a hand placed on it not to study it or map its architecture but to touch it, the way you touch a thing that hurts in order to say *I know this hurts.*

He rolled his sleeve down slowly. He thought about the word under his thumb, the ten letters in cursed silver he could still feel in his skin like an afterimage. He thought about the fact that she hid this every day, from everyone, the glamour cast each morning and maintained through every interaction, and that today she had let him see and was still letting him see, and that this was a trust he had done nothing to earn and had no idea what to do with except sit here in the cooling library and be the person she had decided not to hide from.

Her quill scratched across the parchment. The fire ticked as the last log settled into ash. The frost outside made no sound at all.
