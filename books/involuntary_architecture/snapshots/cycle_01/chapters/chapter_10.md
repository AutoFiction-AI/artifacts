# Chapter 10

Draco made tea at six in the morning because the alternative was his ceiling and the running inventory of things he'd said wrong in the drawing room.

The kitchen was the oldest part of the Manor that still worked like a kitchen — stone floor, scarred counters, a kettle that had sat on the same stretch of counter since before his grandfather's time. He used the Muggle one now. The enchanted kettle needed a heating charm, and his heating charms had been unreliable for weeks, stuttering mid-boil or producing tepid water that sat in the cup like a reproach. He'd rather manage a manual flame than explain to Granger that his household magic was deteriorating along with everyone else's.

He didn't think about the drawing room.

He poured water over the tea bag. Counted. Removed the bag. Added milk — less than he wanted, because the cold-box enchantment was struggling and they were running low.

He thought about the drawing room.

What he'd said. What she'd said. The specific quality of her voice when she'd told him what she remembered — the detail, the precision, as if the trauma had been preserved under glass and she was describing items in an exhibit, except her hands had been shaking. He'd stood in the doorway and had the chance to say any number of things and what he'd actually managed was a defence so thin it had embarrassed them both.

The stairs creaked — third step, seventh, landing — and she came into the kitchen wearing a jumper that didn't fit and her hair in the particular state it achieved when she'd slept too hard and given up.

She should not have been awake. She worked until one or two most nights and slept through dawn with a commitment that suggested dawn had personally wronged her. He had never seen her in the kitchen before seven.

"Morning," she said.

"Morning."

She looked at the kettle. He looked at her looking at the kettle. The space between these two events was nowhere near large enough to contain everything that had happened in the drawing room, so neither of them tried.

"There's water," he said.

"Thanks."

She made her own tea. He already knew how she took it — milk, no sugar, steeped too long because she forgot about it while thinking about something else — and the fact that he knew this was one of several pieces of knowledge he'd been carefully not examining. She sat at the kitchen table, which was scarred oak and older than most of the doors in the building, and wrapped her hands around the mug, and the silence between them was unlike any of the silences that had preceded it.

Not the careful silence of two people maintaining professional distance. Not the raw silence of the drawing room's aftermath, the night before, when they'd gone to separate floors and the house had felt like it was holding its breath. This was something else. Looser. As if the confrontation had stripped a layer of insulation and what was underneath was simply two people in a kitchen, early, tired, drinking tea.

"I didn't sleep," she said. Not a confession. Closer to a weather report.

"No."

"You?"

"Not particularly."

She nodded. Drank. Looked out the window, where the December dark was just starting to thin along the treeline, frost on the grounds catching what little light there was.

"Your heating charm," she said, glancing at the Muggle kettle.

"Unreliable."

"I can look at it. If you want."

This was the first thing either of them had offered the other since the drawing room that wasn't about research, and it was about a kettle. He could have said no. He could have said it was fine, the Muggle method worked, he was managing. All of which was true and none of which mattered.

"All right," he said.

She pulled it toward her across the table and turned it over in her hands, ran a diagnostic — quick, efficient, her wand hand moving with the kind of fluency that came from years of not thinking about the motion. The easy competence was so far from the woman who'd stood frozen in the drawing room doorway that he had to look at the window instead.

"It's not degrading on its own," she said. "The underlying enchantment's solid, it just isn't getting enough ambient throughput. The general decay." She tapped the kettle twice, murmured something, set it back. "That'll hold for a month. Maybe six weeks, then I'll need to redo it."

"Thank you."

"Sure."

He made more tea with the fixed kettle and brought her a cup without asking. She took it without comment. They sat with the new tea and the window showing the frost and the kitchen settling into the sounds it made when the house was at rest — old wood, a faint whistle from the gap above the window that had been there as long as Draco could remember.

"This is better," she said, and held up the mug. The tea, or the morning. She didn't specify.

---

The days after were like that.

She stopped eating in the library. Or rather, she started appearing in the kitchen when he was already there, which meant they ate together, which meant meals became something that happened between them rather than in adjacent solitude. He didn't comment on this. She didn't frame it as a decision. The old choreography of calibrated separation — one eating at the kitchen table, the other in the library, each pretending the arrangement was convenience — had simply stopped, and what replaced it was breakfast with the frost in the windows and neither of them performing anything.

They talked about the research. The anchoring theory she was developing, the decayed infrastructure she'd traced through three centuries of Malfoy texts, the specific notation system his ancestor Septimus had invented that refused to map onto any published schema. But the conversations had a different grain now. She interrupted herself less. He let sentences finish without deflecting into something drier. The professional frame was still there — the work was urgent, the magical decay was not waiting for them to sort out anything personal — but the frame had thinned from a wall to something more like a screen, and what came through it was closer to actual speech.

He brought her tea in the library. Not every time, and not on a schedule, but when he noticed her pace change — the focused stillness loosening into restless movement that meant she needed to stand up and was too stubborn — he made tea and brought it and set it at the edge of her desk, far enough from the texts that a spill wouldn't touch them. She didn't look up. Ten minutes later the mug was in her hand.

She left the library door open. This was a choice — the door was heavy enchanted oak, and leaving it open meant the corridor sounds reached her, which meant she could hear him moving through the house. He chose not to think about why she'd want that.

He started eating dinner at seven because she ate at seven and had mentioned, offhand, that eating alone made food boring. He wasn't sure she'd meant it as an invitation. He showed up at seven. She didn't comment.

On the third morning she brought a passage from the fourteenth-century archive to breakfast and said, "Your ancestor Septimus was a terrible writer but a brilliant engineer. Look at this."

"He was also, by all accounts, a terrible person." He took the page. "Though that's most of them."

She looked at him. Not with pity, not with the careful absence of judgment he'd learned to read in people who were trying very hard not to judge him. Just looked, as if he'd said something she was filing for later reference.

"The binding schema here," she said, pointing. "I've never seen it outside Egyptian work. Centuries ahead of the published European record."

"You can admire the architecture and despise the architect. We're standing in the proof."

A week ago any reference to the Manor's darker qualities would have tightened the air between them. Now it passed through like weather.

"I suppose we are," she said, and reached for the jam. She spread it on toast with the same focused precision she brought to fourteenth-century notation, and he watched her do it and thought that this — the kitchen, the toast, the morning argument about dead Malfoys — was as close to a normal breakfast as the Manor had produced in seven years.

That afternoon he brought her tea and she said, without looking up from her translation, "You always get the timing right. How do you know?"

"Know what?"

"When I need it. You bring it right before I'm about to get up and make it myself."

He didn't have an answer for this he was willing to say out loud, so he said, "The house tells me," which was not entirely false — the Manor did something subtle to the air when she was restless, a faint shift he'd learned to read — and she accepted this with a skeptical noise and went back to work.

One evening over soup she said, "I keep expecting to turn a corner and find a room I haven't seen. But the house has been settled this week. Steady."

He'd noticed. The corridors held their shapes. The library stayed where it was. The route from the guest wing to the kitchen hadn't rearranged overnight. He didn't know if the house was responding to whatever had shifted between them or if the shift was something the house had arranged. The question applied to everything happening under this roof, and he had decided, provisionally and probably unwisely, to stop asking it.

"Maybe it's the season," he said. "It tends to settle in winter."

"Does it?"

"I don't actually know. It might just be quiet."

She looked at him and said, "Quiet's all right," and he couldn't read her expression but he didn't think he needed to.

The Mark ached that night — the dull restless pulse that had become background since the examinations began, the old curse magic shifting under his skin without purpose or destination. During the examinations, when her hands were on his arm and her diagnostic spells were running their slow careful work, the ache went still. Not painless — settled, which was different. The dark magic calmed under her attention in a way no Healer's touch had achieved, and the contrast between her presence and her absence was something he'd been tracking without deciding to. He sat in his room and pressed his thumb against the Mark's centre and the ache sharpened and he stopped pressing. The absence of her hands was not something he was going to think about. He was a person who did not think about people's hands.

* * *

By the end of the first week after the drawing room, the library had become her territory in a way that should have bothered him and didn't.

Not the books — those reorganized themselves at night according to whatever logic the Manor was running that week, and he'd given up predicting where anything would be from one morning to the next. But around and among the books, Granger had built a working habitat. Three translations in progress, each weighted open with whatever she'd grabbed nearest — a teacup, a saucer, once his reading glasses, which she'd appropriated from the side table without asking and returned without apology. Notes in her handwriting, which was atrocious and precise, covering sheets of parchment she arranged in patterns that looked chaotic and weren't. A stack of reference texts on the floor by the south window, because the window seat got the best light before noon and she liked to work there with her feet tucked beneath her.

He knew about the feet because he had been looking.

She'd left a book on his chair — his chair, by the fireplace, the one he'd been sitting in since he was twelve — open to a passage about resonance patterns in enchanted structures. A note in the margin, in her worst handwriting, which meant late at night: *Cf. your notes on the east wing fluctuation — I think this explains the periodicity.*

He sat in the chair with the book pressed against his leg and read the page, then her note, then found his own notes. She was right. The resonance pattern explained the fluctuation he'd been tracking for weeks, and the connection had the quality her best work always had — so clear once articulated that the gap felt almost insulting. He should tell her. He would tell her.

The afternoon passed. The winter light — what there was of it, the sun giving up by half three — came through the library windows at the low amber angle that only happened in early December. In that light the library was the closest thing to a living room the Manor had ever produced. The preservation charms gave the air its permanent base of old paper and dust and the faintly metallic trace of stabilised ink, and layered on top was the tea she'd made an hour ago and the wool of whatever jumper she was wearing and the particular smell of someone else in a space that had been only his for years.

She was on the floor by the south wall, cross-legged, too near the shelf she was referencing to bother with the desk, surrounded by open texts like someone building a nest out of fourteenth-century parchment. Her lips moved when she hit a difficult passage — the Latin, working through syntax — and her right hand was in her hair, twisting a strand without knowing she was doing it.

He was cataloguing her.

He'd been doing it for days. Not deliberately — not the way she catalogued the archive texts, with purpose and a system. This was more the way you catalogued weather: involuntarily, because you were inside it and it was happening and your body registered it whether or not you'd decided to pay attention.

He knew the expressions. Concentration drew her eyebrows together and made her look angry; people who didn't know her would have read annoyance. Frustration made her tap the page with her index finger, a steady rhythm, as if the text were being obstinate on purpose and she meant to let it know. When she found something significant she went very still — the lip-movement stopped, the hair-twisting stopped, everything paused for a held breath — and then she wrote with an urgency that suggested the idea might escape if she didn't pin it down fast enough.

He knew the rhythm of her work. Long focused stretches — two hours, sometimes three, when the library might as well have been empty. Then a burst of movement, sudden and total, crossing the room with the specific walk of someone whose body existed mainly to transport her brain to the next relevant text. Then the slow re-settling. Feet tucked. Hair-twist resumed. Gone again.

He knew she took her tea too strong and forgot it until it was cold. That she cracked her knuckles after an hour of sustained writing, left hand first. That she talked to the texts under her breath when they contradicted each other, arbitrating centuries-old disputes with a patience she rarely extended to the living.

He knew the laugh.

She'd found something in Septimus Malfoy's personal annotations — the man's opinion of French magical theorists, which was unprintable even by the standards of someone who'd been dead for six hundred years — and the laugh had come out of her sudden and unreserved. Head back, the sound of it hitting the high ceiling and ringing in a room that hadn't heard much laughter in years. It had changed the shape of her face entirely, and she'd looked over to see if he'd heard, and he'd been sitting perfectly still with his cold tea and an expression he was not going to examine.

"Sorry," she'd said. "Your ancestor really hated the French."

"Family tradition. We've narrowed it down to the Normans."

She'd laughed again — shorter, more controlled — but the first one was the one he kept. He did not think about why he was keeping it. He was aware that not thinking about it was itself a kind of thinking, and that the recursion was the problem.

One afternoon she read aloud from a passage that was defeating her — the Latin so archaic that the syntax doubled back on itself — and he corrected a verb declension without looking up from his own work.

"Thank you. How'd you know that was wrong?"

"I didn't know it was wrong. I knew what it should say."

"That's the same thing."

"It's not. Knowing the error requires knowing the system. Knowing the answer just requires having read the text before."

She was quiet for a moment. "You've read this before?"

"Parts. When I was younger. My father thought the family texts were important."

"Were they?"

"They were to him." He turned a page. "I don't think he understood what they described. But he understood they mattered."

She didn't push — didn't ask the follow-up question about what his father had been like, what the family legacy meant from the inside. She just nodded and went back to the Latin, and the restraint of it, the discipline of her curiosity, was a kindness he noted without knowing how to return.

On the Tuesday night she fell asleep over a translation past eleven.

He didn't notice at first — the last hour of her work was always quiet, pace slowing as exhaustion overtook precision. But the quill dropped from her hand and she didn't reach for it, and he looked up and her head was on her arm on the desk and her breathing had changed and she was asleep.

The fire was low. The library was cold at the edges after midnight — the preservation charms kept the books at stable temperature but did nothing for the people. He should have rebuilt the fire. Should have woken her, told her the desk would destroy her neck by morning. Should have said goodnight and gone upstairs and maintained the boundary between colleague and whatever word he was avoiding.

He picked up the dropped quill and set it in the ink rest so it wouldn't stain the translation she'd been working on for three days. He took the throw from the back of his chair — the old wool one, there since his mother had put it there when he was fourteen and reading past curfew — and laid it over her shoulders. She shifted under the weight of it. Didn't wake.

He sat back down.

Twenty minutes. He didn't read. He listened to her breathe and watched the fire settle into embers and did not look at what he was doing, because looking at it would require naming it, and the name was not something he could afford. Wanting things was the most dangerous habit he'd ever broken. He'd spent seven years learning not to want — not company, not forgiveness, not for the world to look at him and see something other than the Mark on his arm. He'd gotten good at it. He'd built a life around the absence of want, and the life was small and quiet and precisely controlled and it had been survivable.

He was sitting in a library at midnight listening to a woman breathe and the habit had not broken at all. He had just run out of things to want that the world was positioned to take from him, and now there was something new, and the shape of it was so precise he could feel it like an architectural drawing held up to light, and he would not look at it directly.

He left. The corridor to his rooms was shorter than usual — the house being kind, or efficient, or simply noting what he refused to note himself. Behind him, the fire kept burning. The Manor could manage that much.

* * *

Theo arrived on a Thursday, because Theo arrived on whichever day suited Theo.

He came through the Floo — it held, barely, stuttering for half a second before depositing him in the entrance hall with a film of ash on his coat that he brushed off with more attention than the gesture warranted. The coat was new. Dark, well-cut, worn with the specific ease Theo could make look effortless when it was anything but. He'd always had the talent of presenting well, which was, he'd once told Draco, the entire operating principle of post-war Slytherin life: look like you belong in the rooms that don't want you until the rooms forget to check.

"You have a guest," Theo said. Not a question. He'd seen the second pair of boots by the door, or the notes stacked on the hall table, or something else Draco hadn't thought to conceal. Theo noticed things the way other people breathed — constantly, without apparent effort, with an accuracy that could be unsettling if you weren't used to it.

"Granger's doing research in the library. The magical decay — the archive has texts no one else can access."

"Granger." Theo brushed a last fleck of ash from his lapel. "As in Hermione Granger. The Hermione Granger who's been the subject of three gossip columns this month about why she's spending all her time in Wiltshire."

"She's researching the decay. The library —"

"Has texts. You said." Theo hung his coat on the hall stand. "Are you going to feed me, or should I have eaten first?"

"Are you staying for lunch?"

"I'm staying for lunch, and I'm staying for an hour after lunch to make sure you've actually been eating, and then I'll go." He followed Draco toward the kitchen. "I'd have come sooner but the Floo's been — well, you know. I tried Tuesday and ended up in a pub in Berkshire. The landlord was very confused."

"Were you confused?"

"I adapted. There was a reasonable bitter on tap." Theo settled against the kitchen counter with the ease of someone who'd been in this room a hundred times — after school, during the war, in the awful first year when Draco had been under house arrest and Theo had been the only person who visited. "The network's getting worse. My contact at Floo Regulation says they're running half capacity and still losing ground."

"Granger thinks it's structural. War damage to the foundational anchoring."

"Granger thinks." He said this flatly, no particular emphasis, which was its own emphasis. "You talk about what she thinks."

"She's in my library. We discuss the research."

"Sure."

Draco put the kettle on — the repaired one, which held. The silence between them was companionable in a way he'd missed, the specific quality of being with someone who knew him well enough that silence wasn't a failure of conversation but a rest inside it.

"How's the business?" he said.

"Slowly." Theo took the tea Draco offered. "I've got a few consulting contracts — potions supplier, everything aboveboard, everything documented six ways because when your surname is Nott they check the paperwork twice. And I've got a list of people who won't return my owls. So. Progress, loosely defined."

"And the storefront?"

"Third application for Diagon Alley. Third polite rejection. The landlord sent a letter explaining the space had been let." Theo drank. "It hasn't been let. I walked past it yesterday. The windows are still papered over."

"Theo."

"It's fine. I've got a line on a place in Horizont Alley. Worse location, cheaper rent, and the landlord's Muggle-born, so he doesn't give a damn about my father's sentencing record." He said this evenly — the bitterness present but managed, folded into pragmatism like a crease in good fabric. "I don't need Diagon Alley. I need a door and a counter."

Draco had known Theo since they were eleven. He knew the register shifts — the performative ease that got smoother when the thing underneath was worse. Theo's father had been a Death Eater of minimal conviction and maximal cowardice, which in practice meant he'd done slightly fewer terrible things than Lucius and received a nearly identical sentence. Theo carried the name the way Draco carried his own: carefully, with a constant low awareness of what it cost to put it on a lease or a handshake or a business card.

But where Draco had retreated — the Manor, the isolation, the careful small life of someone who'd decided that wanting less meant losing less — Theo had gone forward. Into the world with the name on him, daring the world to reject him and adjusting the plan each time it did. Draco had never worked out whether this was bravery or a different shape of the same stubbornness.

"You should talk to Granger," he said. "About the lease. She'd know people at —"

"I'm not asking Hermione Granger for help with my commercial tenancy."

"She'd do it. She's —"

"I'm sure she's fair and competent and would handle it with exactly the right amount of principled outrage. That's not the point." Theo set down his cup. "I don't want to be someone's project. I want to rent a shop."

The kitchen door opened.

Granger paused in the doorway. She'd heard voices, Draco realised, and hadn't expected anyone else. She was in the work jumper — the oversized one, the one she wore when she expected to be alone with the texts all day — sleeves pushed up, ink on her fingers. Her hair had abandoned whatever arrangement she'd attempted that morning and was doing what it always did by midday, which was everything at once.

"Oh," she said. "I didn't realise —"

"Granger, Theodore Nott. Theo, Granger."

"We were in the same year," she said, recovering quickly. She crossed the kitchen and offered her hand. "Though I don't think we ever properly spoke."

"We didn't." Theo shook it. "Different social circles. Mine being the ones that were socially untenable." His delivery was flat enough that it took a beat to register as a joke.

Granger's mouth twitched. "I remember your Potions marks. They were better than mine in fifth year."

"Sixth, actually. Fifth year you beat me by two points on the final and I've been bitter about it for a decade."

"Have you been keeping score for ten years?"

"Only on that one. The rest I've made peace with."

She laughed — brief, surprised — and Draco watched this exchange and did not enjoy the discovery that Theo could make her laugh in under a minute, when Draco had managed it exactly once and was still carrying the sound of it around.

"I hear you're solving the magical decay from Draco's library," Theo said.

"Trying to understand it. Solving it's ambitious. I'm not there yet."

"She's being modest," Draco said, and then both of them looked at him, and he wished he hadn't spoken because the expression on Granger's face — surprised, a little pleased, trying not to be either — was precisely the kind of thing he'd been cataloguing, and Theo would have seen it.

Theo had seen it.

"I was going to make lunch," Draco said, which was not true until he said it. "Stay. Both of you."

They stayed.

Lunch was sandwiches, because that was what the kitchen could produce with the cold-box failing and the preservation charms at half-strength. They ate at the scarred oak table and the conversation moved from the Floo failures to the anchoring theory to Theo's storefront search to the specific fourteenth-century text Granger was translating, and it was ordinary. Three people in a kitchen, eating sandwiches, talking. Granger was sharper with Theo than she was with Draco — not hostile, but more willing to push back, to test his precision with hers. Theo matched her, and Draco sat between them and felt something he hadn't felt in this kitchen in years: the particular warmth of people filling a room that had been empty too long.

Theo talked about the post-war commercial licensing climate — the way applications from Slytherin-sounding surnames drew documentation requests that a Bones or a Longbottom would never see — and Granger set down her sandwich and her face did something serious and focused that he recognised as her enforcement-statute expression.

"That's systemic," she said. "Not anecdotal. There's a pattern in the licensing data."

"There's absolutely a pattern," Theo said. "There's also no enforcement mechanism and no political appetite for sympathising with children of convicted Death Eaters while the war's victims are still rebuilding."

Draco looked at the table.

"You're right that it's systemic," Granger said, more quietly. "That doesn't mean it's tolerable."

"I didn't say tolerable. I said real. Those are different problems."

"I know three people at the Enforcement Office."

Theo looked at her. Draco looked at Theo looking at her. The kitchen was warm.

"I'll think about it," Theo said, and Draco could tell he meant it. Theo did not accept help from people he'd just met. The fact that he was considering it from Granger said something about the quality of the last hour that Draco wasn't going to examine.

Granger went back to the library after lunch carrying her tea and a stack of pages she'd been marking during the meal. She paused in the doorway.

"It was good to meet you," she said to Theo. "Properly."

"Likewise."

"Come back whenever you want. The Floo's more reliable from this end — I think the Manor stabilises the local connection."

She left. The kitchen was quieter. Theo let ten seconds pass.

"Don't," Draco said.

"I haven't said anything."

"You're composing something. I can tell."

"I'm absolutely not composing anything." Theo picked up his tea, found it empty, set it back down. "I was going to ask about the Mark. How's it been."

This was not what he'd been going to ask and they both knew it. Draco took the offered deflection because the alternative was whatever Theo had actually been composing.

"Worse in some ways. Better in others."

"Helpful. Very specific. Thank you."

"It aches more. Consistently. But during the examinations, when she's doing the diagnostic work — it's quieter. Calmer. I don't know why." He paused. "She doesn't know why either, which bothers her more than it bothers me."

Theo was quiet. Not Draco's guarded silence, not Granger's thinking silence. Theo's specific quiet — someone deciding how much honesty the moment could bear.

"I'm going to say one thing," Theo said, "and then I'm leaving, and you're not going to argue with me about it because I'll already be in the fireplace."

"That's generous advance warning."

"You look like someone who lives here."

Draco waited for the rest. Theo pushed his chair back and stood.

"That's it?"

"That's it." He found his coat in the hall. Draco followed him. "You've looked like a guest in your own house for seven years. Walking through the place like you were waiting for someone to tell you it wasn't yours anymore." He buttoned the coat with the careful precision he gave everything. "Today you look like you live here. I won't say what's different. You're not stupid."

"Theo —"

"The Floo should hold if I go now. Probably. If I end up in Berkshire again I'll owl you from the pub." He stepped into the fireplace, said the address, and the green flames took him.

The ash settled. The entrance hall was quiet.

Draco stood in the house he'd grown up in and had not felt at home in since the war and thought about what Theo had said. He thought about the library and the kitchen and the woman who was in both of them. The throw on her shoulders. The laugh hitting the ceiling. The fixed kettle. The way she'd said *come back whenever you want* to his closest friend, casually, as if the Manor were partly hers to open.

He went back to the kitchen and washed the mugs. Three of them. He dried them and put them away and wiped the table where the crumbs from their sandwiches were still scattered. The jam she liked was still out. He put that away too, and then stood at the window and looked at the grounds, which were silver with frost under a sky that was almost, not quite, clear enough for stars. The December dark pressed against the glass, and inside the house was warm — not the charged warmth the Manor sometimes produced, just the ordinary warmth of a building that had people in it and fires lit and a kettle that worked.

Somewhere above him she was working and her door was open and the library fire she'd started that morning was still burning. He stood at the window for a long time and didn't name anything, and the house settled around him, and the dark came down, and he let it.
