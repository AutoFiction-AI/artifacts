# Chapter 17

The flat hadn't changed.

Hermione had been gone for five and a half weeks — she knew the number because her brain produced it unbidden, the way it produced everything, whether she wanted it or not — and her flat had waited with the particular patience of a space that didn't care whether she came back. The mug she'd left by the sink had grown a pale film. The stack of post on the hall table had thickened into something architectural. Her bedroom smelled of closed windows and dust, the mild staleness that accrued when no one opened a door for over a month.

She'd been back for three days.

The walls did not shift. This was the first thing she'd noticed and the thing she couldn't stop noticing — the fixed, dumb permanence of plaster and paint. The corridor from her bedroom to the kitchen was exactly as long at midnight as it had been at seven in the morning. The bathroom door opened onto the bathroom. The windows showed the same stretch of wet December street, the same sodium light, the same skip across the road that someone had been filling with broken furniture since before she'd left for Wiltshire.

Ordinary architecture. Ordinary silence. The silence of a space where sound simply stopped, rather than the loaded, attentive quiet of a house that tracked your breathing and decided which rooms you deserved.

She'd washed the mug. She'd gone through the post — mostly circulars, a note from her mother she wasn't ready to answer, two Ministry memos marked URGENT that she'd responded to with the careful, thorough competence that functioned even when the rest of her didn't. She'd tidied the kitchen. She'd alphabetized a shelf of texts she'd already alphabetized before she left, which was the kind of thing she did when she needed to be doing something and the thing she actually needed to do was impossible. She'd stood in the middle of her sitting room at two in the morning on the second night with her wand in her hand and nothing to point it at, and the flatness of that — the absence of any surface that would respond to her attention — had been worse than she'd expected.

The flat was fourteen hundred square feet. She knew this because the estate agent had told her, a year and a half ago, when she'd been looking for somewhere to live that wasn't the house she'd shared with Ron. She'd chosen it for its smallness, its containability, the way every room was visible from every other room. After the divorce, she'd wanted a space she could hold in her head entire. No hidden rooms. No surprises.

The Manor's library alone had been larger than this. Her room in the guest wing had been twice the size of her bedroom here. The corridors between them had been — variable, alive, occasionally conspiratorial, the house shortening the path between the library and her bed on nights she worked late, as if it understood her exhaustion and took pity on it. Or as if it wanted her in the library and was removing obstacles. She'd never been certain which, and the uncertainty had been part of the texture of living there, the way you might live with a cat whose affection you couldn't fully distinguish from its appetite.

She stopped the comparison. It was the kind of thinking that inventoried what she'd lost instead of what she'd chosen to leave.

Except she hadn't chosen to leave. She'd been — no. She had left. He hadn't told her to go. He'd said what he'd said and she'd looked at his face and she'd left because staying after what he knew was impossible, which felt like not-choosing but was, she understood, a choice dressed in the costume of necessity.

She sat on the edge of the sofa and pressed her palms flat against her knees. The upholstery was a grey twill she'd picked because it was inoffensive. It didn't warm under her hands the way the Manor's surfaces had warmed, the leather of the library chairs holding heat like skin, the stone walls in the corridors near her room running faintly warm by the second week, as if the house were adjusting its temperature to hers. This sofa was just a sofa. It was the temperature it was.

The Mark resonance was still there.

Not the way it had been at the Manor — that close, involuntary awareness of him on the floor above, two rooms away, turning in his sleep. Distance had degraded it into something blunter. But it was there. A low ache in her left forearm that wasn't hers, that worsened at odd hours — four in the morning, midday, late evening — with no pattern she could map. The Mark was hurting him. She could feel it the way you felt a pulled tooth: the gap where the thing used to be, the tongue that kept finding the empty socket and couldn't stop searching it.

Without her sustained diagnostic contact, the quiet the Mark had maintained was gone. Whatever her magic had been doing to the curse — soothing it, stabilizing it, or something more structural that she hadn't finished understanding before everything ended — it had stopped when she'd walked out of the Manor. The Mark had resumed its thrashing. She could feel the edges of it from here: restless, aimless dark magic trying to move through a channel that no longer connected to anything. A curse designed to bind a servant to a master, the master seven years dead, the binding fraying into purposeless pain, and the sustained contact that had been the only thing quieting it now forty miles and three days removed.

There was nothing she could do about it from a flat in London with walls that didn't know her name.

She hadn't slept properly since she'd come back. Not the productive insomnia of research — the kind that had kept her up in the Manor's library with a text open on her knees and the resonance telling her he was awake too, two floors up, probably reading, and the strange comfort of that parallel wakefulness. This was the other kind. She lay in her bed in the dark and the dark was just dark and the silence was just silence and her brain ran the confrontation in the same merciless loop, catching on the same moments, wearing them smooth with repetition until the words lost meaning but not force.

_You knew the house was doing this and you stayed._

_After the examination, after the drawing room, after the scar._

_You knew and you didn't think I deserved to know._

She did think he deserved to know. She had. She'd been going to tell him. She'd worked through versions of the conversation — six, maybe seven, each one more carefully constructed than the last — and every version had collapsed under the weight of the part she couldn't frame. That she'd kissed him knowing their feelings might be architected. That she'd kissed him and hadn't wanted to stop, and the not wanting to stop was the thing she couldn't trust, because the house had been warming around them and the resonance had been singing in her arm and she didn't know — still didn't know — how much of what she'd felt was hers.

But that wasn't what he'd been angry about. Not really.

She'd thought about this in the dark, lying on a mattress that didn't adjust itself to her weight, staring at a ceiling that was just stippled plaster. She'd turned it over the way she turned problems — methodically, looking for the structural failure point — and she kept arriving at the same place.

He hadn't been angry about the bond. He'd been angry about the asymmetry.

She'd known something about them that changed the terms of every interaction, and she'd continued interacting on the old terms. She'd touched his arm during examinations knowing the Mark responded to her because the house was engineering a connection. She'd sat across from him in the library knowing the room held because the bond held. She'd kissed him knowing everything she knew and he'd kissed her back knowing nothing, and the gap between their knowledge was a gap in his agency, and she —

She pressed her fingers into her closed eyes until colours bloomed behind the lids.

She'd had reasons. She'd needed to verify. She'd needed to understand the mechanism before presenting it as fact. She'd needed time to think, to be sure, to figure out what to do with information that retroactively poisoned every good thing that had happened between them.

The reasons were real. They were also, she was beginning to suspect, not the point.

---

The Floo went down on Thursday.

Not a partial failure — the stuttering connections and dropped calls that had become background noise for months, the kind of degradation people had started accepting the way you accepted a road with potholes, adjusting your route and your expectations downward. A full system outage. At seven forty-three in the morning, Hermione stepped into her fireplace with a handful of powder and said "Ministry of Magic" clearly into the grate and nothing happened. The fire burned. The powder dissolved. The flames stayed their ordinary orange — no green rush, no spinning, no arrival.

She tried twice more. Then she checked the connection spells, found the network dead at the relay level, and stepped out of the fireplace with soot on the knees of her trousers and a dull recognition that the thing she'd been modelling for weeks had arrived ahead of schedule.

She Apparated to the Ministry — the squeeze and the lurch, the sensation of being forced through a space too small for a human body — and found the Atrium in controlled chaos. The Floo had gone down across London. Parts of Bristol. Most of the West Country. The network technicians were clustered around connection points in the main hall, waving wands at grates that kept going dead under their hands, and the sound of their voices — too many people talking in careful, professional tones that weren't quite hiding the fear underneath — echoed off the tile.

"Granger." Dennis Creevey caught her on the way to the lifts. He was wearing Muggle trainers under his robes — visible at the hem where he'd dressed in a hurry — and carrying a stack of incident reports thick enough to serve as a doorstop. He had the drawn look of someone who'd been in since the early hours: his robe was buttoned wrong and there was a scorch mark on his left sleeve that looked recent. "Thank God. We've had six accidental-magic incidents since last night. Children. The youngest is four."

"Six?"

"The ambient field went haywire around three a.m. — we think the Floo collapse triggered a cascade, or the cascade triggered the Floo collapse, we don't know which direction it runs." He shifted the stack of reports against his chest. He was twenty-three and had been doing a job usually held by someone a decade older since the woman who'd had it before him had taken a leave of absence she wasn't coming back from. He hadn't complained about this, as far as Hermione knew. He had the particular competence of someone who'd learned young that the world did not owe you a reasonable workload. "Two kids are in St. Mungo's. One of them set her bedroom on fire."

Hermione took the reports. Her hands were steady. This was the part of her that still worked — the intake, the sorting, the capacity to absorb data and organize it into actionable sequence when the rest of her was rubble. She could do this. She could be useful in this specific, bounded way.

The reports were bad. Not the clean bureaucratic kind of bad but the frightened kind, the kind written by people whose hands had been shaking. A nine-year-old boy in Dorset had shattered every window in his house during a nightmare. Twin girls in Camden had levitated their kitchen table for forty minutes and couldn't put it down; by the time the Accidental Magic Reversal Squad arrived, one of the girls had been crying so hard the plates had started shattering off the shelves. The four-year-old in Bristol — Ruby, her name was Ruby — had set the fire because she'd been crying in her crib and the ambient magic had responded to her distress by superheating the air around her. She was in the burn ward. Her parents were Muggles. They'd been told it was an electrical fault.

Hermione read this last report standing in the corridor outside the lifts while people pushed past her. She read it again. A four-year-old girl named Ruby, a Muggle-born child whose magic hadn't declared itself yet, burning in her own bed because the ambient field that was supposed to regulate the interface between magical potential and accidental discharge had destabilized past the point of safe containment.

She folded the report and put it at the bottom of the stack and pressed her thumbnail into the pad of her index finger hard enough to leave a mark.

The magical decay wasn't abstract. It hadn't been abstract for months — she'd measured it, tracked its progression, built models of the degradation rate and the cascade thresholds. But there was a difference between a stabilization index declining and a child in a burn ward. The index was a number. Ruby was a four-year-old girl whose parents didn't know she was a witch.

She worked through the morning. The Floo came back at half one — six hours offline — but it was unstable, connections dropping every few minutes, the green fire flickering to orange mid-transit in a way that made everyone reluctant to step in. She coordinated with the Department of Magical Accidents and Catastrophes on the incident reports. She reviewed the field data from the overnight cascade. She attended a meeting in a room that was too warm, where a Ministry official she'd met twice before asked her, with visible discomfort, whether the decay could be reversed.

"I don't know," she said. "I don't have enough data."

"Your research into the anchoring infrastructure—"

"Is incomplete." She kept her voice level. "The relevant texts are in a private collection I no longer have access to."

A pause. The official — Henderson, that was his name, something in Magical Infrastructure she'd never retained — looked at her with the expression of someone who wanted to ask a follow-up question and could tell he shouldn't.

"I see," he said.

"I can work with what I have. The cascade modelling, the field data, the baseline measurements from October. But the deep structural analysis — the original construction records for the anchoring points — those require access to the Malfoy archive."

She said this as neutrally as she could manage. The Malfoy archive. As if it were a simple bureaucratic obstacle. As if the reason she'd lost access were anything other than the fact that she'd lied to the archive's owner about something that mattered and he'd found out.

After the meeting, she sat in her office with the door closed. The office was small — a desk, a chair, a shelf of reference texts, a window that showed an enchanted view of weather that bore no relation to the actual weather outside. Today it was showing sunshine, which felt like mockery.

The research was at the Manor. The texts she needed — the archaic notation documenting the original anchoring infrastructure, the construction records that explained how the foundational magic had been built and how the war had damaged it — were in the gated archive. The archive that opened only when both she and Draco were present. The archive that held when the bond held and closed when it didn't.

She thought about Ruby in the burn ward. She thought about the nine-year-old boy in Dorset, sleeping in a house with no windows now. She thought about the twin girls and the shattered plates and the forty-minute levitation, and she thought about the fact that the research that might — might — lead to understanding and eventually repairing the damage was locked inside a house she'd walked out of because a man she'd lied to had been angry about the lying.

The world's magic was failing. The research was urgent. And she was in a Ministry office staring at an enchanted window's fake sunlight because she couldn't go back — not yet, not like this. Not as the person who'd been right about the research and wrong about the withholding, arriving at the Manor's door with professional necessity as her excuse and pretending that was her only reason. He would see through it. He saw through things — slowly, carefully, but completely once he did. That was what had gotten them here.

The resonance in her arm spiked at half three. A sudden sharp flare, the ache cresting into something acute, the Mark doing something new or something worse on the other end of the connection. She gripped the edge of her desk and her vision went white for a second. He was in pain. Not the low-grade background throb she'd been carrying but something immediate, an episode, the curse flaring in a way that suggested the Mark's magic was destabilizing along with everything else.

She sat very still. She breathed. The flare subsided after four or five minutes, leaving behind the dull residual pulse that had become her constant companion. He was all right. Or he wasn't all right, but he was alive, and she knew this because the resonance was still there, and the resonance was only there because the connection was still there, and the connection —

She opened the bottom drawer of her desk. Inside: a pair of trainers and a clean jumper for the nights she worked late enough to sleep on the office sofa. She'd worn both dozens of times. They were ordinary. They didn't mean anything. She looked at them for a while, then closed the drawer.

She went home at seven. The Floo dropped her connection twice before she gave up and Apparated. Her flat was cold — she'd left the heating off again, the warming charm she'd cast that morning already faded. She pointed her wand at the radiator and the charm took three attempts to hold, which might have been the ambient field or might have been her.

She ate a tin of soup standing at the kitchen counter because sitting down to eat alone at the table felt like a concession to a permanence she wasn't ready to accept. The soup was lentil. It was fine. She'd been eating fine food for three days — adequate, nutritionally sufficient food consumed without attention or pleasure, fuel rather than sustenance. At the Manor, she'd eaten meals that someone had prepared with thought — Draco made a risotto once, late in the third week, and she'd watched his hands stirring and adding stock and she'd thought, absurdly, that he stirred the way he examined texts, with a patience that was almost compulsive. They'd eaten it in the library, sitting across from each other with books pushed aside, and the risotto had been good and the conversation had been about a fourteenth-century annotation they'd found that morning and the evening had been —

She washed her bowl and put it on the rack and did not think about the risotto.

---

Harry came on Friday.

He didn't owl first. He knocked at her door at half six, still in his work robes, with a bag of takeaway from the Thai place near the Ministry and the particular expression he wore when he'd decided something and was prepared to be patient about seeing it through.

"I brought food," he said. "The Floo's being weird again and I don't want to risk it, so I'm here until it sorts itself out."

She let him in. She didn't have it in her to pretend she wasn't glad to see him, and it was a relief not to pretend, because pretending was what she'd been doing for three days — pretending to the Ministry that her absence from the Manor was a logistical problem, pretending to her mother's unanswered letter that she was busy rather than paralyzed, pretending to herself that the ache in her arm was a diagnostic artefact rather than a tether.

They ate on the sofa. The pad see ew was overcooked and the spring rolls were cold, and she ate anyway, which she hadn't been doing reliably, and Harry noticed this and said nothing about it.

"You look like shit," he said, around a mouthful of noodles.

"Thanks."

"I mean it. When did you last sleep properly?"

"Define properly."

"Hermione."

"I'm sleeping some." She pulled apart a spring roll and ate half of it. The wrapper had gone soggy. "It's been a bad week."

"Yeah." He ate. She ate. Outside, it had been dark since four. The flat's windows showed the street, the skip, a car with its hazard lights blinking. "The Floo's been down twelve hours today. Kingsley's getting pressure from the Wizengamot. There's talk about going public with the decay — actually telling people what's happening rather than pretending the Floo problems are maintenance issues."

"They should. People are going to figure it out regardless. The accidental-magic incidents are going to reach the Prophet eventually."

"Dennis said you were in this morning."

"I was."

He paused over his noodles. "He said you looked like you hadn't slept."

"Dennis is twenty-three and he's doing a job that should be done by three people. He shouldn't be spending his time assessing my sleep habits."

"He wasn't. He was worried." Harry set down his container. "The girl in Bristol. Ruby. She's stable, parents are sorted, there's a team with them. But six incidents in one night, Hermione."

"I know." She put her own food aside. "The cascade is getting worse. The ambient field instabilities I modelled three weeks ago — the projections are tracking, but faster. The decay's accelerating."

"Because?"

"Because the research I was doing at the Manor was slowing it down. Not intentionally — the diagnostic work on the anchoring structure, some of the stabilization methods I was testing through the archive texts — it wasn't a cure, but it was applying pressure in the right direction. And now it's not."

"Because you're not at the Manor."

"Because I'm not at the Manor."

Harry looked at her. He had a direct gaze, the kind that created silences other people felt compelled to fill. She'd known him since she was eleven. She was mostly immune.

"So go back," he said.

"I can't."

"Because of what happened."

"Yes."

He picked up his fork again. Ate another bite. The casualness was very deliberate and she knew it was deliberate and he probably knew she knew.

"Do you want to tell me what actually happened?" he said. "Because the disagreement-about-methodology story is bollocks and we both know it."

"Harry."

"I'm not pushing. I'm asking. There's a difference."

She pulled her knees up onto the sofa and wrapped her arms around them. The radiator had finally stabilized but the warmth didn't reach the centre of the room properly. Her flat had never been well-heated.

"I found something," she said. "In the texts. About the Manor."

"Okay."

"The house does something. To the people living in it. It amplifies things. Emotional — connections. Between its inhabitants. It was built to do that, centuries ago, to strengthen the Malfoy line, and I found the texts that documented the mechanism, and I didn't tell Malfoy." She heard the rush of it, the way the sentence ran downhill. She made herself slow down. "Not immediately. I was going to. I needed to verify the texts, I needed to understand what exactly the mechanism was, I needed—" She stopped. "I didn't tell him."

"How long?"

"Weeks."

Harry's fork paused.

"And he found out on his own," he said. Not a question.

"He found the same texts. He worked out when I must have found them — my behaviour changed afterward, I pulled back, and the timing was obvious. He could see exactly when I'd known and what I'd done since."

"Which was?"

"Stay. Work. Be close. Without telling him what the closeness might mean. What the house might be doing to it." She swallowed. "To us."

The word sat between them.

Harry put his food down.

"So," he said.

"So."

A car went past on the street. The hazard lights on the parked car were still blinking.

"Hermione," Harry said, and his voice had shifted into the register she recognized — not gentle, not careful, just direct in the way he was direct when it mattered, the way that sometimes made people flinch. "What are you angry about?"

"What?"

"Right now. Today. The thing you can't stop thinking about. What is it?"

The answer was immediate: he didn't let me explain, he didn't listen, he decided what it meant without —

Except he hadn't decided what it meant. He'd decided what she'd done, which was different, and he'd been right.

"I'm angry that he—" She stopped. Started over. "He didn't give me a chance to explain. He found the texts and he decided I'd been manipulating him, that I was studying him, like he was a — a subject, a variable, and that's not—"

"But you had been studying him."

Harry said this quietly. He wasn't accusing her. He was saying the thing that was true.

"Not like that."

"Like what, then?"

She didn't answer. The radiator ticked.

"Here's what I think," Harry said. He leaned forward, elbows on his knees, the takeaway bag sliding off the sofa arm. "You can tell me I'm wrong and I'll shut up. That's never actually worked, but you can tell me."

"Go ahead."

"I think you're not angry that he found out. I think you're angry that you weren't ready."

She went still.

"You had a plan," he said. "You always have a plan. You found this thing, and you were going to work through it — verify, analyse, build the whole framework — and then you were going to tell him. On your timeline. In your way. With the answers already sorted before you opened your mouth."

She said nothing.

"And he found out before you were ready. He got there on his own and the whole thing came apart before you could present it, and the thing you're actually angry about isn't the fight. It's that you lost control of how the information got to him."

The flat was quiet. The radiator ticked. Outside, the car alarm that had been threatening to go off all week finally did — a thin, repetitive bleat that cut through the windows and then stopped.

"That's not—" she said, and the sentence died because the denial wasn't true and she could feel it dying as she formed it.

"You were scared," Harry said. Simple, the way he said things. No preamble, no qualifying clause. "You found something that meant every feeling you'd had might not be yours, and that's terrifying. I'd have been terrified. Anyone would've been."

"But?"

"But the thing you did with that fear was turn it into work. You turned it into a research problem. And while you were researching it, he was living in it — feeling everything you were feeling, not knowing there was a question about whether any of it was real. He was just... in it."

She pressed her face against her knees. The fabric of her trousers smelled of the lentil soup she'd spilled earlier and not bothered to change out of.

"You weren't protecting him by not telling," Harry said. "You were protecting yourself. From having to sit in the uncertainty before you had an answer. From having to feel it without understanding it first."

The resonance in her arm ached. Low, steady. He was there, forty miles away in a house that had gone cold without her, and the Mark was hurting him, and she could feel it from here — degraded, imprecise, but present, the connection that persisted whether she wanted it to or not.

"It could all be the Manor," she said into her knees. "Everything I feel. Everything he feels. The whole thing could be the house."

"Could be," Harry said.

"You're not going to tell me it's real?"

"How would I know that?" He shifted on the sofa. His shoulder bumped hers and she didn't move away. "I'm not inside it. I don't know what the house does or doesn't do to people. I'm not going to tell you something I can't know."

"That's surprisingly restrained for you."

"Yeah, well." A pause. "Ginny says I'm getting better at not saying things I'm not sure about. She's probably wrong about that."

Something that was not quite a smile passed across Hermione's face.

"I know you, though," Harry said. He wasn't looking at her. He was looking at the takeaway bag on the floor, or pretending to look at it, giving her the periphery of his attention while the centre of it was on her. "I know what you do when you're scared. You don't run. You organize. You collect data. You build a wall of information between yourself and the thing you're afraid of, and you stand behind it, and you call it thoroughness."

"That's not fair."

"It's a bit fair."

She lifted her head. Her eyes were wet and she didn't bother with that. Harry had seen her cry more times than anyone alive and he'd never once made it strange — not when they were twelve and she'd cried in the bathroom about a troll, not when they were seventeen and she'd cried into the dirt of a war, not now.

"I don't know how to go back," she said.

"Yeah." Harry said. "I know."

"The research — the decay's getting worse, the children, the Floo — I know I need to go back. But I can't go back for the research. If I go back for the research it's the same thing. It's me making it about the work because the work is the part I can control."

Harry looked at her. The look was tired and warm and older than either of them should have been at twenty-five, the kind of look that came from a war and from the years after the war and from the specific, unglamorous work of remaining recognizable to each other through all of it.

"Then don't go back for the research," he said.

She wiped her face with the heel of her hand. She took a breath that shook on the way out and steadied halfway through.

"The pad see ew was terrible," she said.

"I know. The spring rolls were worse."

"I ate two of them."

"I saw. That's how I know it's bad."

They sat. The silence resettled. Harry picked up his container again and ate and didn't look at her, giving her the privacy of his inattention while she reassembled something. He was good at that. He'd always been good at that — the specific generosity of a person who didn't need to see you at your worst in order to know you'd been there.

After a while he said, "Dennis is holding things together. He's good."

"He is."

"He reminds me of Colin sometimes. Not—" Harry stopped. "Just the way he shows up. Like it doesn't occur to him not to."

"I know." She pulled her sleeves down over her hands. Habit. Always habit. "Tell him I'll be in on Monday."

"Tell him yourself. I'm not your secretary."

"You said that last time."

"And you still haven't stopped using me as one."

He left at nine. The Floo was still unreliable, so he Apparated from her doorstep — the crack of displaced air, and then the street was empty and she was standing in the doorway in the cold.

She closed the door.

She sat on the sofa for a long time after he'd gone. The takeaway containers were still on the coffee table. She didn't clear them. The flat was quiet in its ordinary way, the silence of a space that held no opinions about her presence.

The resonance pulsed. Faint, steady, persistent. Draco Malfoy was in pain in a house forty miles away and she could feel it in her arm, a low specific ache that matched nothing in her own body — not her heartbeat, not her breath, but something else, a rhythm she'd learned to recognise during those weeks of sustained contact, her fingers on his wrist, the Mark's restless magic under her diagnostic spells, the way it had quieted when she was there and was not quiet now.

She thought about what Harry had said. Not the whole of it — the whole of it was too much, the kind of comprehensive truth that she'd need to take apart and examine piece by piece before she could hold it all at once. But the centre of it. The thing he'd said that had landed like a stone in water and was still sinking.

_You weren't protecting him. You were protecting yourself._

She thought about the bonding texts. The night she'd found them — alone in the deep archive, Draco asleep two floors above, the Mark's resonance telling her he was dreaming, the specific quality of the ache that meant his sleep was restless. She'd read the texts three times. The first time with the rapid comprehension she brought to any new information. The second time slowly, with her analytical framework engaged, cross-referencing what the texts described against what she'd been experiencing. The third time with her hands shaking, because the mechanism the texts described — amplification of genuine emotional resonance, the house reading the bond between its inhabitants and strengthening it, gating access to deeper knowledge based on the connection's depth — matched everything. The rooms that opened only when they were together. The Mark responding to her touch specifically. The growing awareness of his proximity and his pain. All of it consistent with architecture designed centuries ago to forge bonds between the house's chosen inhabitants.

And she'd closed the folio. And she'd gone to her room. And she'd lain in her bed in a house that was warm around her and she'd thought: I need to verify this before I tell him.

She'd believed this. She still half believed it. Verification was how she worked. You didn't present unconfirmed findings. You didn't alarm people with preliminary data. You built the full picture first.

But Harry was right. The verification hadn't been about rigour. It had been about time — time she'd needed not to understand the mechanism but to decide what she felt about it. Time to sit inside the doubt privately, to turn the question over without anyone watching, to figure out whether what she felt for Draco Malfoy existed independent of a sentient house's intervention before she had to say it out loud and let the saying make it real.

She'd needed to know the answer before she asked the question. And she'd needed that because the question — _is this mine_ — was the most frightening question she'd ever faced, worse than the war, worse than the drawing room, worse than the divorce, because the drawing room had been done to her and the divorce had been a mutual failure but this — this was about whether her own inner life was trustworthy. Whether the feelings she was feeling belonged to her or had been grown in her like a culture in a dish, the Manor providing the medium and the proximity and the warmth and her heart doing what hearts did when conditions were optimal, which was attach.

And while she'd been working through this, privately, protectively, behind the wall of thoroughness Harry had named with such plain accuracy — Draco had been living on the other side of that wall. Touching her hand during examinations. Making risotto. Saying something dry about a translation that had made her laugh. Watching her fall asleep in the library and staying, she now knew, for twenty minutes before making himself leave. Doing all of this without knowing that the woman sitting across from him had information that changed the meaning of every one of those moments.

He'd kissed her without knowing.

She'd kissed him knowing.

And the thing she'd done — the thing she could see now, sitting on her sofa in her cold flat with the resonance pulsing in her arm and the December dark pressing against windows that would never shift for anyone — was not what he'd accused her of. He'd said she'd treated him like a subject. He'd said she'd studied their connection instead of sharing it. And that was partly true but it was the wrong frame, the frame that fit his worst fear, the one where things were done to him by people who held power over his knowledge of his own life.

What she'd actually done was simpler and less forgivable. She'd been afraid. She'd found something that threatened to dissolve the realest thing she'd felt in years, and instead of sitting in that fear with the person who shared it, she'd retreated to the only place she'd ever felt safe: the work. The analysis. The frame where she controlled the variables. She'd made herself the researcher and him the data, not because she didn't care about him but because caring about him without certainty was the one thing her considerable capabilities couldn't help her do.

She'd withheld information about his own life from him. She'd decided what he got to know and when. She'd held the power of that knowledge and used it to buy herself time she hadn't earned.

She understood, sitting in the quiet, what that shape looked like from the inside of his history. She didn't compare it to anything. She didn't need to. The shape was enough.

She pulled her sleeve up. The glamour was intact — she'd refreshed it that morning, automatic as breathing. MUDBLOOD was underneath, the word she'd carried for seven years, carved by a woman who was dead now in a room that still existed in a house she'd learned to love and then left. She'd shown it to no one. She'd let Draco see it — let it be seen, more precisely, when the glamour had failed under sustained casting — and the way he'd touched the letters had been the most careful thing she'd ever felt, his thumb tracing the word that his aunt had put there, in his house, while he'd stood against the wall at seventeen and done nothing.

He hadn't apologized. They'd been past apology by then. Past the clean transactional shape of _I'm sorry_ and _I forgive you_. What he'd said was the truth, which was smaller and larger than either of those things.

And even then — even with his hand on her scar and the truth of what his family had done to her exposed between them — she hadn't told him what she'd found in the bonding texts. Even then.

She pulled her sleeve down.

The resonance didn't change. It wouldn't change. It would be there tomorrow and the day after that, because whatever the Manor's architecture had done to them, it had woven itself into the magic that lived in her, and she couldn't cut it out without understanding it and she couldn't understand it alone and she couldn't understand it without going back.

But Harry was right. She couldn't go back for the understanding.

She turned off the lamp. The sitting room went dark except for the street light through the curtains, the sodium glow that turned everything the same flat amber. She lay back on the sofa because getting up and walking to her bedroom felt like more intention than she had. The cushions held the residual warmth of where Harry had been sitting.

She thought about the Manor. Not the library, not the archive, not the research — the house itself. What it had felt like to live inside something that responded to her. The corridors that shortened when she was tired. The library that surfaced texts she needed before she'd articulated the need. The walls that ran warm near her room in a house that had been cold for years before she'd arrived. She thought about whether she missed the Manor or missed what the Manor had contained, and she thought about how the distinction might not matter as much as she wanted it to.

She thought about a man who held very still during examinations and called it trust. She thought about risotto and translations and the particular quality of his laugh when she caught him off guard, rare and startled and over quickly, as if he'd been surprised by his own capacity for it.

She thought about Harry's question. _What are you angry about?_

She was angry because she'd had something. Something that had reached into the parts of her she'd sealed off after the war and the divorce and the years of competence as the load-bearing wall of her identity. Something that had been real in her body — not just in her mind, not just in the analysis, but in the specific physical fact of his wrist under her fingers and his breath catching when her diagnostic spells went deep and the resonance humming between them like a frequency only they could hear. And instead of trusting it — instead of bringing him the doubt and sitting in it together, two people inside the same uncertainty — she'd done the thing she always did. She'd retreated behind the glass. She'd researched. She'd verified. She'd built the careful conversation she'd planned to have, the one with the answers pre-loaded, the one designed to let her present the problem without being seen inside it.

And the cost of that retreat was everything.

She lay on the sofa in the dark. The flat was quiet. The December wind pressed against the windows and the windows didn't press back. The resonance in her arm pulsed with someone else's pain, steady and distant and hers whether she'd chosen it or not.

She did not sleep well. But she slept.
