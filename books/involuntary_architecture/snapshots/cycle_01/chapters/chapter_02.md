# Chapter 2

The Mark woke him at four again.

Not the old way — not the burn that had pulled like a fishhook behind his navel, the Dark Lord's summons dragging every branded body toward whatever room he'd chosen for that night's audience. That signal had died with the man who cast it. What remained was different: a slow, arrhythmic ache that started in the bones of his forearm and radiated nowhere. It was pain without direction — magic trying to move through a channel that no longer existed, pressing against dead ends in his nervous system until it gave up and pooled, hot and restless, beneath the ink.

Draco lay in the dark and waited for it to pass. His bedroom was too large for one person — his parents' old suite, which he'd moved into three years ago not because he wanted it but because the alternative was the room he'd slept in at sixteen, and that room still smelled faintly of the boy who'd knelt on its floor and dry-heaved after taking the Mark. He'd tried airing it out. He'd tried redecorating. Eventually he'd simply shut the door and left it shut and taken the master suite with its absurd four-poster bed and its windows that looked out onto the east lawns where no one walked.

The ache subsided around five. He got up because there was no reason not to.

The kitchen was at the far end of the ground floor — the servants' kitchen, not the formal one. He'd stopped using the formal kitchen two years ago because it was built for a household staff that no longer existed, and cooking a single egg in it felt like performing a monologue in an empty theatre. The servants' kitchen was smaller, stone-floored, with a window above the basin that let in grey light from the courtyard. He made tea. He made toast and didn't bother with a plate. He ate standing at the counter because sitting at the table meant choosing one of four chairs and the other three being empty, and that was somehow worse than no chairs at all.

His route from the kitchen to the library took him through the east corridor, down the back stair, through the gallery with the portraits — several of whom still tried to speak to him; he'd turned most of them to face the wall — and along a narrow passage that connected to the library's south entrance. It was not the most direct route. The most direct route went through the ground-floor reception rooms, and he didn't take it. He hadn't taken it in years. He didn't think about why, the same way you didn't think about which eye you led with or which hand opened a door — the avoidance was structural, built into his body's map of the house so completely it no longer registered as a choice.

There were other rooms he didn't enter. The east drawing room where his father had held meetings. The conservatory where Aunt Bellatrix had sometimes sat in the evenings, humming to herself. The second-floor study where — but Draco did not catalogue these absences, because cataloguing them would mean acknowledging that his home was as much a map of avoidance as of habitation, and he couldn't afford that thought before his second cup of tea.

The library was the one room that justified the house. Two stories of shelved walls, a mezzanine gallery with rolling ladders, tall windows on the west side that flooded the reading tables with afternoon light. The Malfoy collection was extraordinary — centuries of acquisition, some legitimate, some not, texts in Latin and French and English and languages he couldn't identify, magical treatises and mundane histories and things that were neither, exactly, but sat in the uncomfortable space between. Most of it was uncatalogued. His grandfather had maintained a system; his father had not. Draco had made several attempts, abandoned each one when the scale of it overwhelmed him. The books were patient. They had outlasted better Malfoys than him, and they would outlast him too.

He settled into the chair by the south window — his chair, shaped to him by years of the same posture — and opened the text he'd been working through for the past week. A seventeenth-century treatise on sympathetic magic, dry and dense and entirely unhelpful, which was standard. He'd been looking for anything related to the Mark's new behaviour for eight months. The Healers at St. Mungo's had refused to examine it — Dark Lord magic, liability concerns, the very specific discomfort that crossed their faces when he showed them the brand and they understood what it was and what it meant about the arm it was on. Two private practitioners had attempted diagnostic spells. The first had recoiled physically. The second had completed the examination and told him, with genuine regret, that she couldn't help: the magic was layered in ways that exceeded her training, the curse structure unlike anything in the standard literature. She'd suggested he try the Unspeakables. He'd laughed, not unkindly. Unspeakables didn't take walk-ins from former Death Eaters.

So he read. He searched his own family's library for something — a reference, a precedent, a theory — that would explain why a dead man's brand was waking up seven years after the man had died. He found nothing. The Dark Lord's innovations had been his own; the Death Eaters had not been given schematics.

The Mark pulsed, and he pressed his thumb hard against it through his sleeve until the pressure matched the ache and the two cancelled each other out, mostly. It was an old trick. It didn't work as well anymore.

The house was quieter in the cold months. He'd grown up in this building and he knew its seasons — the way it expanded in summer, rooms breathing, curtains lifting in air that came from nowhere; the way it contracted in winter, pulling inward, corridors narrower, certain wings going dark as if the Manor itself were conserving heat. In November the contraction was well underway. The east wing had gone cold weeks ago, the upper corridors drafty, the rooms above the library settling into a silence that felt more chosen than accidental. He didn't anthropomorphize the house. He didn't call it moody or sulking or alive, because those words would have required him to reconsider the basic terms of his cohabitation with the place, and he was not prepared for that renegotiation. But there were mornings — this morning, for instance, the Mark pulsing and the November dark pressing against the windows — when the Manor felt less like a house and more like something he was living inside of, the distinction between container and companion less stable than he'd have liked.

His mother hadn't visited in two weeks. This was deliberate, and he knew it, because Narcissa Malfoy did not do anything accidentally. She'd moved out of the Manor three years ago — a townhouse in Bath, tasteful and proportionate, rooms that stayed where you left them. She'd been diplomatic about it. *The Manor is yours, darling. It needs someone in residence.* She'd never said *I can't live here anymore*, but the way she'd touched the front doorframe on her way out — two fingers pressed flat against the stone, held for a beat too long — had said it plainly enough. The house had done something to her over twenty-five years of marriage, something she didn't discuss, and Draco hadn't asked. Asking would mean acknowledging that the house did things to people, and he still preferred to think of the Manor as architecture. Stone and timber and old magic. Not something with preferences. Not something that listened.

He read until noon. The treatise yielded nothing. He ate lunch alone — soup, bread, an apple he forgot about and found later on the library side table, browning against the wood. He walked the east lawn in the thin November light because his body needed to move and the grounds were the only space large enough to accommodate motion without routing him past rooms he'd rather not see. The Mark ached steadily, a low-grade persistence that he'd learned to fold into the background noise of his body the way you folded a ringing in your ears — always there, beneath everything, no longer remarkable except in its occasional absence.

He was twenty-five years old and he lived alone in a house built for a family and a staff of twelve, and his days had the precise, unvarying quality of someone who had methodically eliminated every variable that might produce surprise, because surprise, in his experience, preceded damage.

This was the shape of his life. He didn't feel sorry for himself. That was a habit he'd broken in the first year after the trial, when self-pity had threatened to become his organizing principle and he'd recognized, with the cold clarity that was either his best quality or his worst, that if he went down that road he wasn't coming back. So he'd built routine instead. Tea, library, grounds, tea, library, dinner, the upper corridor at dusk where the windows caught the last light, then his room, where he slept badly and woke to the Mark and started again. It worked. It was enough. It had to be enough, because the alternative was admitting that it wasn't, and then what.

The owl arrived at half three.

Ministry seal. Departmental stationery — Department of Mysteries, which meant someone with clearance and a specific kind of authority. He opened it at the library desk, because that was where he opened mail, because routine was the architecture of a manageable life.

The letter was from Granger.

He read it twice. The first time for content: she was requesting access to the Malfoy library for research purposes related to the ongoing investigation into systemic magical decay; the Ministry would provide a standard stipend for room and board during the research period; she anticipated a minimum stay of several weeks; she would require unrestricted access to all library holdings including restricted sections. It was formal, precisely worded, on departmental letterhead.

He read it a second time for what it didn't say. That she hadn't set foot in this house since the night she'd been dragged across the floor of a room he no longer entered. That the last time they'd spoken was his trial, seven years ago, where she'd testified on his behalf — measured, factual, unsparing of both of them — and he'd sat in the accused's chair and not said a single word to her, not before and not after, because there was no word in any language that could do the work required of it, and an inadequate word would have been worse than silence. That they hadn't spoken since. That she knew, presumably, what she was asking — to be inside this house, under this roof, working in a room that existed because his family had spent centuries accumulating things, some beautiful, some stolen, some dark, and the distinctions between those categories were not always clear.

And underneath all of it — carefully not written, but legible in the bureaucratic framing, the departmental authority, the professional distance of the request — the fact that Hermione Granger would not have come to him unless every other option was exhausted. He was the last door she'd tried, because this door required entering the building where she'd been tortured.

He set the letter on the desk. The Mark pulsed, sharper than its usual rhythm, and he pressed his thumb against it and thought about the shape of what she was asking.

She needed the library. And he needed — the thought formed before he could redirect it, specific and humiliating in its directness — he needed someone who could look at the thing on his arm and not flinch.

He wrote back that evening. He agreed to her terms. He proposed additional ones.

---

The morning she was due, Draco cleaned.

Not the whole Manor — that project belonged to someone with staff or delusions — but the library, the guest wing, the kitchen, and the corridors between them. He spelled dust from shelves and swept stone floors and replaced the candles in the library's wall sconces because half of them had burned down to nothing months ago and he hadn't bothered, because who was going to notice. He observed, as he worked, that this was the most sustained effort he'd put into the house's appearance in over a year. He observed himself observing this. He didn't like either observation.

The guest wing was in the east arm of the Manor, up a flight and across a landing. He hadn't entered these rooms in — he tried to remember and couldn't place a specific date, which was itself an answer. The beds were held by preservation charms, unchanged since whoever had last stayed. The air had the stale quality of space that had been sealed too long, the particular staleness of rooms where time had not so much stopped as lost interest. He opened a window and the November cold came in like a correction.

He was in the entrance hall by eleven because that was where you received guests, and she was a guest, and he was going to do this correctly if he did nothing else correctly for the rest of the arrangement. The entrance hall was the one formal space he'd maintained throughout his years alone: high ceiling, the floor black-and-white marble in the Malfoy diamond pattern, a staircase wide enough for four people abreast. His family had always understood entrances. It was the rest of the interaction they'd consistently botched.

He heard the Apparition crack from the drive. She hadn't used the Floo, which meant either the network was acting up again or she'd chosen the method that left her outside the wards, where she'd have to walk the drive and ring the bell rather than appearing directly in his fireplace. He thought that was probably deliberate. She was giving both of them the distance of a door between them.

He opened it before she could knock.

She looked the same and entirely different, which was the kind of contradiction that only applied to people you hadn't seen in years. Hermione Granger stood on his front step in a dark coat, a leather bag over one shoulder, her hair pulled back and already escaping in the way it always had. She was smaller than he remembered, or he'd made her larger in the years of not seeing her, built her up into the version that existed in courtrooms and Ministry corridors and the stories the Prophet ran about the war's brightest veteran. The actual woman on his doorstep was just a woman — specific, present, and wearing an expression he recognized: she'd prepared for this, organized her approach, decided how the first thirty seconds would go. He'd seen it at the trial. He'd seen it across a courtroom when she was twenty years old and calmly describing to a panel of wizards the specific ways in which his family's actions had been monstrous and the ways in which his, specifically, had been coerced, and she'd been right about all of it, and he had sat there and let her be right because his own words would have been insufficient.

"Malfoy," she said.

"Granger."

She didn't offer her hand. Neither did he. The absence of the gesture was its own negotiation — two people acknowledging that they weren't going to pretend this was a normal professional meeting between people with a normal history.

"Come in."

She stepped past him and he caught the precise moment her body registered the Manor — a slight change in her breathing, a tension across her shoulders that appeared and was controlled in the same motion. She was in. She was inside the house where it had happened, and her composure was a wall she'd built before she arrived and was maintaining through the kind of effort that didn't show unless you knew what controlled effort looked like. He knew. He'd been building similar walls for seven years.

The entrance hall held them in its formal hush. Granger looked at the marble floor, the staircase, the arched ceiling with the specific gaze of someone who was cataloguing — comparing what she saw now against what she remembered, and finding the differences insufficient.

"The library is through the east corridor," he said. "I'll show you after we've discussed terms."

"Your letter mentioned additional conditions."

"It did."

He led her to the morning room — small, south-facing, used for household business by three generations of Malfoys and therefore as close to neutral as any room in this house could claim. He'd set tea there, which was either hospitality or habit. She sat in the chair across from the desk without waiting to be offered it, and he sat behind the desk because the desk was useful, because it put a surface between them, because he'd been raised in rooms where furniture did political work and the habit hadn't left him.

"You'll have full access to the library," he said. "All holdings, including restricted sections. I've nothing to hide in there that you couldn't get a Ministry warrant for."

"I appreciate that."

"The guest wing is functional. It's in the east arm — I'll walk you through. You'll have your own rooms, private entrance. The kitchen's shared — I eat there, you're welcome to use it. The rest of the house —" He paused. The sentence wanted to go somewhere specific and he redirected it. "The rest of the house is yours to move through as needed. I'd ask you not to enter private rooms without notice, but I don't expect that'll be a problem."

"It won't."

This part was easy. This was logistics, and logistics were a language they could both speak without anything underneath it getting loose.

"My additional condition," he said.

She waited. Her hands were in her lap, still, and he noticed that her left sleeve was pulled down to her wrist — long, covering, the kind of habitual coverage that could pass for style if you didn't know what it was covering. He noticed it the way he noticed everything about her position in this room: with the involuntary hyperawareness of someone who had spent the worst night of his life watching her on the floor of a room not far from this one, and who'd been reading her body language against that memory since the moment she'd walked through his front door, checking for — what. He didn't want to finish the thought. He checked anyway.

"The Mark," he said.

He pushed up his left sleeve. He did it without preamble because there was no version of this reveal that benefited from buildup, and because if she was going to flinch he wanted to know now, before they were deeper into the arrangement. The Mark was there on his forearm, the skull and serpent, darker than it had been in years — the ink, or whatever the brand was made of, had deepened as the pain increased, as though the magic in it were condensing, pulling tighter into itself.

Granger looked at it. She did not flinch. Her eyes moved across the Mark with the precise, lateral scanning that he recognized from the trial — the way she processed information, absorbing the full shape of it before she let herself react. He could see her noting the colour, the slightly raised quality of the skin around the edges, the faint shimmer where the residual magic was active enough to be visible in the morning-room light.

"It's been waking up," he said. "Not the summoning — that's gone. This is different. Pain without a signal. The magic in it's restless. It's trying to do something, but whatever it was connected to is dead, and it's just — " He stopped himself before the sentence turned into something that sounded like asking for sympathy. "I've had two private Healers look at it. Neither could help. St. Mungo's won't touch it."

"When did it start?"

"Eight months ago, give or take. Occasional at first — nighttime, mostly. Now it's more or less constant."

She leaned forward slightly. Not toward the Mark but toward the problem. He could see the shift in her — from the guarded woman who'd stood on his doorstep to something else, something closer to the version of her he remembered from school, the one who'd vibrated at a frequency he could almost hear in the presence of a question she didn't yet have the answer to.

"Can I?" she said.

He understood. He extended his arm across the desk.

Her fingers were warm, which he hadn't expected — or hadn't allowed himself to think about in advance, because thinking about the temperature of Hermione Granger's hands while she touched the brand that defined what he was would have required a level of emotional preparation he hadn't managed. She held his wrist — clinical, professional, her thumb bracing against his pulse point in a way that was entirely diagnostic, and if his pulse responded to the contact she gave no sign of noticing.

She cast a preliminary diagnostic spell. He felt it: a cool wash of her magic against the Mark, careful and layered, nothing like the blunt probes the Healers had used. It moved across the surface of the curse like a hand testing water temperature, and the Mark — the Mark did something it had never done before under examination. It quieted. Not fully, not like the pain was gone, but the restless thrashing that had been his constant companion for eight months eased, pulled back, settled into something almost bearable. And then it shifted, oriented itself toward her spell the way a compass needle finds north. The ache gathered at the point where her thumb pressed against his wrist, concentrated there, as though the dark magic in the brand were reaching for her diagnostic contact.

He nearly pulled his arm back. He didn't. Her fingers tightened fractionally on his wrist, and he could feel her pulse in her fingertips against his skin, or he was imagining it, and either possibility was worse than the other.

"That's not standard curse behaviour," she said.

"No."

"The magic is layered. There's the original brand — the Dark Lord's casting — and then something underneath it. Older, or different, at least. I'd need sustained diagnostic contact to map the structure properly." She released his wrist and he pulled his arm back and rolled down his sleeve. "What are you proposing?"

"You get the library. Full access, for as long as your research requires. I get your expertise on the Mark. Sustained examination, diagnostic work, whatever you need to do to understand what it's doing and whether it can be stopped or managed."

"You want me to treat a curse that two Healers couldn't."

"I want you to study it. You're —" He caught the word *better* before it landed, because it would have sounded like flattery and this wasn't a room where flattery would serve either of them. "You're the most qualified person to examine magic this complex who'd also be willing to examine it on me."

The second clause sat between them. *Who'd also be willing.* Who would look at a Death Eater's mark and not refuse on principle, on disgust, on the reasonable grounds that the man who bore it had earned it and might deserve whatever it was doing to him now. He hadn't meant to say it so plainly. He saw her register the honesty of it — something shifting in her expression, a recalculation he could watch happen, the moment when she heard what he'd actually said underneath the words he'd chosen.

"The terms are professional," she said.

"Entirely."

"Last names. Clear boundaries around the examination work. I'll keep records and share them with you. If I find something treatable, I'll recommend a course of action. If I find something dangerous — to you or to anyone else — I'll tell you directly."

"Agreed."

"And the research comes first. The library is my reason for being here. The Mark work is a secondary engagement."

"Understood."

She looked at him for a long moment. He let her. This was the part where she decided whether he was tolerable — not whether he was trustworthy, because that question had been litigated at the trial and they both knew the verdict, but whether she could exist in proximity to him for weeks without the proximity becoming something neither of them could manage. He didn't know what she saw. He hoped it was close enough to the truth: a man who was tired and in pain and asking for help in the only way he could manage, which was by offering something she needed in return, so that neither of them had to call it what it was.

"All right," she said.

He nodded. She nodded. The quiet that followed was not comfortable, but it was functional — the silence of two people who had agreed on the shape of something and were now sitting with the weight of what the shape contained. He poured tea because the pot was there and because the act of pouring gave his hands something to do that wasn't pressing against the Mark or gripping the edge of the desk. She took the cup and held it without drinking, and the steam rose between them in the morning-room light, and neither of them said the thing they were both thinking, which was that seven years of silence had produced this: a desk and a teapot and an arrangement that was professional and civil and not even close to sufficient.

"I'll show you the library first," he said. "Then the guest wing."

---

The library earned the silence he saw cross her face when they walked in.

He took his usual route — the east corridor, the back passage, avoiding the ground-floor reception rooms — and if she noticed the circuitous path she didn't comment on it. Then through the south entrance, and her reaction was visible in spite of herself: a caught breath, a half-step that slowed, her eyes pulling upward to the mezzanine gallery and the shelved walls that reached the ceiling, two stories of accumulated Malfoy intellect and acquisition and, in some cases, theft.

"Your catalogue," she said, after a moment.

"Nonexistent, mostly. My grandfather kept a ledger. It covers roughly a third of the holdings and hasn't been updated since 1971."

"The restricted section?"

"North wall, behind the screen. Warded, but I'll key you in. Most of it's Dark Arts adjacent rather than strictly Dark — theory, history, magical taxonomy. Some of it's genuinely dangerous. You'll know which."

She was already moving through the space, her hand trailing along a shelf spine, tilting her head to read titles. He watched the transformation happen: the guarded woman from the morning room was still present, still holding, but layered over now by something else — the researcher, the person who'd probably been the real one underneath the composure all along. She pulled a text from a shelf, opened it, frowned at the language, replaced it, moved to the next section. He could see her building a system already — not physically, not yet, but in her head, the preliminary architecture of how she was going to approach several centuries of accumulated disorder.

He let her look. He leaned against the doorframe with his arms crossed and watched Hermione Granger fall in love with his library, which was the only kind of falling any version of this arrangement was supposed to involve.

She turned back to him after a few minutes. "This will take longer than several weeks."

"I'm not going anywhere."

"No," she said, and something in the word acknowledged more than the library. "I don't suppose you are."

He showed her the rest of the ground floor that he was willing to show. The morning room she'd already seen. The kitchen — the servants' kitchen, the real one — where the stone floor was clean but uneven and the window above the basin let in the flat light of a November afternoon. She looked at the kitchen the way she'd looked at everything: cataloguing, measuring, filing. He watched her note the single mug on the drying rack, the single plate, the economies of a solitary life visible in the domestic evidence, and he watched her choose not to remark on it.

The back corridor to the gardens. The passage that connected the kitchen wing to the library. He did not show her the west wing. He did not comment on the doors they passed without opening, or the corridors that branched off the main route and led to parts of the house he hadn't entered in years. She didn't ask.

Then the guest wing. Up the main stair, across the landing, into the east arm. The corridor here was panelled in dark wood, lit by sconces that had been enchanted centuries ago to respond to presence — they brightened as he and Granger passed, which was the standard old charm, keyed to body heat and proximity. The guest rooms were at the corridor's end: a bedroom, a sitting room, a private bath. The windows looked out onto the east lawns and, past them, the tree line at the estate's edge. The November light through the glass was thin and grey, the kind of light that revealed rather than flattered — it made the room feel honest in a way the warmer rooms downstairs, with their charmed sconces and heavy curtains, did not.

"It's adequate," she said, which he understood was her version of generous.

"There are preservation charms on the bedding. The bath plumbing is temperamental — the hot tap sometimes has ideas of its own. I'd call it charming, but I think the accurate word is malfunctioning."

Something crossed her face — not a smile, not quite, but the place where a smile would have gone if the circumstances had been different or if either of them had been someone else. He filed it away. He would take what was available.

"If you need anything adjusted, let me know. I'll be —" He gestured vaguely at the house beyond the doorway, which was absurd, because the gesture encompassed thirty-six rooms and he occupied maybe four of them, and the gap between the sweep of his hand and the reality of his life was visible enough that he saw her see it. "Around."

"Thank you, Malfoy."

"Granger."

He left her to settle in. The walk back to the library was the usual route: east corridor to the landing, down the main stair, through the back passage toward the portrait gallery.

Except that at the base of the stairs, where the passage should have turned left toward the gallery, there was a corridor he didn't recognize.

It wasn't new. The stonework was old, matching the rest of the ground floor — the same grey limestone the whole house was built from. But it was a corridor he hadn't walked in years, a shortcut that connected the base of the east staircase directly to the library's north entrance. He'd used it as a child, he was fairly sure — there was a memory, vague and uncertain, of running through it, his mother's voice behind him telling him to slow down — but it had been closed for as long as he could easily remember. Doors in the Manor didn't always stay where they were. Corridors silted up, filled themselves with whatever the house used to fill spaces it was done with. He'd stopped questioning that years ago. The house had its habits, and he had his, and they coexisted.

But this corridor was open now. The sconces along its walls were lit, warm and steady, the air inside moving faintly as though the passage had drawn a breath. It had not been open this morning. He'd walked past this exact spot on his way to the kitchen and there had been wall here — solid stone, unremarkable, the same wall that had been here every day of his adult life.

He stood at the corridor's mouth and looked down its length to the library door at the far end, which he could see from here, standing slightly ajar, the warm light of the library spilling through the gap. The corridor was perhaps forty feet long. Clean stone. No dust, which was wrong — a passage that had been sealed for years should have been thick with it. The air should have been stale. Instead the corridor smelled faintly of the library itself, of old paper and preservation charms, as if the house had prepared the space and cleaned it before offering it up.

The house had opened this corridor. The house had opened it now — not this morning, not last week, not in the months since his mother had stopped visiting regularly, not in the years since his father had been taken to Azkaban. Now. This afternoon. Within an hour of Hermione Granger walking through the front door.

He felt something then — not the Mark, for once, but something else entirely. A shift in the house's attention, the way you might feel a large animal turn its head in the dark. The walls around him were the same walls he'd known his whole life, and they were not the same, because whatever animated them had stirred — woken up or turned over or simply noticed, for the first time in a long time, that something in the house had changed.

Draco stepped into the corridor. The sconces brightened around him. His footsteps echoed on the stone — a different echo than his usual routes produced, fuller, the sound of a passage that had been quiet too long now carrying noise again.

He walked through to the library. The corridor delivered him to the north entrance, which put him three steps from the reading tables, four from his chair. A shortcut. A gift, if you were inclined to call it that. He was not yet inclined to call it anything.

He sat down and opened his book and did not mention the corridor to anyone.

The house had noticed her. He would sit with that knowledge quietly, turning it over, not yet willing to name what it might mean. The Manor had noticed Hermione Granger and it had changed for her, opened something for her, in a way it hadn't changed for him in all the years he'd lived here alone. He added it to the list of things he registered and did not speak about. The list was long. It was the only filing system in the Manor that was reliably maintained.

Upstairs, he could hear her footsteps moving through the guest wing. The Manor's old stones carried sound unpredictably — some rooms were silent as crypts and others transmitted every shift and creak — and her footsteps reached him through whatever combination of acoustic chance and architectural intention delivered them. She was unpacking, or pacing, or standing in a room that was not hers in a house that was not hers, getting her bearings inside the place where the worst thing that had ever happened to her had happened.

He turned a page he hadn't read. He stared at words he didn't absorb. He thought about the corridor and the sconces and the seven years of silence between them that had ended today with last names and a desk between them and terms that were professional and clear and nowhere near sufficient for what they were actually doing, which was agreeing to live inside the same walls — the two of them, in the house that remembered everything they'd both spent seven years trying to forget.
