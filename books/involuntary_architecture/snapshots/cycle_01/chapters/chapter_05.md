# Chapter 5

She'd been at the Manor for eleven days, and the mornings had developed a rhythm she hadn't asked for and didn't resist.

Up at six, because the light in the guest room entered through an east-facing window the house had angled, she suspected, to catch the first grey of dawn. Tea from the small pot that appeared on the side table — she hadn't worked out whether a house-elf was delivering it silently or whether the Manor was doing it, and she'd decided not to investigate because the answer to either question was uncomfortable in different ways. Dress, check the glamour on her forearm — a quick pass of her wand, the skin smoothing over the scar tissue until it looked like any other patch of arm — and then the walk to the library, which the house had been keeping consistent at about four minutes, though the route was never identical twice.

She was settling in. That was the word for it, and she didn't like it. Settling in implied duration, implied a provisional domesticity that was not part of the arrangement. She was here to research the library and examine the Mark. She had a room in London with a functional kitchen and a bed that didn't rearrange its own headboard, and she would go back to it when the work was done.

But the mornings had a rhythm, and the library was extraordinary, and the work was the kind of work that made her feel like herself in a way she hadn't felt since — she didn't finish the thought. She went to the library.

Malfoy was already there. He was often there before her, which she'd found irritating for the first few days because she was used to being the earliest person in any room and it felt competitive, which was childish, which she recognized and could not entirely suppress. By the second week the irritation had faded into something else — not quite comfort but the reliable presence of someone who took the work as seriously as she did. He looked up when she came in, nodded once, and returned to a text he'd been reading. His tea was half-finished. The lamp nearest his end of the table was lit against the grey morning.

The argument started over a word.

*Deperdere*, in a text dated 1643, written in the cramped hand of Abraxas Malfoy's great-great-grandfather. The standard Latin translation was *to lose, to destroy*, but Malfoy was insisting the family usage was narrower — that in the Malfoy notation, the word had acquired a specific technical meaning closer to *to disperse irretrievably*, and that the distinction mattered for their working model of magical decay.

"It's not a synonym for *amittere*," he said, turning the text toward her. His finger traced the line where the word appeared in a longer passage about anchoring infrastructure. "My grandfather's journals use the same construction. It's about dissipation, not loss. The magic isn't being taken or destroyed. It's bleeding out."

She'd been annotating a parallel text — a theoretical treatise from the 1790s that used standard Latin without the family modifications — and she could see what he meant but didn't agree with his conclusion. "The distinction only holds if you assume the original builders conceived of ambient magic as a contained system. Which these texts don't support." She pulled her own volume closer and found the passage she wanted. "Look — here. *Fundamentum magiae est fluvialis, non lacustris.* 'The foundation of magic is riverine, not lacustrine.' They're describing flow, not containment. So *deperdere* in context means dissipation from a system that was already in motion, not leakage from a sealed—"

"I'm not arguing it was sealed. I'm arguing there's a difference between natural flow loss and structural hemorrhage, and this word" — he tapped the page — "is the one they used for the second."

Hermione sat back. The library was quiet around them, the late afternoon light slanting through the high windows and catching dust motes in long diagonal lines. The desk they'd claimed — the largest of the reading tables, close to the east wall where the catalogued texts were shelved — was covered in books, scrolls, her notes, his annotations, two cold cups of tea.

She reread the passage. He was right. Not entirely — the theoretical framework she'd identified was also correct — but the family-specific usage added a layer she'd missed. The Malfoys who'd built the infrastructure had distinguished between natural attrition and catastrophic failure, and they'd built separate vocabulary for each.

"Fine," she said. "You're right about the word. But the riverine model still holds for the broader system."

"Obviously."

"So we need both frameworks. The macro model is flow-based, but the failure mode the texts describe is structural. Which means the decay we're seeing now isn't the system running down — it's the system breaking."

Malfoy was already writing. His handwriting was small and even, nothing like the erratic scrawl of his ancestor. He noted the distinction in the margin of his working copy, cross-referenced the passage numbers, and added a line she couldn't read from his angle.

She found herself watching him work. He had a way of going still when he was thinking — not the rigid stillness of the Mark examinations, but a quieter thing, his pen paused over the page, his eyes unfocused on the middle distance. Then the pen would move again, and whatever he'd been working through was already resolved in his head before the first word hit the parchment.

It had been years since she'd worked with someone who could do this. Harry was brilliant in his own way — instinctive, brave, capable of leaps she couldn't make — but the research had always been hers alone. Ron had been better at strategy than he'd ever got credit for but had never cared about the puzzle for its own sake. Her colleagues at the Ministry were competent. Some of them were good.

Malfoy was good. He was also fast, precise, and willing to be wrong, which was rarer than being smart. He'd conceded three points to her this week without making a production of it, and he'd been right about two things she'd missed, and neither of them had pretended the score didn't matter because it did, in the way that scores matter between people who are serious about the work.

"There's a third instance," she said, pulling another text from the stack at her elbow. "Seventeen-twelve. Different author — looks like the usage survived across at least three generations."

He reached for it without looking up from his annotation. Their fingers didn't touch — the book was large enough that the handoff was unremarkable — but she was aware of the distance in a way she hadn't been a week ago. An inch of air between his hand and hers over a leather binding.

She turned back to her own work.

They stayed in the library until the light failed. The house did something to the lamps — they brightened gradually as the windows darkened, the transition smooth enough that she'd lost track of sunset entirely the first time it happened and had looked up to find the sky black and the room golden and Malfoy still reading across from her as though neither of them had anywhere else to be.

Which, she supposed, they didn't.

"The Floo in the east parlor has been unreliable," he said, not looking up. "If you need to contact the Ministry, the study fireplace is more stable."

"Thanks."

"Mm."

She made a note about the seventeen-twelve text and added it to her reference chain. The translation work was slow, the kind of slow that felt productive rather than frustrating — each text opened connections to three more, and the network of cross-references she was building had started to take on its own shape, pointing toward something she couldn't quite see yet but could feel the outline of. Like excavating a fossil. Brush away enough sediment and the shape declares itself.

"Are you hungry?" he asked.

She checked. She was, though she hadn't noticed. "Yeah, probably."

"The kitchen puts out supper at seven. It's" — he looked at the clock on the far wall — "twenty past."

"I can eat late."

"You could also eat on time."

There was nothing in his tone. It was a flat observation, the kind of thing anyone might say. She thought about arguing and then didn't, because there wasn't anything to argue with. She was hungry and it was past seven and the text would be there in the morning.

"Fine," she said, and closed the book, and if she noticed that he'd waited until she moved before he stood, she didn't examine why.

---

The meal was simple — roasted chicken, potatoes, greens, bread that was still warm. The house-elves at the Manor operated at a level of quiet competence that she found both impressive and politically uncomfortable, a familiar tension she'd long since learned to hold without resolving. Malfoy ate neatly, without conversation at first. The dining room they used — not the formal one, a smaller room near the kitchen that felt like it had been a breakfast room in some earlier configuration of the house — had windows that looked out over the west grounds, though at this hour there was nothing to see but their own reflections in the glass and the dark beyond.

"The seventeen-twelve text references something called the Tethys Working," she said, because the silence had settled and she'd rather fill it with something useful than let it become an object. "Have you come across that?"

"Once. In my grandfather's library, not here. He had a manuscript — handwritten, no attribution. I think it was a copy of something older."

"Can you get it?"

"I can look. He kept his personal collection at the townhouse. My mother might know where it ended up."

She nodded and ate a piece of potato and didn't say anything else for a while. The bread was very good. She tore a piece and ate it with butter, and the butter dish had migrated closer to her side of the table at some point during the meal, which she registered without comment because the house did things like that now and she'd stopped being startled by small courtesies from the architecture.

"How's the Mark?" she asked.

"The same."

"The same as when?"

"The same as yesterday. Active. Not worsening."

"Is that true, or is that what you think I want to hear?"

He looked at her for the first time since they'd sat down. His eyes were grey and steady and slightly amused in a way that didn't quite reach his mouth. "It's true. I'd tell you if it changed. You'd need to adjust your diagnostic approach."

"Right," she said. "Good."

"Granger."

"Mm."

"Eat the greens."

She ate the greens. They were fine.

They cleared the table together — not by arrangement, but by a kind of mutual practicality that had developed without discussion. She stacked the plates and he vanished them to the kitchen with a flick of his wand, a bit of household magic so casual it was nearly Muggle in its domesticity. The bread basket lingered on the table, and she took the last piece and ate it standing up, which felt like something a person who lived here would do and she pushed that thought down before it could finish forming.

"I'm going to write up the model tonight," she said. "The two-framework distinction. I want to get the terminology right before we go further into the seventeen-twelve text."

"I'll check the notation in the morning. If I'm wrong about the family usage, better to catch it before you've built a reference chain on it."

"You're not wrong."

He paused at the door. Almost smiled — or the muscles near his mouth moved in a direction that, in someone less guarded, would have become a smile. "I might be wrong."

"You're not wrong about this."

"Well." He looked at the door frame rather than at her. "Goodnight, Granger."

"Goodnight."

He left. She heard his footsteps on the stone of the corridor — sharp at first, then softening as the house absorbed the sound, as it always did, folding each person's movement back into its own quiet.

She wiped a crumb off the table and stood there for a moment longer than necessary, in the small warm room with its dark windows and its cleared table and the faint smell of rosemary from the chicken that was already being taken apart by efficient hands somewhere in the depths of the house.

---

It happened on the walk back to the guest wing.

The route the Manor gave her that evening was straightforward — a single long corridor from the kitchen end of the house to the guest wing, lined with portraits that had mostly given up trying to talk to her. She was thinking about the Tethys Working, about the structural-hemorrhage distinction, about whether the seventeen-twelve text's description of magical infrastructure could be reconciled with the theoretical models the Ministry currently used, and she was not thinking about Malfoy at all when she became aware that he was on the third floor.

She stopped walking.

It was not a thought. It was not a guess. She could feel his position in the house the way she could feel the weight of the wand in her pocket — present, specific, directionally certain. Third floor, east side. His rooms. He'd gone upstairs after dinner while she'd stayed to finish her tea, and she hadn't heard his footsteps or marked his direction, and she knew where he was.

Under that awareness, lower and steadier: a dull ache in her left forearm, not the scar. The scar's pain, when it came, had a hot scratchy quality, like a healing burn that had never quite finished healing. This was different. Deeper. A slow pulse of something that felt borrowed, as though her arm had picked up a frequency that belonged to a different body.

She pressed her palm flat against her forearm through her sleeve and felt the ache shift but not subside. It wasn't in her skin or her muscle. It was somewhere else — in the diagnostic trace, maybe, the residual magical connection she'd built over three examinations of the Mark, hours of sustained contact with the curse-magic in his arm. She'd read about this. Sustained diagnostic exposure to heavily cursed objects could create temporary trace bonds — sensory artifacts, usually minor, usually transient. The standard recommendation was reduced contact frequency and time.

She pressed harder and the ache sharpened, briefly, into something that felt like his — the specific restless quality of the Mark's new pain, the magic trying to move through a channel that connected to nothing.

Then it faded to its earlier level. She was standing in a corridor lined with sleeping portraits, her hand clamped around her own forearm, her breathing faster than it should have been. The Manor was quiet around her. A lamp at the far end of the corridor flickered, steadied, and the shadows rearranged themselves along the stone.

She let go of her arm.

Diagnostic residue. That's what it was. She'd been maintaining sustained spell-contact with an unusually active Dark artifact, and the trace connection was an expected side effect. It would degrade with reduced exposure, and if it didn't, she could modulate her diagnostic approach to minimize the resonance.

She walked the rest of the corridor to her room, and when she closed the door behind her she stood with her back against it and made herself think clearly.

The trace-bond literature she'd read at the Ministry — Belby's monograph, the St. Mungo's protocol for cursed-object examiners — described residual sensory artifacts as minor, transient, and non-directional. A faint echo of the curse's quality, usually lasting hours, occasionally days. What she'd just experienced was directional. It had located him in the house with a specificity that residual traces didn't explain. She could either revise her model to account for the Mark's unusual properties — an active, reactivating curse rather than a stable dark artifact — or she could acknowledge that this was something the literature hadn't documented because it wasn't supposed to happen.

She chose the first option, because the first option was the one that let her keep working. The Mark was unusual. Everything about this case was unusual. That's why she was here, and that's why the standard literature didn't apply cleanly.

She unpacked her notes and worked for another two hours at the small desk the house had placed under the window, and she did not catalogue the exact moment the ache returned — faintly, like a radio signal at the edge of reception — or the way it told her, without any mechanism she could name, that he was still awake.

At eleven she put out the lamp. At midnight she was still lying in the dark with her left arm resting on the covers, her fingertips against the inside of her forearm, feeling something that was not hers pulse faintly against the scar that was.

She would tell him if it persisted. There was no reason not to. It was a clinical finding, relevant to their diagnostic protocol, and withholding it would compromise the quality of the data they were collecting.

She rolled over. The mattress was good — better than her bed in London, which had been adequate for years and would have to go on being adequate. The sheets smelled faintly of lavender and something older, a cedar preservation charm that the house had been laying down for centuries.

She'd tell him tomorrow, if it was still there.

She would not tell him tomorrow. She knew this in the same way she knew where he was in the house — not as a decision she'd made but as a fact her body had already settled. The information felt private in a way that had nothing to do with clinical relevance and everything to do with the fact that she could feel him in his rooms, the ache thinning as the Mark's activity ebbed toward whatever passed for rest in cursed tissue, and the intimacy of knowing this — of being inside the peripheral weather of his body without his knowledge or consent — was something she was not prepared to hand over.

It was diagnostic residue. It would fade.

She closed her eyes and let the ache keep her company, which was not the same thing as wanting it.

---

Three days later she met Harry for lunch at a pub in Bethnal Green, a Muggle place he'd found when he and Ginny first moved to East London. It served mediocre food and excellent chips and had the specific virtue of being nowhere anyone from the Ministry would think to look for either of them.

She Apparated to the alley behind the pub and walked around to the front, and the city hit her like a wall — the noise, the diesel, the sheer density of people moving in every direction with their own problems and their own schedules and absolutely no interest in her. After the Manor's watchful quiet, London felt like being dropped into a river.

Harry was already there, sitting at a table near the back with a pint and the look of someone who'd arrived early on purpose to get fifteen minutes of not being Harry Potter. He stood when he saw her and hugged her, and she let herself be hugged, and the ordinariness of it — his familiar shape, the smell of his jacket, his hand on her shoulder — settled something in her that she hadn't realized was unsettled.

"You look all right," he said, sitting back down. "Bit tired."

"Thanks."

"I'm saying you look all right. I could say you look terrible."

"I look fine."

"Yeah, you do." He picked up his pint and drank. "How's the library?"

She told him. Not everything — not the resonance, not the way the house moved around her, not the translation arguments that felt like the most engaging work she'd done in years — but the shape of it: the scale of the collection, the texts on magical infrastructure, the emerging picture of war-damage to the foundational anchoring system. It was easy to talk about the research. The research was clean, professional, the thing she was there for.

"So it's real," Harry said. "The decay has a cause."

"It has a cause. Whether we can reverse the damage — that's what I don't know yet."

"But the texts are there. The information exists."

"Some of it. The Malfoys weren't the only family involved in building the original infrastructure, but they kept the best records. Or the only surviving records, which amounts to the same thing."

Harry nodded. He was good at this — listening to the part she was saying, hearing the part she wasn't. He didn't push. He ate a chip and looked at her with the steady, patient attention that had been exactly the same since they were eleven.

"Are you sleeping?"

"Yeah."

"Hermione."

"I'm sleeping. The guest room's comfortable, the house is quiet at night, I'm getting six hours minimum."

Five of those hours involved lying awake feeling an ache that didn't belong to her, but he hadn't asked about that and she wasn't offering.

"You look tired," he said again, without accusation.

"I'm working hard. It's good work. I'm fine." She reached for her water glass and as her arm extended across the table her sleeve rode toward her elbow. She caught it before the motion completed and pulled it back down with her other hand, wrapping her fingers around the glass. The adjustment was small, habitual — she'd been doing it for seven years. It didn't register as an action. It was simply the way she moved through the world, with one arm perpetually managed.

"Ginny says hi," Harry said. "She also says you should come for dinner when you're back in London for more than an afternoon. She's got opinions about the Floo situation."

"The Floo's been bad?"

"Three failures on our line last week. One of them dropped me in Birmingham."

"Birmingham."

"I'm not joking. The Midlands hub is degrading. The Ministry's pretending it's maintenance, but Ginny talked to someone at the Prophet and they're sitting on two more stories about infrastructure failures. It's getting worse."

The information landed in the space the research had opened. If the Floo network was degrading at the hub level, that was consistent with the anchoring-damage model — the same model she and Malfoy were building from the texts. The hubs were connected to the same deep infrastructure as the ley-line network. If the war had fractured the anchoring points, the hubs would fail in exactly this pattern — peripheral first, then cascading inward.

"I need to see those Prophet reports," she said.

"I'll ask Ginny. She'll have copies." He ate another chip. "Ginny thinks the Ministry's going to have to say something public within the month. She's angry about it, actually — says they should've acknowledged the pattern six months ago instead of letting people think their individual Floo connections were the problem. She had a woman in her department crying because she thought she'd broken her own fireplace."

"That's — yeah, that's consistent with what I'm seeing in the texts. Individual users would notice degradation and blame local causes. But it's systemic."

"You sound like you've been waiting for someone to confirm this."

She had. The texts described the failure pattern in theory. Harry's Floo data was the first field confirmation she'd had that the theory mapped to current conditions, and the alignment between the centuries-old model and real-time infrastructure collapse was, professionally, the most important thing she'd heard in weeks. She also knew that if she explained this at full speed Harry's eyes would glaze at around the forty-second mark, so she compressed. "The research matters more than I thought it did. It's not just historical — it's diagnostic."

"Right," Harry said. He looked at her the way he looked at her when she was running fast and he was trying to keep up without admitting he'd lost the thread. "Good."

"So the Manor. It's all right? The house is treating you all right?"

"The house is a house, Harry."

"That's not what you told me last time."

She had told him, briefly, in a fire-call the previous week, that the Manor's layout was variable. She'd kept it matter-of-fact, framing it as architectural curiosity rather than the unsettling reality of living inside something aware. She hadn't mentioned that the house surfaced books she was looking for, or that the butter dish migrated across the table toward her at meals, or that the corridors between the library and her room shortened on mornings when she woke early and was eager to get to work.

"It's strange," she allowed. "The corridors shift. Some of the rooms rearrange. It's not dangerous, just — variable."

"Variable."

"Like a building with preferences."

Harry considered this. "And Malfoy? How is he with all of it?"

The question was careful. Harry hadn't asked about Malfoy directly until now, and the way he did it — folded into a question about the house, as if the two were obviously connected — was as close to a leading question as Harry got.

"He's fine. He's been helpful with the translations. His family used a specific notation in their texts that I can't read without context, and he has the context." She was talking faster than she needed to, which was a thing she did when she was being precise as a form of evasion. She made herself slow down. "He's a good researcher."

"Okay."

"I mean it. He's rigorous. He catches things I miss."

"I believe you."

"You're doing the face."

"I'm not doing a face."

"The face where you're thinking something and deciding not to say it."

Harry put down his chip. "I'm not thinking anything. I'm glad the research is going well. I'm glad the library's useful. I'm asking if you're all right because you look tired and you've been living in a house that moves its own walls, and I know you, and I know what that house is."

She adjusted her sleeve again. A different motion this time — a pull at the cuff, tugging it down over her wrist while she reached for a chip of her own. The two gestures were mechanically similar and functionally different. The first had protected the scar. This was something else — a tic, a tell, the thing her hands did when she was sitting across from someone who loved her and who she was not telling the truth.

"I'm all right," she said. "Promise."

Harry looked at her for a long moment. He had very green eyes and they were very honest and she could see him deciding to let it go. Not because he believed her, but because he knew the difference between not-ready-to-talk and lying, and he was giving her the benefit of the doubt that this was the first and not the second.

It was the second.

"Okay," he said. "Come for dinner. Ginny'll do that chicken thing you like."

"The one with the lemon."

"The one with the lemon."

"I'll try."

"Don't try. Come." He smiled and it was Harry's smile, the crooked honest one that hadn't changed since he was twelve, and she wanted to tell him everything — the resonance, the ache, the way the house warmed when she and Malfoy worked well together, the way the arguments about Latin felt like the closest thing to play either of them had allowed themselves in years, the fact that she'd started knowing where a man was in his own house by the borrowed pulse in her own arm — but the words were too strange and too new and she didn't have a framework for them yet, and Hermione Granger did not talk about things she couldn't frame.

"I'll come for dinner," she said.

"Good."

They finished the meal. Harry told her about a case — a string of minor enchantment failures on Muggle objects that were supposed to have been decommissioned after the war. "Someone charmed a postbox in Croydon to eat letters and never undid it. Took us three weeks to figure out why the Royal Mail was blaming ghosts." She laughed, and it felt strange — the sound of it, the way it came out easily in this pub with this person when the Manor's quiet had been making her careful with every noise she made, as though the house might be listening and drawing conclusions.

Harry noticed the laugh. She could see him noticing, the way his expression softened into something that was both glad and worried, the specific face of someone watching a friend remember how to do something they'd forgotten.

He paid because he always paid and she let him because the argument was fifteen years old and neither of them was going to win it.

Outside the pub, the afternoon was grey and cold. November had settled into London the way it always did — not dramatically, just a slow withdrawal of warmth. She pulled her coat tighter.

"Come back soon," Harry said. "Not in three weeks."

"It hasn't been three weeks."

"It's been twelve days."

She hadn't counted. She'd been at the Manor for just under two weeks and it felt like longer — not tedious but dense, packed with work and the particular texture of a place that demanded her full attention.

"I'll come back for dinner," she said. "Tell Ginny I'll bring wine."

"She'll say you don't have to."

"I know. Tell her anyway."

He hugged her again, and she held on for a second longer than usual, and if he noticed he didn't say anything. Then he was walking toward the Apparition point at the end of the street and she was standing on a pavement in Bethnal Green with her coat pulled tight, feeling the gap in her arm where the resonance wasn't — because here, in London, miles from the Manor and its corridors and the man who lived in them, her forearm was just her forearm, carrying only its own scar and its own familiar weight.

The absence was information. She filed it and Apparated home.

---

She came back to the Manor at half three. The grounds were cold, the formal garden brown and withdrawn for the season, the house rising behind the hedgerow with its pale stone and its black windows and the look of having been watching the road.

The front door opened before she reached it. The hallway beyond was warm and lit and smelled of wood polish and the faintly sweet undertone of very old stone. She hung her coat on the hook that had appeared for her by the door — a hook that hadn't existed when she'd first arrived, or at least hadn't been visible — and stood for a moment in the entrance, letting the Manor's warmth replace the November she'd carried in on her skin.

The resonance came back between the front door and the staircase.

A low pulse in her forearm, like a second awareness starting up. She stopped on the third stair and pressed her palm against her sleeve. He was in the library. She could feel that much — the directional sense, less sharp than it had been three nights ago but there, insistent, a quiet certainty. The Mark was active. Not badly — a moderate ache, the restless magic cycling through its patterns.

Diagnostic residue. Proximity-dependent, which was consistent with residual trace bonds. In London she couldn't feel him. In the Manor she could. The simplest explanation was that the Manor's ambient magic was conducting the trace — boosting a signal that was too weak to transmit through non-magical space.

She had four possible explanations and all of them were adequate and none of them were quite right, and the mechanism mattered less to her in this moment than the fact that she'd been away from the house for four hours and the first thing it gave back was him.

She climbed the stairs and went to the library and found him at the reading table with a stack of texts she didn't recognize and a pot of tea that was still hot. He'd been working while she was gone — his notes had grown by several pages, and the texts were flagged with scraps of parchment at intervals that suggested he'd been cross-referencing. The sight of it — evidence of his afternoon, the work continuing in her absence — produced a feeling she didn't examine.

He looked up when she came in.

"London?" he said.

"Lunch with Harry. The Floo network's degrading at the hub level — three failures on the Potter line last week. It's consistent with the anchoring-damage model."

"I know. I tried the east parlor Floo this morning. Took four attempts to get a stable connection."

"You didn't mention that."

"You weren't here." He said it without emphasis. A fact, not a reproach. She sat down across from him and pulled the nearest text toward her and opened it, and the library settled around them, and the ache in her arm was steady and his and close.

She didn't tell him. About the Floo, she'd told him. About the resonance — the fact that she'd walked into the house and the first thing she'd felt was his location, his Mark, the borrowed ache that tracked him through stone and staircase — she said nothing.

She opened her notebook and began working, and the room was warm, and the light was good, and the man across the table turned a page and the motion was small enough that she should not have felt it in her own arm, but she did — a brief brightening of the signal, as though his moving hand had passed through the frequency she was receiving, and the resonance had noted it, and passed the notation along to her.

She made herself look at the text. The words were Latin and old and they described a world built on magic that was now dying, and she was the person who could read them, and that was why she was here. The only reason. The ache in her arm was an artifact and the man across the table was a research partner and the house around them was architecture, however animate, and none of this was complicated by the quiet, uninvited fact of her awareness — that she could feel the shape of his presence in the room the way you feel the heat from a fire you aren't looking at.

She worked until the light failed again. They didn't speak much. He refilled her tea without asking and she thanked him and the cup was warm in her hands and outside the autumn dark came early and total and the Manor's windows reflected the two of them back at themselves — heads bent over their separate texts, close enough that the table between them was more bridge than barrier, more shared space than distance.

At nine he said he was going upstairs and she said goodnight and he left, and the resonance tracked him — up the main staircase, through the corridor, up again to the third floor, east side. The signal thinned as he moved further from her, fading like sound through walls, but it didn't disappear. She could feel him settle. The Mark's ache eased slightly, the way it did in the hours before sleep when the restless magic slowed its cycling.

She sat in the library with her books and her tea and her borrowed awareness and told herself it was residue, artifact, the expected consequence of sustained diagnostic contact with cursed tissue.

She almost believed it.
