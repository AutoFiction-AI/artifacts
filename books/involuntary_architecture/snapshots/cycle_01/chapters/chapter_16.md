# Chapter 16

The archive had been letting him in for three days.

Not properly — not the way it opened when Granger was there, the shelves steady, the light warm and constant, the deeper rooms unfolding like a series of answered questions. Alone, the space tolerated him. The ceiling flickered between heights. The reading table materialized but tilted slightly, one leg shorter than the others, as if the room couldn't quite remember its own geometry. The first time he'd tried, Tuesday afternoon while she was down in the library cataloguing spell-failure reports from St. Mungo's, the archive had held for twelve minutes before the walls started contracting and he'd had to leave at a pace that was not running but was not walking either.

He'd gone back the next morning. Twenty minutes. The shelves along the far wall were accessible but the deeper alcoves stayed dark, their contents invisible behind a kind of architectural refusal — not a door, not a wall, just a persistent elsewhere that his hand couldn't reach through.

Today was better. The room was colder than it had been with her, the light a thin grey instead of the amber that pooled on the reading table when they worked together, but the space held. He'd been here for over half an hour and the walls hadn't moved.

Draco stood at the far shelving unit, the one that had surfaced texts on anchoring infrastructure when they were together two weeks ago. His fingers moved along the spines with the practiced care his mother had taught him — Malfoy texts were old, some of them skin-bound, and you didn't grab at them. He was looking for a notation reference Granger had mentioned yesterday, a cross-index between the ley-line maps and the original construction records. She'd described it precisely, as she described everything, and he'd memorized the description without meaning to because that was what happened now when she spoke. The words stayed.

He found the cross-index. It was shelved behind another text, a thick folio in a binding he didn't recognize — dark leather, older than the anchoring records, the preservation charms on it faintly different in texture from the standard Malfoy library wards. He pulled the folio out to reach what was behind it, and the spine cracked as it came free.

Old binding. He should have been more careful. He set it on the tilted reading table and the room shuddered once, the light dimming before catching itself.

The folio was in his great-great-grandfather's hand — Draco recognized the notation, the compressed marginal references, the way the man wrote his Ms with an extra flourish that looked accidental but was actually a ward-signature, a habit passed down through four generations of Malfoy archivists. The title page was in archaic magical notation layered over Latin, and Draco's Latin was good enough to parse it: *On the Residential Bonding Architecture of Malfoy Manor, Its Purposes, Constraints, and Conditions of Activation.*

His hand stopped on the page.

He read the title again. The words didn't change.

*Bonding architecture.*

The folio was organized in sections. He sat down on the chair, which creaked but held, and he read. The archaic notation was dense — he couldn't parse all of it without Granger's theoretical training — but the Latin underneath was clear enough, and his great-great-grandfather had written marginal glosses in plain English for sections he apparently wanted his descendants to understand without scholarly effort.

*The Manor sustains the magical line through forging connections between its inhabitants.* A gloss: *The house reads what is between them and makes it stronger. This is its primary function and the reason it was built.*

*Access to the deeper archives is gated by the resonance between bonded inhabitants. The stronger the bond, the deeper the access.* A gloss: *The house will not open its heart to one alone. It requires two, and it requires them in truth.*

*The resonance manifests as mutual awareness — proprioceptive, emotional, and eventually physical. The inhabitants will feel each other at distance. This is not a side effect. It is the mechanism by which the house assesses the bond's strength.*

Draco's hands were flat on the table. The page beneath his right palm was slightly damp where the leather binding had sweated — or where his hand had.

He turned to the section on conditions of activation. The notation was denser here, layered with ward-diagrams he couldn't fully read, but the marginal glosses continued.

*Activation requires genuine emotional resonance. The house cannot fabricate connection where none exists. However, once resonance is present, the house amplifies it — strengthening attraction, deepening mutual awareness, and creating conditions of proximity that reinforce the bond.*

He read the sentence twice. Three times.

*The house amplifies it.*

The gated archive. The corridors that shortened between her room and the library. The warmth that followed her through the Manor. The way the house went cold when she pulled away and warm when she came back. The Mark quieting under her touch and only her touch. The way he could feel her attention in a room before he saw her — the low hum at the edge of his awareness that he'd been attributing to the Mark's resonance, which he had, which she had said was diagnostic residue, which —

He stopped reading. He looked at the far wall of the archive, where the deeper alcoves still refused him, dark and elsewhere and waiting for a presence that wasn't here.

She'd found this.

The certainty arrived without preamble. It was not an inference. It was the same folio, the same shelving unit, the same texts she had been pulling for weeks. She'd been working in this room for longer than he had. She had read these words before he did. She read Latin and she read archaic magical notation and she was the most thorough researcher alive and she had found this and she had not told him.

He sat in the tilted chair in the cold grey light and the dates arranged themselves with the clarity of something that had always been visible and he had simply failed to see.

Five weeks ago. Her withdrawal — the sudden briskness, the professional distance she'd reinstated overnight. He had interpreted it as rejection, had spent a week quietly destroying himself trying to identify which of his many failures had finally registered as disqualifying. The Manor had gone cold. The corridors lengthened. The archive wouldn't open. He had assumed — he had thought —

She found the texts and she pulled away.

He remembered that week with a specificity that embarrassed him now. He'd spent it auditing his own behavior — replaying conversations in the library, examining meals they'd shared, trying to locate the moment where he'd said the wrong thing or pushed too close or let something show that should have stayed hidden. He'd assumed it was the resonance. That she'd sensed his want through the Mark-bond and been repelled by it, by the naked fact of Draco Malfoy wanting something he had no right to want. He'd eaten dinner alone for five nights and the house had gone cold around him and he'd thought: *This is correct. This is how it was always going to end. You allowed yourself to forget what you are, and she remembered.*

He'd been wrong. She hadn't been repelled. She'd been *informed.* She'd found the texts and pulled back not because she felt too little but because she suspected the house of making her feel too much, and the difference between those two things was the entire distance between the story he'd been living and the story she'd been living, and she hadn't told him they were in different stories.

Then the scar. The glamour flickered during an examination — her sustained casting destabilizing the concealment she'd worn since he'd known her, probably since the war — and he saw what Bellatrix had carved into her forearm. MUDBLOOD. The letters warped by scar tissue that would never be smooth, the cursed wound from his aunt's knife on his drawing room floor. He'd touched it before he could stop himself. She flinched and his hand stayed and they'd sat there in a silence that was the most honest they'd been with each other, and he had thought — he'd been certain — that this was the last wall coming down. That after the drawing room and the Mark memory and now this, the scar she'd hidden from everyone, there was nothing between them that wasn't known.

He'd been wrong about that too. She'd let him trace those letters with his thumb, let him sit with the weight of what his family's house had done to her body, and she knew — she'd known for weeks by then — that the house might be engineering his guilt and her forgiveness alike. She'd let him have that moment and it might have been the architecture's production and she'd said nothing.

After the scar, she came back. Softened. Returned to proximity, to late nights in the library, to their work. He had been grateful. He had been so fucking grateful that she'd come back that he hadn't questioned why she'd left.

And then the kiss. In the library, late, the translation they'd been arguing about, and she looked at him and the distance was gone. He could still feel the exact pressure of her mouth, the way her hand came up and gripped his sleeve, the sound she made when he pulled her closer. He'd been thinking about that sound for three days. He'd been thinking about her hands and her mouth and the way she tasted like the tea she'd been drinking and the way the resonance between them had sung, amplified, so loud in his awareness that he couldn't tell where his wanting ended and the house's began.

She'd known. When she kissed him. She knew the house was amplifying what was between them and she kissed him anyway and she didn't tell him.

The folio was still open on the table. The words hadn't changed. His great-great-grandfather's handwriting was steady and certain, the marginalia of a man who understood what his house was and wrote it down plainly for the people who would come after.

Draco closed the folio. He stood up. The archive room shuddered again and this time the walls did contract, the space narrowing, and he walked out before it could close on him. The library beyond was empty. Late morning light came through the tall windows, weak and winter-thin, and the reading chair where she usually sat had a book open on its arm, spine up, the way she left them when she was coming back.

She was always coming back. The house made sure of it.

---

He found her in the library after lunch. She was at the cataloguing table, surrounded by the St. Mungo's reports and two of the anchoring texts they'd been decoding together, and she looked up when he came in with the expression she always wore when she was deep in something — slightly startled, slightly annoyed at the interruption, already composing the sentence she'd been about to write.

"I found the section on the ward-layer interface," she said. "The third anchoring point has a completely different binding structure than the first two. I think that's why the decay is uneven — the original architects used at least two different foundational—"

"When did you find the bonding folio?"

The sentence stopped. Not trailed off — stopped, like a recording cut mid-syllable. Her hand was still holding her quill and the ink was forming a slow dark circle on the page beneath it where the nib was pressed down.

"What?" she said.

"The folio on the Manor's bonding architecture. It's in the deep archive, third unit from the east wall, behind the ley-line cross-index. I found it this morning." He was standing in the doorway. He didn't come closer. "When did you find it?"

She put the quill down. The motion was careful, deliberate — the way she did things when she was buying herself time. He'd catalogued that too. He'd catalogued everything about her and now the catalogue had a caption he hadn't written: *she knew*.

"Malfoy—"

"When."

"I don't — can we—"

"It's a simple question, Granger. I can count back from when you started acting differently if you'd rather I work it out myself."

Her face changed. He watched it happen — the micro-expressions he had learned to read over weeks of proximity, the way her features organized themselves when she was calculating options. She was deciding how to tell him. She was deciding what version of the truth would cause the least damage, which was what she did, which was what she had always done, and he had mistaken it for honesty because the calculations were so good.

"Five weeks ago," she said. "After the — after the resonance became bilateral. I was looking for an explanation and I found the folio."

Five weeks.

"So when you pulled back. When you went—" He stopped. Started again. "The week where you could barely look at me. That was because you found out the house was engineering this."

"I didn't know that's what it was doing, not for certain, the texts describe a mechanism but the activation conditions are—"

"Stop. Don't do that."

She went quiet. The quill was on the table and the ink stain was spreading on the page and neither of them moved to blot it.

"You found out the house was designed to bond us," he said. "You found out the Manor amplifies what's between its inhabitants. You found out the resonance, the gated archive, the way the corridors change — all of it is the house treating our — treating whatever this is as something to grow. You found that out five weeks ago."

"Yes."

"And you didn't tell me."

"I was trying to verify—"

"You didn't tell me. You pulled back for a week, you came back, you — the scar. The Mark. The examination after the examination. You sat across from me every day and you let me think what was happening between us was—" His voice was doing something he hadn't authorized. Getting louder. He corrected it, pulling it back to the register that cost him more — the quiet one, the controlled one, the one that sounded like his father at his most dangerous, and he hated that but it was what he had. "You kissed me."

Her chin came up. Not defiant — bracing.

"You kissed me knowing the house might be making us feel this. You kissed me knowing our — the resonance, the way I feel you in every room, the way my Mark quiets under your hands — you knew all of it might be architecture. And you kissed me anyway and you didn't tell me what you knew."

"That's not—" She stood up from the cataloguing table. The chair scraped back across the stone floor and the sound was too loud in the library. "That's not what happened."

"Then what happened."

"I found the texts and I panicked. I didn't know what they meant. I didn't know if the amplification was — I couldn't tell if what I was feeling was mine or the house's and I needed to understand before I—"

"Before you what? Before you told me that my house was manipulating us? Before you gave me the chance to decide for myself whether I wanted to — whether what I—" He stopped again. The sentence he'd almost finished would have been too honest and he was not going to be honest right now. Honesty was what she'd taken from him. The right to be honest on his own terms, with full information, making choices he understood.

"You were the researcher," he said. "I was the subject. Again."

"No. That's not fair."

"Fair." The word came out flat. "You found out the experiment was compromised and you didn't report it. You continued running it. You continued running it on me."

"I wasn't running anything on you, I was trying to — God, Malfoy, do you think I was sitting in my room cataloguing data? I was terrified. I couldn't sleep. I couldn't tell if the fact that I wanted to be near you was real or if the house was — I couldn't tell. And I couldn't tell *you* because the moment I did, everything between us would become—"

"Become what? Honest? Informed?"

"Would become about the house instead of about us."

The sentence landed in the library like something dropped from a height. He heard it. She heard herself say it. For a moment neither of them spoke and the only sound was the January wind against the library windows and the faint settling creak of shelves that had been rearranging themselves for centuries.

"There is no us that isn't about the house," he said. "That's the point. That's what the texts say. That's what you knew and I didn't."

"I know."

"You stayed. You came back after the scar, you — you let me touch it. You let me sit there and trace the letters like some — and the whole time you knew the house was doing this to us and you didn't say a word."

She flinched. The flinch was real — he could see it in her shoulders, in the way her hands came together in front of her, and he didn't care. He didn't care that she was hurting because she had made this choice, she had chosen to know and to stay and to not tell him, and every moment of closeness between them was now something he couldn't trust.

"You treated this like a research problem," he said. "You found the variable and you kept it to yourself so you could study it. You could watch me — what, fall? Respond? You could track the bond's progression with a control group of one and I didn't even know I was being observed."

"That's not what I was doing." Her voice had shifted — harder now, the professional precision that meant she was fighting back. "I was living in it. I was living in this house feeling you through the walls and not knowing if any of it was mine. Do you understand what that's like? To want someone and not know if the wanting belongs to you?"

"I'd have liked the chance to find out."

"I'm telling you what it was like."

"You're telling me now. Five weeks later. After I found it myself. That's not confession, Granger, that's getting caught."

The words hit her and he saw them land. Her mouth pressed closed and her hands flattened on the table and for a moment she looked like she was going to walk out. He almost wanted her to. It would have been easier if she'd left then — if the confrontation had ended in the familiar pattern of retreat and door and the sound of someone deciding he wasn't worth the argument.

She didn't leave. She stayed and she said: "You want to know why I didn't tell you about the scar? Before the glamour slipped? Because I wasn't ready. Because showing you what your aunt carved into me while you stood there and watched wasn't something I could do on anyone's schedule but mine. And you understood that. You touched it and you didn't ask me why I'd hidden it. You understood that some things take time."

"That's not the same thing."

"It's exactly the same thing. I had information about myself that I wasn't ready to share, and I shared it when I could. The bonding texts—"

"The bonding texts aren't about you. They're about us. They're about me. They're about my house and my Mark and whether the thing I feel when I look at you is something I chose or something this building decided I should feel, and you had that information and you kept it because *you* weren't ready. My feelings. My agency. My choice. And you made it about your readiness."

She opened her mouth and closed it again. The self-correction was visible — the sentence she'd started that she pulled back from, and whatever it was he'd never know because she swallowed it and what came out instead was smaller and rawer.

"You're right," she said. "You're right about that."

"I know I'm right."

"But you're also — you're making it sound like I was cold. Like I was sitting with this information and calmly evaluating outcomes. I wasn't calm. I was barely functional. I was examining your Mark every week and touching your arm and feeling the resonance sing and thinking *this might not be real, this might not be real, this might not be real* — and then you'd say something and I'd forget to doubt it. That's not calculation. That's someone who couldn't hold two things at once."

"Then you should have told me so we could hold them together."

She didn't answer that. Her eyes were bright. Not crying — he'd never seen her cry and he suspected she'd sooner bleed — but the brightness was there, the surface tension of something held.

"I was scared," she said. The repetition was different this time — not a defense but a fact, stripped of argument. "I was scared that if I told you, you'd look at me the way you're looking at me right now."

He didn't have an answer for that either. They stood on opposite sides of the cataloguing table with the scattered reports between them and neither of them spoke and the silence was not the kind they'd shared after the Mark memory or after the scar — it was not the silence of two people resting in shared understanding. It was the silence of two people who had run out of ways to hurt each other and found they were not done yet.

"So you decided for both of us. You decided I didn't get to know. You decided the timeline. You decided when the information would be shared and on what terms and in the meantime you kissed me and you examined my Mark and you sat in my library and you—" His voice broke. Not dramatically, not a crack that anyone would notice, but he heard it. A fracture in the controlled register where the real sound leaked through — not anger but the thing underneath anger, the thing he was trying very hard not to name because naming it would give her something she hadn't earned.

"Even this," he said. He was quieter now. The quietness was worse. "Even this was something that happened to me. The Mark was done to me. The house was done to me. My father decided I would live here and serve and carry the name and the brand and I did. And then you came and I thought — I thought this was the one thing I chose. That wanting you was mine. That being wanted was — but it wasn't. It was the house's. Or it might have been. And you knew that and you let me keep believing I was choosing."

The library was very still. The winter light had shifted while they talked and the shadows from the tall windows lay differently across the floor now, longer, the day already turning toward the early dark of January. The ink stain on the cataloguing page had dried into an irregular dark bloom.

"I wasn't calculating," she said. Her voice was different. Smaller. The competence stripped down to something raw and insufficient. "I know that's what it looks like. I know that's — but I was drowning. I've been drowning since I found those texts. Every time you touched me, every time I — I didn't know if my own feelings were mine. I didn't know if the fact that I couldn't breathe when you were close was because I wanted you or because the house had decided I should. And I couldn't tell you that because—"

"Because telling me would have meant trusting me with it."

"Because telling you would have meant watching you look at me the way you looked at me in the drawing room. Like I was something the house had done to you."

The sentence hit him somewhere he hadn't braced for. She saw it. He could tell she saw it because something in her face shifted — not triumph, not leverage, just the terrible recognition of having landed a blow she hadn't wanted to land.

"That's what you think?" he said. "That I'd have blamed you for the bond?"

"You're blaming me now."

"I'm blaming you for the secret. Not for the—" He stopped. His hand was gripping the edge of the reading chair and the wood was biting into his palm and he made himself let go. "I'm blaming you for keeping it from me."

"And if I'd told you? If I'd sat you down the day I found the texts and said, 'The house is a bonding artifact and everything we feel might be its construction' — you'd have been fine? You'd have kept working with me, kept letting me examine your Mark, kept sitting across from me at dinner knowing the house might be — you'd have been fine?"

"I'd have been informed. I'd have had the same chance you had to decide what to do with the information."

"You'd have shut down," she said. The words were quiet but steady and he heard in them the specific certainty of someone who had spent five weeks running the scenario. "You'd have gone formal and careful and you'd have stopped letting me near the Mark and the archive would have closed and the research would have ended and we'd have been two people in a house that was getting colder and colder and neither of us would have—"

"You don't know that."

"I know you, Malfoy."

The claim sat between them. She was right — she did know him, in the specific and devastating way of someone who had been paying close attention for weeks. She knew his defenses and his patterns and the way he retreated when threatened. She knew that the formality he was deploying right now was the same formality that would have walled him off if she'd told him the truth five weeks ago. She was probably right about what he'd have done. And the fact that she was right didn't make what she'd done less wrong. It made it worse. She'd used her knowledge of him to make a decision about him, and the precision of her prediction didn't change the fact that it was hers to make and not his.

The brightness in her eyes spilled over — one line, down the left side of her face, and she wiped it with the back of her hand, fast, angry. She knew she'd lost. He could see it in the way her shoulders dropped, the way the fight went out of her posture. Not because his argument was better but because they were both right and being right wasn't going to fix anything.

"I should have told you," she said.

"Yes."

"I should have told you the day I found them."

"Yes."

"I was wrong."

He didn't say anything to that. The admission sat in the air between them and it was true and it was not enough and they both knew it.

"What do you want me to do?" she said.

The question was wrong. He could feel it being wrong — the way it framed the next move as hers to make, the way it put him in the position of deciding what would fix this, as if there were a prescription and she just needed the correct one. It was how she operated. Give her the parameters and she'd solve the problem. But he was not a problem and this was not solvable and the fact that she was standing there asking him to tell her what to do meant she still didn't understand what she'd broken.

"I don't want you to do anything," he said.

"Malfoy—"

"I think you should go."

The words came out in the register he'd been trying to hold — quiet, controlled, the formality that was his oldest defense, the voice that said *I am Draco Malfoy and you cannot reach me from where you are standing.* It was a lie. Everything about the way he was standing, the way he'd folded his arms, the way he was looking at her from across the library with the reading table and the cataloguing table and six feet of cold stone floor between them — all of it was armor. And she knew it was armor because she'd spent five weeks learning what was underneath it.

"Go where?" she said.

"Home. London. Wherever you — just. Not here."

"The research—"

"I don't care about the research right now."

"The decay is getting worse. The Floo failures are escalating. Children are having accidental magic spikes because the ambient field is—"

"I know what's happening to the magic, Granger. I've lived in it my whole life. My family built the infrastructure that's failing. You don't need to brief me on the urgency while I'm—" He stopped. Breathed. The air in the library tasted like old paper and preservation charms and the faint mineral undertone of the Manor's stone walls. He'd been breathing this air his entire life. It had never occurred to him that the air itself might be part of the architecture. "I can't look at you right now. I need you to not be here."

She was waiting. He understood what she was waiting for — not forgiveness, she was too smart to expect that, but something. A word, a gesture, a crack in the formality that would tell her this was survivable. She was looking at him the way she'd looked at the drawing room when the Manor first opened it — the expression of someone standing in front of a thing that could destroy them and not being able to leave.

He didn't give her what she was waiting for. He couldn't. The anger was too large and too specific and underneath it was the thing he would not name because naming it would mean admitting that her five weeks of silence had cost him something he might not get back, and he was not ready to know the size of that loss. Not yet. Not in front of her.

"Go home, Granger."

---

She went upstairs. He heard her footsteps on the stone — not running, not slow, a measured pace that was its own kind of performance. The library corridor amplified sound the way it always had, the high ceilings and bare walls turning every step into a small announcement, and he stood at the cataloguing table and listened until the sound faded into the upper floors.

He looked down at the table. Her handwriting was everywhere — the notes she'd been making, the cross-references in her small tight script, the marginal annotations on the St. Mungo's reports that were more insightful than anything the Ministry's own analysts had produced. She wrote with a slight leftward lean and her lowercase e's were distinctive and he was noticing this because he was not going to think about the sound she'd made when she said *I was scared that if I told you, you'd look at me the way you're looking at me right now.*

He was not going to think about that.

The Manor knew. He could feel it in the walls — a contraction, a pulling-in, the way the house responded to loss. The library light dimmed. Not dramatically, not a plunge into darkness, but a greying-out, the warmth draining from the air. The tall shelves along the east wall shifted, a slow grinding realignment that he felt through the soles of his feet, and two texts fell from a high shelf and landed face-down on the floor.

He didn't pick them up.

He went to the library window instead, the one that faced the front drive. The glass was old — handblown, wavy, the kind of glass that had been in Malfoy windows for two hundred years — and through it the January grounds were flat and grey, the frost on the lawn catching the fading light. The drive curved away from the house and out through the gates and beyond the gates was the lane and beyond the lane was everything that wasn't here.

The bonding folio was still in the archive. He'd left it on the tilted table when the walls contracted. It was probably still there, open to the section on activation conditions, his great-great-grandfather's handwriting explaining calmly and precisely how the house was designed to make its inhabitants fall in love.

Not fall in love. He corrected himself with the precision she'd taught him. *Amplify existing resonance.* The house didn't create. It enhanced. The distinction mattered, or it was supposed to matter, except right now the distinction felt like the difference between being pushed off a building and being pushed off a slightly lower building. You still hit the ground.

He sat down in the reading chair. Her reading chair — the one she'd claimed without asking, the one that always had a book on its arm, the one that smelled faintly of her ink and the hand lotion she used after hours of handling old texts. The book she'd left was still there, spine up, the pages spread. He picked it up and closed it properly because that was what you did with books and he was still himself even if everything else had been a construction.

Upstairs, he could hear her moving. The guest wing was directly above the library's northwest corner and the Manor's stone carried sound in unpredictable ways — she was opening drawers, closing them, the faint thud of what might have been a trunk lid. She was packing. She was actually packing and leaving and the house was contracting around the fact of her departure like a body responding to a wound.

The temperature dropped. Not the gradual cooling of the Manor settling into evening but a sharp, measurable decrease — the stone under his feet was cold in a way it hadn't been since before she arrived. Since the weeks of empty rooms and corridors that went nowhere and the Mark throbbing without relief and the particular silence of a house that was waiting for something and the person inside it who was trying very hard not to be the thing it was waiting for.

She came down twenty minutes later. He was still in the reading chair. He'd thought about moving — about going to his rooms, about not being here when she left — but his body had made the decision for him, which was what bodies did in this house apparently, and he was sitting in the library with a closed book in his lap when she appeared in the doorway with her bag over her shoulder and her coat on.

The coat was Muggle. She'd arrived in it, weeks ago, a dark wool thing with too many buttons. It looked wrong on her now, or it looked right in a way that was wrong — it was the coat of the woman who'd walked into his house as a researcher, a professional, a person with a job to do, and the woman standing in the doorway was all of those things and also the woman who'd kissed him in this room three days ago and he couldn't make the two of them fit together.

"The notes are on the table," she said. "I duplicated everything that was shared. Your family texts are in the second stack."

"Right."

"The anchoring analysis is — there's a section I didn't finish. The third-point binding structure. If you can get the archive to hold, the reference you'll need is—"

"I'll manage."

She stood in the doorway. The light from the corridor behind her was grey and the library light was grey and between the two greys she was a particular shape — coat, bag, hair escaping from whatever she'd tied it in, her face doing the thing where it tried to be composed and failed at the edges.

"I didn't mean to — I know that doesn't matter. I know 'I didn't mean to' doesn't—" She stopped. Restarted, and the restart was worse because it was more precise. "I made a choice. Every day for five weeks I chose not to tell you and I don't have a defense for that that would mean anything to you right now. I was wrong and I was scared and those aren't different things even though I want them to be."

He looked at her. He let himself look, which was the cruelest thing he could do to both of them, because looking at her while she stood in his doorway about to leave was exactly the kind of self-destruction he'd been raised for. The Malfoy inheritance: beautiful things in beautiful rooms that you couldn't trust.

She waited another moment. He said nothing. She turned and walked down the corridor and he listened to her footsteps on the stone — two, four, six, eight, each one fainter than the last — and then the front door. The Manor's main entrance, the one that hadn't opened for anyone but him in years until she came. It closed with a sound that was heavier than wood and iron should produce, a finality that he felt in the floor, and then the house began to grieve.

That was the only word for it. Not the slow cooling of her withdrawal in November but something faster and more complete — a thermal collapse, the warmth draining from the walls like blood from a face. The library shelves groaned and shifted and the window light went from grey to the colourless nothing of deep winter at four o'clock. The corridor outside the library shortened, then lengthened, then shortened again, the house confused by the sudden absence of the person it had been built around for weeks.

The Mark woke up.

Not the low ache he'd been carrying since the resonance began — this was the old pain, the pain from before she came, before her diagnostic magic quieted the curse's restless thrashing. It came back in a wave that made him grip the arm of the chair, the dark magic in his forearm churning without direction, without the channel her sustained contact had carved through it, and the pain was not metaphorical or symbolic or architectural. It was the specific, physical, nauseating burn of a Dark Lord's brand reasserting itself in the absence of the one thing that had made it bearable.

He sat in the reading chair in the cold library in the cold house and he held his arm and the Manor contracted around him, corridor by corridor, room by room, the great house pulling in on itself.

She was gone. The research was unfinished. The magic was still dying. His arm was on fire and his house was grieving and the woman who could have told him the truth had chosen, for five weeks, to study the problem instead.

He thought about getting up. Going to the kitchen, making tea, performing the motions of a life that worked. He'd done it before she came — years of it, the careful choreography of a man living alone in a house built for a dynasty. Tea at seven. Library at nine. Lunch he sometimes forgot. Mark pain managed with a rotation of spells that didn't work well but worked enough. Theo every few weeks, a dinner where they didn't talk about the important things and the not-talking was its own kind of care. It had been livable. It had been the shape of his days and he'd made peace with it the way you made peace with anything — not by accepting it but by forgetting to notice it.

She'd made him notice. Weeks of shared meals and late-night translations and the way she pushed her hair back when she was thinking and the sound of another person's footsteps in the corridor and the specific weight of being known by someone who was paying attention. She'd made the livable thing look like what it was — a controlled diminishment, a life with the edges sanded down until it couldn't cut him — and now that she was gone the old shape didn't fit. The edges were back and everything was sharp.

The book she'd left was still on the chair beside him, closed now, its spine straight. He didn't look at it. He looked at the library windows where the January dark was settling in, early and complete, and the pain in his arm was steady and familiar and not enough to fill the quiet she'd left behind.

The corridor outside the library shortened. Then lengthened. The house couldn't decide how to hold itself without her in it, and Draco understood that because neither could he.

He sat in the chair. The dark came in. The cold did its work, settling into the stone and the shelves and the table where her handwriting was still spread across the reports, her small e's and her precise notation and the ink stain she'd left when he said the words that ended them.

He didn't get up. He didn't make tea. He sat in the cold house and held his burning arm and waited for the cold to become familiar again.

It didn't. It had been too warm for too long. The cold felt like something borrowed from a life he no longer fit into, and that was the worst part — not the anger, not the betrayal, not the Mark's pain returning. The worst part was that the cold had once been comfortable and now it wasn't, and she had done that too.
