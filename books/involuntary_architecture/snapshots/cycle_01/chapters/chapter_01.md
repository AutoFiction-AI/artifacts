# Chapter 1

The filing charm collapsed on a Tuesday evening, and three years of sorted correspondence slid from its shelves and pooled on the floor of Hermione's office in a disordered heap.

She stared at it. The charm was hers — a standard administrative enchantment she'd anchored to the oak cabinet during her first week in the Department of Mysteries, the kind of magic that sat in wood and worked without attention. She'd refreshed it once, maybe twice, in three years. It was not the kind of spell that failed.

She knelt and picked up the nearest scroll. An internal memo from August, the wax seal still intact. She turned it over in her hands and tried to re-engage the filing charm, feeding magic into the cabinet's grain. The spell caught, wavered, and held — but thin, the way a candle looks in a draft. She could feel the difference. The same amount of magic leaving her wand, the same intention, the same precisely shaped enchantment, but the spell sat in the wood like something balanced on a ledge rather than rooted in place.

She filed the memo by hand. Then the next one. Then the next. By the time she'd finished, it was past eight and her knees ached from the stone floor and the office was quiet in the particular way the Department of Mysteries got quiet after hours — not just empty but sealed, the soundproofing charms thick enough to make her own breathing the loudest thing in the room.

The corridor lights thinned as she left. Not a flicker — a slow dimming, as if the illumination charms were being drawn across more space than they could sustain. She'd been watching this for weeks. The preservation enchantments on the east archive had developed a stutter in September. Three diagnostic arrays in Section G had returned anomalous readings that she was no longer confident were anomalous — they might simply be accurate measurements of a field that had shifted outside expected parameters. The ambient-temperature charms in her office had been running two degrees cold since mid-October, which was not noticeable if you were wearing a jumper but was absolutely noticeable if you were Hermione Granger and you measured things.

She'd written the report in September — detailed, thorough, specific to the point of tedium, because she knew Croaker and she knew that anything she submitted would be read once, evaluated for political risk, and filed. She'd been right. Croaker had read it, asked her to continue monitoring, and not mentioned it again. The Department of Mysteries did not acknowledge crises. It acknowledged data.

The data said the ambient magical field across Britain was declining. Not in the dramatic, headline-making way — no buildings collapsing, no mass spell-failures, no Daily Prophet special editions. The decline was the kind you noticed in accumulation: a charm that should have lasted three years failing after two and a half. A Floo connection dropping mid-sentence. A Healer at St. Mungo's needing to cast twice where once would have been enough. Each failure small. Each one individually dismissible. The pattern unmistakable, if you were the kind of person who kept a separate log — date, location, spell type, ambient-field reading at the time of failure. By mid-October, her log was thirty-seven pages and she'd stopped showing it to anyone because the graph she'd plotted from the data was the kind of thing that made people either argumentative or quiet, depending on whether they understood what they were looking at.

Hermione had chased the cause through every archive available to her. The Department's restricted collection, which was considerable. The Hogwarts library on special dispensation from McGonagall, who had asked no questions and imposed no deadline, which was the kindest thing anyone had done for her that year. Two private collections — one from a retired Unspeakable in Devon, one from a Ravenclaw academic who owed her a favor and had been surprisingly generous about it. And everything the Ministry had ever published on ambient magical theory, which was less than she'd expected and more contradictory than she'd hoped.

None of it was enough. The decay was old — older than the Ministry, older than Hogwarts, rooted in infrastructure that predated modern magical governance by centuries. And the records of that infrastructure, the foundational texts that documented how Britain's ambient magical field had been constructed and anchored, were held almost exclusively in private family collections. Most of those families had cooperated with post-war scholarship. Most of those collections had been catalogued.

Most.

She'd spent three weeks in October cross-referencing the Devon collection's notes on pre-Ministry anchoring sites with the Hogwarts library's theoretical frameworks, and the only conclusion she'd reached was that someone, centuries ago, had built something into the deep structure of British magic that no surviving public text adequately described. She could see the shape of it in the margins — references to *anchoring lines*, to *foundational nodes*, to a network of ley-line connections that sustained the ambient field the way a root system sustained a tree. But the primary sources were absent. Every secondary reference pointed to knowledge held privately by the founding families, and those families had either died out, cooperated with post-war scholars and turned up nothing she didn't already have, or were named Malfoy.

She took the lift to the Atrium, which was empty except for a maintenance wizard refilling his cleaning bucket from the fountain for the third time — the charms on the water kept evaporating the supply before the mop could reach the floor. He swore at the bucket and tried again. She watched him for a moment longer than was useful, then turned and walked out through the visitor entrance into the October night.

London was cold and damp and entirely itself. The Muggle streets operated on their own logic, their own infrastructure, their own forms of decline — plumbing and pavement and budgets, not magic. The streetlights worked. The buses ran. She walked past the Tube station and kept walking, because forty minutes of cold air and concrete felt more reliable tonight than any transportation involving enchantment. She'd started walking home in January, after the Knight Bus had lurched so badly one evening that she'd been sick on the pavement outside a Tesco, standing there with her shoes splashed and her wand useless in her coat pocket and the thought arriving fully formed: *I could just use my legs.* The walking had stuck. Forty minutes through Holborn and Camden, past Muggle lives unfolding on their own terms — a chip shop with its windows steamed, a man arguing into his mobile on the corner, a woman carrying a sleeping child out of a minicab. None of it needed magic. She liked the walk the way she liked the gas hob — for its reliability, which was not a quality she had ever expected to prize above convenience.

---

Her flat was in Kentish Town. One bedroom above a shuttered dry cleaner's, the lease cheap enough that she'd taken it after the divorce without negotiating and stayed because momentum and inertia looked the same if you didn't examine them too closely.

The walls were cream. She'd hung nothing on them. The books filled the shelves and overflowed onto the table, the kitchen counter, and the armchair by the window, which she'd stopped sitting in around February when the warming charm on the cushion began bleeding out by midnight. She hadn't recast it. She'd bought a blanket instead, which was still folded on the arm of the chair, unused, because by the time she'd bought it she'd already migrated to the bed for evening reading and the chair had become a book surface. The rest of the flat followed the same logic of accretion. A stack of case files by the door that she'd been meaning to return to the office for two weeks. A Muggle umbrella in the bathroom, left there after a rainstorm in September and never moved, because the drying charm she'd cast on it had held long enough to become invisible and then failed, and now the umbrella was just sitting in the bathtub, dry and purposeless and somehow permanent. She hadn't invited anyone over since she'd moved in. The flat was a place to sleep and read and eat, not a place to be with people, and she'd stopped noticing the difference between choosing to be alone and simply being alone sometime around spring.

She dropped her coat on the hook by the door. The hook was a sticking charm she'd cast in September, and it had begun to peel from the wall — the adhesion thinning the same way everything was thinning, slowly and undramatically. She pressed it back into place with her thumb and heard the charm catch. Barely.

She heated water on the hob. The kettle charm had been unreliable for two weeks — some mornings it worked fine, some mornings the water got to tepid and stopped, and she'd given up the fight with the graceless efficiency of someone triaging her entire relationship with magic. The hob was gas. The gas was Muggle. She stood in her kitchen in her work clothes and watched the flame do what fire did without enchantment, and she did not let herself follow the thought about what it meant that she'd begun to find the Muggle way more trustworthy.

Dinner was toast with the last of the cheddar. Not because she couldn't cook — she was a competent cook when the effort seemed proportionate to the outcome — but because the expansion charm on her refrigerator had failed the previous week, contracting everything back to actual size, and a jar of chutney had cracked against the back wall. She'd cleaned the glass, restocked the basics, and filed the incident under the same word she used for everything now. Managed. Her flat was managed. Her routine was managed. Her days were managed. The divorce had been managed — amicable enough to leave no good stories, clean enough that when people asked, the only honest answer was *it just ended*, which satisfied no one, including her.

Ron had called two months ago to tell her he'd started seeing someone. A witch from his department. He'd been careful about it, decent in the particular way he was decent when he was trying not to be presumptuous, and she'd said the right things and meant them. When she'd put the phone down and looked around the flat, she hadn't felt hurt, or jealous, or anything with enough shape to hold. Just the flatness. The residual zero of a marriage that had subtracted itself so gradually that by the time they'd signed the paperwork, there was nothing left to divide.

She ate the toast standing at the counter and read a journal article on her tablet — ambient-field theory, a reexamination of a conjecture she'd already dismissed twice. The author proposed that magical fluctuation was cyclical, self-correcting, and ultimately benign. The author's data stopped in 2001. She read it to the end because she could not selectively disable thoroughness, and then she set the tablet down and washed her plate and put it on the rack and stood in the kitchen and felt the specific tiredness of a person who had exhausted every available avenue and could not locate the next one.

Her left forearm itched. She tugged at the sleeve — unconscious, habitual, so automatic she was sometimes surprised to catch herself doing it. The glamour was holding. It was always holding. She checked it every morning the way she checked the wards on the flat: touch the skin, feel the smooth surface of the charm, confirm the concealment was intact. She'd been doing this for seven years and the muscle memory was so deep it had become part of getting dressed — wake up, shower, charm the forearm, choose a shirt, leave.

The Floo coughed.

Green flame surged to knee-height in the fireplace and collapsed. She waited. The flame tried again — a wheeze, a flare, three seconds of unsteady connection before the network dropped. She heard a voice in the flare, indistinct, cut off.

She knelt and cast a diagnostic on the Floo. The network was there — she could feel the relay, the low hum of the north London node that served four thousand households — but the link refused to sustain. Harry's fireplace, Grimmauld Place. He'd been trying to reach her.

She could Apparate over. She could send an owl. She wrote a note instead — *Floo dropped again. I'm fine. Talk tomorrow?* — and sent it through the flat's mail charm, which still worked, mostly, though last month it had delivered a letter to the wrong address and she'd had to retrieve it from a Muggle postbox three streets away.

She knew what Harry would ask. He'd ask if she was sleeping. She'd say yes. He'd make that face — the mouth neutral, the eyes saying *I have known you for fifteen years and you are lying to me right now* — and she'd change the subject because she didn't want the conversation where she told him the truth, which was not that she wasn't sleeping but that the sleeping was fine, the eating was fine, everything was managed, and the problem was that managed was all she was and she didn't know what she was for.

She sat on the sofa between two stacks of journals and listened to the Floo cough once more. Green flame flickered. She heard — "Hermione?" — before the connection died again.

She didn't try to reconnect.

---

The memo arrived Thursday morning on pale lavender parchment, interdepartmental seal, the carefully imprecise language that Croaker used when he wanted something to look routine. His handwriting in the margin, small and tight: *Granger — relevant to your research. Last uncatalogued major collection. Authorize at your discretion.*

The substance was simple. The Malfoy family library, held at the family estate in Wiltshire, constituted the last uncatalogued private magical collection of significant size in Britain. The collection was known to contain pre-Ministry records of ambient-field infrastructure, foundational magical theory, and archival material not held in any public institution. A qualified researcher was requested to assess and catalogue the collection for materials relevant to ongoing departmental concerns.

Hermione read it three times. Once for information, once because the words *Malfoy family library* had landed in her chest like something dropped from height, and once to note what the memo did not contain: no mention of the decay, no reference to the stabilization indices she'd been logging for three months, no acknowledgement that the texts she was being pointed toward might be the only surviving documentation of the infrastructure that was currently failing across Britain.

Croaker was being careful. The memo could be filed as routine heritage work. But the margin note — *relevant to your research* — was a directive dressed as a suggestion, and she'd worked for Croaker long enough to know the difference.

She set the memo flat on her desk and pressed her palms against the wood. Oak, Ministry-standard, older than she was. She needed something that was not going to move under her hands.

The Malfoy library. Malfoy Manor.

She had not been back. In seven years she had not set foot on the property, had not spoken to Draco Malfoy since his trial — the spring of 1999, when she'd been nineteen and still carrying the particular fury of someone who had survived something enormous and was not yet willing to let the survival become a story that other people shaped. The Wizengamot had been prepared to send him to Azkaban. Sixteen when he took the Mark, seventeen during the battle, and the prosecution had framed the entire arc as choice rather than coercion, as if any sixteen-year-old in that house had been choosing freely, as if the word *choice* meant anything useful when the Dark Lord was living in your dining room.

She'd testified because it was right. She'd been precise and thorough and she had not talked about what else had happened in that room because the Wizengamot had the record and because saying it aloud would have required her to name the word carved into her arm, and she was not going to do that in a room full of people holding quills. She'd sat in the witness box and said, under oath, that Draco Malfoy had been a child in a war and that when it mattered — when the Snatchers had dragged them into the drawing room and demanded identification — he had looked at Harry's swollen face and lied. She'd said: he was frightened, and he saved us, and I don't think those two things cancel each other out.

The verdict was acquittal with conditions. A two-year wand restriction. He'd stood when it was read, and he'd looked at the bench and then at his mother and then at the door, and he'd walked out. She had been sitting in the gallery with her sleeves pulled down and her glamour freshly cast, waiting for something she couldn't have named, and he had not looked at her. Had not nodded, or glanced, or given her anything at all.

She hadn't needed his thanks. She hadn't done it for thanks — she'd done it because the principle mattered, because the war's moral accounting could not be reduced to sides without destroying something essential about justice. But the silence had caught in her like a hook, and in six years she had not found a way to unhook it that didn't require examining what she'd actually wanted from him, which she was not prepared to do.

She looked at the memo. *Authorize at your discretion.*

The library was the right lead. She'd known for weeks that her research gap was historical — the ambient-field infrastructure was older than the Ministry, built by founding families during the original construction of Britain's magical framework, and the records of that construction had been passed down through the families who'd done the work. The Malfoys were one of those families. Their library was the last uncatalogued collection of that era because the family had refused outside access for generations and because after the war, the politics around the Malfoy name had made any formal request radioactive. Nobody wanted to be the department head who officially dispatched a researcher to a convicted Death Eater's estate. Nobody wanted the Prophet headline.

What she knew about the collection itself was partial and mostly secondhand. Bathilda Bagshot had mentioned it in a footnote in *A History of Magic* — *the Malfoy archive, unmatched in its holdings on pre-Ministry magical infrastructure, remains closed to outside scholarship.* A colleague in the Heritage Division had referenced it over lunch once, half-envious: apparently the catalogue, such as it existed, was handwritten in three languages and organized according to a system that only the family understood. Lucius Malfoy had shown selected texts to visiting scholars during his years of political influence, but always supervised, always on his terms, and never anything touching the foundational infrastructure records. Which was, of course, exactly what she needed.

Nobody except Hermione, who had run out of every other option and who was watching the numbers in her log decline week by week while the Ministry issued memos in lavender parchment and the maintenance wizard in the Atrium fought with a mop that wouldn't wet.

She picked up her quill. She drafted the authorization in her own hand, signed it, attached it to the memo, and set it aside. Then she drafted the access request — a formal letter to the current head of the Malfoy household, outlining the scope of the work, the Department's interest in texts relating to ambient magical theory, and the proposed terms of access. The language was professional, precise. She did not mention the trial. She did not mention the room with the chandelier and the marble floor and the specific cold that she could still feel against her back if she let herself think about it for longer than a second.

She wrote the letter in her best professional hand, the one she used for external correspondence — neat, impersonal, the handwriting of a colleague rather than a person with memories. She'd learned this during the war, the discipline of putting words on paper that did exactly what they needed to do and carried nothing underneath. The letter read like a request between strangers, which was what it was. She hadn't spoken to Draco Malfoy in six years. Whatever he was now, she didn't know him. Whatever she was now — the flat, the toast, the log — he didn't know her either. The letter was accurate in its distance.

She signed it, set the quill down, and pushed her chair back from the desk.

Her hands were shaking. She watched them for a moment — both of them, resting on the arms of her chair, trembling slightly, as if the decision she'd already made hadn't reached her body yet. She put them in her lap, which was absurd — the office was empty, no one was watching — but the shaking was not about being observed. It was her hands catching up to what her handwriting had already committed to. They knew where she was going.

She sat at her desk for a long time. The corridor lights outside her office thinned and steadied and thinned again, the illumination charms struggling with whatever they were struggling with. She sat there and breathed until her hands were still.

---

Harry's note arrived Friday evening by Ministry owl, carried by a bird that looked harrowed. The Floo had been down for most of the day — the entire north London relay offline for the second time that month — and the owl network was straining to compensate. The Ministry's public statement blamed scheduled maintenance. Hermione had read it in the morning Prophet and felt the specific irritation of watching an institution she belonged to lie plainly about something she had measured.

His handwriting was tight and slightly crooked, the penmanship of someone who'd learned to write in a cupboard under the stairs and had never entirely corrected for the cramped conditions.

*Floo's out again. Are you all right? Ginny says come for dinner Sunday. Don't bring wine, we've still got the six bottles from your birthday that no one drank because you left early.*

*Are you sleeping?*

She sat on the sofa between the journal stacks and read the note and smiled, and the smile was genuine in a way that made the quiet of the flat afterwards feel thinner. Harry. The constancy of him, who had known her since she was eleven and bossy and certain about everything and who had watched her become someone less certain and less bossy and had stayed. He didn't ask *how are you* because he'd figured out years ago that the question gave her room to construct an answer. *Are you sleeping* required specifics.

She wrote back:

*I'm sleeping. I slept eight hours last night. I've taken up watercolours. I have a robust social calendar and many leisure interests. Tell Ginny yes to dinner. And I left early because your friend Robards cornered me with a thirty-minute story about ward calibration and I could feel parts of my brain shutting down in self-defence.*

She paused, quill hovering. She could tell him about the Manor. He'd find out — he was senior enough in the Auror office that access requests to notable properties crossed someone's desk, and that someone would mention it, and it would reach Harry inside a week. Better from her. Better framed as what it was: a professional decision, a research lead, the only viable path left.

She didn't write it. She'd tell him Sunday, face to face, where she could manage the conversation and deploy the particular tone she used when she needed Harry to hear *this is handled* without him asking the follow-up questions she didn't have answers for.

She folded the note and sent it and sat in the quiet.

The access request was on its way to Wiltshire. She'd dispatched it that afternoon — a Ministry owl, formal correspondence, her name and her title and her signature arriving at Malfoy Manor by nightfall. The thought of it — her handwriting inside that house, her name on a table in a room she couldn't picture, being read by a person she hadn't spoken to in six years — settled in her forearm with the dull, familiar wrongness that she'd learned to distinguish from ordinary sensation. It was not pain, not quite a phantom — just the place where the scar lived, making itself known the way it did when she thought about things she kept below the surface.

She pulled her sleeve down. The glamour was fine. The Healers had explained it years ago — cursed wounds produced phantom sensation, the nerve damage from Bellatrix's knife meant the forearm would never feel entirely normal, the itch was not the scar opening or the curse reactivating but simply the body remembering what had been done to it. She'd written down the explanation, filed it, and lived with the itch. The intellectual understanding and the physical fact occupied the same arm and had never quite introduced themselves to each other.

She made tea on the hob. Not with magic — the kettle charm had actually worked that morning, for the first time in days, but she no longer trusted it, which was a thought she didn't follow to its conclusion. She sat with the mug on the edge of her bed because the sofa had journals on it and the chair was cold and the bedroom was where she spent her time now, which was another thing she did not examine.

She'd been sleeping badly since the summer, before the decay had become undeniable — since the first diagnostic had come back wrong and she'd sat in her office past ten at night and felt the first real edge of the thing she'd been pushing down for months. Not anxiety, exactly. Something closer to professional vertigo. She was supposed to be able to solve things. That was what she did — she solved them by being more thorough and more prepared and more rigorous than the problem required, and the surplus effort was the margin that let her sleep. This problem was bigger than surplus effort. The margin had eroded, and the sleep had gone with it, and what was left was the toast and the managed flat and the walking home through Camden because at least the walk was something she could finish.

She thought about Malfoy Manor. She thought about it the way she thought about everything: in layers, each one rational, each one defensible, stacked neatly on top of the thing she did not want to look at.

The library was the best remaining lead. The collection was likely to contain the only surviving documentation of the ambient-field infrastructure that was currently failing. She was the most qualified person for this work, and declining would be professionally indefensible when the decay was measurable and accelerating and the Ministry was pretending it wasn't happening. All of this was true.

Underneath the true things was the other true thing, the one she kept placing at the bottom as if layering rational arguments on top of it would make it weigh less: the Manor was the place where Bellatrix Lestrange had held her down on a marble floor and carved a word into her arm with a cursed knife while she screamed until something in her throat tore, and Draco Malfoy had stood against the far wall with his hands at his sides, and the chandelier had thrown cold light across the ceiling, and at some point during it — she didn't know when, the memory was fractured and merciless in unpredictable places — she had turned her head and seen his face and it had been white and fixed and he had not moved.

Not what he'd done — what he hadn't.

She drank the tea. It was too hot and she drank it anyway, and the scald was at least a definite sensation, a thing she could locate.

She couldn't refuse the assignment. She'd known this since Thursday morning, maybe since before Thursday, since the morning she'd watched the corridor lights dim in the Department and thought: *I've reached the end of what the existing archives can give me. The answer is older than what we have.* She had never actually decided not to go back to the Manor — she'd never said it, never declared it, never made a promise. She'd simply not gone, the way you simply don't return to the place where you were hurt worst, and the not-going had hardened over seven years into something that felt like a position even though it had only ever been avoidance.

Now avoidance and professional necessity had collided, and professional necessity was going to win, because it always won with her, because competence was the architecture she'd built her life inside and if she removed that she was a woman in a one-bedroom flat eating toast and checking her glamour and watching the magic fail.

She set the mug on the nightstand. She turned off the lamp — Muggle, plug-in, purchased in January for reasons she'd told herself were aesthetic. The room went dark. The silence was London silence: pipes settling, a distant siren, someone's television through the wall, the low urban hum of a city that ran on electricity and plumbing and did not depend on an ambient magical field that was quietly running out.

Nothing shifted. Nothing watched. The walls stayed exactly where they were.

She lay in the dark and thought about the letter arriving at the Manor. She pictured the owl crossing the wards, landing on a sill, waiting. She tried to picture Draco Malfoy reading her name on Ministry parchment and couldn't get past the image of him at seventeen — the courtroom, the grey suit, the way he'd looked at the door and then through it and then was gone.

He'd be twenty-five now. Her age. She didn't know what he looked like at twenty-five. She didn't know if the sharp angles of his boyhood had settled or sharpened, if he looked tired the way everyone from the war looked tired — that particular exhaustion of people who'd survived something and hadn't figured out what surviving it was going to cost them for the rest of their lives.

She turned onto her side. The pillow was flat. She punched it into a better shape and it didn't help. She closed her eyes and her mind offered the stabilization index, the decay curve, the shape of a system slowly running out of breath.

Then it offered the drawing room. The chandelier. The cold marble against her back. The sound she'd made that she still couldn't think about without her jaw clenching shut.

She opened her eyes.

She thought about the maintenance wizard in the Atrium, swearing at his bucket with the weary, private fury of a man whose tools had stopped working. She thought about the reports she'd read last week — accidental magic spiking in Muggle-born children across the country, the ambient field too unstable to regulate their magical development the way it was supposed to, children setting fires and shattering windows and frightening their parents, who had no framework to understand what was happening. She'd filed the reports with everything else she couldn't fix from her office. She thought about the fact that she was the person most likely to understand what was happening to British magic, and that she was lying awake in a cold flat in Kentish Town because she was afraid of a house.

The fear was not irrational. She could evaluate her own responses — she was a researcher, that was the one thing she could still do. The fear was proportionate to the stimulus. She was going back to the place where she had been tortured, to work in proximity to the person who had watched it happen, and the professional necessity of the trip did not cancel what her body knew about that house. The body kept its own records. Her forearm kept its own records. The glamour was a correction applied to the surface, and underneath it the letters were still raised against the skin, and those letters had been carved in that house, and the house was where she was going.

She stared at the dark ceiling of her managed flat and did not sleep until sometime past three, when she dropped into something that was closer to collapse than rest, and when the alarm went off at six the bedroom was cold — the warming charm had bled out in the night, silently, while she slept — and she got up and showered and stood in front of the bathroom mirror and cast the glamour on her left forearm with the same precision she brought to everything, and the charm settled over the scar tissue and smoothed the skin and hid what was written there. She rolled her sleeve down and buttoned the cuff and got dressed and left for work.
