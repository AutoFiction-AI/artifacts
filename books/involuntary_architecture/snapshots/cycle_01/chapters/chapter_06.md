# Chapter 6

She was trying something new.

Draco had learned her routines by now — the way she arranged her instruments before each session, the angle she preferred for his arm, the habitual push of her sleeves to her elbows that she always corrected before he could register what she was correcting. He'd learned to sit still through the initial passes of diagnostic magic, which ran under the skin like cold water, and to breathe evenly through the deeper probes, which pressed on some bruise he could never locate. He'd learned that her face went remote during examinations, all the restless intelligence narrowing to a single point, and that the silence between them in these sessions was not the same silence they shared in the library. That silence was becoming comfortable. This one was clinical. He preferred the clinical. The clinical had rules.

But today was different, and she had warned him. Sustained contact. Her hand on the Mark itself for the full duration, rather than the hovering wand-guided passes of the previous sessions. She'd explained — precisely, without looking at him — that wand-mediated diagnostics produced an interference pattern that filtered the Mark's raw magical signature. Direct palm contact would bypass the filter.

He'd understood the theory. He understood the implication too: her hand pressed to his forearm, to the stain that had been there since he was sixteen, for however long the session required. Neither of them had mentioned that the last time her hand had lingered on the Mark — during the third session, a brief accidental contact — the magic in it had surged toward her like something recognizing a voice.

They were in the small sitting room off the library's east wall. Not a room anyone would design for medical examinations — a writing desk pushed against one wall, two chairs, a side table — but it had natural light, which Granger had mentioned once, in passing, mattered for the diagnostics. The next morning the Manor's heavy drapes had been gone and the windows clean, winter light coming through glass that hadn't seen a cloth in years. He hadn't asked the house to do that. She hadn't asked the house to do that. The house had done it anyway.

December light. Thin, grey, the sun already failing at half past two. Frost had built along the edges of the glass overnight and the room was cold in the way the Manor's rooms got cold when the house was distracted or indifferent — not aggressive cold, just the absence of effort a building this size defaulted to.

"Ready?" she said.

He rolled up his left sleeve. The Mark was vivid today, the skull and serpent sharp against his skin, the lines almost raised. It fluctuated like this — some mornings nearly invisible, others so dark it looked freshly done. He couldn't predict it. He'd stopped trying.

"How's the pain been?"

"Same. Usual."

"Your usual or regular-person usual?"

He almost smiled. "Manageable."

She didn't believe him. He could tell by the way she didn't argue, which was Granger's particular method of recording disagreement for later use. She set her wand on the table.

"Standard wand-pass first for baseline, then I'll transition to direct contact. The transition might feel strange. I don't know exactly how. Tell me if you need to stop."

He nodded. She picked up her wand and began the initial pass — the familiar cold sweep, the Mark shimmering under the diagnostic light, the sensation of being read by magic that was smarter than it had any right to be. He leaned back in the chair and watched the frost on the window. A thin crack ran through the ice along the lower pane, splitting and branching. He followed it while the diagnostic spells did their work.

The Mark tolerated her. This had been remarkable from the first session and had not stopped being remarkable. The few Healers he'd approached in the years after the war had refused to examine Dark Lord magic — the professional risk, the legal liability, the simple atavistic fear of what the skull on a young man's forearm meant about the young man. The one who'd agreed had lasted ninety seconds before the Mark's defensive response had shattered his diagnostic framework. The man's hands had shaken for an hour afterward. Draco had not tried again.

Granger's spells didn't shatter. They settled into the Mark like water into sand, and the magic in it let them.

"Baseline stable," she murmured, scanning the readings. "Reactivity up slightly from Tuesday. Within your range." She put the wand down. Paused. "Switching now."

She reached across the table and put her hand on his forearm.

He had been expecting it. He had prepared for it. It didn't matter. The contact — warm palm, firm grip, her fingers wrapping around his forearm so that the Mark sat under the center of her hand — hit him with the specificity that only unexpected touch could produce. Not magical. Not painful. Just the fact of another person's skin on his, which should not have been noteworthy and was. Her thumb settled against the inside of his wrist. His pulse was there and she knew it.

"Starting the deeper reading," she said. "Stay still."

He stayed still.

She closed her eyes. No wand now — she was channeling the diagnostic directly through her palm, a technique that required sustained magical focus of a kind most practitioners couldn't maintain for more than a few seconds. She could maintain it. Her brow drew in and her breathing slowed and the magic flowed from her hand into the Mark, and it was nothing like the wand-passes. The wand-passes were cold and analytical, an instrument reading an object. This was warmer. The magic entered the Mark's structure not as an examination but as a presence — something alive meeting something alive — and the dark magic in the Mark, the restless agitated thing that had been his constant companion for months, the ache that woke him at three in the morning and made his hand clench and his teeth set and his sleep a nightly negotiation with pain —

It stopped.

Not gradually. Not the way a headache fades or a cramp releases. The pain was there and then it was gone, like a sound cut off mid-note, and in its place was a silence so complete that Draco couldn't process it.

The absence was louder than the pain had been. He'd been carrying it for so long — not just the recent months of the Mark's reawakening but the years of low-grade ache that he'd dismissed as phantom, as memory, as the body's insistence on remembering what had been done to it — that the relief was disorienting. He had organized his life around this pain. He slept on his right side because the left arm's weight against the mattress made the ache worse. He carried his teacup in his right hand. He dressed with the left arm last because the sleeve dragging over the Mark was a small daily unpleasantness he'd learned to back-load. All of that infrastructure — the minor accommodations, the unconscious adjustments, the constant low-frequency attention the pain demanded — was suddenly obsolete. His arm felt weightless. The Mark under her hand was still and flat and quiet, and the absence of pain had opened a space he hadn't known he was filling.

He must have moved, or his breathing changed, because her eyes opened.

"What happened?"

"It stopped." His voice was rough. He hadn't expected that. "The pain. It's — all of it."

She stared at him. Her hand was still on his arm. In the previous sessions, any disruption had broken her concentration and they'd started over. This time she held on.

"Stopped," she repeated. "The baseline ache, the persistent —"

"All of it. Since the direct contact."

She looked down at where her hand covered the Mark. He looked too. Her grip was clinical, fingers arranged for contact rather than comfort, and beneath her palm the Mark was the stillest he had ever seen it. The dark shimmer was gone. The restless movement of magic through dead channels — gone. The skull and serpent lay flat and inert under her hand, just lines on skin. Just a scar.

She moved her thumb in a slow arc across the Mark's edge, testing something. The magic under the skin didn't react. It should have — even her gentlest diagnostic passes in previous sessions had drawn a shimmer, the Mark's residual defenses flaring before settling. Now there was nothing. Whatever her touch was doing to the dark magic, it wasn't just suppressing the pain. It was quieting the magic itself, the whole restless architecture of it, as if her presence was the answer to a question the Mark had been asking since the Dark Lord died and the signal cut out.

"I'm going to lift my hand," she said. "Tell me when the pain returns."

She pulled her hand away. His forearm was cold where her warmth had been.

He counted. At seven seconds the Mark twitched, a familiar low ache like a muscle remembering an injury. At ten the restless magic was back, not as severe as before the session but unmistakably present, the pain a dull broadcast he couldn't turn off.

"Back," he said.

She put her hand on the Mark again. The pain stopped. Again. Immediate and total, like a switch thrown.

They sat with this. The frost cracked quietly on the window. A tree branch scraped glass somewhere above them, and the thin December light moved a degree across the floor.

"That's not the diagnostic," she said, and he could hear her thinking — the words running behind something that was already ahead of them. "The technique isn't therapeutic. It's observational. The magic shouldn't be interacting, it should be reading. What I'm doing shouldn't have any effect on the pain, let alone —" She shook her head. "It's not responding to the method. It's responding to —"

"To you."

She didn't answer for a moment. Her thumb shifted on his wrist — a tiny adjustment that she might not have been conscious of and that he was extremely conscious of.

"I don't have an explanation yet," she said.

"Yet."

"I want to try something. Maintain contact longer, see if the effect persists after I break contact or if there's a cumulative —" She was talking faster, the cadence he recognized: Granger converting something she didn't understand into something she could measure. "I'll need precise onset and offset data. And a comparison against the wand-mediated baselines from the last three sessions to rule out —"

"Granger."

She stopped.

"You're holding my arm. You don't have a quill."

She looked at the notepad and quill she'd arranged on the opposite side of the table, well out of reach. She looked at her hand on his arm. The problem occurred to her — that logging data required a hand she was currently using to keep his Mark quiet — and something crossed her face that wasn't embarrassment. Something that acknowledged, without naming, that she'd been holding his arm longer than any protocol required and that neither of them had said anything about it.

"Right," she said. "Could you —"

He reached across with his free hand and slid the notepad toward her. She picked up the quill left-handed — she was right-handed, her right hand on his arm — and began writing in a script that looked nothing like her usual careful hand. Cramped, tilted, the letters struggling.

"Onset of pain cessation: immediate on direct palmar contact," she said as she wrote. "Pre-contact pain level —"

"Four. I've been generous about calling it a four."

She wrote that without comment, which told him more than an argument would have. She'd been estimating his pain lower.

"Current level?"

"Zero."

She wrote. The quill scratched against the notepad. Her hand stayed on his arm, warm and steady, and the Mark stayed silent, and the frost built its slow patterns on the glass, and he looked at the ceiling and then at the wall and then at her hand and then at the ceiling again, because if he looked at her face while she was concentrating — lip caught between her teeth, the crease between her brows, her hair escaping the tie she'd put it in that morning — he would have to contend with the fact that her hand on his arm was the most peaceful his body had felt in seven years, and he was not going to contend with that. Not now. Not while she was holding him and writing left-handed and neither of them was naming what this was.

"Gradual release test," she said. "I'm going to reduce contact incrementally. Tell me the moment the pain returns."

She lifted her palm, leaving only her fingers on the Mark. No pain. She curled her fingers back one at a time. Ring finger, middle finger. No pain. Just her index finger and thumb on the edge of the Mark.

"Still zero?"

"Still zero."

She lifted her thumb. One finger. A single point of contact on the serpent's tail.

There. Not the full ache — a whisper of it. A suggestion.

"One," he said. "Maybe less."

She pressed down slightly, increasing pressure without increasing surface area, and the suggestion retreated.

"Pressure component," she murmured, making a note. "Not just contact area. There's a —" She stopped. Shook her head. "I need to think about this."

She hadn't taken her finger off the Mark.

He waited.

She seemed to realize, all at once, the posture they'd arrived at — him leaned back, arm extended across the table, her leaned forward with one finger pressed to his forearm like she was marking a passage in a text — and something shifted in her expression. Not embarrassment. Something adjacent to it, something that registered the shape of what they were doing without having a name for it. She pulled her hand back.

The pain returned fully within ten seconds. He breathed through it. Baseline. The thing he carried. Fine.

"Thank you," she said, standing, gathering her notes. Her movements were precise and slightly too quick, the way they got when she was processing something she wasn't going to discuss yet. "I need to cross-reference this with the ambient resonance literature. There's a text in the east stacks that —"

"Granger."

She paused at the door.

He didn't know what he'd been going to say. Something about the pain stopping. The quality of its absence. That her hand on his arm had achieved what no spell or potion or Healer's incantation had managed in seven years. That the darkest magic anyone had ever put in him went quiet when she touched it, and neither of them knew why, and not knowing should have frightened him more than it did.

He said: "Your notes. They're upside down."

She looked at the notepad in her hand, flipped it, and left without another word.

He sat in the chair for a long time. The frost had built a white border around the window and the light had shifted to the darker grey that meant mid-afternoon in December, the day nearly done at barely three o'clock. His arm ached. The Mark was restless again, the magic probing its dead channels, a phantom trying to complete a circuit that no longer existed.

He pressed his own hand over the Mark. Held it there. Nothing happened. The pain didn't change. His hand was just his hand, and the Mark didn't care about it.

He sat with this. Her hand, and the pain went. His hand, and nothing. There was something in that specificity that he wasn't going to name but that he couldn't quite ignore, either. The Mark — the thing the Dark Lord had branded into him at sixteen, the magic that had been meant to bind him to a dead man's will — recognized her. Not her magic, or not only her magic. Her. The distinction mattered and he didn't know why yet.

The writing desk against the wall held a stack of parchment and an inkwell that had dried years ago. A crack ran through the desk's surface from one end to the other, the wood splitting along its grain. He'd been meaning to fix it or transfigure it or simply move it out, and he'd done none of these things because the room hadn't mattered until she needed it for the examinations, and now it mattered because she was in it three times a week with her hands on his arm, and he was thinking about this too much.

He rolled his sleeve down and went to find her.

***

The library was where she was. Always the library. In the weeks since her arrival, the vast room had become hers more thoroughly than the guest wing the Manor had assigned her. Her organizational system — colour-coded flags, charmed index cards, a fleet of expanding notebooks that seemed to breed when unobserved — had claimed three of the six main reading tables and was encroaching on the fourth. He'd stopped objecting. His table was the one nearest the south window, where the light was best and where she'd left him a cup of cold tea that morning with a note charmed to the handle: *Preservation charm on the Avery folio is failing. Handle with clean cotton.*

He pocketed the note. The previous one had been about an inconsistency in a ley-line construction timeline. Before that, a correction to his translation of sixteenth-century Malfoy marginalia. He was keeping them because they were useful research artifacts and for no other reason, and the fact that he'd started checking the cup each morning the way you'd check for post was an observation he declined to make.

She was cross-legged on the floor between two shelf units, three books around her, the upside-down notepad replaced by a fresh one. She didn't look up when he came in. He sat at his table and opened the text he'd been working through — early Malfoy estate-binding rituals, the archaic notation dense and fussy — and they worked.

The corridor from the sitting room to the library had been short today. Some days it stretched — the Manor adding alcoves and side passages that hadn't been there the morning before, the house's architecture in slow conversation with itself. Today the house had contracted the route, four turns reduced to two, as if it wanted him in the library quickly. He hadn't fought it. He'd stopped fighting most of the Manor's architectural suggestions, partly because resistance was futile and partly because the house's suggestions were usually right, and partly because admitting that the house was usually right was easier than admitting what it was usually right about.

Forty minutes. The productive silence that had become their default: pages turning, quills scratching, the occasional murmured half-question. "The Flemish derivation here — is the root 'vastmaken' or are they using it metaphorically?" "Metaphorical. Look at the compound two lines down." "Mm." "Wait, no. Look at the next page. They're using it both ways." "Hm. Yeah. That's — that's interesting, actually." Back to the text. The silence closing over the exchange.

He'd read for another ten minutes before he realized he'd been following the same paragraph three times, his attention snagging on the same sentence and losing its grip. The passage described how sympathetic resonance between a warded structure and its inhabitants could create — he glanced at the archaic notation, trying to render it in modern terms — a feedback loop. The building responds to the inhabitants, the inhabitants respond to the building, and the cycle reinforces itself until the structure and its people are, in some magical sense, calibrated to each other.

He put the text down and picked up a genealogical supplement instead. Safer material. Names and dates and inheritance records didn't remind him of anything.

It was good, though. The work. Her. The minutes when they shared the room and the silence between them was companionable rather than wary. He could admit that to himself without examining what the admission cost.

He was working through the genealogical supplement, a dry accounting of inheritance transfers, when she said his name. Not his name. The name she used.

"Malfoy."

Her voice was different. He looked up. She was standing at the far end of the room, near the east wall where the Victorian shelving ended and the stone was bare. She was staring at the wall.

He crossed the library, which took thirty seconds, and in those seconds the air changed — a draft that shouldn't have existed, the smell of stone dust and something older. Sealed parchment. Centuries of unbreathed air.

She was standing in front of a door.

There had never been a door there. He'd grown up in this library. He'd spent years of his childhood reading in the window seats along that wall, his back against stone that was solid and unbroken. The east wall was shelving from floor to ceiling on the north half and bare stone on the south, and there had never, in his memory or in any architectural plan he'd seen, been a door.

There was one now. Dark wood, older than the library's Victorian fittings, with hand-forged iron hardware gone black with age. The stone around it was fitted seamlessly — no cracks, no fresh mortar, no evidence of construction. The house hadn't added this door. The house had uncovered it.

"That's new," he said.

"I noticed."

They looked at each other. She was doing the thing she did when discovery and wariness competed for control of her hands — her fingers reaching toward the iron handle while her stance said she was braced for the door to be warded, hexed, or merely a wall pretending to be interesting.

"You want to open it."

"Don't you?"

He did. He reached past her — close enough that his arm nearly brushed hers, a proximity that was becoming habitual in ways he chose not to examine — and pulled the door open.

The room behind it was small. Four metres by five, maybe less. Low stone ceiling, no windows, the air cool and still and carrying the smell of a space that had been sealed for a very long time — not decay but stasis, the absence of time rather than its evidence. The walls were carved with deep stone alcoves rather than lined with shelves, and in them were scrolls, bound codices, loose folios that looked older than anything in the main collection. Older than the library. Possibly older than the current Manor.

Granger made a sound that was not quite a word. She was inside before he'd finished taking in the room, pulling a scroll from the nearest alcove with the particular care she used on texts that might fall apart under a rough hand. She unrolled it partway and her whole posture changed — the body orienting toward the text with a focus that excluded everything else in the room, including him.

"This is anchoring theory," she said. "Original documentation. Pre-Statute, at least — maybe fourteenth century. Look at the notation." She unrolled further. "This is source material. The main collection references these texts but doesn't contain them. This is — Malfoy, this is the primary literature on ley-line construction and maintenance. This is what we've been looking for."

He stepped into the room and felt the house. Not its thoughts — the Manor didn't think in any way he could translate — but its attention. The stone under his feet was warm. The air in the archive was easier to breathe than the library's permanent staleness, as if the preservation was of a different order — not the slow losing battle against rot that the main collection fought but something more deliberate, the air itself held in place.

He pulled a folio from the nearest alcove on his side. The parchment was thick and stiff, hand-made, the ink faded to a brown that was still perfectly legible. Malfoy family notation — he could read parts of it, the letter-forms ancestral versions of the system his father had used in his own records. Estate-binding protocols. The procedures his ancestors had used to tie the Manor's magic to the family bloodline, written in a hand that was careful and professional and six hundred years dead.

"There's more," she said. She had a codex down now, heavy leather cracked with age, and she was turning pages with the kind of reverence that would have looked performative from anyone else. "Ambient field measurements. Fourteenth or fifteenth century. They were monitoring the magical substrate. They knew it could degrade." She looked up at him, and the expression on her face was pure unguarded want — not for him, for the knowledge, for the answer to the question that had brought her to this house — and it was close enough to another kind of want that something caught in his chest, which he ignored. "This room is everything."

"I need the mapping kit," he said. "For the cataloguing protocol. It's in the main stacks."

He stepped out of the archive into the library.

Three paces. That was how far he got before the sound started — stone against stone, low and enormous, the grind of architecture in motion. He turned.

The doorway was narrowing. The door hung open but the stone frame was contracting, the walls of the archive pressing inward. Through the shrinking gap he could see Granger, frozen, the codex in her hands, watching the walls move toward her.

"Malfoy —"

He stepped back in. The contraction stopped. The walls steadied. The doorway held at its original width as if nothing had happened.

Silence.

"Step out again," she said.

"What?"

"I need to see if it's you specifically or either of us leaving."

He stepped into the library. The grinding started immediately — the house learning its own rule, or enforcing it faster. He stepped back in. Stopped.

"My turn," she said. She put the codex down with a care that cost her visible effort and walked past him through the doorway. Three seconds into the library, the ceiling began to drop, the stone alcoves compressing, the scrolls shifting on their shelves. She came back and the room held.

"Both of us," she said. "The room requires both."

"One more test." He was already moving. "Both leave. See if it comes back."

They stepped into the library together. Behind them, the grinding — louder this time, the room contracting fast and sure. The doorway narrowed to a slit and closed, and the wall was bare stone again. Seamless. No door. No crack. No evidence the room had existed ten seconds ago.

They stood in front of blank wall.

"Touch the stone," he said.

She put her palm flat against the wall. Nothing. He put his beside hers. Nothing. They stood with their hands on cold stone and the room was gone.

Then — slowly, the way the house did things when it wanted the process to be felt — warmth under their palms. A vibration in the stone, faint enough to be blood in their own fingers. The outline of the door emerging like something drawn in fog, the iron hardware materializing, the dark wood forming grain by grain until the door was there again and they were both touching it and the smell of sealed air crept through the seams.

He opened it. The archive was intact. The scrolls on their shelves, the codex where she'd set it down, the air unchanged. The room had waited.

"Both present to open," she said. "Both present to hold it. The house is gating access on proximity."

They went back in. The room held.

He leaned against the stone wall. Cool against his back, the surface slightly rough, the texture of hand-cut stone. The archive was quiet — deeper than the library's quiet, the silence of a room that had not heard voices in a very long time and was adjusting to the intrusion.

He thought about his father, briefly and without wanting to. Lucius had used the library every day for years — Draco had childhood memories of his father at the long table, surrounded by correspondence and financial ledgers, the family's business conducted in the room that now belonged to Granger's research. Lucius had never mentioned a hidden archive. Had never mentioned a room behind the east wall, or texts older than the main collection. Either his father hadn't known — which would mean the house had kept this from him too — or he had known and hadn't shared it, which would be entirely in character but raised its own questions about what the house had shown Lucius and under what conditions.

"It knows," he said.

She looked up from where she'd already reclaimed the codex. "What?"

"What we need. The anchoring texts, the decay documentation — the house had all of it, behind a door it never showed me. I've been in this library my whole life." He heard his own voice and didn't like what was in it. "It shows this room now. Today. After —" He didn't finish. After you touched me and the pain stopped. After whatever happened between your hand and my Mark. He wasn't going to say that.

She waited. She was good at waiting when the waiting served her.

"The house opened a corridor for you the first week," he said instead. "Rearranged the shelves. That was hospitality. Preference, maybe. This is different. Both of us, or the room closes. That's not a preference. That's a condition."

"I know."

"It's a requirement. Proximity as a condition of access. The house is making rules about us being in the same room. As a price for what's in here."

"Yes."

"Doesn't that —" He stopped, recalibrated. "Does that not concern you?"

She set the codex down. "Of course it concerns me. A sentient building just showed us that it gates irreplaceable research based on whether we're both physically present. That's not a quirk. That's a policy. That's the house making a structural claim about our togetherness."

The word sat in the room. Neither of them touched it.

"I don't want the house deciding what I can reach in my own library," he said, and the edge in his voice was harder than he'd intended. "I grew up here. I've spent twenty-five years in this building. It never showed me this room. It shows it now, to both of us, with a lock that only opens when we're standing next to each other. That's not hospitality. That's —"

He stopped himself. The sentence he'd been constructing had something honest in it about what the proximity requirement felt like — intimate and imposed, the house treating their closeness as a kind of currency — and he wasn't going to let it land.

"It's a manipulation."

"Maybe," she said. "Or it's a lock, and we're the key. Not the house choosing to manipulate — the house built this way. Like a ward that requires two signatures."

"A ward doesn't have preferences. A ward doesn't open corridors for one person and rearrange shelves for another."

She acknowledged this with a slight movement of her head. Not agreement. Acknowledgement that he'd said something she couldn't dismiss.

"We need what's in this room," she said.

"I know."

"These anchoring texts — if the decay is caused by war damage to the original infrastructure, this is the only record of how that infrastructure was built. This is the only documentation anywhere. And it's behind a proximity gate that requires us both." She paused. He could see her working through the tangle and choosing which strand to pull. "We can object to the house's conditions and still work within them. Those aren't mutually exclusive."

He looked at her. Cross-legged on the stone floor of a hidden archive, surrounded by texts that could explain why British magic was dying, making the pragmatic case for accepting the terms of a sentient house that had decided their togetherness was a resource it could gate and spend. She was right. She was usually right. The fact that she was right didn't change what the house was doing, or what the proximity requirement meant, or the fact that his Mark had gone quiet under her hand an hour ago and the house had opened this room immediately after, and the connection between those two facts was legible to anyone willing to look at it.

Her hand on his Mark. The pain stopping. The house opening its oldest room.

He didn't say any of this.

"Fine," he said. "We work in here."

"Together."

"Obviously together. The room won't hold otherwise."

She picked up the codex. He pulled a scroll from the nearest alcove — the genealogical record he'd returned to, the estate-binding protocols in notation he could mostly read. He sat down across from her. The room was small enough that his knee was near hers, and if either of them shifted the distance between them would close to nothing, and the distance was the same distance her hand had crossed that morning to reach his arm.

She kicked off her shoes and tucked her socked feet beneath her. She was already into the text, murmuring about anchor-point degradation patterns and ambient field tolerances, making notes in her fresh notebook. He watched her mouth a word in what sounded like medieval Latin, try it again, shake her head, and write it down with a question mark. He unrolled his own scroll and tried to read.

The estate-binding protocols were dense and procedural — instructions for rituals that hadn't been performed in centuries, requiring components and conditions that no longer existed. But threaded through the procedural language were observations about the house itself. The ancestor who'd written this — a Malfoy whose name he didn't recognize, someone from before the family started keeping formal records — had described the Manor as *attendant*. The house attends. It watches. It maintains awareness of who is within its walls and adjusts itself accordingly. The specific word the ancestor had used, in the archaic notation, was closer to *listens* than to *watches*, but neither translation was exact. The house perceived its inhabitants through some sense that didn't map onto human categories, and it responded.

Six hundred years ago, a Malfoy had sat in this room and written about the house doing exactly what the house was doing now. The knowledge should have been reassuring — this was precedented, understood, documented. It wasn't reassuring. It meant the house had been doing this for centuries, shaping the conditions of its inhabitants' lives according to its own assessment of what was needed, and the inhabitants had always been inside the shaping whether they chose it or not.

He read. She read. The archive room was cool but the stone where they sat was warming — the house seeping heat through the floor, through the walls, the building doing the thing it did when it was satisfied with the arrangement of its inhabitants. Her foot nudged his ankle as she shifted position and she didn't seem to notice, and he noticed, and the Mark on his arm ached in the old familiar way, and her hand was three feet from his arm and not touching it, and the house held the room open around them and waited.

The winter dark came down outside windows they couldn't see. The archive had no daylight to lose and no darkness to gain, just its own steady sourceless glow, and in that glow they read, and the house held the room around them — stable, warm, watchful — and the distance between them did not change, and neither of them tried to change it, and the house did not explain what it was doing, because it had never explained anything to him, and he had stopped expecting it to.
