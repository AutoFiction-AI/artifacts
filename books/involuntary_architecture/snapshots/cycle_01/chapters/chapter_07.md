# Chapter 7

The room had not been there yesterday.

Hermione was fairly certain of this. She'd walked this corridor twice the previous evening — once to the library and once back — and the door on the left had been a linen closet. She'd opened it by mistake, looking for the bathroom, and found shelves of folded towels and a smell of cedar. Now the door was taller by a foot, dark-paneled, and it opened onto a sitting room she had never seen.

South-facing windows. Two chairs angled toward a low fire. A tea service on a table between them. The winter light was grey and thin, the frost on the garden visible through glass that carried no condensation, which meant heating charms, which meant the room had been prepared. For whom was answered by the woman already occupying one of the chairs.

"Ms. Granger." Narcissa Malfoy did not rise. "Do sit down."

Hermione stood in the doorway a moment longer than was natural, cataloguing: the tea already poured, two cups, milk in neither. The fire steady and low, the kind that burned without consuming its fuel. Narcissa in a grey winter cloak she had not removed, though the room was warm enough. And the room itself — the proportions, the height of the windows, the slight asymmetry of the fireplace surround — looking as if it had occupied this corridor for centuries, not assembled overnight from the house's private reserves.

She sat. She picked up the cup Narcissa had poured for her. The tea was strong, black, unfamiliar in a way that spoke of a blend someone kept specifically for themselves.

"I didn't know you were coming today," Hermione said.

"I did not announce myself." Narcissa's cup rested in her lap, held with both hands. Her fingers were long and bare of jewelry except for a ring on her left hand — gold, plain, the kind of ring that becomes invisible through decades of wear. Her hands were the only part of her that looked her age: veined at the backs, the knuckles slightly swollen, a woman's hands that had done more work than the rest of her composure suggested. "Draco is addressing the ventilation charms in the east wing. He will be occupied for some time."

The precision of this — Narcissa knowing where her son was, knowing how long the task would take, having arrived specifically when he would be elsewhere — was not subtle. Hermione could appreciate the craftsmanship of it even as she recognized she was being managed.

"You want to talk to me alone," she said.

"I thought that would be more productive."

Hermione drank the tea. It was better than she'd expected — the bitterness clean, not stale, the kind of black tea that improved by being left too strong. Outside, the garden lay under frost, the hedges dark, the gravel paths white-grey, the bare trees holding their shapes against a sky that would darken by half past three. Early November had tipped into December while she wasn't paying attention. The days had shortened around her and the Manor's warmth had absorbed the change, so that the outside cold felt increasingly theoretical, a thing that happened to other buildings.

"I want to ask you about the house," she said.

"I assumed you would."

Narcissa's gaze moved to the window, then returned. "The house has been busy since my last visit. The corridor outside your guest room has shortened by a third — it used to run the length of the east facade. And there is a door near the main staircase that was not there when I came for tea in October."

"I've been keeping a log of the changes," Hermione said.

"Of course you have." The tone was not condescending — it was the brief acknowledgement of one careful woman toward another. "I kept one as well, when I first moved in. A full inventory of every shift, every new door, every corridor that changed direction between morning and evening. It lasted eight months before the record became longer than was useful and I admitted that the log was for my sense of control, not for accuracy."

She smoothed her cloak over her lap — not fussiness but the need to have her hands occupied while she said difficult things. Outside, a bird landed on the frozen hedge and left again immediately, finding nothing.

"Last week we found a room in the library that opens only when both of us are present. It destabilizes if one of us leaves. The house is gating access to research material based on who's in the room." She paused, making sure the next part was precise. "Based on both of us being in the room together."

"Yes. Draco mentioned something of this."

"He told you?"

"He told me very little. I asked questions he could not avoid answering." Narcissa's mouth moved — not a smile, but the acknowledgement that a smile might exist in the vicinity. "He has not yet learned how transparent he becomes when he is trying to be opaque."

This was said with the warmth of a mother describing her child's faults and finding them, on balance, endurable. Hermione noted it and set it aside. Narcissa's love for Draco was useful information, not because it could be leveraged but because it explained the architecture of what she would and wouldn't say.

"Has the house done this before?" Hermione asked. "Gated rooms based on who's present?"

Narcissa drank from her cup. She set it down on the table between them — the motion exact, the cup placed so the handle pointed toward Hermione — and folded her hands in her lap.

"The Manor was built by Aurelius Malfoy in 1247," she said. "Though built is generous. Initiated, perhaps. The original construction has been revised, expanded, damaged, and rebuilt so many times that I doubt a single stone from Aurelius's work remains. But the foundational enchantments are his, or derive from his. The house sits on them the way a cathedral sits on its crypt."

This was Narcissa being precise, not digressing. Hermione recognized the approach — her own, when she was building toward a conclusion that required scaffolding.

"Aurelius intended the house to sustain his line," Narcissa continued. "Not shelter it. Sustain it. The distinction matters. A shelter protects what is already present. A sustaining structure..." She paused, and the pause was selection, not hesitation. "Cultivates."

"Cultivates what?"

"Whatever it determines the line requires. Magical strength. Useful alliances. Productive associations." The word associations was chosen over something else. Hermione could hear the gap where a more specific word had been considered and set aside. "The house has a mandate, Ms. Granger. It reads the people inside it. It responds to what it finds between them. It creates conditions."

"And gating the archive room — that's part of the conditions."

"The house rewards what serves its mandate. It withdraws access from what does not."

Hermione set her cup down. The china was thin enough that she could feel the temperature changing through the ceramic, the tea cooling in real time. She was aware that Narcissa was watching her hands and not looking away.

"You lived in this house for twenty-five years," she said.

"I did."

"And the house — cultivated things. Between you and Lucius."

Something recalibrated in Narcissa's posture. Not a flinch — Narcissa Malfoy did not flinch — but a shift of weight, a tightening of the hands in her lap. She touched her cuff. Adjusted it. The motion was small and automatic, the gesture of someone who had been controlling her responses for so long that the control itself had become a tell.

"When I first arrived — nineteen years old, married three weeks — the Manor was very attentive." Her voice had dropped register, losing the lecture quality, settling into something that sounded more like memory than instruction. "Rooms I had never been told about appeared in corridors I walked every day. The library opened sections that had not been accessible to Lucius. The house would arrange the architecture so that we — met. Unexpectedly. In corners and corridors and rooms that hadn't been there an hour before. The Manor was invested in what we might become to each other."

"Invested how?"

"Invested in the way a gardener is invested in two plants sharing the same bed. Whether the plants wish to be there is a secondary concern."

"That's a metaphor."

"Yes. The reality is less tidy." Narcissa picked up her cup, found it empty, set it down. The empty cup remained on the saucer with the precision of someone who never rattled anything by accident. "The house created conditions. Warmth. Proximity. Access. For a time, the Manor was the most generous version of itself — every room golden, every corridor the right length, the library opening further than it had in decades. And eventually, it stopped."

"Stopped."

"Stopped investing. The rooms returned to their fixed positions. The library sealed the sections it had opened. The corridors stopped rearranging themselves. The Manor — settled." She turned the ring on her finger, one slow deliberate rotation. "Lucius preferred the house that way. Predictable. He said the shifting made him irritable. I do not think he noticed when it ended, or if he did, what he felt was relief."

She did not say loss. The word was present in the space where she had not placed it.

"Mrs. Malfoy—"

"I am telling you this," Narcissa said, and the composure was not a mask now but a frame, the thing that held her together rather than the thing that concealed her, "because the house has not stopped for you. In twenty-five years, the Manor never gated a room behind both my presence and Lucius's together. Not once. Whatever this house is doing with you and my son, it is doing something it did not do for us."

The conversation shifted. Hermione felt it — the dynamic moving, the careful economy of information that Narcissa had been managing suddenly inadequate, because this was not strategic disclosure. This was a woman in her fifties telling a woman in her twenties that the house had tried with her marriage and given up. That whatever it was doing now, with Hermione and Draco, it was trying harder than it had ever tried with her.

"What does that mean?" The question came out stripped of Hermione's usual qualifiers, bare.

"I do not know." For the first time, Narcissa sounded as though she was telling the complete truth. "I know the house's mandate is to sustain the line. I know it reads the people inside it and responds to what it finds. I know it creates conditions and gates access. But I do not know the limits of what it will do, or the mechanism by which it chooses." She stopped. Chose her next sentence with the care of someone setting a stone in mortar. "And I do not know whether what it cultivates would exist without the cultivation."

This was the question Hermione had been circling since the gated room — since before the gated room, since the corridors started shifting for her and the library started rearranging its shelves. Hearing Narcissa articulate it, the older woman's voice careful and almost gentle, made the thing solid in a way it hadn't been before. A named doubt was heavier than an unnamed one.

"The house is not malicious," Narcissa said. "I lived here for twenty-five years and I want to be clear. It did not harm me. It did not harm Draco. But it is not neutral." She straightened in her chair — not standing, not yet, but preparing to. "It has preferences. It acts on them. And you should understand that what you feel inside these walls may not be entirely separable from the walls themselves. That is not an accusation, Ms. Granger. It is a fact about where you are living."

"What would you advise?"

Narcissa stood. The motion was unhurried, the kind of standing-up that someone learned to do elegantly before they learned to read. Her gloves lay on the arm of the chair — grey leather, lined — and she picked them up with a deliberation that closed the conversation.

"I do not advise. I do not have the standing to advise you. I am neither pardoned nor convicted — I live in a rented house in Knightsbridge because the Ministry cannot decide whether my war service was treason or not, and until they decide, I am provisional." She pulled on one glove, then the other, smoothing the leather over her fingers with the attention of someone dressing for an audience with no one. "But my son has been alone in this house for three years, and whatever the Manor is doing, he is less alone than he was. I prefer that to the alternative. I prefer it considerably."

This was the warmth — not diffuse, not general, but targeted and precise, the love of a mother who had calculated the risks and decided that Hermione Granger in her son's house was a risk worth tolerating, even welcoming, provided Hermione understood what she was standing inside.

"One more thing," Hermione said.

Narcissa waited. One hand on the doorframe. Her nails were short and unpainted, a practical detail that caught Hermione off guard for no reason she could name.

"When the house stopped — with you and Lucius. What was that like? Afterward?"

The question was too intimate. Hermione knew it as she asked it. But Narcissa had come here to be honest within her chosen boundaries, and this question fell inside them. Just.

"The house became ordinary architecture," Narcissa said. "The rooms stayed where they were. The walls did not listen. It was quieter." She adjusted her cuff again — the same small motion, the tell she could not fully suppress. "Lucius preferred it."

"Did you?"

Narcissa's face was composed, held. Underneath, the expression of someone who had answered this question for herself many times and never once aloud.

"The house tells you what it thinks of your marriage," she said. "Whether you wish to hear it or not."

She left. Hermione heard her footsteps on the stone corridor — unhurried, even, the stride of someone who knew these halls in the dark. The sitting room remained. The fire continued. The two cups sat on the table between them, one empty and one cold.

---

Hermione stayed in the room for ten minutes after Narcissa left, drinking cold tea and watching the frost thicken on the garden hedges.

She wanted to catalogue what she'd heard. Break it into categories, apply the analytical framework that had carried her through every previous crisis of comprehension. Facts: the Manor's foundational enchantments were designed to sustain the Malfoy line. The house read the people inside it. It responded, conditioned, arranged. It had gated rooms for Hermione and Draco that it had never gated for Narcissa and Lucius in twenty-five years of marriage. The house was more invested in whatever was happening here than it had been in a marriage that produced the heir it was supposedly designed to protect.

But the facts had gaps, and the gaps were Narcissa's, deliberately shaped. She had described what the house did — conditions, access, investment — without explaining the mechanism. She had said the house cultivated without saying what cultivation produced. She had described her own marriage's trajectory with the house as a parable — attentive, then withdrawn — but had not said why the house gave up. Whether it was because the marriage lacked something the house wanted, or because Narcissa and Lucius had refused something the house offered, or because the connection between them had simply not been what the house was looking for.

The distinction mattered. If the house withdrew because the raw material wasn't there — if it tried and found nothing between them worth sustaining — that was one kind of story. If Narcissa and Lucius had actively resisted the house's conditions, and the house had eventually stopped trying, that was a different story entirely. Narcissa had not told her which one it was, and the omission was too precise to be accidental.

She wanted this to be data. It wasn't. It was a woman in grey gloves telling her that the house had stopped trying with her own marriage and then watching it begin again with someone else.

The cup was cold in her hands. She set it down, stood, and went to find the library.

Draco was already at their shared worktable, the ventilation charms apparently handled. He'd spread out a stack of texts he'd carried from the gated archive before it sealed — the room closed without both of them, but its contents, once physically removed, stayed in the main library. He had his cotton gloves on and was annotating in pencil, his handwriting cramped and precise.

"Your mother was here," Hermione said.

He didn't look up. "I know."

"She wanted to talk to me alone."

"I know that too." He made a note in the margin of whatever he was reading. "She does that. You don't have to report."

The exchange was brief and did not pretend to be more. He didn't ask what Narcissa had said. She didn't volunteer it. This was the register they'd settled into over the past weeks — functional, workmanlike, with a dry undercurrent that could on good days pass for warmth. She'd noticed, over the weeks of shared work, that he was most at ease when the conversation was about the research — his attention sharpening rather than his guard, his opinions about runic notation offered with a directness he never managed about anything personal. The difference was legible, and she found herself calibrating to it, steering toward the texts when the alternative was silence or the kind of careful circling that neither of them was good at.

She watched him annotate for a moment. He was left-handed — the pencil held in the deliberate grip of someone taught to write with his right who had eventually refused. His left sleeve was pushed to the elbow, the Mark visible on his forearm, dark and motionless, the snake and skull ink-sharp against skin that rarely saw sunlight. She looked at the Mark and then at the cramped, precise handwriting and then at the text he was studying, and did not let herself think about Narcissa in the sitting room, about the house reading what was between the people inside it.

"The archive?" she asked.

"Whenever you're ready."

They walked to the gated room together. The library was vast enough that the walk took two minutes, past shelves that climbed to the ceiling and rolled ladders Hermione had taken to using with a recklessness that she would have found alarming in a student. The door to the archive appeared as they approached — she'd stopped being startled by this — and the room opened with its particular quality of having been waiting for them. The air inside was warm and carried the smell of old vellum and the faint mineral scent of preservation charms working on material that was centuries older than the spells designed to preserve it.

The ledger she'd been translating was where she'd left it, open on the broad oak table to the section recording Tiberius Malfoy's field work from 1364. Draco took his usual seat. She took hers, across from him, close enough to pass pages between them. He kept his cotton gloves on. She wore one and left the other off — the left hand bare so she could grip a pencil, the right gloved against the vellum. This had become habitual without discussion, the way routines do when two people share a workspace long enough.

The notation system that Tiberius had used was dense — Latin prose with runic modifiers functioning like mathematical operators, each glyph changing the meaning of the base text in ways that were systematic but not immediately intuitive. Draco read the Latin. Hermione decoded the runic syntax. They passed pages, annotated on separate sheets, argued about root forms in a dead scholarly language that neither of them could have read alone.

At one point he passed her a page with a runic compound she'd never encountered — the base form looked like a standard preservation glyph, but an additional stroke changed the root meaning.

"I don't know this one," she said.

"Family variant." He reached to a shelf behind him and produced a separate sheet of vellum she hadn't noticed, covered in annotations in a different hand, centuries newer than Tiberius's. "The Malfoys modified the standard runic system for their own records. My grandfather kept a key." He set the sheet between them. "This one means sustained duration — not preserved as in static, but sustained as in actively fed. The magic required regular renewal."

She studied Abraxas Malfoy's handwriting — ornate but legible, the hand of a man taught penmanship as moral discipline. The key decoded fifteen family-specific variants, each a departure from the standard system that would have been invisible to any outside researcher.

"You didn't mention this before," she said.

"I didn't know where he'd left it." A pause. "The shelf it was on wasn't there yesterday."

She could have pressed this. The house surfacing a reference key at exactly the moment they needed it was either convenient coincidence or the Manor doing what Narcissa had described — investing, cultivating, rewarding the work that served its mandate. She let it go. The work mattered more than the question of who was directing them toward it.

They worked in the focused quiet of collaboration that had found its rhythm — when to speak and when to let the other think. She passed him a page. He returned it with three annotations. She disagreed with two and was right about one, and they spent five minutes arguing about a verb form before she conceded that his reading was more consistent with the earlier entries. This was the part she hadn't expected: not the research itself but the pleasure of the mind across the table. He was precise where she was connective, careful where she was willing to leap. They were better together than apart, which was a fact about their complementary training or another thing the house was arranging, and in the warm archive with Narcissa's words still turning in her head, she could not separate the two.

"This isn't possessive," Hermione said, pointing to a glyph clustered in the margin of a construction entry. "Look at the stem. It's got the temporal marker underneath. He's saying the anchoring was maintained, not that it was owned."

"Maintained by whom?"

"That's what the next entry should tell us."

It did. The next page documented a specific site — described in Latin with enough topographical precision that Hermione could match it to modern geography — where Tiberius Malfoy had performed a series of sustaining rituals on what he called a radix. A root-point. A place where magic was anchored to the physical landscape, driven into the earth and bound there by enchantments that were meant to hold for centuries.

"There's another one here," Draco said, three pages further in. "Severn valley. And here — this one's near what would be the Devon coast."

"How many?"

"At least twelve so far. He's documenting a circuit."

They mapped it on a blank sheet, marking each location as the ledger yielded it. The anchor points formed a rough network spanning southern and central England — places where the foundational magic that sustained the ambient field was pinned to geography. The Salisbury plain. Glastonbury. The Welsh marches. A point in London that corresponded to the approximate location of what was now the Ministry of Magic. And Godric's Hollow — the name leaping from the page in Tiberius's careful Latin, and Hermione's pencil stopping on the paper.

"Godric's Hollow," she said.

"I see it."

"The battle sites. Both wars. The amount of magical combat that happened at or near these locations—"

"Would have damaged the anchor points." Draco's voice had gone careful, precise — the register he used when he was following a line of reasoning faster than he wanted to show. "If they're real. If they're still functional."

"They were functional when Tiberius documented them six hundred years ago. The question is whether anyone maintained them between then and now."

"Given that no one appears to know they exist, I'd say not."

They looked at the map they'd drawn. Twelve points, roughly evenly distributed, connected by what Tiberius described as filum — threads of sustaining magic running between the anchor points and maintaining the ambient field across the territory between them. The infrastructure underlying all of British wizarding magic, built or at minimum documented by a Malfoy ancestor six centuries ago. Unmaintained for most of that time. Sitting directly beneath locations where some of the heaviest magical combat in modern history had taken place.

"This is what's failing," Hermione said. "The ambient decay isn't random degradation. It's structural. The anchor points were damaged — probably during the first war and again during the second — and without maintenance, the damage has been propagating through the network. The filum are degrading. The whole system is coming apart."

"And the only documentation of the system—"

"Is in this library. In this room."

The archive hummed around them. The warm air. The faint vibration in the walls that Hermione had felt on each visit and still could not classify as mechanical or magical. The house was holding them in the one space that contained what the wizarding world needed, and gating that access behind their togetherness.

Draco leaned back. His left hand went to his right forearm — the Mark, bothering him, doing the restless low-grade ache it did between examinations. She could feel it. The pressure behind her own sternum that mapped to the Mark's activity, not pain exactly but the shadow of pain, the way you could feel a sound too low to hear. She filed this under things she was choosing not to examine yet, which was a file that was getting thicker by the day.

"The research isn't optional," she said. "This isn't historical curiosity. If these anchor points can be identified, assessed, potentially repaired — this could be the answer to the decay. The whole decay."

"And you need these texts to do it."

"We need these texts. You can read the Latin. I can read the runes. Neither of us can do both."

He held her gaze across the table, the ledger open between them. Something crossed his face that was not the careful formality, not the dry deflection — something less rehearsed, an acknowledgement of what she was saying and what she was not. That she was here. That she would continue to be here. That whatever the house was doing and whatever his mother had come to say about it, the work demanded their proximity, and the work was not a thing either of them could afford to walk away from.

"Right," he said. "Well. Back to Tiberius, then."

They bent over the ledger. The archive held steady around them, warm and humming, and the work continued.

---

She lay in bed that night and listened to the house.

The Manor made sounds after midnight — not the creaks and groans of old construction settling under temperature changes, though it made those too. Underneath was something else, a rhythm that rose and fell with the slow regularity of breath, if breath could come from stone and plaster and the spaces between walls. Hermione had been sleeping in this house for weeks and she still hadn't decided whether the sound was unsettling or, worse, comforting. Whether she was getting used to it or whether the house was getting used to her.

Her room was steady. The house had been consistent about this — her room in the guest wing, the path to the library, the bathroom down the corridor. These things stayed where they were. Small courtesies, she'd started thinking. A word that implied an intelligence behind the giving.

Two floors above and to the east, Draco was awake.

She knew this the way she'd known it for the past week and a half — a low hum of awareness at the edge of her attention, something she could tune into or ignore but could not turn off. The resonance. She'd been documenting it in her research notes under the heading Diagnostic Residue: Mark-Adjacent Sensory Artifacts, which sounded clinical enough to be reassuring and was becoming less accurate by the day. It wasn't residue. Residue dissipated. This was building.

She could feel the Mark's restlessness translated into her own body — not pain but the awareness of pain, a shadow sensation pressing behind her sternum like a hand held flat against a door. She could distinguish, now, between the Mark's active spikes — sharp and sudden, a match struck in the dark — and its baseline unease, the cursed magic moving restlessly through channels that no longer connected to anything alive. She could tell when he got up and paced, because the signal shifted, became kinetic and irregular. She could tell when he settled, standing or sitting, because the resonance went dense and still.

She knew his patterns of insomnia. She hadn't asked for this knowledge and she could not give it back.

Narcissa's words sat with her in the dark. The house reads the people inside it. It responds to what it finds between them. What you feel inside these walls may not be entirely separable from the walls themselves.

The resonance had been faint when it started, those first days after the initial Mark examination — easy to rationalize, easy to file under known artifacts of sustained spell-contact with cursed objects. Three separate reference texts described the phenomenon. She'd reread those passages multiple times, looking for the clinical framework that would make this manageable. But the texts described residual effects that faded with time and distance. What she had was not fading. Each examination deepened it. Each day in the house made the signal more specific, more detailed, more intimate.

She was carrying information about his body that he hadn't consented to share. His sleeplessness, his pain, the rhythm of his nights. She knew things about his private hours that he didn't know she knew, and she kept this knowledge the way she kept the scar under her sleeve — hidden, habitual, a concealment she maintained because the alternative was a conversation she didn't know how to begin.

She turned on her side. The pillow was too soft — a detail she kept meaning to address in daylight and kept forgetting, because in daylight the house's attentions seemed less personal, more architectural. The sheets were warm. The room was quiet, except for the house's slow breathing underneath the silence.

Above her, two floors east, the resonance shifted. He'd gotten up. She could picture it — though she'd never seen his room, had no reason to know its layout — the way he moved at night, restless and deliberate, the pacing that was not aimless but not purposeful either, the walking of someone who was trying to tire himself into sleep and not succeeding. She could feel the Mark flaring faintly with his movement, the dark magic in it stirred by the act of getting up, of being vertical and awake and alone in a house that never quite let him forget it.

She pressed her face into the too-soft pillow and tried to think about the anchor points, about Tiberius Malfoy's six-hundred-year-old field records, about the research that was important and real and not tangled up with the ache behind her sternum. The decay was structural. The infrastructure was damaged. The work was necessary. These were facts, clean and verifiable, the kind of facts she had built her professional life around.

But she was lying in a house that breathed, in a bed the house had warmed for her, feeling the insomnia of a man two floors away in her own chest, and the clinical vocabulary she'd built to contain this was not holding.

The anchor points. The filum. Tiberius Malfoy's six-hundred-year-old maintenance records and the damaged infrastructure that was killing ambient magic across Britain. She'd spent the afternoon and evening building the argument — the research case, the one she would present to the Department of Mysteries when she could get the data out of this library into a form the Ministry would accept. It was solid work. Important work. The kind of work she was built for, that justified the hours and the proximity and the fact that she was sleeping in a house that a woman in grey gloves had warned her not to trust.

But the resonance didn't care about the research. It cared about the body two floors above her, the Mark's dark magic restless and aching, the particular texture of his wakefulness at — she checked the clock on the nightstand — half past one in the morning. She'd started timing it. She knew that his worst nights ran past three. She knew that the Mark usually settled around four, as if the cursed magic exhausted itself and went dormant with the approach of dawn. She knew these things and she had told no one, and the knowing was becoming its own kind of intimacy, unwanted and ineradicable.

The house settled. The resonance hummed. Above her, Draco stopped pacing and was still.

She did not sleep for a long time.
