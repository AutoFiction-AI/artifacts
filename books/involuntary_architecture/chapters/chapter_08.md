# Chapter 8

By half three the next afternoon frost still clung to the east window, and his left forearm was already on the cleared strip of desk while Granger leaned over the rebuilt array, moving her wand through the last calibration marks as if she could irritate sixteenth-century dark magic into behaving like a Ministry instrument.

For three days she had been rebuilding the diagnostic work from the ground up. The old version had taken them only so far. Surface readings told them what the Mark was doing now: command channels burned out and still trying to fire, cursed magic spiking through damaged routes, the whole thing misfiring more often the colder the days got and the worse he'd slept. What she wanted today sat lower than all that. If the original casting still existed under the later damage, if the first layer of the bind had survived intact because the upper damage had buried it rather than destroyed it, then the curse might be carrying more than structure.

Over tea that morning she had said it without looking at him. "I need to know whether the base layer kept anything lived. I don't mean theory or only the spell's shape. I mean sequence, pressure, sensory carryover, whatever was embedded in the first cast. If it did, then every time I probe deeper without accounting for that, I'm guessing with my hands on a live curse."

He had said fine, because there was no answer worth giving besides yes and no other researcher in Britain was sitting in his house rebuilding an illegal diagnostic array for his arm. He had been saying fine for weeks. Fine had come to mean continue, if this is going to hurt let it at least be useful, stop asking me whether I want alternatives neither of us possesses.

The study off the library corridor was small enough that once the desk, two chairs, and a side table were in place there was hardly room to pace. Granger had made every inch of it serve her. Copper weights sat at the cardinal points of the array. A string of old runes in dark ink ran along the edge of the desk and disappeared beneath his wrist. The Marchetti lay open on a stand beside her notes, held down by a stone paperweight shaped like a sleeping lion. Her own notebook had been opened and shut so often that afternoon the spine had given up and stayed crooked. One cup of tea sat untouched at her elbow. A second, colder cup had already been pushed aside.

He had learned something else over the past month: when she trusted the work, she became quieter. When she did not, she narrated every step and sharpened with each revision. She had been talking to the room since luncheon. It was less for his benefit than for her own. Silence would have meant admitting she did not trust the calibration yet.

She checked the angle of one silver pin, frowned, adjusted it by less than the width of a fingernail, then finally looked at him. "We start with a baseline."

"How clinical of you."

"What's the pain now?"

He considered lying on principle and decided he had not the energy. "Present."

Her mouth tightened. "That isn't a scale."

"It's the scale you have."

"Fine. Is it worse than this morning?"

"Not enough to interest you."

"Everything about it interests me," she said, writing anyway. "I'm asking whether it's worse."

"A little."

She nodded as if the answer had fitted a pattern she was already building in her head. "Any numbness in the fingers, any burning above the elbow, any nausea?"

"No. No. A qualified no."

"Qualified how?"

"If you keep asking questions in that tone, I may become nauseous on principle."

That almost got something out of her. It was not a smile, exactly. The corner of her mouth threatened one and failed. "Good. Humor means you're still conscious."

"The afternoon is young."

Her gaze dropped to his forearm. The posture no longer felt shocking in the way it had at the start. It had stopped feeling indecent somewhere around the sixth examination. Routine had not made it easy. It had only taught his body where to put the elbow, how much weight to leave in the shoulder, which muscles to lock so the hand would not jerk under the first contact.

She set her wand aside and reached for him with bare hands because gloves dampened too much and cloth between skin and spell made the readings muddy. Her left hand bracketed his wrist. Her right palm settled over the Mark.

"I'm going past the surface," she said. "The first layers should feel ordinary. If the older magic wakes up, it may not."

"And you'll know what that feels like?"

"No." A breath left her, sharp and annoyed, mostly with herself. "That's why you're telling me the second it changes."

"My pulse is excellent."

"I've felt your pulse lie to me before."

The first pass was familiar by now. Magic moved over the Mark in a controlled wash, reading density, contour, the current distribution of dark magic through skin and muscle. The worst of it was often anticipation. Once she began, the body had something specific to do besides wait. He counted the pressure of her thumb at his wrist. He counted the sting of air coming through the cracked frame and collecting along the knuckles of his free hand. He counted the breaths it took before the usual dull ache under the Mark softened into something quieter under her touch.

That part still unnerved him more than the pain did. The curse should not have quieted for her. It had not quieted for anyone else, not for healers who kept their gloves on and spoke to him as if he were a difficult animal, not for the one Unspeakable Croaker had pushed at him before Granger arrived, not for the half-dozen diagnostic charms he had cast badly on himself at three in the morning after nights when sleep was impossible. Under her palm, though, the Mark behaved as if it recognized the contact and preferred it.

She felt it too. Her eyes flicked up to his and away again. "Upper channels are still resisting. The dead routes are trying to fire through scarred tissue. That's what we'd expect."

"Comforting."

"I didn't say comforting." Her thumb shifted against his pulse. "I said expected."

The array answered her, low and bright. Inked runes along the desk edge silvered at the corners. She pressed deeper, not in force but in precision, and the ache changed character. It gathered itself instead of spreading. The pain that usually wandered up toward his elbow narrowed to one exact point under her hand.

He drew in a slow breath.

"There," she murmured, mostly to the page in her head. "Surface decay where we expected it. Lower channels still active. The upper curse matrix is badly damaged, but it's not the oldest thing in here. There's another layer underneath. Packed tight."

His fingers tightened on the wood.

The look on her face had changed. Anxiety had not gone, but something harsher had joined it now: interest stripped right down to the nerve. She leaned closer to the array, eyes moving between the mark itself, the runes, and his hand like she was trying to catch three truths at once before they slipped out of alignment.

"The damage on top may have preserved it," she said. "That's absurd, but I think that's what happened. The later command channels burned and scarred and all of it sealed the first cast under them."

"You sound delighted."

"I sound right." She swallowed. "Which is not the same thing."

He might have said something dry to that on another day. Instead he watched the pulse in her throat jump once. She was frightened. She was also absolutely not stopping.

Her hand flattened more firmly over the Mark.

"It's old," she said. "Older than the reactivation. Older than the recent damage by decades, obviously, but I mean old in the spellwork. It's dense. It's original. If the first layer preserved any of the installation sequence, then this could tell us what the bind was built to do before it started failing."

"And if it tells us in a way neither of us enjoys?"

"Then I stop." She finally looked at him properly. "I mean that."

He believed she meant it. He did not know whether either of them would get to keep the choice once she crossed the line.

"All right," she said, more quietly now. "I'm going in carefully."

Careful bought her less than a second.

The change started where her skin touched his and dropped through the Mark like a trapdoor giving way under both their weight. Pressure drove inward, hit something old and buried, and came back out with force enough to make the array flare white. The pain did not spread gradually. It opened. One moment the Mark was a narrow wound under her palm, and the next it felt as if the curse had split along some sealed seam and forced its way all the way through him.

"Granger."

"I know."

The Mark convulsed.

Dark magic tore through his forearm, shoulder, ribs, and whatever wall had been keeping the present fixed in place went with it.

---

He was sixteen, and the first thing the memory gave him was not the room but the body carrying him into it. The shoulders under the white school shirt were still too narrow. The wrist bones looked wrong above the hand braced on the desk, as if the rest of him had not caught up yet. Fear lived too high in his chest to count as breathing. He knew the grain of the oak under his palm before he looked up, because he had known that desk all his life.

It had been Lucius's study once. By July it belonged to the Dark Lord in every way that mattered.

Heavy curtains shut out the evening. The desk had been stripped to bare wood except for one candle and a folded length of black cloth. Blank rectangles marked the far wall where portraits used to hang. The painted Malfoys had been removed rather than asked to witness this, or perhaps they had been smart enough to vanish of their own accord. Either way, the room looked abandoned by everything that might have made it feel inherited instead of occupied.

The air smelled of leather, hot wax, old stone, and fear gone sour. His own sweat sat cold along the base of his spine. Lucius smelled the same, though sixteen-year-old Draco did not parse it that way. At sixteen the shaking in the hand on his shoulder registered only as command. Stand still. Do not disgrace yourself. Do not make this harder than it already is.

At twenty-five, dragged all the way back through it with a second consciousness somewhere on the receiving end, he could not miss the tremor.

Lucius's thumb dug into the muscle above his collarbone hard enough to hurt. The pressure was too tight to be reassuring and too helpless to count as restraint alone. The older man was holding him in place because the room required it and because he could not imagine another thing to do with his body. Both truths sat in the grip at once.

His left arm lay across the desk. The cuff had been shoved back in an untidy bunch because no one had thought to tell him clothing might matter today. He had come straight from the rest of the house in a school shirt because there had still been part of him stupid enough to think this would involve words first. The inside of the forearm was pale in candlelight, the veins blue and visible, the skin unbroken. It looked younger than the face he had been carrying around all summer. It looked like a boy's arm, because it was.

Across the desk stood the Dark Lord.

Memory would never hold the whole face at once. It kept the white skin. It kept the red eyes, bright and depthless. It kept the slit of a mouth that never looked shaped for speech people were supposed to survive hearing. The rest blurred every time, as if his mind had decided one clear look was sufficient for a lifetime and would not assist further.

"Your father assures me you're ready."

The lie went through the room without resistance.

Draco opened his mouth and found nothing in it. Whatever answer he had thought he might produce at the top of the stairs had deserted him between the corridor and the desk.

"He is, my Lord," Lucius said behind him.

The voice held. The hand did not.

Draco remembered wanting, right up until that moment, to be equal to whatever this was. He had wanted it in every adolescent way available to him. He wanted the fear to stop looking like weakness. He wanted assignment, importance, an end to the months of being too young to be trusted and old enough to be threatened. He had come into the room already trying to stand like a person who deserved the summons.

The wand touched the inside of his arm and removed the possibility of misunderstanding.

The first sensation was cold. It was not surface cold. It felt driven in, a corpse chill pressed exactly where the skin was thinnest, and his whole body flinched before the incantation had finished because the magic gathering at the point of contact already knew where it meant to go. He never kept the words. The memory stored the shape of the mouth moving and nothing of the sound.

Then the burning arrived.

It punched through skin and did not stop there. It went down through muscle, through the thin vulnerable line between bone and nerve, and then deeper still, into the channels his own magic used, making room for foreign magic by force. He had not known a curse could feel invasive in that exact way, not just painful but territorial, as if something inside his body were being shoved aside to make space for a claim that had already decided it belonged there.

His body tried to wrench away before thought could catch up. Lucius's grip multiplied. One hand locked at his upper arm. The other drove hard between his shoulder blades and forced him down over the desk while the wand wrote its claim into him.

The edge of the oak hit his hip. His teeth struck wood hard enough that the taste of blood flooded his mouth. He heard himself make a noise that had nothing in common with speech. It was raw and shocked and small in a way that still humiliated him now. The sound of it scraped through the room and changed nothing.

The curse kept going. It did not matter that he was already trying not to have a body. It used the body anyway. The cold and the burning had become one thing now, a current forced all the way through the arm and upward toward the shoulder, down again to the wrist, then inward where his own magic ought to have been his. The point was not only to wound. The point was occupation.

His vision blew white. Shapes returned in dirty bursts, not enough to make sense of. The candle lengthened and bent. The red of those eyes stayed where everything else smeared. He could not feel his knees properly anymore. He could feel Lucius still holding on.

For one impossible second he knew two things together: that his father was afraid and that his father was not stopping this.

Then his legs failed completely. Stone tipped sideways. The last clear sensation was the wand still moving against the arm that had been bare and unmarked that morning, and then even that went out.

---

The study returned by pieces. First the desk under his hand. Then the hard edge of the chair against the backs of his thighs. Then the candle Granger had lit against the early dark, and the east window gone white at the corners. He was twenty-five again. Her hand was gone.

Hermione had recoiled so far back in her chair that only the front two legs still touched the floor. Both her palms were lifted off the table as if his skin might still burn through hers. All the colour had left her face. Her mouth worked once before sound came.

"Oh God."

The resonance between them was no longer a distant thrum or a low spill he could ignore when he was tired. It had blown wide open. Her shock hit him before he had his own breathing under control. He felt the afterimage of his memory still ricocheting through her body: the high trapped breath, the pain line inside the arm, the sickened certainty of being too young for the room and inside it anyway. There was no clean border anywhere. Whatever had just happened had used his body as the path in and hers as the place it landed.

He yanked his arm off the desk. The Mark looked unchanged, but the skin around it had flushed dark and the veins stood up under the surface as if the curse had come too close to the skin and stayed there.

He reached for the cuff. The first button missed. The second almost went through. On the fourth try he got his fingers to obey.

"Malfoy."

"Don't."

The word came out flatter than he intended and more frightened than flat had any right to be.

She swallowed and forced her hands down to the desk one at a time, like someone approaching a wild thing from too close. "Can you move your fingers?"

He flexed them because speaking was suddenly more difficult than the motion.

"Any numbness?"

"No."

"Dizziness, nausea, anything tearing further up the channels?"

"Everything hurts," he said. "The rest appears disappointingly intact."

Her eyes flashed, not with amusement but with irritation that he was making her sort precision out of that answer. "Is the pain moving above the elbow?"

"No."

"Is it sharper than before?"

"Yes."

"All right." She took a breath she did not finish properly. "All right."

The candle spat once. Their breathing was the only other sound.

Then she said, with the stiff care of someone placing glass on a stone floor, "I need to say this accurately."

A humorless breath left him. "Do you?"

"Yes." Her voice steadied as it moved toward facts. That was how she saved herself. "What came through clearly was the room, the wand, your father's hand, your age, and the pain. I know you lost consciousness. I know it wasn't symbolic or interpretive. I wasn't seeing around it. I was in the sensory sequence with you. If I say anything beyond that, if I talk about what your father meant or what you meant, then I'm inferring from pressure and context. That would be me, not the spell."

Jaw tight, he looked at the window instead of at her.

"Sixteen," he said before she could ask. "I'd just turned. It was July."

He heard her inhale. The sound was small, but the widened resonance carried the effect of it right through him. "I felt how young you were."

"I doubt that was subtle."

"No." She did not flinch from the admission. "No, it wasn't."

Silence pressed again. He could feel her trying not to say the next thing before she said it. The effort made the line of her mouth harsher.

"Your father held you," she said at last.

He laughed once and hated the sound of it. "He held me still."

That made her go very still indeed. "That isn't the correction you think it is."

"It's the one that matters."

"To whom?"

He turned then, because if he kept looking at the glass he would not be able to tell whether the pressure in his chest belonged to the memory or the present. "To me."

She absorbed that. He could see her doing it. She always did her thinking in the open whether she meant to or not. "All right," she said. "Then tell me what I'm flattening."

He stared at her. The question was too direct, too competent, too much like an invitation he had not granted.

"I walked into the room," he said, each word thin with effort. "I put my arm out. I wanted it right up until the wand touched me. After that he held me there. If you want the accurate version, keep all of it."

The words sat between them. He wanted them back immediately and could not call them.

She did not rush in to improve them. The only sign that they had landed at all was the way her eyes shut once and opened again. "All right," she said. "I will."

He looked away first.

"You were still sixteen," she said after a moment, quieter now. "Nothing about what you just said alters that."

He had no reply that did not sound either defensive or obscene. He pushed his chair back and stood. The room tilted once and righted itself. At the window the lawn beyond the glass had gone colourless under the sinking afternoon. He put one hand against the cold frame because he needed some surface in the present more than he needed dignity.

Behind him she tried, for a few futile seconds, to put what had happened back into language that belonged to the work. He heard it assembling in starts and restarts. "Imprint," she said once. Then, after a beat, "Residual sequence." Then, "Transmission."

None of the words were large enough. A boy had been pinned to a desk while foreign magic was burned into his arm. No academic term would carry the weight of that without turning dishonest.

When she spoke again, the factual register had thinned. "The deeper layer wasn't passive," she said. "It recognized the contact and used your present channels as if the inscription were happening now. I don't know yet whether that's because the curse stores installation context by design or because your magic and mine have been in contact too often and it had a path."

He kept his hand on the frame. "I can't decide which answer I prefer."

"Neither can I." Her chair scraped softly. "I am not doing that version again until I know how to stop it."

He turned his head enough to see her in his periphery. She had not moved closer. If anything, she had shifted back from the desk a little more, like touch itself had become newly suspect. That should have relieved him. It didn't.

"Good," he said.

"At least let me look at the skin once more before you go. I don't mean the memory. I mean the actual arm."

He drew his cuff the final inch over the Mark. "No."

Her mouth set. "If the pain climbs the shoulder or you lose sensation in the hand, send for me."

"Granger."

"I'm aware that you hate being managed. This is not a negotiation."

That almost sounded like the old frame. It almost let him stand inside patient irritation instead of whatever this was, but only almost.

He let the hand drop from the window. "We're done."

"Yes," she said, and the word held more than agreement. "We're done for today."

Neither of them moved for a moment. Then he left the study before either of them could find another sentence and make the room worse.

---

Outside his rooms he put his forehead against the corridor wall and stayed there until the stone numbed the bridge of his nose and the pressure in his skull had something external to answer to.

When he finally pushed himself upright, the house around him looked offensively unchanged. The corridor lamp still burned in its usual sconce. The runner still lay straight along the floorboards. Somewhere below, a door shut with the soft carrying thud the Manor reserved for ordinary afternoons. There was always something absurd about great houses continuing to function after a person had been split open in one of their side rooms.

His own rooms were no better. The washbasin held cold water. The mirror held his face. The chair by the bed had the morning's jacket thrown over it exactly as before, as if the day had taken place without involving fabric or skin or history and he had only stepped out for correspondence.

He stripped the shirt off before he quite knew he had decided to. The sight of the white cuff at his wrist had become unbearable. In the glass his body looked older again and no more convincing for it. The Mark was dark as bruised ink against the flushed skin around it. The channels above it stood out faintly under the surface, a pattern of strain he could see now only because Granger had taught him what to look for.

He bent over the basin and splashed water across his face, then across the arm. The cold shocked the skin and did nothing useful to the rest of him.

On the third attempt he got a jumper over his head without jarring the arm hard enough to swear. The wool was charcoal. It had no cuffs and no schoolboy whiteness left anywhere he could see without trying.

That should have helped more than it did.

Pain could have remained abstract. Plenty of people knew the Mark hurt. Plenty of people knew boys had taken it too young. They knew those things the way the public knows war did damage: as category, as symbol, as one more item in the moral inventory they keep because it helps them sort the dead from the guilty. The specific bodily facts rarely interested anyone for long. The public version of the Dark Mark had room for ideology and spectacle. It had less room for teeth striking wood, for a hand shaking at the shoulder, for the exact raw animal sound a sixteen-year-old made when foreign magic was pushed into him through the arm.

Now Granger had that version. No one had ever had that version of him. His mother did not, though she knew the before and after and had not been inside the room. Theo did not, because he knew enough to stop asking certain questions. The Ministry certainly did not. Granger had the body of it: the breath, the age, the pain.

The worst part, or perhaps only the part he could currently get hold of, was that he had given her the correction himself. He had not let her remain with the simpler story. He had stood there with his arm still burning and said the thing that made him less bearable even to himself.

I wanted it right up until the wand touched me.

There was no use pretending that had been youthful confusion broad enough to wash his hands in. He had wanted what the Mark appeared to promise. He had not wanted the pain, obviously, or the room, or the hand between his shoulders. He had wanted belonging so badly that it had made any price look hypothetical. He had wanted to stop being a frightened rich boy nobody trusted with the real work. He had wanted significance. He had wanted the world arranged into a shape where fear could pass for loyalty and loyalty could pass for adulthood.

Then the wand touched him and the abstraction ended.

If Granger kept only the part where Lucius held him still, she would make him simpler than he was. If she kept only the part where he had wanted it, she would make him simpler too. The whole filthy truth required both. That was what he had been trying to protect when he snapped at her. He had not been defending Lucius. He certainly had not been defending Lucius. Lucius did not require defense from the fact of his own hand. Draco had been defending the complexity because the alternatives were pity or contempt, and he had lived on both long enough to know neither improved the air.

Her answer had not been either. That was what unsettled him most.

You were still sixteen.

It was such a flat fact. There was no absolution in it. There was no argument either. It made no attempt to rescue him from himself, and it showed no eagerness to pin the whole thing on one man so the rest could become narratively convenient. The sentence did not free him. It also did not leave him where he had been. He did not know what to do with that.

He should have stayed upstairs. He should have sent word through the door if the pain worsened and otherwise left the rest of the evening untouched. Instead he found himself back in the corridor fastening the jumper properly with his good hand and setting out toward the library because the thought of sitting alone with the reopened link made his own rooms feel too small.

The walk should have taken the long turn past the portrait stair. It did not. A side door that was usually shut stood open, and the corridor beyond ran straight where it ordinarily kinked. The Manor was giving him the shorter route. He noticed because he noticed everything the house did around Granger now. He was too wrung out to resent the convenience.

By the time he reached the library, the fire had been built up.

Hermione sat in her usual chair with the Marchetti open on her lap, her shoes kicked off near the hearth. She had washed her hands. He could tell because the ink on the side of her thumb was gone and the skin there looked raw where she had scrubbed too hard. Her hair had escaped its knot in several places. She was staring at the same page with enough concentration to make it obvious she was not reading a word of it.

She looked up when he entered. The expression that crossed her face was not surprise. It was not relief either. It was something harder to categorize and less flattering to both of them. She had expected him either way. That was all.

He took the chair opposite without asking whether she wanted company. The widened resonance was still there, thinned a little by distance and walls and time but nowhere near shut. From across the hearth he could feel the leftovers of her body reacting to the afternoon: a sour twist low in the stomach that was not his, a phantom heat across the palm that had rested on the Mark, the kind of exhaustion that settled in the muscles before it reached the face. It made the room feel crowded even in silence.

She watched him sit. Her gaze flicked once to his changed clothes, then away quickly enough that she probably had not meant him to catch it.

He pulled the nearest book off the side table and opened it to the middle. The page might as well have been in Bulgarian for all the use it did him. Across from him, she did not turn her own page.

The library had its ordinary smells: old paper, dust, a faint mineral edge from the preservation charms sunk into the stacks. The room was too large to feel intimate and somehow did anyway when only two people occupied it. Firelight moved over the spines and table edges in slow amber shifts. Somewhere in the distance the house settled with a sound like old joints reluctantly agreeing to another evening.

After several minutes of the same unread line, he said, "It's still there."

Her eyes lifted at once. "Yes."

"Is it going to stop?"

She took time with the answer, which meant it was bad. "I don't know."

He let the book close under his hand.

"I can feel the aftermath of it," she said. "I can't feel the room now or the whole sequence. I only get... afterimages. Bodily ones." She grimaced at the inadequacy of the phrase. "The link's wider than it was before."

"You say that as if we're discussing drainage."

"I'm trying not to say it badly."

"You may have missed that opportunity."

That got her closer to a smile than before and just as unhappy with it. She looked down at the book on her lap instead. "All right. Then plainly. The deeper layer can transmit memory through the resonance, and it can do it hard enough that the person on the other end isn't observing. They're participating."

"I had gathered."

"Yes. I know." She rubbed one thumb hard over the edge of the page and seemed surprised to find paper there. "What I don't know yet is whether it requires bare-skin contact, whether it requires me specifically, whether it was triggered by the older layer, or whether the bond's simply reached a point where the Mark can use it for anything it likes."

"Comforting," he said, and this time she let him have the sarcasm because he had earned it.

"I won't do another deep pass until I can answer at least one of those questions."

"And if the arm gets worse before then?"

Her jaw tightened. "Then we go back to surface work. External readings. Dampening if it spikes. I hate all of those options, so you don't need to tell me they're inadequate."

"I wasn't going to."

"You were thinking it very loudly."

He looked at her.

The faintest flush rose along her cheekbones, whether from the fire or from having admitted what the link was still doing he could not tell. "That wasn't meant as a joke," she said. "I actually can feel more than before."

"Splendid."

"I know."

He should have left it there. Instead he heard himself ask, "What does it mean for the work?"

That was enough to pull her fully upright. Asking a practical question gave her something to stand on. "It means the original bind wasn't just inscribing a symbol or a command structure. It was preserving installation context at the level of the body. That's important. It tells us the curse was built to remember more than obedience. It may be one reason the damaged channels keep trying to reopen. The lower layer remembers a complete event and keeps attempting to reassert the original path."

She pushed the Marchetti onto the table between them and flipped to a page she had plainly not been reading a moment earlier. Notes crowded the margins in her tight hand.

"If that's true," she said, tapping one paragraph, "then the reactivation wasn't only old dark magic waking up. It was a damaged system trying to repeat the moment of inscription without having the intact structure to support it. That's why the pain's so chaotic now. It's not only firing commands. It's replaying injury."

The explanation did not help. It was not supposed to. It did, however, make the afternoon newly inevitable.

"And today you gave it the right path," he said.

Her hand flattened over the page. "Yes."

She did not soften the admission. She also did not overclaim it. "Or rather," she said after a beat, correcting herself the way she always did when precision mattered more than dignity, "I gave it contact with the one person it's been responding to since the first examination and then I pushed into the oldest preserved layer without properly accounting for the bond. That is more accurate and not better."

"You do love accuracy."

"More than is attractive in a woman, I've been told."

He blinked at her. The line had arrived so dry and so unadorned that laughter nearly came before he knew it had been invited. It failed halfway to the surface and left only a rough exhale.

Her eyes flicked up. "That was a joke."

"I noticed."

"Good. I wasn't sure I'd executed it properly."

The room eased by a fraction. Not enough to count as ease, perhaps, but enough that breathing stopped feeling like a task set by hostile bureaucracy.

After a while she said, "Tea?"

The word was so ordinary it nearly undid him.

"Please."

She stood, moved to the side table, and set the kettle going with her wand. The domestic business of it should have been absurd after the afternoon. Instead it was the first thing that had felt remotely survivable since the study. Water heated. Porcelain clicked softly as she chose two cups from the tray. They never matched. Tonight one was cream with a hairline crack running through the glaze and the other dark blue with the gilt worn from the rim.

He watched because not watching would have been more conspicuous. She measured the leaves carefully despite the tremor that still occasionally touched the fingers of her right hand. When she brought his cup over, she did not pass it directly into his hand. She came close enough for the steam to reach him, then seemed to think better of the final inch and set it on the table within reach instead.

The tiny caution in that movement told him more than anything she had said in the study. Touch between them had not been clinical for some time, not really. The examinations had become too familiar, the Mark too responsive, the silence around them too inhabited. But until this afternoon they had been able to pretend the frame still held. Now even handing over tea required negotiation with all the things they were not discussing.

He picked up the cup only after she had taken her seat again.

Warmth reached his hands before the smell did. It was strong black tea, too hot and therefore perfect.

For several minutes they drank without speaking. The heat helped. So did having a task his body understood.

At last he said, "I'm sorry you had to feel that."

Across from him she braced her own cup in both hands. "I'm not. Sorry, I mean. About knowing."

His eyes lifted to hers.

"I'm sorry it happened to you," she said. "I'm not sorry I know."

"You ought to be."

"No." She caught herself, frowned, and started again. "I mean yes, obviously, if the question is whether anyone should have to experience that at all. But if the question is whether I wish I didn't know now, then no. I don't."

"Why?"

She looked down into the tea as if the answer might be hiding there and then, with visible annoyance at herself, answered honestly anyway. "Because someone should've."

He leaned back in the chair. "That is not a medical position."

"No," she said. "It isn't."

The fire shifted. A coal broke inward with a soft bright collapse.

"Most people know the Mark hurts," he said after a while.

"That's not what I mean, and you know it."

He said nothing.

"They know what it meant politically," she went on. "They know what it signaled. Some of them know what it did to people in the abstract. That's not the same as knowing what happened to you in that room." Her fingers tightened around the cup. "I'm not claiming that helps. I'm saying it's different."

He looked back at the book on the table because looking at her had become too exacting. "Different is doing a great deal of work there."

"Fine. It is doing too much work." The impatience in her voice was brief and familiar and, perversely, reassuring. "What I mean is that it shouldn't have been solely yours to carry once it wasn't solely yours to survive."

The sentence was messy. She seemed to know it was messy. She did not tidy it.

He understood it anyway.

Before he could answer, a pulse of queasiness ran through him. He almost blamed the tea until he looked up and saw her swallow against the same sensation. The link had carried it. Her face hardened with the effort of not reacting visibly.

"It's still bleeding through," he said.

"Yes." Her voice came thinner now, because the honesty was exhausting her. "Not thoughts, before you ask. Just body. If I get startled, or if the arm flares, or if one of us gets a spike hard enough to trip the rest of the system, the other one gets a portion."

"A portion."

"I'm choosing that word because the alternatives are worse."

"What are the alternatives?"

She hesitated. "Shared. Or reciprocal. Or invasive."

"Marvelous."

"I know."

He took another drink of tea because otherwise he might have said something not worth keeping.

After a minute he asked, "Did you write any of it down?"

Her gaze moved to the notebook beside her chair. It was closed now. "Only what matters to the mechanics."

"Meaning?"

"It means the deeper layer preserved sensory sequence. It means direct contact with the original bind can trigger full transmission through the resonance." She paused. "I did not write down the room."

"Or my father?"

"Or that."

"You didn't write down the rest?"

This time she held his gaze. "The rest isn't mine to itemize."

He felt something in him recoil from the line because it was too decent and because decency, from her, had always been harder to bear than anger. "You didn't ask permission before going in."

"No," she said, and the word landed without defense. "I didn't. I had permission to examine the Mark. I did not have permission for what happened after the threshold gave way. Those are not the same thing."

He looked down into the cup. Steam blurred the rim for a second and cleared.

"Neither did I," he said.

When he looked up again, she had gone utterly still. She was not startled or pitying. She was simply still in the presence of a fact she was not going to improve with language.

"No," she said after a moment. "You didn't."

It was a worse answer than apology and therefore better.

They let that sit.

Eventually she said, "If you want me to destroy even the mechanical notes, say so."

He considered it. The impulse to erase the entire afternoon had obvious appeal. So did the colder impulse to keep every useful fact and amputate the human cost from it. Both were forms of cowardice, which did not make them untempting.

"No," he said at last. "Keep what's useful."

"All right."

"And keep it accurate."

Her mouth made that almost-smile again, the one that never quite reached comfort. "I know better than to do otherwise where you're concerned."

He might once have answered that with something polished and deflective. Tonight he only nodded.

The books remained open and useless between them. She did not try to steer them back toward work. He did not pretend to read. Outside the high windows the light failed completely and the library turned into fire, shadow, and the pale geometry of shelves. The house made its small evening sounds around them. Somewhere a pipe knocked once behind the wall. Somewhere farther off a door eased shut and held.

Granger tucked one bare foot under herself in the chair, a posture he had never seen her take in the study and probably would not have seen there even on a better day. The gesture was intimate only because it was unconscious. She was too tired to maintain the full professional shape of herself.

He realized, not for the first time and not with any particular gratitude, that the afternoon had destroyed the last plausible version of the old arrangement. She could no longer touch his arm and pretend it was only diagnostic. He could no longer submit to her hands and pretend he was only being useful. Whatever happened next with the research would have to make room for the fact that she had been inside the worst moment of his life and had come back carrying it in her own nerves.

That did not make them kinder to each other by magic. It did not make the work simpler. It did not excuse a single thing that had happened in the room at sixteen or in the years after.

It did, however, make leaving harder than staying.

So he remained in the chair while the tea cooled and the fire settled lower. Hermione sat across from him with her own cup warming in both hands, her book unread, the same ugly knowledge lodged under her skin now. They did not try to return the evening to procedure. They did not promise each other anything about tomorrow. The quiet between them was no longer careful distance. It was the quiet of two people who had been inside the same pain and had not yet found a way to stand anywhere else.
