# Chapter 16

In the three days since the kiss, the archive had been letting him in.

Without Granger, the room held itself together reluctantly. The ceiling shifted by inches. The reading table listed to the left. Twice this week the far alcoves had gone dark while he was standing in them, and he'd backed out before the walls could remember they knew how to close.

Today it held.

At the third unit on the east wall he found the ley-line cross-index she'd mentioned yesterday. Behind it sat a folio thick as a brick, bound in black leather, older than the anchoring records beside it. An old catalogue sigil still glimmered on the inside cover: 14-C. Main library shelving. The house had moved it.

The title page was layered with family notation over Latin. His Latin was good enough.

*On the Binding Architecture of the Manor and Its Proper Maintenance for the Perpetuation of the Line.*

His hand stayed on the page.

The opening sections were legible. His great-great-grandfather had written them in the brisk, compressed hand Draco knew from family records, then added plain-English glosses in the margins for descendants who might need the information without the scholarship.

*The Manor sustains the line by strengthening correspondences between inhabitants.* A gloss beneath it: *The house presses where attachment already gathers.*

*The innermost rooms answer more readily to paired inhabitants than to one.* Another gloss: *It tests relation through proximity, recurrence, and mutual awareness at distance.*

The next page was worse. Water damage had eaten the lower half. Ink blurred into the parchment in a grey bloom that spared fragments and swallowed verbs.

*Activation requires... emotional accord...*

Underneath, in the margin:

*The house does not... where none... Yet once roused it intensifies attention, contrives repeated nearness, and may be mistaken for feeling itself by inexperienced inhabitants.*

Draco read the damaged gloss twice.

He couldn't tell what missing word had sat after *does not*, or whether the house started the fire or found a spark and blew on it until the whole room went up. The uncertainty was the point now. The house shaped conditions. It could make what was happening between them impossible to trust, and Granger had found that out weeks ago.

Five weeks ago she had gone formal overnight. He'd spent that week certain she'd finally seen him clearly and recoiled. The dates arranged themselves with humiliating ease. She found the folio. She pulled back. Then she came close again and never told him why.

After the scar she came back to him. After the kiss she still said nothing.

He put a hand flat on the damaged page and pictured her here alone, reading faster than he could, stopping at the same ruined line. She would have seen more of it than he did, perhaps enough to know, perhaps only enough to panic, but certainly enough to understand that what the house was doing should have been said aloud.

By the time the walls narrowed, he was already on his feet. The folio stayed open on the table as he left. In the main library beyond, her reading chair sat by the window with a book facedown on the arm. She had probably meant to come back to it after lunch. She was always meaning to come back.

He found her in the library an hour later.

At the cataloguing table she was bent over the St. Mungo's reports with two anchoring texts open at her elbow. She looked up as he came in, still half in the work.

"I found the break in the ward layering," she said. "The third anchoring point isn't built on the same structure as the first two, which means the decay may be running through two different systems and if that's true we need to-"

"When did you find the folio?"

The quill stopped moving. Ink gathered at the nib and dropped onto the page.

"What folio."

"Don't." His voice stayed even. "The binding folio. Black leather. Old 14-C mark. It's in the archive now. When did you find it?"

For a second she only looked at him. Then she set the quill down with too much care.

"Five weeks ago," she said. "Maybe a little over. I was looking for an explanation."

"For what."

"For the archive. The corridors. The awareness through the Mark." Her jaw went hard. "For all of it."

She said it looking straight at him. The rest stayed behind her teeth.

"And then you pulled away."

"I panicked."

"You knew enough to panic."

"I knew enough to know the house was in it. I didn't know what it meant."

"You knew it meant there was a question over everything that happened after."

She didn't answer.

He took another step into the room, stopping short of the table but close enough to smell ink and old paper and the tea she'd left untouched beside her notes.

"The activation section is damaged," she said quickly. "I couldn't read the whole clause. I couldn't tell if the house was strengthening an attachment that was already there or if it was-"

"Making it."

"Yes." Her fingers pressed into the table edge. "I didn't know."

"You still didn't tell me."

"I was trying to understand before I put that in your hands."

"It was already in my hands." His left arm had started to ache, a low warning. "It was in my house. In my body. In every room I walked into. You don't get to act as if this was yours to hold until you felt ready."

Her gaze dropped to the blot she'd made on the report. Her shoulders drew in before her jaw set again.

"I know," she said.

"No." The word came out sharper than the rest. "I don't think you do."

Silence stretched. Outside, wind scraped at the tall library windows. Deep winter light, pale and thin, lay across the floor between them.

"I found a mechanism, not an answer," she said. "I kept thinking if I read one more text I'd get certainty and then I could tell you anything useful instead of dropping-"

"Instead of dropping what." His voice flattened. "That the house might have its hand inside my- that maybe nothing between us was-" He stopped.

"Malfoy-"

"You kissed me. Knowing the whole thing was- was contaminated. You kissed me anyway."

"That's not- don't call it that."

"What should I call it."

"I was in it." The sentence came out too fast. "I wasn't- I was barely sleeping. I couldn't be in a room with you without feeling dragged toward you and doubting it while it was still-" She caught herself, pushed through. "I pulled back because I thought if I stayed near you I'd stop thinking entirely."

"And then you came back."

"Yes."

"And still said nothing."

"Yes."

He watched the ink spread through her notes. Neither of them moved to blot it.

"I let you decide when to tell me about the scar," he said.

Her head came up.

"That's not the same."

"Why."

"Because that was your body." He heard his own voice tighten and did nothing to hide it. "This wasn't only yours. This was my house and my Mark and my choice. Or what I thought was my choice."

She flinched, small but visible, her fingers digging harder into the table edge. He didn't soften the point.

"I know I took that from you."

"You did."

"I was afraid."

"So was I." He laughed once, without humor. "I just didn't know what I was meant to be afraid of."

She pushed back from the table and stood. The chair legs scraped over stone.

"You think I was protecting myself," she said. "I wasn't- I was trying to keep working because the magic is collapsing and if I'd told you- if I'd-" She looked at the far shelves instead of him. "I couldn't."

"Couldn't what."

"Watch you decide I was another thing this house had done to you."

The edge of the table pressed into his palm. He hadn't realized he'd been leaning on it.

"Is that what you thought."

"I didn't-" Now she did look at him. "The page was ruined. Narcissa warned me without actually telling me anything. I was already feeling you through the Mark, the house kept pushing us into the same rooms, and then I kissed you because I wanted to and that made everything worse." Her voice had gone thin. "I didn't know what was mine anymore."

"I'd have liked the chance to not know it with you. Instead of after."

Her mouth opened, then closed again. When she spoke, he had to strain to hear it.

"I know."

"No, listen." He heard how formal he sounded and couldn't stop it. "My father decided where I lived. The Dark Lord decided what went on my arm. This house-" His left hand had closed into a fist without his noticing. "I'm always the last one to know. And then you found a text that said maybe even wanting you wasn't-" He swallowed. "You kept it. Five weeks."

"I know."

"You don't get to say that like it settles anything."

"It doesn't." Her hand went to her sleeve, then dropped. "But I do know."

For a moment the only sound in the room was the rattle of bare branches against glass.

"If I'd told you that day," she said, "you would've shut down."

"That's not the point."

"It is a little bit the point." She spoke over him for the first time, not loud, just quick and frayed. "You would've gone cold on me. On the research, yes, but not only that. You would've gone careful and dry and polite and I would've lost you before I understood what I'd found."

"You didn't get to make that call."

"No." The answer came immediately. "I didn't."

She didn't add anything to it. She just stood there with her hands flat on the table.
The pulse in his wrist kept knocking against his thumb.

"I should've told you the day I found it," she said.

"Yes."

"I should've told you before the scar. Before the kiss. Before any of it."

"Yes."

Her eyes went bright, though the rest of her stayed rigid.

"I was wrong."

She held his gaze. His hand stayed locked around the chair arm.

"What do you want me to do," she asked.

He had to look past her, at the darkening window, before he could answer.

"I want you to leave."

She stared at him. "Leave the room."

"Leave the Manor."

Her hand slipped on the strap and caught again.

"Malfoy, the anchoring work-"

"I know exactly what's at stake." He looked at the half-finished reports, the books she'd opened, the notes in her small cramped hand. "I don't care right now. I can't do this with you here."

He waited for the argument, for the list of reasons he was being impossible. None came.

"All right," she said.

He only caught it because the room had gone that silent.

She went upstairs to pack.

He stayed in the library because he couldn't yet trust his legs to carry him anywhere better.

Her handwriting covered the table. Cross-references. Margin notes. Corrections to the Ministry reports that made the official analysis look childish. He recognized the shape of her lowercase e, the leftward lean of her script, the pressure change when she got tired. Noticing those things felt obscene now. He noticed them anyway.

Upstairs, drawers opened and shut. A trunk lid hit wood. Floorboards sounded once, then again. The house carried small noises badly. Every one of them arrived sharpened.

By the time she came back down, the library had gone colder, and too quickly for evening or the ordinary settling of stone after sunset. The drop was abrupt, measurable, the kind the Manor chose when it was unhappy and didn't care who felt it.

He had moved to the chair by the window without remembering the walk.

She stopped in the doorway with her bag over one shoulder and her coat on. The coat was the dark Muggle one she'd arrived in, all clean lines and too many buttons. It belonged to the woman who had entered his house as a researcher. The woman in it now looked less certain of her edges.

"The notes are sorted," she said. "I duplicated the shared material. Your family texts are stacked separately."

"Fine."

"The third-point references are marked. If the archive will hold for you, the cross-index you need is in the-"

"I'll find it."

Her grip tightened on the bag strap.

"I didn't mean for this to happen like this," she said, then shut her eyes for a second as if she hated the sentence as much as he did. "No. That's useless. I made a choice every day I stayed here and kept quiet. I know that."

He said nothing.

"I was wrong," she said again. "I know that's not enough either."

He gave her nothing.

At last she nodded once, a spare movement that looked closer to acceptance than agreement.

Then she turned and walked out.

Her footsteps went down the corridor and toward the front of the house. He counted without meaning to. At the end came the sound of the main door opening for her, then closing.

The Manor reacted at once.

Warmth drained out of the walls. Shelves shifted with a long wooden groan. Through the old window glass the December grounds flattened into grey, then into the early dark that came before anyone was ready for it. The corridor outside the library shortened by several feet, then stretched again, the house failing for a moment to settle around her absence.

Pain tore through his left forearm, the old pain this time, nearer to a burn than the recent low ache, dark magic looking for a command that would never come. It hit hard enough to make him fold over in the chair, one hand locked around his wrist.

So that was back.

He stayed there until the worst of the wave passed. When he lifted his head again the library had lost the coherence he'd mistaken for architecture. The tables and shelves were still in place, but the room no longer knew what to do with itself.

On the table, her notes were still spread around the ink stain. In the archive behind the hidden wall, the damaged folio still lay open to the ruined clause that had broken them.

Maybe the missing word would've cleared her. Maybe it would've condemned both of them. It changed nothing about the fact that she'd known there was doubt in the room and let him walk into it blind.

The dark pressed harder at the windows. Somewhere deeper in the house, a door shut by itself.

Before she came, cold had been ordinary here. That was the shape of his life and he'd learned it. After five and a half weeks of her footsteps in the corridor, her books on the chairs, her voice crossing the library at all hours, the old cold no longer fit. It felt wrong on his skin, borrowed, like the house was handing him back a life he had already, stupidly, stopped knowing how to live.
