# Chapter 11

Mina stood outside with two coffees going sour in her grip and watched Tomas through the office window.

At the county dock the blackwater lost most of its glamour in daylight. It lay under the pilings like used oil, thick with tannin and minnows and floating grass, while above it everything that made night feel illicit and possible turned back into municipal equipment: chain-link, warning placards, a beige fuel cage with a sticker curling at one corner, and the office door with DISTRICT PROPERTY stenciled in black, its handle polished bright where too many damp hands had worked the same cheap latch.

He had put on his good work shirt, which only meant the blue one without old spray drift bleaching the shoulders. The back of it was already dark with sweat. He stood in front of Supervisor Vega's desk with his county cap in both hands, not nervous exactly, but over-controlled, the way he got when an engine started making a sound only he could hear. Vega sat under a wall calendar three months out of date, her reading glasses low on her nose and a nicotine patch lifting at one corner of her upper arm. She corrected numbers with a purple mechanical pencil. Every time Tomas said something she erased a figure, rewrote it, and aligned the pencil with the edge of the paper before looking up at him again.

Mina could not hear much through the glass and the window unit's rattle, only the shape of it. Tomas looked lean and precise in front of the desk while Vega stayed still. A younger man at the back counter, county polo tucked hard into his belt, pretended to inventory larvicide buckets while he listened.

She had offered not to come. Tomas had texted before dawn anyway.

Need you there.

That had been all. Not baby, not come quick, not even a curse. She had read the flatness and gotten dressed in the dark. Now she wished he had not asked. Her presence at the dock felt like a stain spreading through clean water. Everybody here knew her name or could know it with one phone search, and the county did not need more reasons to decide Tomas's fuel numbers attached to something dirty.

He turned his head once and saw her through the glass. His gaze ran over her like a checklist: here, waiting, still where he'd asked.

Vega opened the office door without hurry.

"Miss Cordero," she said.

Her voice was mild in the bureaucratic way that meant the outcome had already been decided somewhere inside her. She wore no makeup except a line of sunblock still white in the seam beside one ear, as if she had rubbed it on while walking from her truck and forgotten that part of her face. "You can come in if you like. I would prefer that we do not do this by relay."

Mina handed Tomas one of the coffees. He did not drink it. A metal fan on the filing cabinet shoved warm air from one side of the room to the other, worrying the corners of the log sheets. Under it hung the usual county blend of old paper, fuel dragged in on boots, and lemon cleaner used to suggest hygiene without paying for enough of it.

On Vega's desk lay the log sheets from the last month, each route block boxed in fluorescent highlighter. Mina knew the dates before she read them: the nights she and Tomas had gone into the channels, the extra fueling after he had signed out the better camera for camp use that was not really camp use and not really legal anything. There was no mystery in the numbers once they were made to sit in a row. What had felt scattered at the time, one can here, one late refill there, turned ugly under fluorescent office light.

Vega tapped the page with her pencil. "Mr. Acosta says there were gauge errors."

"There were," Tomas said. He had gone quiet, which was worse than anger on him. "Pump three sticks. You know it sticks. I reported it."

"You reported it after the discrepancy notice."

"I reported it twice before that to Hector."

The younger man at the back did not turn around. "You told me it was running slow."

"That means sticking."

Vega held up one hand before Tomas could keep going. "Let us try this again with verbs everybody can live with."

She looked at Mina with the flat administrative focus of a woman adding one more name to a form. Mina had crossed the threshold; now she belonged to the conversation. "You worked any official county routes with Mr. Acosta?"

Mina felt Tomas's body harden beside her without touching him. She set her coffee on the desk because holding it had become impossible.

"No," she said.

"You rode his boat on county time?"

"No."

That, at least, was technically true often enough to say cleanly. The lies that survive are the ones with a little swamp water in them.

Vega nodded once. "All right."

Tomas gave a short, humorless breath through his nose. "If this is about the camp camera, say that."

"This is about fuel."

"The camera was county sign-out, county battery, county charger. You want the line item, that is your line item."

"And the gallons?"

He spread his hands. "Storm detours. Idle time. Pump issue. You want me to write down every minute I sat still in a hatch of mosquitoes because route eight flooded and the reeds were hung up in the cage, I can do that for next time."

Vega's purple pencil clicked. "There may not be a next time on nights for a while."

The younger man finally turned. He was not much older than Beto, with a neck reddened above his collar and the sour, embarrassed eyes of somebody who hated being present for another man's humiliation but was not going to absent himself from it either. He set a clipboard on the counter and left the office without being told. The door shut behind him with a thin metal complaint.

Mina stared at the highlighted pages because the alternative was Tomas's face.

Vega pulled one sheet free and rotated it toward them. "On three separate routes your mileage and spray hours support fuel use within a narrow range. You exceeded it every time. Then we have the unauthorized equipment extension on the thermal rig. Then we have fueling after posted return. Then we have the cage log that says you accessed reserve after midnight on a non-emergency date."

"Mosquitoes don't leave off because the sign says office closed."

"No, but forms matter in my world."

Tomas looked at Mina then, not because he wanted her help but because he could not stop himself from locating the other person inside the damage. "Tell her about the channels during the rookery surge. Tell her what that water does to a route."

It was a stupid impulse and he knew it the second it left his mouth. Mina knew it too. If she started explaining spoonbill congestion and root mazes and how a pilot could burn fuel easing around breeding rafts after dark, the question after that would be why she knew so much about his route conditions at night.

She said nothing. Vega noticed everything.

"Miss Cordero does not work for the district," she said. "I am not interviewing her as habitat consultant."

The shame of it moved under Tomas's skin like something hooked. Mina could see it in the angle of his jaw, how he bit once at the inside of his cheek and then stopped because even that looked too close to pleading.

"Fine," he said. "Then write me up for sloppiness and dock it."

"This is larger than sloppiness."

"It is larger because you need it larger. Nobody here is bleeding money except through county stupidity."

Vega's eyes lifted to him over the glasses. "Mr. Acosta, I am trying to keep this from becoming theft paperwork."

That shut the room down for half a beat. Even the window unit seemed to pause and gather itself.

Mina could smell the coffee cooling. It smelled bitter and burned.

Tomas said, "You want the key?"

Vega did not answer immediately. She slid a form from under the stack, already signed at the bottom by somebody farther up the chain. "Administrative suspension from nighttime route assignment pending full audit review. Effective immediately. Turn in fuel key, route tablet, and gate fob. Payroll will process regular hours worked through yesterday. Overtime differential stops today. Field access stops today. You may be called for daytime yard inventory next week if district counsel clears it."

Tomas laughed once. The sound had no air in it.

"Counsel," he said. "For gas."

"For a district employee with documented discrepancies."

He set the untouched coffee on Vega's desk beside Mina's. Very carefully, as if the cup were the only thing in reach he could still manage without breaking. Then he reached into his pocket and laid the fuel key on the paper. It was ugly brass on a float the color of old limes. Mina had touched that float so many times in the dark that she knew the tooth marks on one edge where Tomas worried it with his molars when he was thinking. Seeing it on county paperwork made her feel abruptly stupid, as if she had mistaken a body part for a tool.

"Gate fob's in the truck," he said.

"Bring it in before noon."

"Tablet?"

Vega held out her hand. Tomas unclipped it from his belt without looking at Mina. He passed it over. The little plastic screen was webbed at one corner from an old drop. She remembered holding it against his thigh once while he checked tide notes with his other hand between her legs. The memory arrived wrong and useless, bright as a cut.

Vega stacked the surrendered items into a tray. Her purple pencil rolled and knocked lightly against the key.

"I am not saying more right now than the form says," she told him. "Go home. Do not contact the pump contractor. Do not attempt to access district property after today without written notice. If there is an explanation that helps you, counsel will want it clean."

Tomas's mouth moved like he was lining up six responses and killing them all. He signed the form. His signature looked meaner than usual, each letter pressed through the page. When he straightened, the room seemed to tip around the absence at his belt.

Outside, the sun had climbed high enough to turn the dock planks tacky. Vega stayed in the office. She did not escort them out. The county knew how to do humiliation cheaply.

Tomas walked to the truck and stood with one hand on the open door, staring into the cab as if he had forgotten why he owned it. The mangroves beyond the lot clicked with insects. Somewhere under the dock a mullet slapped once at the surface.

Mina came up beside him. "Tom."

He flinched at the short form, not in tenderness.

"Do not," he said, then corrected himself as if even that much open form wasted energy. "Don't start with sorry unless you've got another eleven-eighty every two weeks and the overtime on top."

His voice stayed low. That was the cruelty of it. If he had shouted, she could have taken the blast and called it weather. Quiet made every word chosen.

"I can cover some of it," she said, though even as she said it she heard how ridiculous it was. Six hundred and forty a week from the camp, less after groceries and her penalty payment. "Not all. Some."

"With what."

"I've got a little put back."

"For your Board payment."

She hated that he knew the exact shape of her poverty. Hated more that she had let him know because intimacy had made accounting feel like honesty.

"I can miss one month."

"And then what."

The truck cab held heat like a toolbox left in the sun. On the floorboard lay a spool of line, two socket extensions, and the envelope from the bank about the skiff loan. Open already, crumpled once, smoothed back out. The fried sweetness of the gas-station pastries he kept buying when sleep got thin still clung to the air. Mina did not need to read it again to know the number. Nine thousand one hundred still hanging from a boat that only made sense if Tomas kept county work steady enough to keep feeding it.

"I said I'd help," she said.

"Help with what, Mina? Groceries for a week? One auto-debit? Great. Then the next one comes. Then the one after that. Rhea's stipend lands once a month and the camp likes to remember housing is a favor when anybody gets inconvenient. This wasn't some sexy risk anymore the second they put my key in a tray."

He shut the truck door harder than he needed to. The sound slapped over the lot and came back from the metal shed.

She looked at his hands because looking higher would have meant seeing too much. Grease still lived in the half-moons of his nails no matter how often he scrubbed. A pale groove showed around his wrist where he usually wore the district lanyard on rough nights.

"I didn't ask you to borrow the thermal rig," she said.

"No. You asked for proof. The rest was just me being useful, right?"

"That's not fair."

"It is exactly fair."

He rubbed the bridge of his nose with thumb and forefinger, the motion abrupt and tired. When he dropped his hand he sounded flatter, which meant he was closer to the truth than either of them liked. "I knew what I was doing. That's on me. But don't stand here acting shocked at the bill."

The dock office door opened again behind them. The young county employee came out carrying a bucket of absorbent compound. He kept his eyes politely away from them and whistled one bar of some old ranchera under his breath as he crossed toward the pumps, the tune breaking because he could not remember the next line. That, more than anything Vega had said, made Mina want to put her fist through something. Ordinary work continuing around the ruin. Somebody else's shy little song.

"What are you going to tell Rhea," she asked.

He gave her a look from the side. "You think I haven't already."

Of course he had. He had texted from the office when Vega stepped out or before Mina arrived. Or maybe he had sent it at dawn the second the district called him in. Rhea would have read the message in the bat lab with the recorder batteries warming beside her and her mouth gone thin.

The heat on the lot thickened. The coffee on Vega's desk would go to skin soon.

"Night channels are done for me," Tomas said. "Maybe for two weeks. Maybe for good. Even if I get back on days, the key isn't mine. The cage isn't mine. Every gallon's going to glow now."

Glow was the wrong word, too close to what had made this possible.

He heard it too and looked away toward the mangroves. "Jesus."

For a second she thought he might touch her. Instead he opened the driver-side door and climbed in.

"Tom."

He leaned across and shoved the passenger door open without meeting her eyes. "I can't drive back with you walking."

Habit opened the door. Weathered tenderness followed. So did the old rule that anger rode home with you instead of leaving somebody to cook on the shell path. Mina got in.

They drove to camp with the windows down because the truck air conditioner had been dying for months and nobody had money to pretend otherwise. Tomas kept one hand high on the wheel, the other braced against the door. Every time the tires struck a pothole the tools under the seat clinked together. He did not put on the radio.

At the turnoff to Rookery Key he said, "Do not ask Rhea for a workaround today."

The formality snagged in the air between them. He knew it too. He corrected nothing.

"I wasn't going to."

He looked at her then, long enough to show he did not believe her or did not trust her not to start believing it in the next hour.

"If you want the creek now," he said, "you find a way that doesn't come out of my hide."

He turned into camp without waiting for an answer.

* * *

By noon the little window unit had given up keeping pace and now mostly snarled. Battery heat, guano, old plywood, and the sour medicinal bite of rubbing alcohol sat in the room, hotter than the day outside. Mina stood in the screened walkway with one hand on the frame, listening to Rhea move around inside among hard-plastic cases and mesh bags and the clicking playback of bat calls slowed into human range.

Rhea did not tell her to come in. She did not tell her to stay out either.

She was at the central table cleaning one of the ultrasonic microphones with a child's toothbrush. She had changed out of field clothes into an old gray university T-shirt with the sleeves cut off, and her wet hair was braided so tight it made the bones of her face look even cleaner. Reading glasses sat low on her nose, one arm wrapped in white veterinary tape where it had snapped months ago. Beside her elbow lay a legal pad divided into columns so neat they mocked the room.

Mina stepped inside. The screen door slapped and bounced once behind her.

"He told you," she said.

"Yes."

Rhea kept brushing dried mud from the seam of the microphone casing. Her voice did not rise. The lack of drama in it made Mina feel more exposed than if Rhea had been crying or furious.

"You saw him?"

"For two minutes before he went to shower. He smelled like county office and bad coffee."

The phrase carried an intimacy that did not include Mina. It marked Tomas as husband, not co-conspirator, and the plainness of it stung.

On the table, under the legal pad, Mina saw a yellow envelope from the university and a folded camp housing agreement with Imogen's signature at the bottom. Rhea followed her glance and laid the legal pad over both without hurry.

"If you're here to tell me this isn't your fault," Rhea said, "save the effort. I'm not in a mood for clean causality."

"I'm not."

"Good."

Rhea set the microphone aside and picked up the next piece of equipment. Her hands were careful even now. That had always been part of what Mina wanted from her, the way she touched fragile things without making a show of gentleness.

"Tom said not to ask you for a workaround today," Mina said.

One side of Rhea's mouth twitched with bleak recognition, the kind reserved for a stupidity so predictable it had become weather. "And yet here you are."

Mina crossed the room because standing in the doorway made her feel like a teenager at a principal's office. The floor flexed under her bare feet. Through the open inner window came the camp's layered noise: a pot banging in the kitchen, somebody laughing too hard out on the guest deck, screens ticking as the wind shifted.

"I'm here because if you step away now, the whole thing changes."

"It already changed."

"You know what I mean."

Rhea looked up at last. Her eyes were tired enough to flatten the room. "Do I."

Mina put both hands on the table, careful not to touch the gear. She could smell the soap she had used after the dock, cheap coconut over diesel residue that would not come fully off. "We still have the recorder. We still have the partial file. Low tide's early this week. The west root path is passable if the rain holds. There are ways in that don't use his route."

Rhea let out a breath through her nose and removed the glasses. She folded them with the deliberate, dangerous calm Mina had started learning over the last few weeks and still hated every time it came toward her.

"You came here to say that today."

"I came here because the window is closing."

Rhea set the folded glasses on top of the housing agreement and looked at her. "Tom came back with the belt clip bare where the route tablet sits. His shirt is in my sink. The county took his key this morning, and your first sentence to me is low tide."

"Don't do that."

"Do what."

"Make this uglier than it is."

Rhea gave a small incredulous laugh. "You didn't need my help with that."

She stood while she said it. Tall, narrow, still wet at the hairline from the shower she must have taken after handling Tomas's shirt or touching his back. On her collarbone, above the T-shirt edge, a fading bruise showed yellow at the center. Mina had put it there days ago with her mouth. It sat now above all this language like a stupid little relic from another economy.

"I know what the room is," Mina said quietly.

"Do you."

"Don't talk to me like I'm a tourist."

"Then stop speaking as if the only vanishing resource in this camp is your access to a creek."

The words landed with clinical force. Rhea did not raise her voice because she did not need to. The lab was too small for sound to do the work.

Mina straightened. Every part of her wanted to answer too fast, to start naming dates and routes and biological windows until precision became a shield again. She made herself hold still. Outside the screen a mosquito whined against the mesh and found no entry.

"I know he got hurt because of this," she said. "Because of me, some of it."

"Some."

"Fine. A lot."

"That's closer."

Rhea rubbed the spot between her eyebrows where the glasses had pressed. "I'm not interested in making you confess for sport. Tom made choices. So did you. But you came in here looking at my cases like they were already yours again."

She crouched, reached under the back table, and dragged out Mina's black dry bag. Salt had stiffened the webbing. A chalky streak of spoonbill shit still marked one strap from the last run. Rhea set it upright between them and shoved the zipper shut with her thumb.

"Take it," she said. "Your flashlight's in the side pocket. Spare batteries too. I'm not storing your route under my lab bench."

Mina looked past her to the foam equipment cases stacked by the wall, the batteries charging, the labeled bags of guano samples. Work everywhere. Real work with protocols and dead grants and tiny exact data points nobody would ever call miraculous. The creek always made her feel that anything not luminous was secondary. Here the opposite pressure held.

"What do you want me to say?" she asked.

"Nothing polished." Rhea's voice lowered. "I'm deciding whether I keep my husband in my bed, whether Imogen can still justify that room to the university, and whether I can look at you tonight without tasting county office coffee. That's enough for one afternoon."

The last word did not sound romantic. It sounded like a specimen she was refusing to mislabel.

Mina said, "And the eel."

"The eel gets to wait," Rhea said. "We don't."

Silence widened between them, filled by insect noise and the slowed-down chatter of bat calls from the speaker, tiny machine screams stretched into something plaintive. Mina remembered Rhea telling her once that the software ruined the bats' elegance but made patterns legible to human ears. That felt like the whole mess in miniature. There was nothing noble about translation, just damage turned readable.

"Do you want me to leave you alone," Mina asked.

Rhea's expression loosened a fraction. Fatigue showed first, then the smaller risk beneath it: a brief unguardedness. She set both palms on the table behind her and leaned her weight there.

"Not entirely," she said.

The answer went through Mina like fresh water and then hurt just as fast because it was not enough to live on.

"I still want you," Rhea said. "That's not the useful fact today, but it's true. I'm also furious enough that every time you say route I taste metal. That doesn't cancel the wanting. It sits beside it."

Mina nodded once because any sentence she attempted would have come out defensive.

Rhea continued, more plainly now, as if anger had burned the formal edges off her syntax. "I'm not helping you get back out there this week. I'm not loaning gear, and I'm not using my permit as camouflage. If Tom is under review, I'm not adding field lies to the pile. If Imogen asks me where I stand, I need to be able to answer without tasting bullshit."

"And us."

"Us is not a route category."

Despite herself, a smile tugged at Mina's mouth. Rhea saw it and did not permit it to become relief.

"Don't mistake language for softness," she said.

"I know better."

"Good."

Mina moved around the table before she had fully decided to. She stopped close enough to smell Rhea's damp braid and the alcohol on her hands. Up close, the bruise on the collarbone was smaller than it had looked across the room, an old bite already migrating toward ordinary skin.

"I wasn't trying to use you," Mina said.

Rhea's eyes lifted to hers. "I believe that," she said after a moment. "You were trying not to stop."

That was worse because it was truer.

Mina looked down. The legal pad partly covered the housing agreement, but one line still showed: OCCUPANCY CONTINGENT UPON ACTIVE PROGRAM PARTICIPATION. Imogen's neat signature below it. A whole domestic life reduced to conditional paperwork on a folding table that smelled like bat shit.

"He hates me right now," Mina said.

"He's angry. Hate is lazier."

"Are you protecting him from me or me from him."

"At the moment I'm protecting my own capacity to think."

The answer was so Rhea that Mina nearly laughed, then did not. She put one hand on the table beside Rhea's and left it there without touching. Rhea looked at the hand, at the scar on Mina's left calf visible through the frayed hem of shorts, at the dried salt on Mina's shin from sweat. Tiny field notes, all of them.

"If you need groceries," Mina said, hating the inadequacy while saying it, "I can cover some until his check clears. I'm not pretending that fixes it."

Rhea's mouth tightened. "That's kind. It's also humiliating, and I'm too honest to pretend otherwise."

"I know."

"I may still say yes later."

Again, Rhea handed her a rationed mercy, slight enough to ache and solid enough to matter.

Rhea reached out then, finally, and touched two fingers to the inside of Mina's wrist where her pulse sat close to the skin. The contact was brief, intentional, exact enough to feel clinical, which made it more intimate than a grab would have been.

"Listen to me carefully," she said. "I'm not done with you. But if you ask me for access again before you ask what Tom needs, I'll be."

Mina swallowed. The room tasted like battery metal and old heat.

"All right."

"And don't come to our room tonight."

There it was: marriage, plain and singular.

"All right."

Rhea dropped her hand, hooked the strap of the dry bag with two fingers, and pushed it into Mina's stomach hard enough that Mina had to catch it. "Go find Imogen before she finds you. She has been in donor voice since breakfast, which usually means the books are bad."

"She told you that."

"She did not have to."

Rhea picked up the glasses and set them back on her nose. The conversation was over with the same economy she used to close sample bags. Mina stood another second anyway, stupidly hoping the room might reverse itself if she waited. It did not.

When she opened the screen door, Rhea said without looking up, "Eat something."

It was the gentlest thing either of them had managed.

Mina stepped back into the walkway and let the heat hit her full in the face.

* * *

Imogen's office had once been a storage room, and it still admitted the truth of that in every line. Shelves meant for cleaning supplies now held donor binders, bird-checklist pamphlets, spare flashlight batteries, three hurricane lanterns polished for ambiance but too dented to trust in a storm, and a box labeled LINENS that contained nothing but extension cords. The desk sat under a patched ceiling stain shaped like Cuba. The room always smelled faintly of mildew, printer toner, and whatever citrus oil Imogen had decided would keep guests from identifying the first two.

By late afternoon the office door stood open because the fan had stopped oscillating and was now aimed permanently at the opposite wall. Mina found Beto on a step stool in the hall outside, replacing a blown bulb in the light over the framed spoonbill photographs. He had a ladder of compressor part numbers written up his left forearm in blue marker and a folded trade-school brochure sticking out of the back pocket of his too-large shorts. One of his kitchen burns, glossy and old on the wrist, flashed when he reached overhead.

"If this thing pops again," he said without looking down, "I'm declaring the house haunted by cheap wiring and emotional repression."

His voice carried the usual quick humor, but it was stretched thinner than usual.

"How bad's the wiring," Mina asked.

"Depends whether you want the guest answer or the human answer."

"Human."

He screwed the bulb in and flicked the switch. Light came on, yellow and weak. "Human answer is the west wall hates us."

He climbed down, not stepping so much as bouncing off the stool in a way that always made every adult around him tense. He held up the dead bulb. The glass was smoked black at the neck.

"Imogen in there?" Mina asked.

"Where else would she stage-manage disaster from."

He lowered his voice and leaned in. "Don't mention the ice machine first. She already lost a battle with it and I think the machine won on points."

There was a clatter inside the office and Imogen's voice, sharper than donor-safe but not yet stripped bare. "If you are whispering about me in my own corridor, at least make it worth hearing."

Beto winced theatrically and grinned at Mina with a crack of real nerves under it. "See?"

He passed her the dead bulb like it meant something and ducked into the kitchen before he could be assigned another task. Through the swinging door Mina saw him stop at the prep counter and pull a small spiral notebook from his pocket. He glanced once at the blue marker on his forearm and copied the numbers over in neat block print before washing his hands. Saving information before sweat took it off his skin. Saving himself inch by inch.

Imogen sat behind the desk in a faded field shirt buttoned all the way up despite the heat. One silver hairpin had come loose, and a coil of hair kept dropping toward her cheek while she worked. She ignored it. Around her lay open ledgers, a laptop propped on two birding guides to get it above the dead angle of the charger cord, and a petty-cash tin with its mouth open like a joke too tired to tell itself.

Without preamble she said, "Shut the door if you are staying. The guests think paper sounds are unwelcoming."

Mina did. The room lost the hall noise and became all fan, paper, and the generator thrum under the floorboards.

"Rhea told me you were looking for me," Imogen said.

"She said the books were bad."

"They are not bad." Imogen took off her glasses, pinched the bridge of her nose, then put the glasses back on. "Bad is a mood. This is arithmetic."

Mina stayed standing. The office had no chair that was not already under paper. "You heard about Tomas."

"Of course I heard."

Imogen's gaze moved over Mina's face, taking measurements. Not sympathy. Inventory again, campwide this time.

"Sit," Imogen said.

Mina cleared invoices off the spare chair and sat. The top page she moved was a repair estimate for the main walkway pilings, the total circled twice in red. The total was twenty-seven thousand four hundred. She knew the number from nothing and then from everything all at once. It belonged to a deferred structural repair invoice carried by a camp that marketed sunrise stillness and artisanal field lunches to rich birders in linen.

Imogen saw where she was looking. "Yes," she said. "That one."

Mina set the page down carefully. "I didn't know it was that deep."

"Nobody ever does when the coffee is good and the roseate spoonbills arrive on schedule."

Outside, the guest deck gave up a burst of polite laughter. Somebody was probably being handed a sweating glass of wine and told a joke about mangrove crabs. Donor theater, Mina thought, then hated the neatness of the phrase because it made the thing sound cleaner than it was. Theater implied budget and rehearsal. This was triage in decent lighting.

"Why are you showing me this," she asked.

Imogen leaned back. The chair creaked under her in the same familiar note the camp made in crosswind. "Because this morning I had to smile through breakfast with two couples from Naples while knowing the west cistern pump is running on a seal Tomas cut out of sheet rubber for me last month, the roof over cabin three still has a soft patch I cannot pay to replace before storm inspection, and the county has just suspended the one man on this island who has been able to get me fuel, parts, and practical answers faster than requisition forms."

She tapped another stack. Payroll. Utilities. A vendor statement with PAST DUE in a block so aggressive it might as well have been profanity.

"And because you," Imogen continued, "have spent the last several weeks treating the eel as private obsession with collateral inconvenience, while I have been trying not to say out loud that the camp has crossed from genteel scarcity into actual exposure."

Mina felt herself brace as if for scolding, then realized this was worse than scolding. Scolding was personal. This was structural.

"I know the eel stopped being just mine once the footage existed," she said. "I know you started seeing money in it."

"Started?" Imogen's expression thinned. "Mina, I have been seeing money in everything for fourteen years. Birds, board members, bat data, celebrity chef fundraisers, tasteful kayaks, memorial benches, children with binoculars. The question is never whether I can see money. The question is whether the thing in front of me can survive being translated into appeal copy before it is ruined."

She opened the petty-cash tin and turned it toward Mina. Inside lay three twenties, a five, and some silver sticking to the bottom in damp air.

"This," Imogen said, "is tonight's margin before the fish delivery clears. We are full for dinner. The ice machine is failing. The board treasurer thinks unrestricted gifts are a myth spread by socialists. Beto wants trade school in the fall and has been taking every extra hour I can invent for him because his mother quite reasonably prefers that he not spend the rest of his life gutting mullet for tips. Rhea's survey contract makes her housing justifiable to the university and barely justifiable to my accountant. Tomas has been quietly fixing things that would otherwise have become line items I could not meet. Your weekly wage is not large, but it is still a wage in a house where every wage comes from somewhere."

The fan pushed hot paper smell against Mina's face. She thought of Tomas setting the fuel key on the desk, of Rhea's housing agreement under the legal pad, of Beto writing compressor numbers off his own arm before soap took them away.

"You could have told us," she said, though even as she said it she heard the childishness.

Imogen laughed without humor. "And when precisely would that have been useful. During donor cocktails. During the morning kayak orientation. In the middle of your sex-fueled illegal wildlife pursuit."

Mina looked down. On the desk corner sat a stack of handwritten place cards for tomorrow night's sponsor supper, each one done in Imogen's severe neat print on thick cream stock. The names belonged to people wealthy enough to mistake a stilt house with patched screens for rustic charm if the napkins were folded correctly.

"I wasn't asking for cocktails," Mina said.

"No. You were asking for night access, county risk, staff attention, and equipment improvisation. Those all invoice somewhere."

Imogen was one of the few people alive who could make the word invoice sound like an accusation with moral depth.

Mina said, "You wanted the footage too."

"Of course I did."

There it was, no donor tone on it at all. The old field-biologist ferocity broke through the manager.

"I wanted it because authenticated footage with county dates and a scientist's name on it can pry open wallets that ignore roof estimates. If I could put protected-site liability, donor ego, and something nobody else could claim in the same packet, the board treasurer would have to stop filing my repair emails under later. I did not start treating your fish as an asset because I had become mystical. I started because I have invoices older than some of my seasonal staff."

She reached for another paper and slid it over.

The generator service estimate had been deferred.

The kitchen refrigeration quote had been deferred.

The septic pump inspection was overdue.

Mina read the numbers until they blurred into one continuous demand. It was all so ordinary that it became obscene. There was no single catastrophe, just a hundred small mechanical truths waiting their turn to become an emergency.

"And donor theater?" Mina asked.

Imogen gave her a sharp look, amused despite herself. "Rhea's phrase, I assume."

"Mine now."

"Fine. Donor theater." Imogen stood and went to the shelf, where she lifted a woven basket full of citronella votives, linen napkin rings, and laminated species cards tied with ribbon. She set it on the desk between them with more force than the basket deserved. "When the east walkway floods, we route guests around it and call the detour a mangrove immersion path. When the guest-room air conditioners start leaking, we stage more time on the observation platform and speak fondly about evening breezes. When the board comes, I move the prettier scopes to the front room and close the door on the storage side. When wealthy people want authenticity, I give them enough creak and guano smell to feel adventurous, not enough to make them ask how close we are to closure."

She touched the basket's ribbon, then let go. "That is not fraud. That is hospitality under capitalism."

The phrase should have been funny. It hit too close to the bone for that. Mina could not get there.

There was a knock on the doorjamb and Beto leaned in with a tray of chipped wineglasses nested in a towel.

"Do you want these out before the Naples people start looking thirsty in a punitive way," he asked, "or are we embracing austerity chic."

Imogen did not turn. "Out in ten. Use the uncracked ones first."

"That narrows the field."

"Beto."

"I'm going."

He shifted the tray against his hip but did not move yet. His eyes flicked to the open payroll sheet on the desk, then away. Quick as a bird, impossible to miss once seen.

"Also," he said, trying for light and landing near it, "if my hours are weird next week, just tell me straight. I've got that school call Friday and I don't want to sound like a liar again."

Imogen's shoulders changed. Just enough.

"Come back after dinner service," she said. "We will look at the schedule."

"That sounds like a maybe."

"It is a maybe."

He nodded once. The joke washed out of his face so completely Mina felt it in her stomach. Nineteen and already fluent in managerial weather.

"Okay," he said. Then, because he could not help himself, "If the rich people ask, I know exactly two things about wine and both of them are lies."

Imogen made a small shooing motion with one hand. Beto left.

The room held the afterimage of him a moment longer than his body did.

Mina said, "You were going to cut his hours."

"I was going to avoid promising hours I cannot fund."

"Same thing if you're nineteen."

Imogen looked at her over the lenses. "Yes."

Nothing in the answer tried to spare either of them.

Rain smell moved through the cracked window then, not rain yet, only the iron-dark warning that a storm front had started building out beyond the bay. The office light flickered. Somewhere down the hall Beto swore at a drawer that had stuck again.

Mina stared at the repair estimate still under her hand. Twenty-seven thousand four hundred. More than four times what she owed the Gulf Wildlife Board and somehow less shameful because it belonged to wood and pipes instead of a body carrying an old disgrace.

"What do you want from me," she asked.

Imogen considered her long enough for the generator to miss once and catch again. Then she reached to the corkboard, pulled down the next day's schedule, and laid it over the ledger.

Across the top, in her own neat hand, was DAWN CREEK - NAPLES / MINA / BETO.

She drew one red line through all of it so hard the pen tore the paper.

"Today, I want dinner service from you and no improvisation. Tomorrow there is no creek launch. The Naples people get coffee on the observation platform, and you and Beto inventory lantern batteries, cracked dock lines, and every flashlight that still works. Log it before breakfast."

She opened the door and called down the corridor. "Beto. Back here."

He appeared with the towel still over one shoulder, looked from Imogen to Mina, then to the red-scored schedule on the desk.

"No dawn run tomorrow," Imogen said. "Move your guest hours to maintenance. I want counts on lantern batteries, dock lines, and the west-walk lights before six-thirty. Mina is helping you."

Beto's face fell before he could stop it. "So the Naples people get coffee and I get corroded D-cells."

"The Naples people get a view and a story," Imogen said. "You get hours I can defend on paper."

He swallowed the joke before it made it out. "Okay."

He took the revised schedule when Imogen held it out. The red line cut straight through his name. He folded the page once and left with it pinched too tight between finger and thumb.

Mina let that sit. The fan moved hot air over the back of her neck. On the corkboard beside the desk hung next week's tide table, a donor watercolor of a spoonbill chick, and a handwritten reminder to move the good binoculars before board weekend. The whole camp sat there in miniature, beauty thumbtacked over procedure over need.

"If the eel paid for the pilings," Mina said slowly, "would you take that deal."

Imogen's face altered with the weariness of being asked a moral question that money had already made practical.

"Depends what else the deal took," she said. "If it brings regulators who shut us for the season, no. If it turns the channels into a harvest site, no. If it buys time without selling the animal to men who think naming a thing is ownership, maybe. I do not have the luxury of pretending purity keeps roofs on."

Mina thought of the annex's concrete throat beneath the mangroves, of the BrightCurrent signage half swallowed by rot. She had been treating the creek like something accessible only by boat, by fuel, by Tomas's county magic. The tide table on the wall suggested otherwise in its own indifferent way. Low tide would hit at 3:17 a.m. A mud route might be possible if the rain stayed north.

"What happens if Tomas can't get back on," she asked.

Imogen's eyes moved briefly to the payroll sheet, then to the ceiling stain, as if the answer might be written in either place. "Then the cistern pump waits until it fails in front of guests. Rhea carries more cash than is fair. Tom takes day labor from men who will smell suspension on him before he opens his mouth. I tape this place together and hope the county keeps its incompetence on its own side of the water. That is usually how these stories go."

The bluntness relieved Mina more than comfort would have. There was no sermon, just consequence.

From the kitchen came the first clatter of dinner service truly beginning, pans set down hard, the sound of Beto laughing too brightly at something somebody richer than him had said.

Mina rose.

"I should help."

"Yes," Imogen said. Then, as Mina reached the door: "Wanting a thing is cheap. Keeping a house open around it is not."

Mina looked back.

Imogen had already put her glasses lower on her nose and returned to the ledger, but some older, rawer self still showed through the management. Maybe it was field anger. Maybe it was grief in an administrative blouse.

"I know," Mina said.

Outside the office the corridor felt cool by comparison, though the storm heat had thickened and the screens were ticking with the first hard scouts of rain. Beto crossed from kitchen to deck with the wineglasses on his tray and gave Mina a quick look that asked three things at once: Are my hours gone, is the house sinking, do adults ever tell the truth before they must. She had no answer fit to hand him in passing.

She went instead to the corkboard by the office door and looked again at the tide sheet. Low tide would hit at 3:17 a.m. The route through the mangrove roots would be mud, cuts, mosquitoes, and probably chest-deep water in one section if the front broke wrong. There would be no engine, no county key, no overtime burning in somebody else's paycheck. There would be only the old BrightCurrent approach on foot if she had the nerve to take her own weight there.

Behind her the camp kept performing solvency as glass settled onto trays, citronella wicks caught, and a guest called the storm smell romantic. The whole place balanced on invoices and posture.

Mina put two fingers on the tide table until the paper dented under her skin, then let go.

If she went again, it would be through mud when the tide let go of the roots. Nobody else got to pay for the motor. If she let the 3:17 pass, that would be on her.
