# Chapter 18

Weeks later, something dripped against metal in the room next to Mina's with the patience of a clock, waking her before the generator came on. The early rains had stopped arriving as an event and settled into habit, and the camp had learned a different set of noises. In the dry weeks the stilts used to complain in thin sharp ticks, heat pulling pitch from the boards and shrinking every nail hole around them. Now the wood gave longer sounds. Water moved inside it. The main house let out slow damp groans before dawn, and the screened walkways snapped when wind shoved rain sideways through them, the whole place flexing sore joints in its sleep.

The spare corner Imogen had handed her after spray night had been the linen closet once. She had taken out half the shelves, left the rest, and called it a practical solution in the voice she used when practicality was covering for poverty. Mina slept under spare mosquito netting, on a narrow camp mattress that smelled faintly of bleach and old salt, with a coil of replacement screen spline hanging from a hook by the door and a stack of folded warning placards under the bed. On the wall above her duffel, clipped to a thumbtack, the latest receipt from the Gulf Wildlife Board curled at one corner where the air never dried. Payment received. Balance still ugly.

Rain had blown in through the outer screen overnight. The toes of her work shoes were wet. She pulled them on anyway and stepped into the hall in shorts and a camp shirt so worn the old logo had faded to a pink smear across her chest. The main house smelled of coffee left sitting on a burner too long, wet rope, and the medicinal bleach they used now on every railing that guests might touch after a morning of leaning into scopes and asking whether contamination transferred through skin.

The camp survived in halves. Cabin Three stayed shut because the south window would not stop leaking and because there were not enough clean mattresses to pretend otherwise. The observation platform was open again, but only on the inland side; the outer corner still carried new boards where Tomas had replaced a section that had gone soft under too many bodies and too much weather. The north rookery walk was limited access now, by permit and by Imogen's temper. A laminated sign hung where the old hand-painted spoonbill welcome had been: POST-SPRAY MONITORING AREA. CLOSED WITHOUT STAFF ESCORT. Disaster had stripped the camp of any noble story. What remained was explicit.

Beto was in the kitchen with one knee on a chair, balancing the way he always did in positions that made older people imagine ambulance sirens. He had a wet dish towel over one shoulder, a pencil behind one ear, and a grease-marked exam-prep workbook spread open next to the coffeepot. He was copying the diagram of a carburetor float bowl into a spiral notebook whose cover had been reinforced three different times with silver tape. The pages had gone soft at the corners from humidity and kitchen steam, but his handwriting stayed neat and blocky.

He looked up when she came in. "Coffee's mean this morning."

"Good. I don't need it to like me."

"That's mature." He slid the pot off the heat before it could scorch further. "Imogen wants the west walkway checked before the extension kids get here. Also Cabin Two has that smell again."

"Everything has that smell again."

"No, this is the dead gecko version."

He said it with the old fast clowning rhythm, but there was less waste in him than there had been in March. After spray night he had not stopped joking; he had started placing the jokes more carefully, like screws he could not afford to strip. Mina poured coffee into a chipped enamel mug and saw the little crescent burns along his wrist where the fry basket had kissed him earlier in the season. He caught her looking and rolled his sleeve down without embarrassment, already reading the next label on the diagram.

"You staying in here all morning with your true love?" she asked.

He tapped the workbook. "If I don't pass the practice section on fuel systems, some nineteen-year-old from Fort Myers whose uncle owns three lifts gets my future. So no. I'm helping you first and then I'm romancing internal combustion until lunch."

"You're disgusting."

"That's why the machines trust me."

Outside, the shell path had turned to a slick paste that sucked at boot soles. Rain still came down in a fine slant, not hard enough to justify quitting and not light enough to ignore. Mina carried a tool bucket in one hand and a roll of screen in the other while Beto lugged the aluminum ladder and kept up a running mutter about people who designed outdoor structures for a coast that had apparently never met salt. The mangroves beyond the cabins held water in all their leaf cups. Every few steps something let go and dropped onto their heads.

The spoonbills were fewer. That was the fact that never softened no matter how often she walked this path. Fewer, with nothing for grant language to polish into resilience. Dawn still caught pink flashes in the channels, and the rookery still had that rank guano sweetness under the rot, but spaces had opened in the noise. The colony no longer hit the body the way it had at peak breeding, like standing too close to an electrical substation made of feathers and appetite. There were mornings when Mina could pick out single calls where once there had been one living wall of racket. Loss had turned legible.

At the west walkway they found two split boards, a screen door that had pulled loose from its lower hinge, and a drift line of mangrove leaves blown in against the threshold. Mina knelt to gather screws from the bottom of the bucket. The wood was damp enough to take the imprint of her knee through her shorts. Beto had the ladder up and the loose hinge braced before she finished cursing at the stripped head on the first screw.

"Give me that," he said.

"I know how to use a drill."

"I know. That's why I'm worried."

She handed it over. He grinned without looking down, the pencil still tucked behind his ear, and worked the screw out slow enough not to chew the wood. He could never stay still in conversation, but with tools he became almost eerily polite. Every movement asked the material what it could stand.

From the main house came Imogen's donor voice, still alive if not exactly thriving.

"No, I would not call it a tour in the usual sense," she was saying. "If you prefer the usual sense, there are better places to spend your weekend."

Mina stood and looked back through the rain. Two women in university-logo ponchos stood on the porch with clipboards hugged to their ribs, the sort of graduate extension students who had enough funding for field credit and not enough funding for comfort. Imogen faced them in a faded khaki shirt buttoned too high against the weather, silver hair pinned up with what looked like a sharpened plant label because she could never find an actual clip when she needed one. She had one hand wrapped around a mug and the other gesturing toward the posted closure maps. Managerial, exact, unsentimental. The camp no longer sold miracle nights. It sold access to damage if you could behave.

The students went with Rhea toward the bat lab half an hour later, heads bowed under the rain. Rhea carried two sample coolers and a packet of preprinted labels tucked inside her shirt to keep them dry. Her braid was darkened almost black from the weather. One nitrile glove hung from the back pocket of her field pants, marked at the cuff with black Sharpie because the lab had ordered the wrong size again and she had taken to sorting her usable ones by hand. When she passed the walkway she did not slow, but her fingers brushed the damp back of Mina's knee where Mina still crouched by the door frame, a touch so brief it could have been balance if you did not know her. Mina knew the heat of it anyway.

"Battery shelf on the west recorder's dead," Rhea said to no one in particular and to Mina exactly. "If you're still alive after the walkway, I need a second pair of hands at low tide."

"I'm hard to kill."

"That hasn't always been your best trait."

The students glanced back, startled by the dryness in Rhea's voice, but she was already moving again, long stride through the rain, one cooler thumping gently against her leg. She smelled as she passed of wet canvas, rubbing alcohol, and the clean metal scent of batteries warming in plastic cases. Mina watched the line of her shoulders for a second longer than she needed to and made herself reach for the next screw.

Beto whistled under his breath. "That was romantic if you enjoy being assigned labor."

"You have work."

"I know. I'm watching how professionals flirt."

"Then watch somebody better."

By ten the rain thinned enough to become steam. The extension students had disappeared into the lab with Rhea. Beto went to deal with Cabin Two and its dead-gecko smell, taking his workbook with him because he would rather carry three extra pounds through humidity than lose an idle ten minutes. Mina hauled the split boards to the scrap pile and found Imogen on the porch rewriting the day's chalkboard schedule to account for another cancellation. The chalk had softened in the damp and kept breaking in her fingers.

Without looking up, Imogen said, "You missed a rot patch by the cistern stair."

"I'll get it after lunch."

"Get it before lunch if you can. The students from Gainesville will interpret a turned ankle as institutional failure."

"They're not wrong."

Imogen snorted once. On her it passed for laughter. She set the chalk down and rubbed moisture off the board with the flat of her palm, smearing half the letters into a white bruise. Up close she looked leaner than she had in spring. The months had taken flesh from her face and left the field lines deeper. She had started wearing her reading glasses on a blue cord around her neck because she was tired of hunting them, and the cord gave her the air of an exhausted headmistress at a school the county kept threatening to close.

"I've redone next week's staffing," she said. "If you are still willing."

Mina waited. Imogen never offered the useful clause first.

"I can put you on paper as utility and restricted guide." Imogen lifted the schedule and read from it with the care she saved for language that might end up in a hearing, which with her it often did. "Boardwalk maintenance, kitchen overflow, low-impact day groups only, no donor dinners, no press, no night water, no independent data collection, and no representation of the camp in any conversation that includes the word discovery."

"Sounds glamorous."

"It is not glamorous. That is one of its advantages."

Mina leaned against the porch post. The wood was warm now under the damp surface. Somewhere behind the house a pump coughed, recovered, and kept going. Imogen looked at her over the top of the paper with the old sharpness still in place, the one that had probably once terrorized trustees and now mostly got used on vendors and men who said things like silver lining.

"The pay is worse," Imogen said. "The title is worse. If investigators come back through, which they may, I will not lie for you. If a guest recognizes you and wants the full disgusting story, I will not stage-manage that either. What I can offer is the bed you already occupy, wages that are bad but regular when the bookings hold, and work that needs doing. If you take it, you take it as it is."

The shame of that should have arrived hot. Earlier in the year it would have. She would have felt it in the back of her neck, in her teeth, in the frantic reaching part of her mind that wanted one clean proof big enough to make every smaller humiliation retroactive and irrelevant. Now it came quieter. A fact, not a wound she could theatricalize. Utility. Restricted guide. No discovery. The words sat on the porch between them like a bucket of fish scales, ugly and honest and not at all abstract.

"You could leave," Imogen said, softer than her usual setting. "I am not saying you should. I am saying I know what it looks like from the outside. Staying here after all this could read as masochism."

"Probably is, some days."

"That is not an answer."

No. Masochism was too neat a word for it. Mina looked past Imogen toward the wet walk to the bat lab, where Rhea had hung two nets to dry under the eaves and the mesh moved in the breeze like black lungs. The inside of Mina's left thigh still carried the faint sting from where Rhea had swabbed a mangrove scrape there after midnight, kneeling between Mina's knees on the camp mattress with field tweezers and an alcohol pad, muttering that infection would be an idiotic way to lose a witness. She had kissed the cleaned skin afterward anyway, open-mouthed and annoyed, as if tenderness had merely interrupted the job.

At the door Rhea had tugged Mina's shorts back into place, gathered her boots by the heels, and said, "I'm sleeping in the lab tonight. Tomas got another county envelope and neither of us is decent company when those show up." By dawn Mina had heard the flat metal knock of a thermos set outside the lab and Tomas's steps moving away down the walk. He had not knocked on Mina's door. He had not spoken through the screen. When she saw him next through the rain he had a post driver on one shoulder and mud to both knees, already working.

That was the arrangement now, or as close to terms as anyone was going to write down. Rhea came to Mina when she wanted her. Tomas and Rhea still kept each other alive in ugly practical ways. Tomas and Mina had not been in bed together since the week after the confession, but he still handed her tools like she was staying for the whole shift, not vanishing at noon. Nobody in it was pretending the shape had gone clean.

The life in front of her was cramped, compromised, and only partly chosen. Her penalty payment came due every month whether or not she achieved wisdom. People still used her name as shorthand for fraud in comment threads she had learned not to read. But the work here had stopped pretending to be anything except the work. Patch the screen. Escort the students. Tell the truth when people asked why the colony sounded thinner. Pick monofilament out of roots before dusk. Keep hands off what had survived.

Mina looked back at Imogen. "Put me on it."

Imogen did not move. "On what, specifically?"

"The ugly version."

"That is still not precise enough."

Despite herself Mina laughed once. "Fine. Put me on utility and restricted guide. Bad wages, bad title, all the dirty parts. I'm staying."

Imogen's mouth tightened, not displeased exactly, just refusing sentiment on principle. "Are you staying because there is nowhere else that will have you?"

The question landed cleanly. That was why Imogen asked things that way. No pretty exit route. Mina could have lied out of pride, but pride had already cost enough bodies to make lying feel cheap. She set her mug on the railing and thought about the closet room, the curled receipt above her duffel, the mangrove stakes, the smell of wet screen in her hands.

"Partly," she said. "And partly because I know where the weak boards are now. I know which channels are still throwing sick fish after a hard rain. I know how to keep people from loving this place in the stupid way."

Imogen considered that. "That is the first useful thing you have said to me before noon in months."

"Happy to improve."

"Do not ruin the streak."

Imogen handed her the softened clipboard. Mina signed on the line where the ink had bled a little from humidity. Her own name looked ordinary there, the loops feathered soft by damp. It tied her to a list of tasks that would recur next week and the week after if the stilts held.

From the dock came the hard metallic ring of a hammer striking bolt. Tomas was under the platform bracing a new support post, shirt already plastered to his back with sweat. He had tied a dish towel around his neck against the rub of salt and wood grit, an old habit from engine work that made him look more tired than he ever had when he still had county payroll. Beto was beside him now, reading off washer sizes from the open hardware box like a litany.

"Three-eighths stainless," Beto said.

"That's quarter-inch."

"No, it's spiritually three-eighths."

"Your spirit can go buy the right bolt."

Beto laughed and dug deeper in the box. Tomas looked up long enough to spot Mina on the porch with the clipboard in hand. His face did not do much; he had gotten quieter since the hearing notices started arriving. But there was still that dry, local lift at one corner of his mouth when he decided not to be mean.

"So that's it?" he called. "They got you sweeping up with the rest of us?"

"Promotion, actually."

"Hell of a ladder."

He said it without venom, which on him counted as tenderness. Rhea came back down the walk with the extension students trailing her, all three carrying sample racks. She stopped at the edge of the dock long enough to set a bottle of water by Tomas's boot. He tapped the toe of his work shoe against hers once in thanks, barely visible if you were not already looking. Mina was already looking. There was grief in that small competence and care in it too. Their history stood there in plain view, all habit and grief and practical care, still knowing the shape of each other's thirst.

One of the students, a narrow young woman with a rain hood big enough to swallow her face, stared at the posted closure map and then at Mina. Mina recognized the hesitation: the half-second when a person was sorting memory from internet scandal. The woman did not say the name out loud. She only asked, "Is the western blind still accessible if we're not collecting specimens?"

Mina took the question before Imogen could. "Only with staff. Tide's undercutting the approach."

"And the contamination risk?"

"Depends what you mean by risk."

The student blinked. Rhea's head turned slightly, listening.

Mina went on. "Don't put your hands in standing water if you can help it. Don't step off the marked boards. If you see a fish on its side, don't decide you suddenly need to rescue it barehanded. You'll be fine."

The answer came out blunt enough to rasp. The student nodded, relieved by plain talk. Mina felt Imogen's gaze touch her from the side like a level being checked. When she looked over, Imogen only said, "Before lunch, if you can," and tipped her chin toward the cistern stair.

Mina picked up the tool bucket again. The rain had stopped. Water kept falling from leaves anyway. She went where the rot was.

* * *

At low tide the camp's edges came into view the way old lies did, their cover washed thin. Roots rose black and knuckled out of the water. Broken oyster shell showed in the mud like teeth. Storm trash collected where the boardwalk shadows crossed the narrow cut on the north side, all of it braided together by line and grass and whatever else the water had decided not to carry farther.

Rhea needed the west recorder battery replaced before dark, but a section of monofilament had turned up in the roots below the restricted walk and Imogen wanted it out before something living found it first. So Mina went with a bucket, a knife, a coil of fresh flagging tape, and Beto, who had finished his workbook section and was insufferable from success.

"Ninety-one percent," he said for the third time as they stepped down the muddy access slope. "Which means if the real exam is fair and God has any respect for struggle, I'm basically licensed."

"That's not what licensed means."

"Mina, don't kill the dream."

"I'm adjusting the dream to code."

He carried the long-handled grabber in one hand and a headlamp wrapped with pink electrical tape in the other. The tape had originally been on a box of donated party supplies and had ended up in the tool drawer because camp life turned everything into repair material eventually. He had written TORQUE IS A LOVE LANGUAGE across the lamp strap in black marker. It sat on his forehead now like a bad vow.

The evening had that heavy washed feel the coast sometimes got after three days of rain, the air wrung out and hung back up damp. Mosquitoes came anyway. They rose from the shadowed grass in gray whining clouds every time Mina's boots sank. She had learned to swat less. Swatting only broke the rhythm and made you feel attacked. Better to keep moving and let irritation turn to background.

The restricted walk ran above a side channel that had once mostly served guests with binoculars and false confidence. Now the planks were streaked with boot mud from sample crews, and bright tags marked the spots where floodwater had loosened the supports. Through gaps in the rail Mina could see the blackwater narrowed by mangrove knees and low branches. The tide had dropped enough that the current showed itself by pulling bubbles under the roots in long patient lines.

Beto stopped short by the second marker stake. "There."

The monofilament had wrapped itself around a cluster of low roots and snagged everything that touched it: eelgrass, a gull feather, one blue crab shell, a fist-sized wad of decomposed leaves. Mina crouched and reached down. The line cut cold and thin across her fingers even through the wet mud. She followed it back and found where it disappeared under a root, sawing in the current. Something had already fought it and gotten free. The bark was scraped pale where scales or shell had torn away.

"Hold the bucket lower," she said.

Beto obeyed. He talked more when afraid, more when embarrassed, more when bored. At tasks like this he got reverent. The bucket knocked softly against his knee.

"Rhea said the recorder on this side's been picking up weird click clusters again," he said after a while. "Not bat weird. Water weird."

"Everything's water weird right now."

"That's not science."

"No, that's experience."

She cut one loop, then another. The line loosened enough that she could pull a rank bouquet of marsh trash free in one wet fist. Mud ran down her forearm. Something tiny and alive darted from the mess back into the water. She shook her hand once and kept going.

Above them a pair of spoonbills crossed the opening between mangroves, late and oddly quiet, pink wings dimmed by the hour. The sight still hit her in the ribs, closer to recognition than guilt. Guilt had become too familiar to retain much glamour. It felt more like the knowledge of what did not come back because humans had paperwork for one urgency and not another. She imagined spray again sometimes when the air shifted wrong: the chemical sweetness under the diesel, the panicked flock noise driven flat by propwash, the soft impacts on boards. Memory did not fade. It just lost its sense of occasion and started living in the body like weather.

By the time they got the last of the line off the roots, Mina's knees were black with mud and the bucket held a mean little collection of evidence: hooks, nylon, bottle cap, two lengths of rusted leader, one child's glow-stick bracelet bleached colorless by water and sun.

"People are pigs," Beto said.

"People are mammals with opposable thumbs and bad impulse control."

"See, that's almost romance coming from you."

She stood and flexed her cramped hand. The tide had turned enough that the current no longer pulled straight out. It eddied now, indecisive. From farther back in the channel came the soft electronic chirp of the west recorder complaining on low battery. Rhea would be swearing at the time stamp already.

"Go swap that battery," Mina said. "I'll flag this support and be right there."

Beto took two steps, then paused. "You ever think about leaving and just not telling this place where you went?"

The question came so casual it nearly passed as idle talk. He kept his eyes on the recorder pole when he asked it, not on her. That was his serious voice now: aimed sideways so nobody could accuse him of wanting too much.

Mina tied a strip of orange flagging to the loosened support brace and cinched it tight with muddy fingers. "Sometimes."

"Yeah?"

"Sure."

"And?"

She looked up at the walk above them, at the patched boards, at the sign warning people back from the roots. Words had never stopped hunger. "And then I think about learning a whole new set of bad decisions somewhere else."

Beto huffed a laugh. "That's inspiring as hell."

"You asked."

"I did." He adjusted the headlamp on his forehead. The pink tape had started peeling at one corner from sweat. "I'm still leaving for school if I can."

"Good."

"You didn't even pretend to be sad."

"I can be sad later. Go save the recorder."

He went, boots thudding hollow on the boards as he climbed back to the walk. Mina watched him reach the pole and start fumbling with the battery box, talking to himself under his breath in the soft coaxing tone he used on stubborn screws. He wanted out of the islands and he wanted to deserve out. That combination in a person always made her want to touch wood for luck.

She bent to the support brace and wrapped the orange tape once, twice. When she straightened, something blue moved under the far roots.

This time the blue came thin as wire, a narrow strip of color threading through the blackwater where the tide curled back on itself. Her phone caught under her palm inside her pocket before she realized she had reached for it. She dragged it halfway free, saw the mud on her own knuckles, and shoved it back.

Above her the battery door clicked open. Beto had gone still on the plank, one hand inside the recorder box, the other already lifting toward the switch on his headlamp.

Mina shook her head.

He let his hand fall and kept the lamp dark.

The blue slid deeper between the roots. Mina stepped sideways off the packed edge to lean around the brace, felt the bank skin over into soup under her boot, and rocked back before the mud could take her. The tape stuck to her wrist. Water tapped from the leaves in slow separate drops.

"You getting that battery or composing a poem?" she said, too low for the line to carry past him.

Beto snorted once, shaky, and seated the new pack. Plastic knocked against plastic. The recorder woke with a thin electronic chirp that sounded absurdly domestic in all that mud.

By then the color had thinned to a seam, then to nothing she could swear to from where she stood. Mina cut the flagging tape with her knife, cinched the brace tighter, and hauled the bucket up to the walk. Beto handed down the dead battery without looking at her for long.

"You saw it," he said.

"Enough."

He glanced toward the roots, then back to the screws. "You gonna tell her?"

Mina hooked the old battery by its strap. "I'll tell her the recorder stayed dead too long in a side cut after rain, and she should check the files before the clock drifts again."

That pulled the corner of his mouth up. "Sneaky."

"Accurate."

They started back with the bucket knocking her shin and the old battery cold in her hand. The boardwalk flexed under their weight. Below them the cut had gone dark except for rainwater sliding off leaves and the ordinary weak metal shine of evening on the tide. Something turned blue once farther in where the mangroves braided tight, too far in for boots and already gone by the time she looked full at it. Mina kept walking. Rhea still needed the cards downloaded before supper, and the north-side supports would want bolts by morning.
