# Chapter 17

The camp had gone quiet in the wrong places.

After spray night there were still spoonbills in the outer trees, still the wet iron racket at dawn when the surviving adults lifted and settled and fought over what nests were left, but the deep constant nursery noise had thinned so much that Mina kept waiting for it to rise again like weather and it never did. The silence had shape now. It pooled under the main house. It clung to the boardwalk where Beto had spent half the afternoon hosing guano, fish scales, and pesticide film out of the slats, his burned wrist wrapped in a strip of blue shop towel because he would not stop working long enough to bandage it properly. It sat inside the little staff room that had been hers until yesterday while she folded shirts into a dry bag that still smelled of diesel, wet rope, and the three of them.

Imogen had taken her staff key after the confession and done it in the voice she used for weather advisories, low and precise and furious enough not to need volume. Mina was out as an employee. Housing would be sorted in the morning. For one night she could keep the cot and not make another public scene on the walkway. That was as close to mercy as Imogen had come.

Mina had packed badly. The field pants went in first because they were closest at hand, then notebooks, then a braid of charging cords she probably did not need, then the old flashlight with the cracked tailcap, then nothing for a while because the room had begun to look too much like evidence. On the wall the pale clean rectangle where the camp schedule had hung made her think of tags on dead birds. Outside, rain moved through in a light hiss, not a storm yet, only the early-season kind that laid a skin over everything and left the wood sweating after.

When Rhea knocked, she did it with two bare knuckles and no apology.

"He's at the kayak rack," she said when Mina opened the screen. Her braid had half come loose at the nape. There was a black streak of Sharpie along the side of her thumb from labeling sample bags, and she smelled faintly of rubbing alcohol under the mangrove mud. "If we're going, it has to be now."

Mina looked past her toward the dark walkway. "Where are we going."

Rhea held her eye for a beat that would have counted as dry amusement on anybody else and counted as strain on her. "Don't waste my time with performative stupidity."

Mina almost smiled. She did not have much left in her for shame, but she had enough to feel the old quick sting of being read clean through. "You think Tomas wants me there."

"No," Rhea said. "I think he wants the thing itself there. That's different."

Rain ticked on the tin over the generator shed. Somewhere below the stilts, mullet snapped at the surface. Mina glanced back into the room at the open dry bag, at the rolled shirts, at the place where her camp polo had hung before she stripped the logo patch off and shoved it in the trash because she could not bear the soft little hypocrisy of folded institutional clothing. If the state people came in the morning, if county enforcement came with them, if BrightCurrent's lawyers had already started calling the right offices, tonight was the last night before the whole creek turned into a case file. She knew that because she had helped do it once before, years ago from the other side of the badge. First you named a thing. Then you narrowed its world to coordinates, chain-of-custody language, transport temperature, tissue viability. Care and capture wore the same gloves for a while.

"Do I need anything?" she asked.

"No containers," Rhea said. "No knives. No spare batteries unless they're already in the light. He was specific about that."

"Christ."

"Yes."

Mina reached for the flashlight anyway, then stopped with her hand over it. Rhea watched that decision and said nothing. The silence between them was not healed. It was only inhabited now. Mina left the flashlight on the shelf, zipped the dry bag shut, and followed Rhea out into the damp dark.

The screened walkway gave under their weight with the soft spring the whole camp had when the tide pushed high under the pilings. Rain had brought the water level up just enough that the lower mangrove roots no longer looked flayed, only exposed and ugly instead of skinned clean to the air the way they had two weeks ago. Through the gaps in the screen Mina could see the bird camp in pieces: the donor deck slick and empty; the kitchen light on, where Beto had likely fallen asleep over inventory again because fear made him talk more until it made him quiet all at once; the bat lab window lit blue-white around stacks of charging units and labeled bags; the dark square of Imogen's office, where somebody had probably already started drafting careful emails about cooperation and preservation and regrettable events.

At the bottom of the steps the air changed. Above the walkway the camp always smelled built, a mix of bleach, old lumber, frying oil, damp screens. Down here it opened into mud, salt, mangrove rot, and the sweet chemical edge that still came and went depending on the wind. Mina hated that smell more than the dead things. Dead things belonged somewhere. Chemical drift sat on top of the world like a lie.

Tomas was waiting by the old kayak rack where the guest boats were chained in season. He had a flat-bottomed skiff half in the water and half on the mud, the kind of cheap fiberglass thing people bought for canals and swore they would maintain. He had stripped it down to almost nothing: one pair of oars, a push pole with the grip taped over in black electrical tape, a short painter, a red-lensed headlamp clipped to the thwart, and a dented plastic bucket that held two water bottles and a coil of line. No net. No cooler. No baitwell. No engine bracket even.

His county shirt was gone. He wore a gray T-shirt darkened at the chest from rain and a pair of work shorts stiff with dried salt. The white rectangle from his suspended county ID badge had left a pale line on the sun-browned skin above his pocket. He was worrying at a split thumbnail with his teeth, not looking at either woman.

"You took your time," he said.

Rhea answered before Mina could. "She packed."

"What'd she pack for."

"Tomorrow," Rhea said.

That made him look up. Not at Mina first, but at Rhea, at the shape of that word. Tomorrow. The morning after consequences. The morning after his suspension stayed a suspension and Mina's firing stayed a firing and none of the three of them got to pretend the confession had burned anything clean.

Mina stepped closer to the skiff. "Where'd you find this."

"My cousin had it upside down behind his trailer with a wasp nest in it." Tomas gave the hull a shove and the skiff slid the last foot into blackwater with a sucking sound. "It floats. That's all I need."

"If county sees you in closed water again-"

"County can kiss my ass from shore tonight." He said it flat, without heat. That was worse than if he had barked. "Listen to me before we go. No phones out unless Rhea says. No light unless we have to. No touching anything if we find it. No taking anything even if it looks dead, loose, easy, obvious, God-given, or like the exact thing that might save your name. If we can't do that, we stay here."

The old impulse in Mina rose so fast it almost embarrassed her. It came up uglier than anger and faster. The need to say that she was not stupid, that she understood field protocol, that nobody had to talk to her like a thief who needed her hands watched every second.

She bit that off because she was a thief who needed her hands watched every second.

"Fine," she said.

Tomas kept his gaze on her a moment longer, gauging whether fine meant fine or only not-yet-arguing. Then he nodded toward the bow. "You up front. You know how to keep your weight quiet."

"That's romantic," Rhea said.

"You want romantic, we can all go back to bed mad."

The line landed dry enough that it might have been a joke in another month. Tonight it came down between them and stayed there. Rhea stepped into the skiff first with the grant-issued handheld GPS looped around her wrist and her phone sealed in a clear dry pouch against her thigh. Her field clothes were the same ones she wore on survey nights, soft from washing and patched at one knee, but she had changed out the camp sandals for old neoprene booties because she never trusted mud to stay where it belonged. Mina noticed stupid things under pressure. The frayed edge of Rhea's braid. The place where Tomas had rewrapped the push pole with tape starting from the middle instead of the end, which meant he had done it in the dark or angry or both.

Mina got in after her. Tomas shoved them off and climbed last, taking the stern with the pole. The skiff rocked once under the changed balance and then settled.

No engine meant no immediate authority. No propwash, no county racket, no hard smell of gasoline blooming behind them. For a few seconds the quiet felt wrong enough to make Mina's body search for the missing vibration. Then Tomas planted the pole in the mud, leaned into it with his broad mechanic's shoulders and old prop-scarred forearms, and the skiff slid forward as if the channel had accepted a condition.

They moved under the camp lights and out beyond them. Rain needled the surface and stitched their wake shut behind them almost as fast as it opened. The world narrowed to wet mangrove walls, the soft thunk of pole to hull, and the distant thin chaos of birds. Without engine noise Mina could hear everything else the creek had been trying to tell them for months: shrimp ticking in the roots, little fish worrying at the tannin film, the drip from leaves into water already full, the occasional cough of a night heron, Tomas's breath when the pole bit too deep and he had to wrench it free.

She had first wanted him for access. That had been the cleanest truth in the mess, and maybe because it was the cleanest it had felt easiest to survive. Then desire had made itself inconveniently real. Then tenderness. Then the ugly arithmetic had come in and named every favor for what it cost. She could still remember chapter and verse of the fight in the cabin after the annex logbook, the exact angle of Tomas's mouth when he asked if she had ever wanted him outside the use of his keys. She had not answered quickly enough. That had been answer enough.

Now he poled them through the dark as if the creek had always lived in his wrists. He knew where the bottom rose by the change in pressure through the fiberglass. He knew when to use the oar instead of the pole because a shell bank would ring. He knew which gaps in the roots were channels and which were traps. Mina hated and wanted the competence in him with equal force. Some nights she still woke with the memory of his hand warm over her lower belly, steadying her against an airboat gunwale. Other nights she woke hearing the spray rotors.

Rhea sat mid-skiff with one hand on the GPS and the other flat on the seat beside her to feel the shift of balance. Under strain she seemed to draw everything inward until even her silence had edges. When the bow nosed too close to an exposed root she laid two fingers on Mina's calf, just above the old prop scar, not as comfort but calibration. Weight left, the touch said. Mina shifted. The skiff cleared.

They went by the first ruined nesting stand without any of them speaking. Even in the dark the damage read. Branches that should have sagged under nest mass instead held ragged bundles like overturned baskets. One spoonbill carcass had snagged high enough that the rain had cleaned most of it down to pale ribs and a wet pink flap of wing. Lower, in the fork of a mangrove, a nest cup held nothing but spray-stuck down and a single chick leg no thicker than a pencil. Crabs had gotten into the roots. Mina heard them clicking.

The smell changed again and made the back of her tongue ache. Mud, guano, rot, then that medicinal bite laid over all of it. She had spent most of spray night helping Rhea rinse live birds with distilled water and crush ice in towels and bag dead ones with labels because once disaster became official it demanded handwriting. In the worst hour Tomas had not been there. He had been out in the county airboat under orders he should have refused and maybe could not have afforded to. By the time he staggered back in, the rookery already looked like a place after shelling. There were chemicals in his arm hair. She had wanted to hit him and hold him at once. She had done neither.

Tomas set the pole and let the skiff glide under a low branch. "Rhea."

"What."

"Tell her about the morning."

Rhea kept her eyes on the dark ahead. "State wildlife called twice after dinner. They want the hydrophone card and any route notes from the week before the spray event. County wants all private watercraft off the restricted channels until the mortality survey is complete. BrightCurrent's insurer has retained counsel. That part came through the university, which is a charming misuse of everyone involved."

"So they know enough to want the place," Mina said.

"They know enough to want anything that can be fenced and billed."

Tomas snorted once. "If they can call it a recoverable line, they'll strip these roots with dip nets and call it stewardship."

Rhea said, "Yes."

The word sat between the mangroves like a hook.

Mina looked down at the water sliding along the hull. Rain made dim silver crowns where it struck. Underneath, the black held its own depth, mirror-dark until the bow disturbed it and then full of brown veils, minnows, broken reflections. Months ago that black had felt like possibility. Tonight it looked like bruised skin.

"Then why are we going?" she asked.

Neither of them answered immediately. Tomas poled them through a narrow cut where the roots reached overhead and turned the rain into slow separate drops. Rhea lifted the GPS, checked the muted screen, then lowered it again without speaking.

It was Tomas who finally said, "Because I want to know what I helped nearly kill."

There was nothing grand in it. No plea for forgiveness. He said it with the flatness he used for fuel numbers and tide tables. The line struck harder for that.

Rhea looked at Mina then, not rescuing him, not rescuing her either. "And because not knowing won't protect it. Only the wrong people prefer ignorance."

Mina let that sit. She had spent years believing the opposite, or pretending to. If nobody could prove the original fish was tank-bred, if nobody could pin the old photo to the cooler run, if nobody could track her exact omissions, maybe ambiguity would protect her. Ambiguity protected institutions better than people. She knew that now. She had stood in front of donors and investigators and told the room what she had done, and the truth had not absolved her so much as pinned her properly to her own life. Maybe that was all truth ever did.

Still, one part of her kept grinding. If the line was breeding here, truly breeding, not only surfacing like a rumor under spoonbill frenzy but entering the creek's actual reproductive mess, then there was a fact in it big enough to reshape everything. Not save her. She knew better than that, or almost knew better. But big enough to make the whole scandal look different under the light. Big enough to say the animal had become more than BrightCurrent's trash and more than her theft and more than a donor brochure miracle. Big enough, maybe, to turn shame into expertise again.

That was the part of her Tomas did not trust, and he was right.

They traveled for what felt like an hour and was probably less. The channel widened, then pinched, then opened into a basin roofed by mangroves and one battered gumbo-limbo leaning white in the dark. Here the spoonbills sounded farther off, their colony noise broken and uneven. Rain thinned to mist. Mosquitoes found them the minute the air lost motion. Mina slapped one off the back of her hand and left a smear of her own blood.

Tomas rested the pole and took up an oar, feathering it so lightly the water barely noticed. "From here on, don't talk unless you need to."

Mina nearly asked from where on. Then she heard it.

A faint crackle came from under the roots ahead of them, like somebody crumpling cellophane underwater. She had heard it once before under the spoonbills at the height of breeding season and thought it was wings reflected through water. Tonight it came in intermittent little bursts, close enough to tighten the skin along her arms.

Tomas let the skiff drift. Rhea lowered the GPS entirely and closed her hand over it until the screen went dark.

The basin had been hit hard. One mangrove on the left side had gone gray at the leaves, not dead yet but failing from the tips inward. Dead fry ringed the sheltered water under it, so small their bodies read more as silver commas than fish. Farther back in the roots something larger had bloated and sunk half under. The smell was not strong enough to identify, only enough to register as sweet rot in wet heat. Above them an abandoned nest sagged open to rain. Feather dust had glued itself to the bark.

Vigil, Mina thought, and surprised herself with the word. Hunt belonged to appetite. Survey belonged to the report waiting outside the basin. Vigil was what people did beside damage when repair was no longer a clean lie but leaving was worse.

Tomas pointed with two fingers to a seam in the roots on the right where the water deepened under a tangle of exposed props. Mina leaned without thinking. The skiff gave a warning creak. Rhea's hand found her calf again, firmer this time.

The first light was so faint she thought it was strain in her own eyes. Then it gathered.

Blue, but not the hard electric ribbon of the first coil she had seen from Tomas's airboat months ago. This was milkier, internal, a soaked kind of glow rising through tannin as if the water itself had taken a bruise and started shining through it. It lived under the roots and not in open water. It thickened, faded, came back. Crackle again. Something moved.

Tomas took the pole and eased the skiff one yard closer, no more. They were tucked now in a pocket where three prop roots arched out of the bank and met again below the surface, making a cage. Inside it the water held still despite the rain.

Mina had spent enough years looking at spawn and fry and egg masses that her body knew before her mind did. The light was punctate, repeated, too scattered for one body alone.

She whispered, "Jesus."

Rhea did not answer. Her breath had gone shallower. Under pressure she often became plainer, the careful clauses burned off until only the load-bearing words remained.

Mina bent lower and saw them.

They were fixed along the underside of the roots in a clear mucous braid, dozens at first glance and then scores once her eyes adjusted, each egg no larger than a lentil, translucent enough to show the pale bead of living blue suspended within. Some glowed stronger than others. Some were only cloudy pearls with the light barely waking in them. Between the clusters, milky threads drifted and caught and drifted again. Neither algae nor any fish spawn she knew from these channels. The whole root pocket looked as if somebody had stitched stars into spit and anchored them where the tide could not tear them free.

And there, moving through them with impossible slowness, was the parent line. Not fully visible, never giving them the whole body at once, only the long narrow passing of it under the eggs, blue running under black skin, then gone behind the roots, then back again. The light in the eggs thickened when it passed. Mina could not tell whether the animal was guarding, tending, spawning again, or simply trapped in the same sheltered instinct the roots offered. BrightCurrent had made a sentinel species. The creek had made whatever this was.

She knew, with the nauseating totality of field certainty, that rumor, sighting, singular specimen were all behind them now. The line had entered the place.

All the air seemed to leave the basin at once.

Rhea reached slowly for the red-lensed headlamp, clicked it on once, and held the beam low across the water so it would not flash the whole channel. Under red the blue only deepened. The eggs remained their own light. Rhea was already watching like a scientist, counting, tracing attachment points, seeing how the roots had sheltered the clutch from current and spray alike.

"Depth maybe twenty centimeters in the pocket," she murmured before she could stop herself. "Attached along red mangrove prop roots. Adhesive matrix. No obvious fungal bloom. Repeated clusters extending-"

She cut the sentence off. Mina heard why a second later. Those were the first lines of a report. The first steps of a seizure.

Tomas said, very quiet, "Don't turn it into a shopping list."

Rhea swallowed. "I know."

The adult line moved again, closer this time. The water under the eggs swelled. Blue light slid over the root bark and onto Mina's wrist where she had braced herself on the bow. For one ugly reflexive second she wanted to scoop. Nothing more complicated than that. Cup both hands under one cluster, lift, run. There were no jars aboard, no cooler, no batteries to keep it stable, no way to make that act anything but theft, and her body wanted it anyway with a force that felt older than reason.

Tomas saw her shift and caught the front of her shirt in his fist.

He did not yank. He only held.

"No," he said.

The word went through her like a hook set clean.

She stayed bent over, heart knocking stupidly in the wet dark, aware of his hand in the damp cotton between her breasts and of Rhea beside her and of the blue eggs under the roots and of the part of herself that could still, after all this, imagine a specimen as salvation. Shame came not as heat but as cold water under the ribs.

"I know," she said.

"You do."

She nearly lied because lying had once been the fastest way to keep love from turning into contempt. Then she heard the crackle under the roots again, saw the adult line's light pass through the clutch like breath through a lantern, and the lie died where it started.

"Not enough," she said.

Tomas let go of her shirt. His fingers stayed curled after, as if they still held fabric. "That's honest, at least."

Rhea had narrowed to pure procedure. Mina looked over and saw that she had the handheld GPS back in her palm, screen on low brightness, a blinking pin marking the basin. Habit. Precision. Insurance. The kind of fact people used to pry a living place open.

"You marked it," Mina whispered.

"Of course I marked it." Rhea's voice stayed quiet, but now it had that deliberate edge exact wording gave her when she was angry enough to be careful. "I've spent two years teaching myself not to trust memory where a coordinate exists."

"If you keep that, they'll get it," Tomas said.

"If I don't keep it, I may lose my work."

There it was, plain as mangrove bark. Ethics, yes, but also money. Permit legitimacy. The university stipend that paid in monthly increments large enough to matter. The lodging it protected. The whole thin scaffolding of a field life that could vanish if she started choosing omission where a grant contract expected data. Rhea had never asked the world to confuse her with a saint.

Mina looked from the GPS to the roots and back. Blue light pulsed up through the eggs, patient as rot, patient as weather. She could already hear how the room would change if Rhea kept the coordinate. Investigators asking for full metadata. Imogen arguing for controlled access and donor leverage. County men in flat-bottomed skiffs with seine bags. Somebody from BrightCurrent pretending concern while pricing out legal exposure. Somebody else wanting embryos, tissue, survivorship rates, reclamation language. Everybody calling it care because ownership sounded ugly in public.

And beneath all that, the smaller dirtier thought that still wanted one clean thing for herself. One location. One undeniable fact. One point on a map that would outlast the stories people told about her.

She hated that thought for surviving. She hated it more because it was not fully wrong. The coordinate did matter. Exactness was what made knowledge usable. Exactness was how the world moved from rumor into public fact.

But she knew exactly what kind of public had its hands on this creek now.

"If you keep it," she said, and found her voice sounding flatter than she expected, "they won't stop at one clutch. They'll peel this whole bank open."

Rhea's mouth tightened. "I know what enforcement does."

"Do you know what donors do?" Tomas asked. Still quiet. That was his meanest register now, the one the audit had stripped down to almost nothing. "Do you know what county does when something gets named and budgeted. They don't arrive with reverence. They arrive with coolers and a reimbursement code."

"I know," Rhea said again, harder.

The eggs brightened all at once, independent of the headlamp. The adult line was there again, just under the foremost cluster, body curving through the root pocket in a long blue flare. This time Mina saw enough of the head to know it was not alone. A second slimmer body threaded in from deeper dark, dimmer but unmistakable, and crossed it once in the cramped water. The light rippled through the suspended clutch. There was no clean word for what the sight did to her. Wonder was too easy. Terror too narrow. It felt like being given proof and accusation in the same wet mouthful.

Rhea sucked in breath through her teeth. "There are two."

"Yeah," Tomas said.

Mina had not realized until then that he had seen this kind of shelter behavior before, or at least guessed it. She turned to look at him. Rain had darkened his lashes into spikes. His face in the red spill of the lamp looked carved down to fatigue and something harsher than grief. Penance, maybe, if penance could have calluses and tool debt and county suspension language folded into it.

"You knew this pocket," she said.

"I knew where the water stayed cold under roots after the spray. I knew where fish went that wanted hiding. Same rules for most living things." He kept his eyes on the clutch. "I didn't know this was here. I hoped."

"Why'd you bring us."

This time his answer came fast. "Because if I only told you, you'd still be able to say later that you didn't really see it. And if I only told Rhea, she'd write it down in the proper tense and then hate herself for it. I needed all three of us stuck with the same ugly fact."

The basin held them. Mosquitoes whined around their ears. Somewhere far off a spoonbill barked once and got no answer.

Mina thought of the first luminous eel photograph, the one that made her and wrecked her. The grainy blue body in a handled moment. The way everybody had argued afterward not over the animal exactly but over custody, provenance, whether she had the right to the image, whether the image had the right to exist, whether a thing glimpsed in the wild stayed wild once a human being framed it properly. She had spent years wanting the creature to testify that she had not lied, not all the way, not about wonder at least. Wanting the animal to stand up in court for her appetite.

The eggs under the roots did not care what she needed from them.

"Take one picture," the bad part of her said aloud before she could stop it. "No metadata. No flash. Just enough to know later we didn't invent this."

Neither Tomas nor Rhea answered right away, and in the pause she heard how naked the request was. Personal, not scientific or institutional. She wanted a relic.

Rhea turned to her first. "And where would you keep it."

"On my phone."

"Which they may seize."

"I could strip the location."

"From your memory too?" Rhea asked.

The question did not sound cruel. That made it worse.

Tomas gave a short breath that might have been a laugh if there had been any humor in him tonight. "If the picture is good, it's bait. If it's bad, it's still bait. All it has to do is make the right asshole think he's one boat ride away from owning a miracle."

Mina looked back at the eggs. The light had dropped to a gentler pulse. In the red dimness she could see some of the outer cluster had clouded over with silt, while the inner ones glowed clean, protected by the roots' angle from direct wash. Survivorship had a geometry. So did destruction. The spray had thinned the rookery, killed chicks, drifted into fish nurseries and bat corridors, and still this pocket kept working at life through the damage.

"Then what do we do," she asked, softer. "Just know it."

Rhea's face changed by fractions. The anger thinned. The precision stayed. "Not just know it. Keep it unhelpful."

"That sounds like cowardice."

"No," Rhea said. "Cowardice is handing a living place to institutions because evidence feels cleaner than responsibility."

Mina opened her mouth, shut it again.

Rhea lifted the GPS a little. Rain beaded on the screen. The blinking point sat there, innocent as any other point. A coordinate was one of the least dramatic things in the world until you understood how many kinds of violence could ride inside it.

"If I delete this," Rhea said, and now her voice had lost even the last of its dryness, gone plain the way it did when she was most exposed, "I'm doing it with both of you as witnesses. I'm not interested in later revision. And I'm not interested in one of you deciding six months from now that restraint would've been more convincing with a private backup."

"I didn't make one," Tomas said.

Rhea kept looking at Mina.

Mina could have lied. She had no coordinates, no hidden recorder, no second phone in a dry bag, no specimen bottle tucked in the waistband of her shorts. She could have said of course not and left it at that. Instead she heard herself say, "I thought about it before I came down the walkway."

Tomas looked at her sharply. Rhea did not.

"And now," Rhea said.

The basin seemed to wait with her.

Mina watched the blue clutch under the roots. Watched the second adult thread back through the dark and vanish. Watched the rain widen and then smooth the circles on the pocket water. Behind her eyes rose every stupid fantasy she had been feeding for months: the paper that would straighten her name back out, the interview where somebody finally asked a different question, the grant call, the relief of not having to flinch when strangers recognized her from the old scandal. Vindicated. The word felt lacquered and false in this water.

Blue kept pulsing through the eggs whether her name ever straightened out or not. The creek owed her no testimony. The blue line under the roots was only alive.

"Now I think if I carry one more thing out of this water," she said, "I stay exactly the same."

Tomas looked away first.

Rhea nodded once. Her thumb moved over the GPS controls with the ruthless competence of somebody deleting more than a point. Menu. Stored locations. Current mark. Confirm. The little screen asked if she was certain because machines loved certainty most where it mattered least.

She pressed yes.

Then she took out her phone, opened the offline map app she used for transects, and deleted the temporary pin there too. After that she turned off the location permission for the camera entirely, thumb sure despite the rain. Not procedural showmanship. A person making future temptation harder on purpose.

"There," she said.

Only the insects answered, and the crackle under the roots, and Tomas's breath, and Mina's own blood loud in her ears.

Something in her eased with painful slowness, the way a cramped muscle lets go one fiber at a time. Her hands stayed empty. The eggs kept burning blue in the root pocket as if nothing human had happened at all.

They stayed longer than she expected after the deletion. Leaving immediately would have turned the act into transaction again. So they held the skiff quiet under the mangroves and kept watch while the eggs pulsed and the adult line or lines moved in and out of the root pocket with slow impossible patience. Mina let herself memorize without cataloging: the angle of the prop roots, the blue thickening in the center clusters, the rain dripping from one dead twig into the lit water and breaking the image apart and letting it re-form. She would remember this basin for the rest of her life and never be able to prove any part of it to people who loved proof more than consequence.

Once, without looking at her, Tomas said, "I'm not taking another living thing out of these channels."

It was the closest he had come to apology.

Mina turned her head toward him. His hand rested on the pole grip, grease still embedded in the lines of his knuckles no matter how often he scrubbed. She knew what it had cost him to say the sentence with her present to hear it: the eel, the spray run, the fuel thefts, the whole ugly chain of wanting and money and county authority and bodies moved from where they belonged.

"I know," she said.

This time he let the words stand.

Rhea shifted on the bench and briefly pressed the back of two fingers to Mina's shoulder blade, right between wet shirt and skin. The touch was small enough to miss if you did not know them, and after months of sex and fighting and side-by-side fieldwork Mina knew exactly how intentional it was. It marked witness, not reassurance or promise.

When they finally backed out of the basin, Tomas did it inch by inch. The skiff scraped once on a submerged root and all three of them froze, but the clutch held, blue steady in the pocket water. Then the channel took them again. The light vanished behind mangrove bark so abruptly Mina could have believed she had imagined it if the cold in her chest had not changed shape.

The ride back was slower. Or maybe silence stretched it. Rain had stopped entirely. Without the need to hide their breath they could hear dawn beginning at the edge of the mangroves, not light yet but the first tentative waking calls from birds that still had enough life left for habit. Once a fish rolled close to the hull and flashed white belly. Once Tomas swore under his breath when the pole sank into muck to the tape grip and came up smelling of sulfur. Rhea tucked the dead GPS screen under her thigh like something buried.

Halfway back, Mina said, "Imogen will ask."

"Yes," Rhea said.

"What are you going to tell her."

Rhea took her time. "That there is reason to keep the restricted zone restricted. That damaged systems do not benefit from spectators. That any report I file will address contamination risk and post-spray reproductive instability in general terms unless compelled otherwise."

Tomas grunted. "You're getting lawyer in your old age."

"I'm getting practical."

"Same disease."

The line almost passed for tenderness. Mina let it.

"And if they compel otherwise?" she asked.

Rhea looked at the wet channel ahead. "Then they can compel me to say I saw enough to justify protection and not enough to map a harvest."

"That's not really true."

"No," Rhea said. "It's not."

Mina let that settle with the damp and the first bird calls. Rhea was about to sandbag her own report. Tomas had given up the last clean excuse he had. Mina, who had built half her misery on wanting the world to understand her properly, was being asked to choose obscurity on purpose.

By the time the camp lights reappeared through the trees, the east had gone from black to a thin rotten gray. The donor deck floated above the water like a stage set nobody wanted anymore. Somewhere under the kitchen windows a pot lid clanged. Beto, then, up too early because grief had ruined his sleep. Mina thought of him gathering fallen spoonbill chicks with both hands after spray night, speaking in clipped nearly formal sentences because something had hurt him past jokes. Childhood ended in stupid places.

Tomas grounded the skiff in mud below the kayak rack and held it steady while Rhea climbed out. Mina followed, boots sinking an inch in the soft bank. Her legs shook when she hit land, spent from an hour or two bent around the hardest fact of the summer.

Tomas hauled the skiff higher with one hard pull and turned it upside down against the rack. Rainwater sheeted out of the ribs. In daylight it would go back to looking like an abandoned cheap boat with a bad patch job, not the vessel that had carried them to the roots and back with their hands still empty.

"They're going to come hard at you," Mina said to Rhea.

"Probably."

"At both of you."

Tomas shrugged one shoulder. "They already did."

Plenty crowded up behind her teeth: his suspension, the spray, the way wanting had tangled itself around access until she could not separate debt from tenderness, the fact that love in this place never came clean. The dawn was too thin for speeches. The camp was waking. A truck would come down the shell road before long with agency plates and coolers and questions.

So she said the only sentence that felt earned.

"I won't tell anybody where."

Rhea met her eyes. "I know."

Tomas did not look at her, but he nodded once.

Mina lifted her dry bag from where she had left it by the steps. It felt lighter than it should have after a night like this, as if some rotten little hope had finally leaked out of it.
By full light Imogen would sort housing and call whatever spare corner the camp still had a practical solution; Mina could already hear the phrase in that weather-advisory voice.

Behind them, beyond the camp and the boardwalk and the office where official language would soon start trying to net the world again, the mangrove basin kept its own blue under the roots. Rain, chemical residue, crabs, tides: the line would live or fail among those and not inside any story she could sell about it or herself. Mina hitched the bag higher on her shoulder and kept walking toward the main house before the first truck came down the shell road.
