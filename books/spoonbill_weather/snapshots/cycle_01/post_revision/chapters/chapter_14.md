# Chapter 14

By the following dusk the camp had learned a new way to be polite.

Nobody told Mina to stay clear of the kitchen. Nobody had to. The main house carried sound too well for that kind of order to survive as an order; it would have become gossip before the sentence was finished. Instead the place had gone careful around her. A pot of rice sat on the stove with the lid tipped so steam could get out. Two fillets of jack were wrapped in foil on a tray, one gone, one waiting. Someone had moved her mug from the drainboard to the far end of the counter, beside the can of instant coffee nobody claimed unless they were furious or broke. Through the screen wall the spoonbills kept up their raw nesting racket in the darkening trees, and under that noise the stilts complained every time the wind leaned.

The argument from the night before had not stopped ringing just because everybody had run out of things to throw at it.

Tomas's silence had been the loudest part. He had not shouted after the first few ugly minutes. He had gotten worse than that. He had gone exact. He had named fuel, hours, and the shape of his own suspension as if he were reading his losses off a county form. He had named his body too, which had been harder to hear. Mina had stood there with the annex logbook on the table between them and understood too late that proof could strip skin faster than lies did. Rhea had not rescued her. Rhea had also not let Tomas turn the whole thing into a neat charge sheet with Mina as sole defendant. That had been its own kind of mercy, and it had not felt kind.

All day Mina had been given work that kept her useful and away from the guest-facing side of camp. She had scrubbed the bat-room sink with diluted bleach until her knuckles smelled like a public pool. She had carried two cracked bins of damp netting out to the wash line and back. She had tightened loose screws on the screened walkway with a driver whose handle had melted a little in some old summer and hardened again lopsided. They were manual things, things that could be finished. Her shoulders were heavy in the clean dumb way labor sometimes gave her, but underneath that sat the live wire she had earned.

She had just lifted the foil and decided she could make herself eat when Rhea tapped the screen doorframe with two knuckles.

Mina looked up too fast. The foil crinkled in her hand.

Rhea stood there with a board paddle under one arm and a dry bag slung across her chest. She had on one of Tomas's old county work shirts over black shorts, the gray fabric washed so thin at the elbows it had gone almost white. Mina knew the shirt because she had once pushed her hands up under it in the dark and felt the square of the stitched county patch against Rhea's ribs from the inside. Tonight the sleeves were rolled with Rhea's usual carelessness, one cuff tighter than the other. A crushed packet of saltines bulged out of the chest pocket. Rhea always kept something plain to eat in her field bag because bug spray on an empty stomach made her nauseous, and she refused to admit it was nausea. She called it chemistry.

"Low water in the west corridors," she said. "The recorder needs a clean run, and a motor would wreck it anyway. If you still know how to paddle, come with me."

Mina set the foil down. "You sure?"

Rhea's face did not soften, but it did something harder to refuse. It became plainer. "I didn't ask by accident."

Mina glanced past her toward the boardwalk. There was no engine noise, no county radio mutter, no Tomas moving around the mosquito dock with his keys hitting metal. Since the suspension, the camp had developed an absence where his airboat should have been, like a missing tooth a tongue could not stop checking.

"Does he know?" Mina asked.

"He knows I'm running bats." Rhea shifted the paddle in her hand. "He knows I asked for a second board."

That was not the same answer, and both of them knew it.

"All right," Mina said.

She did not trust herself to say anything more while Rhea was still standing in Tomas's shirt.

The board shed sat behind the bat lab, where the screened walkway gave up and the shell path took over. Mina changed fast in the little washroom, into long sleeves and old leggings with a seam rubbed shiny at the knee, then met Rhea outside with her hair dragged up wet from the sink. Mosquitoes found the water at her temples before she had the cap back on the repellent. The bat lab door was propped open with a brick. Inside, battery chargers glowed red and green on the counter. Damp netting and guano and rubbing alcohol made the room smell like a wound somebody had kept scientific on purpose.

Rhea was checking batteries against a notepad clipped to a Rite-in-the-Rain board. She wrote small, angular script that made even grocery lists look peer reviewed. Next to her sat the ultrasonic recorder in a foam-lined case, two headlamps with the white LEDs taped over and red filters snapped into place, a waterproof notebook, and a thermos with chipped green enamel.

"You ate?" Rhea asked without looking up.

"Enough."

Rhea slid the saltines out of her pocket and held them out. "Eat more than enough."

It would have been easier if she had sounded tender. Tenderness gave a person something obvious to flinch from. Rhea sounded like a scientist correcting a field error before it propagated through the data set. Mina took the packet and ate two broken crackers while Rhea checked the recorder one more time.

"We're not going near the spoonbill channels?" Mina asked.

"We're not hunting anything tonight." Rhea zipped the dry bag. "We're running transects and trying not to contaminate the file with wishful thinking."

Mina swallowed dry crumbs. "I didn't mean-"

"I know what you meant."

The shell path down to the launch bit through Mina's shoes. On the dark side of the main house, the camp felt smaller than it had with Tomas's airboat in the world. There was no prop cage looming, no fuel sheen, no sense that a bad idea could become motion in thirty seconds if the right man turned a key. The boards made them look almost harmless: two women carrying narrow white bodies under their arms, paddles clicking softly against shafts wrapped in bicycle tape to kill noise. A gecko watched from the railing, throat pulsing. Somewhere across the water a generator coughed itself steady.

They set in beside the mangrove fringe where the tide had dropped enough to expose black root knees. Mina stepped onto her board and felt the old small correction move through her ankles and hips, the body remembering before pride did. The water took her weight with a quiet slosh, then steadied. Rhea shoved off beside her with less visible effort than most people used to stand from a chair.

For the first hundred yards neither of them spoke. They let the boards find the creek's narrow center where the last light sat on the tannin surface like something thinking about silver and changing its mind. With no engine to push them, distance became personal again. Mina felt each yard in her shoulders. The paddle blade entered blackwater with a thick silk resistance and came out smelling of root rot and salt. Tiny fish skipped away from the boards with sounds like fingernails flicked against glass.

She had forgotten how much noise a motor erased. Without it the channels broke into layers: mosquito whine at ear level, mullet gulps in the dark, spoonbills in the farther rookery sounding less like birds than like a room full of people trying not to retch, and crabs ticking against submerged wood. Rhea's paddle dripped in an even metronome a few feet to Mina's left. Every time Mina changed sides, the board gave a soft hollow knock under her feet and sent a circle of tannin out through the reflected branches.

By the time they reached the first station, a low tunnel where the mangroves closed over the water and the air thickened with insects, Mina's triceps were beginning to feel the slower cost of everything Tomas had ever made look easy.

Rhea lifted two fingers without turning her head in the stop signal. Mina dropped to her knees and let the board drift. Rhea did the same. They clipped the boards together with a short line so they would not knock apart in the mild current, then killed the lamps and sat inside dark that was not complete because the water held a little sky and the sky held the afterimage of heat.

Rhea set the recorder between them, its indicator the faintest red bead under tape. She leaned close enough for Mina to smell coffee and DEET and the cool medicinal trace that always lived in the cotton at Rhea's wrists.

"Twenty minutes," she whispered. "Any emergence from the hollow snag on your side, note direction and count. Don't guess. If you lose one, you lose one."

Mina nodded.

Once she stopped trying to hear bats as a concept and started listening for disturbance, they arrived everywhere: tiny leather squeaks beyond ordinary hearing, translated through the recorder's monitor into dry needle-clicks, soft rushes of wing close to the water, and brief impossible changes in shape as something moved through the red-dark in front of her and then became branch again. Three came out of the snag within the first minute, then seven in a loose spill, then more from farther back in the roots where she had not even known a cavity sat.

Counting calmed her in a way thinking never had. She marked one body, then another. One dropped low enough to seam the water. Another broke upward and was gone. The notebook on her thigh went damp under her wrist. Beside her, Rhea made her own marks with the pencil braced against her thumb, never looking hurried, never correcting a movement twice.

When the first pass was done, Rhea touched Mina's knee and pointed farther inland. They unclipped, stood, and poled themselves deeper through a passage too narrow for anything wider than a board or a skiff with a patient fool at the stern.

The quiet changed scale. Airboats turned the mangroves into obstacles. Boards let the place keep its own shape. The roots arched over them in black braided masses that smelled of iodine and old mud. Bats crossed so near Mina heard the pressure of their wings more than the sound, quick compressions of air by her cheek. Twice she had to duck under branches slick with salt and spider silk. Once her board slid over something soft that rolled under the hull and vanished, and her whole body flashed to blue before her mind caught up and named it as a log.

She hated that the creek still knew how to use her. One false texture and she was ready to turn every living thing into a clue.

At the second station they crouched in the lee of a dead palm snag and let the recorder run. Rhea drank from the thermos and passed it over without asking. The coffee had gone milky and bitter. It tasted of chicory and the cap's rubber gasket. Mina drank anyway.

"You don't have to be decent to me tonight," she said softly.

Rhea kept looking into the branches. "That sounds exhausting."

"I mean it."

"I know you do."

Another bat slipped out of the mangroves on a line so clean it seemed drawn. Mina wrote it down and then said, because the dark was doing its job on her and because Rhea had brought her out here instead of leaving her to eat alone with her own shame, "He was right."

Rhea's pencil stopped moving.

"About what?" she asked.

"That I used him." Mina kept her voice low even though there was nobody to overhear but roots and birds and whatever things fed under roots and birds. "I can say I wanted him too. I did. I can say he knew what he was offering, and he did. None of that changes what part of my brain lit up first. He had fuel. He had routes. He had a county shirt and a key and a boat that made the law bend around the edges. I saw access before I saw a man."

Rhea took that in without looking at her. It was one of the things Mina had learned to crave most and fear most in her: the way she let a statement exist at full size before answering it.

"You saw a man too," Rhea said at last. "Don't do yourself the courtesy of simplification."

Mina gave a short ugly laugh. "That sounded nicer in my head than it deserved."

"It didn't sound nice."

There was no cruelty in it. Only calibration.

Mina rested the paddle shaft across her thighs. "It wasn't just him."

Rhea turned then, enough that Mina could see the pale strip of her cheekbone under the red lamp.

"I know," Rhea said.

Mina wished she had not opened the door if she was going to stop in it. The wish passed. There was no point being halfway honest with Rhea. Half honesty was just vanity with better lighting.

"Once you knew," Mina said, "once you knew enough to scare me and you still looked at me like that... I started wanting something from you that had nothing to do with fairness."

Rhea waited.

"Part of me thought if you wanted me after all of it, maybe that changed the shape of what I was." Mina kept her eyes on the notebook because looking at Rhea while saying it would have made her either theatrical or evasive, and she did not have the right to be either. "Like if the wife wanted me too, then I wasn't just the woman who fucked her husband for gas. I was something else. Chosen. Maybe even clean. That's ugly. I know it is."

The current nudged the clipped boards against one another with a rubber kiss.

Rhea exhaled once through her nose. "Yes," she said. "It is."

Mina let the word land. It hurt less than a lie would have.

Three bats burst from the hollow behind Rhea's shoulder, then another two. They both counted automatically. Mina wrote 5 NE. The act of writing steadied her hand enough to continue breathing like a person and not a machine left running.

"I didn't bring you out here to make you bleed for the pleasure of accuracy," Rhea said after a moment.

"Then why did you bring me?"

Rhea capped the thermos. "Because the file needed a second body who can still distinguish a bat from a wish. Because the boards are quieter than a boat. Because if I stayed on land tonight, everybody would keep pretending the only choices are courtroom or bed."

She adjusted the recorder gain with one precise thumb. "And because I wanted your company."

The last sentence went through Mina with more force than anything louder could have managed.

They ran the third station in near silence. A barred owl called once from deeper woods, flat and sour. Mosquitoes needled the backs of Mina's hands where repellent had worn thin. Sweat crawled down her spine under the damp shirt and cooled whenever the night moved. She kept the counts, kept the time stamps, kept her eyes on the exits Rhea assigned her. Work came first. Structure held her together; punishment would only have fed the shame. The difference mattered.

At a shell shelf no wider than a kitchen table, they slid the boards half onto mud and stepped off to stretch their legs. Mina's calves trembled in the sudden steadiness of land. The mud sucked at her soles through the thin water shoes. Rhea crouched by the dry bag and sorted batteries by touch. From this angle the old county shirt hung open enough at the throat for Mina to see a crescent mosquito bite high on her collarbone, angry and raised.

Rhea handed over the saltines again. Mina took one and watched her peel an alcohol wipe open with her teeth.

"Your hands," Rhea said.

Mina looked down. The waxed paddle grip had lifted two blisters at the base of her fingers, not dramatic, just white and mean.

"They'll live."

"So will an untreated fungal rash for longer than either of us wants." Rhea held out her hand. "Give me one."

Mina obeyed before pride could organize itself.

Rhea wiped the raw skin clean. The alcohol bit sharp. Mina hissed.

"I know," Rhea murmured, not unkindly. "Hold still."

She smoothed a strip of hydrocolloid over the blister with the flat of her thumb, pressing from center to edge as carefully as if she were fitting membrane over a specimen slide. Her fingers were cool from the night air and smelled faintly of guano and cheap hand soap. Mina could have made that into a seduction if she had wanted to be stupid. What it felt like instead was worse and better: care with no romance excuse attached.

"I'm trying not to ask for more than anybody can give me," Mina said.

Rhea snorted softly. "That sentence has too much advance credit in it."

Mina almost smiled. "All right. I'm trying to notice when I do it."

"That's better."

Rhea taped the second blister, then sat back on her heels. Behind her the blackwater reflected a ragged copy of the mangroves, roots reaching up and down at once.

"You're not wrong that desire changes the shape of things," Rhea said. "It does. It changes what we can bear to look at. It changes what we excuse. It changes how much stupidity feels briefly survivable."

"That sounds like a scientific term."

"It's adjacent to one."

Mina held the taped hand in her lap. The pad already made the skin feel less exposed, which was exactly the problem with being helped by Rhea: relief arrived before deserving it.

"But desire is not rescue," Rhea said.

The sentence settled between them with the same dry authority as a measurement.

Mina looked up.

Rhea met her eyes. "I wanted you. I want you now. That's real. It survived stress, novelty, even the erotic possibilities of shared contamination. And it won't carry either of us out of this. If I start treating it like a raft, I become cruel. If you do, you become dishonest."

Mina's mouth had gone dry in spite of the wet air.

"I know you aren't leaving him," she said.

"That's not the only thing I mean."

Mina waited.

"I'm not leaving my life for a cleaner story," Rhea said. "Not my marriage, not my work, not the ugly practical lattice of obligations that makes up most actual living. That wouldn't make us brave. It would make us dramatic. There's a difference."

The mangroves ticked with small creatures moving where they could not be seen.

"Some hours I'm furious with Tomas," Rhea went on. "Some hours I'm furious with you. Some hours I'm furious with myself for finding any of this beautiful enough to miss. None of those states absolves the others. I'm not interested in pretending you can replace him, or he can cancel you, or wanting one of you tells me what to do with the other. That's rescue-story thinking. It turns people into exits."

Mina stared at the dark patch on Rhea's shirt pocket where thermos coffee had leaked and dried in an uneven half-moon. It was easier than staring directly into the fact of being seen.

"Then what are we doing?" she asked.

Rhea glanced past her toward the creek, listening for the next pulse of wings. When she answered, her voice was quieter than before, less lecture than confession.

"Witness, if we can manage it."

Mina let out a breath she had not noticed she was saving.

Rhea rubbed one thumb over the rim of the recorder case, a small repetitive motion Mina had never seen her make in front of anyone else. "My job is full of forms of helplessness that have to stay precise. A colony gets sprayed or flooded or stressed out of a roost and there are days when all I can actually do is count who still comes out at dusk. People hear that and think it's a lesser task than rescue. It isn't lesser. It's the part that keeps the damage from being translated into rumor or public relations copy. Somebody has to look directly and say what happened."

She looked back at Mina. "That matters with people too."

The words hit lower than blame. Mina understood, suddenly and without relief, that witness required more of her than apology did. Apology could still be performance. Witness meant staying in the room after the pretty version died.

"You mean you can know what I did and still stay accurate about me," Mina said.

"Yes."

"And I don't get to turn that into sainthood."

"Correct."

The corner of Rhea's mouth moved. It was not quite a smile, but it was enough.

Mina sat down hard on the shell shelf before her knees could decide something embarrassing. The shells pressed through her wet shoes. She welcomed the sting.

"There was a minute last night," she said, "when Tomas was talking about the fuel and the hours and I thought, if you touched me right then, I would survive it. Which was already bad enough. Then I thought if you chose me in front of him, it would make the whole room arrange itself around that instead of the facts. That's the part I hate."

Rhea absorbed this with no visible flinch. "Thank you for not dressing it up."

"Don't thank me."

"I didn't say it was admirable."

Mina barked a laugh so sudden a heron farther down the shelf rustled once in protest.

Rhea's face changed then. Some of the hardness went out of it. She sat beside Mina on the shell shelf, knees up, forearms draped over them, close enough that their wet sleeves touched and stayed touching.

"I need to tell you something in return," she said.

Mina waited.

"Imogen asked for the raw coordinates from the spoonbill sighting file this morning. She didn't frame it as pressure, exactly. She framed it as prudence." Rhea picked a grain of shell off her shin. "I said the metadata were unstable because the file was corrupted. That's true enough to count as truth if I'm feeling cowardly. Less true if I'm feeling rigorous."

Mina turned her head. "You still have them."

"Approximately. Not to the meter."

"And you didn't give them to her."

"No."

The night seemed to tilt a little around that fact, not because Mina wanted possession of the eel back. She did not even know if possession was a meaningful category anymore, but because Rhea had chosen not to hand wonder and contamination over to the nearest institution just because institutions were hungry.

"Why are you telling me?" Mina asked.

Rhea looked mildly insulted by the question. "Because if I'm making a morally consequential decision in a swamp because desire has complicated my judgment, I'd like at least one person present who knows the full contamination profile."

Mina stared, then laughed again, softer this time. "That's the least romantic thing anybody has ever said to me."

"You say that like it's criticism."

"It might be praise."

Rhea bumped her shoulder once with her own. "I'm not offering you exoneration. I'm offering accuracy and involvement. If I decide to keep the location vague for now, I want you to know that's not me being dazzled by you. It's me refusing to let this place become an asset sheet before we understand how much harm is already braided into it."

Mina felt the old reflex rise, the one that wanted to promise usefulness fast, before anyone could change their mind. "I can stay out of it if that helps."

"That's not witness." Rhea's voice sharpened, then eased. "Mina. No vanishing trick. Not with me."

The use of her name, plain and unornamented, moved through her harder than the touch on her blisters had.

"All right," Mina said.

Rhea took a breath. "If this gets uglier, and it will, I need someone around me who won't start lying the second a cleaner narrative becomes available. Tomas can't do that right now, not about this, and I don't blame him for it. Imogen certainly can't. I'm asking whether that can be you."

It was the closest thing to trust Mina had been offered in years, and it arrived wrapped in caveats sharp enough to draw blood. That made it easier to believe.

"Yes," she said.

Then, because the creek and the heat and Rhea's precision had stripped her down to the part that could answer without theater, she added, "I don't know if I can do it perfectly. But yes."

Rhea nodded once, as if the answer had met a threshold she had set privately.

They sat for another moment listening to the dark tick and move around them. Then Rhea said, "Come here," in the tone of voice she used when handing somebody a specimen bag or correcting headlamp angle.

Mina turned. Rhea kissed her with one hand on the back of her neck and the other braced in shell grit by Mina's hip. The kiss landed with the blunt authority of a fact. Her mouth tasted of stale chicory coffee and salt and the faint sweetness of whatever hand cream she used only when her knuckles cracked badly enough to force the issue. Mina opened under it anyway, old hunger flaring so fast she had to grip the shell shelf to keep from leaning all the way over.

Rhea's braid brushed Mina's wrist. The county shirt smelled like mangrove water now, and underneath that, like warm skin. When Mina put her taped hand against Rhea's waist she felt the damp cotton cling and the hard line of the thermos cap digging through the pocket. Rhea made a low sound into her mouth, not helpless, not careful either. It was honest.

Then she pulled back just far enough to look at Mina in the red-thinned dark.

"This isn't a promise of departure," she said.

Mina nodded. Her lips felt salt-burned.

"I know."

"And it's not pity."

"I know that too."

Rhea studied her another beat, as if checking for slippage in the meaning. Satisfied, she kissed her once more, shorter, and stood.

"Good," she said. "We still have two stations."

It was so absurdly characteristic that Mina had to close her eyes and smile into her shoulder before getting up.

The last part of the route took them through water so narrow the boards brushed mangrove leaves on both sides. Bats were thick there, dropping out of the canopy and skimming the surface in quick dark loops that set the monitor ticking almost continuously. The work demanded enough of Mina that her mind stopped trying to turn every exchange into verdict. She watched emergence holes, counted passes, marked time. Once Rhea reached across the clipped space between boards and moved Mina's wrist two inches to angle the recorder away from insect buzz. The correction was practical. The heat it left behind in Mina's skin was not.

Near midnight a low thunderhead walked itself along the inland horizon, far enough away to show without yet arriving. The air changed before the weather did. It felt cooler on the forearms and heavier in the lungs. The mangrove leaves turned their paler undersides. Mina felt the shift in the water first, a subtle flattening as the night held still to hear what the sky planned next.

At the final station, bats poured from a dead gumbo-limbo split by lightning years ago, a ragged streaming knot that made both women abandon talk and just count. Forty-three on Mina's side in under six minutes. Thirty-one on Rhea's. Then smaller staggered exits from farther back in the cavity. The recorder's little hidden light glowed like a clot. By the time the flow thinned, Mina's notebook page had gone soft with humidity and pencil pressure.

Rhea exhaled and rolled her neck once. "That will do."

On the paddle back, they let the current help. The boards slid easier with the creek under them instead of against. Neither woman hurried. Without a motor there was no illusion of outrunning what waited on land, but there was also no prop cage shrieking them back into role. There was only the pull, the dip, the drip, the black lanes opening one by one between roots.

"Where is he sleeping?" Mina asked finally.

Rhea did not pretend not to know who she meant. "Half the night in our bed. Half the night on the porch when he gets tired of being angry in a horizontal position."

The sentence held affection and damage in proportions Mina could not calculate.

"I'm sorry," Mina said.

"I know."

"That wasn't me fishing."

"I know that too."

They rounded the last bend and the camp came into view in pieces: one dim bug bulb at the boardwalk, the main house windows yellowed by old screens, the bat lab rectangle dark except for the charger lights waiting inside. The place looked fragile again, just wood and mesh and debt over water, not the stage set it became when donors were present or a fight made everybody archetypal.

Rhea grounded her board and stepped off into shin-deep mud without complaint. Mina followed, wobbling once before the mud claimed her balance and held it. They hauled the boards up onto the shell lip and stood breathing in the mosquito-thick dark, not touching yet.

"I'm going back to the house," Rhea said.

Mina made herself nod before disappointment could turn adolescent in her face.

"All right."

Rhea set the paddle down and took Mina's jaw lightly in one hand, thumb rough at the corner of her mouth. "I said I'm going back to the house. I didn't say I want distance from you."

Mina looked at her.

"The recorder has to upload," Rhea said. "The files will take twenty minutes if the laptop behaves and an hour if it decides to be a university machine. I can stay for the upload."

It was such a Rhea offer that Mina almost laughed again. Intimacy, in Rhea's dialect, looked like twenty minutes under a progress bar with data as chaperone.

"Okay," she said, and heard how much it meant in that one small word.

Inside the bat lab the air had gone sour and warm from the day's sealed heat. Rhea switched on the red task light over the counter. It painted the room like a wound under skin. They moved around each other with the ease that survived even this: Mina laying out the notebook to dry, Rhea plugging the recorder cable into the laptop, both of them angling themselves automatically away from the place where the extension cord liked to spark if kicked.

The upload bar crawled.

Rhea sat on the edge of the counter and took off her glasses to clean them on the hem of the county shirt. Mina had never asked when Rhea had started needing them for screens. It felt like one of the private domestic facts a person earned by staying. She watched the careful circles Rhea rubbed on each lens, the small crease between her brows when a smear resisted.

"You should wash those bites before you sleep," Rhea said, putting the glasses back on. "The one on your ankle is already ugly."

"Romance again."

"I've got range."

Mina leaned against the opposite counter. The room hummed softly with chargers and laptop fan. Through the screen vent, the night outside kept breathing its wet black breath.

"If it comes apart worse," Mina said, "with Imogen, with the county, with whoever wants the eel to be either miracle or contamination exhibit, I can take my share out loud."

Rhea's gaze moved from the laptop to her face. "You'll have to. That's separate from this."

"I know."

"Good."

The upload reached sixty-two percent and stalled there long enough for both women to notice. Mina felt a childish panic at the thought of this one quiet thing getting corrupted too. Rhea slid off the counter, bent over the laptop, and tapped one key with efficient irritation. The bar moved again.

"There," she said.

Mina let out the breath before she could stop herself.

Rhea straightened and looked at her for a long second. Then she crossed the little room and rested her forehead against Mina's.

"When the facts start costing you," she said softly, "don't rearrange them to make yourself bearable."

The sentence went into Mina like a blade and like relief, both at once. It named the old trick more plainly than confession ever had: not lying outright, just shifting emphasis until she could stand her own outline. No donor-brochure innocence. No glamorous ruin either. Just annex logs and fuel theft and want mixed up with evidence; Tomas on the porch with his anger, Rhea in his shirt, Mina still wanting both witness and touch from the same woman. It did not let anybody out. It also did not ask them to disappear.

Mina put her good hand on the back of Rhea's neck. "I won't," she said. "If I start trying to sand it smooth, stop me."

The laptop chimed. The upload was complete.

Rhea kissed her once, brief and unspectacular and final in the way of things that intended to continue later without needing to advertise it. Then she stepped back, capped the thermos, and unhooked the county shirt from where it had caught on the corner of the recorder case.

"Sleep with ointment on those hands," she said.

"Yes, doctor."

"Not a doctor."

"You say that every time."

"Because it keeps being true."

Mina smiled despite herself. Rhea picked up the dry bag and headed for the door. At the threshold she looked back, braid dark down her shoulder, one hand already lifting to scratch at the mosquito bite on her collarbone now that nobody else was close enough to see her break discipline.

"Tomorrow," she said, "if anybody starts telling a prettier version than the one we have, come find me before you answer."

"I will."

Rhea nodded and was gone down the screened walk, her footfalls swallowed by the old wood almost immediately.

Mina stood in the red-lit room with the finished file on the laptop and the smell of chicory coffee still on her mouth. Outside, the spoonbills raised another burst of filthy, human-sounding noise from the rookery, and farther out in the mangroves the bats kept working the dark whether anybody counted them or not. The facts stayed dirty and fully hers. What the night had given her was narrower than rescue and much harder to misuse.

When she finally turned off the red light and stepped onto the walkway, the paddleboards below the bat lab still dripped creek water back into the black channel one patient drop at a time.
