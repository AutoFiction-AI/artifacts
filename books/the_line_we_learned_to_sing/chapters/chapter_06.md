# Chapter 6

Ina sat cross-legged on the vinyl chair with her laptop balanced on both knees, trimming a line that had already been argued six different ways. Near noon, the studio floor smelled like wet dust and burnt coffee, the way it always did after one of the old wall units coughed itself through another summer morning. She had slept almost not at all after a night of reroute calls and had stopped hearing the dialogue as dialogue. It was only weight now, syllables dragged between legal risk and ratings risk and the quiet threat that a sentence could get somebody hurt three provinces away.

Yura from post-production leaned over the cubicle wall with a half-peeled boiled egg in one hand and her phone in the other. The frog pin on her lanyard had lost one plastic eye months ago, but she still rubbed it with her thumb when she was nervous.

"Did you hear that?" Yura asked.

Ina kept typing. "Hear what?"

"Lobby glass. Big hit. Might be loading gear, but it sounded like somebody kicked the door."

A bass thud rolled up through the floor before Ina could answer. That wasn't loading gear, and it wasn't a delivery cart either. It hit too hard, with too many feet driving it.

She set the laptop down and stood. Through the strip of uncovered window near the ceiling she could see two satellite vans parked crooked against the curb and three broadcast cameras swinging toward the entrance. The sky above the vans was that white monsoon glare that flattened shadows and made every chrome edge painful to look at.

Her phone buzzed with a message from an unknown number.

`Stay at your station. National Intelligence Service on site. Do not destroy records.`

Another buzz came before she finished reading.

`If you run, you will be detained immediately.`

Yura read Ina's face and dropped the egg back into the paper cup. "Shit. Is this tax?"

"No," Ina said, because lying would have sounded more suspicious than the truth she did not have time to explain.

The first officers came through the studio doors in navy windbreakers that still held rain dots on the shoulders. Behind them, two men in business shirts carried hard cases and barcode scanners. One of the officers read from a document without looking up.

"This warrant authorizes seizure of servers, endpoint devices, and physical scripts linked to unauthorized cross-border communications under national security provisions. Everyone stays where you are. Hands visible."

The floor went loud in broken ways: a dropped mug, someone cursing in the color room, the repetitive chirp of a phone alarm that nobody turned off. Ina watched one technician walk straight past the creative desks and kneel by the server rack near the sound booth with the confidence of someone working from a memorized floor plan. He popped the side panel, photographed each drive label, and started pulling trays into antistatic bags. His hands moved with careful boredom.

She heard herself say, "Those are licensed archive packs and subtitle assets. There's client data on those."

The technician did not look up. "Then your clients can contact counsel."

A woman with a clipped bob and a yellow legal pad approached Ina's desk. Her badge read `Captain Shin Mi-rae`. A silver ring had worn a pale groove into the skin on her left index finger, like she had taken it off in a hurry and not put it back.

"Seo Ina?" Captain Shin asked.

Ina nodded.

"Please unlock your machine and step away from the keyboard."

"I want counsel present before any interview."

"You can request counsel at intake. Unlock it now."

The request sounded polite enough that half the room might have mistaken it for optional. Ina placed her fingers on the keys. Ink stains still marked the side of her right hand from the marker she had used that morning to revise scene cards. She typed the passphrase, closed the lid halfway, and stepped back.

By then the hallway had filled with uniforms and two camera operators who had somehow crossed the building threshold despite the guards downstairs. Somebody from accounting started crying in the copier alcove. The sound came in short, angry bursts; she kept trying to clamp it down between breaths and kept failing.

Captain Shin glanced at the desk drawer and tapped it with the end of her pen. "Open it."

"It's office supplies."

"Open it."

Ina pulled the drawer out. Tape, charger cables, a packet of instant coffee, receipts, and beneath those, folded A4 pages with Kwon's signature block and her own name printed in both Korean and English. She had forgotten they were still there. She had meant to move them weeks ago and had told herself she would do it after the next deliverable, after the next meeting, after a night of full sleep.

Captain Shin slid on nitrile gloves before touching the pages. She unfolded the top sheet and held it at eye level.

"Confidential Service Agreement. Restoration Cultural Council. Monthly remuneration seven million won." Her eyes lifted to Ina. "This is yours?"

Ina's mouth tasted like dry paper. "That's old consulting language."

"Do not insult my intelligence in your first answer."

She handed the pages to the evidence clerk, who sealed them inside a transparent pouch and wrote the time in thick black strokes: `12:17`.

Around Ina, people pretended not to look while looking at nothing else. One writer she barely knew kept swiping his phone screen up and up, trying to outrun whatever headline was forming.

Ina reached for her own phone. Captain Shin put out a palm.

"Who are you contacting?"

"My lawyer."

"Name."

Ina gave a name she had never called.

Shin held out her hand. "Phone. You can request legal counsel through intake."

"I need to notify my father."

"Phone."

Ina passed it over. Shin turned it face down on the desk without checking the screen, which felt worse than if she had searched it on the spot.

Three desks over, a junior translator named Jisoo started saying, "I just do punctuation, I just do punctuation," like a prayer she had not believed in since childhood. An officer guided her into a chair with practiced gentleness and a hand hovering near her elbow but not touching.

When they moved Ina toward the elevator, cameras caught the hallway from both ends. She felt each click of shutter through the soles of her shoes, tiny kicks on tile. Someone shouted from the stairwell landing.

"Writer Seo, are you tied to a foreign influence network?"

"Are you under arrest?"

"Did you feed propaganda to entertainment channels?"

Captain Shin angled her body between Ina and the nearest lens. "No statements."

In the elevator mirror, Ina saw herself as the cameras had: damp hair curling at the neck, blouse wrinkled from the chair back, left wrist scar bright against the metal handrail. She was already marked. She wasn't convicted or charged, but she had been marked in public, where charges almost did not matter.

She watched floor numbers descend and thought of the green notebook wrapped in dish towels behind the loose panel under her kitchen sink. It wasn't in this office, and it wasn't in this raid. For now, that bought her a breath.

Outside, monsoon heat slapped her when the lobby doors opened. Diesel rattled in her sternum, tires hissed over wet asphalt, and fryer exhaust from the chicken shop pasted sweet grease onto her tongue. She was put in the back of a gray van with no visible logo. The bench seat had a tear that pinched the back of her thigh through thin fabric.

Captain Shin climbed in across from her and shut the door.

"Am I being arrested?" Ina asked.

"You are being transported for questioning under active warrant authority."

"That's not an answer."

"It is the answer you get right now."

The van pulled into traffic. Ina counted bridges without meaning to, an old commuting habit that had survived years of deadline panic. She watched scooters weave between lanes, watched a bus advertisement for a cosmetic surgery clinic slide past in pink script, watched the city continue like nothing had happened to her life in the last thirty minutes.

Her phone remained in Captain Shin's lap, facedown, silent. Whoever she might have called was already calling someone else. Kwon, probably, with that soft patient voice he used when declaring somebody expendable.

The intelligence building had no sign at street level, only mirrored windows and guards whose suits fit too well for private security. They took Ina through a side entrance where fluorescent glare bounced off waxed tile and the vents pushed coolant-cold air through her damp shirt. Her bag, laptop, and shoelaces went into plastic bins. Her bra underwire set off the scanner; the female guard wanded her chest and hips with bored efficiency while Ina stared at a poster about anonymous corruption hotlines.

At intake they rolled black ink across her fingertips one by one. Her right index finger left a darker print than the others because of the marker stain. The clerk noticed and gave a brief, tired smile.

"Writer," he said.

"Subtitle editor," Ina answered.

He stamped a form and did not smile again.

They put her in a holding room with no clock, two plastic chairs bolted to the floor, and a vent that breathed winter air into her damp blouse until her nipples ached. A paper cup sat upside down on the table. She flipped it over and found lipstick marks on the rim from whoever had been there before.

Minutes or an hour later, the door opened. A man in a government-issue navy suit entered carrying a file thick enough to make a point by itself. He introduced himself as Prosecutor Im Seung-ho from the national security division. His tie was perfectly centered, but one sleeve cuff had a coffee ring the shape of a coin. Behind him came Captain Shin with a recorder and another officer Ina had not seen before, broad-shouldered and balding with runner's calves visible above expensive loafers.

They sat opposite her. The recorder beeped.

Prosecutor Im folded his hands. "For the record, state your full name and current occupation."

Ink still tacky at the edge of her cuticles, Ina pressed her fingertips to the table so they would not slide.

"Seo Ina. Subtitle editor and script consultant."

"For which entities?"

Cool air from the vent crawled under her collar. She gave him the narrowest true answer she could.

"Multiple."

"List them."

She ran the names by legal blast radius and chose the order that bought her seconds.

"MiraeWave, freelance packages, sometimes direct producers."

He opened the top folder and slid a document across the table. Her signature stared back at her from the last page, sharp and stupid.

"Did you sign this agreement?"

Her signature looked confident in a way she had not felt for months.

"Yes."

"Say the entity name aloud."

She wanted to refuse on principle. Principle did not pay legal fees.

"Restoration Cultural Council," she said.

Im nodded, satisfied that she had finally stopped wasting his time.

"This agreement includes confidentiality obligations concerning cross-border media routing and historical narrative operations. It includes compensation beyond declared salary. It also references courier relationships. Do you deny any of that?"

"I deny espionage."

"That is not the same sentence."

The balding officer cracked his knuckles once, then placed both hands flat on the table, forcing stillness over them.

Im continued. "Under the National Security Act and related statutes, undeclared communication with actors facilitating prohibited Northbound transmission can be charged as espionage support, conspiracy, and material aid. Maximum exposure includes custodial sentence and financial seizure. Your bank accounts can be frozen today. Your father's lease guarantor can be audited before dinner."

He let the words sit. He did not raise his voice. He did not need to.

Ina pictured her father's cigarette tin in the kitchen drawer with five thousand-won notes folded inside for rent shortfalls. She pictured the hospital bill envelope still tucked under his microwave because neither of them could pretend it had been paid if she filed it away.

"You called this a questioning," she said. "You came in with punishment already drafted."

"We came in with leverage and options. Which one applies depends on you."

Captain Shin slid a tablet across the table and tapped play. Security footage from the studio showed Ina opening her drawer. Freeze frame: contract pages in her hand. Another clip showed her in a corridor with Kwon three weeks earlier, timestamped, no audio. A still image of a cash transfer ledger followed, with her name in the recipient field and monthly entries of `7,000,000`.

Im said, "Your financial pattern changed in week two of this contract. You began servicing large medical debt more consistently. You also paid down rent arrears linked to your father through intermediary transfers. That money did not come from subtitle residuals."

His tone made debt sound like character evidence.

Ina leaned back as far as the bolted chair allowed. Cold plastic pressed between her shoulder blades.

"I worked," she said. "I got paid. That's not illegal."

"The work may be illegal."

"Then charge me or stop pretending this is a negotiation."

A tiny flicker crossed Im's face, not anger exactly, more like approval that she had arrived where he wanted her.

"Fine," he said. "It is a negotiation."

He pushed a second folder toward her. This one was thinner, two pages clipped together with a yellow tab.

"Controlled Cooperation Memorandum," he said. "You provide prospective operational reporting on designated contacts, communications, and deliverables linked to this council structure. You do not leave the country. You do not delete records. You attend scheduled debriefings. In return, the prosecution recommendation remains suspended while compliance is active."

"Suspended is not immunity."

"Correct."

"So I confess into your file and hope you feel generous later."

"You cooperate and avoid immediate detention."

The balding officer finally spoke, voice rough from either smoking or too many dawn runs. "If you are detained now, your father finds out from TV before you get a phone call."

There it was, the softest knife.

Ina rubbed the burn scar on her left wrist with her thumb until the skin flushed pink. She had gotten that scar at nineteen from a rice cooker lid and had hidden it under bracelets through her twenties. Today it looked like somebody else's timeline written onto her body.

"I want terms," she said.

Im's brows lifted. "You can ask. That is not the same as getting them."

"Then lock me up and explain to your minister why this becomes a courtroom in election season. You don't want open filings naming media companies and intelligence handling in the same week." She leaned forward. "You want a quiet channel. So yes, I've got terms."

Captain Shin watched her with expressionless attention, pen ready.

Im made a small circling gesture with one finger. Continue.

"No contact with my father through his landlord, his boss, or fake debt collectors."

"No manufactured crime offers either. Don't run a loyalty trap and call it routine."

"Counsel at every recorded statement. If you're recording me, my lawyer's there."

"Forty-eight hours' notice for summons unless there's an immediate safety event."

"Written scope starts with council operations and direct channels, and I'd prefer you leave my family, friends, and private life out of it. No, not prefer. Keep them out unless a judge signs off."

The balding officer let out a short laugh. "You think you get to hand us policy."

"I think you've got one witness who can still map this network without lighting up the evening news."

Im turned one page in the folder, slow and deliberate. "Your forty-eight-hour notice request for summons is denied. We call, you answer. Your blanket family firewall is denied. If there is evidence of obstruction, we can still reach your father."

Ina held his stare. "Then we don't have a deal."

"We do if you listen to the language," Im said. He nodded at Shin. "Write this: no landlord or employer pressure on immediate family without division-chief authorization tied to documented obstruction risk. Counsel notice for recorded statements, except same-day exigent debriefs. Scope begins with council operations and can expand only on written probable-cause addendum."

He let the pen scratches fill the room, then looked back at Ina. "Cost: before release, you give us one immediate deliverable. A written map of active council channels and the next seventy-two-hour contact windows."

The demand landed behind her ribs like another camera flash.

"No civilians. No family names."

"One verifiable operative identity by tomorrow's 08:00 debrief," Im said. "That is non-negotiable."

Ina swallowed. "Fine. My concession is one current operative identity tied to an active channel, delivered at 08:00. Nothing peripheral."

Im considered, then shut the folder. "Accepted. Travel restriction remains. Device monitoring remains. Unscheduled summons remains."

"I expected that."

"You do not discuss this arrangement with unauthorized persons."

Ina almost laughed. "Who counts as authorized?"

"Us. Your lawyer."

"And when the council asks why I'm still outside a cell?"

Im closed the folder with precise fingers. "You are a writer. Be creative."

The anger hit hot and sudden, not clean. She saw herself throwing the recorder at his face and hearing the satisfying crack of plastic. Instead she pressed both palms against the table until the tendons in her hands stood out like cords.

"Before I sign," she said, "I want one call."

"To counsel," Im said.

"To Director Kwon. On speaker. Here."

Captain Shin looked at Im. Im considered, then nodded once.

Shin retrieved Ina's phone from an evidence pouch, unlocked it with Ina's passcode, and set it between them. Ina dialed Kwon's known number. It rang twice.

He answered with the smooth courtesy of a man who never rushed anyone unless he wanted them to feel rushed.

"Ina-ssi," he said. "Are you safe?"

Safe. She could have screamed.

"I'm at NIS intake," she said. "They seized the studio and contract pages. I need confirmation of legal support from the council."

A pause, short but unmistakable.

"I am very sorry," Kwon said. "You understand our institution has no formal legal presence. We are an advisory network. Any consulting agreements were individual and exploratory."

Im watched Ina without blinking.

"Exploratory?" Ina said. "You wired me seven million won a month for six weeks. You gave deliverables and deadlines."

"I offered patronage for cultural work. Nothing more. I cannot interfere in a national investigation." Kwon's voice warmed into the practiced tone he used to console younger colleagues after a bad review. "Cooperate where reasonable. Preserve your future. We will revisit collaboration once the weather changes."

The weather changes. He had used that phrase last week in the Seongdong print-shop, staring at Minjae's detention list like rainfall totals.

"You are leaving me out here," Ina said.

"I am preserving what remains possible." He lowered his voice. "Do not turn temporary pressure into permanent ruin."

The line clicked dead.

Nobody in the room moved for a second. Then Im held out a pen.

"Your legal support seems limited," he said.

Ina signed.

Her hand shook halfway through the surname, and she had to rewrite the final stroke. Captain Shin initialed each page, then slid a separate form under Ina's hand.

"Device compliance," Shin said. "We install monitoring software on your primary phone before release. If that phone goes dark for more than four hours without pre-clearance, it is a violation. You carry it powered on."

"I need it for work anyway," Ina said.

"Work has changed."

The balding officer stood and collected the folders. As he turned, Ina noticed neon green thread peeking from the heel of one sock where it had been darned by hand. Such a tiny domestic repair under the polish of a state uniform that it made her stomach twist.

They moved her to a different room to process the paperwork. The technician who installed the monitoring app wore cartoon dog stickers on his ID card and apologized before asking for her unlock code. The courtesy did nothing to clean the fingerprint grease from coercion.

He handed the phone back with a receipt.

"You'll get check-in messages from an encrypted channel," he said. "Do not ignore them."

"And if I do?"

"Then they come get you again."

A clerk returned her shoelaces in a clear bag and pointed to a bench. She sat to thread them through eyelets with fingers numbed by air conditioning. It took three tries to make a knot. The overhead lights buzzed loud enough to feel against her teeth.

When they finally released her, evening had already pushed the sky into dirty violet over the river. Rain had started again, thin needling rain that turned every streetlight into a white smear. Two camera crews waited outside the gate with her release timing down to the minute.

"Seo Ina!"

"Are you cooperating with investigators?"

"Did you take foreign money?"

"Are you tied to anti-state propaganda production?"

The first microphone hit her shoulder hard enough to spin her half a step. She did not speak. Captain Shin had vanished back through the security door before the first question landed, and nobody from the council was there to flank her, nobody from MiraeWave either. Just lenses, rain, and a freelancer with nicotine-yellow fingernails yelling for a quote like she was a vending machine that had finally opened.

Ina lowered her head and walked. The wet pavement sucked at her shoes. A scooter splashed gutter water over her calves at the curb, cold and greasy. She kept moving toward the subway entrance until the reporters peeled off to chase a ministerial sedan turning into the block.

Downstairs, fluorescent light flattened every face to the same tired color. Commuters pretended not to stare while staring anyway. Ina bought a transit card top-up from the machine and saw her own name trending on the news bar above the ticket gates.

`Writer Seo linked to covert influence probe.`

`Council source denies formal affiliation.`

The second line was the one that landed. Denies formal affiliation. A clean sentence designed to leave blood outside the grammar.

She boarded Line 6 and stood by the door because sitting felt like surrender. At Hapjeong a boy in a school blazer whispered her name to his friend and held up his phone screen with her intake photo already clipped and meme-captioned. Someone had added devil horns. Someone else had written `Script traitor` in neon font.

By the time she reached Mapo, her messages were a thicket of unknown numbers and silence from everyone who mattered. MiraeWave HR had sent one email: `Your building access is temporarily suspended pending internal review.` The sender line read `HR Operations`, and the body ended with policy links rounded into corporate comfort.

Her father called once and hung up before she answered. Then he sent a text in two lines:

`Saw TV.`
`Come by if you can.`

She typed `Later tonight` and did not send it. If she went to him now with cameras still sniffing, she would take heat to his door. If she did not go, he would drink and pretend he had not waited.

She exited onto a side street behind the convenience store where the owner kept broken umbrellas in a plastic bucket for anyone who asked. Brine from kimchi tubs slicked the alley concrete, and rain had turned abandoned cardboard into pulpy mats against the wall. Her shoulder bag strap had rubbed a raw line across her neck.

A voice came from the shadow beside the stairwell.

"You walk like somebody's counting your steps."

Minjae stepped into the light, hood up, rain darkening the seams of his jacket. He held two canned coffees and a folded black umbrella that looked too small for either of them. His left hand worried the paper label off one can in thin spirals, a habit she had seen before in server rooms when he was pretending not to be stressed.

Ina stopped three paces away. "How long have you been there?"

"Since before the first camera crew gave up." He handed her a can. "You looked worse on TV."

She snorted once despite herself. The can was cold enough to sting her palm.

"They put you on TV before processing even finished," Minjae said. "That means somebody wanted the image out fast."

"No shit."

"You tailed?"

"Probably."

He nodded toward the crosswalk where a dark sedan idled half a block away with wipers sweeping even though the rain had thinned. "Definitely. Two-man rotation, same car for forty minutes. Amateur by choice. They want you to see them."

Ina took a sip of coffee. Bitter, sweet, metallic from the can rim. "NIS offered me a choice between cooperation and prison theater."

"And?"

"And I'm standing in the rain drinking convenience-store sugar with you, so guess."

Minjae looked at her for a long second, jaw tight. "How deep?"

She shook her head. "Not here."

"Then move." He tipped his chin toward the convenience store. "Talk about groceries and keep your primary phone lit."

He stepped off the curb first, close enough to look like a boyfriend walking her home, not a courier link. They crossed in front of the sedan without turning their heads. Inside the convenience store, Ina grabbed a pack of crackers she did not want and drifted toward the refrigerator wall. In the mirrored glass she saw one man leave the sedan, enter, pretend to read lottery posters, then walk back out without buying anything.

Minjae paid cash for gum, nodded once, and kept walking toward the back. She followed him through the side service door into a loading lane boxed in by compressor cages and wet cardboard. Fan noise chewed up the edges of every word.

"Now," he said.

"You called Kwon?"

"In front of them. He called me exploratory patronage and wished me good weather."

Minjae swore softly, not at her, at the architecture of it.

"He'll feed your name to three factions by midnight," he said. "One to keep them calm, one to flush anyone still talking to you, one in case you survive and become useful again."

"You sound impressed."

"I sound like I've been billed by these people for years." He reached into his jacket and pulled out a cheap black burner wrapped in a pharmacy receipt. "Take this. Battery separate. Don't power it anywhere near your apartment or your father's place."

Ina stared at the phone. "You think I can still run routes after this?"

"I think you can still choose when you move. But we don't say movement details out here." He pressed the burner into her palm. "If they squeeze you hard, send one blank ping from this after midnight from someplace crowded. I'll answer from a clean line with code only."

Ina gripped the burner harder. "I'm not running tonight. If I disappear, they hit my father first. I stay visible until I can move him."

A delivery motorcycle roared through the lane and rattled the metal cages. When it passed, rain ticked against the awning in a thin, regular pattern like fingers on a desk.

Ina slid the burner into the inside pocket of her bag, separate from her monitored phone. Double life in two rectangles of plastic.

"If I talk to you," she said, "you get pulled into this faster."

"Already in it." Minjae peeled the last strip of label from his can and let it stick to his thumb. "The question is whether you want a channel you can trust one percent or zero."

She almost said she didn't trust him at all. What came out was different.

"I need one channel," she said.

"Then use it clean." He lowered his voice. "Don't send me names by text. Don't repeat meeting addresses where your primary phone can hear it. If they make you wear audio, scratch your wrist twice before you ask anything operational. If the cadence feels wrong, I don't answer first contact."

"What phrase gets me through?"

"I'll send it from a clean line tomorrow."

"Tomorrow might be late."

"Then tonight you keep breathing and do nothing heroic."

He said it like an order, but his eyes cut briefly to the bruise forming on her shoulder where the microphone had hit and then away again, the closest he got to gentleness in public.

Ina leaned against the damp brick wall and closed her eyes for a second. Inside her head the day replayed in sharp clips: contract pouch sealed at 12:17, Kwon's voice saying `advisory network`, the pen dragging when her hand shook. She felt very awake and very far from her own body; she'd been cast to play herself and still had not learned the lines.

"They're going to ask for names," she said.

"Give them structure before you give them flesh," Minjae said. "Describe process, not people, until you can't. Buy time."

"Time for what?"

"For leverage to exist somewhere besides their file cabinet."

A window above them slid open and an elderly woman leaned out to shake a mop head into the alley. Gray water spattered near Minjae's shoes. She glanced at them, decided they were not worth a comment, and shut the window.

Ina laughed once, short and cracked. "That's Seoul's blessing, I guess."

"Could be worse. She could've recognized you and asked for a selfie."

"Give it ten minutes."

Minjae checked the street again. The sedan had moved closer, now parked beside a fire hydrant with hazards off. He flattened his empty can against the wall with his palm until the metal popped.

"I need to go first," he said. "If we split with me behind you, they'll tag me too soon."

"You think they haven't already?"

"I think they haven't decided my price yet." He pulled his hood tighter. "Ina. One more thing."

She waited.

"Whatever you signed in there, assume Kwon knows by morning. Maybe by now. He has ears in every office where coffee goes stale under patriotism posters." Minjae's mouth twitched without humor. "Don't confuse silence with safety."

She nodded.

"And don't go to your father tonight if cameras are still parked." He stepped backward toward the alley mouth. "Buy him groceries online so he stays in. Make it look routine."

Practical, unsentimental, exactly the kind of care he could afford to show.

"You're giving me domestic advice now?" she asked.

"I'm giving you survival advice with side dishes."

He turned and was gone before she could answer, swallowed by umbrellas on the main road.

Ina stood alone with the small black burner pressing against her ribs through the bag lining and the monitored phone vibrating every thirty seconds with notifications she did not open. Above the convenience store, the digital billboard switched from cosmetics to a scrolling news feed. Her name appeared again, then disappeared beneath weather alerts for heavy rain in northern provinces.

She took out her primary phone and saw the first encrypted check-in from the channel NIS had installed.

`Confirm location.`

Another message followed immediately.

`Tomorrow 08:00. Debrief Room 3B. Bring full contact list.`

The message came stripped to command verbs and a room number. The threat was already lodged in her wrists from the ink table and in her jaw from clenching through paperwork.

Ina typed back with thumbs still damp from rain.

`Received.`

She opened a grocery app and filled a cart for her father: instant rice, cuttlefish banchan he pretended not to like, nicotine gum he would mock and use anyway, low-sodium soup packets for the blood pressure he ignored. At checkout she hesitated over payment and chose the old card she had used before Kwon's money ever touched her account, because chronology might still matter to anyone auditing intent.

When the order confirmed, she started up the stairs to her apartment. Halfway to the landing she heard footsteps pause below and then continue after a deliberate count of three. Tail maintained.

Inside, she locked the door, turned off all lights, and stood in the dark kitchen listening to the refrigerator motor kick on and off. The loose panel under the sink waited where she had left it. She crouched, pulled it free, and touched the dish towels wrapped around the green notebook without unwrapping it. Cloth damp at the edge from trapped humidity, paper dry inside. Still there.

She replaced the panel and sat on the floor with her back against the cabinet. Rain drummed the window grille. Somewhere in the building a child practiced piano scales badly and stubbornly, repeating the same four notes until they held.

On the table above her, the monitored phone lit again.

`Reminder: disclose unauthorized contacts immediately.`

In her bag, powered off, the burner waited like a second pulse.

Ina closed her eyes and counted the space between raindrops hitting the metal sill, then the space between her own breaths, then the narrowing corridor of choices that still belonged to her. She had avoided the cell for one day. Everyone she loved still sat exposed, and her own safety felt rented by the hour. But she had a channel, a lie she could keep alive for at least another morning, and enough fear left to stay precise.

Outside, tires hissed through standing water. Inside, the fridge motor clicked off and the apartment went very quiet, the kind of quiet that made every coming knock sound inevitable. Before midnight, Ina left the monitored phone lit on the kitchen table, pocketed the burner, and stepped back into the rain.
