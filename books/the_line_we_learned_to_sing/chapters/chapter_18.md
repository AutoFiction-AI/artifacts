# Chapter 18

The predawn reminder buzzed against Ina's hip for the third time as she reached the Seongdong print shop. The sleet had turned the alley into a strip of gray glass and soaked through the canvas knees of her pants. The old signboard still said `Hanil Offset` in flaking blue paint, but someone had zip-tied a white plastic banner over the doorway: OPEN EXCHANGE BETA / PUBLIC ACCESS / SOURCE LOGS VISIBLE.

The banner had been printed with a borrowed church machine that left a faint vertical line through every vowel. She liked that defect. It made the thing look made by people with rent due.

Inside, the room held the same smells it had held when Kwon ran meetings there: wet wool, stale coffee, hot fan dust, ink. The blocked windows were gone now. They had dragged the plywood down two weeks ago and found a courtyard full of mossy bricks and a bent laundry pole. Morning light came in cold and honest and showed every scuff mark on the concrete floor.

Six folding tables stood in a U shape around extension cords and three consumer routers. A handwritten sign on the front table read NO CLOSED BRIEFINGS. NEXT TO EACH FILE: WHO SHOT IT, WHO CUT IT, WHO PAID FOR IT.

Ina set down two boxes of scanner glass and wiped her fingers on her coat. Her left wrist scar prickled in the damp. The donor spreadsheet sat open on the cheapest laptop they owned, and she already knew what had happened before she looked because nobody had bought fresh coffee filters.

Three red rows had appeared overnight.

`Miryang Civic Futures Foundation - KRW 90,000,000 pledged - WITHDRAWN`

`Haneul diaspora grant pool - KRW 40,000,000 pledged - ON HOLD`

`Anonymous legal-defense reserve - KRW 20,000,000 pledged - REVOKED`

A note from Miryang had arrived at 2:13 a.m.: after consultation with counsel, they could not be seen funding an entity under active national-security inquiry. The message had ended with sympathy and a request to reconnect after resolution, as if the country handed those out in envelopes.

Ina checked the account balance anyway. KRW 1,384,110. She could hear that number in machine sounds: one server fan and the old refrigerator in Hyejin's church office that clicked like bad teeth every ten minutes. One arrest would burn through legal fees before lunch.

She was rolling tape over a frayed Ethernet line when the back door kicked open and Seo Hyejin came in sideways, pushing a handcart stacked with catering pans.

"Don't touch the soup yet," Hyejin said before saying hello. "I put too much pepper because my hand slipped and I'm not apologizing for it. People lie less when they sweat."

She had her reading glasses on the silver chain around her neck and wore plastic rain covers over her loafers. In one coat pocket she kept peeled chestnuts in a folded paper cup, and every few minutes she popped one into her mouth and chewed while she read documents. Ina had seen her do that during church budget fights, funerals, and once while telling a landlord he could take his eviction threat and eat it in front of her.

"How bad?" Hyejin asked.

"Three pullouts. Big ones."

"Then we work with people who can't afford to pretend they're neutral."

She parked the handcart, patted Ina's cheek with a cold thumb, and moved straight to the whiteboard where the access rules were listed. She crossed out `trusted partners` and wrote `everyone on camera, everyone in the log` in neat block letters.

Twelve people had arrived by ten-thirty for a launch that was supposed to have thirty-two. The missing ones sent reasons that all sounded like weather and carried the same scared weight.

Six empty chairs stared from the wall like a second attendance sheet. Ina felt the old detention-room chill climb her spine: turnout dropped first, raids came after.

People who had spent a year trying to bury one another still came.

Park Sunwoo arrived first, soaked through his union parka, carrying two hard drives in a zip bag with baby stickers on the corners. At the civic hearing last month he had stood on the courthouse steps and called Ina a broker in whistleblower clothing. Today he kept worrying the cracked skin on his knuckles with his thumbnail and asked if anyone had a clean USB cable because he did not trust the one in his pocket.

"My daughter stuck those stickers on," he said when Ina looked at the hard drives. "She said plain black looked like police equipment."

He set them down and added, almost defensive, "This is detention-family interview footage. Not mine. I only logged it."

Then Lee Minsu appeared, fifteen minutes late, carrying an aluminum camera case and a packet of nicotine gum. During Council days he had run metadata cleaning in Kwon's back room and had refused to make eye contact with anybody lower than director rank. He still wore cuff links out of habit even though his shirt cuffs were frayed.

"Before I hand this over," Lee said, tapping the case, "I need source-chain format fixed. If you let amateurs rename evidence, prosecutors will shred it in thirty seconds."

Sunwoo laughed once through his nose. "Prosecutors are going to shred it anyway."

"They can try," Lee said. "I'm not giving them avoidable help."

Ina watched them square up across the front table, old contempt waking by reflex in both men. One had spent months calling the other a state errand boy. The other had spent months pretending people like Sunwoo were noise.

Now they were the same kind of unemployed.

"No one gets sentimental points for being here," Ina said. "If you brought material, it goes in the public queue with provenance. If your file could expose a minor or someone still inside a detention zone, we redact in the open and we log who asked for what. If you need secrecy to feel important, leave now."

Lee looked at her for a long second, then opened the camera case.

Inside were labeled SSDs, each wrapped in antistatic sleeves with dates in precise handwriting.

"Council internal edits," he said. "Raw and final. You'll see exactly what got cut, by whom, with timestamps. I kept copies because I didn't trust our own archive unit."

Sunwoo pushed his hard drives forward. "Families wanted this released months ago. They said if I sat on it any longer, I could tell my kid I was preserving standards while people disappeared."

Router fans whined into a silence nobody wanted to break.

Park Ji-eun called in on speaker at eleven from a rental rehearsal room in Incheon. Someone had painted cartoon clouds on the studio wall behind her and a strip of kinesiology tape ran from the base of her left thumb to her wrist, bright pink against her skin.

"I can give you cleaned stems from the apology broadcast that the agency buried," she said. "Mic feed, in-ear feed, producer channel. You can hear exactly when they told me to cry."

She smiled because she always could, even after nights with no sleep. "Also, I'm teaching six middle-school kids who dance like caffeinated geese. If you need a chaos choir for the stream test, they're available and they'll demand snacks."

Hyejin leaned into the camera frame with a ladle in her hand. "Bring them. I made too much soup because your uncle pastor still thinks twelve people eat like pigeons."

Ji-eun's smile shifted into something less rehearsed. "I miss your soup, Auntie."

"Then don't miss your train."

The platform repository hit fifty-seven queued uploads by noon, with four arguments in progress.

The number made Ina's shoulders creep toward her ears. More files meant more truth and more ways to get somebody re-identified if one tired person clicked the wrong checkbox.

Argument one: whether all former Council material should carry warning labels for manipulation risk.

Argument two: whether warning labels could become another way of burying useful evidence.

Argument three: whether a truth commission subpoena counted as voluntary release.

Argument four: whether Ina herself should have admin privileges while indicted.

That last one got loud.

A graduate student with a shaved head and a notebook full of statutory citations said, "You're the reason this exists and also the reason people think this is controlled opposition. We can't pretend that conflict isn't there."

She had a chipped green tooth from a bike crash and she spoke in paragraphs, never glancing down, as if she had practiced honesty into a performance discipline.

Heat climbed Ina's neck. The student was right, and rightness never made the accusation lighter.

Ina nodded. "You're right. Put me on a two-key rule. No single-person deletions. I can upload only with co-sign."

"Who co-signs?"

"Not my family. Not Minjae. Pick someone who still thinks I'm dangerous."

Sunwoo gave a dry laugh. "That's my whole personality."

"Good," Ina said. "You get the second key until elections end. Take my admin token now."

The graduate student dragged a plastic evidence tray into the middle of the table. Ina unclipped the hardware key from her lanyard and dropped it into the tray while the student logged the handoff time in blue ink. Sunwoo took the key, clipped it to his parka zipper, and did not smile.

"Any release with your name waits on me," he said.

An alert flashed on Ina's monitor: `UPLOAD REQUEST / DETENTION VAN CLIP / POSSIBLE MINOR FACE MATCH.` She reached to quarantine and got blocked by a red box: `CO-SIGN REQUIRED.` Sunwoo leaned over, read the metadata, and hit HOLD before she could argue.

"Blur first, then publish," he said.

Another donor withdrew at one-fifteen. This one had already sent branded stickers. Hyejin peeled the sticker roll open and used them to seal soup containers.

"Their logo finally became useful," she said.

The brokerage turn came at two, when the room started to sag. People were hungry, wet, and tired of hearing themselves distrust each other with legal vocabulary.

Ina watched eyelids droop over keyboards and recognized the pre-collapse rhythm from strike tents and funeral kitchens: one more insult and half the room would walk. Every contested upload now bottlenecked through the second key on Sunwoo's zipper, including three files she wanted live before curfew.

Hyejin set down three steel trays of pepper soup and hit a spoon against a pot until everybody looked up.

"Eat first. Fight better after," she said. Then she pointed at Lee and Sunwoo with the same spoon. "You two. Bring your drives."

She marched them to a side table, opened her own cloth ledger bag, and took out a stack of photocopied pages bound with hair ties. Church account records. Relief reimbursements. Petty-cash vouchers. Names, dates, signatures.

Ina knew that bag. Hyejin had carried it to landlord meetings when Dohyun's rent went short. When the ledger came out, somebody was about to bleed in public.

"I kept these because everybody in this city lies about money with a straight face," she said. "You were both on opposite sides of my kitchen table for two years. You used to scream at each other while eating my radish kimchi and then ask for seconds. Don't tell me you can't sit and build a public index now."

Lee frowned. "Noona, this isn't the same scale."

"Scale isn't the point. Habit matters most. You both know how to track a number without flirting with it."

She turned to the room. "If you used to work for Kwon, you log what you touched. If you used to campaign against anybody who did, you log what you can't verify yet. We stop pretending purity is available. We settle for traceable."

Lee stared at the copied vouchers, jaw ticking. "Fine. First entry's mine. In March I stripped casualty tags from five Council exports after Director Kwon's office sent the order. My login signed every render."

He pulled a laptop toward him and typed into the open index field where everybody could see. "If I publish that, prosecutors can charge me with evidence tampering. They probably should."

Sunwoo cursed, then dragged one of his hard drives onto the table. "Put this with it. I sat on detention-family interviews for eighty-three days because I wanted a clean legal route and I was scared of burning my union local."

He looked around the room without blinking. "Release forms carry my legal name. If prosecutors call this cross-border coordination, my local loses subsidy contracts and my daughter gets my name on every hate board."

He signed the upload request anyway, pressing hard enough to rip the carbon copy underneath.

Nobody clapped. Nobody smiled. But people sat down with food and laptops and started to work side by side, shoulders still stiff.

Uploads came because Lee and Sunwoo had already paid in public. By late afternoon, once their downsides were named in the log, everyone else's purity speeches sounded expensive and fake.

A former agency paralegal sent scanned trainee debt riders and attached a voice memo in which she admitted she had hidden penalty clauses in plain language because she needed her brother's chemotherapy copays.

An ex-Council caption editor sent internal revision histories with comments that showed who requested euphemisms for casualties.

A neighborhood pastor from Uijeongbu sent three shoeboxes of letters from families split since the nineties, each envelope labeled in blue marker with permission status.

Nothing looked clean. That was the point.

Kang Minjae came in through the loading door at six carrying a weatherproof flight case and a paper cup of burned convenience-store coffee. He had a fresh scrape across one knuckle and the cuff of his black hoodie carried solder flux sharp enough to sting the back of Ina's throat. Somebody had shaved the back of his head too close and missed a line, leaving a pale zigzag above his collar.

The sight of him hit Ina in two directions at once: relief first, then the betrayal ache that still lived in her molars.

He set the flight case on the floor with careful hands, as if setting down a child who had finally fallen asleep.

"Microwave link backup," he said to no one in particular. "If the mountain repeater freezes, this gives us twelve minutes before full drop. Maybe fifteen if nobody breathes on it wrong."

He looked at Ina, then at the room, then back to Ina. "You running this or getting outvoted?"

"Both are true," she said.

He gave a short, tired smile. "Sounds healthy."

Sunwoo did not look up from his laptop. "Tell your guy no hidden tunnels tonight. Full mirror logs."

Minjae bent, plugged in his case, and answered without heat. "I know what transparency means."

"Do you?"

Shoulders squared across the tables, and conversation died mid-breath.

Ina stepped between them before the old script could restart. "No personal baiting. We have a stream in four hours and a legal observer riding with us. If anybody wants a fistfight, schedule it after the kids are home."

Minjae rolled a cable between his fingers, gaze fixed on the floor. "I didn't come here to win arguments."

Ina heard the unsaid part. I came because my sister is still gone and work is what I have left.

At six-fifty-four her lawyer forwarded the waiver that had eaten half the day: `SEOUL CENTRAL DISTRICT PROSECUTORS' OFFICE / EMERGENCY CIVIC PANEL JOINT MOVEMENT PERMIT 2027-12-19`. It authorized Ina's travel outside Seoul metro only between 19:00 and 06:30 for the Seongdong-to-Imjingak relay route, required panel escort and live location pings, and stated that passport seizure plus Monday reporting remained in force.

They loaded gear into two vans at seven-thirty for the DMZ relay site. A civic-panel liaison rode in front with a clipboard and a jaw that looked permanently locked. Behind him, Seoul choir kids in puffy jackets argued about instant noodles and traded throat lozenges. One boy had written all the day’s camera IDs on his forearm with marker so he would not forget where to look when the feed switched.

Wind hit them as soon as they stepped out at the relay compound. The place was concrete and steel and warning signs: RESTRICTED BAND / NO UNAUTHORIZED TRANSMISSION / REPORT ANOMALIES IMMEDIATELY. Wet rust and pine bark rode the air. Every exposed cable hummed.

Inside the control room, fluorescent lights buzzed over racks of patched hardware and a wall of monitors showing mirrored signals from Seoul, a church basement near Kaesong, and two proxy servers abroad. Because direct cross-border channels could still trigger prosecution under armistice law, everything had to route through legal gray corridors and latency buffers that added three to six seconds when the weather behaved. Tonight the weather did not behave.

Snow came in sideways around nine and rattled the metal siding like handfuls of dry rice.

Viewer count had climbed to one hundred and forty thousand by nine-forty across mirrored civic streams and fan channels. Comments scrolled too fast to read. Some sent heart emojis. Some sent death threats. Some demanded arrests in all caps.

A government notice hit the monitor at nine-fifty-two: transmission subject to immediate suspension if classified content appears or if cross-border coordination breaches emergency communications guidance.

The liaison from the civic panel cleared his throat. "You understand this warning is enforceable."

"I understand warnings," Ina said. "I grew up in them."

The Seoul children took their marks in a room that had once stored spare routers. They stood on taped Xs between coiled cables and borrowed acoustic panels. One girl had rabbit patches sewn onto both knees of her sweatpants, and she kept rubbing one ear lobe between thumb and forefinger when she got nervous. Another kid in oversized gloves whispered each lyric under his breath before singing it out loud, as if testing for trapdoors.

Across the feed, the Kaesong group appeared on a grainy camera in a church basement lit by two clip-on lamps. Their director wore a mustard cardigan with one elbow patched in denim and kept patting his shirt pocket to check if his tuning fork was still there.

Old enemies filled the back wall at separate stations: Sunwoo on moderation triage, Lee on source-chain overlays, a former military-band arranger checking pitch, two student volunteers tagging redactions live, Hyejin counting soup cups and extension outlets with equal seriousness.

No one pretended they trusted one another. They trusted the log window because it could not flatter.

The stream opened at ten-oh-three.

The first verse went clean. Children in Seoul and Kaesong traded lines about spring wind, market bells, and mothers waiting at doorways. Sound lag stretched call-and-response into a ragged braid, but viewers leaned in harder when they saw the lag because glitch felt like proof of non-scripted reality.

The second verse had a one-second drop and then recovered.

Five minutes later, at ten-oh-eight, the mountain repeater iced over.

Every screen stuttered. Video smeared into blocks. Audio clipped to a metallic buzz. The room lit red with packet-loss warnings.

"Link B is gone," Minjae said. "Switching to backup microwave. Hold Seoul on ambient mics only."

"Kaesong?" Ina asked.

"Delayed six seconds and climbing."

Outside, wind slammed the building hard enough to shake dust from the ceiling grates. Someone in the hallway cursed. The civic liaison stepped toward the master switch as if preparing to pull it.

"Don't kill it," Ina said without looking at him. "If you cut now, every rumor wins."

"If prohibited content hits," he said, "I've got to act."

"Then watch the same monitor I watch."

The banned lullaby verse was scheduled third.

It had been banned for decades in various forms because it named siblings separated by border and asked who profited from keeping the river unswum. Councils, agencies, broadcasters, and patriotic committees had all found different reasons to call it destabilizing. The words were simple enough for children. That was what frightened institutions.

Seoul got the cue at ten-ten. Kaesong froze on a frame of open mouths.

The kids in Ina's room looked at her instead of the camera. The girl with rabbit knees swallowed and whispered, "Do we stop?"

Ina heard her own voice come out steady. "You keep breathing, then you sing what you practiced."

Minjae's hands flew over the console, routing audio through a spare codec taped in place with blue painter's tape. "I can give them your click track," he said. "They will hear it late, but they will hear it."

"Do it."

A dry metronome pulse hit both channels: tick, tick, tick, stubborn as a cheap clock.

Seoul began the verse alone, thin voices first, then fuller as they found each other.

_When I was small, you tied my scarf twice_  
_and told me wind could not steal names._

Kaesong came in four beats late, the latency turning harmony into echo.

_When the river froze, we wrote on ice_  
_so spring would know where to return._

The lines collided, separated, collided again. It was messy, off by fractions, absolutely alive.

In the control room, nobody moved. Not even the liaison.

The third line landed, then the fourth. Every child held.

When the final word landed, comments exploded across the mirrored feeds in two scripts and three alphabets. Some were curses. Some were sobbing punctuation. Some were timestamps and requests for source files. One account posted, `my mother sang this while hiding the radio.`

Then the Kaesong feed went black.

A second later it returned with only audio, children laughing into the microphone because they had made it through.

Air rushed out of lungs all at once, like steam venting from a cracked pipe.

"Stream stays up for testimony segment," Ina said, because adrenaline needed a task or it would eat people alive.

The first testimony opened on a woman from Paju in a hospital corridor, jaw wired shut under a white mask and a yellow triage wristband still tight on her skin. She held up a release form with her son's name blacked out and spoke slowly so the words could get through the wire.

"They took him on Monday," she said. "Order said seventy-two hours. On Thursday they told me paperwork was delayed. On Saturday they gave him back with two teeth gone and zip-tie cuts that were still bleeding through gauze."

She lifted a plastic envelope toward the camera: ER receipt, dental estimate, transport-security invoice stamped PAID. "I paid 480,000 won to collect my own child. I sold my wedding ring in Paju market at midnight to do it. They still charged me for the van that beat him."

Ina's palm flattened on the console hard enough for her old wrist scar to blanch. She heard her own molars grind and forced herself to unclench before she cracked a tooth.

The woman kept going, voice catching but not breaking. "Sergeant Choi from Unit Nine told me to sign a blank statement if I wanted discharge before morning rounds. I signed. I want that signature in your archive next to his name."

The civic liaison stepped toward the switch panel. "Named active personnel triggers legal-delay protocol."

"Then run protocol, don't cut," Ina said. "Lee, push packet PAJU-07 with affidavit and hospital stamp to main feed now."

Lee's fingers blurred. A red `LEGAL DELAY 12s` bar appeared across every monitor while the source ledger expanded with checksum lines, docket number, and notarized statement images. Viewer count dropped by twelve thousand in ninety seconds, then stabilized.

A delivery driver came next, holding a summons up to his webcam. "I sang one verse on a union stream. They charged me under emergency-communications guidance, fined me three million won, and my company cut me before the hearing."

Under each clip, Lee pushed the supporting files into a scrolling source ledger with live checksum hashes while two student volunteers tagged chain-of-custody notes in real time.

They closed with no anthem at eleven.

Outside, snow had turned to cold rain. The parking lot reflected floodlights and barbed-wire shadows. Two police vans waited near the gate with engines idling.

The idling engines pulled a detention memory up Ina's spine anyway: vans, clipboards, the clean voice that said this part would go faster if she cooperated. She made herself loosen her jaw before she bit down on it.

Inside a narrow hallway where damp concrete sweated through the paint, Ina and Minjae stood across from each other with the stream noise still ringing in their ears.

He held out his phone. On the screen was a message from an unknown number: `route lead seen near Sinuiju checkpoint. female possible. unconfirmed.`

It might have been his sister.

His jaw worked once. "Could be bait. Could be her. Same format either way, same cheap panic."

The old instinct to comfort him rose and stalled. Comfort had been where both of them hid bad decisions.

"Then we run both assumptions until one survives," Ina said. "I know that sounds cruel. It's still the only way we don't miss her."

"I'm tired of building plans that feel like traps."

"Me too."

He put the phone down and looked straight at her, not sliding away this time. "If we're doing this again, we kill the shadow pipes. Full routing table, both sides. NIS pings you, I get the log that day. Broker pings me, you get it before I sleep. No more hero mode."

Relief pricked first. Then anger followed it, clean and useful. She could work with clean anger better than she had ever worked with hope.

Ina nodded once. "Yes. And if your sister reaches you, we freeze operations before either of us moves. That's not negotiable. You don't disappear for forty-eight hours and rename it protection."

His mouth twisted. "I hate that sentence. I also earned it."

"Release governance stays two-key," she said. "Nothing goes live on one signature. No solo edits. No private cleanup at two in the morning because one of us panicked."

"Harsh. Fair."

Her pulse kept skipping, body still stuck half in control-room emergency mode, but she had learned the hard way that blurred terms became wounds.

"Last term, and I'm done softening this: if either of us lies about mission-critical information, the work partnership is over. No fight. No punishment. Over."

He rubbed his scraped knuckle with his thumb. "One lie and we sever the line. Hard cut."

Rain thudded harder on the corrugated roof.

She took a breath. "Now the other part."

He exhaled through his nose. "Yeah. Say it."

"No sex as a ceasefire after fights," she said. "I'm not doing that anymore."

His gaze stayed on hers. "Good. It was fucking up both of us."

"If either of us wants touch, we ask plainly first. No guessing game. No using old habits as consent."

"I can ask."

"If we're working and one of us says stop, we stop. No proving loyalty with pain."

Minjae's eyes flicked shut for one second. "Stop means stop. I can do that."

Ina's hands were shaking now that the stream was over, not from fear exactly, from the after-vibration when your body realizes it can collapse but you still need it upright.

He saw it and did not reach for her.

"Can I touch you?" he asked.

The question hit the panic still rattling inside her and gave it edges she could hold.

"Yes," she said.

He stepped in slow, palms warm through her damp coat, and wrapped her until her forehead rested against the hollow of his throat. Rain and solder clung to him, along with the bitter coffee he kept drinking when sleep became a rumor.

She lifted his hoodie hem and ran her hand over the scarred ridge along his shoulder blade, the old line from hardware work and one bad border night he had never fully described. He flinched at first, then stayed still under her hand.

"Room three is empty," he said against her hair. "Generator noise is loud enough that nobody will hear anything except bad wiring."

"You just made that sound romantic."

"I've got range."

They went to room three, a maintenance cubicle with a narrow cot and a stack of folded emergency blankets. The fluorescent bulb flickered. Somebody had left a packet of barley candy on the windowsill and a screw sorted by size in a paper cup.

Ina locked the door, then unlocked it again and left it that way because locked doors still made her skin go cold after Hongje. Minjae watched her do it and did not comment.

He took off her wet coat and laid it over the back of a chair. She unzipped his hoodie, pushed it from his shoulders, and kissed the scrape on his knuckle before she kissed his mouth. He made a rough sound she felt in her own teeth.

"Tell me if you want me to stop," she said.

"I will. You tell me too."

"I will."

They moved carefully at first, then less carefully, all the control room static still in their blood. She sat on the edge of the cot and pulled him between her knees, hands under his shirt, palms memorizing callus and heat. He kissed the scar on her left wrist, then her throat, then the place beneath her ear where she always held tension like a secret.

When he reached for her belt, he looked up and waited. She nodded once. "Yes."

The cot squealed with every shift. She laughed into his shoulder and bit him when he said, "I told you the wiring would be the loud part."

It was not clean or choreographed. His knee knocked the metal frame hard enough to leave a bruise. Her hair stuck to her lip gloss and she cursed when it got in her mouth. He kissed her through it. When she got too close to panic at the sudden slam of wind against the wall, he held her face in both hands and said her name until the room came back into focus.

After, they lay tangled in an emergency blanket that smelled faintly of plastic and old smoke. Rain ticked across the sill. Somewhere down the hall Hyejin laughed at somebody for trying to pour soup one-handed while checking signal logs.

Minjae stared at the ceiling for a while before speaking.

"I still don't know if my sister is alive," he said.

"I know."

"I keep thinking if I find her, I've got to choose between pulling her out and staying in this."

Ina turned onto one elbow. "If we built this right, it's not only us. That's the whole point of open ownership. You should be able to leave for a month and it still runs."

"That sounds like hope." He gave a tired half-smile. "I'm suspicious of hope."

"Then call it load balancing."

He laughed, low and surprised. "That I trust."

Near midnight they went back to the control room. Most kids were asleep on folded coats while volunteers cleaned cables and labeled drives. Sunwoo sat cross-legged by a power strip, eating cold pepper soup with a plastic spoon and arguing online with a stranger about checksum standards. Lee had fallen asleep upright in a chair, nicotine gum still in one cheek, one hand resting on a stack of drives as if someone might steal them in his dreams.

Hyejin stood at the sink scrubbing pots with arthritic fingers gone red at the knuckles. She did not look up when Ina approached.

"How many uploads?" Ina asked.

"Two hundred and eleven complete, thirty-four pending, six rejected for no source chain. We keep the rejects visible with reason notes."

"Any shutdown notice?"

"One warning. No seizure yet."

Hyejin rinsed a pot and set it upside down to dry. "Also, your father called me three times because he saw your face on television and assumed jail vans were coming. I told him if jail vans were coming, I would hear them before he did."

Ina smiled despite herself. "Thank you."

"Don't thank me. Pay your share for the pepper."

Civic-stream analytics stabilized around one-thirty: viewership had dipped after the outage, then climbed higher once clips of the glitched lullaby spread. Not viral in the old engineered sense. Sticky in a different way. People kept posting timestamps and line references, asking for full source packs, making their own mirrors.

Former rivals stayed to write metadata together because none of them trusted any one person to summarize what had happened.

It was not resolution. It was labor.

Near dawn, Ina stepped outside with Minjae to the fence line where the relay compound met a strip of muddy grass. The rain had stopped. Fog hung low over the dark ridge beyond the wire. On the other side of the valley, a loudspeaker crackled to life with a military weather bulletin and a warning about unauthorized broadcasts. The voice was flat, practiced, almost bored.

Minjae handed Ina a paper cup of vending-machine tea that tasted like hot metal. She held it with both hands and watched steam ghost into the cold.

Her phone buzzed.

A court notice from her lawyer: prosecutors had filed to tighten her existing Seoul-only order with a midnight curfew and ankle-monitor ping checks pending trial.

It was no surprise. Her gut still clenched hard enough to turn the hot tea metallic on her tongue.

She showed Minjae. He read it, jaw tightening, then gave the phone back.

"We expected this," he said.

"Expected doesn't mean easy."

"I didn't say easy."

They stood shoulder to shoulder in silence while the relay tower blinked red above them. Behind the control-room wall, somebody replayed the choir clip to check phase drift, and the children's voices floated out in broken intervals, still slightly out of sync, still carrying.

Ina thought of the old years when one office owned every copy and called that ownership stability. She thought of files hidden in rice bins, dead drops in hymnbooks, lovers turning on each other because the structure rewarded betrayal. She thought of how thin this new thing was: an overworked platform with unstable funding, public logs, and people who barely trusted each other enough to share a table.

Thin was not nothing.

Minjae lifted the edge of his coat and draped it around both of them against the morning cold. It was an awkward fit. Her shoulder did not quite fit under his arm with the tea cup in the way. They adjusted and stayed there anyway.

The first commuter train crossed the bridge south of the compound at six-oh-five, a low iron tremor through the fog. The day started whether or not they were ready.

Ina leaned into him, feeling the rough seam of his hoodie against her cheek, and listened as the children reached the banned line again on loop, voices colliding across delay and weather and law, refusing clean harmony, refusing silence.
