# Chapter 10

Seoul Station smelled like wet nylon and fried squid, the air still thick with tear gas residue from the stadium roads. Ina kept her cap brim low and her mask on, but it did not matter. Every phone around her was tilted up, every screen showing some version of the same freeze-frame: Ji-eun on the Jamsil stage with her mouth open and the hacked lyric line burning across the screen behind her like a threat.

Ji-eun stood half a step behind Ina, hood up, left hand shaking hard enough to make the zipper teeth chatter. She looked like any exhausted twenty-something after a bad night out until someone held a camera too long. Then the trained posture snapped into place by reflex, shoulders square, chin lowered at a practiced angle, even while her fingers kept stuttering.

The departures board flipped to BUSAN, 06:03 KTX. Ina had bought the tickets with an old corporate card Minjae had told her to burn afterward. She had cut it in half in the station bathroom and dropped each half into different bins, because paranoia had become muscle memory.

Her phone lit again.

DIRECTOR KWON: Report to Seongdong at 07:30. Do not move the asset without clearance.

A second message followed before she could clear the preview.

UNKNOWN NUMBER: Agency counsel requests immediate stabilization protocol. Sedation authorized. Prevent unscripted appearance.

Ina locked the screen without replying. The word asset sat in her head like grit under a contact lens. She had pulled Ji-eun out of a tunnel under Jamsil two hours earlier while a handler in a velvet blazer shouted for security and waved a syringe case like it proved professionalism.

"You're reading it again," Ji-eun said.

Ina turned. "Reading what."

"Your murder face. Left eyebrow, tiny twitch." Ji-eun made a little scratching motion over her own brow. "You only do it when somebody older and richer is giving you instructions."

The line was so dry it took Ina a second to catch it. Then, against her own will, she barked a laugh. Ji-eun heard it and gave her a crooked bow from the waist, as if she had just landed a joke on a variety show.

"There she is," Ji-eun said. "Writer Seo has feelings."

"Don't get used to it."

"I won't. You've been emotionally constipated since rehearsal week."

A man in line in front of them glanced back, annoyed, then looked again and did the tiny widening of his eyes people got when recognition and gossip touched in the same second. Ina shifted her body between him and Ji-eun. The gate opened. They moved.

The platform wind shoved diesel and rainwater up from the tracks. Above them, beyond the station roofline, a drone hummed, dipped, and steadied. News channel? Tabloid freelancer? It did not matter. Somebody had already guessed they would run south.

Inside the train, Ina chose the window seat and put Ji-eun inboard so she could not bolt on impulse when panic spiked. Ji-eun accepted the choreography without comment. She folded herself down and wrapped both hands around her phone, screen dark, thumb rubbing the corner where the front glass had cracked months ago.

The train lurched out of Seoul with the low metal groan Ina always felt in her ribs before she heard it fully. Apartment towers slid by, then warehouses, then the gray curve of river water. Her phone buzzed against her thigh every two minutes, a trapped insect.

She did not check it until they were past Daejeon.

There were fourteen new messages. Six from numbers linked to Haneul Entertainment legal staff. Three from Kwon. One from a council operations group chat with no names, only role tags.

OPS-2: If she is unstable, medicate now. We cannot absorb another live event.

OPS-2: Confirm sedation.

KWON TAESIK: You are not authorized to manage this alone. This is becoming disobedience.

Minjae had sent one line at 06:41.

MJ: Exit Busan through platform service stairs. Not main concourse. Camera swarm outside gate B.

Ina typed back with one thumb so Ji-eun could not see.

INA: Got it. You safe?

The read receipt never came.

When she looked up, Ji-eun was watching her without blinking.

"Everybody wants me sleepy," Ji-eun said.

"They want you quiet."

"Same product, different packaging."

Ina let that sit. "Do you want to sleep?"

Ji-eun shrugged. "I want my skin to stop buzzing. I want to stop hearing that crowd go from singing to screaming in one breath. I want to peel this face off and put on one where nobody has had meetings about my obedience since I was fourteen. But no, I don't want to sleep."

"Then we don't sedate you."

Ji-eun's mouth twitched. "You said that like a doctor signing consent."

"I said it like someone who's done being told what your body is for."

They rode in silence for a while. A toddler three rows up dropped a plastic truck; it clattered down the aisle and hit Ina's shoe. She picked it up and handed it back. The boy stared at Ji-eun's hood with solemn suspicion, then decided they were boring and climbed onto his father's lap.

At Dongdaegu, a train attendant pushed a cart through first class. She had a strip of cartoon bandage tape over one knuckle and a keychain shaped like a persimmon hanging from her belt. "Kimbap, fishcake, coffee, cup ramyeon," she sang in a voice that had gone hoarse from repeating itself since dawn.

Ji-eun looked at the tray as if it were museum art. "Do you have the cursed station sandwich," she asked, slipping into bright public politeness on instinct, "the one with too much mayonnaise and one apologetic lettuce leaf?"

The attendant snorted before she could stop herself. "Sold out at Osong. Mayonnaise won."

"Then two fishcakes and one failure coffee," Ji-eun said. "Please."

Ina added triangular kimbap and hot corn tea. When the attendant moved on, Ji-eun leaned over the fold-down table and whispered, "If we die, I want it on record that I died from train coffee, not conspiracy."

"You'd haunt the complaint hotline."

"I would. Very professionally."

They ate with wooden picks and burned fingertips. The fishcake broth was too salty and too good, the kind of station food that tasted like survival because it asked nothing of you. Ji-eun chewed fast at first, then slower as her breathing evened. Color came back into her face in small, grudging patches.

"Tell me something useless," Ina said.

Ji-eun frowned. "What."

"Something not about last night, not about contracts, not about strategy. Give me one useless fact."

Ji-eun thought, then said, "I hate the word ethereal. Every makeup artist who says it means she's about to bleach me until I look dead."

Ina nodded solemnly. "I hate the phrase content vertical. Every executive who says it means my weekend's gone."

Ji-eun pointed her fishcake stick at Ina. "You still alphabetize your spice jars?"

"That was one time."

"You posted a photo."

"I was depressed."

Ji-eun cracked up, sudden and loud, then clapped a hand over her mouth and laughed into her sleeve until her shoulders shook. People glanced over. No one looked twice. This was what fame looked like when it was stripped of lighting: two women with bad coffee, laughing too hard in a quiet carriage because panic had cracked and steam had to go somewhere.

When her laughter tapered, Ji-eun wiped her eyes with the heel of her hand and said, "Okay. Useless fact number two. My stage name almost was Eunice."

Ina nearly choked on corn tea. "No."

"Agency wanted global softness. They said Eunice tested well with aunties in Manila and teen girls in Toronto."

"What happened."

"I told them it sounded like a church soloist who reports tax fraud."

Ina folded forward over the tiny table and laughed so hard the lid popped off her cup. Hot tea spilled onto the napkin and soaked through to her jeans. For ten seconds, maybe fifteen, they were just two women wrecked by a stupid made-up name. Not asset and handler. Not scapegoat and operative. Just exhausted bodies on a train, with Busan still ninety minutes away.

The calm held until Ji-eun's phone lit with a message preview from Manager Choi.

CHOI: Do not consume solid food before statement recording. Face swelling risk.

Ji-eun stared at the screen, then turned it toward Ina. "This is his first concern. My jawline."

She did not cry. Her voice went flat in the way that scared Ina more than shouting.

"I owe them three hundred twenty million won," Ji-eun said. "Did you know how they write it in the monthly statement? Trainee Recovery Account. Sounds like rehab. Sounds like healing."

Ina set down her chopsticks. "Walk me through it."

Ji-eun gave a hard little laugh. "You want the whole humiliation menu?"

"Yes."

She leaned back and spoke to the window, watching her own dim reflection skate over fields and warehouses.

"First year trainee, they booked everything to debt. Vocal lessons, dance classes, dorm rent, stylist, skin clinic, transportation, media training, emergency dentist because one choreographer elbowed me and cracked this tooth." She tapped a canine. "Then they added penalties. Missed weigh-in: fifty thousand. Late to call time: one hundred. Hair color correction because a director said my tone read cheap: three hundred. I was sixteen and paying debt for bleach I never asked for."

The train hummed under them, steady as a ventilator.

"When I debuted, they said congratulations and gave me a bouquet and a debt statement with more zeros than my family had ever seen. We were from a two-room apartment over a stationery store. My mother still cut discount coupons by hand. I thought debut meant paycheck. It meant interest."

She dragged her sleeve over her lip. "Every comeback had recoupables. Music video sets, backup dancers, wardrobe losses, ad campaign penalties if engagement dipped, even fan-meet snacks. One manager charged us for bottled water at rehearsal and put it under image maintenance. I used to hide one bottle in my locker so I would not cost more than my own labor."

Ina kept still. She did not reach across the table yet. Let the line run.

"The rider I slipped you with the ledger? It's real. If I breach behavioral clauses or damage brand confidence, they can call the three-twenty immediately. Plus civil damages. They don't have to prove actual loss first. They only have to say the words national trust erosion in front of a judge who's older than my father."

Her left hand had started trembling again. She pinned it between her knees.

"When the crowd turned last night, I did the media smile. You know the one." Ji-eun bared her teeth in a perfect, dead arc. "And I thought: if I disappear now, maybe they only sue my ghost."

Ina reached over then and held Ji-eun's wrist, not tight, just enough pressure to anchor. Ji-eun let her.

"No disappearing," Ina said.

"Then confession. I go live, I tell them everything. The riders, the scripts, the way they kept beta blockers in a makeup bag like mints. I tell them exactly who handed me what line and who told me to cry on count three."

"If you go live right now, they will frame you as unstable and drug-seeking before you finish the second sentence."

"They already are."

"Yes. And I'm trying to keep you alive long enough to beat them somewhere that lasts more than one trending cycle."

Ji-eun pulled her wrist back, not violently, but with decision. "I'm not a campaign asset either, Ina. I'm not your long game."

The hit landed clean because it was true enough to hurt.

"I know," Ina said. "I'm saying I need one day. Not forever. One day to get you off their legal leash before you light yourself on fire in public."

Ji-eun looked down at the table, at the fishcake stick wrapped in napkin. "One day is how this started, remember? One day for rehearsal, one day for a rewrite, one day for a statement. Ten years later I'm still in one day."

Before Ina could answer, her own phone rang. Not buzzed. Rang full volume because she had forgotten to mute calls during the sprint out of Seoul.

DIRECTOR KWON filled the screen.

Ji-eun watched her with an expression that was almost bored, almost daring. Ina let the call go to voicemail. Kwon called again immediately. She rejected it this time.

A message arrived within seconds, clipped and formal.

KWON TAESIK: If you continue to avoid command contact, I will interpret this as defection.

Ina typed without polishing.

INA: She's not being sedated. She's not recording today. I'll report when she's stable.

Three dots appeared, vanished, appeared again. Then:

KWON TAESIK: You are confusing sentiment with governance.

She did not answer. She turned her phone face-down and kept it there until Busan.

By the time they rolled into the station, rain had cleared and light was coming hard through breaks in cloud, bright enough to turn every wet surface into a mirror. The platform was already crowded with weekend travelers, office commuters, and three men with camera rigs pretending not to scan faces.

Ina and Ji-eun waited until most passengers had moved up the stairs. Then they went the other way, toward a service door marked STAFF ONLY and propped open with a crate of bottled water. A cleaner in yellow gloves smoked beside it, scrolling baseball highlights. She glanced up once, took in Ji-eun's hood and Ina's city shoes, and went back to her screen.

Minjae's directions were precise. Down one concrete flight, through a corridor that smelled of bleach and old fish, up again into a side lane behind a bakery where the shutters were still down. They walked fast, heads lowered, while scooters whined past carrying polystyrene boxes full of octopus and mackerel toward Jagalchi.

At the corner, a woman in rubber market boots lifted her hand twice from the driver's seat of a dented white van. She was in her fifties maybe, with a floral visor and a lipstick shade too bright for dawn. A rosary hung from her rearview mirror, wrapped around a tiny LED flashlight.

"You're late," she said as they climbed in. "I missed the first auction and my sister's going to accuse me of sabotage. Buckle."

"Ms. Bae?" Ina asked.

"People call me that when they owe me money. You can call me Sun-mi." She pulled into traffic with one hand and reached back with the other to pass Ji-eun a mint. "Chew this. You look like you're going to throw up, and this van's suspension is already a moral problem."

Ji-eun obeyed automatically. The mint clicked between her teeth.

Sun-mi kept talking as she drove, not to extract information but because silence made her itchy. She complained about tuna wholesalers, bragged about her daughter passing a civil service exam on the fourth try, and swore she could spot Seoul liars by their umbrella choices. Every sentence ended with the sound of someone who had long ago accepted chaos as normal working conditions.

When they reached the fish-market apartment, she killed the engine and pointed up at a narrow concrete building squeezed between a bait shop and a karaoke bar that would not open until dark.

"Fifth floor. Door code is your birthday if you're honest and your ex's birthday if you're not. I stocked rice, kimchi, and clean towels. No balcony photos. Drones love this block. If anyone knocks and says delivery, ignore. If anyone knocks and says church, maybe open. Depends which church."

She looked at Ji-eun directly then, eyes sharp behind smudged liner.

"I don't care who you are on television. In this building you're a woman who needs sleep and soup. Don't die on my floor. I just had the plumbing fixed."

Ji-eun blinked, then gave a short, real nod. "Yes, ma'am."

"Good girl. Bad hair color, though."

Ji-eun touched her hood and almost smiled.

Upstairs, the apartment was small and clean in the way of places kept for emergencies: no framed photos, no clutter, one extra phone charger in a basket with travel-size toothpaste and two packs of instant seaweed soup. Through the cracked window, harbor air pushed in smelling of salt, diesel, and blood from the fish stalls below. Gulls screamed like hinges that needed oil.

Ina locked the door, checked the second lock, then checked again. She made Ji-eun shower while she scanned local news feeds and clipped blackout curtains over the window with clothespins she found in a drawer.

By noon, the pressure campaign had escalated.

Agency counsel sent a PDF titled TEMPORARY WELLNESS CONSENT with signature lines already filled using Ji-eun's digital stamp.

Council ops sent a location pin for Seongdong and the message CONFIRM ETA.

Unknown numbers sent threats that pretended to be concern.

If she is not controlled she will embarrass everyone.

Take her to private clinic now. She needs chemical rest.

You will both be safer if she does not speak for 48 hours.

Ina deleted none of them. She forwarded everything to an encrypted folder and labeled it coercion_live.

Ji-eun came out of the shower in a faded T-shirt with a cartoon whale on it, borrowed from the emergency drawer. Her wet hair was darker than on stage, nearly black. Without makeup, she looked younger and older at once, the way people did after public humiliation when adrenaline had drained and shame was still in the bloodstream.

They ate reheated pollack soup at the floor table. Ji-eun held the metal bowl with both hands and blew on each spoonful as if this were all she needed from the world.

Then the intercom buzzed once and died. Both of them froze.

Ina put down her spoon, slid to the wall, and checked the peephole through the taped edge of the curtain. No one at their door. Voices from the stairwell. A boy laughing two floors down. A woman scolding him in a Busan accent thick enough to bend vowels.

Ji-eun exhaled slowly. "I hate that my body does this now."

"Mine too."

"No, yours does strategy. Mine does prey."

Ina sat back, anger lifting in her throat like bile. "They taught you that. They can unteach it, or we can."

Ji-eun gave her a look. "You're trying to write a hopeful ending while the building still shakes."

"I'm trying to keep you from skipping to the ending while we're still in the middle."

Ji-eun snorted despite herself. "Terrible line."

"Not my best work."

By late afternoon, the storm that had followed them south came in hard and loud. Rain hammered the metal railing outside and turned the alley below into moving silver. They sat on opposite ends of the futon, each with a phone and no appetite for what was on it.

At 17:12, Ji-eun stood and walked to the window.

"I'm going live at six," she said.

Ina set her phone down. "No."

Ji-eun did not turn. "I wrote it already. Three minutes. Names, amounts, the rider clauses, the sedatives. I say I'm alive and I'm not apologizing. Then I log out."

"You think that ends it."

"I think it starts something honest."

"It starts a feeding frenzy and gives them perfect footage to claim you're unstable."

"I'm unstable. Have you looked at me."

Ina stood. Rain hit the window in sheets, loud enough that they both had to raise their voices.

"Being hurt is not instability. Being trapped and furious is not instability. They will still use those words if you hand them this video."

Ji-eun turned then, phone in her fist. "What do you want from me? Breathe quietly in a borrowed T-shirt while men in suits decide whether I'm useful again? That's your plan?"

"My plan is to buy you legal and physical room."

"With what. Hope. Encryption. Your aunt's church account."

The jab was cruel and accurate and Ji-eun knew it. She winced a half-second too late.

"I'm sorry," she said, then shook her head hard. "No, I'm not sorry enough to stay quiet. I can't carry this by smiling. I can't."

She unlocked her screen and opened the livestream app. Her thumb hovered over START.

Ina crossed the room in two steps and put her hand over Ji-eun's, not wrestling, just blocking the motion.

"If you press that, they sue you by midnight, freeze your family accounts by breakfast, and drag you to a clinic on a welfare order by tomorrow afternoon. They'll say you're delusional and exhausted and they're helping. You know they will."

Ji-eun's jaw worked. "Then I disappear."

"How."

"I walk out and don't come back."

"In this weather, with your face on every homepage. You won't make it six blocks."

"Then I find a bridge."

The sentence arrived without drama, flat as inventory, and that frightened Ina more than tears ever could. She loosened her grip and moved them both down to the floor, knees knocking the coffee table.

"Look at me," Ina said.

Ji-eun stared at the phone.

"Look at me."

She did.

"I'm not going to drug you," Ina said. "I'm not going to tie your hands with legal language and call it care. I'm not handing you back to them sedated. I'd rather get arrested for kidnapping than do that."

Ji-eun swallowed. Her pupils were huge in the dim room.

"But I need you here tonight," Ina continued. "Alive. Angry. Mouthy. I need your brain intact tomorrow, not scorched by a panic broadcast they can spin. Give me tonight."

Ji-eun laughed once, a raw scraping sound. "You keep saying one night like it's cheap."

"It's expensive as hell. I'm paying for it with my own neck."

Outside, thunder rolled over the harbor. The window frame rattled. Somewhere in the apartment below, a radio switched from talk show to trot music and the singer's high vibrato bled through the floor.

Ji-eun closed the livestream app but did not release the phone. "What are you offering me, exactly."

Ina went to her backpack, dug out the folder with the copied contract rider Ji-eun had passed her in chapter eight, and laid it on the table between them. The paper edges were already soft from handling.

"This," Ina said. "I get you out of this debt weapon. Full stop."

Ji-eun stared at her. "Do not do that."

"Do not do what."

"Do not promise heroic impossible bullshit to keep me from a ledge."

"I'm not promising heroics. I'm promising work."

"You can't cancel a contract because you feel bad for me."

"I know. So I won't do feelings. I'll do leverage."

She sat opposite Ji-eun and tapped the rider clauses one by one.

"Clause 4.2, mandatory sedation and behavioral management under reputation risk. That violates medical consent law if we prove coercion. Clause 6.1, immediate debt acceleration on moral harm. Overbroad and challengeable if we show unconscionable enforcement against a minor-signing trainee. Clause 7.3, arbitration venue stacked by their own sponsor firm. That's a conflict. It's ugly, but it's not untouchable."

Ji-eun's lips parted. "You already mapped this."

"I've been mapping it since you handed it to me. I just didn't have enough to move."

"And now."

"Now you're the witness and the harmed party and the public face they can't quietly disappear without making noise. That's risk, but it's also leverage."

Ji-eun looked down at the number printed in the debt schedule: KRW 320,000,000. She traced the commas with one nail.

"I can't pay that if they call it."

"I'm not asking you to pay it."

"Who then. You."

Ina held her gaze. "If it comes to cash before court, I will find it."

"From where."

"I don't know yet. I'll steal, borrow, and burn every favor I've got left. I won't let them use that number as a choke chain."

Ji-eun's eyes filled abruptly, and she got angry at her own tears.

"You're making this worse," she said. "If you fail, I've got to watch you drown too."

Ina gave a tired half-smile. "I'm already in the water."

For a long moment, neither spoke. Rain eased. The harbor noises returned: truck brakes, shouted prices, gulls fighting over offal.

Ji-eun pushed the contract toward Ina. "Say it cleanly, then. No metaphors."

Ina nodded. She put both palms flat on the table, like swearing in.

"I promise I will get you free of Haneul's contract penalties," she said. "I promise I will challenge the debt and block collection. If they call the three hundred twenty million, I take that fight and that cost with you. I do not hand you back. I do not sedate you. I do not silence you. That is my promise."

Ji-eun searched her face for the gap, the caveat, the producer note hidden in the margin. She did not find one.

"Okay," she whispered.

She set her phone down screen-first and pushed it across the table to Ina. Not surrender. Temporary custody. A trust shaped like a bruise.

At 18:03, Ina's phone lit again with a calendar alarm she had ignored all day.

COUNCIL SUMMONS: Emergency strategy review, Seongdong safehouse, 18:30 mandatory attendance.

She looked at it, then at Ji-eun, who was sitting cross-legged on the floor in borrowed whale pajamas, eyelids heavy from exhaustion and no chemicals, cheeks flushed from soup and crying and anger.

Ina swiped the summons away.

At 18:09, Kwon called. She let it ring out.

At 18:15, a voicemail arrived. Ina put it on speaker because secrecy would only rot things faster.

Kwon's voice came through thin and precise, each word clipped like paper.

"Writer Seo, you have failed to appear for a mandatory council review. Your unilateral relocation of Park Ji-eun constitutes operational interference. You are ordered to return to Seoul tonight and transfer her to approved supervision. If you do not comply, support protections may be withdrawn. Consider your next action carefully."

The line disconnected.

Ji-eun hugged her knees. "Support protections. He makes prison sound like customer service."

Ina snorted. "He thinks diction is governance."

Ji-eun leaned her head back against the wall. "Are we fucked."

"Yes," Ina said. "But we're fed, dry, and not sedated, so it's not the worst version of fucked."

A short laugh escaped Ji-eun. "You're a terrible comfort person."

"I'm all we have right now."

"Then stay."

Ina stayed.

Night came early under the weather. She made up the futon with clean sheets that smelled faintly of bleach and sea salt. Ji-eun fell asleep in uneven drops, jerking awake twice at hallway noises, then finally staying down with one hand under her cheek and the other curled around nothing.

Ina sat at the floor table with the contract rider, a notebook, and her phone on airplane mode except for one encrypted channel. She listed names: labor lawyer in Nampo who owed Hyejin a favor from a church bookkeeping mess, journalist in Osaka who loved contract abuse stories, former idol who had won a partial debt challenge and now sold vocal warm-up videos online to pay legal bills.

She wrote numbers in the margin. 320,000,000 principal. Estimated filing costs. Emergency housing. Family transfer buffer if Ji-eun's mother got targeted. Every figure looked obscene and necessary.

Around midnight, the rain stopped. Market trucks started up again below, engines coughing to life one by one. Ina flexed her left wrist where the burn scar tightened in damp weather and kept writing until the page edges grew soft under her hand.

She had missed the council summons. She had ignored direct orders. She had put a public figure under unauthorized protection in a city full of cameras. She had promised money she did not have and legal victories she had not earned.

For the first time since Jamsil, the panic in her chest shifted shape. Not gone. Reassigned.

Across the room, Ji-eun slept without pills, mouth slightly open, hair drying in rough waves against the borrowed pillow. Every few minutes, her left hand twitched once, then stilled.

Ina watched that hand until dawn diluted the window from black to gray. When the gulls started up again and the first fish auction shout bounced off the alley walls, she opened a new page in the notebook and wrote at the top:

GET HER OUT, THEN PAY WHAT IT COSTS.
