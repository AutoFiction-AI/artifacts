# Chapter 11

Ina lay awake on the blanket she had dragged to the floor beside Ji-eun's bed, counting gull cries through walls thin enough to leak everything. Salt came in from the harbor vents. Diesel pooled low in the hallway, damp as laundry left overnight. At four-thirty the birds started up, and by five their cries sounded like old hinges forced open.

Her notebook from the night before stayed open under her wrist, GET HER OUT, THEN PAY WHAT IT COSTS pressed hard enough to ghost onto the page beneath.

Ji-eun slept in short stuttering blocks, one hand curled under her cheek, the left hand twitching every few minutes like she was still hitting marks in front of a camera nobody had turned off yet. On the chair by the window, Ji-eun's stage shoes sat in a neat pair, cleaned with an old toothbrush and never worn outside. She kept them spotless like relics from a religion she had quit but did not trust herself to mock.

Ina's phone lit without buzzing. New meeting notice from Seongdong. Attendance mandatory. Eleven-thirty. Final readiness review: Mercury Night.

She read the line three times and felt the floor vinyl press its cold grid pattern into her bare forearm. She had skipped one council summons yesterday while moving Ji-eun between two taxis and a fish broker's storage loft. Kwon had not yelled on the call. He had thanked her for "protective initiative" in that calm voice he used when he was putting a knife in and wanted you to hold still.

Ji-eun rolled over and opened one eye. "You look like shit. Is it them?"

"It's a review meeting."

"That means you don't want me asking." Ji-eun pulled herself upright, hair flattened on one side, voice rough and almost middle-school plain. "Do I need to pack again?"

"Not yet."

"So yes, then."

Ina stood and stretched her back until it clicked twice. "Stay inside today. Mrs. Guk is coming at nine with lunch and the fake pharmacy receipts. If anyone knocks before she gets here, don't answer."

Ji-eun gave her the smile she used when she knew a director was lying to her and needed her to cooperate anyway. "Don't do the subtitle-editor voice on me. It makes me want to throw things."

Ina almost laughed. Almost. "I'll call you from the train."

She showered in water that never got fully hot. She dried her hair with a towel that smelled faintly of squid stock and bleach, then put on the only blouse she had that still looked like Seoul. In the mirror she looked older than thirty-five and younger than tired. A burn scar crossed her left wrist. Ink shadow stained the side of her finger where pen grooves never washed clean. She looked like somebody who had learned to stay composed in fluorescent light because fluorescent light never asked if she was okay.

Mrs. Guk arrived on time with an insulated pot and a canvas bag full of forged clinic forms. She was sixty if she was a day, with a permanent rubber thimble on her right index finger from mending trawler nets, and she kept hard candy in her bra strap so she could hand one to nervous children without digging through pockets. She did not ask Ina where she was going. She glanced once at Ina's blouse, once at her shoes, and said, "If it runs late, call before dark. The stairwell bulb on the third floor is dead again."

On the KTX north, Ina sat beside a man in a construction uniform who fell asleep before Ulsan and leaned into her shoulder every time the car swayed. She let him. The seatback in front of her had been repaired with gray tape, and the tape edge kept lifting then sticking back down each time her knee touched it. She opened the Mercury brief and read the latest packet timing notes.

Election-night model: high-credibility trigger window from 20:40 to 21:20.

Secondary amplification from pre-seeded fan channels and civic rumor accounts.

Deepfake insert sequence dependent on synchronized testimony leak.

They had finally stopped pretending it was an archival release campaign. They were building a panic event and giving it a historical justification afterward.

Ina typed a page of delay arguments and deleted half of them. She needed to lead with technical points, never moral ones. If she started with harm in that room, Kwon would hear sentiment and everyone else would hear weakness. She rebuilt the list around failure probability: lip-sync phase drift under compression; accent contour mismatch in northern vowels; metadata chain contamination from Jamsil remnants; route security overstretch after recent seizures. She wrote each point in full sentences. Then she wrote one line at the bottom and did not delete it.

If this launches now, people die in the confusion before anyone verifies anything.

By the time she reached Seoul the sky had gone that polished gray that made even expensive buildings look like they had not slept. A taxi took her to the Seongdong print-shop entrance used for old meetings. Two men she had never seen before were stationed by the loading bay with earpieces and patient faces. One of them held out a tray without looking at her.

"Phone. Watch. Access card."

"I need the card for internal doors."

"Not today."

She put everything on the tray and felt the little shame of being searched in front of a parked truck with flattened tires and rainwater pooled in the bed. The guard gave her a paper visitor strip with red ink at the edge. The strip read TEMPORARY.

Inside, the building smelled the same as always: hot server fans, stale coffee, offset ink. Somebody had changed the hallway bulbs to a cooler white, and the place now read as a failing clinic instead of a conspiracy.

The meeting room windows were still blocked with frosted vinyl. Kwon sat at the head of the table in a dark suit and pale tie, silver hair combed back, posture straight enough to make everyone else's spine look soft. To his right sat legal and comms. To his left sat technical operations. Song Mira from election analytics had three sharpened pencils lined up parallel to her notebook and a tiny brass inhaler on a chain around her neck; she took one measured breath from it every fifteen minutes as if she had timed her own lungs.

Kwon looked up when Ina entered. "Writer Seo. Thank you for coming on short notice."

She took the last open chair. "I was already on my way."

That was a lie, and it made nobody here safer.

Kwon nodded to Song. "Give us the volatility frame."

Song stood, projected the district map, and began in a voice that sounded perpetually one cup of tea away from steady. "On current models, undecided concentration is still highest in river-adjacent precincts and military-family clusters. Sentiment keeps swinging with live media cues between 20:30 and 21:30. If we miss that window, post-result consolidation cuts narrative elasticity by about forty percent."

She clicked to the next slide: turnout lines, rumor velocity curves, channel overlap. Every color trend ended at the same black bar labeled Mercury Night.

Kwon folded his hands. "We are not discussing whether to proceed. We are discussing precision."

Ina opened her notebook. "Then that's exactly why we need a delay."

She kept her tone level. "Current face model still breaks at broadcast compression. Mouth edges jitter on hard consonants. You can hide some of that with motion blur, but not enough for live scrutiny. Accent modeling still leans south on open vowels, and I'm the only person in this building who can hear that in real time. Route security is unstable after Jamsil and the raid cycle. If this packet drops while channels are raw, we get traceable leaks before we get uptake."

Bae cut in before Park could speak. "People don't run phonetics in a panic. They forward whatever matches the mood."

Ina turned to him. "They don't run phonetics, no. They smell a fake in two seconds and hand us to every counterintel desk in Seoul."

Park from legal tapped his pen. "Your recommendation?"

"Minimum three-week delay. We run falsification drills, then push a decoy rumor event first and measure correction speed."

Comms chief Bae snorted quietly. He wore fingerless cycling gloves indoors because his hands shook when they got cold, and he kept rubbing his thumb across the seam like he was trying to sand it smooth. "Three weeks and we miss the only night people are primed to believe a betrayal clip."

Ina did not look at him. "Three weeks and we don't torch the whole operation on a bad render."

Kwon watched her like he was waiting for a student to locate the answer he had already written on the board. "What you call obvious is often sufficient. Public cognition under electoral pressure does not require flawless artifacts. It requires emotional timing and institutional hesitation."

"Institutional hesitation means people make stupid decisions in the gap." Ina heard her own voice tighten. "At Jamsil, the rumor spike hit at 20:52. By 21:06 Songpa police had five North-born delivery drivers facedown in a parking lot, and one left with his jaw wired shut. That's fourteen minutes. That's why I'm asking for three weeks."

"No, this isn't abstract. At Jamsil--"

"Writer Seo." Kwon's voice stayed soft. "We have data on many things. Data does not govern history. Windows do."

She reached for the folder she had brought from Busan and slid a page across to Song: error captures, frame stills, timing drift. Song scanned it quickly, then looked at Kwon, not Ina.

"The artifacts are real," Song said. "Not catastrophic yet, but real."

"Of course they are real," Kwon replied. "Every instrument has failure signatures."

Ina leaned forward. "Then why are we acting like this is ready?"

Kwon tilted his head. "Because readiness is not perfection. It is alignment with consequence. Election night offers us ambiguity dense enough to survive first contact. After certification, every institution closes ranks around whichever story won. We either force reconsideration in the volatile window or we spend two years shouting at a locked door."

His language always did this: took a person and replaced them with a horizon line.

Ina tried another route. "Then let me run control-room oversight. If timing is fixed, at least give me final veto on release if the render glitches."

Silence moved around the table like a draft.

Kwon opened a slim aluminum case and turned it so they could all see what was inside: a matte-black hardware token the size of a lighter, etched with a tiny winged Mercury and a serial strip. The payload key. The room's center of gravity shifted one inch toward it.

He closed the case with a click. "Final authorization will be held by Technical and Security as of today."

Ina stared at him. "You're cutting narrative review out of a narrative operation."

"I am reducing cross-pressure in the last phase."

"That is a pretty phrase for blind launch."

Kwon's eyes did not move. "You are fatigued, Writer Seo. You have had two months of divided loyalties, one detainment sequence, and one high-profile extraction in Busan that was operationally useful but structurally undisciplined. I do not question your intelligence. I question your current fit for terminal decisions."

There it was: the bureaucratic language for removal, ironed flat.

Ina heard chairs shift, paper move, someone swallow. She could have thrown the folder. She could have stood and called him what he was. Instead she forced herself to count the fan rotations she could hear through the wall, one low whine every three seconds, until her mouth stopped shaking.

"You recruited me because I could hear when a line sounded wrong," she said. "Now you're telling me that hearing wrong is disloyal."

"I am telling you that urgency has replaced your discipline."

"Urgency is what you're selling right now."

For the first time he looked almost tired. "We are past the stage where delay equals prudence. Delay now equals surrender to the status quo."

Ina laughed once, short and ugly. "You keep saying status quo like it's weather. Fourteen minutes of bad signal put five men on asphalt and one in surgery."

Kwon folded the aluminum case into his portfolio. "I have said it many times. I simply do not recite it as apology."

Song inhaled from her brass capsule and kept her gaze on her notebook. Bae checked his watch. Park closed the legal file without reading the last page.

Kwon stood, signaling end of meeting. "Mercury Night launch is fixed for by-election night, twenty-one ten release queue, twenty-one seventeen full dissemination. Writer Seo will continue script and language assets under revised permissions. Access changes are effective immediately."

The line hit with the blunt finality of a stamp on wet paper. The launch was fixed. Her permissions were revised. The change was effective immediately.

Ina stood too fast and the chair legs scraped. "I want that in writing."

"You will receive protocol memo C-11 within the hour."

"And if I object formally?"

"You may object formally."

He offered the ghost of a courteous smile. "It will not alter schedule."

The meeting broke. People gathered cables and folders with the subdued speed of staff who had already decided what they would tell themselves about what happened here. Nobody made eye contact with Ina except Song, briefly, and Song's look was not pity. It was warning.

In the hall, a junior access coordinator named Hwang Yoori waited by the badge station. Ina remembered her from payroll signatures: twenty-something, blunt bangs, little silver stars painted on two fingernails and chipped down to points. She kept a roll of mint gum clipped to her lanyard and offered one to everyone who was about to be angry, as if menthol could preempt violence.

"Writer Seo," Yoori said softly, already apologizing with her posture. "I need your old credential."

Ina handed over the plastic card. Yoori fed it into a slot, watched the red light blink, then printed a new one with a yellow stripe.

"Editorial zones only," she said. "No server floor, no launch room, no comms bridge. Elevator access capped at level three unless escorted."

Ina took the card. It weighed nothing and still felt heavier.

"Who else got revised?"

Yoori glanced down the corridor before answering. "Only you."

Then, because she was young and could not stop herself from being a person inside this machine, she added, "My brother follows your old drama with my mom. She likes the grandmother character."

Ina swallowed and slipped the card into her wallet where the old one used to sit. "Tell your mom she had better taste than this building."

On the taxi ride back to Seoul Station, protocol memo C-11 arrived in her encrypted inbox. Three paragraphs of legal paste, two bullet points on compliance, one line confirming launch schedule fixed to by-election window. She read it again on the platform while a teenage couple argued about whose turn it was to buy fish cakes. Their fight was petty and alive and had nothing to do with national destiny. She wanted to step into it and stay there.

By the time she reached Busan it was night and raining hard enough to erase the top floors of the harbor cranes. The apartment stairwell smelled of mold and fried mackerel. Third-floor bulb dead, just as Mrs. Guk had said. Ina climbed by phone light and found Ji-eun sitting at the table in an oversized sweatshirt, scraping nail polish off her thumb with a butter knife.

"You're late," Ji-eun said. "I made ramyeon and forgot to eat it."

Ina set down her bag. "Did anyone come?"

"Only Mrs. Guk, and she yelled at me for chopping garlic wrong." Ji-eun studied Ina's face. "How bad?"

Ina could have lied cleanly. She could have bought one more night of quiet. Instead she pulled out the new yellow-striped credential and dropped it on the table.

Ji-eun let out a low whistle. "They benched you."

"Editorial confinement."

"You look like someone took your teeth."

"Close enough."

Ji-eun pushed the cold ramyeon bowl toward her. "Eat first. Fall apart after."

Ina took two bites because the noodles were there and her body demanded tribute before despair. Then she called Minjae from the burner he had given her in chapter-seven trust and chapter-nine panic, and told him to meet before dawn at the auction docks. He did not ask why. He only said, "Pier 4, back stairs. Wear shoes you can run in."

The harbor before sunrise was all metal noise and cheap light. Forklifts beeped in reverse. Men in rubber aprons dragged crates over wet concrete. Loudspeakers barked lot numbers while gulls tried to steal fish heads from the sorting bins. Ina waited under a corrugated awning where rain bounced off the edge in thick ropes.

Minjae came in from the service lane carrying a plastic tool case and a black umbrella turned inside out by the wind. He had not shaved. His hair was flattened by rain. One sleeve rode up enough to show the ridge of scar tissue across his shoulder, pale against wet skin. He set the broken umbrella in a bin and walked up without kissing her.

"You sound wrecked on the phone," he said. "What happened?"

She handed him the yellow card.

His mouth tightened. "Jesus."

"Launch fixed. By-election night. Twenty-one seventeen full push."

He stared at the card a second longer, then gave it back. "Kwon did this himself?"

"In front of everyone."

"And you asked for delay."

"Technical delay, controlled drills, anything."

He rubbed rainwater off his jaw with the heel of his hand. "He was never going to give it to you once he smelled dissent."

"I didn't ask what he smelled." Ina stepped closer so the auction noise could not eat her words. "I need a sabotage route."

Minjae looked at her as if she had switched languages mid-sentence. "Define sabotage."

"Checksum corruption at ingest. Spoofed clock skew. Kill a repeater node right after first release. I don't care which lever, I care that the clip doesn't propagate cleanly."

He gave a short, incredulous laugh. "That's not a lever, that's twelve crews getting interrogated and maybe disappeared."

"If it propagates cleanly, we get panic sweeps. You know that."

"I know exactly what panic sweeps look like." His voice stayed low but got sharper. "I also know what a network purge looks like when brokers think somebody burned the route on purpose."

He opened the tool case and took out a folded map laminated with clear tape. Route marks in red and blue crossed city names and coast lines like cracked veins.

"These two channels pay my sister's floor rent in Moran and the police envelope that keeps her landlord from reporting her phone." He tapped twice near the edge of the map. "This one moves antibiotics and memory cards together because customs ignores medicine crates half the time. If I jam Mercury from inside, broker audits hit all three routes. Not maybe. Always."

Ina stared at the map until the marker colors blurred. "Then we do it from outside."

"Outside still points back to me."

"Everything points back to you because they built it that way."

"Yes." He snapped the map shut. "That is exactly my point."

Rain hammered the awning. A forklift screeched turning too fast. Somewhere behind them two buyers started shouting over a missing crate and the words dissolved into dialect and curses.

Ina said, "If you do nothing, Mercury launches and your sister lives in the aftermath anyway."

"If I sabotage, she doesn't get to have an aftermath." He took a breath, then another, like he was counting bolts on a panel. "Ina, listen to me. I can harden one neighborhood channel. I can move warning texts through church lists before the clip drops. I can prep two extraction vans. I can't torch the core route. I won't."

"Won't or can't?"

His face shut down for a beat. Then he laughed once, bitter. "Both. Happy?"

She hated the question as soon as it left her mouth. She hated his answer more because it was true.

"I'm out of decision rooms," she said. "I'm asking you because there's nobody left to ask."

"Then stop asking the machine for mercy and get out before it eats you."

"Don't give me survival slogans."

"I'm not giving slogans." He stepped in, voice rough now, no shield. "Run. Take Ji-eun and run south or west or anywhere without this signal radius. I can get you on a cargo ferry under crew manifests by tomorrow night. You disappear for two weeks, maybe three, you come back when the first crackdown cycle burns out."

She stared at him. "You're asking me to abandon everyone in range."

"I'm asking you to stay alive long enough to do something later."

"Later is what people say when they want today to happen to someone else."

"Later is what people say when they've seen bodies and know they can't stop the first hit." He dragged a hand through wet hair, suddenly exhausted. "I'm not Kwon, Ina. I don't have speeches for this. I've got route math and a sister with one winter coat and a landlord who counts every visitor at her door."

There it was, the private register he almost never let out in daylight: not cynical, just terrified and practical in the same breath.

Ina stepped back under the awning. Water dripped from the corrugated edge onto her sleeve and ran cold down to her wrist scar. She thought of Ji-eun's stage shoes lined up by the window, right shoe toe split where she kept pivoting on bad stage floors. Hyejin hunched over the church ledger with swollen knuckles, pressing numbers so hard the pencil snapped because computers made evidence easy to seize. Her father kept stacking rent notices under grocery flyers until the envelopes turned red and men came to the door before breakfast.

She could run.

The word sounded clean, and clean was suspicious.

"If I run," she said quietly, "Kwon gets uncontested launch. No language check, no internal friction, nothing. He already cut me out because I said no. Running would prove him right about me."

Minjae kicked the leg of an empty crate hard enough to tip it. "'Fuck Kwon's schedule. Fuck this launch.' He jabbed a finger at the yellow-striped card in her hand. 'This isn't about your pride, Ina. It's triage.'"
A buyer two stalls over glanced at them, then yanked his cart the other way, pretending he had not heard.

"It's not pride. It's leverage."

"You don't have leverage anymore."

"I've got memory. I've got documents. I've got people who still pick up my calls."

"For now." He looked toward the dock gate, then back at her. "If Mercury goes live and they need a culprit, they'll pin it on whoever's easiest to narrate. You're almost gift-wrapped."

"I know."

"Then why are you standing here asking me to break the only lines feeding my family?"

She met his eyes. "Because I'm out of clean options and I thought you might still choose ugly with me."

His jaw jumped. He looked away first.

When he spoke again, his tone was flatter, operational. "I can't sabotage. That answer doesn't change if you ask me ten different ways."

A fish cart rolled past them trailing brine and crushed ice. The worker pulling it had one earbud in and was singing off-key to himself, cheerful in a place built from exhaustion.

Minjae closed the tool case and clipped it shut. "I'll push warning prep through non-attributed lists. I'll move two backup phones to your aunt by noon. I'll send you district heat guesses every three hours on launch day. That's what I can do without signing my sister's death warrant."

Ina nodded once. Agreement felt like loss in her mouth.

He touched her elbow, then did not let go. "Come with me anyway. Just this once. I can take you to the old motel above Yeongdo, no records, no cameras in the stairwell. Sleep for eight hours. Then decide."

"I can't. Ji-eun's alone."

"Bring her."

"And then what? We hide in mold and cigarette curtains while the city tears itself open?"

He did not answer right away. Wind snapped a tarp loose on the next dock and men shouted to pin it down.

"And then maybe you're alive when the tearing stops," he said.

She wanted to lean into him. She wanted the simple body answer: warmth, breath, temporary oblivion. The two of them had used each other that way before and called it trust because they needed the word. But she could not make herself step forward.

"I can't run," she said.

"You can. You won't."

"Fine."

"Fine."

The word clanged between them, dropped metal on concrete.

He let go of her elbow. "I've got to move before daylight checks start."

"Minjae."

He waited.

"If it goes bad faster than expected, if they sweep in your sister's block first, call me before you call anyone else."

His mouth twisted into something close to a smile and nothing like one. "You still think you can fix timing with language."

"Sometimes I can."

"Sometimes." He lifted the broken umbrella from the bin, looked at it, threw it back. "Don't trust any message tagged with weather emojis on launch day. They're burning that code set."

That was his goodbye, practical and intimate at once.

He walked toward the service lane without turning around.

Ina stood under the awning until her socks were wet through. Then she went back up the hill to the apartment, bought coffee from a machine that tasted like scorched plastic, and climbed the dark stairwell by memory. Inside, Ji-eun was awake and practicing smile variants in the bathroom mirror, soft and bright and apologetic and defiant, one after another like scales.

"You saw him," Ji-eun said from the doorway.

"Yes."

"Did he fix it?"

Ina put the yellow-striped card on the table beside last night's cold ramyeon bowl. "No."

Ji-eun exhaled slowly. "Then what now?"

Ina opened her laptop, entered her council credentials, and watched three access requests fail in sequence. The launch room was denied. The comms bridge was denied. The server floor was denied. A fourth prompt arrived from legal requiring acknowledgment of revised permissions before she could download even transcript drafts.

What now was this: smaller windows, thinner tools, more expensive decisions.

"Now," she said, "we stay awake."

She signed the acknowledgment with a hand that had started to cramp, then forwarded protocol memo C-11 to two off-book addresses she trusted enough to endanger: the labor-rights attorney in Nampo and the Osaka investigations editor she'd flagged the night before.
The attorney replied in forty-three seconds with a court-duty stamp and a deadline: send full headers by 14:00 or lose emergency filing priority. The editor sent one line after that: "Once this lands, they'll know it was you."
A minute later, Hyejin sent a separate line: "Haneul Self Storage. Striped awning. Bring your mother's keyring."

Outside, the harbor loudspeaker switched to weather warnings for afternoon wind shear, voice metallic and calm. Inside, the apartment refrigerator clicked on and rattled with a loose-tooth chatter. Ji-eun sat across from her and peeled the label from a water bottle in one long careful strip, not looking up, listening to the keys as Ina typed.

Mercury Night had a date now. The room where it would be born had no chair for her.
