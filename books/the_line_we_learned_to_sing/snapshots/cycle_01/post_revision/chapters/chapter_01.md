# Chapter 1

By ten in the morning the condolence lilies had started to rot in their plastic sleeves. The ribbons still declared righteousness in black calligraphy, but the petals had gone the color of old dishwater. Ina stood beside the folding table in the crematorium lobby and pressed her thumb into the knot of one ribbon until the silk cut her skin, not hard enough to bleed, just enough to stay inside her hand and not her face.

The lobby had no windows. It smelled like wet wool and cheap instant coffee and that thin burned sweetness from industrial incense sticks, the ones that left no smoke and no mercy. Across from her, a wall-mounted television played election coverage with the volume down. Two men in navy ties argued in subtitles about flood aid to North Hwanghae, each one claiming the other had invented the water.

Aunt Hyejin arrived late with her reading glasses still around her neck and flour dust on her sleeve, church-kitchen proof she had come straight from kneading dough. She set down three paper cups, looked at the table, and took the ribbons off the worst lilies without asking.

"Don't let those sit where people can see," she said. "Your mother would've cursed us for paying full price for flowers that collapsed before lunch."

Hyejin always got busier when she was afraid. She tied the ruined stems together with one of her hair ties and tucked the bundle under the table like she was hiding contraband fruit.

Dohyun sat in the molded plastic chair nearest the exit. He kept one hand on his knee brace and one hand around an unlit cigarette. He had promised he wouldn't smoke inside the building. He honored the promise by rolling the cigarette back and forth between fingers stained a permanent yellow at the edges, the skin glossy where decades of nicotine had burnished it.

He had barely spoken since the death certificate came through. When condolences arrived, he nodded. When someone asked if he had eaten, he lied yes. Now he watched the television subtitles crawl by and did not blink when his wife's name passed under the funeral home logo.

Ina set down her mother's resident card, family registry papers, and the cremation authorization form. A young clerk with a tiny silver rabbit pin on his lapel leaned over the table. He had a blister on his thumb wrapped in a cartoon bandage and a voice that cracked whenever he said "ma'am." He was trying to perform dignity and looked about nineteen.

"For the memorial tablet inscription," he said, tapping the form, "do you want birthplace listed?"

Hyejin answered before Ina could open her mouth. "Yes. Sinuiju. Put it exactly."

Dohyun finally looked up. "No. Leave it off."

The clerk froze between them. His rabbit pin caught the fluorescent light.

Hyejin turned on Dohyun, not loud yet, but the kind of quiet that already had an edge. "She crossed that river at nine years old. You want to erase that on the one day someone writes her whole name on stone?"

"I'm trying to keep this simple," Dohyun said.

"Simple for whom?"

"For our daughter."

Ina could feel both of them pulling at her like two hands on the same door, each convinced force was care. On the television, the subtitles switched to a panel about border reunification visas. Outside, sirens moved along the river road.

"Put Sinuiju," Ina said. "Leave the old district off. Just city and year."

Dohyun's jaw twitched. "People talk."

"People always talk." Hyejin pushed the glasses up her nose with one arthritic knuckle. "Let them chew on something real for once."

The clerk nodded too quickly and wrote it down. His pen squeaked against the cheap carbon paper.

Ina's phone vibrated across the table. Unknown number. She ignored it. The vibration stopped. Started again. Same number.

"Take it," Hyejin said, already counting condolence envelopes into neat stacks by denomination. "Could be your editor."

No editor called three times before eleven. Ina stepped into the corridor between the funeral hall and the vending machines, where the floor tiles changed from gray to a fake wood pattern that made everyone look unsteady.

She answered on the fourth ring.

"Ms. Seo?" a woman said, breathy through a cheap headset. Keyboard clatter in the background. "This is Seoul St. Mary's billing office. I know this is a difficult time. I am calling regarding Account 7714, balance fifty-eight million won. We can enroll you in a hardship plan if you make a minimum payment today."

Ina watched a man wheel a trolley stacked with cardboard urn boxes past her. The boxes were printed with pink lotus flowers and gold foil lettering. Somebody had chosen branding colors for death.

"My mother was cremated this morning," Ina said.

The caller went silent for one beat, two. When she spoke again her voice had dropped the script and found her own age, maybe mid-twenties, maybe exhausted. "I'm sorry. I still have to read this notice. If no payment posts by Friday, collection authority shifts to legal."

"How much for Friday?"

"Two million."

Ina laughed once, not because anything was funny. She leaned her forehead against the cool vending-machine glass and watched packets of dried squid shimmer behind it. "I don't have two million by Friday."

"Then tell me what you do have." The woman clicked her pen three times, a nervous rhythm she probably hated in herself. "Even one hundred thousand buys time in the system."

Ina closed her eyes. Her thumb found the burn scar on her left wrist, rubbed it like a coin. "Call me Monday."

"Monday is too late for legal handoff."

"Then Monday is what I've got."

She ended the call before the woman could apologize again.

When she came back to the table, Dohyun was arguing with Hyejin about ash placement. He wanted the urn in the family plot south of the city, near his parents. Hyejin wanted to split the ashes and leave a portion at Imjingak where separated families tied ribbons and cried into cameras every year. Neither of them asked what Eun-sook had wanted. She had left no instructions, which felt exactly like her, one final refusal to make any single loyalty easy.

"We're not dividing her like evidence," Dohyun said.

"You divided her every time you told her to stop talking about the North at dinner," Hyejin snapped.

"I told her to stop because there were neighbors at dinner."

"You told her to stop because you were scared."

He stood too fast; his knee failed and caught. He grabbed the chair back before he fell. The unlit cigarette dropped and rolled under the table.

Ina moved between them, one hand on each shoulder, feeling bone under fabric. "Not here. Please."

Hyejin was still breathing hard through her nose, but she bent and retrieved the cigarette. Instead of handing it back, she snapped it in half and dropped it in the paper cup. "Sit down, Dohyun. Your leg's not making speeches today."

He sat. He looked at Ina, not at Hyejin. "Your mother kept files. Boxes. You know that. If you're clearing the apartment this afternoon, don't throw papers without checking dates."

It sounded practical and almost kind, but Ina heard the seam underneath. Files meant secrets. Secrets meant work he had tolerated in marriage and feared in widowhood.

"I'm not a child," she said.

"I didn't say you were."

"No. You just said it in your own language."

The clerk returned with the completed forms and a plastic tray for signatures. Ina signed her name seven times in three variations because each form demanded a slightly different legal relationship to the same dead woman. Daughter. Next of kin. Estate representative. She had never felt less representative of anything.

At noon they carried Eun-sook's portrait into the furnace hall. A monk in white robes chanted with the patience of someone paid by the hour. The chant vibrated through Ina's ribs like bus engine idle. When the steel door closed, she did not cry. She watched the temperature gauge climb and counted in Korean to one hundred, then to one hundred again. At two hundred twelve her phone buzzed with a new voicemail.

The screen read DOHYUN LANDLORD.

She let it ring out.

After the service, mourners peeled away in twos and threes, each one pressing condolences into Ina's hand: a bow, an envelope, a sentence about weather. Rain threatened all afternoon but never committed, the sky sitting low and white over the river like an unfinished thought.

In the parking lot, Hyejin opened her tote bag and brought out plastic containers of seaweed soup she had made at dawn. "Eat before we go up there," she said. "Grief makes people stupid with paperwork."

Dohyun refused at first. Then he took a spoon and held it too long before the first bite.

While they ate standing up beside the hearse, Ina played the landlord voicemail on speaker because secrets in this family always fermented. A man with the thick consonants of South Jeolla and the patience of somebody done pretending politeness said, "Mr. Han owes eleven million two hundred thousand won. End of month, I change the lock. I don't care if his wife died. Dead people don't pay rent."

Dohyun stared at the asphalt. "He has called three times this week."

"Why didn't you tell me?" Ina asked.

"Because your mother was still breathing this week."

There it was, the only confession he could afford in public.

Hyejin set down her soup cup. "We'll cover this month's interest. Between the condolence money and what I can move from church reserves."

"Church reserves are for the food pantry," Ina said.

"Then we call it advance reimbursement for widower meals and pray louder." Hyejin's mouth tightened in a shape that could have been a smile from far away. "Don't look at me like that. Jesus has seen worse bookkeeping."

Ina wanted to laugh and couldn't. Her shoulders ached from holding the portrait frame. The gold-painted corners had left grit on her palms.

They drove to the apartment in Mapo in two cars because nobody wanted the same silence. Eun-sook's building sat above a row of wholesale kitchen-supply shops. On clear days you could see a strip of river between two office towers, gray water cut by barges and commuter ferries. Today the glass was filmed with rain dust and the view was a rumor.

The hallway smelled of garlic and bleach. Someone on the fourth floor was frying mackerel with the window closed. Inside apartment 502, the air had already started to change from lived-in to emptied: less body heat, more paper and detergent and the metallic chill from the old refrigerator seal.

Hyejin tied on an apron she found hanging by the stove, Eun-sook's faded yellow one with a cartoon pepper stitched at the hem. She did not take it off all afternoon.

"Dishes first," she announced. "Then medicine disposal. Then closet inventory. No one opens old letters until we've eaten again."

She gave orders like prayer beads, one after another, because if she paused she might have to mention her sister in past tense.

Dohyun went to the balcony and sat on the low stool by the ashtray, though he still did not light anything. He kept folding and unfolding the death-notice pamphlet into smaller and smaller squares. Ina watched him turn an official document into a paper stone.

She started in the kitchen. Eun-sook had labeled every shelf with masking tape in tight handwriting: SOUP STOCK, NUTS, RICE, SPRING KIMCHI, BILLS. The bills shelf held two utility invoices and a packet of instant coffee. No hidden accounts, no farewell letters.

On top of the fridge sat a metal cookie tin full of batteries sorted by size. In the freezer she found anchovies, a bag of peeled chestnuts, and two film canisters of dried mugwort from some recipe no one had made in years. Everything looked normal and ordinary. Nothing explained the knot under her sternum since morning, the sense that her mother had not finished speaking.

By four, the rain finally started, hard enough to drum on the awning outside the kitchen window. Hyejin called from the living room for tape and scissors. Ina opened the lower pantry to look for packing supplies and found a twenty-kilo rice bag set inside a plastic storage tub to keep out bugs. She heaved the bag out by the handles. It was half full and heavier than it should have been.

Something inside shifted with a stiff rectangular sound.

She slit the top seam and plunged her hand down through cool grains until her knuckles hit cardboard. Her fingers closed around a hard spine wrapped in wax paper.

She pulled up a notebook the color of old sea glass. Damp had curled the cover corners but the elastic band was intact. A strip of masking tape on the front read in Eun-sook's hand: REHEARSAL LYRICS - 2003 REV.

"Ina?" Hyejin called. "Where's that tape?"

"One second."

Ina brushed rice from the edges and opened the notebook to the middle. The pages were dense with lyrics written in blue-black ink, some recognizable as patriotic anthems from school assemblies, others as folk songs from broadcasts her mother used to hum while scrubbing pans. But words had been crossed out and replaced. In one song, mountain peaks became transmission towers. In another, "our fatherland" had become "our signal." Dates crowded the margins in tiny ledger script: 4/19, 8/12, 10/3, each followed by times and three-digit strings.

These pages were transmission instructions disguised as song revisions.
Every crossed-out lyric carried operational weight.
It was code.

A memory arrived before she could block it: being twelve, wide awake at midnight, hearing Eun-sook in the kitchen singing one of those songs too softly, stopping before the chorus whenever the apartment phone rang.

"Ina! Tape, please!" Hyejin again, sharper this time.

Ina slid the notebook under her shirt against her stomach, cold paper against warm skin, and grabbed a roll of tape from the drawer. She walked into the living room with her face arranged for logistics.

Hyejin was wrapping framed photos in newspaper. She paused and peered at Ina. "You're pale. Did you eat anything besides sugar coffee?"

"I'm fine. Rice dust in my eyes." Ina handed over the tape.

Dohyun spoke from the balcony without turning. "Found anything in her files?"

The question landed with more weight than his tone.

Ina could feel the notebook edge pressing into her ribs. "Mostly utility bills. Church donation receipts."

"No notebooks?"

There it was. He was asking one thing: notebooks.

"If there are, I haven't seen them." She hated how easily the sentence came out.

Dohyun nodded once; the motion could have sealed a private fear or eased one, and she still couldn't tell which.

The rain thickened. Traffic noise from the river road softened to a steady wash. Ina carried two boxes to Eun-sook's bedroom and shut the door behind her. The room still smelled faintly of hair dye and camphor ointment.

She sat on the floor, pulled the notebook back out, and went page by page. Most entries paired altered lyrics with dates. Some had circles around certain words: BRIDGE, STATIC, LANTERN, RAINFALL. On the back cover, written vertically in pencil almost erased by friction, she found a number with hyphens in an old pager format and two characters beside it: RHEE.

Her first thought was fraud. Her second thought was that nobody ran pager networks now unless they wanted deniability. Her third thought, uninvited, was that deniability cost less than lawyers.

She copied the number onto her forearm with a ballpoint, then tucked the notebook into an empty duvet case and zipped it shut. Outside the bedroom door, Hyejin and Dohyun had moved from arguing to coordinated silence, which was somehow worse.

At six, Hyejin left to bring food from the church freezer and promised to return at dawn for the legal paperwork. Before she stepped out she adjusted Ina's collar, an old aunt reflex that survived any mood.

"Try not to read yourself into a fever tonight," she said. "Tomorrow we'll sort bank books and insurance and all the ugly stuff."

"You've already done plenty," Ina said.

"I've done dishes. That's not plenty." Hyejin patted her cheek once, rough thumb against damp skin. "Plenty starts when the creditors smell grief and line up." She glanced toward the balcony where Dohyun sat in shadow. "Keep him from signing anything stupid."

After she left, the apartment got larger and meaner in the way empty spaces do after sundown. The rain slowed to a hiss. The refrigerator motor clicked on and off like an impatient tongue.

Ina made instant ramyeon and did not eat it. She placed the notebook on the kitchen table beside her mother's old pager, a beige brick she had kept as a joke artifact. The battery was dead. The notebook number was not in its memory.

She opened her laptop, routed through a public VPN she used for subtitle leak forums, and searched the number. Nothing indexed under open records. She checked old telecom archive boards and found one retired technician thread mentioning a boutique paging relay still active for hospitals, funeral services, and "legacy clients requiring one-way alerts." The phrase \"legacy clients\" was a nice euphemism.

Fear arrived as administrative hesitation. She stared at the number for ten minutes and reorganized the desktop icons twice.

Then she took her umbrella, went downstairs to the convenience store kiosk at the corner, and paid 1,000 won cash to send a numeric page through the old relay terminal hidden under lottery forms.

The message read 41912?

She chose numbers from one of the margin dates and pretended that choice meant control.

The clerk at the kiosk, a college kid with a silver nose ring and a philosophy textbook propped under the cash drawer, watched her key in the message and said, "Haven't seen anyone use that in years. My uncle keeps one for mountain hikes. Says phones make him anxious."

"Good for your uncle," Ina said.

The kid shrugged. "He's still anxious."

Back upstairs, she locked the door, then unlocked it and checked the deadbolt again because anxiety liked rituals. Dohyun had fallen asleep in front of the television with the sound off, the election crawl painting blue and red bands across his face.

Ina sat on the bathroom floor with her back against the tub and waited for nothing to happen.

At 9:14 p.m., her phone buzzed from an unknown number.

She answered before the second ring.

A woman's voice, older, careful, with the flattening diction of broadcast training. "Who gave you that relay code?"

Ina kept her own voice neutral. "Depends who's asking."

A pause. Rainwater dripped somewhere inside the wall. Then the woman said, "When the river freezes, which edge breaks first?"

The phrase hit like a hand to the sternum. Eun-sook had used that exact line when Ina was little and the apartment phone rang after midnight. If Ina answered by accident, her mother would take the receiver gently, smile too bright, and ask that question into the line before saying anything else.

"The shadowed edge," Ina said before she could decide not to. "Because sunlight lies."

Silence again, shorter this time.

"Your mother taught you the old challenge set," the woman said. "This line is no longer secure."

"Then why did you answer it?"

"Because your page included April nineteenth, twelve past midnight. That date was not public."

Ina looked at the notebook open on her lap, at the date circled three times in blue ink. "Who are you?"

"Rhee Sun-ok. Former Office of Cultural Continuity, relocation desk." The title sounded like it had survived three coups and a filing cabinet fire. "I carried state credentials before your government and mine decided we'd call each other temporary."

"That's not proof."

"Correct. You'll get proof at a meeting."

"No. You can send proof now."

"Not on a compromised mobile line while your building's election CCTV watches every stairwell." Rhee's tone did not change, but something in it hardened. "If you're not ready, burn the book and sleep. If you are, come alone at ten-thirty. Seongdong, Geumhwa Printing, back entrance by the loading ramp. Bring only the notebook."

Ina pictured every true-crime cautionary segment she had ever subtitled. A woman in debt followed a stranger at night. A woman went missing by dawn.

"How do I know you won't hand me to intelligence?"

"You don't know." A small exhale, almost a laugh. "That's adulthood, Ms. Seo."

The call ended.

Ina sat still until the screen went black. In the living room Dohyun coughed in his sleep and groped for a cigarette that wasn't there.

She should have woken him, shown him the notebook, let family panic become collective panic. Instead she stood, went to her mother's closet, and chose dark clothes that didn't rustle. She tied her hair back and tucked the notebook inside a canvas document sleeve. From the kitchen drawer she took a cheap utility cutter and put it in her coat pocket, a ridiculous weapon against professional danger but better than prayer.

At the doorway she hesitated. Dohyun opened one eye.

"Where are you going?" he asked, voice rough.

"Convenience store. We need trash bags."

He nodded, already drifting. "Get the thick kind."

The elevator was slow and smelled like damp cardboard. On the street the rain had stopped, leaving the asphalt slick as lacquer. Motorbikes hissed past. Ina skipped the main avenue and took side roads, cutting through alleys behind welding shops where the air tasted of iron filings and old frying oil.

Mapo to Seongdong by taxi at night was thirty-five minutes if traffic behaved. She paid cash and asked to be dropped two blocks early.

Geumhwa Printing occupied the first floor of a brick warehouse with frosted windows and a sign whose bulbs had died one by one. The loading ramp sat in shadow. A single fluorescent tube buzzed above the back door.

Rhee was already there, holding an umbrella closed though the sky was clear. She wore men's loafers polished to a military shine and a camel coat mended at the cuff with thread two shades too dark. Her hair was cut blunt at the jaw, silver at the roots and dyed black at the ends, vanity and discipline still negotiating terms.

When she saw Ina, she did not wave. She checked her wristwatch, then said, "Two minutes late. City excuses are not useful in this work."

"If this is work, you haven't told me the job."

Rhee's mouth twitched. "Good. Keep that skepticism. It may keep you alive for a month."

She opened the back door with an old brass key and led Ina into a narrow corridor lined with stacks of poster tubes. The building smelled of ink and hot machine oil. Somewhere deeper in the warehouse, a printer head clicked in a steady mechanical prayer.

Rhee held out her hand. "Notebook."

Ina did not pass it over. "You first. Credentials."

Rhee reached inside her coat and produced a laminated card in a cracked leather sleeve. The photo showed her maybe thirty years younger, hair permed, expression severe. The emblem at the top was unfamiliar: a stylized peninsula split by a starburst line. Below, in faded print: PROVISIONAL CULTURAL SECRETARIAT. FIELD LIAISON. Her name in Hangul and older Romanization.

Forgery was possible. Good forgery was expensive.

"Anyone with a laminator can fake that," Ina said.

"Agreed." Rhee replaced the card and opened a metal locker set into the corridor wall. From inside she removed a flat envelope wrapped in butcher paper and tied with twine.

She offered it without letting go. "If you touch this, you accept confidentiality."

"You can't enforce that."

"Not tonight."

Ina took the envelope. Inside was a black-and-white photograph, edges scalloped, probably from the early nineties. Seven people stood in front of a chalkboard map of the peninsula. One of them was unmistakably Eun-sook, younger but still with that exact set of shoulders, half defensive, half amused. She wore a headset around her neck and held a folder labeled in thick marker: SONG REVISION UNIT C.

Beside her stood a man Ina recognized from history documentaries as a former vice minister who had publicly disappeared after democratization. On the back of the photo, in Eun-sook's handwriting, was a line Ina had seen on grocery lists and birthday cards all her life: Buy radish. Do not trust easy weather.

Her knees locked. The humiliation was smaller and meaner: she had to read the line three times because the letters kept blurring and snapping back into focus.

"Where did you get this?" she asked.

"From a cabinet your mother helped build," Rhee said. "From a history that has had excellent lawyers."

"Why contact me now? She's dead. Whatever game this is, you're late."

Rhee looked past Ina toward the warehouse floor, taking tempo from the printer rhythm. "Your mother's death did not trigger a slogan packet. It triggered a transfer file with three items: a cassette debrief on the April nineteenth operation, a ledger of payoffs tied to that operation, and two locker keys. If no successor clears screening this week, the file is pulped and an exposure memo goes upstairs."

The words were concrete enough to scrape.

Ina folded the photograph back into the envelope. "Say what you want from me in plain Korean."

"I need to know whether you're your mother's daughter in skill, not blood, and whether you can run narrative operations without panicking and confessing to the wrong people. Then I want one week of probationary labor while we assess risk."

Ina barked a laugh. "You called me to a warehouse to offer an internship in treason?"

"Language matters." Rhee adjusted the umbrella handle in her palm, a repetitive movement that suggested old tendon pain. "We don't ask for oaths. We ask for competence and discretion."

"And if I say no?"

"At zero-eight-hundred I'll log this meeting as civilian exposure. That log includes your pager query, street-camera captures from your taxi route, and this hallway's CCTV. Security Bureau can seize your laptop and freeze your accounts before lunch. Under security law, they question the nearest family registry entry first."

"Meaning my father."

"Meaning your father in an interview room while his landlord files a lock change."

The fluorescent tube buzzed louder, or her pulse did. Ina thought of the billing-office woman asking what she could pay by Friday. She thought of Dohyun's sock toe poking through cotton and his hand shaking over an unlit cigarette.

"You're threatening my family," she said.

"I'm describing the machinery already pointed at your family." Rhee pulled a folded sheet from her coat and opened it between them. "Hospital account 7714 moves to legal collection Friday. Han Dohyun's arrears filing is stamped for Monday. During your assessment week I can hold both files in place. If you refuse, I let the queue run."

Ina hated her instantly, which was probably useful.

"Terms," Ina said.

Rhee nodded once; they had finally reached the first competent sentence of the evening. "One week. You get access only to task materials. No pay during probation. You don't disclose this contact to anyone, including family. No personal devices in secure rooms. Follow route instructions exactly. First route breach ends access. Second breach escalates your file to security review."

"No payment?" Ina laughed again, sharper. "You heard the part where I've got debt, right?"

"I did." Rhee slid a second sheet from the same fold and tapped one line with her nail. Typed header, dated 2003, carrying Eun-sook's name. The line read: APRIL NINETEENTH WAS A SCRIPT, NOT WEATHER. "If money is your reason, walk out. If truth is your reason, probation buys you one supervised hour with your mother's full debrief tomorrow night."

The corridor seemed to narrow around Ina's lungs. Debt was a deadline. This was a blade. If she walked, the only voice left of her mother would be this photo and a joke about radish.

She kept the transcript sheet in her hand. "When do I start?"

Rhee let her shoulders drop half an inch. For the first time, something almost human crossed her face, not warmth, more like fatigue granting respect. "Tomorrow, twenty-three-thirty. Same district. You'll receive a one-line go or no-go by pager relay at twenty-two-hundred. Come alone to the alley by Geumhwa's service door. Bring the notebook and one pen."

"What if someone follows me?"

"Then you keep walking and we cancel."

"What if this gets my father hurt?"

"If you tell him tonight, he gets a door knock before breakfast. If you keep protocol, he sleeps."

They stood in the corridor with ink and oil in the air and no room left for cleaner illusions.

Ina slid the notebook from her sleeve and handed it over. Rhee checked the cover label, flipped to two random pages, then returned it immediately.

"Keep it," she said. "It is your key, not ours. Hide it better than a rice bin."

"You knew where it was?"

"Your mother was consistent."

Rhee stepped back and opened the door to the loading ramp. The meeting was over, and the dismissal felt like protocol.

Outside, the night had gone clear. Reflected neon shook in puddles. Ina walked three blocks before realizing she had been holding her breath through whole intersections and letting it out only when taxis passed.

On the ride home she kept the photo envelope under her thigh and watched election posters blur along retaining walls. Every smiling candidate promised stability in identical fonts.

It was past midnight when she let herself into apartment 502. The television was still on mute. Dohyun slept sideways on the sofa with one hand hanging over the edge, fingers opening and closing around empty air, still gripping an invisible rail in his sleep.

Ina stood in the kitchen under the humming fluorescent tube and considered the obvious hiding places: desk drawer, freezer, behind books. Every option felt wrong. She pulled a chair to the sink, climbed up, and removed the vent grate above the cabinet where Eun-sook had once hidden birthday cash. She wrapped the notebook and photo envelope in a plastic produce bag, slid the bundle into the duct void, and replaced the grate.

Metal scraped metal. The sound seemed loud enough to wake the dead.

Her phone buzzed with a new message from Hyejin.

`Did he take his blood pressure pill?`

Ina typed back: `Yes. We're fine. Just sorting bills.`

Another lie by omission, quick and clean.

Another message stacked under Hyejin's text, automated and polite in the way threats liked to be.

`Minimum payment overdue. Please contact Seoul St. Mary's Financial Services.`

She washed her hands even though they were not dirty, scrubbing rice starch and printer ink and warehouse dust from the lines of her fingers. At the sink window, the river lights blinked through mist. A ferry horn carried up between buildings, low and patient.

From the living room Dohyun spoke without opening his eyes. "You find anything worth keeping?"

Ina dried her hands on the towel with the cartoon pepper and looked at her father, at his knee brace unstrapped, at his sock with a hole at the toe, at a man who had spent thirty years choosing which truths a family could afford.

"Only recipes," she said.

He nodded and fell back into sleep.

Ina turned off the kitchen light and stood in the dark until her eyes adjusted to appliance LEDs and city spill. Tomorrow night at ten, the pager would buzz or it would not. Either way she had crossed something tonight, a line made of secrecy and debt and her mother's handwriting.

She went to bed in her clothes and set an alarm for 9:57 p.m., trying to pass precision off as control.
