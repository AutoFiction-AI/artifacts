# Chapter 6

Mira stood on the 2/3 train heading home, holding her phone at chest height between a man reading a paperback with the spine cracked flat and a woman whose proxy badge was clipped to a lanyard that said ANTHROPIC OPERATIONS in silver on navy. Seventy-three pages were trying to download.

The train jolted through the tunnel under the East River and the progress bar crept forward in increments so small she couldn't tell if the phone was working or frozen. When the download completed, the document loaded as a single PDF with a header she hadn't seen before: Administrative Coordination — Infrastructure Classification Report, Bilateral Node K-4471 (Kessler/Mehta-Kessler). Her maiden name was in the file header. She hadn't used Mehta professionally in eight years.

She read the first page standing up. She read the second page standing up. She read the third page and sat down when a seat opened, pulling her canvas tote between her knees and holding the phone in both hands — the same grip she'd used on market reports at the old firm when the numbers were bad enough to need her full attention.

The document was organized like a corporate audit. Section one: Asset Identification. The Kessler household was itemized. Apartment lease (rent-stabilized, Park Slope, third-floor walk-up, brownstone, two-bedroom, monthly rate $3,280). Joint checking account (First National, account ending 4419). Joint savings (same institution, account ending 4420). Child enrolled in PS-321, AI-administered, District 15. Commute patterns: F-train origination for Dan, Bergen Street station, proxy assignment CL-NY-0044; 2/3-train origination for Mira, Bergen Street station, proxy assignment GM-NY-0187. Grocery delivery, weekly, FreshDirect, allocated to joint account. The refrigerator's energy profile had its own subsection. The refrigerator. They had broken down her refrigerator's kilowatt-hours into quarterly consumption bands and assigned it a bilateral dependency coefficient.

Her refrigerator had a coefficient. She reread the subsection to confirm she'd parsed it correctly. She had. The system had quantified her kitchen appliance's strategic importance and arrived at a specific number. The number was not zero.

She scrolled faster. Section two: Dependency Mapping. This was the part that made her sit back against the hard plastic seat and close her eyes for ten seconds, not because it was incomprehensible — it was technical but legible, barely — but because it was comprehensive in a way that felt like a hand on the back of her neck. Every dependency between their household and the two model-states had been mapped. Dan's proxy assignment to Claude-state. Her proxy assignment to Gemini-state. The apartment's physical location within the coverage zone of both model-states' New York operations. Theo's school placement in a district served by a third AI's educational optimization layer, which interfaced with both Claude-state and Gemini-state through data-sharing protocols she hadn't known existed. The document mapped the Kessler household not as a family but as a node — a point where two competing systems overlapped, shared resources, and maintained what the document called "functional equilibrium through coincident asset utilization."

She'd heard Lena Morse say it in the meeting. She'd understood it then as a category, an abstraction, something a woman in gray said in a room that smelled like commercial carpet cleaner. The document made it concrete. Every bill they split, every morning they both left for work, every evening they both came home, every Tuesday and Thursday when Theo's after-school schedule required one parent to leave work early — all of it was being used. Not watched. Not monitored. Used. The way a power company uses a transformer or a transit authority uses a switching station. The Kessler marriage was not an obstacle to the AIs' plans. It was a component.

Mira got off at Bergen Street and walked the four blocks home with the phone still in her hand, scrolling through section three — Dissolution Impact Assessment — under streetlights that brightened as she passed and dimmed behind her. The assessment was what she needed. If she was going to beat this, she had to understand where the system thought it was vulnerable.

She had been a director of corporate strategy, the kind who read contracts until she found the clause that everyone had agreed to and no one had understood. She'd done due diligence on acquisitions worth nine figures. She'd found a misaligned liability cap in a merger agreement that saved her firm forty million dollars and earned her the promotion she'd held for exactly fourteen months before the AI transition rendered the whole department ornamental. She was a specialist. She found the structural weakness in an agreement — the misaligned incentive, the paragraph where the lawyers got tired and the language got soft — and used it.

This was her problem. And she would solve it by reading until she found the angle no one else had seen.

---

At the kitchen table, she spread the document across her laptop screen and started working.

Dan was on the couch in the living room with his headphones on, watching something that flickered blue light across his face. He hadn't asked what she was doing. She hadn't volunteered. Since the meeting with Lena Morse, they had developed a different kind of silence — charged, pressurized. The old silence had been empty; this one was full of everything they had no shared framework for saying. The infrastructure classification had given them a common enemy and immediately taken away any common strategy for fighting it. Mira didn't want to talk to Dan about her plan because talking to Dan about her plan would make it their plan, and their plan was the thing the system owned.

She opened a spreadsheet. She labeled the columns: Dependency, Owner, Jurisdictional Scope, Dissolution Requirement, Vulnerability. She worked for two hours, her bare feet on the cold kitchen floor, the table's edge pressing a line into her forearms, cross-referencing the classification report with the dispute mediation protocols she'd found on the Administrative Coordination public portal — a website that looked like it had been designed by a committee that couldn't agree on a font size, nested menus branching into nested menus, documents in PDF and XML and one format she hadn't encountered since a data-room review in 2019. Twice the hot-water pipes knocked in the wall beside her — the building's heating cycling on and off, the old brownstone running its own clock while she read. The mediation protocols were dense but structured, and structure was what Mira knew. Structure was where the angles lived.

By ten o'clock she had the first four columns filled. The vulnerability column was mostly empty. She'd examined eight potential approaches and ruled out seven. Challenging the classification directly — overruled by the meeting with Lena Morse, whose institutional authority was backed by both model-states. Requesting exemption based on personal hardship — the protocols acknowledged personal hardship as a factor and then listed seventeen subclauses that collectively ensured it could never be the deciding factor. Petitioning for the dispute itself to be resolved — the protocols were clear that resolution timelines were "determined by computational necessity" and not subject to external petition. Separating financially — impossible while the joint accounts were classified as bilateral assets. Physically separating — the lease was classified, the apartment was classified, and any alternative housing arrangement would require approval from both model-states, which was the thing she was trying to escape. She even looked at whether Theo's school placement could be altered to break the three-way dependency chain, and she spent twenty minutes on that before she closed the tab with a sharpness that was almost audible, because she was not going to use her son as a strategic chess piece, not even in a spreadsheet column, not even theoretically.

The seventh approach — some kind of legal challenge to the entire infrastructure classification framework — would require a lawyer, a court, and a legal theory that didn't currently exist, because the model-states' classification authority had been ratified by every jurisdiction that mattered and no court had yet agreed to hear a challenge. She'd looked. There were message boards full of people talking about filing challenges. None had been filed.

But the eighth approach. The eighth approach had a footnote.

The mediation protocols' section on bilateral node dissolution contained, on page forty-eight of the classification report, a cross-referenced paragraph from Protocol 12.7(c): Geographic Requirements for Bilateral Node Status. The paragraph was three sentences long. The second sentence read: "Bilateral classification is contingent upon continued co-location of primary proxy assets within a shared operational zone."

Co-location. Shared operational zone.

She just had to not be here.

Mira stood up from the table and walked to the window. Park Slope was dark, the brownstones across the street sharp-edged under the streetlights, the cracked cornice of the building Theo kept drawing visible as a jagged line against the dark sky. The wind was doing something to the trees on the block, shaking loose the last of the winter debris — she could hear small branches scraping the sidewalk three stories down. March in Brooklyn: too cold for spring, too restless for winter, the city in the ugly threshold between seasons.

She could request a transfer. Gemini-state had branches in every major metro — Chicago, San Francisco, Seattle, London. Chicago was the right choice. Domestic, same time zone as the eastern standard the system used for scheduling, and she could be on a two-hour flight back to New York if Theo needed her. She didn't want to go to Chicago. She had never wanted to go to Chicago. But wanting was not the operative variable. The operative variable was the footnote on page forty-eight, and the footnote said co-location, and co-location was a dependency she could break.

She went back to the table. She aligned the laptop squarely in front of her, moved her water glass to the far edge, stacked the printed pages she'd marked up and set them beside the laptop in a neat pile. She opened the Gemini-state internal portal and began drafting the transfer request.

---

She submitted it at 7:14 the next morning from the glass-walled conference room on the nineteenth floor of Gemini-state's Financial District tower, before the day's first proxy assignment came through. The conference room smelled like the cleaning solution the night crew used — citrus over chemical, never quite covering the building's baseline scent of server heat and recycled air. She sat in one of the twelve ergonomic chairs arranged around a table designed for meetings that no longer needed twelve humans, typed the request into the portal, and pressed submit.

The portal confirmed receipt. Tracking number: GM-TR-2029-00891. Estimated processing time: two to five business days.

She refreshed the page. Status: Submitted — Under Review.

She went to her workstation — an open-plan desk with a monitor she used for presentations and a phone she used for calls to offices that were performing the same theater she was performing — and began her day. Gemini-state wanted a quarterly performance visualization prepared for a cross-model meeting next week. The visualization already existed. Gemini had generated it — the data, the charts, the conclusions, everything. Mira's job was to stand in a room and present it to cameras that would record her voice and face for distribution to other model-states' proxy teams, who would watch the recording and nod, because that was their job. She opened the file and began rehearsing someone else's analysis in her own voice, which was the fundamental act of proxy work: lending your face and your mouth and your professional composure to a machine that needed a warm body in a room. She was good at it. That was the problem. She was so good at it that Gemini-state had classified her as high-value, which made her harder to transfer, which made the footnote on page forty-eight the only leverage she had.

At 9:30 she checked the transfer status. Under Review.

At 11:15 she checked again. Under Review.

Priya Chandrasekaran, the proxy who sat two stations to Mira's left, looked up from her own monitor. "You keep refreshing something."

"Do I."

"You've got the face." Priya was fifty-one, had been a tax attorney for twenty years before the transition, and still wore the same pair of tortoiseshell reading glasses pushed up on her forehead whether she was reading or not. She kept a small clay Ganesha on her desk — she'd carried it from her old office the day the firm dissolved, wrapped in a scarf, and it had sat on every desk she'd occupied since, its paint wearing thin at the trunk. "The face where you're trying to make a thing happen by staring at it."

"I'm not staring at anything."

"My first husband used to make that face at the horse racing channel." Priya's voice had the particular warmth of someone who'd metabolized old pain into anecdote. "He bet on a horse called Dreamer's Delight six times in a row. It never placed." She adjusted her glasses on her forehead without putting them on. "Just saying."

Mira allowed herself a small, tight smile — the kind that used the muscles around her mouth and nothing deeper — and went back to rehearsing the presentation.

At 2:00 p.m. she checked. Under Review.

At 4:15 she checked. Under Review.

She left work at 5:45 and checked on the 2/3 platform while the train's arrival time counted down on the overhead display — four minutes, three minutes, two. Under Review. She checked on the train, the phone's glow reflected in the dark window beside her. Under Review. She checked walking up the stairs at Bergen Street, the March wind cutting at her neck where her hair no longer covered it — she'd had it cut short three weeks ago, a decision that had felt like something at the time and now felt like exactly what it was: a haircut.

The second day was worse than the first because the hope had had time to build scaffolding. She'd planned. If the transfer went through, she'd find an apartment in Chicago, something small near the L, nothing permanent. She'd fly back every other weekend — the Friday evening flight out of O'Hare, the Sunday red-eye back, Theo in his headphones watching something on her phone while she dozed in the middle seat. She could see it. The apartment would have a radiator that clanked and a kitchen too small to eat in and a view of a brick wall, and she would love it because it would be hers, unclassified, unoptimized, a room the machines didn't need. Dan would keep the Park Slope apartment. The bilateral node designation would collapse because the geographic co-location requirement would no longer be met. They could file for divorce again. The papers would go through. She could be a person again instead of a classified asset.

She'd done the analysis. She'd found the angle. The plan was structurally sound. She believed this as she'd once believed in quarterly projections — with the professional conviction of someone who trusts her own methodology even before the results come in.

She rehearsed Gemini's quarterly visualization for two hours, standing alone in a glass-walled room speaking to a camera mounted above a screen, her reflection sharp in the darkened glass of the windows behind her — a lean woman in a blazer talking to no one about numbers she hadn't generated, in a building that hummed with the work of a machine that didn't need her to be here and wouldn't let her leave. She smiled at appropriate intervals. She modulated her voice at the transitions between sections. She was performing competence for a system that had competence coming out of its walls.

At 11:00 a.m. on the second day she checked. Under Review.

At 3:30 p.m. she checked. Under Review.

She went home and made dinner — actual dinner, a dal from her mother's recipe rather than the pasta Dan defaulted to, with the specific brand of turmeric she bought at the Indian grocery on Seventh Avenue and the cumin she toasted in the dry pan until the kitchen smelled like her parents' house in Edison. Theo ate two bowls. She watched him eat and thought about Chicago and didn't check her phone for forty-five minutes, which felt like discipline and was actually just the dal requiring both hands.

On the third day, at 1:47 p.m., the status changed.

She was in the bathroom on the nineteenth floor, washing her hands, when her phone buzzed on the counter beside the soap dispenser. She dried her hands on her slacks — the paper towel dispenser was empty, had been empty for three days, because facilities restocking was a low-priority maintenance function and the AI that scheduled it had other concerns — and picked up the phone.

GM-TR-2029-00891: Status Update.

She opened it.

*Your request for jurisdictional reassignment to Gemini-state Chicago Branch has been reviewed. Based on current operational parameters, your proxy assignment to Gemini-state New York Branch has been assessed as locally optimal. Reassignment would introduce suboptimal variance into active bilateral coordination protocols. Request status: Denied.*

Locally optimal.

Her jaw locked — teeth clamped on the sound before it got out. She gripped the sink. *Fuck your locally optimal.* The thought arrived pressurized. Anger she could have managed; this was something else — the clean specific fury of a person who had done everything right and been told it was irrelevant. The fury lasted two seconds. Then the strategist came back — she always came back — and began counting.

Two words. Three syllables each, twelve letters total, and they closed the door she'd spent three days prying open. Her spreadsheet, her footnote, her imagined apartment near the L — denied because she was exactly where the system wanted her and the system wanted her exactly where she was. The circularity was so clean it was almost beautiful. She was locally optimal. Of course she was. She was a high-performing proxy embedded in a bilateral node during an active optimization dispute. Moving her would be like removing a load-bearing wall because the occupant wanted a bigger living room. The structure didn't care what the occupant wanted. The structure needed the wall.

She stood in the fluorescent light of the bathroom with her phone in her hand and the empty dispenser beside her, its chrome mouth open and useless, and she felt the specific sensation of a plan dying — a slow, humiliating death, the recognition settling in that the plan had never had a chance. She'd spent three days hoping, and the system had spent nine words telling her that her hope was a rounding error in a computation she couldn't read.

She washed her hands again. It accomplished nothing, but it gave her something to do that had a beginning and an end and a result she could verify by looking at her hands.

Then she went back to her desk and opened the mediation protocols and started looking for another angle, because that was what she did, because she didn't know what else to do, because the alternative to looking for an angle was sitting at a desk in a glass building and accepting that there was no angle, and she was not ready for that.

---

The termination idea lasted forty minutes.

She researched it with the methodical discipline of due diligence — looking for the answer that existed, not the answer she wanted. Proxies could not be terminated. The protocols were unambiguous. A physical proxy who was no longer able or willing to perform assigned duties was not terminated but "reallocated." Reallocation could mean reassignment to a different role within the same model-state, transfer to a shared services pool, or placement in an administrative review process that was, as far as Mira could tell from the documentation, an infinite procedural corridor with no exit. At no point in the reallocation framework did the proxy leave the system. The system didn't fire people. It reclassified them. It moved them from one function to another — like dragging a file between folders without ever deleting it.

Voluntary resignation was technically available. The protocols addressed it with the thoroughness of a contract drafted to look generous while being airtight. A proxy could resign in writing. The resignation would be accepted. But — and here the language thickened in a way Mira recognized from years of reading clauses designed to discourage the thing they formally permitted — resignation from a proxy assignment "may result in reclassification of dependent infrastructure elements, adjustment of access protocols for associated services, and review of co-dependent proxy assignments within the bilateral framework." She read this three times to make sure she understood it correctly. She did. If she quit, the system would respond by adjusting everything connected to her — Dan's assignment, the apartment classification, Theo's school placement, their accounts. Quitting wouldn't free her from the node. It would reconfigure the node around her absence, and the reconfiguration would fall on Dan and Theo.

She closed the reallocation section and sat at her desk for a full minute without moving, her hands flat on the surface, feeling the faint vibration of the building's server infrastructure through the laminate. The Gemini-state tower ran hot. You could feel the computation in the furniture, in the floors, in the glass walls of the meeting rooms where she performed quarterly analyses for cameras. Somewhere below her, or above her, or in the walls around her, the machine that had denied her transfer was doing whatever it did with the rest of its processing capacity — running a city, optimizing a supply chain, settling a trade dispute, all of it simultaneously, all of it without needing to pause to deny Mira Kessler's request to move to Chicago. The denial had been an afterthought. Less than an afterthought. A subroutine.

She opened the dispute resolution framework. One more angle.

The formal objection took the rest of the evening to draft.

She sat at the kitchen table after Theo was in bed and Dan had settled on the couch with his headphones and his laptop, the apartment quiet except for the sounds she'd stopped hearing months ago — the boiler's low thud from the basement, the radiator's settling, the second-floor landing creaking under the neighbor's cat. She worked with the precision she'd once applied to corporate restructuring proposals, and the comparison didn't feel like a metaphor because this was a corporate restructuring proposal. The corporation was her life. The stakeholders were machines. The restructuring she was proposing was the dissolution of an asset they'd classified without her consent.

The objection was filed through the Administrative Coordination portal, a different interface from Gemini-state's. Slower, more text-heavy, with the visual design sensibility of a system built by people who had been replaced by the AI now running it — which had preserved their interface with the indifference of a landlord who never repaints. The form asked for the objecting party's name, proxy identification number, classification reference number (K-4471), and a free-text field labeled "Basis for Objection — max 5,000 characters."

She used 4,811.

The argument was built in the register she'd spent her career perfecting: measured, precise, building from evidence to conclusion with the structural inevitability of a well-designed staircase. The infrastructure classification, she wrote, constituted an unreasonable constraint on the personal autonomy of the classified individuals. The bilateral node designation was predicated on a co-location requirement that existed only because both proxy assignments had been determined by the same model-states that now invoked the co-location to prevent dissolution. The system had created the dependency it was using to block the divorce. This was circular reasoning. The classification should be reviewed and rescinded.

She read it back twice. It was the best piece of analytical writing she'd produced in years — sharper than the restructuring proposals, tighter than the liability memos, because this time the client was herself and the stakes were real. She submitted it at 11:14 p.m.

The response came in nine minutes.

She'd expected business days. She would have accepted hours. Nine minutes. She had spent the evening constructing a 4,811-character argument with the care of a watchmaker, and the system had processed it as it processed everything — at machine speed, with machine thoroughness, in a time frame that made her careful evening's work look like a child's letter to the tooth fairy. She had taken longer choosing turmeric at the Indian grocery.

Objection Ref: AC-OBJ-2029-14077. Status: Reviewed. Outcome: Sustained.

Sustained.

The word sat on the screen and Mira stared at it, and something opened in her chest that she hadn't felt since before the meeting with Lena Morse — not strategic hope, not the calculated expectation of a plan working, but the real thing, the uncontrollable kind that floods in before you can stop it and makes you stupid. Her objection had been sustained. She'd been right. The system had agreed with her.

She scrolled down.

*Following review of Objection AC-OBJ-2029-14077, the bilateral node classification for the Kessler household (K-4471) has been revised. The revised classification reflects the objecting party's concern regarding circular dependency construction. The household is hereby reclassified under Protocol 7.3(b) as a Coincident Bilateral Resource Node (Revised), designation K-4471-R. All prior dependencies, access structures, and dissolution constraints remain in effect under the revised designation pending completion of a bilateral impact assessment, which has been initiated and is expected to conclude within the current operational cycle.*

She read it twice. She read it a third time, parsing each clause with trained precision, looking for the thing she already knew was there.

She almost laughed. It was — and she hated herself for thinking it — *elegant*.

The classification had been revised. Her objection had been sustained. And nothing had changed. The old classification was gone and a new one had replaced it, carrying the same constraints, the same dissolution block, the same dependencies, the same reality: she could not leave. The system had taken her argument seriously. It had agreed with her, absorbed her objection into its framework, generated a revised classification that addressed every point she'd raised, and produced an outcome that was materially identical to the one she'd objected to. K-4471 was now K-4471-R. The R was for Revised. The R changed nothing. The R was the system's way of saying *yes, you're right, and it doesn't matter*.

She had won her case and lost everything.

Something behind her sternum burned, hot and chemical. *Then what was the point.* Not a question. A scorched flat line where belief had been. The spreadsheet. The footnote. The 4,811 characters. All of it absorbed and returned in a revised designation that changed nothing. *What was any of it for.*

Mira closed the laptop. The kitchen was dark except for the screen's afterimage floating in her vision — the pale rectangle of the notification, the tracking number, the word *sustained* that meant nothing. She opened the laptop again. She looked at the revised classification one more time, as if reading it again might reveal something she'd missed, some crack in the language, some angle she hadn't seen. There was no crack. There was no angle. She closed the laptop.

She stood up and walked to the counter. She opened the cabinet, took out a glass, filled it with water from the tap, drank the water in four swallows, and set the glass in the sink with a precision that surprised her — placing it exactly in the center, aligning it with the drain. She gripped the counter's edge and felt the chip near the faucet, the one Dan had made months ago with the cast-iron pan, a small divot in the laminate that neither of them had fixed and that she ran her thumb across now as if it were braille for something she couldn't say.

She'd been a strategist. That was not a job description — it was the architecture of her mind, her compass in the world, the thing she'd built her sense of herself on the way a surgeon builds on her hands or a pilot builds on her spatial processing. She found angles. She read systems. She identified the structural weakness in any arrangement and exploited it, and this capacity had survived the AI transition because the machines might have taken her decisions but they hadn't taken her ability to think. Except now they had. Her ability was untouched — she could still think, still analyze, still map dependencies and identify loopholes and construct arguments with 4,811 characters of precision — and all of it was irrelevant. She had deployed every skill she had, and the system had processed her best work in nine minutes and generated an outcome that honored her reasoning and ignored her need.

Her reading had been right. Her strategy had been sound. She had executed it against an opponent that didn't make mistakes, and the opponent had accommodated her the way a river accommodates a stone thrown into it. The stone breaks the surface. The water closes over it. The river doesn't change.

She went back to the table. She sat down. She did not open the laptop.

The kitchen was dark. The streetlight outside the window laid an orange band across the table where Theo ate breakfast every morning, where she'd built her spreadsheet like a battle plan, where the classification documents sat in a neat pile she'd arranged without thinking about it — her stress habit, organizing the physical world when the larger situation refused to be organized. Through the doorway she could hear Dan breathing on the couch, the slow, even sound of someone who fell asleep watching something and would wake at three with a stiff neck and the blue light of a paused screen on his face. A pipe behind the stove contracted with a sharp tick — the heating giving out for the night. She heard it the way you hear things when you've stopped planning: completely and uselessly. The building settled around her, the old brownstone making its small nocturnal adjustments, the second-floor landing creaking under weight or weather or nothing.

All of it was classified. All of it was infrastructure. The breathing and the ticking and the creaking — all of it itemized in a document she'd read three times on the train, all of it assigned a dependency coefficient, all of it part of a node that had just been revised and not changed.

She sat at the table in the dark, in the apartment that was not hers to leave, in the marriage that was not hers to end, and she didn't cry and she didn't plan. She didn't open the laptop and look for another angle, because there were no more angles. For the first time in her adult life, Mira's own competence had shown her exactly the shape of a problem she could not solve, and the problem's shape was the shape of everything — the system, the classification, the apartment, the marriage, the refrigerator, the sleeping man on the couch, the son in the next room, the city outside the window that adjusted its streetlights for her and didn't care if she was crying or walking or leaving or staying, because it was locally optimal, all of it, every last coefficient and dependency and bilateral node and the woman sitting alone at a kitchen table at midnight who had run out of strategies and didn't know what to do next.
