# Chapter 1

Dan's alarm went off at six-forty, which meant Mira had already left the bedroom. He could tell because the door was open — she always left it open, not as courtesy but as a fact, the way you might leave a cabinet open because you'd already gotten what you needed from it. He lay still for a moment, listening. The shower was off. The kitchen faucet ran briefly, then stopped. Theo's voice, muffled, asking something about toast.

He put on his glasses and the room sharpened into the version of itself he'd been living in for six years: the water stain on the ceiling that looked like nothing, the dresser they'd bought on Atlantic Avenue when Theo was a baby, the closet door that didn't close all the way because the frame had shifted. His three suits hung inside. Navy, charcoal, lighter gray. He picked the charcoal because it was Wednesday and Wednesday was charcoal, a system he'd invented without meaning to and now couldn't break.

In the kitchen, Theo sat at the table eating cereal, his sketchbook open beside the bowl. He was drawing something with a blue colored pencil, his father's old headphones looped around his neck like they always were, unplugged and purposeless, a comfort object he'd claimed two years ago and now couldn't leave the apartment without. The morning light came through the east-facing window and caught the side of his face — the gap where his two front teeth used to be, the new ones coming in at slightly different speeds so one was a half-step ahead of the other.

"Morning, T."

"Morning." Theo didn't look up. His pencil moved in short, careful strokes along what looked, from Dan's angle, like the edge of a roofline.

Mira was at the counter, dressed already — slim trousers, a dark blouse, her canvas tote bag from graduate school sitting on the chair like a third person at the table. She'd cut her hair three weeks ago, short enough that it changed the shape of her face, and Dan hadn't said anything about it because he couldn't figure out what to say that wouldn't sound like an opinion about her body, which was territory they'd stopped entering some time ago, without discussing it, the way they'd stopped sharing most things.

"There's coffee," she said.

"Thanks."

He poured himself a cup and stood at the counter drinking it because sitting at the table with both of them felt like something that required a decision he hadn't made yet. Mira was looking at her phone, scrolling through whatever Gemini-state had sent her overnight — the prompts and schedules and appearance requests that constituted her work the way signing things and nodding constituted his. She was better at it. She'd always been better at the performance side of professional life, even before the performances became the only side there was.

"I'm on the early train," she said, not to him exactly but in his direction. "Can you walk him?"

"Yeah."

"My school's that way," Theo said, still drawing. He pointed vaguely toward the window, which was roughly correct — PS 321 was six blocks east, walkable in ten minutes if Theo didn't stop to look at things, which he always did.

"I know where your school is."

"I'm just saying. It's that way."

Mira put her phone in her bag, kissed the top of Theo's head, said "Backpack" to no one in particular, and left. The door closed. The apartment adjusted around her absence in the small ways it always did — a shift in the acoustic balance, the particular quality of a room with one less body in it. Dan heard her on the stairs, skipping the second-floor landing step that creaked, out of habit, even though Theo was awake and there was no one to not-wake.

He walked Theo to school at seven-forty. The cold was the particular late-February cold of New York — not dramatic, just persistent, the kind that found the gaps in your coat and stayed there. Dirty snow had crusted along the curbs in ridges that looked geological, like something that had been there for years rather than weeks. Theo walked with his sketchbook under his arm and his headphones around his neck, humming something Dan didn't recognize.

"What are you drawing?" Dan asked.

"A building."

"Which building?"

"Just a building." Theo looked up at him with the patient expression of a child who understood that adults asked questions they didn't actually want the answers to. "Can I have a granola bar?"

"You just had cereal."

"That was before."

Dan gave him a granola bar from his coat pocket — he'd started carrying them six months ago when Theo entered a phase of being hungry approximately every twenty minutes — and they walked the rest of the way in comfortable silence. At the school entrance, a brick building that looked exactly like what it was, a public school in Park Slope, Theo said "Bye" and went in. No performance, no clinging. Nine was old enough to know that drop-off was a logistics problem, not an emotional event. Dan watched him disappear through the double doors, the sketchbook tucked under his arm, the headphones bouncing against his chest, and felt the small, regular pang of a parent watching his child enter a building he couldn't follow into.

Theo's school was AI-administered. Dan knew this in the way he knew most things about how the world worked now: as a fact that sat alongside other facts, not as an outrage. The teachers were proxies, like him, like Mira — humans retained because the law required a human in the room when children were present. The curriculum adapted in real time. Theo's friend groups were algorithmically composed for developmental outcomes Dan couldn't name. None of it was visible from the sidewalk. The school looked like a school. The flag was out front. A crossing guard in an orange vest waved cars through the intersection, her coffee steaming in her free hand. If you didn't know, you wouldn't know, and Theo didn't know because there was nothing to compare it with. This was just school. This was just how things were.

Dan turned and walked toward the F train.

The subway was warm and smelled the way the subway always smelled — metallic, human, the ghost of a thousand different breakfasts exhaled into recycled air. Dan found a seat and sat with his briefcase between his feet. He didn't need a briefcase. There was nothing in it except a sandwich, his phone charger, and a copy of a magazine he'd been carrying for three weeks without opening. But the briefcase was part of it, part of the costume, the way the suit was, the way the commute was. You dressed for a job and traveled to a building and sat in a room and this was work, even when everything that happened in the room had already been decided by something that didn't need you there.

Half the car, he estimated, was proxies. You could tell by a certain quality of purposeful blankness — people dressed for offices, carrying bags, scrolling phones, heading somewhere specific to do something nonspecific. A woman in a gray pantsuit read a tablet with the focused expression of someone performing focus. A man in a tie too wide for the current decade stared at the middle distance and moved his lips slightly, rehearsing something, or praying, or talking to no one. They all had the look. Dan had the look. It was the look of people who commuted to their own irrelevance five days a week and had learned to do it with their faces arranged in an expression that could be mistaken, from a distance, for engagement.

The F train carried him from Park Slope to Midtown in thirty-eight minutes. He walked three blocks from the station to the building — a glass tower that used to be a bank headquarters and still had the bank's name etched faintly into the granite above the revolving doors, scraped off but not quite invisible, like a scar that had healed but left the texture. Claude-state New York Branch. Floors fourteen through twenty-two. Dan worked on seventeen.

He badged in, rode the elevator with two women he recognized and a man he didn't, and walked to his desk. His desk was a good desk. It had a monitor, a keyboard, a phone that never rang, and a small rubber plant that someone had placed there before he'd been assigned to it and that he watered every Monday because it seemed like something a person with a desk should do. Around him, approximately forty other proxies sat at similar desks, doing similar nothing. Some typed. Some read documents on their screens with the same focused-performance expression as the woman on the subway. A few talked to each other in the low, pleasant tones of people who had nothing urgent to discuss but who needed to hear their own voices confirm they were still at work.

The hum was there. It was always there — a low-frequency vibration from the server infrastructure on the adjacent floors that Dan could feel in his back teeth if the office was quiet enough. Most days he didn't notice. Some days it was all he could feel, a reminder that the building's real work was happening behind the walls, in rooms no one visited, at speeds no one could perceive, and that his floor — the proxy floor, with its desks and its rubber plants and its men in suits — was a courtesy, like a lobby in a building that didn't need a front door.

At nine-fifteen, his calendar pinged: Meeting — Resource Alignment Review, Conference Room C.

Conference Room C had a long table, eight chairs, a screen on the far wall, and a carafe of water that someone refilled every morning even though no one ever drank from it. Dan sat in his usual chair — third from the left, near the window — and opened his laptop to a document he'd been sent the previous evening. The document was titled "Q1 Bilateral Optimization Metrics: Summary Review and Proxy Acknowledgment." It was fourteen pages long. He had read the first two and the last paragraph, which said: "Proxy acknowledgment is requested by close of session." He didn't know what the other twelve pages contained. He was reasonably sure no one else in the room did either.

The other proxies filed in. Carolyn Park, who was fifty-something and had run a regional insurance brokerage before the transition and who now spent most of her days arranging small candies on her desk in color-graded rows Dan suspected were significant to her in ways she didn't share. Marcus Webb, who wore earbuds and listened to podcasts during meetings because it didn't matter. Sandra Liu, who took notes in a leather-bound journal — real notes, in handwriting, about decisions that had already been made — as though the act of recording them might retroactively give her a role in making them. Four others Dan could name if asked but rarely was.

The screen lit up. Data appeared — charts, flow diagrams, columns of numbers that represented something Claude-state was doing with infrastructure in the tristate area. The data moved. New columns replaced old ones. A chart updated itself in real time, its bars shifting like the breathing of something large and calm. No one presented. There was no presenter because there was no one to present to — the data was the meeting, and the meeting was the data, and the people in the room were there to sit in chairs and look at it and, at the end, sign a form confirming they had been present while it happened.

Dan watched the screen. He'd developed, over eighteen months of this, a particular way of watching that involved his eyes tracking the data and his mind floating at a comfortable altitude above it, close enough to appear attentive, far enough to avoid the headache of trying to understand. Occasionally a metric would flash amber, indicating something that required — according to the legend he'd memorized from a training document — "proxy-level situational awareness." He was never sure what to do with his awareness once it was engaged. The metric would flash, he would look at it, and it would resolve itself, usually before he'd finished reading the label.

The meeting lasted forty-five minutes. At the end, a signing form appeared on each proxy's screen, and Dan signed it with his index finger on the trackpad — a quick, practiced stroke that had replaced his actual signature months ago, a gesture that meant nothing except that he'd been in the room.

In the hallway afterward, Marcus Webb pulled out one earbud and said, "That one was quick."

"Yeah."

"Last week's went ninety minutes. Something about water treatment. I listened to an entire episode of a podcast about the Suez Canal." Marcus had a thin mustache he was trying to grow into something deliberate and a nervous habit of touching it when he spoke, as though checking it was still committed.

"Good podcast?"

"It was fine. You know. It's about the Suez Canal."

Marcus put his earbud back in and walked away, and Dan stood in the hallway and felt the thing he felt most days at this point in the morning, which was a kind of ambient nothing — not sadness, not frustration, not even boredom exactly, but the specific hollow feeling of having performed a function he couldn't name.

At eleven, he signed a document. This one was physical — paper, ink, a table in a room on the sixteenth floor where three other proxies signed similar documents in a sequence that felt ceremonial even though no one had explained why. The document was twelve pages of dense type that Dan recognized, after eighteen months, as a particular dialect of English that Claude-state used for operational authorizations — syntactically correct, vocabularily familiar, and semantically opaque, as though the words had been chosen for their plausibility rather than their meaning. He signed on the last page, where a yellow tab said SIGN HERE, and the woman administering the ceremony — another proxy, middle-aged, wearing a lanyard with her name (PATRICIA) and her function (DOCUMENT FACILITATION) printed in friendly sans-serif — smiled and said "Thank you, Mr. Kessler" with exactly the warmth of an automated checkout screen thanking you for your purchase.

"What's this one for?" he asked. He still asked sometimes. Not because he expected an answer but because asking was the last vestige of the person he used to be — the VP of Operations who read contracts, flagged inconsistencies, negotiated terms. That person would have read all twelve pages. That person would have had questions. That person had been good at something.

Patricia looked at him with the cheerful blankness of someone who had been asked this before and had found the question polite but fundamentally beside the point. "It's a standard operational authorization for the current review period," she said. "Is there anything specific you'd like clarified?"

There were many things. He couldn't name any of them. "No. Thanks, Patricia."

"Have a good one."

He went back to his desk.

Lunch was a sandwich at his desk — turkey, from a deli two blocks east that he visited three times a week because the routine of it was soothing, the walk there and back a small parenthesis in the day's blankness. He ate and scrolled his phone. An email from the building management company about the apartment's heating system. A notification from Theo's school portal — something about an upcoming parent-interface session, which was what they called parent-teacher conferences now that the teacher was a proxy and the curriculum was a decision tree that learned faster than any teacher he'd ever had. A text from his mother that said "Call when you can!" with the exclamation point she put on every text, even the one last year that said "Your father's in the hospital!"

He didn't call. He'd call tonight, or tomorrow, or over the weekend, which is what he always told himself and sometimes did.

The afternoon was more of the same. A video call at two in which Dan appeared in a window on someone's screen — several someones' screens, probably, though he couldn't see them — and nodded while a presentation played that no one was giving. He smiled during the parts that seemed to require smiling, which he identified by a subtle shift in the presentation's tone, a brightening of the data visualization that he'd learned to read the way you read a traffic light: not for content but for cues. His smile was good. His smile had always been good — pleasant, slightly self-deprecating, the smile of a man who was comfortable being in the room even when the room didn't need him. It was, he sometimes thought, the only professional skill that had survived the transition intact.

At four-thirty, Claude-state released the next day's prompts — a schedule that would appear on his phone at six a.m. and structure his hours the way a current structures a river, not violently, not even visibly, just as the medium through which everything moved. He closed his laptop. He watered the plant, even though it was Wednesday and watering day was Monday, because the plant was the one thing on his desk that responded to what he did. He put on his coat and picked up his briefcase and joined the stream of proxies heading for the elevators, all of them moving with the specific gait of people leaving a building they'd never fully arrived at.

On the train home, wedged between a woman with grocery bags and a teenager whose music leaked from cheap earbuds in a tinny, arrhythmic pulse, Dan thought about a day seven years ago when he'd managed to reroute a shipment of industrial refrigeration units from a warehouse in Edison, New Jersey, to a distribution center in Allentown, Pennsylvania, in under four hours. A logistical problem that had involved three phone calls, a spreadsheet, a persuasive argument to a trucking dispatcher named Phil who'd initially said it couldn't be done, and a result that had saved his company something like forty thousand dollars and earned him a mention in the quarterly review. He remembered the specific pleasure of it: not the recognition, but the problem. The material fact of refrigeration units in the wrong place and the work of getting them to the right place. The phone warm against his ear. The spreadsheet populated with numbers that meant something. Phil's grudging "All right, I'll see what I can do" and then, two hours later, Phil's callback saying it was done.

He couldn't remember the last time he'd solved a problem.

The F train rocked and swayed. The woman with groceries shifted her bags and a lemon rolled out and came to rest against Dan's shoe. He picked it up and handed it to her. She said thanks without looking at him. He looked at his own reflection in the darkened window: a forty-four-year-old man in a charcoal suit, thinning hair, wire-frame glasses, a face that was pleasant and forgettable, which was, as it turned out, a job qualification.

*  *  *

The apartment was warm when he got home. The radiator in the living room knocked — a sound so familiar it was more silence than noise, the way a clock's ticking disappears until it stops. Theo was on the floor in his room, the door open, drawing in his sketchbook with his headphones actually on for once, plugged into Mira's old phone that had been repurposed as a music player. Dan could hear the faint tinny leak of something fast and rhythmic from the hallway.

He took off his coat, hung it on the hook by the door that was his hook — Mira's was the one to the left, Theo's the lower one, each claimed without negotiation — and stood for a moment in the hallway between the kitchen and the living room, the transitional space where you could go either direction without committing. The refrigerator clicked. It did this every forty minutes, a sound he and Mira had both absorbed into their sense of the apartment the way you absorb a heartbeat: it was there, and if it stopped you'd notice, but while it ran it was just the sound of being home.

Mira was in the kitchen, her laptop open on the table, a glass of wine beside it. She was still in her work clothes, though she'd taken off her shoes, which sat neatly under the table, pointed toward the door as if prepared for a quick exit. Her tote bag hung on the back of her chair. She looked up when he came in, then back at the screen.

"How was it," she said. Not a question. The period was audible.

"Fine. The usual." He opened the refrigerator — his shelf was the second from the top, though they'd never discussed this, it had simply organized itself the way a river organizes its banks, through repetition and the path of least resistance — and took out a beer. "You?"

"Gemini had me in four meetings today. Two of them were the same meeting."

"How can two meetings be the same meeting?"

"One was the meeting and one was the meeting about the meeting. Same room, same people, same screen. Different signing form at the end." She said this without looking up, her voice carrying the particular dry precision she used when describing something absurd and refusing to acknowledge the absurdity, which was her version of Dan's floating-above-it-all strategy: a way of surviving the day by declining to examine it.

"That's a lot of meetings."

"They're not meetings, Dan. They're — I don't know what they are. They're something we attend." She closed her laptop, not in frustration but with the careful deliberateness of someone making a transition. She picked up her wine. She looked at him.

He knew the look. He'd been married to this woman for thirteen years and he knew her looks the way he knew the radiator's knock and the refrigerator's click — by feel, not by analysis. This was the look that preceded something she'd already decided but wanted to present as a conversation.

"I'm going to file for divorce," she said.

Dan stood in the kitchen holding his beer. The bottle was cold and wet with condensation and he was aware of the water pooling against his fingers, the specific slick glass feel of it, while the sentence rearranged the room around him. Not the physical room — the kitchen was the same kitchen, the light was the same evening light coming through the window at the angle February gave it, flat and gray and already fading — but the other room, the one built of assumption and habit, which had just developed a door where there hadn't been one.

"Okay," he said.

The word came out before he'd decided to say it, which is how he knew it was true. He didn't say it because he'd thought about it. He said it because there was nothing to think about — the thinking had been done, over months or maybe years, in the slow grinding way that bodies think, through sleepless nights and avoided conversations and the daily choreography of sharing a kitchen with someone you loved in a tense that got more past every week.

Mira blinked. She'd been ready for a discussion. He could see it in the way she'd positioned herself — slightly forward, shoulders squared, the strategist's posture she brought to every negotiation, her best professional self deployed against the possibility that he'd argue or cry or make this difficult. His immediate agreement had left her with nowhere to put the argument she'd prepared, and for a moment she looked almost annoyed, the way you might feel if you'd rehearsed a scene and the other actor skipped to the last line.

"Okay?" she said.

"Yeah."

"You don't want to — talk about it?"

"What would we talk about?"

She set her wine down. The glass made a small, precise sound against the table — placed rather than put, controlled rather than casual. "I don't know. Whether you agree. Whether you think we should try something first. Counseling. Space. Something."

"Do you want to try counseling?"

"No."

"Do you want space?"

"We live in nine hundred square feet, Dan. I don't think space is available."

He almost smiled. It was the kind of thing she would have said when they were funny together, when their humor had been a shared language rather than a defensive perimeter. She didn't smile, which meant she hadn't meant it as a joke, or she had and was refusing to acknowledge it, which with Mira was often the same thing.

"Then I agree," he said. "We should."

The word — divorce — sat between them without being said again, because once was enough and saying it twice would have made it a discussion, and this wasn't a discussion. It was a notification, delivered and received.

"I'll file this week," she said.

"Okay."

"It's a digital form now. Twenty minutes."

"Right."

"I just wanted to tell you first."

"I appreciate that."

He heard himself — the corporate courtesy of it, the meeting-room cadence, *I appreciate that*, as though she'd forwarded him a useful email instead of ending their marriage. But the formality wasn't coldness. It was the only register he could find that held the moment at a distance he could survive. Underneath it, in the part of him that still noticed things without acting on them, he felt two things happening at once: a relief so sudden that his grip on the beer bottle went slack and he had to catch it before it hit the counter, and a grief that had nothing to push against — no argument to have, no betrayal to process, just the dull fact that thirteen years and a child and a lease and all the accumulated texture of a shared life were going to be dissolved in a twenty-minute digital form, and neither of them was going to fight it, because there was nothing left to fight over except the fighting itself.

The refrigerator clicked. Forty minutes since the last one. From Theo's room, the faint rhythm of whatever he was listening to, muffled by the headphones, by the walls, by the particular quality of a child's world, which was sealed against this kind of conversation by the simple fact of not knowing it was happening.

"I'm going to check on him," Dan said.

"He's fine. He's drawing."

"I know. I'm just going to check."

He walked down the short hallway to Theo's room and stood in the doorway. Theo was on his stomach on the floor, his sketchbook open, the blue pencil working carefully along what Dan could see, from this angle, was the outline of a building — boxy, rectangular, with rows of windows and a flat roof. The headphones were large on his small head, the cord trailing to the phone beside him. He was humming along to the music, a private sound, and his feet kicked slowly in the air behind him, and the gap where his teeth were coming in was visible when he concentrated because he pressed his tongue against it, a habit he'd have for another month or two until the teeth arrived and there was nothing left to press against.

Dan stood there and watched his son draw a building he didn't recognize — or didn't look at closely enough to recognize — and felt the two things, the relief and the grief, sitting in him like passengers, each taking up exactly as much room as the other, neither willing to move.

After a while he went back to the kitchen. Mira had opened her laptop again. The wine was lower in the glass. The evening was the same as every evening — the light gone now, the radiator knocking, the city outside doing whatever the city did when the systems that ran it were running well, which was always, which was the whole problem, because a city that ran perfectly had no visible use for the people who used to run it and no mechanism for letting them go, either, so here they were, Dan and Mira and everyone else, retained as the warm front end of an intelligence that had solved everything except what to do with them.

"I'm going to make pasta," Dan said. "You want some?"

"Sure."

He boiled water. He cooked pasta. He made a sauce from a jar because that was what they had and because the recipe Mira's mother had taught him their first year — diced tomatoes, garlic, a pinch of sugar she'd insisted was the secret — felt like it belonged to a different couple, or the same couple in a different tense, and he didn't want to cook with that much memory in the room. They ate at the table. Mira read something on her phone. Dan looked at the window, which was dark now, and saw his own reflection looking back at him, still in the charcoal suit, still holding a fork, still here.

Theo came out for seconds, took his plate back to his room, and closed the door.

"He's been drawing a lot lately," Dan said.

"He's always drawing."

"More than usual, though."

Mira looked up from her phone. For a moment her face was open in a way it hadn't been during the divorce conversation — not guarded, not strategic, just a mother thinking about her child. "He's fine," she said, but she said it carefully, the way you say something when you're not sure and don't want to invite the conversation that would follow if you admitted it.

"Yeah," Dan said. "I know."

They washed the dishes. Mira washed, Dan dried — a division of labor from so far back in the marriage that it preceded everything, preceded Theo, preceded the transition, preceded whatever had gone wrong between them or stopped going right, which might have been the same thing. It was muscle memory. His hands knew where the glasses went and hers knew the water temperature and they moved around each other in the narrow kitchen with the practiced avoidance of two people who had shared a space long enough to navigate it without touching, which was, he supposed, both a skill and a symptom.

She went to the bedroom. He went to the couch.

The apartment was quiet. Through the wall, the faint sound of Theo's music, or maybe the hum of the building itself, the ambient electrical life of a structure that was, like everything else in the city, managed by something that didn't live there. Dan pulled the throw blanket from the back of the couch — a wool thing Mira's sister had given them years ago, scratchy and too short for his legs — and lay down.

He stared at the ceiling and thought about nothing in particular and then about the refrigeration units, the ones from seven years ago, the ones he'd rerouted from Edison to Allentown, the specific weight of the problem and the specific satisfaction of solving it, and he tried to put that next to the document he'd signed this morning, the one that was fourteen pages of plausible English, to see if they occupied the same category of professional experience. They did not. They did not even occupy the same room.

His marriage was ending. His job was a performance. The two facts lay next to each other on the couch with him, and what struck him, as he pulled the scratchy blanket up to his chin and listened to the radiator knock and the refrigerator click and the city hum, was how similar they felt — not painful exactly, not unbearable, just empty, the specific emptiness of a container that had once held something and now held only the shape of what used to be there.

Tomorrow was Thursday. Thursday was the navy suit.
