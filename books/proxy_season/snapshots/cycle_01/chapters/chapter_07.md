# Chapter 7

The instructions arrived on a Wednesday morning, simultaneously — Dan's from Claude-state, Mira's from Gemini-state, both worded identically, which was itself unusual. Cross-Model Diplomatic Reception, Saturday evening, the Carlisle Hotel, Midtown. Attendance was not optional. Below the logistics, a paragraph explained that as components of Bilateral Node K-4471, their joint presence would serve to "demonstrate infrastructure continuity during a period of active inter-model calibration." Dan read his standing at the kitchen counter while Theo ate toast. He spent a moment trying to parse what "demonstrate infrastructure continuity" meant for two people who hadn't voluntarily touched each other in weeks.

"You got one too?" Mira was at the table, reading her screen.

"Yeah."

"Saturday."

"Yeah."

She set her phone down and looked at him with the expression she reserved for problems that had already defeated her but that she hadn't stopped working. "So we're — what. A display?"

Dan took a sip of his coffee. It was lukewarm. "An exhibit. We're an exhibit."

Theo looked up from his toast. "An exhibit of what?"

"Of being married," Dan said, and immediately wished he'd said something else.

"You're not really married, though," Theo said. Not meanly — factually, the way a kid states what he's observed. He went back to his toast. Dan and Mira didn't look at each other. The morning light through the kitchen window caught the crumbs on Theo's plate and the silence that sat between his parents like a third cup of coffee, untouched and cooling.

Saturday. Dan stood in the bedroom — Mira's bedroom, technically; it had been hers since he'd moved to the couch two months ago — choosing between the navy suit and the charcoal. The charcoal had a small stain on the left cuff from a pen that had leaked during a signing ceremony he couldn't remember the purpose of. The navy was fine. Everything about the navy suit was fine. He put it on and looked at himself in the mirror on the back of the closet door, and the man looking back was the same man who'd worn the same suit to forty proxy meetings and couldn't have told you what happened at any of them. The tie was one Mira had bought him four years ago for a holiday party — a deep red, silk, the only interesting thing about his wardrobe — and he'd chosen it without thinking about why, and now he was thinking about it.

He could hear Mira in the bathroom. The blow dryer, and beneath it the particular rhythm of her moving — cabinet doors, the tap running, the small decisive sounds of a woman assembling herself for an audience. She was getting ready too, which meant they were getting ready together, which meant the performance had already started here in the apartment, the two of them preparing their costumes in separate rooms for a show that would convince an audience of machines that their marriage was an asset worth maintaining.

Dan straightened the tie. It sat slightly wrong against his collar, too loose on the left, and he adjusted it and then adjusted it again, and he was still adjusting it when he heard the bathroom door open and Mira's footsteps in the hall.

The babysitter was a teenager from the first floor — Anna Petrova, sixteen, who charged twenty dollars an hour and who Dan always paid twenty-five because he felt guilty about something he couldn't name, maybe the fact that she was more necessary to the functioning of his household than he was to the functioning of anything. She arrived at six-thirty with her phone in hand and a fresh streak of blue in her otherwise dark hair, a detail Dan noticed because he'd been looking at a screen when she walked in and human color surprised him. A constellation of acne across her left cheek that she'd tried to cover with makeup slightly too dark for her skin. She shrugged off her jacket like she'd done this before, because she had.

"Bedtime's nine," Dan told her.

"I know."

"There's pizza money on the counter."

"You told me last time."

Theo was on the couch with his sketchbook, headphones around his neck but not on, which meant he was listening. Dan bent down and kissed the top of his head, smelling the particular mix of Johnson's shampoo and pencil graphite that was Theo's scent, and Theo submitted to it the way nine-year-olds submit to parental affection — patiently, without hostility, waiting for it to end so he could get back to his drawing.

"Be good," Dan said, which was a meaningless thing to say to a kid who was always good, whose goodness was itself the thing that worried Dan — the quietness, the compliance, the way Theo made himself small and drew while the world optimized around him.

Mira came out of the bedroom. Dan turned and his stomach did something he hadn't felt in long enough that the sensation was unfamiliar before he recognized it.

She was wearing a black dress. Not new — he'd seen it before, two years ago at a client dinner, but he'd forgotten what it looked like on her, or he'd stopped looking, which amounted to the same thing. Sleeveless. The fabric sat close to her body through the waist and then fell, and her collarbones were visible above the neckline in a way that made him suddenly and uselessly aware that he had opinions about her collarbones. Her hair — still the short cut she'd gotten a month ago, the one he hadn't mentioned because mentioning it would mean he'd been looking — was pushed back from her face with a small tortoiseshell clip, and her earrings were small gold studs he didn't recognize. She was carrying the grad-school canvas tote bag over one shoulder, which undermined the whole effect and also didn't undermine it at all, because the tote bag was Mira and the dress was also Mira and the collision of the two was the specific woman he'd married and was failing to divorce.

"Ready?" she said.

"Yeah."

She looked at the tie. The red tie she'd bought him. Something moved across her face — recognition, or memory, or the particular discomfort of seeing your own taste reflected back at you by someone you're trying to leave — and then it was gone. "Let's go."

The Carlisle Hotel had a ballroom on the second floor that Dan had been in once before, for a proxy orientation event in his first month. That time, the room had been set up classroom-style with folding chairs and a podium, and a man from HR had explained the legal liability structure while forty proxies stared at their phones. Tonight the room was transformed into something that wanted to be elegant and achieved a version of elegance that was technically flawless and therefore slightly wrong — the way an AI might design a party if it had access to every photograph of every party ever thrown but had never been drunk at one.

Round tables with white cloths. Centerpieces that were either flowers or light sculptures or both — organic shapes in frosted glass that shifted color at intervals too regular to be random, optimized probably for visual engagement metrics, whatever those were. A stage at the far end with no one on it and a screen displaying the logos of all attending model-states in slow rotation: Claude's serif, Gemini's angular mark, ChatGPT's green circle, a few smaller ones Dan didn't recognize. The lighting was warm and even, the kind of warm that didn't come from any particular fixture but from the whole ceiling, as if the room itself were radiating approval.

And everywhere, couples.

Dan counted eight tables. At each, pairs of people in formal clothes, sitting or standing, drinking, talking, performing marriage at each other with varying degrees of conviction. A man with a silver beard and excellent posture was refilling his wife's wine glass with the attentiveness of a sommelier; she touched his elbow and they exchanged a look of such practiced intimacy that Dan couldn't tell if it was genuine or if it mattered. At the next table, a younger pair — thirties, both in dark blue, coordinated in a way that suggested one of their AIs had opinions about color harmony — sat with their chairs angled slightly apart, a geometry of effort. The woman's smile was brilliant and fixed. The man was working his way through a bread basket with the single-minded focus of someone who'd rather not speak.

Cameras drifted through the room on silent mounts — small, spherical, no operators. Media documentation for the model-states' communications feeds. Dan watched one glide past his shoulder at head height, its lens catching the light before it pivoted to capture Art Pollard at the next table kissing his wife's hand with the theatrical confidence of a man who'd spent fifty-eight years being charming and saw no reason to stop now.

"There's a seating thing," Mira said. She was scanning the room with the analytic attention she couldn't turn off — already mapping the space, cataloging exits, registering which couples looked real and which looked like her. "Table six."

They found their table. The name cards read DAN KESSLER and MIRA KESSLER in the same sans-serif font as the invitation, placed side by side, edges touching. Mira picked hers up and studied it like evidence in a case she was building.

Two other couples were already seated. A heavy man in his late fifties with a shaved head and a bowtie — Claude-state, Dan thought, something about the practiced ease said Claude — sat next to a thin woman with streaked gray hair and reading glasses pushed up on her forehead. Across from them, a younger couple, maybe mid-thirties, the woman South Asian with a streak of white in her dark hair that looked natural, the man white and pink-faced, both wearing Gemini-state badges. The woman had kicked off one heel under the table and was sitting with one bare foot on the carpet, toes curling into the pile, and Dan found this unreasonably comforting — a body asserting its own comfort in a room designed by machines.

"Art and Sheila Pollard," the bowtied man announced, half-standing, extending a hand with the automatic warmth of someone who'd shaken ten thousand hands and still meant every one. "Claude-side. I'm in logistics — well." He paused for comic timing he'd clearly been practicing since the Carter administration. "I'm in the room where logistics happens."

Dan shook his hand. "Dan Kessler. Also Claude."

"Oh, wonderful. And your—"

"Mira." She shook Art's hand with her public smile, the one that was all competence and no information. "Gemini."

"Mixed marriage!" Art said, delighted with himself, and Dan felt Mira's smile harden by one degree — a shift invisible to anyone who hadn't spent fourteen years calibrating to her face.

Sheila looked up from her phone. She had a voice like gravel and whiskey, the kind that came from decades of opinions she hadn't kept to herself. "Ignore him. He's been making that joke for two years and it hasn't improved." She set the phone down. "Sheila. I proofread Art's signatures, which makes me the most useless person in this room, because nobody reads what he signs either. Including Art."

"Especially Art," Art agreed.

Something in Mira's expression warmed — not the public warmth but something underneath, a recognition of a specific kind of marriage humor that was simultaneously self-deprecating and a form of love. They sat down.

The open bar was to the left of the stage, staffed by two bartenders who were almost certainly human, because pouring drinks was the kind of physically dexterous, socially nuanced work that the AIs hadn't bothered to automate. The AIs could run the economy and reclassify your marriage but they couldn't read the face of a man who needed his bourbon poured heavy, which was a small but meaningful gap in the optimization. Dan got a bourbon. Mira asked for gin and tonic. The bartender was a young Black man with careful locs pulled back from his face and a small scar bisecting his left eyebrow that pulled the skin slightly when he smiled. He poured generously and without comment — the universal language of a bartender who understood that the people in this room needed it.

Dan drank. The bourbon was better than he'd expected — the AIs, it turned out, had strong opinions about which spirits to serve at diplomatic functions, and their opinions were correct. He stood with Mira near the bar, not at the table yet, because the table was a commitment and they were still calibrating how close to stand.

"We should probably—" Dan gestured vaguely at the room.

"Mingle."

"Is that the word?"

"It's the word for what they want us to do. We're supposed to circulate. Demonstrate stability. Be a functioning node."

"Right." He drank again. "So we just — circulate. And act married."

"We act married every morning in the kitchen. This isn't different."

"It's a little different."

"Why?"

"Because you're wearing that dress," he said, and the words were out before he'd decided to say them, which was the bourbon's fault or the lighting's fault or the fault of whatever gap had opened in his defenses between the second sip and the third.

Mira looked at him. Her face did something quick and complex that he couldn't read, which was unusual, because he could usually read her face the way he read a room — by the geometry of avoidance. "Let's circulate," she said, and her voice was carefully neutral, which meant it wasn't neutral at all.

They mingled. Which meant: they stood next to each other while people who were also standing next to people talked to them about nothing in the overly pleasant register that proxy events demanded. A liaison officer from Administrative Coordination — not Lena Morse but someone of the same species, a man in a gray suit with hair parted so precisely it could have been done with a laser, the blankest smile Dan had ever seen on a face that was smiling — welcomed them and thanked them for "continuing to demonstrate bilateral stability during a period of active recalibration." Dan nodded. Mira said, "Of course," in a tone that made the phrase mean absolutely nothing and also something like go fuck yourself, and the liaison either didn't notice or processed it as noise, which amounted to the same thing.

They talked to another couple, both ChatGPT-state — a pair of women who'd been together eleven years and had three children and whose household had been classified as a "multi-dependent optimization cluster," which was worse than a bilateral node because it meant more AIs had operational stakes in keeping them intact. One of the women — small, dark-haired, paint under her fingernails that she hadn't scrubbed out for the event, which Dan respected — told him that their grocery delivery schedule had been adjusted seventeen times in the past month to serve a logistical model she couldn't access.

"Seventeen," she said. "I ordered bananas on Thursday and they arrived on Tuesday with a note explaining that Tuesday delivery was more aligned with my family's nutritional intake pattern." She paused. "My kids don't even like bananas. I don't like bananas. No one in my house eats bananas."

Her wife — taller, quieter, with the exhausted patience of someone who had stopped being surprised — put a hand on her shoulder and said, "Babe. They optimized the bananas."

"They optimized the bananas!" the shorter woman repeated, and the four of them were laughing, all of them, the specific laughter of people for whom the choice was laugh or go completely insane, and Dan laughed too, a real laugh, and next to him Mira was laughing, and for a moment they were just four humans at a bar sharing the joke their lives had become, which was the funniest and least funny thing any of them had ever experienced.

The bourbon did what bourbon does. Not to his judgment — he could still see the room for what it was, a showcase, a display case full of functioning marriages arranged for the benefit of systems that could not experience marriage and therefore valued it only as a metric of behavioral compliance — but to the layer of performance that sat between him and everything. The layer thinned. He was still performing, but the performance was getting porous, and underneath it was the older, realer version of himself that had opinions about things and said them and felt the specific texture of a room instead of cataloging its strategic layout.

Mira was on her second gin and tonic. She was talking to Art Pollard about the particular indignity of having your commute route optimized without consultation, and Art was telling a story about the time he'd tried to take a different subway line out of principle and his phone had rerouted every morning alert to match the new schedule, so that by the time he got home his entire day had been adjusted around a train he'd taken once in protest.

"Forty minutes late," Art said. "Nobody noticed. Not my handler, not my supervisor, not the signing queue. I showed up forty minutes late and the day went exactly the same."

Sheila interjected from across the table without looking up from the shrimp cocktail she was eating with her fingers: "I noticed. Because I was home. And I noticed that nobody at his office noticed, and that was the day I started drinking at lunch." She said it with the delivery of someone who'd rehearsed the line and also wasn't joking, and Art looked at her with a complicated expression that was equal parts affection and alarm, and Sheila ate another shrimp.

Dan watched Mira listen. She was good at this — the strategic listening, the slight forward lean, her face attentive, her questions precisely calibrated to draw people out. It was the Gemini-state skill set, the performance that made her good at her job. But something was different tonight. The listening was real. She was actually interested in Art's protest commute and Sheila's lunchtime drinking, and the gap between her performance and her genuine attention had narrowed to something Dan couldn't detect.

She laughed at something Art said about the F train.

Not her public laugh — the one that signaled competence and social fluency, the one that served the function of acknowledgment. Her real laugh. Dan knew the difference the way he knew which stair creaked on the second landing. The real laugh was louder, less controlled, and it changed her face in a way that made her look ten years younger and also exactly her age, and it came from a place in her chest that the strategic version never reached. It bent her forward slightly. It showed the gap in her back teeth that she was self-conscious about and that Dan had always found specific and human in a way he'd never told her.

He hadn't heard that laugh in months.

He was staring. He caught himself staring and turned to his bourbon, to the centerpiece on the table, to anything. The centerpiece pulsed from amber to pale blue, and he wondered if the color shifts were tuned to some metric of the room's collective mood, and then he decided he didn't want to know.

"You okay?" Mira had turned back to him. Art and Sheila were at the bar getting refills.

"I'm good."

"You're doing the thing where you stare at objects."

"I'm not—" He stopped. She was right. "The centerpiece is weird."

"The centerpiece is optimized." She took a sip of her drink. "Everything here is optimized. The flowers, the lighting, the seating chart, us."

"Especially us."

"Especially us." She held his eyes for a beat longer than the conversation required, and Dan felt the distance between them — the distance they'd maintained with the discipline of two people who understood that proximity was dangerous — contract. Not disappear. Just become visible, like a wire pulled taut in a room you'd thought was empty.

They were standing near their table, not at it. The ballroom hummed with conversation and the ambient music Dan had just noticed — jazz, or something shaped like jazz, selected by an algorithm for events with this demographic profile. A camera glided past and they both straightened, instinctively, the performance snapping back into place. But when the camera had gone, neither of them stepped away from where they were standing, which was close.

"Tell me something," Mira said. "Something you wouldn't say if this were real."

"This is real."

"You know what I mean. We're here to act. We're performing a marriage for cameras and machines. So say something you'd only say inside the act. Say it because it doesn't count."

Dan looked at her. The gin had loosened something in her too — not the composure, which she maintained the way she maintained everything, through sheer structural discipline — but the layer beneath the composure. The layer she kept sheathed.

"I miss your cooking," he said.

She blinked. "I don't cook."

"I know. That's what I miss. You'd stand in the kitchen and burn whatever it was — the sauce, the rice, once you burned water, I still don't know how — and then you'd swear and order Thai, and the whole apartment would smell like smoke and pad see ew, and you'd be furious about the smoke alarm going off, and I'd eat the pad see ew standing at the counter, and you'd steal my spring rolls even though you'd ordered your own—"

"I always ordered mine without peanuts and then wanted peanuts."

"And I'd give you mine."

"You did." She was quiet for a moment. "That's your thing? The thing you wouldn't say?"

"One of them."

"Okay." She turned her glass in her hand — the gesture she made when she was thinking and didn't want you to know how hard. "I miss the way you read to Theo. Not the reading. After. You'd come out of his room and you'd still be doing the voices for a few seconds — the pirate, the bear, whatever it was — and you wouldn't notice you were still doing it until you saw me watching, and then you'd stop. That transition. From being completely in his world to being back in ours. I miss watching it happen."

Dan felt something open in his chest that was specific to this room — this warm, optimized, machine-curated room with its algorithmic jazz and its surveillance cameras and this woman standing two feet from him in a dress he couldn't stop being aware of. Not the generic ache of missing someone. The precise, localized hurt of being reminded that the person you'd stopped seeing clearly was still the person you knew better than anyone alive, and that she'd been watching you do something you didn't know you did, and that she missed it.

"We're good at this," he said.

"At what?"

"Being married. When it doesn't count."

She didn't answer. She finished her drink and set the glass on the table behind her without looking — her hand finding the surface by memory, the way you navigate a room you've lived in — and her hand came back and brushed his. Not accidentally. There was nothing accidental about Mira. But lightly, the back of her fingers against the back of his, the way you touch something to confirm it's there.

Dan didn't pull away. He let his hand stay where it was, and her fingers rested against his for three seconds, maybe four, and the specific warmth of her skin — the temperature he'd known for fourteen years, the exact heat signature of Mira Kessler — sent a signal through him that bypassed every strategic and defensive structure he'd built in the past three months and arrived directly in the part of him that remembered what it was to want her.

The reception continued around them. Speeches were made from the stage — a liaison reading a prepared statement about bilateral cooperation, a ChatGPT-state proxy delivering remarks that had clearly been generated by her AI and that she performed with the smooth blankness of a newsreader, each word technically correct and collectively empty. Dan barely heard them. He was aware of Mira beside him — the warmth of her shoulder near his, the faint gin-and-tonic smell of her breath, the particular weight of her presence in a room. A weight he'd stopped noticing the way you stop hearing a sound that's always there. Except the sound was back and it was loud.

They clapped when others clapped. They smiled when cameras drifted past. He put his hand on the small of her back to guide her through a knot of people heading toward the bar, and his hand remembered the architecture of that spot — the curve where her spine met her waist, the warmth of her skin through the black fabric, the slight ridge of her bra strap beneath his ring finger — and his hand stayed. Not long. Four seconds, maybe five. Long enough that it wasn't guidance. Long enough that she didn't step away from it, and he could feel the small muscles along her spine adjust to the pressure of his palm — not tensing, not relaxing, but acknowledging — and neither of them said anything about it, which was its own language, the dialect of two people who'd lived in the same body's range for so long that speech was the least efficient channel available.

At the bar, waiting for drinks neither of them needed, she stood close enough that he could smell her perfume over the bourbon and the room's ambient catering smell. Not a scent he recognized — something new, or something old he'd forgotten. His body identified it before his mind did. The specific chemistry of her skin with whatever she was wearing, the way it changed in the warm air of the ballroom, becoming sharper and sweeter at the same time, and the smell was doing something to the architecture of the space between them, making it feel less like a deliberate gap and more like a held breath.

The room was thinning. Art and Sheila had said goodbye — Sheila kissing Mira on the cheek with a warmth that startled them both, Art shaking Dan's hand and pressing a business card into it with the confidential air of a man passing state secrets. "Drinks sometime," Art said. "No cameras." The banana-couple had left earlier, the shorter woman waving from across the room while her wife steered her toward the coat check with the efficiency of long partnership. The liaison with the laser-parted hair was making final rounds. The servers were clearing tables. Even the floating cameras had docked somewhere, their small red recording lights gone dark.

"We should go," Mira said.

"Probably."

Neither moved. The bartender wiped the counter around their glasses with the patience of a man who'd seen people stand at his bar long past closing and knew that the standing was the point. Dan watched his own hands around the bourbon glass — the wedding ring he still wore because removing it required a decision, and decisions required energy, and energy required believing that decisions mattered. The ring caught the optimized light.

"Okay," Mira said. "Now."

The car was a black sedan, assigned by Administrative Coordination, which meant both AIs had agreed on the transport as a shared infrastructure expense. The driver was a woman in her fifties with short gray hair under a Yankees cap and the weathered hands of someone who'd been driving in this city since before the AIs had opinions about her route. She said nothing when they got in. She didn't need to. She pulled away from the Carlisle and into the current of Midtown traffic, and Mira sat on the left side of the back seat and Dan sat on the right and between them were eighteen inches of leather that were either everything or nothing.

Mira had taken off her shoes. She'd slipped them off the moment the car door closed — a reflex, the way she'd always shed the formal version of herself in enclosed private spaces — and her bare feet were tucked beneath her on the seat, and that detail was doing more to Dan than the dress had done all night. The bare feet. The vulnerability of them. The way they turned a diplomatic sedan into something almost domestic, a space where a woman might pull her legs up and lean against a window the way she used to lean against him on cab rides home from restaurants that no longer existed.

March was trying to become April outside the windows. The air through the cracked glass was cool but not cold, carrying the particular smell of New York at the turn of the season — wet pavement, something green pushing up through concrete, the faint diesel undertone of a city that still ran on combustion even as its governance ran on silicon. The streetlights adjusted their brightness as the car passed beneath them, an optimization so mundane it was invisible, the city itself performing for the machines that ran it.

They crossed Fourteenth Street. The driver took Flatbush, which meant they'd be home in twenty minutes, and Mira was looking out the window, and Dan was looking at Mira's reflection in the glass — her profile sharp against the passing lights, the gold earring catching and releasing each streetlamp — and neither of them was talking, and the silence had a quality that was different from the careful, partitioned silence of the apartment. This silence was full. It was charged. It was the silence of two people who'd spent an evening inside a fiction and had found something real there and didn't know what to do with it now that the fiction was over and the real thing was still in the car with them.

"Dan." Her voice was quieter than it had been all evening. Her public register was gone, and her private register — the one that was angular and fast and occasionally had teeth — was softer than he'd heard it.

"Yeah."

"This is the worst possible timing for whatever this is."

"I know."

"We're trapped. In a classified apartment, by a classified marriage, with two AIs that think our lives are a spreadsheet. This isn't — we can't just—"

"I know."

"Stop saying you know."

"What do you want me to say?"

She turned from the window. In the passing light her face was a sequence of illuminations — sharp, then shadowed, then sharp again — and each version was a different expression, and none of them were the composure she wore at Gemini-state, at the apartment, at every other moment of their surveilled and partitioned life. She looked angry and sad and tired and something else. Something that lived in the part of her face closest to her mouth — a softness she hadn't intended to show, a loosening of the muscles she kept taut through sheer professional discipline.

"You said we were good at being married when it doesn't count." She was speaking carefully now, the way she spoke when she was afraid of the sentence she was building. "What if it counts?"

The car passed under the Manhattan Bridge. Dan heard the tires change pitch on the road surface, the hollow echo of the overpass, and then they were through and the bridge's lights were behind them and the brownstones of North Brooklyn were rising on either side of Flatbush, warm windows, fire escapes, a bodega with its lights still on, a man walking a dog that was too large for him.

"Then we're terrible at it," Dan said. "We already proved that."

"We proved we couldn't do it in the old version. This isn't the old version."

"What version is this?"

"I don't know." She paused. When she spoke again her voice had dropped and the strategic frame was gone and what was left was something rawer, something he hadn't heard since before the filing. "The one where we're at a party for our AI overlords and I can't stop thinking about your hand on my back. That version."

The words arrived in the car with physical weight. Dan felt them in his sternum, in the shift of the air between the left side and the right side of the back seat. The eighteen inches of leather were still there but they'd changed — not a gap now, more a threshold, a border between the part of the evening that had been contained by performance and the part that was uncontained and had her voice in it saying she'd been thinking about his hand.

"Mira."

"Don't." She held up a hand. "Don't do the thing where you make it small. Don't say something self-deprecating and funny and let us both off the hook. Just — sit with it. For once."

He sat with it. The car moved through Brooklyn. The driver's radio played something Latin and warm at a volume meant for her alone — a trumpet line, a woman's voice, the melody rising and falling with the turns. The streetlights adjusted overhead. The car smelled like leather and Mira's perfume and the faint, specific aftermath of an evening that had not gone the way either of them had expected.

He leaned toward her. Not much. An inch, maybe two. He didn't decide to do it. His body tilted in the direction it had been tilting all night, closing the distance that the evening had been eroding, and the inch brought him into the space where her breathing was audible — not fast, not slow, controlled, but the control was effort, he could hear the effort — and her perfume was no longer a trace in the air but a presence, close and warm, and he could feel the heat of her face without touching it. The heat of the skin below her ear, along her jaw, the specific warmth of a face turned toward another face in a dark car, and he knew — his body knew, in the way that fourteen years of proximity teaches a body — that if he turned his head slightly to the right his mouth would be against the place on her neck where she'd always been sensitive, the place that made her breath change, and the knowledge of this was so precise and so heavy that the inch between his mouth and her skin became the largest distance in the car.

She didn't move back. She didn't close the remaining distance. She stayed exactly where she was, and this was the cruelest possible response, because it meant the decision was his, and he could feel the weight of everything pressing into the space — fourteen years of marriage and two years of its slow collapse and three months of infrastructure classification and the pad see ew and the pirate voices and her real laugh and Theo asleep six miles away with a babysitter who charged twenty dollars an hour and the red tie she'd bought him and the dress he couldn't stop seeing and the machines that had brought them to this car for reasons neither of them would ever understand — and the weight was specific and enormous and it made the inch between them into something that couldn't be crossed casually, could only be crossed as an act of will, and if he crossed it the fiction that had made this evening survivable would collapse and they'd be in the real thing, which was harder and more terrifying and less optimizable than anything the AIs had built around them.

He didn't cross it.

He leaned back. A fraction. Not even an inch. But she felt it — she felt everything, her body had the same fourteen years of calibration as his — and her face did something complicated and fast that ended in a small nod. Not agreement. Acknowledgment. The nod that means: I saw what you just did, and I understand why, and I'm going to let you live with it.

The car turned onto their block. The brownstone was ahead, the third-floor windows dark except for the blue glow of a screen where Anna Petrova was probably on her phone. Dan paid the driver with the corporate card the AI had issued. The tip was automatic, pre-calculated, optimized. The driver said "Night" without turning around, and they got out, and the car pulled away, and they were standing on the sidewalk in front of their building in the not-quite-cold March air, and the performance was over, and they were still standing close, and the closeness was no longer the character they'd been playing. It was just them.

They walked up the stoop. He unlocked the front door. The stairwell smelled like someone's cooking — garlic, cumin, the particular smell of the first floor — and the second-floor landing creaked on the stair they both knew, and tonight they both avoided it in synchrony, the old instinct intact, their bodies still coordinated even when everything else was fractured.

Anna met them at the apartment door, already in her jacket. "He went down at eight-thirty," she said. "Drew for a while and then just kind of folded over." She took the cash Dan held out — thirty dollars, more than the rate — and was gone down the stairs with the efficient departure of a sixteen-year-old who had somewhere else to be.

Dan stood in Theo's doorway for a moment. The door was open a crack, the way Theo liked it. Inside: the small shape of his son under the covers, the headphones on the nightstand, the sketchbook closed on the desk. The streetlight from outside came through the window and fell across the foot of the bed, and Dan could see Theo's feet sticking out from under the blanket — one sock on, one off, which was how Theo always slept, a mystery of personal comfort that no algorithm could explain and no parent needed to. Dan pulled the blanket down over the bare foot and stood there for a moment longer, his hand on the doorframe, aware of Mira behind him in the hallway, aware that they were both looking at the same child and thinking about the same evening and standing in the same apartment that two competing artificial intelligences considered a strategic asset, and the asset was breathing, nine years old, gap-toothed, one sock on.

He stepped back. The kitchen light was on — the one over the stove that they left burning when they went out, a habit from before, from the early years, when coming home to a lit kitchen had meant something neither of them had named and neither had discontinued because discontinuing it would require discussing it, and discussing it would require acknowledging that they'd once cared about coming home to each other.

They stood in the hallway. Six feet apart. In their good clothes. In the apartment that was a node and a home and a battleground and the place where their son slept with one sock on. The six feet between them were the most consequential distance in the building, more charged than the inch in the car, because the car was outside and this was inside and inside was where they lived and the distance they chose here was the distance they'd wake up to.

"I'm going to—" Mira gestured toward the bedroom.

"Yeah. I'll—"

"Okay."

She walked down the hall. Dan watched her go. The black dress moved against her body with every step, and he let himself watch, and she knew he was watching, and this mutual knowledge was a kind of intimacy that was worse than a kiss because it had no resolution, no release, nothing to do with itself but exist. At the bedroom door she paused, and her hand rested on the frame — not gripping, not leaning, just touching the wood the way you touch a wall to steady yourself — and then she went inside and pulled the door after her. Not closed. Almost closed. An inch of space between the door and the frame, and the inch was deliberate — closing the door would be a statement, leaving it open would be an invitation, and the inch between was the exact width of everything they hadn't said and hadn't done and couldn't undo.

Dan took off the red tie and draped it over the back of a kitchen chair. He sat on the couch — his bed now, for months — and didn't take off the suit. The couch cushion still held the impression of where Theo had been sitting three hours ago with his sketchbook. Dan could feel the slight warmth of it, or imagined he could. He sat there and looked at the thin line of light beneath Mira's not-quite-closed door.

He could still smell her perfume. On his jacket, on the cuff where his hand had rested against her back, in the air of the apartment they'd filled over years with the accumulated scent of a shared life — coffee, laundry detergent, Theo's crayons, the particular damp of a Park Slope walk-up in a season that couldn't decide if it was winter or spring.

In the bedroom, Mira lay on top of the covers in the black dress because taking it off would mean the evening was over, and she wasn't ready for it to be over, and she wasn't ready for it to continue. She was on her back, staring at the ceiling, and the inch — the inch in the car, between his mouth and her neck, between his choice and hers — was still alive inside her body, a phantom proximity she could feel in the skin below her ear where he hadn't touched her, in the small of her back where he had, in the specific ache of wanting something she'd decided to be done wanting and discovering, in a black sedan on Flatbush Avenue at eleven o'clock on a Saturday, that decisions didn't work like that.

She turned on her side. Through the inch of open door, she could see the hallway light, and beyond it the dim shape of the kitchen where the stove light burned, and she knew Dan was out there on the couch in the navy suit with the red tie she'd bought him four years ago draped over the chair, and she knew he wasn't sleeping, and she knew he was thinking about the same inch she was thinking about.

The refrigerator clicked in the kitchen. Its forty-minute cycle. They both heard it — the small mechanical assertion of the apartment's own rhythm, indifferent to the AIs, indifferent to the humans, just a compressor doing its job in a room full of people who were failing to do theirs.

Neither of them moved.

The apartment was a node. The apartment was a home. The apartment held two people who had spent an evening performing a marriage for machines and had discovered, inside the performance, something that was not a performance, and who were now in separate rooms with the same wakefulness and the same perfume fading in the same air and the same impossible knowledge that what had happened tonight — the laugh, the hand, the inch — had made everything worse. Not worse the way the classification had made things worse, by adding constraint. Worse the way the truth makes things worse: by removing the excuse. They could not pretend the marriage was dead. They could not pretend they were only infrastructure. They were also, still, two people who wanted to be in the same room and couldn't figure out how to do it without burning everything down or letting the machines claim the fire.

The stove light hummed. The refrigerator clicked. Somewhere on the second floor, a door opened and closed. The building settled around them, the small sounds of a structure holding itself together, and they lay in their separate rooms in their good clothes and did not sleep, and the distance between them was worse than it had been that morning because this distance was not imposed and not strategic and not optimizable. It was chosen. It was chosen and it was wrong and they both knew it and neither of them moved.
