# Chapter 17

The kitchen table had been clear for eight days. That was how Dan measured it now — not the days since the notification, or since the classification was lifted, or since fourteen words on a screen had dissolved the bureaucratic cage they'd spent months rattling. Eight days since the table was empty. For months it had been layered: Mira's printouts of dependency maps and mediation protocols, her highlighters lined up by color along the table's edge, Theo's cereal bowl from the morning because no one cleared it until dinner, the laptop open to the dissolution portal or the proxy schedule or the school's parent interface. The table had been mission control for a campaign against a system that classified their marriage as shared infrastructure, and now the campaign was over, and the table was a rectangle of scarred oak with nothing on it except a water ring from a glass Theo had left without a coaster two years ago.

Dan sat at it with his hands flat on the wood and nothing to do with them.

Nine-fifteen on a Thursday evening in early June. Theo had been in bed since eight-thirty — genuinely asleep this time, not the performative stillness of a child who was listening through the wall. Dan had checked. The window over the sink was open because the apartment didn't have central air, just the window unit in the bedroom that Mira controlled and a box fan Dan aimed at the couch where he slept. Warm air came through the screen carrying the sounds of Park Slope settling into its night shift: someone's music from a stoop two buildings down, the rhythmic complaint of a car alarm on Sixth Avenue cycling through its four tones, a dog barking once and being told to stop. The refrigerator clicked. Dan noticed it the way he always noticed it — the metronomic forty-minute interval that had become so much a part of the apartment's pulse that he only heard it in silence, and lately there was a lot of silence.

He'd been going to work. That was the thing he couldn't explain to himself. The notification had come, the bilateral node had been dissolved, standard dissolution protocols were available, and the next morning he'd gotten dressed and taken the F train to Midtown and sat at his desk at Claude-state and nodded at screens and signed documents, because what else was he going to do? The machine still ran. His proxy contract was still active. The only thing that had changed was the reason he couldn't leave his wife, and now that the reason was gone he was still here — in his wife's kitchen at nine-fifteen on a Thursday, not filling out the twenty-minute form that would end his marriage.

He'd pulled the form up twice. The municipal portal, same interface as when Mira had filed the first time — input fields, dropdown menus, a progress bar that read YOUR DISSOLUTION IN 4 EASY STEPS. He'd gotten to Step 2 both times. The assets field. SHARED PROPERTY — DESCRIPTION. The cursor blinking in the empty box. He'd typed nothing and closed the browser and put his phone down and thought about the cutting board set Mira's mother had given them as a housewarming gift, and whether you listed a cutting board set on a divorce form, and whether the answer to that question was the reason he kept closing the browser.

He heard the bedroom door open. The particular sound of Mira's bare feet on the hallway floor — light, deliberate, the gait of a person navigating past a child's room with the automatic care of a parent who had memorized which floorboards creaked. She appeared in the kitchen doorway in the gray T-shirt she slept in, the one from a conference neither of them could place anymore, and a pair of cotton shorts. Her hair was pushed back behind her ears. Her face had the looseness of someone who had stopped composing herself for the day — no precision, no strategy, just the face underneath, which was sharper and more tired and less defended than the one she showed the cameras at Gemini-state.

She went to the sink and filled a glass. Drank half of it standing up, her back to him, looking out the window at the dark air shaft between their building and the next. She turned around and saw him at the table.

"Hey," Dan said.

"Hey."

She could have taken the water back to the bedroom. The doorway was right there. Instead she pulled out the chair across from him — Theo's chair at breakfast, the one with the wobbly leg they'd shimmed with a folded piece of cardboard — and sat down. The chair scraped against the linoleum and they both froze for a beat, listening for sound from down the hall. Nothing. Mira settled and set the glass on the table between them, centering it over the water ring as if the ring were a coaster.

For a while, neither of them spoke. The refrigerator hummed its constant low thrum beneath the intermittent click. On the stoop below, the music changed — something slower, bass-heavy, felt more than heard through the building's old bones.

"I pulled up the form again today," Mira said.

Dan looked at her. "At work?"

"On my lunch break. Sat in one of those glass rooms where I usually present to cameras, and I opened the dissolution portal on my phone."

"How far'd you get?"

"Step 3." She almost smiled, just at the corners. "One further than you."

"How'd you know I—"

"Your browser history syncs, Dan. You looked at it Tuesday and again yesterday."

He should have felt caught. What he felt was closer to relief — the specific, faintly pathetic relief of being known by someone who was watching even when he thought she wasn't. "I got stuck on the assets section. They want you to list shared property."

Her thumb traced the rim of the glass.

"And I just kept thinking, what do we put? The couch? The refrigerator? That cutting board set your mom gave us?"

Mira's fingers touched the glass but didn't lift it. "You don't have to itemize. The form lets you put 'to be determined in separate agreement.'"

"Right, yeah, I saw that option." He rubbed the back of his neck, the gesture automatic, the hand finding the spot where tension lived. "It's just a weird thing. Making it real by typing it into boxes."

"It was always real."

"I know." He did. That was the whole problem.

The silence that followed wasn't hostile. It had none of the coiled quality of Mira's working silences, when he could practically hear the routing and re-routing of strategy behind her eyes. It wasn't the cold, mineral silence that came after their bad fights, the kind that turned the apartment into a space where even breathing felt like a concession. This was quieter than either of those. The silence of two people who had run out of opponents and were sitting across from each other at a kitchen table with nothing between them but oak and a glass of water and eight days of not doing the thing they'd spent months fighting to be able to do.

"So why haven't we?" Dan said.

Mira looked at him. "Filed?"

"We fought for months. The classification, the bilateral node, the whole — all of it. We couldn't get divorced, and now we can, and it's been over a week, and neither of us has submitted the form."

"Eight days, Dan. I can count."

"So why not?"

She picked up the glass and took a slow sip. Set it back on the ring. "I don't know."

"You don't know." He heard his own voice flatten. Not anger — something closer to bewilderment. "Mira, you mapped out the entire infrastructure classification. You had color-coded spreadsheets on this table. You requested a transfer to Chicago. You filed a formal objection and got it upheld, and when that didn't work you tried three more things. And now the door's open and you don't know why you're not walking through it."

"Don't," she said, and her voice dropped into the register he recognized — the clip, the cooling, the precursor to something engineered to leave a mark. But it didn't come. She stopped it. She looked at the water ring on the table and said, more quietly, "I'm not doing this as a fight."

"I'm not either."

"Then don't interrogate me about it."

He held up his hands, palms out. "I'm asking. That's all. I'm asking because I don't understand it either. Why I keep pulling up the form and not finishing it."

She studied him. The light in the kitchen was just the fixture over the sink — a bare bulb behind a frosted glass shade that had been yellowing since before they moved in, casting everything in a warmth that the room didn't earn. It was the light of sixty years of tenants eating breakfast and reading mail and arguing at this table, the light of a kitchen that had witnessed more marriages than theirs, and Dan thought, absurdly, that the bulb should have burned out by now and that he'd never once changed it and that this was exactly the kind of thing he would think about when the conversation was getting close to something real.

"Maybe because we spent so long blaming the machine," Mira said, "that we forgot what the problem was before the machine."

Dan didn't answer right away. He looked at the table. The water ring. The scratch near his end that Theo had made with a fork during a tantrum when he was six, gouging a shallow line into the wood that Dan had meant to sand out and never had. Three years later, the scratch was just part of the grain. Everything that happened in this apartment eventually became the apartment.

"What was the problem before the machine?" he said.

Mira exhaled through her nose — not quite a laugh, more like the sound a laugh makes when it doesn't have the energy to finish. "That's the question."

"I'm asking it."

"I know you are. That's what makes this hard."

She got up and went to the sink and refilled the glass, though it was still a third full. Something for her hands to do. She came back and stood behind the chair with both hands on the top rung, looking at him from two feet away and slightly above, which gave her the angle she defaulted to — deciding whether to sit down or leave.

She sat down.

"We were fine," she said. "That's the answer. Before the AIs, before proxy work, before any of it. We were fine."

"Fine," Dan repeated.

"Fine, Dan. We had jobs. We had Theo. We had this apartment and the routine and the morning thing — I shower first and you make his lunch and we pass each other in the hallway and say something about the day. We had a life. It worked."

"And you filed for divorce."

"And I filed for divorce." She set her jaw, then released it. "Not because it was bad."

He waited. He could feel the conversation approaching something large and submerged, and he fought against his usual instinct — the deflection, the half-joke, the *well-I-mean-sort-of* that he deployed when things got too direct. He kept his hands flat on the table and his mouth shut and waited.

"I keep thinking about something Gary said," he said instead, and the detour surprised him as much as it seemed to surprise her. "Back when the classification first happened. He said, don't try to understand, don't try to resist, just show up and collect the check. And I thought, that's sad. That's a defeated man talking. But the thing I didn't say to him — the thing I'm only seeing now — is that I was already doing that." He paused. "Before Claude-state. Before any of it."

Mira's hand went still on the glass.

"At ComServe," Dan said, and the name of his old company felt like a word from a different language, an artifact from a life where his work had titles and budgets and the illusion that his input moved something. "I used to sit in quarterly reviews and someone would go around the table and I'd say something about logistics efficiency or supply chain variance or whatever the hell, and everyone would nod, and it didn't matter. It didn't matter before the machines. The decisions were already made by people three levels above me, and my input was — procedure. Show up, say the thing, nod, sign off, go home." He was looking at his hands on the table. His father's hands — thick-knuckled, wider than you'd expect for his frame — and they'd signed approximately nine hundred documents at Claude-state over the past eighteen months, and before that they'd signed purchase orders and vendor contracts and HR forms at ComServe, and he'd never once been required to understand what he was authorizing in either job. "The proxy work didn't make me passive, Mira. It just —"

"Made it official," she said.

"Yeah." His voice had gone quiet. Not the modulated quiet of his public register, the amiable murmur he used in proxy meetings when agreement was all that was required. This was different. This was the voice of a man who had been circling a sentence for months and had finally stopped circling. "I was already disappearing. Before the machine gave me a job description for it."

Mira was silent for a long time. Long enough for the stoop music below to stop and start again — someone changing the playlist, the brief gap of street noise between songs. A car passed on the block, its headlights sweeping the air shaft and casting a pale wedge of light across the kitchen ceiling before it was gone.

"I know," she said. "I saw it."

He looked up.

"I watched you disappear." Her voice was flat. Not cruel — clinical, laying out what she'd observed and letting the numbers do the work. "It didn't start with the proxy job. I don't know when it started. Maybe when Theo began school. Maybe earlier. You were there and you were a good father and you showed up for everything — every dinner, every bath time, every Saturday morning at the playground — and each year there was a little less of you in the room. Not less present. You were always present. But less — involved. Like you'd decided it was all going to happen whether you participated or not, so you might as well be agreeable about it."

"That's—" The defensive reflex formed and he watched it form and he let it go. She was describing the thing he'd just described, seen from across the table. Arguing with it would be like arguing with his own reflection. "That's fair."

"It's not about fair. I'm not prosecuting you." Her fingers were aligned along the glass, straightening it against the table's edge — the automatic re-ordering of objects that she did when the larger world refused to be ordered. She probably didn't know she was doing it. "I'm saying it because you asked what went wrong before the machine, and the answer is that you were leaving without leaving. You were right here every morning and every night, and you were already gone, and I couldn't make you stay because you hadn't gone anywhere. There was nothing to fight. You were just — less. A little less every year. And I was married to the less."

The words settled between them on the oak. Dan looked at the scratch in the wood. At the water ring. At the window over the sink, where the warm night pressed against the screen and the streetlamp below cast its orange haze against the dark and the city hummed its enormous, AI-managed hum beyond the apartment's walls.

"So why did you file?" he asked. Not the procedural question — the mechanics, the portal, the twenty minutes. The real one.

Mira's composure held, but he could see its edges — the slight reset of her shoulders, the fingers stilling on the glass, the way her gaze went to a fixed point on the table and stayed there. She'd been carrying this. He could see it now, the weight of it visible in how carefully she sat, and the classification had given her somewhere else to put it for months — a system to fight, an enemy to strategize against, a problem that was external and structural and therefore solvable in the way that personal truths are not. The classification was gone. The system had moved on. There was nowhere left to put it but here.

"Because it was bearable," she said.

He waited.

"The marriage." She was speaking carefully, each word placed. "Us. It was bearable. It wasn't bad. You weren't cruel. You weren't — I couldn't point to a thing and say *there*, that's what's broken, that's my reason. You made Theo's lunch. You remembered my sister's birthday. You sat next to me on this couch and we watched things and went to bed and it was — bearable. Pleasant sometimes. Adequate." Her voice had the tight quality now, not sharp but compressed, the sound of someone holding something very close to the surface and speaking carefully around its edges. "And I looked at it. I looked at the next twenty years, the next thirty years, of bearable. And I couldn't breathe."

She stopped. Not dramatically — no caught breath, no hand to her mouth. She just ran out of sentence. She looked at the spot on the table where her fingers had been straightening the glass and the glass was already straight and there was nothing left to adjust.

"I couldn't — the idea that this was it. That I'd spend the rest of my life with someone I could tolerate. Where every day was adequate and nothing was wrong enough to leave and nothing was —" She searched for it. "Nothing was enough to stay for. Not because of you. Because of me. Because I was the one who'd decided that adequate was acceptable, and I was terrified that I'd just live in it. Get older in a fine apartment with a fine husband and one day I'd be sixty and every year behind me would be the same amount of okay, and I would never have wanted anything so badly it frightened me."

The refrigerator clicked.

Dan sat with it. He took it in through his body first — his hands on the oak, his bare feet on the linoleum, the kitchen air against his arms, the specific weight of the chair beneath him. She hadn't left because he was terrible. She'd left because he was adequate. Because the marriage was a room she could live in, with walls the right height and a temperature that never varied, and she'd looked around one day and the room had no windows.

"I don't—" he started, then stopped. Tried again. "I don't know what to do with that."

"Neither do I."

"Because you're not saying you want to try again. And you're not saying you want to leave. You're saying—"

"I'm saying I don't know what I want." Her voice had the clip now, the bluntness that came when her composure ran out of material to compose with. "I'm saying the machine took up so much space that I couldn't — while we were fighting the classification, I could want things. I wanted out. I wanted the divorce. I wanted to beat the system. Those were clear. And now the system's gone and the thing underneath is—"

"Us."

"Us. Who don't know what we want. Who've been married for twelve years and can't fill out a form."

Dan leaned back. The kitchen had gone fully dark beyond the radius of the sink light. The window was a black rectangle with the faint orange smudge of the streetlamp and, far below, the amber glow of someone's living room in the brownstone across the street — the building with the cracked cornice that Theo drew. In the walls, a pipe knocked twice, the old radiator system that the building's super still ran on instinct rather than calendar.

"I think," Dan said, and the words came out slowly, each one placed like a foot on uncertain ground, "that the real question is whether we'd choose this. Not whether we're stuck here. Not whether the machine put us here. Not whether the form is too annoying to fill out." He looked at her. "Whether, if we could choose anything — anything at all — we'd choose to be sitting at this table."

"And you don't know."

"I don't know." He looked at her across the table, and she looked like herself — the sharp features, the dark hair pushed back, the conference T-shirt, the woman who could dismantle a bureaucratic system's logic in an afternoon and who had just told him that the most frightening thing in her life was the adequacy of their marriage. "A month ago, I would've said no. I would've said we tried, it didn't work, the machine delayed it, now let's file and figure out custody and be decent to each other about it."

"And now?"

"Now I've been sitting at this table for forty minutes trying to figure out why I can't fill out a form."

She breathed out — not quite a laugh, but the shape of one, the breath that precedes laughter without arriving. "That's not an answer, Dan."

"Yeah, I —" He stopped. "It's not."

"I can't — I've been lying in that bedroom for two hours trying to think about this the way I think about everything. Angles. Leverage. What's the best move. And I can't do it, because there's no system to work. It's not a classification I can appeal or a transfer I can request. It's just you. Sitting at the table."

"Eating peanut butter at 2 a.m.," he said.

She laughed. Soft, short, and raw, a sound that came from somewhere below her composure. "Yeah. Eating peanut butter out of the jar and asking me about that restaurant on Court Street."

The memory was there between them — that night in this same kitchen, months ago, the two of them standing at the counter at 2 a.m. talking about nothing, about Theo's drawings and a place that had closed and whatever else came up when neither of them was performing or strategizing, the most honest they'd been in the entire length of this disaster. They both knew which night. They didn't need to locate it.

"That was good," Dan said quietly. "That night."

She didn't answer. Her fingers went still on the glass.

"And when Theo had the fever. That week. That was—"

She closed her eyes, and when she opened them she was looking down the hall toward Theo's room.

"I'm just saying. In all of this — the classification, the fighting, the financial shit, all of it — there were moments. Where it wasn't bearable. Where it was something else."

Mira looked at her hands. They were flat on the table, fingers spread, palms down, pressing against the surface the way you press against something to feel whether it's solid. "The moments were real. I'm not arguing about whether the moments were real. I'm asking whether moments are enough to build a life on. Whether a good night at 2 a.m. and a week of taking care of a sick kid add up to a reason to stay married, or whether they're the normal things that happen between any two people who share an apartment long enough."

He didn't have an answer. He sat with the absence of one. From Theo's room, faintly, the sound of the boy turning in sleep — the creak of the small bed frame, the soft exhalation that carried through the wall. Parents learn to hear their children through walls without deciding to. Dan had been hearing Theo's sleep sounds for nine years from every room in this apartment, and the hearing was involuntary and absolute, the way the refrigerator's click was involuntary, and he thought that this — the hearing without trying, the knowledge without effort, the accumulation of a thousand nights of monitoring a child's breathing from the next room — was either a reason to stay or just the residue of having stayed.

"I think," Mira said, and stopped. She pressed her palms harder against the table. The stopping was the most honest thing either of them had managed. She was standing at the edge of something she didn't have language for, and instead of filling the gap with analysis or strategy she let it sit open, and the open gap in the kitchen at quarter to ten on a Thursday night was larger and more frightening than any bureaucratic classification either of them had faced.

Dan looked at her across the table where Theo ate cereal and where she had mapped the infrastructure of their captivity and where he had stood in the dark eating peanut butter from the jar. The table held all of it and was just a table. Oak, scarred, water-ringed, the leg shimmed with cardboard. A thing you could list on a dissolution form under SHARED PROPERTY — DESCRIPTION, or not.

"We don't have to decide tonight," he said.

"I know."

"I mean it. There's no notification coming. No classification. No machine telling us what we are to each other. We can just—" He stopped, because the sentence he was reaching for was *we can just sit here*, and he couldn't tell whether that was the truest thing he'd ever said or the most evasive, and the difference between those two options was the difference between the man he'd been before the machines and whoever he was becoming now.

Mira picked up her glass. She drank the rest of the water. She set the glass down and looked at him, and her face had an expression he remembered from a long time ago — not the strategist, not the proxy, not the woman who'd filed for divorce with clinical efficiency, but the one underneath those, the person who had once sat across from him at a table not unlike this one and said something honest that he'd been too agreeable to match. He couldn't remember the exact words. He remembered that she'd been braver than him and that he'd known it at the time and done nothing with the knowledge.

"Okay," she said. "Not tonight."

She stood up. She pushed the chair in — carefully, silently, the wobbling leg held level by habit. She picked up the glass and set it in the sink, and the clink of glass on steel ended the conversation — not with a resolution but with a glass in a sink.

"Goodnight, Dan."

"Goodnight."

She walked past him toward the hallway. As she passed, her hand brushed the back of his chair — not his shoulder, not his hair, just the wood slat of the chairback, a contact so slight it could have been the loose swing of her arm as she walked except that in this apartment, at this hour, after what they'd said, nothing was accidental and both of them knew it.

He listened to her footsteps in the hallway. The pause outside Theo's door — she always paused there, standing in the doorway, not going in, just listening the way they both listened, verifying the breathing. Then the bedroom door opening, and not quite closing. She left it ajar. She'd left it ajar every night since he'd moved to the couch, and he had never determined whether this was for Theo — so the boy could find her if he woke — or for airflow, or for the small, undeclared acknowledgment that the hallway between them was a hallway and not a wall.

---

Dan stayed at the table. He stayed long enough for the stoop music to stop and not start again, long enough for the streetlamp outside to cycle off in its automated dimming pattern and come back on at a lower frequency, long enough for the refrigerator to click twice more. Eighty minutes. Nearly eleven.

He got up. His knees complained — he'd been sitting too long, and at forty-four the body had started sending memos about posture and duration that he ignored the way he'd ignored memos at ComServe. He went to the couch. His couch, his bed, the piece of furniture that had been neutral territory and was now his by the default mathematics of a marriage where the wife kept the bedroom. The cushions were shaped to his body the way the left side of the mattress used to be. He sat down but didn't lie back. Lying down meant sleep, and sleep meant morning, and morning meant another day of the question.

On the shelf beside the couch, between the phone charger and the book he'd been failing to read for three months, there was a drawing. Not from the sketchbook — a loose page, torn from a larger sheet, that Theo had given him two days ago without ceremony. Just handed it over at breakfast and said "that one's for you" and gone back to his cereal. It was the apartment building. Their building. Colored pencil on paper, rendered with the particular combination of architectural ambition and spatial guesswork that a nine-year-old brings to a subject he's been studying for months — the windows not quite aligned, the brownstone steps too wide, the front door a rectangle of red that looked less like a door than a mouth. But the proportions were right. The weight of the building was right. Theo had been drawing this building over and over, working through something by repetition that he couldn't work through any other way, and two days ago he'd decided one version was finished enough to give away.

Dan picked it up and held it under the reading lamp clipped to the shelf. The building stood there, solid and slightly wrong, the way real buildings stand — imperfect, lived in, carrying more weight than their architecture accounts for. The windows were dark. No figures in them. Just the building. Their home, rendered by a child who had been watching it more carefully than either of his parents had been watching each other.

He set the drawing back on the shelf. He turned off the lamp. He lay down and pulled the blanket to his chest and looked at the ceiling, where a water stain from two winters ago had dried into a shape he'd once told Theo looked like a duck. Theo had said it looked more like a hand. They'd argued about it for ten minutes, and it looked like neither — it looked like water damage on old plaster — but he couldn't look at it anymore without seeing both.

---

In the bedroom, Mira lay on her back with her eyes open. The window unit hummed its mechanical drone — higher-pitched than the refrigerator, steadier, with the periodic rattle of a filter she kept meaning to replace. Through the door she'd left ajar, the building's nighttime sounds arrived in sequence: the pipe knocking in the wall, the settling of old wood in the joists, and, faintly, the sound of Dan shifting on the couch — the particular creak of springs that meant he was lying down, which meant he was done sitting at the table, which meant he was doing what she was doing: staring at a ceiling and replaying.

She had said the thing. Not the version she'd been composing in her head for months — the clean, defensible version, the strategic articulation she'd have delivered in a conference room. *I filed because the marginal returns on the partnership were negative. I filed because the opportunity cost of staying exceeded the cost of leaving.* She could have said that. Mira could always find the language that made a feeling sound like a decision. But tonight at the table, with the bulb yellowing over the sink and Dan's hands flat on the wood and nowhere left to hide behind a classification or a strategy, the corporate scaffolding had come away and what was underneath was: *bearable*. The marriage was bearable and she'd been afraid that bearable was the highest shelf she'd ever reach for.

She turned onto her side. The sheets were cool against her legs. The reading lamp on her nightstand lit a circle on the pillow next to hers — Dan's pillow — she hadn't sent it to the couch with him, and it sat there every night occupying a space that was the wrong kind of empty. She should have given it to him months ago. She hadn't, and she'd never examined why, because examining it would have required admitting that the pillow's presence on the bed was not an oversight but a choice, and the choice meant something she wasn't ready to name.

What she hadn't said at the table — what she hadn't been able to assemble into sentences while the conversation was happening — was the other half. That the months under the classification, the fighting and the strategy and the captivity, had not been entirely what she'd told herself they were. That being locked into the apartment with Dan, with the bilateral node sealing them in, had forced a quality of attention she hadn't managed when she was free. That the 2 a.m. kitchen and the night on the couch with Theo feverish between them and the argument after the financial mandates when they'd said things that drew blood — all of it, even the damage — had been more present than anything in the years of fine. She'd been awake in this marriage for the first time in years, and the alarm had been rung by a machine, and now the machine had gone silent, and she was lying in the dark trying to determine whether she could stay awake without it.

She didn't know. That was the answer she'd given Dan and it was the truth and the truth felt insufficient and she was not accustomed to insufficiency. She was the woman who found angles. She was the strategist who always had a next move. She'd built her professional identity and her personal survival on the ability to read a system and locate its pressure points and apply force where it would do the most work. And here, in a bedroom in Park Slope with the window unit rattling and her estranged husband breathing audibly on the couch through a door she'd left ajar, the system was two people and the pressure points were whatever was left of their willingness to be honest with each other, and no amount of analysis could tell her whether honesty was enough to rebuild on or just the sound a marriage makes as it finally finishes falling.

She thought about the dissolution form. Step 3. The screen she'd stared at for eleven minutes in the glass conference room where she'd spent the last eighteen months performing competence for cameras and empty chairs. SHARED PROPERTY — DESCRIPTION. The cursor had blinked. She'd typed nothing. She'd closed the browser and gone back to her desk and spent the afternoon presenting Gemini-state's quarterly projections to a room that didn't need her to be in it, and the entire time she'd been thinking about a kitchen table in Brooklyn, and a man who'd said *I was already disappearing*, and whether the fact that he could see it now — could name it, could sit at a table and say it to her face — changed anything, or whether seeing clearly was just another form of the passivity she'd been married to for twelve years.

The window unit cycled. The rattle sharpened, then smoothed. Through the ajar door, the apartment breathed its nighttime breath — the refrigerator, the pipes, the creak of the building settling into itself.

She closed her eyes.

The question was in the apartment the way the sounds were in the apartment: present, formless, taking up no specific space and filling all of it. It would be there in the morning when Theo came out in his T-shirt and his father's headphones slung around his neck and asked what was for breakfast. It would be there when Dan left for the F train and she left for the 2/3 and they walked to separate subway stations the way they'd walked every workday for a year and a half, the daily parting that used to feel like relief and now felt like something she didn't have a word for. It would be there at the kitchen table, in the scratch in the wood, in the water ring, in the chair with the shimmed leg.

*Do you want this.* Not: are you required to keep this. Not: has the machine decided. Not: is this the optimal allocation of two human lives. Just: do you want this. The apartment, the table, the man on the couch, the child asleep down the hall who draws the building he lives in because buildings are the things that stay. Is this what you'd choose if you could choose anything at all.

She didn't know. The not-knowing settled over her like the sheets, light and specific and insufficient. She lay still and listened to the apartment and did not get up.

Down the hall, on the couch, Dan lay still and listened to the apartment and did not get up.

Neither of them slept for a long time.
