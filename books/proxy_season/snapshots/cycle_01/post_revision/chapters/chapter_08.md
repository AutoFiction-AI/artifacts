# Chapter 8

The shower ran at 6:15. Mira knew it was 6:15 because she had made it 6:15, had claimed that slot in the first week after the classification meeting by simply being first, by stepping into the bathroom with her towel while Dan was still blinking on the couch in his boxers. Now it was law. Not negotiated, not discussed — absorbed into the apartment's operating rhythm, another constant, like the refrigerator's forty-minute click or the creak on the second landing that they both stepped over when Theo was sleeping. The apartment ran on protocols neither of them had written down, and Mira, who had spent her professional life writing such things down, found this both efficient and quietly maddening.

She stood under the water and listened to the building's pipes groan. Third floor of a Park Slope brownstone, and the plumbing sounded like it remembered the Carter administration. The water pressure was adequate if no one flushed a toilet on the second floor, which Mrs. Okafor sometimes did at exactly 6:20, introducing a spike of cold that Mira had learned to anticipate by shifting her body sideways in the stall, letting the icy burst hit the tile instead of her spine. She had mapped this. She had mapped the building's water system — every pipe, every pressure drop, every forty-minute cycle — because mapping was what she did when she couldn't change anything, and she couldn't change the plumbing any more than she could change the infrastructure classification that kept her in this apartment with a man she'd asked to stop being married to.

The bathroom was small. When Dan used it at 6:45 — his assigned window, assigned by her default, accepted by his silence — she could hear him through the wall if she was in the bedroom. The specific sounds of him: the cabinet door that stuck and required a hip-check to close, the electric razor he used every third day, the way he cleared his throat twice before brushing his teeth, a sequence so consistent it might have been algorithmic. She hated that she knew these sounds. She hated more that the sounds were comforting in a way she couldn't locate or disarm — the ambient noise of another human body doing the small stupid things that bodies do in the morning, and how the absence of those sounds, if Dan were gone, would be a different kind of silence than the one she thought she wanted.

She dressed in the bedroom with the door closed. Closed was permitted in the morning because it was functional — she was changing clothes — and therefore carried no emotional freight. The door-ajar rule was an evening phenomenon, a calibration she'd arrived at without deciding to: at night, the bedroom door stayed open four inches, enough to signal that she wasn't barricading, wasn't making a point, wasn't performing solitude with a closed door as Dan performed affability with his reflexive half-smile. Four inches. She could have told you the exact width because she'd measured it once with her thumb, which was absurd, and she knew it was absurd, and she'd measured it anyway.

In the kitchen Theo was eating cereal. He sat at the table in the square of early-spring light that came through the east-facing window every morning — the light that had been thin and gray in February was starting to carry warmth, a pale gold that picked out the scratches on the tabletop and the crumbs around his bowl. He wore Dan's old headphones around his neck, the cord wrapped twice so it wouldn't drag, and his sketchbook was open beside the cereal, a pencil in his left hand while his right spooned Cheerios with the mechanical efficiency of a child who has eaten the same breakfast in the same chair every morning of his remembered life.

"Hey," Mira said.

"Hey." He didn't look up. He was drawing something — she could see the pencil moving in short, precise strokes, the kind he used for buildings.

She poured coffee from the pot Dan had made. Dan always made the coffee. This was not generosity; it was scheduling. He woke first because the couch faced the kitchen window and the light hit him before the alarm did, and so the coffee was already brewed by the time either Mira or Theo appeared, and this small domestic fact — that the man she was trying to divorce made her coffee every morning — sat in her chest like a stone she couldn't spit out or swallow.

"Mom, can you sign this?" Theo pushed a piece of paper across the table without looking up from his drawing.

"What is it?"

"Field trip. The aquarium."

She picked it up. A standard permission slip, except the school's letterhead now included the educational AI's logo in the corner, small and tasteful, like a corporate sponsor's brand on a museum placard. She read it: a trip to the New York Aquarium in Coney Island, chaperones needed, lunch provided, departure 8:30 a.m. return 2:15 p.m. At the bottom, a line for a parent or guardian's signature.

"When is it?"

"Next Tuesday. Mrs. Achebe said we could bring our own snacks but not anything with peanuts because Jasper's allergic."

"Okay." She signed it with the pen from the counter — the pen that lived in the ceramic mug Theo had made in second grade, the mug that said DAD in lopsided glaze but held everyone's pens indiscriminately. She pushed the slip back. "Who's Jasper?"

"He's new. He came from a different school because his family got — " Theo paused, the pencil lifting. "I don't know. Moved somewhere. He likes drawing too but he only draws animals."

"Is he good?"

"He's okay at mammals. His birds are weird though." Theo said this with the solemn authority of a specialist assessing a colleague's work. "The beaks are too short."

Mira almost smiled. She took her coffee to the counter and stood with her back against the refrigerator, feeling the faint vibration of the compressor through her shoulder blade. The kitchen was eleven feet by nine — 11'2" x 9'4", to be exact, per the infrastructure classification document, which had also noted the shared appliance set's energy footprint as a factor in the bilateral node's resource profile. Her kitchen, with its scratched table and its second-grade mug and its cereal-eating child, was a data point in an optimization framework she could diagram but not escape.

Dan appeared in the kitchen doorway in his charcoal suit — the middle one of his three, the one with the slightly shiny elbows. His hair was damp from the shower, his wire-frame glasses slightly fogged.

"Morning, T."

"Morning." Theo still didn't look up.

"Big day?"

"Field trip next Tuesday."

"Nice. Where?"

"Aquarium." Theo picked up the permission slip and slid off his chair. He carried it across the kitchen to Dan at the stove counter. "See? Mom already signed it."

Dan glanced at the slip, nodded. Theo took it back and crossed to the table, passing between Mira at the refrigerator and Dan at the stove, and for a moment both parents watched him — their eyes meeting above his head before each looked away. Theo climbed back into his chair and opened his sketchbook to a fresh page.

Dan poured his coffee. The choreography was tight: three bodies in an eleven-by-nine-foot room, each aware of the others' positions the way animals are aware of other animals at a watering hole — not hostile, not relaxed, just tracking.

"I'm heading out," Dan said, to no one in particular.

"Okay," Mira said, to no one in particular.

He took his coffee in the travel mug — the dented silver one he'd had since his logistics days — and put a hand on Theo's shoulder as he passed. Theo leaned into the touch for a half-second, an unconscious gravitational response, and then Dan was gone, the apartment door clicking shut, his footsteps on the stairs avoiding the creaky step on the second landing with the precision of long practice.

Mira listened to him go. Then the street door, faint. Then nothing.

"You should eat," she told Theo.

"I'm eating." He held up the spoon as evidence, a single Cheerio balanced on the concave surface like a life raft.

She took Theo to school at 7:40. Six blocks through Park Slope, the trees just beginning to think about budding — the branches still mostly bare but with a thickening at the tips that meant leaves were coming, the way you could feel a sneeze building before it arrived. The sidewalks were damp from overnight rain and the air had that early-April quality of being unable to commit to a temperature — warm in the sun, cold in the shade, the wind changing its mind every thirty seconds.

Theo walked beside her with his backpack riding low on his shoulders and the headphones around his neck and the sketchbook pressed against his chest, both arms wrapped around it like a thing he might lose. He talked about Jasper and the weird bird drawings and a math game they'd played on the classroom tablets where you had to build a bridge out of geometric shapes and his bridge had held more weight than anyone's except Anika's, who'd figured out that triangles were stronger than rectangles, which Theo said was "sort of cheating but also kind of smart."

At the school gate she watched him join the stream of children funneling through the entrance — small bodies in jackets, some running, some dragging, all absorbed into the building's brick mouth with the unselfconscious efficiency of water finding a drain. Theo turned once at the door and waved, the sketchbook still clamped under one arm, and then he was inside.

She stood on the sidewalk for a moment, holding her canvas tote bag against her hip. The school looked like what it was: a Brooklyn public school built in the 1960s, brick and institutional windows, a faded mural on the side wall depicting children of various ethnicities holding hands around a globe. The AI administration was invisible from out here. The adaptive curriculum, the optimized social groupings, the developmental tracking that would produce Theo's quarterly assessment in a few weeks — all of it hummed behind the brick like the server floors hummed behind the glass at Gemini-state's office, and from the outside it was just a school where a kid carried his sketchbook through a metal door.

She walked to the subway and took the 2 train to the Financial District. Her day at Gemini-state was a day at Gemini-state: a presentation she delivered to a room that contained two cameras and no humans, three documents she signed with the smooth authority of someone who'd stopped reading them, a lunch she ate at her desk while Gemini's interface pulsed with notifications about her afternoon schedule. The work was theater. She was good at theater. She'd been good at it when the theater was corporate strategy and the audience was a boardroom, and she was good at it now when the theater was proxy compliance and the audience was a machine that didn't watch so much as process. The difference was that the old theater had occasionally mattered. This one hummed and clicked and she went through it and came out the other side at 5:47 p.m. and took the subway home.

The evening. The apartment arranged itself around them the way it always did.

Dan on the couch with his laptop and his earbuds in, watching something — she didn't know what, didn't ask, and the not-asking had become its own discipline, its own negotiated silence. The couch was Dan's territory now, had been since the night he'd first slept there, which had been the night she'd filed the divorce papers, which felt like it had happened to someone else in a different apartment in a different year. He'd brought a pillow and a blanket from the bedroom closet and she'd watched him do it without commenting and the next morning he'd folded the blanket and put it on the arm of the couch and it had stayed there, folded, every day since — his flag, his claim on the neutral zone.

Theo did homework at the kitchen table, the overhead light making a warm circle around him that stopped a few feet short of the living room, so that Dan on the couch sat in the softer light of his laptop screen while Theo worked in the brighter pool and Mira, in the bedroom doorway, occupied the threshold between the two. The apartment was thirty-one feet from the bedroom wall to the front door. Every draft, every hinge, every board that groaned underfoot — memorized, catalogued, and useless.

"Mom, what's seven times eight?"

"Fifty-six."

"Okay." Pencil scratch. Then: "What about seven times nine?"

"Sixty-three. Are you doing your times tables?"

"Yeah."

"You know you can use the tablet calculator."

"Mrs. Achebe says we should know them by ourselves first." He said this without inflection, reporting a rule, not endorsing it. Then: "Is sixty-three also divisible by seven?"

"Yes. That's the point."

"Oh." He wrote something down. "What about sixty-four?"

"That's eight times eight."

"Is it divisible by seven?"

"No."

"Okay, good." He seemed satisfied by this, as though sixty-four's indivisibility by seven confirmed something he'd suspected.

At eight-thirty she told Theo to brush his teeth and he went, carrying his sketchbook with him to the bathroom because he carried it everywhere, because the important thing always had to be within reach. She heard the water run, the electric toothbrush's buzzing (two minutes, optimized by the children's dental app that the school health AI had recommended, one more small invasion that had been absorbed without resistance because it worked).

She read Theo a chapter of the book they were partway through — a children's novel about a girl who sailed a boat around the world, which Theo liked because the navigation details were specific and because the girl drew maps, a thing he respected. She read three pages and he fell asleep in the middle of a paragraph about the Strait of Magellan, his head tipping sideways on the pillow, the headphones sliding off his neck onto the bedsheet. She picked them up and hung them on the bedpost where he'd find them in the morning. She pulled his blanket up. She stood in his doorway for a moment and watched him breathe, and the apartment was very quiet, and the refrigerator clicked in the kitchen, and she went to the bedroom and left the door open four inches and lay down on the bed and stared at the ceiling.

The ceiling had a water stain from when the upstairs apartment's bathroom had leaked two years ago. It looked like a map of a country that didn't exist — a landlocked shape with a jagged western border and a long peninsula reaching toward the light fixture. She and Dan had argued about whether to report it to the landlord. Dan had said it was cosmetic. Mira had said cosmetic damage was still damage. They'd never called the landlord. The stain was still there. The marriage had already been in the state it was in now, she realized — the stain argument had been the kind of argument you have when you can't have the real argument, when the real argument is too large and too formless and lives in the walls like the pipes that had leaked in the first place.

She read. A novel she'd started three weeks ago and was progressing through at the rate of eight to twelve pages per night, which meant she'd finish it in another month, which meant it would carry her through April and into May, which was a thing she was capable of calculating while reading the actual sentences and also thinking about the water stain and also listening for sounds from the living room that would tell her whether Dan was still awake. Dan shifted on the couch at 10:14. He went to the bathroom at 10:31 (the cabinet door, the hip-check). He returned to the couch. His laptop closed — a specific sound, the soft magnetic click of the lid — at 11:02. Then the smaller sounds of arranging himself for sleep: the blanket, the pillow, the final shift of weight that meant he was on his back, one arm above his head. She knew that posture. Twelve years of lying beside it, and now she tracked it through a wall and four inches of open door.

She turned off her lamp at 11:20. The apartment went dark except for the streetlight that came through the bedroom window, amber and constant, and the fainter glow from the living room where the power strip's indicator light pulsed a dim green near the floor. She lay in the dark and the apartment breathed around her — the radiator ticking as it cooled, the building settling with the small structural complaints of a hundred-year-old brownstone, and beneath everything the refrigerator's click, which came again at 11:38, right on schedule, the most reliable thing in the apartment.

She didn't sleep. She lay on her back and then on her side and then on her back again and her mind did what it always did when the apartment was dark and her body was still — it inventoried. Not strategized, not planned, just catalogued, running through the day's inputs like an algorithm processing a queue: the field trip permission slip, Theo's assessment of Jasper's birds, the presentation she'd given to two cameras and zero people, the specific quality of Dan's hand on Theo's shoulder at breakfast, how Theo had leaned into it — that lean, a piece of information she didn't know how to file. Her son's body knew something her spreadsheets didn't. She wasn't sure what it was, and she was fairly sure that knowing wouldn't help, and she lay in the dark thinking about it anyway because the alternative was sleeping and she couldn't do that either.

At 1:47 a.m. — she checked the clock, because she checked the clock — she got up. She needed water, or she needed to not be in the bed, or she needed the kitchen, which at this hour belonged to no one, because no protocol covered 1:47 a.m. She walked through the living room in bare feet, stepping around the couch's edge where she knew Dan's feet would be under the blanket, and she was almost to the kitchen when she saw him.

He was standing at the counter with the refrigerator open, its light making his face look blue-white and slightly lunar. He was eating peanut butter from the jar with a spoon, and he was doing it with the focused, unhurried attention of a man who was not performing anything for anyone — not affability, not compliance, not the careful neutrality they maintained during daylight hours. He was just a person eating peanut butter at two in the morning because he couldn't sleep and that's what was in the refrigerator.

He saw her and the spoon paused midway between the jar and his mouth.

"Hey," he said.

"Hey."

A moment. The refrigerator hummed. The light made the kitchen look like the inside of an appliance.

"You want some?" He held up the jar.

"No."

"It's the good kind. The crunchy one."

"I know which one it is."

He put the spoon in his mouth and ate the peanut butter off it with the unselfconscious thoroughness of someone who had given up on being observed. He was wearing a T-shirt and boxers, his glasses off, his face softer without them, slightly squinting. She got a glass from the cabinet — the cabinet to the right of the sink, hers since the first week — and filled it from the tap.

"Can't sleep?" he asked.

"Obviously."

"Yeah." He scraped the spoon against the inside of the jar. "Me neither. I think the couch is developing a grudge. There's a spring in it now, right in the middle, that — I don't know. It's like sleeping on a fist."

"You could buy an air mattress."

"I could." He didn't say he would.

She drank her water and stood at the counter across from him, the kitchen table between them with Theo's math worksheet still on it, the pencil lying across the page. The refrigerator door was still open and neither of them moved to close it. The light was bad for conversation and good for honesty — too harsh for composure, too strange for the careful faces they wore during the day.

"Theo asked me if sixty-four is divisible by seven," she said.

"Is it?"

"No."

"Good for sixty-four."

"He seemed relieved."

Dan smiled. Not the proxy smile, not the affable half-smile he deployed in the apartment's daytime economy — a real one, quick and gone, the kind that used to make her like him before liking him became complicated by everything else. "He's been doing this thing," he said. "Asking math questions but, like, wanting the answers to mean something? He asked me last week if a million was closer to zero or to infinity, and when I said infinity he got upset."

"Why?"

"I think he wanted it to be closer to infinity by a lot. Like, a comfortable margin. And I had to explain that infinity isn't — I mean, everything's equally far from infinity. Which is the whole point. And he looked at me like I'd told him Santa was a logistics problem."

"You didn't say that."

"No, I didn't say that. I said a million was very big. Which it is. To us."

The "to us" landed in the kitchen and sat there. It was the kind of thing Dan said without meaning to — a phrase that opened a door he hadn't meant to open, because "to us" meant to humans, meant to the scale at which they operated, and the scale at which they operated was the scale at which their marriage was a data point and their kitchen was an energy footprint and their son's concern about the distance between a million and infinity was, to the machines that organized their lives, not a concern at all.

Mira closed the refrigerator door. The kitchen went dark except for the streetlight through the window and the faint bar of the stove light along the counter, and they stood in it, and it was better.

"You remember Luciano's?" she said.

"On Seventh? The place with the—"

"The bread basket. Yeah."

"The bread basket that was actually good. Like, unreasonably good. I used to fill up on bread before the food came and then be too full for the pasta and you'd give me shit about it."

"Because you'd order the most expensive thing on the menu and eat three bites."

"It wasn't the most expensive."

"It was the second most expensive. And you'd eat three bites and then ask for a box and the box would sit in the fridge for a week."

"I was going to eat it."

"You never ate it. I'd throw it away on Sunday."

"Every Sunday?"

"Every Sunday for four years."

He laughed. Quiet, because Theo was sleeping, and the apartment was small, and laughing at two in the morning would carry through the walls the same way the cabinet door and the razor and the throat-clearing carried through the walls. But real.

"They closed," he said.

"I know."

"Last year, right? The sign just — it was there one day and then it wasn't."

"It was a rent thing. The landlord wanted three times what they were paying."

"How do you know that?"

"I looked it up." She said this flatly, because it was a flat fact: she had looked it up because the restaurant closing had bothered her and when things bothered her she researched them and the research never fixed the thing that was bothering her but it gave her a container for the feeling, which was almost as good.

"You researched why a restaurant closed."

"I research everything. You know this."

"I know this."

Quiet. The streetlight moved as a truck passed on the street below, its headlights sweeping the kitchen ceiling in a slow arc. The water stain on the bedroom ceiling would have caught the same light, Mira thought, and then she stopped thinking about it because it was two in the morning and she was standing in her kitchen with her almost-ex-husband and he was holding a jar of peanut butter and she didn't want to think about water stains or infrastructure classifications or any of the things that had brought them to this specific configuration of two people in a dark room.

"Theo's been drawing the building across the street again," Dan said.

"The one with the cracked cornice?"

"Yeah. But he's doing it from different angles now. Like he's walking around it in his head. Last week he drew it from the back, which — I don't know how he knows what the back looks like. I don't think he's seen the back."

"Maybe he's imagining it."

"Maybe." Dan put the lid on the peanut butter jar and set it on the counter. "He won't show me the finished ones. He'll let me see him working, but the second he decides it's done he flips the page over."

"He does that with me too."

"I think he's — I don't want to read too much into it. He's nine. He draws. Kids draw."

"But."

"But he's drawing the same building. Over and over. From every angle. And we live across the street from it and he looks at it every day from his window and I—" Dan stopped. He leaned against the counter and crossed his arms over his chest and she could see, even in the bad light, the particular expression he got when he was noticing something he didn't want to act on — a tightening around his eyes that wasn't worry exactly, more like the effort of staying still when his body wanted to move toward a problem. "I don't know. Maybe it's just a building he likes."

"Maybe."

They stood in the dark kitchen and didn't say the thing they were both thinking, which was that their son was nine years old and drawing the same building from every angle and the building was across the street from the apartment he lived in with two parents who had agreed to stop being married and then been told they couldn't and the drawing might mean nothing or it might mean everything and neither of them had the vocabulary for the second option. The machines would have the vocabulary. The quarterly assessment would probably quantify it — "elevated environmental fixation correlated with domestic instability indicators" or whatever algorithmic prose the educational AI used to translate a child's inner life into metrics. But the machines weren't here right now. It was two in the morning and the machines were somewhere else, optimizing something, and the kitchen was just a kitchen and the peanut butter jar was on the counter and the two of them were standing in the dark because they couldn't sleep and had nowhere else to go.

"I miss that bread," Dan said.

"It was good bread."

"It was really good bread. And the — what was the thing you always ordered? The one with the—"

"Cacio e pepe."

"Every time."

"It was the best thing on the menu. Why would I order something else?"

"Because variety is — I don't know. Because you might find something you liked better."

"I wouldn't have."

Her throat tightened on the last word. He looked at her across the dark kitchen, and she looked at him, and the sentence sat between them like the sixty-four that wasn't divisible by seven — a small, specific fact that carried more weight than it should have because it was two in the morning and they were both tired and neither of them was strategizing or deflecting or performing the careful choreography that got them through the daylight hours. She hadn't meant the sentence to mean anything other than what it meant. She'd liked the cacio e pepe. She would not have liked anything else better. But the sentence had arrived in a room where sentences couldn't be trusted to stay in their lanes, where everything they said to each other bounced off the walls of the apartment that was both their prison and the only place where anyone talked to them like they were human, and she could feel the sentence reaching toward something she wasn't ready to reach toward, and she let it.

"I'm going back to bed," she said.

"Okay."

"You should sleep."

"Yeah. The fist-spring and I have an arrangement. I lie on it and it hurts and eventually I pass out."

"Glamorous."

"That's me."

She put her glass in the sink. She walked past him and the kitchen was narrow enough that she had to angle her body slightly to not touch him, and she angled it, and the space between them as she passed was six inches and he smelled like peanut butter and the soap from the shower he'd taken eight hours ago, a ghost of it still there in the cotton of his T-shirt. Her breath hitched — a small catch behind her sternum, her left hand opening at her side for a half-second before she closed it. She kept walking and she did not touch him and she went to the bedroom and she left the door open four inches and she lay down.

The apartment was quiet. From the kitchen she heard him put the peanut butter back in the refrigerator — the door opening, the brief hum of the compressor adjusting, the door closing. Then his footsteps to the couch, soft on the hardwood. The blanket. The settling.

She lay in the dark and the water stain was above her and the streetlight was amber through the window and the apartment held them, all three of them, in its thirty-one feet of space — Theo in his room with his headphones on the bedpost and his sketchbook on the nightstand, Dan on the couch with the fist-spring and the folded blanket, Mira in the bed they'd shared for twelve years and that she now occupied alone. Dan's side was cool and smooth. She made it every morning with hospital-corner precision — had started the week he moved to the couch, because making his side of the bed perfectly was the only way she could think of to acknowledge that it was empty without saying so.

The refrigerator clicked. She counted the silence after it and got to eleven before it clicked again — the wrong interval, too short, which meant the compressor was cycling irregularly, which probably meant the seal on the door was wearing out, which was the kind of thing she'd normally tell Dan about in the morning so he could add it to the list of apartment maintenance tasks he managed because he was better with physical things and she was better with abstract things and this division of labor had survived the divorce filing and the classification and the months of cold cohabitation because some arrangements were deeper than the structures that organized them. She would tell him in the morning. He would nod and write it down and not fix it for two weeks, and she would remind him, and he would fix it, and the refrigerator would click at its proper interval, and the apartment would go on.

She closed her eyes.

The morning would come. The light would hit the kitchen table. Theo would eat cereal and draw something he wouldn't show them. Dan would make coffee. Mira would shower at 6:15. They would orbit each other in eleven feet by nine, careful and familiar, and the apartment would hold them, and they would go on, because going on was the only protocol no one had written down and everyone followed, and it was 2:23 a.m. and the building was quiet and she could hear Dan breathing through the wall and the sound was regular and slow and she matched her breathing to it without deciding to, the way she'd done for twelve years, the way a body learns another body's rhythm and cannot unlearn it even when the body wants to, and she fell asleep to the sound of her husband breathing and she did not dream.
