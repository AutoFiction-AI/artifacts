# Chapter 6

Silas woke on two flour sacks in Junie's back room with his cheek stuck to rough canvas and Patent Row still in his nose, though the smell rising under it did not belong in any honest kitchen. There was broth in it, yes, and old onions, lamp smoke, river damp, vinegar, yeast, all the ordinary Ferrymouth perfumes that got into your clothes and stayed there until rain beat them out. Under it sat the sweeter note, the one he had learned to distrust the day before: warm pond rot with a sugared edge, as if somebody had poured syrup over ditch water and called it progress.

Pink light quivered under the prep table.

He rolled onto an elbow too fast, banged his head against the shelf above him, and heard Junie say, from somewhere near the sink, "Good. You're alive enough to scrub."

The back kitchen looked as if Patent Row had vomited through it in the night. Three lamp trays had been hauled off the range and laid upside down on old towels. Their rims wore a fur of pale slime, not one color but several failing to agree: bridal pink at the edges, a sickly milky white where the tin had held heat, a ribbon of mourning blue in the seams. A handbill with half of GIDEON WREN'S VERDANCY HOUSE gone to pulp clung to the leg of the wash table. The word CROWN still showed in a curl of green ink before the paper dissolved into jelly.

Junie stood at the sink in shirtsleeves, broad shoulders shining with sweat, her hair pinned hard and crooked because one side had already worked loose. The small healed split in her left eyebrow had gone white with heat. She scraped at a ladle with a fish knife and counted under her breath as if she were keeping score against God. "One good kettle. Two trays maybe. One ladle gone to hell. One brother too stupid to die when it'd be useful."

Silas pushed himself up. His coat, stiff with dried splash from the launch, hung over the lamp rack. The cuff nearest the floor had gone tender pink and was putting out a faint expensive glow, the kind Gideon would have liked under glass and Junie would have liked nowhere near supper.

"How bad?" Silas asked. His mouth tasted like old metal. The back of his neck still remembered the crowd laughing when the wall had outperformed his brush.

Junie did not waste time giving the answer shape. "Bad enough that if I had let the first ferry crew in an hour ago, I'd have served stew by diseased moonlight. Bad enough that your lovely coat spent the night teaching my trays bad habits. Bad enough that you can stop looking ashamed and start carrying water."

"I didn't mean to bring it here."

"You brought it here, laid that coat on my rack, and slept like a saint in a painting while my stove learned new tricks. Don't hand me remorse when I need your hands."

He got to his feet. His shirt had twisted around him in the night. The room was close already though the day had barely started, and outside the half-open door Ferrymouth was waking in knocks and gull shrieks and a ferry bell struck flat by damp. He picked the handbill off the table leg. It stretched before it tore. A clot of white slime dropped onto his boot and crept, deliberate as gossip, toward the leather seam.

Junie pointed with the fish knife. "Cold bucket first. Pump's still clean. Hot water makes it throw spores."

"Since when do you know that?"

"Since sunrise. Move."

By the time he came back with two sloshing buckets cutting his shoulders raw, Junie had stripped the front range down to iron and hauled the bread board onto a flour barrel. She had not once, he could tell, permitted herself the luxury of standing still to be miserable. Ruined things had been sorted into piles: salvage, doubtful, gone. There were bones for stock, onions worth cutting, and aprons clean enough to lie with. The kitchen had the grim order of a battlefield if a battlefield also needed to serve eel pie by noon.

She jerked her chin at the buckets. "There. Now strip off that coat before it courts my dish line."

"You say the sweetest things to me."

"I say them because your father is dead and nobody else did the job well enough."

Silas peeled the coat off without answering and held it away from himself. Wet light clung to the nap in streaks. Yesterday, under the buyers' parasols and the shouting, it had seemed almost handsome for a minute, the wall running faster than paint, color leaping from one insult to the next. Here it looked diseased, worse than that, ridiculous, like a clown's skin after the clown had drowned.

Junie tossed him an apron. "Wear that. If the coat still answers to those fools, I don't want it within spitting distance of the soup."

He caught the apron and tied it on. "What did you do with the customers?"

"Told breakfast they could eat bread and cold herrings or starve with dignity elsewhere. Most of them chose bread. Two chose to complain. They're still alive, so perhaps I was merciful."

She set one of the trays in the cold basin and the water answered with a nasty little hiss. The slime on the rim tightened to pearled beads, then loosened again as if reconsidering. Junie looked at it with flat dislike.

"It started in the rack," she said. "You left your coat there. One of the launch cards was stuck in the cuff. By dawn the left side of the stove was growing lace. Then the lace got ideas."

"Shit."

"Yes." She scraped with the fish knife and a flap of pinkish mucus lifted, then snapped back and slapped her wrist. "And if you call it beautiful, I'll break your nose."

Silas took a rag and reached for the basin. The tray was cold at the center and meanly hot at the handles, as if it had opinions about where a man ought to touch it. He nearly dropped it.

Junie barked a laugh without any amusement in it. "There. You finally named something true with your face."

He wanted, stupidly, to explain the launch. He wanted to say the wall had gone bad before the chant was half set, that children on the balconies had yelled other names and the moss had flashed through colors quicker than he could mix them, that Damaris Pike had looked delighted enough to kiss him and Gideon grim enough to invoice the weather. He wanted to say he had walked down from Patent Row wearing half the city's laughter like a second shirt. Junie would have heard none of it. The ruined ladle at the sink made that plain.

Instead he said, "Can it be cleaned?"

"I sent for Miss Cordero."

He looked up too fast. "You sent for her."

"I sent for the person who knows how not to poison a kitchen. Your vanity wasn't in the neighborhood."

He bent over the basin before Junie could see the heat that answer kicked through him. Want came in braided tight with dread. Inez arriving to find him asleep in the wreckage of his own public performance felt like standing up for judgment with dishwater on his hands. He ran the rag along the tray rim. The slime bunched under the cloth and let out a smell like old flowers left in bilge water.

Junie said with the rough mercy she used instead of comfort, "Save the face for later, Sile. She has seen worse than you."

"That's a comfort."

"It ought to be."

He scrubbed. The tray fought him with small obscene persistence, as if each stringy edge preferred the kitchen it was ruining. Twice he thought he had the seam clean and twice the blue came back up from the metal like a bruise surfacing. Junie worked beside him in hard efficient strokes. They spoke only when something needed moving. The hush between them was not peace. It was labor.

When the back door opened again, river noise came in with a cooler strip of air and Inez Cordero behind it.

She had tied a dark work scarf over her hair and looped a length of copper wire through the knot, so it rode against her shoulder like something she might need before noon. Her skirt was hitched for walking on planks. Mud striped the hem in careful dried fans. She carried a canvas tool roll in one hand and a sack under the other arm that left wet salt dust on her sleeve. The crescent scar on the inside of her right wrist showed when she set the sack down.

Her gaze went first to Junie, then to the trays, then to Silas in the apron. The order of that stung more than if she had started with him.

"How many?" she asked.

Junie held up three fingers. "Three trays touched. One ladle gone soft. Kettle skirt only, I think."

Inez crossed to the basin and put her knuckles against the tray rim instead of grabbing it. Then she bent and listened, as if the tin might answer through sound before it answered through color. "You were right to keep it cold." She glanced at the handbill pulp near Silas's boot. "And wrong to let that paper stay in the room."

Junie snorted. "By the time I found it, it had married the floorboards."

"It'll come up."

"So will rent," Junie said. "That doesn't make me cheerful."

Inez's mouth moved at one corner into something drier than a smile. "No. It doesn't."

Silas said, "If you're here to enjoy yourself, you've chosen a poor house for it."

She looked at him then, full on. Her face gave him nothing cheap. The room, already close, seemed to take one slow breath and hold it. "If I wanted enjoyment, Mr. Vale, I'd have stayed where people know better than to sleep in a kitchen wearing active moss."

Junie pointed the fish knife between them. "No public quarrel in my back room. If either of you starts performing, I'll put your heads in separate buckets. Miss Cordero, tell me what can be saved. Sile, stop bleeding pride on the floor and fetch the coarse salt."

Inez untied the sack and turned it out: salt, two strips of old burlap, a narrow scraper with a bone handle worn smooth by years of thumb pressure, a stoppered bottle of sour whey, and a bundle of dried reeds cut to brush size. She laid them out in a straight line on the table, each tool placed as precisely as a syllable.

Silas went for the salt crock. When he came back, Inez had already shaved a thumbnail of white growth off the tray and pinned it under the scraper blade. It twitched once. He would have sworn it twitched.

"Don't use the fish knife," she told Junie. "It'll nick the tin and give it somewhere to start again."

"Then your man can have the fish knife and his own hand if he wants excitement."

"He's had enough of that," Inez said.

Junie barked a laugh this time and meant it.

Silas set down the crock harder than necessary. "Am I in the room?"

"Unfortunately," Junie said.

Inez did not bother with him. She tipped sour whey onto the tray seam. The slime recoiled from the acid smell and thinned from milky white to a washed-out green. "Hold it there," she said.

He took the tray, careful now. Her fingers brushed his for less than a second. Hers were cool from the walk outside, callused at the pads, the skin damp with mist or sweat. The contact was practical and it ran through him like a lie.

"There," she said, and scraped. A wet ribbon came free with a soft tearing sound that made his shoulders climb. "Good. Keep the rim level."

"I'm keeping it level."

Junie looked over. "You aren't."

"I'm close."

"Close burns soup."

Inez glanced once at his hands and adjusted the tray with two quick pushes of her thumbs. "Now you're close."

They fell into an ugly little rhythm. They soured the seam, salted the scrape, wiped it down, moved it into the second basin, and brought it back to the table. The slime changed manners every few minutes. Sometimes it clung stubborn and gelatinous. Sometimes it broke into pearls that rolled for the cracks in the boards. Once a line of pink shot up the handle toward Silas's thumb and Inez slapped salt onto it so fast he felt more startled than burned.

"Christ," he said.

"If you were going to pray," Junie said, "you had all yesterday."

The handle cooled under his hand. Inez had not removed her palm from the metal yet. He could feel the shape of her strength through the tray, steady as a brace under a shelf. She let go only when she was certain the heat had settled.

"What did you call it?" he asked.

She wiped the scraper on burlap. "Nothing out loud."

"You said something."

"I told it to hold."

"That worked."

"Three voices in a hot room with live metal under them can nudge things. It won't remake a district. It can keep a kitchen from biting you." She reached for more salt. "That's different from a crowd teaching a wall to blush for money."

Junie pointed her chin at Silas without looking up from the kettle skirt she was scouring. "Hear that? She's trying very hard not to say I told you so."

"I don't waste effort on the obvious," Inez said.

That should have annoyed him. It did, a little. Mostly it made him want to laugh, and because there was nowhere to hide in the room and no crowd to feed, the laugh came out small and true.

"Fine," he said. "What do I say, then?"

Inez looked at him as if assessing whether he was capable of carrying a bucket without naming it after a duchess. "Name the job and the limit. Tell it how it has to behave if supper's going out of this room."

"For example."

She pointed at the tray in his hands. "Low hold."

"That sounds like an order, not a name."

"In a kitchen those are often the same."

Junie said, "Low hold," in the tone she usually used for getting dockhands to sit before they fainted.

Inez repeated it once, quieter.

Silas, feeling foolish enough to be obedient, said it too. "Low hold."

The tray's rim went from uncertain blue to a steady workman's green no brighter than old bottle glass. It did not transform. It settled. The difference was almost insulting in its modesty. There was no flourish and no gasp from a crowd, just metal cooling to something a woman could use without losing skin.

He stared at it.

Inez had already moved on to the next tray. "Again."

"That was all?"

"No," Junie said. "There was also labor. Keep up."

The second tray proved meaner. Its seams had held more of the launch muck and some of the white had gone inside the lip where the tin folded. Inez had him hold the tray so close his forearms ached. Junie scraped at the opposite side with a reed brush, cursing under her breath in the same clipped rhythm she used for chopping onions. By the time they won it back to serviceable green, Silas's apron was smeared with slime, whey, dishwater, and his own sweat. A clot of something pale and stringy hit the floor near his shoe and made a tiny indecent wobble before Junie stamped it with the heel of her boot.

"There," she said. "Now it's as dead as your sense."

"You don't get to be pleased about this."

"Who's pleased? I'm still down one ladle and a morning."

Inez stood and flexed her right wrist. The scar there paled and darkened as the tendons moved under it. "You may lose the ladle entirely. Don't let anyone eat off the small spoons from that rack until they've had ash water and a second rinse. The big kettle can stay if you bank the heat low through service."

Junie said, "Can I get through supper?"

"Yes."

Junie's shoulders dropped a fraction. It was the closest she came to relief. "Then I can insult my brother in a less hurried spirit."

Silas had been looking at Inez's wrist too long. She caught him at it with the alertness of someone guarding her own tools, as if he had reached toward one that wasn't his. He looked away first and hated that she had seen him do it.

"I owe you," Junie said.

"You owe me two bowls and those three trays back tomorrow so I can reseat the rims."

"Done."

"I can bring them," Silas said, before he knew he meant to.

Inez turned the bone-handled scraper in her hand. "Can you arrive without carrying a parade on your coat?"

"If I strip first."

Junie made a low sound that might have been a laugh or a warning. "Behave, both of you. I still have pies to warm."

The third tray took longer because fatigue had set into all three of them and because the slime, now sensing defeat or maybe only the kitchen settling into one practical mood, got stubborn in a petty domestic way. It clung under Junie's nails. It webbed between the tines of a fork. It made a wet kissing sound when Silas pulled the burlap away too fast and Junie, without pausing, said, "If that thing starts flirting with you, charge it rent."

He said, "I would, but I hear it pays in adjectives."

That got the laugh from Inez at last. It came out short and unwilling, like a knot breaking loose in rope. He looked up before he could stop himself. Her head had tipped back a little. The scarf had slipped enough at the temple to show damp hair flattened there by work. The laugh sharpened her in his eyes, because it let him see what she looked like when control relaxed and returned.

Junie, hearing it too, said, "Good. Now that the room has witnessed a miracle, somebody hand me the onion pot."

By the time the first dinner bell from the ferry slip rang through the lane, they had three trays back on the range, green and low and ugly as they ought to be. Junie slid the soup kettle into place over them and watched the first slow bubbles gather at the edge. No one spoke until the boil held steady.

Then she shoved a clean towel into Silas's chest. "Front room. If anybody asks why the kitchen smells like a swamp saint's drawers, tell them it's fashion."

"You don't want the truth?"

"The truth is not paying tab two and wants more bread. Move."

He moved. Behind him, Inez was rinsing the last of the tools, Junie was already ladling broth, and the kitchen that had nearly gone strange under his coat answered them now with the plain useful noises of service: tin on iron, knives on board, bowls set hard on wood, the range breathing its banked green breath.

The rush ran long because Ferrymouth was full of men who had spent the day hearing about Patent Row and wanted supper with a side of scandal. Junie gave them stew, bread, and no gossip worth carrying. Silas worked the room in his shirtsleeves and towel, fetching bowls, slicing pie, taking coins greasy from rope hands and dock tar. Twice somebody tried to tease him about the launch. Twice Junie called for more broth before he had to answer. By the time the last regular limped out into the blue-green dusk, the kitchen lamps had burned low and steady, his feet felt hammered flat, and his shoulders had found muscles he usually let brushes do without.

Junie dropped the bar into place across the front door and leaned her forehead against it for one brief second before turning back toward the kitchen. That one second was all the self-pity she allowed herself, and even then it looked more like calculation than grief.

"Sit," she said.

Silas and Inez were both still standing with the foolish endurance of tired people when somebody finally grants them rest. Junie pointed two fingers at the table. "I wasn't speaking in metaphor."

The meal she put before them was what the day had left: eel ends pulled into bean mash with browned onions, hard bread fried in drippings till it almost pretended to be fresh, pickled green tomatoes chopped rough, and the heel of a custard pie that had collapsed on one side and therefore could not be sold to anyone proud. She set down three bowls and then her tally book, because Junie never ate without her numbers close enough to touch.

Silas looked at the book. "You aren't adding this to my debt."

"I add everything to your debt. The question is only which column."

Inez sat with a small sound at the back of her throat as her knees bent, a blunt report from the joints after the day's work. She had loosened the scarf and retied it lower, enough that a dark wave of hair had escaped at the nape of her neck and stuck there with sweat. The copper wire was gone from the knot now, laid with the tools to dry. Her forearms, scrubbed clean, still carried faint green shadows in the fine lines around the wrists.

Junie caught Silas looking and slid him his bowl hard enough to jar the spoon. "Eat before you invent another disaster."

He ate. The first mouthful hurt in the way good food hurts when a man has spent the day smelling it and not earning any of it. He tasted salt, eel fat, onions, and vinegar from the tomatoes. The recovered trays held their low green under the pot with no sign of the day's mischief. That almost undid him more than if one had failed.

Junie took up her spoon. "We lost the ladle. One pie tin's suspect. Two small spoons are in disgrace till morning. I'm charging Patent Row in spirit if not in coin."

Silas swallowed. "Charge me in coin. You've earned the pleasure."

"I already do."

Inez said, "The spoons will come back if you ash them twice."

"Good," Junie said. "I hate buying spoons. They vanish faster than husbands."

That got another short laugh out of Inez. Silas smiled into his bowl before he could help it.

Junie noticed and stabbed a piece of fried bread. "Don't look so blessed. One clean-up and I still remember you dragged the trouble in here."

"I know."

"Do you?" She was not sharp about it. She was tired past sharpness. "Because men like you often know a thing very loudly for an hour and then forget it by market dawn."

He could have defended himself. Once he would have. Instead he said, "I know enough to smell it in my own coat."

Junie nodded once, accepting that much and no more. "Good."

Silas ate another spoonful. Outside, the river knocked at hulls in the slip. Somewhere down the lane a child tried out one of yesterday's launch names in a sing-song dare and got shushed by an older voice before the second repetition. The city was full of mouths. That was the whole damned problem.

He looked at the trays under the stove. "When we said low hold. Why did that work when all the pretty names yesterday did nothing but turn the wall stupid?"

Junie said, "There he is. He's discovered questions."

Inez wiped her spoon on bread before answering. In the front room she had been all economy and correction. Fatigue altered her without softening her; her sentences lengthened just enough to make room for example. "Yesterday was a market crowd trying to shove six appetites through one word. Men wanted show. Buyers wanted certainty. Children wanted to heckle. The moss took the only thing all of them shared and showed off." She tipped her spoon toward the range. "This tray had one job: hold a slow pot through second ferry."

"So a kitchen beats a crowd."

Inez shook her head. "No. A kitchen just knows what it needs sooner."

Junie said, "Plainer."

Inez looked at the stove instead of at him. "A useful word gets repeated by burnt fingers and empty plates. A pretty one only needs mouths."

He could see the distinction plain as wet boards on Lantern Steps, somebody's grandmother choosing a safe path by habit older than posters.

"And I've been giving people words to admire," he said.

Junie snorted. "You've been giving rich men words to invoice."

He took that. She was right.

Inez did not rescue him. She never had. "And rooms remember the useful ones longer," she said.

He thought of Gideon's launch cards, thick paper, elegant letters, the crowd chanting Celadon Crown while the wall flashed funeral blue and bridal pink and one ugly diseased white that made the silk buyers recoil. He thought of the retainer folded in his room, the advance already warming one corner of his future, Havelock Pigments waiting on payment, Junie's fuel notes hanging over her like a wet ceiling. He did not say any of that. The omission sat between his ribs like swallowed tin.

Junie was watching him with the kitchen's low lamp turned in her eyes: enough light to work by, not enough to flatter. "Don't go noble on me now. It doesn't suit you."

"I wasn't trying for noble."

"Good. Because I need you housebroken, not transformed."

That startled a laugh out of him, and when he looked over Inez was smiling into her bread with the expression of a woman who would deny it in court.

"What do you call this stuff in Sump Ward," he asked her, nodding toward the bucket by the sink where the day's scrapings sat under salt, "when it gets in everything and won't die with dignity?"

"Depends where it starts."

"That's infuriating."

"It's accurate." She counted on damp fingertips. "If it climbs a lamp rack and leaves the tray too hot at the lip, one house calls it widow's bite. If it webs a wash line and starts dropping cold light on the sheets, another house calls it nun silk. In the propagation sheds, Aunt Vero calls the same thing bastard lace when it thinks it's being clever."

Junie nearly choked on a bean. "Bastard lace. That's better than mine."

"What's yours?" Silas asked.

"Dock scurf. Because I don't have time to poetry every nuisance."

"You have time to improve everybody else's."

"That's different." Junie scraped her bowl clean and set the spoon across it with decision. "And before you start collecting those, understand this: names like that belong to the rooms that earned them."

Silas looked from his sister to Inez. He could feel the correction in it before either woman sharpened it further. It was no speech, just a boundary laid on the table beside the bread.

"I heard you," he said.

Inez met his eyes. Hers were dark in the low light, steady enough to make lying feel clumsy. "Did you."

It was not a question he could answer honestly without dragging Gideon's contract into Junie's kitchen and letting it foul the room all over again. He looked down at his bowl. "I heard you."

Junie clicked her tongue, saw straight through him, and left him to stew in it. That was worse. She rose and took up the bowls. "Then hear this too. If either of you turns my supper into a tribunal, you'll wash every pan in the place before sunrise."

"There are not enough pans left for that threat," Silas said.

"Then I'll borrow some."

She carried the dishes to the sink and shoved her tally book aside with the heel of her hand. Silas half rose to help, but she cut him off. "Sit. If you stand up out of guilt one more time tonight, I'll nail you to the bench. Miss Cordero, you can help me in a minute. He still owes me the use of his ears."

Inez stayed seated. That in itself felt like a choice.

From the sink came water, crockery, Junie's muttered arithmetic. Silas and Inez had the table between them and very little else. Without Junie's voice filling the room, Ferrymouth came back in: rope bumping posts, a late shout from the slip, the soft hungry insect whine around the back door screen. The lamp over the table showed Inez's face differently than daylight had. Fatigue had taken the public edge off it. The caution remained; she had not misplaced that. But something had unclenched enough that he could imagine, for one dangerous instant, what warmth from her might feel like when it was not handed out in the form of instructions.

He said, because the honest thing was suddenly easier than the polished one, "You didn't have to come."

"Junie asked."

"Still."

She dragged a fingertip through a smear of bean left in her bowl, considering. "A kitchen losing light is everybody's business in weather like this."

"That's a very civic answer."

"Do you want the uncivic one?"

He did not trust his voice for a second, which annoyed him. "Yes."

She looked at him directly, and when she spoke her tone had the same precision it had used all day, only warmer under it, as if heat had moved closer to the surface of the metal. "I wanted to see whether you were all mouth when there wasn't a crowd to feed."

He sat back. The room felt smaller. "And?"

Inez broke off a piece of fried bread and held it between finger and thumb while she decided how much truth he could carry. "You're worse with a crowd."

He laughed once through his nose. "That bad."

"That bad."

"But not all mouth."

"No." She ate the bread. "Not today."

The compliment, because it came from her and because it had cost labor to earn, hit deeper than applause ever had. He wished, absurdly, that he were cleaner, that the day had not left salt under his nails and dishwater in his sleeves. Then he thought of her hands in the basin, the burn scar at her wrist, the damp hair at her neck, and understood cleanliness had nothing to do with it.

Junie came back to the table carrying a wrapped parcel of bread ends and pie scraps. "For the walk," she told Inez. "I know better than to send a woman home on principles alone."

"I brought salt, not holiness."

"Same difference to some men." Junie set the parcel down and pointed at Silas. "You. Carry the trays to the back landing in the morning. Not tonight. Tonight you're too tired and therefore liable to become poetic on the stairs."

"You make poetry sound catching."

"In your case it spreads."

Inez stood. When she reached for the parcel, her sleeve slipped back and the crescent scar on her right wrist showed again, pale against damp skin. He had noticed it before. Now he wanted to ask about it and knew that wanting was already too intimate for the day they had had. He stayed where he was.

Junie saw everything because Junie always did. "First bell," she said to Inez. "If he doesn't appear, I'll sell him for lamp mash."

Inez adjusted the parcel under her arm. "If he appears dressed for Patent Row, send him home."

"Cruel woman," Silas said.

"Practical woman," Junie corrected.

Inez looked at him over the table. "Come in clothes you can ruin. There are rims to reseat and two lamp lines in Sump Ward still acting foolish after yesterday. If you want to understand what you touched, use your hands."

Morning took shape at once: reseated rims, two foolish lamp lines, his hands finally wanted before his mouth. He had been asking her for that place since Lantern Steps, though he had not always known enough to ask it right.

"I'll come," he said.

Inez's gaze moved over him once, taking inventory the way she had taken inventory of the trays: what could be saved, what still wanted scraping, where the weak seam lay. "Good," she said. "Try not to arrive glowing."

Junie laughed outright then, tired and mean and fond. "Out with you before I start charging rent for courtship."

"This isn't courtship," Inez said, already at the door.

"No," Junie said. "That's why it has some chance."

Silas followed them to the back landing because the room had become too full to stay in. The night outside pressed warm and damp against his face. Ferrymouth lay under its ordinary working lights now, patched green along the slips, a safer blue on the farther stairs, no trace from here of the overbright public idiocy of Patent Row except what lived in his memory and on the coat Junie had hung as far from the stove as possible.

Inez paused on the top step and looked out over the lane before setting her foot down, steady on the wet wood the way only locals and fools ever were. The parcel was tucked against her ribs. The tool roll knocked lightly against her leg.

"Miss Cordero," he said.

She half turned.

The apology crowded up, wanting shape. So did the contract. So did desire, raw and badly timed. He chose the only part he could say without fouling the night. "Thank you."

Her face gave him the same dry composure it had worn at the door, but the lamp from Junie's kitchen touched one cheek and made the restraint legible rather than cold. "You worked," she said. "That helped."

The words gave him footing. Pity would have been easier to bear.

She went down the steps. At the second landing she glanced back once, a quick look over her shoulder, deliberate as a fingertip testing a seam. Then she kept going toward the lower streets where the practical lamps held and the names he had not earned yet lived in people's mouths without asking permission from men like him.

Behind him Junie said, from the doorway, "Shut your mouth, Sile. Mosquitoes will think you're courting them too."

He shut it. He stood one moment longer in the damp dark with his hands still smelling of sour whey and tin and the faint impossible trace of Inez's skin where practical work had put them briefly together. Then he turned back into the kitchen, where tomorrow had already begun in stacked bowls, drying burlap, three rescued trays cooling to a low unglamorous green, and the uphill share of the day he still owed Patent Row.
