# Chapter 10

By the time Silas reached the last plank over the drainage cut, the night had gone so hot the boards sweated under his boots. Sump Ward lay low and shining under a skin of damp, every catwalk plank dark with old overflow, every wash line hanging slack as dead eels. The grow houses gave off their own weather. Warm green light leaked through the slats. Ferment steam rolled out sweet and rotten, apples left too long in a barrel, yeast, mud, the faint metallic bite of lamp chimneys cooling. He had come without a speech. He had left his hat at home, his mouth shut, and his good hand empty.

Aunt Vero waited at the west gate with a paper twist of fennel seed in one broad brown hand. She chewed it on long shifts because the digester steam turned her stomach mean, and the sweet anise smell sat oddly against the ward stink. Her white braid was looped up and pinned with a bent brass spoon. One eye tracked him cleanly. The other wandered toward the cut as if it had a better opinion of the evening.

"If you came to kneel," she said, "kneel where somebody can mop around you. If you came to lift, the west house is going pretty, and nobody here needs that shit."

Silas nodded once. "I came to lift."

"We'll see."

She let him through.

Inside, the heat thickened at once. The west house had two rows of shelf beds, a central table slick with cuttings, and a long ferment wall breathing through iron coils. The practical trays along the outer shelves should have been a workman's green, sturdy and ugly, good for kitchen flame and stair landings. Half of them had thrown a pale frill over the surface instead, a delicate gold-white lace that looked expensive from a distance and useless up close. The stuff crisped at the edges when it dried and spat too hot when burned. Fashion had gotten into the beds again.

Inez stood on the far side of the table with her skirts pinned up to mid-calf and her sleeves rolled hard over the forearms. Her scarf had slipped back enough to show damp dark hair at the nape. Mud striped one shin. She had a hooked trimming blade in her right hand and a crate braced against one knee. She looked at Silas once, from face to boots and back, took in the lack of hat and excuses, and pointed with the blade.

"Take that stack to the drain bench," she said. "Not the pale top growth. The whole trays. Hold them level or you'll shake spores loose."

He moved before she finished talking. The trays were heavier than they looked. Wet root mats dragged against the wood, and his right wrist complained at the first lift, a hot twist under the old sprain that traveled halfway to the elbow. He shifted the weight into his left hand and his shoulder and kept walking. The drain bench stood under the outer shutters where the night air could get at the bad growth. He set the stack down, came back, lifted another.

Nobody thanked him. Good. Thanks would have made the whole thing theatrical.

Women he did not know worked the opposite shelves with their mouths set tight. One of them hummed under her breath, not a tune, just enough sound to keep time with her knife. Another had pinned her hem with fishbone clips and kept blowing curls out of her eyes with small irritated bursts. Silas had never seen either woman before. Tonight he noticed the fishbone clips because one was painted blue. He noticed the humming because it stopped whenever he passed too near. The ward had not forgiven him. It had merely run short on hands.

Aunt Vero shoved a pump handle down with both palms and spat husks neatly into the gutter. "Lantern Steps boys stood on the outer walk all afternoon saying Celadon this, Celadon that, like it was a prayer and their own mothers weren't cooking by these beds. Now the shelf thinks it's a chandelier. Cut under the lace. Save the root. Throw the vanity in the drain."

"Yes, ma'am," Silas said.

She snorted. "Don't sweet-mouth me, painter."

Inez dragged a tray toward herself and slit the pale crust away in long practiced strokes. Underneath, the real moss showed wet and dense, darker green, still alive. "If the root smells sweet, it's gone," she said without looking at him. "If it smells like wet iron, keep it. And don't mash the runners. We need them by morning."

He took the next tray to her table instead of the bench. "Show me the first one."

That brought her eyes up again. Suspicion first. Then calculation. She angled the tray so he could see the seam where useful growth ended and the fashionable lace began.

"Here," she said. "Not here. The white edge lies. It looks shallow. It isn't."

Her hand came over his for a moment to set the knife angle. The inside of her right wrist brushed his knuckles. He felt the ridge of the old burn scar, smooth and abrupt as fired clay. Then she stepped back and he cut where she'd shown him, slow because of the wrist, careful because if he ruined even one tray in front of her she would never let him touch another.

The work settled them into a rhythm that was not peace but could pass for it in low light. Lift, brace, cut, sort, carry. Mud made a sucking sound under every boot turn. The ferment wall knocked softly behind the iron coils, a deep wet glug as if the house had its own patient stomach. Now and then a tray flashed a sicker pale under the lantern and had to go whole into the drain cart. More often the ugly useful green held under the branded skin. Silas cut and saved what he could. Sweat ran down his ribs and soaked his waistband. His shirt stuck to the river scar under his left side. The room smelled of boiled pennies, old leaves, lamp oil, wet rope.

He lasted near twenty minutes before the words rose hard enough to need letting out.

"I signed Wren's paper before Lantern Steps," he said.

Inez did not stop trimming. "Lift that corner."

He did. Together they shifted a sagging tray onto the table.

"Before you first dragged me through a working house," he said. "Before Junie cleaned slime off her soup pots. Before I heard one name in here that meant more than copy to me."

"I heard you the first time."

"Then hear the rest."

Her blade kept moving. "Talk and work, then. If you slow down, I won't listen."

So he talked and worked. It was the only decent arrangement he could think of. He told her the plain frame first because ornament would have made him sick. Gideon had put a retainer in front of him. Exclusivity. Stable name rights, campaign rights, first use on any public surfaces Silas lettered for the strain. Enough money to clear Havelock Pigments and keep collectors off his door for a while. Enough to buy Junie a few quieter weeks if he stayed careful. Enough to make the paper feel like relief when it should have felt like a hand at his throat.

He expected her to cut him off. She did not. That was worse.

"How long did you mean to keep it from me?" she asked.

He shaved pale lace away from a runner root and dropped it into the refuse pail. "I told myself I'd say it when I had something useful to bring with it."

"That's not a time."

"No." He did not look up. "It isn't."

She waited.

He hauled a full refuse pail to the drain bench, dumped the glittering waste into the black water where it hissed and curled, came back empty. "I meant to keep it from you until the contract stopped shaming me," he said. "That's the ugly truth. I kept thinking if I solved the thing fast enough, the paper under it would matter less."

"To who?"

"Mostly me."

The answer sat between them a moment, ugly and unadorned. He had never heard a confession sound less noble.

Inez hooked two fingers under a sagging runner and held it up for him. "Cut there."

He cut.

"And after you knew what the names did here?" she asked.

The question hit deeper because it did not let him hide inside the first bad choice. It forced him to account for every choice after it.

"After that, I kept taking his money because I liked not being poor for five damned minutes," he said. "I liked walking into Havelock's and watching him go polite when I laid the crowns down. I liked not owing Junie every spoon of stew I swallowed. I liked being the man they paid first when a room needed a mouth."

The knife slipped a little and nicked his thumb, shallow but sharp enough to sting. He sucked the blood off without thinking and tasted salt, iron, fennel from the air.

"I wasn't starving," he said. "That's the part I don't get to dress up. I was vain and scared at the same time, and I let those two things climb into bed together. Then I stole from you because it was easier to call theft research than to say I had sold the listening part of myself."

At that, Inez finally stopped moving.

The lantern threw a low green shine under her cheekbones. Sweat had loosened a curl against her temple. He could not read forgiveness anywhere near her face. He had not expected to. What he saw instead was attention, sharp and cold and more dangerous because it was being spent on him rather than withheld.

"How much did he tell you?" she asked.

"About what he wanted?"

"About what he meant to do with the names once he had them."

He put the knife down before answering. "Enough. He never said it all in one sentence. Enough to know he wanted the city to forget where it learned anything useful. Enough to know pretty sold faster than safe."

"And you still helped."

"Yes."

She looked back to the tray, as if the tray at least had the decency to be specific. "Don't ask me to call that weakness."

"I wasn't going to."

"Good."

He lifted another tray. His wrist barked again. He made the sound one makes when a nail catches in rotten wood, brief and involuntary. Inez's eyes flicked to the wrapped joint.

"Give me your hand," she said.

He did. Her fingers were muddy and cool at the tips, hot in the palm. She untied the rag around his wrist, inspected the puffed bone with one quick frown, then retied it tighter and flatter, using the strip so it sat firm under the heel of his hand instead of cutting the swelling.

"You came at night because you thought darkness would help?" she asked while she worked.

"No. I came at night because Aunt Vero's boy said the west house was in trouble and I knew you wouldn't throw out a body while the trays were going."

"That's not the same as being wanted."

"I know."

She tied the knot with her teeth, spat the loose thread, and dropped his hand. "Hold the heavy end from now on. No fine cuts unless I hand them to you. If you go stupid and tear something living, I'll put you out myself."

It was not pardon. It was work. It felt, in that room, larger.

They kept going. Once the outer shelves were stripped, they carried the salvageable trays into a cooler aisle and re-racked them by use: low-heat kitchen stock, stair lamps, wash-yard light, nursery jars. Inez named each class without ornament. Pot-green. Step-cool. Soaplight. Silas repeated them only when she asked him to mark a crate. He heard how each phrase sat differently in the mouth, not built to charm a crowd but to keep someone's hand from blistering over a stove.

Aunt Vero came through twice more, once with a bucket of sand and once with two boiled eggs in her apron pocket that she ate standing up, tapping shell bits into her palm and muttering about men from the ridge who wanted their lamps to look delicate. "Delicate," she said, crushing the word as if it had bones in it. "You can't fry smelt on delicate." The second time she passed Silas a tin cup of ditch-cooled water without comment. He drank. The water tasted of zinc and algae. It was the best thing he had put in his mouth all day.

Near midnight the women from the far shelves began peeling away to other houses. Fishbone clips. Humming. One by one gone into the dark. The worst of the outer rot had been cut back. The drain cart stank with discarded lace. The saved trays glowed a steadier workman's green. Silas was carrying the last stack toward the propagation shed when Aunt Vero blocked the doorway with her broad shoulder.

"Inner bed next," she said to Inez. "The mother shelf threw a fever stripe by the flue."

Silas looked up.

Vero caught it and showed him her teeth, yellowed and wicked with fennel dust. "Don't flatter yourself. You're not touching the mother tray. You're lifting what she tells you and keeping your damned shadow off the roots."

"Fine by me," he said.

"No, it isn't. But it'll do."

She leaned close enough for him to smell the egg salt and anise on her breath. "If you sell one more home-name out of this ward, boy, I won't trouble the saints with the matter. I'll handle it myself."

Then, because she was Aunt Vero and dignity had never once slowed her down, she patted his cheek with the same hand she'd been using on the sand bucket and left a cool muddy print there before shoving past him toward the outer walk.

Silas wiped it off with the back of his sleeve and heard Inez make a sound that might have been the first nearly-laugh she had given him in weeks.

It did more to his balance than the shifting plank under his boots.

* * *

The propagation shed sat lower than the rest of the house, half sunk into packed river mud, its walls lined with warm brick channels that carried digester heat past the shelves. The room was narrow enough that when Silas turned with a tray he had to do it on the balls of his feet or crack the corners against the posts. Condensation beaded on the inner glass and ran down in crooked silver threads. A single hooded lamp burned over the bench, throwing light on rows of starter jars and clay labels stacked in chipped blue saucers. The heat in there was different from the workroom heat. Less air, more skin. It lay on him like a wet hand.

Inez slid the bar home behind them, not to keep him in, only to keep the outer draft from shocking the bed. Still, the sound of the wood settling into place changed the room.

"Set the salvage on the lower rack," she said. Her voice had gone quieter with the hour, but not softer. "These are the runners that matter. If the fever stripe gets into the root shelf, tomorrow turns ugly."

He did as she told him. The lower rack was crowded with shallow trays and one long narrow shelf under muslin, the cloth damp and breathing faint green through the weave. He kept his shadow off it as ordered. When he straightened, the top of his head nearly brushed a hanging line of drying tags.

For a while there was only the work again. Inez loosened the hot striped section with two fingers and a blade thin as a fillet knife. Silas steadied the tray, moved jars where she needed space, packed damp grit around healthy runners, carried bad growth to the cull bucket. The room forced them close. Her hip brushed his when he reached for the grit bowl. Once she said "Behind you" and laid both hands on his ribs to shift past him in the narrow aisle, and the press of her palms went through the wet shirt straight into his back like a brand.

He thought about nothing. That was the only way to keep his hands useful.

It almost worked.

"You should have told me before the feast," she said.

He kept his eyes on the tray. "Yes."

"You should have told me before I took you through the lower houses."

"Yes."

"You should have told me before you let me think the worst thing about you was vanity."

That one took him a second. He let the grit bowl down too hard. A little spray of wet sand hit the bench.

"That wasn't the worst thing?" he asked.

Inez's mouth moved, not quite a smile. "No. The worst thing was that you listened well enough to learn what hurt people and then used that gift to make money from the hurt."

He stood very still in the damp heat, one hand on the bowl. "That's fair."

"I know."

Silas nodded. The motion made sweat break free from his jaw and drop to the bench. There was nothing to defend. That, more than the accusation itself, left him strangely empty, all the old frantic words gone out of him. He had spent weeks inventing speeches for this room and could not remember one. Good.

Inez touched the cloth over the mother shelf with the backs of her fingers, checking warmth the way another woman might have checked a child's forehead. "Why did you come tonight, really?"

He could have lied prettier. The room was small enough that any lie would have fouled the air.

"Because I couldn't stand carrying it alone anymore," he said. "Because I kept hearing Otis say those polished bastard words from school like they belonged in his mouth. Because I knew you had every right to shut me out and I still came hoping work might get me through the door your pity shouldn't."

She considered that while she peeled a bad stripe from the tray and laid the living part on fresh grit. "You didn't come for absolution, then."

"No." He paused. "If I'd gotten that, I'd mistrust it."

She looked at him fully then, and the attention in her face changed, not gentled or made safe but deepened, the way river water darkened under a cloud and let a man guess how far down he'd go.

"Hand me the muslin," she said.

He did. She spread it over the shelf, tucked the damp edge under the tray lip, and flattened it with both palms. As she worked, the scarf at her neck finally came loose and slid down between her shoulder blades. She pushed it off impatiently and set it aside on a hook. Her hair, flattened and damp from hours under cloth, showed its own life at once, heavy and black and waved where the tie had held it. Silas watched one bead of sweat travel from behind her ear to the hollow above her collarbone. He looked away so fast he almost laughed at himself.

"Hold this," she said.

It took him an idiot second to realize she meant the lamp, not the room between them. He took the lamp and lifted it while she checked the underside of the shelf. The stripe she'd cut back had stopped at the clay chip she'd shoved under the tray edge, no longer climbing pale toward the root bed. She pressed the mat with her thumb, brought that thumb to her nose, and breathed in. Iron, mud, no sweet rot. "It's holding," she said, more to the tray than to him. "Till morning." The light caught the inner line of her right wrist again, that pale crescent against brown skin, and the curve of her thumb wet with grit. It made him think, unwillingly, of the white pull of his own rib scar under a wet shirt and the way old hurts announced themselves when heat got at them.

"Silas."

He had to drag his attention back to her face. It was the first time she had used his name that night. Maybe the first time in weeks. Maybe longer.

"What?"

"You're shaking."

He was. The room had cooked every clean reserve out of him, and his arm had begun to tremble from the weight of the lamp and the hour.

"Set it down before you drop it," she said, and something in the way she said it had more care in it than the words themselves admitted.

He set the lamp on the bench. When he straightened, they were close enough that if either of them breathed deep their shirts touched.

"I'm all right," he said.

"You're not all right," she said. "You're standing. That isn't the same thing."

Her hand rose, stopped once, then went on and touched the muddy print Aunt Vero had half left on his cheek. She rubbed the last dried edge of it away with her thumb. The gesture was so small it split him worse than the earlier accusations had. He looked down at her hand because it was safer than looking at her mouth. The burn scar on her wrist sat under his eye. Without thinking too hard, because thinking too hard had ruined enough already, he turned his head and pressed his mouth to that pale crescent.

She drew in breath.

She didn't flinch or recoil. She only drew breath.

"That scar came from a pipe burst," she said, her voice lower now. "I was seventeen. It peeled half the skin off. Vero said I had been trying to work faster than heat does."

Silas looked up. "What did you say?"

Her thumb moved once along his jaw, testing the rough there. "I said the pipe could go to hell."

He laughed, quiet and wrecked with tiredness. It came out more honest than anything else he had managed that night. She heard the break in it. Her eyes changed again.

"Don't you dare turn gentle on me because you've told the truth once," she said.

"I wasn't planning to."

"Good."

Then she kissed him.

There was nothing polished in it. No slow permission seeking, no pretty surprise. Her mouth landed hot and direct on his, tasting of ditch water, salt, fennel drifting in from the workroom, and the long shift they had both just done. He set one hand on the bench behind her because his wrist would not bear a grab, and she caught his shirt front in both fists and pulled him close enough to knock the clay labels together. They clicked in their saucer like little teeth.

He kissed her back hard enough to tell the truth with it. She opened for him at once, not soft but hungry, and the whole room seemed to narrow further around the sound of their mouths and the slow wet plunk of condensation from the glass. When she shoved his shirt up, her palm found the scar under his left ribs and paused there, mapping it. He made a rough sound into her mouth. She kissed the scar through the cloth first, then lifted the shirt higher and kissed bare skin. The tenderness of that nearly undid him. Then she bit just above it, sharp enough to make his knees loosen, and he understood what kind of mercy she meant to offer and what kind she did not.

He got her to the bench in two awkward steps because the floor was too slick and the room too narrow for grace. She sat back against the wood with jars rattling behind her, skirts bunched in both fists as she yanked them clear of her knees. "If your wrist gives, say so," she said, breathing hard. "Don't prove anything stupid on me."

He nodded. "Help me with the buttons."

She did, quick and practical, working open his damp trousers while he pushed fabric up her calves and found the warm inside of her thigh with his left hand. Mud had dried in a thin map over one knee. He kissed it because it was there. She made a sound low in her throat that might have been his name or might have been only relief that he had finally learned where to put his mouth and where not to put words. Her drawers were already wet where his fingers slid under, heat and slick and her body admitting what the rest of her still held at blade point. When he touched her properly she shut her eyes and leaned her forehead to his shoulder for one breath, just one, then caught his wrist and guided him harder.

"Like that," she said. "Don't make a ceremony of it."

He obeyed. Ceremony was for showrooms. This was work of another kind, no less exact for being desperate. He rubbed her until her breath went ragged and her heel thudded once against the bench leg. Then she dragged him between her knees, took him in hand, and the room tipped. The hooded lamp threw their shadows huge and warped across the sweating glass. Outside, somebody crossed the outer walk and the boards complained, a brief groan of wood over water. Neither of them stopped. If anything, the danger of being overheard stripped the last of the distance from the air.

She pulled him into her on the first try, muttering "There" like she was seating a stubborn tray. The bench edge bit the backs of his thighs. He braced more on his forearm than his bad hand and moved carefully once, then not carefully at all because she wrapped her legs around him and bit down on the meat of his shoulder through the shirt and said, against his neck, "Again." So he did. The propagation shed answered in little noises: glass ticking, bench scraping, the wet slap of his thigh against her skirt, the breath torn out of both of them in turns. Her hair had come wholly loose now and kept sticking to her mouth; he pushed it back with clumsy fingers and she laughed once, breathless and mean in the best way, before she grabbed his face and kissed him until he lost count of everything but the motion.

His wrist flared once when the bench shifted. He hissed. She felt it at once and turned them with more strength than looked possible, dragging him down onto the stack of empty jute sacks by the wall and coming over him without breaking the rhythm. The sacks smelled of damp grain and old moss dust. Brick heat pressed through them into his back. Her skirt spread over his hips. She rode him with one hand braced on his chest, the other clamped over her own mouth when a sound got too close to carrying. He reached up and caught that hand away, kissed the center of her palm, then held her by the waist while she moved, hard and exact, until the careful part of him went white and useless. When she came, it was with her forehead driven into his shoulder and the muscles of her stomach knocking against him in short fierce pulls. He followed a moment later, half choking on her name because the room had left no extra air for grace.

Afterward they stayed where they had landed, tangled on the hot sacks, the lamp still burning, both of them listening to see if the house had heard. Nothing came but the ferment wall's patient knock and the far water in the cut. Silas laughed once under his breath, not from comedy exactly, more from the fact that his whole body felt beaten and newly built at the same time.

Inez pushed herself up first. Her hair was in her face, her mouth red where he'd kissed it, and there was a smear of green grit along one cheek. She crossed the narrow room barefoot and laid the backs of two fingers on the muslin over the mother shelf. Then she peeled one corner high enough to look. Underneath, the root bed lay dark and even. The cut line by the flue had stayed where she'd left it, a dull yellow bruise no wider than a thumb. The clay chip marker was still dry. No sweet rot smell rose.

"Still holding," she said.

Only then did she look back at him. "Don't mistake this," she said.

He looked at her. "I won't."

"Good." She drew two fingers down his sternum, slow, almost absent, then pinched him hard enough to keep the point in it. "Because I wanted you. That's true. I also wanted the truth out of your mouth and your body tired enough not to dress itself in silk while it gave it."

"That worked," he said.

"It did."

She rolled off him and sat up, dragging her skirt down with brisk hands. For a moment he watched the line of her back as she reached for her scarf and then stopped, leaving it where it hung. The room smelled like sex now under the moss and heat, unmistakable and dangerous. He buttoned himself slowly one-handed. His skin felt flayed open to the air.

When she spoke again, her voice had changed once more. Warmer, yes. Also more exact, which he would not have thought possible.

"Wash your hands," she said. "Both of them. Then sit up."

He did not ask why. There was a basin by the inner wall with a trickle pipe above it. The water came down warm from the house cistern. He scrubbed grit and her slick from his fingers, watched muddy swirls gather and go, came back wiping his hands on a clean rag from the shelf. Inez had moved to the bench. She knelt, reached behind a stack of cracked lamp chimneys, and pulled out an oilskin packet tied with faded blue cord.

Even before she untied it, he knew it was not some casual workbook. The oilskin had the shine that came from years of palms, not months. One corner had been stitched by hand in two different threads, one old brown, one newer black. The whole thing smelled faintly of lamp oil and basil and the locked-warm scent papers took when they lived near ferment heat.

Silas stayed standing. He had just had his mouth on her throat. The packet still felt more intimate.

Inez glanced up at him. "Sit."

He sat on the bench opposite her.

She laid the packet between them and rested one hand over it for a second, not ceremonious, just steadying herself. "My mother started this," she said. "I kept it after she died. Vero added to it a little when my hand was still ugly. Most of the newer pages are mine. It's names, uses, warnings, where a strain was first taken, which households say it different, which names travel safe and which don't. It doesn't cover every house or every ward. It covers enough to keep people lit."

Silas looked at the blue cord. "Why are you showing me?"

Her mouth tightened, then eased. "Because if you're in this with me, I need you dangerous in the right direction. Because I'm not going to let you put your hands on me and then leave you ignorant of what you've been stealing from. Because next time you choose, I want you to know the full weight of what you are choosing against."

She untied the cord and opened the ledger.

The pages were thick and gone soft at the edges from years of damp fingers. Some entries ran in a compact older hand, straighter and more school-taught than Inez's. Others broke into her own practical slant, English braided with Spanish abbreviations, small sketches of leaf edges and flame heights in the margins, grease fingerprints, dates keyed to flood years instead of calendars. Names filled the pages, but never alone. Every one had company. Pot-green: low blue edge, safe under iron, do not use in sleeping rooms. Step-cool: holds under fog, weak for boiling, good near children. Soaplight: bright, bitter smoke if overfed. A page farther in carried crossed arrows between three names for the same strain, each tied to district, weather, and burner type. Another had a note in the older hand: never sell cuttings on a dry week, they lie about heat.

He let out a breath he had not known he'd been holding.

"This isn't flavor," he said.

"No," Inez said. "It never was."

He touched the page edge with one clean finger, careful as if the paper had a pulse. "And I thought I understood names."

"You understood how to make strangers repeat them." Her tone was not cruel now. That made it cut cleaner. "That's different."

He turned another page. More lineages. More notes. A page marked with two hands, one older, one younger, arguing in the margin over whether a certain riverbank cutting should be called shade-milk or ferrygreen after the flood. A later correction, practical and exasperated: both, depending on the burner. Then, near the back of that section, a phrase in the older hand circled twice, underlined once in Inez's darker ink years later: mothers in drain, do not carry to market. His eyes stopped there.

Inez watched him notice it. "Leave that line alone tonight," she said.

He nodded. He understood enough to obey the edge of that no.

She folded her hands on the table. The burn scar showed pale in the lamplight. "You read here. You don't take it out of this ward. You don't copy a line. You don't give Gideon one word from these pages, not directly, not dressed up, not by accident because your vanity gets hungry again. If one line from my mother's hand reaches him through you, I'll break the left hand too. I need you to hear that plain."

Silas looked from the page to her face. There was no heat left in the threat, which was how he knew it was real.

"I hear it," he said.

"Say it back."

"I don't take it out. I don't copy it. I don't repeat from it without you. Nothing from here goes to him, not even dressed up."

She held his eyes another moment, checking not romance but comprehension. Then she pushed the ledger a little farther toward him.

"Good," she said.

He read until the words blurred from tiredness. He made it through three pages of crossed arrows and burner notes, past a warning doubled in two hands about sleeping rooms, to the circled line that had caught him before: mothers in drain, do not carry to market. By the time he lifted his head, he had a faint smear of old ink on his thumb where he'd pressed the page edge too hard.

When he finally looked up, Inez had not moved away. She sat with one bare foot tucked under her skirt, watching him with the same wary steadiness she had watched him lift trays, fuck her, confess. There was no soft afterglow to hide in. The room still smelled of labor. Dawn had not yet broken. The city outside still wanted one name clean enough to own.

"Why me?" he asked quietly. "For this, I mean. Not the other part."

Inez glanced at the ledger, then back at him. "The other part is easier."

The page edge bit a white line into his thumb. He eased his grip and dragged in a slower breath.

She went on. "You know how words travel. Better than anybody I trust less. You also know now what they cost when they travel wrong. That may make you useful. Or it may make you a worse disaster than before. I haven't decided which. I decided only that I would rather choose my risk awake."

Silas closed the ledger with both hands and immediately gave it back.

"That's fair," he said.

"Yes." She took it, retied the blue cord, and slid the packet back behind the chimney stack. "Don't make me stupid for having wanted you."

He stood with his thighs aching and her bite still damp through his shirt at the shoulder. "I won't."

Inez gave him a look that said promises were cheap even now, maybe especially now. Then she rose, gathered her fallen scarf, and moved to unbar the door.

When she lifted the wood free, the first thin gray of morning was leaking into the outer walk. Somewhere beyond the grow houses a ferry horn sounded over the cut, tired and long. Silas followed her out carrying nothing in his hands. Moss and lamp oil stayed on his fingers, and the line from the ledger would not let go of him: mothers in drain, do not carry to market. At the bend in the walk he looked back. Inez was already half inside the propagation shed again, one hand on the bar, the warm dark and the hidden oilskin behind her. Whether he deserved it or not, noon had already fixed him back under that lamp with the book open.
