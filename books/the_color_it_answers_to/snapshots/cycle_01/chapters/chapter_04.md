# Chapter 4

By the time Silas reached the first plank walk above Sump Ward, his trouser cuffs had taken on the ward's smell: warm mud, lamp slime, old soap, and the sweet-sour breath of digesters working too hard in weather that had not yet learned mercy. The boards under him sweated through their grain. Below, the drainage cut moved like thick tea, carrying peelings, reed scraps, and a pale skim of algae that caught every color the ward gave it and made none of them cleaner.

He had come looking for a useful theft. He would not have called it theft, even to himself. He would have called it a principle, a pattern, one true hinge hidden under all the neighborhood babble, something he could carry back uphill and sell to men who liked their mysteries trimmed flat and lettered in gilt. He wanted one good phrase, perhaps two. He wanted enough to make the runaway moss stop embarrassing everybody by acting like a crowd.

Aunt Vero waited at the landing with a tobacco tin open in one hand, fennel seeds shining against the metal. She crunched one between her back teeth, eyed him with the mild contempt she seemed to reserve for men who still believed dry shoes were a form of virtue, and tipped the tin toward him without generosity. Her thick white braid was looped over one shoulder. One eye drifted a little off him and back again, as if even her face was not prepared to spend both its sights on Silas Vale.

"He'll slip in the first ten yards," she said to Inez. "Let him go under once. The ward might improve him."

Inez had her dark hair wrapped in a work scarf the color of old olives, though the damp had already darkened it nearer black. She stood easy on the planks, weight where the nails still held, one hand on a tray strap cutting across her shoulder. There was nothing theatrical in the way she waited. He had seen women in Patent Row shops perform indifference like it was lace. Hers was laboring indifference. It had things to do after he left.

"If he falls," she said, "you can fish out his boots. That's the expensive part."

Silas put two fingers to the brim of an absent hat. "Nice to see my education has become a public project."

Vero snapped the tin shut. "Only because funerals cost."

He might have answered that with something better if the air had not been so wet it felt poured. Instead he smiled the smile that had sold soap powders, eel bait, and one regrettable line of patent corset hooks, and watched it die against Inez's face without leaving a mark.

"You wanted me to look," he said to her. "I'm looking."

"No," she said. "You wanted a clean answer. Looking is the part before that."

Then she turned and started along the catwalk without checking whether he followed. Of course he followed. Vero gave him a small shove between the shoulder blades for luck or insult. In Sump Ward the two seemed to travel together.

The first glow house sat half below grade, built against brick that stayed damp even in dry weeks. Inside, the heat struck like a body. Fermentation pipes ticked along the walls. Wet burlap hung over racks. Rows of shallow trays gave off different colors the way tired people gave off moods, not one light but several, all related and none obedient. A low green lived close to the floor where the cook pots sat. On the shelves above it ran a colder wash of blue-white, bright enough to find a dropped needle. In the far corner, a patch the size of a dinner plate pulsed whitish gold with an ugly little hunger that made Silas think of irons left too long on cloth.

Three women worked the room without wasting a glance on him until they had decided he was worth it. The first, a heavy-hipped woman with one shoe unlaced because her left ankle had swollen against the damp again, lifted a pot lid with a rag and leaned her face into the steam as if it could tell her whether supper would stretch. Her wedding ring hung on a black cord over her apron instead of on her hand. "That's him?" she asked Inez, not lowering the lid yet. "Patent Row mouth."

"That's him," Inez said.

The woman looked Silas up and down, taking especial note of the yellowed paint caught in the moons of his nails. "Marta Bell," she said. "If you learn something useful, it'll be the first clean miracle I've seen come down from that hill."

Silas spread a hand. "I try not to promise miracles before noon."

Marta snorted and pointed with her chin to the green bed crouched beneath her stove ledge. "Potback," she said. "Here, anyway. We call it that because it sits low and holds. Doesn't spit. Doesn't race. You can leave beans on it and go slap a child or fetch wash and come back to food instead of cinders."

She said the name toward the bed the way some people spoke to a mule: not tenderly, but with a settled agreement. The green answered by evening out along the tray, a dull rich color under the iron, heat more felt in the shins than seen.

Silas crouched. The growth smelled faintly of pond skin and hot copper. "And if somebody called it something else?"

"Then I'd have to cook by whatever foolishness they chose." Marta set the lid down harder than needed. "Last week boys on the upper stairs kept shouting that pink nonsense of yours. By dusk the edge of this tray had gone rosy and skittish. Took twice the mash to keep it under the pot, and the stew still sulked."

Inez crossed her arms. "Patent Row heard color and thought decoration. Here color means your supper keeps or doesn't."

Silas glanced toward the blue-white shelf. "So what do you call that one?"

"Threadshow," said the second woman before Inez could answer. She sat on an upturned crate with a baby pinned to one hip and a needle flashing in her right hand. Her spectacles had lost one arm and were tied in place behind her ear with black thread to match the mourning dress she had cut down twice already. She had a habit of wetting the thread between her lips before every stitch, quick and absent as prayer. "It shows black on black. Good for mending cuffs and finding lice eggs if the school sends them home again. Cool enough that the baby can stare at it without blistering himself stupid."

The baby, offended by being included in the sentence, kicked once and got his foot tangled in her apron tape. She untwisted him without looking down.

"Nessa Quill," she said. "Don't write my name anywhere."

"I wasn't writing," Silas said.

"Yet."

He looked to Inez for rescue and got none. She went to the blue shelf, lifted a tray, checked its underside, and set it back with a touch so practiced he could see the whole weight of the motion before it happened. "Threadshow is bright, not warm," she said. "On Lantern Steps they use a cousin bed for reading slates. Here it stays in kitchens and sewing corners. Different feed. Different trim. Different name because different use."

"But still the same family," Silas said. "Near enough."

Inez's mouth flattened the way other people's might have widened. "Near enough is what men say before they break something that wasn't theirs."

Marta laughed without kindness. Nessa bit the thread off with her teeth.

Silas stood. The room's heat had taken hold under his shirt. "All right," he said. "Say I've been a fool. Not hard to say. But if you're trying to keep the whole city from pulling these beds every which way, doesn't that argue for fewer names, not more? One for heat. One for bright. One for the nasty hot bastard in the corner."

That got him his first full look from Inez since the landing. It was not affection, and it was not even respect. But it was a cleaner attention than contempt, which at that moment felt almost luxurious.

"Come on," she said.

He hated being hauled like a schoolboy and followed anyway.

They went back into the open ward where the afternoon had thickened toward evening without cooling. Wash lines crossed the narrow spaces between houses. Somebody above beat a rug against a railing with the grim concentration of a woman imagining a face in it. Children moved on the planks in bare or near-bare feet, careless only in the way the practiced are careless. Silas had to watch where he set his boots. The boards had whole dialects in them: slick, split, sound, soft, rotten at the edge, safe if you were born to it and a lie if you were not.

Inez took him over a narrow run above the drainage cut. The planks bowed under his height. She did not look back until his sole skidded on a patch of green-black sheen and his shoulder clipped the rail.

"Center nails," she said.

"You might've mentioned that before I started negotiating with the river."

"You knew enough to bring boots. I thought you might know enough to see the nails."

She said it without triumph. That was the part that bit. Mockery was easy company. Her accuracy made him feel large and expensive in all the wrong ways.

At the bend of the walk a warning board had blistered up from the wet. The old paint had bubbled around the words until the letters looked diseased. Below the sign, a ribbon of pale blue moss ran the length of the catwalk edge, thin as knifing and bright where the wood went treacherous. A boy of maybe ten balanced on the far end, fishing bent nails out of a tin with the solemn greed of a magpie. He had one ear patched with a scrap of red flannel and the patient expression of a child used to adults talking over him while he made himself useful.

"Timo," Inez said, "what's that edge called here?"

Without looking up the boy said, "Stepblue. Near the Quinter walk it's shinshow because old Mrs. Quinter kicks every dark thing she sees."

"And why not potback?" Inez asked.

Timo looked at Silas then, pitying him the way only children could. "Because you don't cook on a walkway."

Silas barked a laugh before he could stop it. The boy returned to his nail tin, satisfied that instruction had been accomplished.

Inez touched the warning sign. The board sagged under her fingers. "This one should've been redone three rains ago."

"You've got the paint?"

"No."

"Brush?"

"No."

He gave her a look and got the smallest ghost of weariness in return. "So you've got a rotten sign and no paint," he said. "Very efficient."

"We've got me," she said, and pulled a clasp knife from the pocket of her skirt. "And apparently we've got you."

She handed him the knife handle first. It was not trust. It was work.

There were old skills in his hands that even shame could not chase out. He scraped the blistered paint back to wood while she knelt along the edge and trimmed the overreaching stepblue with short hard cuts, keeping the living strip from swallowing the board. The knife rasped. Her little pruning shears clicked. Below them the cut water knocked bottle glass against stone. It was the kind of labor that made speech optional without making company false.

He found a stub of blue chalk tucked behind the sign post and sketched the letters in before he cut deeper with the blade: KEEP TO CENTER. LOW EDGE ROTS. The wording was not pretty or clever. It was meant to be read by somebody carrying wash with one hand and a sleeping child with the other.

When he straightened, Inez was looking at the board instead of at him. The damp had darkened the scarf at the nape of her neck. He could see the crescent burn scar inside her right wrist when she lifted the shears, a pale curve against brown skin, old hurt gone practical.

"You left room," she said.

"For what?"

"For the blue. Most sign men cram the whole board because empty space offends them."

"Empty space offends landlords. I'm less principled."

Her mouth shifted, not quite a smile. "That might be the first sensible thing you've said."

He should have answered with something easy. Instead he heard himself say, "I know how water eats paint."

"You do?"

"I make my living on walls that sweat."

That made her look at him directly again. There was no flirtation in it. It was something slower, a measuring. She stepped close enough to brace the board with her shoulder while he cut the last groove of the T. Her arm brushed his side once, by necessity. Even through wet cloth he felt the steadiness of her, the way she held pressure without advertising it. Patent Row liked women arranged for looking. This one had been arranged by use, which was not the same thing and did worse things to his concentration.

When the letters were done she ran a thumb through one groove, testing depth. "It'll hold a few weeks."

"If your nail thief doesn't steal the whole post."

Timo looked up, scandalized. "I only steal bent ones."

"A moralist," Silas said.

"He sells them by weight," Inez said. "To buy sugar twists."

The boy shrugged. "My sister likes the red kind."

There it was, the human reason tucked under petty crime. Silas liked him at once and distrusted the liking, because his trade had taught him how quickly affection could turn into harvest.

They moved on.

The next kitchen stood open to the walk, hotter than the first and more crowded. A laundry frame leaned over one wall. Flatirons sat on a brick shelf. The light in here was the whitish gold he had noticed before, but held in smaller dishes, trimmed close and mean. It gave off a dry heat that bit the backs of his fingers when he passed too near.

A young woman was slicing stale bread into thin soldiers for a child at the table, making each piece the same length with a precision that suggested the child would reject any variation as personal insult. Her sleeves were rolled unevenly, and on the crook of one elbow she had drawn a row of tiny blue stars in ink to keep the older boy from using the skin as a slate. The older boy sat with his tongue pressed out the gap in his teeth, pushing the bread soldiers into rank instead of eating them.

"Ruthie Cale," the woman said, glancing once at Silas and then back at the bread. "If you've come to name anything, name my husband home before midnight."

"Can't manage husbands," Silas said. "Too many existing claims."

"Shame. That's the only miracle I'd pay cash for."

Inez nodded toward the dishes of gold-white growth. "Tell him."

Ruthie slapped the older boy's hand away from the nearest tray without turning. She did it by sound. "Fingerbiter," she said. "Because if you hear the word, you don't pet the pretty lamp. It runs hot enough for irons and kettle plates. Good when the baby cough keeps us up and I want hot water fast. Bad for every fool child and every fool man who thinks white means gentle."

The older boy scowled at Silas as if the category might be shared.

Silas held his hand over the tray and felt the bite of it immediately. "And if somebody called it threadshow?"

Ruthie gave him a flat look. "Then I'd scorch the shirts and leave the nits alive."

"And if they called it bridelight?" Inez asked.

Ruthie made a face like she'd bitten soap. "Then it goes vain. Lifts color toward the edges. Wants looking at. Doesn't stay where it's fed."

Silas watched the tray. The growth did have that restless gleam at the rim, a dangerous prettiness like polished metal on a crowded dock. He thought of Patent Row shopgirls leaning in to admire a lamp by color alone and hated them a little for being convenient, which was unfair and therefore familiar.

The boy at the table finally shoved one bread soldier into his mouth and said around it, "Ma says pearlglass is for people who don't do their own shirts."

Ruthie did not hush him. "Ma says lots of true things."

Inez looked at Silas. "Do you want one word now?"

He looked from the irons to the child to the tray and imagined some high-window showroom trying to sell all three rooms at once with one syllable and a ribbon. He could do it. That was the filthy part. He could hear the sentence forming already, smooth as oil: a household radiance, bright for every need. Men had paid him to tell worse lies.

"No," he said, and because the answer surprised him it came out plainer than most things he said. "Not one."

Inez's face did not soften. But the pressure in the room changed, slightly, the way a pipe changed pitch when some obstruction had been cleared.

She took him from there into the back propagation run, where the house walls sweated green and the trays sat in tiers over warm water channels. Here she stopped talking to the residents and began talking to him as if he might, in limited quantities, be able to follow.

"The hill men hear names as labels," she said. "Something to print on a box, something to shout once and own. Here a name tells you how to touch it, where to set it, what not to trust it near, whether it'll cost too much mash for what it gives back. Sometimes it tells you whose hand got burned learning that. Sometimes it tells you what hour to feed it if you don't want it waking the house."

She reached past him to pinch away a pearled clump trying to climb the lip of a tray. Her knuckles brushed his shirt. The motion was unadorned and made his skin pay ridiculous attention.

"So the names are instructions."

"And memory."

"Same thing, half the time."

"Not if you're the one who gets to forget."

That landed. It landed because she said it without heat, which left him no cheap shape to kick against. The people up on Patent Row did get to forget. They forgot what kept them lit. They forgot what happened below the ridge unless the below produced a spectacle worth climbing down for. He made part of his living feeding that forgetfulness better words.

At the last house they climbed to a lofted walk over a line of wash tubs and open barrels where spent mash soured for reuse. The evening was coming on. Across the ward the lights were waking in their various tempers: low cooking greens under stove ledges, sewing blues in windows, the dangerous little whites banked close where irons waited, and stranger mixed colors where somebody's household had argued with itself or the neighborhood had cross-bred too much talk into one poor tray. From up here the place did not look chaotic. It looked annotated.

Silas leaned on the rail and then straightened at once when it gave a wet complaint under his weight.

"You'll go through one of these days," he said.

"Not today."

"That's not a system."

"It is if you know the boards."

The answer irritated him because it was true and because it refused the kind of truth he could bottle. He looked out over the lights again. On the ridge, even from here, Patent Row shone in flatter colors, a few rich signs forcing themselves above the rest. Down here the ward glimmered by need. No one had bothered to make it graceful for distance. It only became beautiful once you understood what each ugly little patch was doing for the hands around it.

"Suppose," he said slowly, "some bastard with wall space decides to press one name hard enough to drag the whole city after it. Signs, handbills, newsboys, drunks, the lot."

"That bastard wouldn't be the first."

"Suppose he does it well."

She turned to him. The river air had found a little cool at last, enough to lift the damp hair loose against her temple. "Then people get hurt before they get obedient. Which is why I asked you to come look instead of standing uphill and talking about elegance."

"Asked," he said.

"All right. Allowed."

"That's kinder."

"No, it isn't."

He laughed once under his breath. It felt rusty. "You really don't miss much."

"I miss plenty," she said. "I just don't enjoy it."

For a moment neither of them spoke. Below, Ruthie Cale's older boy began reciting some schoolroom numbers in a sing-song moan until somebody told him to either mean them or shut his mouth. Farther off, a ferry bell carried over the cut. The ward smelled of soap, hot metal, cabbage, and the thick vegetal sweetness of the grow houses. It should have offended him. Instead it made Patent Row seem oddly bloodless, all those clean gloves and dry ledgers pretending they were not built on wet work.

He put his fingers on the repaired sign groove still ghosted in his skin. "If I said I understood now," he said, "you'd know I was lying."

"Yes."

"If I said I understood more than I did this morning?"

She considered him long enough to make flattery impossible. "That might be true."

He looked at her wrist where the old burn scar curved pale as moonlit bone. He looked away before the look turned stupid. "Then tell me what the useful next step is."

"For whom?"

There it was again. The clean knife. He had half a dozen answers prepared by habit and none he wanted to hear himself say. He could have said that the next step was for him, for the contract in his pocket notebook, for the advance already walking around inside his debts wearing respectable shoes, for the part of him that still believed there had to be a single key because single keys got you paid.

He let most of that rot where it stood.

"For the moss," he said at last. "For the people cooking by it. For not making things worse than I've already made them."

Something in her expression changed then, though not into trust. Trust would have been childish and unearned. What came instead was narrower and more useful.

"You come back tomorrow without trying to coin anything," she said. "You listen when people answer instead of shopping their mouths for what will look good on a wall. You carry trays if I tell you to carry trays. You mend boards if they're failing. You don't call any bed by a name you haven't heard used in the room where it's living. And if you get a clever sentence in your head, you let it die there."

"That's a punishing apprenticeship."

"Then don't take it."

He watched the ward lights settle deeper into themselves as dusk took the sky apart above them. Potback sat under one stove, threadshow glowed in a window, stepblue found the wet edge of a walk, and fingerbiter banked mean under an iron plate. None of them sounded elegant. That was not their failure. That was their virtue. They did not ask to be admired first.

Up on Patent Row, a man could rename a thing until it forgot its mother. Down here the words had mud on them and instructions in their teeth.

"I'll come back," he said.

Inez nodded once, as if accepting delivery of a bundle she had not ordered and might still return. "Early. Before the heat turns everything stupid."

"That includes me, I assume."

"Especially you."

He should have reached for swagger then, some filthy little bow to leave the exchange on familiar ground. Instead he only stood there with her a moment longer, shoulder to shoulder above the ward, looking at the different lights doing their different work. The attraction he felt was not clean and not especially comfortable. It had very little to do with her mouth and a great deal to do with the fact that she could walk him through a whole city of living argument and never once confuse knowledge with performance.

When she went back along the planks, he followed her center-nails careful this time, watching where she set her feet and hating how much he liked being taught. Behind them the repaired warning board caught the first full wash of evening and held its letters plain against the damp.
