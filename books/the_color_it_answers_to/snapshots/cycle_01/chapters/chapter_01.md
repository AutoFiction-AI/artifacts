# Chapter 1

Patent Row smelled the way a liar's mouth smelled after a hot day: sweet at first, then yeasty, then rotten once you got close enough to know better. The digesters had been running hard since noon. By late afternoon every wall on the avenue sweated through its posters. New handbills curled over old ones. Blue paint bled into red. Paste shone in the heat. The whole street looked as if it had been licked and left out.

Silas Vale liked it best when it looked that way.

He stood on a tea crate outside a rented patent stall, a mahlstick under one arm and a lettering brush between nicotine-yellowed fingers, while the crowd considered disappointing him on purpose. They had that look. Patent Row wore it most days now. Too many inventors. Too many miracle glazes, steam salves, eel tonics, glow powders, rat jars, widow's corset stays, and bridge bolts said to laugh at rust. Every week wanted a sensation. Every week half the street pretended it had not been fooled the week before.

The woman paying him for today's miracle held a squat tin in both hands as if it might leak through the seams by shame alone. Mrs. Clary Dobb had glue blisters along the heels of her thumbs from pasting her own notices and a clove stem tucked in one corner of her mouth because the digester smell made her stomach pitch. Her best hat had a wilted jet bead fringe that clicked when she nodded.

"Mr. Vale," she said for the third time, "I paid for memorable. I did not pay for vulgar."

Silas did not look down at her. Looking down made clients think they had him. He looked past her into the crowd instead, where two ferry clerks in rolled sleeves had stopped to watch, and a pair of boardinghouse girls were already grinning behind their hands, and a newsboy had tucked his remaining sheets under one arm so he would not miss the fall if there was one.

"Those are neighboring districts, Mrs. Dobb," he said. "There's no clean road from one to the other on this street."

The newsboy barked a laugh. One of the ferry clerks called, "He's right there."

Clary Dobb's mouth flattened around the clove. "It's for brass polish."

"Everything's for brass polish until it isn't." He crouched, held out his free hand, and she surrendered the tin. The paste inside was green-brown and glossy as river scum under moonlight. It smelled of oil, salt, and something sharp enough to strip skin if a fool kept his hand in it. Useful. Maybe. Not his problem. His work was not truth. His work was the half second before a stranger reached for a purse because a word had made the object in front of them feel inevitable.

He dipped two fingers in the polish and turned toward the crowd with the smear glistening on his skin.

"Look at this," he said. "Look honest."

"Honest?" said one of the boardinghouse girls. She was carrying a bundle of cuffs and had a damp ring at the throat of her cheap blue dress. "On Patent Row?"

"You can go to church for the other thing." He pointed with the glistening fingers at a tarnished handbell hanging beside the stall. "What did she call it before she paid me?"

Clary Dobb answered with the pained obedience of a woman saying her own weak child's name in public. "Dobb's Algal Brass Restorative."

The crowd groaned on schedule.

Silas let them. He could feel the moment ripening. It came up through a crowd the way heat came up off brick: first a shimmer, then a pressure. He knew the exact instant their meanness became hunger. That was his actual talent. The brush mattered. The ear mattered more.

He stepped off the crate, took the handbell off its hook, and rubbed the paste across the dull metal with the heel of his palm. The green-brown sludge turned black for a breath, then thinned under his pressure and woke a bright gold underneath. The ferry clerks leaned in. Even Clary Dobb forgot the clove for a second and let it rest in the wet corner of her lip.

"There," Silas said. "That is not restorative. That is not algal. That is not anything any living mouth wants to ask for twice."

He handed the bell to the boardinghouse girl. "Hold that up."

She did, laughing already.

"What she's selling," Silas said, climbing back onto the crate, "is not polish. If it were only polish, every shipwright's boy from here to Ferrymouth would make it in a bucket and she'd be ruined by Thursday. What she's selling is the exact shine a front latch gets when mourning has lasted long enough and company may call again."

The crowd made the small scandalized noise he wanted.

Silas looked at Clary Dobb then, finally, and gave her the sort of smile that made nervous people think he was on their side and sensible people check their pockets.

"She's selling Widow's Rub."

There was a beat. A real one. Patent Row took good names the way a boxer took a clean punch: with surprise first, pain or pleasure a moment later.

Then the newsboy yelped, "Widow's Rub!" and one of the ferry clerks slapped his friend's shoulder, and the boardinghouse girl nearly dropped the bell laughing. A driver sitting high on a dray farther down the avenue shouted, "I'll take a tin for my landlady, see if she shines herself into mercy." Somebody whistled. Somebody booed. The booing was not hostile. It was appetite in work clothes.

Clary Dobb turned the phrase in her mouth once. Silas watched the horror in her face give way by increments to arithmetic.

"Widow's Rub," she said again, with less pain and more ownership.

"There you are." Silas set a fresh board against the front of the stall and loaded his brush. "Now keep up."

He did not usually enjoy the painting more than the naming. Painting was slower, and slower work meant the part of him that kept accounts woke up and started clawing at the door. Havelock Pigments had become too patient lately. Patience from a creditor was never mercy. It was measurement. But a live crowd changed the whole business. Then the brush was not a tool of trade but proof. If he only shouted, they could call him a drunk with luck. If he lettered the thing in front of them, in one unbroken series of strokes while the word was still hot in their mouths, that was craft. That was a man earning the superstition around him.

He laid down the W with a fat arrogant downstroke and a lean uplift that made the ferry clerk breathe out through his teeth. The I and D snapped together. By the time he got to the apostrophe-less S he could hear the rhythm of the word settling into the crowd. Wid-ow's-rub. Three beats, one slide. Dirty enough to travel. Simple enough for a child. Strong enough for a shop window.

He painted the second word larger.

When he stepped back, the board read:

WIDOW'S RUB

in cream letters on an eel-black field, with a thin green line under the whole thing like river light under a closed door.

The boardinghouse girl clapped. The newsboy started chanting it toward the people still walking past as if the product had existed for years and he had merely been patient enough to find the proper corner from which to sell it.

"Widow's Rub! Widow's Rub! Makes your brass behave!"

"That last part is yours," Silas told him.

"You'll thank me when it catches."

"If it catches, I'll deny knowing you."

But it had already caught. A dockman bought a tin for the joke and then bought a second because the bell truly had shone. One of the ferry clerks asked whether it worked on buckles. Clary Dobb, who five minutes earlier had looked near to apologizing for existing, began answering with an authority she plainly had not possessed until the name arrived to dress her in it. She tucked the clove behind one ear, squared her shoulders, and lied with improving skill.

"Works on buckles, hinges, lamp collars, and any brass a decent house keeps above the touch of damp."

"You've never had a decent house in your life," said the boardinghouse girl.

"Then I'm overdue one."

More laughter. More hands going into pockets.

Silas felt the little inner lift he always felt at such moments, a rising at the back of the neck that had almost nothing to do with pride and everything to do with power. Not moral power. Not respectable power. Nothing any judge would write down. Just the indecent fact that a thing could be worthless at four o'clock and saleable at four ten because he had put the right dirt on it.

That was what Patent Row paid for. Not genius, exactly. Nerve, timing, and a willingness to put his mouth where other people's shame lived.

Clary Dobb pressed coins into his palm before she forgot herself and became cautious again. Her hand was damp and trembling. "If the matrons of Gilt Crescent come asking for it," she said, "I shall send flowers to whatever church still admits you."

"Send money instead."

"I'm not ungrateful, Mr. Vale."

"No," he said, weighing the coins without counting them yet. "Only improved."

He slipped the money into his trouser pocket, tucked the brush behind one ear, and considered departure. A crowd loved you best in the first six minutes. Stay past ten and they began asking for charity. Another line. Another trick. Another proof that the rabbit was really in the hat and not in your sleeve all along.

***

He had just taken the crate under one arm when something struck the back of the fresh sign with a wet smack.

The sound was meaty enough that half the crowd laughed before they turned.

Above them, an errand boy on the narrow gallery between two buildings had frozen with a split bucket still in his hands. He could not have been more than twelve. One suspender hung loose, and his left ear stuck out from his hair as if it wanted to escape him first. He had that guilty stillness children got when they knew apology would only make adults more accurate.

"Oh, hell," he said.

The bucket's contents had slid down the back of the signboard and through a crack in the side frame, then burped around to the front in three slow glistening tongues. At first it looked like ordinary drain slurry: black water, pale mash, a rind of green filth where too many things had been mixed and heated and forgotten. Patent Row saw that all day and cursed it with no particular imagination.

Then the green rind shivered.

Silas stopped moving.

The stuff on the sign began climbing.

It did not do it fast. That would have been easier to understand. It lifted itself by the patient half-millimeters of something testing a surface, throwing out thin frilled edges and finding grip on the drying paint. One tongue spread over the lower corner of the W. Another reached toward the green underline and brightened there. The black water dripped away. What remained had the live, treacherous sheen of spill-born moss, the kind that showed up after digester surges and gutter floods and made three blocks of fools insist they had discovered a new strain. Usually it flared, stank, and died.

This one did not look interested in dying.

The crowd drew closer. Patent Row could smell novelty faster than fire.

"What's that, then?" the ferry clerk said.

The boy on the gallery found his voice. "Sweepings from Latcher's lamp room! I only meant to tip them in the cut!"

"Well, you tipped them in my sales pitch," Clary Dobb snapped.

The boardinghouse girl held the polished bell up higher as if that might scare the slime back into obedience. "It is eating the widow."

"A fitting courtship," said the driver on the dray.

Another laugh. The newsboy was already bouncing on the balls of his feet. "Name it, Vale."

Silas did not answer.

He had seen live gutter growth before. Enough to know when the crowd wanted fright and when it wanted fun. This was still fun. Barely. The moss had climbed across the curve of the W and was now throwing a faint green wash over the cream paint, not dissolving it, not covering it, only altering the light on it. The effect was pretty in the cheap dangerous way a broken bottle could be pretty in sun.

"Go on," said one of the ferry clerks. "You named the polish. Name the leavings."

"I don't christen every damp mistake on the avenue."

"You will if we clap," said the boardinghouse girl, and the crowd rewarded her with exactly that.

Silas felt the pressure change. It had been admiration a moment ago. Now it was appetite with teeth. Patent Row had decided he was not only useful. He was reusable.

He hated that part. He loved it too. Those were neighboring districts as well.

The moss had reached the downstroke of the R in RUB. Its frilled edges were paling, not green now but a translucent shell color, the sort of blush a cheap pearl tried to fake and never quite managed. The whole board looked suddenly indecent in a way he had not planned. Widow's Rub, haloed by shy luminous filth.

Silas was aware of the entire street in separate pieces: the damp on the tea crate handle under his palm, the clove breath still leaking off Clary Dobb beside him, the creak of the gallery above where the boy dared not move, the far ferry bell from Ferrymouth carrying through the hot air, and underneath all of it the crowd waiting for his mouth to turn accident into commerce.

He should have walked away. That would have been the wise thing, and wise things were not always beneath him. Sometimes they even happened.

But the crowd was looking at the pinking edges of the moss, then back at him, and a joke arrived whole.

He snorted. "Look at it."

"We're looking," Clary Dobb said through her teeth.

"No, really look. The little whore is blushing."

That got the proper shock. Three women laughed. The errand boy on the gallery made a strangled noise, scandalized on principle and delighted in spite of himself.

Silas pointed with the crate handle. "Came out of a gutter, climbs where it's not invited, throws on a pretty face the instant strangers crowd near. That's not drain-light. That's bridelight."

The newsboy said it first, because boys always did. "Bridelight!"

The boardinghouse girl repeated it with her hand over her mouth. One of the ferry clerks tried it in a mock-genteel accent. The driver on the dray bellowed it down the avenue to people who had not seen any of this and needed only the shape of the word to want in.

"Bridelight!"

"Bridelight on Dobb's board!"

"Oi, come see the bridelight!"

The word went out in layers. Street cry. Laughter. Questions. A woman passing with two hat boxes paused long enough to say, "What is bridelight?" and the newsboy, with the speed of the professionally ignorant, answered, "The newest thing in Nacre Bend, missus," before turning and shouting it again.

Silas felt the first prickle of wrongness then.

The moss at the bottom of the board was no longer only pale. It was pink. Not a trick of the lowering sun. Not paint reflection. Pink from inside itself, a flushed nacre color that thickened along the frilled margins and pooled bright where the word had been repeated loudest. Another tongue of growth slipped over the D and answered with the same change. The green underneath thinned as if retreating from embarrassment.

Clary Dobb stepped back so hard her hat fringe clicked against her cheek. "That wasn't there."

"No," Silas said.

He kept his voice short when he was truly paying attention. The performance went out of it all at once. "It was not."

The boardinghouse girl reached toward the sign, then stopped an inch short when the warmth hit her knuckles. "It's hot."

"Don't touch it," Silas said, and there was enough flat fact in his tone that she obeyed.

The crowd obeyed only by becoming louder.

"Bridelight!"

"Look there, it likes it!"

"Say it again!"

They did. God help them, they did. Half of Patent Row spent its days trying to prove that repetition could make a bad thing profitable. The avenue had trained itself for exactly this kind of mistake.

By the time Silas pushed through them to get closer to the board, the moss had gone from shame-pink to full flushed rose along the lower half of the sign, as if someone had lit a coal behind the paint. The green line he had laid under WIDOW'S RUB now glowed like a river weed seen through wine.

The errand boy on the gallery said, almost reverently, "It heard him."

Nobody answered because that sentence had gone farther into the room than any of them wanted to admit.

Silas looked down into the gutter. A second clot of the spilled stuff had landed there among cigarette stubs, horse droppings, and shredded handbills. It had caught in the ironwork around a drain mouth. While the crowd shouted over the sign, that smaller clot had begun to pink as well.

He could have called it trickery if there had only been one patch.

He could not call two patches trickery with a straight face, and on Patent Row a man needed at least the option.

Clary Dobb grabbed his sleeve. "Mr. Vale. If this ruins my board-"

"If it ruins your board, you will have the most visited ruined board in the district by supper."

"That's not reassuring."

"It wasn't meant to be."

The coins in his pocket suddenly felt too light.

He should have gone then. He knew he should. The crowd was turning from customer to witness, and witnesses never paid enough. But now people were arriving from farther down the avenue and asking the word before they asked the cause. Bridelight had jumped the original moment already. It was moving on its own legs.

Silas set the crate down again.

That was his mistake, or only the first one he had made in full daylight.

***

For the next hour Patent Row did what it did whenever something truly new entered the air: it converted astonishment into commerce before astonishment could change its mind.

A pie seller dragged her barrow ten yards closer to Clary Dobb's stall and announced hot eel hand-pies for bridelight viewing. Two boys started taking wagers on whether the glow would turn the whole sign pink by sunset. A man from a competing polish shop arrived pretending concern and stayed to see whether he might buy whatever remained if Clary Dobb fainted. Someone tried to scrape a thread of the moss into a bottle and yelped when the glass grew warm in his fingers.

Silas endured it because leaving had become harder than staying. Also because he was having a good time, which was the uglier truth. His nerves were up and singing. A thing he had said in a half-joke had altered a living surface in front of witnesses. He knew enough to be afraid and not enough to be bored.

That was almost happiness.

He took a second fee from a soapmaker three doors down for naming a floral wash while the crowd kept one eye on the glowing board. He called the wash Churchstep because it promised cleanliness without innocence, and the soapmaker nearly kissed him. The ease of that second christening should have calmed him. It did the opposite. Ordinary work felt suddenly cheap. He kept looking back at the pink glow under WIDOW'S RUB the way a gambler kept glancing at a horse that had started speaking in a human voice.

The moss did not spread fast. It spread steadily. That was worse.

By the time the sun slid low enough to catch on the ridge above Gilt Crescent, a lace of the same flushed pink had appeared in the mortar seam between Clary Dobb's stall and the neighboring print shop. A strip of gutter growth under a hatter's window answered to the word when a passing apprentice shouted it just to see. Women in work aprons began saying bridelight with practical skepticism, as if naming it right away might keep the thing from becoming somebody else's property first. A pair of schoolboys came running from halfway up the avenue swearing there was some on a bridge piling too, and one of them had chalk on his cuffs from writing the word where he should not.

Silas finally counted his coins while pretending not to care. Enough for supper, enough for tobacco, enough to make Havelock's clerk stop using that soft voice for one more day. Not enough to satisfy the sharper hunger the glowing board had woken in him. He tucked the coins back away feeling cheated by success itself.

That was when Damaris Pike found him.

She emerged from the doorway of the Evening Tin Sheet with printer's smut on two fingers and widow's black gone shiny at the elbows from years of wearing and refusing to replace. Her front tooth had a neat chip out of it that caught the pink glow when she smiled. She had a folded proof tucked under one arm and a stub of pencil anchored in hair that had once been pinned for respectability and had since decided against the effort.

"Don't go home yet," she said. "I can smell tomorrow from here."

Silas took his brush from behind his ear and used the handle to scratch at a drying spot of paint on his wrist. "I was considering drink before destiny. It is a known preference."

"Take both." She looked past him at Clary Dobb's stall, where the crowd had only thickened. "Tell me you didn't plan it. If you tell me you planned it, I've got to print that. If you tell me you didn't, I can imply worse."

"You make a widow's trade look honest."

"And you make honesty sound underdressed. There, we're square." She leaned in, close enough for him to smell ink, clove cigarettes, and the stale starch of old mourning. "How long till it reaches the ridge, do you think?"

"You are asking the wrong bastard."

"No, I'm asking the bastard who put the word in its mouth."

"Its mouth?" Silas looked back at the board. "You have upgraded slime into a witness faster than I expected."

"Witnesses sell. Slime sells too, but less well above the second floor." Her eyes flicked over him with the quick accounting habit of an editor: hair loose from the heat, coat too thin at the cuffs, pocket weighted by one small payment and not enough more. Nothing in her face softened. Damaris Pike did not trade in softness. She traded in angle. "I want three mornings out of this at least. Four if children start frightening one another with it. Give me a line."

"A line for what?"

"For the headline, naturally. I can't put BRIDELIGHT STIRS PATENT ROW unless you hand me something nastier beneath it."

"You already had the word before you came over."

"That is because I employ boys who can hear."

He grinned despite himself. "All right. Print this: It blushes under scrutiny and climbs where it's not wanted. Which makes it more qualified for society than half the ridge."

Damaris's chipped tooth flashed. She wrote it down in a rattling burst, then paused only long enough to look at him over the proof sheet. "If that thing reaches a church wall by morning, I shall own this week."

"If it reaches a church wall by morning, the church may own us."

"That has never stopped me."

She tucked the pencil back into her hair and snapped her fingers at a printer's devil hovering in the doorway. "Ned. Set BRIDELIGHT big enough to make shopkeepers swear."

The boy darted away at once, already mouthing the word as he ran, as if speaking it were part of setting type. Silas watched him go and had the uncomfortable thought that the street had just found another throat.

"Do not look ill," Damaris said. "You have the face of a man who has finally done something worth regretting. It is attractive on you. Temporarily."

"I'm always attractive. Regret's only weather."

"Keep telling women that. It saves them time."

She turned her head slightly, not enough for a full glance, and Silas followed the line of her attention.

Gideon Wren was standing across the avenue in front of a carriage he had no need to have stepped out of except that stepping out of it improved the composition. Lean, silvering at the temples, gloved despite the heat, coat dark and immaculate where everyone else's cuffs had already surrendered to the day. Patent Row's damp seemed to respect him against its nature. Even his boots looked wiped by a servant between each puddle.

Silas had seen him before, because anybody who sold names or wall space in Nacre Bend eventually saw Gideon Wren, but rarely this near. Up close the man's civility had structure to it. You could have set a ledger edge against it and trusted the line.

Damaris muttered, "There is the undertaker of pleasure itself."

"You say that like you have dined with him."

"Twice. He chews as if appetite were a rumor about poorer people."

Gideon crossed the avenue without haste. People shifted for him. Not out of affection. Out of the same instinct that made pedestrians step aside for a horse likely to kick: respect misfiled under caution.

"Mr. Vale," he said.

He inclined his head first to Silas, then to Damaris. "Mrs. Pike."

"Mr. Wren," Damaris said. "Do come purchase the moon. The avenue is nearly out of ordinary commodities."

"You print jokes because you cannot invoice silence," Gideon said mildly. "I am here on more practical business."

Damaris made a small bow with two fingers. "My condolences."

Silas let himself enjoy that for a breath, then turned to Gideon. "If the business is polish, you're late. The widow's been rubbed."

Gideon's mouth moved almost not at all. On another face it would not have counted as a smile.

"So I hear. And now there is this." He looked toward the glowing signboard. He did not gape. That was one of the many expensive things about him. "I understand the name is yours."

"The shout was mine. The rest belongs to the gutter, apparently."

"Nothing belongs to the gutter for long once it has proven marketable."

Damaris snorted. "Now there is a sermon for the age."

Gideon ignored her with enough polish to make the exclusion visible. "Mr. Vale, novelty becomes value only when it can be asked for twice without confusion. A street can shout many things. A city must be taught."

The line put a cold little hook into Silas somewhere under the ribs. Not because it was frightening. Because it was true in the way that ugly commercial truths often were.

He shrugged to hide that he had felt it. "You may teach the city what you like. I only supply conversation."

"Conversation is an undisciplined distribution channel."

"That sounds lonely."

"It sounds profitable." Gideon glanced at the widening crowd and then back to Silas. "If the growth persists past tonight, I should like a word with you tomorrow."

"You are having one now."

"A better one, then."

Damaris clicked her tongue. "Be careful, Silas. Men like him don't buy mouths. They buy the hours around them and call the arrangement civilization."

Gideon turned to her at last. "Mrs. Pike, you mistake stewardship for theft because theft is the only durable grammar your paper recognizes."

"And you mistake a leash for architecture."

Silas almost laughed. The two of them were lovely together in the way acid and silver were lovely when brought too near. He filed that away. Everything on Patent Row could be turned to use if you had enough shamelessness and somewhere to write it.

Gideon returned his attention to Silas. "Tomorrow morning, then. If the phenomenon remains."

"And if it does not?"

"Then you have still proved yourself interesting in public, which is no small thing."

That was almost flattering. More importantly, it sounded like money dressed for church.

Silas said, "I keep irregular mornings."

"I do not." Gideon touched two fingers to the brim of an invisible hat, because his actual hat remained in the carriage where a man like him left unnecessary objects until they were required for theater. "Do not let Mrs. Pike print you into a corner before then."

He went back across the avenue. The crowd closed after him. Even his exit seemed ironed.

Damaris watched him go and let out a breath through her nose. "There. Now the day has turned expensive."

"You make everything sound like a debt."

"Everything is a debt. Some of it merely dresses better." She folded the proof under her arm again. "If he offers you a retainer, ask for more than your appetite says. Appetite is a cut-rate negotiator."

That made Silas glance at her sharply. She only smiled and turned away.

"Tomorrow's sheet," she said over her shoulder. "Buy it from a child, not a man. The children shout clearer."

Then she was gone into the print shop, where type and rumor were already beginning their night work.

Silas stood a moment longer in the guttered light, listening.

Patent Row had changed key.

The usual evening clatter was still there: dray wheels knocking through ruts, hawkers dragging out the last of the day, ferry bells from the river, glass clinking in upstairs rooms, some idiot singing half a chorus from a music hall song he did not know well enough to survive. But over and through it now came the new word. Not everywhere. Not yet. Enough places to matter.

"Bridelight down by the print shop!"

"No, look, there is some by the saddler too!"

"Mind your hem, it's warm-"

"Who named it?"

"Patent Row Silas."

He hated hearing that name in strangers' mouths. He always had. It made him sound both larger and cheaper than a man ought to be.

He drifted with the crowd instead of leaving it, which was another mistake. The dusk thickened. Lamps along the avenue began to come up one by one, ordinary household green in most chimneys, but where the spill-born patches had taken hold the light was altered. Under the hatter's window a line of moss in the mortar joints had gone the same flushed shell pink as Clary Dobb's sign. On the brick flank of the print shop, a damp patch no one had noticed at noon now glowed soft rose around the edges of a handbill for a boxing exhibition. Farther down, on the iron brace of a pedestrian bridge over the drainage cut, a smear of living film had brightened as if somebody had laid bridal ribbon under soot.

Every new sighting made people say the word again.

Every saying seemed to feed the thing it named.

Silas stopped at the mouth of an alley where two shopgirls and a coal runner were staring at a strip of pink glow climbing the wall above a busted crate. The coal runner had one shoe tied with cord and a healed burn on the back of his hand shaped like a crooked flower. He looked over when Silas drew near and did not recognize him, which was almost restful.

"You think it will spread?" the boy asked nobody in particular.

One of the shopgirls answered before Silas could decide whether to lie. "Everything does if rich men notice."

That was a better line than most paying clients ever bought from him. He nearly told her so. Then the patch on the wall pulsed once, a soft flush through the pink, and all three of them laughed in the same startled key people used when a dog unexpectedly spoke.

Silas did not laugh.

He looked back up the avenue. Clary Dobb's glowing board had become a small lantern in the crowd. Beyond it the ridge windows of Gilt Crescent held the last gold of the sun. Below, Patent Row was taking on its night colors: algae green under common lamps, river black in the gutters, and now, in scattered answer across brick and iron and damp timber, that impossible bridal pink.

He tried the ordinary explanations in order because he was not an idiot. Reflection. Mineral in the sweepings. Trick of warm paint under a cooling sky. Coordinated fraud by persons unknown and too underfunded to deserve the effort.

Each explanation failed on contact with his eyes.

He put his hand against the alley wall beside the glowing strip and felt the heat in it, not enough to burn, enough to declare itself alive. The brick sweated under his palm. Pink light climbed over the web of old mortar and made the ridges look almost tender.

He had named products before. He had named pests, washes, varnishes, appetite tonics, a line of lamp collars so ugly the name had been the only honest part of them. He had put words on things until they sold faster, shone brighter in shop windows, or seemed better bred than their materials justified.

He had never before watched the thing itself lean toward the word.

That should have sobered him clean through.

Instead a dangerous pleasure opened low in his gut, hot and bright and mean with possibility. If a shouted word could do this on a street corner, what could a printed one do by morning? What could a whole city be talked into? He thought of walls. Lamps. Ferry rails. Whole neighborhoods lit one color because the right phrase had caught. Then he thought of the same force escaping whoever first named it, answering not to a patron or a shop but to the nearest hundred mouths, and the pleasure turned at the edges.

This was bigger than a successful christening.

That meant it was worth more money than anything he had touched in months.

It also meant money might not be the first hand on it.

A girl half a block away shouted, "Bridelight!" just to test the air. Three voices answered. On the bridge brace the pink deepened.

Silas took his hand off the wall.

For the first time since he had started selling words, he felt the old stupid superstition trying to rise in him, the childish conviction that language was not only trade or wit or leverage but a kind of handling. A way of touching the live world without laying skin on it.

He did not like the thought. He liked it far too much.

Night settled another notch. Patent Row glowed back.

Silas stood in the damp alley mouth with Clary Dobb's payment in one pocket and somebody else's future trouble moving pink through the walls around him, and he could not tell whether he had just made the best joke of his life or the first one that would keep answering after he shut up.
