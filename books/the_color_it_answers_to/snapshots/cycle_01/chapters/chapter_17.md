# Chapter 17

By morning Patent Row looked hungover from its own miracle. The blessing bills Damaris's boys had pasted for Bellglass Bridge hung in wet curls, and every slick wall along the hill held some leftover quarrel of color: pale gold in one seam, kitchen blue under it, a feral pink climbing the mortar where somebody had shouted bridelight just to see if the city would blush again. People were still stopping in the street to test words against the moss. A teamster in a cracked hat called one patch ferry-veil and got a stripe of green so cold it almost looked silver. Two laundresses laughed, tried widow's lantern, and turned the same stripe blue as skimmed bruises.

Silas kept walking.

He had slept a little at Ferrymouth, face-down on Junie's spare mattress with the ferry bells punching holes through what was left of the night. The chorus from Bellglass Bridge still ran in his skull, and not the sound of it only, though there had been enough sound to shake rust from iron. He kept feeling a whole city answer at once. He kept feeling what it had been to stand inside that answer and not have to own it. He did not know yet if that felt like relief or bereavement. His mouth tasted of old paste and lamp smoke and the bitter heel of envy. There were still parts of him cheap enough to miss the stage.

Damaris had sent for him at dawn by a runner with one suspender hanging and yesterday's ink on his ear. Gideon had sent for him too, by card, thick cream stock, Verdancy seal pressed in green wax. The card requested a private discussion at his immediate convenience.

That alone told Silas what sort of morning it was.

Damaris's pressroom stood open to the alley for air and got none worth having. The place smelled of hot iron, wet paper, stale chicory, and the human grease of boys who had slept in their shirts. The widow herself was at the long proofing table with her mourning collar unpinned and spread open because press heat raised hives on her neck. She had tucked the loose collar points down with a brass safety pin as if respectability were just another sheet to trim when it snagged. One compositor dozed upright on a stool. Another was setting type with his lips moving around the copy like prayer.

"You look awful," Damaris said without looking up. "Which is useful. A handsome man after a public victory makes people suspicious."

"Morning."

"It's late enough to be morning, yes." She slid a sheaf across the table. "Read that before you go uphill and let him try to wash you."

Silas took the pages. They were office copies on thin paper, violet with transfer ink in places where a clerk had pressed too hard. Verdancy House headings sat over distribution tallies and runner instructions. One list of ward names had, beside each entry, another column in a tidier hand: market substitute, simplified form, approved packaging color. He saw names he knew were never meant for labels because Inez had once told him what they were for in a room so hot the ferment pipes sweated on the walls. One meant keep children away from the heat lip. One meant burn only in damp weather. One meant funeral use, low and blue, for households sitting with a body through the night. Verdancy had turned it into moon-silk.

Under those was a stack of purchase notes. Arrears had been bought cheap from fuel men and landlords: kitchen paper, wash-house paper, digester liens. Junie's name sat halfway down one page in a clerk's obedient block hand, her debt entered like a crate of soap.

Silas looked up.

"Where'd you get these?"

"A clerk with a grievance and a fondness for cards," Damaris said. "Also for oyster pie, which is how grievances become print. Don't romanticize me. I'm a grocer of resentment."

He flipped farther. There were internal memoranda too. Language harvest from low wards before autumn cooling. Repeatable variants for district segmentation. Recommended use of Mr. Vale's public wit to convert resistance into aspirational difference. He could feel Gideon's mind moving under the page, neat as a knife through fruit.

"These are copies."

"Of course they're copies. I'm reckless, not stupid." Damaris finally looked at him, chipped tooth showing because she was not in the mood to hide anything. "What I don't have is the packet he will slide in front of you today when he explains why theft becomes vision if the stationery is costly enough. I want that packet. I want whatever names he thinks safe to show you now that he thinks the bridge business has made plurality fashionable. And I want the contract gone if you're not going to sign another. If he still owns your mouth on paper, he will pretend anything I print is a lover's squabble between businessmen."

"You think he brought me up there to hand you a confession."

"No. I think he brought you up there because he still knows exactly what kind of hunger made him money."

That landed because it was true, and because Silas had spent half the walk from Ferrymouth trying not to taste it. Damaris tapped the copy with one inked finger.

"He's going to offer you a cleaner version of your old sin. Maybe a smaller one. Maybe a prettier one. That's the dangerous sort." She leaned back and rubbed at the welt rising under her collarbone, irritated more by her own skin than by him. "If you take it, take it honestly and I will print around you. If you don't, bring me the papers and let the city see the seams. Either way, don't go up there thinking the man has discovered a conscience. He's discovered a pivot."

Silas set the copies down. The rollers thudded twice in the back room where someone tested a plate. He thought of his old shop on Patent Row, the window with his best lettering ghosted in reverse on the inside pane, the north light he had cursed all winter and loved every month he could pay for it. He thought of how simple it would be to have a door again, a bench, a rack of clean sable, a place where his name was not currently being shouted by newsboys as half a warning and half a joke.

"If I bring you the papers," he said, "you print all of it. Not only him."

Damaris's mouth tipped. "There you are. I was wondering if shame had finally made you dull. Sit with that comfort now: I've no intention of making you pretty. I'm a widow, not a seamstress."

He almost laughed. It came out as breath.

"All right."

"Good. Now go uphill before his courage improves."

Verdancy House kept its private rooms above the showroom in Gilt Crescent, where the ridge caught more wind and less honest smell from the river. The clerk downstairs knew Silas by sight and disliked him for reasons that had nothing to do with morality and everything to do with class. Silas had always had that effect on clerks who could not decide whether he was vulgar or enviable. Today the man only held the door with extra care, as if touch itself might leave a mark.

Gideon's office looked the same as the first day Silas had signed away his neutrality: blue carpet thick enough to quiet boots, green-shaded lamps, a coal grate polished around the mouth, paper arranged in weighted squares on a walnut desk broad enough to seat a family. The windows were cracked against the damp, but the room still smelled more of starch and lemon peel than of Nacre Bend. Gideon was bare-handed because he had been handling paper. The left cuff button of his shirt had worked loose again; he worried it with his thumb while he read, and the silk around it had worn a small grey halo from the habit.

"Mr. Vale," he said. "Please sit."

Silas stayed standing until Gideon looked up. Then he took the chair opposite the desk because he refused to play harder to get than he felt.

Gideon studied him with calm professional disappointment, as if last night's public defeat had chiefly injured the room's arrangement. "You should be congratulated. Few men survive a crowd by surrendering authorship and remain useful afterward. Fewer still do it by instinct."

"That what I did?"

"It is what the city thinks you did. The city is wrong in fascinating ways, but one can work with wrongness if it is energetic enough."

He slid a decanter and glass Silas's way. Silas did not touch either. His hands were steady enough until he looked at the desk and saw, off to one side, a ribbon-tied stack of papers thick as a fist. His own contract sat on top, watermark visible where the light caught it. His name. Gideon's. The old green wax seal he had once pressed like a man sealing up his own throat.

Gideon saw him see it and gave the smallest smile.

"I thought it best to keep the morning concrete."

"You always did like paper more than people."

"Paper keeps its shape longer."

There was the offer, then, laid out with the kind of civility men used when they expected greed to do the rough work for them. A new retainer, not exclusive in the old sense because the old sense had broken on Bellglass Bridge in front of God and ferry traffic. Better, Gideon said. More sophisticated. Verdancy House would cease trying to force one name and instead license district usage under a central arrangement. Public plurality, private ownership. Silas would serve as principal designer of public surfaces and authorized variants. He would have a monthly salary, a percentage on licensed boards, and a restored workshop on Patent Row under a lease held safely through Verdancy. Not the little room he had lost. Better rooms. Better glass. Better foot traffic. Damaris could be fed selected copy as needed. Junie's fuel notes would be returned and cleared. Miss Cordero, if she chose not to be foolish, could be retained quietly as a technical consultant.

As Gideon spoke, he laid mock-ups in front of him: paste-up sheets, sample layouts, a handsome broadside with layered type and ornamental borders. Beneath the printed names, in a script Silas recognized as his own old public hand because Gideon had paid him enough times to imitate it, the words read: Usage arranged by Silas Vale.

The bastard had even chosen the blue he liked best.

Silas felt the pull of it so hard it shamed him. It was not only the money, though the money was real enough: sixty crowns at signing, twelve a month, materials on account. It was enough to put meat in Junie's stew without the arithmetic scowl between her brows. It was enough to buy Otis boots that did not leak at the toes. It was enough to stand in a shop with north light on a fresh board and feel his own name come back into his body by the hand instead of by rumor. It was enough to stop being a man who lived on other people's spare corners and apology.

Worse than the money was the acquittal. Gideon was offering him a way to come back elegant. It would not be forgiveness exactly; forgiveness was for churchwomen and fools. But it would be explanation, reframing, installation again in the city as someone whose public failure had turned out to be foresight.

He hated how much he wanted it.

Gideon watched that hatred pass over his face and mistook it for deliberation. Men often had.

"You understand," Gideon said, folding his hands, "that last night's display changes nothing fundamental. People still require placement. They still require a trusted hand. They may shout six names from a bridge, but by market day they will want to know which packet to buy, which lamp to hang, which district mark means money instead of mold. There is no shame in order. There is only profit or waste."

"And the ward names?"

"What of them?"

"You strip them, polish them, sell them back."

"We refine them. Context is expensive, Mr. Vale. Most consumers do not buy context."

"Most consumers don't have to keep a stove on with it."

Gideon gave a slight shrug. "Then they are not the profitable consumers."

Silas looked at him. Really looked. The man's silver at the temples, the immaculate tie, the loosened cuff button under the worrying thumb. The office had the same green shade on the lamps as a hundred rooms below the ridge, but here the light was obedient. Here it answered to money first and mouths second. Last night at Bellglass Bridge the city had broken that obedience for one public hour. Gideon had come out of the wreckage already looking for a smaller cage.

"You'd sell them the rebellion too," Silas said.

"If rebellion becomes a habit, it is merely another route of distribution."

The honesty of it made Silas's stomach go cold.

Gideon leaned back. "You should not sentimentalize Miss Cordero's victory. Her kind of plurality is laborious. It soils the hand. It does not scale. What I am offering is an end to chaos with enough local ornament to satisfy the sentimental poor and enough coherent billing to satisfy the men who matter."

There it was: Miss Cordero's victory, the sentimental poor.

Silas heard the bridge again for a second, not as music this time but as proof of number. Children shrieking names wrong on purpose. Aunt Vero bawling something rude enough to make a priest choke. Inez's voice cutting straight through all of it because she had not asked the crowd to admire her, only to use the right words for the right light. Gideon had stood there and learned nothing except that next time he ought to invoice the noise.

He let his gaze drift to the ribbon-tied packet.

"What's in the rest?"

"Enough for you to see the architecture."

"Show me."

Gideon untied the ribbon himself. Perhaps that was the vanity of the thing. He wanted Silas to admire the mechanism. He spread the papers over the desk in neat fans: extraction schedules from Sump Ward and Lantern Steps, runners' reports on which names had commercial charm and which carried too much death or kitchen-specific use to sell cleanly, notes on debt acquisition timed to pressure local adoption, print orders for relabeled cuttings with color recommendations, a memorandum headed Language Simplification Strategy for Autumn Uptake.

Silas saw his own past work threaded through it like disease. Marginal notes in Gideon's hand beside phrases Silas had once brought back loose in his mouth, thinking himself a clever bastard and not yet a thief. One sheet had a list of possible taglines under his byline. Another had household warnings severed from the warnings and retained only for sound. The man had built a whole accounting language for theft.

And there, halfway down one ledger page, were Junie's notes, marked held pending leverage deployment, then transferred internal, then recoverable upon cooperation.

Silas tapped that line.

"Those come back first."

Gideon looked almost bored. "Of course. Provided we are speaking seriously."

"Put them with the rest."

"Mr. Vale."

"If we're being concrete, be concrete."

For the first time all morning Gideon seemed to consider not just the outcome he wanted but the shape of the man in front of him. Then, because he believed so thoroughly in appetite, he made the fatal choice and slid the notes out from the ledger pocket. Junie's fuel papers were soft at the folds from too much handling. Somebody had added Verdancy House marks in the corner where her own blunt numerals ought to have been left alone. Silas picked them up. Gideon did not stop him.

"You are angry," Gideon said. "That is not unusual. Men are often angry at the point of return. They call it conscience because they want a prettier noun. Take the morning if you require it. Take the papers and read them. By evening you will remember that outrage does not pay rent."

Silas kept holding the notes. With his other hand he lifted the old contract from the top of the stack.

The paper felt heavier than it had when he signed it. Maybe shame added weight. Maybe he had more hand to feel it with now. He looked at the scrawl of his name and remembered the first shock of that advance in his palm, how quickly compromise had felt like relief when Junie needed coal and Havelock Pigments wanted their money and every wall in Patent Row still pretended there was virtue in being paid well for ugliness.

He had told himself at the time that everyone sold something, whether it was brushwork or back or mouth. Why should he be the one bastard in Nacre Bend too pure to take a retainer?

Because sometimes the thing you sold kept reaching after you.

He stood.

Gideon did not move. "Sit down."

"No."

"Do not perform integrity for me. I funded you through better performances than this."

Silas smiled then, a mean little thing that hurt his own face. "That is the trouble, isn't it? You still think the room is yours if the carpet's thick enough."

He turned to the coal grate. Gideon rose fast enough then, chair legs scraping the blue carpet.

"Mr. Vale."

Silas fed the lower corner of the contract to the grate's open mouth.

The paper caught slowly at first, the edge browning, then all at once the green seal blistered and burst. Fire ran under his own signature until the ink rose black and curled. Heat bit his knuckles. Gideon crossed the room with a speed that would have looked ungentlemanly to anyone else. Silas stepped back before the man could snatch the sheet free and shoved the burning contract deeper with the poker. It broke at the fold. One half dropped into orange coals and vanished in a bright, ugly lick. The other hung for a second, his name splitting open along the S, before it went too.

For one absurd instant he felt grief. The grief was real, and it was not for Gideon's money. It was for the shape of himself that had once fit so neatly inside a signature and a reputation and a room full of men waiting to be charmed. It went up with the paper. He stood there and smelled the old life burn: rag cotton, sealing wax, ink, the fat paper rich men trusted more than they trusted bodies.

Gideon had stopped two paces away. He had not shouted for the clerks. He had not grabbed Silas by the coat. His face had gone very still, which on Gideon meant fury stripped of sermon.

"You are making a theatrical mistake."

"No," Silas said. "I made that when I signed it."

He took the packet of extraction papers off the desk with his burned hand. Gideon's eyes flicked down to it.

"Leave those."

"You just told me to take the morning."

"Those are not yours."

Silas tucked Junie's notes inside the packet. "That has not stopped Verdancy yet."

Now Gideon did reach, not wildly, just enough to show what he wanted. Silas stepped back again. The desk hit his thigh. Somewhere downstairs a bell rang from the showroom and went unanswered.

"If you print a scandal sheet with stolen office papers," Gideon said, every word clipped and exact, "you will ruin yourself beyond any possibility of repair."

"I think that part's already in motion."

"And Miss Cordero will still not thank you."

That found flesh, but it found it too late.

"I'm not asking her to."

Gideon's mouth thinned. "You should have stayed with what you were good at."

Silas looked at the grate where the last ribbon of contract had collapsed into cinder. "Maybe I am."

He left before the man could choose between dignity and pursuit. Gideon chose dignity. Of course he did. By the time the clerk downstairs half rose from his stool, Silas was already through the outer door and into the ridge wind with the packet under his arm and his right hand starting to smart in earnest.

Damaris did not say a word when he came back into the pressroom. She only looked from his face to the papers and then to the pink welt across his knuckles where the fire had kissed him. The rollers in the back had started for real now, a heavy turning heartbeat that made the floor hum.

"Well," she said. "That's a better expression."

He handed her the packet. She moved fast once the paper was in reach, spreading sheets, sorting, calling for copy boys, barking for the compositor to wake fully or go drown himself somewhere less expensive. She read as she moved, eyes hardening into pleasure of the coldest useful kind.

"Language simplification strategy." She whistled once through her chipped tooth. "He really did write the confession as a memorandum. Men with orderly desks should be horsewhipped."

Silas laid Junie's fuel notes on top. "Print those too."

Damaris paused. "You are certain."

"If you scrub me out of it, I will hate you."

"That would ruin my month." She gathered the notes in with the rest. "Don't fret. I know the difference between martyrdom and evidence."

He stayed because leaving felt cowardly and because the pressroom was the only place in the city where his old appetite and his new shame were both being put to use without pretending they were clean. Copy boys ran. Type clicked into sticks. Damaris wrote the opening in three bursts, crossing nothing out at first and only then going back to sharpen the nouns. She chewed anise seed from a twist of paper while she worked because the metallic taste of the press made her swear too much, and she swore plenty anyway.

She let him read the proof before the forme was locked.

Verdancy House Purchases Household Debts to Strip Ward Names for Sale.

Below it, in tighter columns, the packet laid open: the instructions to runners, the lists of practical names severed from use and repackaged for fashion, the bought arrears, the timed leverage, the note on Junie's kitchen. Damaris had not spared Silas. She named him as Verdancy's paid mouth, as the man who had helped turn ward language into product, and as the man who had now burned the contract and supplied the papers. There was no saint there, and no clever rebel scrubbed clean by one decent morning. There was only consequence, widened.

"Good," he said, and meant it through the wince.

"I know." Damaris sanded the page, shook it off, and slapped it toward the waiting boy. "Run that to the stone. Headline first, and if Barlow drops another comma I will break his thumb."

The first sheet came off wet and hot enough to steam faintly in the damp air. Silas took it by the edge. The ink marked him anyway. Outside, before the second dozen had been stacked, he could already hear the first newsboy trying the shout on his lungs.

"VERDANCY BOUGHT YOUR KITCHEN PAPER!"

Another boy answered farther down the alley, voice cracking with delight at the obscenity of good copy.

"WARD NAMES SOLD LIKE SOAP!"

Damaris listened with one hand braced in the small of her back, her face gone suddenly older and lonelier than it ever did while she was actively cruel. Then she shook it off.

"Go to her," she said. "If you wait until the city has finished reading, you'll turn this into another audience."

Silas folded the fresh broadside once, tucked Junie's freed notes inside his coat, and went.

By the time he reached Sump Ward, the paper had outrun him.

Two women were reading it aloud outside a wash house, taking turns where the type ran small. A ferryman had pinned one copy to a post with a fish knife and was explaining the column on debt leverage as if he had invented debt himself. A boy on Lantern Steps was testing Damaris's headline at different volumes to see which version made the moss nearest the rail ripple green. The city had got hold of the scandal in exactly the way Gideon feared most: not as a private disgrace among respectable men, but as something with legs and mouths and cheap shoes.

Silas crossed the plank walk into the grow-house yard under a sky already thinning toward cooler blue. The night's damp was coming earlier now. Autumn was somewhere upriver, not here yet but thinking about it. The yard smelled of wet burlap, pond heat, and the faint sweet rot of cut stems left too long in a bucket.

Inez was at the long worktable under the lean-to roof, one boot off, rubbing a thumbful of camphor tallow into the arch of her bare foot before she shoved it back into the leather. Wet planks made her feet seize by evening; he had seen her hide the limp before, but never the maintenance of it. Her scarf was off and hanging from a nail. Dark hair had come loose at the nape in a damp rope. On the table beside her sat three boards sanded clean, a tin of white ground, a pot of blue-green paint, and the first broadside from Damaris folded under a stone so it would not crawl away in the damp.

She looked up once.

"You are late," she said.

He stopped. "You knew I was coming?"

"Damaris printed you in half the city. It seemed likely the other half would deliver the body." She set her boot down and pulled it on. "Come here. The edge on this plank is rough."

That was all the welcome he got. It was more than he had any right to expect.

He came to the table. The plank was for Ferrymouth by the size and scuff of it, scavenged ferry wood with old bolt holes filled in. One end had already been underpainted. She had ruled pencil lines for three stacked fields of text and left the bottom strip blank.

"What's the blank for?"

"What people add when they aren't asked permission." She tipped her chin toward the paper under the stone. "I read your morning entertainment. Did it hurt?"

He looked at his hand.

"Some."

"Good."

He almost smiled. "You don't waste sugar."

"I've kitchens for that." She slid him a scraper. "Take the old paste off the back. Not with the burn."

He obeyed. The work was ugly and small, which was probably why she had chosen it. Old newsprint and paste came away in gummy curls. The board smelled of river salt and something older, maybe fish oil, maybe rope. Inez opened the broadside with flat fingers and read one paragraph again, though he could tell by the set of her mouth that she already knew it nearly by heart.

"Damaris made him sound taller," she said.

"She made me shorter."

"That part was overdue."

He scraped. She sorted brushes by stiffness and laid them in a row. Her movements were exact without ever looking delicate. The burn scar on the inside of her right wrist flashed when she reached for the paint tin, pale against the rest of her skin like a crescent moon somebody had tried to file down.

When she spoke again, her voice had lost the dry edge just enough to be dangerous.

"Junie?"

"Got her notes back."

He took the folded papers from his coat and laid them beside the broadside. Inez looked at Junie's blunt numerals, at the Verdancy marks in the corner, and then at him. She was not grateful. She was measuring.

"All right," she said. "That is one thing where it belongs."

"And the contract's gone."

"I read that too."

"I burned it in his grate."

Something moved in her face then, quick and gone. Satisfaction, maybe. Or only the relief of hearing a fact out loud instead of through Damaris's appetite. She dipped two fingers in the tallow pot and caught his burned hand before he could pull it back. The ointment was cool, greasy with camphor and something minty underneath. She spread it over the red welt with competent pressure, not tender enough to flatter him and not rough enough to punish.

"You should have done it months ago," she said.

"I know."

"I'm not saying it so you can agree prettily."

"All right."

She let go. "Good. Then listen."

That word changed the yard. Even the drip from the gutter seemed to move aside for it. She picked up the board, set it straight between them, and tapped the three ruled fields.

"This goes to Ferrymouth tonight if the paint dries fast enough. Top line for the common use-name there. Second line for heat warning. Third for cuttings source and the night it was last checked. The blank strip stays blank unless the kitchen adds something after. We are doing two more tomorrow, one for Lantern Steps and one for the lower wash path."

The word was we.

Silas kept his scraper still.

"If that is an offer," he said, "say it like one."

"If it were only an offer, I would not bother saying it at all." She picked up the pencil and wrote on the back of the plank in tight practical script: white, blue-green, lampblack, paste, nails, two labor hours. "You asked me, once, what a future with me would look like if it did not depend on speeches. It looks tedious at first glance. That is one of its virtues."

He felt the old instinct rise, the one that wanted to turn a line like that into flirtation, wanted to make her smile and himself forgivable in the same motion. He let it die.

"Tell me the terms."

She nodded once, as if a student had finally found the right side of the question.

"First: no sole credit. Not on a board, not in print, not in a room full of investors, not in your own head. If a sign comes from ward knowledge, the ward gets named or the sign stays bare. If the work is ours, it is ours. If it is mine, your name does not crawl onto it because you are handsome with a brush."

"Fair."

"Second: no stripping context. If a household gives us a name and a warning with it, the warning stays with the name. If the story matters to use, the story stays too. If somebody says no, that means no. You do not harvest a thing because it sounds expensive."

"Fair."

"Third: money gets written down every time. Materials first. Shared labor next. One part in six goes to the grow houses until the mother trays are steady through the cool weather. If Junie's kitchen takes a job board, she pays when she can, not when some man in a clean office decides to teach her discipline."

"All right."

"Fourth." She set the pencil down and looked at him properly for the first time since he had come into the yard. "You do not come back to me because you burned one paper and felt noble watching it. I am not a medal for improved conduct."

He met her eyes. "I know that too."

"Do you." It was not a question. It was a blade laid flat, waiting to see whether he would press his own throat to it. "If you want my body, you come to it awake. Not hiding. Not as a way to wash off what you did. Not to prove to yourself that a woman with sense has pardoned you. You ask me. I answer. Some nights I will say no, and you will not mope like a dock boy denied a kiss behind the fish shed."

Against his will, despite everything, he barked a laugh.

"I wouldn't dare."

"You have dared stupider things."

"True."

Her mouth twitched and settled. "If I say yes, you stay the night through. No crawling out before dawn because remorse has made you dramatic. If you are in my bed, you are there because tomorrow morning you are still on the ladder beside me, or under the sink fixing paste, or out in the yard cutting dead growth with your wrists aching. Work before declarations. Shared signage before shared mythology. Life before speeches about life."

The last of the light had dropped low enough that the yard lamps were starting to answer. One tray near the drain gave off a stripe of diluted gold that the blue paint on the table made greener by contrast. Somewhere beyond the fence Aunt Vero was shouting about somebody's useless nephew and a stolen bucket. The city did not stop to make room for a turning point. It went on being damp and rude.

Silas put the scraper down very carefully.

"And if I say yes to that," he said, "what am I to you?"

Inez leaned both palms on the board. There was paint under one thumbnail and a little crescent of dried mud at the edge of her sleeve where she had wiped a hand hours ago and forgotten it. He loved her most, he thought, when she looked most like a person halfway through the day's real work.

"A man I want," she said. "A man I do not trust cheaply. A man who is useful when he tells the truth and dangerous when he starts admiring himself. A partner, if you can stand the second word without trying to crawl back into the first."

The first word was author, perhaps, or genius, or star, or bastard. Maybe it was all of them.

He looked at the blank strip on the bottom of the board. Room left for other mouths.

"I can stand it," he said, and knew as he said it that standing it was not the same as liking the loss. The honest future was smaller than the one Gideon had laid out in blue mock-ups and monthly numbers. Smaller and wetter and more public in the least glamorous way. No brass plate on a shop. No easy version of innocence handed back. No sole crowd. He grieved it. He did. The grief did not make the answer less true.

"I want the work," he said. "I want you. I don't know yet how not to miss the rest, but I'm done selling the missing."

Inez studied him long enough to make the yard feel narrow.

"That is almost a decent sentence."

"You know how I feel about almost."

"I know how you feel about applause."

She came around the table then, close enough that he could smell the tallow on her hands, the wet plant smell caught in the cotton at her shoulder, and the faint salt of her own sweat under all of it. It was not perfume or anything bought to mean desire. It was the body that had spent the day doing necessary things and had not apologized for it. She touched the front of his shirt where the broadside was folded in his inner pocket.

"Are you keeping that as proof," she asked, "or as a souvenir of your own reform?"

"Proof."

"Good."

She slid the paper out, unfolded it, read the headline once more, then set it under the stone again. After that she took his face in both hands and kissed him with none of the softness he had spent the morning half craving and half despising in himself. It was not absolution. It was selection. Her mouth was warm and a little rough from coffee. When he made the mistake of turning the kiss grateful, she bit his lower lip hard enough to correct him.

"Do not do that," she said against his mouth.

"Do what?"

"Thank me with your face."

"I wasn't."

"You were."

He put his forehead to hers and laughed once because the only alternative was something worse. "All right."

She kissed him again, slower this time. His burned hand hovered useless for a second until she caught it and put it at her waist herself. The motion was deliberate enough to feel contractual in the cleanest possible sense. He felt the heat of her through the worn work dress, the solid line of her side, the breath she took when his thumb found the knot of muscle above her hip. Her own hand went under his coat and up along his ribs until she found the old scar there and pressed it once, not gently.

"Still there," she said.

"Most things are."

"Not the contract."

"No."

She stepped back first. "Good. Then earn the rest of the evening."

He made a noise low in his throat that would have embarrassed him yesterday and did not now. "Cruel woman."

"Tired woman. Hand me the white ground."

So they worked.

He laid in the top line while she mixed the warning blue darker for Ferrymouth's damp. The board was for Junie's kitchen after all, though Inez had not said so until the first letters began to show. FERRY-BED over the top, in a plain strong hand fit to be read through steam and fatigue. Below it, KEEP THE BLUE STRIP FOR STOVE HEAT. DO NOT CUT GOLD GROWTH INTO THE POT BEDS. The last line named the source house in Sump Ward and left the date blank for morning's inspection. The bottom strip stayed empty. While the paint set, Inez ruled two smaller boards for Lantern Steps: one for safe rail light, one for children's fingers.

The work steadied him more than any speech could have done. He brushed, lifted, breathed, turned the stroke before the grain caught, and remembered his wrist before it complained. Inez corrected spacing without apology and once took the brush from him to tighten an S that had tried to become showy. He let her. Outside the fence, voices kept lifting with pieces of Damaris's broadside: Verdancy, kitchen paper, bought debts. Some of the shouts were gleeful. Some were angry. None of them sounded like private catharsis. Good.

When the Ferrymouth board was done they carried it inside to dry on chairs in Inez's room because the night damp had turned mean. Her room was narrow and warm with stored heat from the wall pipes. A chipped blue cup sat on the sill full of screws and clipped nail heads because she saved what could still be useful. Her work scarf hung over the bedpost. On the little table by the bed lay the oilskin ledger, closed and tied. Beside it she had set out another book, cheap brown paper boards, blank except for two ruled columns on the first page.

She saw him looking.

"That one is for jobs," she said. "Not names. Hours, materials, who paid, who did not."

"Romantic."

"I told you what my virtues were."

He set the drying board down across two chairs. The room had only one lamp lit, shaded with tin so the glow fell low and useful. It turned her face into planes and left the corners in green shadow. He could hear the ward outside through the thin wall: a baby objecting to sleep, somebody laughing at the broadside again, a bucket knocked over, the far river answering in hull knocks and one bored bell.

Inez took the cheap ledger to the table and opened to the first page. In the left column she wrote Ferrymouth kitchen board. In the right: shared labor. Then she held out the pencil.

"If you're saying yes," she said, "write it by doing something boring."

He took the pencil and added the materials in his own hand. White ground. Lampblack. Blue-green. Nails to come. His lettering wanted to flourish out of habit. He kept it plain. When he set the pencil down, Inez nodded as if witnessing something more intimate than a vow.

"All right," she said.

"That all?"

"For tonight."

The want in him had not gone anywhere during the work. It had changed shape. Less plea, more hunger with its boots on. He came to her slow enough to be asked away if she wanted. She did not ask. She put one palm flat on his chest and felt the beat under it like she was testing a wall for hidden pipe.

"You are staying?" she said.

"If you'll have me."

"I just spent an hour making rules for it. Do not make me repeat myself."

He kissed her again, and this time when she opened to him there was no gratitude in it, no theatrical wreckage. Only desire with its eyes open. He got her dress loose at the throat and put his mouth on the burn scar inside her wrist because he had watched that pale crescent flash at him for months and wanted to know the skin around it. She hissed once and tipped his head where she wanted it, which was better than tenderness, better than pardon. Her fingers found the buttons of his shirt and fumbled only once at the burn on his hand before choosing the other side. By the time they reached the bed they were both half-undone and still arguing in the smallest practical ways.

"Mind the lamp," she said.

"I'm watching it."

"You are watching me."

"That too."

"Less poetry. More care."

He laughed against her shoulder. Then she pushed him down on the edge of the mattress, stripped the coat from him, and kissed him hard enough to shut him up. The bed ropes complained under their weight. Outside, somebody in the alley tried out a new name for the bridge light and got no answer at all.

Later, when the lamp was turned low and the Ferrymouth board dried in the chair shadows like a third quiet witness, they lay under one blanket with the damp night pressing the window. Inez was on her side facing away, not asleep; he could tell by the change in her breathing when voices rose outside. After a while she reached back without turning and put her hand on his forearm.

"We start at dawn," she said.

"I know."

"This is not repaired."

"I know that too."

Her hand stayed where it was. "Good."

Silas looked past her to the jobs ledger on the table, to the oilskin name book beside it, to the chair where the board for Junie's kitchen waited for morning. He could still feel the ghost of the old life burning in Gideon's grate. He probably would for a long time. But here was the smaller thing, the harder thing: a room with no brass plate on the door, a ledger that would record hours instead of glory, a woman who had made space for him without pretending the making cost her nothing, and work already drying for the morning.

Down in the alley, two drunk men argued over what to call the same strip of moss climbing the drainpipe. One said saint's broth. The other said dock-gleam. Neither won. The strip held both colors badly and stubbornly at once.

Inez's heel found his shin under the blanket, warm and accidental or maybe not. He lay there listening to the city refuse to settle, and when sleep came it did not feel like absolution. It felt like having somewhere exact to wake.
