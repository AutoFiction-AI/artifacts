# Chapter 8

Silas reached Sump Ward with his clean shirt already pasted to his spine and the mud named for the feast sucking at his boots as if the ward itself wanted an answer before he got a step farther. Mid-summer heat had kept the digesters lively all day. Even at dusk the air above the drainage cuts smelled of yeast, sweet rot, lamp smoke, and hot river weeds. Lanterns were going up on clotheslines between the grow houses, green and blue and one sudden vulgar blush-pink where some patch had answered to city gossip again. He had told himself the Feast of Mud Names was the one night a man might disappear inside other people's noise. He could carry tables. He could keep his mouth shut. If fortune grew unexpectedly charitable, he could find Inez at the edge of the crowd and say one honest thing before she told him to go to hell.

The feast always came after the first hard weeks of summer, when the ward mud stopped pretending it might dry and settled into its true profession of swallowing shoes, stools, pails, tempers, and sometimes whole hens. Sump Ward answered by feeding itself on top of the mess and naming every fresh indignity something memorable enough to laugh at. By the time Silas ducked under the first wash line, doors had already been laid across casks for tables, planks had been bridged over the worst soft patches, and somebody had chalked names onto slates tied to the serving bowls. A tureen of crab stew wore the title *Bridegroom's Shame.* A crock of pickled okra had become *Saint Judas Fingers.* A pan of corn cakes steamed under the pious lie *Mudfast Virtue.*

Aunt Vero stood at the middle of it with her thick white braid looped down her back like mooring rope and her broad hands deep in a bowl of pepper mash. Heat and algae stain had browned her wrists nearly to walnut. One eye drifted a little when she was amused, which it was now, because a knot-kneed fiddler on an upturned crate had just missed three notes in a row and was trying to bluff the fourth. She saw Silas before anyone else announced him.

"If you came to stare, charge admission," she said. "If you came to work, take that end."

She jerked her chin at a door table leaning crooked against a wall. Silas obeyed before she could reconsider. The opposite end lifted in the hands of a girl with cropped hair and a split thumbnail stained purple from berry dye. She didn't offer her name and he didn't ask. Together they hauled the door through a lane of children who were trying to rename a puddle beside the privy by democratic insult.

"It's Bishop's Bath," one boy declared.

"It's not," said another, missing a front tooth. He held a spoon like a gavel. "It's Courting Soup."

Silas set down his end of the table, wiped his palms on his trousers, and heard himself say, "It's Tax Collector's Kiss. It sticks to you and leaves less than it took."

That bought him a burst of laughter so quick and easy it hurt. Aunt Vero barked once through her nose. The gap-toothed boy slapped the mud with the spoon as if ratifying law. Somebody down the line shouted that Patent Row Silas could stay if he kept that sort of sense and none of the expensive kind. The remark came with no poison in it yet.

He had not understood until then how badly he had wanted that lack of poison.

The ward moved around him with the loose efficiency of people who had done this every year and expected to do it in old age if the city let them. Women spread oilcloths with one hand and kept babies off the trough rims with the other. Men came in from the catwalks carrying jugs, lamp cages, and a whole skinned carp on a board. The knot-kneed fiddler found his tune and stayed with it, sawing through a reel while a woman with her sleeves pinned above strong freckled elbows kept time on a spoon against a crock. The planks sweated underfoot. Mosquitoes hovered at calf height. Every few minutes the Sallow River announced itself with a ferry bell or the hollow knock of a hull against pilings.

Silas took a stack of bowls where someone pointed, then a basket of crusts, then a barrel hoop that had come loose from a beer cask. Working was easier than arriving. Work gave his hands somewhere to live while his mind tried not to circle the week behind him. His mind kept circling Gideon's office, the good paper, and the little kick in his gut when the advance cleared Havelock Pigments and made his worst creditor suddenly courteous. The nastier memory under all the rest was of himself on a Sump Ward catwalk, still as a rat in the reeds, listening to women use a language built for keeping light from hurting people, and storing it in his head like lifted silver.

He was carrying a crate of cups when he saw Inez.

She came out of the side grow house with a tin basin on one hip and a length of pruning twine still looped around her left wrist, as if she had changed for the feast only as far as the door and no farther. She had traded her work apron for a dark skirt and a clean blouse with the sleeves rolled anyway. Her scarf was fresh tied, indigo with a small tear near one edge where it had been caught on a nail and mended badly. The crescent burn on the inside of her right wrist flashed pale when she shifted the basin higher. She moved across slick plank with the same steady placement she would have used in a flooded cellar. One of the little girls trailing her kept trying to match steps on the narrow boards and failing.

Inez caught the child by the shoulder without looking down. "Heel first on the dry stripe," she said. "Not in the shine. You want supper or a mouthful of trough?"

The girl corrected. Inez let go. Then she saw Silas with the cups.

Nothing softened in her face. Nothing hardened either. She looked at the cups, at the line of people waiting for them, and made a practical choice.

"Those go by the cider cask," she said. "Not there. Unless you want everybody drinking crab grease."

He set them by the cask. "I can avoid that much ruin."

"We'll see."

It was not forgiveness. It was not even kindness. Still, she had spoken to him as if he existed inside the work instead of outside it. He took the scrap as greedily as a beggar takes crust.

For half an hour the feast let him keep it. He fetched more jugs. He lettered two fresh slates with a carpenter's pencil because the rain of last week had blurred the first batch into gray ghosts. He kept the lettering broad and plain, without Patent Row flourishes, and when Aunt Vero looked over his shoulder at *Last Ferry Eel* and *Cold Pot Green* she grunted as if he had at least not made an ass of the nouns. A woman with a cracked alto voice demanded a better name for the beer that had soured in one cask and turned sweet in the next. Silas said *Widow's Surprise,* and the knot-kneed fiddler nearly dropped his bow laughing.

The warmth of it was the dangerous part. It was not dangerous because they laughed at him. That happened everywhere. It was dangerous because they laughed with him and then went back to their own talk as if he might, if he behaved, be one body among others instead of the sort of man whose mouth had to stand on a crate and collect coin. The Feast of Mud Names turned naming into common property for an evening. A child could improve a grandmother's joke. A grandmother could spit on a man's contribution and replace it with something better. Nothing stayed owned long enough to go stale. Silas had spent years making a trade out of sticking a phrase to a surface until nobody could imagine the surface bare. Here the pleasure ran the other way. Names were thrown, used, revised, laughed off, and born again two bowls later.

It made the thing he had done sit in him worse.

By full dark the lanterns were lit. Blue light lay cool along the plank edges. Green lamps under the tables made bare ankles strange. Somebody had hung strings of polished mussel shells that ticked together whenever a warm draft came off the ferment rooms. Silas had a tin cup of sour cider in one hand and a heel of cornbread in the other when he noticed the paper packets.

At first he took them for favors or betting slips. Two boys had strung them through a cord and were wearing them like medals across their chests. A woman used one to fan smoke away from a grill. Another had folded a packet into a little scoop for salt. The paper was cheap and stiff, but the corners carried a gilt crown stamped so thin it rubbed off on fingers. The mark was Verdancy House, and his stomach went hollow before he saw the words.

The printer had set the letters, not his hand. But the block capitals had come from the sample alphabet he had sketched for Gideon's label man, broad at the shoulder and mean little spurs on the R and K. He knew them the way he knew the notch in his left thumbnail. And on the front of the nearest packet, in green ink already smudged by grease, sat a name he had heard behind Aunt Vero's south wall three nights earlier.

*RAIL-BLUE.*

Below it, in smaller print: For steady crossing light.

He might still have walked out then if his body had obeyed the thought. Instead he stood with cider halfway to his mouth and watched the rest of the ward see what he had already seen.

Inez was the first to stop moving.

She had been reaching past one of the boys to take a ladle from the table. The packet string caught on her knuckles. She glanced down with irritation, then took one between finger and thumb. Her gaze moved once over the lettering. Then she read it again. The air seemed to narrow around her.

"Where'd you get this?" she asked the boy.

He grinned, pleased to have produced an object worth adult attention. "Man at Lantern Steps. Three for a farthing if you said the names out loud."

His friend chimed in, "He gave us six because Nilo said *rail-blue* faster than me."

The knot-kneed fiddler was still playing, though softer now because people close to him had turned their heads. Inez plucked the next packet off the string. Then the next. Her hands were steady. That was worse than if she had torn them.

*PAN-COOL.* For kitchen lamps.
*WASH-LINE GREEN.* For damp rooms.
Then she reached the one that made the color leave her face without making her pale.

*MOTHER-HUSH.* For nursery calm.

Silas knew that one too. He had heard it through slatted wood, Aunt Vero saying it low to a young mother whose baby would not sleep near hot light. He had remembered it because the phrase sat oddly in the mouth, soft first, then shut. He had sold it because Gideon had wanted everything useful and because Silas, in the moment that mattered, had wanted to be the kind of man who got to keep the advance more than he had wanted to deserve Inez's trust.

Inez lifted her head and looked straight at him. There was anger in it, but it was not the bright clean anger he had earned from her on Lantern Steps. This was flatter and worse. The look of a woman seeing her own wash hung out in public with strangers' hands on it.

"Who gave you that word?" she asked.

Noise still moved at the edges of the feast. A baby cried once. Somebody set down a bowl too hard. But the middle of the ward had gone quiet enough that he heard a shell string tap against itself in the warm air.

Silas's first instinct was the old one: talk, rename, make the thing a little sideways so he could get his foot out from under it.

"Inez, those are Gideon's packets," he said. "He'll print saint names, dog names, a drowned man's last cough if he thinks it sells mash. I didn't know he'd scatter them down here tonight."

Her voice stayed level. "That wasn't what I asked."

He felt the crowd turn toward him the way water turns when a bigger hull cuts through it. The boy with the packet string stopped smiling and looked from one adult to another, trying to find the rule he'd broken.

Aunt Vero was beside Inez now. She held out her hand. Inez gave her the *MOTHER-HUSH* packet. The older woman read slowly, lips moving over the printed phrase, not because she could not read it but because she meant to make each part answer for itself. The drifting eye settled hard enough to seem straight.

There was no use lying. He had already stepped past any lie that would save him. "I heard things," he said. "That's the truth of it."

Aunt Vero looked up. "Heard."

"Yes."

"Not told."

There was no use denying that either. "Not told."

The fiddler let the tune die mid-bar.

Aunt Vero turned a little so the people beyond her could hear. When she spoke again, her voice had gone oddly formal, the way some old women prayed before they cursed. "This fool listened where he was not invited. He took words off working mouths. Then he sold them uphill so they could come back wrapped in trash paper."

That broke the stillness because the feast had dozens of people inside it who knew at once what they were hearing. A woman by the eel grill snatched a packet from her salt scoop and squinted at it. A man near the cider cask dug two more out of his pocket with the baffled disgust of a person finding lice in a cap. Voices began to rise, not yet in one shout, but in separate recognitions.

"Pan-cool is ours."

"My sister says wash-line green in the damp room."

"Who the hell printed rail-blue?"

"They're selling crossing words now?"

One of the older men laughed once, ugly and unbelieving. "For a farthing apiece, by the look of it."

Silas saw the movement travel through them as memory. Each packet called back a room, a hand, an instruction passed between women over steam or between neighbors on wet steps after dark. The names were not ornaments here. They were habits for keeping light useful, for keeping skin unburned, for finding the rail before a drunk uncle found the canal instead. He had known that. That was what made it theft instead of cleverness. He had known and done it anyway.

Inez had not taken her eyes off him. "Who gave you *mother-hush*?" she asked again.

He could still have spared himself the full shame by refusing the question, by letting the crowd decide the rest from his silence. Instead he answered because the word itself had become too filthy in his mouth to hide behind.

"No one gave it," he said. "I heard your aunt say it in the south house. Through the slats."

For a second he thought Inez might strike him. It was not because she stepped forward; she did not. It was because something in her face finally moved, and the movement was smaller than rage. It was hurt stripped of surprise, a clean cut. He had not been prepared for that. His own shame had kept him imagining her anger as heat. It turned out to be cold workmanship instead, the precise knowledge of where a blade went.

"You listened at a nursery wall," she said.

He opened his mouth and nothing useful came out. "I..."

"You listened," she said, "at a nursery wall."

That landed harder than any public insult he had ever collected. The crowd heard it land. So did he. In the silence behind it he could picture the place exactly: damp boards, one lamp trimmed low, a fretful child, Aunt Vero teaching a new mother which light ran coolest. He had made it sound to himself like research. Standing there with a hundred bodies and supper smells around him, he saw it plain. A grown man crouched by slats, stealing household caution because a rich bastard wanted new copy.

He said the worst honest thing before he could dress it up. "Yes."

The uglier voices came then.

"Thief."

"Patent Row rat."

"He took nursery words."

A woman Silas knew only as the one who always wore a brass thimble on her necklace because her hands shook too much to sew without losing it said, "My niece blistered two fingers when the wrong name went around last month. You selling the right ones too now? Is that the trick?"

He had no answer she could use.

Aunt Vero did. She held up the packet between finger and thumb as if it were damp laundry. "This one told women which lamp stayed cool in a fever room," she said. "This one told boys which blue would hold to a handrail in flood dark. This one" - she shook *MOTHER-HUSH* once - "never belonged to a market at all. He did not take poetry. He took instructions. He took the names that keep people from getting hurt and sent them to a man who'd print them by the hundred with a crown in the corner."

The cheap gold flashed under the lantern and seemed suddenly obscene.

Silas heard himself start up again, the old reflex dying hard. "I told Gideon not to strip the use off them. I told him if he printed them, he had to keep the point..."

Inez laughed, once. It was not pleasant. "You told him."

He shut his mouth.

"Did he pay extra for your concern?" she asked.

"No."

"Did he pay enough for you to stop?"

He thought of Havelock Pigments wiping his slate, of Junie's thin margin, of the way need had looked respectable while he was standing in Gideon's office. Those things were true. None of them helped him here. None of them should.

"No," he said.

Aunt Vero snorted. "Look at that. He can answer plain when the whole ward hears him."

A pressure had built in the bodies around him. It was not a theatrical circle or the neat spacing of a show crowd on Patent Row. This was worse because it was household close: elbows, bowls, lamp handles, damp skirts, working boots, the ordinary distance at which people fed one another and argued about salt. Silas could feel heat off them. Somebody behind him set down a jug with deliberate care, freeing both hands. The knot-kneed fiddler was no longer holding his bow at all. He had laid it across his lap and was looking at Silas with the pinched pity a man gives a dog before somebody else kicks it.

The shame turned physical in stupid little ways. The cider in Silas's stomach went sour. Sweat trickled down his ribs under the shirt. His right hand wanted a brush or a railing or anything to occupy it. He hated that some part of him still looked for the shape of escape, some final line that might win the room back. There was none. A room that had been bought from him before could be bought again. This one had to live by what he had stolen.

A man stepped out from the cider cask. Silas had seen him before on the ward walks, a lamp trimmer maybe, narrow in the hips and broad in the chest, with lye whitening the hairs of his forearms and a baby's knitted sock tied absurdly to one vest button for luck. He did not say anything. He only planted both palms in the middle of Silas's chest and drove.

Silas hit the plank edge with one heel, skidded, pinwheeled once, and went over backward into the fermentation trough.

Warm mash closed over his shoulders with a sound like a crowd inhaling. It was thinner than mud and thicker than soup, alive with bubbles and stray skins of algae. It forced itself up his nose, into his ears, between his collar and neck. The trough had taken a spill-born chorus strain earlier in the week, and the mash answered the lamp light in an oily green shot through with blush-pink. When Silas came up choking, hair plastered flat and one boot gone, the stuff made him glow like a cheap saint painted by a drunken apprentice.

The ward laughed.

They could not help it. Neither could he, if he had been standing dry and somebody else had come snorting out of that trough with cabbage strings on his shoulder and luminous scum hanging off his lashes. The laugh cracked out of fury and sat inside it, ugly and genuine both. Someone shouted, "Name that, then," and somebody else said, "Call it Patent Baptism," and even Aunt Vero's mouth twitched before it flattened again.

Silas planted his palms on the trough rim to haul himself out.

His left hand held. His right slid on mash, caught against the iron hoop banding the trough, and folded under him. Pain shot from wrist to elbow in a white hard line so bright it cleared every thought from his head. He made a sound that was too raw to count as a word and dropped half back into the slurry. The laughter snapped short in the people nearest him. He clawed again with the left, dragging his useless right arm clear. The wrist had already started swelling under the smear of luminous mash.

"Don't grab him," Inez said sharply.

That stopped the two men who had moved forward, whether to help or to shove again Silas could not tell. She was still standing where the crowd had first opened around her, packets crushed in one hand now, green ink bleeding onto her palm. The precision had returned to her voice. It did not sound merciful. It sounded like a woman refusing to let the scene turn sloppy.

"He can get out," she said. "Let him feel where the edge is."

So he did. He hooked his left elbow over the trough rim, got a knee under him, and slid out onto the planks in a shining ruin. Mash ran off his sleeves and dripped from his nose. His stocking foot hit wood with a slap because the trough had eaten one boot. The missing boot bobbed once in the luminous slurry like a dead animal refusing burial.

The crowd had gone from laughter to something meaner and quieter. People could see the injury now. They could also see that it was not enough.

Silas cradled his right wrist against his chest and tried to stand straight. He was aware of every detail with humiliating clarity: the way the mash stank sweeter close up, the way wet shirt cloth sucked at the scar under his ribs, the way a thread of pink-lit slime crawled down from his ear to his jaw. He spat on the plank. Bright green spit joined the cracks between boards.

Aunt Vero came close enough to inspect him as if he were a spoiled batch. The drifting eye had started to wander again, this time from fatigue or disgust. When she spoke, the ceremonial note was gone. Only the ward remained.

"Now you look priced proper," she said.

No one laughed.

She lifted the packet string from the boy's neck and dropped it on Silas's shoulder. The little rectangles slapped wetly against his mash-coated shirt.

"You want labels, wear them."

The boy started to object, then thought better of it.

Inez stepped forward at last. She did not come all the way to him. She came just far enough that he could see the green ink ground into the lines of her hand from the crushed packets. He noticed absurd things even now: a loose thread at the edge of her cuff, the small damp crescent where sweat had darkened the scarf knot at her neck, the fleck of cornmeal on one knuckle. She looked tired more than enraged. That, more than anything, made him understand the size of what he had done. He had taken work from people already carrying too much of it.

"You don't get to stand in this ward and act surprised," she said. "You saw what the names were for. You saw the kitchens. You saw the crossings. Then you took what wasn't offered and let it come back as a trick."

"I know."

It was the only honest answer. It was too small. It was worthless. It was true.

Her mouth tightened. "No. You know now because we're making you hear it with witnesses. Before now you just wanted the money and the room."

The line found him exactly. He did not bother denying it.

Aunt Vero tipped her chin toward the north path, the one that climbed out of Sump Ward toward Lantern Steps. "Out," she said. "Eat your supper uphill. If you come down here tomorrow, you'll go in deeper. If you come with apologies, save them till they can carry water, cut rot, or mend a lamp. Your mouth is shut to us."

A murmur of agreement traveled the planks. It was not a cheer or the blood sound a mob makes when it smells a better injury ahead. This was household judgment. It was worse because it would last.

Silas looked once at Inez because some stupid part of him still asked for a smaller sentence from her than from the others. She gave him none.

"You listened where children sleep," she said. "Don't come back asking me to understand why."

There was room in that line for nothing but distance, not hatred. Hate at least admitted appetite. This was denial of claim.

He bent for his fallen hat and almost blacked out when instinct brought his right hand down. Pain burst hot and nauseating. He swore, low and useless. The thimble-necklace woman kicked the hat toward his left foot so he would not have to stoop so far. It was the only kindness left in the place and it shamed him nearly as much as the trough had. He picked it up with his left hand, jammed it against his mash-slick thigh, and started toward the north path with the packet string stuck to his chest and one wet stocking squelching on the boards.

No one touched him on the way out. They did not need to. The crowd held open just enough space to make plain that it was not company. Behind him he heard bowls lifted again, benches dragged, somebody telling the children to keep back from the trough. The fiddler did not resume at once. Then, after ten paces, he did, tentatively, like a man testing whether a broken thing could still carry tune.

Silas reached the last plank before the slope and turned despite himself.

The feast had already begun the work of closing without him. Aunt Vero was back at the tables, barking for more pepper mash. The knot-kneed fiddler found his reel again. The boy with the missing tooth leaned over the trough with two other children, pointing at Silas's lost boot and arguing some new ridiculous name for the shining slop around it. Inez had her back to him, one hand braced on the cider cask while she spoke to a young mother holding a drowsy child. She did not look up.

The ward had made its decision and gone on feeding itself. That was the shape of the truth. Patent Row shame had always required an audience that stayed with you long enough to enjoy itself. This was different. These people knew exactly how much of him they needed to waste: a shove, a trough, a closed path, then back to supper because kitchens still wanted tending and lamps still wanted the right words. He was not important enough for grand violence. He was only dangerous enough to bar.

By the time he climbed into Lantern Steps, the mash on his clothes had started to cool and tighten. His right wrist throbbed with each heartbeat, thickening under the skin. The packet string slid down and tangled at his elbow. He tore it off with his left hand and almost flung it into the drainage cut. Instead he looked at the wet paper stuck together there, his own borrowed alphabet blurring green into gold, the ward's working names turned to trash in a fist.

He shoved the whole mess into his pocket.

From below, past the plank walks and grow-house roofs, the Feast of Mud Names rose back into full sound: fiddle scrape, crockery knock, a woman's laugh sharp as a spoon on tin, children yelling over whatever name they had found for the trough that had baptized him and spat him out. The night kept moving. Sump Ward had its feast. Silas had a ruined shirt, one boot full of mash, a wrist already failing him, and no place left in that light.
