# Chapter 14

By the third morning after the regatta, Ferrymouth glowed like a rich man's tooth.

The light in Junie's kitchen had gone pale gold in the night and stayed there, thin and flattering and useless. It made the steam above the stockpot look expensive. It made yesterday's grease on the stove lids shine like lacquer. It made the floorboards look drier than they were, which was the mean part. The boards still sweated river damp, and when Silas put his bad wrist against the prep table to lever himself up from the bench, his palm came away with flour pasted to it and a smear of yellowish slime from the lamp hook.

Junie had been up long enough for anger to settle into method. She stood over the range in her apron and underskirt, hair pinned high with a trussing skewer because she could not find the proper comb in the dark. Steam had dampened the little healed split in her eyebrow white. She kept lifting the lid off the stew, staring into it as if staring harder could make the boil come back.

"It won't hold," she said.

Silas knew she was not talking to him in the first instance. She was talking to the firebox, to the pale-gold cakes stacked in the coal cradle, to Gideon Wren somewhere up on clean Gilt Crescent with his gloves and his sermon voice and his purchased city. Even so, the sentence landed on Silas because most things did now.

He crossed to the stove. The heat coming off it was wrong. There was warmth, yes, enough to comfort the skin if all you wanted from a stove was an agreeable hand. It did not have the hard push of kitchen fuel. It did not get under the pot and commit.

Junie thumped the ladle against his knuckles when he reached for the fire door. "You'll spill it."

"I was looking."

"You can look with your eyes."

He looked. The branded fuel sat in a crusted nest behind the grate, not green as household mash should have been, not blue-white as the cooler strains sometimes burned, but a pretty waxy gold like sugar pulled too thin. The cakes blistered and collapsed quickly, leaving a lace of bright residue that hissed without doing much work. Somebody in a parlor might have admired the color under a shade. In a kitchen it meant the stock never climbed higher than a fretful burble. Fat clotted. Bones sulked. The onions took forever to surrender.

Otis sat at the table with his slate and his one good school copybook, not dressed for school. He had one suspender hanging and a dark dock stain on the knee of his trousers where it never came out, no matter how Junie scrubbed. He was tracing the loop of a capital C, tongue tucked in the gap where his front tooth used to be.

"You said I could go if the lower steps dried," he said without looking up.

"They didn't."

"Mick Harlan went."

"Mick Harlan has a father who'll carry him if he breaks his fool neck."

Otis erased too hard and tore the page. He did not cry. He only rubbed the place with his thumb as if paper might heal if you were polite. Silas watched him do it and had the ugly thought that even the child had learned not to expect repair from him before breakfast.

Junie jerked her chin at the ledger propped open by the bread bin. "Read me that line."

He moved closer. Her accounts were written in the blunt clear hand of someone who did not romanticize numbers. Yesterday's fuel. Today's fuel. Two columns, same supplier, different world.

"Seven coppers a scoop yesterday morning," he said.

"And?"

"Twelve."

"Say the rest."

There were four scoops marked before dawn and three more with question marks beside them.

"If the heat keeps dropping, you need seven before the late ferry."

"At twelve."

"I can multiply, June."

"Then do it where I can hear it."

He did. Twenty-four, thirty-six, forty-eight, sixty, seventy-two, eighty-four. The numbers made the room feel smaller than the walls. Junie took each one with a short nod as if he were naming parts cut off a carcass.

"That's fuel only," she said. "Not fish. Not onions. Not bread. Not rent. Not the note that bastard bought just so he could stick his hand up your back and move your mouth."

She said it evenly. That was worse than if she had shouted. Her voice had gone dock-flat, the voice she used when tallying spoilage after a bad weather run.

Silas leaned his hip against the counter and nearly went down because the boards under him had been slicked with condensed steam. He caught himself on the flour barrel. The barrel rocked. Junie slapped it steady with the side of her hand without even turning.

"I'll go to the yards," he said. "There has to be common mash somewhere."

"There isn't."

"Somebody's holding stock back."

"Of course somebody's holding stock back. There are women on the ridge paying parlor prices to make their supper lamps look clean. Meanwhile I've got a stove full of saint's piss and a pot of eel that won't remember it's dead unless I threaten it for another hour."

The joke would have drawn a snort from him last week. This morning it only made him stare at the stew. The eel rounds rolled slow in the weak boil, skin silvering then dimming under the gold light like coins in bad water.

A man rapped on the side door and came in before being asked, because ferry crews treated Junie's kitchen as weather, not property. Doss Mercer, who unloaded flour barges with one shoulder permanently higher than the other, ducked under the lintel and stopped short.

"Christ," he said. "Looks like somebody boiled a chandelier in here."

He had a clove tucked in one cheek for his breath. He worried it with his molars when he was tired, and today the clove had stained a wet brown crescent at the corner of his mouth.

Junie pointed the ladle at him. "If you've come to praise the look, I'll feed you outside."

"I came to eat."

"Then you'll wait."

Doss glanced at the stove, at the fuel cradle, then at Silas. He was not a subtle man, but misery had made subtle men of half the city in three days.

"Same at ours," he said. "Pitch won't stay soft at the caulking shed. Miko blistered his thumb leaning too close because the lamp looked hotter than it was. Then the tar skinned over cold as spit. Boss had us scraping it off plank by plank."

The caulking shed had gone bad with everything else, one more place where elegant light made stupid damage. The city had no other conversation left.

Junie held out a bowl without looking at Doss. "You want breakfast, you get yesterday's heel and broth from the top. Hot enough if you don't dawdle."

"Pay you tonight."

"You'll pay me because your mother would rise out of her grave and box your ears if you didn't."

Doss gave a tired grin. "That she would."

He sat, hunched over the bowl as if to trap heat with his body. Otis watched the steam thread away and vanish before it reached Doss's face.

Silas reached for his coat. Junie saw it and did not tell him not to go. That, too, was worse. If she had stopped him, there might have been room for argument. She only said, "If you come back with pretty fuel, I'm throwing it at your head."

"I know the difference."

"Do you."

He left before the answer had to be spoken aloud.

Ferrymouth usually smelled of yeast, brine, rope, old soup, bilge, and whatever the river had decided to surrender in the night. The new gold phase had laid another smell over it, faintly sweet and powdery, the odor of something trying to pass for cleaner than it was. It came off lamp housings and packet shops and the wet strips of moss brightening along the dock pilings. Richer, maybe, if you were a fool. To Silas it smelled like paste left too long in a warm jar.

The light did wicked things to depth. It gilded puddles without showing their holes. It slid across the iron on ferry ramps and made dry rust look slick and slick rust look dry. Men who had worked these planks twenty years were planting their feet like strangers.

At the bottom of Lantern Steps, a woman in a red shawl had set down a market basket and was rubbing one knee with furious little circles. Two eggs had broken in the basket and glazed the turnips. She was old enough that her braid had gone steel gray under the shawl, but she still wore a hatpin shaped like a swallow's wing, absurdly jaunty for six in the morning. Silas knew her face from somewhere, one of the women who had once heckled a soap launch and improved it.

"You all right?" he asked.

She looked up and recognized him quickly enough to make the answer interesting.

"No," she said. "Your fancy corpse-light took the edge off the step."

He crouched, because standing over an injured woman while she cursed you was too easy a version of the moment. Her knee had already darkened under her stocking. She kept pressing it with the heel of her hand, then stopping because it hurt worse.

"Let me see."

"What for? You'll name the bruise and sell it back to me?"

He almost told her she had earned that one. Instead he took the basket and set the unbroken eggs straight.

"Mrs. Caskey," he said, remembering. "Your place is two flights up by the wash rail."

"Three now that I can't trust my own damn feet."

He offered his arm. She took it after a pause long enough to state terms. Her hand was light and dry, the fingers nicked from years of needlework, and she smelled sharply of lavender water deployed against the river with no hope of victory.

"My daughter hems collars for half Patent Row," she said as they climbed. "She starts before the sun because the boys carrying bundles knock by seven. This gold rubbish makes white thread vanish against white cloth. You know what she did this morning? She stitched a gentleman's cuff shut. The man had to rip himself out of it like he was being born."

On a better morning it might have been funny. Here it sat between them without lifting.

At the landing outside her rooms, a young woman with a tape measure looped around her neck stood by the door holding a shirtfront up to the lamp and squinting until one eye nearly closed. Her hair had escaped its pins on one side only, giving her the look of somebody who had fought half a battle before breakfast and lost the wrong half. Mrs. Caskey jerked her chin toward her.

"Told you," she said. "Nera, this is the bastard."

Nera Caskey lowered the shirtfront. "I know him."

She said it without theatrical hatred. That was almost unbearable. She sounded like somebody naming weather she had not invited but still had to work under.

Silas left them there and kept climbing because there was nowhere else for the motion to go.

By the time he reached the fuel yards above the market, the queue already bent around the block. Men with scuttles. Women with wrapped baskets. A barber from Sallow Cut carrying his lamp tray like an invalid. The branded packets were stacked under an awning in neat cream paper wrappers stamped with a crown and a field of curling leaves, beautiful work, his own lettering bastardized into something cleaner and more obedient. Common mash, the rough green household kind, sat in a single half-emptied bin behind the counter. A chalk sign over it read RESERVED.

Nels Tarrow ran the yard in the mornings. He had a cough that whistled on the inhale and one clouded eye from a childhood fever, which made it seem as though he was always looking at two truths and distrusting both. Today he was sweating through his vest before breakfast, tallying orders with a carpenter's pencil tucked over one ear.

"Don't tell me you're buying retail now," he said when Silas reached the front.

"Don't tell me you've sold your spine for wrapping paper."

Nels gave a dry little bark. "Spines don't keep stock. What do you want?"

"Common mash."

"You and forty others."

"That bin says reserved."

"That's right."

"For whom?"

Nels spread both hands. The gesture displayed the burns on his thumbs, old white ovals from years of handling hot pans. "For people with standing orders and advance payment."

"My sister feeds half this dock."

"Your sister owes half this dock, depending who counts."

Silas leaned in. "There has to be more in back."

Nels lowered his voice without changing his face. "There's more in back. It's going uphill by cart because Gilt Crescent wants matching table lamps and veranda bowls. They like the gold. Makes their wives look expensive at supper. You want me to say it slower?"

Silas looked past him. Two boys were indeed loading crates marked for the ridge, careful as altar servers with the branded cakes, careless with the rough stuff. One crate of household mash had split at the corner. Dark green clumps bled onto the cobbles. A woman three places behind Silas stared at that spill like a starving dog watching gravy run into straw.

"Give me four scoops of the rough," Silas said.

"I can't."

The words still landed stiff in his mouth. Fear had a way of straightening a man's speech.

"Then give me whatever will boil a pot for longer than a hymn."

Nels slid a wrapped gold packet onto the scale. "This is what's there."

"It's trash."

"It's trash that people with cash have decided is elegant. Which makes it stock."

The woman behind Silas laughed once through her nose. No one else bothered.

He bought two packets because pride had become a luxury item. The paper wrappers felt dry and costly in his hand, as if they had no business riding in his coat pocket beside cracked chalk and old tobacco. Nels counted out the change and, after a moment, pushed one extra loose cake across the counter.

"That one's crumbled," he said. "Use it first."

Silas looked up.

"Don't thank me," Nels said. "I'm only tired of hearing your sister call me a grave robber."

On the way back down he saw the city learning the new light with its shins and ruined breakfasts. A lamplighter on a side lane held his ladder farther out than usual because the gold glow erased the rung shadows. Two coopers outside a barrel shop had carried their steaming glue pot into the street to steal heat from the sun, swearing over a seam that would not set. In a basement laundry a woman stood with both forearms plunged into suds gone yellow at the surface, blinking hard over other people's collars because the pale gold made every stain look faint and every scorch look decorative until you turned the cloth and saw what was actually there.

When he came back through Junie's door, the kitchen had divided itself by necessity. The front half stayed lit. The rear tables sat in a dim greenish murk because she had extinguished two lamps to save fuel. Customers bent over bowls in alternating colors, gold at the counter and swamp shade at the back wall, as if the room had been cut into classes with a knife.

Junie took one packet from his hand, hefted it, and closed her eyes.

"How much."

He told her.

She did not swear immediately. She set the packet down by the ledger, flattened the wrapper, and read the crown stamp as if reading the face of the man who had printed it. Then she took up the pencil. Otis watched her write.

"Four scoops before noon, maybe seven by late ferry," she said. "At this price, by Thursday I'm selling broth for the privilege of smelling fish. By Saturday the bread goes smaller. By Monday I choose between fuel and rent. That's if the stove doesn't choke first on this painted sugar."

She looked at Silas then, properly. "This is what your big word bought. Not for the ridge. For here."

He had no answer that deserved air.

Doss Mercer, already back for seconds because men who hauled for a living were built around repetition, raised his bowl a little. "She's right. My mother says the upstairs lamp makes her saints look younger and the hallway dark as a ditch. She'd rather see the ditch."

"Eat," Junie snapped, and he did.

She broke open one of the packets with the carving knife. Inside, the fuel cakes were molded in little scalloped rounds fit for display. He hated them on sight. They looked like something a confectioner would pipe for a baptism. Junie rammed two into the stove with the poker as if trying to insult them into honest labor.

"Otis, put your books away."

"I didn't finish my page."

"You'll copy receipts instead."

He gathered the slate and copybook slowly. His mouth had gone soft around the missing tooth, the way it did when he was frightened and trying not to be young about it.

"Uncle Sile," he said, "is the gold sick, or are we?"

Junie made a sound in the back of her throat that might have become a laugh in a less tired house.

Silas took the boy's slate because his own hands needed occupation. The chalk dust streaked over the yellow residue on his fingers and turned paste-gray.

"The gold's not sick," he said. "It's spoiled."

"Can you fix spoiled?"

Junie answered before he did. "Not by talking at it."

Silas set the slate down. Outside, a ferry bell knocked twice through the damp and somebody shouted for breakfast from the alley. The day was beginning whether the kitchen was ready or not.

Junie tied a fresh rag around the spoon handle where the heat ought to have been too fierce to touch barehanded and was not. "Go see your shop," she said. "Boy from Murtagh Buildings came by at dawn. Said the wall's blooming through the paper and the landlord has found religion about clean tenants."

"I paid through last month."

"Then enjoy the memory."

"Junie."

She did not soften. Not because she had stopped loving him. Because love was what made softening impossible while the pot still needed coaxing and the room was full of men who wanted feeding.

"If there's anything left worth carrying," she said, "carry it before somebody else decides it already belongs to them."

He stood there another second, listening to the weak hiss of the branded cakes. They burned fast, bright at the edges, almost eager to be consumed. The stew finally rolled into something like a boil, but the sound of it had changed. It was not the deep working thump of honest heat. It was a fretful chatter, all surface, no force.

He went.

Patent Row had always looked as though language had been pasted over itself until the street forgot what its own brick color was. Usually that pleased Silas. He liked walls carrying old lies under new ones. He liked paint ghosts. He liked the vulgar confidence of paper slapped over paper, promise on top of promise, whole fortunes trying to dry before the rain found them.

This afternoon the row looked curated.

Celadon Crown placards had gone up in the night or the dawn, cream stock with pale-gold borders and the same obedient lettering everywhere. Too much breathing room in the design. Too much confidence that the eye would come where it was told. The gold moss had answered all over the district. It glazed cornices, window putty, sign hooks, the iron scrolls over awnings. It made the whole street look richer from a distance, tidier if you did not know what you were seeing. Up close the damage showed. Paste bubbled under posters. Paste boards swelled. Shop lamps glowed pretty in broad daylight because they no longer had enough practical business to do.

A jeweler on the corner had dragged his stool outside because the new light inside his shop made the brass backs of watch cases shine but left the pin springs invisible. Across from him, a map copier worked with the shutters open so wide the sheets kept trying to take flight. A line had formed outside a packet dealer selling half portions of branded fuel to clerks' wives and boardinghouse keepers who could not afford more and could not go dark. Every face in the line held the same insulted concentration, like people being overcharged for their own blood.

Silas's shop sat halfway down the block between a monogram printer and a dealer in patent lamp mantles. Or it had sat. The place was still there. The sentence was what had gone.

The window bearing his name had been scraped from the inside, not cleanly. The word VALE remained in ghosts and scratches, a few stubborn white loops where the brush had once ridden slick and proud. Pale-gold moss threaded through the putty lines in the pane, branching into delicate fans that would have been beautiful if beauty had not currently meant failure. The door stood open. Inside, somebody was moving his things.

Silas stopped hard enough to send pain up his wrist. He did not feel the wrist first, though. He felt the empty place where outrage ought to have been and found something colder.

Mr. Brindle, the building agent for Murtagh Buildings, stood near the counter with a handkerchief held discreetly over his nose. Brindle was a soft man made to look firmer by starch. His collars were always too tight, and when he grew flustered a red bloom crept up from under them in exact finger widths. Today the bloom had reached his ears. He removed the handkerchief when he saw Silas and folded it away as if manners might yet save the scene.

"Mr. Vale," he said. "I had hoped to catch you earlier."

"By taking the hinges off while I slept?"

"There is no need."

"There's every need."

Two boys had stacked his sample boards by the back wall. One of them, a freckled apprentice from the printer's next door, would not meet his eye. The other kept staring at the gold growth on the ceiling beam as if waiting for it to drip.

Silas crossed the room. The shop smelled wrong. Under the usual blend of oil, turpentine, damp paper, dust, and lamp smoke lay the same sweet false odor from Ferrymouth, stronger here because Patent Row had chosen to lean into it. The moss had climbed the wall behind his main worktable where old paste and summer damp met. It had got into a tray of rabbit-skin glue and turned the surface into a pale film like cooled marrow. Two bundles of handbills had fused together at the edges. His white lead had warmed toward butter. The brushes left overnight in rinse water wore gold threads along the hairs as if age had arrived all at once.

He lifted one and watched the bristles separate in a limp fan. No line with a point left in it. No good for lettering. No good for anything but smearing.

"Rent is sixteen days late," Brindle said behind him. "The contamination is worsening. The owner has accepted an advance from a new tenant who can make immediate use of the frontage."

Silas turned. "I can scrape the wall."

"It is in the joists."

"I can cut it out."

"You cannot work fast enough with one hand, Mr. Vale, and the owner is not obliged to finance your recovery."

There it was, crisp and bloodless. Silas almost respected the wording. Brindle had likely practiced it on the walk over.

"Who is it?" Silas asked. "Which genius needs my door so badly?"

Brindle hesitated. A woman standing just outside the threshold answered for him.

"Me, if it matters."

She was small, maybe thirty-five, maybe older in the way city hunger sanded people down into approximation. Her mourning dress had been brushed until the black went shiny at the hips, and she wore a spray of lacquered cherries on her hat that had lost one fruit and did not apologize for it. Under one arm she carried a flat tin cash box. Under the other, a sleeping child too big to be carried easily and too tired to object. The child's sock dangled half off one heel.

"Bess Morrow," she said. "I had a notions stall under the arcade until the damp got in. Verdancy's giving depot rights to small sellers now, on percentage. I can pay three months if I hang their packets and keep the lamp trimmed. That's the whole romance of it."

She did not sound proud. She sounded winded.

Silas looked from her to the cash box to the child. There was no villain there worth the expenditure.

"Congratulations," he said, because cruelty always arrived first. "You get a room full of spoiled brushes and a wall that answers to insults."

Bess shifted the child higher. "If the wall answers to coins, I'll call it darling."

Brindle took half a step between them, not from courage but because he feared raised voices in rentable spaces.

"Mr. Vale, if you remove your personal effects today, the owner is willing to forgive the disposal fee."

"How Christian."

The freckled apprentice snorted once and then pretended to cough.

Silas went to the counter because his knees had developed a private tremor he did not care to exhibit. Under the counter sat the strongbox where he kept sign gold, imported pigments, receipts, and the few clippings that proved he had once mattered enough to be reviewed. The box remained untouched. At least Brindle had not sunk that low. Or perhaps he had and found nothing worth pocketing.

He opened it. Two small tins of color were still sound, lampblack and river green. The clipping from Damaris's sheet announcing him "Patent Row's Mouth of Miracles" had gone tacky along one edge where damp had licked it. He almost left it. Instead he folded it once and put it back because hatred was still a form of possession.

At the worktable lay the mahlstick he had used on fine jobs before the wrist sprain. He picked it up with his right hand on instinct, dropped it, cursed, then bent too fast to retrieve it and banged his shoulder on the drawer pull. The pain came specific and stupid, not tragic. Behind him, no one made the noise of pity. For that he was grateful.

"I could still paint the front," he said, mostly to the room. "I could do one last board and rent out the back."

Brindle's silence answered first. Then Bess Morrow, from the threshold:

"Not with that wall you won't."

She nodded toward the sample rack by the window. He followed the line of her chin and saw that the gold growth had reached the finished pieces. A wedding banner half rolled on the rack now glowed pale at the edges where the white cloth should have stayed white. A funeral drape sample had picked up a warm sheen that turned solemn black into something almost festive. His trade depended on a color meaning the same thing two days in a row. Here the city had taken that away as neatly as if it had cut the handles off his brushes.

He stepped closer to the window. Outside, two women paused to admire the light in the pane.

"That's the color," one said. "Not so vulgar as the pink was."

"Makes everything look dearer."

They moved on.

Silas put his forehead against the cool glass for a second. The pane vibrated faintly with street traffic. Through the gold thread in the putty he could see his own reflection half erased, hat low, shirt sticking to the spine, fingers stained from work he could not properly do. Patent Row Silas. Mr. Vale. The man with a shopfront and a window and a chair customers waited in. He had thought the essential part of his ruin happened on the regatta platform when he gave Gideon the word aloud. That had been the mouth of it. This was the hand closing.

"Take the shelf hooks if you want them," Bess said after a moment. "Fresh iron costs stupid money this month."

That line, more than Brindle's notice, nearly undid him. Not because it was kind. Because it was practical. The city had moved past his shame into salvage, and salvage did not stop to admire a fallen man.

He stripped what he could in the next hour. Tins into the chest. Chalks wrapped in old proofs. The one serviceable mahlstick under his arm. Sample alphabets, though he nearly laughed packing them, all those beautiful confident letters with nowhere to go. The good sable liners were ruined. The wall easel had swollen at the feet and would not come free of the floor without splintering. He kicked it once, then stopped because tantrums were expensive in public.

The freckled apprentice finally found enough nerve to help him carry the chest to the door.

"Sorry, Mr. Vale," the boy muttered. He had ink under one thumbnail and a tiny silver medal pinned inside his vest, visible only when the cloth shifted. Some church prize, maybe, or a dead sister's token. Something he kept for luck while moving another man's life into the street.

"Don't be," Silas said. "Learn from it."

"From what?"

Silas looked around the shop, at the gold blooming through the paper seams, at Brindle with his handkerchief, at Bess setting her cash box on the counter he had stained with vermilion three summers ago, at the child waking and immediately reaching for the bright packet display because children trusted color before consequence.

"From how fast a nice name can eat a room," he said.

The boy frowned as if he would like to understand and could not afford the time.

When the chest was outside, Silas went back once more for the signboard that had hung over the door before he rented the place, a long painted panel with his own hand on it, the lettering flourished just enough to declare talent without admitting vanity. He had painted it the week the lease began. He had stood in the street afterward and admired the line of the V until dusk.

The panel came down heavier than he remembered. The back had gone damp and soft near one end where the gold growth had crept up the mounting bracket. As he lowered it, the softened corner tore off in his hand. A flake of painted wood drifted down onto his boot like dead bark.

He stared at it.

Brindle said quietly, "Mr. Vale."

Silas laughed then, a short ugly bark with nothing amused in it. "Of course."

He set the broken sign against the wall instead of trying to save it. There were losses too late for gentleness.

By late afternoon the light in Patent Row had turned richer, the way bad ideas always did once enough money stood behind them. Gold in the upper windows. Gold at the packet stalls. Gold edging the moss in the drains. From the ridge it would have looked like prosperity traveling downhill. On the street it looked like women buying half measures, men misjudging stair edges, glue failing in workrooms, stewpots refusing the boil, and a sign painter carrying his trade home in a chest because the room that had made him visible now belonged to packaged light.

He took the key Brindle held out and laid it on the counter anyway, though the gesture was theater and both men knew it. Then he picked up the chest with his left hand at the side handle and his right forearm braced uselessly under the weight. The strain bit into the old scar under his ribs and made him walk careful, as if he had aged twenty years since breakfast.

At the corner he stopped to rest and looked back once.

Bess Morrow had already propped a pale-gold packet pyramid in the window. The sleeping child, now awake, sat on the counter sucking two fingers and watching the street with solemn, fever-bright eyes. Behind them Brindle supervised the hanging of a cream banner that read CELADON CROWN in letters so trim and civil he could have spat.

Silas did not spit. Spit washed away. This was staying.

He turned toward Ferrymouth. The chest handle cut a fresh groove into his palm. Evening traffic thickened around him, workers, buyers, errand girls, boys with news cries, all moving through the same pale gold that made the ridge look fashionable and the lower wards look tired. Somewhere behind him, somebody admired the color again. Somewhere ahead, Junie was still feeding people on fuel that cost too much and did too little. The city had chosen a pretty word, and now kitchens paid for it, stairways paid for it, workrooms paid for it, and men like Silas discovered that a mouth could lose a shop long after it lost the argument.
