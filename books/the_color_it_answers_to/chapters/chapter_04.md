# Chapter 4

The first plank walk above Sump Ward bowed under Silas's boots and gave him the ward at once: warm mud, lamp slime, old soap, and the sweet-sour breath of digesters working too hard in weather that had not yet learned mercy. The boards under him sweated through their grain. Below, the drainage cut moved like thick tea, carrying peelings, reed scraps, and a pale skim of algae that caught every color the ward gave it and made none of them cleaner.

Need, folded into Gideon's envelope in his inside pocket, had sent him looking for a useful theft. He would not have called it theft, even to himself. He would have called it a principle, a pattern, one true hinge hidden under all the neighborhood babble, something he could carry back uphill and sell to men who liked their mysteries trimmed flat and lettered in gilt. He wanted one good phrase, perhaps two. He wanted enough to make the runaway moss stop embarrassing everybody by acting like a crowd.

Aunt Vero waited at the landing with a tobacco tin open in one hand, fennel seeds shining against the metal. She crunched one between her back teeth, eyed him with the mild contempt she seemed to reserve for men who still believed dry shoes were a form of virtue, and tipped the tin toward him without generosity. Her thick white braid was looped over one shoulder. One eye drifted a little off him and back again; even her face refused to spend both its sights on Silas Vale.

"He'll kiss muck inside ten yards," she said to Inez. "Let him gulp a little ward water first. Men come in dry and puffed up; one swallow of drain slop settles them lower than prayer."

Inez had her dark hair wrapped in a work scarf the color of old olives, though the damp had already darkened it nearer black. She stood easy on the planks, weight where the nails still held, one hand on a tray strap cutting across her shoulder. She waited with no theater in her. He had seen women in Patent Row shops perform indifference like it was lace. Hers was laboring indifference. It had things to do after he left.

"If he falls," she said, "you can fish out his boots. That's the expensive part."

Silas put two fingers to the brim of an absent hat. "Nice to see my education has become a public project."

Vero snapped the tin shut. "And because hauling his long carcass back out would sour my afternoon. Funerals cost besides."

He might have answered that with something better if the air had not been so wet it felt poured. Instead he smiled the smile that had sold soap powders, eel bait, and one regrettable line of patent corset hooks, and watched it die against Inez's face without leaving a mark.

"You wanted me to look," he said to her. "I'm looking."

"No," she said. "You wanted something you could carry away clean. Looking's what happens before that."

Then she turned and started along the catwalk without checking whether he followed. Of course he followed. Vero gave him a small shove between the shoulder blades for luck or insult. In Sump Ward the two seemed to travel together.

The first glow house sat half below grade, built against brick that stayed damp even in dry weeks. Inside, the heat struck like a body. Fermentation pipes ticked along the walls. Wet burlap hung over racks. Rows of shallow trays gave off different colors, one family of moods rather than a single obedient light. A low green lived close to the floor where the cook pots sat. On the shelves above it ran a colder wash of blue-white, bright enough to find a dropped needle. In the far corner, a patch the size of a dinner plate pulsed whitish gold with an ugly little hunger that made Silas think of irons left too long on cloth.

Three women worked the room without wasting a glance on him until they had decided he was worth it. The first, a heavy-hipped woman with one shoe unlaced because her left ankle had swollen against the damp again, lifted a pot lid with a rag and leaned her face into the steam, reading there whether supper would stretch. Her wedding ring hung on a black cord over her apron instead of on her hand. "That's him?" she asked Inez, not lowering the lid yet. "Patent Row mouth."

"That's him," Inez said.

The woman looked Silas up and down, taking especial note of the yellowed paint caught in the moons of his nails. "Marta Bell," she said. "If you learn one useful thing before my beans catch, I'll call it a miracle and serve it with supper."

Silas spread a hand. "I try not to promise miracles before noon."

Marta snorted and pointed with her chin to the green bed crouched beneath her stove ledge. "Potback," she said. "In this kitchen, anyway. It sits where I leave it and minds the pot. Doesn't spit in my face. Doesn't run off. I can fetch wash, box a child's ear, come back, and the beans are still beans instead of black scrape."

She pitched the name toward the bed in the tone reserved for mules: not tenderly, but with a settled agreement. The green answered by evening out along the tray, a dull rich color under the iron, heat more felt in the shins than seen.

Silas crouched. The growth smelled faintly of pond skin and hot copper. "And if somebody called it something else?"

"Then my stove pays for whatever foolishness they chose." Marta set the lid down harder than needed. "Last week boys on the upper stairs kept hollering that pink nonsense of yours. By dusk this tray had gone rosy and jumpy. Took twice the mash to hold supper under the iron, and the stew still sat there mean as old laundry."

Inez crossed her arms. "Patent Row heard color and thought trim. Down here it means supper holds or the pot scorches."

Silas glanced toward the blue-white shelf. "So what do you call that one?"

"Threadshow," said the second woman before Inez could answer. She sat on an upturned crate with a baby pinned to one hip and a needle flashing in her right hand. Her spectacles had lost one arm and were tied in place behind her ear with black thread to match the mourning dress she had cut down twice already. She had a habit of wetting the thread between her lips before every stitch, quick and absent as prayer. "Because it lets me see black thread on black cloth, God finally being useful for once. Cuffs, hems, lice eggs if the school sends them home scratching again. Cool enough this greedy little soul can moon at it all evening and keep his skin."

The baby, offended by being included in the sentence, kicked once and got his foot tangled in her apron tape. She untwisted him without looking down.

"Nessa Quill," she said. "And don't put my name in any notebook that walks uphill."

"I wasn't writing," Silas said.

"Yet."

He looked to Inez for rescue and got none. She went to the blue shelf, lifted a tray, checked its underside, and set it back with a touch so practiced he could see the whole weight of the motion before it happened. "Threadshow is bright, not warm," she said. "Lantern Steps runs a cousin bed for school slates. Here it stays by mending baskets and cradle corners. Different feed. Different trimming. Different name, because the room asks different work of it."

"But still the same family," Silas said. "Near enough."

Inez's mouth flattened; on somebody else the line might've widened. "Near enough is what men say right before they crack a tray and ask why the room's cursing them."

Marta laughed without kindness. Nessa bit the thread off with her teeth.

Silas stood. The room's heat had taken hold under his shirt. "All right," he said. "Say I've been a fool. Not hard to say. But if you're trying to keep the whole city from pulling these beds every which way, doesn't that argue for fewer names, not more? One for heat. One for bright. One for the nasty hot bastard in the corner."

That got him his first full look from Inez since the landing. The look came stripped of contempt, plain and level, and at that moment it felt almost luxurious.

"Come on," she said.

He hated being hauled like a schoolboy and followed anyway.

They went back into the open ward where the afternoon had thickened toward evening without cooling. Wash lines crossed the narrow spaces between houses. Somebody above beat a rug against a railing with the grim concentration of a woman imagining a face in it. Children moved on the planks in bare or near-bare feet, careless from long practice. Silas had to watch where he set his boots. The boards had whole dialects in them: slick, split, sound, soft, rotten at the edge, safe if you were born to it and a lie if you were not.

Inez took him over a narrow run above the drainage cut. The planks bowed under his height. She did not look back until his sole skidded on a patch of green-black sheen and his shoulder clipped the rail.

"Center nails," she said.

"You might've mentioned that before I started negotiating with the river."

"You knew enough to bring boots. I thought you might know enough to see the nails."

She said it without triumph. Mockery was easy company. Her accuracy made him feel large and expensive in all the wrong ways.

At the bend of the walk a warning board had blistered up from the wet. The old paint had bubbled around the words until the letters looked diseased. Below the sign, a ribbon of pale blue moss ran the length of the catwalk edge, thin as knifing and bright where the wood went treacherous. A boy of maybe ten balanced on the far end, fishing bent nails out of a tin with the solemn greed of a magpie. He had one ear patched with a scrap of red flannel and the patient expression of a child used to adults talking over him while he made himself useful.

"Timo," Inez said, "what's that edge called here?"

Without looking up the boy said, "Stepblue. Near the Quinter walk it's shinshow because old Mrs. Quinter kicks every dark thing she sees."

"And why not potback?" Inez asked.

Timo looked at Silas then with the pity children reserve for obvious grown fools. "Because you don't cook on a walkway."

Silas barked a laugh before he could stop it. The boy returned to his nail tin, satisfied that instruction had been accomplished.

Inez touched the warning sign. The board sagged under her fingers. "This one should've been redone three rains ago."

"You've got the paint?"

"No."

"Brush?"

"No."

He gave her a look and got the smallest ghost of weariness in return. "So you've got a rotten sign and no paint," he said. "Very efficient."

"We've got me," she said, and pulled a clasp knife from the pocket of her skirt. "And apparently we've got you."

She handed him the knife handle first and turned back to the board. The gesture put him to work.

There were old skills in his hands that even shame could not chase out. He scraped the blistered paint back to wood while she knelt along the edge and trimmed the overreaching stepblue with short hard cuts, keeping the living strip from swallowing the board. The knife rasped. Her little pruning shears clicked. Below them the cut water knocked bottle glass against stone. It was the kind of labor that made speech optional without making company false.

He found a stub of blue chalk tucked behind the sign post and sketched the letters in before he cut deeper with the blade: KEEP TO CENTER. LOW EDGE ROTS. Somebody carrying wash with one hand and a sleeping child with the other had to catch the warning in a single glance.

When he straightened, Inez was looking at the board instead of at him. The damp had darkened the scarf at the nape of her neck. He could see the crescent burn scar inside her right wrist when she lifted the shears, a pale curve against brown skin, old hurt gone practical.

"You left room," she said.

"For what?"

"For the blue. Most sign men cram the whole board because empty space offends them."

"Empty space offends landlords. I'm less principled."

Her mouth shifted, not quite a smile. "That might be the first sensible thing you've said."

He should have answered with something easy. Instead he heard himself say, "I know how water eats paint."

"You do?"

"I make my living on walls that sweat."

That made her look at him directly again, slow and measuring. She stepped close enough to brace the board with her shoulder while he cut the last groove of the T. Her arm brushed his side once, by necessity. Even through wet cloth he felt the steadiness of her, pressure held low and firm with no wasted show. Patent Row liked women arranged for looking. This one had been arranged by use. That fact did worse things to his concentration.

When the letters were done she ran a thumb through one groove, testing depth. "It'll hold a few weeks."

"If your nail thief doesn't steal the whole post."

Timo looked up, scandalized. "I only steal bent ones."

"A moralist," Silas said.

"He sells them by weight," Inez said. "To buy sugar twists."

The boy shrugged. "My sister likes the red kind."

The boy said it like a receipt. Sugar twists for his sister, bent nails by the tin. Silas liked him at once and distrusted the liking, because his trade had taught him how quickly affection could turn into harvest.

They moved on.

The next kitchen stood open to the walk, hotter than the first and more crowded. A laundry frame leaned over one wall. Flatirons sat on a brick shelf. The light in here was the whitish gold he had noticed before, but held in smaller dishes, trimmed close and mean. It gave off a dry heat that bit the backs of his fingers when he passed too near.

A young woman was slicing stale bread into thin soldiers for a child at the table, making each piece the same length with a precision that suggested the child would reject any variation as personal insult. Her sleeves were rolled unevenly, and on the crook of one elbow she had drawn a row of tiny blue stars in ink to keep the older boy from using the skin as a slate. The older boy sat with his tongue pressed out the gap in his teeth, pushing the bread soldiers into rank instead of eating them.

"Ruthie Cale," the woman said, glancing once at Silas and then back at the bread. "If you're handing out names, send one after my husband and shove him through the door before midnight."

"Can't manage husbands," Silas said. "Too many existing claims."

"Shame. That's the only miracle I'd put real coin on."

Inez nodded toward the dishes of gold-white growth. "Tell him."

Ruthie slapped the older boy's hand away from the nearest tray without turning. She did it by sound. "Fingerbiter," she said. "Say that and even a half-wit ought to keep his hand back. It runs hot enough for irons, kettle plates, a coughing baby at two in the damned morning. Good for that. Bad for children, bad for curtains, bad for any fool man who sees white light and thinks it's tame."

The older boy scowled at Silas, plainly ranking him with every other fool man in reach. On the walk outside, a knot of children went by chanting some skipping rhyme. Most of it blew apart in laughter. The one word that stayed whole was bridelight.

The nearest dish answered ugly fast. Its close mean white went pearly at the rim. Dry heat sharpened off it. One bread soldier browned where it sat on the board.

"Don't you start," Ruthie said, already moving.

The boy lunged toward the prettier shine. Silas caught him under the armpits and hauled him back against his own legs while Inez clapped a cracked plate over the dish and packed wet mash around the lip with the heel of her hand. Heat licked across Silas's knuckles hard enough to sting. The abandoned bread slice blackened at one edge and curled.

"That's your bridelight," Ruthie said. "Sits there pretty till I turn my back, then I've got cold irons and a scorched board."

The children were already halfway down the walk, still singing. Silas smelled scorched bread and hot mineral and heard, with obscene professional ease, the sort of sentence Gideon had paid for yesterday: a household radiance, bright for every need. Men had paid him to tell worse lies.

Inez looked at Silas. "Still want your one clean word?"

"No," he said, and because the answer surprised him it came out plainer than most things he said. "Not one."

Inez's face did not soften. But the pressure in the room changed slightly, a pipe note easing after a blockage gave way.

She took him from there into the back propagation run, where the house walls sweated green and the trays sat in tiers over warm water channels. One of the upper beds had furred pearly at the seam, an ugly compromise between sewing blue and iron white. Inez planted her palm on the tray rim and jerked her chin at a cedar slat propped against the wall.

"Hold that there."

He wedged the slat where she meant. The tray shivered under his hand, warm and faintly alive, like muscle deciding whether to cramp.

"This is where pretty costs," she said. She scooped wet mash from a bucket and packed it hard along the seam. "Somebody hears a sweet word in the lane, tries it in the wrong room, and then the bed wants to be three things at once. We spend a week teaching it manners again."

"Men uphill are already sniffing after a word they can sell to every room," he said.

Her thumb drove the mash deeper. "Then don't feed them one from here."

"It's not that clean." He kept the slat steady while the tray hissed against the new packing. "I've got money spoken for before I've earned the rest of it."

"So does Marta. So does Ruthie. So does every child prying bent nails out of the rot." She finally looked up at him. "Need explains you. It doesn't wash you."

He had no joke for that either. The tray settled by degrees under her hands, the pearled seam dulling back toward usefulness. When she nodded, he eased the slat away.

At the last house they climbed to a lofted walk over a line of wash tubs and open barrels where spent mash soured for reuse. The evening was coming on. Across the ward the lights were waking in their various tempers: low cooking greens under stove ledges, sewing blues in windows, the dangerous little whites banked close where irons waited, and stranger mixed colors where somebody's household had argued with itself or the neighborhood had cross-bred too much talk into one poor tray. From up here the ward read like a page worked over in the margins, every glow a notation keyed to need.

Silas leaned on the rail and then straightened at once when it gave a wet complaint under his weight.

"You'll go through one of these days," he said.

"Not today."

"That's not a system."

"It is if you know the boards."

The answer irritated him because it was true and because it refused the kind of truth he could bottle. He looked out over the lights again. On the ridge, even from here, Patent Row shone in flatter colors, a few rich signs forcing themselves above the rest. Down here the ward glimmered by need. No one had bothered to make it graceful for distance. It only became beautiful once you understood what each ugly little patch was doing for the hands around it.

"Suppose," he said slowly, "some bastard with wall space decides to press one name hard enough to drag the whole city after it. Signs, handbills, newsboys, drunks, the lot."

"Then people get hurt before they get obedient," she said. Her hand landed flat on the rail between them, not dramatic, just braced, telling the damp wood the same thing she was telling him. "A stove starts spitting. A lamp runs too hot in a nursery. Somebody thinks one pretty word ought to do every job in the house."

He kept his eyes on the ward. "I've got hill money leaning on me to help with exactly that."

Her fingers tightened once on the damp rail. "Then you don't get to call this a visit."

"I wasn't going to."

"Good."

For a moment neither of them spoke. Below, Ruthie Cale's older boy began reciting some schoolroom numbers in a sing-song moan until somebody told him to either mean them or shut his mouth. Farther off, a ferry bell carried over the cut. The ward smelled of soap, hot metal, cabbage, and the thick vegetal sweetness of the grow houses. The mix sat heavy on his tongue and made Patent Row seem oddly bloodless, all those clean gloves and dry ledgers pretending they were not built on wet work.

He put his fingers on the repaired sign groove still ghosted in his skin. "If I said I understood now," he said, "you'd know I was lying."

"Yes."

"If I said I understood enough to know a clean answer would gut this place?"

She considered him long enough to make flattery impossible. "That'd be closer."

He looked at her wrist where the old burn scar curved pale as moonlit bone. He looked away before the look turned stupid. "Then tell me what the useful next step is."

"For whom?"

The notebook in his inside pocket seemed to gain weight. The contract folded beside it had already turned itself into rent, fuel, and the next week's food in his mind. He let the easier answers rot where they stood.

"For the moss," he said at last. "For the people cooking by it. For not handing the hill the whole city clean and easy."

Her expression cinched tighter, the look of somebody deciding he might be worth another hour's labor.

"You come back tomorrow without trying to coin anything," she said. "You listen when people answer instead of shopping their mouths. You carry trays if I tell you to carry trays. You mend boards if they're failing. You don't call a bed by a name you haven't heard used in the room where it lives. And you don't take what you hear here uphill to starch and sell."

"That's a punishing apprenticeship."

"Then don't take it."

He watched the ward lights settle deeper into themselves as dusk took the sky apart above them. Potback sat under one stove, threadshow glowed in a window, stepblue found the wet edge of a walk, and fingerbiter banked mean under an iron plate. None of them sounded elegant. That was not their failure. That was their virtue. They did not ask to be admired first.

"I'll come back," he said.

Inez nodded once, with the look of a woman taking charge of a bundle she had not ordered and might still return. "Early. Before the heat turns everything stupid."

"That includes me, I assume."

"Especially you. If you're late, I use the morning on somebody useful."

He should have reached for swagger then, some filthy little bow to leave the exchange on familiar ground. Instead he only stood there with her a moment longer, shoulder to shoulder above the ward, looking at the different lights doing their different work. The attraction he felt was not clean and not especially comfortable. It had very little to do with her mouth and a great deal to do with the fact that she could walk him through a whole city of living argument and never once confuse knowledge with performance.

When she went back along the planks, he followed her center-nails careful this time, watching where she set her feet and hating how much he liked being taught. Behind them the repaired warning board caught the first full wash of evening and held its letters plain against the damp.

Morning belonged to Sump Ward now, if he kept his word. By noon the clean answer he'd already sold would still be waiting uphill, along with the contract folded in his pocket and the rent and fuel already leaning on it. He walked on with the ward's work-words in one ear and, against his will, felt the smoother lie testing itself behind his teeth.
