# Chapter 11

The ledger stuck to Silas's forearm when he turned the page, then gave with the little kiss of paper peeling off itself. Noon heat had packed the propagation shed into a wet lung. Pipe warmth breathed through the plank walls, burlap sweated on the tray racks, and the oilskin cover had gone tacky with old lamp grease. He had never seen a book that felt so much like a tool. Its corners were blunted by years of wet hands, and one scorched patch near the spine showed where somebody had set it too close to a burner.

Inez weighed the ledger flat with two stone sinkers and bent over it in her work shirt, sleeves rolled, hair wrapped up and damp at the nape. The burn scar inside her right wrist shone pearly when the shed light hit it. She had been warmer with him before dawn, warmer and lazier and willing to laugh into his throat when he said something stupid with his face in her shoulder. By daylight she had gone back to precision, sleeves rolled and ledger pinned flat. The warmth was still there; it had tightened into the kind of trust that expected him to keep up.

"Stop reading the names first," she said.

He looked up from a column that ran mudglow, widow-step, pan-blue, drain-hush. "What else did your mother put in here worth my humiliation?"

"The part that keeps people from getting burned."

He made a face, but he obeyed. The names stood at the edge of each entry, small and practical, as if embarrassed by how much men liked them. The real body of the page was measurement, heat behavior, smoke quality, where a cutting would hold steady, which houses used it, what not to do if children were in reach. Some entries had a little hooked mark in the margin. Some had three pin dots, close together like nail holes. Beside several of those, in different hands and different years, ran the same notes in different wording: answers hard in chorus; unstable if street-sung; keep inside household speech when possible.

Silas touched the three dots with his left index finger. His right wrist still had the ache of bad weather in it, and the old trough sprain woke mean whenever he tried to grip too hard. "These are all the strains that turn when a crowd mouths at them."

"Not all. The ones we knew enough to fear."

"And you wrote them down anyway."

"We wrote them down because fear isn't useful if it dies with the person holding it."

He looked at her. She had not raised her voice. She almost never needed to. He went back to the page. Once he quit admiring the names and started reading like a man trying not to make another ruin, patterns rose out of the ledger the way old lettering rose from a wall after fresh rain. The hooked mark showed up beside half a dozen entries scattered over years. One note would say split from old M.D. stock. Another said mother line still steadier in wash cellars. Another, in a hand he guessed was Inez's mother's, said ridge buyers sniffing around, keep the better trays off the main walk.

"M.D.," he said. "That isn't market drain."

"No."

"Mother digester?"

That got him half a glance. "You're guessing like a man who used to get paid for it."

"Used to and still would, if I had less character."

"You don't have enough character to brag that way."

He laughed despite himself, then frowned again at the page. The same initial sat beside too many useful things to be an accident. It promised low smoke and even heat, took to plank shade, held brightness after splitting, and answered more than one local name without going white. One entry had a slash through the name column, as if the writer had changed her mind about what to call it and decided the use mattered more. Under that slash: from M.D. mother, fourth split, keep from men with sample jars.

Silas read it twice.

Then he turned three pages and found a later entry: fourth split lost, likely carried upslope. Another hand had added, years afterward, if the bastard comes back pretty, do not trust it.

The shed seemed to tighten around him. He could hear bubbling from the ferment pots in the back room and the soft wet nips of trimmed moss dropping into a pail somewhere behind the racks. He had spent half his adult life pretending invention began in the mouth of the man quickest to speak. The ledger made that vanity look cheap. Names had parents. Strains had grandparents. Labor sat behind every bright public surprise with its sleeves rolled and its shoes ruined.

"This isn't about one runaway patch," he said.

"No."

"This thing already had a line. A real one. It didn't crawl out of a gutter last month and ask the city to adopt it."

"No."

He tapped the note about the lost split. "And somebody walked one off."

Inez took a slow breath, not weary, not patient either. "Somebody walked something off. Or sold it. Or let a buyer look too long. The old notes don't tell stories because stories get improved when people are ashamed."

"You know what M.D. means."

She set a finger on the page, just below the line, on a grease stain that had gone almost black with age. "I know enough to know I don't know all of it. My mother wrote what she needed for work. When something belonged to grief she left it to older mouths."

"Vero."

"Yes."

He shouldered back from the table. The bench bit damp through his shirt. "Vero's been waiting for this, hasn't she."

"Vero's been waiting for men like you to learn how often they arrive late and call it discovery."

"That was elegant. Spend the whole night making a sinner honest and by noon you're carving him up with file work."

"You were not honest the whole night."

"No," he said, and the word came out rougher than he meant. "Not the whole night."

Something moved in her face then, a quick sharp recognition that told him she had heard the break in his voice. She closed the ledger, tied the strap, and slid it under her arm. "Come on. If we ask Aunt Vero before she's eaten, she'll insult us faster and talk longer. After she eats, she only insults."

He followed her out into the glare. The catwalk planks outside the shed had gone slick under the high summer damp. Down below, wash lines sagged between houses, and the drainage cut carried a skin of green where the light missed it. A woman on the far landing shouted for more lamp cloth. Two boys argued over a game and called the same patch of stair moss by two different names without stopping to notice they had done it. The moss at the stair edge gave a faint thread of blue, then settled back into workaday green.

Silas looked after it. The ledger had opened something uglier and larger than fraud. It had opened lineage.

***

Aunt Vero was in the hottest house of the row, knee-deep in trays and cursing at a burner valve with the kind of concentration some people saved for prayer. She had hitched her skirt with a strip of old ferry rope and woven a sprig of rue into the end of her thick white braid because, as she once told a room full of doubters, bad bargains liked a clean scalp. One eye drifted a little when she was tired or amused. At the moment it gave her the look of judging them from two directions at once.

"If you came to ask blessing on whatever happened in that shed last night," she said without turning, "take your damp shame outside. I am busy with work that feeds people."

Inez stepped over a trough rim and shut the door behind them. "We came about the ledger."

"That is almost better. Less vulgar."

"Not by much," Silas said.

Vero looked at him then, broad brown hands on her hips, algae stain sunk permanent into the creases. "You still talk like a brass band in a pantry."

"I can leave."

"You could. It wouldn't improve your understanding."

She went back to the valve. Silas watched her crack it loose with a strip of cloth and the heel of her palm, then listen with her head bent until the hiss evened out. She did not hurry because somebody else's urgency had entered the room. When she finally turned, she held out her hand for the ledger. Inez gave it over. Vero did not open it at once. She weighed it, thumb on the scorched patch near the spine, and for a moment the room changed around her. The rudeness stayed, but something older rose under it.

"Your mother always tied the strap too tight," she said to Inez. "As if paper could run."

"What was M.D. stock?" Inez asked.

"Straight to the bone, then." Vero snorted. "Good. Saves me the trouble of pretending anybody here came for the weather."

She flipped through the pages with a speed that made clear she had seen the hands before, maybe even corrected them. When she reached the notes Silas and Inez had been tracing, she stopped. Her drifting eye settled. "You found the lost split entry."

"What does M.D. mean?" Silas asked.

Vero looked at him over the top of the ledger. "You hear one set of initials and already your mouth wants a proper noun. That is how men get robbed by their own habits."

"I can survive a lesson if it gets to the point."

"Can you."

She shut the book and jerked her chin toward the back room. "Bring that pail first. The one with the spoiled cloth. Since you want history, you can carry some stink with it."

Silas carried the pail. The spoiled cloth in it had gone sour with trimmed runoff and old mash. It smelled like hot coins left in ditch water. He hauled it into the back room, where the heat thickened and the walls were lined with older racks, older jars, older pieces of apparatus too useful or too broken to throw away. Vero followed with the ledger under one arm. Inez came last and set the bar across the door without being told.

That made Silas look at her. "We locking out saints or speculators?"

"Whoever reaches first," Vero said.

She crouched by the lowest rack and pulled out a shallow tray wrapped in damp burlap. The tray itself had once been decent tin. Time had hit it with dents, patches of solder, and a wire handle twisted from three different repairs. Under the burlap lay a thick mat of moss darker than the runaway city's patches, almost bottle green at first glance, until the room heat shifted and a hidden seam of color breathed under it: blue in the ribs, a blush of old rose at the tips, one pale gold thread where a split had recently healed.

Silas's breath hitched hard enough to sting. His hand hovered over the tray, close enough to feel the trapped heat, and would not come down. Then his knees hit the wet boards before he knew he had bent. Cold soaked through at once. The thing was not magical in any storybook way. It had the face of a family member you only noticed after years away. He saw the vein pattern along the frond edges, the little silvering at the root knots, and the way the color did not sit flat but seemed to wait for company. He had painted the runaway in six streets and called it novelty. Here was its elder laid out in tin, alive and unimpressed.

"Mother tray," Inez said quietly.

Vero nodded. "The one that lasted."

"Christ," Silas said.

"No," Vero said. "Women."

The word landed like a verdict. It stripped the room of its last joking breath.

She set the ledger beside the tray and tapped two entries with a blunt fingernail. "M.D. was Mothers' Drain. Not official. Nothing written on a city map. Just the cut behind the wash houses where the digester runoff pooled after the old flood year. Fuel came thin that summer. Bought stock turned mean, smoked children black in the nose, burned too hot in pans and too weak in lamps. Men on the ridge kept saying supply would settle if we paid proper and waited proper. Meanwhile supper still wanted making."

Her mouth tightened. For one beat, when she said the next part, her English went almost formal. "So the women did not wait."

Then the roughness came back in. "We skimmed the scum from the cut, the good luminous strings that held after dawn. We fed half the city in our heads while we bred it. Tula Mays could tell heat from smell before a tray even blushed. Nena Ortiz washed her feet three times a day and still cursed like a carter. Your mother, God keep her troublesome, wrote everything down because she did not trust memory once men started asking questions."

Inez looked at the tray instead of Vero. "How many of you?"

"Enough to make a strain and not enough to own one. That is the point you should keep in your teeth." Vero scratched at the burlap edge. "Six steady hands. More on flood nights. We bred for kitchens first, stairs second, sickrooms third. Pretty was for people with money and mirrors. Useful was for us."

Silas touched the tin rim, careful not to touch the moss. It was warm, warmer than the room. "When'd the buyers come in?"

Vero barked a laugh. "They were there before the roots thickened. Buyers are flies. You don't ask when they arrive. You ask what they've already landed on." She leaned back against the rack. "At first they came with compliments. They said women in the low wards had a genius for domestic refinement. They said somebody ought to protect such promising work from spoilage. One patent man even brought sugared almonds in a box and spoke to us as if he was lowering himself into charity. I ate the almonds. Then I told him to take his polished shoes out of my mud."

Despite himself, Silas smiled. It died fast.

"We hid the better trays," Vero went on. "We split the names house by house so nobody outside the ward could hear one word and carry the whole use home. We kept the hot-burn notes in mouths, not on paper. We were not saints. We were tired and suspicious and already learning. But one split got away."

Inez lifted her head. "Taken?"

Vero's drifting eye went vague with memory. "Taken, traded, lost, sold. Pick the version that keeps your sleep. A tray does not need a villain with a silk hat. Sometimes it only needs a cousin who wants rent money. Sometimes a husband with a bottle in his eye. Sometimes a boy who thinks one clipping from the edge is not the mother herself. The split went upslope. A year later pretty little jars started showing up in refined windows. Less heat, more shine. Always the way."

Silas felt old shame move through him and find a newer place to settle. "And the runaway line."

Vero reached down and pinched a dead brown scrap from the mother tray, rolled it between finger and thumb, and let it fall. "Descended. Not identical. Not pure. Nothing alive stays pure in this city unless it's already dead. But yes. Same mother. Same old chorus temper in the bones. Same appetite for public mouths. They bred some steadiness out, some prettiness in, then commerce did what commerce does. It copied badly, cheapened fast, spilled often. Now the bastard is back on the walls, and everybody up-ridge is acting like the river invented it for their benefit."

"Does Gideon know?" Inez asked.

"Know this exact history? Probably not. Know he's selling stolen labor with a new ribbon on it? Men like him always know that much. They think if enough years pass between theft and sale, the taking goes clean."

Silas stared at the tray until the blue in its ribs throbbed faintly with the sound of voices in the next room. It was all there if a man stopped admiring his own cleverness long enough to look. The useful names had been split by house. The missing tray sat in the notes like a tooth knocked out. The market had prettied the strain, and public mouths had made it unstable. He felt almost insulted by how much of the city's suffering had been legible to anyone with patience.

"If Damaris prints this," he said, "if people see the tray, hear the history, maybe it cuts the ground out from under Gideon before the regatta."

Vero gave him such flat contempt he wished she had thrown something. "And then what. The city picks one holy widow to stand up in a clean collar and say this is mine? A judge asks which woman first touched the root and therefore owns the lamp in every kitchen? Foolishness. We didn't make it alone then, and we won't make it alone now."

"He can't own it either."

"Of course he can't. But markets don't need truth. They need a face and a word and enough fools repeating both." She pushed the tray toward him half an inch. "Don't use my dead against him as if I handed you a better brand."

Silas looked down at the patched tin and kept his mouth shut. The tray sat between them warm as an accusation. He had spent months turning other people's mouths into his trade. The instinct to fix this by finding the rightful author was only the cleaner version of the same hunger.

Inez put two fingers on the old tin. "Then why keep the tray."

Vero's expression changed again, a small dark shift. "Because work deserves witnesses. Because your mother is gone and Tula is gone and Nena is gone, and if every useful thing gets sold twice and remembered never, then the city belongs entirely to men with printers." She rubbed at the ferry rope around her waist. "And because it still works. We use splittings off the edge when a bed turns mean. History that does not light a stair is just vanity with dust on it."

The room hummed with heat. Outside, a child laughed, then yelped when someone shushed him. Silas looked from Vero to Inez and back to the tray. He had come in wanting proof. What sat before him was worse and better than proof. It was obligation that had outlived witness.

Vero jerked her chin at him. "Don't start apologizing now. I've heard enough men make sorrow sound like performance."

"Wasn't going to."

"Good. Save your mouth for when it can carry something besides itself."

She rewrapped the tray with more tenderness than she had shown either of them, though she did it roughly enough to preserve her reputation. When she rose, her knees cracked loud as snapped twigs. "Now get out. I want broth before the valve acts cursed again. And if you're taking this history anywhere near the ridge, go filthy. Let them smell where it came from."

***

Damaris's note found him on the walk back from Sump Ward, folded into a printer's proof and tucked beneath the strap of his good shoulder. He did not feel the newsboy do it. One minute he was crossing the narrow plank above the drainage cut, thinking about old theft and warm tin; the next there was paper against his neck and a boy already halfway down the alley yelling somebody else's headline.

The note was in Damaris's quick black hand.

If you want to see how Mr. Wren means to shove one word down the city's throat, meet me at the lower gate of Gilt Crescent at dusk. Comb your hair. They search for class first.

He laughed once when he read that, then folded the proof into his pocket. By the time the sun went red through the river haze he had washed, failed to make his shirt respectable, and borrowed a collar from Junie's best shelf that hated him on sight. The ridge climb put sweat under it anyway.

Damaris waited in the lee of a carriage arch where rich people could pretend they were outdoors without being touched by the street. She wore widow's black gone shiny at the elbows, and one glove had been mended along the thumb with blue thread so visible it looked defiant. Ink still lived in the creases of her fingers. When she smiled, the chipped front tooth flashed like a mean little lamp.

"You look like debt trying to pass for linen," she said.

"And you look like a funeral that learned shorthand."

"Fair. Come on."

She did not offer him an explanation until they were moving. That was Damaris at her best and worst. She treated secrets like headline type, worth more if held one breath longer than decency allowed.

"How'd you get this invite?" he asked.

"By being useful before the man remembered to despise me." She kept her pace easy, not quick, because quick meant guilt in neighborhoods built on watching. "He wants press after the launch, not before. He wants society women now, dry-goods men, ferry concessionaires, choir leaders, the kind of buyer who likes a product better if it arrives with music and a napkin. But one steward still owes me because I kept his sister's divorce off page three."

"You're a public service."

"At times."

The lower gate of Gideon's preview opened into a boathouse so polished it almost denied the river. The place sat on a cut off the main channel, under a glass roof beaded with evening condensation. Water held black and still in the basin beneath, thick enough to reflect the gas globes and the lantern boats moored in a bright row. Flower arrangements tried hard to conquer the algae smell and did not quite manage it. Under the sweetness sat mud, ferment, hot lamp metal, the real city refusing eviction.

Damaris passed her card to a steward with side-whiskers damp from the heat. "My copy clerk," she said, touching Silas's sleeve as if he belonged to her entirely. "He notices where phrasing goes wrong."

The steward barely looked at him. Class, as Damaris had promised, was the first search and often the only one. A man in a collar and cleanish cuffs could walk through more gates than a woman who had built the thing he was admiring.

They climbed a narrow service stair to the catwalk running above the basin. From there Silas could see the whole arrangement at once, which was exactly when he understood what Damaris had dragged him here to suffer.

Nothing about the arrangement below belonged to river pleasure. Gideon had laid out an occupation in ribbons and copy.

Six sample skiffs floated below, each fitted with lantern cages twined in pale gold moss and each carrying the same gilt nameboard at the prow: CELADON CROWN in letters slim enough to flatter wealth and broad enough to read from a ferry. Behind the boats, printers' boys stacked bundles of handbills tied by route. One stack was marked Ferry South in grease pencil. Another was marked Bridge stalls. A third was marked School allotment. On a long side table, paper sleeves held damp little splittings in sawdust, each one tagged with the same printed card and a line of usage instructions so simple they insulted the city: For elegant domestic light and refined evening display.

Silas stared at that line until disgust turned almost admiring. It was clean theft. It stripped out heat, labor, warnings, every practical distinction that had kept people safe, and replaced them with aspiration.

"Wait," Damaris murmured.

A small hired chorus took position at the far end of the dock, scrubbed into summer white and borrowed respectability. Girls in summer white stood beside two boys whose collars sat wrong because they were river children hired up for the afternoon. A music man tapped the stand. They ran the refrain once, then again. The melody was simple enough for drunks and children, which was to say dangerous. By the third repeat Silas had it against his will.

Celadon Crown, carry it home.
Celadon Crown, let the whole house glow.

"Christ," he muttered.

"Yes," Damaris said. "A hymn for shoppers."

Below them buyers drifted between the skiffs with the pleased attention of people inspecting a future difficulty for somebody poorer. A dry-goods matron in pearl earbobs held one of the free cuttings up against her sleeve to judge the color. Two ferry men bent over a route map tacked to cork and argued in undertones about where the launch boats could catch the densest evening traffic. Nobody here was stupid. That was the filth of it. They understood perfectly that a word caught better when it came home in more than one form.

Gideon Wren stepped onto the dock with his gloves in one hand and the room quieted around him as if it had rehearsed that too. He was as neat as ever, silver at the temples, suit untouched by the climb or the damp. He did not raise his voice. He never needed to. Control did the carrying for him.

"My friends," he said, "the error of the earlier launch was not ambition. It was insufficiency. One name offered once is a suggestion. One name encountered from morning to night becomes habit."

He paced once along the dock. Gaslight touched the edge of his cheek and left the rest deliberate.

"People do not adopt by argument. They adopt by sequence. They hear a phrase at breakfast, they see it on the bridge at noon, they sing it without thinking by evening, and then they carry the living thing home in their own hands. After that, the market merely records what memory has already accepted."

Silas could have admired the construction if it were not aimed like a knife at half the city. Signs carried it. Songs carried it. Hand-to-hand cuttings, ferry routes, school allotments, house displays: Gideon had bought or hired or seduced every channel that strengthened a chorus strain into orderly line.

One of the ferry men below asked about distribution to the lower wards. Gideon answered without a pause. "Especially the lower wards. A habit that flatters only the rich is fashion. A habit that enters the stair districts and the kitchens is ownership."

The gas globes hissed overhead, and the fresh printer's ink kept climbing the catwalk, metallic and hot. Men below nodded at ownership as calmly as if Gideon had said weather.

Damaris had been whispering smart little funeral lines all the way in. Now she shut her mouth. Silas glanced at her. Her eyes were fixed on the table of cuttings, and the theatrical amusement had drained off her face. One gloved hand lay flat on the rail. She only got that still when the copy stopped being about other people's trouble and started walking on her side of the street.

"You knew about the song," he said.

"I knew there was a song. I didn't know he was feeding splittings into it."

Below, the chorus took up the refrain again while a steward placed sample namecards into buyers' hands and another uncapped a pot of printer's ink so fresh the metallic tang climbed all the way to the catwalk. On the far wall hung full-size posters already pasted to temporary boards for inspection, pale gold field, elegant lettering, a skiff all shine and no mud. In the corner, a schoolmistress examined little folded sheets printed with the refrain and the brand name in large type for copying practice.

Silas felt the collar sawing at his neck. He thought of Otis sounding out branded words at Junie's table. He thought of Vero's mother tray breathing in dark tin while this bright clean theft rehearsed itself under glass. Gideon was not planning an event. He was planning a week in which the city would not get a free mouth.

"How many papers?" Silas asked.

Damaris swallowed, then found her voice again. "Three friendly sheets for sure. Maybe four if he buys the morning boys. I heard two street song men downstairs comparing tempo. And those ferry allocations mean he's paying for the route callers too."

"Bridge stalls, school copy, free cuttings, songs." Silas looked over the rail at the route bundles. "Posters by every choke point worth the wall rent. He wants the word to hit a person before they know they've chosen it."

"Yes."

He looked down again at Gideon, who was smiling now in that composed devotional way he wore when speaking of markets as if they were weather sent by God to reward tidy men. Buyers laughed lightly at something he said about tasteful domestic illumination. A steward set one lantern boat adrift across the basin. The moss in its cages held a pale, disciplined gold, not because it preferred that color but because enough money had already started asking it to.

Damaris touched his sleeve. "You've seen it. Don't get greedy and stay for more."

"Greedy."

"Men like you and me always think one more look will turn into a weapon. Mostly it turns into being noticed."

She was right. He hated that she was right. They slipped back down the service stair while the chorus kept polishing the refrain. By the time they reached the gate, the tune had lodged under his ribs like grit. Damaris stopped under the carriage arch and pulled off the blue-stitched glove finger by finger.

"I can print some of this," she said. "Not all before he launches. He has bought too much silence too neatly. But I can make people laugh at the refinement of it."

Silas thought of the mother tray, of Vero saying proof was not a better brand. "Laughter won't stop the catch."

"No." Damaris looked toward the dark falling over the lower wards. "But it can rough the surface."

He studied her a moment. "Why show me."

Her chipped tooth worried at her lower lip before she answered. "Because I started this season treating your mess like copy, and now the copy is walking into kitchens. That makes it my business whether I enjoy the assignment or not."

Redemption would have asked for apology and applause both, and Damaris was too honest for either. She was only admitting the story had reached her own door, which was better.

"All right," he said.

"If you get us both hanged in print, at least spell my name right."

Then she was gone into the ridge traffic, widow black swallowing into carriage shadow. Silas stood another second under the hot stone arch, listening to the song spill faintly from the boathouse and thinking how cheaply a city could be taught to mistake repetition for truth.

***

Inez was waiting for him on the plank walk behind the wash houses, where the ward thinned into dark and the old drainage cut took over the speaking. The air down there smelled of wet rope, soap gone soft in its dish, and the green rot that never quite lost to usefulness. Somebody two houses over was frying onions. Farther off a ferry bell struck and let the sound travel through the boards.

She had her arms folded, braced against mosquitoes and news alike. A lamp tray sat beside her boot on the rail, hooded against the damp. When he stepped onto the walk it tipped a little under his weight, and the water below made a low sucking sound against the pilings.

"You were gone long enough," she said.

"The rich schedule even their theft."

"You saw it."

He told her, though he did not keep it in order because order made it sound smaller than it felt. He gave her the song first, because that was the filthiest part. Then the school copy sheets. Then the ferry allocations, the free splittings, the posters waiting on their boards, the buyers nodding over routes as if discussing weather. He gave her Gideon's line about habit, and when he repeated the word ownership he heard it come out of his own mouth like a bad taste.

Inez listened with her head a little bowed. In the half light he could make out the set of her scarf and the shine on the burn scar at her wrist where she held the rail. She did not interrupt until he got to the cuttings.

"Free?" she said.

"Tagged and damp and ready for anybody who wants to feel rich in a kitchen too hot to breathe in."

"So he'll put the word in their mouths and the thing in their hands together."

"Yes."

The drainage cut gurgled under the planks. A window opened somewhere above them and a woman dumped gray water into the night with a muttered curse at whoever had left soap rind in the pail. Silas thought again of the service catwalk, of rich people admiring a city as if it were a decorative appetite. He wanted very much to hit something expensive.

"Vero was right," he said. "He doesn't need the truth. He needs sequence."

"I know what he needs." Inez looked at him then. "What do you think we do."

He had been carrying one answer all the way down from Gilt Crescent and knew, even before he spoke it, that it was the old poisoned answer in a cleaner coat.

"Pike prints the history. We show the tray. We say where the line came from before he turns it into a parade."

Inez did not move. That was worse than anger. "And by breakfast every buyer on the ridge is asking which widow gets to sign the cuttings."

The answer sagged in his own mouth before she finished it. He looked down over the rail. Along the cut wall a strip of moss caught somebody's laughter from upriver and shifted blue at one edge, green at the other. The change was slight, but it showed how fast the stuff answered company.

"If we prove the mother line," he said, "the market turns it into deed paper."

"Into licenses and ration lines," she said. "One blessed tray, one clean collar, the rest of the ward waiting permission to cook."

He rubbed at the ache in his wrist and let the old hunt for the rightful owner die where it stood. The pain gave one mean pulse and settled. "Then use. Kitchen names in kitchens. Stair names on stairs. More than one useful word alive at once. He can't own a thing that won't stay singular."

Inez uncrossed her arms. The walk gave a little under both of them when she shifted closer to the rail. "Better. No sainted mother board. No official widow. The names stay tied to what they do and to the hands that keep them working."

"Pike can print the buy, then," he said. "The song sheets, the ferry routes, the school copy. Make people see he's paying to stuff one word down every throat."

"Yes. And Vero keeps the tray buried and sends word through the wash houses tonight. No showing the good splits to stewards. No answering a printed card with silence. The ward answers with the names that cook, heal, and hold stairs."

He looked at her in the half light. "Tomorrow we start asking who still remembers which names hold."

This time she nodded once. "Now you're talking like a person who has finally met a kitchen."

He laughed, low and tired. "That wasn't praise."

"No."

"Thought so."

He looked at her profile in the dark, the straight practical line of it, the mouth that had bitten his shoulder before dawn and was now set on strategy with the same seriousness. Whatever had opened between them in the shed had not thinned under the weight of the day. It had changed shape. That felt truer and more dangerous than comfort.

For a little while they stood without speaking. The night moved around them in layers. Ferry chains knocked. A mosquito whined at his ear and he slapped it too late. Somewhere up on Lantern Steps a man called for more wick. Somewhere else a woman answered with a different moss name than the one Silas had heard in the boathouse song, then another voice corrected her for use, not for elegance. The growth along the cut wall breathed at the edges of color, refusing to settle into a single obedient glow.

Silas thought of Gideon's basin under glass, every skiff arranged, every route bundle tied, every poster already dried to the board. He thought of Vero's patched tin, warm through his fingertips. One room tried to make a city behave. The other had kept a city alive without ever pretending it belonged to one hand. For the first time since this whole mess had turned his mouth into civic weather, he could see the shape of the fight clearly: one market mouth trying to swallow every useful one.

"We'll need more mouths than ours," he said.

"We've got them," she said. "We only need them saying what works before his cards reach supper."

"That'll be noisy."

"Good."

The word settled between them with more force than anything louder could have managed.

Below the walk the drainage cut carried old runoff south toward the river. The water took scraps of light from every window it passed and broke them up on the surface. One house threw green onto it. Another laid blue across it. Farther down, some child had insisted on a name at supper and left a bruised pink thread in the current. The colors never held for long. The water kept breaking them apart.

Inez set her hand on the rail beside his, a plain offer of shared balance. Shared weight was enough. "Then that's the work," she said. "Keeping it in use so nobody gets to own it alone."

He looked at their hands on the wet wood, her scar silver in the low light, his stained fingers, the city breathing around them through a hundred uncoordinated mouths. Under the deeper theft, under the heat and shame and coming spectacle, purpose finally took a shape he did not need to sell.

Tonight the word would go house to house. By tomorrow it would need Junie's tables.
