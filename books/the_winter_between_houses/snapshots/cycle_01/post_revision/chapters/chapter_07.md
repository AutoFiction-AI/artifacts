# Chapter 7

The knock came before full light, three hard raps that did not wait for courtesy. Noreen had just shoved the last borrowed tin behind Peter's account books and gone to scrape frost from the inside of the pantry window with the back of a teaspoon. She had the spoon in one hand and a damp tea towel in the other. The towel had frozen stiff where it hung over her shoulder. Peter was at the table behind her, drawing columns on the back of an old gas bill and adding up figures that looked cleaner than their life.

He did not look up. "Answer it."

He was still in shirtsleeves and waistcoat, spectacles low on his nose, the sort of neat that made her feel as if she had left fingerprints on something she did not own. He had shaved already. He always shaved before noon on weekdays even when the buses were off and half the town was walking on ice.

Noreen set the spoon down and flexed her fingers, the knuckles burning now that she had stopped moving. On the front step stood a young council clerk she had seen once at the clinic queue, and behind him a woman in a maroon coat with a clipboard tucked into the crook of her arm. The woman's lipstick had cracked into pale lines, as if she'd painted it in a moving lorry.

"Mrs Walsh?" the woman said. "Edith Jeffries, emergency office. We've an accommodation order for this address."

The clerk held out the paper like it was hot. A red stamp from Calderbank Borough Council covered the top half under the heading Emergency Billeting and Utility Support. The ink had run where melted snow had touched it and dried again.

"No," Peter said from the hallway behind Noreen, close enough that she felt his breath move her hair. "There is a mistake. My wife is expecting."

Miss Jeffries did not blink. "I am aware. We are placing electricity board personnel nearest failure corridors. Mr Arthur Hale, linesman, is assigned to this row. You have a spare room and a functioning grate."

"Functioning is generous," Peter said.

"You may file a complaint when roads open and offices resume full hours." She tapped the form with her pencil. "Today, this is not optional."

A man had been waiting by the gate, cap in his hands. He stepped in when Jeffries moved aside. He was lean enough that his coat seemed to hang from his shoulders rather than fit them, with a yellow-striped scarf wound twice around his throat, knitted too short to tuck in properly. One end kept slipping loose. Every few breaths he rubbed that loose end smooth with his thumb. His knuckles were raw and split white at the joints.

"Arthur Hale," he said. "Sorry to land on you."

Noreen recognized him then: the same board man from the Harrows' Christmas table, the one Tom had called Arthur Pike all evening through ale-thick politeness, though the council form in her hand said Hale in blunt block capitals.

His voice was plain Yorkshire with the edges worn off from years of talking over wind. He held a canvas tool bag and a small battered suitcase with one buckle mended in green thread. The thread was too bright for the case and too careful to be accidental.

Peter did not move to let him through. "How long?"

"Until restoration phase eases," Miss Jeffries said. "Could be days. Could be longer. You know what this weather's done."

She turned to Noreen, not Peter. "If you need paraffin priority because of pregnancy, report through your district midwife. She's listed on the back." She lowered her voice by half an inch. "Keep your hallway clear. They call out men at all hours."

Noreen took the paper because someone had to. Her fingers left damp marks at the corners. She knew the exact moment she stopped hoping this was negotiable. It was when Miss Jeffries signed the duplicate copy with a blunt pencil and tore it out for them with the calm of a woman who had done this door after door and heard every version of no.

Peter shifted his weight and gave Arthur a smile that looked as if it had been measured with a ruler. "Mr Hale, of course. Public need takes priority. Come in. Mind the runner."

Arthur wiped his boots on the mat until the mat gave up taking dirt. "I'd rather leave the boots by the door if that's all right."

"You'll catch your death," Noreen said before Peter could answer.

Arthur's mouth twitched. "I've kept this lot attached through worse than wet socks."

He stepped inside in thick grey socks, steam lifting off his trousers as heat reached them. The hallway smelled suddenly of outside things: diesel, sleet, iron. He kept his bag close to his leg, as if it might bolt.

Miss Jeffries gave Noreen a final nod. "Spare room must be available by six. If he is not housed, we assign enforcement." She looked over Noreen's shoulder at Peter and did not soften. "Do not test that."

When they were gone, the house sounded bigger for a few seconds. Then Peter shut the door very carefully and said, "Get the room sorted."

Noreen did not move.

"We use that room," she said. "The linens are there. The baby bundle is there."

"Then move them."

Arthur stood in the hall holding his cap with both hands, eyes on the umbrella stand.

"I can wait outside while you clear it," he said. "Or in the yard. I'm no trouble."

"You'll do neither," Peter said. "Mrs Walsh has this in hand."

He turned to Noreen, that same smooth smile still on his face because there was now an audience. "You have this in hand."

The words sat on her skin like cold coin.

She carried the billeting form into the kitchen and laid it under the sugar tin to stop her own hand from tearing it. The spare room held two years of things she had not had the heart to throw out. It held her mother's rosary beads in a cracked blue box, a stack of mended infant vests from her sister in Hull, a jar of buttons sorted by color in old jam glasses, Peter's old account books tied with string, and the blanket she had started for this baby and abandoned halfway when the bleeding started in week three of the freeze and she was afraid to finish anything that could be folded around a body.

Arthur set his suitcase down by the stairs but did not follow. "Tell me where not to stand," he said.

Noreen looked at him then. Up close his eyelashes were pale with melted frost. There was a white crescent scar behind his left ear where skin had once split and healed badly. He was trying so hard not to be in their way that it made him clumsy.

"Just don't touch the top shelf," she said. "That's mine."

"I wouldn't dream of it."

Peter had gone back to the kitchen and his sums. Noreen could hear his pencil crossing and recrossing a number until the paper thinned. Every time she carried a box past, he looked up and counted her trips rather than the boxes.

By noon she had moved the baby things into the corner of her own bedroom, stacking them behind the wardrobe where Peter would not look unless he needed a reason. The spare room was small, with a bed narrow enough to make any grown man feel apologetic. The wallpaper had cabbage roses faded to bruised pink. She shook out the quilt, beat dust from it in the back yard, and watched the powder freeze mid-air before it reached the ground.

Arthur came to the doorframe and waited for her nod before stepping in. "Need a hand with the grate?"

"I'll do it."

"You are doing it," he said, not correcting her, just stating. "I can black the bars while you're elsewhere."

Noreen moved aside. He knelt, took a rag from his pocket, and polished the iron with quick practiced circles. He did not ask where to put his shirt, where to keep his tools, where to hang anything. When she brought him a chipped washbasin and a mug, he thanked her as if she had given him a room at a hotel.

At one point he took the yellow scarf off and folded it into a square, precise as a handkerchief, then tucked it under his pillow. He did not drape it over the chair or fling it with his other damp things. He kept it under the pillow, where no one would spill tea on it.

Peter appeared in the doorway with his ledger voice on. "We keep breakfast at seven, supper at six-thirty. No smoking upstairs. Bathroom use before ten if possible to conserve coal."

Arthur stood. "Understood."

"If there is night call, do not wake my wife unless absolutely necessary. She needs regular rest."

Noreen laughed, thin and wrong in her own ears. Peter glanced at her once and she felt the warning in it.

Arthur nodded anyway. "I'll step lightly."

Peter turned to Noreen. "Tea."

Arthur said, "No need for fuss."

"It's no fuss," Peter said. "My wife is efficient."

Noreen went to boil the kettle with her jaw set so hard she felt the hinge grind. She could hear the men in the spare room discussing fuse loads and line collapse out near Stainforth Road, and she could hear, through the shared wall, Elsie coughing in her own kitchen and opening the coal scuttle. Sound traveled like gossip on Barrow Lane; it did not ask permission.

She wanted to knock three times on the back wall in the old pattern she and Elsie had started using during the false labor night, one for safe, two for come if you can, three for now. She did not knock. Arthur's bag was three feet from the wall and Peter was in listening distance.

At half two Elsie came by the front with an empty flour tin under her arm, cheeks chapped red from wind. She stopped dead when she saw Arthur at the hall table re-soldering the broken clasp on his own torch with a spirit lamp no bigger than an eggcup.

"Oh," Elsie said. "You've company."

Noreen saw her take in everything in a blink: the extra cup, Peter's posture, the billeting paper still under the sugar tin where the red stamp showed like a wound. Elsie's hand tightened on the tin handle.

"Council order," Noreen said. "Mr Hale's in the spare room."

Arthur stood so quickly he bumped his knee on the table. "Afternoon. Arthur Hale." He wiped his hand on his trouser seam before offering it. "Sorry if I'm in your way."

Elsie shook it. "Elsie Harrow. Next door."

"Your wall's warmer than ours," Arthur said, then gave a small embarrassed laugh. "Sorry. First thing I notice in a house is where the heat leaks. Bad habit."

"Could be worse habits," Elsie said.

Peter came in from the scullery, eyes going from Elsie to the flour tin to Noreen. "Mrs Harrow. If this is about another cup of sugar, we are all on strict account this week."

Elsie lifted the tin. "It's your own flour from Tuesday. I'm returning it."

"How conscientious."

Noreen stepped in before the silence thickened. "I'll put the kettle back on."

Elsie's glance flicked to her, quick and bright with warning that this was not the place and not the moment.

When Noreen came back with tea, Elsie had moved to the hallway and was discussing paraffin queues with Arthur, both of them careful, ordinary, as if they had always done this. Peter sat at the table with his fingertips pressed together and listened to each sentence as if it might be a bill arriving unpaid.

That evening, when Elsie left, she did not slip through the yard as she usually did. She buttoned her coat in full view of the front room window and walked home by the pavement, even though ice made it slower.

Noreen watched until her shape disappeared behind the hedge and understood, with a cold that had nothing to do with weather, that this was how it would be now.

She and Elsie would still exchange what needed exchanging, but never in the old easy line from scullery to scullery, never with backs turned, never without counting who had eyes.

Arthur unpacked after supper. He laid out one shirt, two wool jumpers, an oilskin rolled tight, spare socks darned at heel with mismatched thread, and a paperback detective novel swollen from damp. He set a framed photograph on the bedside table: a woman in a nurse's cape, smiling into sun that had no place in Calderbank. Then he noticed Noreen at the doorway and turned the frame face down without fuss, as if he did it every day.

"Sorry," he said quietly. "Didn't mean to make it personal."

"It's your room," she said, then corrected herself because it was not, because that was the point. "For now."

He gave her a look she could not read and said, "For now's what most of us get."

She lay awake that night listening to a stranger breathe on the other side of the landing and to Peter breathe beside her in clipped, controlled counts, in for four, out for four, as if even sleep had to pass inspection.

She did not knock on the wall.

* * *

By the third day Arthur's boots had left two half-moons of dried mud near the door no matter how often he cleaned them, and Peter had started talking as if mud were a moral category.

"It is not difficult," he said at breakfast, buttering toast in exact edges, "to keep common areas orderly."

Arthur looked at the floor, then at Noreen with apology already there. "I'll scrub it before I go out."

"No, you will eat while it's hot," Noreen said.

Peter folded his napkin once. "Noreen, do not manage the guest by contradiction."

Arthur opened his mouth, then closed it. He chewed in silence.

Noreen passed him the marmalade anyway. The jar was nearly empty, rind and syrup clinging to glass like amber. Arthur took a teaspoon and scraped carefully, leaving enough that someone else could pretend there was still jam tomorrow. It was the kind of habit people learned from long years of not being first at a table.

Outside, the lane had narrowed to a black runnel between snowbanks gone grey with soot. Men from two streets over dragged a dead scooter to the corner and left it there like driftwood. Every afternoon someone shouted for paraffin and someone else shouted back that there was none. Every evening a baby cried from the Hodgsons' house at number six and stopped only when someone sang hymns off-key.

Inside, Peter performed generosity for Arthur and correction for Noreen in the same breath.

"Take another potato, Mr Hale."

"Noreen, thinner slices please, we're not feeding navvies."

"Mr Hale, do use the fire in your room, we insist."

"Noreen, account for the extra coal in your next list."

He did not raise his voice. He did not need to. The table itself carried him, the way it had been set and counted and paid for by him, that was his argument in wood and crockery.

Arthur answered with yes and thank you and I can manage. He never argued, never sympathized too quickly, never looked away when Peter's tone sharpened. That last part unsettled Noreen most. Most men looked politely past another man's house-rules. Arthur watched them land.

On Friday Noreen asked Peter for milk money before shop closing. He was at the sideboard with his wage envelope open, notes squared, coins sorted in stacks by denomination.

"How much?" he asked.

"One and eight. If she's got any left."

"For what quantity?"

"A pint and maybe a gill if she cuts me slack."

He lifted two shillings, held them, then put one back. "You had one and four yesterday."

"For bread and paraffin." She kept her voice flat. "You watched me pay."

"I watch many things. It does not follow that all are legitimate."

Arthur came in then from the yard with a coil of cable over his shoulder. He stopped, took in the open envelope, Peter's hand still above the coins, and set the cable down by the door as softly as if there was a sleeping child nearby.

"Wrong place?" he asked.

Peter smiled without warmth. "Not at all. Household accounting."

"Right." Arthur nodded once. "I've seen whole substations go down for less than a missing shilling."

Peter gave him the single shilling and three pennies, not enough. Noreen did not reach for them.

"One and eight," she said.

"You will make one and three stretch." He tilted his head toward Arthur. "Our arrangement now includes a third mouth."

Noreen took a breath that hurt along her ribs. "Your arrangement included your pub tab last Tuesday as well, and that stretched."

Peter's face did not change, but his voice turned formal enough to frost. "Do not address me in that tone in front of a guest."

Arthur said, "I can eat at depot when I'm out, truly. It's no burden."

"You are not the burden," Peter said.

For a second no one moved. Noreen heard, through the wall, Elsie dropping a pan and swearing low, the word clipped off before it carried.

Arthur cleared his throat. "Mrs Walsh, if you pass me that screwdriver, I'll fix your back-gate latch before dark. It catches on the frost and that's asking for trouble."

He had shifted the room half an inch with one line. Peter was still standing over his coins, but now he had to decide whether to refuse a useful repair and look small, or concede the moment.

He set down another sixpence. "Very well."

Noreen picked up the coins and felt heat rise in her face, not gratitude, never that. Rage and relief often wore the same coat.

She went to Mrs Bhatti's with the shopping bag and the money damp in her glove. Elsie was at the flour bins arguing quietly about yeast. They did not stand together. They did not touch sleeves. Elsie said, "Morning," in the same voice she used for women she barely knew.

Noreen answered in kind.

At the till, with Mrs Bhatti's pencil tucked behind one ear and her radio coughing static near the cigarettes, Noreen slid her list over and said, "No credit this week. Cash only."

Mrs Bhatti lifted one eyebrow. "Since when?"

"Since now."

Elsie did not turn around, but Noreen saw her shoulders go tight under her coat.

On the way home Noreen took the longer route past the chapel rather than cut through Elsie's yard. The snow there had gone to slush over frozen stones, and her boots filled at the seams. She did it anyway. She could feel the shape of Arthur in the house now the way she felt the mantel with her hip in the dark: not always in sight, but always there.

When she got back, Arthur had fixed the back-gate latch. Peter tested it twice, then threaded stiff garden wire through the latch and tied it to a brass handbell hung on a scullery nail. Any lift of that gate would ring in their kitchen. "No casual traffic through our yard," he said, not looking at her while he spoke. "If Mrs Harrow needs anything, she comes by the front."

In the afternoon Elsie came by to return a potato masher and borrow a pan lid. She stood in the hallway with her gloves on.

"Can't stay," she said. "Tom's due."

"You all right?"

"Fine." Elsie looked past her at the stairs, where Arthur's coat hung on the peg below Peter's. "You?"

"Fine."

The lie passed between them smooth as soap.

Arthur came down carrying a cracked plug head and paused when he saw Elsie. "Mrs Harrow, your front room lamp, if it's still flickering, I can look after tea. No charge."

Elsie gave a quick smile. "It'll keep till tomorrow."

"Tomorrow might not keep," Arthur said, then seemed to hear himself sounding like a noticeboard and added, "Only if you want."

"Thanks," she said. "I'll send for you if it goes."

She left before Peter came in from the lavatory. Noreen watched her through the glass panel in the door, crossing the lane with shoulders tucked and each step measured to look ordinary. A woman who had learned exactly how quickly ordinary movement could be called suspicious.

That night at supper Peter asked Arthur about line failures around the estate and listened with the solemnity of a committee man.

"So if residents overloaded sockets with electric bars, they contributed to outages," Peter said.

Arthur buttered bread with the wrong side of the knife, absentminded. "Contributed, yes. But mostly it's ice load and old kit. People are cold. They do daft things when they're cold."

"Discipline prevents daftness."

"Sometimes," Arthur said. "Sometimes folk just need heat before they need lectures."

Peter gave a soft snort. "That is a generous philosophy for a utility worker."

"It's not philosophy," Arthur said. "It's what I see when I knock doors at two in the morning."

Noreen had not heard anyone answer Peter like that in his own house. A simple correction, level and unbowed, laid down like a plank over bad ground.

Peter turned to her. "More tea."

She stood and reached for the pot. Arthur stood at the same time and took it from her before she had to lean across the table.

"I've got it," he said.

Peter's jaw worked once. "You are a guest, Mr Hale."

Arthur poured anyway, steady hand, no spill. "Tonight I'm a man with two hands and a kettle."

Noreen sat back down and looked at the steam rising between them. She did not thank him. Thanks made a debt. She did not need another ledger in this house.

Later, in bed, Peter said into the dark, "I do not want you encouraging familiarity with him."

Noreen stared at the ceiling crack shaped like a river map. "By letting him hold a kettle?"

"By performing solidarity against me."

She rolled onto her side to face away. "Maybe stop giving strangers a reason to notice your manners."

There was a long pause. Then the mattress dipped as he turned and his hand landed on her hip in that practiced claim men called gentle because it did not bruise, an ownership touch, a stamp.

"Do not provoke me, Noreen," he said softly. "You are not in a position to test boundaries right now."

She went still because moving would start a fight and she had no room left for one. Through the wall she heard Elsie and Tom beginning their evening argument, his voice loud with drink already, hers low and tight as wrapped string. Arthur's floorboard creaked once above, then quieted.

By Saturday morning Peter had moved the handbell to the kitchen sill where he could see it and left the curtain half open while he wrote at the table.

Noreen carried potato peelings to the bin and saw Elsie at her own step with a folded apron over one arm.

"Morning, Mrs Walsh," Elsie called, too bright and too loud, the way you spoke when you wanted windows to hear. "You still wanting that drippings tin back, or should I leave it at Mrs Bhatti's?"

Noreen felt Peter's gaze through the glass before she answered. "Leave it at the shop, thanks. Easier that way."

A beat passed. Elsie's mouth tightened and smoothed. "Right then. Weather's turning."

"Looks it."

Arthur came down the yard path with a coal scuttle, glanced between them, and looked away, giving the exchange the shape of ordinary neighbor business.

Peter rapped once on the window frame. "Noreen, inside."

She carried the peelings back in and hated how fast her body obeyed.

* * *

The blackout hit at ten past seven on Sunday evening with a sound like someone biting foil, a hard little crack from inside the walls, and then all at once the lane went dark.

Noreen had just lifted the pot from the stove. Steam ghosted up and vanished into black. Peter swore, formal even in anger. "Damn these incompetent schedules."

Arthur was already in the hallway, torch in hand. "Stay where you are. I'll check your board first."

"You are not on duty," Peter said.

"Electric doesn't care about duty hours."

Noreen set the pot down by touch and felt for the match tin. Peter took it from her hand and struck first, as if even flame had to be his. The match lit his face from below, giving him hollows under the cheekbones. He handed the candle to her only after he had his own cigarette lit from it.

Across the wall Tom's voice came through, slurred and high. "Elsie? Elsie, where've you put the bloody lamp?"

A heavy thud followed, then another, cupboard doors or fists.

Noreen moved toward the back door. Peter caught her sleeve.

"You will not go over there in the dark."

"She's near term."

"So are you."

Arthur had the meter cupboard open now, torch clamped between his teeth, fingers quick on the fuses. "Main in this house is intact," he said around the light. "Looks street-side. Could be branch fault."

Tom shouted again, nearer now, and then the shared back gate slammed. Boots scraped on their step. Before Peter could answer, Tom banged on their door with the side of his fist.

Noreen opened it first.

Tom filled the frame with wet coat, red eyes, and the thick sweet stink of ale. Snowmelt ran off his cap brim onto the mat Arthur had scrubbed that afternoon. He had no gloves on. His hands were shaking, whether from cold or drink she could not tell.

"Your bloody lodger's nicked the power," Tom said. "Whole row's dark and you've got a torch like Christmas."

Peter appeared behind Noreen. "Lower your voice."

"Don't you tell me what to do in my own lane." Tom leaned past Noreen, peering into the hall. "Where's Elsie?"

"At your house, I assume," Peter said.

"She weren't when I come in."

Noreen felt the air leave her chest. "What do you mean she wasn't?"

Tom waved a hand, impatient at his own confusion. "She was there. Then she weren't. Lamps gone. Pan on boil. You think I can keep track when it's pitch-black?"

Arthur stepped into view, wiping his hands on a rag. "Mr Harrow, if she's come over, she's not here yet. Let's check your place and the lane."

Tom's eyes narrowed at him. "Who asked you, spark boy?"

Arthur did not flinch. "No one. But shouting won't bring the lights up."

Peter said, too quickly, "Mr Hale is board staff. He is here by council order."

"Yeah, and now we've all got to salute?"

Tom took a step forward that might have been toward Peter, might have been toward the room itself, toward any surface he could knock to prove he existed. Noreen smelled stale beer and cold tobacco and, underneath, fear sour as old milk.

"Tom," she said, keeping her voice level. "Where did you last see Elsie?"

"Kitchen. Then I went for lamp oil and she starts on at me about the money and then all this goes and—"

He broke off, looked at Arthur's torch, and seemed to remember an audience. "None of your business anyway."

Arthur crouched, unlatched his toolkit, and held up a coil of insulated wire. "Could use your hands if they're steady enough. Need someone to hold the lantern while I check the feed at your meter. Five minutes."

Tom snorted. "My hands are fine."

"Good," Arthur said. "Prove it."

It was not a challenge in tone, but it landed like one. Peter shifted sideways to watch. Noreen saw the trap click into place and hated that she was grateful for it.

Tom looked from one face to another and found no easy exit. "All right then. Come on."

They moved as a knot through the yard: Arthur with tools, Tom with lantern, Peter with his coat thrown over pajamas and pride held high like a collar, Noreen behind because no one had told her not to and she was past waiting for permission. Wind cut through the passage and lifted her nightdress hem around her calves. The snow in the yard had crusted hard enough to hold footprints for days.

Elsie was in her own scullery when they got there, crouched by the sink with a candle stub and a wet tea towel wrapped around one hand. Plaster dust freckled her skirt. The wall above the breadboard had a fresh fist-sized hole punched through the paint and lath, and the clock hung crooked on one nail with its glass face cracked inward. She looked up sharp as a trapped bird, then saw Noreen and let breath out.

"Pan boiled over," she said. "I grabbed it wrong."

The towel darkened where skin had blistered. The burn was not bad, but it was angry.

Tom started in at once. "I was calling and you weren't answering and then you've got yourself scalded because you can't leave things be for one minute—"

"Leave off," Noreen said.

"It's my wife."

"It's her hand."

Arthur was already at the meter box by the back door, lantern light cutting his face into planes. "Mr Harrow, hold this steady, at my shoulder height. Not there. There."

Tom held it too low. Arthur repositioned his wrist with two fingers, not rough. "If you wobble, I can't read the contacts."

"I ain't wobbling."

The lantern shook enough to make shadows jump.

Peter stood by the table, lips thin. "This scene is unnecessary."

"The scene is your lane in blackout," Arthur said. "I'd take unnecessary over fire."

He popped the cover, checked the cartridge fuse, then swore once, low. "Feed's iced on the branch. Not internal fault."

Tom made a triumphant noise. "See? Not my bloody wiring then."

"Not tonight," Arthur said.

Noreen caught the words and so did Tom. His face changed, a flare and collapse in one beat.

"What's that meant to mean?"

Arthur did not look at him. "Means I still need that lantern steady." He reached deeper into the box, fingertips numb and precise. "Peter, can you fetch the dry cloth from my bag? Left pocket."

Peter hesitated. It was the first time anyone had given him an instruction in that yard. Then he turned and did it, because refusing would look childish in front of the two women.

In the kitchen Elsie sat on the stool, jaw tight, injured hand in a bowl of cool water now. Noreen knelt beside her and checked the palm. Skin lifted in one narrow strip near the thumb pad.

"You'll need clean dressing," Noreen said.

Elsie nodded once. "Tom came in roaring before the lights went. Wanted to know where his wage packet had gone." She kept her voice low, eyes on the water. "He'd put it somewhere and forgot. Then he accused me."

"Did he hit you?"

Elsie looked up at that, offended and tired at once. "He swung at me and hit brick when I ducked." A beat. "Then he held his fist in my face and said next time he'd pick bone." She looked back to the bowl. "Cup rack came down when he hit the wall. I grabbed the boiling pan wrong trying to save the plates."

From the doorway Tom barked, knuckles split and chalked white with plaster, "I can hear you whispering."

"Then hear this," Elsie said without turning. "Shut your mouth and hold the light like the man told you."

The silence that followed was thick enough to chew.

Arthur said quietly, "Good level. Keep it there."

Noreen almost laughed, not because it was funny but because it was the first useful command Tom had obeyed in weeks and it had come from a stranger in a borrowed bed.

Five minutes later the branch feed coughed back and the bulb above Elsie's sink flared weak yellow, then steadied. A cheer went up somewhere down the lane. Someone banged a saucepan in celebration. Somewhere else, a child started crying at the sudden light.

Arthur closed the box and rubbed his hands together hard. "That'll hold if no one overloads. One heater per socket. No daisy chains."

Tom tossed the lantern onto the table too hard. "Right. You heard the man."

Peter said, "We all appreciate your expertise, Mr Hale."

Arthur slung his bag over his shoulder. "No expertise in not freezing. Just procedure."

Tom leaned in toward him, smile wet and ugly. "Think this makes you boss round here? Sleeping in Walsh's spare and telling men what to do in their own houses?"

Arthur met his eyes and spoke in the same calm he had used for the fuse. "No. It makes me tired and still on call." He nodded toward Elsie's bandaged hand. "And it makes me want fewer accidents."

Tom laughed once, sharp. "Accidents."

He patted his coat pockets, then his trouser pockets, faster each time. "Where's my packet then?"

Elsie closed her eyes. "Inside your cap lining. You stuffed it there at Mrs Doyle's and forgot."

Tom snatched off his cap, shook it, and a folded wage envelope slid onto the floorboards between his boots. No one moved to pick it up.

Peter made a small sound in his throat that might have been pity and might have been satisfaction.

Tom bent for the envelope, missed it on the first try, got it on the second, and stood with his face gone mottled red. He looked at Arthur, at Peter, at the women, seeing exactly what each of them had seen.

"Long day," he muttered. "Anyone could do that."

"Course," Elsie said.

The softness in her voice cut deeper than shouting would have.

Tom stuffed the packet into his inside pocket this time and buttoned the coat wrong, button into wrong hole, hands thick. "We're done here," he said to no one in particular, and pushed past Noreen, shoulder clipping the doorframe hard enough to shake the hanging cups.

When he had gone into the front room, Arthur crouched by Elsie. "May I?" he asked, indicating her hand.

She gave him the towel. He unwrapped it, checked the burn without fuss, and said, "Keep it cool ten more minutes, then dry dressing. No grease."

"You a linesman or a nurse?" Elsie asked.

"Brother to three sisters," he said. "You learn things."

There it was, the detail that made him a man and not just a coat on a peg. Three sisters somewhere who had burned themselves on pans and irons and kettle spouts. People who had taught him where to put his hands and when not to.

Peter had gone silent in the corner, watching Noreen and Elsie close together by the sink. Not touching, but near enough to feel. Noreen stood and took one step back, then another, widening the space on purpose.

Arthur saw it. Of course he saw it.

He packed his tools and said, "I'll check number six before I turn in. Their baby's night-light plug was sparking yesterday."

Peter recovered first. "Commendable diligence."

Arthur gave him a level look. "It's not commendable. It's the job."

Noreen walked him to the back step. The lane hummed now with restored current: radios crackling back to life, kettles relit, voices louder because fear had somewhere to drain.

"Thank you," she said, because this debt she would name.

He shook his head. "Don't thank me. Tom's meter tail is loose, and your gate-bell wire is stripped where it bends. I'll swap both in daylight."

She frowned. "Is it dangerous?"

Arthur adjusted the yellow scarf at his throat. "Not if you're careful. One heater per socket tonight, no daisy chains, and keep a bucket by the stove in case that wall plug spits again." He looked toward the two houses, mirror fronts with one shared spine of brick. "If he starts shouting next door, knock my door first. I'll come."

Before she could answer, he added, gentler, "I've got sisters. I know that kind of quiet."

Then he went down the step into the lane, torch beam low and steady, leaving Noreen with the cold and the truth of it.

Inside, Peter was already at the table with pen and paper.

"From tomorrow," he said, "there will be no yard chatter, no wall tapping, and no visits after dark. This lane lives on gossip and I will not have our name fed to it."

"Our name?"

"Do not play innocent."

Noreen looked at him, then at the shared wall, then at the doorway where Arthur's coat hook waited empty for his return.

"You can draw all the columns you like," she said. "It won't make this house private again."

Peter's mouth flattened. "Privacy is maintained by discipline."

"No," she said. "Privacy was maintained by no one looking. That's over."

He stood so fast the chair scraped. "You are becoming insolent."

"I'm becoming observed," she said. "There's a difference."

He reached for her wrist and stopped short, hand open in the air between them as if he had remembered there were now two kinds of witness: the man in the spare room, and the woman he had married who had started saying things aloud.

She went to the stove, lifted the kettle, and poured out the water gone metallic from too much boiling. In the sink the steam rose and struck the window where frost was already feathering back across the glass from the edges in.

Across the wall Tom and Elsie were speaking in voices too low to catch words. The rhythm was wrong for peace. Noreen set the kettle down and listened anyway, because listening was what women did when doors were thin and consequences were thicker.

When Arthur came back in, he paused in the hallway and wiped his boots with slow deliberate strokes, twice each sole, as if ritual could hold a line against mess.

Noreen went upstairs without speaking. She took the half-finished baby blanket from behind the wardrobe and sat on the bed with it spread across her knees, wool rough against her palms. She added twelve stitches, then had to pull back four where her count had slipped. From the spare room she heard Arthur turn a page in his damp detective novel. From the kitchen below she heard Peter fold paper and unfold it again.

She did not go next door.

She did not knock on the wall.

Before she slept, she placed the billeting order in the bottom drawer under her chemises and stockings, where Peter would not look because he assumed he already knew what mattered.

In the morning she would walk to the shop by the long route again. Elsie would wave from her own gate as if they were only neighbors, not co-conspirators in hunger and fear. They would talk about potatoes and paraffin and never say the names of things that kept them alive.

The stranger in the spare room would hear enough, and he would hear too much.
