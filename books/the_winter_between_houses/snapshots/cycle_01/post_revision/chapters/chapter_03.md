# Chapter 3

On Christmas morning, Elsie set six plates on the Walsh table and held the seventh over the oilcloth, turning it until the crack caught the light. The line went from rim to center and forked near the flower in the middle, a split ready to run farther the minute hot gravy hit it. If she put that one in front of Tom he would joke and eat; if she put it in front of Peter he would look at it like an insult with gravy on top.

Noreen came in from the scullery with her sleeves rolled to the elbows and a damp lock stuck to her cheek. She carried a stack of saucers that did not belong to either house, all pale green with a hotel crest rubbed almost away.

"Mrs Bhatti found these in the back room," she said. "Said they were from her cousin's cafe and we can have them till New Year if we don't chip the lot."

Elsie looked at the crest again. "Cafe saucers for Christmas dinner. That's class."

Noreen grinned. "We're continental now. Stop staring and lay them out."

The room smelled of onions, coal smoke, and the paraffin heater Peter had insisted on moving closer to the wall "for safety," though it made the wallpaper sweat. Outside, snow had crusted hard against the step. Every time somebody opened the front door the cold came in like a person with a bad temper.

Tom stamped in with his cap in his hand and his ears pink as raw ham. He did not have a butcher's parcel.

Elsie felt the gap where that parcel should have been before he spoke.

"Queue wrapped twice round the block," he said. "Then Hargreaves comes out and says the delivery lorry never got through from Leeds. No turkey, no goose, no nowt but two bony rabbits and a tongue that looked embalmed."

"You put our order in three weeks ago," Elsie said.

"I know what I did." Tom dropped his cap on the sideboard and rubbed his hands over the paraffin heater without taking his coat off. "He says he'll make it right after Boxing Day."

"After Boxing Day is when nobody needs a Christmas bird."

Tom blew a breath through his teeth. "Don't start, Els."

She wanted to start. She wanted to pick up his cap and beat him over the face with it for that soft don't start that always meant do the hard bit for me. But Noreen was there, and Noreen had her own husband and her own performance to mount before dusk. So Elsie said, "Fine. Then we need another main."

Noreen had already reached for the flour tin. "Lentil pie."

Tom laughed once. "On Jesus's birthday."

Noreen pointed the flour scoop at him. "Jesus was a carpenter's son. He'd eat what was put in front of him and ask for seconds."

He held up both hands. "I surrender to theology."

Elsie caught herself smiling and hated that, because smiling felt like agreeing to the terms again. Noreen saw it anyway and bumped her hip with the edge of the flour bin, a small deliberate shove that said keep moving.

By ten o'clock both kitchens were running hot and mean. Elsie chopped onions till her fingers stung; Noreen rinsed lentils in three changes of cold water, shaking the colander so hard the metal clattered. Tom peeled potatoes with the speed he only had when there was an audience. Peter, still in his pressed shirt with his tie on because he believed in proper lunch even on Christmas morning, stood in the doorway with a notebook and asked practical questions in that clerk's voice of his.

"How many portions are we serving exactly?"

"Eight if Arthur comes," Noreen said. "Seven if he sends his apologies and eats at his mother's."

Peter wrote 8/7 in pencil. "Then use one and three-quarter cups for the dried lentils. If you use two, we shall have leftovers and nothing for tomorrow's supper."

Tom snorted. "Listen to this. Christmas by actuarial table."

Peter did not look at him. "Food planning is not theatrics, Tom."

Elsie kept chopping. The knife nicked her thumb and she sucked the blood quickly so nobody saw. She knew that if she looked up she would catch Noreen's eye and they would both have to decide whether to laugh or shut up. Both options cost something.

At half eleven Noreen tied on her coat and said she was walking to the shop for more onions and black pepper. Elsie knew what that meant and followed her into the street before Peter could object.

The lane was white packed down to blue where boots had polished it. Breath smoked out of mouths and chimneys alike. Mrs Bhatti's corner shop had a queue that reached past the empty milk crate by the door. Inside, the bell gave its thin frantic ring each time someone squeezed through.

Mrs Bhatti stood behind the counter in a maroon cardigan with one sleeve patched in darker wool, her reading glasses at the end of her nose and a yellow carpenter's pencil hooked behind her ear. She did not write totals till she had licked her thumb and flattened each receipt against the counter with unnecessary care. Her left thumb wore a strip of white sticking plaster where she had sliced herself opening a crate of turnips; she used the good hand to pass change and clucked every time someone tried to hurry her.

When Noreen asked for lentils on credit, Mrs Bhatti did not pretend surprise. She reached under the till and brought out a sack already weighed into a paper parcel, folded and waiting under the drawer for exactly this request.

"You settle after New Year," she said. "Not before. Before New Year, you're all liars."

Noreen laughed. "Fair enough."

Mrs Bhatti peered past her at Elsie. "And you, Mrs Harrow, no carrying two bags. I saw you last week with paraffin in one hand and potatoes in the other. You think I'm blind because I'm old?"

"You're not old," Elsie said. "You're terrifying."

"I'm both." Mrs Bhatti slid two onions and a packet of pepper across the counter, then, after a beat, added a small jar of preserved lemons with a chipped lid. "For brightness. Your men will call it foreign and eat all of it."

On the walk back, Noreen tucked the jar inside her coat like contraband and said, "She'll put that on the tab and fight me if I mention it."

Elsie looked down at their two sets of footprints crossing and recrossing where the lane narrowed. "She's right about one thing. We're liars."

Noreen did not answer at once. Wind pushed loose snow off the roofline in a dry hiss. When she spoke her voice stayed light and almost convincing.

"Today's not for truth. Today's for looking like we never had to ask anybody for anything."

Back in the Walsh kitchen, they built the lentil pie in a deep enamel dish whose handle had been repaired with wire. Noreen fried onions and celery in the bacon fat she had saved since November, scraping every browned bit from the pan with a wooden spoon worn thin at one edge. Elsie mashed boiled carrots with mustard powder and margarine and folded that over the lentils for a crust that would brown if the oven held its heat.

Tom came in, sniffed the pan, and said, "Smells better than turkey."

Peter, entering behind him, corrected him without pausing. "Smells stronger than turkey."

Noreen said, "If either of you criticizes this dish before you've eaten it, you can go gnaw frost off the back wall."

Tom gave a theatrical shiver. Peter sat down at the table and polished his glasses with a folded handkerchief, jaw set, taking his time over each lens. Elsie watched the two of them and thought, not for the first time, that men could hold grudges like cold water in the mouth, swilling it around for hours.

By two the house had warmed enough for the window glass to run at the edges. Elsie and Noreen set the table with mixed crockery and the cafe saucers. There were seven knives that matched and one blunt table knife with a bone handle. Noreen wrapped the handle in a napkin and put that place setting at Peter's chair without comment.

She looked at what they had assembled: lentil pie, roasted potatoes in goose fat saved from last year and rationed spoon by spoon, braised red cabbage with cloves, one jar of beetroot, and a pudding small as a child's head. It looked meager and stubborn and alive.

"It'll do," Elsie said.

Noreen adjusted a fork by half an inch and said, "It'll pass."

The knock at the front door came exactly as the church bells started in the distance, muffled by snow. Time to perform.

* * *

Noreen opened the door with flour on her sleeve and a smile already pinned in place. Vera and Len Brennan stood on the step stamped white to the knees, both carrying parcels wrapped in newspaper. Vera's lipstick had bled at one corner where she kept worrying it with her teeth; she did that when her chest tightened, along with tapping the lid of the peppermint tin she carried in her coat pocket. Len smelled of damp tobacco and the antiseptic violet he dabbed on his racing pigeons' feet every winter, and his fingers carried the stain in the half-moons under the nails.

"Merry Christmas, love," Vera said, handing over a squat bottle. "It's not sherry. It's cooking brandy. Don't make faces. It burns either way."

"It'll do us fine." Noreen took the bottle and kissed Vera's cheek, then Len's. "Get in before your knees freeze solid."

They crowded into the front room with coats and steam and the raw happy noise people make when they are determined to be good company. Tom shook Len's hand hard enough to rock both their shoulders. Peter inclined his head and took Vera's parcel to the sideboard, where he lined up gifts by size before opening any of them. Elsie had seen him do this before and thought it looked like an inspection.

For ten minutes the afternoon worked exactly as planned. Tom produced paper crowns from a box of stale crackers and put a green one on Peter's head when Peter was not looking. Len told a story about trying to cycle on black ice with two sacks of bird feed hanging from the handlebars and ending up in Mrs Kendrick's privet. Vera laughed hard enough to wheeze, then thumped her chest and said she had given up cigarettes "except on days ending in y." Even Peter smiled at that, thin but real.

Noreen moved between table and stove, touching shoulders as she passed, refilling cups, keeping the room in motion. She had always been good at this kind of choreography. If people were laughing, they did not listen for the scrape under the laughter.

When she brought in the pie, Tom whistled.

"Now that," he said, "is a proper center piece."

"It is not a center piece," Peter said. "It is supper."

Noreen set the dish down and gave him a look sharp enough to shave with. "You can call it what you like after you've thanked me."

Peter folded his napkin on his lap. "Thank you, Noreen."

The serving started well enough. Elsie spooned cabbage. Vera passed beetroot. Len reached for potatoes and said, "Give me the burnt ones. Burnt's where the flavor hides." Noreen nearly dropped the spoon laughing when Tom announced in his bus-conductor voice, "Move down inside, please, make room for gravy."

Then Peter began to portion.

He held the carving spoon over the pie and measured each scoop by eye, the same neat arithmetic he used at the council office. One rounded spoon for Len. One and a half for Tom "because of his shift work appetite." Three-quarters for Vera. Three-quarters for Elsie. One for himself. Whatever remained scraped to Noreen's plate in a thin crescent.

"It's easier if we keep things even," he said.

Noreen stared at the crescent and said, lightly, "Even seems to mean hungry where I'm sitting."

Peter adjusted his glasses. "You were tasting while you cooked."

Tom let out a low laugh that might have been warning or agreement; with him it could be either. Elsie looked at Noreen's plate and then away. She knew the math of the dish. Peter's arithmetic was not about hunger.

Vera, trying to bridge the moment, lifted her glass. "To making a feast out of nowt, then."

"To ingenuity," Peter said, and drank.

The conversation limped, then found its feet again because people wanted it to. They moved on to church services, frozen pipes, rumors about the buses restarting by New Year. Tom told a story about a passenger smuggling a live hen under her coat; Len topped it with one about a fox in his loft. Noreen fed the room little lines, small jokes, prompts. Elsie watched her do it and thought of somebody stitching a split seam while wearing the dress.

Peter waited until pudding was served to make his move. He set down his spoon and turned to Len with that mild schoolmaster tone.

"Len, you are from Leeds, are you not?"

"Born and bred."

"You can hear the variance in local speech better than I can. Tell me, does Noreen still say 't' for 'th' when she gets excited, or has marriage corrected that?"

Len froze with custard halfway to his mouth. Vera's peppermint tin clicked once in her pocket.

Noreen put on her host's smile and bought herself a breath. "What a thing to ask over pudding."

Peter kept his gaze on Len. "It is an ordinary linguistic observation."

Tom cut in too loudly. "Pete, give it a rest."

Peter lifted one hand. "No, really. I am interested in pronunciation drift. Council office gets all sorts now. You can almost map postcode by vowels."

He turned to Noreen at last. "Say 'thirty-three,' darling."

Heat rose up Noreen's neck under her collar. She felt it as a rash, not a blush. "No."

"It is harmless."

"Then you say it."

Peter gave a tiny impatient sigh and enunciated, "Thirty-three."

Tom laughed into his glass, desperate and ugly. "There you are. A Christmas parlour game from the grammar school."

Noreen kept her spoon in her hand because setting it down would have shown it shaking. "You're making a show."

Peter's voice went gentler, which meant crueler. "I am trying to help you, Noreen. People judge. You know they do. If you wish to be taken seriously by better circles, the old Ballymuck habits do not assist."

There it was. Ballymuck. The estate outside Galway where her father had worked road crews and her mother scrubbed floors at the convent when there was work. Peter used the name when he wanted to remind her he had rescued her from something. He had never seen the place. He pronounced it like a stain.

Vera stared at her plate. Len drank water in two careful swallows, face pinched.

Noreen looked across to Elsie. Elsie had gone very still, not the soft stillness of listening but the held-breath stillness before someone breaks a glass in their hand. Tom watched both women and did not know where to look next.

Noreen heard herself laugh. It came out thin and metallic.

"Better circles? Peter, we're eating lentils off borrowed saucers while wind comes through the skirting board. If that's society, I must've missed the invitation."

Len barked a laugh before he could stop it. Vera kicked him under the table.

Peter did not smile. "Mockery is a poor substitute for improvement."

Noreen leaned forward. "And contempt is what, exactly? Good husbanding?"

Peter's jaw moved once. "Do not dramatize."

She opened her mouth to answer and then closed it. She felt the room tilt by half a degree, not enough for anyone else to notice, enough for her to know if she spoke now she would either cry or spit. She did neither. She lifted her pudding spoon and finished the last of the custard anyway, thick and lukewarm on her tongue.

Elsie broke the silence by asking Vera whether her sister in Huddersfield had got coal through this week. Vera answered with grateful speed. Conversation restarted, shaky and overbright. Noreen stayed in it, because not staying in it would have been a scene and a scene would have been exactly what Peter would later call her fault.

When Len stood to help clear plates, Peter said, "Leave those. Noreen and Elsie can manage. Let us not upend all tradition in one afternoon."

Len sat back down slowly. Vera's lips pressed white.

Noreen carried dishes to the scullery with her face arranged in pleasant neutral. She could hear each voice in the front room through the doorway. Tom too loud. Peter steady. Elsie clipped and polite. The sound of cutlery against china was a small ongoing attack.

She stood at the sink, hands in cold grey water, and whispered under her breath in Irish, one line her mother used when storms took the roof tiles: God save us from the men and their good intentions.

* * *

Elsie followed Noreen to the scullery with the stack of plates too high for safety, because if she did not move she would march back in and throw Peter's careful face into his own pudding.

The scullery window was furred white except for a scraped oval at eye level. Through it she could see the back lane where bins sat buried to their handles. Noreen had her forearms in suds up to the wrist and was scrubbing the same plate twice.

"You all right?" Elsie asked.

Noreen did not look up. "Fine."

"Liar."

"Professional."

Elsie set the plates down and lowered her voice. "You don't have to keep this going."

Noreen scrubbed Peter's pudding spoon twice, then set it apart from the others on the drainer. "I do for another hour. Then they all go home and call it a lovely afternoon."

Before Elsie could answer, Tom's voice boomed from the front room. "Here she is, the kitchen marshal. Come rescue us from his worship's lecture on municipal budget deficits."

Noreen wiped her hands and lifted her chin. "Back to the front, then."

The men had moved from pudding to cigarettes and brandy. Smoke hung below the ceiling like gauze. Len tapped ash into an empty saucer and said, "In fog, a good cock bird keeps low, follows the canal line, and comes home by sense, not swagger."

"On Route Six I don't get canal lines," Tom said. "I get bald tyres, forty wet coats, and a conductor's smile."

Peter corrected him. "By luck."

"Luck's in the rota, mate. You still keep the bus out of the ditch."

"Luck is what men invoke when they have not prepared."

Tom gave a short laugh. "And there's the office leaflet talking."

Vera said quickly, "Anybody fancy another bit of pie?" though there was almost none left.

Peter ignored her. "Preparation keeps households intact, Tom. Wages logged, bills met, habits controlled. It is really not mysterious."

Elsie felt the words like fingers searching for her pocket. Noreen had gone very quiet beside the sideboard. Elsie waited for her to soften it with a joke, to rescue Peter from himself the way she always had. Noreen did not speak.

Tom sat forward, grin thinning. "You giving me a sermon in my own neighborhood now?"

Peter folded his hands. "I am making conversation."

"No, you're taking attendance. Seeing who passes your little exam."

Len cleared his throat. "Come off it, lads. It's Christmas."

"My birds settle faster than this when a hawk cuts across the loft."

Tom pointed his cigarette at Peter and ash dropped on the rug. "You think because you wear a tie to eat lentils you get to grade the rest of us."

Peter's voice sharpened. "At least I do not gamble away heating money and call it weather."

The room snapped tight. Elsie heard Vera inhale, a tiny drag of breath through her teeth. Noreen's hand found the edge of the sideboard and gripped.

Tom stood up so fast his chair skidded back. "Watch your mouth."

Peter did not stand. "Sit down."

"Say it again."

"I have no need to repeat myself."

Tom came around the table and caught Peter by the tie knot before anyone could reach him. Peter's chair went over backward. He hit the sideboard shoulder-first and a stack of Mrs Bhatti's borrowed cafe saucers slid, struck the boards, and burst into pale green shards.

Vera cried out and rose half from her seat. Len shoved himself between the men and got both hands on Tom's chest. "Leave it, Tom. Leave it now."

Peter straightened, face gone chalky, tie hanging half torn. "Get out of my house."

Tom lunged again. "Say Ballymuck one more time and I'll put your teeth on that rug."

Noreen dropped to her knees among the crockery before either man could answer. A shard nicked the heel of her palm; blood striped across the tea towel she grabbed from the rail. Elsie crouched with her, swearing under her breath, and pulled her back from the worst of the glass.

Then the front door banged under a hard knock and a gust of cold ripped through the hallway. Len kept one arm across Tom's chest while Tom twisted toward the sound.

Tom swore and strode to the door. He yanked it open.

On the step stood Arthur Pike, Tom's relief driver from the bus depot, cap in one hand, paper parcel tucked under his arm, coat powdered white at the shoulders.

"Sorry I'm late," he said, teeth chattering. "Buses stalled at the viaduct. Had to walk from town."

He lifted the parcel. "Your mum sent trifle sponge and told me to keep my fingers out of it."

His smile faltered when he saw the room: overturned chair, torn tie, Len braced like a constable, and Noreen's blood on the tea towel.

"Right. Where do you keep the brush and pan before someone stands in this?"

No one answered for a beat. Then Vera moved first, fetched the dustpan from beside the range, and handed it to him without looking at Peter.

They cleaned in silence. Arthur swept the long shards first, then chased the green grit from the board seams with the edge of the pan. Elsie wrapped Noreen's palm in sticking plaster. Tom stood by the mantel, breathing hard, while Peter sat rigid with his jaw set and his tie stuffed into his waistcoat pocket like evidence.

No one asked for another drink. The trifle sponge stayed wrapped on the sideboard. After ten strained minutes Vera asked Len for their coats, and Len, buttoning up, said to Peter in a voice too level to miss, "You don't invite people to Christmas to watch you strip your wife for sport."

By five the Brennans had gone with stiff goodbyes. Len shook Tom's hand once and left Peter standing. Vera hugged Noreen hard around the shoulders, careful of the bandaged hand, and whispered, "You ring if he starts again." Arthur left with a wrapped slice of pie for his mother and said he'd return the dish on first run tomorrow, "if the viaduct opens."

When the door finally shut and footsteps crunched away down Barrow Lane, the house did not exhale. It held.

* * *

Noreen stacked cups in the sink one-handed and listened to Peter bolt the front door. The click of the bolt sounded final in a way that had nothing to do with weather.

She and Elsie worked shoulder to shoulder without speaking. Grease cooled on plates in iridescent skins. The tap water came out thin and bitterly cold; the boiler had given up an hour ago. The sticking plaster over Noreen's palm went pink again in the dishwater. In the front room Tom said he ought to check the fire at home and Elsie answered, "A minute," then dried her hands and came into the scullery.

"I'll leave you to it," Elsie said softly. Her eyes searched Noreen's face, landing nowhere, then searching again. "You send through if you need anything tonight. Anything."

Noreen wanted to say stay. She wanted to say if you go next door I will be alone with him and I do not have enough skin left for another hour. Instead she nodded because wanting and saying had never been the same thing in this house.

After Elsie left, Peter came in and closed the scullery door behind him. He removed his glasses, polished them, and put them back on. He only did that when he was preparing a statement.

"That business with Tom putting his hands on me and smashing Mrs Bhatti's saucers was unnecessary," he said.

Noreen kept drying a plate she had already dried. "You mean the part where you asked me to perform my accent for the neighbors."

"They are not my friends. They are neighbors."

"Fine. For the neighbors."

Peter leaned one hand on the back of the chair, posture tidy, voice tidy. "You were over-sensitive. I made an observation. You chose to turn it into a grievance."

Noreen set the plate down with care because if she set it down hard it would shatter and then he would talk about temper for a week.

"I want an apology," she said.

He blinked, surprised not by the request but by the plainness of it. "For what specifically?"

"For making me small in my own house, and for standing there while I bled into a tea towel over your tie."

Peter's mouth tightened. "This is exactly the theatrical language I mean."

"No. It's plain language."

"You are tired. We all are. Supplies are erratic, the weather is intolerable, and everyone is under strain. Tom chose violence. I will not be put on trial for his lack of restraint."

She stared at him. "You think that's what happened."

"I know what happened." He straightened a fraction. "I have carried this household through three weeks of shortages and panic. I will not apologize for maintaining standards."

Standards. The word landed like a shut drawer. Noreen saw, with a calm so clean it frightened her, the whole mechanism laid out: the tidy voice, the ledgers, the way he could make hunger sound like discipline and cruelty sound like correction. He was not having a bad holiday. He was being himself with witnesses.

Behind the wall, someone in Elsie's kitchen dropped a pan and swore softly. The everyday sound slid through the plaster and sat between them, intimate as breath.

Noreen put the tea towel over the chair back.

"All right," she said.

Peter frowned. "All right what?"

"All right. I heard you."

He waited for the rest: the argument, the tears, the bargaining he could win. It did not come. Noreen moved past him to the pantry and began putting away leftovers. Two spoonfuls of lentils in a jam jar. Half an onion wrapped in paper. A heel of bread hard enough to plane wood. She labeled the jar in pencil, not because anyone needed labeling but because writing gave her hands a job.

Peter spoke again, sharper. "Do not sulk. It is childish."

She did not turn. "I'm not sulking."

"Then what are you doing?"

"Accounting," she said, and let him decide whether that was a joke.

He stood there another moment, then gave a short disbelieving exhale and left the scullery. She heard him settle in the front room with the evening paper, pages snapped straight.

Noreen stayed where she was until the cold reached through her stockings and up into her knees. Then she wiped the table, banked the kitchen fire, and went upstairs. In the bedroom Peter had already laid out his shirt for Monday and hung his tie over the chair back. He had done it every Sunday and holiday of their marriage. Prepared, always.

She undressed without turning on the lamp, folded her dress over the end of the bed, and slid under the blankets with her back to his side. When Peter came up ten minutes later he smelled of smoke and brandy and Pears soap. He got into bed carefully, barely shifting the mattress, and after a while put a hand on her shoulder through the blanket.

"Nora," he said, using the short name he wore like proof of tenderness. "You know I only want what is best."

She looked at the wall. Paint bubbled above the skirting where damp had come through in November. From next door came the muffled scrape of Elsie moving coal in the grate, shovel against scuttle, three measured scoops. A back door latch lifted, then dropped.

Noreen lifted Peter's hand and set it back on his own side of the bed.

"Sleep," she said.

He lay still a moment, then turned away with a rustle of sheets and a quiet irritated huff through his nose. Soon enough his breathing settled into the shallow rhythm she knew better than prayer.

Noreen stayed awake. She counted the sounds through the wall: two floorboard creaks, the kettle lid rattling, the back gate clicking once, then silence, then Elsie's low voice saying something she could not make out. She had spent years translating muffled noises into stories that made marriage look solid from outside. Tonight she did not translate. She let each sound remain exactly what it was.

In the morning she would pour tea, warm his shirt by the fire, and answer to Nora when he called. She would do the tasks because tasks kept the day moving and because the baby turned heavy under her ribs and needed calories, not speeches.

But as she lay there with her back to him and the cold seam of mattress between them, something quiet and irreversible settled in. She did not need him to admit what he was. She had seen it with eight place settings and witnesses, measured out beside lentils and pudding, as tidy as accounts.

Downstairs the house ticked and cooled. Outside, snow tightened again on Barrow Lane, hard as crockery.
