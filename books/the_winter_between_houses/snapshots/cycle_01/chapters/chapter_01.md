# Chapter 1
The frost had come inside in the night.

Elsie saw it first on the parlor window, ferning up from the sill in white ribs that reached almost to the latch. When she touched the glass with one finger, her skin stuck for a second and pulled free with a sting. The room still held yesterday's cold, not this morning's. The hearth had gone black to ash, and the ash had a skin on it like old grease.

She kept still under the blanket and listened for the street. Milk bottles usually chimed against front steps by six. A bus usually pushed through the corner by half past, laboring in second gear up the rise. This morning there was nothing except a scrape and then another scrape: someone trying to clear frost from the inside of a pane with a teaspoon.

Tom slept on his back with his mouth open and a paper taste of ale on his breath. His left hand rested over his chest as if he were posing for a photograph. A conductor's whistle, cracked at one edge, hung on the bedpost where he could grab it before work. He had polished his boots last night and lined them up under the chair, toes toward the door, like if he pointed enough things at the day it might obey him.

Elsie eased herself up, one hand under her belly, and shuffled into her stockings. The baby sat low and hard as a loaf not yet risen. In the scullery, the tap gave her two coughs of air before a spit of brown water and then nothing. The bucket under the sink had a crescent of ice along one side. She snapped that with the poker and carried the pieces to the kettle.

By the time Tom came in, buttoning his shirt wrong and not noticing, she had got a reluctant flame in the grate with newspaper twists and the last two good lumps from the coal box.

"You'd think we'd moved to bloody Norway," he said, rubbing his arms. He had his old bus depot scarf on, orange thread gone gray, and he rubbed at the smoker's cough in his chest before he coughed. "Road's white from here to Bhatti's. Not one set of tracks. I've got to leave early."

"Your wages in the blue envelope?" she asked. She kept her tone flat, like she was asking about his clean vest.

"On the mantel. I told you."

"You told me Friday."

"It's Monday now, Els." He grinned as if she had set up a joke and he had delivered the punch line kindly. "I brought it, didn't I?"

He kissed the side of her head, missed by an inch, and took his mug to the fire. His hair had a parting combed straight with spit and determination, one side always trying to spring free. She watched him blow on tea that was mostly hot water and tannin and thought of how men could make comfort out of the shape of a mug even when it held nothing worth drinking.

When he left, stamping twice in the doorway to break the leather, she stood in the cold hall with the envelope in her hand before she opened it. She knew the weight of a week in notes. This did not weigh like that.

Two pounds ten should have sat in the fold after rent and his tobacco. There was one pound and a few shillings in silver, with a sixpence tucked in like a joke. She counted twice at the table because counting wrong had become the fear that shamed her most. Same total both times.

Outside, the lane had gone bright and hard, every slate roof edged white, every drainpipe bearded with icicles. The cold made the terraced houses look smaller, as if winter had squeezed them together. Elsie wrapped her coat over her belly, pinned her scarf with a safety pin that caught in the wool, and set off with the coal sack folded under her arm.

At Bhatti's corner she found the door propped open with a crate of turnips, each turnip crusted with ice that glittered under the strip light. Mrs Bhatti stood at the till in fingerless gloves she had knitted from maroon wool and mended twice at the thumb. She kept a carpenter's pencil behind one ear and licked it before she wrote in the credit book, no matter how dry her tongue must have been in this weather.

"No milk till noon if we're lucky," she said, before Elsie asked. "Van got stuck by the viaduct. Bread lorry is not coming at all."

"Any paraffin left?"

"Three tins. Two spoken for. One for Peter Walsh because he has the voucher letter and he waved it at me like a warrant." Mrs Bhatti sniffed and slid a packet of tea over the counter. "You can have this on tab. Pay me when the world thaws."

Elsie laid down two shillings anyway, because credit in ink felt heavier than coin in hand. The coins looked small and helpless against the black till tray.

At Calderbank Fuel Yard, the queue bent round the brick office and halfway up Chapel Street. Men with sacks stood stamping and swearing through their scarves. Two women had brought prams with the hoods taken off so they could heap coal where a baby should have gone. Lorry wheels spun in place by the gate, sending up black slush.

By the time Elsie reached the hatch her toes had gone numb in her shoes, and the baby had found a rib to press against as if to remind her she had no right to stand this long.

The clerk knew her by name. Mr Lomas had a fingertip missing on his right hand from a loading accident years before; he used the flat of that finger to turn ledger pages and left gray marks where coal dust sat in his skin.

"Morning, Mrs Harrow." He opened the club book and ran his flat finger down the lines. His forehead folded. "You are in arrears."

"That can't be right. I paid last Thursday."

"You paid three and six. Then Saturday there was a delivery ticket signed Harrow, two sacks, and no balance settled." He turned the ledger to show her. Tom's quick signature slanted over the line like it was trying to run downhill. "You are one pound two shillings overdrawn."

The words made no sense for a second. Overdrawn belonged to other people, people who had accounts and pens and men behind desks. Not her. Not on coal.

"I've one pound and six," she said.

"Then I can put you down for slack and a quarter sack when stock comes. No full bags till arrears are covered. Maybe tomorrow if roads clear."

A man behind her swore at the delay. Someone farther back shouted that children would freeze before paperwork warmed them.

Elsie gathered her notes slowly so her fingers would not shake. "Put the quarter, then."

"It won't carry you long."

"Nothing does."

He almost smiled at that, then looked away in the way of men who did not know where to put sympathy when it came.

The quarter sack looked pathetic in her hallway. Coal crumbs had already leaked through the seam and marked the linoleum like spilled pepper. She stood over it, coat still on, and did arithmetic against her own breathing. If she banked the fire through the day and fed it only at dusk, she might keep one room livable. If the temperature dropped further like the radio said, she would not.

The radio had called it a historic front before Tom left, the announcer careful and bright over the words severe and prolonged. Schools likely to close. Trains delayed. Nonessential travel discouraged. It had sounded like someone reading from a card in a warm booth while his tea steamed beside him.

She lasted an hour before she went next door.

Noreen opened to the second knock with a dishcloth over one shoulder and flour on both wrists. Her hair was tied back with green ribbon, the ribbon darkened in a damp patch where steam from the kettle had caught it. She had taken to wearing Peter's old cardigan because it reached over her stomach and still buttoned. One button was missing, and she had replaced it with a smooth brown one from a different garment, too big and slightly comic.

"You look half froze," Noreen said. "Come in out of that."

"I can stand a minute." Elsie kept to the step so she would not have to look at Noreen's warmer hallway. "Could you lend me a scuttle? Just till tomorrow. Lomas has put us on quarter till stock comes in."

Noreen did not ask why quarter, or why tomorrow. She glanced over Elsie's shoulder toward the lane, where snow was already filling yesterday's boot prints, then disappeared and came back with a full black scuttle in both hands.

"Take this and bring it back when you can," she said.

"I'll settle it."

"I know you will."

The weight of it pulled on Elsie's shoulder all the way home. Not just coal. The fact of being seen needing it.

By afternoon the routines that made a day had collapsed one by one.

No post came. The washing she had hung in a burst of optimism came in stiff as boards and smelling of iron. She could not scrub the hall tiles because the bucket water skinned over between trips to the grate. She chopped one onion and stretched it through potatoes and stock until the pan looked full enough to pass for supper. Then she turned the empty flour sack inside out and shook the corners for the last clinging dust.

She pushed the back row of tins to the front in the pantry and set the jars in a neat line, labels forward. One tin of salmon to look prosperous. Two jars of pickled beetroot from summer to look deliberate. No one needed to know there were only three potatoes in the sack and none of them clean.

When Tom came in after dark, he brought cold with him and pub noise still in his throat. Snow had melted into his coat and then refrozen at the hem. He smelled of damp wool, tobacco, and the sharp sweet reek of betting slips handled all day.

"Depot shut early," he said, hanging his scarf. "Buses like ice skates out there. Saw a lorry kiss a lamp post. Whole town's gone to pot in six hours."

"You stopped at the King's Arms," Elsie said.

"For ten minutes to thaw my fingers. You'd have done the same."

He rubbed his hands over the grate and gave her that stage grin he used with inspectors and pub men and anyone else he needed to win before they asked a question.

She set bowls on the table. Thin stew steamed and then stopped steaming before either of them sat.

"Lomas says the coal account's overdrawn," she said. "He has your signature for two sacks Saturday."

Tom blew on his spoon and tasted before he answered. "Does he now."

"Did you take two sacks?"

"I sorted it."

"With what money?"

"With money that was mine before it was his and yours and anyone else's." He grinned again, smaller this time. "Don't look like that. I had a line to cover, that's all."

"A line?"

"A marker. Christ, Elsie, you know how it is. One week bad, next week good."

"We're in a freeze. This isn't one week bad."

He pointed at her bowl with his spoon. "We've got food. Fire's lit. You're warm enough to nag."

"I'm not nagging. I'm asking where the pound went."

"And I'm telling you it'll come back. You pick the worst time to start counting pennies like a headmistress."

The wall between their dining room and the Walsh parlor thinned when voices rose. Everyone on Barrow Lane knew that without saying it. Elsie lowered hers and hated the lowering.

"I'm counting because the child kicks when the room drops and because I stood in a queue for two hours to be told I'm in arrears on my own hearth," she said.

"Our hearth," he said quickly, as if pronouns could repair things. "And you weren't told that on your own. Everyone's in arrears this week."

"Not everyone signed for two sacks and left me with six shillings."

He stared at her for a beat, spoon half-raised. The banter fell off him so fast she heard the clink when it hit the table.

"I said it'll come back." His voice had gone low and urgent, the one he used when he needed sympathy from men he had already lied to. "Don't make this bigger than it is."

She could have said then that she had found the betting docket in his coat when she hung it by the fire, folded twice and damp at the edge: Regal Turf, stake two pounds, race three, lost. She could have laid it between them beside the bread crusts and made him look at his own handwriting.

Instead she heard a laugh from next door stop in the middle. Noreen's laugh, cut off like a thread bitten through. Then Peter's chair scraped once and went quiet.

Everyone was listening.

Elsie stood to fetch more bread she did not have. She opened the pantry door to its careful display and held it there as if choosing, as if there were choice.

"We'll manage," she said, without turning. "I just need the real number."

Behind her, Tom pushed his bowl away. "You're doing my head in." He tried for light and missed. "What number do you want? Tell me that."

"The one that keeps us warm."

He snorted through his nose, almost a laugh, almost contempt. "Then you should've married a foreman."

Something tapped on the shared wall. Once. Then again, softer. Not an accident. A signal from next door that they could hear and would pretend they could not, if given half a chance.

Tom glanced at the wall and raised his voice deliberately. "Anyway, Walsh says council's moving coal wagons by priority now. Might get easier. Might all blow over by weekend."

He wanted performance restored, neighbor ears reassured, the script back in place where husbands knew things and wives kept pans hot.

Elsie turned from the pantry with the empty bread board in both hands and matched his volume exactly. "Let's hope so," she said.

The argument ended the way a lamp goes out when paraffin runs dry. Not solved, just dark.

Tom went to smoke by the back door, collar up, muttering at the weather. Elsie cleared bowls into gray dishwater and felt each scrape of spoon against pottery as if she were filing metal. Her fingers were numb and then abruptly burning when blood came back.

In the scullery she took his coat from the peg to shake off snow and found the docket again in the inner pocket, soft with damp. She flattened it on her palm, read the stake, read the race, read LOST in block capitals stamped hard enough to bite through paper.

The heating money had not vanished into confusion or wages held back. It had gone down a bookmaker's hole in ten minutes.

She folded the slip smaller than before and pushed it into the hem of her apron where the seam had come loose. Then she went back to the sink.

When Tom came in he was bright again, all weather talk and gallows jokes.

"Road'll clear tomorrow," he said. "You'll see. I'm on early turn. I'll square Lomas before dinner."

"All right," Elsie said.

He kissed the side of her neck and reached for tobacco. The affection felt like a hand placed on someone else's shoulder by mistake.

Later, when he slept, she lay awake and counted debts in shapes instead of numbers: the quarter sack in the hall; the scuttle from next door; the damp betting slip stitched into her apron like a hidden label; the sound of Noreen's laugh stopping at the wall.

A marriage could keep standing long after the joints had gone loose. She had seen chairs do it for years, one leg packed with folded newspaper, everyone careful not to lean too hard. It still counted as furniture until someone sat without thinking and the whole thing collapsed under them.

In the morning she woke before dawn to the baby pushing against her ribs and the cold pressing the blanket flat against her shins. Tom had already gone, or not yet come back from whatever errand he had invented before first light. His side of the bed held no warmth.

The tap in the scullery was silent. Ice had locked the basin drain. She chipped at it with a spoon until the spoon bent.

From next door came the thud of a pan set down too hard, then Noreen's voice, low and rapid, and Peter answering with that schoolmaster calm he used when he wanted a room to submit. Elsie could not make out words through the wall, only tempo.

She told herself she would keep her head down today. Sweep, mend, stretch soup, bank the fire. No scenes. No borrowing. No more evidence gathered in secret pockets.

At half nine, a knock came at the back.

Noreen stood there with a tin caddy in one hand and a shawl over her cardigan. Her cheeks were bright from cold, and she had taken the trouble to pin her hair with two pearl-headed clips that did not match, one cream and one yellowed near brown. She wore them anyway, like a private insistence that the day would not strip everything down to utility.

"I've made tea too strong for one house," she said. "And Peter's gone to the office to be useful about nothing. Can I come in?"

Elsie stepped aside. "Course."

Noreen looked once at the fire, once at Elsie's face, and set the caddy on the table without comment. She moved through the kitchen as if she had known it all her life, found two mugs, rinsed them in the little water left in the kettle, and poured.

"You two gave the wall a proper show last night," she said eventually, not unkind.

Elsie stared into her tea. The surface shivered with each wind gust up the flue.

"Sorry," she said.

"Don't apologize to me. I heard enough to know sorry isn't the useful part."

That was Noreen's way when she stopped joking: the line came quick and square and left no room to wriggle.

Elsie wrapped both hands around her mug. Heat seeped into the heel of her palms and stopped there, as if unsure it should go farther.

"He took money meant for fuel," she said. The words scraped. "He told Lomas he'd settle. Didn't."

Noreen nodded once. "I guessed as much."

"I don't want a row in front of the lane."

"Then don't give them one." Noreen blew across her tea and took a careful sip. "But don't starve your grate for his pride either."

A kettle lid rattled next door as the boil rose and fell. Somewhere down the lane a child cried, then coughed hard enough to stop herself. Boots passed outside, slow and crunching.

"Peter got paraffin from Bhatti because of his letter," Noreen said. "He'll crow about it all week. Let him. We can use what we have and keep him thinking he's saving us." She leaned back and pressed one hand into the small of her back, wincing before she smiled again. "I've got an extra sack from my brother's van run last month. It's in our shed behind the old pram frame. Take half today and half when this front decides to leave."

Elsie looked up sharply. "I can't take that."

"You can and you will. The baby doesn't care whose initials are on the coal ticket." Noreen's tone warmed, then hardened at the edges. "And if you try to pay me this minute I'll be insulted for real, and you don't want me insulted while I'm this tired."

Despite herself, Elsie gave a short laugh. It hurt in her throat from disuse.

"You'll get it back," she said.

"I said I know you will."

They drank in silence for a while, the good silence, with the table between them and the weather pushing at the windows like an animal that had found the weak spots in every frame.

Noreen broke it first. "My ankles are twice themselves by tea time," she said, matter-of-fact. "If this keeps up I won't see my own knees till Easter."

"You should put them up."

"On what? Peter's principles?" She snorted and rubbed the side of her calf where the stocking elastic had left a deep red groove. "I didn't mean that for your wall."

"It can have it," Elsie said.

Noreen studied her over the rim of her mug. "You don't have to tell me everything. But if he leaves you short again, you knock. Day or night."

The offer sat between them, plain as a loaf on a board.

Elsie nodded. She did not trust her voice not to crack into gratitude, and gratitude made her feel young in the worst way.

After tea they carried the coal together because doing it separately would have looked furtive from the lane. Noreen took the shovel and filled Elsie's sack in short measured scoops, resting every third scoop with a hand on her back. Elsie held the sack mouth open and felt grit collect under her fingernails. Their breaths smoked together in the shed air.

"That's enough," Elsie said when the sack reached half.

"That's what you said at the caddy and look where we've ended up," Noreen replied, and tipped one more shovel in.

They crossed the shared yard with the sack between them, each gripping a handle, waddling and swearing softly when they slipped. By the time they reached Elsie's door both were laughing through their breathlessness.

From an upstairs window across the lane, Mrs Clegg twitched her curtain aside and then let it fall. News in winter traveled on less than that.

Inside, Elsie poured the coal into the box and watched the level rise to something that looked, for one afternoon at least, like competence.

Noreen tugged on her gloves and headed for the door. "I'll send over a heel of yesterday's loaf if Peter has not eaten it with his sums," she said.

"I've got potatoes."

"You have three. I heard the sack when you moved it." She lifted a shoulder. "Don't be proud with me. Be proud with men if you must."

After she left, the house seemed larger and emptier at once. Elsie wiped the table, folded the dishcloth, and took her apron from the peg.

She unpicked the loose hem with a bread knife tip and slid out the betting slip. The paper had dried stiff and wavy from yesterday's damp. The ink on LOST had bled slightly at one corner, making the word look as if it were trying to run.

She held the slip over the fire for a second, close enough to curl the edge, then pulled it back. Burning it would not return the pound. Keeping it would not either. But a thing with handwriting on it could anchor memory when charm and apology started doing their work.

She tucked it into the bottom of the tea caddy under the old leaves, where Tom would never look. Then she sat at the table and opened the housekeeping notebook to a clean page.

I owe Noreen half a sack of coal.

She stared at the line until the nib blot spread, then added below it:

Lomas arrears are one pound and two.

Tom said he would square it tomorrow.

She left a gap and wrote one more sentence in smaller letters, pressing hard enough to score the page beneath.

Do not ask tonight. Keep heat first.

The rule looked sensible in ink, almost calm. On the lane, a council lorry ground past, chains clattering against frozen road. Somewhere a man shouted for someone to mind the curb. The sound bounced house to house and came thinner by the time it reached her kitchen.

Elsie closed the notebook, slid it under the breadboard, and fed two careful lumps into the grate. Flame climbed, caught, and settled low. She held her palms out and watched the skin over her knuckles pink back to life.

Normalcy, she thought, was not a state. It was work. It was choosing what to hide and what to keep breathing long enough to face tomorrow.
