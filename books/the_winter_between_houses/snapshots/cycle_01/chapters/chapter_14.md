# Chapter 14

Noreen woke to the sound of rain that was not rain.

It was ice gritting against the front window, a dry furious hiss, and the window answered with little clicks like teeth. The coal in the range had sunk to a red eye. Her feet were already swollen before she put them on the rug, the skin stretched shiny where her slippers rubbed. She sat up slowly because the headache from the clinic warning had come back behind her right eye, a steel pin turned one quarter at a time.

The first pain crossed her belly while she reached for the chamber pot. It did not rise and fall. It took hold low and deep and dragged her breath out of her like someone pulling wet linen through a wringer. She stayed bent over the mattress until it eased. Then she counted. Keane had told her to count anything she could trust: clock ticks, kettle whistles, the distance between pains. Numbers did not panic.

She got to six minutes. Then five. On the third pain she stood and warm water spilled down her calf, clear at first, then clouded pink when it hit her heel.

"Elsie," she said to the wall before she remembered a wall did not answer.

Arthur was in the spare room on the other side of the landing. She knocked once and he was up at once, boots in his hand, shirt half buttoned. He slept in a wool cap with a rent at the crown because the back room chimney drew wrong and dropped soot on the pillows.

"It's started," she said. "It's not practice. It's proper." 

Arthur did not ask whether she was sure. He looked at the water on her nightdress hem and nodded once. "I'll fetch Mrs Harrow and ring Keane. Phone at the substation still works if the line hasn't iced." He jammed his cap down and glanced at her feet. "You want the fire up now?"

"Yes. And the kettle. Two kettles if they boil." She gripped the bedpost until another pain climbed through her spine. "Where's Peter?"

"Town Hall side. He said he'd walk back after the late ledger shift." Arthur's mouth flattened. "No one walks quick in this."

He was gone before she could answer. She heard his tread on the stairs, then the front latch, then the storm swallowing him. Noreen braced both hands against her belly and breathed the way Keane had shown her, in through teeth, out in a slow thread that made room for the next one.

Elsie came through the back door in Tom's old duffel, her own coat hanging open over her stomach because nothing buttoned now. She had wrapped a scarf over her hair and forgotten to tie the ends, so the wool tails swung when she moved. Her face was pale in the firelight, the color of skimmed milk, but her hands were steady.

"I'm here," Elsie said. "Arthur's gone for Keane and ambulance. Sit. No, don't sit that far back, you'll trap your tailbone. Here." 

Noreen laughed once at that and the laugh broke in the middle because the next contraction had started. Elsie crouched in front of her and put both palms on Noreen's knees, grounding them. She smelled of smoke, Pears soap, and the sharp iron scent that came when snow hit hot coal.

"You're shaking," Noreen said when it passed.

"Course I'm shaking. Doesn't matter. Tell me how far apart."

"Five. Then maybe less. My water went." Noreen touched her own mouth as if she could keep words from spilling too quickly. "My head's bad again. I've got lights on the edge."

Elsie swore under her breath, a small hard word. "Keane said headache with lights means no waiting about. We'll set the front room and keep you warm. Have you bled much?"

"Only streaks so far." 

Elsie nodded and stood with one hand braced at her back. The movement cost her; Noreen saw it in the pause at the top, that tiny freeze while she caught her own breath around her own child. Then Elsie was all task.

She pulled sheets from the airing cupboard, rejected the first set for damp, took the older coarse pair that had dried nearest the grate. She sent Noreen to the privy before pains grew stronger, then changed her into Peter's old clean shirt because buttons were easier than a nightdress knot with wet hands. She brought towels from both houses, counted them out loud, and folded them by size on the sideboard.

"These small ones are for the baby. The large ones are for you. Clean rags go in this basin. Dirty ones go in the coal scuttle once it's empty. Don't argue."

"You're bossy when frightened," Noreen said, breathless.

"I'm bossy when there isn't time."

Another contraction hit and Noreen leaned into Elsie's shoulder. Through the wall, the Harrow clock struck two. Somewhere on Barrow Lane a bottle crate blew over and clattered down the cobbles until it smashed.

Arthur came back twenty-five minutes later white to the brows, eyelashes crusted with frost, carrying Keane's canvas bag and a paraffin tin under his arm.

"She'll be five minutes behind me," he said. "She made me take these because she said I'd drop her and not the bag. Ambulance knows. They said maybe an hour, maybe more. They've got a jackknifed lorry near the viaduct."

"Did you get through clear?" Elsie asked.

"Clear enough. Operator had to patch me twice." He put the paraffin by the range and flexed his fingers one by one, as if checking each still belonged to him. The knuckles were split and dark where old skin had opened again in the cold. "I primed the spare lamp. Want it in here or landing?"

"In here," Noreen said before Elsie could answer. "I don't want shadows tonight."

He nodded and lit the lamp. His hands stopped shaking only when flame steadied in the glass.

Keane arrived as promised, cheeks raw from wind, hair escaping her pins in silver loops that had gone damp and then stiff. She carried her own boots in one hand for the last step over the threshold and came in stocking-footed so she would not track slush to the bed. The socks were men's grey work socks darned three times at the heel.

"All right," she said, not loud, not soothing. "Tell me what happened and when."

Noreen gave her the times between breaths. Keane listened with the brass watch she wore pinned upside down on her bodice, then warmed the stethoscope head inside her sleeve before setting it on Noreen's skin. She always did that. Noreen had noticed it first at clinic and nearly cried from gratitude at that tiny refusal to make a woman flinch.

Keane examined her quickly, efficiently, eyes narrowing only once. "You're in established labor. Early for dates, yes, but labor all the same. We don't move you unless I lose either heartbeat. Roads are a hazard and you've got pressure signs. We deliver here."

Noreen nodded because there was nothing else to do. Elsie asked, "How early?"

"Likely thirty-four weeks, maybe a shade less. Small baby expected. Cold is our second enemy, bleeding is third. First enemy is delay." Keane snapped open the bag. "Arthur, boil everything that can be boiled. Knife, scissors, enamel bowl. Keep a rolling kettle, not a polite one. Elsie, I need you gloved and ready with me."

"I've got washing gloves," Elsie said.

"Good. Wash your forearms to the elbow with carbolic. Nails now. Noreen, pass urine if you can. Empty bladder gives me room."

Another pain took Noreen and she gripped the mattress ticking. She could smell carbolic soap, paraffin, wet wool, and beneath all of it the old sour mattress heat of the room. Keane spoke over the contraction in short steps she could hold onto.

"Breathe down low. Keep your jaw loose. Don't waste it in your throat. Save it for the push when I ask." 

From the front room Arthur fed the fire until the grate glowed orange-white. Coal was low. He split two broken chair rungs and fed those too, muttering apologies to the furniture like it might forgive him. Every few minutes he stepped to the back door and listened for engine noise that never came.

By three o'clock pains were under four minutes and biting hard. Noreen labored kneeling over the back of a dining chair because lying flat made her dizzy and made the pin behind her eye drill deeper. Sweat ran down her spine though the room stayed cold at the skirting boards. Elsie stood behind her, fist pressed into the small of her back through the shirt, counting breaths in a calm voice that thinned only at the ends.

"Three in, six out. Again. Three in, six out. You've done this all week with the stairs. Same lungs."

"Stairs don't split a woman in two," Noreen said.

"No," Elsie said. "But you swore at those as well and beat them."

Keane checked the fetal heart between contractions, shifting the horn and frowning at the rhythm. "There are dips on the tail. It's not catastrophic yet. Keep going."

Outside, wind hammered the guttering so hard that soot rained down the chimney breast. Arthur set a pan lid over the nursery basin to keep grit out and went back to the door. He had moved from question to routine, and routine was how he managed fear: check lamp, check fire, check road, check kettle, repeat.

At half past three, Noreen vomited thin tea into the basin and immediately apologized. Keane ignored the apology.

"Good," she said. "Body's working. You're near transition."

Noreen wanted to strike her for the word good. She wanted to sleep standing up. She wanted Peter there and not there at once. Another contraction cut through thought and left only noise, a low animal sound she did not recognize as hers until Elsie said, "That's right, use it." 

The front door banged. Arthur went to it and came back with two ambulance men who looked more like dockworkers than saviors, both red-faced from the cold and carrying equipment wrapped in army blankets. One of them had half his moustache singed off, a clean crescent over his lip where he had leaned too near a heater some other night. He said, "Road's shut at the top. We parked by the chapel and hauled this lot on a handcart." 

Keane did not look up from Noreen's knees. "Good. Set oxygen by the hearth. Bring your transport cot in and warm it. Baby will likely need transfer at once." 

"And mother?" the other man asked.

"Mother stays till she's stable. You don't get both in one trip with those roads." 

The man glanced toward Noreen and then away, not from squeamishness but from arithmetic. He was counting distance, snow depth, fuel, and time.

Noreen was already past embarrassment. She clenched the chair back and pushed when Keane told her, not before. The room narrowed to six commands: breathe, hold, curl, push, pant, rest. Elsie's hand never left her shoulder.

She smelled singed wool and realized belatedly that Elsie had leaned too close to the fire, coat sleeve smoking at the cuff. Arthur slapped it out without comment and moved the coal shovel farther from her foot.

"Don't you dare catch fire in my house," Noreen gasped.

"Bad timing, that," Elsie said, and Noreen laughed again, then bore down into another contraction so fierce it seemed to pry her pelvis apart with iron bars.

Keane's voice sharpened. "Head's there. Slow now. No heroics. Pant."

Noreen panted, cursed, panted again. She felt the burn and then a sliding release that emptied her in one hot rush. Keane's hands moved fast. The room went quiet for half a second that lasted a year.

Then a small wet cough.

It was not a cry. It was a cough like a kitten bringing up milk.

"Baby's out," Keane said. "The baby's tiny, very tiny. Come on, little one, breathe for me." 

She rubbed the baby's back with a rough towel, suctioned the mouth and nose with a rubber bulb, rubbed again. The baby's skin was blue at the lips, red-black at the knuckles, the limbs no thicker than Elsie's wrist. Keane clamped and cut the cord, handed the child to the singed-moustache ambulance man at the warming cot, then changed her mind and took the child back to give two measured breaths through a tiny mask, counting under her breath.

"One and two. Pause. Again." 

A weak cry came then, high and reedy, and Noreen's whole body jerked toward the sound.

"Let me see." It came out hoarse, less command than plea.

Keane brought the baby close enough that Noreen could touch with two fingers. The child's foot was no larger than a matchbox, heel translucent. Noreen slid a fingertip along the sole once, as lightly as if she might tear it.

"You're here," she said. "Stay here."

The ambulance man fixed an identification band around the tiny ankle and another around Noreen's wrist, a strip of stiff paper inside plastic, numbers written in blunt pencil because ink froze. The band was too big on her from edema gone to slack. It rattled when she moved.

"Special care unit," Keane said. "They're set for prem babies tonight. You won't be with the baby straight off. Hear me? That's not abandonment. That's treatment."

Noreen nodded, tears and sweat and hair all the same wet now. "Tell them I sing off-key," she said to nobody and everybody. "Tell them the baby can complain about that later." 

Elsie bent and kissed her forehead, quick and fierce. "I'll tell them worse than that."

The ambulance men wrapped the baby in warmed blankets around a hot-water bottle tucked safe at the feet and lifted the transport cot. The weak cry faded down the hallway and out into storm. Noreen turned her face toward the door until she could not hear it at all.

Then she felt blood pour out of her.

It was warm at first, then cooling as it spread under her hips. It was too much, and it came too fast. Keane's hand was between her legs instantly, pressing, assessing.

"Placenta not complete," Keane said. "And we're flooding. Elsie, gloves now. Arthur, fresh boiling water and every clean towel left. No one panics."

No one panics, Noreen thought, and began to shake so hard her teeth knocked.

Keane's voice dropped lower, more deliberate, the cadence she used when systems failed and only hands remained.

"Noreen, listen to me. You're hemorrhaging. I need your permission to do painful things quickly."

"Do it," Noreen said.

"Say yes clearly."

"Yes. Do it."

Keane gave an injection into Noreen's thigh, ergometrine from a glass ampoule she snapped with her teeth because her fingers were wet. She massaged Noreen's uterus hard through the abdomen, knuckles pressing deep until Noreen screamed and nearly bucked off the bed.

"Stay with me," Keane said. "Pain means muscle's waking. This is good pain now. This is good pain." 

Elsie climbed onto the mattress behind Noreen, bracing her shoulders with her own body despite the weight of her belly. "Bite this," she said, pushing a folded towel to Noreen's mouth. "Not your tongue."

Noreen bit and tasted bleach and old sun from line-dried cotton. She felt pressure, pulling, another hot gush. Keane worked with relentless hands, not cruel, not kind, exactly enough.

"Placental piece retained," Keane said through clenched teeth. "I'm going in. Hold her steady."

Elsie swore again, softly, and tightened her grip. Arthur stood at the door with a stack of towels and looked as if he wanted to run through a wall for them and found no wall that would help.

He set the towels down and turned his back without leaving, giving privacy where he could in a room that had none. He stared at the wallpaper roses and recited transformer fuse sizes under his breath like prayer.

Noreen left her body for a few seconds at a time. She was on the rug in her mother's kitchen at nine years old, kneading bread with a chipped mug for a cutter. She was sixteen and laughing in the rain outside the dance hall. She was nowhere. Then Keane's hand dragged her back with brutal certainty.

"Look at me," Elsie said into her ear. "Look at my coat button. The blue one. Count the holes."

Noreen focused on the button as if it were a lighthouse lens. It had four holes, and its rim was chipped. The thread was doubled because Elsie never trusted a single line to hold. She counted through another contraction of pain that was not labor now, only survival.

Blood soaked two towels, then three. Keane packed, pressed, waited for tone, pressed again. She asked Arthur to fetch the paraffin lamp closer and then cursed when the flame guttered.

"Fuel's nearly gone," Arthur said.

"Then warm bricks in the grate and wrap them. Keep her from crashing." 

He did it at once, using tongs and old pillowcases, moving like a man handling live wires. The room smelled of hot clay and blood and paraffin smoke thick enough to sting eyes.

At last the flow slowed from gush to heavy seep. Keane kept one hand on Noreen's abdomen, feeling for firmness as if she could hold life in place by will and pressure alone. She checked Noreen's pulse at the neck, counted silently, checked again.

"You're still here," Keane said.

Noreen opened her eyes. "Would've annoyed you if I wasn't," she whispered.

"Very much," Keane said.

Elsie laughed once and then had to sit down hard on the chair because dizziness washed over her. Keane saw it immediately.

"You all right?"

"Just stood too quick." Elsie pressed a palm to her own belly until it passed. "Baby kicked. Didn't like the shouting."

"Your baby can file complaints later," Keane said, echoing Noreen without smiling. "Drink this." 

She handed Elsie sweet tea gone lukewarm. Elsie drank and went back to Noreen's side.

It took another forty minutes for a second ambulance to claw its way into Barrow Lane. The men brought plasma and a stretcher, swearing at the drift in language stripped of poetry. Keane gave report in clipped sequence, each point exact: premature delivery, neonatal cyanosis, transfer initiated, postpartum hemorrhage estimated over a liter, uterotonics given, retained tissue removed, pulse thready then improving, immediate hospital admission required.

They shifted Noreen carefully onto the stretcher. The mattress beneath her looked butchered. Elsie could not stop staring at the shape her blood had made, a dark map branching out toward the bed edge like river deltas in school atlases.

Keane snapped her fingers once near Elsie's face. "Stay with me. Help me with blankets." 

They layered Noreen in wool and two coats. The plastic transfer band knocked against the stretcher rail each time the men lifted, a hard dry clack that sounded indecently small after all that blood.

At the front hall, the door burst inward with another gust and Peter stumbled in, glasses fogged, overcoat crusted white to the thigh. His cast was wrapped in a grocery bag tied with string. He took in the stretcher, the blood on Keane's apron, Elsie's coat, Arthur's face, and his own house reduced to triage.

"What has happened?" he said. Then, louder, "What has happened here?"

Noreen tried to answer and only coughed.

Peter moved toward the stretcher, already assembling authority around himself like a coat. "I am her husband. You should have waited for me before making transfer decisions."

Keane stepped between him and the medics without raising her voice. "You were unavailable. Labor established at approximately two fifteen. Fetal distress worsened by three forty. Infant transferred at four eighteen. Maternal hemorrhage at four twenty-three. I documented each intervention and witness."

Peter's jaw set. "You had no legal right to remove my child from this address without my consent."

"I had clinical duty to prevent death," Keane said. "Your child needed oxygen and heated transport. Your wife needed to not bleed out on this floor." 

He turned to Noreen. "Nora, tell her to stop speaking to me as if I am a bystander."

Noreen looked at him through half-lidded eyes, skin grey with exhaustion. She lifted her wrist with effort and showed him the transfer bracelet.

"You are a bystander," she said. Her voice rasped, but each word landed. "Tonight you are."

Peter stared at the band, then at Elsie, as if he could reorder the room by deciding where to place blame first. He drew himself up, grammar tightening with rage.

"This household has been managed in my absence by people who do not understand consequence. I will address that when she is stabilized."

Arthur spoke before Elsie could. "Consequence is she and the baby are alive. That's what was managed." 

Peter wheeled on him. "You are a lodger under emergency order. Keep to your assigned status."

Arthur shrugged once, tired. "Assigned status tonight was boil water and not let them die. I kept to it." 

Keane handed a folded sheet to one of the ambulance men, then another to Peter. "Here is the transfer destination and the neonatal identifier. Visiting will be restricted until mother is fit. Here is my report copy. If you challenge any part of timeline, challenge me directly at clinic in writing." 

Peter took the paper with his good hand and did not look at it.

"You are overstepping," he said.

"No," Keane said. "I'm stepping exactly where your wife needed somebody to stand."

The stretcher moved. Noreen caught Elsie's sleeve as they passed, fingers weak but insistent.

"Don't let him tidy this," she whispered. "Don't let him turn it into numbers."

Elsie bent close. "I won't. I'll go to the hospital as soon as roads open. I'll bring you news if they won't let you travel." 

Noreen's mouth twitched, an attempt at her usual crooked smile that only half arrived. "Bring proper tea. Hospital tea tastes like mop water." 

"I'll bring leaves, not bags," Elsie said.

The ambulance men carried Noreen into wind and white grit. The door shut behind them on a sudden silence that felt obscene after hours of commands and cries.

Peter stood in the hallway holding papers that shook in his hand despite all his effort to appear still. On the table beside him lay the basin of bloodied towels, the clipped cord, Keane's empty ampoule wrappers. Proof refused to become polite.

Keane stripped off her gloves and dropped them into the scuttle. "Mr Walsh, the fire must stay up. Pipes will freeze if it falls and she'll return to a broken house when she is discharged. You will clean that bedframe with boiled water and carbolic. Don't let neighbors in to gawp. Do you understand?"

He looked at her as if she had insulted his lineage. "I understand instructions perfectly well."

"Good. Then follow these."

She turned to Elsie. "You need to lie down before you fall down. Your face is paper." 

"Not yet, I won't." Elsie was already gathering clean cloths and a bucket. "Not while he's standing there like an inspector."

Peter made a small disbelieving noise. "Mrs Harrow, I would advise restraint in your tone."

Elsie did not look at him. She wrung out a cloth in boiling water and laid it over the mattress stain. Steam rose pink. "I'd advise you to heat your front room before your wife comes home," she said. "Advice for advice." 

Peter opened his mouth. Keane cut him off.

"Enough. She's not your employee." 

For a long second no one moved. Wind rattled the letterbox. Somewhere down the lane a child cried and was hushed. Then Peter stepped aside because there was nowhere else for him to step, and because he had just watched three people do work he had always claimed as his right to direct.

Arthur carried the coal bucket in from the yard and set it down with a grunt. "Last of it," he said. "I'll queue at Bhatti's when she opens, if she opens." 

Keane nodded. "Put it on Peter's tab and mine if needed." 

Peter flinched at that, tiny but visible. It was money again. It was always money. Elsie saw it and filed it away, not out of malice but because this was how the future would be negotiated: receipts, signatures, who stood where at four in the morning when blood was on the floor.

Keane washed her hands in water gone rust-red and wrote one final note on a ruled card, pressing hard enough to tear the paper at the corners. She left the card weighted under the sugar tin where Peter could not pretend he had not seen it.

Before she left for the clinic she crouched by Elsie's chair and spoke low.

"You did well. You followed direction under pressure. If anybody questions that, send them to me."

Elsie nodded. Her hands had begun to tremble now that there was time for tremor. "Will the baby make it?"

Keane did not offer kindness she could not guarantee. "The baby reached special care alive and breathing. That's where certainty ends for tonight." 

She stood, pulled on her boots, and lifted the canvas bag that seemed heavier now though half its supplies were gone. At the door she paused.

"Sleep in shifts," she told Arthur and Elsie. "One ear on the phone if line returns. He'll try to run this as narrative. Don't let him erase sequence." 

Then she was out into the white dark again.

Arthur banked the fire. Peter went into the front parlor and shut the door with measured care, as if gentleness with hinges might repair the rest. Elsie stayed in the bedroom alone a moment longer, cloth in hand, staring at the transfer bracelet number copied on Keane's scrap: tiny digits, blunt pencil, proof of a life carried out before sunrise.

She pressed the cloth harder against the mattress until her shoulders hurt. Through the wall she heard Peter on the telephone at last, voice low and formal, giving names and dates, trying to build the night into a ledger he could survive.

In the bed, the hollow where Noreen had labored was still warm.

Elsie laid her palm there and kept it there until the heat went thin.
