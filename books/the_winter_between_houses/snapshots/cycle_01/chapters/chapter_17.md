# Chapter 17

By midafternoon the snowbanks had lost their shape and turned into a dirty, collapsing wall along Barrow Lane. Water ran under them in brown veins. The gutter outside Elsie’s front step had been full since breakfast and now it had risen over the iron lip, spread across the lane, and started feeling for lower ground. Every few minutes there was a heavy slap from the eaves as another sheet of meltwater dropped and smacked the paving stones.

She had rolled two old towels and pushed them hard against the back door, then three flour sacks over those, and the water still found a way in. It came cold and thin and determined, a steady silver line under the warped wood, making the flags shine. When she bent to wring out the first towel, her belly tightened so hard she had to brace her palm on the table and wait for the line of pain to finish writing itself from her spine to her pubic bone.

She stayed still until the pain dropped away. Then she looked at the clock over the mantel, not because that one was right but because it was louder than the kitchen clock and she could hear it over the gutters. The second hand dragged its red tip around and around while she breathed through her nose and told herself it might still be the kind of practice pain that warned and then went quiet.

The next one came nine minutes later and stole her breath halfway through lifting the coal scuttle.

“Not now,” she said to the room, to the water, to the child. Her voice sounded too polite for what was happening.

She set the scuttle down, both hands on its rim, and waited until she could straighten. Across the wall she could hear nothing from the Walsh side but a faint, regular tapping that might have been Arthur in the spare room knocking rust off something metal. Peter had kept the front curtains closed most days since Noreen left for Saint Brigid House. The house next door looked occupied by furniture and rules, not people.

Elsie wiped her hands on her apron, pulled on her coat, and stepped into the lane with one hand under her belly. The water was above her ankles at once, cold enough to numb and then sting. She made for Mrs Bhatti’s shop because the telephone in the back there still worked when the lines in the terraces did not, and because she knew she could not stand in her own kitchen waiting for the next pain without doing something that looked like action.

The lane smelled of wet ash and rot where the snow had been holding everything down for months and had suddenly decided to give it back. Coal smoke lay low, heavy in the damp. Somebody had set two kitchen chairs in a doorway as a makeshift barrier and one had already floated sideways, wedged against a drain cover.

Mrs Bhatti was at the till with her sleeves rolled to her elbows and a tea towel around her waist. She had taken off her cardigan and hung it over a hook above the bread crates. The muscles in her forearms looked harder than the butcher’s.

“You should not be out,” she said as soon as Elsie came in dripping. “Sit. I will ring.”

“It’s starting,” Elsie said. She hated how flat it sounded. “Can you get Nurse Keane?”

Mrs Bhatti had already lifted the receiver and was thumbing the dial. “Clinic first. Then district board. Then your husband.”

Elsie leaned on the counter while the ringing buzzed in her ear. The contraction came in front of witnesses this time. She shut her eyes and gripped the edge of the sweet jar rack. Through the glass she watched her own hand whiten around striped barley sticks.

Mrs Bhatti did not touch her. She stood close enough to block view from the door, her body making a wall, and spoke into the phone with clipped certainty. “Yes. Mrs Harrow, Barrow Lane. Established pains. Thaw flood in the road. No, I’m not saying she can travel.” She listened, nodded, then held the receiver out. “Keane.”

Keane’s voice came through crackling and distant, as if she were standing under rain. “Elsie, listen carefully. How far apart?”

“Nine minutes. Then eight. Maybe less. I can’t keep still through them.”

“Any bleeding?”

“No. Not like before.”

“Good. Stay in the house. Keep warm. Get clean sheets, basin, kettle on. I’m coming from Saint Brigid side and I’ll bring Noreen if she’s up to it. Don’t try the clinic. The low bridge is under water and they’re turning ambulances back.”

The line hissed.

“And Tom?” Elsie asked before she could stop herself.

A pause, then Keane again, practical as a cupboard latch. “If he’s useful, send for him. If he’s not, we proceed anyway.”

The line clicked dead.

Mrs Bhatti set a hand lightly between Elsie’s shoulder blades when the next pain came. “My Rafiq will carry your coal up while you wait. He can run a message to Tom’s brother too.”

Elsie nodded because nodding was easier than saying thank you. She had not seen Tom in four days. The last word from him had come through a boy from the depot: sleeping at his brother’s, sorting shifts, back soon. Back soon had stretched and thinned and then vanished.

By the time Elsie got home with Rafiq sloshing behind her carrying half a sack of coal against his chest, the water had reached the first brick of the doorstep. Arthur was outside the Walsh house with his trousers rolled and boots unlaced, clearing sticks from a blocked drain with the handle of a screwdriver.

He looked up, saw her face, and dropped the tool at once. “It’s started?”

She nodded.

“I’ll do your sandbags,” he said. “Then I’ll go for whoever you want.”

Arthur always spoke like he was offering a wrench, not his opinion. Even now his voice had that linesman habit of no waste in it.

“Tom first,” Elsie said. “Brother’s flat on Wakefield Road. If he’s not there, check the Fox and Crown.”

Arthur gave one short nod. “I’ll take the utility bike. Might make it if the culvert isn’t gone.” He hesitated, then added, almost shy, “I’ve got spare dry blankets in the Walsh airing cupboard. I’ll bring them.”

Inside, the house felt too warm near the range and too cold by the floor, a split climate that made her shiver while sweat ran down her ribs. She stripped the bed, carried sheets to the front room, and folded them with care she did not have for anything else. During each contraction she counted nail heads in the skirting board to keep from thinking ahead.

Noreen came with Keane just before dusk, both of them wet to the thigh and breathing hard. Noreen had tied a scarf over her hair but loose strands had escaped and stuck to her cheekbones. She still wore the narrow plastic hospital bracelet from her own baby’s transfer, turned inward on her wrist so the label did not show. Her coat bulged with folded cloths and two bottles wrapped in newspaper.

“You brought half the linen cupboard,” Elsie said, trying for lightness.

Noreen kicked the door shut with her heel. “I brought what mattered.” Her smile was quick, then gone when Elsie bent over another pain. “Ah, love, that one’s sharp. Lean on me.”

Keane had already dropped her canvas bag on the table and was snapping open metal clasps. She set out a blood pressure cuff, forceps wrapped in boiled cloth, a bottle of disinfectant, cord ties, notebook, pen. Her wedding ring was looped on a thin chain around her neck, tucked out of the way inside her blouse.

“Get your coat off,” Keane said. “We need your pulse before you pretend you’re fine.”

“I’m fine.”

“You are laboring in a flood. You are not fine. Sit.”

Elsie sat.

Keane’s hands were rough and steady. She took blood pressure, timed two contractions with a watch whose glass was cracked through one corner, listened low with the Pinard horn, then checked dilation with brisk economy and no apology.

“Four centimeters. Proper labor. Baby’s head is down and engaged. We stay put.” She glanced toward the window, where floodwater reflected a trembling strip of sky. “No carriage, no car, no ambulance is getting through tonight. We make this room work.”

Noreen had already dragged the narrow mattress from Elsie’s bedroom and laid it near the range, covered first with oilcloth, then folded blankets, then the clean sheet Elsie had ironed two weeks ago when waiting had still felt like a controllable thing.

Elsie watched the setup as if it belonged to another woman. “Tom should be here,” she said quietly.

Noreen looked at her and did not lie. “He should.”

Keane tied her apron tighter. “He can hold the basin if he arrives. He is not required for delivery.”

Another contraction took Elsie by surprise and folded her over the table edge. She heard herself make a short animal sound she had not planned. Noreen was beside her at once, palm hard on her lower back in circles that found the one place the pain would answer to pressure.

“Breathe with me,” Noreen said. “In, out, in, out. You’ve done hard things before. This is one more and we do it together.”

Elsie breathed. She felt Noreen’s hand trembling from exertion and held still anyway.

Arthur came back once with sandbags and blankets, then left again for Tom before full dark. Keane sent him with a note for the civil defense post and another for the clinic to say home confinement had been initiated under flood obstruction. She dictated as she wrote, each word precise. The habit calmed Elsie more than soothing did.

Outside, the water kept climbing.

***

By nine o’clock the lane was a moving brown sheet lit by window lamps and the occasional sweep of a torch. Water touched the bottom rail of Elsie’s front door and made it thud in its frame every time a bigger wave came down from the top of the street. The gutters did not sound like gutters anymore. They sounded like kettles poured out continuously.

Keane kept time and sequence. Empty bladder. Sip water. Change position. Rest jaw. Count through the peak. She gave instructions in short chains, then explained when Elsie demanded a reason. That was how Elsie knew she was scared: she kept asking why for things she already understood.

“Because if you lock your jaw your pelvic floor locks too.”

“Because if you lie flat your back labor worsens.”

“Because pain does not mean danger by itself.”

Noreen stayed close enough for Elsie to smell the sour-sweet milk stain drying on her blouse. Between contractions, Noreen squeezed milk by hand into one of the wrapped bottles and set it near the coolest part of the room, face turned away for privacy she no longer got much of. She apologized once for taking time.

“Don’t,” Elsie said. “Feed him when you can. You don’t need my permission.”

Noreen gave a short laugh with no humor in it. “I know I don’t. I still ask.”

When the next contraction rose, Elsie bit down on a clean corner of sheet and pushed her forehead into Noreen’s shoulder. Noreen smelled of rain, soap, and the yeasty ghost of the bakery where she had worked before marriage put her indoors full-time.

At ten Arthur returned alone, soaked through and breathing like he had been running stairs.

“Brother’s flat was dark,” he said from the doorway, careful not to drip onto the clean linen. “Neighbor said he hadn’t seen Tom for two nights. Fox and Crown closed early because the cellar flooded. No sign.”

Elsie gripped the chair back until her knuckles hurt.

“Did you check the depot?”

“Gatekeeper said he’s not rostered this week. Didn’t know where he was sleeping.”

The contraction hit then, huge, and she could not answer. She leaned over the chair, knees bent, and felt the pain take all language from her body and replace it with pressure. Noreen counted her through it. Keane’s hand was on her belly, feeling the contraction and the baby’s position at once.

When it passed, Elsie looked at Arthur and heard herself say, “Don’t go again.”

Arthur nodded once. “Right.”

It landed in her then with a clean, cold certainty: Tom was not delayed. Tom was absent. It was not weather. It was not roads. It was him.

She had thought she was past the part where she waited for him with a table laid and excuses ready. Labor stripped that thought down to bone. Every minute of pain made the waiting look stupid.

Keane sent Arthur to the scullery with a bucket and bleach to keep busy and out of the way. He obeyed without offence, sleeves rolled, moving like a man who had learned the value of being told exactly what to do in a crisis.

Near midnight Elsie’s waters broke in a warm flood that ran down her thighs and pooled dark on the oilcloth. She looked down and saw a green-brown stain mixed in, thin but there.

Keane saw it too. “A little meconium. Not heavy. We keep moving.”

“Is she all right?” Elsie asked.

“She’s coping. You keep coping and we meet her.”

The answer was honest enough to hold.

Contractions came every three minutes then two. Time stopped being clock and became pattern: rise, hold, crest, tear, drop. In the drops she shook, teeth chattering though sweat soaked her shift. Keane checked dilation again and said, “Eight. Nearly there.”

“Nearly where?” Elsie said, and then laughed once because the question was pointless.

Noreen wiped her face with a flannel cooled in basin water and wrung it out with both hands. Her own hands had the faint yellow tint from chlorhexidine that never quite left after hospital visits. Elsie noticed everything because there was nothing else to do between pain storms but notice.

At one point the electricity dipped. The lamp in the hall dimmed to orange thread, then recovered. Arthur appeared with two hurricane lamps lit, set them on high shelves out of splash range, and vanished again without needing thanks.

Pressure changed. It stopped being all-around pain and narrowed into a downward force that made Elsie bear down before she chose to.

“Not yet,” Keane said. “Pant through it. Blow.”

“I can’t.”

“You can. Short breaths. Like this.”

Noreen demonstrated, quick shallow puffs, eyes locked to Elsie’s. “Copy me. Come on. You are not alone in this room.”

Elsie copied because copying was easier than arguing. The urge passed. It came back stronger.

Keane checked once more. “Ten. Good. Now you push when I say and not before. On your side first.”

Elsie rolled with help, one leg braced on Noreen’s thigh. The first push burned but did not move enough. The second moved something deep and final. Keane’s voice cut through rain and gutter roar.

“Again. Chin down. Put it where it hurts.”

Elsie pushed and felt her body split around the child’s head. She yelled, a raw sound that startled even her.

“There,” Keane said. “Head is there. Stop. Pant. Good girl.”

The phrase should have annoyed her, but Keane said it like a worker praising another worker, not a priest blessing a penitent.

She panted. She felt stretch, sting, then release as shoulders rotated. The room narrowed to three faces and one task.

With the next contraction she bore down and the baby slid free in a wet, shocking rush into Keane’s hands.

Silence for half a second.

Then a rough, outraged cry tore through the room.

Elsie started crying at once without deciding to. She laughed and cried together, chest hitching. Noreen made a sound like a prayer breaking in the middle.

“Girl,” Keane said. “Strong voice. Good tone.”

Keane suctioned mouth and nose, rubbed the baby’s back with a dry cloth until color deepened from dusky to red, then laid her slippery and furious on Elsie’s chest. Heat bloomed where skin met skin. The baby’s fists were the size of walnuts, clenched so tight the knuckles blanched.

Elsie touched her with two fingers first, as if checking whether she was allowed.

“You are here,” she whispered. “You are actually here.”

Noreen tucked the sheet around both of them and pressed her lips to Elsie’s damp temple, quick and firm, before stepping back to hand Keane cord ties.

The work did not end with crying. Keane watched for placenta, measured blood on pads by eye and touch, palpated uterus until it hardened under her palm. Elsie hissed when cramps came with each massage.

“Stay with me,” Keane said. “Placenta needs to come complete. Push once more.”

Elsie did, exhausted and shaking. Keane inspected the placenta under lamp light, turning it with two gloved hands.

“Intact,” she said. “No retained bits. Bleeding is acceptable.”

Acceptable. The word felt miraculous.

When Keane finally sat back on her heels, it was past three and the rain had thinned to a steady drizzle. Floodwater still moved in the lane, but slower now, less angry.

Arthur knocked softly and asked through the door if they needed anything from the relief van parked at the top road.

Keane answered before Elsie could. “Tea if they have it. Sugar for the mother.”

Arthur said, “Right,” and went.

Elsie looked at the child on her chest, then at Noreen. “He never came.”

Noreen met her eyes. “I know.”

There was no softening left in either of them for the fact.

***

Dawn came as a gray dilution in the window glass. The lane looked flayed open, every rut and pothole visible under shallow moving water. Someone upstream had lost a coal bin lid and it drifted past like a black raft. A pair of children in rolled trousers poked at floating debris with broom handles until a woman shouted them inside.

The baby rooted and fussed against Elsie’s chest. Keane helped position her to feed, guiding tiny mouth to nipple with fingers gentler than her voice ever was. Pain bit hard at first latch and then eased into a heavy tug.

“She knows what she’s doing,” Keane said. “You both do.”

Elsie watched the small jaw working and felt a terror so sharp it was almost clean: this could still be taken, even now. By fever, by flood, by a signature in the wrong hand.

Keane packed used instruments into a tin with boiled water, then opened her notebook on her knee. “We need a plan if roads stay blocked and you deteriorate, or if baby needs transfer before you can travel. Who’s your emergency contact for consent if you’re unconscious?”

Elsie stared at her. The question sounded administrative but carried a blade inside it.

“By default it would be husband,” Keane said, not unkindly. “Unless you specify otherwise in writing. Given current circumstances, I’m asking now while you’re alert.”

Current circumstances. A phrase that included everything without saying betrayal out loud.

Noreen had moved to the table to wash cloths in a basin, sleeves pushed up, forearms thin and marked with new needle bruises from recent blood draws. She kept her eyes on the water as if giving Elsie privacy and witness both.

Arthur returned with a dented kettle from the relief van and a loaf wrapped in ration paper. He set them down and stepped back, cap in both hands. His knuckles were cracked in red grids, deeper where old frost splits had reopened in the damp.

“Road chief says no vehicles down here till afternoon,” he said. “If rain comes hard again, maybe longer.”

Keane nodded and kept writing timings from memory: full dilation, delivery time, placenta complete, estimated blood loss.

Elsie looked toward the mantel where her wedding photograph still stood in its cheap frame, tilted because one corner of the backing had warped. Tom in his good suit, grin too broad for the camera. Her own face younger, tighter around the mouth.

She felt no impulse to protect him anymore. Labor had burned that reflex off.

“Give me paper,” she said.

Keane looked up. “For what?”

“For what you asked.”

Keane tore a clean sheet from the back of her notebook and slid it over with a fountain pen. “State your full name, child, and instruction. Date and time. I will witness.”

Elsie shifted the baby to Noreen, who took her with both hands and an expression Elsie had only seen once before, when Noreen first held her own son before transfer and would not blink in case blinking counted as looking away.

Elsie sat at the table. Her legs shook from effort and blood loss, but her hand stayed steady enough once she started.

_I, Elsie Harrow of 14 Barrow Lane, being in full possession of my mind after delivery, direct that if I am unable to give consent for my daughter, Noreen Walsh is to act as emergency guardian for medical and immediate care decisions until I recover._

She stopped, then added one line more.

_This instruction is made because my husband, Thomas Harrow, is absent and not reliably reachable._

She signed with the same careful script she used on cleaning ledgers and school forms.

Keane read it once, lips moving. “You understand this may provoke dispute later.”

“Yes.”

“You understand I can attach it to my clinical record and notify clinic intake when transport opens.”

“Yes.”

Keane signed beneath as witness, date and time precise to the minute. Her pen scratched hard on the paper. She blotted the ink with gauze, folded the note once, and slipped it into a waterproof sleeve from her bag.

Noreen stood by the stove with the baby at her shoulder, swaying from habit more than intention. Her own body still moved cautiously, protecting a core that had not healed. Milk had soaked through one side of her blouse again in a pale crescent.

“You are sure?” she asked quietly.

Elsie met her gaze. “I’m done waiting for men to arrive in time for decisions. I was sure the first contraction that took me to my knees.”

Arthur had turned his face away to the window, giving them dignity, but his ears reddened. In a lane of thin walls, nothing stayed private for long.

Keane closed her bag with a metallic click. “Then that is the instruction. I’ll state it to relief control as part of handover, and I’ll repeat it at clinic. If anyone challenges, they challenge me first.”

There was power in that, not legal in the grand sense, but immediate and practical, the only kind that had mattered all winter.

A knock came at the door. Mrs Bhatti stood on the step in gumboots, skirt hem soaked, holding a parcel of clean nappies wrapped in newspaper.

“I boiled these at dawn,” she said. Her eyes went from Elsie to the child to Noreen and then to Keane’s open notebook on the table. She missed nothing. “Do you need more coal?”

“We need quiet and dry cloths,” Keane said.

“You will have both.” Mrs Bhatti set the parcel down, lowered her voice, and added to Elsie, “My boy says your husband was not at his brother’s yesterday either. If anyone asks, I will say what is true: women got this child born.”

She left before thanks could become sentiment.

Keane rose to check the door seals and sandbags once more. Noreen brought the baby back and settled her against Elsie, careful of the tender stitches and afterpain.

Elsie looked at her daughter’s face, crumpled and furious and alive, then at the note tucked safe in Keane’s bag.

It was not law enough to protect them from everything that could still come. It was enough to mark the line of who stood where when the water rose.

Outside, runoff kept moving down Barrow Lane toward the river, carrying winter away in cold brown sheets. Inside, the room held new heat: kettle steam, milk breath, damp wool, and the stubborn pulse of two women who had learned to choose each other before the world approved.

When the baby fell asleep at last, mouth parted, hand unclenching on Elsie’s skin, Elsie let her own eyes close for one minute exactly.

She did not pray for Tom to appear.
