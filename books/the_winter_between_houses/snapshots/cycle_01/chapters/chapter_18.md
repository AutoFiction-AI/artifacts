# Chapter 18

The thaw had a sound she had forgotten was possible: not wind, not wheels, not men shouting down the lane, but steady dripping from every eave on Barrow Lane, a patient tapping that went on before dawn and kept on after dinner as if the houses had started leaking time. Elsie woke to it with the baby on her chest, damp hair stuck to her own skin, the room smelling of milk and stale coal and the iron tang she still carried between her legs ten days after labor. She eased herself upright, biting down when the pull in her belly caught, and sat until the room settled into one shape. The child made a thin goatish complaint, opened her mouth, and rooted blindly into Elsie's shirt.

In the kitchen, envelopes had swelled in the damp and curled off the table edge. The top one was from Calderbank Baby Goods, the red print feathered at the corners where meltwater had got into the hall and then dried again. Under it lay a note from Saint Brigid House, folded sharp enough to cut. The last was from Mrs. Bhatti, not a bill this time, only a line in blue pencil: *Tell me if you need lentils before Thursday van. Do not wait.*

Elsie set the kettle, hooked her thumb under the baby's nappy tie, and checked it by touch. She had stopped being surprised by how often she touched everything now to know if it was wet, too cold, missing, crusted, split, emptied. Her fingers had become instruments. The first note she opened was the shop one because she trusted the hand that wrote it.

Outside, a shovel scraped and then knocked on stone three times. Arthur Hale stood in the Walsh front garden in his board jacket, the knees gone shiny, rolling extension cord around his elbow in neat loops. The council van idled at the curb with mud up to its wheel hubs. He looked up when Elsie pushed the lace curtain aside and gave her a nod that meant both morning and goodbye.

He came over ten minutes later when she left the door on latch for Noreen. He had his kitbag on his shoulder and the tobacco tin in his left hand where he kept spare screws, fuses, and mints all in one rattle. A peppermint sat in his cheek and moved when he spoke.

"Council pulled the billet order," he said. "Road teams are back. They want us on the high lines near Dewsbury before the afternoon melt. I left two dry sockets in your scullery and one in next door. Keep anything electric off the floorboards till the drain clears."

Elsie held the baby closer because the hall still breathed cold up through the tiles. "You eating before you go?"

"I had bread in the van. Proper treat." He gave a short smile, glanced at the child, and pulled one knitted glove halfway off, as if asking permission with his hands before touching anything. "She looks sturdier than yesterday."

"She's loud, anyway."

"That helps." He tapped the doorframe with two fingers, habit more than fidget, then looked toward the Walsh side where water had pooled at the step. "I laid a plank over that rut. It'll float if it fills, so test it before you commit your full weight."

He spoke like that even in farewell, in fixes and warnings, offering what could be done in the next half hour and not one inch beyond. He smelled of wet wool and metal filings. When he reached into his breast pocket for the key, he took out a folded photograph by mistake first, saw it, and slid it back without looking at it. Elsie pretended not to notice. He did not talk about himself unless a question was strictly practical.

"Tell Noreen I'll drop her suitcase at Saint Brigid after shift if she wants," he said. "The green one with the busted wheel. It's still in my room."

"I'll tell her."

"Right." He stepped back, then added, almost shyly, "And if Tom turns up and starts something, send for me through Mrs. Bhatti. She knows where our crews are this week."

The van took him away in a spray of dirty water. Elsie watched until it turned at the end of the lane. When she closed the door again, the house felt both safer and less defended, as if one layer of witness had been stripped off.

Noreen knocked with her heel because both her hands were full: a string bag of jars, a wrapped loaf, and her own satchel with the strap darkened where rain had soaked through. She came in breathless, face paler than her old winter pallor, hair tucked under a scarf that had once been cream. The edema had gone from her ankles, but blood loss had left its own mark. She moved carefully at first, then too fast in little bursts, as if she could trick her body into not requiring recovery if she kept it occupied.

"I've brought the heel of yesterday's soda bread and one decent onion," she said. "Don't praise me, I charmed it out of Sister Pauline by claiming you had no appetite."

"I don't have one."

"Exactly. I did not lie."

She set the bag down, kissed the baby's forehead with lips dry from the cold, and then held out her right hand. Around her finger hung the plastic hospital bracelet from the special care nursery, the tiny tag creased where she'd bent it too many times.

"They're changing visiting hours again," she said. "That's what this morning was. More rules."

Elsie poured tea before asking because tea gave her something to do with her eyes. "How bad?"

"Depends if you're fond of humiliation before noon." Noreen took the cup and cupped it with both hands, inhaling steam like medicine. "The matron says I can keep my bed and still go to the nursery if I produce three things: a priest's note, a fixed address, and proof I'm not running loose with disreputable company."

She gave the last phrase with bright mockery and then looked at Elsie's face and softened. "Her words were kinder than that, but not by much."

Before Elsie could answer, another knock came. Three hard raps, measured and official. The baby startled and spat milk down Elsie's sleeve.

The man on the step wore a black raincoat and shoes too clean for Barrow Lane. A leather folder sat under his arm, edges protected by a clear plastic sleeve. He introduced himself as Mr. Yates from Calderbank Baby Goods and asked, without greeting, "Mrs. Harrow?"

Elsie stood in the doorway with the child on her shoulder. "Yes."

"I have called regarding the arrears account. Twelve pounds and eight shillings outstanding on your pram hire-purchase. We posted warning in January and notice in February. Our warehouse has released spring stock. Management is now reviewing delinquent contracts for recovery."

His gaze dropped to the infant, then to the hallway where the pram chassis stood against the wall, one wheel tied with string where the rubber had split.

"You intend to recover that?" Elsie asked.

"I intend to prevent court escalation," he said. "If we can agree schedule today."

Noreen had come up behind Elsie without noise. "You can agree schedule with her and leave the speeches in your folder," she said pleasantly. "She's just fed."

He ignored her. "The contract is in both names. Your husband has not responded to summons by post."

"He's not here," Elsie said.

"Then he should be found."

The baby began to cry in earnest, long outraged pulls of breath, and Elsie felt her own body answer in ache and leak under her blouse. She shifted the child, looked at the man, and said, "Tell me what payment keeps you from turning up with bailiffs while I'm bleeding on my own floor."

Mr. Yates blinked once. It was the first expression that looked human on him.

"One pound ten this week, and one pound weekly thereafter, reviewed at month's end."

"I haven't got one pound ten this week."

"Then I file non-cooperation."

Noreen put her tea down on the hall table with a small click. "She has one pound today," she said. "No more. You'll take that and write the week as goodwill, because any magistrate you bring this to will ask why your company sent a collector to a postpartum woman before she could stand straight."

Yates turned to her then, annoyed now, and took stock: hostel coat, parish badge pinned crooked, wet shoes leaving marks on the tile. "And you are?"

"A witness with a good memory."

For a second Elsie thought he might refuse on pride alone. Then he opened the folder, drew out a printed schedule, and took a fountain pen from behind his ear.

"One pound today," he said. "Then one pound weekly every Thursday by four o'clock. Miss two installments and we proceed."

Elsie fetched the coin purse from the tea caddy shelf. The coins were warm from the stove shelf where she'd hidden them in an old sock. She counted them into his palm while the baby screamed against her neck. He wrote the receipt in cramped block letters and tore off the top copy. His cuff carried a frayed stitch at the wrist. He tucked the pound away with hands that shook slightly. It struck Elsie that he was likely under his own numbers somewhere, sent down lanes like theirs to keep his seat.

"Good day," he said, and stepped back into the rain.

Noreen shut the door with her hip. "I would've liked five minutes alone with him and his warehouse spring stock."

"You got us a week." Elsie set the baby down in the basket by the range and pressed her hand to her own breast until the ache eased. "That's better than liking anything."

They sat at the table with the receipt between them like a medical report. Elsie took out Arthur's old outage map, turned it blank side up, and drew lines with a blunt pencil.

"Right," she said. "Spring terms. Proper this time. We can't keep winging it by whoever panics first."

Noreen nodded. "Say it and I'll argue where needed."

Elsie wrote headings in block letters because that made the page feel less like fear.

**Money. Heat. Babies. Beds. Witnesses.**

"Money first," she said. "I've got six shillings till Friday after Yates. Mrs. Bhatti is carrying us for lentils and bread but I'm not stretching her tab past Easter. I can start with Mrs. Danvers two mornings next week if I can stand and if she doesn't flinch at blood on the mop water."

"I'll do your stairs on those mornings and say it's for me, not charity." Noreen rubbed the nursery bracelet with her thumb until the plastic squeaked. "Saint Brigid takes one-third of any wages if they call it board contribution. Cash in hand is safer if we can manage it."

"Heat."

"I'm still at the hostel nights. Curfew's half eight, door bolts at nine. Sister Pauline keeps the key on a chain at her belt like she's jailer in a play, but she does it because two girls got followed back last month. She's not wrong."

"Can you stay here some evenings?"

"If I sign out and back by curfew, yes. Not every day. They watch patterns."

Elsie nodded, adding marks. The baby grunted in her basket and waved one fist as if objecting to paperwork.

"Babies," Elsie said, softer. "Mine's here. Yours is in that nursery with glass between you and skin. What do they actually allow?"

"At the moment? Thirty minutes if a nurse likes your face. Longer if your husband arrives and behaves. No evenings if bus is late. No carrying unless weight's up and doctor says."

"And Peter?"

Noreen's laugh came out thin. "Peter says he'll 'consider cooperation' if I come home and stop making scenes. He had that phrase ready in his mouth before I sat down."

Elsie wrote Peter's name and circled it hard enough to tear the paper. "We'll need his signature for now. That's the law, not consent."

"I know."

A shadow crossed the frosted glass of the door. Then a softer knock, two taps and a pause. Sister Agnes from Saint Brigid stood there in a navy coat shiny at the elbows, a knitted beret pinned too tight, and a paper parcel under her arm. She smelled of lavender soap and damp cellar stone. When she talked, she sorted rosary beads in her pocket with fast practiced fingers, one bead per clause.

"Mrs. Harrow, forgive the intrusion," she said. "Mrs. Walsh, Matron asked me to deliver your amended attendance card."

Noreen took the card but did not invite her in. Sister Agnes looked past her anyway and saw the map-paper budget spread on the table.

"I should remind you," she said gently, eyes staying on Noreen, "that Saint Brigid expects residents to pursue reconciliation where possible. The parish is willing to support legal mediation and spiritual counsel. There are concerns in the neighborhood about appearances."

"Neighborhood has concerns about everything," Noreen said. "Last week Mrs. Coates told me floodwater was God's verdict on laundry left overnight."

Sister Agnes almost smiled, then steadied. "This is not about gossip alone. Nursery access paperwork must show stability. The hospital has tightened safeguarding after the January abduction rumor."

"Rumor," Noreen repeated.

"Rumor, yes. But policies follow fear."

Elsie felt anger come up clean and hot, without fuzz. "What does Noreen have to do to see her own baby?"

Sister Agnes met her gaze now. "She must remain resident at Saint Brigid, keep curfew, attend weekly interview with Father Doyle, and avoid overnight stays outside approved addresses. If husband contests, case conference may require his attendance."

"So she can live apart," Elsie said.

"Conditionally."

"Then that's what she's doing."

Sister Agnes nodded once, then held out the paper parcel. "For the baby," she said. "One wool cap. It came from donation box, but I mended the seam myself. It should keep ears warm in this wet wind."

The cap was yellow with one green stripe and a tiny darn near the crown where someone else's name had once been stitched. The detail landed harder than the warnings. Noreen took the parcel and said, with no irony at all, "Thank you."

After she left, they listened to her shoes squelch down the path, then to Mrs. Coates' gate creak across the lane and not close for a suspiciously long time.

"Witnesses," Elsie said, pointing at the last heading. "Arthur's gone. Keane's still ours. Mrs. Bhatti if needed. Sister Agnes maybe, depending which way the wind's blowing."

"And each other," Noreen said.

Elsie looked at her. "That's not maybe."

Noreen sat back, one hand on the table edge, knuckles pale. "Say it plain then. If Peter asks who comes and goes, if Tom asks what this is, if Father Doyle asks why I'm not home. Say it so we can keep saying it the same way."

Elsie thought of the note in her tin, the guardian declaration she had signed while pushing her daughter into the world, Keane's witness line still damp when she'd folded it. Tom did not know. Peter suspected too much. The lane heard everything except what mattered.

"You're kin to us," she said. "Not church kin, not blood kin, ours. You mind her if I'm stuck or ill. I keep a bed and kettle for you if Saint Brigid throws you out. We don't ask husband's leave for that."

Noreen's eyes filled and then cleared before tears could fall. "Right," she said. "Then I mind her Tuesdays and Fridays till curfew, and Sundays after nursery. You come to Saint Brigid laundry room Wednesdays with bottles and we'll pretend it's domestic instruction."

They bent over the map and made marks until the page looked like a military plan, arrows between Harrow House, Saint Brigid, clinic, shop. By noon the rain had hardened, and children ran boats made from cigarette packets down the gutter stream. Mrs. Coates watched from behind her curtain. Elsie opened the front door wide on purpose and shook out a cloth nappy where everyone could see.

"Mrs. Walsh left her basket," she called, loud enough for two houses either side. "She'll be back after nursery forms."

Noreen gave a tiny snort. "You lay it on thick when you're angry."

"I like to save it for public service."

When Noreen finally left for the bus, she crossed Arthur's plank with arms out slightly for balance, laughing under her breath when it bobbed in the pooled water. Elsie watched until she reached the corner and turned toward town, one hand pressed to her satchel where the bracelet and new attendance card rode together.

The house settled into its afternoon noises: drip from the eaves, the kettle ticking on the hob, the baby hiccuping herself awake. Elsie put the signed payment schedule in the tea tin beside the guardian note and closed the lid hard enough to ring.

By evening, she had terms on paper and no idea whether paper could hold against men, law, or weather. But paper was what existed, and she had chosen it instead of begging.

Noreen sat in Saint Brigid's office at three-forty with damp stockings cooling against her calves and a headache that came in a bright line over her left eye. The office had one gas fire that worked only when Sister Pauline kicked the valve with her heel, and two framed prints of saints who looked as if they'd never had to queue for a bus in slush. A brass clock above the ledger ticked too loudly for the size of the room.

Matron Quinn was a broad woman with forearms like a baker and ink stains on two fingers. She kept a tin of butterscotch drops in her top drawer and handed one to anxious residents as if sugar could settle law. Today she offered one to Noreen, then one to Peter without comment.

Peter declined. He had come in his municipal coat, collar pressed sharp, right wrist still in a plaster cast wrapped now in a clean linen sling. Rain had beaded on his glasses, and he had not wiped them yet. He looked both careful and furious, which was his best suit.

Margaret Keane leaned against the filing cabinet in a dark cardigan, bag at her feet, reading the nursery form as if she expected it to be wrong and wanted to catch it in the act.

"Let's keep this practical," Keane said. "Baby needs maternal contact for feeding progression. Mrs. Walsh needs consistent access. Transport is still patchy. We are not using the unit as leverage in a marital argument."

Peter folded his left hand over the cast. "I have not done that. I have asked for order."

"You have asked for obedience," Noreen said, before she could decide to be strategic.

Matron Quinn raised a palm. "One voice at a time. Mrs. Walsh, your request is for daily nursery access between four and six, plus one weekend morning. Correct?"

"Yes."

"Conditioned on residence at Saint Brigid and curfew compliance. No overnight absences. You may nominate one support person for transport if approved by matron."

Noreen heard the opening and took it. "I nominate Mrs. Elsie Harrow."

Peter made a sharp disbelieving sound. "That is not appropriate."

Keane did not look up. "Appropriate is whoever gets her there on time and keeps her fed."

Peter turned to Matron Quinn, formal voice tightened to wire. "If this institution intends to facilitate the breakdown of a lawful marriage, I shall write to the council board and to Father Doyle."

"Write where you like," Quinn said. "The question on this line is transport."

"Then put me down."

Noreen laughed once, no humor in it. "You missed three visits in a fortnight and blamed rain on clear days."

"Because I was working to pay for your keep."

"You're paying because the child needs milk and I need a bed you can't lock from outside."

The cast hit the arm of his chair when he shifted. Pain flashed across his face before he hid it. "If you return home, all of this resolves immediately."

Noreen felt the old pull for one second: the map in her head where easiest route was always back through his kitchen, his money tin, his temper schedule, his apology tea. Then she remembered blood running down her thighs onto the stair runner in January and Peter calling it nerves. She remembered him at her hospital bed calculating visiting fare before he touched her hand.

"No," she said. "I'm not returning home now."

He went very still, then more formal still. "Then I cannot endorse irregular companionship that exposes my household to ridicule."

"Your household is not where I sleep."

"You are my wife."

"On paper," she said. "That's what we're handling today. Paper."

Keane set the form down and looked at Peter square. "Mr. Walsh, you can sign parental acknowledgment and maintenance commitment without cohabitation clause. It's standard when placement is separate."

"Standard for whom?"

"For mothers who are safer away." Keane's voice was dry as lint. "We have many."

Matron Quinn slid the form across the desk with one blunt finger. "Sign section three and four. Section five remains blank pending legal counsel. Mrs. Walsh keeps access under house rules. If she breaches curfew, pass is suspended forty-eight hours. If she attends as scheduled for four weeks, pass extends and support person approval stands."

Peter stared at the page long enough for the clock to tick twelve loud beats. Then he took the pen with his left hand and signed in rigid schoolmaster script. The signature slanted uphill by the last letter where writing left-handed fought him.

"This is temporary," he said.

"Everything's temporary," Noreen said. "Even winter."

Quinn stamped the card, tore off the copy, and handed it to Noreen. The paper was still warm from her palm: *Resident permitted nursery access, conditional*. It was not freedom or justice or mercy. It was access.

Outside the hostel, rain had stopped and the sky had opened into a dirty silver light that made puddles look deeper than they were. Noreen walked to Barrow Lane with her shoes in her hand for one stretch where floodwater had eaten the pavement edge. Her stockings clung cold around her knees. By the time she reached Elsie's door, her calves trembled from the effort and she hated that they did.

Tom Harrow stood in the hallway holding a bunch of daffodils wrapped in yesterday's racing pages. He had shaved for once, nicked his chin, and tried to hide the cut with a square of toilet paper that had come loose and stuck now to his jaw. He smelled of cheap soap over ale.

"Nora," he said too cheerfully. "You look done in."

"Hello, Tom." She hung her coat and kept her bag on her shoulder.

Elsie sat at the table with the baby in the crook of one arm and a bottle warming by the stove. Her face had the set she wore when she was choosing exact words and would not be moved off them.

Tom held out the daffodils to her. "For the house," he said. "Couldn't find anything decent this late."

Elsie looked at the flowers, then at him. "Put them in the sink."

He did, awkwardly, and splashed muddy water on the tiles. "I've come to talk proper," he said. "No rows. I know I fucked it. I know what I did. I've started back on shifts, part rota, and I'm sorting the bookie in chunks. I can come home and take nights with the baby, give you rest."

Noreen moved to the stove, turned down the flame, and let silence work. Tom hated silence more than shouting.

Elsie spoke without raising her voice. "How much do you still owe? Not slogans, number."

Tom rubbed his neck. "Around ninety. Maybe a touch over once interest bastards get their bite."

"Any police at the depot?"

"No charge yet. Maybe none. Supervisor said keep my head down."

"Where are you sleeping?"

"Len's sofa. His missus wants me out by Sunday."

"And your plan if I let you back?"

He gave a helpless spread of hands. "Work, pay, keep straight. What else?"

Elsie shifted the baby to her shoulder and looked past him at the sink where the daffodils had already begun to shed yellow dust on the enamel.

"That's not a plan," she said. "That's weather talk."

Tom's voice softened, pleading now. "Els, I missed the birth. I know. I hear it in my sleep every night. I came back because I want to be here. Isn't that worth anything?"

She closed her eyes once, briefly. Noreen knew that look; it was the moment Elsie stepped from hurt into decision.

"It's worth visits," Elsie said. "Tuesday and Saturday, five to seven, here, with me present. You bring one pound for arrears each Thursday and proof on paper. You don't get a key. You don't sleep here. If six weeks pass and you keep every term, we review."

Tom stared as if he'd misheard. "I'm her father."

"Yes. Act like it."

"You're punishing me."

"I'm pricing risk."

The phrasing hit him harder than any insult. He took a step toward her, then stopped when Noreen set the kettle lid down with a hard clang.

"Don't crowd her," Noreen said.

Tom turned on her. "This is my house."

"No," Elsie said. "It's my tenancy till you prove otherwise. You left."

A knock came before Tom could answer, crisp and impatient. Peter stood on the step in a dark overcoat, cast cradled in its sling like evidence. He glanced from Tom to Noreen to Elsie and seemed to reorder his whole approach in one breath.

"I will be brief," he said. "Mrs. Walsh, I have signed your nursery access under protest. I am prepared to resume financial support at full rate if you return to the marital home by Sunday vespers. If you choose continued separation, support will be limited to statutory minimum pending counsel."

Tom gave a short bark of laughter. "Hell of a sales pitch, Pete."

Peter ignored him. His attention stayed on Noreen, controlled and cutting. "I am offering restoration of respectability and stability. That offer is not indefinite."

Noreen felt tired enough to shake. She put her satchel on the table, took out the stamped pass, and laid it flat beside Elsie's payment schedule from Baby Goods. Two pieces of paper with different logos and the same threat written underneath.

"You are offering control in exchange for obedience," she said. "I'm done buying that."

"You cannot support yourself."

"Not alone. I'm not alone."

Peter's jaw moved once behind closed lips. "Do you understand what allegations could be made in custody proceedings?"

"Perfectly." She kept her voice warm, almost conversational, because rage would blur her and he liked blur. "That's why I'm keeping curfew, signing everything, attending nursery, and documenting every pound you send. You can threaten court. I can read forms too."

He looked at Elsie then, as if naming her would soil the sentence. "And this arrangement with your neighbor is to continue?"

Elsie answered before Noreen could. "Yes."

Peter shifted to legal diction, each word clipped to full shape. "Mrs. Harrow, you are actively interfering in my marriage."

"No," Elsie said. "I'm feeding a woman who nearly died in my front room and helping her see her child. That's not interference. That's memory."

For a moment no one spoke. Rain began again, soft against the back window.

Peter drew himself up. "Then we proceed formally."

"Do that," Noreen said. "And send maintenance by postal order every Friday. Address on file at Saint Brigid. If it misses two weeks, Keane says magistrate first hearing is quick these days when infants are involved."

Keane's name landed where argument could not. Peter knew exactly who documented what in this town.

He nodded once, stiffly. "You have chosen your course."

"I have," Noreen said.

He left without looking back, boots striking hard on the flooded path. Tom lingered, uncertain now, two fingers worrying the loose paper on his chin until it peeled away and showed the small cut bleeding again.

"So that's it," he said to Elsie, quieter. "I'm timetable and receipts."

"For now."

"You don't trust me at all."

Elsie met his eyes. "No."

It was the cleanest mercy she had ever given him.

Tom swallowed, nodded once as if agreeing to terms at a betting window, and pulled his coat collar up. At the door he hesitated.

"I am sorry," he said, full form strange in his mouth, deliberate this time. "Not sorry-for-being-caught. Sorry."

Elsie's face changed, not toward forgiveness, not away from it either. "Then keep the terms," she said. "That's the only language left."

After he went, the house felt suddenly larger and poorer, emptied of male volume but full of consequences. Noreen sat down hard on the nearest chair. Her legs had gone to wool.

Elsie poured tea, then changed her mind and poured broth instead, thick with yesterday's lentils and too much salt. Noreen drank it standing up by the range because if she sat she might not rise before curfew.

"You got the pass?" Elsie asked.

Noreen held up the stamped card. "Four weeks on probation like I'm a delinquent choirgirl. Curfew, interviews, no missed sign-ins. But it's daily access if I keep every rule."

"You'll keep them."

"I'll keep enough." Noreen tucked the card into the inside pocket of her satchel, then added with a crooked smile, "I can be good when good gets me in the door."

Elsie laughed once, tired and real. She lifted her daughter from the basket and settled her against Noreen's shoulder for a minute, just long enough for the tiny body to grow warm and trusting against both of them.

"Listen," Elsie said quietly. "If I don't wake one night, if fever comes back, the guardian note stays in the tea tin behind the barley. Keane knows."

Noreen's eyes cut to hers. "Don't say that now."

"I'm not being dramatic. I'm being exact."

"Fine." Noreen kissed the baby's head and passed her back. "Then you hear me exact as well. If Saint Brigid throws me, I come here first. If your payments slip, I pick up shifts and we split bread before pride. If Peter files anything, we answer in writing and in person with witnesses."

"Done."

"Done."

Curfew pressed at the edges of the clock. Noreen wrapped the yellow wool cap around her hand so she would remember to take it to nursery in the morning and pulled on her coat. Elsie walked her to the step with the baby under a blanket.

Barrow Lane at dusk looked like a country after fighting. Snow still clung in grey heaps along the wall bases, but the middle of the street had gone to slick black mud, rutted by vans and boot heels. Gutter water ran fast now, carrying coal grit, cigarette ends, and one child's paper boat that had somehow survived half the lane. Windows opened for the first time in months, letting out cabbage steam, radio static, arguments, laughter. Life was noisy again and no less precarious for it.

Across the lane, Mrs. Coates pretended to shake a rug while watching them. Farther down, Mr. Patel from number nine set bricks in a line to make a dry path for his twins and cursed affectionately every time one kicked them loose. Above, clouds broke in strips, pale gold behind soot.

Noreen stepped onto Arthur's plank, tested it with her heel, then crossed. Halfway over she turned back.

"Tomorrow after nursery," she said. "I'll bring the bigger bottles and that mending needle you like."

"I'll have your share of lentils soaked," Elsie said.

"And the map. We forgot Sundays."

"We won't forget again."

Noreen lifted two fingers in salute and went toward the main road, coat hem dark with spray. Elsie stood in her doorway with her daughter asleep against her collarbone and watched until Noreen disappeared past the corner shop light. Then she looked down the lane where thawwater kept moving, not clean, not certain, but moving in the direction they had chosen.
