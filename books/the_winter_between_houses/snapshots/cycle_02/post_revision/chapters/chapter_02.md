# Chapter 2

The hall letter slot clicked in the wind, then the one next door answered, and Noreen woke before the alarm because the baby had found a hard corner under her ribs and kept nudging until she sat up. The room was black except for the pale rectangle where the frost had thinned on the window. Peter still slept on his back, lips parted, breath dry and regular, one hand on the counterpane, palm up, waiting for paperwork even in sleep. She swung her feet down, felt cold boards through the thin soles of her slippers, and stood very still until the room stopped tipping. The child shifted once, then settled.

In the kitchen she struck a match and cupped it to the fire grate. Coal caught slowly, then all at once, a low red mouth under clinker. She set the kettle on, pulled her cardigan tight over the front of her nightdress, and reached for the morning post where it had slid halfway under the door with a line of blown snow. There was a milk circular, a parish notice, and an envelope with heavy red letters on the front.

FINAL DEMAND, the red stamp said.

The paper was damp at one corner, and the ink on the address had bled where a glove must have pressed it. She could make out the lane, not much else. Peter sometimes had rate-office correspondence marked urgent in angry print, and he hated when post sat unopened. She slit the flap with the blunt knife she used for butter and eased out two folded sheets and a carbon copy slip that left blue dust on her fingertips.

Calderbank Baby Goods, Hire Purchase Department. She read the name once and then again because her eyes refused the words the first time.

Account holder: Mrs Elsie Harrow.

Arrears outstanding: GBP 12 8s.

Final notice before retrieval action.

She held the letter closer to the stove light. The paper had that office smell, stale paste and ink ribbon and hands that had counted too many forms. Beneath the demand was a typed line about an extension request, approved on the strength of a signed authorization dated the tenth of December. The carbon slip was attached to prove it. She flattened it against the table with the heel of her hand.

Signature of purchaser: Elsie Harrow.

It was not Elsie’s hand. Noreen had seen Elsie write lists for the shop, careful block letters pressed so hard the pencil point snapped. This signature slanted right as if it was leaning into a pub counter. The first E was broad and lazy. The y in Harrow hooked under itself with a flourish that tried too hard. Noreen knew that y. She had watched Tom make the same tail when he signed for two sacks of coal in November and laughed about not being trusted with chalk, let alone money.

The kettle knocked its lid and rattled like teeth. Noreen lifted it off too late and water hissed across the ring, then ran in a line toward the edge of the range. She reached for a cloth, wiped, and stood there with the damp rag in her hand while the red stamp on the envelope sat accusing her from the table.

It had come to the wrong door, and she had opened it, and now she held something that could not be unknown.

She told herself she would carry it straight next door, hand it to Elsie, and that would be that. There would be no talk at the threshold, no faces at windows, and no chance for Mrs Prentice three houses down to tell her sister that bailiffs were circling Barrow Lane. She could do this in two minutes and keep the morning ordinary.

By the time she had dressed, tugged her wool skirt over her hips, and pinned back her hair, the lane had started its daily scraping noises. Buckets knocked against step-stones. A shovel edge grated on packed snow. Someone coughed deep and wet through the terrace wall. She slid the envelope inside her cardigan, where the paper scratched when she breathed, and stepped out the back way to avoid the front path where everyone could see everyone else.

She was halfway along the side ginnel when Mrs Bhatti lifted her shop shutter from the other end. Metal banged, then rose in jerks. Mrs Bhatti wore her husband’s old army greatcoat over a flowered house dress and had wrapped a green scarf over her hair so only her eyebrows showed. She kept a tin of clove sweets in her pocket and sucked one before she opened each morning; Noreen could smell it now, sharp and sweet in the frozen air.

“Morning, Nora,” Mrs Bhatti called. “If your Peter wants his Players, tell him he’s paying cash today. I’m not a bank.”

Noreen smiled because that was what the lane required. “I’ll tell him. Has the milk come?”

“It came,” Mrs Bhatti said. She set a crate down and rolled her shoulders. “Half the bottles were cracked. The driver said the road to Ledley is glass. We will have turnips and complaints and that will be our feast.”

She always said bleak things like jokes and then watched your face to see whether you could carry your end. Noreen usually could. This morning the envelope rubbed her skin like a rash.

“Put me down for two turnips, then,” she said.

“Done. You look pale.” Mrs Bhatti squinted at her. “Did the baby keep you up?”

“All night boxing.”

“Good lungs, then. Better than most men on this lane.”

Mrs Bhatti laughed at her own line, a sudden bark that turned into a cough. Noreen laughed too, too late, and kept moving before another question landed.

At the Harrows’ back door she paused with her fist raised. Heat leaked around the frame in a thin greasy smell of boiled cabbage and damp coal. She heard a chair scrape, then a pan put down harder than needed. She heard no voices. She knocked once with her knuckles and waited.

Elsie opened after a moment with her sleeves rolled and soap up to her elbows. A line of wet nappies hung over the fireguard behind her, steam lifting from them. Her hair had worked loose from its pins and curled damp at the temples.

“Nora. You’re early.”

“I’m early. I’m sorry. I won’t keep you.”

Elsie checked the yard behind her, then faced Noreen again. “Everything all right?”

Noreen had expected to hand the envelope over with one sentence and go. She had not expected Elsie’s face, open and tired and trying not to ask for anything. She pulled the envelope out from inside her cardigan. The red stamp flashed between them.

“This came through ours. I opened it before I saw.”

Elsie did not take it. Her eyes dropped to the red print, then snapped back up. Color climbed her throat.

“Was Tom with you?” she asked.

“No. He went out before light, I think. I didn’t see him.”

“Right.” Elsie wiped one hand on her apron and reached at last. She held the envelope by the corners as if the center might stain. “Thanks.”

Noreen should have let it rest there, but the carbon slip in her head would not sit still. She heard herself say, “There’s a form in it. About the pram account.”

Elsie’s jaw moved once, not quite a swallow. “He said he settled that Friday.”

“He didn’t.”

Steam from the drying nappies made the doorway close and wet around them. Elsie turned her body so Noreen could step in out of the yard. Noreen hesitated, then came through. The room was only a few feet wider than two outstretched arms. Every surface had a task on it: cracked bowl of pegs, iron turned on its side, stack of folded flannels, two pennies set apart from other change on the sill.

Elsie opened the letter there at the table without taking off her coat. She read the first page in silence, then the second, then the carbon authorization. She put the pages down very carefully, one over the other, lining each edge to the grain in the table, buying herself three silent breaths.

“I didn’t sign this,” she said.

“I know.”

Elsie touched the signature with the side of her finger, not the tip. “I never signed any extension either.”

Noreen felt relief and nausea in equal measure. Relief meant she had not misread it, and nausea meant being right made everything worse.

“Then he signed in your name,” she said.

Elsie barked a laugh that died as soon as her eyes hit the total again. “He signed in my name and spent the week telling me not to fret because he had it sorted. Christ.” Her fingernail clicked against the typed figure. “Twelve pounds and eight. We haven’t had twelve pounds in this house at one go since August.”

Noreen looked at the two pennies on the sill. She did not need asking to know what they were for: tomorrow’s bread when Mrs Bhatti stopped carrying the tab another day. Economic shame was not dramatic on Barrow Lane. It sat in plain view, two coins on a windowsill, while women talked about weather.

“If anyone else sees that envelope…” Elsie said, then stopped.

Noreen thought of the lane, which loved a crisis provided it was next door. “No one saw me come in.”

Elsie folded the letter once and once again, too small, then flattened it back out as if folding had offended her. “I need to think.”

Noreen nodded. “I’ll go.”

At the door Elsie spoke behind her. “Give me till tonight. I need to catch Tom sober before this runs round the lane.”

There it was, the ask sitting between politeness and plea. Noreen turned. Elsie was still by the table, one hand over the paper, the other braced on the wood near the iron.

“All right,” Noreen said.

She meant it when she said it. She did not yet know what “all right” would cost.

Back in her own kitchen she set about the noon meal with the discipline she used when she needed her hands to occupy her head. She peeled potatoes, saved skins for stock, and cut onions thin so they would sweeten before they burned. The baby rolled once and then lodged low, heavy as a wet loaf. Outside, children shouted where the lane widened near the corner, then one started crying with the outraged force of a fresh fall on ice.

Noreen took Peter’s shirt from the line near the range, still damp at the cuffs, and pressed it against the warm oven door to finish it. When she reached for the sewing basket to mend a loose button, she found last Christmas’s cards tied with red thread and shoved into the side pocket. Tom’s card to Peter sat on top, because Peter kept cards by sender and year like receipts. Noreen untied the thread and read the line under the printed robin.

To Pete and Nora, keep warm, keep laughing.

The cheer of it made bile rise in her throat. She shoved the cards back and retied the thread so hard it bit her fingertip.

By four the light had thinned to lead. She lit the lamp and set the table for two. Peter came in at half past, stamping snow from his boots, collar up against his jaw. He laid his ledger satchel on the chair before he took off his coat. He always did that first, as if the bag needed to be sheltered before his own hands.

“You are late with the lamp,” he said, rubbing his glasses with his handkerchief. “The room is dim.”

“It’s lit now.”

He hung his coat, sat, and looked at the stew pot. “Is this all the beef we have left?”

“It’s what there was.”

He nodded once, the way he did when recording a figure he did not like. They ate for a few minutes with only spoon and plate noise between them. Noreen could feel the envelope through the table, though it was not there. Elsie had it. The fact of it still sat in the room.

Peter set his spoon down. “Mrs Bhatti told me you were at the Harrows before eight.”

Noreen kept eating. “I was.”

“For what purpose?”

“To return a bowl.”

He waited, eyes on her face, not blinking enough. “You did not bring home a bowl.”

“I left it there.”

“That is inefficient.”

She almost laughed at that, at the perfect Peter word dropped into a winter kitchen. Instead she took another spoonful and burned her tongue.

He said, “Noreen, I am asking you directly. What is happening next door?”

“Nothing that’s yours to manage.”

He leaned back, folded his hands over his stomach. It was the posture he used when a clerk brought him figures that did not add. “What happens next door becomes mine when it touches this house. Debt collectors do not stop at property lines. Reputation does not stop at property lines either.”

“There’s no collector at our door.”

“Yet.”

She met his gaze then because avoiding it made him stronger. “You want me to report on Elsie now? Is that where we’re at?”

“I want transparency in my own house.” He said each word with clipped care. “I bring in the wage. I am entitled to accurate information.”

Noreen felt heat rise that had nothing to do with the stove. “I’m not your office, Peter.”

“You are my wife.”

“And I’m the one on my feet all day in this place while you balance columns and call it life.”

For a second he went blank, a clerk caught by a voice from the cupboard. Then his jaw set.

“You will lower your voice.”

“No.”

Silence held, stretched thin as spun sugar. Through the wall someone next door coughed, three short barks, then a kettle whistled and was lifted. Terrace life did not permit private battles; it only let people pretend.

Peter picked up his spoon again, laid it back down untouched. “If there is financial irregularity with Thomas Harrow, and you know of it, withholding that information from me is foolish.”

She could hear the bureaucrat in him choosing terms, each one polished to avoid the words he meant: shame, scandal, leverage.

“He’s not your case file,” she said.

“Do not be childish.”

“Don’t talk to me like that.”

He kept his eyes on her for a long moment, then said, very calm, “I will ask once more. Did you see correspondence addressed to the Harrows?”

Noreen kept her face still. Lying to Peter thinned the air in her chest. One wrong word and the floor of the house could crack under her.

“No,” she said.

His eyes narrowed behind the clean circles of his glasses. He knew she was lying. He also knew that proving it would require making a scene large enough for the lane to taste. He valued authority, but he valued appearances more.

He pushed his plate away. “Very well. If a man from collections comes here because you decided to protect your friend, you will answer to me first.”

He stood, took his satchel, and went upstairs with the careful, angry tread that meant he was controlling himself by force. The bedroom door shut. A minute later the tap ran, then stopped.

Noreen sat with both hands flat on the wood until she could feel the grain under her palms again. The baby kicked once, hard and offended, registering her objection. When Noreen stood, the room tipped and flashed at the edges. She caught the dresser and held there, breathing through her teeth until her knees steadied.

She took a chipped mixing bowl from the shelf, wiped the flour ring off with the heel of her thumb, and set it by the back door. If Peter asked again in the morning, she would have a bowl to point to and a lie to keep straight. Then she cleaned the plates, but the spoon Peter had left rang against the sink and jumped from her wet fingers twice before she could hold it.

At nine, with Peter’s breathing thick overhead, Noreen wrapped a scarf over her hair, picked up the chipped bowl, and let herself out through the back. Snow had crusted to ice in the yard, and each step made a small crack she felt in her teeth. Light showed from the Harrows’ scullery. She crossed, knocked once, and went in when Elsie opened without asking who it was.

Elsie had changed into an old cardigan that used to be blue and now was every color of washing water. A needle was stuck in the front, thread trailing down her side. She had been darning one of Tom’s socks and stopped halfway.

“Is he back?” Noreen asked.

“No. Maybe not till late.” Elsie gestured to the chair by the table. “Sit. You look frozen.”

Noreen sat. The scullery smelled of soap, coal smoke, and the faint sourness of milk gone just off. On the table lay the opened letter, weighted by the sugar tin.

Noreen set the bowl on the table. “For the record. Peter pushed at me over supper.”

Elsie dragged the chipped bowl into line with the letter as if setting evidence in a row. “Then we’re past maybe. I read every line. They’ve marked us for a field call Friday, six to eight.”

Noreen felt the cold again despite the stove. “If he knocks while Tom’s out, the whole lane will know by breakfast.”

Elsie nodded without lifting her eyes. “I told myself maybe I signed and forgot. That was stupid.”

“It wasn’t stupid. It was hopeful.”

Elsie gave her a look that said hope had become expensive. “He said they wanted a witness for a delivery date. He took my name off the hall hook, you know, on that little card Nurse Keane left, and practiced my writing while he smoked. I told him to stop dafting about.” She pressed her thumb into the corner of the table until the nail whitened. “I watched him do it and I still didn’t see it.”

Noreen let the words settle. The betrayal had not arrived in one dramatic blow; it had been built in ordinary gestures, in a husband entertaining himself with his wife’s name.

“What can they do?” Noreen asked quietly. “On the letter it says retrieval action.”

“They can take the pram before the baby’s in it. They can send a man to stand in this room and list what’s worth carrying off. They can send it to court if they want.” Elsie blew out a breath that shook at the end. “A court over a pram. Imagine.”

Noreen could imagine it too easily: a man in a hard hat clipboarding through dishes and blankets while neighbors pretended not to watch. Poverty always had witnesses.

“You could go to the shop manager,” she said. “Show this. Say it was forged.”

“And then what?” Elsie asked. “They call police. Tom says I’m lying because I’m angry. We spend Christmas in statements. Whole lane hears by dinner. My mother hears by Sunday and tells me I’ve made my own bed with a gambler.”

Noreen rubbed her fingers together under the table. Carbon paper dust still clung in the lines of her skin. “You didn’t make this.”

“I married it.” Elsie looked up then, eyes bright in a face gone gray with tiredness. “That still counts against me.”

There was no clean argument against that. In Noreen’s own house, legality and duty arrived as the same sentence in Peter’s mouth.

Elsie reached for the letter again, smoothed it flat, and spoke without looking up. “Did Peter ask?”

“Yes. Calm at first, then not. ‘For what purpose.’ ‘What is happening next door.’ Then, when I wouldn’t hand him anything, ‘Did you see correspondence.’ Like he was chairing a bloody meeting.”

“What did you tell him?”

Noreen thought of Peter’s hands folded over his stomach, the polished words, the warning about answering to him first. “Nothing useful.”

Elsie stopped smoothing the page. The room held still.

“Nora,” she said, and now there was plea in it plain and unhidden. “I need you not to tell him. Not him, not Mrs Bhatti, not anyone. If Peter knows, he will call it prudence and then he will use it when he needs to. He won’t even mean to half the time. It’ll just come out as fact in the wrong room. Please.”

Noreen looked at the sugar tin weighing the letter down. There was less than an inch of sugar left; she could hear grains slide when Elsie shifted the tin. Everything in these kitchens was measured: tea, paraffin, what could be said aloud.

“If I keep it,” Noreen said, “I’m lying in my own house. You know that.”

“I know.” Elsie’s voice cracked, and she cleared it. “I’m asking anyway.”

Noreen did not answer at once. She listened instead: the faint thump of Peter turning in bed through one wall, a bicycle passing at the lane end, wind finding the gap under the back door and making the flame in the lamp bend. The house pressed close on all sides, as if bricks themselves were listening.

“What will you do?” Noreen asked.

“Tomorrow I’ll be outside Baby Goods before they lift the shutter. I’ll cut through the side ginnel so Mrs Prentice doesn’t clock me, ring the side bell, catch the manager before the shop girls come down, and put every coin I can scrape on his blotter so he marks us held till the end of January.”

“With what?”

Elsie looked toward the mantel where two envelopes sat behind a china spaniel missing one ear. “I’ve seven shillings from cleaning, hidden from Tom since October. Two and six in the sugar tin. I can pawn the wedding chain at Oates if he pays fair weight.”

She dragged a hand over her mouth. “That’s it.”

Noreen made a face. “Don’t give him the ring either, if he asks.”

Elsie twisted her wedding ring until the skin at her knuckle blanched. “He already asked last week for coal money and called it borrowing.”

Noreen felt anger rise, sharp and clean. “He forged your name. That’s not borrowing, Elsie. Don’t let him dress it up. It’s theft, and he’s hiding behind you.”

Elsie closed her eyes briefly, as if the plainness of that sentence hurt more than the letter. “Say it quieter.”

Noreen realized she had leaned forward and raised her voice. She sat back. “Right. Sorry for the volume. Not for the word.”

“No.” Elsie opened her eyes again. “Don’t be sorry. I just can’t bear hearing it loud.”

Noreen reached across the table without planning to and put her hand over Elsie’s. Elsie’s fingers were raw from soap and cold, knuckles split in two places, and still warm underneath. For a moment neither of them moved.

“Fine,” Noreen said. “I’ll keep it. From Peter too.”

Elsie squeezed once, quick and hard, then let go. “Thank you.”

“That doesn’t mean I like it.”

“I don’t need you to like it.” Elsie pulled the letter toward herself and folded it along the old creases. “I just need to get through this week.”

Noreen watched her tuck the papers into an empty tea packet, then slide the packet behind the flour tin where no man on a casual search would look. It was a small domestic trick, learned and refined in houses where women hid things to keep them alive.

At the door Noreen pulled her scarf tight and turned back once. “If he comes in drunk tonight and starts promises, don’t let him near that paper.”

Elsie drove the darning needle through Tom’s sock and left it there. “When did promises ever require sobriety?”

Noreen smiled despite herself. “Fair.”

She stepped into the yard. Snow-light gave everything a false clean glow. Behind her, Elsie set the bolt. Ahead, in her own house, Peter waited in the dark with his low, regular snore and his demand for accurate information. Noreen stood between the two doors long enough for cold to bite through her stockings.

She crossed back to her kitchen, knelt by the back step, and scrubbed her wet boot marks out of the frosting boards with the tea towel she had folded for morning. Upstairs, Peter turned once in bed. Noreen stopped scrubbing and held her breath with the tea towel knotted in her fist until his snore settled back into its low, regular drag. Then she counted, without wanting to, how few nights were left till Christmas morning.
