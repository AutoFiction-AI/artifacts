# Chapter 4

Elsie scraped a clear patch in the kitchen window with the edge of a spoon and kept listening for the back latch. Tom still had not come through the back door with his boots stamping and his voice already explaining where he'd been. Outside, Barrow Lane turned whiter by the minute; by eleven the snow came sideways, not down, a chalky sweep that erased doorsteps and swallowed the gaps between houses. The lane looked uninhabited, each frontage sealed under a chalk-white crust.

The wireless on the shelf above the sink hissed through a weather bulletin from Leeds. The announcer sounded too calm for the words he was reading. There was severe drifting. Non-essential journeys were discouraged. A local authority notice was expected by afternoon. Elsie stood with one hand under her belly, thumb pressed into the seam where her coat buttons strained, and listened for the part where they said it would ease by tea time. They did not say that part.

She had gone to bed at half one with Tom still out and had woken at six convinced she had heard him at the latch. It had been the milk crate in the yard banging in the wind. Since then she had made tea, washed two cups out of habit, poured one away, and checked the clock so often that the battery had become the loudest thing in the room.

Anger had carried her through the first two hours. It was useful anger, the kind that let her fold nappies and count potatoes and curse him in complete sentences while she split a heel of bread with the old knife. By late morning the anger was thinning into something meaner. She knew Tom's tricks. She did not know this silence.

She layered herself in wool, then her brown coat with the mended cuff, then the scarf Noreen had handed her across the wall at Christmas because it was warmer than pride. The hem of her skirt snagged on a nail by the pantry door. She pulled free, swore once under her breath, and made herself stop at the table long enough to write on a torn envelope: Gone to find Tom. Back soon. She weighed it with the sugar tin in case Noreen looked in and panicked.

On the lane she moved carefully, boots feeling for kerb through packed slush. Her breath made the scarf damp against her mouth. At number six, old Mr Henshaw was already out with a coal shovel, carving a trench from his step to the road in inch-by-inch strokes as if he thought effort alone could keep the weather honest.

"Morning, Mrs Harrow," he called, not looking up. His cap was pulled down to his eyebrows and one sleeve had been darned with green wool that did not match the coat. "You heading far?"

"Depot first," she said. "You seen Tom?"

He shook his head and leaned on the shovel for air. "Haven't seen my own son since Sunday and he lives two streets over. If you've got someone indoors, keep him there. This lot's got a nasty mind to it."

That was as close to sympathy as Henshaw ever gave anyone. Elsie nodded and kept moving.

Mrs Bhatti's corner shop was dark except for the back room, where a strip of yellow light lay across stacked crates of onions. The front shutter was down to shoulder height. Elsie bent and ducked under.

Mrs Bhatti stood at the till in fingerless gloves, ledger open, pencil tucked into the knot of her braid like a hairpin. She had tied her sari hem up over her ankles for traction and was still in her house slippers, the felt soaked at the toes. A kettle steamed on a single gas ring near the biscuits.

"You're out in this?" Mrs Bhatti said. "You should be at home with your feet up."

"Have you seen Tom this morning?"

"Not since last night, and he was not buying groceries then either." She snapped the ledger shut with two fingers and studied Elsie's face, not her belly. "He was with Blackwell and that little ferret from the tyre place. Smelled like stale stout and damp dogs."

Elsie felt the answer land low in her stomach. "What time?"

"After ten. Before I closed." Mrs Bhatti poured tea into a chipped mug and pushed it across the counter. "Drink this. Your lips are blue."

"I can't stay."

"Then take it with you." Mrs Bhatti lowered her voice. "My eldest rang from Bradford at dawn. Lorries are folded across the road there and nobody's settling accounts on time today. Don't spend your last dry hour chasing a man who's already put himself in the red."

Elsie wrapped both hands around the mug anyway, letting the heat bite her palms for three breaths before she set it down. "If he made it to the depot, he'll have to be there."

Mrs Bhatti gave a short, irritated hum, the sound she made when a customer asked for credit she could not afford to extend. "If you pass back this way, knock hard. I'll be in the storeroom counting tins and pretending I trust people to pay me in spring."

That, too, was kindness.

The bus depot sat behind a wire fence at the edge of town, a yard usually loud with engines, conductors shouting routes, men smoking in clumps by the gatehouse. Today it was muffled and wrong. Three buses stood angled in the snow like grounded boats, windscreen wipers lifted, windows furred white. The big doors were chained. No diesel tang, no roar, only the clatter of a loose sign on one hinge.

A man in a railway coat emerged from the gatehouse when she rattled the chain. She recognized him after a second: Stan Doyle, dispatch office, a narrow face and the same nicotine cough as Tom but deeper in the chest. He wore his scarf twice round and still kept pressing two fingers at his throat between sentences.

"Mrs Harrow?" he said. "Shouldn't be out."

"I'm looking for Tom. He was on early shift."

Stan's eyes flicked away toward the frozen yard, then back. "No one had a shift after seven. They pulled all runs."

"He signed in before that?"

Stan moved his weight, boots crunching. "Didn't sign in at all."

For a moment she thought she had misheard him because of the wind. "Check again."

"I did." He hesitated. "Look, I'm not trying to be difficult. We had four men sleep in the break room last night because they couldn't get home. Tom wasn't one of them."

She gripped the chain so hard the links imprinted her palm through the glove. "Who was he with yesterday?"

"Could've been anyone." Stan coughed into his fist, spat into the snow, then seemed to remember who he was speaking to and wiped his mouth with the back of his hand. "Maybe try the Lion. Some of the lads went there after wages."

"After wages were late again."

He gave a bleak little half-smile. "Aye. Hard to spend what you haven't got. Doesn't stop them trying."

She watched him, waiting for him to add something else, something useful, some scrap that would let her walk in one direction instead of all of them. He only pulled his coat tighter and glanced toward the office door where a telephone sat on a shelf inside. She followed his look.

"Can I use your line?" she asked.

"Dead since nine. Whole exchange keeps dropping."

He said it quickly, like apology and warning together. Elsie nodded once, turned, and started back down the hill.

By the time she reached Calderbank High Street the weather had thickened into white grit. She kept one hand on walls when she could, reading shopfronts by memory because signs blurred out at ten feet. The butcher had sandbags piled against his door. The pharmacy window had been boarded from inside. A boy no older than thirteen skidded past hauling a sack of coal on a pram chassis and grinned at her, face red with cold and triumph.

The Crown and Shuttle had half its windows steamed over and the other half filmed with frost. Someone had spread sawdust across the threshold to keep people from breaking their necks on the flagstones. Elsie pushed in and the warmth hit her hard enough to make her eyes water.

The room smelled of wet wool, pipe smoke, and spilled bitter gone sticky at the edges. Men were lined three deep at the bar though it was not yet midday, coats on, hats on, all of them listening with one ear to the radio propped near the optics. No one laughed. Even the regular domino table was empty.

Behind the taps, Peggy Lomas was polishing the same glass in furious circles. Peggy had broad wrists, a silver St Christopher around her neck, and a way of seeing trouble before it crossed the room. She looked up, took in Elsie's coat and belly and white face, and came round the bar without being asked.

"You all right, love?"

"I'm looking for Tom Harrow. He was in last night."

Peggy's mouth tightened. "He was."

"When did he leave?"

"Late."

"With who?"

Peggy glanced at the men nearest the door, then back at Elsie. "Not here."

"Not here where?"

"Not this side of the room." Peggy took Elsie by the elbow and steered her toward the corridor by the gents where the noise dropped by half. "You don't need a lot of eyes on this."

Elsie pulled free, not hard. "I need to know where my husband is."

"And I need this bar kept on its feet." Peggy lowered her voice until it was almost a growl. "He left around half eleven with Blackwell. They were mouthing off about a card table in the old rail sheds. That's all I've got."

"Rail sheds are shut in this weather."

"I know that and you know that. After six pints, every shut shed turns into a card room in their mouths." Peggy's expression changed, softened one inch. "Listen to me. Council rang before the line went to hell. Full closure likely by two. You need to get home."

A man from the bar called out, "Peg, you serving or sermonizing?" and got a few low chuckles for his trouble.

Peggy didn't turn. "You want another pint, you can wait till my hands are free." Then to Elsie, quieter: "If he stumbles in, I'll send someone straight down Barrow Lane."

Elsie heard herself ask the stupid question anyway. "He wasn't hurt?"

"Not when he left."

That was not comfort; it was a narrow strip of floor over a deep place. Elsie nodded, thanked her, and left before the room could start watching again.

The old rail sheds were a blind idea and she knew it, but she walked halfway there before the wind beat her back. Snow had drifted thigh-high against the embankment. A constable waved her off from the bridge, face wrapped in a scarf, eyes streaming.

"Road's closed past this point," he shouted. "Turn around now."

"My husband's missing."

"And he'll stay missing if you freeze on me, won't he? Home, madam."

He said madam like he'd been trained to, not because he believed it. She hated him for being right.

On Barrow Lane the snowplough came through once and gave up, leaving a ridge of compacted ice down the middle. Doors were shut. Curtains twitched and fell still. At number three, Mrs Caulfield opened her top window an inch and leaned out in a maroon dressing gown with a fox-fur collar rubbed thin at the tips.

"Elsie!" she called. "You found him?"

"No."

"Try his brother in Hebden. Men always go to brothers when they've done something stupid."

"I don't have that number."

Mrs Caulfield sniffed and drew her gown tighter. "Neither do I. Peter Walsh might. He keeps a little black book with all his useful people in it."

The window shut. Elsie stood a second in blowing snow, wanting to throw something at the glass and having nothing in her hands.

She let herself into Harrow House with fingers so numb she had to shoulder the door twice before the latch caught. Indoors felt colder than the street. The fire had slumped to dull red and a film of ice feathered the inside of the scullery tap. She bent to rake the grate, then had to sit on the kitchen chair with the poker still in her hand because the baby had shifted heavy and low and her back had gone into a hard, warning ache.

It eased after a minute. She breathed through her nose until she could stand again.

On the back of the torn envelope she'd left under the sugar tin, she wrote in pencil with her hand still shaking: Dawn if no Tom. Noreen to use Peter's line for Tom's brother in Hebden and Stan at the depot if the exchange came back. Elsie to start at the rail sheds by mill bend, then the bridge police post. Ask Henshaw to check viaduct ditches with his shovel. She folded the scrap and shoved it into her coat pocket. Random knocking had got her nowhere; in the morning they would split the ground and ask by name.

The telephone at the Walshes was the only private line on the row. Peter had it because Peter had forms and authority and friends who mattered after office hours. Elsie hated needing his things, but need was not interested in her pride. She crossed the yard, knocked, and waited with snow melting down her collar.

Noreen opened in a cardigan over her dress, hair pinned up any old way, flour still on one wrist. She had been baking, probably because kneading gave her something to push against that wasn't her own life. The house behind her smelled of yeast and onions and paraffin.

"Christ, look at you," Noreen said, pulling her in by the sleeve. "You're frozen through."

"Can I use your phone?"

"If it works." Noreen shut the door with her hip. "Peter rang at dinner to say council might keep him in town overnight if the roads close. Then the line started crackling in sharp, greasy bursts."

Elsie wiped her boots and followed her to the hall table where the black phone sat under a framed wedding photo turned slightly askew. She lifted the receiver. No dial tone. Only a faint rushing hiss, thin and hollow in her ear.

She tried the operator anyway. Then depot. Then the Crown and Shuttle. The same dead hiss, then nothing.

"Any luck?" Noreen asked.

Elsie set the receiver down too hard and the bell gave a small, useless jangle. "Line's gone."

"You're shaking. Sit down and I'll get tea."

"I should keep trying."

"With what, telepathy? Sit."

Noreen's humor usually came with a grin. This one did not. Elsie sat.

They took tea at the kitchen table with the lamp already lit though it was barely four. The snow outside reflected enough light to make the room look underlit from below, all hard shadows and pale walls. Noreen slid a plate over with two slices of soda bread spread with dripping.

"Eat," she said. "You haven't all day, have you?"

Elsie opened her mouth to deny it and stopped. The lie was pointless between them. She ate, because the baby kicked when she did not.

"Where've you checked?" Noreen asked.

"Depot first. He never signed in. Then Mrs Bhatti's, then the pub. Peggy says he left with Blackwell for the rail sheds. Could be rubbish." Elsie wiped grease from her thumb with the edge of her napkin. "Henshaw hasn't seen him. Caulfield hasn't. The constable on the bridge sent me home before I could get near the sheds."

Noreen listened the way she always did, chin tipped down, eyes on Elsie's hands more than her face. "So we know he was alive at half eleven last night and stupid enough to go where there was cards."

"That's your comfort?"

"No." Noreen blew over her mug. "That's the shape of it. Comfort can come later if we find any."

Outside, something heavy scraped along the lane. They both turned. A council lorry crawled past with a loudspeaker lashed to the roof. Static cracked, then a man's voice broke through.

"Emergency notice for Calderbank district. All bus routes suspended with immediate effect. Main roads closed to private traffic until further instruction. Residents are advised to remain indoors after dark. Repeat, remain indoors after dark."

The voice faded and came back as the lorry moved away, and the words carried between houses in pieces: suspended, closed, indoors.

Elsie stared at the steam rising from her tea and did the sums she could not stop doing. No buses meant no depot shift. No shift meant no pay packet. No pay packet meant coal first, food second, pram arrears letter still waiting at the bottom of the tin where she had hidden it. She pressed two fingers into the wood grain of the table to stop herself listing the rest out loud.

Noreen broke the silence. "You're not going back over there tonight."

"I'm going. If he comes home and I'm not there—"

"If he comes home, he'll find a note and two feet of common sense between his ears." Noreen set her mug down hard enough to slosh tea. "Elsie, don't be dense for once in your life. The yard at yours is a slip of ice and your fire's barely going. You stay here."

"Peter won't like it."

"Peter can write a memo to God about it." Noreen stood, already reaching for paper. "We'll leave him one as well, for his precious records."

She wrote quickly, her handwriting round and neat: Elsie staying due closure and failed line. Tom missing since last night. Back in morning if roads clear. She pinned it to the hall mirror with Peter's brass cufflink, then laughed once at the impropriety of that and did not bother apologizing.

As dusk thickened, they crossed to Harrow House together to collect what Elsie needed. Wind shoved at them in the yard. Snow had crusted over the coal scuttle by the back step. Indoors, Elsie moved fast, naming tasks as she did them because naming kept panic from setting up house.

"Nightdress. Comb. The wool socks from the drawer, not the thin ones. I need the tonic bottle. If I skip two doses I go light-headed. Nappies should stay by the range in case the roof leaks."

Noreen watched her shove things into a market bag and did not interrupt until Elsie opened the pantry and stood looking at two onions, half a bag of flour, and three tins as if she could shame them into multiplying.

"Pack it in, Elsie," Noreen said, quick now. "Leave the pantry. You'll count the spoons next and freeze doing it."

"Tomorrow I might be digging him out of a drift."

"Or tomorrow he'll crawl back stinking of stout and cards and you'll skin him with the bread knife. Both options can wait till morning."

The joke landed better this time. Elsie almost smiled.

They banked the Harrow fire deep, checked the back latch twice, and left the kitchen clock wound on the mantel where Tom could see it if he came in. Elsie wrote a second note and propped it in front of the sugar tin: At Noreen's. Lines down. Knock hard.

By seven the power failed.

It went in a single snap, not a dimming, the bulb over Noreen's stairs dying mid-flicker so that one moment they were two women in a narrow hallway and the next they were shadows with matches. Wind thudded in the chimney. Somewhere down the lane a child started crying, then was hushed.

Noreen lit two candles, one in a saucer on the table and one in a jam jar by the sink. "There," she said. "Grand hotel." She pulled the curtain tighter against the window and fed paraffin into the heater with careful hands. "We'll keep one room warm and shut the rest."

The kitchen shrank around the flame. Every sound from outside arrived sharper for the lack of electricity: slate rattling, the gate chain tapping, the far bark of a dog that would not settle. Elsie sat with her back to the range and both hands around a hot-water bottle wrapped in a tea towel. Heat spread slowly through her coat and stopped at her knees.

Noreen was never still for long. She kneaded dough for morning rolls, wiped flour from the table with the side of her hand, checked the paraffin, rewrapped the kettle lid with cloth to keep the heat in. She talked as she moved, little practical things about whose pipes had burst last year and how to keep oranges from freezing if you buried them in flour, like she was laying planks over a hole.

Elsie let her. Listening was easier than speaking.

Near nine there was a knock so sudden and loud they both flinched. Noreen opened the door to a gust of snow and a young postman in the wrong coat, one sleeve pinned up where his left arm should have been. He held out two telegram envelopes in his gloved hand and looked embarrassed before he spoke.

"Not for this number," he said. "For next street, but I can't make that lane now. Can you hold them till morning?"

Noreen took the envelopes. "Of course. Come in and warm yourself."

"Can't. They've got me doubling route and I still need to clear three roads." He shifted his satchel higher with his shoulder stump in one practiced motion. "You ladies stay inside, yeah? They've closed the viaduct. One bus already slid sideways near mill bend."

He gave a brief, almost shy nod and stepped back into the storm before either woman could answer.

Noreen shut the door slowly. "I thought we'd heard all the bad news there was. Turns out weather finds new pockets."

They ate stew from the pot, thin but hot. Elsie tasted mostly pepper and tin. Halfway through, the baby rolled hard enough to make her suck air through her teeth.

"Pain?" Noreen asked at once.

"No. Just elbowing for room."

"Cheeky little thing." Noreen's smile was brief, then gone. "You're not bleeding?"

Elsie shook her head.

Noreen looked relieved and then guilty for showing it. "Sorry. I ask everyone that now. I asked Mrs Caulfield that this morning when she said she'd slipped in her yard. Woman's sixty-two and I asked if she was bleeding like she was one of the clinic mothers."

"You can ask me." Elsie scraped her spoon around the bowl. "I'd rather that than people saying, 'You'll be fine,' when they don't know."

Noreen sat down across from her with her own bowl and did not eat yet. Candlelight sharpened her face, turning her mouth serious. "Did nobody ask you last time?"

The question was simple and hit with the blunt shock of metal on tile.

Elsie looked at the candle, then at the spoon in her hand, then at nothing. She could have said, There isn't a last time. She could have said, It was years ago and not worth dragging through this kitchen. She could have got up and stacked plates and made herself useful until midnight. Instead she heard herself say, "No. They asked Tom if he wanted tea while I was still pushing."

Noreen's fingers stilled around her bowl.

Elsie set the spoon down carefully. Her palms had started sweating against the pottery. "I was twenty-four. First baby. We were in rooms off Myrtle Street then, above a butcher's, and I thought the pain meant I was doing it right because everyone said pain was the job. We waited too long to call anyone because Tom kept saying first labors take ages and I was making a fuss. By the time the district nurse came my waters had gone green and she swore straight at him before she even got her coat off."

The room pressed closer. Wind shoved at the window and the frame answered with a thin creak.

"They got me to St Luke's in a taxi with no heater," Elsie said. "I remember the vinyl seat sticking to my legs and the driver opening his little flap every five minutes to ask if I was done yet like he had another fare waiting. I pushed for... I don't know. Long enough that the clock changed shifts and I heard two sets of cleaners in the corridor." Her breath snagged. She pressed the heel of her hand to her sternum and swallowed before she could keep going. "I remember everything useless. The pattern on the curtain. The nurse's lipstick bleeding at the corners. The way the metal stirrup was colder than outside."

Noreen did not move.

"When he came out he did not cry," Elsie said, voice flat now, because flat was all she could manage. "He was heavy enough, dark hair, fingers like Tom's mother. He just... did not start. They rubbed him. They put that little mask on and pumped and one of them counted out loud like numbers might bully him into it. Then one nurse put him under a blanket and looked at the wall instead of me."

Elsie dragged her thumb through a ring of spilled stew on the table and watched it smear. "They told Tom first. He stood at the end of the bed with his coat still on and said, 'Right, well, we'll have another,' like he'd missed a bus and was planning the next one."

The shame she had kept boxed for seven years came up not as tears at first but as heat, a dry furious heat behind her eyes. "I asked to hold him and a doctor said it might be better if I remembered him peaceful. Peaceful. Like I was asking for a photograph, not my own child."

Noreen made a small sound in her throat, not a word.

"They gave me pills to dry my milk," Elsie went on, because stopping now would make all of it pointless. "It came in anyway. Through my nightdress, through the sheet, soaking everything. Every woman in that ward with a living baby had a bell by her bed and all night I listened to bells and crying and nurses saying, 'Good girl, latch him,' while my breasts swelled till my skin shone and there was no mouth to put to them."

She could hear that ward now as clearly as if the storm had stripped the years away: metal trolleys, rubber soles, infants rooting and failing and finding. She had never told this whole of it to anyone. Not her mother before she died, not the clinic midwife at checkups, not the women who said sad thing and touched her elbow in the queue.

"I bled for three weeks," she said. "Tom couldn't stand the sight of the cloths drying by the range. Said it put him off his tea. On New Year's Eve he went out and came back singing and I scrubbed the chamber pot while he slept. We never named the baby because Tom said names make a thing harder to forget."

There it was: the ugliest part, the one that still made her feel disloyal to the dead.

Noreen stood and came around the table. For a second Elsie thought she might hug her and stiffened on instinct. Noreen did not. She set a folded dish towel in Elsie's hands first, something to grip, then crouched so they were level.

"He was your son," she said quietly. "Named or not."

Elsie looked at her. Candlelight trembled over Noreen's cheekbone and caught in the damp at her lash line.

"I never say him out loud," Elsie whispered. "If I say him, it feels like I'm stealing."

"You're not stealing." Noreen's voice roughened. "You're carrying. There's a difference."

That cracked whatever was left of Elsie's control. She bent over, elbows on knees, and cried into the towel with her mouth open because the sound had to go somewhere and she could not bear to wake the lane with it. The first sob tore her throat. The second folded her farther down until her forehead hit her wrists. Snot and tears ran together into the cloth; she gagged once on her own breath and coughed without getting air. Noreen kept one hand between her shoulders, steady pressure, nothing theatrical.

When the wave passed, it left hiccups knocking through her chest and a fine tremor in both forearms. She tried to speak and only got, "I-" before her voice split into a rasp. Noreen fetched water and held the mug steady until Elsie's fingers stopped skidding on the handle.

The kitchen sat in an awkward, honest quiet: candle spit, wind at the sash, spoon ticking where Elsie had dropped it against the bowl. Elsie wiped her nose on the dry corner of the towel and tried again. "Shouldn't have dumped all that on you."

Noreen waited until the whole sentence was out. Then she wiped the table in slow circles with the hem of her apron because there were no clean cloths left and housekeeping was how she made mercy practical.

"I'd rather you drop truth on me than feed me lies like stale cake," she said. Her voice stayed rough, not soft. "Don't apologize for telling me what's real."

The candle burned low. They banked the range and carried blankets into the front room where the chimney drew better. Noreen made up the sofa with two quilts, then added her own wool throw without comment. Elsie took off her boots and sat, rubbing warmth back into her toes. The baby shifted again, gentler this time.

"You want me in here with you?" Noreen asked.

"You'll freeze in your own bed if the fire drops."

"Answer the question."

Elsie considered the dark ceiling, the sound of wind finding every crack in the frames, the knowledge that Tom might be in a ditch or in another pub or under another story she would never verify. "Stay till I sleep," she said.

Noreen nodded and sat on the floor with her back against the sofa, quilt wrapped around both of them where it trailed. They listened to the storm settle into its late rhythm, less gust now, more continuous shove. Once, from the other side of the wall, came the deep cough of Mr Henshaw followed by a muttered oath. Ordinary noise. Proof of other bodies enduring the same night.

After a while Noreen said, "When roads open, we'll go looking again. Properly. Depot, sheds, brother, all of it."

"We?"

"Aye, we. Don't make that face. I can still walk and shout and frighten men."

Despite everything, Elsie laughed once into the quilt. It hurt, but it was a laugh.

"Sleep," Noreen said, and squeezed Elsie's ankle through the blanket, brief and matter-of-fact.

Elsie drifted in and out with the candle guttering by the hearth. In one waking moment she heard Noreen in the kitchen, writing something at the table, pen scratching steady on paper. In another she heard the front latch rattle and held her breath until she recognized the pattern as wind, not hands.

Near dawn the storm eased enough that she could hear individual flakes striking the window in dry ticks. The house had gone cold at the edges, but the center held. Noreen had fallen asleep with her head against the sofa, mouth slightly open, one hand still hooked in the quilt as if holding a rope.

Elsie watched her for a long time before she moved. Then she reached down and tucked the blanket higher over Noreen's shoulder, careful not to wake her.

Tom was still missing. The roads were still closed. Nothing practical had been solved.

Elsie sat up, took Noreen's pencil from the table, and wrote the dawn split on the back of a telegram envelope: Elsie to rail sheds and bridge post on foot, Noreen to wake Henshaw for the viaduct ditches, Peter's black book for Tom's brother in Hebden the moment the line breathed. She did not lie down again. She watched the front latch and waited for enough light to start.
