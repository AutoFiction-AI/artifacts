# Chapter 10

In Noreen's back room, Elsie woke before the light had chosen a shape with her fingers still locked around Noreen's wrist from the night. The room was all one color, a thin iron gray, and the blanket over her had gone damp where two breaths had soaked it through. Her left hip hurt from the bed frame. Her mouth tasted of ash and salt and the tannin of tea gone cold in a cup she had never finished.

Noreen lay on her side facing the window, knees pulled up, one hand under her cheek. In the half-dark she looked younger and more tired at the same time. Her hair had come loose from its pins in the night, black against the pillow, and there was a red mark at her shoulder where Elsie had held her too hard and not let go. Elsie knew the exact shape of that mark because she had put it there with her own hand, with her own hunger. She had not been drunk. She had not mistaken a body for a blanket. She had known what she was doing each second of it.

The floorboard outside the room gave one long complaint. Then came a cough from down the landing, a man's cough trying not to be loud. Arthur. Billeted men learned to cough politely, as if lungs could be taught manners.

Noreen opened her eyes.

"He's up," Elsie whispered.

"I heard." Noreen's voice was rough from sleep. She did not pull away. She looked straight at Elsie. Her gaze dipped to Elsie's mouth and snapped back to her eyes. "You're shaking. That's cold, not regret, yeah?"

Elsie put her hand under the blanket and found Noreen's wrist. The skin was warm, pulse quick. "Both," she said, because anything else would have sounded like acting.

Noreen gave a breath that was almost a laugh, almost not. "Fair enough. Come on, then. We need the grate lit before he clocks we were dawdling." 

They got up in a silence full of small accidents: a sleeve inside out, Elsie's stocking twisted at the ankle, Noreen hissing when she bent to pick up her slip and her back pulled. Elsie saw the bite mark she had left on Noreen's collarbone and had to look away to knot her apron string properly. She tucked the sheet hard under the mattress, smoothing the middle where their weight had left a trough. Noreen flipped the top pillow and turned the lower one so both looked slept in from one side.

"Settle downstairs," Noreen said, practical now, fastening her cardigan at the wrong button first and fixing it fast. "If he asks, you took the settle after the fire went." 

"If he asks, I'll say I snored and kept you up," Elsie said.

"You did not snore. You did other things louder." Noreen's eyes flicked up, wicked and tired together. "Don't go pink now. We haven't got time." 

A kettle lid clinked in the kitchen below. Arthur moved like a careful lodger, never slamming, never claiming noise. Even so, every sound came through the house because the terrace walls carried and the cold made timber talk.

Noreen took one step toward the door and stopped with her hand on her belly. Her face changed. She swallowed twice.

"Bucket," she said.

Elsie got the enamel bucket under her just before Noreen retched. There was not much in her stomach, only sour water and yesterday's potato. Noreen spat, wiped her mouth with the back of her wrist, and said, "Well. Romance." 

Elsie rinsed the bucket with the little water left in the wash jug. "You've not kept breakfast down three mornings this week. That's not romance, either." 

"I know what it is. It's this child making a nuisance before it's got lungs." Noreen leaned against the dresser and closed her eyes for a second. "You all right?"

The question landed in Elsie like a hand pressed to a bruise. She nodded, then shook her head. "I don't know what to call it yet." 

"Don't call it a mistake. That's all I'm asking this minute." Noreen opened her eyes again. She was fully awake now, that quick brightness she got when she decided on a line and stood on it. "You can call it madness, bad timing, sin if it helps you argue with God. But don't tell me we slipped. We didn't slip. We climbed into it." 

Elsie looked at the frozen corner of the window where ferned ice had grown overnight on the inside glass. "I won't call it slipping."

"Good. Now move. If Arthur gets to the scullery first he'll see the lamp oil on your hem and ask why you were kneeling on my floor at two in the morning." 

They went downstairs together, not touching. In the kitchen the range was dead, ash white and soft. Arthur stood by the table with his sleeves rolled and his braces hanging, rubbing goose fat into his knuckles from a tobacco tin before he pulled on his gloves. The cracks across his fingers were deep enough to shine pink.

"Morning, Mrs Harrow," he said, nodding at Elsie with the careful courtesy of a man who slept in strangers' houses for a living. "You made it through then? Thought your side had gone black about midnight." 

"It did," Noreen answered before Elsie could, crossing to the range with the poker. "She came over when her fire dropped. Slept on the settle wrapped like a parcel. Look at her, still blue." 

Arthur checked Elsie's face, took in the dead grate, and seemed to make whatever ledger he needed. "Right thing to do. Board says we're on staggered cuts again tonight. I'll be out by five if roads hold." He rubbed his hands once, sharp. "I've left half my bread in the tin if either of you run short at dinner. It's stale enough to tile a roof, but it soaks." 

That was Arthur: offers instead of opinions. He said it as if leaving bread was a minor clerical adjustment, not kindness.

"Keep your bread," Elsie said. "We'll manage." 

"You'll do no such thing," Noreen said. "You'll keep your bread and we'll keep ours and everyone'll pretend this is normal. Sit down a minute and warm your hands. Your fingertips look like candles." 

Arthur did not sit. He stood with his mug and watched steam choose the least cracked path up the kitchen air. "Pipe outside's got a skin of ice," he said. "I can break it before I go. Save you wrestling it." 

"Please," Noreen said, and meant it.

Elsie knelt by the grate to lay paper, slack coal dust, two small lumps, one larger at the back. Her fingers worked by habit while her mind snagged on scraps of the night: Noreen's breath caught against her neck, the way her own name sounded in Noreen's mouth when she was close to sobbing and close to laughing at once. She pushed a match under the paper and watched flame take reluctantly, then commit.

Arthur drank fast and set the mug down. "If Mr Walsh asks, tell him I checked your outside pipe at six and Mrs Harrow crossed for heat. Better he hears one story than invents his own." 

He pulled a bent queen of hearts from his pocket, flattened it once with his thumb, and tucked it back. "My little girl called this the liar's queen. Keep your story matching and you'll be left in peace." 

"Right. Outside pipe." 

The back door shut. Cold came in around it anyway.

Noreen moved beside Elsie at the grate and fed in two more pieces of coal. Their sleeves brushed. Elsie felt heat in the place between her shoulder blades that had nothing to do with fire.

"He'll keep his own counsel," Noreen murmured.

"Maybe. People keep counsel until there's something to buy with it." 

"You're not wrong." Noreen crouched slowly, one hand braced on her knee. "You need to go next door before anyone sees your boots still here. Take the potato sack and my ash bucket. Looks like trade, not trouble." 

"You've got enough potatoes as it is." 

"You've got less. And don't start noble nonsense before tea." 

Elsie took the sack. It was heavier than it looked, dirt still on the skins. Noreen passed her the ash bucket and, under cover of the bucket handle, pressed two fingers hard into Elsie's palm, then let go.

"Tonight?" Noreen asked, barely sound.

Elsie stared at the blackening paper in the grate. "I don't know if tonight's safe."

"I asked if tonight. Not if safe." 

Elsie lifted her eyes. "We'll talk this afternoon. Properly. Terms." 

Noreen's mouth tightened, then eased. "Terms, then. Go on." 

Elsie crossed the yard with ash and potatoes and the small private ache of leaving a warm room for one where nobody had breathed beside her in weeks. Frost squeaked under her soles. Her own back door stuck; she had to shoulder it twice. Inside, Harrow House held the kind of cold that had edges. The sink had iced in a crescent where yesterday's rinse water sat. Tom's coat was not on the hook. His boots were still gone.

Tom was still gone, and there was no explanation and no relief either.

She set the potatoes in the pantry, emptied ash to the metal pail, and began morning inventory aloud because saying numbers pinned her mind down. "Coal: eleven full scuttles if careful. Paraffin: three quarters of a bottle. Oats: two and a half jars. Dripping: one crock. Bread: one loaf if no mold." She pulled the loaf from its cloth. One corner had gone green overnight. She cut it away and fed the spoiled bit to the stove.

From next door came the thud of Arthur's boot against the outside tap and his muttered, "Come on, you stubborn bastard," to the pipe. A man swore at metal differently than at people, with less performance and more patience.

Elsie scrubbed the settle blanket where her knees had left grime in case anyone checked Noreen's story against this room. She arranged the cushion with the faded rose print and put her own scarf over the arm. She hated that she thought in evidence now. She hated more that she was good at it.

A knock came on the shared wall: once, pause, once. Noreen's rhythm.

Elsie knocked back with the poker handle.

Noreen's voice came through plaster, muffled but clear enough if you knew the tune of it. "You lit yours?"

"Just now." 

"Arthur got the pipe free. He's nicked one of my good spoons to lever ice." 

"He can keep it if it keeps water running." 

Silence, then Noreen again, softer. "I meant what I said upstairs." 

Elsie leaned her forehead against the wall, paint cold as tin. "So did I." 

She heard Noreen move away; boards creaked under her weight. A moment later the kettle whistle rose and fell in two houses at once like an argument and an answer.

By ten the lane had woken into its freeze-day habits. Shovels scraped. A pram wheel squealed somewhere down by the corner. Mrs Lilley shouted at one of her twins for licking an icicle off the drainpipe. Elsie took in coal, shook out a rug, peeled potatoes for broth. After that she carried two jars of rendered fat and a strip of bacon rind next door in plain sight. They moved around each other in Noreen's kitchen with the ease of women who had worked side by side for months, only now every accidental brush had a second meaning under it.

No one would have called it dramatic. That was the trouble. A life could split without fanfare, right in the middle of peeling vegetables.

---

By afternoon the sky had gone the color of dishwater and stayed there. Elsie stood in her scullery with both sleeves rolled above her elbows, washing nappies in water she had boiled twice and still did not trust. Soap grated under her nails. The line in the yard was stiff as wire. When she pegged cloth up it froze into shape before she reached for the next one.

Noreen rapped at the back door and came in carrying the blue enamel basin between her hip and forearm.

"Can I steal your dolly peg? Mine's split," she said, then shut the door behind her with her heel and lowered her voice. "Peter's still not back. Arthur's gone to the substation. We've got half an hour." 

Elsie wiped her hands on her apron. "I said we'd talk."

"Then talk." Noreen set the basin down. Her cheeks were wind-burned from hanging wash and her hairline was damp with sweat. Pregnancy made every chore two chores. "Don't make me guess what face you'll wear tonight." 

Elsie glanced at the window. The frost she had scraped from the pane an hour ago had grown back in white veins. "In here?"

"No. Yard. If anyone hears, it'll sound like washing talk." 

They went out to the strip of backyard between privy and coal shed where the wall blocked half the wind. Elsie could smell damp brick, smoke, and the sharp ammonia of old snow where someone had let chamber water out too near the door.

Noreen pulled a basket of clothespins close and reached across it to catch Elsie's chapped hand. Her thumb dragged over a split knuckle and Elsie hissed. Noreen bent and kissed that knuckle once, quick and hard, before she let go.

"All right," Noreen said. "Terms. You first. You go listy when you're scared." 

Elsie pulled her hand back under her apron and looked at the wall where Mrs Lilley's line ran above the bricks. "Term one: no touching in open yard. Not by this wall, not by any window. One child on a milk crate sees us and we're finished." 

"Fine. Hands decent in public, sinners in private." Noreen tilted her head. "Next." 

Elsie rubbed the sting out of her knuckle against rough cotton. "Both houses still run. If your fire drops, I cross with coal. If mine goes, you bang the wall. We still show where we're due. Last night doesn't buy us a holiday." 

"Agreed. Fires first, faces straight." Noreen sorted pegs into neat little ranks and snapped a warped one clean in two. "What else?"

"Babies come first, always. If either pregnancy turns bad, pride can go hang. We call Keane, we call each other, and if the men object they can object while we walk past them." 

Noreen nodded once. "Yes. No heroics." 

Elsie swallowed. This was the line that would either keep her alive or ruin her. "And we stop calling last night a slip." 

Noreen closed her fist around the broken peg until stove polish marked her palm. She looked up slowly. "Good. Keep going. Plain, Elsie, not chapel." 

"I'm trying." Elsie took a breath that hurt her ribs. "I wanted you. I still do. That's true. Also true, if this lane gets a smell of it, we're finished. Peter'll call it duty and pin you in with a prayer book and a ledger. Tom'll sell the story for beer money if it clears one debt. So outside, we're neighbors. Inside, we keep our heads and we don't get careless near windows." 

Noreen held her gaze without blinking. "I know what they'll do. I live with one of them." She shook her head once. "But Christ, Elsie, you can turn wanting into pantry sums." 

"Pantry ledgers tell me who's fed by morning," Elsie said.

A half-smile pulled at Noreen's mouth. It dropped before it could settle. "You left out one term. Mine." 

"Go on." 

"Don't come to me only when you're freezing or frightened and then button yourself up by breakfast. I can't be your emergency stove." She tapped two fingers against Elsie's breastbone, right over the hammering beat there. Her voice roughened. "If we're doing this, we're doing the waiting too. The dull bits, the scared bits, all of it. Not just the parts that burn." 

Elsie felt the back wall at her spine, cold through wool. "I don't know how to do all of it. I know how to keep us fed and out of sight." 

"Neither do I. I'm not backing out." 

From two gardens down came the squeal of a gate and a child singing off-key about oranges. Both women turned their heads by reflex. When the singing skipped into a shout, they looked back at each other.

Elsie said, "All right. We don't lie to each other. We don't promise what we can't pay. If one of us needs space, she says it instead of vanishing." 

"Better." Noreen dug in her apron pocket and brought out three clothespins painted black at the tips with stove polish. "Now don't roll your eyes at me. I made code." 

Elsie stared. "You've already made code."

"I was awake at dawn, wasn't I?" Noreen held one peg up between finger and thumb. "One black peg on my line, left side by the drainpipe, means I need you after dark. No knocking till the top room curtain's shut. Two black pegs means don't come near, house is unsafe. If you need me, same on yours." 

Elsie reached for the peg. The polish smudged her fingertip. "And if someone asks why black pegs?"

"We say I ran out of proper ones and Arthur gave me these from depot stores. Men are always hauling home ugly bits of something and calling it useful." Noreen shrugged. "It's stupid. That's why it works."

Elsie turned the peg over, feeling the spring bite. "We need a spoken one too. For when we're at the front and everyone's looking." 

Noreen thought for a beat. "'Blue soap.' I ask, 'Have you got blue soap?' and it means I need you alone. You answer, 'Used the last of it,' and I know it's not safe yet. You answer, 'I've a sliver left,' and it means yes, later." 

"Is that too clever?"

"Maybe. But we're women on a lane talking laundry. We could run a whole conspiracy over nappies and no one would hear it." 

"Fine. One black peg for tonight, two for danger. Blue soap for words." 

Elsie put the peg in her apron pocket like it was hot.

Noreen leaned back against the wall and shut her eyes for a second. "There. Domestic espionage. My mother would cross herself, then ask for updates." 

"Mine would pray till her knees gave out." 

Noreen opened her eyes. "Do you want to pray?"

Elsie surprised herself by answering fast. "No. I want both babies breathing by spring, and I want us still speaking when we get there." 

"Amen, then." 

They stood with the basin between them and did not touch. The wanting sat in the yard like another person, patient, inconvenient, impossible to send home.

Mrs Lilley's voice cracked over the wall. "Nora? You got any bicarbonate? Little Alfie swallowed snow with soot in it and he's sick as a dog." 

Noreen's face changed in an instant to neighbor-bright. "Coming, love!" she shouted back. To Elsie, quieter: "See? No one lets you brood on this lane." 

She grabbed two nappies from the basket and walked to the fence with the easy sway she used in public, half flirt, half harmlessness. "I've got some, aye. Tell him if he licks gutter ice again I'll box his ears myself." 

Elsie took that cue and lifted the basin, headed for her own back door, and nearly collided with Peter Walsh coming through his gate from the alley.

He wore his office coat though it was Saturday, collar buttoned, hat rim rimed with sleet. His shoes were city shoes, wrong for this lane, thin leather already whitening at the seams. He had a packet of papers under his arm tied with green tape and a fold-out umbrella snapped at one rib.

"Mrs Harrow," he said, tipping his head exactly enough. He did not smile. "You are industrious." 

Peter always sounded as if he were dictating minutes to an invisible clerk.

"Trying to stay ahead of it," Elsie said, shifting the basin on her hip.

"Quite." His gaze moved from her face to the basin to Noreen by the fence and back again, calculating. "Noreen informs me there was a power failure of uncommon duration. I gather you sheltered here." 

Elsie kept her mouth neutral. "My grate died before midnight. Noreen had enough coal to keep one room warm, so I came across."

"Sensible in extremis." Peter's glove creaked as he tightened his hand around the paper packet. "I prefer prior notice when my household acquires overnight guests, but weather is untidy by nature." 

Noreen turned from the fence and came toward them carrying a little orange biscuit tin of bicarbonate for Mrs Lilley. "Peter, don't start accountancy with breathing people," she said, light as butter over hot bread. "Elsie would've frozen next door. You'd have done the same if it'd been Mr Hale with no heat." 

Peter looked at her for one long beat. His gaze dropped to the tin in her hand. "You are dispensing our supplies again." 

"Half a spoon for a child. We're not financing a revolution." 

His jaw moved. "You are always amusing when you are refusing instruction." 

Anger flared in Elsie, clean and dangerous. She tamped it flat under practicality. "I'll send over a pinch from mine tonight," she said.

Peter inclined his head. "Unnecessary. We are not destitute." 

Noreen's laughter thinned just a fraction. "No one said we were." 

He stepped aside to let Elsie pass but did not take his eyes off her until she had crossed into her yard. She could feel them between her shoulder blades like cold nails.

Inside her kitchen she set the basin down and braced both palms on the table edge until the tremor in her wrists eased enough to lift a kettle. Through the wall came muffled voices: Peter's measured baritone, Noreen's quicker answers, then a scrape at the dresser and the dry click of wood on wood.

"What is this black grease on the pegs?" Peter asked, every word neat.

"Stove polish. I marked the split ones."

"You will not mark household items like dock tags. Give them here."

A drawer thudded shut in the Walsh kitchen. The coded pegs were gone before dark.

Elsie peeled turnips for stew and nicked her thumb. Bright blood welled and looked black in the low light. She sucked it clean, wrapped it in rag, and went on. She kept the pot moving so it would not catch, banked the fire before it died, and stitched the hem she had torn on the coal scuttle. Frost furred the pane again. Every few minutes she paused mid-wring with a dish cloth twisted tight and bent her ear toward the wall, then forced herself back to work.

At dusk she took washing from the line with numb fingers and brought the pegs in by handfuls. Mostly plain wood, swollen from wet. One black-tipped peg from her own basket fell into her palm and left a dark stripe across her skin.

On Noreen's line, left side by the drainpipe, there was nothing but bare cord. Through the Walsh parlor curtain, Peter's shadow crossed once, then again, angular and slow.

She should have waited for daylight and kept to the terms she had set. Instead she slid the black peg into her apron pocket, poured tea into a chipped mug, and crossed the yard.

Noreen opened the scullery door on the second tap and pulled Elsie in by the sleeve. The room smelled of boiled cabbage and paraffin. Heat from the range had not reached the stone floor; cold climbed through Elsie's soles.

"He took all three," Noreen whispered. "Counted them like coins and put them in his desk. No more line code while he's in the house."

"Then tonight's gone," Elsie said. "No wall knocks, no back-door runs. We reset in daylight." 

"Tonight's gone," Noreen repeated, then caught Elsie's wrist and guided her hand under her cardigan to the hard warm curve of her belly. "Feel that? She kicks every time he starts on ledgers."

The kick landed once against Elsie's palm, sudden and intimate enough to hitch her breath. She bent and pressed her mouth to Noreen's knuckles, tasting soap and salt.

A floorboard in the passage gave a sharp crack.

"Noreen?" Peter called. "Who is in there?"

Noreen moved first. She shoved a chipped blue soap bar into Elsie's hand and raised her voice. "Mrs Harrow's run out. I said she could have a sliver."

Elsie forced her own voice steady. "Sorry to bother. Mine went soft by the stove."

Peter appeared in the doorway with the hall lamp held high, his glasses flashing. His gaze landed on Elsie's face, then the two women standing too close to the sink.

"You will ask in daylight next time," he said. "This house is not a corridor." 

Noreen smiled without warmth. "Then keep the roads passable and I'll keep perfect hours." 

His mouth tightened. "From this evening, the back gate is bolted at six. If Mrs Harrow requires anything, she asks at the front door and in daylight. I will answer." 

"You're posting office hours on my kitchen now?" Noreen said.

"I am setting order," Peter said. "Do not be insolent."

Elsie went out with the soap biting damp into her palm. In her yard she turned once. Noreen had not shut the door yet; she stood in the gap with one hand on the frame and the other on her belly, watching.

Elsie did not lift a hand. She closed her fist around the black peg in her pocket and went inside to lay out a lie she'd now have to keep.

In her kitchen she gave the shared wall one light knock, their old check beat. Nothing came back. A second later she heard Peter slide the Walsh back bolt home, metal on metal, final as a lockup gate.

Outside, the thin run of meltwater on the Walsh step had begun to glass over.
