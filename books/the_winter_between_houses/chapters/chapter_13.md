# Chapter 13

Elsie tipped the dwindled coins from her coat pocket onto the kitchen table and flattened three pound notes under the sugar bowl. She counted the notes a third time before she even unbuttoned her coat.

The bus cold from bringing Noreen home still clung to her coat hem, and frost had climbed halfway up the kitchen window from the inside, feathery and white as cheap lace. A little hill of silver sat to one side, sorted into shillings and sixpences, and the copper had gone green at the edges where damp had got in. She kept a stump of pencil near her elbow and wrote figures on the back of last week's fish wrapper because she had used the clean page from Tom's old notebook for Noreen's clinic times.

Rent first. Coal second. Mrs Bhatti before Saturday, even if it was only a part payment and a promise. She wrote 

`rent 2-6`  
`coal 1-4`  
`Bhatti ?`

and then she stopped, because there was no arithmetic symbol for a man who came home with empty pockets and a grin that wanted to pass for charm.

The house sounded wrong without his tread. She could hear next door clearer than her own hallway. Noreen's kettle lid clicked and settled, clicked and settled again. A muffled male voice followed, Peter's clipped schoolroom tone even through the wall. Then silence, and the kind that meant someone had decided speaking cost too much.

Elsie tucked the money into the tea caddy and shut the lid. Her belly shifted hard to the left under her dress, a slow drag as if the baby had rolled to listen too. She rubbed the side where the heel had pressed and went to stir the pot on the hob, mostly cabbage and two rashers cut thin enough to lie about themselves.

When Tom came in, he banged the front door too loud, as if noise could stand in for confidence. Cold rode in with him, and the wet-wool smell from his overcoat, and ale, and tobacco ground into his scarf. He stamped his boots once and coughed into his fist.

"You should've seen North Bridge," he called, voice bright in that practiced way. "Buses crab-walking like old ladies on Sunday shoes. Whole route was a circus."

Elsie did not answer from the stove. She listened for the jingle of wage coins in his trouser pocket. There was none.

He came into the kitchen, grin set, hair flattened by his cap. "Smells decent in here. You put meat in? Christ, that's luxury."

She set the spoon down. "Post came."

"Oh aye? Anything nice?"

"From Calderbank Depot office. Brown envelope with your name on it. It's on the mantel."

He looked past her toward the front room, then back. The grin stayed but his eyes moved fast, quick as a boy checking whether his hand had been caught in the biscuit tin.

"Probably rota nonsense," he said. "They keep sending paper because they love the sound of themselves."

"Open it."

"Later, Els. Let me sit down first, I'm frozen through."

"Open it while I'm here."

He scratched his jaw, eyes flicking to the mantel. "You'd make a decent foreman, you know that?"

She did not move.

Tom sighed and went through to the front room. She heard the envelope tear, then nothing, then the paper fold once, carefully. He came back with it in his hand and an expression he tried to keep blank.

"Administrative thing," he said. "They've made a fuss over a short bag on Tuesday."

"Short by how much?"

"Not much. Bits. A few quid that'll turn up in some bloody drawer."

"How much, Tom?"

He slid into the chair by the table and spread the letter as if the paper itself might defend him. "They're saying fourteen pounds and ten from remittance, and another six and change from a late shift float. It's nonsense."

Elsie stared at him. "Twenty pounds is not bits."

"I didn't say twenty, did I? I said they're saying it. Auditors count one way in the morning, another way by tea."

"Did you take it?"

"I moved money around." He held up a hand before she spoke. "Listen to me first. I moved money because I needed two days to square a private matter. Two days, Els. It got caught because that jobsworth Martin came in early and did his little hero routine."

She could hear her own breath pulling sharp through her nose. "What private matter?"

"Nothing you'd thank me for dragging indoors."

"Try me."

"Bookie pressure." He said it quickly, like spitting out a fish bone. "There. Happy now?"

The kitchen seemed to tilt for a second and then set itself right again. The pot lid rattled and she did not reach for it.

"How much do you owe?"

Tom rubbed both hands over his face, as if he could warm them that way. "One hundred and forty-seven to Billy Cragg. That's the hole."

She sat down opposite him because her knees had stopped trusting the floor. "And you took depot money to feed it."

"Don't say stole. Christ."

"What word do you want?"

"Borrowed against Friday. I had a line coming in."

"You had a bet."

"A line," he snapped, and then softened at once. "Love, I know how it sounds. But you didn't see the board that day. It was a banker. Anybody would've taken it."

"Anybody with my name on his pram papers too?"

He held his breath.

She had not planned to bring it in yet, but the line had come up out of her before she could choose it. She watched his face rearrange itself around the new calculation: how much she knew, who had told her, which lie still had room to breathe.

"That again," he said at last. "We sorted that."

"No. You told me not to fuss and I wanted the room to stay standing, so I let you walk away. That's not sorting."

"You're carrying enough as things stand. I kept the uglier bits off you."

"You put the uglier bits in my name." She leaned over the table until the edge pressed hard into her stomach. "Dates, Tom. When did you start taking depot money?"

He swallowed. "Three Fridays back. First time was five quid. Paid it Monday. Then twelve, then..."

"Then?"

"Then it got away from me."

"How many times?"

"Four."

"And who knows?"

"Depot office. Maybe transport police if Martin keeps barking. Nobody's gone to a station yet. This is still internal if I make it right before Monday hearing."

Elsie looked at the letter. The words jumped around, but she picked out enough: `disciplinary suspension`, `pending inquiry`, `attend 8:30 Monday`. At the bottom, typed clean and merciless, `Failure to account for public monies may result in police referral.`

She could taste metal from the kettle steam. "You're suspended."

"Temporary."

"Which means no wages this Friday."

He flinched. "Half pay while they look. Maybe."

"Maybe is not rent."

He slapped his palm flat to the table and forced cheer into his voice again. "All right. That's why we're talking now, eh? That's why I'm straight with you. We fix it before Monday and they can't prove intent. That's the game."

"Game."

"Figure of speech. Don't start hanging me with grammar." He leaned in, palms open, matey grin trying to crawl back. "I've got a route. I need a little bridge money, just enough to put back what they're shouting about. Then I show up clean, say records got crossed in storm overtime, and we're done."

Elsie heard next door's wall clock strike four, muffled and officious. The baby kicked once, a hard knock under her ribs.

"Where does this bridge money come from?"

He hesitated for a beat. "Your ring."

The word landed heavier than the coins in the tea caddy.

Tom rushed on. "Only for a week or two. Alfie Dent gives fair rates if he knows it's me. You pawn it Friday, I clear the short, I buy it back once wages restart. Nobody sees anything."

She turned her left hand over on the table. The wedding band sat loose now because her hands had thinned despite the swelling everywhere else. The gold was worn white at one point where it rubbed against the bucket handle when she scrubbed floors.

"Not that ring," she said. "You don't get to spend it."

"You haven't even heard the full plan."

"No, Tom. Not for your patch job."

"Elsie, don't be stubborn for pride. It's metal. It's a circle."

"It's mine."

"It's ours." His voice sharpened. "Unless that changed and nobody told me."

She held his gaze. "You want the truth? It changed when you signed my name and called it protection. It changed when you took fare money from a public bag and came home smelling like stout."

He pushed his chair back hard enough for one leg to scrape and shudder on the stone floor. "I came home, didn't I? That's more than some. I came home and told you what was what."

"Because a typed letter did half the telling first."

Tom's mouth twisted. Shame looked like anger on him; she had learned that years ago and still found herself checking for the bottle when he raised his voice.

"You don't get to stand there pure," he said. "You knew cash was tight and still you handed bits over next door every other day. Soup, tins, coal. You think I didn't notice?"

"I kept a pregnant woman fed."

"You've got one in your own house." He jabbed a finger toward her belly and then dropped his hand, as if embarrassed by it. "I'm trying to keep us out of court, Els. Help me do that."

She stood, slow and careful, hands on the table to lever herself upright. "Tell me everything before you ask me to bleed for it. Names. Amounts. Who's pressing you."

He stared at her, breathing through his mouth.

"Billy Cragg runs the book for this side," he said finally. "His runner's little Len with the cracked front tooth. I was in deep before Christmas and kept chasing because I thought one win would pull me clear. Cragg wanted twenty by Thursday night or he'd start calling here. I took depot money to keep him off the door. Lost again on Saturday match that never should've turned. There. That's everything fit to print."

Elsie pressed her fingers against the table edge until the varnish bit into her skin. The numbers lined up now. They made a shape she hated because it looked solid.

"You put us under a man who sends boys to doors," she said.

"He sends reminders, not knives. Don't make it East End melodrama."

"And the depot hearing?"

"Monday morning."

"And if you can't replace it?"

He looked away first. "Then it's transport police. Maybe court. Maybe prison, if they fancy an example."

The kettle hissed dry enough to scream. Elsie reached past him and lifted it off the ring.

"Tea?" she asked, voice flat.

Tom blinked. "What?"

"You're shaking. Tea won't fix theft, but it'll stop your hands rattling long enough to listen." She set the kettle down and faced him again. "We're not done."

She did not put milk in either cup. There was half an inch left in the bottle for morning and she had made that decision before he asked.

Tom drank too hot and burned his tongue and swore at the cup. By the time he slammed it down, the window had gone dark and the cold had thickened in the corners.

"Right," he said. "Here's the bit you won't like."

Elsie sat opposite with her cardigan buttoned to her throat, hands in her lap so he could not see them tremble.

"You've already done that bit."

"No, listen. Ring gets us maybe twenty-five. I can raise another five off Stan."

"Stan?"

"My brother."

"The one who still owes you from Easter?"

"He'll cough if it's family disgrace on the line."

"He'll cough if you tell him a version with you as victim."

"Call it what you like. Between that and a story at Monday hearing, we can contain it." He leaned forward, lowering his voice as if the wall itself took notes. "If they ask where short money went, you say you had me hold housekeeping cash because you were worried about thieves in the lane. You say I mixed bundles by mistake with depot takings during overtime. With weather chaos, they might buy that."

Elsie felt heat rise up her spine, not from the stove. "You want me to stand there and lie for you."

"I want you to keep your husband out of a cell," he said. "Out of court. Out of something the whole lane chews on."

"By putting me in it." She tapped the table once. "I've got three pounds twelve and six in that caddy. Rent's two-six. Coal's one-four if Langley still has sacks. There's no mystery housekeeping bundle for your Monday story."

"Then don't give figures," he said quickly. "You keep it broad. You say you were scared after those lane thefts and shoved notes at me in a hurry. Snow week, overtime, everyone mixing things up."

"Mine aren't mixed."

"Els, come on. You're my wife."

"I'm your wife, and I'm the one they'll question if you use my name."

Tom dragged a hand across his mouth, buying a second. "All right, different route." He pointed toward the front room where their wedding photograph sat on the mantel, silver frame gone black at the corners. "Ring goes to Dent's till payday. One week, maybe two. You keep the ticket in your apron. If anybody asks, you needed coal money."

She leaned in, voice low. "You're not taking this ring, and I'm not feeding another lie into your depot file."

"Don't talk like you're clean in this if I go down," he snapped. "Barrow Lane won't split hairs. They'll call you thief's wife, and they'll put it on the baby too."

"They already count every potato in my basket when I buy on credit. A police statement is new trouble, and you know it."

The urge to throw the cup at him came so suddenly she almost smiled at it. She set the cup down instead, carefully, and laced her fingers together until the knuckles hurt.

"No," she said. "I kept this house warm when there was no coal. I kept food moving when your wages wandered. I took shame in spoonfuls because I thought we were on the same side. You made me collateral without asking, and now you want my ring and my mouth as well."

He rubbed his hands over his scalp, flattening his hair and lifting it again in the same motion. "Christ, Els, don't make me beg in my own kitchen."

"Then stop bargaining with things you've already spent." She held up her hand and folded one finger after another as she spoke. "No pawning the ring, and no statement with my name in it. Tomorrow you go to Stan and raise what you can without forging, gambling, or touching this house's coupons. Monday you tell the depot hearing what you took and when."

"You're insane." He jabbed two fingers at the tabletop. "You just wrote my conviction speech."

"I wrote the only version I can repeat when they put me under a light."

"You want me to confess intent?"

"You took it four times. Call it what you like."

"Don't use that word at me again."

"Then give me another word that isn't a lie."

The silence that followed had weight. Outside, someone dragged a dustbin along the frozen flags and the metal shrieked all the way to the corner.

Tom stood and began pacing between stove and sink, three strides each way. "All right. Alternative. I pawn it myself on my name, not yours. Hear me out. I bring back the slip, you lock it in the tin, done. If everything settles, I buy back before anyone sees."

"No. If that ring leaves this house, Dent takes his cut and I have nothing left to show for ten years except the gap on my hand."

"You'd rather nurse a principle while transport police knock?"

"I'd rather stay out of the lie that gets typed."

She felt her pulse in the scar line under her wedding band, a hot quick beat that had nothing to do with the stove. He was trying to make panic sound like common sense again.

"You think truth protects you?" He turned, face blotched with cold and rage. "Truth's for men with solicitors. People like us survive on stories that pass inspection."

"Your stories are why we're here with no wages on Friday."

He stopped pacing. "Say the line," he said.

"What line?"

"Say you'll stand with me Monday. Sit there, look worried, keep it simple. That's all I need."

"I'm not sitting in that depot office nodding through a story about cash I never gave you."

She pictured Monday's office in one hard flash: wet coats steaming on chair backs, men pretending not to stare at her belly while they waited for her to lie cleanly.

"Don't give me chapel talk."

He stepped closer until she could smell stale ale sweetening on his breath. "Then say it plain. You with me or not?"

Elsie met his eyes and felt the room narrow to the width of the table between them.

"Not like this," she said. "Not through my ring. Not through my mouth lying for you."

He stared as if he had misheard a foreign language.

"After all this," he said, quiet now, almost gentle, which frightened her more than the shouting had. "After years, you'd choose a piece of gold over me."

"I'm choosing rent paid Friday and my name off your betting mess."

He gave her a look she had seen only once before, when the stillbirth had ended with clean sheets and no baby cry and he had stood at the foot of the bed unable to speak. Hurt and fury braided together until they were one rope.

"Fine," he said. "You want lines? Here's one. If I walk out tonight, I don't carry this rent next week. I don't carry coal. I don't carry your little social smile with the neighbors either. You can explain my absence however clever you like."

"That's Friday rent gone and next coal sack gone. Say threat if you mean threat."

"It's arithmetic." He snatched his cup and hurled the dregs into the sink. "You think you've been doing sums alone?"

She got to her feet too fast and had to catch the table edge. Pain pulled low across her abdomen, a hard belt, then eased.

Tom saw it and softened for one beat. "Sit down," he muttered. "Don't start anything with the baby because of this."

"Don't point at my pregnancy to win this." Her voice dropped until it almost vanished. "You want to leave, go. You want to stay, you stay under terms I can live with."

"Terms." He showed his teeth with no humor in it. "You've turned into a solicitor overnight."

"No. I turned into the person who has to remember exactly what happened when men start asking questions."

He flinched as if she had slapped him.

"Jesus," he said. "Who are you now?"

She thought of Noreen's swollen ankles in clinic shoes, of Keane's dry voice in the hallway yesterday: `If she seizes in this weather, you may not get a second chance.` She thought of the pram receipt with her forged name. She thought of the baby inside her and how little room was left for pretending.

"I'm the person left counting coins and answering doors when you've spent your charm," she said.

For a moment he looked ready to strike the table again, maybe the wall, maybe anything that sounded louder than shame. Instead he grabbed his coat from the hook, shoved his arms into it, and headed for the front room.

Drawers opened, shut, and opened again.

Elsie followed, one hand on the hallway wallpaper to steady herself. "What are you doing?"

"You said go. I'm going. Stan's got a settee."

"Take your own clothes. Leave the ration book."

He turned with a shirt half-hanging from his bag. "Don't be ridiculous."

"Ration book stays here."

"I need my coupons."

"You can get replacements through your brother if you're out of household. You don't take this house's food with you."

His mouth opened, shut. "You're serious."

"About food? Always."

"Fuck this, Elsie. Fuck your bloody terms," he said, not even trying to keep it down. The words smacked the plaster and carried straight through the wall; next door, a chair scraped and then stopped dead. The baby jolted hard under Elsie's palm, and heat climbed up her neck.

"Happy now?"

"No."

He jammed socks and a shaving tin into the bag, grabbed his bus cap from the peg, and paused at the door with his hand on the latch.

"When they come asking," he said without turning, "remember you had a chance to help me make this small."

Elsie's hand went to her ring by reflex. She pressed the band into her palm until the edge marked her skin.

"When they come asking," she said, "I'll tell what I know and no more."

His throat worked around something he did not say, then he yanked the door open.

Cold blew in sharp enough to sting her teeth. His boots pounded down the two steps, suitcase knocking against his knee, and then the gate banged once, twice, like punctuation.

The house settled around the silence he left.

*

For five full minutes Elsie did not move. She stood in the hallway with one hand on the peeled banister and listened to every sound she had not noticed while he was filling them: the tick of the clock, the soft simmer from the kitchen, a cough through the shared wall, the steady drip where thaw had started to find a crack above the front window.

On the sixth she lurched to the sink and gripped enamel hard enough to squeal it against the wall. Her heart kicked at her throat in fast dirty thuds. She bent over the basin and retched up pepper broth and spit, strings of it hanging from her lip before she wiped them with the heel of her hand. The baby drove one blunt heel into her side and held it there until her breath came back in thin pulls.

Then she went to the table and opened the tea caddy.

Three pounds, twelve and six in silver, one and nine in copper. Not enough for rent and coal together. Not enough if Monday brought a police knock and a landlord with opinions. She drew Tom's letter toward her and laid it beside her own scrap list.

She dragged the scrap closer and wrote if no Tom wage down the side. The pencil bit so hard it tore the wrapper and kept going. She came up fourteen shillings short on rent before she even counted coal, with only half a scuttle left and milk for maybe two days if she stretched it thin. A drip from the thaw crack above the window hit her wrist as she wrote clinic fare for Nora?.

She stared at the last line until the word blurred, crossed out `Nora`, and wrote `Noreen` back in, firmer this time, as if the full name might make the problem sturdier.

From next door came a faint knock on the shared wall: two taps, pause, one tap. Their new code since chapter ten's quiet mornings, a question dressed as plaster.

Are you there?

Elsie tapped back once.

In a minute the back latch lifted and Noreen slipped in through the scullery door, wrapped in a grey cardigan worn inside out so the seam would not bite her swollen wrists. She carried an enamel mug and a folded dish towel under one arm. Her hair was pinned up badly, escaping in damp curls at her neck, and one slipper heel had been crushed flat from being stepped on in a hurry.

"I heard the gate," Noreen said quietly. "And him saying Christ knows what in the lane. You all right?"

Elsie nodded, then shook her head because there was no useful shape for the truth.

Noreen set the mug on the table. "Barley broth. I put too much pepper in because Peter won't eat it that way. Lucky you."

Even now, even with her ankles puffed above her socks and a bruise-yellow tiredness under both eyes, she found room for mean little jokes like that. It was one of the things Elsie trusted most in her.

"Sit," Noreen said. "You're white as the sink."

"I'm fine."

"You're vertical. That's not the same."

Elsie sat.

Noreen lowered herself onto the opposite chair with both hands braced on the table, breath hissing once as her back protested. She glanced toward the front hall, then back. "Did he hurt you?"

"No." Elsie touched her ring again before she could stop herself. "He wanted this pawned. Wanted me to lie for him at depot hearing."

Noreen's face hardened, then flickered with something like pity for Tom before she smothered it. "And?"

"And he stole from work to pay gambling debt. About twenty from depot short bag so far. Owes bookie one-forty-seven. Hearing Monday."

Noreen let out a breath through her teeth. "Sweet Jesus."

"He said if I stood with him we'd make it small."

"By making you an accessory." Noreen rubbed her lower belly absentmindedly, fingertips tracing slow circles through the wool, the way she did when worry climbed too high. "What did you say?"

"No ring. No lie. Confess or don't, but without me in it."

Noreen gave a tiny nod, then reached into her cardigan pocket and pulled out a wrapped eucalyptus sweet. She always kept one there because the iron tablets made her gag and mint made it worse. She unwrapped it, sniffed, and set it on the table without eating it.

"So he left," she said.

"Settee at his brother's."

"Then he left." Noreen's voice went flat on the word, no soft edges. "That's the fact."

Elsie felt tears sting and hated them instantly. They came anyway. Her chest hitched twice, then locked; she set the mug down too fast and broth slopped over her fingers as a cramp cinched low across her belly.

Noreen was up before the mug stopped rocking. She cupped Elsie's jaw with one hand and pressed the other into the small of her back. "Breathe with me. Slow. In through your nose. That's it. You're here."

"I told him to go," Elsie said when she could force words out. "I wanted him gone for months, and when the latch went I wanted to run after him and drag him back by the sleeve. What's wrong with me?"

"Nothing's wrong with you. He made home feel like a fire and a blanket in the same hour for years. Your body doesn't drop that habit because he slammed one door."

Elsie scrubbed her wet face with both hands. "I'm furious at him. I'm scared for him. I'm scared I'll carry his mess in my name till this child's grown."

"You can be scared and done," Noreen said. "Both fit in one body. I've been carrying both since Peter started counting out my bus fare like parish dole."

"I keep hearing the gate."

"You'll hear it all night." Noreen pushed the mug back into Elsie's hands. "Drink first. We can bully tomorrow into shape when it shows up, not marry what your heart wanted tonight to be."

Elsie swallowed broth that tasted of pepper and bay and cheap pearl barley, thick enough to feel like food. Warmth spread into the hollow behind her ribs where panic had sat all evening.

"I don't know what to tell people," she said.

"Tell them what keeps you safest." Noreen shrugged, grimacing when the movement pulled her side. "Night rotations, brother ill, depot overtime. Pick one plain story and wear it threadbare till spring."

"If police come, story falls apart."

"Then you don't start with lies you can't carry. Keep it mean and dull." Noreen rubbed her thumb against the eucalyptus wrapper, eyes on Elsie. "You know he had debt. You know he walked after a row. After that, you've got nothing to add. That's true, and true is lighter to haul."

Elsie stared at her. "You're good at this now."

Noreen barked a laugh. "Practice."

For a moment they sat in the kitchen's thin warmth, shoulders hunched against drafts, the wall clock ticking them forward whether they were ready or not.

"Peter knows?" Elsie asked.

"He knows there was shouting. He pretended not to hear words because hearing words means obligations." Noreen reached for the eucalyptus sweet, then put it back untouched. "He'll ask in the morning and I'll tell him as little as possible. Arthur heard the gate too, likely. He was in the spare room mending that ridiculous cuff of his where sparks burned it again."

Elsie pictured Arthur with his frost-cracked knuckles and patient quiet, stitching work shirt cuffs with electrician's tape on the table because thread kept snapping in the damp. Another set of eyes she had not chosen.

"Social cover's gone," she said. "It's one thing when a husband vanishes for a night. Bag in hand is another."

"Aye." Noreen looked at her directly. "And cover isn't food."

Elsie let that sit.

Noreen leaned back carefully, one hand at her lower spine. "Let's do sums. What have you got cash and coin?"

"Three twelve and six, plus coppers."

"Rent's two-six."

"And coal's one-four if Langley still has sacks."

"Then rent gets paid first, so nobody's got an excuse to nose into your rooms." Noreen lifted one shoulder in a tired half-shrug. "I'll stretch ours for a week and walk to Bhatti with you Saturday. She'll carry tab if she can see your face and not just Tom's promises."

"You need your own food. Keane said salt down, rest up, no drama."

Noreen rolled her eyes. "Keane says many correct things in one breath. Rest is the one this street never stocked."

"Nora."

"I know." She pressed her lips together and nodded once. "I know. I've been seeing little lights at the edge again when I stand too quick. I won't pretend." She gave Elsie a look half-defiant, half-weary. "But I'm not leaving you alone in this kitchen tonight with that face."

Elsie reached across and laid her hand over Noreen's. The skin was warm and dry, knuckles rough from washing nappies in water that never stayed hot long enough.

"Thank you," Elsie said.

Noreen's thumb moved once under her palm, a private stroke gone as soon as it happened.

From next door Peter called, voice formal even in irritation. "Noreen? Where have you gone?"

Noreen closed her eyes for a second. "There he comes, as reliable as tax." She stood, using the table as leverage, and winced. "I'll come back first light. We'll get your words straight before milk rounds."

At the door she turned. "Hide the ring somewhere stupid, not obvious. Men always check sewing tin first."

Despite everything, Elsie gave a breath of laughter. "Where then?"

"Flour bin if you've got any flour left. Nobody digs there unless they're saints or starving."

When Noreen had gone and the scullery latch clicked, Elsie took the ring off for the first time since her wedding day.

The pale line beneath it looked indecent, skin that had not seen air in ten years. She held the ring between finger and thumb, felt its heat fade, then walked to the larder and pushed it deep into the last three inches of flour in the tin, under a folded tea cloth so she could not see the spot and make herself mad by checking every minute.

She washed the cup, banked the fire, and laid Tom's depot letter flat under the breadboard where damp would not curl it.

At half six, Mrs Pritchard from the end house knocked to return the enamel pie dish from Christmas and let her eyes travel once over the hallway where Tom's boots were missing.

"Quiet in here," Mrs Pritchard said.

"Tom's with his brother tonight," Elsie said, voice neutral. "Family matter."

"Oh." Mrs Pritchard held the dish a little tighter. "Right. Well. If you need paraffin, our lad got an extra can."

The offer carried curiosity like a second handle.

"Thank you," Elsie said. "I'll remember."

After she shut the door, she leaned her forehead against the cold wood and counted to twenty.

Before she got back to the table, three hard knocks hammered the front door, too square for a neighbor and too sober for Tom. A narrow lad in a wet cap stood on the step with his collar to his ears, cracked front tooth showing when he spoke: Len, Billy Cragg's runner.

"Tom Harrow in? Cragg says twenty by dinner tomorrow or he stops being polite."

"He's not here."

Len leaned to look past her into the hallway. "Then I'll ask next door. Folks answer faster when everyone's listening." Through the shared wall came a thump of movement from Walsh's side, chair leg or boot heel, and Elsie caught the door before he could turn away. If he started naming numbers at that wall, Peter would hear every one, and Noreen would pay for it in blood pressure and questions.

"Wait." She went to the tea caddy, peeled one pound note from under the sugar bowl, and folded it twice before pressing it into Len's gloved hand. "You give Cragg this and tell him no more doorstep theatre."

Len rubbed the note between thumb and forefinger. "One quid buys you till Sunday noon. After that he comes himself."

He left with his boots loud on the flags, no hurry in him at all.

By eight the street had gone muffled again, snow under fresh crust, distant shouts from the pub corner blown flat by wind. Elsie sat at the table with the ration book open and a list of meals for seven days written in her careful hand: cabbage broth twice, potatoes and onion if Bhatti had any, barley stretch, bread heels toasted, egg only if she could trade for one.

Under it she wrote, hard enough to score the table through the paper, tell no lies you can't keep straight.

She started a fresh scrap and wrote, "He left after a row about money," then stopped when the front latch jumped under a hard shove.

Tom's voice came through the wood, thick with drink and cold. "Open the fucking door, Els. I left my key."

She stood so fast the chair legs screamed on stone. The baby rolled hard under her ribs. She slid the bolt across, palm slick, and kept her mouth near the panel. "You'll come back tomorrow sober. Not tonight."

"Don't you make me a spectacle out here." His fist hit the door again. Across the lane a curtain twitched. In Walsh's front room floorboards creaked once, then twice.

"Go to Stan's," she said. "You're not coming in shouting."

He cursed, kicked the bottom panel, then went quiet enough for her to hear him breathing.

"You lock me out now, it's done," he said.

"It's done because you made it done."

Something metal scraped through the letter slot and clattered across the hallway tiles. His house key spun once, rang, and stopped against the skirting board.

"Keep it then," he shouted, the words already moving away toward the gate. "Keep your bloody house."

Bootsteps slipped on the frozen flags, then the gate slammed and the lane swallowed him.

Elsie picked up the key. It stayed warm from his hand for one second and then went cold as sink water.

In bed, the mattress felt too wide and too cold on the side where he had slept. The springs complained every time she turned. Through the wall she heard Noreen vomiting quietly into a basin, then the soft murmur of water, then Arthur's low voice in the hallway offering to fetch more coal bricks from the shed. Peter answered with a curt no that meant yes if no one named it that.

Elsie lay on her left side and watched the moonlight make bars across the ceiling through frost-scratched glass. Her baby moved once, then settled.

She did not pray. She tucked Tom's key under the mattress seam and listened while pub shouts thinned to coughs and the drain outside began ticking with meltwater, one drop, two, then a run.

By morning she would be a woman with a husband elsewhere. By Monday she might be a thief's wife in every mouth on Barrow Lane. By next week, if Tom's wages stopped fully, she would be choosing between coal and clinic fares with a child pressing down in her own body and another neighbor's blood pressure rising next door.

She put her hand over the hard curve of her belly and spoke into the dark, not loud enough for anyone else to hear.

"We'll keep the door," she said. "We'll keep the truth as near to plain as this street allows."

In the kitchen, her torn sums sat under the sugar bowl: cash 2-14-3, rent 2-6, coal 1-4. Fifteen and nine short before milk, before clinic fare, before morning.
Somewhere after midnight, rain turned to the dry hiss of ice against the glass.
