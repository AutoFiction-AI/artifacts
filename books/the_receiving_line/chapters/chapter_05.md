# Chapter 5

Bleach had dried white in the creases of Marisol's knuckles by the time she looked up at the departure board. CAR 8 RELEASE in 00:18. SOUTHBOUND SLOT CONFIRMED. The red digits hung over Caldera's platform big enough to shame everybody. Under them the bay floor sweated old coolant, mop water, and a sweetness from the vending-machine syrup line that had been leaking for weeks. She stood at the wall terminal with her supervisor passcard clipped to her pocket. The clock lost a minute while she watched it. The line did most of its violence that way, with the digits stepping down while nobody touched the board.

She had spent twenty years learning which messes were considered real and which were only labor. Blood on restraint vinyl was labor. Urine pooled under a toilet flange was labor. Vomit in a family bunk was labor unless it got into the foam, and even then there were grades, there were codes, there were ways to write a body back into dispatch. But once waste was described as airborne, once contamination crossed from surface to system, no one in compliance wanted to own the release. The company could forgive filth. It could not forgive the words that made filth legible.

Car 8 was amber, not red. The aft-lavatory ammonia reading was high, and moisture sat under the lower bunks. The sensor history showed one brief backspit after intake, then a stable line. It was a tired car and a hot day. Nothing unusual. Marisol touched the incident field. The rubber of the glove caught on the screen.

`Cross-surface fecal aerosol following blackwater backspit during hold. Soft material exposure unresolved. Full deep clean required before dispatch.`

The system asked for source witness and authorizing supervisor. She entered her own number twice.

The first honest part of the lie was the filth itself. It had existed, as it always did. She invented the threshold, the point at which discretion ended and protocol became compulsory. She selected FULL COMPARTMENT SANITATION, acknowledged the warning that bedding, restraint foam, and temporary detainee displacement would be required, and pressed submit before she could start naming what the order would cost.

The red banner hit the board with a hard electronic chirp. CAR 8 RELEASE disappeared. HOLD - SANITATION REVIEW replaced it.

A custody sergeant came up the platform so fast the ring on his left hand kept striking the rail. He was a broad man with a peppermint swollen into one cheek and the damp collar of somebody who had quit cigarettes three times already this year and hated everyone for noticing. He looked at the board, then at Marisol.

"Tell me you hit the wrong box."

"I didn't. Aft backspit, soft surfaces, possible vent exposure. I need the compartment cleared."

"We sealed the manifest."

"Unseal it."

He laughed once, without humor. "You want me to pull thirty-one people back into holding because a toilet burped?"

"I want you to move them because if aerosol got into the vent track, you're about to spend six hours breathing shit for a schedule nobody's going to thank you for."

"You just found this now."

"I found it when I found it."

The sergeant stared at her long enough to show he heard the edges in that answer. The clock above them was already converting delay into money, incident emails, supervisors called away from better forms of contempt. Then he turned and shouted for the officers to crack the car back open.

Marisol moved before the echo finished. Authority, once spoken, had to keep moving or it turned into argument. She keyed the sanitation locker, issued two more Tyvek sleeves than the shift had budgeted, and told the cleaners to strip every soft surface in 8 from door gasket to rear lavatory. "Blankets first, then foam, then straps. Double-bag anything wet enough to drip. Don't leave me one dry-looking corner unless you're ready to sign your name to it."

A younger cleaner with a cracked phone screen glanced down at a text alert and swore softly in Spanish. The day-care app on the glass showed a late-pickup fee climbing by the quarter hour. He shoved the phone back into his pocket and took the bags without complaint. Another woman, older and already limping in one ankle boot, pulled her braid over her shoulder, tucked it into the neck of the Tyvek, and asked, "Whole compartment?"

"Whole compartment," Marisol said.

The woman blew air through her teeth. "Christ."

The shift had already run long enough that everybody's calculations were exposed. The cleaners did not talk about housing deductions in front of management, but Marisol knew them as well as she knew bleach ratios. Miss the crew shuttle and you paid for your own ride back to the dorm block. Miss enough scheduled turns and you lost the heat bonus that made June survivable. Every extra half hour on a hold climbed into somebody's groceries. The company loved sanitation work for that very reason. It made harm look like arithmetic.

Marisol did not apologize. The moment the order existed, apology was only vanity.

By the time the first officer got the side door rolled wide, the smell reached them in layers: old urine cooked into metal, bleach from the last turn, the sourness of milk spit, and beneath it all the wet-sewer heat of a blackwater system that had been asked to pretend it was fine too many days in a row. The car had family bunks on one side and standard restraints on the other, a compromise design that pleased nobody except the people who wrote procurement notes. The lower mattresses were dark in patches. A bucket sat under the aft sink because someone had given up trusting the drain.

There were thirty-one detainees in the car, and the hold made all of them harder to place. Officers unlatched chain points, cut two temporary ties, barked everybody upright. A toddler who had been half asleep started screaming the moment the door opened all the way and the platform light hit him. A man with diarrhea stains dried down one calf tried to ask for a toilet and got handed a specimen bag instead. A woman with a bandaged wristbone under a paper hospital strip kept saying she needed her inhaler from the property sack and nobody could tell her whether the sack had already gone to reseal.

Meal trays that should have come down the line in twenty minutes were delayed because nobody served into a red-tagged compartment. A blood-pressure round stopped halfway through another car when the officers got pulled to supervise displacement. Marisol heard a child asking in a flat stunned voice whether the blanket would come back with the cartoon dogs still on it and knew the honest answer was probably no. Deep clean translated every small possession into either evidence or trash.

Marisol signed the displacement sheet and hated how steady her handwriting looked.

While the lavatory seals stayed open, officers passed down specimen bags and told people to hold it. A boy tried squatting behind the yellow chain and got yanked upright by the elbow. At the service cart one of the cleaners shouted that the rinse drum was empty. Marisol signed for another without checking stores. Extra water meant another quarter hour before anybody could call the car habitable.

A cropped-haired girl from the middle bunks sat down on the concrete the moment an officer let her and began retying her paper wristband with the patient concentration of somebody repairing fishing line. Two fingernails were missing from her right hand. She used the torn edge of a meal-wrapper strip to reinforce the knot. Gabriel was three bodies beyond her, wrists linked, the old scar through his left eyebrow gone white while the chain between his cuffs pulled tight and his gaze caught on the stripped bunks. He had seen the red banner on the board and understood whose hand had done it.

Marisol kept moving. If she met his eyes too long in public, the whole lie would slant toward him.

The cleaners went in fast. They stripped bunks until the car looked flayed, foam slabs stacked by the door like pale organs, damp blankets slung into orange bio bags, restraint straps dropped hissing into bleach tubs. One of the men climbed onto the upper frame and wiped the vent track with a white pad; when he brought it down, a brown fan of spray bloomed across it, exactly the pattern Marisol had needed and had not actually found. That almost made her sick. Dispatch could have photographed that pad and called the whole hold obvious.

The custody sergeant stalked beside her reading updates off his tablet with the ring still tapping the rail. "Dispatch wants ETA. They want to know whether this is thirty minutes or ninety."

"Tell them I know when the vent swabs come back."

"They're going to ask for images."

"Then send them images."

He looked at the stripped bunks, the overflowing orange bags, the open lavatory panel. "You like this one too much, Anaya."

She turned on him before the thought on her face could become confession. "You want to send them south with aerosolized fecal mist in a closed car, do it under your badge. I won't write it under mine."

That shut him up because liability was a language he believed in.

It also copied the hold to compliance.

A small icon flashed at the top of her terminal: regional review queue notified. Teresa would not be sitting there staring at it yet. Teresa had bigger fires and cleaner shoes. But the notice existed now. If the hold ran long, if there was a discrepancy between the sanitation log and the blackwater history, if anyone got curious enough to pull the raw sensor trace, the order would harden into a real thing with her name on it.

Marisol climbed down to the service side and checked the aft panel herself, crouching with her stiff knee complaining against the metal stair. The line pressure reading was boringly stable. The valve seated clean. The backflow flap had left a smear, but not the kind that justified the words she had used. Deep clean would buy time. It would not buy enough. Once the soft surfaces were swapped and the vents re-swabbed, dispatch would pressure them to release on reduced comfort status. The company could tolerate pissed-off officers, sick children, lost meal windows. It could not tolerate a missed southbound corridor if there was any plausible way to keep steel moving.

A cleaner came out with a mattress skin rolled under his arms like a butchered hide. "You want these all incineration, boss?"

If she marked them salvage, the car would clear faster. If she marked incineration, replacement inventory would have to be counted, scanned, and hauled up from stores, which meant more delay, more cost, and more people sitting on hot concrete.

"Incineration," she said.

The cleaner swore, not at her exactly, but near her. "That was the last stack from Bay Two."

"I know."

He hefted the dripping bundle higher and went.

Each destroyed foam set would hit somebody's route budget before the week was out. Management loved to call that accountability when they wanted supervisors policing one another with shame. Marisol knew where the memo would land if Teresa started tracing expense spikes: first on sanitation, then on maintenance, then on whichever worker had signed the wrong minute in the chain. Sabotage did not remove anybody from the ledger. It only rearranged who got billed.

The hold turned mean fast. Twenty minutes in, the chemical mist from the portable sprayers mixed with rail dust and sweat until everybody tasted pennies and soap. An officer shouted at a boy for leaning against the yellow chain. The boy was maybe twelve and had only one shoe because the other had gone into a contamination bag with his blanket. He hopped back onto the hot sole of one foot and tried not to cry. A mother crouched over the screaming toddler and fanned him with a manifest copy somebody had dropped. The paper went soft in her hand.

One of the portable sprayers coughed dry. The cleaner with the cracked phone lifted the empty canister at her like an accusation. Marisol signed out another concentrate pack and told him to run a second pass on the lower frames and restraint rails. The bleach hit the air sharp enough to water everybody's eyes, and the new wet sheen gave dispatch one more reason not to put bodies back inside.

The board updated again: HOLD REVIEW 00:34. Dispatch had given her half an hour before escalation, more than she had expected and less than she needed. Southbound release after that would still make the next slot at reduced priority, still keep Gabriel moving, still keep the ancestry packet alive.

The sergeant's radio snapped. He listened, jaw working around peppermint.

"They want mechanical cross-check on the aft waste line," he said. "Procedure change after the Brownsville spill."

Marisol took the radio from him without asking and heard the dispatcher repeat it in that dry neutral voice people used when they were already saving messages for later blame. "Supervisor Anaya, confirm whether the blackwater return assembly has been cleared by rolling stock. Sanitation red alone does not justify corridor loss."

Of course it didn't. Somebody in compliance had tightened the rule since the last public embarrassment. Pure dirt never outranked schedule for long.

Marisol handed the radio back. "Get them a mechanic."

"From where?"

Caldera Junction was full of mechanics. Only one of them mattered.

She watched the cleaner on the upper bunk scrape along the vent seam, watched orange bags swell with the smell of other people's bodies, watched Gabriel standing in the holding line without looking at her, and felt the choice settle in her like a swallowed bolt. Under her badge, the lie was already dangerous. Once another badge sat under hers, it would start dragging other people onto the track beside her.

By the time the aft swab came back and told her exactly nothing she did not already know, Marisol had made the decision. She clipped the tablet to her belt, told the sergeant nobody reboarded until she said so, and cut through the service corridor toward the maintenance bay with bleach on her sleeves and dispatch chewing through the last of her clean options.

***

Ivo was halfway under a commuter carriage when she found him, boots sticking out from under the chassis and a socket wrench ticking against the railbed in patient irritation. The maintenance bay at Caldera always smelled different from the cars: hotter metal, axle grease, old rain caught in ballast, coffee burned into a hot plate somewhere nobody admitted owning. A fan turned overhead with the dignity of a dying bird. On Ivo's bench a pair of drugstore reading glasses lay folded beside a maintenance manual, one arm repaired with blue electrical tape. Next to them, held flat under a magnet shaped like a heron, was a small crayon drawing of three black birds on a wire and the crooked printed word `forage` in a child's hand.

Marisol stopped long enough to see that and nothing else. Then Ivo rolled out, wiping his forearm across his face and leaving a dark crescent of grease by his temple.

"If this is about Bay Four, tell them the compressor can wait till Jesus." He saw her expression and his own changed. "What happened?"

"I need a blackwater return valve on Car 8 to fail."

He stayed on the concrete another second, like the floor had become useful. Then he stood. Tall, heavy through the shoulders, gait still carrying that rolling mechanic's sway even when he had not been moving, he looked past her toward the departure-board glow at the end of the corridor.

"Need," he said. "That's a generous verb."

"It's the car under sanitation hold."

"I saw the red. Thought maybe you'd finally lost your patience with toilets."

"Ivo."

"Don't `Ivo` me like I'm a wrench." He reached for the blue-taped glasses, put them on, squinted toward the departure-board glow, then stripped them off and pinched the bridge of his nose hard enough to leave grease there. "What kind of fail?"

"One that buys time. One that looks bad enough not to clear fast."

"Those two aren't the same thing."

He knew, which was part of why she had come. Ivo did not talk in abstractions when machinery was involved. He believed in parts, wear, what load did to metal, what fraud looked like when it had to survive inspection. Marisol heard the dispatch clock even in here. She also heard herself choosing the next words and knew she could still lie smaller if she wanted.

"My son is in 8," she said.

The bay seemed to narrow around that. Somewhere further down, an impact gun barked twice and cut off. Ivo worried the taped arm of the glasses between thumb and forefinger. His mouth opened, closed, and when he looked up again the joke had burned off his face.

"Gabriel."

"Yes."

He rubbed the back of his neck at the little burn scar high on the left side, a habit she remembered from years ago, from nights when he was trying not to tell her he was afraid of money. "I saw a kid with his eyebrow, but I told myself I was making ghosts."

"I need the car held."

"You need the whole fucking treaty held."

"I need this first."

He leaned against the bench and looked past her again, toward the corridor, toward the train she had not named and he did not need named. When he got cornered, he usually joked harder. This time the humor came and went before it could settle.

"If I hang a red mechanical tag on a waste return, it sits under my badge until reconciliation," he said. "They read those logs in August when my pension clocks over. Family court reads my pay stubs at the end of that same month. You know what I've got left on that file."

She did. Six thousand eight hundred dollars was not an abstraction when you earned it with summer overtime and a back that had started answering every ladder in the morning. Ivo had once shown her a spreadsheet on a paper napkin because he did not trust the pension-office website. He had drawn columns for wages, child support, fuel, and rent, then one skinny column labeled only `L.` for his daughter, too superstitious or too scared to spell her name out inside the math. He had not been embarrassed to show her. That made the memory worse.

"I know," she said.

"Then say the number in your head before you ask me again."

She did not. She could not make his arrears into a test she passed by reciting them. Instead she said, "If they clear 8 on schedule, Gabriel's southbound by noon."

Ivo stared at the heron magnet on the bench, at the taped glasses, at the crayon birds. His daughter was twelve now and obsessed with urban raptors one month, drainage ditches the next, any living thing that made a home out of civic neglect. He had started carrying sunflower seeds in his pocket because she had once told him city birds recognized generosity faster than people. Sometimes Marisol found the striped shells on the dashboard of his service truck.

"Paper won't do it," he said finally. "Not anymore. After Brownsville they'll read pressure, seat time, sensor lag. Teresa sees a clean history and I'm dead."

"What will do it?"

He took a brass shaving off the bench with the edge of his thumb. "A dirty history."

She took that as the closest thing to yes she was going to get.

He moved fast once he chose, because mechanics had the decency to know indecision only made later work harder. He grabbed a service kit, a roll of red tags, and a replacement seal still in its plastic. "Come on."

They walked back through the service side under the car line where the blackwater pipes ran warm and low enough to take scalp if you forgot yourself. Ivo crouched at the aft assembly of Car 8, clipped his work light under the housing, and listened with the side of his screwdriver pressed to the valve body. He could have been diagnosing a real fault. He was diagnosing what kind of lie could live through scrutiny.

"Backspit's bullshit," he murmured.

"I know."

"Seal's worn but not failed. Sensor's fine. Return arm's sluggish because nobody flushed the line proper after intake." He glanced up at her. "You want a delay or you want an audit?"

"I need delay without a whistle today."

"Modest ambitions."

He opened the housing, drew the valve plate out half an inch, and let Marisol see how clean it was. It was not perfect, only ordinary. Then he took the brass shaving and lodged it against the seat so small it almost vanished. He reseated the plate, tightened it down, ran the test cycle once. The return motor whined, hesitated, clicked back. On the gauge the pressure dropped wrong.

"There," he said. "Now it's ugly."

"You can fix it later."

"If later comes before they impound the car, sure." He held up the untouched replacement seal in its plastic. "I'm not burning a good part unless I've got to. But I'm tagging this assembly for replacement anyway. Inventory will show I pulled one from stores and returned it unused. That means questions."

Marisol watched his hands move: grease black under the nails, motions economical even when he was committing a crime. He handled the valve with the same plain efficiency he brought to a bearing change, accepting load and consequence together. In his hands the red tag became one more part that had to be seated right the first time.

He keyed his mechanic ID into the tablet and began dictating. "Aft blackwater return valve. Intermittent seat obstruction under load. Incomplete closure on cycle. Release unsafe pending clean-out and manual verify." His voice on the log was flat, almost bored. That frightened her more than if he had whispered. Boredom was how durable lies got written.

He signed. The system stamped his number under hers.

The lie left her body then and became infrastructure. It lived in another worker's badge, another department's ledger, another future meeting where men with polished shoes would decide whether a red tag had been justified. Marisol felt that change with almost physical clarity. The first lie had been panic wearing procedure. This one was alliance, whether she deserved the word or not.

"That's it?" she asked.

"That's enough to hold them if nobody gets ambitious." He peeled a red tag, looped it through the housing, and pulled it tight with his teeth. "If Teresa gets ambitious, we're both fucked and I'll still have grease under my nails in the disciplinary photo."

He stood, joints cracking, and looked at her directly for the first time since she'd said Gabriel's name. "Does he know?"

"That I'm doing this? Yes."

"That you're asking me to?"

"No."

"Good. I'd rather he hate me for the right reason later."

Marisol almost smiled and could not make it happen. "I didn't ask you because of old times."

"I know. Old times never paid a pension anyway."

He shouldered past her toward the platform steps, then stopped and pulled the folded crayon bird drawing out from under the heron magnet. He slipped it into his breast pocket before anyone else could splash solvent on it. Only then did he head up to present the fault.

That small motion undid her more than the signature had.

By the time Marisol got back to the holding lane, the mechanical cross-check had already hit the board. HOLD - SANITATION / WASTE RETURN FAILURE. Dispatch would hate the pairing. Compliance would hate it more. The officers hated it immediately, because it converted waiting into procedure. Nobody reboarded while a waste return sat red-tagged.

The detainees had been shifted into a fenced service strip beside the car, shaded only by the train itself. Heat lifted off the concrete in visible curls. Officers had clipped a long waist chain through the adults on one end and seated the children against the wall on the other where a portable fan pushed chemical air over them. Somebody had passed out paper cups of water too small for anything except argument. The toddler who had been screaming before was quiet now, face slick and stunned with heat, asleep against his mother's thigh in a position that looked painful for both of them.

A little boy with only one shoe had gotten hold of a grease pencil and was coloring the exposed side of the wall until an officer noticed. Two women argued in low exhausted voices over whose property sack had come back missing a sweater. The cropped-haired girl with the repaired wristband sat cross-legged and counted knots into a bread-twist wire, flattening each turn with the blunt pad of her missing-nailed thumb before starting the count over.

Gabriel leaned against the fence with his wrists clipped in front now instead of behind, because somebody had made the reasonable decision that people waiting in heat got uglier when they could not swat flies. His shirt was damp between the shoulder blades. The old scar through his left eyebrow went pale when he looked at her.

She took a property-inventory board from the nearest officer, tucked it against her chest, and started down the line with her eyes on the grid, checking sacks, verifying contamination losses, and accounting for what the hold had turned from possession into waste. It gave her an excuse to stop in front of each person without seeming to stop for Gabriel alone. It also made her look directly at what she had done.

The board filled with the same blunt entries: blanket contaminated, pillow contaminated, one left shoe contaminated, inhaler pending, family photo pending, two cloth diapers contaminated, cheap paperback destroyed in bleach. A woman asked whether she would get her dead husband's letters back dry or wet and Marisol had no honest answer. The inventory box had only PRESENT, DESTROYED, or UNKNOWN.

When she reached Gabriel, he did not move off the fence. "You made everybody sit out here," he said.

His voice was low, almost conversational. That made the line harsher, not softer.

"You'd be moving already if I hadn't."

He looked past her to the toddler, to the old man hunched over a specimen bag behind his knees, to the woman fanning herself with the ruined manifest paper. Then back to her. "Don't sell me the discount version."

Marisol kept the board between them and ticked a false mark she could not later read. "I bought time."

"You bought them time too."

"For what?"

"For another manifest review. Another change of cuffs. More chances to lose a name." He tilted his head toward the stripped-open car. "And for that kid to piss himself because your deep clean shut the lavs."

The words landed without any old family theater in them. Gabriel was looking at the toddler, the specimen bag, the woman with the paper fan, and that made the point harder: the hold had a body count too, measured in smaller units than death.

"I know what it costs," she said.

"No. You know what it costs when the company does it. That's different. When you do it, you still want credit for meaning well."

She almost snapped back at him then, something ugly about keeping him off a port he had never seen, about how meaning well was not the phrase anybody on this line would use for her. But the officer two places down had turned away to answer a radio, and Gabriel's right hand came off the fence long enough to press a folded strip of waxed wrapper paper against the bottom edge of the inventory board.

"Take it," he said.

She pinned it with two fingers without looking down.

"What is it?"

"Berths that don't match the file labels. This car and one in 7. Maybe more after they reshuffle. Follow the bunk tags, not the names. The names get cleaned faster."

The wrapper strip sat warm against her palm, slick with his sweat, exactly his kind of evidence: memory cut down to something that could disappear into a fist. Marisol slid the wrapper into the cuff of her glove and moved her body half an inch to cover the exchange.

"How'd you get these?"

He gave her the smallest glance toward the holding lane, toward the heat-stunned cluster of people she had helped create. "You made them wait. Waiting is when people compare paperwork."

Two places down, the cropped-haired girl was still working the wire, tightening each twist before easing it flat against her palm. When Gabriel said "people," his eyes went there briefly and then away again. Marisol understood. Not all of this had come from him.

"I can't fix every file from a platform hold," she said.

"Then don't pretend the hold itself is the fix."

An officer started back toward them. Gabriel let his expression go blank so fast it was like a door shutting in her face.

She should have moved on. Instead she heard herself ask, very quietly, "What do you want from me?"

Even asking felt obscene. She had spent years translating care into competence because competence at least left a record. Want was another matter. Want had made her stupid before. Want had made her clean scenes she should have left dirty. But the question was out now, hanging between the inventory board and the fence.

The question narrowed him into precision.

"If you're going to do this again, do it for something bigger than me getting one more bad hour in a different place." His words stayed low and quick, the cadence of somebody used to making speech survive surveillance. "Get records. Get tags. Get word between cars. Use the time so it belongs to somebody besides them. And if you come back asking me to walk out alone, don't. I won't."

He glanced once more down the line. "Not while they keep the rest of the car on the bill."

The officer was close enough now to catch tone if not content. Marisol dropped her gaze to the inventory board and dragged one gloved finger down the column until it landed on a random sack number. "You think I can turn a sanitation hold into something bigger?"

"I think time with no use is just another room they built."

The officer stopped beside them. "Problem?"

Marisol flipped the board. "Missing sweater. Property discrepancy."

Gabriel said nothing. His silence could pass for hatred, which on this line was ordinary and therefore useful.

The officer grunted and moved on.

Marisol stepped to the next detainee in line, but the wrapper paper inside her glove had already changed weight. She could feel the recorder in her pocket on the other side, still stolen, still unheard. Together they felt like evidence against the fabricated assignment, names pressed up against the mechanism that erased them. Her hand smelled like bleach and hot paper.

Behind her, the service radio on the wall clicked and Ivo's voice came through with that same infuriating plainness: "Return assembly remains unstable on manual verify. Hold until repeat cycle and flush." There was no drama in it and no hint of favor, only a mechanic doing his job badly on purpose and well enough to make it stick.

One of the cleaners staggered past carrying another bagged mattress skin toward incineration, Tyvek pasted to his back with sweat. The day-care fee on his cracked phone would be climbing. The custody sergeant was already on a second peppermint, jaw working like a man grinding money between his teeth. The woman with the manifest fan had stopped fanning and was simply staring at the concrete. The old man with the bag between his knees did not look up at anyone.

Marisol finished the inventory sweep because that was what the role required and because stopping halfway made suspicion. She marked destroyed what had not yet been destroyed, present what she prayed might turn present later, unknown where the truth could not be reached in time. By the time she handed the board back, the car behind her was gutted to metal and the hold had swollen beyond anything she could call clean.

At the platform edge, she looked up at the departure board again. Ivo's number sat under the mechanical tag and hers under the sanitation order, red beside red. The system had room now for both lies and none for mercy.

She folded the wrapper strip smaller and slid it into the pocket with Gabriel's recorder. The two objects clicked softly against each other when she walked. Around her the train kept extracting payment for the hour she had stolen: from the workers in sweat and late fees, from the detainees in heat, wet clothes, and vanished property, from Ivo maybe in August when men in clean offices would decide whether his service record had remained acceptable enough to let him grow old less poor. When dispatch finally let the steel move again, Marisol rode south in the service alcove, and that debt rode with her in her pocket beside Gabriel's recorder.
