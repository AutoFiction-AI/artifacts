# Chapter 10

By noon the storm water had gone warm in the blackwater traps and the south half of the train smelled as if the marsh had crawled up under the floor to die. First maintenance whistle had come and gone while storm cleanup sprawled and Teresa staged her quarter-close theater, and Marisol still had not made it to the machine-shop annex. She stood in the trauma-room sluice with a deck brush in one hand and a quarter-close notice in the other, reading Teresa's neat signature above the words SUPPORTING LOG COMPRESSION - LOCAL RETENTION WINDOW CLOSING 18:00. Anything older than ten days would be normalized into summary tables for concession renewal review. Handwritten addenda would not be retained unless tied to active litigation. Supplemental sanitation variance notes were to be discarded after supervisory reconciliation.

Teresa had a way of making erasure look like housekeeping.

Paloma came through the inner curtain backward, one hand holding a tray of used hemostats, the other steadying the door with her hip. Her braid had come half loose, and the camphor on her clothes was losing its fight with disinfectant and stale blood. "If you're going to stare at that memo," she said, "do it while you scrub. I need this room clear in six minutes."

"Who's coming in?"

"No one new. Follow-up dressing change." Paloma set the tray down and flexed her right hand once, trying to shake the tremor out of it. "And before you ask, yes, it's her."

Marisol folded the notice into her back pocket. The paper kept its crease like thin tin. "You gave me six minutes. Don't waste two of them."

Naila sat on the exam cot with her shirt lifted to the bottom of her ribs while Paloma unwound yesterday's bandage. The bruising from the refusal crackdown had turned from black-violet to a yellowed green that reminded Marisol of oil on old dishwater. Naila's cropped hair had grown into a rough shadow around her head. She had twisted the silver lining from an oral-rehydration packet into a narrow cord and was knotting it around her left forefinger with the two fingers that still had nails. It wasn't nerves. She was counting, making something hold.

When she looked up, her face stayed still except for the eyes. "You brought paper."

Marisol took Gabriel's scrap notebook out of the inner pocket of her coveralls. It was not a notebook in any proper sense. It was wrapper paper flattened and layered, a ration label peeled clean and used twice, part of a laundry ticket, two bunk tags gone soft from sweat. Gabriel's handwriting crossed over other people's penciled marks. Some names had only initials, some had a bunk and a guessed country, some had nothing but a date and a corner notch.

Paloma peeled tape off Naila's side with slow fingers. "Talk while I work. If she starts bleeding again, both of you shut up."

Marisol spread the scraps on the instrument trolley. "These four. Here, here, here. They show up in Gabriel's list, then nothing. No transfer confirmation, no reassignment on the manifest. You told him some people disappeared before the port."

Naila leaned in. The room's fluorescent light made the sun marks across her cheeks look like bruises laid down years ago and kept. She touched one tag with the pad of her missing-nailed finger. "Not before the port. Before the country. Different thing."

"Then say it clean."

"They went out through clinic or wash." Naila tapped the tag again. "This woman was Celia. Bunk 6-F on the intake bus, then 3-C on the train. She kept the salt packets from the rehydration mix and ate them dry because the sweet drink made her vomit. After the latrine backed up into the aisle, they took her with the ones who had rash and fever."

Marisol heard herself slip into command voice. "Who took her, what color band, which station."

"A custody woman with the throat tattoo. A medic with green tape on her shears." Naila spoke as if laying knots one after another, no hurry, no wasted lift. "White temporary band first, then blue over it after clinic. At Caldera, not on the public side. By the service fence near the linen yard."

Paloma kept her eyes on the bruising, but Marisol saw the pause in her hands. It lasted only half a second, but it was enough.

"You saw her there?" Marisol asked.

"I saw her two days later through the water-fill window." Naila nodded toward the tracks beyond the steel wall, as if the sight line were still open. "She wore a yellow municipal apron. Her wrist was wrapped so thick she used the other hand to shake sheets. She had tucked two salt packets into the seam at her chest. She hid them the same way."

Marisol wanted to say it could have been another woman, another yellow apron, another chest seam hiding contraband. The objection came all the way to her teeth and stopped. Naila did not look like a girl guessing. She looked like a worker whose whole life had depended on noticing which thing in the pile was still usable.

Paloma pressed fresh gauze over the worst of the bruising. "Breathe shallow."

Naila obeyed without complaint. "This one too," she said, pointing at a tag with only M. REYES and a bunk number. "He had one sandal split at the toe. He tied it closed with red bread wire. Coughed all night, then bled from the nose when they hit the brakes too hard outside Marsh Crescent. They called him nontransportable because he would not stop staining the blanket. After that I saw him on the drainage crew by the commuter tracks. It was the same sandal, the same red wire. He was shoveling mud that smelled worse than this room."

Marisol put two fingers on the trolley to steady it. The metal was tacky from the steam Paloma had been running through the wound-cleaning tray. "You told Gabriel all this?"

"Some." Naila rolled the silver cord tighter around her finger. "I didn't have enough words. We had wrappers. We had numbers. We had the way people stopped answering when the lights came on."

Paloma tied off the bandage and sat back. Her eyes looked older in the fluorescent wash than they did over cafeteria coffee. "I can give you three more minutes. After that I have to make it true that she needed the room."

Marisol lifted one of the bunk tags. The corner had been clipped square. She had assumed damage, or boredom, or some detainee habit of marking what belonged to them with the only tool they had. Now the square corner felt intentional in her fingers. "What does this cut mean?"

Naila looked at it once. "Clinic hold."

"You know that how?"

"Because they changed the tags after rescan. Whole corners for families, triangle for transfer group, square for clinic. We learned fast." She glanced toward Paloma, then back to Marisol. "You don't survive rooms like this by waiting for people in uniform to explain the symbols."

Paloma gave a dry short laugh with no humor in it. "No, you don't."

Marisol gathered the scraps into order again: Celia, Reyes, two others with clipped corners and blank transfer fields. The paper smelled of hand oil, old sugar, and the mildew that lived in the train's seams no matter how often she threw caustic at it.

"Why municipal?" she asked. "If they defer transfer for medical, they should go to hospital intake or court hold."

Naila's expression barely changed. That was what made the answer land. "Hospitals let people go. Work yards don't."

The words sat in the room like something dragged in on a boot.

Paloma stripped her gloves off and dropped them in the red bin. "Your six minutes are dead. Take the scraps and get out before someone decides to audit my tenderness."

Marisol tucked the wrapper papers away. "I need Gabriel."

"You always need Gabriel." Paloma reached for the used instruments. "What you need right now is a reason to be at his car."

"Sanitation pull."

"Then pull something." Paloma bent to open the pathology cupboard and, without turning around, said, "The square-corner tags started showing up after Teresa's audit week. I noticed because they made my counts wrong."

Marisol looked at her.

Paloma kept arranging sealed sample jars inside the cooler, her silvering braid slipping against the nape of her neck. "I said I noticed. I didn't say I understood." She shut the cupboard with her hip. "Go."

The higher-security car had been resealed after the storm with fresh strips of tamper tape over every service hatch. Marisol broke the tape on the meal-waste port because sanitation outranked optics when the bins were full enough, and because nobody argued with a smell that had already started to turn. Inside, the air came out hot and wet, thick with old sweat, bleach from the rushed respray, and the sweet sick rot of fruit cups opened too early and eaten too slowly.

Gabriel was three bunks in, one wrist cuffed to the side rail. The bruise along his upper arm had gone yellow at the edges. His eyebrow scar cut through the damp dark of his face like a mark made years before this and kept. He did not stand when he saw her. He smiled once, without warmth.

"Supervisor Anaya," he said. "You look radiant."

"You look like hell."

"That too." His eyes dropped to the port in her hand. "You didn't come for the bins."

Marisol kept her voice low. The custody guard at the far end of the car was flirting with the water-service contractor through the outer door. For the moment, their laughter covered her. "Square notch on the tags. Naila says clinic hold."

Gabriel's expression changed before he answered. It wasn't surprise. It was recognition, and then anger that she had taken this long. "You finally asked the right question."

"I asked what I could prove."

"No. You asked what fit in the box they gave you." He shifted closer, chain dragging against the rail. "Clinic hold, med deferral, civic recovery, municipal stabilization. They keep changing the label because the label isn't the point."

Marisol slid the clipped tag and one page of the scrap notebook through the port. Gabriel glanced once toward the guard, then laid the paper flat over his knee.

"Paper House heard the rumor on three corridors," he said. His voice had none of the performative heat he used when he wanted to hurt her. That made it worse. "People who got too sick, too injured, too contaminated for clean transfer stopped moving south but didn't come home either. They turned up cleaning flood drains, sorting city linens, hauling shelter cots, always under some local contract with a different badge and the same case number buried under it."

"Rumor is not proof."

"No shit."

He tapped the clipped corner. "This isn't a detainee mark. Intake staff use it when they don't want the change obvious from a distance. You can see it in the track labels too. Listen to the recorder. Track seventeen, the woman from North Basin? She says they moved her after a restraint sore on her ankle got infected. She thought deferment meant a ward bed. What she got was a city laundry with a locked dorm and bleach burns up both arms because they had her stripping contaminated sheets bare-handed."

Marisol had heard the recording in the dark at Marsh Crescent, but she had been listening then for the wound in the voice, for the fact of a person naming what the train had done. She had not known what piece of machinery hid inside the nouns.

"Track twenty-two," Gabriel went on, already there, already sorting sound into evidence. "Man from the estuary camps. Broken finger, fever, transport postponed. Four days later he was patching storm screens for Caldera municipal works. He laughed when he said it because he thought he had cheated the ship. Then he said the guards on the work van still carried rail restraints."

Marisol looked past him at the car's white paint failing along the window seam. Rust had come through again, thin as dried blood under skin. "Why didn't you say it plain?"

He gave her a look that would have gutted a weaker person. "I did. You wanted a family exception, not a business model."

The laughter at the outer door stopped. The contractor wheeled a water cart away. Marisol crouched to yank a waste bin free so if anyone looked in she would be doing the thing she was supposed to be doing.

"They purge local supports at eighteen hundred," she said. "Quarter-close compression. Teresa signed it herself."

Gabriel's silence lasted long enough to become its own shape in the air between them. Then he said, "Of course she did. The summaries don't bleed. The raw logs do."

"If this is real, I need event codes, not rumor."

"Then stop thinking like compliance." His hand came through the slot just far enough for her to see the ink still ground into the sides of his fingers from some pre-arrest life that had not finished leaving him. He pointed to the page she had given him. "The names matter, but the categories matter more right now. Match clinic holds to sanitation incidents. Not just beatings. Look at vomit events, lice wash, blood on restraint foam. Anybody who makes the car look unfit to show. Dirt is what moves them sideways."

The phrase hit her in the place where professional knowledge lived: not just beatings, but vomit events and blood on restraint foam, all the things she had spent twenty years learning to clear fast, clear quiet, clear before a camera or inspector or civilian nose could catch them. The train had not merely reacted to those thresholds. It had learned to earn from them.

She stood with the sloshing bin in both hands. The lid rattled from the train's low floor vibration. "If I find it?"

Gabriel leaned back from the hatch. His voice went flat and almost gentle, which was the register he used when he was naming the worst thing in the room and refusing to blink. "Then you get it out of company hands before they turn it into a better spreadsheet."

* * *

The clinic log cage sat behind the oxygen closet and smelled of paper mold, iodine, and the mouse poison somebody had tucked under the lowest shelf in the dumb hope of discipline. Marisol had supervised the cleaning of that room for years before she had ever been allowed to touch the folders inside it. She knew which floor tile rocked, which lock stuck in humid weather, which shelf bowed under the older binders. Losing passcard reach had not scrubbed that map out of her.

Paloma opened the cage with her own credential and left the door on the latch. "You have eleven minutes before the courier starts screaming about signatures," she said. "If you crumple anything, I'll hit you with a tendon hammer."

Marisol already had gloves on. "I'm not crumpling."

"Everybody crumples when they're afraid."

Paloma set a stack of blank prescription pads on the desk and a clinic pencil sharpened down to the metal ferrule. The tremor had come back into her right hand now that she had stopped moving fast enough to hide it. She pressed the heel of that hand against the desk until it quieted. "You can't take originals. If anything goes missing, Teresa will have the whole wing strip-searched on principle."

"I know."

"Do you." Paloma looked at her at last. "Marisol, I'm already hanging by thread and paperwork. So if what you find in there is enough to make you stupid, decide not to be."

Then she was gone, curtains whispering shut behind her.

Marisol pulled the first storm-week binder and laid it flat. Trauma-room entries ran in two columns: intake case number, presenting condition; disposition, signatures, cross-reference. Her own whole career was buried under forms like this, under the faith that if the language stayed exact enough the damage might count as managed instead of chosen.

Case 5128-C: scalp laceration, restraint impact, cleaned and closed.
Case 4410-A: diarrheal contamination, dehydration, observed.
Case 4471-R: rib bruising, intake restraint, no transfer delay.

Gabriel's number caught in her throat before the rest of the line did. The chart listed no transfer delay, as if his bruised ribs were a minor intake inconvenience and not her son cuffed three bunks into a sealed car. She forced herself past his entry and kept hunting the clipped-corner cases Naila had named.

Celia was not listed as Celia. She appeared as 3182-M, dermatologic spread with GI contamination, offloaded under MED DEFERRAL - TEMP CIV STAB, seventy-two-hour review pending, cross-referenced to Sanitation Exposure Event MW-CAL-77, with a destination field that read municipal receiving clinic. The phrase sat there with the clean dead confidence of a lie that had passed through legal twice.

Marisol copied the case number onto the prescription pad, then the incident code. Beneath it she wrote the bunk Naila had given her, 3-C, and the note about the salt packets because if this ever had to become more than paper she wanted the person back inside the code.

She pulled the next binder. Reyes was 4417-L, persistent epistaxis post restraint, linen contamination, MED DEFERRAL - LABOR SAFE PENDING RESPIRATORY CLEARANCE, cross-referenced to MW-MAR-12, destination municipal recovery berth.

Marisol stopped writing. The phrase LABOR SAFE was not medical language. It was warehouse language. Her chemical-burn scar dragged against the page as she steadied her hand. It was how management talked about a pallet with one broken corner that could still carry load if you did not stack it too high.

She copied the line anyway.

The sanitation office kept its own record books in the next room over because blackwater incidents, foam replacements, and contamination holds all touched dispatch-safe status. Those were her books, or had been, before Teresa decided she could no longer be trusted with the full anatomy of the route. Marisol crossed the hall with the prescription pad tucked inside her sleeve.

The office was empty except for the fan in the corner pushing hot air from one side of the room to the other. Someone had left a cup of instant noodles half eaten on the desk, the surface filmed pale with congealed broth. The smell of ammonia from the floor drums cut through it.

Her old key still worked on the lower file cabinet. Teresa had been thorough about electronic permissions and sloppy about metal.

Inside were the NCR log books in triplicate stacks: white copy to compliance, yellow to route office, pink retained until quarter close. Marisol pulled the week of the storm hold and flattened the pages one by one, fingers moving with the memory of shifts when she could do this half asleep. MW-CAL-77 recorded a Car 3 latrine reflux into the central aisle, contaminated linen removal, and four passengers designated nontransportable following exposure review. MW-MAR-12 logged a restraint-soil incident and nosebleed contamination, bedding discard, and one adult removed for municipal respiratory clearance.

The words on the sanitation side were worse, in their way, than the trauma-room lies because they were plainer: reflux, bedding discard, nontransportable. They named the body only when the body had become a problem for surfaces.

Marisol wrote the matching codes down, then opened the top drawer of the desk where route-cost folders sat waiting for signoff. Those should have held contractor receipts for bleach drums, pump seals, biofoam replacements. They did hold those. They also held a thinner folder she had never opened because it had always been Teresa's hand initialing the cover: MUNICIPAL OFFSETS / CONTAMINATION RESPONSE.

She knew before she touched it that this was where the rot would show its bones.

The first invoice came from Caldera Civic Linen Recovery, incident reference MW-CAL-77: four adult temporary placements, two days laundering contaminated public-stock bedding, custodial supervision surcharge payable to RailPassage Logistics, net municipal reimbursement $672.

The second came from Caldera Drainage Authority, incident reference MW-MAR-12: one adult temporary placement, debris-screen clearing and pump-house wipe-down for forty-eight hours, medical observation externalized, net payable $186 plus transport hold credit.

There were more behind them. Enough more that her stomach gave a small practical warning the way it did before she climbed bad stairs. Marsh Crescent heat-fever cases turned into shelter laundry support. A family-unit scabies hold became municipal mattress reclamation. Pregnant detainees coded unfit for immediate ship transfer were routed into climate-center kitchen prep under custody waiver until the next review window. Every page had the same trick in it. The medical line declared temporary unsuitability for onward movement. The sanitation line declared contamination or exposure. The invoice converted the waiting body into local revenue.

The company earned on the southbound transport contract. Then it earned again when a body became too messy, too swollen, too visibly injured, too contagious-looking for clean transfer optics. It was not release or treatment. It was a side door paid by the city and hidden under cleanup language.

Marisol sat down hard in her old chair. Its vinyl had split years ago; the foam underneath scratched the back of her thighs through the coveralls. She looked at the columns again until they stopped trying to become some less ugly arrangement.

It was all there in the order she herself had helped make normal: a mouthful of blood on the blanket, a vomit event in summer heat, lice eggs in the seam of a family bunk, a wrist restraint rubbed raw to the wet meat because nobody loosened it fast enough. Once the car crossed a threshold of visible filth or infection, the person attached to that threshold moved into a different category. Medicine named them unstable. Sanitation named them nontransportable. Accounting named them payable.

She had spent two decades believing the dirtiest work on the line was concealment: scrub the floor, replace the foam, vent the compartment, write the variance clean. That had been bad enough. This was meaner. The line hid what happened to damaged people and built a second market out of the damage.

"Fuck."

The word came out soft and total.

Over the speaker in the corridor, Teresa's voice clicked on in both languages, clipped and polished even through cheap metal. All supplemental route logs were to be stacked for courier pickup by seventeen-thirty. No local copies were to remain outside approved archival channels. Staff were reminded that quarter-close review was an audit-sensitive period and unauthorized reproduction of chain-of-custody documents would be treated as evidence tampering.

Marisol stood before the announcement finished in English. Outrage could wait. Paper could not.

The NCR books solved part of the problem. She tore the pink retained copies for MW-CAL-77 and MW-MAR-12 from the binding with a practiced slow pull that left the stubs looking old rather than freshly robbed. From the trauma-room files she could not tear anything without exposing the theft. She went back to the clinic cage, found the copier no one used because its toner left soot on fingers, and laid the relevant pages one at a time on the glass.

The machine coughed like a smoker and spat out grey crooked ghosts, but the codes were legible: 3182-M, 4417-L, MED DEFERRAL, LABOR SAFE, MUNICIPAL RECOVERY BERTH.

It still wasn't enough. She copied two more, then three more, enough to show pattern rather than anomaly: a scabies wash detail, a heat-collapse kitchen assignment, and one case that made her stop again because the patient age line read sixteen and the invoice buried the fact under juvenile family support. She copied that one too and hated the paper in her hand for staying dry.

Paloma slipped back in with a box of suture packs and shut the cage door with her heel. "Courier is here," she said. "He smells like hair oil and grievance."

Marisol held the copies up.

Paloma's tired eyes took in the words and sharpened in a way Marisol had not seen since the worst nights in the trauma room, when the doors opened too fast and bodies came in stacked by urgency. "Oh," she said.

"It's worse than rumor."

"I can read." Paloma set the box down. Her right hand trembled once, then flattened against the metal shelf until it obeyed. "How many do you have?"

"Seven case pages, two sanitation copies, four invoices."

"That's not enough for court."

"It's enough for a leak."

Paloma looked toward the ceiling as if she could see Teresa's office through it. "If you send anything through a terminal, she'll know before the file finishes leaving the outbox."

"I know."

"If you take originals, she'll lock the whole wing down."

"I know that too."

Paloma picked up one of the photocopies and ran her thumb over the black streak the copier had left in the margin. "There's a parish archive by the commuter viaduct. Old baptisms, dockworker marriages, death books, all the paperwork people suddenly need once a treaty turns grandparents into border hardware. Gabriel talked about it once when he was twelve and bored in my waiting room. He said the church kept records better than the state because the church still believed paper had a soul."

A laugh rose in Marisol's throat and died there. "Paper House."

"Probably." Paloma handed the copy back. "If you go, go with paper. Don't use a drive or a photograph. Paper can rot, but it doesn't notify compliance on the way out."

Marisol gathered the pages into order and slid them between two ordinary sanitation variance sheets so anyone glancing down would see only pump failures and bleach inventory. "If they ask, you never saw this."

Paloma's mouth tightened. "If they ask, I'll tell them I was busy stopping a girl from coughing blood onto my floor. Which, for the record, is true often enough that nobody should doubt it."

She reached into the cabinet, pulled out an empty sleeve from a pack of adult incontinence briefs, and shoved the copied pages inside it. "Use this. Men in uniforms don't like digging through anything that suggests age or shit."

Marisol took it.

Paloma added, "When this comes back on us, and it will, don't insult me by apologizing like a priest. Just make sure it lands somewhere that can hurt them."

* * *

Santa Marta parish crouched under the commuter viaduct three blocks from Caldera Junction, its stone front permanently filmed with rail dust and candle soot. People came there for mass, for cheap soup on Tuesdays, and for records that had become more dangerous than relics. The side door to the archive opened off an alley that smelled of wet cardboard and frying oil from the stand across the street. Gabriel had written the directions on the back of a meal ticket in pencil so faint Marisol had nearly missed them: ask for the archive, not the office. Say Avelar if somebody else is listening.

She crossed from the employee side of the station with the packet inside the incontinence-brief sleeve, the sleeve inside her cleaning tote, the tote under a folded yellow caution vest. Every set of footsteps behind her sounded, for three strides, like security. Her left knee had stiffened in the evening damp and made the stairs a negotiation. Heat still rose off the pavement though the sky had gone the color of a bruise turned silver at the edges.

The archive room was cooler than the street by only the width of a lie. Two fans turned overhead, pushing paper smell, wax, and old mildew in patient circles. Metal shelves ran to the ceiling. Baptism ledgers sat beside freight-union death books, shipping parish registers, marriage packets, and acid-free boxes labeled in a hand more careful than any company clerk's. At the central table, a woman in a sleeveless black dress was dusting the edges of a ledger with a makeup brush. Her glasses were held together at one hinge with beige athletic tape. A battery radio on the windowsill muttered a baseball score to itself.

She kept dusting the ledger edge when Marisol came in. "Office is closed."

"I need the archive."

"That's where you're standing."

Marisol shut the door behind her. "I'm looking for an Avelar certificate."

The brush stopped moving. The woman raised her head then and looked Marisol over without hurry: coveralls, route badge, cleaning tote, the posture of someone who knew where the exits were. One of her hands wore a rubber finger cot on the thumb to keep oil off the pages. "For court," she said, "or for memory."

Marisol thought of Gabriel saying labels were never the point. "For survival."

The woman's mouth twitched once, not into a smile. She switched off the radio. "You shouldn't bring rail uniforms in here."

"I came from work."

"That's not a defense." She set the brush down and got up, moving with the careful economy of somebody who had spent years carrying brittle things. "Did Gabriel send you?"

Marisol nodded.

"He usually sends students and granddaughters." The woman came around the table. Up close she smelled faintly of clove cigarettes and book glue. "I'm Lidia."

Marisol kept her hands at her sides. "Marisol."

"Do you have originals?"

"No."

"Good. Originals get noticed fast." Lidia pointed to the table. "Put down whatever is making your shoulders do that."

Marisol set the tote down, pulled out the sleeve, and slid the papers free. The absurdity of the wrapper made Lidia glance at it once and then away with professional respect.

She spread the packet in silence: one photocopied case page, then another, then a pink sanitation copy, two invoices, one more photocopy, and the handwritten cross-reference sheet from Marisol's prescription pad.

Lidia's eyes sharpened on the codes almost immediately. "These are local."

"All from the same corridor."

"And partial."

"Because I had twenty minutes and a copier that wants to die."

Lidia picked up the page with LABOR SAFE on it and read to the bottom. Her taped glasses slid down her nose. She pushed them back with the finger-cot thumb. "This isn't enough for publication by itself."

"I know."

"It's enough for corroboration if Paper House has testimony."

"They do."

"Do they have dates?"

"Some of them do. They have names, bunks, voices." Marisol heard the strain enter her own voice and cut it off. "Listen to me clearly. They're purging local supports tonight. The summaries will stay, but the summaries hide the mechanism. I need this somewhere Teresa Ordonez can't recall with one call."

Lidia gathered the pages into three piles by document type. Her face had gone very still. "Sit."

"I don't have time to sit."

"Then stand quietly while I make your bad theft less stupid."

Marisol stood.

Lidia crossed to an old overhead scanner rigged beside the shelves. It was the size of a sewing machine and looked nearly as old. No rail-company terminal would have recognized it as worth monitoring. She snapped on the lamp, and the bulb threw a white square over the cradle. "Digital copies on isolated storage, a paper duplicate in the sacramental overflow, and a courier packet to the city node. We keep it in three places. One fire doesn't kill a thing if it's already bred."

The phrasing was not churchy. It was archival, which Marisol trusted more.

"Will Paper House get it tonight?"

"If the local runs on time for once in its miserable life." Lidia fed the first invoice under the glass, squared it with two nicotine-yellow nails, and brought the platen down. "Don't ask for more than that."

The scanner clicked and whined. On the small attached screen the invoice bloomed up in hard black letters, uglier and more convincing than it had looked in Marisol's hand.

Net municipal reimbursement.
Labor safe pending respiratory clearance.
Custodial supervision surcharge payable to RailPassage Logistics.

Lidia scanned the rest with the concentration of a surgeon or a forger. Marisol watched her hands. That was how stress worked in her now. It wasn't pulse or dizziness. It was hands, the useful ones in the room: her own, cracked and bleach-rough; Lidia's, steady over the platen; Paloma's, roughened by tremor and still precise enough to close flesh; Naila's, knotting silver wrapper into memory because paper could be taken but counting had to live somewhere.

"Why a church archive?" Marisol said before she meant to speak.

Lidia kept feeding pages under the glass. "Because when governments started sorting people by grandparents and ports and dead fathers, everybody came running to churches for proof. Baptisms, marriages, burial books. Old dock unions did the same. Paper House figured out fast that the safest place to hide testimony was among all the other documents power pretends are sacred." Another click, another page swallowed into duplicate. "Also because no compliance officer under fifty can tell one ledger room from another. They think mildew means irrelevance."

That tracked so perfectly with Teresa's kind of intelligence that Marisol almost respected the elegance of it.

When the last page had gone through, Lidia printed two thin thermal receipts with checksum strings Marisol couldn't parse and tore them off. One she tucked into an envelope with the paper originals. The other she held out.

"What is that?"

"Proof I took custody, if I die inconveniently before the packet moves."

Marisol took it. The strip curled warm in her palm like something alive and temporary.

Lidia slid the scanned pages into one acid-free folder marked MUSIC MINISTRY DONATIONS and the originals into a second marked DAMAGED REGISTERS - HOLD. Then she pulled a canvas diocesan mail pouch from under the table and put the donation folder inside with three other packets. "City node gets the pouch on the nineteen-ten local. Paper copy stays here tonight. If the train is watched, the archive still has a set. If the archive burns, the city has a set."

"And if both burn?"

Lidia finally looked straight at her. The tape on her glasses had started to peel with sweat. "Then your problem is no longer documentation."

From outside came the rattle and brake-whine of an arriving commuter train. Lidia cinched the pouch and called through the open inner door, "Mateo."

A boy of maybe sixteen appeared from the back stacks carrying a crate of hymnals. He had a smear of blue paint on one wrist and the solemn put-upon face of someone who had expected a quiet evening with damaged paper and had instead been given live trouble. Lidia handed him the pouch.

"Platform three. Direct to the city desk. Not the station office. If anyone asks, parish records."

Mateo rolled his eyes with the reflex of long practice. "They always ask."

"Then answer better than they do."

He slung the pouch over his shoulder and went out at a trot, hymnals forgotten on the floor. The side door banged once behind him.

Marisol listened to his steps hit the alley, then the station stairs, then vanish into the larger iron sound of Caldera Junction swallowing and releasing bodies as if that were all a station ever did.

Lidia was already restacking the shelves, putting the room back into its ordinary disguise. "You should leave separately from your fear," she said. "It makes noise."

Marisol stayed still for a second. In the station beyond the church wall, the nineteen-ten gave its departure chime, that clipped synthetic politeness the corridor used to make orders sound like service. She pictured the canvas pouch knocking against Mateo's leg as he boarded, the folder inside it rubbing against parish donation sheets and wedding banns and the paper proof of dead people once married under kinder bureaucracies. It wasn't enough for court, and it wasn't yet enough to tear the line open. But it was enough to live outside company custody, enough that Teresa could not sweep this version of the dirt down a digital chute and call the floor clean.

When Marisol finally reached for her tote, her hands smelled of copier soot, candle wax, and the ammoniac trace of the train that never fully left her skin. She thought of the invoices turning in some other room now, being read by somebody who had never stood in a blackwater spill with their pants wet to the calf, somebody who might still have the luxury of disgust unsalted by a paycheck. Fine. Let them have it.

The southbound train would still leave in the morning if nothing else stopped it. The detainees in the cars would still wake to heat and straps and announcements. Gabriel would still be inside. Naila would still be counting. None of that had changed.

What had changed was smaller and harder. The line's dirtiest secret had crossed a threshold of its own. It was no longer only inside Teresa's folders, or the trauma-room binders, or Marisol's head with the rest of what she had survived by not naming fast enough. It had gone out into paper that belonged to other hands.

By the time Marisol stepped back into the alley, the nineteen-ten was already moving south to north, away from the concession office, carrying the pouch toward the city with the evening crowd and their groceries and their sweat and the ordinary loose change of after-work life. She stood in the rail dust and listened until the sound thinned. Then she turned back toward the yard, where the white cars waited under their lights with rust coming through at the seams.
