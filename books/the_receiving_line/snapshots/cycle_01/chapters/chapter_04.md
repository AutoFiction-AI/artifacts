# Chapter 4

The records alcove at Caldera Junction sat above the sanitation bay, and everything in it sweated by association. The floor was dry, the cabinets were dry, the forms were dry, but the heat from the wash line climbed through the metal anyway and turned the room into a place where paper curled at the corners before noon. Marisol let herself in with her passcard, felt the old catch in her left knee on the threshold, and locked the door behind her. Through the wall she could hear the blackwater pump on Car 6 taking its load in slow surges, a wet-choked thump followed by the cleaner whine of the recovery line. It was southbound prep. She had two hours, maybe less, before dispatch wanted every signature in place and every body counted as movable.

She set Gabriel's case number on the blotter, not because she needed to remember it but because numbers behaved if you pinned them down. 4471-R. The terminal woke under her hand with a patch of dead pixels in one corner and the yellow RailPassage logo opening like bad milk. She logged into route review, then into treaty mirror, then into the secondary custody screen that still ran on old government architecture and always lagged when the corridor got busy. The train below her vibrated through the floor in a steady floorboard hum. Somebody dropped a wrench in the bay and swore. A synthetic announcement repeated boarding restrictions in English and Spanish, polite as a church volunteer and just as deaf.

She did not need hope. She needed a sentence. Somewhere in all the forms there had to be one sentence that let a living woman tell the machine it had the wrong country.

The file opened on the fourth try.

RAFAEL AVELAR.

CASE 4471-R.

RECEIVING STATE: SANTA AVELINA.

ROUTING BASIS: A-3 PARENTAL REGISTRY.

PRE-TRANSFER REVIEW: UNAVAILABLE WHILE ANCESTRAL CLAIM IS ACTIVE.

The yellow ancestry icon pulsed in the corner of the screen. Marisol hated that icon. It made a person look like weather, something already decided higher up than a body. She clicked into the supporting packet anyway. The system thought for a long moment, then gave her a stack of scans: a dockworker certificate from Santa Avelina, payroll fragments from a port authority she had only seen on manifests, a death record with Tom Avelar's name printed so cleanly it looked more official than he had ever been alive. The document edges showed salt rot and old fold lines. Someone in archives had flattened them, whitened the background, boosted the seals. The machine had polished the dead man for reuse.

She zoomed in until the letters blurred. Tom's surname was still the one Gabriel carried when he had been small enough to let other people decide who he belonged to. Marisol pressed two fingers against the bridge of her nose until she saw white.

She backed out and opened the challenge menu. Everything that mattered was gray. DESTINATION ERROR. LOCKED. IDENTITY CONTEST. ROUTE VIEW ONLY. FAMILY INTERVENTION. COMPLIANCE RESTRICTED. The only live box was ADD NOTE, and the warning below it made her stomach pull tight: ROUTE-LEVEL NOTES BECOME PART OF TRANSPORT CHAIN OF CUSTODY AND ARE AUDITABLE ACROSS MEMBER-STATE REVIEW.

If she wrote he is my son in that box, the sentence would never belong to her again.

She picked up the desk phone instead. The handset cord was so stiff with age it dragged the base an inch when she lifted it. She keyed through treaty review, listened to three levels of menu options that treated emergency, discrepancy, and appeal as if they were products she might prefer, and finally got a human voice.

"Pre-transfer desk."

The woman sounded as if she had a sunflower seed tucked in one cheek. Marisol could hear the shell click lightly against her teeth between words.

"Meridian West route supervision," Marisol said. "I need an immediate destination challenge opened on case 4471-R. The receiving state is incorrect."

Keys tapped. A swallow. "That case is ancestry-verified."

"Yes. It is still wrong."

"That is not the same issue."

Marisol flattened the file number sheet with the heel of her hand. "The paternal record is a dead man's dock certificate. The detainee has never lived in Santa Avelina, never transited there, never worked there, and I need the route hold authority for reassignment before departure."

The woman clicked her tongue, not unkindly. "Supervisor, route hold is not available on an active ancestral hook. If the parentage packet has been accepted by the receiving state, corridor transport cannot suspend on disagreement alone."

"Then what can suspend it?"

"A court stay. Contradictory parentage documentation. Verified fraud in the packet. A receiving-state withdrawal, which you are not going to get before dispatch."

Marisol looked at Tom Avelar's death record on the second monitor. "The father is dead."

"Death does not dissolve parentage."

"The son has never been there."

"Presence is not required for ancestral admissibility."

The phrase landed with the weight of something recited a hundred times a week. Marisol could picture the woman at the other end without seeing her: low partitions, bad air, a plastic cup sweating rings into a desk, a line of cases waiting to be converted into the right phrases.

"What does required mean here?" Marisol asked. "Required by whom? The car is still on my line. The receiving state does not have him yet."

"The receiving state accepted the basis at 04:18. After that, your corridor is transport custody. You are not adjudication. If there is an appeal, it attaches after handoff."

After handoff. After the southern port. After the ship or the bus or whatever uniformed stranger met the train and took the bodies farther out of reach. Marisol knew all the words. She had been working inside them for twenty years. Hearing them applied to Gabriel made them sound cheap and handmade.

"Open the fraud review anyway," she said. "I will send the contradictory note."

"What contradictory note?"

The truth crowded her mouth and would have ruined her if she let it out wrong. His father had not raised him. His father's country had never been home. His father's papers had spent more years in a drawer than the man had spent sober. None of that mattered to a field with locked drop-downs.

"There is a family discrepancy," she said.

Silence, and then the woman's voice sharpened by a degree. "If you are immediate family, you need to disclose conflict and recuse from case contact. I can transfer you to compliance."

Marisol kept her eyes on the screen. The departure column on the side panel had already turned from blue to green. Seal checks in ninety-two minutes. Water load in seventy-six. Southbound release after that if nothing failed.

"Transfer me," she said.

The hold music came on, a small bright tune with no business existing in a room like this. While it played, the system threw a new banner over Gabriel's file.

CASE ESCALATED TO REGIONAL COMPLIANCE REVIEW.

AUTHORIZATION: T. ORDONEZ.

Of course Teresa's name sat there before the call connected. Teresa had likely been watching the alerts already, maybe with one of her careful coffees going cold beside the keyboard, maybe with her expensive glasses somewhere she could not find them again. Marisol set the handset down before the transfer finished. She did not need Teresa through speaker static. She needed to see exactly how much door was left.

She pulled Tom Avelar's scans back up one more time. There was no hidden seam in them. The port seal had been verified. The death record had been linked. The receiving state had pressed Accept, and the machine had locked every route-level question behind that acceptance as cleanly as if it were closing a hatch. Gabriel's case had not slipped into the wrong country by clerical accident. Somebody had built a lane for cases like his and taught the system to call that lane lawful.

Marisol printed the packet cover sheet, then regretted it as soon as the page slid out warm and damp from the tray. Paper made things feel changeable. Paper could still be lost, stained, misfiled, crumpled in a fist. This page only gave the lock another body. She folded it once, put it in her pocket anyway, and went downstairs to Teresa.

* * *

Teresa Ordonez had taken over the old pension office beside dispatch, which still had a mural of smiling retirees fishing under a blue lake no one on the corridor had ever seen. The office glass was too clean for the building around it. Even the heat felt better behaved on Teresa's side of the door. A portable cooling unit hummed by the filing shelves, pushing cold air over stacks of compliance binders and sealed evidence envelopes. Teresa stood at the table in her immaculate gray uniform with her reading glasses nowhere visible, turning one folder over, then another, while her bitten cuticles flashed under clear polish when the light hit them.

"You are early," she said without looking up.

Marisol closed the door behind her. "You flagged the case."

"You touched it seven times in twenty-three minutes. I did not have to work very hard." Teresa finally found the glasses tucked into the front of her own jacket, put them on, and scanned Marisol over the rims. "Sit down."

Marisol stayed standing.

Years ago, when Marisol still spent most shifts with bleach on her sleeves and a mop handle digging a permanent callus into her palm, Teresa had been the one who noticed that her incident reports came in complete and on time. Teresa had signed the recommendation that moved her from sanitation attendant to lead, then from lead to route supervision. She liked workers who did not make her chase missing numbers. She liked people who knew how to turn chaos into paperwork that investors could survive. Marisol had mistaken that preference for faith exactly once and had not repeated the error.

"Is case 4471-R your son?" Teresa asked.

There was no point using the wrong noun now. "Yes."

Teresa set both hands flat on the table. Her nails were neat. One cuticle at the thumb had been worked raw enough to show a half-moon of pink underneath. "Then you have already made this worse than it needed to be."

"I asked for an exception."

"You asked for an exception after accessing a sealed file repeatedly without disclosing direct family conflict."

"I'm disclosing it now."

"You are pleading it now," Teresa said. "Those are not the same thing."

Marisol felt the paper in her pocket stick against the fabric with sweat. "The assignment is wrong."

"Wrong is not a route category."

"He has never set foot in Santa Avelina."

"That is irrelevant once the parental basis is accepted."

Teresa said it the way she might have said the train had already been fueled or the quarterly numbers had already gone upstairs. Not cruelly. Not with pleasure. With the calm of a person who had spent years surviving by naming the wall and then standing beside it.

Marisol put both hands on the back of the empty chair and leaned into the metal. "Then tell me what is relevant."

Teresa took off the glasses again, cleaned one lens with a square of cloth from her sleeve pocket, bought herself a breath. "At this stage, relevant means this. The receiving state has recognized his father's certificate. The Compact lets them claim provisional admissibility through parentage whether or not he has lived there. Once that recognition is entered, Meridian West is transport custody only. We move him. We log condition. We do not litigate belonging from a junction office over a cooling unit that leaks into the carpet."

Her voice never rose. That made it worse. Anger could at least be pushed back. Teresa's control made every sentence sound pre-approved by somebody with a better desk.

"There has to be a discretionary review," Marisol said. "Medical vulnerability, misidentification, humanitarian hold, something."

"Medical holds require instability a clinician will sign and defend. Misidentification requires evidence that he is not who the file says he is. Humanitarian holds require external jurisdiction or a scandal no one in this building can contain. What you have is a blood tie and a bad outcome."

Marisol heard herself before she meant to speak. "He's not a bad outcome."

Teresa's eyes shifted, just once, and some older knowledge passed through them. She remembered Gabriel at sixteen, maybe, hanging in the employee lot with a backpack and a mouth that already looked hard to persuade. She remembered Marisol bringing leftovers to double shifts and never talking about the boy unless someone else forced the subject. Whatever Teresa knew, she did not use it tenderly.

"Do not do this in nouns you can love," she said. "It will not help him."

Marisol let go of the chair. Her chemical burn scar pulled pale on the inside of her wrist. "What helps him, then?"

"A quiet file. A transport that does not attract review for the wrong reason. A post-transfer petition from counsel who is not his mother and cannot be accused of contaminating chain of custody."

"Post-transfer." Marisol laughed once, with no humor in it. "You mean after they dump him somewhere he has never been and call it due process."

"I mean after he is in a jurisdiction that can hear the petition attached to the category you are objecting to." Teresa folded the lens cloth into a precise square. "If you force this into an undisclosed-family incident here, security will isolate him, the file will be frozen for internal review rather than reopened, and your own access will end today. They will not free him because you are emotional. They will simply move him under a harder set of hands."

Marisol thought of Gabriel's bruised ribs, the mark at the top of his arm where intake restraint had slipped and tightened on skin instead of sleeve. Harder hands were easy to picture. Harder hands were the line's answer to most delays.

"You're asking me to do nothing."

"I am asking you not to confuse motion with leverage." Teresa put the glasses back on. "There are times when the cleanest thing a mother can do is refrain from putting herself into the record."

It was the word mother that made Marisol want to strike something. Teresa knew that. Teresa used family language the way she used concession language, as a pressure tool meant to guide behavior toward the least expensive outcome.

"He's my record," Marisol said.

"No. He is a detainee on a sealed route and your son on your own time." Teresa's voice flattened. "If you mix those categories inside the file, I cannot protect you."

Protect. Teresa always knew which words insulted less if she polished them. Marisol saw the shape of the threat under it anyway: her badge, her housing deduction, the health coverage that had paid for knee injections when the stairs got bad last winter, the years it had taken to climb out of mop water and into something with an office key. Teresa did not need to describe the whole fall. Both of them had watched enough people take it.

"You promoted me because I knew this line better than anyone," Marisol said. "You knew what I was when you signed those forms."

"Yes," Teresa said. "Which is why I am speaking to you here instead of letting internal security discover for itself why a route supervisor suddenly cannot stop touching one file."

That landed cleanly because it was partly true. Teresa was containing her. Teresa was also tightening the walls.

Marisol took the folded cover sheet from her pocket and put it on the table between them. Tom Avelar's name sat there in block print, useful after death in a way he had never been alive.

"He hated the sea," she said before she could stop herself. "Tom got sick on ferries. He had one certificate from one season unloading crates at a port he wanted to leave. Now a dead man gets to drag his son across a border he never even crossed sober."

Teresa looked down at the page but did not touch it. "The file is not a biography."

"No. It's worse."

For the first time something like impatience broke Teresa's surface. She took off the glasses again and set them on the folder too hard. "What do you want me to say, Marisol? That the Compact is just when it is efficiently administered? It is a transport treaty built from categories. Categories are always cruder than lives. I do not have the authority to repair that from Caldera Junction, and neither do you."

The room cooled the backs of Marisol's damp arms and left the front of her body burning. The old pension mural behind Teresa showed a painted woman laughing with a fish on the end of a line. Marisol wanted to tear the mural straight down the middle.

"Then give me the route authority to delay pending review."

"No."

"Because you can't?"

"Because I will not create a discretionary lane that every lawyer on this corridor can later argue should have existed for every other file." Teresa's hand moved to the corner of the table where her tablet sat face-down. "And because if I open that door for your son because he is yours, I am no longer even pretending to be able to defend what comes after."

That was the nearest Teresa had come to honesty, and it left a strange small space in the room, not sympathy exactly, but the outline of something a little rawer. She was not denying the ugliness. She was saying the ugliness had already been costed.

Marisol took a breath that tasted like machine-cooled dust. "So that is it."

"No," Teresa said. "This is it. I have placed the case under compliance watch until southbound release. Any further route access to 4471-R pings my terminal. That protects you as much as it limits you. You will finish your dispatch responsibilities. You will not add family language to chain-of-custody. You will not freelance counsel. If you need to speak to the detainee for operational reasons, you do it with a custody officer present and the contact remains brief."

"Operational reasons."

"Those are the only reasons that still exist on paper." Teresa picked up the glasses again, then frowned and searched the table for them before realizing they were already in her hand. She gave a short annoyed exhale at herself, put them back on, and continued as if Marisol had not seen. "After transfer, I can route a petition marker through receiving-state review. Quietly. That is what is available."

Marisol imagined the marker traveling south in some clean electronic lane beside the bodies, thin as thread and just as easy to snap.

"You think I should be grateful."

"I think gratitude is not the point." Teresa reached for the folder at last and slid Tom Avelar's cover sheet back across the table with one finger. "Do not contaminate the record with family feeling. If you want to help him, stay useful."

Marisol took the page, folded it smaller, and put it away. The conversation had done what Teresa wanted. It had turned rage into a space problem, too little room for it in the chest, too many consequences attached to where it might spill.

"You always did know how to say no like it was a favor," she said.

Teresa's mouth tightened, almost a smile and not one. "That is one of the reasons they pay me."

When Marisol opened the door, Teresa added, "And Marisol."

She turned back.

"Do not make me choose between reporting you properly and preserving what is left of your career."

The word preserving sat in the room like bleach with the cap off. Marisol left before she had to answer it.

* * *

The custody car had the afternoon smell already, wet cloth beginning to sour, old urine worked up from seams by heat, the sweet plastic note of restraint foam warming after the morning wipe-down. The vents were running, but not well enough to flatten the human air. Bodies made their own weather in there. Every bunk carried a different arrangement of it: fever, fear, stale milk, cheap soap, anxious sweat, someone trying not to vomit again.

Marisol signed in at the car gate under the pretext of checking water allotment before seal. The custody officer on the latch side was young enough that his jaw still looked unfinished. A tiny purple crescent moon sticker clung to the back of his watch as if a child had pressed it there and he had forgotten to scrape it off. He saw her look and turned his wrist inward.

"Two minutes, Supervisor," he said. "Captain wants the aisle clear for count."

"Then don't spend forty of my seconds talking."

He almost smiled. Almost. "Two minutes."

Gabriel was on the lower bunk by the window slit, knees angled up because the space had been built for compliance, not comfort. Intake had left yellowing bruises along his upper arm and the dark spill under one rib where someone had knelt him too fast against the bench edge. His fingers were stained with old ink deep in the cuticles, as if the body still remembered the recorder and the notebooks even without them. He looked thinner in the car than he had in the trauma room. The line did that to people fast. It pressed them down into what could be transported.

He watched her come without moving to meet her. "You look terrible."

"I'm working."

"That's what I said."

The guard took two steps away for the sake of pretending not to hear. The aisle still carried noise enough to blur them, a child fussing farther down, somebody coughing up something wet, the automated station note repeating hydration guidance nobody could follow with any dignity.

Marisol kept her voice low. "I found the policy hook."

"Congratulations."

"It's your father."

Gabriel's face barely changed. Only one corner of his mouth twitched, not toward a smile. "He's been dead a long time and he still can't mind his business."

"His Santa Avelina certificate is what locked the route."

"Not the route. The country."

"Both." She looked down the aisle, then back at him. "They call it ancestral admissibility. Once the receiving state accepts the parentage packet, nobody on the corridor can hear a challenge before handoff. I tried review. I tried compliance. There's no lawful way to stop the transfer from here."

He let that sit between them for a second, maybe to see whether she would decorate it with apology. When she did not, he nodded once.

"So they ship first," he said, "and later some office decides whether the ship was a lie."

"Yes."

"That sounds efficient."

There it was, the humor that only showed up when he was close to rage. It was one of the last things about him that still felt like childhood to her, not because he had been funny then but because he had always learned the shape of a trap faster than other people in the room.

"Did you tell Teresa?" he asked.

"Enough that she's watching the file."

"That was generous of you."

"Don't start."

"What, with accuracy?"

The old fight wanted to open under everything else, mother and son falling back into the grooves they knew best, his contempt, her command voice, each one using what hurt because it was quick and already in reach. Marisol forced herself to stay with the fact.

"I'm telling you because you needed the truth before the door shuts," she said. "I don't have a petition. I don't have a judge. I have nothing clean to offer you."

That got his attention more than any promise would have. He looked at her properly then, at the sweat in her hairline, the paper cut on her thumb from Teresa's cover sheet, the way she was holding her shoulders too still.

"Nothing clean," he repeated.

"No."

"Good."

She blinked. "Good?"

"If you had come back with some neat little plan for me alone, I was going to tell you to go to hell again. This is at least the right size of ugly."

The guard shifted by the gate. One minute left, maybe less.

Gabriel slid his hand under the thin mattress edge and brought out a folded scrap no bigger than two postage stamps laid together. It had once been the silver backing from a meal-wrapper label. He had written on the white side in lines so fine Marisol could barely see them until he pressed it into her palm.

"What is this?"

"The start of a count."

The paper was soft with handling and stiff at one corner where dried soap had been used as glue. Names ran down it in cramped block letters with bunk numbers and destination codes beside them.

M. CARDENAS, 6-B, S.A.

H. FUNES, 4-A, B.M.

QUISPE, 8-C, P.A.

There were more on the reverse, half initials, half full surnames, all reduced the way frightened people reduced themselves when paper was scarce and guards liked to search.

Marisol looked up sharply. "What am I looking at?"

"People tagged to the wrong countries," Gabriel said. "People who know it because they know where they are from, which is still allowed for now. Some are ancestry hooks. Some are old labor-sponsor hooks. One man got assigned off a transit record from when he was nine and got put on the wrong bus for six hours. The system doesn't care what kind of wrong it uses if the receiving state says yes."

"How do you know these are wrong?"

He gave her a flat look. "I listen."

Then, because he saw she needed more than that, his voice changed. It lengthened, picked up detail. "Cardenas gets seasick from the fish slurry they serve on intake days and starts gagging before the tray gets near her. She's from dry inland cane country and has never seen a port bigger than a loading canal. Funes asked me three nights ago whether Belmora snow burns your lungs because he's only ever known heat. Quispe in 8-C knots wrapper strings around her bunk post every time somebody gets moved so she can remember who has vanished from where. None of them speak about the places on their tags like people who belong there. They speak about them like labels torn off crates."

The names on the paper blurred for a second before Marisol's eyes adjusted again. She realized Gabriel had not been making himself singular in the car. He had been keeping witness even here, in the smell and the heat and the constant counting. The instinct had survived intake, confiscation, bruises, all of it.

"Why give this to me?" she asked.

"Because now you know your case isn't your case."

"I knew that before."

"No." His voice stayed low, but the edge came back. "You knew the machine could hurt strangers and then it came for your son. That's not the same knowledge."

The line stung because it was exact. Marisol could have argued chronology, could have listed every body she had seen hauled through the trauma room and every time she had eased a threshold where she could. None of it would have touched what he meant. The system had been ugly for years. This was the first time she had let herself see design instead of damage.

She folded the scrap once along an existing crease. "How many?"

"More than I could write small enough before they searched the blankets. There are names on other cars too. If you get time, I can get you more. If you don't, start with those."

"Start how?"

He gave a short breath that might have been a laugh in another life. "You're the one with the keys."

"Keys don't open treaties."

"No," he said. "But they open doors and closets and maintenance panels and all the small stupid places a line depends on. You know what they respect. It's not us. It's stoppage."

The word sat in her chest with immediate physical weight. Not because it was new. Because it was the first honest thing anyone had said to her all day.

Down the aisle a baby started crying with the exhausted mechanical rhythm of a child who had gone past the point of wanting comfort and only had sound left. The guard at the gate lifted two fingers. Time.

Marisol put the scrap inside the cuff of her sleeve, against the burn scar where sweat would hold it flat. "If I do this," she said, keeping her voice so low it was almost breath, "I can't do it only for you."

Gabriel met her eyes. For the first time since intake, he did not look away first.

"Then don't," he said.

The guard came back down the aisle. "Supervisor."

Marisol straightened. Her knee bit hard when she turned. The car gave one long shudder underfoot as the coupling test started on the next line over. Everyone in the bunk row felt it. A dozen bodies changed posture at once, listening through the floor. They knew trains the way trapped animals knew weather. They could tell from vibration what might move them and what might break.

At the gate, the young officer with the moon sticker held out the sign-out tablet. He smelled faintly of medicated baby powder under the regulation soap, as if he had dressed in a hurry after leaving some domestic task half done. Marisol signed her name so hard the stylus skipped.

"Everything fine?" he asked, routine more than curiosity.

"No," she said.

He looked at her, uncertain whether that required action. She was already walking away before he could decide.

The dispatch corridor seemed narrower than it had an hour earlier. Freighted carts clogged one side, water drums the other. A deep-clean rig waited near sanitation stores with its tamper seals still bright red, the bleach canisters strapped tight, the clipboard fat with forms that could stop a route cold if any one box on them turned from preventative to active. Beyond it, through the glass, the departure board counted down green minutes toward southbound release.

Marisol stood with the names damp against her wrist and listened to the building. Pumps. Vents. Wheel checks. The two-language politeness of the announcements. Teresa had been right about one thing. The file was not a biography. It would not open for grief, or blood, or the fact that Tom Avelar had become more useful dead than he had ever managed alive. Law had shut every door that used its own name.

But the train still belonged to pressure, to waste, to inventory, to all the ugly practical nouns the company feared because they cost money and left residue. Marisol knew those nouns better than any lawyer ever would. She knew what had to be scrubbed, sealed, vented, carried, and kept from overflowing. She knew how close the whole corridor lived to stoppage while pretending it lived on order.

The southbound clock kept moving.

So did she.
