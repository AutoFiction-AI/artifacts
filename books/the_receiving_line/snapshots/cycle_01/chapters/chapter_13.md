# Chapter 13

The lights failed hard at 21:17.

Not the soft brown shiver the corridor had been living with all month, not the blink that took one row of carriage lamps and gave them back with a complaint from the ballast. This cut took the fans, the badge readers, the bilingual courtesy in the announcement loop, and for one long second the train seemed to forget what shape it was supposed to hold. The floor vibration dropped out under Marisol's soles. Somewhere forward a child started crying before the emergency strips came up red and mean along the baseboards.

She was in the sanitation corridor between Car 7 and the trauma module with both hands inside an access box, feeling for the manual pump switch that never sat where the diagram said it sat. When the power died, the blackwater line gave a thick backward gulp. She heard it through the pipe before she smelled it. A second later the smell hit: hot sewage, enzyme foam, old metal cooked all day and suddenly opened.

The radio on her shoulder cracked with three voices at once. Manual count. Stay where you are. All movement suspended pending tier verification. Custody wanted every car sealed. Dispatch wanted a systems assessment. Somebody in Spanish was swearing about the south lavs backing up. The synthetic announcement tried to come back and managed only Please rem before cutting into static.

Marisol got the pump lever down, felt the line catch halfway, and understood two things together. First: the outage was regional, bigger than one train, because the signal relay should have jumped to reserve by now and had not. Second: if the cameras were blind and the readers were dead, every body on the line was about to become a handwritten problem again.

Gabriel was in high-security restraint because Teresa had wanted him where every movement took a signature after the pathogen failure. After the old man died in transfer, after Paloma was walked off under interview hold, after Gabriel told her the dead belonged partly to her ledger now, Marisol had stopped pretending there was a clean edge left anywhere. She had still kept moving. She had still signed her name where the line required one. But the fantasy that competence could outpace damage had gone bad in her hands.

Now the train had stalled in storm dark with the locks dumb and the count going manual.

Temporary possibility, she thought, and hated the phrase even while she used it. That was management language. That was how people got turned into windows that opened briefly and shut on fingers. But the thought stayed. If the count went bad enough, if custody started rebanding and shifting bodies by flashlight, Gabriel could vanish south before morning under some emergency consolidation Teresa would call necessary. Or he could come off the line in the one stretch of dark the system had not budgeted for.

Her hands moved before the argument finished.

She shut the panel, wiped her palm on her coverall thigh, and took the side locker key from the cord under her shirt. Teresa had downgraded her passcard weeks ago; Teresa had not thought to strip the old metal habits out of her. The decon locker still kept three sealed sanitation coveralls in plastic, two respirators, and a roll of yellow hazard tape. Marisol tore one package with her teeth. Size large. The line bought for bulk, not fit. Good enough. She shoved the suit, a respirator, and a pair of nitrile gloves into a red-labeled absorbent sack, then added a coil of gauze and a pump wrench because carrying a sack without tools looked like theater.

At the next junction a custody officer swung her flashlight into Marisol's face. The beam washed her features flat, but Marisol recognized the green crocheted lizard clipped to the woman's lanyard. Jessa from reserve detail. She kept fennel seeds in an aspirin tin and sucked on them through double shifts so she would not smoke.

"Hold there," Jessa said. "Manual verification in progress."

"Then verify faster," Marisol said. "South blackwater's backing into the restraint drain. If it crosses the sill you can count boots floating."

Jessa looked past her down the corridor as if smell might become visible. "My order is no cross-tier movement."

"Your order can explain to Teresa why Car 9 gets shit under the seats."

The name did it. Not because Jessa feared Teresa in any grand moral sense. Everybody feared Teresa in the practical one. Jessa swore once, low and sincere. "I need an escort with you."

"Find one. I'm not waiting for the tanks to make their own decision."

Marisol walked before permission settled. That was the old trick of authority: make the other person decide whether to stop a moving body in crisis and gamble on being wrong. Jessa followed, muttering into her radio. By the time they hit the service vestibule outside Car 9, another surge of stink had worked ahead of them and somebody farther down was already gagging.

The restraint tier door stood half-open on manual override. Two officers were in the aisle with flashlights and a wet paper manifest clamped under a plastic bread bag. The younger one kept smoothing his mustache between thumb and forefinger every time a name came back answered. He looked twenty and furious at the weather for existing. Behind him the detainees sat in the red emergency wash of the floor strips, wrists cinched, faces coming and going with each swing of light.

Gabriel was not hard to find. He sat against the bulkhead near the auxiliary sink because his upper-tier latch had jammed and they had moved him out to keep the aisle clear. One cuff held him to a seat bracket. His head was down, but Marisol saw the scar through his left eyebrow even before he lifted his face. Under the bad light the bruising along his ribs showed through his thin shirt like old fruit left in a bowl too long.

"Restraint bay first," Marisol said. "I need the panel."

The mustached officer frowned. "We're counting."

"And I'm telling you the line is backing up." She crouched at the service hatch without waiting, set the wrench into the wing nut, and felt the man's beam on the back of her neck. "If pressure's hit this branch you've got aerosol risk in a sealed car. Do you want that in writing?"

Nobody wanted anything in writing that sounded like aerosol risk during an outage.

The hatch came free with a sticky pull. Heat breathed out. Marisol reached inside, found the bypass valve by touch, and turned it past what procedure allowed. The pipe shuddered. A second later a black rope of foul water spat from the inspection seam and slapped the floor between her boots.

The younger officer jumped back with a sound too startled to be dignified. Jessa swore full voice. The smell thickened until it felt textured.

"Absorbent now," Marisol snapped. "And get me the utility fan if the reserve still has one that works."

"Power's out."

"Then bring the dead fan so I can prop the compartment with it. Move."

The older officer backed toward the corridor, dragging the younger one with him by the sleeve. Nobody wanted to stand over erupting waste any longer than duty forced. Jessa pulled her shirt up over her nose and leaned into the radio, trying to sound official through disgust.

Marisol did not look at Gabriel until the others were moving. Then she was already beside him, dropping the sack between his feet.

"Get this on," she said.

He stared at the coveralls, then at her. "What are you doing?"

"What it looks like. Left arm first."

"You said no private rescue."

"I said a lot of things before the train lost power. Move."

His mouth tightened. For one second she thought he would refuse on principle and damn them both. Then another wet cough came from inside the opened panel and he bent forward with a wince, worked the sleeve over his cuffed arm, and let her tug the rest around him. He had lost weight since intake. The coveralls hung and still caught on the ridge of his shoulder.

Her fingers shook once at the cuff key. Not from tenderness. From speed. The red-seal key set was not supposed to be in her pocket. She had stopped caring about supposed a while ago. The bracket cuff popped. She caught the chain before it struck metal.

"Respirator," she said.

"That won't fool anybody."

"It doesn't have to fool everybody. It has to buy thirty seconds at a time."

She shoved the mask over his face, pulled the hood up, and wound gauze around his right wrist where the detainee band glowed pale in the red light. She wrapped too fast the first time, cursed, unwound, wrapped again tighter.

"Don't hunch like a prisoner," she said. "Carry the sack and look pissed off, not scared."

His eyes stayed on her over the rim of the mask. "You still know the difference?"

She did not answer because Jessa was coming back, one hand over her nose, carrying a dead utility fan by its handle like a carcass.

Marisol shoved the sack into Gabriel's hands and turned the opened pipe so another foul splash hit the floor. "Contaminated worker," she called before Jessa could ask a question. "He took the spray. I need him through decon before he vomits in the corridor."

Jessa looked at Gabriel, then at the sewage climbing toward her boots. Fennel sweetened her breath under the stink. "I don't have him on the crew list."

"Because I pulled him from reserve scrub on Car 10 when your officers locked half my access points. Do you want names now or a dry floor later?"

It was not a good lie. It was not even a tidy lie. It landed because the outage had made everybody temporary and because Marisol's voice still carried the shape of command when she needed it.

Jessa swung the flashlight up. Gabriel kept his head down and shifted the sack on his shoulder the way any exhausted worker would shift something heavier than promised. The hood shadowed his face. The gauze covered the band. One of the gloves she had given him was already smeared black at the palm.

"Straight through," Jessa said. "No detours. I need a visual when he's sealed in."

"Then come watch if you've got time to spare."

Jessa did not.

They moved.

Marisol went first because people parted faster for somebody who looked as if she belonged to the problem. Gabriel came behind with the absorbent sack and the dead fan. The corridor was dim enough that faces existed as mouths and cheekbones, then vanished. At the junction to the storage spur, the clerk with the bread-bag manifest blocked them by instinct more than suspicion.

"Need a tick on that one," he said, lifting the wet ledger. Rain had got into his boots somehow; each step made a small insulted squeak. "Everybody crossing tier lines gets a mark."

Marisol kept walking. "Mark him contaminated and get out of my way."

"I need an identifier."

"You need dry socks. Nobody's getting that either."

He glanced down as if remembering his feet. It gave her the half second to shoulder past. Gabriel followed so close their sleeves brushed. Marisol felt the contact like a live wire. If the clerk turned, if he called, if anybody asked for the respirator to come off, they were finished in a space too narrow to run.

Nobody called.

The service corridor narrowed behind the trauma module into the strip the passengers never saw: pipe runs, folded spill screens, crates of enzyme tabs, a rack of replacement restraint foam still in clear packaging. The train groaned around them each time the storm wind hit the sidewall. Water found a seam somewhere above and began dripping in steady taps into an empty solvent bucket. Gabriel stumbled once when the car lurched under a braking check.

Marisol caught his elbow. The bruise heat under the coverall sleeve rose into her palm.

"I'm fine," he said, too quick.

"You're not. Keep moving."

At the rear service hatch she set down the dead fan and listened. No radios close. No boots on the metal ladder. Outside, rain hammered the shell so hard it flattened every smaller sound.

She cracked the hatch. Wind shoved it wide and threw water into her face. The train sat on a service siding just north of Caldera's freight throat, far enough from the main platform that the public lights were only a dull stain through the rain. Beyond the ballast the maintenance lane shone black. Every yard lamp was out. Lightning turned the rails silver for a blink and then left them gone again.

There should have been a lockout pin on the ladder cage. There was not. Some careless bastard had left it looped open.

For once, carelessness helped.

"Down backward," Marisol said. "Use both hands."

"You first."

"If I go first and my knee goes, I take you with me. Down."

He bared his teeth at her through the respirator like he wanted the fight more than the instruction. Then he slung the sack lower and started down, boots feeling for the rungs. Halfway he sucked air hard between his teeth. Rib. She heard it.

Marisol came after, left knee complaining at the second drop, burn scar on her wrist stinging where rain got under the glove. By the time her boots hit ballast the water had found every seam in her clothes. The storm had heat in it still, summer refusing dignity even while it broke. Wet rust. Creosote. Mud lifting from a hard-packed summer.

The service truck sat where she had prayed it might: angled beside a stack of bagged absorbent and a pallet of hose couplings, white paint filmed brown with yard dust, company logo half-peeled from the door. Spill response. Nobody inventoried those carefully in outage weather because no one liked admitting how often they were needed.

The magnetic key box was still under the chassis lip, gritted with dirt. Marisol's fingers slipped once before she got it open.

Gabriel leaned against the truck side, breathing through the respirator, one arm wrapped hard around his ribs. Rain pasted the hood to his skull. Under the white suit he looked less like a worker than a body waiting for bagging. The sight hit her so fast and so low she had to look away to keep functioning.

"In," she said.

"You planned this?"

"No."

"That's supposed to help?"

"It means if somebody asks later, they won't find a neat line to follow."

He gave a breath that might have been a laugh if either of them had more spare air. She shoved the key home. The truck started on the third try with a cough that sounded public enough to wake the yard. But thunder rolled over it, and then they were moving through the rain with the wipers dragging two narrow clean bands across a world mostly made of water.

Retiro Sur was not on any route map the company handed new supervisors. It was where decommissioned sleepers and dining cars went after the fittings worth stealing had been stolen and the fittings worth invoicing had been invoiced twice. Marisol knew it because twenty years on the line taught you where useful things got forgotten. Three times she had signed transfer sheets for stripped sanitation tanks headed there. Once she had gone with Ivo to look for an obsolete valve casting, and he had stood in the rain reading the embossed car names like they were headstones, amused and tender both, his mechanic's hand resting on dead steel. She had not thought of that night in months. Retiro Sur belonged to the file in her mind marked later.

Now it rose out of the storm as dark hulks behind a sagging fence, windows blind with dust and old glue, weeds up to the axles. The gate chain had been cut long ago and retied with wire. Marisol drove through without headlights the last stretch, following memory more than sight, until the truck nosed up against a sleeper whose side door hung a few inches off true.

They got out into rain that had turned colder.

The sleeper car still carried the faded gold line of an old luxury emblem under the rust blooms, a lie from a different era left to peel in public. Marisol jammed the pump wrench into the door track and heaved. Metal shrieked. The door gave enough for them to slip in sideways.

Inside smelled of wet dust, pigeon droppings, old fabric, and the mineral rot of standing time. Lightning showed narrow berths with mattresses long removed, brass hooks dulled green, a washbasin cracked like a dry lake. Water ticked through the roof into a dented service pan somebody had left under the leak years ago.

Marisol set the sack down. Gabriel pulled off the respirator and bent with both palms on his thighs, drawing breath carefully so his ribs would not seize. She got the door shut most of the way behind them. The rain on the roof thickened until the whole car sounded like it was being sanded back to bare metal.

Only then, in the dark shell of the dead sleeper with the storm holding the world a few feet away, did Marisol let herself understand what she had done.

She had taken a detainee off the line.

She had taken her son off the line.

She had no credential left big enough to cover it.

The thought should have landed as panic. Instead it landed as silence, deep and mechanical as a train engine finally cooling.

* * *

Marisol went back to the truck for the emergency torch and hung it from an old reading-lamp bracket above the berth. The amber casing turned the car interior nicotine-stained and patient. Gabriel sat on the edge of a lower berth frame while she cut open the absorbent sack and spread its clean side over the rusted slats so he would not split his skin on them. He let her do it. That was not trust. That was fatigue with bones in it.

She had a half bottle of water, gauze, and a packet of antiseptic wipes gone almost dry in her pocket. The first wipe turned pink on the scrape at his jaw. The second came away brown from corridor dirt. His shirt stuck when she lifted it from the bruise line. He sucked air once and then held still.

"Nothing's broken," she said after feeling along the ribs as carefully as she could with hands made for bleach buckets, not medicine.

"That's a very comforting sentence."

"If it was broken, you'd know."

"I do know. It hurts."

"Hurting isn't a diagnosis."

He looked at her then, real irritation finally cutting through exhaustion. "You took me off a custody train in a sewage suit during a blackout and now you're doing route-supervisor triage on my chest."

"I'm telling you what I can tell from here."

"Yeah." He leaned back against the berth frame. "You always were."

The line could have turned into another fight. It did not because the storm would not leave room for small lies. Rain hammered the roof, and every so often lightning washed through the clouded windows and showed the next dead car over, its corridor tilted, its curtains hanging in strips the color of old teeth. Retiro Sur held the shapes of travel after travel was over: luggage racks with nothing in them, berth numbers leading nowhere, hooks waiting for coats that would not come back. The whole siding looked paused in a sentence the company had decided not to finish.

Marisol wrung water from her sleeves. Her left knee had stiffened on the drive. When she straightened, it clicked loud enough for Gabriel to hear.

"How long can we stay here?" he asked.

"Till morning without anybody thinking to look. Maybe longer if the outage keeps half the yard blind."

"And after morning?"

She folded the used wipe in on itself. "We take the service road west. There are depots past the gypsum lots where nobody asks much if you've got cash."

"How much cash do you have?"

She did not answer fast enough.

Gabriel nodded once. "Exactly."

"Enough to get you out of Caldera."

"Me."

The word sat between them with all the room around it. Marisol busied her hands on the gauze roll, though there was nothing left to wrap.

"You needed off that train tonight," she said.

"I did."

"Good. Then stop pretending this wasn't necessary."

"Necessary and enough aren't the same thing."

She looked at him. His hair was plastered flat from the rain. Without the respirator he looked younger for a second, not because the bruises were gone but because exhaustion had rubbed the sharpness off his face. Then he moved and the age came back. Twenty-seven. Underfed. Ink ground into the sides of his fingers that intake soap had never fully taken out.

"Naila's still there," he said. "Half the car's still there. Paloma's out because of us. That old man is still dead. So tell me what this is supposed to be, because if it's just you finally grabbing the one piece you can carry, say that plain."

Marisol felt command voice rise in her out of habit, ready to sort, correct, reduce. She hated herself for how quickly it answered.

"If the count went manual and they reclassified under emergency transfer, you could've been moved before dawn. Teresa would've buried you in the outage paperwork and called it continuity of custody."

"I know."

"Then don't stand there and act like I pulled you for a scenic drive."

"I'm acting like you pulled me because the machine got close enough to your own house again that you couldn't pretend it wasn't ordinary."

"It wasn't ordinary."

"No. That's the problem. For you it never gets to be real until it's personal."

She opened her mouth with a list already forming. The dead man. Paloma. The logs she had leaked. Naila's wrists. All the things she had done since June to prove her moral enlargement to the son who did not owe her belief. The list died before it got out because she heard how it would sound: another ledger, another effort to balance one body against another and call the books harder than they were.

Gabriel watched her not with anger now but with a kind of practiced endurance. He had learned to hold silence the way other people held tools. Let the other person work against it. Let them hear themselves.

Marisol sat down across from him on the opposite berth frame because her knee was trembling and because standing over him felt too close to the line she had brought with her. The weak amber lamp turned the chemical burn scar on her wrist pale.

"You want plain," she said. "Fine."

He did not move.

"Years ago," she said, and stopped because that was already too abstract. She started again. "It was August. Heat worse than this summer, if you can believe it. Before Meridian West got the new cars. Before Teresa. I was still sanitation lead, not supervision. Car 5 restraint bay on the old northbound transfer. A man came in alive at Puerta Seco and dead by the next county line."

The rain on the roof filled the pause. Gabriel's face did not change. That frightened her more than if he had flinched.

"They had strapped him too long after intake," she went on. "Wrists, ankles, chest belt. He fought. One of the contract officers broke his nose on the seat arm. He vomited when they cinched him again because he couldn't get his head clear. By the time they opened the bay his face was stuck half in it. Vomit, blood, one tooth loose in the mess, and he'd shit himself when his heart quit. That's what was there."

She had not meant to go that fast. Once the memory opened, detail came with the smell still attached.

"County investigators were on the way because another detainee had got a call out before they took phones," she said. "Dispatch wanted the car secured. Secured meant clean enough that the first thing anyone saw was a body on a stretcher instead of what they'd done around it. My supervisor looked at me and did not give an order in words. She did not have to. If I made trouble I was done. If I refused I lost the job, the housing advance, the health coverage. Gabriel, you were still carrying an inhaler in every room. I had already spent half a month begging the pharmacy to split a refill because the co-pay hit before payday. So I stayed."

Her throat had gone dry. She swallowed and kept going because stopping here would turn the whole thing back into shape without consequence.

"I scrubbed the bay before the investigators came. Not mopped. Scrubbed. I took a brush to the seat bolts because blood had clotted in the threads. I pried shit out of the floor channel with a wooden tongue depressor because the mop head would only smear it. I changed the restraint foam on the right wrist because he'd bled through it and there was skin stuck under the buckle. I bagged his shirt and put the bag in ordinary contaminated linen so it would leave with the wash loads instead of the evidence stack. I sprayed citrus neutralizer over the bleach because the bleach smell alone would have told them the room had been hit fresh. Then I signed the waste log with my own name and wrote bodily-fluid containment after emesis event. That's what I did."

The lamp hummed faintly. Rain found another roof seam and began dripping into the cracked basin in slow mineral plinks.

Marisol stared at her own hands. Even now she could feel the tongue depressor flexing, the awful give when it caught something soft in the channel and then dislodged it. She had gone home after dawn and stood in the laundry alcove in her bra and work pants, rinsing pink water out of the cuffs until the water ran pale enough to lie.

"I told myself it was once," she said. "I told myself the man was already dead and I was cleaning aftermath, not helping kill him. I told myself every ugly thing that lets a person go make coffee after. But the truth is simpler. I made the room look bearable. I helped the line say nothing had happened that a solvent and a form could not fix."

When she finally looked up, Gabriel was still watching her with the same exhausted steadiness.

"Yeah," he said.

Not shock. Not triumph. Just that.

"Yeah?"

"I know."

For a second the words did not land as meaning. Then they did, and the whole car altered. Not because she was judged. Because she had misjudged the dark again.

"What do you mean, you know?"

He leaned his forearms on his thighs carefully, guarding his ribs. When he spoke now his voice shifted into the longer, precise weave she had heard when he talked about testimony instead of himself.

"I knew there was a night like that when I was sixteen because you came home at sunrise smelling like bleach laid over penny metal, and you stripped in the laundry alcove before you got to the bathroom. You had somebody else's blood dried in the seam above your shoe sole. I asked what happened, and you said a pipe burst in transit. Not even a good lie. A pipe burst doesn't leave you shaking so hard you miss the sink twice." He looked past her for a second, into the dark between berths. "I didn't know the file then. I knew the lie."

Marisol remembered the morning in a flash so physical it bent her forward. Gabriel at the kitchen threshold, all elbows, hair in his eyes, rescue inhaler still clipped to the belt loop of his school trousers because he never remembered where he had left the last one. She had told him not to stand there in bare feet. She had told him to go eat. She had not looked at him while she said it.

"Years later," Gabriel said, "Paper House got a packet from a sister looking for what really happened to her brother on the north transfer. She had rejection letters, autopsy summaries with half the pages missing, one bad photocopy of a county intake sheet, and a sanitation log somebody should've destroyed but didn't. Your signature was on the cleanup line. Not the death line. The cleanup line. That was almost worse."

He gave a short breath through his nose. Not laughter. Recognition sharpened against itself.

"So when you came into the trauma room this summer talking about fixing my paperwork quietly, I knew exactly what quiet costs with you. I knew you could care. I never doubted that. I knew you'd carry. You carried everything. But I stopped believing you'd tell the truth before the truth had a boot on your neck."

Marisol sat very still. The old sleeper car pressed close with its stripped berths and stale rain smell. Outside, thunder rolled farther off now, not gone but moving south.

"Why didn't you say it?" she asked.

"To do what?"

"To me."

"Would you have answered?"

She wanted to say yes. The want was almost comic. It had no evidence under it.

"No," she said.

"Right."

He leaned back, winced, adjusted. "And I didn't want a confession. Not the kind you make at the kitchen table so everybody gets to cry and call it honesty. I wanted to know whether you could ever get all the way to the fact without somebody dragging you there."

"And now?"

"Now we're sitting in a dead sleeper car because the power failed and you finally had a chance to break the pattern in a direction you could live with for an hour."

The line hurt because it was accurate in the small before it failed in the large. Marisol had broken pattern. She had also repeated it. She had taken one body she loved out of sight and called that action. If they stayed here, Retiro Sur would become another cleaned room, just bigger and full of rain.

She rubbed the heel of her hand over her mouth. Her skin tasted of rust, old antiseptic, and truck grime.

"You think I don't know what this looks like," she said. "I do. I know exactly. I know if we stay hidden, Teresa calls you absconded, dangerous, unstable, whatever phrase keeps her investors from imagining a mother stole a detainee through the shit corridor. I know the old man's death gets folded into outage complications and Paloma sits under suspension for nothing. I know Naila and the rest keep riding south while I tell myself I saved what I could carry." She looked at him. "I know."

He let the echo of his own earlier words sit a beat before answering. "Then say the rest."

"The rest is that I wanted you off that train even if it made me a hypocrite."

"That's true."

"The rest is that I wanted one thing in my hands that wasn't already lost."

"Also true."

She took a breath that snagged halfway and had to start it again. "And the rest is that if I choose you alone now, after everything you and Naila and all those names made visible to me, then I'm doing the same job I did in Car 5. Different tools. Same disappearance."

Gabriel's face changed at that, not softened but opened a fraction, enough to show the statement had landed where argument had been trying to get.

"There it is," he said quietly.

Marisol hated how much relief lived inside hearing that. Not forgiveness. Just the relief of finally touching the right edge of a thing.

Rain softened to a steadier fall. Somewhere outside, loose metal banged once against another car and then settled.

"So what now?" Gabriel asked.

She looked around the sleeper as if the dead fittings might advise her. Berth numbers in flaking enamel. A luggage net split at one corner. The cracked basin catching roof water one patient drop at a time. Retiro Sur had been built to hold people overnight in transit, bodies arranged for temporary comfort between departures. Now it held only delay. There was no life to build in it. Only a pause.

"I go back," she said.

He studied her as if checking the sentence for hidden clauses. "Back where."

"Caldera first. Then wherever Teresa's put the center of herself tonight. She'll be counting exposure, not bodies. If I'm absent too long she sends this into fugitive protocol and closes every door we need open."

"You think she'll let you walk in?"

"No. I think she'll let me hang around long enough to see what she can use me for."

That got the smallest twitch at one corner of his mouth. "That sounds more like her."

"And more like me for knowing it."

"Yeah."

He was tired enough that the word held no sting.

Marisol reached into the inner pocket of her wet overshirt and took out the folded scrap she had kept there through months of delays and recounts: names, bunk numbers, partial destination codes, the paper softened by sweat and rain and rereading. She set it between them on the berth frame.

"You remember more than what's here," she said.

"Most of it."

"Write what you can while I'm gone. Not the company labels. The things they'll strip out. Who limps. Who hums. Who's got children that only answer to one language when they're scared. All of it."

He looked at the scrap, then back at her. "You trust me to keep the record."

"I've trusted you with the record since before I understood that's what I was doing."

"That's almost nice."

"Don't ruin it."

For the first time that night, a real near-smile moved through him and vanished. He took the paper. His fingers were still stained at the cuticles, archive ink surviving custody soap, surviving the train.

"If you go back," he said, "don't go back to patch anything. Don't give her another cleaner version. If she offers you me for the others, if she offers you any private mercy, you know what that buys."

Marisol thought of the old restraint bay. The seat bolts. The citrus laid over bleach. The entire career built one acceptable surface at a time.

"I know," she said.

"No." He met her eyes. "You know in theory. I need you to know in practice."

She let that stand because argument here would only be a wish to feel less seen.

"In practice, then," she said after a moment, "I go back dirty."

He looked down at the sodden coveralls, at the smear still drying on one glove, at the rust flake caught in the crease of her knee.

"Good," he said. "Stay that way."

Marisol stood. Her knee protested. The lamp flickered once and held. She found an old service pencil in the berth cubby, the wood swollen but usable, and laid it beside him. He took it automatically, archivist reflex outrunning everything else.

At the door she stopped and looked back.

Gabriel had bent over the scrap already, not writing yet, just staring as if listening for the right order of names. Rain shone in the broken edge of the window behind him. He was still bruised, still underfed, still somebody she had dragged half by will and half by panic through a sewage corridor and into a cemetery for obsolete travel. None of that had changed. What had changed was smaller and harder. There was no longer any fantasy left in the room that she could save him by making him singular.

"There are protein bars in the truck glove box," she said. "And a thermal blanket behind the seat. I'll bring them in before I go."

"All right."

"If somebody comes before I get back, don't wait to learn whether they're kind."

"I don't."

That, too, was true.

Marisol opened the door far enough for the rain noise to rush in again. The yard outside lay under storm light and rust, car after stalled car waiting without hope of departure. She understood with a clarity so plain it felt almost stupid that truth now demanded not speech alone but exposure: the unsheltered version, the one that left the stain where others could trip over it.

At first light she would drive back toward the line she had helped keep running and make herself visible inside it again.

Not to get forgiven.

Not to get her son pardoned in private.

To stop helping the room look bearable.
