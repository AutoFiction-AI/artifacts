# Chapter 3

By the time the layover meal opened at Caldera Junction, the cafeteria smelled of fryer grease, wet denim, and the thin ammoniac sweetness that lifted off a crew after a full intake day. Marisol carried her tray with both hands because her left knee had gone wooden on the metal stairs and because the stew trembled in its compartment like something barely set. The windows facing the commuter platform were filmed white from old detergent. Beyond them, on the maintenance track, the deportation cars sat in their patient row with the white paint failing at the seams. On the other side of the glass a commuter train slid in clean and silver, doors opening for people who still got to decide where they were going.

She took the corner table by the dead vending machine because it gave her a view of both doors. That was habit, not paranoia. Or at least it was not only habit. Her hair was pinned so tight the skin at her temples ached. Bleach had dried the backs of her hands into a fine white nap, except over the old burn on her right wrist, where the skin never roughened evenly again. She set down the tray, sat carefully, and listened to the room: forks on plastic, boots dragging, a cook cursing in the steam line, the constant low vibration from the yard under all of it. The whole junction seemed built to remind a body that metal did not rest merely because people did.

Paloma arrived first, compact and square in her scrubs under an unbuttoned work shirt, silver braid darkened with sweat down her back. She carried her own tray in one hand and two little rinse cups in the other, each holding a basil cutting in water gone green at the stems. She set the cups in the strip of evening light on the table before she sat, more carefully than she set down the food.

"If I leave them in the dorm," she said, "those idiots will strip them for noodles."

Marisol looked at the cups. "You've got a whole clinic for plants now."

"I've got four survivors. That isn't a whole clinic. That's triage."

Ivo dropped into the third chair with the rolling gait Marisol had known for years, as if every floor had loose bearings somewhere under it and he was accounting for them. He had grease under the nails no brush ever reached, and the small burn scar high on his neck had gone pink in the heat. A dog-eared bird guide stuck halfway out of his coverall pocket beside a folded maintenance docket. He slid a heel out of one boot under the table and made a face like a man removing a nail from lumber.

"What's dying?" he said.

"Everything on your tray," Paloma said. "Also this basil if the dorm boys find it."

Ivo looked into the stew. "No, the basil's safe. Nothing grows in that."

Paloma snorted. Marisol almost did. The laugh stayed shallow in her chest, not because the line was bad but because Gabriel's face kept rising underneath every ordinary thing that tried to sit on top of it: the old scar through his eyebrow, the bruising under one eye already yellowing toward green, the clear property bag hanging from a hook in intake with the recorder inside it like an organ sealed for transport.

Paloma noticed what Marisol had not touched before Marisol did. "Eat while it's hot."

"It's not hot," Ivo said. "It's historically warm."

"Eat while it's historically warm, then."

Marisol broke the skin of the stew with her spoon. Steam rose carrying onion, powdered stock, and something that tried to be beef and never had the muscle for it. Paloma pinched off two basil leaves, tore them with careful fingers, and dropped them into the broth. The smell sharpened at once. It did not improve the food so much as make its lies more specific.

"You stole that from the trauma room?" Marisol asked.

"I propagated it from the trauma room," Paloma said. "Stole is for narcotics and payroll."

"Same building," Ivo said.

He took out the bird guide and used it to level the bad leg of the table. The cover showed a gull in winter plumage, its head turned with suspicious dignity. Paloma pointed at it with her spoon.

"You've got that thing with you again."

"Lina sent me a picture from the school yard." He dug his phone from his breast pocket, thumbed to a blurred image, and set it between the cups. "Wanted to know if it's a kestrel."

Paloma leaned over. "That is a pigeon."

"A talented pigeon," Ivo said.

"A sick pigeon," Paloma said. "Look at the feathers."

He glanced at Marisol. "Tell her."

Marisol looked down at the screen because looking at a bird was easier than looking at anything with paperwork attached to it. The photo showed a lump of gray-brown feathers on a chain-link fence, breast puffed against the heat. In the corner a child had caught half her own thumb.

"Pigeon," she said. "Kestrels don't sit like that."

"Thank you," Paloma said.

Ivo took back the phone with exaggerated hurt. "My own table. Two women against me."

"Your daughter asked a question," Paloma said. "Now you can answer it without lying."

"She likes the hawk answer better."

"Children like candy better than soup too," Paloma said. "That doesn't make soup a conspiracy."

"This soup is absolutely a conspiracy."

The room around them kept up its own tired commerce. A group from the refit bay argued about coolant leaks. Someone at the next table was filing a torn nail with the edge of a keycard. A ceiling fan chopped hot air into slightly differently shaped hot air. Over the cafeteria speakers a synthetic voice announced the commuter departure for Platform C in English and Spanish, clipped and pleasant as if it had never had to wipe vomit off restraint foam, as if none of the white cars on the maintenance line had ever carried a child.

Marisol ate because they were looking at her and because not eating read as worry in a room where worry was contagious. The spoon clicked once against her teeth. She kept seeing Gabriel in the trauma room, shirt lifted, ribs mapped under skin that had gone mean and lean from transport food. He had always been thin. Even as a boy he had looked built from long twine and grievance. It was the bruises that would not leave her alone. They had shape. She knew from one glance what buckle had made one mark and what grip had made the other. She knew too much about what left color on a body and how long each color took to clear.

Paloma had begun rubbing her right hand with the heel of the left, working the tremor down into the knuckles as if it were a muscle she could persuade. The movement was small, practiced, almost private.

"You're favoring the knee again," she said without looking up.

"Everybody's favoring something," Marisol said.

"That wasn't the question."

Ivo pulled his boot back on and stamped his heel once. "My right foot feels like somebody hid a bolt head in the sole."

"That's because your fascia is shot and you buy boots like a martyr," Paloma said.

"I buy boots like a man with court arrears."

She lifted her eyes. "How much left?"

"Too much to be dignified, not enough to be dramatic."

"That's not a number."

"Six-eight, last time they mailed me something threatening."

Paloma made a face that mixed pity with diagnosis. "And you spent part of your layover at the pension office anyway."

"Had to. They lost a quarter again."

"A quarter of what?"

"A quarter year. On paper I apparently took three months off in 2037 to enjoy myself."

Paloma smiled. "Did you?"

"If I'd done that I wouldn't still be here eating a gravy crime under fluorescent light."

Marisol said, "It was the axle seizure in spring. They booked the shutdown wrong."

Both of them looked at her. The answer had come too fast, too exact. She could hear it herself, the way stress turned her into a woman who corrected labels while the room burned.

Ivo tore bread. "See, that's why I sit with you. I've got a memory problem and you were born mad at a ledger."

Paloma studied Marisol's face a beat longer than she needed to. "What did you come in here carrying?"

The question was soft. It went nowhere soft inside Marisol. She set down the spoon, wiped her mouth with the paper napkin, and bought herself the two seconds it took to lay a smaller lie over the larger one.

"A manifest mismatch," she said. "One of intake's scans bounced twice and came through with the wrong property count. If I don't fix it before southbound, it'll breed another six forms."

Paloma kept watching her. "Property count doesn't put that look on you."

"Blackwater alarm did," Marisol said. "Two of them. Car Nine burped back through the rear stack and the new kid on hose detail panicked and kinked the line. I spent an hour ankle-deep in somebody else's breakfast. There. Better story?"

It was too detailed, which made it more believable to anyone who did not know her stress well. Paloma knew it. Paloma also knew when not to pry where a body had already braced.

"Fine," she said. "Then after this you need heat on the knee and six hours horizontal."

"You prescribing horizontal now?"

"For workers? Constantly. Nobody listens."

Ivo pointed with his spoon. "See, that's why medics go mean. You keep giving orders to people who refuse repair."

"Mechanics don't refuse repair?"

"Mechanics postpone repair until catastrophic failure. It's a more honorable trade."

That got a real laugh out of Paloma, brief and low. It bent her over the tray for a second and showed the tiredness in her neck. Marisol held on to the sound because it was ordinary and because ordinary was suddenly difficult work. Paloma smelling of basil and disinfectant, Ivo grumbling about pension math, the three of them under a bad fan with a tray between them: it belonged to a world where the worst thing in front of Marisol was a swollen knee and not the fact that her son was sitting restrained two tracks over under a country's name that was attached to him by a dead man and a treaty formula.

Ivo ate two more spoons of stew, gave up, and pushed the tray away. "Storm's still sitting north."

"You can tell by your magic pigeon?" Paloma asked.

"By the swifts over the gantry. They were skimming low all afternoon."

"Birds are not a barometer."

"Everything is a barometer if it wants to live."

Paloma picked up one of the basil cups and turned it so the leaves faced the window. "You say things like that and then act surprised when people think mechanics are superstitious."

"We're not superstitious. We just know failure announces itself before management hears it."

That one landed closer to Marisol than he intended. Or maybe he intended it exactly there. Ivo had a deadpan that let things pass as jokes until they sat down inside a person. Marisol looked at him. He was tearing the paper sleeve off his plastic fork into narrow strips, eyes on the platform outside where commuters crossed with shopping bags and office shoes. If he suspected more than she had told him, he had the decency not to ask in front of Paloma. Or maybe he had only seen that she was wound too tight and was giving her a line broad enough to walk away on.

Paloma finished half her bread and wrapped the rest in a napkin for later. She wasted nothing edible and almost nothing green. "I'm going to move these before the night porter turns the window vent on full and strips them."

"You love those plants more than most patients," Ivo said.

"The plants don't lie about their symptoms."

"Neither do I."

"You lie constantly. You call a broken foot a tight sock."

"Not broken. Advanced annoyance."

She stood, gathering the cups. Her right hand shook once when she lifted the tray and she shifted the weight to the left without comment. Marisol stood too on instinct, then regretted it when the knee caught and sent a hot grind up her thigh.

Paloma saw that and clicked her tongue. "Heat, Sol. Not stairs, not pride, not another shift of pretending your joints are made of policy."

"I've got one call to make."

"Then make it sitting down if you can."

"Look at you," Ivo said. "Medically bossed by a woman carrying salad in specimen cups."

"Basil," Paloma said. "And if either of you touches it, I'll prescribe something ugly."

She left with her cups and wrapped bread, moving with that compact quickness she had even when exhausted, shoulders already angling toward the trauma-room corridor as if bodies could summon her through walls. Ivo watched her go.

"She's going to end up running an illegal garden out of the medication fridge," he said.

"She practically does now."

"Good. Somebody ought to keep one living thing on this route that isn't under inventory."

The sentence put a fine wire through Marisol's chest. She kept her face still. Ivo looked back at her then, really looked, the way a mechanic listened with his hands before he took a panel off.

"You sure it's just paperwork?" he asked.

She folded the napkin once more, exact along the crease. "I'm sure it's paperwork enough."

He sat with that, not satisfied but not pushing. "All right. If they tell you to fill six forms, fill five and lose one. That's my advice."

"Your advice is why bearings fail in public."

"No. My advice is why they fail after my shift."

There was the ghost of a smile at one corner of his mouth. It made him look younger and more tired at the same time. She had known that expression in a bunk once. In the junction light it was only an old tool left in the wrong drawer. He picked up his tray.

"I've got to check on a coupler complaint before night crew invents religion around it," he said. "If you still look like that after your call, find me on the platform and I'll lie about the storm until you calm down."

"You should call Lina," Marisol said, because it was safer than answering what he had actually offered.

He grunted. "I will. I'll tell her the hawk is a union pigeon with bad posture."

"Tell her pigeons survive everything," Marisol said.

"That's not a comfort."

"No," she said. "It isn't."

When he had gone, the cafeteria grew louder for having fewer known faces in it. Marisol stacked her tray, left it at the return slot, and took the corridor toward the administrative wing where the crew phones hung. The hall was colder than the cafeteria, not because the air worked better but because fewer bodies had breathed into it. Fluorescent tubes buzzed above her. A bulletin board outside the pension office held notices about hydration, a summer injury poster with a cartoon spine, and a payroll reminder about housing-deduction schedules. Someone had drawn a halo over the cartoon spine in blue pen.

She used the wall phone instead of her handheld because the route devices logged too cleanly and because she did not want this call attached to her name any more than it had to be. The handset smelled faintly of citrus sanitizer and older hands. She keyed in her supervisor number, then the case-services extension, then sat through the recorded menu in both languages while a synthetic woman explained that quality and compliance monitoring might occur. By the third prompt Marisol had dug the edge of her thumbnail under the paper label on the phone cradle and lifted half of it away.

A live voice came on at last. It was female, tired, neutral, mid-shift somewhere far from the heat in the yard.

"Corridor case services. Employee number, please."

Marisol gave it.

"How can I help you, Supervisor Anaya?"

"I've got a live southbound file out of Puerta Seco. Case four-four-seven-one-R. The receiving-state designation appears mismatched to subject history. I need a correction before release."

Keyboard clicks. Marisol could hear each one as if the woman were tapping little teeth onto glass.

"Did intake validation clear the ancestry packet?" the voice asked.

"Yes. That's the problem."

"If the packet validated, the designation is not treated as a clerical error."

"It is an error."

"It falls under case-integrity review, Supervisor."

Marisol leaned her forehead briefly against the cool cinder-block wall. "Open one."

"This desk does not open review on a live route."

"Then who does?"

"Regional compliance can submit a case-integrity request. A court order can interrupt status. Receiving state refusal can interrupt at transfer."

Each sentence arrived smooth and properly spaced, no malice in it, which made it worse. A human voice had said the words, but the shape of them had already been decided somewhere else, in a template, in a training file, in a room full of people who never had to stand two feet from a restrained body and watch a bruise come in.

"I need somebody to look at the file now," Marisol said. "The train is at Caldera. We roll south tonight."

"I can log concern language and timestamp the contact."

"That won't hold dispatch."

"No, Supervisor."

"Then what good is it?"

There was the smallest pause. It was not defiance. It was not sympathy either. It was just a worker checking which answer belonged to the slot.

"It preserves sequence," the woman said.

Marisol looked at the bulletin board across from her: hydration poster, payroll notice, cartoon spine with its stupid halo. She kept hearing the phrase preserve sequence. The line knew how to preserve sequence. It preserved sequence on restraint logs, on waste manifests, on the chain of custody for a child's inhaler taken at intake and never returned in the same country. Sequence was what systems said when they meant nobody was coming.

"Put it in, then," she said.

"I need concern language."

Marisol shut her eyes. What language could hold it? My son is not from the place you are sending him. A dead man's paper is driving a live body south. I saw the marks your intake team left on him and I need them not to become foreign soil. Instead she heard herself say, clipped and procedural because that was the only register the phone respected:

"Subject history appears inconsistent with designated receiving state. Request supervisory review of ancestry validation and attached source documents prior to transfer."

More keys.

"Logged," the woman said. "Ticket number K-nine-two-seven-one-six."

"Does regional compliance see it tonight?"

"I can't guarantee review time."

"Can you flag urgency?"

"It's already classified as active transport."

That was as far as the desk would bend. Marisol thanked her because years on the route had trained politeness into the muscles that took abuse and kept moving. She hung up before the woman could offer any scripted closing. The dial tone returned, level and indifferent. In the silence after it, the junction gave her back its real noises: a brake squeal from the commuter line, a cart rattling over a floor seam, someone laughing too hard two doors down.

She stood there with the useless ticket number in her head and understood the narrowness of what had just died. It was not hope exactly. Hope had already taken hits. What died was the idea that this could be corrected quietly, by competence, through the sort of back-channel adjustment that kept operations from embarrassing themselves. If she took this upward now it would not be a fix. It would be a file, a named challenge, a record with her own access badge all over it. Teresa would see it. Maybe she had to. Maybe that was next. But something in Marisol had already started moving sideways, away from permission.

The property room sat thirty feet down the hall behind a wire-glass door with a keypad most supervisors knew by feel. She knew which digits stuck in humid weather and which one needed the nail, not the pad. Inside, the air was stale with plastic, laundry powder, old pockets. Metal shelving ran to the back wall. Clear bags hung from hooks or lay in bins under car numbers: belts, phones with batteries taped to them, medicine bottles, cheap jewelry gone gray at the clasp, one pair of dentures in a cloudy cup, a canvas shoe with cartoon stars on the toe. There was nothing dramatic there. It was just the little household matter that got shaved off a life when the state wanted the life portable.

Marisol let the door close softly behind her. She listened first. There were no steps in the corridor and no voices close, only the refrigeration unit kicking on in the adjoining records closet and the far murmur of the junction beyond the wall.

She found Car Eight's overflow bin where intake had shelved the unreturned electronics and paper items pending southbound manifest confirmation. The tags were in numerical order until they weren't. She moved them with the same hands she had used for twenty years to sort what could travel with a body and what had to be logged away from it. Her own breathing sounded too loud in the room.

Then she found 4471-R.

The bag was lighter than she had expected, a clear zip pouch with a barcode sticker curling at one corner. Inside sat the recorder she had seen hanging in intake, matte black, thumb-sized, with its batteries taped in a smaller inner sleeve and a strip of masking tape on the back carrying Gabriel's block print: NORTH / JUNE / WITNESS 3. The writing undid her more than the machine did. He had always labeled things as if disorder were a personal insult. As a boy he wrote his name on cassette cases, on school notebooks, once on the underside of a kitchen chair because he said everything in the apartment migrated when she was at work and somebody had to mark the survivors.

Marisol slid the bag onto the metal shelf and held still until the tremor left her fingertips. This was theft, not a paperwork adjustment, not a borrowed look, not a supervisor's discretion stretched past comfort. The route had a thousand words for seizure and none for return.

She peeled the tape strip back from the battery sleeve with painful care, the adhesive making a tiny dry sound in the room. She took the batteries first and put them in the coin pocket of her work pants. Then she opened the larger zip, removed the recorder, and held it in her palm. It was warmer than the room from trapped heat in the plastic, absurdly light for something that had already pushed her out of the last safe excuse. It was a field tool, a witness box small enough to disappear in a closed fist.

Footsteps crossed the corridor outside.

Marisol flattened herself against the shelving, recorder in hand, listening. The steps passed, slowed, moved on. Somebody coughed. A cart wheel clacked over the threshold strip and kept going. She waited until the silence settled again, then slid the empty battery sleeve back into the pouch, folded the top once, and rehung the bag with the barcode facing out. A lazy check would see plastic and label and move along. A careful check would find the loss. Let careful arrive later.

She tucked the recorder inside the waistband of her work pants, under her shirt, where the hard rectangle rested against the soft lower belly she still thought of as the part of her body Gabriel had once occupied without asking permission. The thought came and she nearly flinched from it. She shut the property-room light off, keyed the door behind her, and walked back into the corridor at the pace of a woman who had completed a boring errand.

On the platform side window, dusk had gone from yellow to a flat iron blue. A commuter train pulled out, taking its clean passengers home. Farther down the yard she could see Ivo near the edge of the maintenance lane, one shoulder against a post, phone to his ear at last. He was listening more than speaking, head bent, the posture Marisol had seen once before when Gabriel was ten and had split his chin on a sink and Ivo had stood outside the clinic calling his daughter to explain why he'd miss dinner. In the trauma-room corridor on the other side, Paloma was adjusting her basil cups on a sill under a light, turning the leaves toward whatever weak brightness the building gave.

The recorder pressed against Marisol with each step, small and undeniable. The hotline had given her a ticket number and nothing else. The machine at her waist had weight. It could be carried. It could be hidden. It had survived somebody else's hands. By the time she reached the dorm stairs, it had already warmed to her body.
