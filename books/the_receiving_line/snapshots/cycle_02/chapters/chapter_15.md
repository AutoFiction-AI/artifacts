# Chapter 15

Marisol slid a stack of cream cardstock into the service printer beside the coffee urn, and Car Three answered with orange peel, hot plastic, and the lie that linen could make any part of Meridian West gentle.

RailPassage had turned the observation car into a hotel version of itself. The wipe-clean benches were hidden under cream covers that slipped each time the train shivered on its suspension. A catering contractor had set out folded napkins with brass rings, bowls of glossy fruit, and narrow glass diffusers that hissed bergamot into the air hard enough to sting the back of Marisol's nose. Someone had borrowed potted rosemary from the cafeteria patio and lined it along the windows so the car looked herbaceous instead of custodial. The rosemary was already dropping needles onto the floor.

Teresa Ordonez stood in the aisle with a clipboard and no patience left in her face. Her uniform looked immaculate from the collar down, but the clear polish on her bitten cuticles had chipped white at the tips, and she had set her expensive reading glasses on top of a bowl of oranges as if the fruit worked as furniture.

"The minister's office has twelve minutes on board before the Q and A," she said. "The investor group has been promised a visible demonstration of family-tier accommodations. The press questions have been pre-cleared. I need no improvisation from anyone. I need sequence."

The printer had two live queues riding the same tired bilingual software, one for hospitality menus and one for transport nutrition slips. She had already moved the divider tab half an inch to the left. The machine would do the rest with bureaucratic obedience.

"Sequence is easier when the printer isn't sharing a brain with detention meals," she said.

Teresa did not look at her. "Today is not a day for commentary, Supervisor Anaya."

The amber clip on Marisol's partly restored passcard bumped her sternum when she turned. Amber meant escort access, limited doors, every movement logged. Teresa had given her enough credential to make the performance credible and not enough to trust. It was a careful cruelty. It also kept Marisol close to the systems she needed.

Before dawn, she and Ivo had walked Gabriel back aboard through the catering gate behind a linen cart tall enough to hide a man if he folded himself hard enough. The recorder and a coiled patch lead had ridden under the cart's false bottom with the tonic bottles; Gabriel had ridden in the dead wedge behind a narrow service cabinet strapped to the frame, knees to chest, breathing through starch and rosemary. At the platform door a wand check struck the cabinet side hard enough to ring, and the officer only waved them through because the press shuttle was backing traffic all the way to the freight lift.

Marisol had kept one hand on the cart rail and whispered through the towels, "Last chance. We can still get you off."

"No," Gabriel had whispered back, his voice flattened by the wood panel inches from his mouth. "If they want a clean family coach, they can clean it with me inside it." Then he had pushed the recorder into the deep pocket sewn inside her coveralls and gone so still that, when the dog handler paused at the rosemary pots, Marisol could hear only the leash chain tapping the guard's belt. Now the same cabinet stood by the display berth, and he was still folded into the dead space behind it, one cough away from wrecking the whole morning.

At the far end of the car, Ivo lay half under the commentary console with his boots sticking into the aisle. His grease-black thumb wore a blue adhesive bandage printed with tiny parrots, the kind sold cheap in kiosk packs for children. He had a flashlight in his mouth and one earbud dangling loose against his neck.

"You got that patch?" Marisol asked.

He rolled out far enough to spit the flashlight into his hand. "It'll ghost on the third channel when she asks for the comfort track. Not enough to fry it. Just enough to embarrass a vice president."

"How long?"

"Long enough for somebody important to say, What the hell was that."

He slid back under the console. Metal clicked. Wire insulation rasped against the housing. Marisol took Gabriel's recorder and the short patch lead from the same deep pocket in her coveralls, the recorder no bigger than a deck of cards now that the tape wrap had been changed twice, and palmed them low beside the panel. Ivo's hand came out without his face. She passed both in. He plugged the splitter by feel.

"You loaded only the scraps?" he asked.

"Only the scraps."

"Good. I don't need a revolution before lunch."

"Neither do I."

That was not true, but timing was a kind of plumbing. Open a valve too early and all you got was a wet floor and a disciplinary report.

Outside the window, Caldera Junction baked under a pale white heat. The maintenance sheds beyond the platform looked powdery with dust, and the public-side awnings shimmered over tracks that had not cooled from the night. Porters in borrowed waistcoats hurried with silver coffee urns. Security had moved the detainees two cars back and shut the connecting fire doors so the guests would only see the demonstration tier and the polished end of the route. Marisol could still hear the synthetic announcement voice beyond the seal, clipped and falsely kind: hydration service in ten minutes, remain at assigned berth, thank you for your cooperation.

Officer Ledezma came through with the wand scanner in one hand and a plastic cup of yogurt in the other. He always ate dairy like he was taking medicine. There was raspberry caught in the bristle of his mustache.

"Director," he said to Teresa, "press is here. The woman from City Ledger brought another photographer."

Teresa held out her hand for her glasses, found orange instead, then found the glasses by feel with a tightness around the mouth Marisol had learned to recognize as real irritation.

"No flash in the family car," Teresa said. "No unscheduled photography near the custody partition. And keep Anaya visible."

Ledezma glanced at Marisol, then at the amber passcard. "Always a pleasure."

Marisol did not answer. She was looking through the narrow wired-glass pane in the next door. Far down the adjacent car, where the real family bunks ran hot and close under fluorescent strips, Naila sat on the edge of a berth with her hands folded around a length of candy-wrapper string. She had tied three knots into it and then a fourth, her cropped head bent as if she were counting under her breath. When she lifted her face, the sun marks on her cheeks flashed gold and gone. She did not wave. She only touched two fingers to the metal frame once, a sound too small for anyone but Marisol to hear through the train's floor vibration.

It was not time yet.

The guests came aboard in a gust of perfume, wool, and platform heat. A deputy minister from the Mobility Secretariat led with the expression of a man arriving late to a funeral he had not wanted to attend. His name was Baeza. He had a narrow striped tie and the cautious step of somebody whose expensive shoes pinched. Behind him came two investors, a municipal liaison, a communications consultant with peppermint oil shining at her temples, and Sena Valdes from the Ledger weekend desk, carrying a leather notebook fat with old inserts and a tin of cardamom pastilles that clicked in her pocket each time she moved. Her photographer wore suede loafers already dusted white at the toes from the platform grit.

Teresa's smile arrived as if clipped into place from a case file.

"Welcome to Meridian West," she said. "We are grateful for the opportunity to show you what humane mobility looks like at operational scale."

The phrase landed in the bergamot haze and sat there.

Marisol took her station near the coffee service, hands folded at her waist the way Teresa had requested, as if she were another amenity. The train lurched once. Outside, the platform started sliding backward. The rosemary pots rattled against the windows.

Teresa began the script in the tone she used for audits and condolences. "This corridor integrates dignity, sanitation, continuity of care, and family-sensitive transport design under a unified compliance framework."

Sena Valdes uncapped her pen. "Family-sensitive" sounded like something she intended to use later.

Teresa moved them first to the display berth. RailPassage had made the bunk with a white waffle blanket no real passenger would ever keep white for more than twenty minutes. A picture book lay open on the pillow. Marisol recognized it from property hold. Somebody had wiped the crayon off the cover and put it back into circulation as decor.
Beside it, the tall service cabinet sat loaded with spare blankets and water glasses, ordinary enough to disappear into the display.

"Our family accommodations prioritize privacy, hydration access, and reduced sensory strain," Teresa said.

Deputy Minister Baeza bent to touch the rail. "These are standard restraint anchor points."

"Multi-use safety hardware," Teresa said at once.

Marisol heard Ivo make a sound under the commentary console that could have been a cough or a laugh.

The catering contractor's server, a young woman with a strawberry barrette hidden under her hairnet, started distributing menus. The card stock was thick, cream, embossed with the RailPassage seal. For six seconds the room held.

Then Baeza looked down and frowned.

"What is 'Tier B transport-soft protein, utensil restriction active'?"

On the opposite bench, one investor turned his menu over as if the answer might be on the back. It was. The back listed beverage service under internal abbreviations: HYD-250, CAFF-LTD, NO GLASS, NO LOOSE METAL. Sena Valdes smiled without any warmth and read aloud from the Spanish side.

"Property retained until final disembarkation. How festive."

The communications consultant moved first, snatching lightly at the nearest card. "That is an unfortunate print collision."

"It appears to be a prison menu," Baeza said.

Teresa took one menu, scanned it, and for a single beat the polish left her face. Then it returned cleaner than before.

"It is a backend template issue," she said. "Nutritional compliance language populated the service footer. You are still being offered citrus chicken, fennel salad, and almond cake."

"Under utensil restriction?" Valdes asked.

"Under ordinary hospitality."

All eyes turned, as they always did, to the worker with the badge closest to the machine.

Marisol said, "The printer pulls from whichever live queue got updated last. Somebody should've separated the hospitality feed from the transport feed before they promised embossing."

That made one investor look up from his card. Marisol recognized the shift in his face: this was about systems, shared infrastructure, the language that hid under cost savings and surfaced later under scandal.

Teresa's head turned a fraction. "We will correct it before the station segment."

"You might want to keep one," Sena said, slipping the menu into her notebook. "Purely for the scrapbook."

The train took a curve and the rosemary needles skittered across the sill. Beyond the tinted window, the maintenance yard gave way to low warehouses and the hard bright backs of parked commuter cars. The photographer raised his camera. Ledezma put a hand over the flash housing without needing to say anything.

"If you'll take your headsets," Teresa said.

Each seat held a thin black set of bilingual tour phones. Teresa had planned a carefully scored sequence: soft strings under an introductory voice-over, translated legal context, then a clipped testimony from a vetted former transport subject now working inland under company partnership. Marisol knew the order because Teresa had rehearsed it twice in front of her like a threat.

She also knew where Ivo had laid the ghost.

The first thirty seconds worked beautifully. Low tasteful strings slid in under a warm studio voice describing continuity of care. Through the headset on Marisol's own ear, the corridor sounded almost tender.

Then the comfort track stuttered.

A different voice came in, flat and synthetic, too loud in the right channel. "Car Eight, emesis kits restock. Child-size cuffs count short by one."

The strings came back, then vanished under a woman's recorded voice saying, "No, that is not my country, you wrote the wrong sea on the paper-"

Static clipped the sentence in half. Guitar returned for two bars. A male voice, older, breathless, said, "My shoes were in the blue bag, both of them, I had both-"

Teresa slapped her own headset off as if it had bitten her. Across from her, Deputy Minister Baeza had gone very still, one earpiece hanging loose against his tie. Sena Valdes did not remove hers until the synthetic voice said, "Biofoam replacement in family tier, visible blood event logged," and by then she was already writing.

"Director?" the communications consultant said, peppermint shining under her eyes.

Ivo rose from the console with studied slowness. "Cross-patch contamination," he said. "Old board. She asked for three channels live, board gave her three channels live."

"Can it be corrected?" Teresa asked.

"Depends how honest you want the answer."

"I want the useful answer."

"Then yes. Two minutes."

He knelt. Marisol stayed where she was, because moving too quickly looked guilty. Her skin prickled under the coveralls. From the hidden recorder in the panel came one last leak, almost too soft to catch: a young voice saying, "Write the name on your hand if they take the paper." Then the strings swelled back in and stayed there, bright and vacant.

Sena pulled off the headset and looked directly at Marisol. "Was that internal operations?"

Marisol said, "Some of it."

"And some of it?"

"Old audio sitting in the wrong cache."

"What kind of old audio."

Teresa answered before Sena could push again. "Irrelevant material that should not have been in a demonstration environment. You have my assurance this is not representative of current passenger care."

Marisol felt Baeza looking not at Teresa now but at the sealed door beyond the rosemary pots. The wrong voices had done their work. They had given the train a seam.

Teresa touched Marisol's elbow with two fingers, the polite pressure she used when she wanted control without spectacle.

"Walk with me," she said.

They stepped into the vestibule between cars where the air lost the bergamot and found the real train underneath: hot gasket rubber, old piss, wet metal. Teresa shut the sliding door behind them and dropped the smile at once.

"You were told exactly what today required."

"I printed nothing and wired nothing."

"Do not be insolent with me. Not now."

Her voice had gone thinner, harder. Through the wired glass, the journalist's pen moved. Through the door behind Marisol, the other car breathed with bodies kept out of frame.

Teresa said, "You are standing in borrowed grace. I brought you back under escort when any other director would have turned you over to security and called it prudence. I kept your housing file from acceleration. I am still the one person in this corridor who can alter the shape of what happens to your son."

There it was: not the bargain in full, just the hook sunk back into the same flesh.

Marisol looked at Teresa's hand. She was holding her glasses now and had nearly snapped one temple arm clean through.

"If you could alter the shape," Marisol said, "you would've done it quietly and billed it as mercy."

Teresa's jaw shifted. "You are very close to making yourself useless."

"Not today."

That was the problem, and both of them knew it. Teresa needed the woman who knew every flush line, restraint cabinet, bad hinge, and maintenance lie on the route. The train could not convincingly perform humanity without the people who had spent years cleaning up after its actual work.

Ledezma stuck his head into the vestibule. "Director, the minister wants to use the lavatory before the station approach."

For the first time all morning, Teresa looked afraid of something stupid.

"The VIP one?" she asked.

"I assume he's not asking for the staff compartment."

The fear was reasonable. The VIP lavatory had been refitted with brushed-steel fixtures, a rosemary hand soap, and a sign that called it the Hygiene Suite. The plumbing underneath was the same vacuum temper as every other toilet on the train, only hidden behind better paneling. Before boarding, Marisol had loosened the sensor collar by less than half a turn and fed a twist of catering twine into the seal where the suction would worry it soft. Nothing about it was catastrophic. It was just enough to make the bowl fail at a moment when apology had to wear a blazer.

"Go with him," Teresa told Marisol. "Stand there if you have to."

"That usually makes people more nervous."

"Then fix whatever is waiting to become my afternoon."

Deputy Minister Baeza came down the aisle with the careful gait of a man pretending not to be in physical discomfort. Up close, Marisol saw the sweat darkening the strap line at his back. He nodded to her, already distracted by the need.

"This way," she said.

He went in. The door shut. The train moved through a patch of rough track and shuddered once from coupler strain. Thirty seconds later the bowl gave a long swallowed grunt audible from the corridor, followed by silence, followed by another grunt with wetter conviction.

Baeza opened the door with the expression of a man who regretted trusting any institution at all.

"It does not flush," he said. "Or perhaps it does, but only morally."

The first ribbon of smell touched the air then: bergamot oil, chemical hand soap, and the sweet deep sewage note of blackwater dragged backward through a line that had lost its seal. It cut cleanly through the diffusers. From the observation car, conversation faltered.

Teresa was already moving toward them.

"Can it be handled discreetly?"

Marisol looked past her at the assembled passengers, the menus, the headsets, the photographer pretending not to angle for a picture, the journalist no longer pretending anything. Then she looked at the base of the lavatory wall where the first brown thread of backwash had touched the threshold.

"Not if discreet means invisible."

Ivo arrived with a tool roll and a face like he had expected this all morning. He crouched without ceremony, listening to the pipe with one knuckle against the panel.

"Vacuum's burping on a bad seal," he said. "Could be twine. Could be cheap ambition."

"Can you clear it?" Teresa asked.

"Sure. With time."

"You have ninety seconds."

"Then I can improve it."

Marisol got down on one knee. The cream bench cover brushed her shoulder. The fabric had already picked up a wet gray print from somebody's shoe. She popped the service panel and the real train breathed straight into her face: ammonia, shit, orange oil, the mineral damp of old scale. Inside, the clear hose quivered with brown froth. The catering twine had worked itself into a pale knot around the sensor throat, pulpy now, almost innocent-looking.

She put two fingers in without looking at anybody.

Behind her, somebody made a small disgusted noise. That was good. Let them have the exact object lesson. Humane mobility knelt in the aisle and reached barehanded into the throat of a dressed-up toilet.

"Gloves?" Baeza said weakly.

"Gloves slip in suction housings," Marisol said. "Then you get another problem."

Sena Valdes was at her shoulder before Ledezma could stop her, notebook open, eyes bright in a hard professional way.

"How often does this happen?"

"On this car?" Marisol twisted the twine free, felt the line shudder, and kept her voice level. "Rarely. On the route? Depends how much paper people promise a system that was built for fear and speed."

Teresa said, "Supervisor-"

Ivo cut across with his head still down. "She means load tolerance. Fancy fixtures don't change pipe diameter."

Sena wrote that too.

Brown water coughed once against Marisol's wrist and then drew backward with a sound like somebody swallowing a lie. She tightened the collar, not all the way. It was enough to hold through the next segment if the line stayed lucky, and not enough to forgive the train. When she slid her hand out, the chemical burn scar on her wrist was striped with thin brown threads. The rosemary soap from the sink dispenser had already leaked down the wall in a green smear.

"Flush it," Teresa said.

Marisol pressed the sensor.

The bowl cleared and held for two beats, then a third. Then a bubble rose from the trap and burst with a soft obscene pop.

Ivo looked up at Teresa. "That is what ninety seconds bought you."

One investor laughed before he could stop himself. It was a tired laugh, more surrender than amusement, but it cracked the room. The communications consultant shut her eyes as if prayer might be an operational tool.

Teresa said, very evenly, "Close the door. Place the car temporarily out of service. We continue."

"With one lavatory closed on a moving hospitality demo?" Baeza asked.

"With one lavatory undergoing maintenance," Teresa said. "Minister, if you have ever toured a public facility older than this one, I am sure you have survived worse."

That almost won the room back. It was a good line, clipped with just enough shared insult in it. Even Sena's mouth moved. Teresa saw it and pressed harder.

"You came to observe a complex transport environment, not a perfume ad. Complex environments contain maintenance events."

Marisol dried her hand on a towel that immediately dissolved into lint and damp. Through the panel under her palm, the flush line trembled again. The train was beginning its approach sequence. She could hear the floor vibration changing pitch under the wheel load, the small advance warning her body always caught before the announcement did. Somewhere below and ahead, the couplings were taking the curve toward the public platform lead.

Ledezma's radio crackled. "Station prep in eight."

Teresa turned on him. "No unscheduled stops before the demonstration platform. I want the exterior camera feed checked. I want the alternate lavatory sign removed from frame. And I want no one speaking to press without clearance."

Sena popped a cardamom pastille between her teeth. "Then you shouldn't keep inviting press onto trains."

"Ms. Valdes," Teresa said, "you requested operational candor."

"I did. You gave me a menu about property retention and a toilet with a conscience."

Even Baeza looked away to hide the smile.

Marisol felt something shift there, not in the room exactly but in the arrangement of force inside it. Teresa was still in charge. The officers still had cuffs. The doors still opened for some bodies and not for others. But the polished version of the corridor had started taking on water, and now everyone could smell the leak.

The observation door rattled open from the far side. For half a second, before Ledezma moved to block the sightline, Marisol saw into the next car. Rows of actual bunks. A mother lifting a paper cup to a child's mouth. Naila near the aisle, wrapper string looped around her fingers, looking straight through the gap with a steadiness that made the rosemary pots and linen covers behind Marisol look like stage props in a school play.

Then the door shut.

Teresa saw what Marisol had seen. The color left her lips and came back painted as authority.

"We do not cancel," she said quietly, more to herself than anyone. "A cancellation reads as system failure. The station delegation is already assembled. The livestream notice is out. We proceed, we shorten remarks, and we move to the platform sequence."

That was the sentence Marisol had been waiting for since dawn.

Ivo rose with the tool roll in one hand. "If we're proceeding, I've got to get under before the public track switch."

Teresa snapped to him at once. "Then go."

He looked at Marisol only once on his way past. He gave her no nod and no signal, only the brief flattening of his mouth he wore when fear had become calculation. The parrot bandage flashed blue against the tool roll and vanished through the lower access hatch.

Sena said, "What exactly is the platform sequence now?"

Teresa was smiling again. The smile cost her more this time.

"A brief live demonstration of transfer-interface dignity with municipal observers present."

"Transfer-interface dignity," Sena repeated.

"You may quote me."

Marisol could feel the recorder still warm inside the console panel where Ivo had hidden it. The scraps on it were not enough to break the corridor. They had only dirtied the white gloves. Soon, when the train reached the right piece of track and all the compromised people kept their nerve, the same system would carry more than scraps.

For now she did what Teresa needed done. She pulled the ruined towels, reset the service cart, lifted the fallen rosemary pot, and turned the closed lavatory sign inward so the camera would not catch it on the next pass. Sabotage was not always an explosion. Sometimes it was knowing exactly how much function to restore so power would walk farther out onto a rotten beam.

The overhead announcement chimed in its clipped synthetic politeness. Public station approach. Prepare for demonstration stop. Remain in assigned area.

In the observation car, the investors sat straighter. The photographer checked his batteries. Deputy Minister Baeza asked quietly for water and was given it in a paper cup stamped with the RailPassage seal above a line of tiny legal text about indemnity. Sena tucked the corrupted menu deeper into her notebook and looked, for the first time all morning, less entertained than hungry.

Marisol unclipped the supervisor passcard from her chest and stood by the sealed door, listening to the train carry its separate truths in the same frame: rosemary and blackwater, linen and restraint hardware, Teresa's polished vowels and the cough from the hidden car behind them. The station roofs were coming into view now, white in the noon glare, and the sewage note was beginning to outlast the citrus. Nobody on board could pretend not to notice.

That was enough. The run was wobbling, and because too many important people had already promised themselves a clean story, they were going to carry the wobble straight into public sight.
