# Chapter 1

By 5:12 the white cars were already sweating through their seams. Heat worked fast at Puerta Seco. It lifted the old urine out of floor bolts, turned restraint foam tacky, and swelled the blackwater tanks until every gauge lied a little high. Marisol Anaya stood on the ladder under Car 6 with her left knee complaining at the third rung and listened to the pump cough through the service line. After twenty years on the concession she trusted sounds before numbers. A clean pull gave a hard vacuum whine and then steadied. This one shuddered, spat back, and took a wet breath like something drowning.

"Shut it. Open the inspection cap first," she said.

The youngest yard hand on the line crew twisted the valve closed and looked at her for approval before touching the cap. He had taped his wedding ring to the inside of his glove because he was still not used to the tan line on his finger without it. Marisol did not know if the marriage had ended or if he was only keeping metal away from the machinery. She did not ask. She held the hatch light low and waited while he loosened the cap one quarter turn. Pressure sighed out, rank and warm. A clot of paper, shit, and pink vomit threads pressed against the mesh.

"There it is," Marisol said. "Don't jab it deeper. Hook it and bring it clean."

The yard hand nodded, swallowing through his mouth. He had made that mistake once, on a family car in August, and spent three hours elbow-deep in a backflow nobody was paid enough to touch. He used the bent steel hook this time and worked slow, pulling out the blockage in a slick rope that slapped the tray. The smell came up thick enough to taste. Marisol did not step back. She checked the level float herself, knuckle against the casing, then reset the sensor and watched the gauge settle from red to amber.

Amber would pass if the other two tanks held. Amber would pass if the trauma room was turned over and the dispatch-safe checklist stayed dry enough for a signature. Amber would pass because the train had a departure slot at 7:40 and RailPassage had already stacked too many bodies into the morning for anything gentler than passable.

She climbed down, wiped the underside of her wrist across her forehead, and caught the sting where old chemical damage crossed the skin inside her right arm. Her hands smelled like bleach through the gloves. They always did. The gloves only changed the temperature of it.

The yard around her was coming awake at full irritation. Forklifts beeped between stacked supply crates. Intake officers shouted lane counts over the synthetic station voice that kept repeating welcome language in two languages no one on the ground believed. Beyond the chain fence, buses were unloading the first surge from the overnight holds: men with wrists zip-linked for the walk, women carrying plastic property sacks, two children already crying hard enough to vomit. The corrugated shade over intake did not touch the air. It only striped it.

Marisol took the stairs into the center service car two at a time despite her knee and keyed the trauma room open with her passcard. Cold air should have met her there. Instead she got tired fluorescence, the sweet-metal smell of cauterized skin, and Paloma Rivera standing at the sink with one gloved hand braced flat while the other shook around a bottle of enzymatic cleaner.

Paloma had twisted her silvering braid up with a green tubing tie. A paper cup of basil sat beside the sharps bin under the vent, leaves blown half-sideways by the weak fan. She kept trying to grow things in rooms that were built to forget bodies. Most of them died. She kept bringing more.

"You're late to my funeral," Paloma said without looking up.

"I was inside a blackwater lie."

"Then you were in management."

Marisol crossed to the restraint chair. They had wiped the straps, but a dark crescent still sat in the seam where a shoulder blade had ground against vinyl. There were singed hairs in the floor drain. Someone on the night shift had used the cautery wand too close to skin again or too long. The room always held that smell after, not exactly meat and not exactly plastic, something in between that stuck to the back of the tongue until coffee turned bitter.

"Who was it?" Marisol asked.

"A man who kept sawing at his cuff until he cut himself to the tendon." Paloma capped the cleaner and flexed her right hand once, hiding the tremor by turning it into a reach for gauze. "They wanted him transportable. I made him transportable."

The line's real clinical term for mercy was transportable. He did not have to be healed, safe, or even stable. He only had to keep moving.

Marisol bent, pried the strap seam apart with a gloved thumb, and worked the cloth edge through until the blood smear came up. "You need a new chair pad."

"I need six more nurses and a room with a window."

"You can put both in the requisition."

"I did. I'm sure the requisition is being sung to sleep somewhere."

Paloma said it deadpan, which made it funnier than it deserved. She squared the gauze stack with the side of her wrist, turned the cleaner label to face front, and then hid the shaking hand by tucking it under her opposite elbow. Even the basil cup had been nudged back into the strip of air from the vent.

Marisol stripped the paper from the tray, checked the suction canister, and cleared the cautery tip from the instrument table. She had done this job herself long before she carried supervisor authority. She knew where blood hid after a rushed wipe, where foam held vomit, where the company inspectors ran fingers when they wanted to prove the line had failed. She signed the surface check on her slate only after she had leaned close enough to make sure the room smelled mainly of hospital citrus and not of burned person. It still did not pass for clean. It passed for certified.

The intake alarm went off before she pocketed the slate. Lane Three went first. Lane Two followed. A child began retching in the corridor with the hollow barking sound that meant there was nothing much left to bring up.

Paloma closed her eyes once. "They started early."

"Heat buses always do."

Marisol was already moving. She grabbed a sleeve of emesis bags, a pack of absorbent powder, and the red-seal restraint key set from the hook by the door. The key set rode against her hip when she walked, heavy and familiar. In theory it was for emergency releases only. In practice it was a reminder that every lock on the train could be opened by someone who drew a salary.

Lane Three was backed from scanner to fence by the time she got there. Intake had split into three lines under the canopy and all three were failing in their own ways. In Lane One an old man had pissed straight through the leg of his paper suit and was standing in the runoff because nobody had moved him yet. In Lane Two a little girl with a melted cartoon rabbit on her T-shirt had vomited orange electrolyte across her mother's property sack and the mother's shoes. The mother was trying to clean both with the hem of her own transport shirt while a custody officer told her to keep the line moving. In Lane Three the wristband reader kept chirping amber and spitting out duplicate binds.

"Stop sending them through wet," Marisol said to the scanner clerk. "You're smearing the chips."

"They came wet."

"Then dry the bands before you scan them."

The clerk shoved a towel dispenser toward the lane without taking his eyes off the screen. Sweat had already pasted his bangs to his forehead. Marisol stepped around him, wiped the scanner bed herself with a square of gauze, and started doing what she always did when volume got stupid: triage the process harder than the bodies.

"You." She pointed at the officer nearest the vomiting child. "Bag the clothes now or you'll be carrying the smell onto the car. If she throws again inside, Paloma will want the whole bench stripped. Do it once."

The officer opened his mouth to argue, saw her badge, and shut it.

"You two, separate the dry sacks from the wet ones. Anything wet rides outside the bedding net. If you pack damp clothes into heat, you get rot by Caldera."

Hands started moving. They usually did when Marisol spoke in that voice. She did not raise it. She only made everything sound like the step before a larger mess.

Another amber chirp sounded from the reader.

The man under the scanner was lean enough that the paper suit floated off him at the ribs. One sleeve had ridden up, showing finger-shaped bruises yellowing over newer purple along the upper arm. His hands were bound in front with soft restraints for intake, and despite the dust and the holding-cell soap his fingers were still stained blue-black at the nails as if ink had lived there longer than dirt. He tipped his face away from the scanner's light and the old white split through his left eyebrow flashed.

Marisol's body knew him before her mind allowed it. The scar was small. It had always been small. She had held a kitchen towel over that brow while he swore at fourteen and told her he did not need stitches if she would just stop pressing. She had salted pasta water that same night because there had been no money for urgent care after rent. She had forgotten the date of it and not the shape.

The scanner chirped amber again.

The man lifted his head against the custody officer's flattening palm. He did that too the way he had met everything he resented since childhood: neck gone rigid, mouth shut, refusal running through him so plain he would not offer one extra inch of himself to anyone asking badly.

Marisol looked full into Gabriel's face.

His cheekbones were sharper. He had lost weight from the jaw and neck first. There was a healing split at the corner of his mouth and the flat, mean look of somebody who had slept under strip lights and then been handled by men paid to hurry him. The scanner light changed nothing. Gabriel stood there with his father's surname nowhere visible and his own name nowhere in the room.

"Supervisor?" the scanner clerk said.

The wristband printer spat half a label and jammed.

Every intake failure demanded a decision. She could rescan him, pull him, flag a hold, make paperwork, or slow the lane. Marisol felt the entire line turn toward the point where she had stopped moving.

She picked the ruined band off the tray. "Manual bind later," she said. "Push him through on visual. Lane can't hold here."

The clerk frowned. "Procedure says-"

"Procedure says we don't stack a hundred people under broken shade for one bad chip. Mark him manual and move the line."

The custody officer shoved Gabriel forward. For one second Gabriel's eyes met hers. Recognition hit clean and ugly. Gratitude never crossed his face. Surprise never made it that far. A door slammed hard behind his eyes, one she had not known was still there.

Then he was through the gate, into the holding pen for Car 8, and the line surged to fill the space he left.

Marisol realized the ruined wristband was in her gloved hand. The band still held the chip pellet, warm from his skin. She closed her fist around it hard enough to feel the edges through latex.

The little girl in Lane Two vomited again, more liquid this time, down the front of her mother and onto the concrete. The mother made a sound Marisol did not let herself hear as anything but operational.

"Powder first," she said to the nearest attendant. "Then scrape. Don't mop puke into the drain."

She kept the lane moving. She got the child onto a cooler bench and the mother a clean shirt from stores. She rerouted two family groups away from the jammed scanner, called for extra water at the canopy, and sent one custody officer to fetch fresh foam pads before the wet ones went sweet in the heat. Her hands did exactly what they had been trained to do by repetition and necessity.

All the while the ruined band sat in her pocket like a live battery.

By 6:03 the queue had shortened from impossible to merely dangerous. The blackwater gauge on Car 6 held amber without climbing. The trauma room was cleared. Intake was recovering the shape of a system. Marisol signed three partials, denied a request for a cosmetic delay on Car 4, and stepped into the dispatch office with the excuse already built in her mouth.

The manual bind had to be corrected before seal.

That much, at least, was true.

* * *

The dispatch office had one fan that only stirred paper and one window painted over on the outside to keep detainees from reading screen glow at night. By dawn the room already smelled like dust warming under electronics. Marisol shut the door, stripped one glove off with her teeth, and laid the ruined wristband chip on the desk beside the keyboard.

The manifest system took her passcard and then asked for reason code. She selected `SCAN FAILURE / MANUAL BIND` and entered the band number. The file came back greyed out with a compliance lock across the receiving-state field.

Of course it did.

Ordinary intake cases could be corrected from route supervision if the printer swallowed a number or a chip lost signal. Receiving-state assignments were sealed higher up. There were too many treaty hooks, too much liability, and too many ways for the wrong person on the floor to notice what country had just been invented for someone.

Marisol keyed in the yard override she was not supposed to know and had learned because managers liked practical miracles while pretending miracles had no mechanics. The lock trembled, shifted, and opened one layer deeper. Her slate flashed with an incoming compliance ping before the screen fully loaded.

Teresa Ordonez appeared in the upper corner from some brighter office farther north, face already made for the day, fatigue tucked under foundation that never quite covered it. She was looking past the camera with irritation sharpened by speed, one hand sliding files aside until she found the pair of reading glasses she was forever misplacing. When she set them on, Marisol could see the bitten skin around her cuticles.

"Supervisor Anaya," Teresa said. "Why are you inside a receiving-state lock from intake?"

Marisol kept her eyes on the loading field below Teresa's image. "Manual bind failure on Lane Three. The band won't hold. I need the destination code before seal."

"You need the chip rewritten."

"The chip pellet is damaged."

"Then replace the band and move him. Caldera can reconcile."

Marisol clicked deeper while Teresa spoke, using the pause between Teresa's clauses as cover. "If the replacement throws in-car, it becomes a restraint inventory exception instead of a yard correction. I'm not sending a rolling exception over one bad bind."

Marisol heard herself keep going, adding restraint inventories and rolling exceptions, each clause fitted tight against the next until Teresa had no clean place to interrupt. Teresa stopped riffling files. She slid the reading glasses higher on her nose and listened the way management listened when procedure started protecting them from exposure. Competence bought Marisol thirty seconds.

"Thirty seconds," Teresa said. "Do not alter the file. Confirm the code, rewrite the band, and close it."

The full manifest opened.

The photograph was not the intake camera still warm from the lane. It was the arrest image from first processing, harsher light, more bruising. Gabriel looked straight into it with blood drying at one nostril and contempt already organized. The line below read:

`AVELAR, RAFAEL`

`CASE 4471-R`

`RECEIVING STATE: SANTA AVELINA`

`BASIS: ANCESTRAL ADMISSIBILITY`

`SUPPORTING PACKET: TOM AVELAR / DOCK LABOR CERTIFICATE / DECEASED`

For a second the room narrowed to the letters alone. Tom Avelar had been dead eleven years and was useful again. RailPassage had found one old dockworker certificate in the archive, found one son who had never set foot on that island, and made paperwork do the rest.

Teresa was still talking. "Is there a sanitation relevance here, Supervisor, or did intake send me a clerical vanity project before breakfast?"

Marisol clicked into the packet and saw the embedded certificate. Thomas Avelar, badge photo in his twenties, before the cough took hold in him, before the union blacklist, before he spent his last year on a couch with an oxygen bottle stuttering at his feet. Gabriel had his mouth there around the corners when he was trying not to show pain. Everything else came from her side.

"There's a relevance," Marisol said. "The printer jammed the country line."

"Then print it again."

Marisol closed the packet one layer and entered the bind rewrite field. Her thumb hovered over `CONFIRM`. If she rewrote the band cleanly and let the case disappear back into compliance lock, she would have nothing in her reach except a name she was not supposed to know. The train would seal, the file would travel, and by the time the next correction point opened Gabriel would be a moving problem owned by somebody else's schedule.

She hit `MIRROR TO YARD EXCEPTION CACHE`.

The system asked for authorization. She entered her passcard again and chose the lowest-priority sanitation code she could find, the one used when a manifest needed to follow a contaminated blanket or bloodied restraint into local chain-of-custody review. It was not meant for people. It accepted him anyway. A second access line flashed green in the corner of the screen before settling into the audit log.

There it was: one duplicate trace, one stupid, visible breadcrumb telling anybody who checked closely that Marisol Anaya had touched a case twice when once should have been enough.

"Did you hear me?" Teresa asked.

"I heard you."

"Then close the file."

Marisol rewrote the band from the chip fragment, printed the fresh strip, and let the thermal paper spit out `RAFAEL AVELAR` in clean black letters that made her want to tear the machine off the desk. She did not. She pressed the band flat against the table instead, smoothing the adhesive with two fingers until the letters became just another task requiring pressure.

"The bind is corrected," she said.

Teresa studied her through the screen a beat longer than comfort allowed. Teresa was good at that. She could make silence feel like documentation.

"Dispatch is tight this morning," Teresa said. "Do not bring me preventable holds."

"I don't."

One of Teresa's eyebrows moved. She logged the answer the way she logged every small risk, with a silent mark beside it. Then the call cut. The corner of the screen went dark.

Marisol sat with the fresh band in her hand and the mirrored file still cooling in local cache. She opened the case once more from there, quicker now, without permission, just to make sure the details had not changed because part of her still wanted the machine to admit an absurd error and hand back somebody else's son.

It did not.

Gabriel Anaya had become Rafael Avelar inside the system. Santa Avelina had accepted him by treaty hook. The dead father had been attached to the living son like cargo paperwork. If the train rolled clean and on time, the lie would cross state lines with her signature under the sanitation check.

She printed the replacement band, pocketed it, and stood.

Outside, the yard horn gave the six-thirty warning for load finalization. She had less than an hour before seal and less than that before custody officers stopped tolerating supervisor errands inside the cars.

She keyed the door open and went back into the heat.

* * *

Car 8 sat on the east side of the consist, nearest the loading cages and furthest from the yard office, which was one reason intake liked it for overflow. Another reason was that nobody with authority visited Car 8 unless they had to. It was where the line stuffed the mistakes that volume made: families split and recombined wrong at the fence, single adults moved beside minors because the manifest had outrun the bodies, people too sick for general restraint and too healthy for Paloma to requisition. The white paint around the door seam had bubbled from old heat and old bleach. Rust showed through like dried tea.

Marisol crossed the ballast with the replacement band in one pocket and the red-seal keys hitting her hip. The platform was busy enough to cover purpose. Officers were counting heads against slates. Two attendants hauled wet bedding into a mesh bin. Someone farther down the consist was hosing vomit off a stepwell and sending diluted orange back onto the gravel.

At the foot of Car 8, a young custody officer with a dinosaur bandage wrapped around his thumb put out an arm when he saw her coming. "Seals in eight."

"Then I'm early," Marisol said. "Manual bind correction on Lane Three."

"Do it fast."

He stepped aside. He could not have been more than twenty-three. There was sunscreen missed at both ears, bright red against his neck. He smelled of mint gum and stress sweat. Marisol had supervised men like him for years, boys paid to become hard before their faces settled.

Inside, the air sat on the skin like a damp cloth left in an engine room. The overhead fans turned and only redistributed breath. Families had been assigned to the near bunks, single men to the center benches, overflow women and older children toward the rear partitions where sight lines were worst. People had already begun the small irreversible work of transport: tucking property under thighs, rationing water by the sip, deciding who would get the window slit. A baby cried at the pitch of actual pain. Somebody nearer the back was coughing up something thick.

Marisol could read a car in one glance the way other people read weather. Too much moisture near the floor meant a leak or a child who had not said he needed a bucket. Too much silence in one section meant somebody hurt worse than the guards had logged. She took all of it in while moving straight for the middle bench where the manual-bind list had put `4471-R`.

Gabriel sat with his wrists looped to a waist chain and his shoulders held carefully away from the seatback. That meant rib pain, or bruises worse than she had seen at intake. The paper suit had been traded for a transport shirt that did not fit his chest and made him look even leaner. The fresh split at his mouth had opened again. Someone had wiped the blood and missed the corner.

He saw her before she stopped in front of him.

Recognition moved through his face so briefly that anyone else would have missed it. His eyes sharpened. His jaw set. Something older and more private crossed there, and then it was gone behind a stranger's regard so flat it felt practiced.

Marisol kept her own voice low. "I had to rebind the chip."

He did not look at the band in her hand. He looked at the badge clipped to her shirt, then at the keys on her belt, then back to her face. He took inventory first. He had always been his father's child in that.

"Your ribs?" she asked.

"You checking cargo condition?"

The line hit clean under her breastbone. Gabriel had always known where to put the blade.

The dinosaur-bandage officer leaned in from the aisle. "Problem?"

"No problem," Marisol said before Gabriel could answer. She bent as if only to inspect the old band, close enough to smell dried sweat and the bitter holding-cell soap still trapped in his collar. Under it there was another smell, metallic and stale, the smell of a night in restraints. She saw the deepening shadow over his left side where fingers or a baton had landed earlier.

"Listen to me," she said, barely moving her mouth. "When med rounds come through, tell them it hurts to draw breath. Don't be proud today."

His eyes flicked once to hers, and for a second she thought the old reflex might survive in him somewhere, the part that had once handed her an inhaler without argument because air was air and pride could wait.

Then he straightened against the chain and spoke loud enough for the officer and half the bench to hear.

"My name's Rafael Avelar," he said. "If you need the plastic to tell you that, read it slower."

Several heads turned.

Marisol felt the room tilt around a still point. He had not called her mother. He had not even called her liar. He had taken the false name and used it as a wall.

"Keep your voice down," she said.

"Why." His mouth moved at the cut and split it wider. "You don't know me."

The officer snorted as if detainee attitude belonged to weather and not to history. "Band on and move, Supervisor."

Marisol took Gabriel's wrist. His skin was hotter than it should have been. The bruising around the restraint line was fresh enough to feel slightly raised under her thumb. When she wrapped the new band over the old one, he did not pull away. He let her touch him exactly long enough to know the permission meant nothing.

She fastened the adhesive and kept hold of his arm one second longer than procedure required. "When they ask destination, don't sign anything extra."

That one sentence was all she had to offer: bad advice, thin advice, the kind a woman gave when she controlled seals and tanks and had no control at all over what mattered.

Gabriel looked at her hand on his wrist until she let go.

"You should know that already," he said.

He kept this one low, pitched for her alone.

Her hand closed again before she told it to stop. Bruised heat and the raised welt from the restraint bit into her palm; the waist chain gave one hard click. She let go so fast her knuckles struck the bench rail. The sting flashed up her arm and brought him back in one ugly shard: Gabriel in her apartment doorway with a duffel bag on one shoulder, twisting free while something burned on the stove behind her.

The seal timer chirped from the door panel.

"Out," the officer said. "We're closing."

Marisol stepped back. In the next bunk a mother was wetting a rag from her own drinking bottle to wipe sour milk off a toddler's chin. Across the aisle an old man kept one hand over a paper bag as if it were money. A teenage boy at the rear partition was mouthing the station announcements half a beat behind the speaker, either mocking it or teaching himself the words.

This was what Car 8 always held: overflow, misfit categories, bodies the system had sorted fast and badly and then declared organized because the doors could still shut.

Gabriel had turned his face away by the time she reached the threshold.

The officer pulled the door in. Gasket met frame. The latch dogs bit one by one with the same sequence Marisol had heard for years and never once mistaken for anything human. She waited for the seal light to go from red to green. It did.

On the platform outside, load-final horns were sounding up and down the consist. She checked them automatically: Car 3 green, Car 4 green, Car 6 still amber on blackwater but holding, Car 8 green, trauma room clear, dispatch window intact. The train was becoming a fact, and it was her fact, built this morning by signatures, wipes, thresholds, bags changed in time, straps dried enough to pass inspection, one manual bind corrected instead of challenged.

Her slate buzzed with the departure matrix: Southbound Meridian West, Puerta Seco release 7:40, status `ON SCHEDULE`.

She stood with the ballast crunching under her shoes and looked through the narrow reinforced slit in Car 8's wall. She could not see Gabriel from there, only moving shadows, the blur of a child's hand, one face turning and gone.

The duplicate case sat in local cache under a false sanitation code. The fresh band on Gabriel's wrist said Rafael Avelar. Tom Avelar's dead certificate was doing live work in the system. Teresa had warned against preventable holds. The blackwater alarm might still climb if the heat kept up. The trauma room might fill again before noon. The entire line was sliding toward departure on the rails Marisol knew better than any room she had ever called home.

The worst part was that none of the train's numbers cared.

Another horn sounded. The board dropped to final two-minute.

Marisol touched the passcard clipped to her breast pocket as if the plastic might tell her which part of the morning still belonged to her. The plastic came away slick under her thumb. She had spent half her life learning how to keep this corridor dispatchable, how to make blood into paperwork and waste into thresholds and panic into a schedule nobody above her had to smell.

Now her son was inside the cleaned car under another man's country, and the train was still on time.

Medical could still interrupt a clean departure. It could not do it for the lie in his file or for the dead man's certificate. It could do it for pain, for breath, for a body that refused to stay transportable. Marisol turned toward the service car and started walking. By the time she hit the stairs, heat complaints were already backing up into the trauma-room hall.
