# Chapter 17

Nate got to Eastbridge Plaza a little before seven, early enough that the police cruiser had its light bar off and the orange barrels were still spaced loose and crooked, more setup than closure. The chain-link sections he'd seen stacked in the site photos were up now, threaded with plastic mesh, all of it silver and fluorescent against the gray morning. The windows had been emptied. Paper darkened the insides. A loose strand of Christmas tinsel skated along the curb in the wind and snagged on one of the sawhorses.

No one from Kassos had told him to be there. There was nothing for him to sign, nothing for him to inspect. Demolition belonged to other men now, men in insulated coveralls and hard hats with coffee cooling in their trucks. His part had been two weeks earlier in Victor's conference room with a legal pad under his hand. The pen had moved. That was all. He had kept his job. The rest of it only needed machines.

He parked across the street because there was nowhere else he could stand. He could not stand with the crew or with the people who'd lost the place. The Honda's heater took too long to matter. He shut it off after a minute because the fan made the windshield smell like hot dust and old pennies, and he didn't want one more machine in his ears.

The stores looked smaller with the signs still up and the insides gone. Gail's Diner had its red script over the awning, but the windows below it were blanked out with butcher paper. The barber pole outside Al's shop had been unplugged and stood there dead and striped. Lazar's Hardware still had the hand-painted sign on the end unit, the L a little washed out, exactly the way it had looked the first day Nate drove here with the road atlas open on the passenger seat and the impossible fact of his father alive on the other side of the glass. Back then he had sat in the car for forty minutes and told himself there was time: five months, then four, then weeks, then paperwork, then a date circled on calendars that did not care what he wanted.

A flatbed rolled up first with a compact excavator chained down on the back, then a larger one behind it, the kind with a hydraulic thumb bolted to the arm so it could grab and peel instead of only smash. A water truck idled at the curb. One of the workers hopped down with a thermos in one hand and a clipboard in the other, his knit cap pulled so low it bent his ears. He looked about twenty and had a blue ink stain across the side of his wrist like he'd lost an argument with a pen that morning. He talked to the cop, laughed at something, then went back to unhooking chains.

Nate stayed in the car until the windows started fogging. Then he got out and stood with his hands in his coat pockets on the opposite sidewalk, his breath showing. Cold came off the pavement even harder than it came out of the sky. It had rained two days earlier and frozen at the edges of the lot. Mud in the potholes wore a skin of white. Every sound carried: diesel idle, chain drag, a metal ramp clanging down onto asphalt, someone coughing deep enough to sound structural.

He had not told Sarah where he was going. He had said he needed to stop by the office, which was true in the way most of his life was true now: not false enough to argue with, not true enough to survive contact. She was still in bed when he left, one foot out from under the blanket, hair over her face, winter break from school turning her alarm irrelevant for the week. He had stood in the doorway longer than he should have, looking at her and thinking there were whole categories of damage that did not announce themselves at the moment they were done. Some of them asked what time he'd be back. Some of them mumbled okay and rolled over.

The first of the former tenants arrived at seven-twenty, not in a cluster but singly, the way people showed up for wakes if they were not sure how much grief they were entitled to. Mrs. Park came from the side street in a navy wool coat with a strip of dry-cleaning tape still stuck near the hem, the white paper wrinkled but never peeled off. She carried her handbag in both hands like she was afraid somebody might make her check it. Al came next in a tan overcoat shiny at the cuffs, his barbershop keys still hanging on the shoelace he wore around his neck because he'd once lost them in a snowbank and never trusted pockets again. Cesar crossed from the bus stop with his Mets cap pulled low and a paper cup of the Dominican coffee he stocked for his wife, the dark roast smell reaching the street before he did.

They gathered by the barricade without touching it. Nobody said good morning. Gail arrived last, still moving fast, even in the cold, even with nowhere to get to. She had her hair pinned up crooked with two metal combs and had come in the same white diner shoes she wore behind the counter, the backs mashed flat from stepping into them without using her hands. She carried a stainless steel coffee carafe under one arm and the pink order pad from the diner shoved into her coat pocket, the cardboard corner bent soft from years of handling. For a second Nate thought she'd forgotten she did not need it anymore. Then he understood she had brought it because she did not know what else to bring to the death of a place where she'd spent half her waking life.

They stood together and watched men they didn't know set ramps and back machines into position. A city truck went past, slowed, and kept going. The cop moved one barrel six inches to the left with the care of a man performing his own small portion of the ritual.

"You'd think they'd wait till after New Year's," Cesar said.

Al shrugged into his collar. "Why."

"Because people got Christmas," Cesar said. "Kids. Stuff."

Gail unscrewed the carafe and poured coffee into the cap. Steam came up between her face and the dark storefronts. "You think that matters to those assholes."

"Not to them," Cesar said. "I mean normal people."

Mrs. Park said, "My son said maybe weather delay."

"Your son optimistic again?" Gail asked.

"He got that from his father."

Nobody laughed. Mrs. Park didn't look like she'd expected them to.

Nate knew all their voices now, though not intimately and not with the ease he knew Sarah's, or the involuntary precision with which he'd learned the shapes Renata and Tommy made in a room. He knew them well enough to hear Gail lose some of her speed when she was tired, well enough to hear Al answer most questions in the tone of a man who had already been arguing with them alone in his shop for ten minutes, well enough to know that Mrs. Park always mentioned her son when she was trying to make a situation more manageable by putting another person in it. He had eaten turkey clubs at Gail's counter and listened to these people complain about delivery schedules, about parking, about the nail salon's busted hot-water heater, about teenagers who bought one soda from Cesar and loitered in front of everybody else's windows. He had built his attachment the stupidest possible way, through lunch.

A pickup with the demolition company's name on the door pulled in beside the excavator. Nate recognized the last name, not the brother-in-law's first name, which Victor had mentioned once in that flat, explanatory tone he used when he wanted Nate to understand that corruption had already hardened into weather, but the family name: Dreher. It was stenciled in blue block letters on white metal: `DREHER CONTRACTING`. Under it, smaller: `SITE PREP / DEMO / REMOVAL`.

There it was, not hidden or whispered or slid under a table, just painted on a truck parked in front of his father's store.

Gail looked up and saw him across the street.

It took him half a second to realize she was looking at him and not through him. Her face shifted by half a degree, quick math behind the eyes as she fit him into the morning. She said something to Al, handed him the coffee carafe, and crossed at the gap between the barrels.

Nate's first thought was not what she might ask. It was what his face looked like to her right now. Dominic's nose going red in the cold. Dominic's mouth. Dominic's body standing on the wrong side of the street while men unpacked the machinery that would take the place apart. It had been one thing to shake Mort's hand wearing this face, to sit at Gail's counter and be nobody in particular. It was another to bring Dominic's body to the demolition as witness. That changed the face. Maybe only for him. Maybe not.

Gail stopped a few feet away. Up close he could see she'd put lipstick on out of habit and then worried it off her lower lip.

"You hear anything?" she asked.

He shook his head.

"Nothing at all."

"Nothing useful," he said. His voice came out flat from the cold and from how carefully he was keeping it level. "I'm sorry."

She looked past him to his car, then to the machinery, then back at him. "You with them today?"

"No."

"Office?"

"No. I just came."

Gail absorbed that. She had a face built for impatience, quick and readable, but right now it went still in a way he'd only seen once before, when a kid in the diner dropped a glass and cut his hand badly and she turned from waitress to triage nurse without showing anybody the jump in her pulse.

"Okay," she said.

He waited for her to ask the next question, the one that would make him either lie outright or tell a truth useless enough to count as lying anyway. She did not ask it. She tucked both hands into her coat pockets, glanced at the strip mall again, and said, "Mort and Linda are on their way."

His hand closed inside his coat pocket until the nails bit his palm.

"Yeah," he said.

She nodded once. "All right."

Then she went back across the street, took the coffee carafe from Al, and poured for everybody else before she poured for herself.

By eight, there were maybe a dozen people if you counted the ones who'd come only to stand at the edge for a minute and then drifted farther down the sidewalk. A woman from the nail salon Nate only knew as Tran had shown up with a cigarette she never lit and a puffy silver coat that flashed every time she turned. Two guys Nate had seen in Gail's diner but never spoken to stood with their hands in their pockets and kept talking about the Giants game because silence would have been worse. They kept handing each other obvious lines just to keep air moving.

"They start at eight?" one of them asked.

"Looks like."

"Thought it'd be nine."

"Why would it be nine."

"I don't know. Feels like a nine thing."

The cop checked his watch. One of the workers climbed into the excavator cab. The engine turned over thickly, coughed, caught. Its first full growl rolled through the cold air and into Nate's chest with a pressure he felt in Dominic's sternum, not his own. Dominic's body knew construction noise. It met the sound without the flinch Nate expected from himself, and that unnerved him more than the sound did.

Linda's car came five minutes later.

Nate recognized it before he saw them because Mort still drove every route like part of it might turn into a delivery run, slow into turns, careful over potholes, braking early. The car eased against the curb on the far side of the crowd. Linda got out first and came around to the passenger side before Mort had even opened his door. He walked fine. She kept hold of his arm anyway, anchoring both of them.

He had worn the good coat.

It was dark wool, brushed clean and pulled out for church and funerals and weddings and apparently for this, though this was none of those and all of them. The collar sat awkwardly against his neck. Mort spent most of his life in canvas and denim and only put on wool when the day had already gone bad. Nate could see the reading glasses on the chain tucked under the lapel, the outline of them pressing against the fabric. There was probably still a carpenter's pencil in one pocket. Mort forgot things in coats for months at a time. Linda once found a spark plug in the freezer and nobody could explain how it had gotten there.

Linda kept one hand through Mort's elbow as they crossed the sidewalk toward the others. She was as small and brisk as Nate remembered, her short hair flattened under a knitted hat, her store keys looped around the blue plastic coil bracelet she wore when she had too much to carry and no free pocket. The keys hung against her glove with every step. She was crying already, not hard, not dramatically, just steadily enough that she kept taking sharp breaths through her nose in the cold and wiping under her eyes with the back of her wrist like she was annoyed at her own face for doing this in public.

The crowd made space for them. Gail put a hand on Linda's shoulder. Al touched Mort's upper arm and then took his hand away right after, because there was not really anything to do with a man's arm under those circumstances.

From across the street Nate could not hear the first few words. He could only catch mouths and movement. Linda's mouth pinched and moved quickly. Gail said something short back. Mort nodded once to whoever was speaking and took his place at the barricade.

He stood with his hands in his coat pockets and his jaw set so hard it changed the lower half of his face.

That was the posture. Nate knew it before he could name it. It was the stance Mort used when the furnace went out on the coldest day of the year and there was no money to replace it, when a supplier had shorted an order and then insisted they hadn't, when fourteen-year-old Nate knocked over an entire shelving unit in the garage trying to impress nobody. Mort got quieter, put his hands away, and kept the rest of himself locked down so the practical part could finish the job. If his hands were free, he would use them. He would sort, measure, pick something up and fix it. Here there was nothing to pick up yet and nothing to fix, so he kept them buried.

Nate stepped farther back into the shadow of the bare sycamore at the curb, though he knew it would not help. Mort was not looking at him. Mort's eyes moved over the storefronts, catching on doors, window frames, the seams between units, taking inventory of what was still standing.

The foreman, a thick-necked guy in an orange jacket with reflective strips gone grimy at the edges, walked the line with the cop and pointed at the fence, the hydrant, the overhead wires. He talked with one gloved finger, not dramatic, just competent. Men nodded. The water truck pulled closer. A hose came off the side and uncoiled over the sidewalk, laying black and rubbery through the slush.

Nobody in the crowd raised a voice. Nate had expected, if not a fight, then at least some sharp phrase thrown toward the machinery, something human and inadequate hitting metal. Instead everybody watched the way people watched surgeons through glass when there was nothing to be done but see whether the body opened on the table was one you loved.

A worker cut the power line to the barber pole and dropped it into the cab of a pickup. Another peeled away caution tape from the side door of the diner and wound it around his arm because it would tangle in the tracks otherwise. They were not cruel. That made it worse. Cruelty would at least have been a choice. This was efficiency. The men were doing a day's work in good faith, and the day's work was to erase the physical record of other people's days.

The excavator went into the nail salon first.

Its bucket punched through glass with a crack too sharp for how heavy the machine was. Then the hydraulic thumb closed, gripping aluminum frame and mullion together, and the whole front of the unit came away in a shriek of metal. The water truck sprayed a fan of mist that turned immediately to cold grit on the asphalt. Pink insulation showed, then a waiting-room chair tipped sideways and disappeared under falling block. Someone near Gail said, "Jesus," softly, with the reflexive hush people brought into church, and the machine kept moving.

Nate had seen demolition before in clips online, sped up, narrated by men who loved heavy equipment and talked about reach and torque and cycle times. This had none of that stupid distance. Every bite took a room with it: a counter, a sink, a mirror bolted to a wall. Fluorescent tubes burst in brief white flashes under the falling ceiling. The sound was not one sound but a sequence: impact, metal complaint, the patter of smaller debris, the hiss of water, the diesel rev as the arm lifted and reset.

Tran from the nail salon smoked her cigarette down to the filter without seeming to notice she'd finally lit it. Mrs. Park crossed her arms tighter under her coat. Cesar kept taking the lid off his coffee and putting it back on, peering into the cup each time before snapping it shut again.

The barber shop went next. Al watched the old chrome chair roll halfway out of the blown-open front and topple sideways into plaster dust. "Son of a bitch," he said, but with no real force behind it. He sounded more surprised than angry, like he'd expected the chair to know better after all those years.

He took the keys off his neck and held them in his fist for a while. Nate's eyes kept catching on things he had no use for and wouldn't forget. The dead barber pole against the pickup tire. Mrs. Park's dry-cleaning tape fluttering at her hem. Gail's order pad working loose from her pocket every time she shifted her weight and her pushing it back in without looking.

Mort did not move except to plant his feet a little wider when the excavator hit the block between units and the vibration carried through the ground.

Linda said something to him. Nate couldn't hear it. Mort answered without turning his head.

Then the machine took the front of the diner.

The awning came away first, bent down the middle like cardboard, and the red script sign held for one extra second after the bolts underneath had already failed. It dangled stupidly, one side lower than the other, `GAIL'S` still readable and `DINER` tipped toward the pavement. Nate had seen that sign from the parking lot, from the diner stool, from Mort's side of the counter when they drank bad coffee and talked about nothing. He had seen it in summer heat and October rain and under the yellow parking-lot lights that made the whole plaza look flatter after dark. Now one remaining fastener snapped and the sign came down in a flat metal slap against the curb, bounced once, and lay there faceup in the spray.

Gail made a small sharp sound, and Nate only caught it because he'd already been listening for every small break in every human face on the block. It came out of her the way pain came out when cold water hit a bad tooth.

She handed the carafe to nobody. It dropped onto the curb and rolled once without opening. Then she turned and walked back toward her car.

Al said, "Gail."

She shook her head without looking at him.

"Nope," she said. "I'm done."

And kept walking.

No one tried to stop her. There was no reason they would. The sign had fallen. That had been her line.

The carafe stayed on the curb, a little puff of steam still escaping where the cap hadn't sealed right.

The diner collapsed inward faster than the first two units. Booth vinyl flashed red in the dust and disappeared. A section of counter he knew by touch showed for a second, the spot where the laminate had bubbled from an old coffee-burn and Gail always set the pie plate two inches to the left of it. It broke into three pieces under the excavator track. He had the nauseating certainty that if the machine had paused and somebody had asked him which fragment had once held the sugar dispenser and which had held the napkin caddy, he could have answered.

Linda started crying harder when the diner went. The hardware store was next now, close enough to hear in the way she breathed.

Mort took one hand out of his pocket long enough to touch the inside of her elbow. The movement was small and automatic, not showy comfort, just contact. Then his hand went back into the coat.

"It's all right," he said, and did not sound like he believed it in any direction.

The excavator repositioned. Workers dragged twisted awning frame out of the way with chains. A section of cinderblock wall leaned outward, hesitated, then came down into itself in a booming collapse that kicked dust across the street hard enough to salt the hood of Nate's car.

Then they got to the end unit.

Lazar's Hardware sat larger than the others and deeper. It took longer to open. The machine worried at the front corner first, cracking block and peeling back metal frame. When the face of the store split, Nate could see farther in than he ever had as a customer or as a kid. The place looked skinned. Pegboard, stud bays, the fluorescent run over aisle three, a slice of back room with cardboard boxes stacked to shoulder height. Shelving tilted, held for a second by stubborn bolts, then tipped forward and emptied itself.

Specialty fasteners came down in metal streams from busted bins, a granular silver rain into plaster dust. Bolts, washers, anchors, and screws lost their categories and turned into weight and noise. The wall Mort loved most came apart by category: machine screws, carriage bolts, toggle wings. The blue-and-white bin labels flashed and vanished. Somewhere in there were the left-hand threaded screws Mort had once explained to him with the patient pride of a man showing a kid that the world contained more particularity than he had guessed. Those were the parts other stores stopped bothering with. Mort kept them anyway, because the whole point of a place like Lazar's was that a man could walk in needing one impossible little thing and walk out with it in a paper bag.

Linda covered her mouth with both gloved hands. Her keys clacked against her bracelet.

Mort kept looking.

He barely blinked. He never touched his face. The muscles at his jaw kept bunching. The machine kept tearing the store open, room by room, and Mort stood there in the good coat with his hands in his pockets while sixteen years of rent, inventory, arguments, coffee, bookkeeping, deliveries, and ordinary Tuesdays came apart into block and wet dust. He didn't shout. He didn't cry. That almost made it harder to look at.

At some point Linda followed his gaze across the street and saw Nate.

Nate knew the moment because her face altered around the crying and a new question settled over it. She leaned toward Mort and said something. Mort looked, finally, across the road.

Their eyes met.

For one blind second Nate thought Mort knew something. Nate did not mean the impossible part, not time travel or overwritten consciousness or anything so grand and stupid. He meant something smaller and worse: that Mort knew this face belonged somewhere inside the damage, that the stranger called Dom had promised to look into things and had failed and had come anyway because failure did not cancel witness.

Mort gave him the smallest nod.

The nod was recognition, brief and plain. Mort had seen him and wanted him to know it.

Nate could not nod back. His neck had gone rigid. He stood under the sycamore and took the weight of that tiny public acknowledgement in Dominic Altieri's face while Dominic Altieri's signature rode in a file drawer across town and in the tendons of the hand inside his pocket.

The hardware sign came down almost gently compared to everything else.

The machine got under the awning, lifted, and the old wood sign tore from two brackets at once. It dropped face-first into the debris with a muffled crack rather than a crash, and the dust swallowed the spot where it landed. Linda made a broken sound and stepped in closer to Mort. He didn't put an arm around her. He stood where he was, hands still pocketed. If he moved one inch now, Nate thought, he might never stop moving.

"Those bins'll be everywhere," he said after a while, so quietly Nate almost lost it under the engine noise.

That was what he had to say. He didn't say this is my store or sixteen years or bastards. He talked about the bins, the inventory, the practical world surviving one last second in language even while it was being ground down.

The machine worked the remaining walls from the corners, methodical and unhurried. Roof trusses folded. Ductwork gleamed suddenly in the open air. A bent rack of shovels slid out of the rubble and vanished under a track. The register counter tilted and went. The back-room door, the one Linda used with her shoulder when her arms were full, broke in half at the hinge side. A carton marked `PAINT THINNER` burst and darkened a patch of dust into mud. One broom landed bristle-first and stood that way for a second before the next section of roof crushed it flat.

By the time the excavator was pushing the last broken block into rough piles, half the crowd had gone. Tran flicked her cigarette into the gutter. Mrs. Park left with both arms folded tight across her coat. Cesar stayed until twisted refrigeration pieces from what had been his bodega went up onto a truck, then headed down the block with his empty coffee cup still in hand.

Al lingered with Mort and Linda. The three of them stood at the barricade looking at the scraped rectangle, the broken block, the twisted refrigeration, the wet dirt where the rooms had been that morning. Eventually Linda touched Mort's sleeve and he nodded. Before he got into the car he looked once across the street. Nate was still there.

The cleanup sounded smaller: chains cinching, backup beepers, men talking about lunch in ordinary voices while they raked other people's square footage into heaps. When the workers shifted to the far end of the lot, Nate crossed through a loose fence panel near where the hardware store had been.

Mud sucked at his shoes where water and gypsum dust had made a paste. The air inside the fence smelled wrong for December: wet concrete, broken insulation, electrical dust, the faint medicinal sweetness of spilled solvent. A section of diner menu board still said `TURKEY CLUB` under gray slurry. A barber-shop mirror held the sky in broken pieces. Blue plastic hardware bins lay split open in the muck.

He found the screw beside a splintered sheet of pegboard, half buried and dull until his glove wiped one thread clean. The threads ran left-handed. Mort had shown him that once when he was a kid, laughing at how offended Nate had been by the idea that a screw could be backwards on purpose. Most stores quit carrying them. Mort kept them because every few months somebody came in with one in his palm and said nobody else had it.

Nate pocketed it and got back to the Honda before anyone yelled. Dust sat in the seams of his coat and on the dashboard. The screw knocked lightly against his keys when he turned onto the main road.

At a red light he took his left hand off the wheel and pushed it into his coat pocket. The scar on the knuckle caught on the screw's threads before his fingers closed around it. When the light changed he let go and drove home.

He let himself into the apartment a little after noon. The radiator knocked in the wall behind the couch. Sarah sat at the table in thick socks and a sweatshirt, a red pen in her hand, a stack of quizzes fanned in front of her. She looked up at him, then at the gray paste drying on his shoes.

"I thought you were going to the office."

"I did." He bent to untie one shoe. "I had to stop by one of the sites after."

"Which site?"

He worked at the knot until it loosened. Gray water spotted the mat. "Eastbridge. Just for a minute."

Sarah set the pen down and looked at him a beat too long. "Okay."

He got the other shoe off and stood. Dust shook from the seams of his coat when he slid it off, a pale spill onto the chairback.

"How was it?" she asked.

The screw in his pocket caught on the scar across his knuckle when he shoved his hand in there.

"Fine," he said.

Sarah looked at his hand, then at the dust on the chair and the wet print darkening on the mat. She capped the pen, gathered the quizzes, and took them into the bedroom with her, leaving him in the kitchen with the coat over one arm.
