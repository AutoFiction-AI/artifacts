# Chapter 1

By 4:58 on a Wednesday, Nate had told nine different adults to restart their computers and only one of them had admitted that yes, actually, the computer had not been on.

He clicked `Resolve Ticket`, took off the headset, and sat still in the foldout chair at his kitchen table while the work laptop spun for a second and froze on the company logo. The logo was a blue gradient circle meant to suggest seamless support. It suggested a pill. On bad days it suggested being digested.

The apartment had one real room and a bedroom that felt temporary even after four years in it. The kitchen table was the office. The couch was where he ate. The narrow counter by the window held a Keurig, a drying rack, two bowls, one plate, and the chipped mug he used for everything because there was no one left around to be disgusted by that kind of thing. The place still carried the geometry of the divorce, which turned out not to fade much with time. There was the lighter rectangle on the living room wall where a framed print had hung before his ex-wife took it. There was the half-empty bookshelf that made every book he still owned look more important than it was. There was the closet in the bedroom where her side had been empty for two years and still managed to look like an accusation.

His personal laptop was already open beside the work one. It had been sleeping on the desktop. As soon as he touched the trackpad, the screen came up on the folder labeled `ALTIERI`.

He had not opened it during his shift. He was not stupid enough to get fired from tech support for revenge research. But he had thought about it during the shift, the same way he thought about it while brushing his teeth or waiting for coffee to drip or standing in line at CVS buying garbage bags and ibuprofen and batteries. The folder had stopped being an activity a long time ago. It was background process. It ran under everything else.

He clicked into `Photos` first because that was what he always did.

There were three usable images of Dominic Altieri and seventeen versions of the same two bad ones, cropped and enlarged and sharpened until the pixels looked like aquarium gravel. One came from a Kassos Development staff page that had been redesigned twice since he'd saved it. Dominic wore a blue button-down, no tie, and the expression of a man doing his best in a room with bad coffee. The other came from a tagged photo on a dead social account Nate had found years ago, Dominic standing half turned at somebody's wedding with a drink in his hand and a woman tucked into his side, dark hair against a bare shoulder, both of them looking toward something outside the frame. The comments under that one had given Nate the name Sarah.

The company headshot kept refusing to become what Nate needed. He could zoom in until Dominic's face broke into squares and it still would not look like a destroyer. It looked like a guy who knew how to make small talk with your aunt. It looked like a guy who held doors for people. Nate had spent years trying to bully the image into something uglier. The image never cooperated. So he used the documents.

Documents behaved.

He clicked back out and opened `ProjectDocs`, then `Eastbridge`, then `Tenant_Notice_Scans`. There it was: the letter. He knew every line by position now, not even by reading. Kassos Development letterhead at the top. Formal language in the middle. Thirty days' notice. Redevelopment. Future opportunity. Thank you for your cooperation. At the bottom, a signature block over the typed name DOMINIC ALTIERI, Project Coordinator.

The scanned copy was not the original his father got in the mail. Nate had never seen that one again after the house got cleaned out and the garage boxes went into storage. This copy had come from a public-records request and then a lawsuit docket and then the corner of the internet where municipal paperwork went to fossilize. It was enough. The signature matched the one on other project filings. It matched the name that sat in Nate's head like a splinter.

Mort Lazar had run Lazar's Hardware in Unit 7 of Eastbridge Plaza for sixteen years. Nate knew the number from the lease documents. He knew the square footage. He knew what the assessed parcel value had been before the rezoning vote. He knew which council members had spoken in favor of the redevelopment and which ones had pretended to ask hard questions before voting yes anyway. He knew the demolition permit had been issued in December 2005. He knew the strip mall came down. He knew his father spent the winter trying to sell old stock out of the garage and refusing to call it that. He knew his father was dead by March.

He did not know what it would have changed if Dominic Altieri had died first. That was the weak point in the whole thing, and Nate knew it. One dead junior developer did not obviously stop a project with city approval and financing and scheduled contractors and a half-dozen signatures on every page. But the human brain was a talented litigator. It would keep filing appeals long after the judge had gone home.

If Dominic had not signed the notice, if Dominic had not handled the project, if Dominic had not existed for Kassos to use, then maybe something in the future would have shifted. Everything Nate had lived inside for years reduced itself, finally, to that one word.

Nate closed the document and rubbed his eyes. He still wore glasses in 2025, cheap rectangular frames with one arm slightly loose. They slid down his nose whenever he got tired, which was often. He pushed them back up and looked around the apartment like he had just arrived in it and couldn't believe the place belonged to him.

He was thirty-four: remote tech support for a company whose main product he could not have described in a way that made it sound necessary, divorced, no kids, a mother who called every Sunday and had stopped mentioning the folder because she knew what mentioning it did. He lived in an apartment where the nicest object was a coffee table he'd found by the dumpster and repaired because he'd grown up around a hardware store and there were some humiliations that still came easier than buying new furniture.

The bitterness had outlived the event. That was maybe the most embarrassing part. He was not a boy anymore. He was not even especially young. His father had been dead nearly twenty years and Nate had arranged his entire interior life around the idea of one man he'd never met. He had spreadsheets. He had parcel maps. He had a folder of screenshots from Dominic's LinkedIn profile progression, as if endorsements for negotiation and project coordination were clues in a murder case instead of the internet version of office handshakes.

He had an address history spreadsheet too. That one mattered more.

He opened it. Rows, dates, towns: public-records scraps stitched together from people-search sites, old directory archives, voter rolls, a cached insurance document that had coughed up Dominic's 2005 Bridgeport apartment address after Nate spent three consecutive nights cross-checking ZIP codes and abbreviations like it was a second job. Dominic had rented the same place for almost three years. Nate had found a grainy rental listing with a photo of tan carpet, vertical blinds, and one window air conditioner shoved sideways into the bedroom.

The spreadsheet sat on the screen while he listened to the apartment make its evening noises. The refrigerator compressor kicked on. Somebody upstairs dragged something heavy across the floor. A siren passed somewhere far enough away to be scenery.

On the folding table by the living room window, under a dish towel he'd thrown over it when the super came by last week, the machine waited.

He looked at it for a long time before getting up.

***

The dish towel came off in one pull. Underneath was a setup that still looked, even to him, less like advanced technology than like the before photo in an insurance claim. Copper coil wound around a cylindrical form. A boxy metal housing with the magnetron bolted into a scavenged frame. Raspberry Pi in a plastic case with heat sinks stuck to it like silver Chiclets. Relay board. Power supply. Two cooling fans zip-tied to a bracket he had cut crooked. A little touchscreen mounted at an angle because he had drilled one side of the plate wrong and decided not to care. Wires everywhere, color coded in the optimistic sense that he'd once intended to keep them organized.

He had built it in this room over six weeks with an AI assistant, a credit card, and the kind of confidence people got from not understanding what was supposed to be impossible.

It had started as boredom, which was not a noble beginning but was at least a common one. One Friday night after his shift, after microwaving leftover lo mein and eating it out of the container and thinking about absolutely nothing he wanted to do next, he'd opened the assistant and typed a question half as a joke.

If I wanted to model localized time distortion with consumer-grade hardware, what would a harmless prototype even look like?

The assistant did not laugh at him. That was the first hook.

It responded in patient, cheerful prose, the tone of a person helping a neighbor build shelves.

You would start with simulation, not hardware.

If you insist on a benchtop concept, think in subsystems: field generation, timing reference, shielding, control software.

Do not attempt any live-biological testing.

The phrasing should have scared him. Instead it made the whole thing feel tantalizingly bureaucratic, like the only real barrier between him and a temporal field generator was a shopping list and some caution tape. Nate had spent enough of his life online to know that confidence in paragraph form could substitute for expertise in the receiving brain. He also knew, dimly, that the assistant could hallucinate. It helped people write wedding vows and cover letters and SQL queries and weird fanfiction. It also told people to use glue where screws belonged. He knew all that. He knew it every single night he kept going.

The problem was that the assistant was calm in exactly the way his own life was not. Nate typed confused questions. The assistant answered without irritation. Nate asked if he could simplify the physics. The assistant simplified. Nate said he had a soldering iron, a multimeter, and a mediocre grasp of electricity. The assistant said that was enough to begin with concept validation if he stayed under safe thresholds. It gave him a bill of materials.

He ordered most of it from Amazon that night: copper magnet wire, ferrite cores, shielding fabric, high-voltage warning stickers, ceramic standoffs, thermal paste, a Raspberry Pi, a relay module, EMI tape, a replacement microwave magnetron sold for a Whirlpool model he did not own, insulated gloves, and a cheap radiation meter with reviews split between this saved my lab and does not work. It arrived broken. Over the next week boxes kept showing up at his door in a stack that made the hall look like he'd developed either an electronics hobby or a personality disorder. Both were true.

He built after work because that made the whole enterprise feel less deranged. Men with jobs built things in the evening. Men with jobs learned to solder by watching a fourteen-minute video twice and then burning their thumb anyway. Men with jobs asked an assistant to explain how to calibrate a phase-reference array "like I'm smart but not physics smart" and then copied code into files with names the assistant had invented: `controller.py`, `field_mapper.py`, `epoch_offset.py`.

He could handle the physical part better than the software, though even that was generous. He had grown up watching his father repair window screens, rehang doors, square cabinets, replace a garbage disposal without turning it into a project. Mort believed in measuring twice because cutting once was already trouble. Nate measured once, looked at the assistant's diagram again, and drilled into the metal bracket while it shifted under his knee. The hole went crooked. He made it work.

The code was worse. Nate lived on computers and still did not know how people who actually built software held the whole architecture in their heads. He could troubleshoot. He could search error messages. He could copy commands from a forum and understand, in a folk-magic way, which part mattered. The assistant was perfect for that level of ignorance. It never asked whether he understood what a parameter meant before telling him where to put it. It generated Python in a tone of warm inevitability. It told him to create a virtual environment and he did. It told him to install libraries and he did. It told him to replace a default safety lock with a custom threshold based on his power supply and he did that too, which was maybe the first actual crime in the sequence.

At first the machine did nothing.

That was almost a relief. He energized the coil and got heat. He ran the test routine and got a fan spinning up on the wrong side of the assembly. He tripped the breaker once and had to go into the hall in socks to reset it while pretending not to notice his neighbor's door crack open. He burned through one power supply and a decent surge protector. The Keurig died for two days before mysteriously reviving, which he mentioned to the assistant and received a whole block of text about stray field effects, line noise, and insufficient shielding.

He asked if that meant he was close.

It said, Closer than before.

That answer did more damage than anything technical.

He rewound the coil. He added shielding. He moved the whole rig farther from the fridge. He followed a generated calibration routine that had him syncing system time to three public atomic-clock servers and then comparing drift across a sensor loop he'd built from a breadboard and parts that came in little plastic envelopes. The process was tedious enough to feel real. His nights turned into steps: winding, soldering, stripping, crimping, rebooting, copying blocks of code, replacing values, tightening connections. He stopped noticing how strange it was to be taking instructions from a text box on how to assemble a device that might, if the assistant was even partially right, deform time in a one-bedroom apartment with bad ventilation.

The first anomaly did not look like science. It looked like cheap ceramics.

He had left his chipped mug on the folding table because there was nowhere else to put it. The machine was powered down when he went to bed. In the morning the mug looked ten years older. Fine crack lines veined the glaze. The chip at the rim had gone darker, dirtier. The handle had a hairline split that had not been there the night before.

He held it under the sink light for a long time, turning it in both hands. The thing had been ugly already. It was possible he'd missed the cracks. It was possible the heat from the rig had done something weird. It was possible he wanted to believe so badly that he was prepared to hallucinate deterioration in a mug from a regional bank.

He threw the mug away, then took it back out of the trash ten minutes later and photographed it.

The photos didn't help, except that the metadata on one of them listed the capture year as 2032 before correcting itself when he reopened the file.

He asked the assistant what sort of fault could cause apparent material aging combined with timestamp corruption.

The assistant's answer was longer than usual. It included phrases like local temporal shear and reference-clock instability and nonuniform frame exposure. It also said this:

If the effect is repeatable, stop treating it like noise.

So he repeated it.

He bought a white ceramic ramekin and a cheap digital watch from Target because he wanted test objects he hadn't already lived with long enough to doubt his memory. He put both near the field boundary during a low-power run. The watch came out seven minutes fast and never recovered. The ramekin developed a yellowed patch in the glaze like old nicotine staining. Nate sat on the floor with the machine humming in front of him and laughed once, not because it was funny but because there was nobody else in the room and his body needed somewhere to put the charge.

After that the whole project tilted.

He stopped asking the assistant whether it was theoretically interesting and started asking what the power requirements would be for larger displacement. The assistant grew more cautious in wording, which Nate took as evidence of seriousness rather than a final off-ramp. It talked about energy density and targeting precision and containment envelopes. It suggested simulation. Nate pushed. It suggested inanimate test payloads only. Nate pushed again. At one point it warned him that temporal translation of complex biological systems raised unresolved issues of continuity, substrate mapping, and transport fidelity.

He skimmed that answer while eating crackers over the sink.

It was not that he thought he understood it. It was that understanding had become optional. He had built a thing that made objects come out wrong in time. He had proof in his hands. The rest of the argument felt procedural.

Once the possibility existed, Dominic Altieri entered the room almost immediately.

Nate did not stand up and announce to himself that he was going to commit murder. That would have required a cleaner mind than he had. The decision came the way mold did, by degrees. One night he asked what sort of temporal anchor would improve target convergence for a human subject. Another night he asked whether fixed-location targeting was more stable than body-relative targeting. Another night he pulled up the address history spreadsheet and compared years. He told himself he was just seeing if it could be done. Then he told himself that of course it could be done, in theory. Then he stopped bothering with the distinction.

If he could get to July 2005, before the demolition, before the winter in the garage, before the silence that spread through his parents' house like rot, then what exactly was he supposed to do with that ability besides use it?

The answer should have been anything else.

Instead he looked at Dominic's headshot and thought about the notice letter and the boxes of unsold stock in the garage and the way grief hardened into routine if nobody interrupted it. The man in the photo had become the hinge Nate hung nineteen years on. That was probably unfair. Nate knew it was probably unfair in the same useless way people knew cigarettes were bad and exes stayed blocked for a reason. Knowing and stopping were different species of act.

He began tightening the target.

The cached records gave him the apartment address. Old tax records and a voter-registration scrape gave him enough corroboration to trust it. Dominic had been living there in July 2005. Nate needed a date and time. The assistant's targeting notes said stationary subjects were preferable. Sleep reduced movement variance. Overnight electrical loads were often more stable. Nate chose 3:47 AM because it sat in the blank middle of the night and because once he typed it into the touchscreen it looked specific enough to be science.

He told himself he would figure out the rest when he got there.

That was the ugliest part, maybe. He did not own a gun. Connecticut gun laws and Nate's own inertia had taken care of that. He had a kitchen full of knives, a hammer in the junk drawer, and a head full of revenge scenes that blurred out at the practical step where a real body had to be harmed by his real hand. The machine spared him from clarifying the plan. He would arrive. Dominic would be asleep. Something would happen. He would make it happen. Men had killed for worse reasons than wanting their father back.

He could think that in the abstract because Dominic still existed only as a file tree and a face that would not become monstrous on command.

The afternoon before he activated the machine, Nate tried one last time to imagine not doing it.

He cleaned the apartment instead. That was what his nervous system did when it wanted virtue without sacrifice. He took down the recycling. He wiped the counter. He ran the vacuum over the rug under the folding table and found a missing screw, three zip-tie ends, and a stale Cheez-It under the couch. He showered. He shaved badly. He made boxed mac and cheese and ate half of it standing up. The whole time the machine sat under its dish towel like something domestic, some embarrassing hobby project that was one better paint job away from never needing explanation.

At 11:00 he opened the `ALTIERI` folder again.

He looked at the wedding photo this time. Dominic in profile, smiling at somebody beyond the camera. Sarah under his arm, her face turned up slightly, amused by something Nate could not see. There was no reason to linger on her except that she made Dominic seem like a person who existed off paperwork. Nate almost closed the image. Then he zoomed in, irrationally, as if there would be a warning hidden in the grain. There wasn't. Just a woman with dark hair, one bare shoulder, and the look of somebody tolerating a photographer because the night itself had been decent so far.

He minimized the image and brought up the control interface.

The touchscreen on the rig displayed the parameters in ugly open-source fonts: target date, target time, latitude and longitude, anchor description, power threshold, compensation mode. A small amber warning icon in the lower corner pulsed because the software always had something to complain about. Nate tapped it.

BIOLOGICAL TRANSPORT FIDELITY UNVERIFIED.

CONSCIOUSNESS CONTINUITY UNKNOWN.

SUBSTRATE CONFLICT RISK ELEVATED FOR OCCUPIED TARGET VECTORS.

RECOMMENDED: UNINHABITED COORDINATES / INANIMATE PAYLOADS ONLY.

He read every line and absorbed almost none of it. Fidelity sounded like tissue damage. Continuity sounded philosophical. Substrate conflict sounded like a lawyer version of don't appear inside a wall. He had learned how to move through bad news in language that exceeded his patience. Half of adulthood was seeing a paragraph you didn't fully understand and deciding whether your real life could afford the delay required to understand it.

He tapped through the warning.

The machine needed a warm-up cycle. Fans started first, then the higher insect whine of the power section ramping up. The copper coil picked up a heat shimmer almost immediately, the air above it wobbling. Nate sat in the kitchen chair he'd dragged over and watched the values crawl into the green. He had cable-managed some of the wiring during the cleaning spree, which made the whole rig look even more ridiculous, like he'd organized the scene of his own felony.

Outside, somebody in the lot slammed a car door. The sound arrived strangely flattened. Nate frowned and looked toward the window. The vertical blinds were still. The air conditioner in the bedroom clicked off, and the sudden absence of its rattle made the whole apartment feel suspended, as if the room had leaned forward to listen.

He checked the parameters again. The screen still showed July 11, 2005, 03:47, Bridgeport, and the apartment address.

He had entered the GPS coordinates by copying them from a map into the interface one number at a time. The assistant had recommended redundant location descriptors to improve convergence, so he had added the street address too. It felt absurdly administrative. He was filing a travel request to commit murder.

He put both hands on his knees and realized they were damp.

This was the moment where a better man would stop. That thought came and went with no force behind it. Nate was so far past the stage where morality sounded like a new idea. He knew what he was about to do. He also knew what would happen if he stood up, powered everything down, and went to bed. Tomorrow would be another day of password resets and coffee pods and the `ALTIERI` folder waiting for him like a second spine. He could keep living exactly as he had lived, inside the grievance, until he aged into one of those men with a single story and nobody willing to hear it again.

He was tired of his life being all aftermath.

He reached over and hit `Arm`.

The touchscreen flashed red, then settled. A progress bar appeared. Numbers ticked upward. The fans got louder. Somewhere inside the metal housing the magnetron began a hard electrical buzz that he felt in his molars before he really heard it. The cheap radiation meter on the floor chirped once and then, unhelpfully, displayed zeros across the screen.

The air changed.

It was not temperature at first but texture. The room developed a pressure to it, as if the apartment had been wrapped in plastic and the plastic was being pulled tighter. The hairs on Nate's forearms rose. The copper coil shone with a brighter, more expensive color than copper had any right to. The dish towel, crumpled on the couch arm, lifted at one corner and settled again. His work laptop, still open on the table, flickered between the ticketing system and a blank white screen and then back.

Nate should have backed away.

Instead he stared at the center of the rig, at the slight distortion in the air above the field assembly, and thought with absolute stupidity: it worked.

The progress bar hit ninety percent. A new warning banner rolled across the bottom of the screen.

OCCUPIED TARGET VECTOR. RESOLUTION FAILURE POSSIBLE.

He leaned closer to read it.

That was when the apartment seemed to skip.

The refrigerator sound cut out. The upstairs dragging noise repeated itself, same scrape, same pause, same scrape, like a corrupted audio file. The digits on the microwave blinked twelve-zero-zero, then eleven-fifty-nine, then twelve-zero-zero again. Nate opened his mouth to say something to nobody. What came out was a gasp because the pressure in the room had moved inside him. His vision doubled at the edges. The machine's buzz dropped lower and lower until it was not sound anymore but force, a hand pushing at the center of his chest.

He grabbed the chair seat.

The metal under his fingers vibrated so hard it blurred. Light from the monitor smeared sideways. The apartment did not disappear exactly. It seemed to pull apart along lines he had never noticed before, the edges of cabinets, the seam where the wall met the ceiling, the cheap laminate wood grain on the floor breaking into parallel versions of itself. Dust burned on the heating elements with a smell like old pennies and a toaster left on too long. The air was so bright with static that when Nate blinked he saw the rig's outline printed on the back of his eyelids.

The banner on the screen changed again. He only caught part of it.

SUBSTRATE OCCU-

Then the whole room folded flat.

He did not feel movement through space. There was no tunnel, no rush, none of the special-effects garbage his brain had rented for the occasion. There was a violent instant of being exactly where he was and somewhere else trying to occupy the same coordinates. It felt mechanical, not mystical, which made it worse. There was no grandeur to it. He had not opened a portal. He had triggered a conflict.

Then there was nothing at all.

***

The sheet was wrong.

That was the first thing, not darkness or confusion or memory but the sheet itself.

It had more weight than the thin gray set on his own bed, and it was tangled around his legs in a way his sheets never got because he slept alone and mostly still. His bare calf touched another bare calf. A body beside him breathed once, softly, with the unconscious confidence of somebody who belonged exactly where she was.

Nate's eyes opened.

The room was almost dark. Streetlight seeped through blinds and made pale stripes on one wall. Somewhere nearby a window air conditioner rattled with a tired metallic rhythm. The ceiling above him was not his. He knew that before he knew anything else. It had a water stain in one corner and a fan where his apartment had a cheap dome light. The air smelled faintly sweet and clean, shampoo and cotton and summer-night freon.

He went still so fast it hurt.

The body beside him shifted closer in sleep, a warm hip and shoulder under the sheet, dark hair spilled against the pillow. A woman's hand lay curled near her face, thumb tucked under her jaw. There was a streak of red along the side of her middle finger, as if she'd gotten pen on herself and not noticed.

It was Sarah.

He knew her from the photo before he admitted it was her. The wedding picture, the bare shoulder, the face tilted toward Dominic with unguarded ease. In the dark she was softer, realer, stripped of pixels. Her breathing touched his upper arm.

Nate made a sound and swallowed it.

He pushed himself up on one elbow. The motion came wrong. His arm lifted him too easily and from a different place in his shoulder than he expected. His balance tipped forward harder than it should have. He caught himself with a hand on the mattress and saw, in the red digital wash of the alarm clock, broad fingers splayed against the sheet.

It was not his hand.

It was not his at all.

The knuckles were heavier. The wrist wider. A pale scar crossed the left knuckle in a diagonal slash he had seen only in a zoomed-in company photo where Dominic stood holding a softball trophy with some office team. Nate stared at it until the hand stopped looking like a glove and started looking like a fact.

He looked down.

It was a naked torso, not his pale narrow chest with the soft dip at the sternum and the ribs that showed too quickly when he'd had a bad week. This chest was broader, the body heavier through the shoulders, a dusting of dark hair disappearing under the sheet. Even in the dark he could tell he was looking at someone else's proportions. Someone else's life of walking around inside gravity.

Then he thought of his glasses.

The thought arrived so sharply he reached for the nightstand on instinct, searching for frames that were never there. He found only a clock radio, a paperback facedown, and a glass of water gone warm. But the room stayed clear. The numbers on the clock stayed clear. The water stain in the ceiling corner stayed clear.

He could see.

Nate's breath came faster, louder. Sarah stirred at the sound, made a small sleepy noise, and turned her face deeper into the pillow. He froze, every muscle locking so hard the mattress gave a tiny complaining creak under him. Her hand slid across the bed until her fingers touched his side and settled there, automatic, proprietary, intimate in a way that made his stomach drop so fast it felt like missing a stair.

He looked at the clock.

3:47.

For a second he thought the machine had failed in some stupid symmetrical way and dumped him back into his own apartment at the exact minute he'd launched it. Then he saw the smaller display under the time.

JUL 11 2005.

He stared until the numbers blurred and sharpened again.

The air conditioner rattled. A car hissed past on wet pavement somewhere below, though he hadn't heard rain. His own pulse thudded in his ears, but even that sounded wrong, deeper in a chest that was not built the way his had been. He put a hand over his mouth because he was suddenly afraid of hearing his own voice and finding that wrong too.

He was in the room.

No. That was not right.

He was not in the room he had targeted. He was in the body that had been in the room.

The distinction hit with sickening precision. He had expected travel. Matter, clothes, skin, maybe a knife if he'd forced himself to choose one, all of him stepping into Dominic Altieri's bedroom like a man entering a bad thought. But he was not wearing his clothes. His body was not here. His glasses were not here. His bad shoulder from sleeping wrong last winter was not here. He had not arrived as himself.

He touched his face in the dark.

His jaw was different. His nose was different, crooked at the bridge under his fingertips, broken once and healed a little off-center. The stubble was rougher than his own would have been at this hour. His mouth felt wider. The simple act of placing his hand on his own skin made his head ring with panic, because it was not his skin and yet the nerves reporting it were somehow his.

Sarah breathed in, out. Her fingers flexed once against his side and went slack again.

The machine warning unscrolled itself in his head, too late to matter. The phrases came back in sequence: occupied target vector, consciousness continuity unknown, substrate conflict.

He had read those words like signage at a construction site. He understood them now with the blunt, animal clarity that came after the damage. There was no extra space in this body for Dominic. There was no second set of eyes somewhere behind his eyes. Nate had not joined him. He had replaced him.

Dominic Altieri was gone.

The knowledge did not arrive as guilt first. It arrived as mechanics. One thing had been written over another. A file deleted and saved. Nate's mind tried to reject the comparison because it was glib and disgusting and also because it fit too well. He had killed a man with jargon. He had committed murder through a user interface. No blood on him. No struggle. Just a warning dialog and an `Arm` button and now a sleeping woman with her hand on the ribs of the body she believed belonged to the man she loved.

He had wanted catharsis. Wanted confrontation, maybe even some ugly speech in a dark room, Dominic waking up to the face of the son whose family he'd ruined. Wanted, if he was honest, something cinematic enough to justify how long he'd carried this around.

Instead he had done something pettier and more absolute. He had deleted Dominic so completely there had not even been anybody left to hear the accusation.

Nate swallowed against the taste in his mouth. It was not metallic or dramatic. It tasted like sleep and old water and panic warming up.

He needed to move. He needed air. He needed a mirror. He needed ten impossible things in an order he couldn't establish.

But Sarah's hand was still on him, and the instant he shifted her fingers tightened very slightly, the reflex of somebody surfacing just enough to notice a change in the bed.

"Dom," she murmured, not waking, only shaping the name with her lips before sinking again.

The sound went through him like a blade turned gently.

He shut his eyes.

When he opened them, the clock still said 3:47. The date still sat below it in calm red digits, smug in their clarity.

JUL 11 2005.

Twenty years away, in a city where his father was alive and fourteen-year-old Nate Lazar was asleep somewhere under a cheap summer fan, and Nate himself was in Dominic Altieri's bed wearing Dominic Altieri's body while a woman named Sarah slept against him and the only machine that could have returned him existed, if it existed at all, on the far side of two decades.

He looked down one more time at the broad left hand on the sheet, at the scar crossing the knuckle, and understood the shape of what he had done.

He had not gone to Dominic Altieri.

He had taken Dominic Altieri's place.
