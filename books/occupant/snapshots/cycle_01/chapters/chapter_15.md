# Chapter 15

By eleven the apartment had gone quiet in the careful way it did now, not peaceful, just arranged around not touching the sore parts. Sarah had gone to bed with a stack of essays and one of her red pens clipped into the neck of her T-shirt. Nate sat alone at the kitchen table under the yellow overhead light with Dominic's checkbook open, the latest bank statement flattened under his palm, and the December rent coupon pulled from under the fruit bowl.

Checking: twenty-two hundred.

Savings: eight hundred.

Wallet: forty-three dollars and a gas receipt.

Three thousand dollars was not money. Three thousand dollars was a delay. It was rent, the car, groceries, a little breathing room if Victor decided the weirdness had become more trouble than Dominic was worth. Without the job there was no backup plan hiding behind the math. There was Dominic's apartment, Dominic's Honda, Dominic's half-broken relationship, Dominic's name on the badge that got him into the office where his father's death had been filed into manila folders and signed for.

He had written the numbers twice already on the back of an electric bill, like rewriting them could make them expand.

The intercom buzzed hard enough to rattle the cheap metal frame by the stove.

He looked at the clock on the microwave. 11:07.

The buzz came again. Then Tommy's voice, flattened by static and hallway echo.

"Dom. C'mon."

Nate pressed the talk button. "What happened?"

"Nothing. Open the door."

He buzzed him in anyway.

From the bedroom Sarah made a small sound, not awake all the way, just registering disruption. Nate stood in the doorway and listened to Tommy take the stairs too fast, a hand smacking the rail on the landings. When he reached the apartment door he leaned on it instead of knocking.

Tommy's lip was split in the middle, a neat dark cut that had puffed the whole upper lip around it. Blood had dried into the stubble at the corner of his mouth. His leather jacket hung open over a wrinkled Knicks sweatshirt, and he kept rubbing the little gold Saint Christopher medal at his throat with two fingers, working the chain back and forth until it had left a red groove in his neck. He smelled like cold air, beer slopped on a bar top, and the sweet mint gum he chewed when he was trying not to smoke.

"You look great," Tommy said.

"Jesus."

"Yeah, no, not ideal."

Tommy smiled when he said it, but the smile came out thin and wrong. Nate stepped aside. Tommy came in talking, because of course he did.

"Before you do the whole thing, let me just sit down for a second. I had kind of a weird night. Not weird ha-ha. Bad weird."

Nate shut the door softly and glanced toward the bedroom. "Kitchen."

"Right. Sure. Secret summit."

Tommy followed him in. He moved fast in the useless, scattered way of somebody who wanted to look casual and could not stop checking the exits. Nate pulled open the freezer, found a bag of peas gone mostly to frost, wrapped it in a dish towel, and set it on the table.

Tommy looked at it. "Very nurturing. You and Sarah watch one episode of something medical and now we're triage."

"Hold it on your mouth."

"Bossy."

He did it, though. He sat with the peas pressed to his lip and winced. The kitchen light made his face look more boyish than it had at Sunday dinner months earlier, the charm stripped down to its cheap studs. Twenty-eight, Nate thought. Younger than Nate had been when his own life started going bad in the boring irreversible way. Tommy's hair had dried into cowlicks from sweat and cold. There was a damp ring at the collar of his sweatshirt where somebody had dumped a drink or water over him.

Nate stayed standing.

"What happened?" he asked again.

Tommy breathed through his nose for a second. "I got clipped."

"By who?"

"Some guys."

"That's a child answer."

"OK, sorry, Professor. Some guys I owe money to."

Nate waited.

Tommy lowered the peas. "Four."

"Four what."

"Thousand."

Nate let out a short breath through his nose. It was not even surprise. Tommy saw it and started talking faster, his usual rhythm slamming back into place like he'd found a gear.

"I know how it sounds. I do. It sounds like a lot. It's a lot. But it's not like I lost four tonight tonight. Some of it's old. It stacked up. There was football, and then this college thing, and then I had one really stupid blackjack weekend where I got cute for no reason, and then these assholes started floating me because they know me, which in hindsight, terrible sign, if a guy's like no rush, Tommy, that's actually all rush, turns out."

"Who hit you."

"Nobody hit me."

Nate looked at the mouth.

Tommy touched the split with the heel of his hand, then shrugged. "OK, a little. It was more instructional than violent."

"Instructional."

"Yeah."

"That's not better."

"I know it isn't better."

He said it quietly. That was new enough that Nate felt it in his chest before he understood why. Tommy usually argued on instinct, like silence was a tax he'd never agreed to pay. Tonight the silence kept slipping through him between sentences.

"They found me at O'Rourke's," Tommy said. "Out back, near the Dumpster. One of them asked if I was deaf. The other one put my face into the wall. Then they got very specific about a week."

Nate looked at the red groove the medal chain had cut into Tommy's skin. Tommy saw him looking and tucked the medal under his sweatshirt like he'd been caught doing something private.

"Don't tell Ma," he said.

"That your face looks like that?"

"Any of it. She'll know from my voice. I can usually get around that. Not with this."

From the bedroom came the faint sound of mattress springs. Sarah turning over. Nate lowered his own voice.

"Why are you here."

"Come on."

"Why are you here, Tommy."

Tommy stared at the peas for a second, then laughed once through his nose. "You're right. Fair. Because I need help."

"How much."

"I said four."

"How much can I actually give you."

Tommy's eyes flicked up. Relief moved through his face so fast it looked like pain. "See, this is why I came here first. I know you're pissed, I know, but I also know you're not gonna let me get buried over four grand."

Nate almost said, I might. Instead he looked down at the bank statement already on the table, Dominic's life reduced to columns and an end-of-month cheerful slogan from Fairfield County Savings.

"I don't have four thousand dollars."

"Dom."

"I don't."

"You always say that first."

"Because it's usually true first."

Tommy spread his free hand. "OK, fine, not four in the apartment. I don't need you to peel it off a mattress. I'm saying put something together. Checking, card, whatever, and then I got a game Sunday, all right, not bullshit, actual clean read, Pats-Bills, the line is soft and I can get the rest, plus your money back, plus maybe a little extra so this entire humiliating family event becomes, I don't know, a temporary liquidity issue."

It was almost impressive how fast Tommy could wrap disaster in language that sounded like he should be wearing a headset and pitching venture capital. The same look, Nate thought, that every doomed app guy had in 2021 when he said monetization like the money already existed.

"No," Nate said.

Tommy blinked. "No what."

"No game. No Sunday. No you turning this into six or eight or some other number you say like it's a weather report."

"I wasn't saying gamble with it."

"You were absolutely saying that."

"I was saying I can cover the overhang if you help me get right now handled."

"With what money."

"Dom, come on."

"With what money."

Tommy dropped the comedy for half a second and looked exactly like Dominic around the nose and eyes, the family resemblance suddenly mean instead of charming. "I don't know yet."

"Right."

"That doesn't mean there isn't money."

"That's pretty much exactly what it means."

Tommy slapped the peas back onto his mouth and spoke around them. "You're in a mood."

"I'm in my apartment at eleven at night with your blood on my towel."

"So wash the towel."

Nate stared at him.

Tommy pulled the peas away. "Sorry. Sorry. That was stupid."

The refrigerator hummed behind them. The percolator sat dry on the counter. Nate could hear the building pipes ticking, heat coming up too hard because it was December and the super solved every temperature problem by making the radiators act like murder weapons.

"How bad is it," Nate asked.

Tommy looked at the table instead of him. "Bad enough."

"That's another child answer."

"You know what, maybe I'm not crushing it right now conversationally."

"Do they know where Ma lives."

Tommy didn't answer.

Nate felt his spine go cold in a very local, mechanical way, as if some switch inside the borrowed body had clicked from standby to active.

"Tommy."

"They know things." Tommy swallowed. "One of them asked if Renata still opened before dawn. Which, OK, maybe he was guessing, maybe every mother in Connecticut is up at five making sauce or whatever, but I don't think he was guessing."

Nate sat down.

Tommy watched that happen and nodded once, almost to himself, like: yes, there it is, now we are in the real part.

"I wasn't gonna tell her," Tommy said. "I wasn't gonna let it get near her. I just need to get enough in front of them that they stop having fun with it."

"Fun."

"You know what I mean."

Nate did. He looked at Dominic's checkbook. He looked at the numbers he had been studying before the buzz came. Three thousand dollars wasn't money. Three thousand dollars was time. It was one bad month not becoming immediate catastrophe. It was the only padding between him and the exact shape Victor had laid out in that calm office voice: drop it, or the firm could stop supporting you. It was also, inconveniently, sitting on the table while Dominic's brother held frozen peas against a mouth somebody else had opened for him.

Tommy leaned forward.

"Listen to me," he said. "I'm not asking because I had a rough weekend and I wanna chase it. I know that's what it sounds like because, frankly, I've curated a strong brand over the years. I get that. This isn't that. They gave me a week because they know I can get it from you. That's embarrassing, by the way. For me. Just so we're tracking the humiliation distribution here. But if I don't show something, they're gonna start making calls. They're gonna show up places. I need them off me."

"How much gets them off you."

"Four."

"How much gets them to wait."

Tommy hesitated. He was doing math and also lying and Nate could watch both processes compete in his face.

"Three," Tommy said finally. "Probably."

"Probably."

"Three definite. Three says I'm not running. Three says Sunday is about the rest, not the whole thing."

"There isn't a Sunday."

"Fine. Whatever. The point is three buys me air."

Nate reached for the statement and turned it toward Tommy. He tapped the checking line with one finger.

"Twenty-two hundred."

Tommy leaned in.

Nate tapped the savings line.

"Eight hundred."

Tommy said nothing.

"That's it."

"What about the cards."

"What about them."

"Advance."

"At what ATM, Tommy, a magical ATM where interest doesn't exist?"

"OK. OK."

He sat back. For a second he looked older than Nate had seen him look, not because the face changed but because the performance lost power and the wires showed. He kept one hand on the peas. The other worried the saint medal back out from under the sweatshirt and flattened it against the table with his thumb like he was smoothing a receipt.

"You got more than this before," he said.

"Before what."

"Before. Other times."

The wording was careful. Tommy had reached the point where even he knew he could not say last time without admitting there had been too many last times.

Nate rubbed at the bridge of his nose. His head hurt. He had spent months learning Dominic's calendar, Dominic's handwriting, Dominic's dead man's body. He had learned what cereal Sarah bought, what kind of coffee Renata preferred, the route to the office without using the atlas, the measured angle of Dominic's smile at work. Now here was another part of the life, uglier than the family dinners, and he knew it too. Not factually. Structurally. He knew this kitchen had hosted versions of this argument before. The script was in the cabinets.

"I can't do four," he said.

Tommy nodded quickly, like agreement had already happened. "Three then. Great. Great. Three's workable. I can make three work."

"I'm not done."

Tommy went still.

"If I give you anything," Nate said, "you're not taking one dollar of it to a game. Not a game, not a card table, not some guy's cousin with a lock of the century. You don't need one good weekend. You need to stop doing this. Every time you get buried you act like one more bet is the ladder out, and it's never the ladder. It's just next week again."

The sentence came out whole. Too whole. Old, maybe. Used.

Tommy's eyes lifted to his face. The room changed a notch. The joke reflex did not just fail to appear; it physically left him.

"Yeah," Tommy said after a second. "All right."

Nate knew, with the ugly certainty of instinct, that Dominic had said some version of that before. Maybe here. Maybe ten times. Maybe at a bar, in a car, on Renata's front steps with Tommy swearing this one was different while Dominic held the family line with both hands. Nate had not inherited the memories, but he had inherited the grooves. He could feel them under his own words, worn smooth by repetition.

Tommy looked down at the saint medal again.

"You always know exactly where to stick it," he muttered.

Nate almost corrected him. Not always. Not me. Instead he pulled the checkbook closer.

"Three leaves me with nothing," he said.

Tommy nodded, once.

"I know."

"No, I don't think you do."

"I know enough."

"Rent's next week."

"Yeah."

"Victor is already halfway to firing me."

Tommy frowned. "What."

"Nothing."

"No, what do you mean."

Nate shook his head. He was not going to do this too, not tonight, not hand Tommy the private architecture of his fear so Tommy could step around it on the way to the check.

"It means I don't have room for this."

"I know."

"And I'm doing it anyway."

Tommy swallowed. "Yeah."

Nate wrote the amount slowly: 3000.00.

The signature came easier now than it had in July. That remained one of the quieter horrors. His hand did not hesitate over Dominic Altieri anymore. It made the name in one practiced sweep, the same hand that had signed work forms, grocery slips, maybe birthday cards, definitely the notification letter to Morton Lazar, definitely whatever checks Dominic had written before to keep his brother from being eaten alive a thousand dollars at a time.

Tommy watched the pen move as if stillness itself might spook Nate out of it.

"It's linked to savings," Nate said. "It'll clear."

"Dom."

"Shut up for one second."

Tommy shut up.

Nate tore the check out and held it between two fingers for a beat before letting it go. Tommy took it carefully, unlike anything else in his life. He looked at the number, then at Nate, then back at the check like the number might resize itself if he blinked wrong.

"Jesus," he said.

"Do not say you're good for it."

"I wasn't gonna."

"Do not say thank you like this fixes anything either."

"OK."

"And if they come near Ma anyway, you call me first. Before you get a plan. Before you get clever. Before Sunday, before whatever. Me first."

Tommy nodded.

"Say it back."

"You first."

"Me first."

"You. Yeah. You first."

Nate stared at him until Tommy rolled his eyes a little and corrected it.

"You first, then. Fine."

The apartment had gone so quiet that when Tommy laughed once under his breath it sounded like someone else in the room. He folded the check with absurd care and slid it into the inside pocket of his jacket.

"I hate this," he said.

"Good."

"Not because of you. I mean, partly because of you. You're being a prick in a way that's not helping the overall vibe. But I hate this. Just so that's noted for the record."

"Noted."

"I had it for a minute, too. That's the stupid part. I was up. Not enough, but up. Then I decided to get cute."

"You keep saying that like it's a weather event."

"It's a disease, actually. I think if I get diagnosed they have to stop charging me juice."

Nate did laugh then, against his will, once and hard through his nose. Tommy caught it and smiled in immediate relief, the old family skill of treating laughter as provisional amnesty.

"There he is," Tommy said softly.

Nate hated the warmth that moved through him at that. It was not earned. It had been purchased secondhand with a murder and maintained with a thousand daily lies. Tommy was relieved because his brother had surfaced. Sarah stayed because some version of Dominic still lived in the apartment. Renata kissed his face because the body was right. None of it belonged to him. It still worked on him anyway.

"Go home," he said.

"I'm gonna go to Jimmy's. Crash there till morning."

"Do not go back to O'Rourke's."

"I wasn't planning on hanging a sign."

"Tommy."

"I'm kidding. Jesus."

He stood and swayed a little before finding his balance. The peas slid off the towel and hit the table with a wet thump. Tommy looked at them and then, because he was Tommy, said, "You want me to put these back or are they medically compromised."

"Get out."

"Right."

At the door Tommy stopped. The hallway light from the apartment building was harsher than the kitchen light, greener, and it carved his face back into something bruised and young. For the first time since coming upstairs he did not seem to be performing toward anyone.

"Thanks, Dom," he said.

Then he stepped in and hugged Nate hard enough to force air out of him.

It was not a graceful hug. Tommy was all elbows and cold jacket and the sharp smell of winter and bar smoke. But it was real. Nate felt the full weight of another man's brother landing on him: history he had not lived, forgiveness he had not earned, the stupid durable love that kept surviving in this family despite evidence. Tommy's hand thumped once between his shoulder blades, awkward, heavy, grateful. Nate put his own arms around him a second too late and held on because not holding on would have been its own betrayal.

When Tommy pulled back his eyes were bright in a way that made Nate think of fever.

"I'll call you tomorrow," Tommy said.

"Do that."

Tommy nodded and left, footsteps fast on the stairs this time, check in his jacket, three thousand dollars gone out of the apartment before midnight.

Nate shut the door and kept his hand on the knob.

He could hear the building again. A television through the wall. Somebody running water. A baby coughing two units down. In the kitchen the checkbook lay open on the table with the carbon imprint still visible under the next blank check. Three thousand dollars. Balance pending: nothing worth the name.

* * *

When he turned around Sarah was standing in the hallway in one of Dominic's old college sweatshirts, hair flattened on one side from sleep, the side of her hand marked with a red streak where she had apparently gone to bed still holding the grading pen. She had one essay folded under her arm by mistake, as if she'd brought part of her job out with her without realizing it.

"How bad?" she asked.

Nate looked at her for a second, then back at the kitchen.

"Pretty bad."

She nodded once and went past him.

Nate followed her in. Sarah opened the cabinet over the fridge, took down the bottle of bourbon they mostly kept for company and bad weather, and found two glasses without asking if he wanted one. That was the closest thing to a ceremony they had left.

She poured. The bourbon hit the glass with a heavy medicinal smell that took over the room for a second, warm and blunt.

"Did you give it to him?" she asked.

"Yeah."

"How much."

"Everything."

Sarah handed him a glass. "Checking too."

"And savings."

"OK."

That was all. No why. No I told you so. Not even the courtesy version of disbelief. She sat down at the table, tucked one foot up under the opposite knee, and pulled the essay out from under her arm. It was a blue-book answer about the New Deal, half-graded, the student's handwriting slanting downhill by the last paragraph. She looked at it as if she did not recognize the object, then set it aside.

Nate stayed standing a moment longer. The room still held Tommy in traces: diluted blood on the dish towel, the thawing bag of peas, the sharp sweet mint of his gum. Sarah noticed the peas and moved them with two fingers into the sink.

"He looked scared," she said.

"He was."

"I know."

Nate sat across from her. The chair gave its usual little squeak. He drank. The bourbon burned all the way down and then sat in his stomach like a coin.

"He said they asked about Renata," he said.

Sarah's mouth flattened. "Of course they did."

"Has that happened before?"

"Not that I know of."

"You don't sound surprised."

She took a drink first. "I'm surprised by the specifics. I'm not surprised by Tommy getting right up to the edge where specifics start."

Nate nodded.

"Was it really four?" she asked.

"That's what he said."

"Which means maybe five."

"Probably."

She looked toward the door Tommy had gone through. "The first year we were together, he called here at one in the morning from some off-track place in Milford because he'd lost his car keys and six hundred dollars and somehow those felt like the same emergency. Dominic drove out in pajama pants."

Nate could see it too easily: Dominic still alive, irritated, competent, already reaching for the keys before the call ended.

"Did he pay him back?" Nate asked.

Sarah gave him a look over the rim of the glass.

"Right," Nate said.

She set the glass down carefully. "Sometimes he'd bring groceries over after. Like that counted. A sack of oranges, cereal, three kinds of deli meat nobody asked for. Once he brought a lamp."

"A lamp."

"Still had the price tag on it. He said he won it in a raffle. Dominic said, this is from Target. Tommy said, yeah, Target had the raffle."

Nate laughed despite himself, softer this time. Sarah did not quite smile, but the corner of her mouth moved.

"He always comes late," she said. "That's his thing. If he shows up before dinner, it's a favor. If he shows up after ten, it's triage."

"You heard all of it."

"Most of it."

He looked at her. "Why didn't you come out."

Sarah rolled the glass once between her palms. "Because if I'd come out he would've started performing for me too. He would've gotten cute. He would've said he was fine. He would've tried to make me laugh. You had a better shot at getting the actual number."

"I still don't know if it was the actual number."

"No." She shrugged. "But you got the scared version of him. That's closer."

Nate took another drink. The heat in the apartment had gone overboard while Tommy was there. The radiator hissed under the window like it resented the whole household.

"I'm broke now," he said.

"I know."

"I mean actually."

"I know what actually means."

There was no accusation in it. Just precision. Sarah had always been good at separating tone from fact. She could hand you the ugly truth without polishing it into drama.

"I've been doing the math every night," Nate said. "Since Victor."

She glanced up at that. "You didn't tell me that."

"I didn't tell you a lot of things."

"That one I noticed."

He nodded. Fair enough.

"Three thousand was the cushion," he said. "Not even a good cushion. A floor mat, basically. But it was there."

Sarah looked at the statement on the table. "You were counting months."

"Weeks."

"Yeah."

She drank again. Her hair had fallen forward over one cheek. She pushed it back with the wrist that still had the red pen streak on it. Nate kept looking at the mark. It was so specifically Sarah: evidence of a day she had lived somewhere else, in a classroom with fluorescent lights and teenagers who thought Reconstruction happened to annoy them personally, carried by accident into this kitchen after midnight because life did not stop separating itself into chapters just because one family had another crisis.

"You couldn't not do it," she said.

It was not a question.

"Could I not?" he asked.

Sarah considered that. "I could've. Maybe. That's one difference between me and Dominic."

The name sat between them. Dominic, not you. Dominic, the person she still thought she was measuring him against every day. Nate looked down at his drink.

"But he couldn't," she went on. "And whatever is going on with you, that part seems to have survived."

Nate let out a breath. "That's not comforting."

"I wasn't trying to comfort you."

"I know."

They sat with that. Somewhere below them a car alarm chirped and then stopped. The essay on the table had a note in Sarah's handwriting at the top: Be specific. Nate could read it upside down.

"Do you hate him?" he asked after a minute.

Sarah frowned a little. "Tommy?"

"Yeah."

"No." She thought again. "I hate the version of every room that shows up with him sometimes."

"That's very teacherly."

"What, accurate?"

"Annoyingly."

She gave him the smallest real smile of the night, then let it go.

"He's not bad," she said. "He's just never met an emergency he couldn't turn into a story about himself until it gets close enough to bite him. Then he's twelve."

"Tonight he looked younger than that."

"Yeah."

Sarah rubbed at the red streak on her hand with her thumb. It only smeared wider.

"When he gets quiet, that's when I'm afraid for him," she said.

Nate nodded. He knew what she meant. The loud Tommy, the pitchman Tommy, the guy making jokes with a split lip and frozen peas on his face, that Tommy still believed in the audience. The quiet one had run out of tricks.

"Did Dominic ever say no?" Nate asked.

The question came out before he could stop it.

Sarah looked at him, not suspicious exactly, but alert in the old way, the way that meant she was hearing the shape of what he asked in addition to the words.

"Sometimes," she said. "Not often. Usually after. Usually with a speech."

"A speech."

"A lecture. Whatever." She tipped her glass toward him. "You gave one tonight, by the way."

"Did I."

"Mm-hm."

"Was it good."

"No." Another tiny smile. "It was familiar."

That landed deeper than if she'd meant it cruelly. Nate stared into the bourbon. Familiar. He had spent months terrified of every unfamiliar move, every wrong drawer, every missed memory, every phrase Sarah caught and turned over like evidence. Now the worst things were becoming easy. The signature. The lecture. The way Tommy had folded into his arms at the door and thanked the right dead man.

Sarah followed his gaze to the checkbook.

"What are we short on immediately?" she asked.

"Rent, depending on timing."

"I can cover groceries for a while."

"You don't have to."

"I know I don't have to."

He looked up.

She met his eyes and said, more quietly, "I'm not doing a wife thing. I'm doing math."

"OK."

"And before you say it, yes, I know the difference."

"I wasn't gonna say it."

"You were absolutely gonna say it."

"Maybe."

She took the essay back into her lap and flattened the bent corner with her thumb. It was such an ordinary motion that it almost broke him. Not because it was tender. Because it was not. Because it meant the night had not become a movie. Tommy's blood, three thousand dollars, men asking about Renata before dawn, and Sarah was still here smoothing some kid's bad paragraph about Roosevelt because tomorrow existed and papers still got graded and rent still had to be paid.

"You should get some sleep," she said.

"You too."

"I did. Briefly."

"Sorry."

"I know."

They drank. The bourbon level dropped. The apartment cooled by degrees as the radiator shut itself off and the windows took back the night.

"Do you think he'll pay the three?" Nate asked.

Sarah snorted softly.

"Right," he said.

"He might mean to."

"That's somehow worse."

"It's definitely sadder."

He rubbed his thumb over the condensation ring his glass had left on the bank statement until the paper started to pill.

"I'm so tired," he said, and heard how flat it came out.

Sarah looked at him for a second. Not pitying. Just looking.

"I know," she said.

That was the moment of solidarity, and because it was Sarah it arrived without decoration. No touch. No speech about family, no rescue fantasy, no declaration that they would figure it out. Just the fact of her staying in the chair across from him while the apartment smelled like thawing peas and bourbon and Tommy's bad decisions, while the numbers on the statement sat there unchanged except for the check he had already written against them.

They stayed at the table until the bourbon was gone from both glasses.

When Sarah finally stood, she gathered the essay and the red pen from the counter, frowned at the smear on her hand, and headed back toward the bedroom. At the hall she stopped.

"If he calls tomorrow," she said without turning around, "make him tell you the actual name of the place he owes. Not just the guys. The place."

"Why."

"Because if he lies about that, he's still playing."

Nate nodded even though she could not see it.

"OK."

She disappeared into the dark.

Nate sat alone a little longer with the checkbook, the statement, and the ugly clean space where the cushion had been. Outside, somewhere down on the street, a man laughed too loudly and then coughed until the laugh became work. Nate finished the last swallow in his glass and looked at the numbers one more time, not because they might change, just because looking away would have been a kind of optimism, and he was done with that for the night.
