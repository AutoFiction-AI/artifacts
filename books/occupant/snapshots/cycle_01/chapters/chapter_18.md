# Chapter 18

The lot started changing before Nate let himself look at it directly.

First came the chain-link fence, with green privacy mesh threaded through it until the whole wrecked rectangle turned opaque and official. Then came the trailer with KASSOS DEVELOPMENT in blue vinyl on the side, as if there had been any danger of forgetting whose job this was. Then came the survey flags, orange and pink, stabbed into frozen mud in a grid so clean it made him think of vector lines, a wireframe model laid over the place where Gail had poured coffee and Al had flicked Barbicide off his comb and Mort had sold the odd stock nobody else cared enough to carry.

By the second week of January, concrete trucks were backing in before eight. Rebar cages rose from the footings like wire ribs. Men in brown duck jackets stamped their boots and drank from foam cups and shouted measurements into the cold. Where Gail's counter had been there was a pit with rainwater skinned over in the mornings. Where Lazar's Hardware had been there was plywood, diesel exhaust, and a stack of cinder block wrapped in plastic that snapped loud in the wind.

He drove by more than he needed to. Sometimes on the way to work, sometimes on the way home, sometimes because he told Sarah traffic was bad on the other route and she looked at him for half a second too long and let him keep the lie. The place looked smaller fenced in. Eastbridge Plaza had taken up most of his childhood imagination. In memory it had been almost municipal, a whole district of his life. In January it was an address with a permit trailer.

The community did what communities did when nobody with any power cared whether they survived. It rerouted itself.

Gail reopened two blocks west in a narrow storefront that had been a luncheonette, then a tax office, then nothing for a while. The new diner had six stools instead of twelve and booths so tight you had to turn sideways to get in. The grill hissed too close to the register. The coffee maker was louder and worse. Gail worked the counter in a navy cardigan with one pocket hanging by a thread and reading glasses held together at the bridge with a strip of clear tape she insisted still had years left in it.

"This place has no elbows," she told Nate the first time he came in. "You try carrying three plates through there and tell me this isn't a labor violation."

"Food's good," he said.

"I didn't ask about the food."

But she kept the place full. It was not full the way the old diner had been, with half the strip mall drifting in and out because it was there and because Gail knew who liked rye and who needed decaf after lunch. This was a smaller, more deliberate full. Mrs. Park came in once with her son's science fair board under one arm and took soup to go. Al left the address of his new barbershop on the back of a losing Keno slip and told Gail to put a stack by the register. Cesar sent over a crate of plantains and then made her pay for them because, as Gail said with hard admiration, the man would rather die than do charity where somebody could see it.

Nobody said Eastbridge Plaza much. They talked in new addresses now, cross streets, parking situations, which landlord was a bastard, whose heat worked, whose didn't. The old place survived as logistics.

At home the apartment kept being home whether he deserved that word or not.

Sarah was back to full school days after winter break. She came in smelling like cold air, dry-erase marker, and the fried oil from the place next to the faculty lot. She dropped student papers on the kitchen table, toed off her boots, and went straight to the sink to wash her hands like the building itself was on her skin. Most nights they cooked because eating out cost money Nate did not have and because cooking had become one of the few things that made the apartment feel like a place time passed in instead of a set he had broken into.

He got good at onions. He got decent at rice. He was still bad at timing anything involving fish.

One Tuesday in late January, Sarah opened the hall closet to put away a scarf and stood there looking at the accumulated hardware with her mouth slightly open.

"Why do we have weather stripping?" she asked.

"Drafts."

"For what?"

"Future drafts."

She looked back into the closet. There were two boxes of outlet covers, a pack of brass hooks, a caulk gun, shelf brackets, a coil of sash cord, and three small boxes of screws in sizes nobody without a hardware store upbringing would buy by accident.

"Are we renovating some secret second apartment?"

"Not that I know of."

"Then why do we have three boxes of cup hooks?"

"I didn't realize there were three."

"There are definitely three."

He went over and looked like this might somehow improve the situation. It did not.

"Maybe I lost track."

"You're hoarding extremely boring things."

"That's probably a good sign, right?"

She shut the closet with her hip. "Compared to what?"

That was the kind of conversation they had now. It was not the brittle, forensic exchange pattern of October, when every wrong note had felt like evidence in a case she had already built. It was not the easy, inherited shorthand she had once had with Dominic either. What they had now was less stable and more real than both. She did not test him anymore. She did not bring up Cape Cod or Providence or old fights with his mother to see what answer came back. She talked to the person in front of her. He answered as himself as much as he could while still answering to a dead man's name.

Sometimes that meant they argued about nothing.

He left a pan soaking too long and the rice glued itself to the bottom like drywall compound. She got annoyed because she had asked him twice to stop doing that and because she had spent all day telling fifteen-year-olds to follow instructions and had no remaining patience for adults who did not. He got defensive because he was tired and because being corrected in Dominic's kitchen by Dominic's girlfriend still found ways to hit nerves he did not have good names for. They bickered for six stupid minutes over a saucepan and did not speak while eating, then she reached over after dinner, took his plate when he stood up too fast, and said, "You're washing. Since apparently we're starting over from scratch on domestic competence."

"Fair."

"I'm serious."

"I know."

He washed. She dried. That was it. There was no lesson in it and no symbolic breakthrough, just two people in a kitchen with soap drying on their hands and a local news anchor murmuring from the other room.

Other nights were softer. She graded at the table while he chopped scallions or peeled garlic, the red pen cap between her teeth when she was thinking. She read in bed with one knee up, papers spread over the blanket in dangerous stacks. Sometimes he lay beside her with his eyes closed, listening to pages turn and pretending to be asleep because it was easier than deciding whether to speak.

One night she touched his arm without looking up from the book in her lap.

"You're doing it again," she said.

"What."

"The fake sleeping. Your breathing gets weird."

He opened his eyes. "Sorry."

"Don't apologize. Just either be asleep or be awake."

"Seems a little binary."

She gave him a look over the top of the page. "You work in development. I assumed you liked categories."

He laughed before he could stop himself. It came out tired and real enough that she smiled, then went back to reading. After a minute she tucked her cold feet under his calf. He shifted to make room automatically. She did not thank him. He did not make a thing of it. Her heel rested against his leg, the familiar intimate pressure of a life shared by repetition rather than declaration, and he lay there very still, feeling both included and fraudulent in exactly the same moment.

There were whole evenings now when he forgot to compare himself to Dominic because there was nobody left to compare against. There was just him rinsing rice in Dominic's hands while Sarah graded DBQs at the table, sitting in traffic in Dominic's Civic with a box of weather stripping on the passenger seat, and reaching for her in the dark because she was there and because she reached back. The moral fact of what he had done had not softened. It had settled deeper, like something dropped into wet concrete. But daily life kept curing around it.

Sundays still belonged to Renata. She fed him until his belt complained and sent him home with foil trays of baked ziti balanced on his knees. Her knee had started bothering her in the damp weather, so she sat more often while she worked now, icing almond cookies on a cutting board in her lap and yelling directions from the chair like a foreman who refused to acknowledge injury. She had stopped staring at him for signs of wrongness. Or if she still saw them, she had accepted them as part of the son in front of her. "You're pale," she told him one Sunday, pressing a paper bag of sesame bread into his chest. "It's winter, I know, but still. Go outside." Then she patted his cheek twice and turned to ask Sarah whether the principal at school was still an idiot.

That was the pattern: borrowed life, ordinary day, borrowed life, ordinary day. January went by like that.

At work Nate stopped trying to think big. Big had been for the months when there might still have been a lever somewhere, a permit to delay, a meeting to derail, a signature to refuse before refusing it would only mean somebody else signed faster. Big had ended in December, under a bucket of concrete and the weight of his own name on paperwork.

Small was what remained.

So he listened for small things.

A contractor finishing a kitchen remodel in Fairfield needed old brass cabinet pulls to match the ones a homeowner was too cheap to replace. A framing foreman wanted joist hangers in a quantity too annoying for the big box stores to bother with. Somebody working on a church basement needed thirty feet of weather stripping and did not want to spend half a day driving around for it. When the opening came, Nate slid Mort's name in as casually as office gossip.

"Try Lazar," he said one morning, flipping through a site packet for show while Ray argued with a plumber on hold. "Guy's got old stock. Sells out of his garage now."

The contractor on the other end laughed. "A garage."

"Yeah."

"This legit?"

"Depends what you need. If you need cheap hinges and a lecture about hinges, probably."

The guy laughed again, wrote down the number, and that was that. There were no heroics in it, no forged checks, no secret fund, no grand sabotage. There was just a name passed across a desk and into the world.

He did it again three days later with a drywall sub looking for odd-sized anchors, and again the week after that with a roofer who needed one box of galvanized nails because he'd come up short on a job and was furious about it. Nate kept his voice light. He never pitched. Pitching drew attention. These were crumbs and he treated them like crumbs.

The first time Mort mentioned one of the referrals, it was almost an accident.

Nate had gone to the garage on a Thursday after work, the sky already dark by five. Snow that had fallen two days earlier still clung in gray ridges along the curb. Mort had the side door propped open with a brick. A brass service bell, salvaged from somewhere in the old store, hung crooked above the frame. It gave a small thin ring when Nate pushed through.

The garage smelled like cold metal, cardboard gone damp at the corners, and the sharp electric dust of a space heater working too hard. Shelves from the store had been reassembled along the walls, not quite level on the concrete floor. Plastic bins sat where paint cans and motor oil probably used to be. The old handwritten aisle cards were taped up above them in Linda's block letters. FASTENERS. ELECTRICAL. WEATHERPROOFING. A battery radio on the workbench murmured low enough that the voices blurred into weather and sports scores.

Mort stood at the bench in an old UConn sweatshirt under the canvas apron he had worn in the store for years. He had a yellow price gun in one hand and a box of cup hooks in the other. Before peeling off a crooked sticker, he licked his thumb. The exact same absent-minded gross habit he had done all through Nate's childhood, despite Linda saying every winter that if he would stop licking his fingers in a hardware store he might make it to spring without getting sick.

"Hey, Dom," Mort said, like he had expected him. "Come on in. You're letting the cold inside."

"Sorry."

"You need something?"

The answer was yes and no and not in any way he could say. Nate picked up a roll of plumber's tape from a bin by the door because it was there.

"Maybe this."

"Good thing to have."

Mort set down the price gun and rubbed his hands together once in front of the heater. The motion was slow, almost thoughtful. His fingers were red and rough, the skin split white at the knuckles. Nate had known those hands his whole life. He had also known the later version of them, the version that sat too long at a kitchen table wrapped around coffee that went cold. The frightening part was how little separated one from the other.

Linda came through the mudroom door carrying two mugs of coffee, although there was only one person in the garage who drank coffee at that hour. She still wore the old ring of store keys clipped to her belt loop. There was no store left for them to open, but the keys knocked lightly against her hip when she moved, a habit the body had not gotten permission to drop.

"Dom," she said. "You want one?"

"No, I'm good."

She handed the mug to Mort anyway and watched until he took it. Her face changed as soon as she thought nobody was looking at it. It was not dramatic fear and it was not tears. It was a continuous small monitoring, the expression of somebody listening through a wall for a sound she hoped not to hear.

"Had a guy in from Fairfield this morning," Mort said after a sip. "Needed joist hangers. Could've gone to Home Depot, I guess, but he said somebody told him I had better stock."

Nate kept turning the spool of tape in his hand. "Yeah?"

"Bought more than he needed, too. Which I respect." Mort smiled a little. "Couldn't back his van for shit. Took him three tries."

"That so."

"Mm-hm."

The radio gave the traffic report. Linda glanced toward it, then back at Mort.

"Tell him about the church," she said.

Mort frowned, not irritated, just searching. "What church."

"The church basement."

"Right." He nodded once, as if this had been his own thought all along. "Guy from St. Andrew's needed weather stripping. Said he'd be back Saturday if the pastor approved the color."

Linda looked at Nate then, directly, and he had the brief sick sense that she knew more than was possible. Not who he was. Nothing like that. Just that she knew he had become one of the people helping Mort keep the garage open, and that she was grateful enough not to ask how or why.

"That's good," Nate said.

"It is," Linda said. "Every little bit."

Mort took the plumber's tape from Nate, squinted at the box, and said, "Two twenty-five."

Linda said, very gently, "It's one seventy-five, Mort."

"One seventy-five," he repeated at once. "Sorry."

"No problem," Nate said too fast.

Mort reached for the cash box, then paused with his hand hovering over it like he had forgotten what came next. It lasted only a second, less than a second. Then he opened it, made change, and nodded.

"Anyway," he said, sliding the quarters across, "you need anything else for the house, just say it. I've got some finish nails on sale."

The evasion was so transparent Nate almost loved him for it. Mort always went practical the second anything in a room threatened to become emotional. He had done it when Nate was eight and split his lip on the driveway. He had done it when Linda's sister got sick. He had probably done it the week before he died, and fourteen-year-old Nate had mistaken it for steadiness.

"Not today," Nate said. "Maybe next time."

"Next time, then."

That became the rhythm.

It was weekly at first, then sometimes twice in a week if Nate could justify it to himself. He stopped on the way home from work or on Saturdays after Gail's. He bought things he did not need because buying nothing would have been too naked a form of pity and because Mort would not have tolerated pity anyway. He bought a pack of utility blades, picture hangers, a brass doorstop, a bundle of shims, and two switch plates the apartment had no use for. The closet filled up. A drawer in the kitchen filled up after the closet did. Sarah found a bag of masonry anchors under the bathroom sink and held them up in disbelief.

"Why are these in here?"

"Storage overflow."

"This isn't storage. This is where we keep toilet paper."

"Now it's both."

She shook her head, but she was smiling when she put the bag on the counter. "You know you can tell me what this is."

He was rinsing lettuce. Water hit the steel sink in a flat silver spray.

"I know."

"Okay."

She did not push. That was part of what had changed between them too. She could still press like a prosecutor when she wanted. He had seen that and earned it. But she had started making different decisions about what to leave alone, not because she trusted him completely, which would have been impossible and stupid, but because trust was not the only thing people built a life out of. Sometimes they built it out of repeated noninterference. Sometimes out of showing up to dinner. Sometimes out of saying I know and meaning both I know you are not telling me everything and I know you are trying, in whatever damaged way you have available.

Later that night, after they ate, he installed one of the switch plates just to justify its existence. The old one in the hall had a hairline crack. Sarah stood in the doorway with her arms folded, watching him work.

"You realize replacing one object so you can defend buying six of them is not normal behavior."

"I'm not defending it. I'm demonstrating value."

"That's worse."

"Probably."

She was quiet a second. Then she said, "It looks better."

"Yeah."

"You missed a spot with the screwdriver."

"I know."

"Do you."

"Now I do."

She came over, put two fingers against his wrist to move him aside, and straightened the plate herself by less than a degree. It was teacher precision in domestic form. When she stepped back her shoulder brushed his chest. She stayed there a second longer than necessary, not leaning, not not leaning. He could feel the warmth of her through his shirt and also the whole brutal architecture underneath it: Dominic's body, Dominic's apartment, Dominic's life, this woman who had not agreed to any of it and was still here.

He put his hand lightly at the back of her neck. She let him. That was all.

By late January the garage had taken on its own weather. The space heater glowed orange in the corner. The concrete floor sweated when the temperature swung. Mort sorted and resorted screws into clear bins as if order itself might be saleable. He got quieter, though not all at once. That was what made it hard to point to and impossible to ignore.

He still knew the names of things. If anything he clung harder to them: ring shank, toggle bolt, finish washer, left-hand thread. He still lit up, briefly, when somebody asked for something obscure enough to prove the world had not gone entirely to prepackaged bullshit. But the stretches between those moments lengthened. He sat more often. He stared at the radio without hearing it. Sometimes Nate would ask a question and Mort would answer after a beat too long, as if he had been somewhere else and had to walk back.

In the original timeline, Nate had not understood any of this while it was happening. He had been fourteen and angry in the selfish, blameless way fourteen-year-olds were angry, furious that the garage smelled like cold steel and damp cardboard, furious that his father kept saying maybe next week and later and we'll figure it out. March had arrived in memory like a jump cut. Adult Nate knew better. Nothing jumped. A man lost footing by inches.

One Saturday near the start of February, Nate found Mort on a stool behind the workbench with the radio turned down almost to silence. Snowmelt dripped steadily from the eaves outside. Mort was looking at a ledger pad without writing in it.

"Slow?" Nate asked.

Mort looked up and took a second to place the room around himself. Then he gave a small shrug.

"People don't build much in February."

"Some do."

"Some do."

Nate picked up a box of sandpaper because it was nearest.

"How's business otherwise?"

Mort rubbed his thumb along the edge of the ledger paper. "All right."

The word was flat enough to make Linda appear in the mudroom doorway before Nate heard her coming. She had flour on one sleeve and a pencil tucked behind one ear. She must have been doing the books at the kitchen table again, the house and the garage folded together into one anxious little business.

"The cabinet guy from Milford called," she said to Mort, too casually. "Said he might need more pulls next week."

"Did he."

"He did."

Mort nodded and kept his eyes on the pad.

Linda looked at Nate. "You hear of anybody needing odd stock, you tell them he's here."

"I will."

"Anything. Hardware, trim, old fixtures, weird sizes. People don't know where to go for weird sizes anymore."

"I know."

She pressed her lips together like she had more to say and no place to put it. Then she went back into the house.

Mort cleared his throat. "She's worried people forget about us if they can't see the storefront."

Nate set the sandpaper down, then picked it up again because setting it down had felt too much like giving up the script.

"I don't think they will."

"Maybe." Mort finally looked at him. His eyes were tired but direct. "Funny thing is, I don't miss the rent. I don't miss the parking lot. I don't miss sweeping the damn sidewalk every time it snowed because the landlord suddenly couldn't find his shovel. It's the store I miss."

He said it simply. There was no performance in it and no prepared sadness, just a fact compact and dense as a nail head.

"Yeah," Nate said.

Mort looked back at the ledger. "Anyway. You need anything for the apartment, I got doorstops in brass and rubber both."

There it was again, the veer into hardware the instant he had gone too close to himself.

"Brass," Nate said.

"Brass is better."

"I figured."

Mort stood, slower than he should have needed to, and went to the shelf. As he moved, Nate could hear Linda in the kitchen through the half-open door, running water, setting down a mug, the tiny domestic noises of somebody staying within earshot on purpose.

Nate started taking longer lunches after that. He did not take them long enough for anybody to comment, and he did not take them often enough to create a pattern Victor would notice. He took them just long enough that once or twice a week he could drive over, ring the bell, buy one stupid thing, stand in the heated corner of the garage and talk about weather or the Red Sox or whether people still bought actual storm windows anymore. Sometimes there were customers. Sometimes there were not. Sometimes Linda brought coffee and did not sit down. Sometimes Mort talked for five minutes about the difference between finish nails and common nails, the heads, the way they vanished under trim, the reasons one was for force and the other for not being seen. Those were the best days. Those were the days Nate could almost believe the campaign was working, if campaign was not too grand and stupid a word for a man buying unneeded hardware and handing out a phone number when he got the chance.

On the worse days, Mort lost track of a sentence halfway through it and started again from a point farther back. Or he misheard a question and answered another one. Or he went still while Nate was talking, his eyes on the heater, and came back with a tiny embarrassed shake of the head like he had nodded off in church.

Nate never said what he was thinking. He never said he knew where this went. He never said he'd already lived through the version where Mort did not make it to spring. He stood there with Dominic's face and Dominic's wallet and Dominic's useless purchased items, and he asked about hinges.

That was the limit of justice. That was what he had.

By the first week of February there were actual walls starting to rise at the old lot. Stud framing was up. Plywood sheathing followed. A shape was coming up where absence had been, as if replacement were the same thing as repair. Nate drove past one morning on the way to the office and had the ugly thought that the new building was being erected on top of a deleted file, not a demolished place, with all the metadata gone and only the footprint left.

That night Sarah found him at the bedroom window with the blinds open and his tie loosened, looking at nothing visible from the apartment.

"Long day?" she asked.

"Kind of."

She came up beside him and folded her arms against the cold glass for a second before stepping back. "I had two kids call the New Deal communist propaganda today."

"In history class?"

"Where else."

"Maybe gym."

She made a tired sound that was almost a laugh. Then she said, "You want dinner now or in twenty minutes?"

"Whatever's easier."

"That wasn't one of the options."

He turned to look at her. Her hair was still tied back from school. There was a faint smear of blue marker near the cuff of her sweater. She looked like herself in the exact unglamorous way people only ever did in their own homes. He felt, suddenly and with no usefulness at all, how much had been stolen from her as well.

"Now," he said.

"Okay."

She took a step toward the kitchen, then stopped and came back. "You can tell me if something's wrong."

The answer assembled itself in him, huge and unusable. Instead he said, "I know."

She nodded once. "All right."

Then she went to start the rice, and he followed because that was what following looked like now. There was no revelation and no confession in it, just a man moving into the kitchen behind the woman who had given him another ordinary night.

By then Mort had started setting aside the things Nate tended to buy: a pack of utility blades on the counter, a box of picture hangers near the register, finish nails because they moved slowly and because Nate never had a good reason not to take them.

He kept coming.

On the first Saturday he went in February, the sky had that flat white look that made every house on the block seem temporary. Nate parked at the curb outside Mort's place and stayed in the car with the engine off, the heat fading from the vents in stages. The paper bag on the passenger seat held the things he had written down at breakfast so he would not arrive empty-handed in his intention: finish nails, sanding sponge, maybe weather stripping if there was any left.

He left the bag where it was and looked at his hands on the steering wheel.

They were still Dominic's hands, broad and capable-looking, with the scar across the left knuckle white against winter skin. The calluses had softened in the months Nate had been wearing them because office work, the steering wheel, grocery bags, and Sarah's back under his palm at night had not asked the same things of them. There was still enough roughness left to remind him the body had belonged to a man who did more with it than type and lie, but the edges were rounding off. Time had been sanding Dominic down into him.

These hands had signed the demolition paperwork. These hands had handed Tommy three thousand dollars they did not have. These hands had taken bowls from Sarah after dinner and straightened switch plates and passed business cards across desks and shaken Mort Lazar's hand like a stranger's. Nate turned them over, looked at the creases in the palms, the half-moon dirt stain that never quite left the edge of one thumbnail, and had the brief stupid wish that bodies kept audit logs.

They did not. They just kept moving.

He got out of the car.

The side path to the garage had been salted badly, more in clumps than spread. His shoes crunched over old ice. Someone in the neighborhood was running a snowblower even though there was barely any snow left to move. He could hear the radio before he reached the door. It was not music this time. It was a man on AM sports talk arguing about the Yankees bullpen like it mattered enough to get angry over.

The brass bell above the side door chimed when he pushed it open.

The heater was already going. It made the corner by the workbench feel almost warm while the rest of the garage stayed a few degrees above outside. The bins were more organized than the last time he had come. That was Linda's doing, probably. Masking tape labels replaced some of the old cardboard ones. A stack of flattened boxes leaned against the freezer. The old Lazar's Hardware sign was propped face-in to the wall now, like nobody wanted its lettering looking around the room.

Mort was at the bench sorting fasteners into the clear bins, his reading glasses low on his nose, a coffee mug within reach and cooling fast. He moved with care rather than speed, pinching screws between thumb and forefinger, letting them click into plastic one by one. The radio host took a commercial break. In the brief static gap the bell's last thin ring seemed to hang in the air longer than it should have.

Mort looked up.

"Hey, Dom," he said. "What do you need today?"

Nate reached for the box nearest his hand, because that was how this worked, because this was what showing up meant, because there was nothing else to do that was not either impossible or cruel. The box was finish nails, one and a quarter inch, the cardboard soft at one corner from damp air. He turned it over in his palm, feeling the slight dry rattle inside.

"Just these," he said.
