# Chapter 13

At Gail's, Nate had learned to keep his eyes on his coffee until Mort's voice passed behind him and went away again. If Mort happened to be out front sweeping leaves off the sidewalk, Nate could wave without slowing down. He could listen to Gail complain about the city putting up another temporary sign for the future development and never once look toward the end unit. Avoiding Lazar's Hardware had turned into something with the complexity of a bad software workaround. There was a whole stable operating system for it now: perimeter only, no entry, no touching anything he remembered.

It failed on a Saturday because there were three dead tulip bulbs on the windowsill over the kitchen sink and because Sarah had said, without looking up from the stack of papers she was grading, "If you're going out, can you pick up a level? The shelf in the bedroom is still crooked."

She had said it in the same tone she might have used to ask for milk. Nothing had been resolved after the fight two weeks earlier. She had moved back into the bed three nights ago, but not all the way back into him. They talked. They ate dinner. She handed him things and asked for things and sometimes her face softened when she forgot to be wary, but the apartment still had the feeling of a room after somebody threw a glass in it. The shards were gone, but the room still felt braced. He had said sure because it was a shelf and a level and a thing a boyfriend could buy on a Saturday without screwing it up.

Then he stood in the strip mall parking lot with the engine off and realized where he had come.

The morning had the thin, metallic cold that made every sound carry. Wind pushed old leaves against the Civic's tires in dry little bursts. Eastbridge Plaza looked bleaker than it had in September. The trees behind it had given up and the light had gone flat. Gail's windows steamed from the grill. Mrs. Park's dry cleaner sign buzzed faintly. Outside the barbershop, the red-white-blue pole turned with a patient uselessness. At the end, Lazar's Hardware sat under the hand-painted sign with the faded L, exactly where it had always been in his head and somehow smaller now that he had Dominic's adult body around him instead of the body of a kid who had once believed the place was infinite.

Nothing trapped him there yet. The engine was off, not dead. Two towns over, a Home Depot would sell him the same level under bad fluorescent light and with nobody behind the counter he had ever loved. Sarah wouldn't ask where it came from. Mort would stay safely on the other side of the glass. That was the smart version.

He got out of the car.

The bell over the door still gave the same tired double-jingle. It hit him in the center of the chest so hard he almost looked around to see if anybody else had noticed.

The store smelled like cut wood and cold metal and WD-40 and the rubber mats by the door, all of it mixed into one smell that did not exist anywhere else in the world. Nate had spent years remembering it wrong in broad strokes, hardware store, dust, oil, but memory had compressed it. The real thing was more specific and meaner. The sawdust was sweet at the edge. The machine oil was sharp enough to sting the back of his nose. Damp wool from somebody's coat hung near the key-cutting machine. There were narrow aisles and pegboard walls and yellow shelf tags with Mort's block handwriting on some of them where the original labels had gone missing years ago. There was a bin of loose hinges, paintbrushes in a dented wire barrel, and a display of furnace filters with one package sun-faded from sitting in the window too long.

He had the dizzy, stupid thought that he could map the store with his eyes closed. Three steps to the left for picture hangers. Six down the center aisle for electrical tape and extension cords. The little glass case by the register with the expensive drill bits and the lock cylinders. He kept his face arranged into what he hoped passed for a man seeing a normal store for the first time.

Mort was behind the counter in a canvas apron over a gray flannel shirt, reading glasses on the end of a chain, a yellow legal pad laid flat beside the register. He was writing down something from a clipboard and licking the side of his thumb every few lines so he could separate the paper, a habit Nate had forgotten until right then. His hair was thinner than it would be in Nate's strongest memories of him because the strongest memories of a parent always seemed to pick a year and refuse to update. The hands were the same, wide-palmed, knuckles thickened by work, with a pale scar along one finger where a utility knife had slipped when Nate was maybe ten, maybe eleven, the year the back fence got rebuilt.

Mort looked up. "Morning."

"Morning." Nate's voice came out flatter than he wanted. He added, a beat late, "How you doing?"

"Doing all right." Mort nodded toward the aisle behind Nate. "Let me know if you need a hand with anything."

"Yeah. Thanks."

From the back room came the sound of cardboard rasping over concrete, then a woman's voice saying, "Mort, did the brass cup hooks come in or no?"

"Top of the box on the dolly, I think."

Linda said something Nate could not catch, then laughed once at herself. He looked without meaning to. She was half visible through the open door to the back, smaller than he remembered, hair cut short and pushed behind one ear with a pencil stuck through it, a box cutter clipped to the waistband of her jeans. She had a peppermint in one cheek, enough to round it slightly as she talked to herself and worked. She flattened empty cartons with one hard push of her heel before stacking them. Nate had no memory of that habit, which made it somehow worse. It was proof she had existed in all the minutes he had not been there for.

He moved deeper into the store because standing still at the threshold was turning into its own kind of performance. The levels were where they had always been, hanging in descending sizes near the hand tools. Yellow aluminum frames, red plastic end caps, little green tubes inset with bubbles that looked toy-like until you actually needed one. He reached for a twenty-four-inch level and his hand stopped an inch away because eight-year-old Nate had once been so small he needed both hands to hold one of these flat.

That memory had no warning on it. Mort in the basement of their house on Porter Street before the mold in the corner got bad, showing him how to mark a line for shelves that were not going to hold anything heavier than paint cans and old textbooks. Mort kneeling behind him, one hand over each of Nate's wrists, just steadying. "Wait for the bubble," he'd said. "Not almost. Center." The bubble had swum and hesitated and settled between the lines like it wanted to please him. Nate had been proud in the complete, uncomplicated way children sometimes were, because a man they loved had shown them how the world could line up if you held the tool still long enough.

He picked up the level. The metal was colder than he expected. The bubble trembled once as he raised it.

"That's a good size for shelves."

Mort had come around the counter without Nate noticing. Up close he smelled faintly of coffee and sawdust. He took the other end of the level automatically, not snatching, just steadying it while he turned it so Nate could see the vials.

"Unless you're doing trim work," Mort said. "Then you want longer if you can manage it. But for a shelf, picture frame, cabinets, this is fine. Better than the little torpedo ones. Those things are handy but they make people overconfident."

Nate made himself smile. "Yeah?"

"Yeah." Mort glanced at him, mild, practical. "They fit in your pocket, so people figure, close enough. But close enough isn't level. You check a shelf with one of those, it looks fine, and then you put something heavy on it and the whole thing drifts."

Nate laughed a little too fast, then had to dial himself back.

"I need it for a shelf," he said. "In the bedroom. It's been off for, I don't know, a while."

"Anchored into studs?"

"I think so."

"You think so."

"I mean, probably. I didn't put it up."

"All right." Mort took the level back from him long enough to check the edge against the shelf behind them, his thumb rubbing an imaginary burr. "If it's wall anchors and not studs, level might not be the problem. Weight could be pulling it down. What are you putting on it?"

"Books. Some books."

"That'll do it. How long's the shelf?"

Nate held out his hands without thinking and then stopped because Dominic's hands were not his hands and the span meant nothing. "Maybe three feet?"

"Maybe." Mort smiled, not mocking him. "You want to bring the hardware in if it gives you trouble. No charge, I'll tell you if it's wrong."

"Thanks."

"You need brackets too?"

"No, I think I'm good."

"All right."

Mort did not move away immediately. He looked pleased to be in the aisle with a customer asking about an ordinary job. Nate had forgotten that too, or maybe he had never understood it properly as a kid. Mort liked it when the conversation got specific. Selling something barely entered into it. If you named the problem clearly enough, there was usually a right answer somewhere.

Nate's eyes slid past him to the back wall and stopped at the fasteners.

They were still there. Rows and rows of shallow drawers, each with a white label and a part number, machine screws, lag bolts, carriage bolts, washers, cotter pins, every small metal fact of the world sorted by thread and length and head type. A hardware store could fake a lot of things with a few seasonal displays and the right smell, but not that wall. That wall was where Mort's actual religion lived.

He went to it before he could decide whether that was wise. Mort followed, hands tucked into the apron pockets.

"Jesus," Nate said softly, then corrected himself. "Sorry. That's a lot."

Mort's face came awake in a way Nate knew as well as his own name. "Yeah, well. This is the part I like."

He pulled one drawer open and tipped it slightly so Nate could see. Tiny brass set screws, bright as new coins against the yellowing label. He pushed it shut with the side of his knuckle and opened another with short black machine bolts lined up inside.

"You go to the big places now, they carry what moves. Drywall, deck screws, contractor boxes, all that. Fine. But if your storm window spits out one stupid set screw on a Saturday, they're not helping you."

"Throw out the window, probably."

Mort made a face. "Exactly. Whole country's set up that way now."

He touched one of the handwritten labels with a blunt fingertip. "I keep the fussy stuff. Weird thread pitches, odd little brass pieces, the parts people bring in loose in their pocket because one thing went missing and now the whole job's dead in the water. If I can get somebody out of here for sixty-eight cents and save them buying a whole new fixture, that's worth the drawer space."

Nate stared at the tiny drawers because if he looked at Mort's face too directly he was going to miss whatever expression a stranger was supposed to have. He remembered kneeling on a milk crate behind the counter, sorting washers into coffee cans because Mort said it would keep him busy for half an hour. He remembered the dry little ring of screws hitting metal. He remembered being told not to mix Phillips and slotted because then a job went bad before it started. His hands around the level were slick now, as if the store had found a way under his skin and was oiling the joints.

"You built all this?" he asked.

"Over time." Mort shrugged, but the shrug did not hide his pride very well. "Started small. Then people ask for one thing, then another. You keep notes. You order a little more next time. Sixteen years later, here you are."

"Sixteen."

"Nineteen eighty-nine." He nodded once. "This unit was empty. We had enough for first month's rent, security, and not much else. Linda's brother helped me build the shelving. Ugly work, but it held. We upgraded piece by piece."

He said it the way a person might talk about replacing the roof or redoing a porch. He did not say it like an origin story, and he was not asking to be admired. Nate could see the whole accumulation behind the words anyway. He could see pallets split open in the back, Saturdays at the register, winter mornings getting the lock to turn, and his mother doing books at the kitchen table. Sixteen years translated into pegboard, shelf brackets, bins of nails, and the fluorescent hum over their heads.

"That's a long time," Nate said.

"It is."

Linda came out of the back carrying a carton against her hip. She gave Nate a quick appraising glance that was somehow warmer and less forgiving than Mort's whole face. "You finding everything?"

"Yeah," Nate said. "He helped me."

"Good." She set the carton on the counter and looked at the level. "You got the decent one, not the junk one."

"There's a junk one?" Nate asked.

"There's always a junk one," she said, and went back through the door before he could answer.

Mort watched her go with an expression Nate recognized too, the tiny private smile of a man who had heard the same type of sentence for thirty years and still liked the person saying it. Then he nodded toward the counter.

"You want me to ring that up for you?"

"Yeah. Sure."

He followed Mort back to the register carrying the level with both hands because that was how he'd carried one when he was eight and because apparently the body stored humiliations and tenderness in the same place.

At the counter Mort wrote something on the yellow pad before he touched the register. He was doing inventory, just counts. Nate could see columns of numbers and abbreviations in the narrow, careful handwriting that once filled every take-home note stuck to the fridge with a magnet advertising a roofing company that no longer existed. Mort set the pencil down, then turned the level over again and scanned the sticker.

"You local?" he asked.

It was casual. The floor of the conversation opened under him like a trapdoor.

"Sort of," Nate said. "I work nearby."

"Nearby nearby, or everybody says nearby and means Fairfield."

Nate let out a small breath through his nose. "Bridgeport nearby."

"All right, then. You're one of ours."

That should not have hurt as much as it did.

Mort named the price. Nate pulled cash from Dominic's wallet because using a card felt insane, like showing up in a ski mask. Mort counted out his change into Nate's palm with the same neat pressure he would have used on anybody. The coins were cold. A nickel slipped against the base of Dominic's thumb and for one second Nate had a visual overlay so clean it made him dizzy: his father's hand now, thickened and rough, and his father's hand fifteen years from now shaking loose change into a dish on the kitchen counter while bills piled up under a magnet. The future sat inside the present like a bad layer in Photoshop, impossible to unsee once it loaded.

He closed his hand around the change before he dropped it.

"You need a bag?"

"No, I'm okay."

"All right."

Mort slid the receipt across, then looked past Nate toward the front windows. The light there had gone whiter. Cars moved through the lot in fits, customers at the diner mostly, a woman coming out of the nail salon in a quilted vest with her keys already in her hand. It was just enough of a pause that the store sounds rose around them: fluorescent hum, cardboard shifting in the back room, the bell faintly ticking against the door in the draft.

Nate should have left. The transaction was over. The level was in his hand. A sane version of the morning had him out the door, back in the apartment, shaking quietly while Sarah fitted books onto the shelf and said, maybe, Thanks.

Instead he heard himself ask, "How's business?"

Mort looked back at him. "Business is business."

That was a stock answer, the kind a man gave when he meant to keep the rest for himself. Nate nodded like that settled it. Mort tucked the receipt book straighter against the register and reached for the pencil again.

Then, maybe because they had already talked about shelves and fasteners and local geography, maybe because Nate had paid cash and asked a real question and did not feel like a pure stranger anymore, Mort said, "We're hanging in."

He said it to the legal pad, not to Nate.

* * *

The first thing Nate noticed after the break in the conversation was that two shelves near the window were thinner than they should have been.

They were not bare. Lazar's Hardware would never be bare while Mort had a pulse. But there were gaps where there had not been gaps before, little openings between boxes of weatherstripping and faucet washers, more broom handles leaned together in one bucket than on hooks, a cardboard carton under a display rack with PACK UP written on the side in Linda's block capitals. Once he saw it he saw the rest: a stack of flattened boxes by the back door and blue painter's tape flags stuck to three shelving uprights, maybe inventory marks, maybe something else. The store had the faintly provisional look of a house where somebody had started sorting the closets before saying out loud that they were moving.

Mort followed his glance without embarrassment. "Getting a jump on some things."

"For Christmas?"

Mort gave him a tired, honest look. "No."

Nate set the level on the counter because holding it had started to feel too revealing, like hugging somebody in public. "Right."

That was where a man in his position was supposed to nod and let it die. Dom from the diner, the guy from nearby, would take the hint and leave it there. But he had been listening to Mort talk about inventory with that quiet life in his face and now the store itself looked like it had heard bad medical news and was trying not to make a scene.

"Gail mentioned something," he said. "About redevelopment."

Mort's hand stayed on the pencil. The chain on his glasses clicked once against the zipper of his apron when he shifted. He did not get angry. That would have been easier to stand there and receive. Anger would have had heat. This was flatter than that.

"Yeah," he said. "That."

Linda came through the back room door carrying a sleeve of receipt paper. She heard enough of the tone to stop. "Mort."

"It's fine."

She looked at Nate, then at the level on the counter, then back at Mort. "I know it's fine. I'm saying don't stand there on that hip."

Mort moved his weight obediently, and Linda, satisfied, put the receipt paper under the register, patted once at the pencil behind her ear as if checking it was still there, and disappeared again. Nate watched the doorway a second too long. She had not stayed to see if he listened. Mort was already off that hip before the back-room door finished swinging.

Mort rubbed one thumb over the edge of the legal pad. "We got the letter in June. Official notice. Thirty days and all that. Which was funny, because thirty days to do what exactly, I couldn't tell you."

His voice stayed even. The store held the words without helping him with them.

"You got to fight it?" Nate asked.

"Tried." Mort lifted one shoulder. "Linda talked to a guy from church. Not a real estate lawyer, just a guy who knows paperwork and had opinions. We went to one council meeting. Sat there two hours while they talked about tax base and revitalization and how this corridor is underutilized. Underutilized." He gave a small, dry laugh that had no humor in it. "There are six businesses here and every one of us works six days a week."

Nate looked at the level. The bubble sat between the lines as if it had all the answers in the world.

"What are they putting in?"

"Mixed-use, supposedly." Mort said the word like he was quoting instructions for a machine nobody wanted. "Retail on the bottom, apartments above. Better parking. Better visibility. Better this, better that. They got renderings with trees in front and a couple pushing a stroller where Al's barber pole is now."

The line should have been funny. Nate felt the pressure of it and let it pass. Mort sounded like a man reporting the weather.

"That sucks," Nate said, which was a thin little phrase for the job but also the only one that came out honest.

"Yeah." Mort nodded. "It does."

He turned the pencil in his fingers once. The eraser was bitten flat. Nate had not noticed that as a child because children do not clock the exact damage adults do to office supplies while trying to stay calm.

"You know what gets me?" Mort said.

Nate looked up.

"Not even the money. I'll figure out the money. I don't know how yet, but you do what you do. It'll be ugly for a while. Maybe longer than a while. Fine." He gestured at the walls with the pencil, at the bins, the shelves, the years compacted into merchandise. "It's that nobody asked us. That's the piece of it. Sixteen years, and they mail a letter. They don't come stand in the store and say, hey, this is what we're doing. They don't look you in the face. Just paper."

Nate had read the letter. He had held it at Dominic's desk under fluorescent lights, Morton's name typed cleanly across the top, Dominic Altieri curled at the bottom in the signature block with the careful courtesy of a man who had argued and lost. He knew what paper felt like when it was all the system could spare.

The broad hand on the counter now, Mort's, not Dominic's, flexed once against the Formica. Nate saw the nails, the black half-moons of old grease that never completely came out, the callus at the base of the thumb from years of grip. Those hands had opened every drawer in the store a thousand times. They had taught him how to seat a screw, how to pull a tape measure straight without kinking it, how to tap a nail first with your wrist loose and then drive it. In the original version of his life, those hands would hold the notice, hold the empty time after the store was gone, hold nothing long enough that one March afternoon they would not hold on anymore.

Nate said, because he could not not say something, "Did they give you any options?"

"Not real ones."

"Relocation assistance, or..."

"Some stipend, maybe." Mort's mouth twitched. "You know how those letters are. Maybe this if you sign by this date, maybe that if you provide this, maybe a reimbursement after expenses if a committee approves whatever. I run a hardware store. I don't have time to become a specialist in how somebody else is putting me out of business."

Nate nodded like a customer, like a man hearing about municipal nonsense over a counter on a Saturday morning. Inside him, everything had gone still in the particular way it did when pressure got too close to the seam. His body stopped being body and became position data. Where his elbows were. Where Dominic's shoes were on the rubber mat. How far the back room door stood open. The click of Linda's box cutter as she retracted it somewhere out of sight.

Mort looked down at the level again. "Sorry. I don't usually unload on people buying hand tools."

"No, it's okay."

"You ask somebody how business is, I guess that's the answer lately."

"Yeah."

There was room there for him to say something normal. That sucks, again. Maybe it'll work out. Bureaucratic sympathy from a stranger. His fingers tightened on the receipt. That was the place to nod, say something useless, and go. He almost did. Then Mort, maybe feeling the need to make the conversation useful again, pointed toward the back wall.

"If that shelf gives you trouble, bring me whatever anchors are in it. If they used the cheap plastic ones, throw them out. Half the products in this country are built on the assumption that nobody will come back and complain."

That did it.

The offer was so automatic, so ordinary, so completely his father that Nate felt something in him move before language caught up. Mort was losing the store. Mort had been served the letter Nate had spent nineteen years building a mythology around. Mort was still, right now, offering free advice about drywall anchors to the man wearing the face attached to the signature on the paperwork that would kill him. Nate saw the old milk crate behind the register, coffee cans full of washers, Mort sliding a bent screw into his palm and telling him to start with the part that was wrong. Somebody came in with a crooked shelf and you tried to fix the shelf first.

Nate heard himself say, "I work in real estate. I could look into it."

The sentence crossed the space between them before he had fully assembled what it meant. It arrived with the sick instant clarity of something you could never call back once it left your mouth.

Mort blinked. "You do."

"Yeah." Nate could feel the lie trying to branch, put out roots, become biography. He kept it short on instinct. "Development side, mostly. Not, you know, municipal stuff. But I could maybe see if there's anything in the process, or if somebody's jerking you around on the relocation part, or..." He stopped because the sentence was building toward details he did not actually control. "I could look into it."

Linda had come back into the doorway again. Nate did not know how long she had been standing there.

"Mort," she said, careful now.

Mort did not take his eyes off Nate. He was not dumb. That was part of the problem. He was a man who lived among exact objects. He knew when something fit and when it only looked like it fit from a distance.

"That's kind of you," he said finally.

Nate made himself hold the eye contact. "It's nothing. I mean, I can ask around. See what I find."

"Why would you do that?"

There it was. The kind of practical question Mort asked when something didn't quite fit.

Because I'm your son. Because I killed the wrong man. Because the right man sits in an office ten minutes from here and calls me Dom. Because your life got flattened by a machine and I crawled inside one of its parts.

What he said was, "I don't know. You seem like good people."

It sounded thin to his own ears. Mort kept looking at him anyway, measuring.

Linda spoke from the doorway. "Gail said he works nearby. Comes in for coffee."

"I know who he is," Mort said, still looking at Nate.

Linda crossed her arms. "I'm just saying."

Then, because she was Linda and apparently incapable of letting any room hold only its heaviest thing for long, she added, "And he bought the decent level."

Mort's mouth moved, not quite a smile. He let a breath out through his nose and looked down at the counter.

"Well," he said. "I'd appreciate that."

It was worse, somehow, that he said it carefully. He set the sentence down between them the way he might set down a glass item that mattered. Nate had the insane urge to tell him not to appreciate it yet, not to put any weight on it, the warranty on this promise is void, sir, product may fail under normal use. Instead he nodded once because his throat was full of the store smell and the WD-40 and the dust and his own stupidity.

"Okay," he said. "Yeah. I'll..."

He almost said I'll call. He had no number that made sense. He switched directions hard enough that the sentence barely held together.

"I'll stop by," he said.

"All right."

Another pause. Linda uncrossed her arms and came to the counter, setting a roll of register paper beside the till. Up close Nate could see there was blue ink on the side of her hand from some leaking pen and a small burn scar by her wrist he had never seen before.

"Shelf in the bedroom, huh?" she said.

It took him half a second to remember his own cover story. "Yeah."

"Books?"

"Books."

"Then bring the bracket in if it keeps sagging." She gave him a narrow, practical look. "People blame the wall when it's usually the bracket."

"Okay."

"And don't put all the big hardcovers on one side. That's asking for trouble."

"All right."

She nodded as if he had just received adequate instruction in a matter that could now be closed. Mort picked the level up and handed it to him again, and the transfer of weight nearly undid him. The tool barely weighed anything, but Mort gave it over with the absent trust of a shopkeeper returning a purchase, and Nate's hands closed around it with the knowledge that this object was going to sit in Dominic's apartment tonight, maybe on the kitchen table while Sarah cleared space to fix the shelf, and every time he saw it after that he would know exactly where it had come from and whose fingers had last touched the frame besides his own.

"Thanks," he said.

"Sure." Mort nodded toward the door. "Stay warm. Wind's nasty today."

Nate turned and walked out before he said anything else catastrophic.

The bell gave the same tired jingle over his head. The air outside felt cleaner and less breathable. He got three steps into the parking lot before he stopped because he was suddenly aware of the level in his hand and the promise attached to it and the fact that they had about the same practical value.

Through the front window he could still see Mort moving behind the counter, head bent over the yellow pad, already back in counts and columns. Linda passed behind him carrying another box and said something Nate could not hear. From outside they looked ordinary, which was worse. Mort Lazar now knew a man named Dom who bought a level, drank coffee at Gail's, and said he'd ask around. Linda had heard it too.

He walked to the Civic and laid the tool carefully across the passenger seat. The vinyl had gone cold. When he shut the door, the level rolled an inch and tapped against the road atlas still lying there from summer, old paper and bright aluminum knocking once like a reminder he did not need.

He got behind the wheel but did not start the car.

I work in real estate. I could look into it.

The words replayed with the dead, unstoppable quality of something already committed to a database. Monday was no longer optional. He was going to open the Eastbridge file again because Mort was waiting, because Linda was waiting, because now there was a version of this story in somebody else's head where help might still arrive.

He pictured Victor's voice, patient, well-fed, talking capital and timelines. He pictured trying to pry something useful out of a machine designed to turn useful people into paperwork. He pictured himself back in the store saying, There's nothing, I checked, and seeing exactly what that made him.

A woman in a quilted vest crossed in front of the Civic carrying a takeout bag from Gail's. She glanced at him, saw nothing worth noting, and kept moving.

He started the engine.

On the drive back to the apartment, the level clicked softly every time he braked. At a red light he reached over and set a hand on it to stop the noise. The aluminum warmed under his palm. He kept it there for the rest of the trip.
