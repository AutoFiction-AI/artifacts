# Chapter 3

By Wednesday morning, Nate was calling Kassos Development from the kitchen phone Dominic and Sarah kept bolted to the wall beside the refrigerator. Sarah was in the bathroom with the hair dryer running, which felt like cheating. He could hear her cabinet door open and shut, the little clink of bottles, the ordinary apartment noises of a life that had started before him and kept going after the murder. He pressed the receiver harder to his ear and listened to the ring.

Voicemail.

Good. Better than a person.

"Hey, it's Dom." Even now the voice startled him a little, not because it sounded wrong but because it sounded right. It came out of Dominic's throat with no effort at all, warm and rough in the morning, the voice of a man people probably believed the first time. "I'm still not feeling great. I think it was something I ate. I'll try for tomorrow."

He hung up before he could say anything else stupid. He stood there with the receiver in his hand until the dial tone turned mean.

Sarah came out of the bathroom tying her dark hair into a ponytail, one red pen clenched between her teeth while she tugged the elastic tight. She wore a short-sleeved blouse for summer school and carried a stack of photocopied packets against her hip with the resigned care of someone transporting something already doomed to become more work. When she saw him still holding the phone, she took the pen out of her mouth and said, "You call in?"

"Yeah."

"You still look like shit."

"Thanks."

"That wasn't an insult." She crossed to the counter, set the packets down, and checked the stove knobs with her fingertips, one by one. She had done the same thing yesterday before leaving, and the day before that, apparently because apartment fires and idiots were both real. "If you're sick, stay sick at home. Don't be noble about it."

He nodded.

She pulled a square of toast wrapped in foil from beside the breadbox and slid it toward him. "Try that in an hour or something. If it comes back up, that's between you and God."

There it was again, that dry tone that never asked permission to be funny. Nate had no practiced Dominic response for it, so he said the safest thing.

"Okay."

Sarah narrowed her eyes for half a second, not suspicious exactly, just taking inventory. Then she grabbed her tote bag and keys. "You need anything?"

No exit strategy, no original body, no explanation for the fact that he had killed her boyfriend and was now eating her toast.

"I'm good."

"Right." She leaned in and kissed his cheek, quick and absentminded and intimate enough to make his skin go cold after she stepped back. "Drink water. Not coffee. Actual water."

After she left, the apartment settled into its daytime sounds: the refrigerator hum, a muffled television from another unit, a dog somewhere down the hall barking in offended bursts. Nate put his palm over the spot on his cheek where she'd kissed him and then took it away, because what the hell was he supposed to do with that.

He wasn't sick. He had used sickness because it was available, because the body had looked pale enough in the mirror and because "I recently erased a man from existence and now I live in his apartment" did not fit into office etiquette.

He wanted out of the apartment. He wanted, more than that, a map.

Dominic's keys hung from a ceramic dish by the door. The Honda key was on a ring with a CVS card, a tiny plastic St. Christopher medal rubbed nearly blank at the face, and a bottle opener from a liquor distributor Nate didn't recognize. He stood there a second with the keys in his hand, feeling the absurd trespass of every object: the body, the voice, the toast, and now, apparently, the patron saint of travelers dangling from his fingers. Dominic Altieri had gone to church, or his mother had insisted on protection, or somebody had once pressed the medal into his hand and said here, keep this on you. Nate didn't know any of that. He was learning a dead man from leftovers.

The Civic was in the lot behind the building, silver and a little sun-faded on the roof. When he opened the door, a wave of trapped heat came out carrying the smell of old upholstery, dust, and the sweet synthetic pine of an air freshener that had long ago stopped pretending to be a tree. There were parking receipts in the cup holder, a cracked pair of sunglasses in the center console, and a Rand McNally road atlas jammed into the glove compartment under the registration.

He stared at the atlas for a full five seconds before taking it out, as if some smaller, meaner version of Google Maps might be nested inside.

It was Connecticut. Just Connecticut, folded and stapled and hostile.

He carried it back upstairs and spread it on the kitchen table because the car felt too cramped for whatever this was going to be. The pages kept trying to close on his hands. Streets broke off at the gutter and resumed three inches away in a different square. Bridgeport was all pale grids and names in tiny print. He found the apartment's street by checking Dominic's license, then found Kassos Development from the business card he'd taken out of the wallet yesterday, then sat looking at the page until he understood that there would be no little blue dot. No calm synthetic voice telling him to continue for two miles. If he wanted to know where he was, he had to learn it the old way, with his own eyes and a finger moving along paper.

He felt suddenly, stupidly angry at the twenty years he had lost. Not just the conveniences. Not just the phone. The whole invisible infrastructure of the future, all the stupid things that worked so well you only noticed them when they were gone. Search bars. GPS. Group texts. The ability to stand in a parking lot and know what anything was. He had come back in time to commit a murder and had somehow wound up defeated by folding paper.

He got the atlas into something like the right shape, took it downstairs, and started driving.

The first few minutes were all body memory and none of it his. Dominic's hands sat easily at ten and two. Dominic's foot found the brake with the light, even pressure of someone who did not spend his life jerking to a stop because he was reading directions off a phone. Nate kept waiting to do something wrong and have the car announce it. Instead the Civic just moved through morning traffic with insulting competence.

Bridgeport in July 2005 looked less like time travel than like somebody had quietly removed a layer of static from the world. People on the sidewalk weren't bent over screens. At a bus stop three teenagers stood shoulder to shoulder with nothing in their hands, just existing there in public boredom, one of them kicking the metal bench leg every few seconds hard enough to ring it. A man in painter's whites read a folded tabloid while he walked. Two women outside a bakery actually looked at each other while they talked.

Nate reached for his pocket at a red light, wanting the phone by reflex, wanting to search where am I, wanting a map to bloom obediently under his thumb. His hand found Dominic's wallet instead. Broad leather, warm from his body. Forty-three dollars cash. The wrong century.

He drove with the atlas open on the passenger seat, glancing down at it and then immediately back up, because he was not eager to die in 2005 by rear-ending a landscaping truck. The map slid every time he turned. Twice he missed streets because a line that looked thick on paper turned out to be some narrow one-way lane squeezed between brick buildings and a laundromat. Once he found himself under a train bridge he had not meant to pass under, the concrete sweating old damp, the radio dropping to static for three seconds like the city itself had swallowed the signal.

He turned the radio off and rolled the window down instead.

Hot air came in carrying exhaust, salt from somewhere off the water, fryer grease, cut grass. Summer in Bridgeport had always smelled like too many things happening in too little space. He had forgotten that. Or maybe forgotten wasn't the word. The memory had been compressed, zipped into some useless folder in his head while he spent his adult life narrating the city as wound, as failure, as the place things got taken from him. He had not thought much about the summer smell.

On Fairfield Avenue he passed a Blockbuster Video with its blue sign bright as a toy. The windows were plastered with movie posters instead of cell phone ads. A man in cargo shorts was walking out with two plastic cases in one hand and a soda the size of his forearm in the other. Nate slowed enough to stare, which earned him a horn from the car behind him.

"Yeah, okay," he muttered, and kept moving.

At the next corner a gas station sign showed regular at $2.30. He looked at it once, then again, then actually laughed, a single ugly sound. There were years in his twenties when he had eaten instant noodles three nights in a row and still paid more per gallon than that. The laugh died as quickly as it had come. Cheap gas. Dead father. Nice trade.

He pulled over near a row of newspaper boxes because the date had become a physical itch. The digital clock in the apartment and Sarah's summer-school schedule had already said the same thing, but he wanted something public, something printed, something the world had made for itself without reference to him. He fed quarters from the cup holder into a red metal box and tugged out a copy of the *Connecticut Post*.

July 13, 2005.

The ink came off on his fingertips immediately. He stood on the sidewalk beside the car reading the dateline and the first few inches of the front-page story, not processing a word of it beyond the date. A cyclist in a neon windbreaker coasted past, humming tunelessly to himself. A woman in nurse scrubs waited at the crosswalk with one white sneaker unlaced, reading the horoscopes over Nate's shoulder without apology. The day was happening with complete confidence that it was real.

He folded the paper badly and threw it onto the passenger seat. It landed on the atlas, two proof systems stacked on top of each other. Map, date, era. Fine. He was here.

He kept driving.

He told himself he was learning the radius of Dominic's life: apartment, office, grocery store, family house, eventually, if he could find that. The lie lasted maybe ten minutes. He knew exactly where he wanted to go. He had known before he took the keys from the dish. The address had been in his ALTIERI folder in 2025, in scanned permits and rezoning notices and tax assessments. Eastbridge Plaza. He had typed it so many times his fingers could probably still do it without him.

Now there was no search bar to catch the name for him. There were only roads and wrong turns and commercial strips that blurred into each other, low brick, vinyl awnings, chain pharmacies, stores with hand-painted signs. He followed the atlas east, then south, then doubled back when he realized he had tracked the wrong route with his finger. The city shifted around him in pieces he recognized and pieces he didn't. A storefront church where he remembered a check-cashing place. An appliance repair shop that had later become, or would later become, something with smoked glass and impossible rent. Billboards advertising phone plans with free nights and weekends, like the future had not yet figured out how to make every hour belong to work.

Every few blocks he got the sensation that his actual life was not gone but merely loading badly somewhere offscreen. It was a stupid, tech-support way to think about irreversible metaphysics, but it was the only one he had. Surely the right refresh would bring back his apartment, his laptop, his own hands on a steering wheel with cracked knuckles and bitten nails. Surely there was a hidden menu, a rollback option, a restore point.

There wasn't. There was a Civic and a paper map and a dead man's saint medal knocking against the steering column.

By the time he found the commercial road that led to Eastbridge, his shirt was sticking to the middle of his back. He passed a self-storage place, a carpet warehouse, a squat diner with mirrored windows, then saw the plaza sign ahead on a low brick pillar: EASTBRIDGE PLAZA, black letters on a white board sun-bleached almost gray.

His hands tightened on the wheel before he had consciously decided to turn in.

* * *

The strip mall was smaller than it had been in his resentment.

That was the first shock. The plaza hit him before his father did.

In Nate's head, Eastbridge had swollen over the years until it was practically a civic landmark, the site of the original sin. He had built speeches around it in his own mind. The demolition. The redevelopment. The machine that took the store, took Mort, took whatever thin strip of future Nate still believed in at fifteen. But from the parking lot it was just what it had always been: a single-story row of practical units with sun-faded awnings, a pothole patched twice near the entrance, and a handful of businesses trying to hold a commercial block together through force of repetition.

Gail's Diner sat near the middle with two painted coffee cups on the window and a handwritten pie list taped crookedly beside the door. Next to it was a dry cleaner with prom dresses sealed in plastic along the wall, then a nail salon with pink cursive lettering already peeling at the edges, then a barbershop with a real spinning pole that made the whole place look more permanent than it probably was. A bodega at the far side had a cardboard coffee display in the window and a hand-lettered sign advertising money orders. And at the end, bigger than the other units and set a little apart by the angle of the lot, was Lazar's Hardware.

The sign was exactly the one he remembered, hand-painted on a white board in red block letters, the L in Lazar's slightly faded as if the sun had liked that letter best and eaten it first.

He parked three rows out and did not shut off the engine.

From this distance the plate-glass windows reflected enough sky that the interior took a second to resolve: pegboard, fluorescent light, the high front counter. Then a shape moved behind it, and the shape was a man in a canvas apron, and the man turned sideways to lift a box from under the counter, and Nate saw the reading glasses swinging on the chain against his chest.

It was Mort.

Nate only realized the engine was still running because the wheel trembled under his hands.

Mort was talking to a customer, a heavyset man in a sweat-darkened Yankees cap holding a carton of deck screws against his stomach. Nate couldn't hear the words through the glass, but he knew the rhythm immediately. Mort listened with his head tipped, patient, then answered by showing rather than telling. He took one screw from the open box, held it up between thumb and forefinger, tapped the threads with his nail, then pointed toward something on the pegboard wall. He had always talked that way when the subject was materials, like the thing itself deserved a proper introduction.

Alive was a cheap word for what Nate was seeing, but there was no fancier one that made it more true. Mort was fifty-two, with his thinning hair combed back and his reading glasses on the chain because he was forever taking them off and losing them, his forearms browned from work and roped with little old scars. The canvas apron was tied too high because Linda always said he wore it like an old man and he always ignored her. He didn't look young or restored. He looked present, fully there in the bright store air, explaining screws to a man who needed a deck.

Nate had spent years imagining this moment without knowing he was imagining it. Not this exact moment, obviously. There had been no version of events where time travel worked, where murder worked, where he arrived here wearing the body of the man he'd blamed. But there had been some underground fantasy, persistent as mold, that if he could only get to the source of the damage early enough, before it all happened, he would feel something clean, maybe purpose or vindication, some line drawn straight from pain to action.

Instead he was in a hot Honda staring through glass at his father demonstrating screw threads.

He put the car in park and left the engine running. He still didn't open the door.

The customer in the Yankees cap laughed at something Mort said. Mort smiled, not big, just enough to crease one side of his face, then rounded the counter and led the man toward an aisle Nate could not see. For three seconds the space behind the register was empty. Then Linda crossed the back opening carrying a ledger and a yellow pencil tucked over one ear, moving fast in the compact, efficient way she always had, like every room with work in it belonged partly to her. She disappeared again before Nate had even fully registered her.

His mother too.

He put a hand over his mouth and pressed hard, not because he was going to cry, although maybe he was, but because his face had lost its instructions.

A bell jingled as a woman came out of the store carrying a gallon of paint and a pack of sandpaper. She had a baby balanced on one hip and a cigarette tucked behind the ear not supporting the baby. She crossed behind Nate's car without looking at him and shifted the child higher with a grunt that carried through the glass. The normality of it was obscene. The whole plaza was going on being itself. A man came out of the barbershop brushing loose hair from the back of his neck. Somebody in the diner laughed loud enough for Nate to hear it even with the Civic closed up. Heat shimmered over the asphalt. A cart from somewhere rolled two feet by itself and stopped.

Eastbridge Plaza sat there intact and functioning, not a grievance or an empty lot with a plaque nobody read, not scanned paperwork in a folder named ALTIERI on a laptop in 2025, but a place with bad parking and pie and acetone and old men buying screws one at a time because they didn't need a whole box.

His father reappeared at the counter. Mort set the larger carton down, tore the flap, counted out screws into a small paper bag, and folded the top over twice. It was the same movement Nate remembered from childhood, though memory was too soft a word for it. It lived deeper than memory, down in the body where repetition becomes law: fold twice, press flat with the heel of the thumb, set the bag down like it mattered.

Nate killed the engine.

The silence hit at once, then filled with smaller sounds: the ticking metal under the hood, the muffled hum of window units, a gull making one rusty complaint somewhere overhead. Sweat slid down the side of his ribs. The vinyl seat held the heat of his back. Newsprint had transferred from his fingertips to the steering wheel in faint gray streaks.

The terrible part was that he could go in.

Mort would not look up and say my son. He would look up and say can I help you, or maybe just be right with you, and that would be that. Nate could stand in the store where he had spent half his childhood, buy nails or ask a question about anchors or caulk, and listen to his father answer him like a stranger, then walk out carrying proof of the exchange on a little white receipt.

He reached for the door handle and stopped with his fingers resting on the plastic.

No.

Because what would he even do once he was in there? Tell the truth? Hi, Dad, it's me, I know I look like a guy named Dominic Altieri because I used a machine I built with AI to come back from 2025 and kill him, and now you have to listen to this in between helping customers with deck repairs. Mort would not believe him, and even if he did, there was nothing either of them could do with belief. Or maybe Nate would lie. Maybe he would say he was just browsing, just looking for something around the house, and then his father would spend ten minutes helping him find it. That felt somehow worse.

He let go of the handle.

Inside, Mort bent to reach below the counter and came back up slower than Nate remembered, not old exactly, just fifty-two, carrying the accumulated wear of decades on concrete floors. It was nineteen years ago and no time at all. Nate had seen him in a coffin, powdered and arranged and not there. He had not seen him breathe since before the funeral home smell got into his clothes and stayed there. Now Mort was breathing right in front of him and scratching the side of his nose with the back of one rough hand because he probably still had sawdust on his fingers.

Nate looked away and then immediately looked back.

A teenage kid in a grocery apron ducked into the store, came back out with a roll of duct tape, and jogged toward the bodega. Mort had already turned to the next customer, an older woman with a folded umbrella though the sky was clear. She was talking a lot; Nate could tell from the motion of her free hand. Mort nodded. Waited. Nodded again. He had always been good at letting people take the full long route to what they actually needed.

The front door opened and for a second the sound carried.

"No, those'll rust on you," Mort said. "You want the coated ones if it's staying outside."

That was all. One sentence. Then the door shut and the words flattened back into glass.

Nate dropped his forehead against the steering wheel.

The leather was hot. The horn almost sounded and didn't, because he caught himself in time. He stayed there bent over, breathing into the crook where his arms crossed, and the first clear thought he had was not about Dominic or the plaza or even the impossible fact of time travel. It was that Mort's voice still had that exact rasp in it, the little drag at the back end of a sentence when he'd been talking all day in the store. Nate had carried that sound around for nineteen years without understanding he still knew it this precisely.

When he straightened up, the plaza had not changed. Of course it hadn't. A woman from the nail salon was outside on her smoke break, fanning at the polish smell with a folded appointment book. The barbershop door stood open on a burst of talk radio. Somewhere grease popped. Life was not waiting for him to finish falling apart.

He checked the dashboard clock. Twelve minutes had gone by.

He stayed.

He watched Mort take cash from one customer and a check from another, bend over the register with the reading glasses finally on, then push them back up to his forehead as soon as he was done. He watched Linda emerge again carrying a shrink-wrapped case of something small and metallic, stop to say a few words that Mort answered without turning, and go back into the rear room. They had been married thirty years and communicated mostly in angle and timing. Nate could not hear them, but he knew the shape of that marriage from thousands of nights at a dinner table with both of them tired.

He tried, once, to imagine going in as Dominic Altieri specifically. Giving the name if asked. Hearing whether it meant anything yet. The thought made his stomach fold in on itself. Maybe Mort had already seen the letter, maybe he hadn't. Maybe Dominic was still just some name in a file downtown. But Nate knew. Nate knew whose body was in the driver's seat, whose hands were on the wheel, whose signature would eventually sit at the bottom of paperwork with Lazar's Hardware typed above it. He was not ready to bring that body through the front door.

The steering wheel's textured seam dug into the base of his thumbs. He welcomed the small pain. It was at least current.

Somewhere in this city, within maybe fifteen minutes if the lights cooperated, fourteen-year-old Nate Lazar was alive.

That thought arrived so abruptly he sat back as if somebody had spoken into the car.

Fourteen-year-old Nate, not theoretical, not a memory. A living kid on a July afternoon. He was somewhere eating cereal out of the box or arguing with Linda about nothing or wasting hours in front of a square old monitor. He still had both parents. He still had a hardware store to be embarrassed by. He had not yet turned the demolition into the organizing principle of his life. He had not spent years feeding the story of Dominic Altieri until it got large enough to swallow a marriage, a career, whatever small decent parts of him had once existed.

The city was containing both of them at once. That was what made it unbearable. Nate was not looking backward at childhood from a safe adult distance. He was here with it, breathing the same thick air, close enough to maybe drive by his old house if he wanted to ruin himself properly. Close enough that some other version of him might be outside right now, walking to a friend's place, sunburned, hungry, not yet aware of the shape the world was going to take around him.

He laughed once under his breath, with no humor in it. He had come back to prevent a death and had accidentally created the worst family reunion in history.

A customer pushed out of Lazar's Hardware with a box fan under each arm. Mort followed him to the doorway to hold it open and stood there a second after the man left, one hand on the frame, looking out over the lot the way store owners do when business slows: not resting exactly, just measuring. From where Nate sat, it was possible that if Mort lifted his chin a little he would look directly at the Civic.

Nate slid down in the seat on pure reflex.

Ridiculous. Mort would not recognize him. There was nothing to recognize. But his body had already made the decision. Dominic's broad chest folded inward, Dominic's head ducked, Dominic's hand came up to shield the side of his face. Nate stayed that way until the heat became stupid, until the seat belt buckle burned against his forearm. When he finally looked again, Mort had gone back inside.

Twenty-six minutes had gone by.

He stayed.

A gull landed on the hood of the car, strutted twice, and pecked at a dead bug near the windshield wiper. Nate watched it with the blank concentration of a man whose brain had exceeded its design capacity. When the bird finally flew off, he checked the clock again.

Forty minutes.

He started the engine. The sound felt too loud. Inside the store, nobody reacted. Mort was bent over the counter helping the umbrella woman count out exact change from a coin purse.

Nate kept the car in park another ten seconds. He wanted one last impossible thing, though he couldn't have said what exactly: a look up, a sign, some crack in the machinery of the world that would let a father know his son was thirty yards away in another man's face.

Nothing happened.

Mort folded the receipt, handed it over, and turned toward the next aisle.

Nate backed out carefully and drove to the exit at a speed so reasonable it made him hate himself. At the sign he glanced once in the rearview mirror and saw the plaza sitting there in the reflected glass, sunstruck and ordinary and already receding, with Gail's pie list, the spinning barber pole, and the faded L still legible.

He drove without the atlas now. He didn't need it to get back. The route had printed itself into him the brutal way important things did.

Traffic thickened near the main road. At a light, a boy on a BMX bike shot between lanes and cut across in front of him, skinny knees flashing, no helmet, no sense. Nate's chest gave one hard, useless thump. The kid wasn't him. Of course he wasn't. Wrong face. Wrong posture. Still, for half a second the city had offered the possibility and then taken it back.

By the time he reached Dominic's apartment, the newspaper on the passenger seat had curled at the edges from the heat. He parked, shut the car off, and stayed where he was. The engine ticked and ticked and then slowed. Upstairs, somewhere above him, a toilet flushed. Somebody dragged furniture across a floor. A siren moved distantly through town and then away from it.

On the dashboard, the *Connecticut Post* date stared up at him. July 13, 2005.

His father was alive, his mother was alive, and somewhere under the same sky fourteen-year-old Nate was still small enough to think his life was going to happen in order.

Nate sat in Dominic Altieri's car with Dominic Altieri's hands on the wheel until the metal under the hood went quiet.
