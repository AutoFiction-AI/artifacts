# Chapter 2

Nate was standing in the doorway of the guest room in his boxers and a podcast-branded T-shirt, and Paul knew before Nate said anything because Nate wasn't performing. Nate was always performing, at the draft table, at the grill, on the phone to his eleven listeners, in his sleep for all Paul knew. But the man in the doorway was small and still and his mouth was working without sound, and that was how Paul Decker learned that his best friend was dead.

"Something's wrong with Martin." Nate's voice came out at a register Paul had never heard from him — flat, airless, like the words had been squeezed through something too narrow. "I went up to — I was gonna ask him about the side bet, and he's — Paul, I think he's —"

Paul was already moving. Bare feet on the cold hallway floor, the bathroom nightlight throwing that sick yellow glow Martin always left on for guests who drank too much and needed a beacon to piss by. Martin's bedroom door was open at the end of the hall. Nate must have left it open. The light from the nightstand lamp spilled into the hallway in a thin stripe.

Martin was in bed. On his back, one arm hanging off the mattress, the other folded across his chest like he'd arranged himself for a photograph. The sheets were pulled up to his waist. His mouth was open. His eyes were closed. Paul would be grateful for that later, grateful he didn't have to see whatever the eyes looked like, but right now he wasn't thinking about later. The color of Martin's face was wrong. Martin was always ruddy. Flushed from lake sun, from scotch, from yelling about someone's draft pick, from the blood pressure he joked about and never treated. Now his skin was a grayish yellow that Paul had never seen on a living person, and the seeing of it was what told his body before his brain caught up.

Paul put his hand on Martin's neck and felt nothing. Warm skin, slack muscle, no pulse. The warmth was the cruelest part: it meant recent, it meant close, it meant that somewhere in the last hour Martin Kessler's heart had stopped and the heat was still leaving his body and Paul had been asleep twenty feet down the hall.

"Call 911," Paul said.

"I don't have — my phone's downstairs, I left it on the —"

"Go get it. Go."

Nate didn't move. His face was doing something Paul had never seen from him, a kind of involuntary disassembly, the muscles pulling in directions that had nothing to do with comedy or performance or any expression Nate had practiced. His eyes were wet and he was gripping the doorframe with one hand like it was the only thing keeping him upright.

"Nate. Phone. Now."

Nate turned and went. Paul heard him on the stairs, heard the third step creak, the one everyone knew to skip, because Nate was not thinking about stairs right now, and then Paul was alone with Martin.

CPR came next because you start CPR. That was what you did. You didn't check for a pulse a second time or stand there weighing odds the way Paul did at a desk all day. You put your hands on your best friend's chest and you pushed. Paul's training was a weekend course twelve years ago, mandated by his company for field adjusters who might encounter a medical emergency at a claim site, and what he remembered was: thirty compressions, two breaths, don't stop, don't second-guess the depth. The practice mannequin had smelled like new rubber sneakers, and the instructor had said "you can't make it worse" with the cheerful certainty of a man who had never done this on someone he loved.

Paul laced his fingers and set the heel of his right hand on Martin's sternum and pushed. The first compression produced a sound that was not the cinematic crack of breaking ribs but a soft wet yielding, a thud that Paul felt travel through his palms and up his forearms and into a place behind his eyes where it would live for months. Again, he pushed. The counting was supposed to help: one, two, three, four. What it actually did was give his brain something to hold while the rest of him processed the fact that the body moving under his hands was the person who had driven four hundred miles to pick him up from a motel in Fargo when Paul's rental car died during his father's funeral trip. Who had shown up at Paul's house the night after Anne's miscarriage with a bottle of Maker's Mark and a roll of trash bags and had cleaned the kitchen without being asked and without saying a single thing about why the kitchen needed cleaning. Who had been Paul's best man and given a toast that made Paul's mother cry, and Paul's mother did not cry, and Martin had known exactly which story would do it, the one about Paul falling asleep in the library junior year and Martin covering him with his own coat and leaving a note that said "you looked cold, you looked stupid, I'll be at the bar."

Fifteen, sixteen, seventeen. Paul tilted Martin's head back and sealed his mouth over Martin's and breathed. The air went in and Martin's chest rose and the air came back tasting like Lagavulin and something underneath the Lagavulin that was metallic and intimate and terrible. Air went in again, and then Paul went back to compressions. Twenty-five years of friendship being expressed as a rhythm Paul could not maintain because his arms were already burning and his breath was already ragged and the counting was already losing itself in the gap between what he was doing and what it was accomplishing.

Because he knew. He knew the ugly small percentages, knew how quickly a room turned from emergency to aftermath, had once had to explain that difference to twelve jurors staring back at him. What he was doing was not medicine. It was not even hope. It was community theater: a forty-seven-year-old man in flannel pajama pants performing chest compressions on another forty-seven-year-old man whose heart had stopped long enough ago that the warmth was leaving and no amount of rhythmic pressure was going to reverse whatever had already been decided.

Still he pushed. On it went until his shoulders felt like they were separating and the count dissolved into meaningless numbers and the sound of his own breathing was louder than anything the body was producing. Soon the effort became its own reason, not to save Martin but to not be the person who stopped trying.

Then he stopped.

On his heels, Paul looked at Martin's face, which had not changed, which would never change again, and said, "Fuck."

Quiet. Almost conversational. Not addressed to Martin or to God or to the obscenity of dying alone in bed at forty-seven with a half-finished scotch on the nightstand and your friends sleeping down the hall. Addressed to the room. To the lamp and the sheets and the gap between Martin's front teeth that Paul could see because Martin's mouth was still open and there was nothing dignified about death, nothing composed, nothing like the careful phrases from work Paul used when he needed death to sound tidier than it was.

Then he leaned forward and put his forehead on the edge of the mattress. The sheets smelled like Martin's laundry detergent, the blue bottle, the cheap one, the same brand Martin had used since their freshman dorm at the University of Minnesota. Paul had seen that blue bottle under every sink Martin had ever owned. Apartments, the first house, Linda's house, this lake house. A stupid detail, a nothing detail, and Paul pressed his face into it and his eyes burned and his throat closed and his nose ran onto the cotton, and he stayed there with his forehead on the bed and his hands on the mattress three inches from his dead best friend and he did not move for what felt like a very long time.

---

Nate came back with his phone and Tommy and Joel behind him, all of them half-dressed and blinking in the hallway light. Tommy in coaching shorts and nothing else, the scar on his left forearm visible, his crew cut flattened on one side from the pillow. Joel in boxers and a University of Minnesota T-shirt that had to be twenty years old, its collar stretched and the maroon faded to something closer to brown. They filled the doorway and looked past Paul to the bed, and Paul, who was standing now, who had wiped his face with the back of his hand and straightened up because that was what you did, shook his head.

"Jesus Christ," Tommy said. It came out the way Tommy said everything, loud, definitive, but his face was wrong for the volume. His face was the face of a man who had just been told something he already feared.

"Is he —" Joel started.

"Yeah."

Tommy stepped into the room and put his hand on Martin's chest, his enormous hand flat on the sternum Paul had just been compressing, and held it there like he was checking for himself, like the determination required a second opinion from a man who coached high school football for a living. The hand stayed there a beat and then came back, and as Tommy looked at his own palm the fingers curled slightly and Paul watched something move behind Tommy's eyes that was not grief. Not yet. It was closer to recognition, like a man hearing a sound he'd been listening for.

"Did you call?" Paul asked Nate.

"I tried — there's no signal in the basement. I came back up and got these guys first."

"There's signal on the deck," Paul said. "Or by the road."

Nobody moved toward the deck.

Doug appeared in the hallway behind them, gaunt and blinking, his sweatshirt on inside out, the tag sticking up from his collar like a small white flag. Doug didn't ask. His gaze went past the bodies in the doorway to the bed and to their faces and back to the bed, and his mouth made a small shape that didn't become a word. Then he stepped back against the wall to make himself smaller, which was what Doug did when rooms got too full.

Cliff was last, arriving in pressed pajama pants and a T-shirt that still had a crease from the fold, because Cliff packed his T-shirts in tissue paper, because Cliff was Cliff, and he stood at the back of the group and looked at Martin's body on the bed and then his eyes moved to the nightstand and the scotch glass and the room's layout, and Paul recognized the expression. Paul had seen it on men who walked into a bad room and went straight to angles instead of grief. Cliff was calculating. Someone was dead in the room and Cliff Haywood was already doing math.

"We need to call," Paul said. The word came back to him, and the repetition made it sound less like a plan and more like something a man says to a room that isn't listening.

"He's gone, Deck." Tommy's coaching voice, the one for halftime when a kid was hurt and the injury was real and what mattered was keeping everyone else from falling apart. Flat, declarative. "You can see he's gone."

"That's not the point. We're supposed to —"

"What's the ambulance gonna do?" Tommy had both hands on the back of his neck now. "It's a thirty-minute drive from Detroit Lakes and he's — Paul, he's gone. You know he's gone."

"We still have to call."

"He's not wrong." Joel, from the far side of the doorway. His voice had shifted into that careful, mortared register — the legal architecture engaging. "We call now, we get a county deputy in forty minutes taking statements till dawn. Six guys in their underwear who've been drinking, trying to —"

"Yeah, and if we don't call, it looks like —"

"We call at seven, it's the same deputy." Joel's hands came up, the narrowing gesture. "Business hours. Same result. The only difference is —"

"The only difference is five hours where it looks like we sat on it."

"It looks like six men who were woken up at two in the morning and handled it badly." Joel held Paul's look. "Which is what this is."

The room was quiet. The refrigerator hummed downstairs in its forty-minute cycle. A loon called from the lake, that sliding, deranged sound that tourists recorded on their phones and locals tuned out, a bird screaming into the dark for no reason a human could use.

Paul looked around the room. At Tommy, who was staring at the nightstand now with an intensity that didn't match the moment. At Joel, whose legal reasoning was sound and whose reasons for wanting it sound were his own. At Cliff, who had stopped calculating and was now looking at the doorway like he was measuring how long it would take to get through it. At Doug, pressed against the hallway wall, arms crossed over the inside-out sweatshirt, saying nothing. At Nate, who was holding his phone with both hands like it was something that had been taken away from him and given back wrong.

Paul wanted to argue. The impulse was to pick up the phone and call himself and let the county handle it. But Joel was making enough sense to sound like permission, and Paul's chest and forearms were still wet from the compressions. His saliva was on Martin's mouth. His tears were on Martin's sheets. Some part of his brain, traitorous and trained, started laying the pieces out anyway: Nate gone for the phone. Paul alone with the body. Martin's arm knocked out of place. Wet marks on the nightstand. His own spit on Martin's mouth.

Martin's body was not where Paul had found it. One arm hung wrong. The sheets were twisted. The pillow was half off the bed. Paul looked at all of it and saw interruption. What he saw was a room he had touched too hard.

"OK," Paul said. "Morning."

The room held its shape for another minute, six men and a dead man in a bedroom at 2 AM, none of them knowing what to do with their hands. Then Doug said, quietly, that he'd make coffee, and the word "coffee" was enough to break whatever was keeping them there, because it was specific and practical and had nothing to do with the body on the bed. The group moved to the kitchen in the useless, shuffling procession of people who have been told there is nothing to do and have not yet accepted it.

Paul stayed and told them he wanted a minute. They gave it to him.

The minute lasted twenty-five.

What Paul did in Martin's bedroom in those twenty-five minutes was methodical and sickening. First he repositioned the body, moving Martin's arm back across his chest, or close to where it had been; he couldn't remember exactly, and the trying made sweat run down behind his ears. Then he smoothed the sheets and straightened the top sheet across Martin's waist. Every adjustment was a guess, and each guess felt like dragging the room another inch away from whatever the truth had looked like at 2 AM.

Then came the pillow and the scotch glass, tipped sideways against the lamp base after CPR. The glass went upright. At the wet shine around Martin's mouth, he hesitated and wiped it with the corner of the top sheet. The cloth caught on Martin's lower lip. Paul stopped with the sheet still in his fist, seeing what the gesture had become, then finished wiping because half-done felt worse.

Next the surfaces: nightstand, bedframe, doorknob, the hem of his flannel shirt wrapped around one finger. The motions were small and practiced-looking, which made them worse. A man on television did this after murder. Paul did it after trying to save his friend.

By the time he reached the hall bathroom, the tap was already on. At the sink, he scrubbed his hands with Martin's Ivory soap until the smell changed, sweat and Lagavulin replaced by that flat clean bar smell Martin had carried from apartment to apartment for twenty-five years. Water ran over his wrists. Foam slid into the drain. When he dried his hands on the towel and turned off the light, the room upstairs was tidier and less true.

The kitchen was lit by the fluorescent tube over the sink that nobody liked and nobody replaced because it was Martin's kitchen and Martin got to decide what was harsh. Paul sat at the table with both hands around a mug of Doug's punishing coffee and looked through the sliding glass door at the deck.

Tommy was outside, pacing. Even from behind glass, even in the dark, Tommy was unmistakable, the size, the athletic restlessness, the way his hands gripped the back of his own neck and then dropped and then gripped again, a man whose body could not process whatever his brain was running and whose only solution was locomotion. Back and forth across the deck. Back and forth.

Nate was in the great room, visible through the kitchen doorway, sitting on the couch with his phone on his lap and his hands on his knees. Not looking at the phone. Not performing. Just sitting there.

Joel had taken the chair by the dead fireplace and was already asleep, or pretending to be, his legs stretched out, his chin on his chest, his breathing even. Joel's ability to shut down under pressure was either a lawyer's skill or a dissociative gift, and Paul had never been able to tell which.

Cliff was somewhere. Paul could hear him in the hallway for a moment, and then he couldn't.

Doug sat across from Paul with his own mug of terrible coffee, and they looked at each other over the table, and neither of them said anything for a long time. Doug's face was the same face it had been all weekend, hollowed, the bags under his eyes almost purple in the kitchen light, the weight loss that nobody was asking about because the asking would require an answer and the answer might be worse than the not-knowing. Doug looked like a man who hadn't slept well in months, and the not-sleeping had nothing to do with tonight.

"You OK?" Paul asked, because that was the question you asked in kitchens at 3 AM even when the answer was obvious and the asking was performance.

Doug looked at him. "No." A beat. "Are you?"

"No."

"OK."

That was the whole conversation. Then they sat with it, drinking bad coffee in a dead man's kitchen while the lake lapped at the shore fifty yards away.

The coffee maker gurgled through its cycle. Paul sat there and he listened.

That was when it started.

The first sound was a creak from the stairs. Not the third step. Everyone knew about the third step, everyone had been skipping the third step for years, it was one of the house's shared facts, as known and automatic as which burner ran hot on the stove and which bathroom door you had to lift to latch. This was the fourth or fifth step, higher up, and the creak had weight to it, someone heavy, someone who'd remembered the third step and skipped it but whose mass was enough to make the next one protest.

Paul checked. Tommy was still on the deck. Joel was in his chair. Doug was across the table. Nate had been on the couch. Paul leaned back to look through the kitchen doorway, and Nate was still there, hunched forward now, phone in his hands, the screen casting a blue wash across his face.

Someone else was on the stairs. Or the stairs were settling.

Then the upstairs hallway. Footsteps, heavy, deliberate, the stride of a big man trying to walk quietly and failing because big men walk heavily even when they try not to. A pause. Then the sound of Martin's bedroom door, which Paul had left almost closed, swinging open on its hinges. That specific hinge, the top one, always gave a faint metallic moan that Martin had said he'd oil and never did.

Someone was in Martin's room.

Paul's first thought was flat and practical: someone's checking on him. Paying respects. Having their own version of the moment Paul had just had. His second thought arrived with the sour taste of Doug's coffee climbing back up his throat: someone's doing what I just did.

For a second Paul almost got up. Almost went to the stairs and called out. But that would mean admitting he was listening, and listening meant admitting he recognized the shape of what was happening upstairs. A man did not sit in a dead friend's kitchen cataloging footsteps unless some part of him already knew why the footsteps were there. Paul kept his chair and kept his mug.

Over the next two hours, the house moved. Not the way houses move when they settle, the pine beams adjusting to the temperature drop, the A-frame groaning against the wind off the lake, the sounds you stopped hearing after the first night. The house moved the way a house moves when people are trying to move through it without being heard and are not quite succeeding.

At 3:22, Paul noted the time because once he'd started listening he couldn't stop pinning things down. At 3:22, a toilet flushed upstairs. Not a casual flush, not the half-pull you give at night when you're trying to be quiet. The handle held down hard, the full-tank flush, the kind of flush you commit to when you need everything to go down on the first try. The plumbing rattled in the walls for a beat afterward, the old copper pipes that Martin was always going to replace.

At 3:48, the study door. Paul knew it was the study because the study had an old-fashioned thumb latch instead of a knob, a wrought-iron piece that Martin's parents had installed in the seventies, and it made a specific metallic snap that was different from every other door in the house. Distinct as a fingerprint. Paul was sitting twelve feet from that latch and he heard it click open and he did not get up because getting up would have meant he'd heard it and hearing it would have meant he cared and caring would have meant there was something in the study worth caring about at 3:48 in the morning.

At 4:10, footsteps overhead again. Lighter this time, not the heavy tread from earlier but someone moving with the particular caution of a person carrying something, their weight redistributed, their steps shortened. Then the soft rattle of the shared bathroom's medicine cabinet, the hinge on that mirror-door that squeaked in a way Martin's mother had probably complained about before Paul was born. Something small and hard met the tile floor, a bottle, a container, and was picked up quickly, the tiny percussion of plastic on ceramic and then the scrabble of fingers retrieving it. The footsteps retreated, lighter than they'd come, moving with the careful rhythm of someone who now had something in their hands they didn't want to drop.

At 4:30 or 4:40, Paul couldn't pin it exactly. His timekeeping had started to fray at the edges, the way precision frays when you've been awake too long and the only clock is the coffee maker's cycle. A phone screen glowed in the hallway outside the kitchen. Not a flashlight, not an overhead light. The specific blue-white of a phone screen, held close to someone's body, the glow moving and pausing and moving again and then going dark. Someone was standing in the hallway doing something on a phone that could not wait for daylight.

Paul held all of it in his head whether he wanted to or not. The heavy footsteps on the stairs were not the light footsteps near the bathroom, which meant at least two different people. The toilet flush was upstairs, consistent with the guest bedroom wing. The study latch was ground floor, twelve feet from the kitchen. Paul had not seen or heard anyone pass through the kitchen to reach the hallway, which meant they'd come through the great room or down the back stairs from the basement and approached the study from the opposite direction. The phone screen in the hallway could have been anyone, but the glow was low, waist-height, held by someone who was standing still and deliberating, and the whole posture had the look of deletion, the fixed attention of a person making something go away.

All of it sat with him. He did not discuss it with Doug, who was still across the table, who was on his third cup of terrible coffee, and whose inside-out sweatshirt was now right-side-out, which meant Doug had taken it off and put it back on at some point when Paul was listening to the house and not watching Doug, and Paul thought about that and then set the thought aside in the same mental drawer where the rest of the night's observations were accumulating.

By 5 AM the house was quiet. Or the version of quiet a house achieves when you've been listening so hard that the absence of human sound feels like its own kind of noise. The pines moved outside the kitchen window. The lake lapped at the dock, the rocks, the shore.

The first gray light came through the window and Paul realized he had been sitting in the same chair for two hours, both hands on a mug of cold coffee, listening to his oldest friends move through Martin's house one by one and leave it changed.

When he finally stood, his knees cracked. His lower back had locked into the shape of the chair and protested the new configuration. Then he walked down the hallway.

The study door was ajar. Hours earlier Paul had walked past it on his way to the kitchen and it had been closed. Now it was open two inches, and through the gap Paul could see Martin's desk and the landscape print on the wall, the one with the lake and the pines, the one Martin's parents had bought at a craft fair in Duluth in 1983. The print was slightly crooked. It was tilted a quarter inch to the right, as if someone had bumped the wall behind it or moved it and tried to put it back and misjudged the angle. Paul knew the angle because he'd sat in Martin's study dozens of times over twenty-five years, talking about nothing, and the print had always hung straight.

From there he continued to Martin's room. The door was as he'd left it. Paul pushed it open.

Martin was on the bed, in the position Paul had arranged. The arm across the chest, the sheets smooth, the pillow centered. Paul's work. Paul's theater. But the nightstand was different. The scotch glass was there, the lamp was there, but something was absent, and Paul stood in the doorway staring at the nightstand the way you stare at one of those spot-the-difference puzzles, knowing something has changed but unable to isolate it until the absence resolves itself into a shape.

The pill case was there, Martin's small brown leather supplement case, fish oil, B-12, the vitamins Martin took every morning with the earnest optimism of a man who believed he could drink like a twenty-two-year-old and compensate with capsules. But next to where the pill case sat, there was a gap. A circle of dust-free nightstand where something had been and was not. Paul had seen it during CPR without registering it: a small orange prescription bottle, the standard pharmacy kind with the white child-safe cap, sitting next to the pill case. The label never registered. During CPR, he'd been busy trying to restart a heart.

The bottle was gone. Someone had taken it.

Backing out of the room, he went to the shared bathroom at the other end of the hall. The medicine cabinet was open, not swung wide, just unlatched, the mirror-door floating a quarter inch forward like a drawer that hadn't been pushed fully home. Paul didn't open it further, but he could see the shelves. His own toiletries kit on the second shelf, a tube of toothpaste, Nate's brand of mouthwash that no one else would use. The third shelf, where there had been, Paul was almost certain, other things. More things. Paul couldn't itemize what was missing because he hadn't inventoried what was there, but the shelf had a cleaned-out quality, a sparseness that felt deliberate.

Back in the kitchen, Doug was still at the table, in the same position, as though he had not moved since Paul sat down. But his mug was empty and the coffee pot was lower and the sweatshirt was different and the silence was different, and Paul looked at Doug and Doug looked at Paul and neither of them said any of the things that the looking contained.

Tommy was no longer on the deck. Paul could hear the shower running upstairs, the pipes shuddering the way they shuddered when you turned the hot water past halfway, a sound so specific to this house that it might as well have been part of the walls. Tommy was showering at 5:15 in the morning.

Joel hadn't moved from the chair by the fireplace.

Cliff was in the great room now, sitting where Nate had been, scrolling through his phone with an expression of focused calm that Paul recognized from twenty years of watching Cliff work rooms and deals and dinners: the face Cliff put on when the numbers were bad and the other party hadn't figured it out yet. Cliff looked up when Paul entered the kitchen and gave him a nod that contained nothing, no grief, no solidarity, no meaning Paul could read. A nod like a closed door.

Nate was gone from the couch. Paul looked around and didn't see him on the deck, in the hallway, or in the kitchen. Gone somewhere. Doing something. At 5 in the morning.

Paul poured himself another cup of Doug's devastating coffee and stood at the kitchen window. The lake was visible through the pines, iron-gray in the predawn light, flat and still except for the small lapping at the dock that was the lake's constant, the sound that you stopped hearing after the first hour of every visit and that Paul was hearing now as if for the first time. The dock reached out into the water. The Adirondack chairs on the deck were wet with dew. The propane grill where Martin had made burgers eighteen hours ago, scraping the grates with the perfectionist's attention he gave to every physical task, scolding Tommy for leaving grease on the drip tray, and Tommy had called him a psychopath and Martin had said "I'm a psychopath with clean grill grates" and everyone had laughed and the burgers were perfect. Eighteen hours ago.

"Deck."

Tommy was in the kitchen doorway. Showered, dressed in jeans and a polo shirt that was wrong for Tommy, too neat, too deliberate, the outfit of a man who had made decisions about his appearance at 5 AM that were not about appearance. His face was scrubbed and raw. His hands were in his pockets, and Tommy's hands were never in his pockets. Tommy's hands were always doing something, holding a beer, gripping a shoulder, gesturing at a point he was driving home. Hands in pockets was a man containing his hands.

"We should call," Tommy said. "Should've called hours ago."

"Yeah."

"I'll do it. I'll call from the deck, there's better signal."

"OK."

Tommy stood there another beat. The look he gave Paul was something Paul would spend weeks trying to classify, guilt or something that wore guilt's face but moved differently behind the eyes, something that used guilt as a shelter for whatever the actual feeling was. Then Tommy went out the sliding door and Paul heard his voice on the deck, his coaching voice, reporting the death of Martin Kessler to the Becker County 911 dispatcher with the clipped factual cadence of a man who had called in injuries before: name, location, situation, time discovered. No emotion in the delivery. All the emotion in the hands that Paul could see through the glass, gripping the deck railing until the knuckles went white.

Paul looked around the kitchen. The fluorescent tube buzzed. Morning came through the pines in thin gold lines that hit the lake and turned the gray to something brighter that had no right to be beautiful and was.

Upstairs, the study print was crooked. The orange prescription bottle was gone from the nightstand. The bathroom shelf had been thinned out. Those changes had happened in separate rooms, at separate hours, by separate hands. Paul had handled the body himself. By daylight it no longer looked like a man had died in his sleep. It looked like men had been through the house in shifts, each one straightening or pocketing or scrubbing something.

The sun cleared the tree line. The lake turned from gray to gold. Paul put his cold coffee in the sink and rinsed the mug and set it on the drying rack the way Martin would have wanted, because Martin was particular about his drying rack. That was what finally broke Paul. Not the death. Not the CPR. Not the sounds in the night. The drying rack, the stupid wooden drying rack that Martin had bought at a kitchen supply store in Fargo and been unreasonably proud of. Paul stood at the sink with water running over his hands and cried without making a sound while the sun hit the lake and Tommy's voice carried through the glass door, talking to the dispatcher, giving directions to a house that none of them would ever come back to the same way again.
