# Chapter 17

Paul watched Tommy through the sliding glass door for a long time before he went out.

Tommy was in the Adirondack chair closest to the railing, Martin's chair whether anybody called it that or not, with a beer balanced on the armrest he wasn't touching. The propane heater was off. The temperature had dropped into the low fifties after sunset, and Tommy was sitting there in a T-shirt and jeans like cold had quit mattering. His hands were on his knees. He was looking at the lake.

Joel was in the study. He'd been there since they read the fire document, going through Martin's careful handwriting again, making notes on his legal pad, building the case against a man he'd known for twenty-five years with the same flat precision he used for people he'd never met. Joel had wanted to go straight to Mallick.

"We hand her this tonight," Joel had said. "The document, the photos on Cliff's phone, the timeline. She's got it."

"I know."

"Then let's go."

"Give me an hour."

Joel looked at him the way he looked at clients who were about to make their own worst decision. "Why?"

Paul didn't have a clean answer. Martin was dead. Martin had spent twenty-two years protecting Tommy. Paul needed to look at Tommy's face when the protection ran out.

"Because I need to."

Joel didn't push it. The job had taught him what arguing couldn't move. "I'll be in the study. If he..."

"He won't."

"You don't know that, Paul."

"I know Tommy."

"You didn't, though." Joel's voice was flat, almost tired. "None of us did."

Paul left him with the legal pad and the cold coffee and went to the kitchen. It smelled like old coffee, stale bread, and the cedar note of Martin's cologne that had somehow worked itself into every room.

He stood at the sliding glass door. Through the glass, Tommy was perfectly still. The kitchen light threw a yellow block across the deck boards behind him. Paul had known him for twenty-five years and had not known this. That was the hurt now. Martin being dead was one piece of it. The rest was having to do the thing Martin would never have done.

The door ground in its track when he opened it.

Paul stepped onto the deck in his socks. The boards were cold and slick with dew. He'd forgotten shoes. Dew soaked through his socks before he even noticed.

Tommy didn't turn.

Paul crossed to the chair beside him and sat. The armrests were rough with old paint. Martin had been saying for three summers that he'd sand and restain these chairs. He never got around to it.

"Hey," Paul said.

Tommy lifted the beer an inch and set it back down. "Hey."

The silence between them had Martin's handwriting in it and Cliff's body at the examiner's and the knowledge that Tommy had walked down to the dock Monday night and kept a man under in water too shallow to pass for an accident.

"I need to talk to you," Paul said.

"Yeah." Tommy kept looking at the lake. The word barely carried across the two chairs. "I figured."

Paul had planned an approach, sequential and fair, the way he planned assessments. He dropped it the second he opened his mouth.

"I know about the fire, Tommy."

Tommy's hand went rigid on his knee. Tendons stood out along his forearm, and the scar tissue above his left wrist caught the light from the kitchen window. The rest of him stayed still.

"I don't know what you're talking about."

"Washburn County. 2002. Cabin on the chain of lakes." Paul's own voice sounded strange to him, all the qualifiers gone. "Ray Toomey."

Tommy didn't move. The beer bottle clicked once against the armrest.

"Martin wrote it down," Paul said. "He kept it in the safe for twenty-two years. Joel and I read it this afternoon."

"Martin wouldn't."

"He did."

"Martin wouldn't do that to me."

"He didn't do anything to you. He wrote it down and locked it up and never showed a soul. Cliff found it. Cliff photographed it. Cliff brought it to you on this deck, and you killed him."

Killed sat between them on the wet boards.

Tommy stood up so fast the chair scraped hard. He went to the railing and grabbed it with both hands. When pressure hit, his body always did the same thing: bigger, wider, ready to take over the field.

"You went through Martin's things." His voice was louder now. The coach was back. "You went into his study and into his safe and you read..."

"The safe was open. Cliff left it open."

"So you read one piece of paper Martin wrote twenty-two years ago and now you think you know what happened."

"I know what I read."

"You don't know shit, Decker." He turned. Anger flashed across his face and slid off. "You weren't there. You don't know what that night was. You don't know what it..."

"You started the fire."

"I was twenty-five."

"A man died."

"I know a man died." Tommy's voice cracked on died. "You think I don't know that? You think I've gone one fucking day..."

He broke off. His mouth stayed open. His eyes were wet. His chest worked too hard.

Paul waited until Tommy's grip loosened on the rail.

"Tell me what happened," he said.

Tommy breathed in through his nose and out through his mouth, sideline breathing, the kind Paul had watched him do after a blown play. Then he looked at his palms as if they might still have something on them.

"We were up at the cabin," Tommy said. His voice had gone small. "Martin's uncle's place. Weekend up north. Drinking, cards, acting like assholes. I was gone. Drunk and smoking in bed, which is exactly as smart as it sounds. There was a propane line running along the wall by the heater. I dropped the cigarette. Maybe I knocked it with my hand. Maybe it rolled. I remember smelling gas. I remember smoke. Then nothing."

"And Martin?"

"Martin got me out. Came in yelling, dragged me by the arm." Tommy swallowed. "Ray was in the front cabin, the one by the road. Fire got there fast. Old wood, dry pine, all of it ready to go."

"Ray Toomey died," Paul said.

"Yeah." Tommy kept looking at the lake. "Fifty-three. Three kids. Worked parks in the off-season." The details came out like something memorized past feeling. "Martin went back the next morning and pushed it toward electrical fault. Bad heater. Old wiring. That's what went on paper. That was the lie. The cigarette was me."

"Until Cliff," Paul said.

Tommy's face shut down again.

"Don't."

"Cliff came to you with the document."

"I said don't."

"He came to you on this deck and told you what he wanted."

Tommy's hands curled into fists at his sides.

"He wanted forty thousand dollars," Tommy said. "From Martin's estate. Plus interest. Plus what he called goodwill. He sat here with a beer and talked like he was closing on a condo."

"And Monday night?"

Tommy stared past the railing.

"He came down to the dock still talking. Same words. Arrangement. Goodwill. Manage it. Manage me." Tommy shook his head once. "He put a hand on my arm like we were in this together. I shoved him off."

Paul said nothing.

"He slipped in those stupid loafers and hit his temple on the bench going over. Went in. Came right back up, though. Spitting, cussing, reaching for the boards." Tommy's breathing roughened. "He had one hand on the dock and one on my wrist. I could've pulled him out. I didn't."

The wind moved through the pines behind the house.

Tommy stared at his own hands. "I got on my knees and pushed his head back under. He was fighting. Kicking the posts. Shoes scraping wood. He kept trying to get up and I kept him down. Then he stopped."

Paul felt the cold boards through his socks again, hard and immediate.

Tommy rubbed a hand over his mouth. "I didn't go down there planning it. That's true. But I held him there. I held him under."

"Martin spent twenty-two years on this," Paul said. "Making sure you came back every September. Making sure nobody looked too close."

"Managed."

"Protected." Paul's throat hurt. "He protected you. His whole life, Tommy." He swallowed. "He never told me."

Tommy turned enough to look at him. His eyes were red and raw. "He loved you too," he said. "He did, Paul."

"He loved you more."

Paul let the words sit.

"Yeah," Tommy said. His hands hung between his knees. "I know."

Tommy's shoulders dropped. He looked sick, exhausted.

---

Wind came harder off the lake now. It smelled like cold water and leaf rot from the shallows. Paul's socks were soaked through.

"Martin left you the house," Paul said.

Tommy's head jerked toward him. "What?"

"The will was in the safe. He left you the lake house. He left it to you."

"He left me the house?"

"Yeah."

"And you?"

"The house is yours."

Tommy stared at him.

"You were his best friend."

"I thought I was."

Tommy backed into the chair and sat hard. The beer bottle rolled off the armrest, crossed the deck, and tapped against Paul's foot.

"He left me the house," Tommy said.

"It's in the will."

Tommy pressed both hands over his face. "This place. The deck. The room with the..." His voice came out muffled through his fingers. "Every year. Every year he made sure I came back here."

Paul said nothing.

Tommy dragged his hands down his face. "All those calls. You good? Kids good? Denise good?" He swallowed hard. "He was taking attendance. Making sure I still answered."

He looked at the house.

"Keeping me on the roster." His hands went to his knees. "Without that, Deck, I'm the guy from that cabin. I'm the guy from the dock."

Paul had nothing he trusted enough to say back. He sat there in the cold and let the silence hold.

"I'm not telling you it's OK," he said at last. "Ray Toomey. Cliff. None of it is OK."

"I know."

---

"Mallick knows about the tampering," Paul said. "All of ours. She has Cliff's phone. She has the photos of the fire document and the texts to his lawyer. She'll get to the dock with or without help."

Tommy looked back at the lake. "What do you want me to do?"

"Go to her in the morning. Sit at that kitchen table and tell her what happened to Cliff."

"The fire?"

"I'm talking about Cliff. The fire is twenty-two years old and you need a lawyer for that. Cliff is tonight."

"And if I don't?"

Paul looked at him. Tommy in Martin's chair, the paint peeling under his hands, the house around him now his for the worst possible reason.

"Then I tell her. Six AM."

Tommy nodded once.

"You're not going to do what he did."

"No."

"He would've found a way."

"Yeah." Paul looked at the flakes of paint on his palm. "He would've. I'm not going to."

"So that's it."

"That's it."

"I'm giving you the night," Paul said.

Tommy gave a short nod. "That's generous, Deck."

"It isn't. Six AM."

Paul went inside. The door ground shut behind him.

In the hallway, one of the framed photos caught him: Martin on the dock, one hand on Paul's shoulder, both of them squinting into hard summer sun. Paul kept walking.

Joel looked up when he entered the study.

"He's got until six," Paul said.

Joel set his pen down. "OK."

Paul sat in Martin's desk chair. The safe hung open behind the lake painting. His wet socks left dark marks on the hardwood. He kept his eyes on Joel's legal pad.

Joel turned a page on the legal pad. Outside, the deck gave one small creak and then nothing.
