# Chapter 14

Doug folded the same flannel shirt for the third time. The sleeves wouldn't square right, the cuffs refusing to line up, and he gave up and stuffed it into his duffel. His other shirts were already in there, along with the prescription bottles he'd moved from the upstairs bathroom Saturday morning. The Lexapro and the Ativan sat buried between a pair of jeans and his shaving kit, where they'd been since this whole thing started. An hour ago he'd taken his evening dose in the basement bathroom with the door locked, the way he'd taken every dose this weekend: quickly, quietly, the bottle opened and closed in a single motion so the rattle of pills wouldn't carry through the thin walls. Twenty-five years of friendship and he still couldn't swallow an antidepressant unless he was sure the coast was clear.

The great room had the feel of a waiting room where the appointment had been cancelled. Paul sat at the kitchen table with his reading glasses and his phone, texting Anne about departure times, pausing after every line to read it back before his thumbs moved again. Joel had his laptop on the couch, answering the work emails he'd been ignoring since Thursday, the blue light catching the sharp angles of his face. The draft board was still there, and nobody had suggested taking it down. Nobody had suggested much of anything today. The day had passed in the half-speed of an airport delay, too awake to sleep, too tired to do anything useful, suspended in the lag between catastrophe and departure.

Mallick hadn't been back since Sunday evening. No follow-up calls, no uniformed deputies in the driveway, no casserole. The implication had settled over the house like weather: the investigation into Martin's death was winding down. Natural causes, suspicious tampering, no murder charge. Doug didn't know the legal specifics. Joel did. Doug understood the shape of it anyway. Martin died of a bad heart, five of his closest friends panicked in the dark, the panicking made a natural death look sinister, and now they were all going to go home and spend the rest of their lives knowing they'd made themselves look guilty of something that never happened. Tilt your head and it was almost funny. Doug didn't tilt his head.

Doug zipped the duffel. The sound was loud in the room and Paul glanced up.

"You're set?"

"Yeah. Early flight." This was a lie. Doug's flight wasn't until two-thirty tomorrow afternoon, but the lie gave him a reason to end the conversation, and ending conversations was what Doug did best. Twenty-five years in this group and his primary contribution had been his willingness to go get more ice.

Nate came in from the deck, sliding the glass door shut behind him. He'd been on the phone, Mia probably, or his producer, or whichever version of his future he was negotiating with at any given moment. At the kitchen counter he poured himself a glass of water, took a couple of distracted swallows, and stared through the dark glass as if the call had kept going out there.

"Mia says hi," Nate said, to no one in particular.

"Hi back," Paul said, without looking up.

"She wants to know if we're all okay." Nate said it flatly, the performance drained out of it, which meant someone who loved him had asked the question genuinely and he hadn't known what to do with that. He drank the rest of the water and put the glass in the sink. "I told her we're fine."

"Are we fine?" Joel asked from the couch, with enough edge that it could have been a real question or a lawyer's question, and Nate didn't answer either version.

"Where's Tommy?" Paul asked, and Doug heard the question carry more weight than Paul meant it to. Paul was asking about logistics, whether Tommy needed a ride to the airport, but the name landed differently now. Everything landed differently now.

"Deck, I think," Joel said. "Or the firepit. I saw him walk that way twenty minutes ago."

"And Cliff?"

"Took his phone into the study." Joel closed his laptop and set it on the cushion beside him. "Calling someone. His office. His lawyer. Does Cliff have a lawyer? Everyone has a lawyer now, I guess."

That was a Joel observation, dry and self-aware, the sarcasm pointed at himself as much as anyone because Joel was a lawyer and the week had made lawyers of them all. Nobody picked it up. The room let it die and moved on.

Doug sat on the arm of the stairs that led down to the basement and watched the room. Watching rooms was what he'd always done. Forty-seven years of reading them from the edges: the doctors' offices during his pharmaceutical sales calls, where you learned in the first three seconds whether the receptionist would let you wait or send you home; the living room of the apartment in Roseville where Steph had told him she wanted a divorce and he'd stared at the coffee table, cataloging every ring and scratch on its surface because cataloging surfaces was easier than hearing the words; the shared bathroom upstairs in this house, where he'd taken his prescription bottles out at four in the morning because the shame of other men seeing his medication was worse than any shame the investigation could produce.

The great room was lying to him. Everything in it said the weekend was over: the packed bags by the front door, Paul's careful logistics, Joel's email, Nate's glass of water where a beer would have been on Thursday. The draft board said it was over. Martin's empty chair at the kitchen table, still pulled back slightly from the table as if he'd just gotten up for a refill, said it was over. But Doug could feel something in the room that hadn't resolved. Not in any one person. It was in the distances between people. Tommy alone outside. Cliff in the study with his phone. The way Paul and Joel were careful with each other in a way that was new since Sunday, a carefulness Doug couldn't identify as alliance or suspicion or something in between. The group's geometry had shifted, and from the edge Doug couldn't see the shape of it.

Cliff came back from the study with his phone in his hand and poured himself two fingers of Martin's Lagavulin. The bottle had been on the counter since Thursday night. Nobody had put it away. Scotch smell mixed with the general smell of the house, pine paneling, lake damp, the ghost of Martin's presence that lived in the walls the way old smoke does. Cliff drank standing up, the way he did everything, leaning forward slightly, one hand in his pocket, already half turned toward the next room. Pressed chinos, navy polo, and the leather loafers he wore indoors because he was the one person in the group who gave a shit about shoes.

"Paul." Cliff raised the glass an inch, a small toast or a small acknowledgment. "Joel. Doug."

"Hey," Doug said.

"We should talk about the house at some point," Cliff said. "Not tonight. But someone's got to deal with Martin's things. The estate, the property taxes, all that. There's a process. I've gone through it with clients. I'm happy to help coordinate, if that's useful."

"Let's get through the drive home first," Paul said. The qualifier he'd swallowed, the _in my experience_ he'd left out, the _to be fair_ he hadn't added, made the sentence sound like a door being held politely shut.

Cliff nodded. He drank. He looked at the draft board and said, "He'd have won the league this year. Those picks were solid." Then he took his scotch and went out to the deck, and Doug watched him go. Chinos, polo, loafers making a soft sound on the hardwood as he crossed the room. The sliding door opened and closed. Through the glass, Cliff settled against the railing with his drink and looked out at the lake where the last light of the day had gone and the moon was putting a silver path across the water.

Joel looked at Paul. Paul looked at his phone. Neither of them said anything about what Cliff had said or the way he'd said it, which told Doug that whatever was wrong in the room, they'd already discussed it between themselves and decided not to include him. This was fine. This was what always happened. Doug was the one you told things to when you needed someone to nod, not when you needed someone to act.

People drifted. Nate went down to the basement and said something about packing. Paul made a last round of the kitchen, washing the dinner dishes nobody had finished, wiping down the counter, the caretaking that was Paul's way of grieving. Joel closed his laptop into his bag and said he was going to read upstairs. Tommy was still outside somewhere, a large silent shape Doug had lost track of.

The house settled into the sounds of its own machinery: the refrigerator cycling on with its forty-minute hum, the wind through the pine trees pressing against the walls, the third stair step creaking under Joel's weight as he went up. Doug sat on the arm of the basement stairs and felt the quiet accumulate. It was a different quiet from the dock quiet. House quiet was full of people trying to sleep and failing, full of the sounds they made when they thought no one was listening. The dock quiet was empty. That was what made it clean.

Downstairs, Nate was on one of the pull-out sofas, phone in hand, thumb scrolling without purpose. He didn't look up when Doug came down. Doug sat on his own pull-out and took off his shoes and lay on top of the sheets in his sweatpants and hoodie and stared at the basement ceiling, which was low and paneled and had a water stain in the corner that looked like a map of somewhere he'd never been.

Sleep wouldn't come. The Ativan should have helped. It usually softened the edges enough that he could slide into a shallow unconsciousness that passed for rest, but tonight the edges stayed sharp. Doug lay there and listened to Nate's breathing slow into sleep and the lake lapping at the shore outside the basement window and the house making its noises above him, and he thought about Steph and about the apartment in Roseville that he'd moved out of three months ago and about the one-bedroom in Burnsville that he'd moved into and about the fact that he'd come to this weekend because he needed to be around people and the people he'd come to be around were either dead or going home in the morning and then he'd be back in the Burnsville apartment with the Lexapro and the Ativan and the pharmaceutical sales calls he couldn't seem to care about anymore.

Finally Doug got up. Nate didn't stir. Doug put his shoes back on and pulled the hoodie tighter and went upstairs through the dark house and out the sliding door onto the deck. The deck was empty. Cliff's scotch glass sat on the railing where he'd left it, a finger of amber still in the bottom. The lake was out there, invisible except for the moonlight on its surface and the sound it made, which was the sound of water being patient.

Doug walked to the dock.

---

The stone path was uneven under his feet, the flagstones sunk at different depths from twenty years of Minnesota freeze-thaw. Doug walked it from memory in the dark. Eighty yards through pine and birch, the trees close enough on both sides to block the house lights after the first thirty feet. The lake reached him before the dock did, the small persistent lapping that was Elbow Lake's answer to the wind, not waves exactly, more the water reminding itself that it was there.

The moon was three-quarters full and high enough to reach the path in broken pieces, the light cut by the tree canopy into bright shapes on the stone. September night in northern Minnesota. The warmth of the afternoon was gone and what replaced it was a damp cold that came off the lake and settled into the hoodie and found his skin. Doug shoved his hands in the front pocket. Pine needles underfoot, brittle and sharp. Their smell, turpentine and sugar, was the smell of every September he'd spent at this house, and for a moment it was Thursday again, the arrival, the gravel driveway, Martin's voice from the deck: _Whitfield! You look like shit. Get up here._

Martin had noticed right away. One of the few, maybe the only one, who'd looked at Doug at the airport on Thursday and seen what was underneath the hoodie and the forced smile. On the deck that first evening Martin had asked, privately, _You eating?_ and Doug had said _yeah, I'm fine_ and Martin had looked at him the way you look at someone who is not fine and both of you know it and let the lie stand because pushing it would make it real.

Martin was dead now. The man who noticed was dead.

The trees thinned and the dock appeared, extending thirty feet into the lake, the swim platform at the end a dark rectangle against the water. The bench was where he'd left it, facing the lake. Everything was where he'd left it.

Except there was something in the water.

Doug stopped where the stone path met the dock's first planks. The wood was pale in the moonlight, the grain visible, and the water on either side was dark and moved with the slow deliberation of a lake that had been moving this way for ten thousand years. In the water to the left of the dock, six or seven feet from the edge, there was a shape that did not belong to the lake.

His first thought was a log. Deadfall from the north shore, stripped of bark and waterlogged, floating at the surface. Martin had complained about them. They fouled the swim ladder. They bumped against the dock at night and made sounds that woke people up. Doug's brain offered this explanation the way it always offered the explanation that hurt less. _Contraindicated_, his pharmaceutical vocabulary said, before the word dissolved. The shape was not a log.

It was a person. Face-down. Arms extended at wrong angles, not floating so much as resting on the surface with a terrible limpness that was different from sleep or relaxation or any other state that a living body produces. The legs were extended at a slight downward angle, the feet nearly touching the bottom. The back of the shirt, a light-colored collared polo, sat just above the waterline, the fabric dark with water and clinging to a back and shoulder blades that were motionless in a way that motionless does not mean for a living person.

Doug took three steps onto the dock. The wood creaked under his weight. The sound felt obscene.

The silver hair. The width of the shoulders. The collared polo. It was Cliff. Doug knew it before he assembled the evidence, the way you know someone from behind by the set of their body, the particular proportion of shoulder to waist, the details you've absorbed across twenty-five years of draft weekends without ever deciding to learn them. Cliff Haywood was floating face-down in three feet of water beside the dock at Martin Kessler's lake house, and the lake was lapping at his sides with the same patient rhythm it had been using all week.

Doug knelt on the dock. The planks were damp and cold through his sweatpants. He leaned over the edge and reached down until his hand found Cliff's shoulder. The shoulder was rigid, not the give of sleeping muscle but the stiffness of flesh that had stopped being operated, the meat and bone of a body without a person in it. The water was cold. Minnesota-lake cold, September cold, a cold that went past his wrist and seized his forearm and held it while he pulled.

Cliff's body moved sluggishly in the water, heavier than Doug expected or Doug weaker than he remembered, and the pull rotated Cliff's face half out of the water. The gash on the right temple was the first thing Doug saw. It was dark in the moonlight. Blood reads black under moonlight, not red. The wound was wide, the skin split in a way that looked like impact against something hard and angular, not the gradual scrape of a fall but the concentrated force of a single blow. The eye on that side was open. The other eye was still in the water. Cliff's mouth was open too, slightly, the jaw slack, and there was lake water in it.

Doug let go. His hand came back wet and smelling of the lake and something underneath the lake, something organic, something that was Cliff and the lake and the process that had already begun, the process Doug's pharmaceutical training gave him clinical names for and that he did not want to name here on this dock in the dark with his hand dripping.

Doug looked at Cliff's feet. The loafers. Cliff's leather loafers, the indoor shoes, the ones he wore on the hardwood floors of the great room, the ones that had made that soft careful sound an hour and a half ago when he crossed the room with his scotch and went out to the deck. Not deck shoes. Cliff had a pair of deck shoes, canvas and rubber-soled, the kind you wore on a dock because dock wood got slippery with dew and deck shoes gripped. Doug had seen them by the sliding door every day this weekend, kicked off on the mat. Cliff did not walk down to the dock in his loafers. The stone path was uneven. The dock was wet. Cliff cared about his shoes.

Doug stood. His hands were shaking. The tremor had a quality he recognized from the first week on the Lexapro, before his body had agreed to the new chemistry, a vibration that seemed to come from somewhere deeper than his muscles, from the wiring underneath. But this was not medication. This was the thing that happened when his body understood what his mind still refused to assemble. His knees were wet from the dock planks. His right hand was wet from the lake. Cliff was in the water. The water was three feet deep. There was a gash on his temple. His shoes were wrong.

When Doug opened his mouth, a sound came out that he did not recognize. It was not his voice. It was not any voice he had ever used, not the pharmaceutical sales voice, not the self-deprecating murmur, not the monosyllable, not the trailing-off half-sentence he used as social currency in a group where everyone else was louder and more certain and more willing to finish a thought. The sound was raw and enormous and it came from somewhere below his diaphragm, behind his ribs, and it crossed the water and hit the far shore and came back as an echo that sounded like someone else screaming from across the lake. It came again. And again. Three times Doug screamed on the dock beside Cliff Haywood's body, and by the third time his voice was breaking and the scream thinned into something hoarse and wrecked that was closer to the sound he actually made in the world, which was barely a sound at all.

The echo died. The lake absorbed it. The loons, if they were out there, stayed quiet. Somewhere across the water a dog barked twice and stopped. The silence that followed was not the dock silence he'd come here for, the clean, empty silence that asked nothing of him. This silence had a body in it. This silence was the kind that waits.

Doug stood on the dock and his legs felt strange beneath him, not weak exactly, but disconnected, as if his weight were a problem of engineering that his knees had decided not to solve. He sat down on the wet planks. Cold went through his sweatpants immediately. Doug sat and looked at Cliff in the water and waited for the sound of footsteps on the stone path, because they would come. They had to come. That scream had been the loudest sound he'd ever made in twenty-five years of friendship, the loudest sound this lake had heard all week, and somewhere in the house there were four men who had just heard the quietest person they knew make a noise that did not belong to him.

Water lapped at the dock. Cliff's silver hair moved slightly with the lake's pulse, a slow drift that was almost gentle, almost peaceful, like the hair of a person sleeping face-down in the water, which was not a kind of sleeping at all.

---

Nate reached the dock first. Doug heard him before he saw him, bare feet on stone, slap and hiss and another slap as the flagstones bit him.

"Doug? Doug, what happened?"

Nate's phone light hit the dock and swung across the water and found Cliff and stopped.

"Oh, shit." Nate's voice dropped from his chest to his stomach. "Oh, shit. Oh—"

"He's dead," Doug said. His voice was back to its normal register, barely a sound, and the words came out flat and colorless after the screams that had produced them. Doug was still sitting on the dock. His sweatpants were soaked and his hands were in his lap, the right one still damp from the lake.

Nate stood at the foot of the dock with his phone light on the water. The beam caught the silver of Cliff's hair and the fabric of his polo and the stillness of his arms, which were out at angles that suggested reaching, as if Cliff had been trying to grab something when he stopped trying. Nate's mouth stayed open, but nothing came after the first oh, shit. Usually he could fill any silence with stats, bullshit, a whole panicked halftime show. Barefoot on the cold stone, he had nothing. His light hand trembled slightly and the beam wavered on the water, making Cliff's shape shift and blur.

"Is he—" Nate started, and didn't finish.

Paul arrived next, at a run, breathing hard, carrying a real flashlight because Paul knew where the flashlight lived. The beam was brighter than Nate's and when it found the water and found Cliff it stopped wavering, pinned there the way Paul's hands always went still around broken things. Insurance adjusters look at wrecked things for a living.

"Jesus Christ," Paul said.

Paul didn't wait. He stepped off the dock's edge and into the lake up to his shins, the water soaking his khakis to the knee, and he got a hand under Cliff's shoulder and turned him. Cliff's body rotated slowly in the shallow water. The face came up, white and slack, the eyes unevenly open, the gash on the right temple visible now in Paul's flashlight beam, wider and more ragged than it had looked from above. Paul put two fingers against the side of Cliff's neck and held them there for what felt like a long time and was probably ten seconds. He moved his fingers. He tried again, a quarter inch to the left. Then he took his hand away.

"He's cold," Paul said. His voice was careful, calibrated, the voice he used for information that mattered. "He's been in the water for..." He stopped. Doug watched Paul's insurance brain start the calculation: water temperature, body temperature, rate of cooling, duration. Then something in Paul's face overrode the math and left him standing in the lake with one hand on his dead friend's arm and no idea what the number was or whether the number mattered. "A while. He's been in here a while."

"Paul, come out of the water." Joel's voice came from the foot of the dock. Doug hadn't heard him arrive. He'd come down the path with careful, placed steps, fast enough to matter but not fast enough to slip. His phone light found the water and held on it with the same steadiness as Paul's flashlight.

"I was checking."

"I know. Come out of the water. Don't move him any more." Joel said it with the clipped authority Doug had heard from him only a few times in twenty-five years, the voice that belonged to the courthouse, not the lake house. "This is a crime scene."

The word settled over the dock. Nate looked at Joel. Paul, standing in the water, looked at Joel. Doug, sitting on the wet planks, looked at Joel.

"How do you know that?" Nate said. His voice was raw and smaller than usual and the question had no performance in it.

"Look at his shoes." Joel crouched at the edge of the dock and aimed his light at Cliff's feet. The leather loafers were visible beneath the surface, the dark leather taking on water, the thin soles entirely wrong for the stone path and the dock wood and every surface between the house and the lake. "Those are his indoor shoes. He didn't walk down here in loafers."

Nobody spoke. Joel's light stayed on the loafers. Water moved around them with gentle indifference.

"And the water's three feet deep," Joel continued. His voice was precise and quiet, the voice that assembled facts the way Paul assessed damage, not to understand but to contain. "A conscious person doesn't drown face-down in three feet of water. He was either unconscious when he went in, or he was held."

"There's a wound." Paul was still in the lake. He looked down at Cliff's face, half-lit by the flashlight beam. "Right temple. It's not a scrape. It's a gash. Something hit him. Or he hit something hard."

"Or something was used," Joel said.

The sentence ended and nobody added to it. Joel stood up from his crouch. Paul waded out of the water and onto the dock, his khakis dripping, his shoes ruined. He stood there breathing through his nose in short, counted pulls, like he was trying to keep a gauge needle from slipping into the red.

Tommy came down the path last.

His footsteps were heavier than the others, each one landing hard on the flagstone. At the foot of the dock he stopped with his phone in his hand and the light still off. No question came out of him. He just stood there, a dark shape against the tree line, breathing through his nose like he'd run harder than the short walk from the house required.

Then Tommy turned on the light and found Cliff.

"No," Tommy said.

Not loud. Not the coaching bark. Just the word. Tommy came as far as the first wet plank and no farther. His free hand flattened once against his thigh and stayed there. When Joel said _held_, the muscle in Tommy's jaw jumped hard enough for Doug to see it in the bad light.

"Tommy," Nate said, and the name had the tone of someone reaching for a person who might not be reachable.

Tommy didn't answer. His light dropped from Cliff to the boards and then went dark again. Everybody else had asked some version of what happened. Tommy hadn't asked anything. Doug didn't know what that meant. It only sat wrong.

"We need to call Mallick," Paul said. He was out of the water now, standing on the dock, his flashlight pointed at the planks rather than the body, giving Cliff the privacy Cliff no longer needed. "Right now."

"It's midnight," Nate said, and the absurdity of the objection, as if there were a polite hour to report a body, registered on his own face the moment the words left him. "Yeah. Obviously. I know."

"I'll call," Joel said. He already had his phone out, his thumb on the number before the sentence was finished. He stepped off the dock and walked a few paces down the path and put the phone to his ear.

Doug sat on the dock and looked at Cliff. The moonlight had shifted or his eyes had adjusted, and the skin was already a different color from living skin, a pallor underneath the permanent tan that had always made Cliff look like he'd just come back from somewhere expensive. Cliff's watch was still on his wrist, the crystal intact, catching the moonlight. His hands were open. The polo was a pale blue, the same polo Cliff had been wearing earlier that evening when he'd poured himself the Lagavulin and talked about the house and the estate and the process and said _he'd have won the league this year_ and gone out to the deck with his drink.

Two hours ago. Maybe less. Cliff had been alive in the great room two hours ago, drinking Martin's scotch, wearing those loafers on the hardwood floor, making plans for a future that now included neither Martin nor himself.

Behind him, Joel's voice, flat and clear: "Detective Mallick. It's Joel Beckman, at the Kessler house. We've got a second body."

The words carried across the dock. _A second body._ Martin had died. Cliff had been put here. Wrong shoes. Shallow water. Gash on the temple. Somebody had done this, and the somebody was one of the men breathing in the dark with him.

Doug looked up from the water. The four men had sorted themselves without discussing it. Paul on the dock with the flashlight angled down, as if he could keep doing one useful thing. Joel at the foot of the dock, phone still in his hand. Nate lowering himself beside Doug because sitting beside the body-finder was the only help he had. Tommy one step back from the wet boards, his phone dark again, his face turned toward the lake instead of Cliff. Even the way they were standing had started to matter.

Joel came back to them. "She's on her way. She said not to touch anything." His glance went to Paul's soaked khakis and Doug's wet right hand. "I told her we already touched things."

"I was trying to help him," Paul said, and his voice cracked on the last word. Not a sob. Just the sound of a measured man's measurement giving way for a second.

"I know," Joel said.

Nate sat down on the dock next to Doug. Not close, three feet between them, but on the dock because Doug was on the dock and that was the point. Nate's bare feet dangled over the edge, inches above the water where Cliff was floating. He realized it, muttered "shit," and pulled them back under him.

The wait settled over them in separate pieces. Paul kept the flashlight on the planks. Joel stood at the path, listening for tires. Tommy stayed where he'd stopped, too still, his hands opening and closing at his sides only when he thought nobody was looking. Doug kept seeing Cliff as he'd been in the great room two hours earlier, dry loafers on hardwood, Martin's scotch in his hand, talking about taxes and process like the future still existed.

Headlights appeared at the far end of the gravel driveway, distant at first, then the crunch of tires reached the house. Joel straightened. Paul's flashlight dropped to his side. Nate stayed seated for another second before pushing himself up.

Doug stood. His sweatpants were soaked, his knees ached from the dock, and his right hand still smelled like lake water and Cliff's skin. One last look at the water: hair drifting in the moonlight, loafers dark below the surface, the gash a black line. Then Doug turned away and walked up the dock toward the house and the headlights.
