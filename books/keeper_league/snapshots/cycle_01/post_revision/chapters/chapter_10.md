# Chapter 10

The kitchen in Martin Kessler's lake house had the sort of refrigerator that announced itself. Every forty minutes or so the compressor kicked on with a shudder that rattled the glasses in the cabinet above the sink. Mallick had timed it. Twenty years of police work had made her the kind of woman who timed refrigerators. She'd been sitting at the kitchen table since seven-thirty, and the machine had cycled four times, which put it close to ten o'clock, and she was nowhere.

Her leather notebook was open to a page that would have embarrassed her if any of her colleagues at the county office saw it. She'd drawn a grid. The columns were labeled with initials: PD, CH, NG, DW, TR, JB. The rows were labeled with the physical evidence. She'd filled in check marks, question marks, and, in two cases, small x's that meant confirmed absent. The grid looked like a badly designed crossword puzzle, and it told her less than she wanted and more than she expected.

She took off her reading glasses and set them on the table. The beaded chain caught on her coffee mug and she untangled it with the patience of a woman who'd been untangling the same chain from the same kinds of things for a decade.

Martin Kessler was dead of cardiac arrest. The medical examiner had been clear: hypertrophic cardiomyopathy, undiagnosed, aggravated by alcohol, sildenafil, and prolonged heat exposure from the hot tub. It was a natural death. People died like this. Healthy-looking men in their late forties dropped dead and their friends stood around looking stunned, and the medical explanation was usually sufficient.

But Martin Kessler's house had been rearranged while his body cooled.

She put the glasses back on and looked at her grid.

The body. Martin had been found in bed, and by the time the deputies arrived Saturday morning, he was on his back with his arms at his sides. Tidy. Composed. People who died in their sleep did not usually end up tidy and composed. They ended up in whatever position the last involuntary movement left them: twisted, half off a pillow, one arm thrown, a leg bent. Paul Decker, bless his insurance-adjusting heart, had corrected her on Saturday about which way Martin's head had been turned. "I think it was more to the left, actually, toward the window." The correction told her two things. Paul had seen the body in its original position. The body was no longer in that position when the deputies arrived. Someone had moved Martin and straightened him out, and Paul either didn't realize he'd just told her that or his training in accurate claims assessment was stronger than his instinct for self-preservation.

The wiping. She'd had to wait until Sunday morning for the BCA team to drive up from St. Paul, which was its own little joke on county resources. They found partial cleaning on the bedroom doorframe, the edge of the mattress, and the headboard. Partial. Whoever wiped got the right side of the headboard and missed the left. They got the doorframe at shoulder height and skipped the handle entirely. This was not professional cleaning. This was someone on hands and knees at three in the morning, wiping surfaces he'd touched, probably crying. The wiping matched the body work. Same room. Same panic. Same person. Paul.

The safe. The wall safe in Martin's study was hidden behind a lake landscape print, which was exactly where a lake house wall safe would be hidden. Martin had left the combination dial one tick off from locked, so anyone who jiggled it would find it open. Cliff Haywood had corrected her on Saturday about which room was the study when she'd pretended to confuse it with the half bath. "The study's the second door, not the first." She hadn't confused anything. Cliff's correction placed him in a room he had no stated reason to enter, and it placed him there with enough familiarity to know the order of the hallway doors. Financial records were inside the safe. Cliff was owed forty thousand dollars by the dead man. She didn't need to be the county's best detective to draw that line, and she wasn't the county's best detective. She was the county's only detective, which was a different thing.

The voicemail. Nate Griggs had spent forty-five minutes telling her about his podcast, his audience metrics, his recording setup, his microphone preferences, and the exact linguistic conventions of fantasy football trash talk, and in the middle of minute thirty-eight he'd referenced "the thing I left on Martin's phone, the voicemail, which, I mean, not that it matters, because it was just draft stuff." He'd said it with the desperation of a man who had rehearsed not mentioning a detail and then mentioned it anyway. Martin's phone, now in evidence, showed a deleted voicemail from Nate's number, received at 8:47 PM Friday and deleted at approximately 3:15 AM Saturday, after the death and before the call to police. Nate had deleted a message from a dead man's phone because the message contained the words "dead to me" in the context of fantasy football, and he was terrified a county detective in rural Minnesota would not understand the genre.

She took a sip of coffee. It was lukewarm and she drank it anyway.

The medications. The shared upstairs bathroom had a medicine cabinet that was too clean. Six men in their late forties sharing a bathroom for a weekend and the cabinet held a tube of toothpaste, a disposable razor, and a bottle of Advil. No prescription bottles, no allergy medication, no heartburn tablets, no sleep aids. Nothing. She'd opened the cabinet on Saturday afternoon and clocked the emptiness the way she'd clock a rug that had been freshly vacuumed in a house where nobody admitted to vacuuming. Someone had cleared that cabinet out, and the only reason to do that was if it had held pills somebody didn't want found. Given the age and habits of the men in this house, the missing bottles most likely said depression, anxiety, blood pressure, or sleep. They said struggle in a language the group did not like speaking out loud.

She wrote "meds: shame, not guilt" in her notebook and underlined it.

And the Viagra. Sildenafil in Martin's blood, and no Viagra anywhere in the house. Not in his bedroom, not in his bathroom, not in his luggage. The ME had confirmed the level was consistent with a single dose taken four to six hours before death. Whoever got rid of the rest had not started in the half bath where she'd heard the flush Saturday morning. He'd started in Martin's room, because that was where the pills would have been before they became a problem. Tommy Renaldi had come out of that bathroom looking like he'd run a half mile in a sauna. She'd asked if he was alright and he'd said, "Fine, yeah, fine," in the way people said fine when they were not fine and couldn't think fast enough to build a better lie.

Five.

She looked at the grid. Five separate acts of tampering. Five separate people. Five completely different motivations.

The body-mover was scared of physical evidence. The safe-opener was looking for money. The voicemail-deleter was scared of his own words. The medication-remover was scared of his own prescriptions. The Viagra-remover was scared he'd killed his friend with a joke.

She took off her glasses again. The chain clinked against the table.

She had been working this for thirty-six hours on the assumption that somebody had either killed Martin Kessler or helped cover up his killing. Every instinct she'd developed over twenty years told her that a scene this contaminated pointed to one plan, one motive, one person leaving his fingerprints all over the aftermath.

But this wasn't one cover-up. It was five.

And none of them knew about the others.

She sat with that for a moment and then laughed. It came out louder than she'd expected in the empty kitchen, a genuine helpless sound that she covered with her hand out of habit. There was no one to hear her, but the habit persisted, the way her cats still knocked things off the nightstand even though she'd stopped keeping things on the nightstand three years ago.

This wasn't a conspiracy. It was a committee that never met.

Five middle-aged men with bad knees, reading glasses, weak bladders, and private humiliations, sneaking through the same lake house between two and six in the morning. Paul in the bedroom straightening Martin's body while Nate worried over a voicemail that had seemed funny at eight-forty-seven and threatening by three-fifteen. Cliff in the study with the safe. Doug slipping his bottles out of the upstairs cabinet. Tommy carrying a problem out of Martin's room and into the half bath. All of them hearing the same floorboards, the same doors, the same third stair from the top that everybody knew to skip. All of them freezing when they heard somebody else in the dark. All of them deciding, with the confidence unique to frightened men, that the sound had to be nothing.

It was the most absurd case she'd ever worked. She'd once investigated a snowmobile reported stolen in Richville that turned out to have been repossessed by the dealership, reported stolen by the owner who was six months behind on payments, and then actually stolen by the dealership owner's nephew, who rode it across Otter Tail Lake in March and went through the ice forty yards from shore.

This was better. Panic made people fix only the part with their fingerprints on it. It meant the lies wouldn't line up. One man would remember a door, another a flush, a third would swear the hallway had been empty because he needed it to have been empty.

She got her casserole dish from the fridge. She'd made the green bean casserole Saturday morning, after the call came in about a death at a lake house on Elbow, and she'd brought it because that was what she did — somebody died, she cooked something, she brought it. The foil was still on top, crimped neatly at the edges. Nobody had touched it. Twenty years of bringing casseroles to grief and she could count on one hand the number that actually got eaten, but she kept making them because the gesture mattered, or because she liked the cooking, or because it was easier to walk into a room full of suspects holding a Pyrex dish than holding a badge.

She peeled back the foil and ate a spoonful standing at the counter. It was better the second day. It was always better the second day.

She put the dish back and sat down at the table. She wrote in her notebook: "Natural death likely. Separate tamperings. Rebuild bedroom/hall traffic." She underlined "bedroom/hall traffic" twice.

But the pen hovered.

The theory held everywhere except the dead man's bedroom.

Paul had Martin's body and the wipe marks. Nate's voicemail was deleted at 3:15 AM, after Martin died and before anyone called police. The missing Viagra had started in Martin's room before it ended in a toilet. Three separate errands touched the same space around the same dead man, and no one she'd spoken to had given her a clean account of who saw whom in that room, or in the hallway outside it, while the house was awake and hiding from itself.

That was not intuition. That was a witness gap.

She could explain the motives. She couldn't explain the gap.

She closed the notebook, put on her blazer, and went to find someone precise.

---

Mallick found Paul Decker on the back deck, sitting in one of the Adirondack chairs with a beer he wasn't drinking. The September evening had turned cool enough to see breath, and Paul had a fleece pullover zipped to his throat. The lake was a flat dark sheet beyond the tree line. A loon called once from far across the water, and the sound hung there until Mallick let the screen door bang shut behind her.

"Oh, Mr. Decker. I'm sorry. I didn't realize anyone was still out here."

She had realized. She'd seen him through the kitchen window for the last twenty minutes.

"Just getting some air," Paul said. He didn't look at her.

"I don't blame you. It's close in there tonight." She settled into the Adirondack chair beside his, which required a small negotiation with the chair and with gravity. "Would you look at that lake, though. My mother had a place up near Park Rapids when I was growing up. Not as nice as this. An A-frame, though. She loved an A-frame."

Paul glanced at her. She could see him calculating whether this was a conversation or an interview, whether Park Rapids had wandered in here honestly or tactically. Paul Decker was the kind of man who calculated because his profession ran on risk assessment and the accurate categorization of incoming information.

"Martin's parents built this one in the seventies," he said.

"You can tell. The paneling's got that warm look. Pine, is it?"

"Knotty pine."

"Knotty pine. There we go." She adjusted her blazer against the chill. "I should let you sit in peace. I just had a question, and it's probably nothing. You know me and my questions."

Paul set his beer on the armrest. His posture shifted by a degree somebody less attentive would've missed. The shoulders squared. The spine straightened against the chair's slant. "Sure."

"I was looking back over Saturday morning, the sequence of events. I wanted to clarify one thing about Martin's phone. Did you happen to notice anyone near it after you found him?"

Paul's brow creased. It was not the crease of a man building a lie. It was the crease of a man searching an honest memory and finding nothing there. "His phone? No. I wasn't thinking about his phone. I was with him. With Martin. I was trying to—" He stopped. "I was in the bedroom."

"Of course you were. You were right there with him. So if anybody picked it up after that, you didn't see it."

"No." He rubbed his thumb against the wet label on the bottle. "No, I didn't."

She nodded and looked out at the lake as if the answer had put a pleasant little check mark in her notebook.

"While I'm bothering you, the upstairs bathroom. The one you all share."

"What about it?"

"Did you happen to notice what was in the medicine cabinet before Saturday morning? Friday, say."

He frowned. "No. I don't think so. We've got our own stuff in our bags, mostly. The cabinet's more for communal things. Tylenol. Toothpaste. That kind of stuff."

"Sure. And you wouldn't know if somebody took prescription bottles out of there after Friday night."

"No." He paused. "Why? Is there something about the bathroom?"

"Probably not. I'm just checking boxes. You know how it is. Paperwork. My office wants every little thing accounted for."

Paul sat with that. A gust moved through the pines and then was gone.

"Can I ask you something?" he said.

"Of course."

"The safe in the study. Did somebody open it?"

She looked at him. This was useful. She hadn't asked about the safe, and he'd brought it up on his own.

"What makes you ask about the safe?"

"Martin always left the dial one click off. He did it every year. He'd close it and then not quite spin it all the way. We all knew." Paul took a breath. "If somebody was going through the house that night, they would've found it. I should've mentioned it before. I didn't think of it until today."

"And you didn't go into the study that night."

"No."

"But you knew about the safe."

"Everybody knew about the safe. Martin showed it to us years ago. He was proud of it. The combination, the mounting, the whole thing. He'd show somebody and say don't tell anyone, and then he'd show the next person. We'd all pretend we hadn't seen it before." The faintest smile touched his mouth and disappeared. "That was Martin."

"That's nice," she said, and she meant it.

"So you didn't go in the study. You didn't notice the medicine cabinet. And you weren't near Martin's phone."

Paul looked at her then. He was still holding the beer, but his hand had stopped moving. Condensation ran over his knuckles and he didn't seem to notice.

She could see him arrive at the pattern. She had asked about the phone, the medications, the safe. Three separate tamperings in three separate parts of the house. He had been honestly blank on all of them. Which meant she already knew he'd done something else. Which meant she knew the other acts had been done by different people.

"Detective," he said.

"Mm-hmm?"

"You're asking about different parts of the house."

"I am."

"Because you don't think it was one person."

She let the silence hold.

"Mr. Decker, I think you moved Martin's body. And I think you wiped the surfaces you touched during CPR."

He didn't deny it.

"I think you did that because your training told you your DNA at a death scene was an exposure you couldn't account for. And I think that's all you did."

Paul was quiet for a long moment. "You asked me about the phone," he said. "The medicine cabinet. The safe."

"I did."

"Three things in three different rooms." His voice had tightened, the qualifiers gone. "And I didn't know about any of them because they weren't mine. Those were three different people."

"At least."

He stared at the water. His jaw worked once. The beer was still in his hand. He hadn't drunk from it.

"I heard people that night," he said. "Moving around. Doors."

"And you told yourself it was the house."

"Yeah." He let out a breath. It hung in the cool air for a moment and dissolved. "It was a natural death. The ME said so."

"The ME said so."

"Then why are you still—"

"Because Martin's phone was handled at 3:15 in the morning, and those pills were taken out of his room before somebody flushed them. You were in that bedroom, or just outside it. So either people were moving through that space without you seeing them, or somebody's lying to me about when they were there."

Paul said nothing.

"I still think it was a natural death," she said. "But I don't close a file with that much traffic around the body unaccounted for."

He looked back out at the lake. Somewhere deeper in the house, a floorboard popped and settled.

"The casserole," Mallick said. "Did you try it?"

"What?"

"My casserole. The green bean. It's in the fridge."

"No." He blinked. "No, I didn't."

"It's better the second day," she said. She stood from the Adirondack chair with a small graceless push off both armrests that she'd stopped being embarrassed by years ago. "You should try it. There'll be a third day too, the way things are going."

She went back inside. The screen door banged behind her. Paul stayed on the deck with his warming beer and the knowledge that the detective knew exactly what he'd done and thought it was only one piece of a larger mess.

In the kitchen, Mallick sat back down at the table and opened her notebook. She wrote: "PD confirmed for body/surfaces only. Blank on phone, meds, safe. Bedroom traffic still unaccounted for."

She underlined "bedroom traffic" and looked at the page.

Paul had done what she needed him to do. He'd owned the body and the wiping, and he'd gone honestly blank on the phone, the medications, and the safe. That was useful.

What he hadn't given her was a sequence that made the bedroom make sense. The room at the center of the whole mess still had too many hands in it and not enough witness.

She could sleep on likely. She could not close on likely.

The house settled around her, creaking the way old lake houses creak when the temperature drops and the wood contracts. Through the kitchen window she could see the stone path that led through the pines and birches to the dock, eighty yards of pale stone in the dark, ending at the wooden dock that ran thirty feet into the lake. The dock was a dark line against the darker water.

Maybe in the morning the bedroom would make ordinary sense. Maybe Nate got in and out between Paul's trips to the hallway. Maybe Tommy did. Maybe grief had narrowed Paul's world to Martin's face and left the rest of the room unrecorded. All of that was possible.

Possible was not the same as accounted for.

She closed the notebook, rinsed her coffee mug, dried it, and set it on the counter next to the coffeemaker because she'd want it first thing and it was already clean and why open the cabinet twice. She pressed the foil down over the casserole dish with both hands, just to have a task for them, then turned off the kitchen light and went upstairs to the guest bedroom the deputies had cleared for her.

The third step from the top creaked under her weight, and she wondered how many of the five had remembered to skip it in the dark.
