# Chapter 9

Joel Beckman had been counting the hours since anyone last asked him about the argument. Fourteen. A personal best for this weekend, though the bar was roughly ankle-high. The previous record had been six hours, overnight, because even these people had to sleep eventually.

Joel sat at the kitchen table with a turkey sandwich he wasn't eating and watched Nate try to scrape the last of the mustard from the jar with a butter knife. Cliff was at the counter cutting an apple into mathematically precise slices. Tommy was on his second sandwich, maybe his third. Joel had stopped tracking. Paul sat across from him with a cup of coffee he kept warming in the microwave, the same cup he'd been nursing since morning, which Joel found either disciplined or insane.

Doug was eating a bowl of cereal, because Doug had apparently decided cereal was a valid Sunday lunch option and nobody had the energy to argue.

The chair at the head of the table was empty. Nobody had mentioned it. Everyone had noticed.

"Mustard's done," Nate announced, holding up the jar. "We're officially post-mustard. I want that noted for the record."

"There's more in the garage fridge," Paul said.

"The garage fridge. Of course. Because Martin had a condiment annex." Nate set the jar down and took a bite of his dry sandwich. After a while he said, "His garage fridge had better beer than my actual fridge. That's not a bit. That's just true."

The kitchen was quiet except for the refrigerator's cycling hum and Tommy's methodical chewing. Afternoon light came through the windows at a low angle and caught dust above the table. Martin's scotch, the Lagavulin, still sweetened the air in the great room beyond the kitchen, a smell Joel had started to associate with the specific flavor of being accused of something he didn't do.

"So what happens now?" Tommy asked the table. It came out in the tone coaches used when they already had an answer and wanted the room to arrive there on its own.

"Mallick said she'd be back this afternoon," Paul said. "She's at the station going through her notes."

"I mean with us. The house. All of it."

"Martin's estate will handle the house," Cliff said, and Joel heard the smoothness in it, the way Cliff's voice could flatten any question into terms of property and transfer. Cliff knew things about Martin's finances that the rest of them didn't. Or thought he did, anyway. For two days now Joel had watched him move through the house like he was appraising it.

"I just want to go home," Doug said quietly. Then he caught himself. "Not that. I mean. We all do. Obviously."

"Mallick hasn't cleared us yet," Joel said.

Tommy looked at him. "She hasn't cleared anybody, Beck."

"She's cleared some of you more than others."

"What's that supposed to mean?"

Instead of answering, Joel took a bite of the sandwich. The turkey was dry. Martin always bought the good deli turkey, the kind you had to ask for at the counter, and somehow it was already going stale. Two days. That was how long it took for the provisions to start outlasting the host.

Nate was watching him with the expression Joel had learned to recognize as the lead-in to a bit: eyebrows up, mouth slightly open, the podcaster warming up. This time Nate didn't launch. He just kept chewing and looked at Joel with something that wasn't quite sympathy and wasn't quite curiosity.

"Can I ask you something?" Nate said.

"You can ask."

"Are you, and this is just me genuinely asking, not for anything, just between us, gonna tell us what you and Martin were fighting about?"

The table went quiet. Not silent. Tommy was still chewing. The fridge was still humming. But the conversational temperature dropped in a way Joel could feel against his skin.

"I told Mallick it wasn't relevant."

"You told Mallick it wasn't relevant to her investigation," Paul said carefully. "That's not the same as it not being relevant."

"It's not relevant."

"Bullshit," Tommy said. Matter-of-fact, not angry. Coaching mode. "You show up Thursday night already pissed off, you're in his face at midnight screaming loud enough that I could hear it from the hot tub, and Martin's dead twelve hours later. That's relevant."

"Martin died of a cardiac event."

"We don't actually know that yet," Paul said.

"We effectively know that."

"Joel." Paul set his coffee down. The microwave-warmed cup made a soft thud on the table. "We've been here for three days. Mallick keeps coming back. Nobody's been cleared. And you're sitting here telling us the argument everybody keeps circling isn't relevant."

"The investigation isn't centered on the argument."

"It's centered on you," Cliff said without looking up from his apple. "Which is the same thing."

Joel pushed his plate away. The sandwich sat there with one bite missing, reproaching him with its dryness.

The truth was that Joel had been waiting for this conversation. He hadn't wanted it, but he'd been waiting for it, the way you waited for a dental appointment you'd already rescheduled twice. The argument was the one piece of information that still belonged to him and not to the investigation, and holding it had become physically exhausting in a way he hadn't expected. His jaw ached. His shoulders had been knotted since Saturday morning. For three nights he'd slept maybe three hours at a time in a guest room that smelled like Martin's fabric softener, lying in the dark and rehearsing both the speech he'd planned to give Martin and the speech he now had to give these men. Lately the two speeches kept bleeding into each other until he couldn't tell which one belonged to which audience.

"It's personal," he said.

"We know it's personal," Nate said. "That's why we're asking."

"You don't know what kind of personal."

"Then tell us what kind."

Doug was staring at his cereal. He hadn't said anything since the I want to go home admission and the quick retraction after it. Joel could still see Doug's eyes moving from one man to the next, the quiet surveillance Doug had perfected over twenty-five years of being the one nobody watched.

"Look," Tommy said, leaning forward, elbows on the table. "We're all stuck here. Dead friend, detective, same questions. And you're the piece that doesn't add up."

"I'm not the—"

"Yeah you are. You know you are." Tommy tapped the table once. "So say it. Whatever you got, just say it, because the rest of us sitting here pretending we're not wondering? That's bullshit."

Joel almost laughed. Pure Tommy — the halftime challenge, the coaching certainty. Tommy Renaldi, who had flushed evidence down a toilet while a detective stood on the other side of the door, lecturing Joel about transparency.

"Okay," Joel said. The word came out smaller than he intended. "You want to know what the argument was about."

"Yes," said four voices at roughly the same time. Doug's mouth moved but no sound came out.

Joel looked at the empty chair at the head of the table. Martin's chair. The chair where Martin Kessler had sat for twenty-five drafts, running the league, managing the group, making sure everyone had a drink, making sure Tommy came back every year, making sure the whole machine kept turning. The chair where the most generous, most controlling, most thoughtfully selfish man Joel had ever known had presided over a weekend that was equal parts reunion and performance.

"Martin was fucking my ex-wife," Joel said.

---

The sentence hit the table like a dropped plate.

Nate's sandwich stopped halfway to his mouth. Tommy's jaw paused mid-chew. Cliff set down the knife. Paul closed his eyes, not like a man flinching, more like a man bracing for impact he had sensed coming without knowing from where.

Doug looked straight at Joel. It was only a look, but Joel recognized it because he'd been seeing it in the mirror for a month now: the face of a person trying to understand a betrayal big enough to rearrange the furniture in his head.

"Karen?" Paul said.

"That's the one."

"Jesus Christ," Tommy said.

"For eight months. Since January." Joel heard how steady his own voice was and found it strange. In all the versions of this conversation he'd rehearsed, he'd been shouting by now. Instead the facts came out flat and precise, the way he delivered an opening statement, leading with the evidence and leaving the emotion for later. "She told me a month before the draft. We were in the parking lot of Ellie's dorm, dropping off her stuff for fall semester. Karen waited till Ellie went inside and then told me in the car. Slichter Hall parking lot. She said she wanted me to hear it from her."

"Hold on," Nate said. "A month before the draft. So you came here knowing."

"I came here to talk to him."

"You came here to confront him," Cliff said.

"I came here to talk to him. I had a whole plan for being calm about it. I was going to wait till Saturday morning, when everybody was hungover and slow, take him down to the dock, and ask him why."

"Instead you screamed at him Thursday night," Tommy said.

"I didn't scream."

"Beck, I heard you from the hot tub."

"I raised my voice. There's a legal distinction."

"There really isn't."

Joel rubbed his face with both hands. Legal precision was habit. Sarcasm was habit. Neither one helped him explain what it felt like to sit in a dorm parking lot watching your daughter wave from a second-floor window while your ex-wife told you that your friend of twenty-five years had been inside her since New Year's.

"Martin and Karen," Paul said. "How."

"How what?"

"How did it start? How long did you know? How did Karen even..."

"I don't know how it started. Karen said it started at a New Year's party. I wasn't there because I had Ellie that weekend. They ran into each other and one thing led to another and apparently one thing kept leading to eight months of another." Joel could hear the cross-examination tone taking over, sharp and clipped. "She said it wasn't planned. She said she didn't mean for it to happen. She said all the things people say when they absolutely meant for it to happen."

"Did Martin know you knew?" Doug asked.

His voice was so quiet everybody turned to him. It was the first interruption Doug had made all day.

"Yeah." Joel swallowed. "I called him three weeks before the draft and told him I knew. He said he was sorry. He said it was over. He said he'd ended it in August."

"Had he?" Cliff asked.

"I have no idea. Karen says yes. Martin said yes. I can't cross-examine a dead man."

"So Thursday night," Nate said. "That's what the argument was."

"Thursday night I got drunk and all the calm shit I'd planned to say turned into the actual shit I wanted to say. Which was louder and less articulate." Joel picked up the abandoned sandwich, looked at it, and put it back down. "I called him a piece of shit. Several times. In several configurations. He stood there and took it because that's what Martin did. He absorbed things. Hands in his pockets. Head tilted. Like he was listening to a weather report he found mildly interesting. I wanted to hit him so badly I could taste it."

"Did you?" Tommy asked.

"No. I went to bed. I lay there for three hours thinking of every goddamn thing I should've said and didn't, and then Paul banged on my door at two in the morning and Martin was dead."

The refrigerator cycled off. In the sudden absence of it, the house went properly quiet. Joel could hear the lake through the windows, that small steady lapping that didn't care what was happening inside.

Paul opened his eyes. Grief was still there. So was something new. Joel couldn't name it yet, but he could see the crack forming.

"I'm sorry," Paul said.

"Don't be sorry for me. Be sorry that he died before I could finish telling him what I thought of him." Joel's voice tightened. "That's the part that gets me. I had things to say to him. I drove up here with them loaded and ready. And then he just fucking died."

Tommy leaned back and blew air through his teeth. His gaze went to the ceiling, then to Joel, then to the empty chair.

"Shit, Beck," he said.

"Yeah."

---

By Joel's professional assessment, what followed was the most structurally bizarre conversation he'd ever participated in, and he'd spent sixteen years in Hennepin County criminal court.

They moved out to the deck because the kitchen had gotten too small for it, and because Nate said he needed air. Joel suspected that meant Nate needed room to process at the volume his personality required. Tommy came back from the garage fridge with beers, the good IPAs Martin hoarded. Paul brought his coffee. Doug took the Adirondack chair farthest from the center of things, his cereal still sitting inside and going soft in its bowl.

The September afternoon was golden and ridiculous. The lake was flat. The trees were just starting to turn at the edges. Two loons called somewhere out past the dock. It was exactly the kind of afternoon Martin built the whole weekend around, the kind that could almost trick a man into forgetting he was forty-seven and his friend was dead and a detective kept returning to ask how dead friends wound up in altered rooms.

Nate settled into a chair with his beer. "Okay. I have to ask a gross question."

"That's how we know you're alive," Joel said.

"No, I'm serious. Was this like... were they just fucking, or were they doing couple shit too? Because those are different categories of offense."

"Jesus Christ," Paul said, at the same time Tommy said, "Doesn't matter."

"What? They are. One is a drunken horrible thing. The other is your friend sticking his dick into your life for eight months."

"Please don't phrase it like that," Paul said.

"How would you like it phrased?"

"I'd like not to hear 'sticking his dick into your life.'"

"I don't know the details," Joel said. "I didn't ask whether he left a toothbrush there."

Tommy pointed his bottle at Nate. "Doesn't matter anyway."

"It obviously matters."

"No, it doesn't." Tommy sat forward. "You don't fuck your friend's ex-wife. That's the whole rule. I don't care if they've been divorced six months or six years. I don't care who filed. I don't care if she sent engraved invitations. You don't put your dick there. End of rule."

Cliff leaned against the railing with his apple. The careful slices were gone. He was eating the core now like a man who didn't believe in waste. "She's his ex-wife, Tommy. Not a burial plot."

Tommy turned. "What the fuck does that mean?"

"It means Joel doesn't own her. None of us own her. There's a difference between betrayal and property law."

"Nobody said property. I said friendship."

Nate held up both hands. "I think there's still a waiting period, though. Minimum. There has to be. You can't just see divorce papers and go, great, green light, let's start fucking."

"That's not better," Paul said.

"It's worse, actually," Joel said.

"Thank you," Paul said.

"Look, I'm not defending it," Nate said. "I'm trying to figure out the penalty structure."

"The penalty structure is you spend the rest of your life as the asshole who did it," Tommy said.

"Okay, but there are levels," Nate said.

"There aren't," Tommy said.

"There are," Cliff said, talking over him. "Eight months isn't the same as one night."

"They're both bad," Tommy snapped. "One's just more bad."

Joel sat there listening to five men he'd known for a quarter century argue about the ethics of his dead friend sleeping with his ex-wife, and something in his chest loosened with one small click. Not because anyone had found the right language. Nobody had. Tommy's moral certainty was blunt and a little performative. Cliff was doing his transaction thing, reducing damage until it fit a frame he liked. Nate was trying to turn pain into a rules discussion because rules were safer than feelings. But they were doing it out loud. Joel's humiliation had stopped being secret. It was on the table now, vulgar and undeniable, and for the first time in a month someone besides Karen and his therapist knew the exact shape of it.

"What about Karen?" Nate asked. "She gets a vote in this. She's not just the field everybody's arguing over. She chose it too."

"Karen's choices are Karen's business," Joel said. "I'm not mad at Karen."

Nate blinked. "You're not?"

"No. We're divorced. She doesn't owe me loyalty. She can sleep with whoever she wants. Martin was the one who sat at my table for twenty-five years and then put his cock in my ex-wife anyway."

Paul made a low sound in his throat and took a drink of coffee that had to be cold by now. Tommy stared out at the lake like he was trying to find somewhere to throw the whole conversation. Even Cliff lost his smoothness for half a second.

"Okay," Nate said. "Yeah. When you say it like that, the hearing gets very simple."

Joel laughed before he meant to. It surprised all of them, him most of all. "Hearing. Great. Good. Do I get compensatory picks or what?"

That got Nate first, a short bark that escaped before he could stop it. Tommy followed with a low laugh of his own. Even Paul's mouth twitched. Cliff smiled the thin smile of a man who appreciated a structure holding under stress. Joel folded over Martin's beer and laughed into the neck of the bottle, brief and helpless. Foam slicked his thumb. He wiped it on his jeans and took a swallow while the deck kept going, because that was easier than acting like the joke hadn't gotten him.

"The Kessler Rule," Nate said, warming to it against his better judgment. "There has to be a Kessler Rule."

"There does," Tommy said immediately. "You touch a friend's ex-wife, you lose all picks for a year and everybody gets to call you a cunt to your face forever."

Paul closed his eyes. "That's not a bylaw."

"It should be."

Cliff bit off the last clean piece of apple. "Does it apply to ex-girlfriends? Because if we're drafting sex legislation, scope matters."

Tommy stared at him. "You hear yourself, right?"

"I hear a vague law."

"I hear you being an asshole."

"No, you hear precision," Cliff said. "There's a difference."

Doug had barely moved. He sat with his hands folded in his lap, looking not at whoever was speaking but at Joel, with the steady attention of a man who knew something about having the life you thought you had fall away from under you in a single conversation.

"You know the worst part?" Joel said.

"Please say yes, because I have several candidates," Nate said.

"The worst part is I came here to fight with him. I planned the whole weekend around it. I drove four hours with a speech in my head. And then he died in his sleep like he just opted out. Like he looked at the whole thing and decided, no, I don't feel like sitting for this one."

Doug looked down, then back up. "That's really fucked up," he said.

Nobody stepped on the line. Nobody laughed. Doug had finished a sentence and the deck went quiet around it.

"Yeah," Joel said. "It is."

Tommy cracked his knuckles, sharp in the afternoon air. "Martin was wrong. I loved him. But he knew what he was doing or he wouldn't have hid it."

"Martin wasn't a bad person," Paul said. The qualification sounded automatic, almost exhausted. "He did a bad thing. Those aren't the same."

"Maybe not," Tommy said. "But this one counts."

Cliff tossed the apple core into the woods. "Come on. He had an affair with a divorced woman. That's ugly. It's not treason."

Tommy turned so fast his chair legs scraped wood. "Treason?"

"What would you like me to call it?"

"I'd like you not to act like Beck's pissed because somebody misfiled paperwork."

"I'm not acting like anything." Cliff's voice stayed level, which only made it worse. "I'm saying the man is dead. Joel's still here. Karen's still in Minneapolis. And now everybody wants to hold a tribunal like Martin broke into Beckman's house and fucked her on the kitchen counter."

Joel laughed once, without humor. "That would've at least saved time."

"Jesus Christ," Paul said.

"No, seriously," Cliff said. "What do you want here? You want him dug up so Tommy can yell at the corpse? You want us to strip his name off the trophy? Fine. Say it. But don't act like sleeping with an ex-wife is the same as stabbing a guy in an alley."

Tommy stood. "Nobody said that either. You keep doing this thing where you make it tiny by comparing it to something worse."

"I'm not the lawyer here."

"Then stop talking like one."

Paul had been staring into his coffee. Now he set the mug on the deck rail with a small hard click and stood up too. That got everybody quiet faster than Tommy's volume had.

"He was my best friend," Paul said.

No one answered.

"And he hid this from me too." The qualifiers had dropped from Paul's voice. "I've been defending him for two days and I don't even know what I was defending." He looked straight at Cliff. "I'm done with that."

Cliff's eyes narrowed. "Paul."

"No." Paul's voice stayed measured but stripped. "Next time Mallick asks about Thursday night, I'm telling her why. I'm not covering for him."

The lake made a soft, stupid sound against the dock posts.

Cliff let out a short laugh. "That's insane. You're going to hand the detective motive on a plate because you're mad at a dead friend?"

"I'm going to stop lying for one."

Tommy nodded once. "Good."

Nate looked from Paul to Joel and back again. The performance dropped clean off his face. "I think that's right," he said quietly. "Honestly."

Cliff turned to Joel. "That's what you want? You want Karen's name in a police notebook because everybody out here suddenly discovered a code?"

Joel looked at Paul at the railing, Tommy standing with his beer and his jaw set, Nate smaller than usual in his chair, Doug still watching like witness had finally become a job instead of a personality trait. Whatever answer the group had been giving all weekend, it was gone now. The room had split in directions Martin would have managed if he were still alive.

"I want it to stop sounding like I picked a fight for no reason," Joel said. "After that, I don't really give a shit how tidy it is."

For the first time all afternoon, Cliff had nothing smooth ready. He grabbed his beer off the arm of the chair and went back inside without offering to get anyone else one.

Nobody called after him.

Paul stayed at the rail. Tommy sat back down, but he turned his chair toward Joel instead of toward the lake. Nate stared at his bottle. Doug gave Joel a single nod.

Joel kept the bottle in his hand and let the quiet sit there. The deck ticked in the sun. The lake knocked softly at the dock posts. Nobody asked him another question.

The loons called again, farther out this time. Whatever happened when Mallick came back, it wasn't going to be the same six men handing her the same story.
