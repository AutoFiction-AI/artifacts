# Chapter 6

Nate Griggs had been holding his phone toward the basement ceiling for three minutes, tilting it at different angles like a man trying to divine water from a satellite that had better things to do. The search page loaded in pieces: Google logo first, then a white void, then the top result emerging letter by letter like a telegram from someone who wanted him to suffer.

*Can police recover deleted voicemails from a phone*

The first result was a legal advice subreddit, which was exactly the kind of source a serious person would not consult and exactly the kind of source Nate was going to read with the focus of a man studying his own biopsy results. He thumbed it open. The page loaded in strips, each paragraph arriving on its own schedule.

*Depends on carrier. Verizon keeps metadata (call time, duration, number) for 1-2 years. Actual voicemail audio is generally retained on their servers for 30-40 days after the user deletes it. Law enforcement can access recordings through a subpoena or court order.*

Thirty to forty days. He was on day one.

Nate sat down on the pull-out sofa. The mattress was thin enough that the metal frame bit into the backs of his thighs through the fabric. Doug's duffel bag sat on the other pull-out, zipped tight as a vacuum seal. Doug packed like a man trying to prove he took up no space in the world. The lake was audible through the small casement windows, a steady lapping that Nate's brain, helpfully, converted into a countdown.

The voicemail. The goddamn voicemail.

He'd left it around eight on Friday evening, after the draft was done and they were all on the deck with beers and Martin was doing his thing where he pretended to be above trash talk by simply not responding to trash talk, which was Martin's most infuriating move and had been for twenty-five years. Nate had taken his phone to the basement bathroom, the one with the shower that smelled like lake water and old PVC, and he'd called Martin's cell and left a voicemail because that was their thing, the bit, leaving long ranting voicemails about each other's draft picks because who leaves voicemails anymore, that was the joke. They were the last seven guys in America who used voicemail on purpose.

Six guys. Six.

He'd gone up to Martin's room two hours ago, thinking he could check the phone one more time, make sure the deletion had taken, see if there was a "recently deleted" folder he'd missed in the dark at three-thirty in the morning. The nightstand had been bare. Martin's phone, charger cord, watch, wallet: all of it bagged and taken after the deputies finally got there, well after the seven a.m. call to dispatch. The bedroom door was shut now with a strip of yellow tape across it that looked like it came from a hardware store. Martin's phone was in an evidence room somewhere in Detroit Lakes, and Nate couldn't check whether the deletion had actually worked, couldn't verify that the voicemail wasn't sitting in some purgatory folder waiting for a detective with a subpoena to bring it back to life.

Which brought him to the Quora answer.

*Great question! Even after a user deletes a voicemail, carriers typically retain the audio file for a minimum of 30 days. Standard law enforcement procedure involves serving a preservation request followed by a subpoena, with typical turnaround of 5-10 business days.*

Five business days. Next Friday. Mallick could have the voicemail playing through her car speakers by next Friday.

The voicemail. What he'd actually said. He could hear it in his own voice, the podcast cadence, the buildup rhythm he used when he was doing his weekly "Roster Autopsy" segment, and the word *autopsy* was another one he wished he could take back from the universe:

*Kessler. Martin. My guy. I've been staring at your roster for like fifteen minutes and I am — I'm composing your obituary right now. This team is dead. You are dead to me, Kessler. Dead. You took Addison in the fourth — the fourth, Martin — when Nico Collins was right there, just sitting there, staring at you, begging you to take him, and you went with a guy who had six targets last week. Six. That's not a wide receiver, dude, that's a bystander at the scene of a crime. That's a guy who showed up to the stadium and accidentally ended up on the field. And then — AND THEN — you took the Vikings defense in the seventh round. A defense. In the seventh. I can't — I actually can't with you right now. I'm at your funeral, man. I am standing at the podium delivering the eulogy and I'm telling people, 'He was a good man. A generous man. And he took a defense in the seventh round and deserved everything he got.' And I haven't even — shit, wait — the Pacheco pick, which I'm saving for the pod, because I want to do a full segment on how a man murders his own season in front of six witnesses and nobody intervenes. Your roster is a corpse, Kessler. A goddamn corpse. I'm performing the autopsy Monday morning. Rest in peace, your playoff hopes, 2024 to 2024.*

It went on. It went on for another ninety seconds after that. He'd riffed on Martin's backup quarterback as a "death wish," called the bench "a graveyard," circled back to the Addison pick a second time because he'd lost track, and signed off with "this message will self-destruct, much like your season and your legacy and honestly your dignity as a human being." He'd said *dead* four times, *kill* twice, *murder* once, *funeral* once, *obituary* once, *corpse* twice, and *rest in peace* at the end like a sign-off for a man who was going to be dead in six hours.

Nate put his phone face-down on the mattress and pressed his palms against his eyes. He could still see the afterimage of the search results through his eyelids. Thirty to forty days. The voicemail was out there, sitting on a server in some Verizon data center in Iowa or wherever they kept their servers, and he had sent it there himself when he'd deleted it from Martin's phone in the dark at three-thirty in the morning, thinking that was sufficient, because he was a fantasy sports podcaster with twelve hundred subscribers, not a person with a working knowledge of telecommunications infrastructure.

What he'd done at three-thirty in the morning: he'd gone into Martin's room, where Martin was still in the bed, still dead, his arm hanging off the edge at the angle that had made Nate check on him in the first place. He'd picked up Martin's phone from the nightstand, the screen protector cracked in one corner, the home screen still a photo of the lake at sunset, and his hands had been shaking so badly he'd had to use both thumbs to navigate the voicemail app. He'd found the message, his own name next to it, 8:17 PM, three minutes forty-one seconds. He'd pressed delete. The phone had asked him to confirm. He'd confirmed. And then he'd stood there in the dark room next to his friend's body and thought, with the involuntary clarity of a man who could not turn off the part of his brain that formatted experience into content: *this would make an incredible episode.*

He hated himself for that thought. Even the self-loathing came packaged like a segment outline: confession, vulnerability, producer-Nate whispering that honesty played. His friend was dead in a lake house in Becker County and Nate's brain was still pitching beats for a show with a 2.3-star rating on Apple Podcasts and four hundred dollars a month in revenue before Mia's student loans.

His phone buzzed. A text from Mia, timestamped six hours ago: *How's the weekend going? Miss you.*

He hadn't answered. He hadn't answered because what was he going to say, *Martin's dead and there's a detective upstairs who brought a casserole and I committed a felony at three in the morning*? He left the phone on the mattress.

The ceiling above him creaked. Someone crossed the kitchen. A cabinet door clicked shut, then a mug touched the counter upstairs. Nate caught himself sorting the sounds into layers, footsteps, ceramic, lake water under all of it, the way he did when he was listening for clean tape, and that made him angrier than the sounds themselves.

He needed to go upstairs. He needed better signal. He needed to find out if Verizon kept the actual audio or just the call metadata, because if it was only metadata they'd see a three-minute call from Nate's number to Martin's at 8:17 PM and that was nothing, that was friends calling friends. If they had the audio file with Nate saying *you are dead to me, Kessler* and *I'm at your funeral,* that was something else.

He stood up too fast. Head rush. Six beers and a scotch last night, maybe ninety minutes of sleep total, and twelve hours of adrenaline working on him like a stimulant he hadn't consented to. His "Griggs on the Grid" cap was on the floor by the sofa, the logo that Paul once called "the saddest thing in podcasting." He picked it up, put it on, took it off. The cap was a prop, part of the show, and the show was the last thing he needed right now, but his hand wanted it the way his mouth wanted to narrate and his brain wanted to riff. It was all the same muscle, and the muscle did not know how to be still.

He went upstairs. The third step creaked. He always forgot about the third step and he always stepped on it, twenty-five years of stepping on that step, and now there would not be a twenty-sixth year of anything in this house.

Paul was in the kitchen, standing at the counter with a glass of water and the particular expression of a man trying to project that he was simply a man drinking water and not a man who had rearranged a crime scene nine hours ago. Nate recognized the expression because his own face was wearing something similar.

"Hey," Nate said.

"Hey." Paul took a sip. "You doing all right?"

"Yeah. No. I mean, yeah." He held up his phone. "Trying to get a hold of Mia, but the signal down there, it's nothing. I don't know how Martin dealt with it." He heard himself use past tense and, absurdly, another part of him tagged it as usable. He wanted to walk into the lake.

"Deck's better," Paul said. "Or up by the road."

"Right. The deck. Yeah."

Nate didn't move toward the deck. He stood in the kitchen doorway and looked at Paul drinking his water, and he thought about asking Paul what carrier Martin used, whether voicemail servers kept audio after deletion, whether deleting a voicemail from a dead man's phone counted as destroying evidence. He asked none of it. Martin was at a morgue in Detroit Lakes, being examined by somebody who would decide cause of death and whether the scene had been touched.

"I'm gonna go to the deck," Nate said.

"OK," Paul said.

The deck was getting cold. September in northern Minnesota did that. The afternoon had been golden and warm, the lake flat and bright, and then the sun dropped behind the white pines and suddenly you needed a hoodie and your breath was considering becoming visible. Nate sat in one of the Adirondack chairs and held his phone out and watched the signal bars flicker between one and two.

He loaded a new search: *Verizon voicemail retention policy law enforcement subpoena*

The results confirmed what the Quora answer had promised. All major carriers retained voicemail audio for a minimum of thirty days. Law enforcement access required a subpoena with a standard turnaround of five to ten business days. One article from an industry blog noted that some carriers had begun retaining data for up to ninety days as part of updated compliance protocols.

Ninety days.

Nate put the phone in his lap and looked at the lake. A loon called from across the water, clean and lonely enough that Nate immediately heard how it would sound under narration. He thought about how the voicemail would sound played back in a courtroom. He thought about how it would sound on a true crime podcast, not his podcast, a real one, one with listeners, one that would play the recording with the key phrases captioned on screen:

*"You are dead to me, Kessler. Dead."*

*"I'm at your funeral, man."*

*"Your roster is a goddamn corpse."*

And a narrator, in that measured true-crime voice that every podcast had now: *Six hours later, Martin Kessler was found dead in his bed. The voicemail was discovered missing from his phone.*

Nate could hear the episode. He could hear the ad breaks. He could hear the Reddit thread.

The sliding glass door opened behind him. He flinched hard enough to knock his phone off the armrest.

"Mr. Griggs?" It was Mallick. She was holding a coffee mug and smiling like a woman who'd run into a neighbor at the co-op. "I was hoping we could have a little chat, if you're not in the middle of something. Oh, did you drop your phone? These chairs, they're beautiful but the arms are so narrow."

---

She'd set up at the kitchen table. Her leather notebook was open, her reading glasses were on the beaded chain around her neck, and the green bean casserole she'd brought that morning sat on the counter behind her, still covered in foil and untouched. Nobody was going to eat a casserole brought by a detective to the house where their friend had just died. Also because it was green bean casserole and nobody actually liked green bean casserole.

"Sit, sit," Mallick said, pulling out the chair across from her. "I'm so sorry to bother you. I know this has been just an awful day. Can I get you anything? I think there's still coffee in the pot. Oh, shoot. I think I turned it off. Did I turn it off? I'm always doing that at the station, Debbie gets so mad at me."

"I'm good," Nate said. He sat down. The chair legs scraped on the floor and the sound was too loud for the room.

"So." She put her reading glasses on and looked at her notebook. "Nathan Griggs. You go by Nate?"

"Nate. Yeah. Just Nate."

"And you mentioned you're a podcaster? That's so neat. What's the show, 'Griggs on the Go'?"

"'The Grid.' 'Griggs on the Grid.' It's a fantasy football show. Draft strategy, weekly matchups, start-sit decisions, that sort of thing."

"Oh, I'm sorry, 'the Grid.' Like the football field? The lines?"

"Sort of. More like the roster grid, the player, it's a spreadsheet thing, kind of." He watched her write something in her notebook. She'd gotten the name wrong and he'd corrected her and she'd written something down and Joel had told them all, two hours ago, that she did this with everyone, the wrong detail followed by the correction followed by the note.

"How fun," Mallick said. "My nephew Tyler does something like that, I think. The thing where you pick the players?"

"That's it. Yeah. So you draft real NFL players onto a fake roster, and then their real-game stats generate points for your team. It's a huge industry. Thirty-three million Americans play. I've done a twelve-part series on the market size alone. Not that that matters." He heard himself doing it, giving her the long version because silence made his skin itch. Mallick sat there with her coffee and let him keep going.

"Thirty-three million," Mallick said. "My goodness. And you all came up here to do that? To pick your teams?"

"Twenty-five years. Same weekend, same house, same guys. Martin set it up our first year out of college and it became the thing. The one thing nobody missed." He stopped. "Well. Martin's the one who can't. Who didn't." He stopped again. Death had always been show language for him, a segment title, a tagline, and now every version of the word felt filthy.

"Twenty-five years," Mallick said, and it sounded warm rather than evidentiary. "That's real loyalty. And how did the draft go? Thursday night?"

"Good. It was fun. The usual, guys arguing over picks, Tommy yelling, Joel trying to rules-lawyer every trade. Cliff doing Cliff stuff where he offers you something that sounds great until you realize you just gave up your first-round pick for a backup tight end and a promise."

"And Mr. Kessler? Did he have a good draft?"

Nate laughed before he could stop it, reflexive and genuine, and then heard it land in the kitchen and it was wrong, a man laughing about the draft performance of a man who was in a morgue. "No. He had a terrible draft. Martin always had a terrible draft and then somehow made the playoffs anyway, which, I've actually done the numbers on this on the pod, his projected finish versus his actual finish over twenty-five years, and the variance is..."

"Oh, that's thorough. What made it so bad? His draft?"

He could feel the slide starting. The things that made Martin's draft terrible were the same things he'd ranted about in the voicemail, and silence was still the one skill Nate Griggs had never learned.

"He reached for guys. He always reached. He took Addison in the fourth when there was, look, clear value sitting there on the board, and he went with the name he recognized instead of the guy the numbers said to take. Then a defense in the seventh. That's a cardinal sin. You don't draft a defense until the last two rounds. Everyone knows that. And the kicker in the tenth. I mean, we all gave him shit about it. That's the whole point. The draft isn't just about picking players. It's..." He caught himself. His brain had been accelerating toward *the trash talk is the game, the insults are the love language, when I said his team was dead that was just...* "It's the arguing. The competition. It's the only time most of us see each other."

Mallick nodded, sipping coffee. "Now, you mentioned you record things for your show. Were you recording Thursday night?"

"Parts of it. No, it wasn't a hidden camera situation. Ambient draft audio, reactions, the group ripping each other. I always get some tape for the pod. Martin was fine with it. Everyone was fine with it." He heard the extra explanation coming out of him and couldn't stop it. Anxiety was already three sentences ahead of the conversation.

"Oh, that's wonderful. We might want to have a listen to that, if you wouldn't mind."

"Sure. Absolutely. It's just the draft. It's just us being us."

"Great." She made a note. "And your phone, was that the only way you'd be in touch with Mr. Kessler? Texting, calling, that kind of thing?"

"Yeah, texts mostly. We've got a group chat that's been going for, I mean, years. Thousands of messages. Draft talk, trades, the league standings. The group chat is basically the league and the league is basically the group chat."

"Mm-hmm. And phone calls?"

"Less. We're all texters mostly. Sometimes you'd call to say come out to the deck, or ask somebody to bring more ice up from the basement, that kind of thing."

"So no calls to Mr. Kessler that Friday? The day before?"

The question arrived clean and Nate could feel every door in it, the three or four ways he could answer that would be short, functional, and safe: *Maybe, I don't remember. Probably a quick one. Nothing important.* He had clean exits and his mouth walked past all of them because Nate's mouth did not take direction from the part of his brain that understood consequences.

"I mean — yeah, probably, at some point? We were all here, you call people. And voicemails, that's — we do this thing. A voicemail thing. Where you leave long, stupid voicemails about each other's picks, because nobody leaves voicemails anymore, right? So it's this ironic — it's a bit. Commentary on the medium. You leave a three-minute rant about somebody's terrible roster because the format itself is funny, because voicemail is basically a dead—" He heard it, the word *dead* arriving in the sentence like a siren, and he tried to steer around it. "—a thing nobody uses. So we use it. As a joke."

"That is funny," Mallick said. She was looking at him with the warm, full attention of a woman who had absolutely nowhere to be. She was not writing anything, which was either because what he was saying didn't matter or because she was letting him build speed, and he didn't know which was worse.

"And the language — this is the thing I want to explain, because it sounds — if you heard it without context, the language we use, it's — you say things like 'your team's done,' 'you're finished,' 'rest in peace your season,' and that's not — when I said — look, when you say a guy's roster is dead, you don't mean—"

He stopped. His hands were frozen mid-gesture, both of them, in the air between him and the detective, shaped around a word he was trying to take back. He'd said *when I said it.* Not *when you say it.* Not *when someone says it.* *When I said it.* Past tense. First person. A man describing a thing he had done.

Mallick set her coffee mug down on the table. She picked up her pen.

"I'm sorry," she said. Her voice was the same voice it had been, the neighborly voice, the my-nephew-plays-that voice. "When you said his roster was dead? To Mr. Kessler?"

"That's just how we — everyone says that—"

"Oh, sure, sure. I'm just, I want to make sure my notes are right. I didn't have any messages from you to Mr. Kessler that evening. But you're saying there was a voicemail?"

The kitchen went quiet. Through the window above the sink, the last light sat flat on the lake. Mallick's notebook was open to a page of small, even handwriting, and Nate had the sudden, sick certainty that every lawyer on earth would tell him the same thing right now: stop talking. He was already past that.

"I left a voicemail," Nate said. "Friday night. On his phone. I was — it was a bit on his draft. How bad his picks were. And I said things like 'you're dead to me' and 'I'm at your funeral' and 'your roster is a goddamn corpse,' and that's just — that's how you talk about fantasy football. That's the dialect. Thirty-three million people talk like that."

"I understand," Mallick said. She was writing.

"And I deleted it."

He said it fast, like getting the worst part out before his nerve caught up. Once it was in the room, the rest followed.

"I went to his room. After we found him. Early, like three-thirty in the morning. And I went on his phone and I deleted the voicemail. Because I heard it back in my head after he died and it was — I'm saying 'you're dead to me' and 'I'm at your funeral,' and that's just sports, that's just the game, but if someone listened to it who didn't know—"

"Without the fantasy football context," Mallick said.

"Yes. Exactly. That's exactly it. Without context, it sounds insane. So I went in and deleted it. That's what I did."

Mallick's pen stopped moving. She looked at what she'd written, then looked at him over her reading glasses.

"Mr. Griggs, I just want to make sure I have this right. You entered Mr. Kessler's room after he was found deceased, you accessed his phone, and you deleted a voicemail you had left earlier that evening. Is that accurate?"

Put that way, it sounded like exactly what it was: a man going into a dead man's room and tampering with a dead man's phone. Five minutes of context, gone in one sentence.

"It was a fantasy football voicemail," Nate said. His voice was smaller now. The performance was over. The pacing, the hand gestures, the build. The sentences were short and had nowhere to go. "Three minutes. Just me being an idiot about his picks. That's all it was."

Mallick closed her notebook. Not abruptly. She closed it the way you'd set down a book at the end of a chapter, the stopping point already chosen. She took off her reading glasses and let them hang on the beaded chain.

"Well," she said. "I really do appreciate you telling me that, Nate. Thank you."

She used his first name. She hadn't used his first name before. He wasn't sure whether that was better or worse and he couldn't ask because asking would also become information, and everything he said to this woman became information, and the only way to stop giving her information was to stop talking, and stopping was the one thing he had never learned to do.

"I think we might need to follow up on a few things," she said. She stood and took her mug to the sink. "Oh, can I warm you up some coffee? Shoot, I did turn it off. I'm sorry about that. I always do that."

Nate sat at the kitchen table and looked at the foil-covered casserole on the counter and the draft board still hanging on the great room wall, Martin's picks circled in red marker.

His phone pressed against his thigh. He still hadn't answered Mia's text. Through the kitchen window the lake was going dark, the amber light pulling off the surface, and from somewhere down the hall came the scratch of Mallick's pen starting up again.
