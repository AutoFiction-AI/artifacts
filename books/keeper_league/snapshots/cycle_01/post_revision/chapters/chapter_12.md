# Chapter 12

Tommy was alone on the deck for the first time since Saturday morning. Monday afternoon, sun low enough to turn the lake copper, and maybe fifteen minutes before somebody came out to check on him or ask if he wanted another round of Mallick's questions or start talking about Martin in that careful tone everybody had found since the body came out of the bedroom.

He had dragged an Adirondack chair to the rail so he could put his feet up and look at the water. A warm can of Busch Light sat in both hands. He wasn't drinking it. He was palming it the way he palmed a football before a snap drill, not because the beer mattered but because empty hands felt worse.

Two nights since Martin. Not even three full days, and the house already felt like a locker room after a loss nobody knew how to explain. Men kept picking up cups, setting them down, starting sentences, dropping them halfway through. Paul had spent the morning in Martin's study doing whatever Paul did when he was processing, which usually meant turning grief into categories. Joel was somewhere in the house, still angry at a man who couldn't catch any more of it. Doug had been on the couch with the same magazine page since breakfast. Nate was in the basement with his phone, which Tommy was trying not to think about.

Mallick had gone back to Detroit Lakes. Working the case, supposedly. Tommy didn't buy it. That woman filed people the way Tommy filed opponent tendencies. Tells, mistakes, weak side, strong side. She'd be back.

The screen door opened and banged shut behind him.

"Hey." Cliff's voice. Smooth as ever. "Brought you one."

A cold Busch Light came into Tommy's peripheral vision. Cliff stood there freshly shaved and too put together for a lake house, like he'd stepped out to show a shoreline lot instead of sit on a dead friend's deck.

"Thanks." Tommy took the cold can, set the warm one on the boards, and cracked it. Cliff lowered himself into the chair beside him, closer than he usually sat.

"Hell of a view," Cliff said.

"Yeah."

"Martin always had taste. I'll give him that. The man knew property. The rest of life, maybe less."

Tommy drank. Cold enough to register.

"You doing okay?" Cliff asked.

"I'm fine."

"You look like hell."

"I look like a guy who's been trapped in this house since Saturday with a dead friend and a detective who thinks I redecorated in my sleep. So yeah. Hell."

Cliff laughed, not the showroom laugh, an actual short bark. "Fair. Listen, I need to talk to you about something."

"Okay."

"It should've been a simple conversation. Now it's not."

Tommy turned toward him. Cliff had gone into work posture, shoulders pitched forward, beer on his knee, listening face already in place. Tommy had watched him do this at cookouts and draft nights and open houses. Cliff sold things. That was the center of him. He sold condos, he sold confidence, and right now he was trying to sell Tommy a version of what came next.

"What conversation?"

"The estate. The forty Martin owed me."

Tommy shrugged. "Call a lawyer. That's not me."

"Sure, and I will. But by the time probate finishes, I'll have more fires than I can put out." Cliff took a drink. "You've got weight with this group, whether you like hearing it or not. If you tell Paul Martin meant to square me up, if you tell Joel this isn't me inventing a debt over a corpse, that changes the temperature."

"We were friends."

"You were close in a way the rest of us weren't." Cliff lifted a hand. "Not that. I mean he watched out for you. Everybody saw it. He made sure you came back every year, even when you tried to skip. You two had your own side conversations. Out here, usually."

"What's your point?"

"My point is you say Martin meant to pay me, people believe it faster." Cliff looked back at the lake. "I've got three projects stalled and one note already in default. Forty grand matters right now."

Tommy watched the water. A north breeze had come up and chopped the copper light into pieces. Pine, lake water, garlic from inside. Paul was probably making pasta again, because feeding people was how Paul pretended he wasn't panicking.

"I can talk to Paul," Tommy said. "If Martin owed you the money, he owed you the money."

"He did."

"Then it's not a hard conversation."

Cliff nodded. Silence sat between them longer than Cliff usually let silence sit.

"There's one more piece," he said.

Tommy waited.

"Sunday morning I went into Martin's study. The safe was practically open. Dial one click off."

"Okay."

"I was looking for paper on the debt. Found some. Found an envelope too."

Tommy tightened his grip on the can. "What envelope."

"Manila. Marked RT."

The lake kept moving. Somewhere past the dock, a loon called. Inside the house the refrigerator kicked on with that old mechanical groan Martin had been promising to fix for fifteen years.

"Doesn't mean anything to me," Tommy said.

"Yeah. I figured you'd say that." Cliff's voice stayed smooth, but the polish had a harder backing now. "I opened it because I thought RT was a property code or an address. It wasn't."

Blood beat in Tommy's ears, the same thick rush he used to hear on the sideline when a kid stayed down too long and the whole field got quiet.

"I don't know what you're talking about."

"It was a letter. Martin's handwriting. August 2002." Cliff spoke in the careful, even tone he used when he was telling buyers about rot under a porch. "Cabin fire. Washburn County. Caretaker named Ray Toomey. Who started it. What Martin did after."

Tommy set the beer on the armrest. His hand stayed steady. The scar along his left forearm had started itching for the first time in years.

"You read it."

"I did."

"You took pictures."

Cliff met his eyes. The salesman face was still there. The math behind it had changed. "I document things. Habit."

"Fuck you."

"Call it whatever you want." Cliff spread one hand. "It's relevant."

"You're blackmailing me."

"No. Blackmail is cash or cops. I'm talking about an arrangement." He kept his voice mild, almost patient. "You want that letter to stay buried. I want the money Martin owed me. Those goals don't have to fight each other."

Craft beer. Leather loafers. Hands soft enough to look moisturized. Tommy outweighed Cliff by forty pounds and every pound had been built on purpose, in weight rooms and on practice fields. Across from him sat a man who thought leverage was cleaner than force because leverage left his hands looking nice.

The coaching part of Tommy's brain had already stopped hearing the sales pitch. It was doing field-position math instead. Distance to the dock. Sound over water. What kind of body could do what to what other body and how long it would take.

The shape came into his head and stayed there.

"Okay," Tommy said.

Cliff blinked. "Okay?"

"I'll talk to Paul. I'll help move it."

"That's a smart answer."

"It's not about smart. Martin owed you. He should've paid you."

"He should've, yeah."

"So I'll help."

Cliff studied him for a beat, deciding which version of Tommy he was looking at. Angry Tommy. Cooperative Tommy. Too-still Tommy. Then some private ease came over his face. He'd heard yes, and yes was all Cliff ever really listened for.

"Good," he said. "And the letter stays where it is."

"Nobody sees it."

"Right."

---

The memory never came back as a story. It came back as pieces his body had kept after his mind refused them.

The cabin. August 2002. He was twenty-five. Martin was twenty-five. A weekend in Washburn County at a buddy's family place, or maybe a buddy of a buddy's, the ownership chain so loose nobody checked. In your twenties that counted as permission.

Friday night. Bonfire. Too much lighter fluid, because Tommy had always been the guy who made fires too big, who couldn't just start one and leave it alone. The same force that made him good at football made him bad at stopping. Doors, drywall, arguments, all of it.

Martin said, *that's enough, that's enough,* and Tommy squeezed more onto the base because the more Martin said enough the more Tommy needed to show him enough didn't apply. Not at twenty-five. Not eight beers deep. Not with Wisconsin dark all around them and the idiot conviction that the world was there to be grabbed.

Then the wind shifted. Or the flame found dry brush between the pit and the porch. Or the lighter fluid can had been closer than Tommy thought. Twenty-two years later he still couldn't build the exact sequence. He only knew the result. The fire jumped for the cabin.

The porch went first. Dry cedar. Old wood. It caught with a crackle so loud it sounded amplified, not like a barrel fire or a pit fire but like an animal finally getting fed.

Martin yelled. Tommy ran toward it because that had always been his first instinct. Go at the problem. He snatched the hose from the side of the cabin and got nothing, no pressure, just a loose handle spinning in his hand. Then he dropped it and ran toward the propane tank without any plan beyond movement.

That was when he saw the truck.

Green Ford pickup. Dull paint. Mud on the wheel wells. Parked behind Martin's car.

It hadn't been there when they arrived. Or it had and they hadn't looked, which was worse. They had lit a bonfire beside a cabin with a truck in the driveway and never checked if anybody was inside because checking was not the kind of thought twenty-five-year-olds had before they started drinking.

Ray Toomey. Tommy didn't know the name then. He learned it from the newspaper three days later, in a McDonald's parking lot off Highway 53, when Martin held up the local paper and read the headline in a flat voice: WASHBURN CO. CARETAKER DIES IN ELECTRICAL FIRE. Martin's eyes had gone dead in a way Tommy had never seen before. No anger. No panic. No grief. Just shutdown. Then Martin said, "They're calling it electrical. We don't talk about this again."

In the moment, though, Tommy only knew there was a truck in the driveway and a cabin on fire. He ran to the front door, grabbed the handle, and got hit by heat so hard it felt like a shove. His left forearm clipped the metal frame and the skin cooked there on contact. He screamed, dropped back, and the door slammed shut. Inside, a beam or part of the ceiling came down. The room sealed itself. After that, no voice. No movement. Just the fire.

He didn't go back in. He stood in the gravel with his arm already blistering, white and waxy where it should've been red, and Martin came around the corner, grabbed him by both arms, and said, "We have to go. Now. Tommy. Now."

"There's somebody in there."

"I know. We have to go."

"Martin."

"Look at me." Martin's hands dug into his shoulders. "There's nothing you can do for him. We need to go."

And they went. Martin drove. Tommy sat in the passenger seat with his arm throbbing and his mind doing the one thing it would never fully undo, which was accept that he had killed a man. Not on purpose. Not knowing anybody was inside. Still killed him. He had been drunk and loud and stupid and too pleased with force to bother checking a driveway. The result was a dead man in a burned cabin and Martin driving thirty miles under the limit because Martin was already thinking about the next step while Tommy was still inside the first one.

Martin took him to a clinic in Spooner the next morning. Grease burn, Tommy told the nurse. Deep fryer accident at a friend's place. She either believed him or didn't care. The scar healed into a white ridge that always announced itself to a hand before it announced itself to an eye. Denise asked about it on their third date. Tommy said, "Cooking accident. I was twenty-five and an idiot." Both parts were true. Neither part was the truth.

For twenty-two years Martin checked the seal. Every September there would be a moment on the deck or by the fire pit or halfway down the dock path, a hand on Tommy's shoulder, a look held one beat too long, a quiet "You good" or "We're okay" or just Tommy's name in Martin's voice. Tommy would nod. Martin would nod. Another year would go by. Martin was the valve. Martin wrote the whole thing down, sealed it in an envelope, put it in the safe, and never explained why. Tommy had never asked. Why Martin did things was Martin's department.

Now Martin was dead. The valve was gone. Cliff Haywood was sitting on the same deck, talking about twenty-two years of ruin like it was a debt stack he could restructure if everybody stayed calm.

---

Cliff rolled the cold can against his knee. "One housekeeping item."

Tommy looked at him.

"The photos from Sunday are on my phone, and my phone backs up automatically. I'm only saying that because I don't like misunderstandings."

"So if the letter disappears, you've still got it."

"That's right."

"Anybody else have it?"

"Not the images." Cliff held his gaze. "I'm trying to keep this contained."

Tommy let that sit there. Phone and cloud. Maybe lawyer too, maybe not. Cliff was the kind of man who believed a paper trail was what separated professionals from morons.

"You'll get your money," Tommy said. "This week I talk to Paul. Then we're done."

Cliff nodded once. "That works for me."

"You don't say Toomey's name again. You don't come back to me with this again. No second conversation."

"Clean break."

"Yeah."

Cliff stood, stretched, and set his empty can on the deck rail for a second before thinking better of it and putting it on the boards. Even now he was staging surfaces. "I appreciate you being reasonable."

Tommy didn't answer.

"I'm going to see what Paul's doing about dinner," Cliff said. "You coming in?"

"In a minute."

"Don't sit out here too long. It drops fast once the sun goes."

"I know how September works, Cliff."

A quick smile. Small, private, satisfied. Then the screen door banged and the house took him back.

Tommy stayed in the chair. Same chair he'd used every September for twenty-five years, closest to the rail, armrests worn smooth by beer cans and forearms and men pretending a lake view could absorb whatever they didn't want to say. Martin had bought the chairs the year after the fire. Tommy had spent two decades not asking himself what that meant.

The scar on his forearm had stopped itching. Both hands lay flat on the armrests, palms down, fingers spread. Tommy looked at them the way he looked at his players in film sessions when he wasn't judging technique but capacity. What could this body do if the call asked for it.

Cliff's phone lived in Cliff's pocket. The backup lived somewhere Tommy couldn't reach. A server. A building. A room with humming lights and people he'd never meet. Cliff, though, wasn't abstract. Cliff was a hundred and seventy-five pounds in loafers who called a treadmill a workout.

Tommy thought about the dock. Thirty feet of wood into the lake. Slight give under the planks. Swim platform at the end. Water beside it was three feet deep, maybe less where the silt mounded around the pilings.

Deep enough if a man went in hurt. Deep enough if another man kept him there.

The idea changed texture. It wasn't strategy now but weight, footing, timing. Cliff in loafers on wet boards with Tommy standing right there.

Out past the dock the loon called again. The lake had lost its copper and gone flat gray. Once the air dropped, the boards would sweat slick. A man in loafers on wet wood at night could lose his footing. People in the house wouldn't hear much if nobody was listening for trouble.

Tommy picked up the beer Cliff had brought him. The can was still half full. He tipped it over the rail and watched the spill flash once in the last gray light before it vanished into the pine needles.

He set the empty can on the armrest and wiped his hands on his shorts.

Then he went inside.
