# Chapter 15

The call came at 11:47 PM. Mallick was on the couch watching the ten o'clock weather, a front pushing in from the Dakotas, rain by Wednesday, and thinking about whether the tomato cages would hold. She picked up on the second ring.

Dispatch gave her the address and she knew it before the numbers finished. Martin Kessler's lake house. Elbow Lake. Second call to the same property in four days.

She wrote it down anyway. She always wrote it down.

She changed out of sweats. Blazer, polo, slacks, low boots. She clipped her reading glasses to the chain around her neck, took her leather notebook from the kitchen counter, and left through the garage. The casserole dish sat in the cabinet above the stove. She left it there.

The drive from Detroit Lakes took twenty-six minutes. County Road 6 was empty. Deer eyes flashed at the tree line and vanished. She drove with the window cracked. The cold had that September edge that told you October was already practicing. She didn't turn on the radio.

Saturday kept replaying anyway. The green bean casserole. Kessner instead of Kessler. Men lining up to correct her. Paul with the careful insurance cadence. Cliff with the smooth courtesy of a man who sold people the future for a living. Thirty-seven corrections across six interviews. Thirty-seven small disclosures from men who thought they were fixing her mistakes instead of showing her where they'd been.

Saturday had been a suspicious death with a natural heart under it and five separate panic attacks on top. She'd walked away with a contamination map and a case she could close.

Now somebody had put a second man in the lake.

She turned onto the gravel drive at 12:13 AM. Two county cruisers were already there, light bars washing the A-frame blue, then red, then blue again. Every window in the house was lit. Figures moved behind the glass.

The lake was loud from the yard. On Saturday it had been background. Tonight it sounded like pressure.

Deputy Arneson met her at the door. Same deputy as Saturday. Big, competent, face set a little tighter now.

"Detective. Same house."

"What do we have?"

"Cliff Haywood, forty-eight. Found face-down beside the dock around eleven-thirty. Doug Whitfield pulled him out before we got there. There's a cut on the left side of the head."

"How bad?"

"Hard to say in this light. Good-sized split. Also..." Arneson glanced past her toward the dark out back. "He was wearing tassel loafers."

"On the dock."

"In the water beside it."

"How deep?"

"Three feet. Maybe a little more right off the edge."

That kept accidental drowning off the easy list.

"Who found him?"

"Whitfield. He's in shock. Other four are in the great room. Kelleher's with them."

She walked in.

The room she'd worked on Saturday was still crowded with men and light. No one in it looked in charge of anything.

Doug Whitfield sat on the couch with a fleece blanket around his shoulders, face drained gray by shock and overhead light. Paul Decker stood near the island with his hands folded, already arranged into usefulness. Joel Beckman leaned against the mantel and watched her come in, all the lawyer sarcasm gone. Nate Griggs paced in one narrow lane across the rug, cap still on, words building in his throat. Tommy Renaldi sat in the Adirondack chair by the deck door with his hands between his knees.

Saturday Tommy had been all movement. Tonight he looked bolted down.

Mallick stopped where all five could see her.

"I'm Detective Mallick. I was here Saturday investigating Martin Kessler's death." She let the correct name sit there. "I understand Mr. Haywood was found in the lake."

They heard it. Paul looked at Joel. Nate stopped walking in mid-step. Doug raised his eyes for a second, long enough to register the missing casserole, the missing apologies, the missing act. Tommy didn't move at all.

"Nobody leaves this property," she said. "Nobody touches anything. I want each of you separated for the rest of the night."

Paul took a step toward her. "Detective, if there's anything we can do-"

"Stay available, Mr. Decker."

She turned to Arneson. "Mr. Beckman in the study. Mr. Griggs in the basement. Coach Renaldi stays here. Mr. Whitfield stays on that couch. Keep Mr. Decker in the kitchen."

Nate lifted both hands. "I can give you the whole night. Start to finish. Dinner, who went where, who was outside, all of it."

"I'll come get you."

No warmth. No neighborly flutter. Nate's mouth shut.

Mallick looked back to Arneson. "Call dispatch. I want the BCA crime scene unit headed this way. Tell them possible homicide and a compromised water scene. Nobody goes near the dock until I see it."

Arneson nodded and stepped out.

Kelleher started moving the men. Doug got up slowly, blanket still around him. Joel went toward the study without complaint. Nate muttered once under his breath and headed for the stairs. Paul stayed near the island, hands still folded, waiting for instructions that weren't coming.

Kelleher touched Tommy's shoulder. Tommy lifted his head, looked at Mallick, and gave her a flat stare, mouth set hard.

She went out the back door.

---

The route to the dock vanished into the trees ten feet off the deck. Her flashlight found wet gravel, pine needles, roots, the dark shine of cedar rail at the bottom. Good enough for a person who knew the property. Good enough to carry a man down without wanting to be seen. She'd need the BCA to photograph the ground before daylight traffic ruined whatever it was holding.

The air by the water was colder than the yard. Her breath showed. Lake smell came up strong: mud, weeds, cold mineral water, churned-up silt.

Arneson had left a battery spot at the end of the dock. Cliff Haywood lay on his back in that hard white circle, arranged into more order than death usually allowed. Deputies had straightened him after pulling him out. She could see it in the hands, in the shoulders, in the shirt twisted across his chest.

Froth had dried from one nostril and the corner of the mouth. His eyes were closed. She would have preferred them open.

She crouched, pulled on gloves, and started where the scene wanted not to be looked at.

Shoes first.

Leather loafers. Tasseled. Expensive. Cliff wore them in the house. She remembered the click of them on Martin's hardwood on Saturday. Even his worst weekends had a dress code. Sunday afternoon she'd seen him cross the deck in beat-up boat shoes with wet-darkened seams and soft rubber soles.

These were not dock shoes.

She lifted one foot. The sole was clean through the arch. No grit. No wet sand worked into the edge. No pine needles stuck to the leather. Only a scuff at the toe and the drag-smear of somebody else's handling.

Either Cliff hadn't walked down here in those shoes or somebody had changed them after he was dead.

She lowered the foot and moved to the wound.

The split sat on the left temple, a little above and behind the ear, about two inches long. Clean enough to be hard-edge impact. Not rock, not branch. She looked at the railing. Old splinters, weathered gray, nothing fresh. She looked at the bench at the end of the dock. Smooth wood, no blood, no hair, no scrape she trusted.

If he had fallen on his own, the wound should have argued for it. Forehead. Chin. Bridge of the nose. Maybe a shoulder. This one sat too far back. Side and behind. Not where a simple fall would land.

She shined her light over the edge of the dock. The beam reached bottom fast. Pale sand close in, then a seam of churned silt, then darker water beyond. Three feet, maybe a little more where the dock dropped off. Not enough to drown a sober grown man who wanted to live. Enough to drown a man who'd been stunned first. Enough if somebody kept a hand on the back of his neck and waited out the fight.

The froth mattered too. Not definitive without the autopsy, but it pushed the same way. He had taken water in alive or close enough to it.

Not proof yet. Plenty to take accident off the easy list.

She stood and backed up a step. In the white light, Cliff's body looked split between two stories. The dock said slip, stumble, bad night, cold water. The loafers said indoors. The wound said help.

She walked the planks to the far end and back again. Nothing obvious on the bench. Nothing loose on the swim platform. At shore, where gravel gave up and grass started, her light caught half a boot print. Bigger than Cliff's shoe. Clear enough to note, not clear enough to own. Could be Doug. Could be a deputy. Could be the man she was out here for.

From the dock, the house was mostly windows and glow through branches. From the house, after dark, the dock disappeared. The trees and water did the rest.

A person could hit a man out here. A person could hold him down. A person could walk back to the house wet to the elbows and never be heard over the lake.

Mallick took one last look at Cliff's face, then turned back toward the lights.

---

The kitchen table was where she set up. Same chair as Saturday. Same cheap overhead fixture. The old refrigerator kicked on with its forty-minute wheeze as she opened her notebook to the blue-tabbed section marked *Kessler residence, corrections.*

Thirty-seven entries. Every one of them a moment when a man in this house had fixed an error she planted and, in fixing it, told her what he knew.

She ran her finger down the page.

*Decker: corrected body position. Knew original orientation. Reached Martin before police.*

*Haywood: corrected study location before she gave it. Had been in the study.*

*Griggs: referenced the voicemail he said he never left. Started explaining before she asked for it.*

*Renaldi: corrected nothing. Said little. In bathroom flushing when officers arrived Saturday.*

*Whitfield: corrected nothing. One-word answers. Shut down when pushed.*

*Beckman: corrected the timeline to the minute. Angry, but the facts stayed clean. No obvious tampering.*

Saturday that map had led to a natural death under a ridiculous pile of amateur evidence tampering. Five guilty consciences, five separate bad ideas, no murder.

Now Cliff Haywood was on the dock with a head wound and house shoes.

She turned to a fresh page and wrote *Haywood, early assessment.*

Cliff's correction still mattered. He had been in Martin's study. The safe had been open on Saturday. At the time she'd filed it under money: Cliff looking for forty thousand dollars from Martin's estate before the estate even existed.

Money still might explain it.

So might whatever else had been in that safe.

She wrote: *Reopen safe. Inventory contents. Determine what Haywood saw.*

Then she started interviews.

Doug Whitfield stayed on the couch, blanket up to his chest, both hands around a mug of water he wasn't drinking. Up close the gauntness in his face looked worse than it had on Saturday. The lake had left a dark line halfway up his sweatpants.

"Mr. Whitfield. Start with when you went down there."

Doug swallowed. "Around eleven-thirty. Maybe a little before."

"You go every night?"

"Most nights. It's quieter."

"What did you see?"

"He was in the water." Doug stared at the mug as he said it. "Face down. Left side if you're walking out. I thought maybe he was swimming and then I saw it wasn't that."

"What wasn't."

"The way he was just there." Doug's fingers tightened around the paper cup. "No movement. No sound."

"What did you do?"

"I yelled. I went in. Water wasn't deep. I got him turned over and his face..." Doug stopped, mouth open for a second, then shut it. "I dragged him up. Then I yelled again and ran back."

"Did you notice his shoes?"

A blink. Slow. "No."

"Anyone on the path? Any voices? Splashing?"

Doug shook his head. "No. Just the lake."

She let that sit. Shock had narrowed him down to the size of a nail. No point prying harder yet.

"All right."

Doug nodded once and looked back at the mug.

Nate Griggs was downstairs with Kelleher. The basement smelled like detergent, damp carpet, and a house built too close to water. Nate stood as soon as she came in.

"Detective. I've been going through it and I can pretty much give you the whole night if you want the director's cut."

"I don't."

Nate stopped.

"What time did you last see Cliff Haywood alive?"

"Dinner. For sure dinner. Six-thirty to, I don't know, seven-fifteen, seven-twenty. Everybody was there. After that it broke up. Some guys stayed upstairs, some went out back. I saw Cliff on the deck later when I came up to use the bathroom. Maybe nine. Nine-fifteen."

"Who was with him?"

Nate tugged the bill of his cap. "I think he was alone. Maybe Paul had just come in. I can't swear to it."

"You were recording dinner."

His face changed at once. Performance pulled back. What remained was the man under it, smaller and more alarmed.

"Yeah."

"How long did you record?"

"Dinner. Waiver wire talk. Mostly bullshit." Nate rubbed his palm on his shorts. "I usually let it run a little after because sometimes somebody says one useful thing when they think we're done."

"Did you stop it yourself?"

"I think so. I mean, I thought so. I'd have to look at the file."

"I'd like the phone."

"The whole thing?"

"The whole thing."

Nate winced, then reached into his pocket and handed it over. "Passcode's 4421."

"Any other audio from tonight?"

"I don't think so. If there is, it's because I forgot to kill the app all the way. That's not me hiding anything. That's me being bad at software."

"I'll sort it out."

Nate nodded. "Okay."

"Did you notice any trouble between Cliff and Tommy?"

"Trouble like yelling? No. Trouble like one guy talking too low and the other guy acting like his skin didn't fit? Yeah."

"When?"

"All day. Worse at dinner."

She wrote it down, bagged the phone, and went back upstairs.

Joel Beckman was in the study, sitting in Martin's desk chair with his hands on the chair arms instead of the desk. Careful without making a show of careful.

"Mr. Beckman."

"Detective." Joel stood. "No casserole this time."

"No."

That answer seemed to satisfy him more than silence would have.

"When did you last see Cliff Haywood?"

"Dinner. Roughly six-thirty to seven-fifteen. I went upstairs after that. I came down when Doug screamed."

"What time?"

"Eleven thirty-two. I checked my phone."

The precision sounded native to him, not built for her.

"Any tension today between Cliff and anyone else?"

"Tommy."

"How so?"

Joel considered it. "Too polite. That's the first part. Cliff gets smooth when he wants something. Tommy gets louder when he wants out of a conversation. Today Cliff was smooth and Tommy was quiet."

"All day?"

"Since Sunday, really. Tonight it got hard to miss."

"Did you hear an argument?"

"No. I heard the absence of a normal one." Joel shifted his weight. "I don't have proof of anything. I have an impression. Cliff had leverage. Tommy knew it."

She wrote that down too.

"Leverage about what?"

"If I knew that, I'd tell you. Or I'd bill for it." Joel's mouth tightened for half a second. "But yes. Start with Tommy."

"I'm starting with everybody."

"Sure."

He didn't believe her, which was fine. She didn't need belief from Joel Beckman. She needed time stamps and clean observations, and he'd just given both.

Tommy was where she'd left him, same chair, same posture, hands still jammed between his knees like he didn't trust them loose. His sleeves were shoved up. The scar along the left forearm showed clean and pale under the light.

"Coach Renaldi."

"Yeah."

"Where were you between ten and midnight?"

"In bed."

"What time did you go up?"

"Nine. Nine-thirty."

"Did you leave the room after that?"

"No."

"Did you hear Cliff go out? Anybody go out?"

"No."

He didn't hedge. Didn't add. Didn't perform indifference. Every answer came flat and clean. Tommy was usually louder than this or already leaving the room. This version of him was wrong.

"How would you describe your relationship with Mr. Haywood?"

"He was a friend."

"Any disagreement today?"

"No."

Mallick watched his face while he said it. No shift. No heat. No coaching bark. Saturday he'd been half-motion even standing still. Tonight every muscle was locked.

She asked a few more narrow questions and got the same dead-flat answers back. No witnesses. No movement. No conflict. Bed by nine-thirty. Nothing to add.

He had the size for it, and he was holding himself so still it looked painful. Neither one was evidence. The case wasn't going to hold on an old scar and a bad feeling.

"All right," she said. "I'll speak to you again in the morning."

Tommy looked at the floor. "Yeah."

Back in the kitchen, the casserole was still there, covered in foil, untouched after four days. No one had eaten it. No one had thrown it out. That tracked.

She opened to the fresh page and wrote the cleanest version she could defend.

*Dock scene: head wound inconsistent with simple fall. Froth at nose and mouth. Water shallow. Loafers inconsistent with victim walking to dock. Treat as staged drowning pending ME and BCA.*

*Haywood: confirmed study access from Saturday correction. Safe may matter. Reopen.*

*Whitfield: finder. Pants wet to the thigh. Saw the body face-down on the left side of the dock. Shock limits the rest.*

*Beckman: says Cliff got smooth and Tommy got quiet. Heard no argument, only the lack of their usual one. Leverage is his read, not a fact.*

*Renaldi: says he was upstairs by 9:30 PM and never left. Denies disagreement. Hands jammed between knees, answers clipped to one word.*

Then she put the pen down and looked at Nate's phone.

The audio app opened to a list of files. Most of them had Nate all over them: `WEEK3_SLEEPERS`, `MAILBAG_FINAL_v2`, `MON_DINNER_RAW`. The dinner file was longer than he'd implied. Twenty-nine minutes.

She tapped it.

At first it was exactly what he'd promised: beer bottles on wood, somebody arguing about waiver priority, Nate laughing too hard at his own line, someone calling someone else an idiot with real affection under it. The group before the second death. The group when it could still pass for itself.

Then plates started moving. Chairs scraped back. Voices spread out, farther from the phone.

Cliff's voice came in close and low, not meant for the whole table but caught anyway.

"Ten-thirty. Dock. You and me."

A pause. Three seconds of silverware and chair legs.

Tommy answered from farther off, rough enough that she would have known him without the weekend of interviews.

"Not in here."

Cliff again, just as low. "Ten-thirty."

Another pause. Then Tommy, flat and final.

"Fine."

Mallick hit pause.

Not a confession. More than a hunch. Tommy had lied about the disagreement. The rest of his timeline had just gotten a lot harder to believe. Cliff had expected him at the dock.

She picked up her pen and wrote one more line under Renaldi's name.

*Audio: Haywood and Renaldi set dock meeting for 10:30 PM during dinner file.*
