# Chapter 5

The casserole was still warming. Mallick had asked the tall one, Decker, the insurance man, to put it in the oven at two-fifty before she started with the lawyer, and now it was ticking away in there, the green bean smell curling through the kitchen like a guest who'd arrived too early and was making itself comfortable. She could smell it from the great room, where she'd set up at the long pine table, her notebook open, her reading glasses pushed to the end of her nose, and the county's cheapest ballpoint in her right hand.

She'd finished with Beckman. That had been interesting, interesting the way a locked door was interesting. A public defender who knew exactly how much silence was worth. She'd thanked him and let him go and now she was looking at the draft board on the wall. Martin Kessler's picks circled in red marker. Somebody had pressed hard with that marker. She could see the indentation from here, the way the whiteboard surface dimpled under each circle. Fantasy football mattered enough to scar the board. Now he was dead upstairs in a room that looked like it had been tidied by a committee.

Mallick wrote: *Beckman: clean. Too clean? Or genuinely clean?*

She underlined *genuinely* and drew a small question mark next to it. Then she capped the pen and went to the kitchen.

The oven light was on. She bent to check the casserole through the glass. The cheese was browning nicely along the edges, and the fried onions on top had crisped into those little curls she liked. Her mother's recipe, technically, though Mallick had switched from canned green beans to frozen ones in about 2008 and hadn't told anyone. She straightened up, felt the pull in her lower back that she felt every time she bent at the oven, and poured herself a glass of water from the tap. The water here tasted like iron and pine. Lake house plumbing.

Paul Decker was standing in the doorway of the great room. He'd been waiting. She could tell because he was holding a coffee mug he'd already finished, the handle turned outward, gripped with both hands, trying for relaxed and missing by a mile.

"Oh, Mr. Decker," she said. "Is it Decker? I'm so bad with names. Hold on." She flipped her notebook back a page. "Paul Decker. Insurance, you said?"

"Claims adjuster," he said. "Mutual of Omaha, but the Minneapolis office."

"Claims adjuster." She wrote it down. She'd already written it down twice. "Now that's a job. My neighbor Gary had a tree come down on his garage last March, big old ash, just split right down the middle, and his claims adjuster was just wonderful. Do you do homeowners, or is it more commercial, or whatever walks in?"

"All lines," Paul said. "Homeowners, auto, commercial. Whatever walks in."

"Whatever walks in. Sure." She pulled out a chair at the kitchen table and gestured to the one across from her. "Do you want coffee? I think there's still some in the pot. It might be a little old by now, but I can make fresh if you want."

"I'm fine."

"You sure? I can't drink old coffee myself. My stomach. But some people prefer it stronger, so." She shrugged and sat down and opened her notebook to a fresh page. "Now, Mr. Decker. Paul. Can I call you Paul?"

"Sure."

"Paul. I just want to go over a couple of things, if that's all right. Nothing formal. I'm just trying to get the picture straight in my head because, honestly, I get confused sometimes with these, when there's a lot of people in a house and everybody's got a different version." She smiled. "I'm sure you understand. In your line of work."

"I do." He sat down across from her. His posture was good, straight-backed, hands flat on the table, the coffee mug set to one side. He had the tabletop stillness of somebody used to getting the story in order. She noted this.

"So. Last night. You all had the draft, the football thing, and then drinks, the hot tub, and bed. Is that about right?"

"That's about right." He paused. "The draft went until about ten-thirty. Then we were on the deck for a while, maybe until midnight. Martin went to bed before the rest of us. Tommy went up maybe twelve-thirty, and I think I was one of the last ones. Maybe one, one-fifteen."

She wrote the times down. "And you slept where?"

"Upstairs. The guest bedroom on the right side, if you're facing the lake."

"The right side. And Martin was in the master?"

"Yes."

"And that's on the left?"

"That's on the left. At the end of the hall."

She nodded and wrote. "Now, Nate, Mr. Griggs, he said he found Martin at, oh, he said around two. Does that sound right to you?"

"I think it was a little after two. He woke me up. He was standing in the doorway and he said Martin's not breathing, or something like that. Not breathing. I got up immediately."

"And you went to Martin's room?"

"I went to Martin's room. I tried CPR. For several minutes. But he was already gone." Paul's jaw worked. Not for effect, she thought. She'd seen enough grief to know the mechanics of it, and this was the real thing, the muscle clenching because the words were in the way. "His skin was cool. Not cold, but. He'd been dead a while."

"I'm so sorry," Mallick said, and she meant it, in the way she always meant it, which was genuinely but with her pen still moving. "That must've been awful. I can't imagine. My husband passed in '09 and I wasn't even there for it. He was at work, just dropped, and I still sometimes think about whether it would've been better to be there or not. I go back and forth."

Paul looked at her. The mention of a dead husband had the effect she expected, a softening around his eyes, a slight dropping of the interview posture. People gave her things when she gave them something first. It was transactional and also true. Pete had dropped at work. She did go back and forth.

"I'm sorry," Paul said.

"Oh, it's been a long time now. But you don't forget how it feels." She flipped a page. "Now, when you went in and found Martin, Nate was already there, right? And you said you tried CPR. When you went in, was he on the bed?"

"He was on the bed."

"On his back?"

Paul paused. She watched the pause. In her experience, a pause before a factual answer meant the fact was being measured, not remembered.

"On his side," Paul said. "His left side. Facing the window."

"His left side. Facing the window." She frowned and flipped back in her notebook. "Oh, that's funny. I thought the deputies' report said he was on his back when they got there this morning. Flat on his back, arms at his sides." She looked up. "But you're saying he was on his left side?"

"He was on his left side when I got there."

"Huh." She wrote it down slowly, her handwriting deliberately large so he could see she was recording it. "So between when you got there at two-something and when the deputies arrived at, what was it, eight-thirty, he went from his left side to his back."

The silence in the kitchen was thick enough to hear the oven ticking and the old refrigerator starting its next cycle, that grinding mechanical sound that probably woke everyone in the house every forty minutes.

"I moved him," Paul said. "For the CPR. You have to put the person on their back for chest compressions. So I rolled him onto his back."

"Oh, of course." She wrote it down. "That makes perfect sense. CPR. You'd have to, wouldn't you. And the arms at his sides?"

"I positioned him. For the compressions. You put the heel of your hand on the sternum and you do the compressions there."

"No, sure, I understand. I took a course once. At the YMCA, if you can believe it." She smiled. "Although I think they've changed it since then. When I learned it there was the breathing part, but now I hear they don't."

"They still recommend rescue breaths for people trained in it," Paul said. The insurance precision was coming through now, the need to have the right figure, the right protocol, the right claim. She could practically hear the form fields filling in. "I was trained. I did compressions and breaths for about four minutes. Then I stopped because there was no response and his, I could tell it wasn't going to work."

"Four minutes. That's very precise."

"I was looking at the clock on the nightstand. The digital one. It said two-fourteen when I started and two-eighteen when I stopped."

"Two-fourteen and two-eighteen." She wrote both numbers down, then drew a small box around them. "See, that's what I mean. Most people would say a few minutes. You've got timestamps. That's your training, I bet."

"Probably."

"My husband was a roofer. When he estimated a job he'd measure to the quarter-inch and then add ten percent for waste. He said people who round up are lying and people who round down are selling you something." She wrote *clock on nightstand: verify position, verify model* in the margin of her notes and then drew a small circle around it.

"Paul, that's really helpful," she said. "I appreciate you being so specific. In my line of work, most people say a few minutes or a while, and that's not always helpful when I'm trying to put a timeline together." She paused, then added, as if it had just occurred to her: "You know, it's a little like claims work, isn't it? Getting the details right."

"I suppose it is."

"Do you mind if I ask, when you left Martin's room, did you close the door?"

"I closed the door."

"And you went back to bed?"

"I went back to bed."

The answer came too fast and too flat, and they both heard it. Paul's hands were still on the table but his thumbs had moved, tucked under his palms, a small compression that she filed away with the body position and the four minutes and the clock on the nightstand. He knew exactly how the body was oriented, exactly how long he'd performed CPR, exactly what he'd touched. Then he'd adjusted a corpse, closed the door, and gone back to bed.

"Well," she said, standing up. "I think that's all I need for now. Would you send in, oh, who's next on my..." She checked her notebook as if she hadn't planned the order three hours ago. "Haywood? Is Mr. Haywood around?"

"Cliff," Paul said. "I think he's on the deck."

"Cliff. That's right. Cliff Haywood. Would you mind? Oh, and Paul, try the casserole if you get hungry. I always make too much."

"Thanks."

"The secret is the fried onions on top. French's. Don't let anybody tell you the fancy ones are better."

Paul nodded and stood and carried his empty coffee mug to the sink and rinsed it. She noted that too: the quick rinse, the clean sink, the automatic tidy-up. His hands erased themselves from things without asking permission.

---

Mallick moved the interview to the deck. The kitchen smelled too much like casserole now, and she wanted Haywood in a different setting than Decker. People behaved differently depending on where you put them. The kitchen made Decker precise. She suspected the deck would make Haywood expand.

She was right. Haywood came out with his own coffee, a fresh cup, and pulled one of the Adirondack chairs around to face her at a companionable angle rather than across from her. He'd changed his shirt since this morning. She'd seen him in a navy polo at seven, and now it was a sage green linen thing, open at the collar, the kind of shirt that cost more than it looked like it cost. She knew the type. Her second cousin sold commercial real estate in Fargo and dressed the same way, studied casualness with a pitch always warming in the wings.

"Detective, I appreciate you taking the time with all of us," Cliff said, settling into the chair. "I know this can't be easy, coordinating, what is it, six interviews? Seven, if you count the deputies and everybody."

"Oh, I lose count." She laughed. "I'm not very organized, honestly. My sergeant says I'd lose my own head if it wasn't attached, which, you know, he's probably not wrong." She flipped through her notebook, passing Decker's pages. "Now, you're Cliff. Cliff Haywood. And you do..." She trailed off and looked at him expectantly.

"Real estate development. Commercial and residential, mostly outer-ring suburbs. Plymouth, Maple Grove, that area."

"Oh, nice. Good market out there?"

"It's got its challenges." The smile was practiced. Not fake. Practiced. He wore confidence the way other men wore a watch, something visible and checked often. "But we're positioned well."

"Positioned well. I love that." She wrote it down. She wrote down everything. Most of it was useless, but the act of writing put people on a tape delay. They watched her pen and forgot to watch her eyes. "So. Last night. The draft, the drinks, the hot tub. Same as everyone else?"

"Same as everyone else. Although I turned in a little earlier than the rest, maybe eleven-thirty. The hot tub does me in. My back."

"Oh, I hear you. My back is terrible. You know, this house, the deck is beautiful, by the way. Is this original cedar?"

"I think Martin had it redone maybe six, seven years ago. He mentioned it."

"It's gorgeous. And the view." She looked out at the lake. The September light was hitting the water at an angle that turned it amber near the shore and steel-gray farther out. A breeze was coming off the water, warm enough for September but with something underneath it, the first real suggestion that October was a thought the weather was having. Two loons were working the shallows by the dock, and she could hear one of them calling, that sound like someone laughing at a joke they didn't want to be laughing at. "I'd never leave."

"It's something."

"Did Martin use the place a lot? Year-round, or just summer and early fall?"

"Mostly summer and early fall. The draft weekend, obviously. Some winter weekends, I think, for ice fishing. He wasn't up here full-time."

"And he kept it nice. The inside, I was admiring the study. That little room off the hallway, is that the..."

"That's not the study," Cliff said. "The study is two doors past the half bath. Farther down the hall."

She stopped writing. Looked up. Let the look hold for exactly the amount of time it took Cliff's expression to adjust, a quarter-second of openness replaced by the smooth surface reforming.

"Two doors past the half bath," she repeated. "I thought it was that first room, the one with the coats."

"No, that's a closet. The study is the next one. It's got a desk, bookshelves, a painting of the lake."

"A painting of the lake. Of course." She smiled and wrote it down and didn't look up. "Thank you for that. I always get turned around in these big houses. My place is eight hundred square feet, so anything with more than one hallway..." She laughed. "You've been to the study, then? During your visits?"

The pause was shorter than Decker's had been. Haywood was faster at recalibrating. "Over the years, sure. Martin and I had some business together. He'd show me paperwork sometimes. It's not, I mean, we're friends. Were friends. People go in each other's rooms."

"Of course they do. I wasn't suggesting anything. Oh, goodness, no. I was just impressed you knew the layout so well. I've been to my sister's cabin a dozen times and I still can't find the bathroom." She shook her head, self-deprecating, and flipped to a new page. "Now. When you went to bed last night, eleven-thirty, you said, did you hear anything during the night? Anybody moving around, doors, that sort of thing?"

Cliff took a sip of his coffee. The sip lasted a beat too long. "I'm a heavy sleeper. Once I'm out, I'm out."

"Oh, I envy that. I haven't slept through a whole night since about 2004. The cats wake me up. I've got two, and they..." She stopped herself, waving a hand. "Sorry. Nobody needs to hear about my cats. So you didn't hear anything. And you woke up when? What time this morning?"

"Six-fifteen, six-thirty. Around there."

"And Martin was, you knew already? Somebody told you?"

"Paul told me. He was in the kitchen when I came down. He said Martin died during the night."

"Paul told you in the kitchen. Was anybody else there?"

"Tommy. Tommy was there. And I think Nate was somewhere. I could hear him on the phone, maybe in the other room."

She wrote it all down. Haywood had relaxed again. The study correction sat in her notebook like a seed she'd water later.

"Cliff, I really appreciate this. You've been so helpful. Can I ask you one more thing?"

"Of course."

"The casserole I brought, is it still in the oven? I can't remember if I asked somebody to take it out."

"I think Paul put it in."

"Bless him. I hope it's not burning. My casseroles tend to, well, they're better a little overdone, honestly." She stood up. "I'll go check on it. Would you mind pointing Whitfield my way if you see him? Doug Whitfield."

"He went down toward the dock a while ago."

"The dock. All right. Thank you."

Haywood nodded and stood and shook her hand. He shook her hand, which nobody else had done, which told her everything she already suspected about the way he moved through the world. A handshake to end an interview. A deal closed. His grip was firm and calibrated, like he'd settled on this exact pressure years ago and never revised it.

She returned the grip and smiled and went inside. On the way through the great room she passed the big one, Renaldi, the coach, coming out of the half bath. He was drying his hands on his jeans and he looked at her the way you'd look at a car coming the wrong way down a one-lane road: total focus, no expression. She said, "Oh, excuse me," and he said, "Yeah," and moved past her with his shoulders turned slightly, the way a large man does when he doesn't want to touch you in a hallway. She filed that away too, the shoulders, the single syllable, the hands still damp on the denim.

---

The casserole was fine. A little dark on the left side where the oven ran hot, but the structural integrity was sound, the green beans still distinct under the cheese. She pulled it out with a dish towel, she'd forgotten to bring an oven mitt, which was typical, and set it on the stovetop to cool.

Then she stood at the kitchen counter with her notebook open and her reading glasses pushed up on her forehead and she was not folksy anymore.

Decker knew the body's position. Left side, facing the window. The deputies found the body on its back with arms at his sides. Decker said he'd rolled Martin for CPR and positioned the arms, which was perfectly reasonable and also meant that between two and eight-thirty in the morning, Paul Decker had been close enough to the body to reposition it and had chosen to leave it tidy, the way people imagine a quiet death looks. That was not how people looked after four minutes of chest compressions. She'd seen CPR aftermath. There should've been bruising on the sternum, displaced clothing, maybe vomit. The body upstairs was tidy. Too tidy for somebody who'd been worked on.

Haywood knew the layout. Not just knew it. Two doors past the half bath. No hedge words, no rummaging around in memory. He said it like directions he'd given recently.

Through the deck doors she could see Whitfield down at the dock bench, seventy or eighty yards away through the pines and birch. Still as fence wire. Head bent toward the water.

She took her notebook and went out.

The stone path to the dock was warm under the late sun. Pine needles popped under her flats. Whitfield didn't turn when he heard her coming. Up close he looked even rougher than he had from the house. Hollowed cheeks, the gray scrape of beard, that wired-tired look you got when your body stopped remembering how to sleep.

"Mr. Whitfield?"

He looked up.

"I'm Mallick. I was hoping we could talk for a few minutes."

He held her eyes, then looked back at the lake. "Forgot meds."

Two words. Quiet enough that she almost missed them.

"You need a few minutes."

He nodded once.

Not evasive, she thought. Logistical. Or chemical. The line of his jaw held that clenched patience of somebody waiting for his own body to catch up with him.

"All right," she said. "Come find me when you've got them in you."

He gave her a quick glance at that, something like embarrassment and gratitude getting mixed together and canceled out, then nodded again. "Yeah."

She walked back up to the house before he'd had to decide whether to say anything else. He didn't call her back. He didn't need to. She had what she came for.

Mallick sat at the kitchen table and turned to a fresh page in her notebook. The refrigerator kicked on with its grinding cycle. She wrote:

*Decker: knows body position. Says CPR. Scene too clean for CPR. Check bruising, sheets, clock.*

*Haywood: knows study. Two doors past half bath. Been in there recently. Debt? Papers?*

*Beckman: lawyer. Angry. Won't explain argument. Maybe clean hands, maybe careful ones.*

*Renaldi: half bath when I came in. Wet hands. Next.*

*Griggs: later today. Talks too much. Will correct himself.*

*Whitfield: dock. "Forgot meds." Wants delay, not escape. Later today.*

She looked at the list. Six men. Five of them said they went to bed, slept through the night, and woke up to the news that their friend was dead. By morning the body had been handled, the house had been handled, and at least two of the stories in front of her had already been adjusted to fit the daylight.

She tore the page out, folded it into her blazer pocket, and went to find her phone. She wanted the BCA to move on the autopsy before supper, and she wanted Renaldi at the table before the others had another hour to listen to each other breathe.
