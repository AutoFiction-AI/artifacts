# Chapter 4

The two deputies from Becker County had made seven trips up and down the stairs. Joel counted from the kitchen table, where he'd been sitting since quarter to eight with a mug that kept getting refilled because Paul kept refilling it. Every trip, both deputies skipped the third step from the top. Someone had warned them about the creak, which meant someone had already started managing the scene, and Joel's money was on Paul.

He was on his fourth cup of coffee. His hands had the specific tremor that came from too much caffeine layered on no sleep and no food, and twice he'd caught himself doing the thing his clients did in arraignment — sitting very still, watching people with authority move through a space that used to feel safe. Seven years as a public defender in Hennepin County and he'd never once considered what it felt like from the inside. Now he knew. It felt like shit.

The great room still smelled like Martin's scotch — the Lagavulin he'd poured too generously last night, the way he poured everything too generously, the host who couldn't stop hosting even when people told him to sit down. The draft board was still on the wall, Martin's picks circled in red Sharpie, and nobody had touched it because touching it would mean deciding what the board was now. A record. An artifact. Evidence. The morning light through the A-frame windows was gold and stupid, the kind of light that belonged on a lake weekend postcard, not on whatever this had become.

Tommy was on the deck with his hands wrapped around a coffee mug like it was the only solid thing left. His face had a gray, clammy quality Joel had seen before on defendants who'd just heard the charges read. Nate was in the kitchen doorway, explaining something to a deputy about the lake house's history that the deputy had not asked about and did not care about. Doug was on the couch in the corner with his hood up and his knees drawn to his chest, looking at his phone with the particular blankness of a man who wasn't reading anything on it.

Cliff was in the Adirondack chair by the fireplace, legs crossed, coffee balanced on the armrest. He looked like a man waiting for a slightly delayed flight. Everyone else looked like they'd been in a car accident.

Paul came back from the hallway and sat down across from Joel. His wire-rimmed glasses were smudged and he hadn't cleaned them, which was how you knew Paul Decker was not all right — the man cleaned his glasses on a forty-minute cycle, same as the refrigerator.

"Medical examiner's been called," Paul said. "They said a couple hours."

"From Detroit Lakes?"

"Yeah." Paul rubbed the bridge of his nose where the glasses sat. "The deputies are being — they've been professional. Thorough. I told them Martin had some health concerns."

"Did he?"

Paul looked at him. "He was forty-seven and he drank like he was twenty-two. He didn't exercise. You saw what he ate last night."

"That's not a health concern, that's a lifestyle."

"Joel."

"I'm not being a lawyer."

"You're always being a lawyer." Paul said it without malice. He'd been saying it for twenty years.

Joel didn't argue because it was true. He picked up his mug and set it down again without drinking. "Has anyone called Linda?"

"I've got her number. I've been waiting until — I don't know what I'm waiting for. There's no protocol for this."

"No."

"Yeah." Paul stared at the draft board. Martin's first-round pick, circled in red, with the little annotation Martin always made — projected points in his neat handwriting, the numbers that didn't matter anymore. "I know."

The gravel driveway popped and crunched under tires. Both deputies turned toward the front windows. Joel saw a maroon Ford Explorer pulling up behind the sheriff's cruiser, and behind the wheel was a woman in a brown blazer reaching across the passenger seat for something.

What she was reaching for turned out to be a casserole dish.

She came through the front door carrying it two-handed, a Pyrex 9x13 with a foil top, and she was already talking before anyone could figure out who she was. "Oh, good morning. I'm Detective Mallick, Becker County Sheriff's. I'm so sorry about your friend. Is this the Kessner residence?"

The silence was specific. Six men who had all heard the same wrong thing, waiting for someone else to fix it.

"Kessler," Paul said. "Martin Kessler."

"Oh, Kessler! I'm sorry, I had it wrong in my notes somewhere. Kessler. Thank you." She shifted the casserole to one arm and extended her free hand toward nobody in particular. She was maybe five-six, stocky, gray-streaked brown hair pulled back in a low bun, reading glasses on a beaded chain around her neck. She looked like someone's neighbor who'd come over because she heard a commotion. "Now, is there a place I can set this down? It's green bean — my mother's recipe, and I know that sounds like a thing people say, but she really did make it better than anyone. Needs about twenty minutes at three-fifty if you want it later."

Nate was the first to move. He took the casserole from her the way you accept a dish from an aunt at a funeral — reflexively, without processing the absurdity of the gesture. "Yeah, the fridge is right here."

"Thank you so much. And before anything else — I'm sorry, this is embarrassing — could someone point me toward the restroom? The drive from Detroit Lakes and two cups of coffee and I just — well." She smiled. Warm, slightly sheepish. Joel's neck prickled. "Anyway."

"Half bath's by the front door," Paul said. "Just past the—"

"I'll find it. Thank you." She set her leather notebook on the kitchen counter and disappeared down the hallway with the unhurried ease of a woman looking for the bathroom at a friend's house, not at a death scene.

Joel looked at Paul. Paul looked at Joel.

"What the fuck," Tommy said from the deck doorway. He'd come inside and was standing there with his mug, staring at the hallway she'd gone into. "Did she just bring us a casserole?"

"A detective," Nate said, and Joel could hear the podcaster's machinery already whirring, already organizing this into a segment, a bit, something he could narrate. "A county detective brought a green bean casserole to a — I mean, is that standard? Is that a Minnesota thing?"

"It's a casserole thing," Doug said from the couch without looking up.

"Thank you, Doug, yes, it's a casserole thing. A casserole thing. At a death investigation. From a woman who called Martin the wrong name."

"Keep your voice down," Joel said.

"I'm just—"

"I know what you're just. Keep it down."

Mallick came back from the bathroom, drying her hands on the sides of her blazer. She retrieved the notebook from the counter and clicked open a pen, and one of the deputies — the younger one, crew cut, the one who'd been asking about the draft board earlier — gave her a nod that Joel read as deference. Interesting. She outranked them, or they respected her, or both. Hard to tell from a nod.

"Now," she said, pulling up a chair at the kitchen table as if she'd been invited. "I'm sorry about getting here late — there was road work on 59 that had everything backed up, if you can believe that on a Saturday. I know Deputy Sievert and Deputy Kohn have been taking good care of things." She opened the notebook to a fresh page. "So I understand Mr. Kessler was found earlier this morning?"

"Around two AM," Paul said. "Nate found him."

"Nate." She looked around the room. "That's—"

"Me. Yeah. Nathan Griggs." Nate stepped forward like he'd been called on in class. "I found him at — well, it was closer to one-fifty, I looked at my phone right when I—"

"One-fifty. OK." She wrote it down. "And he was in the downstairs bedroom? One of the—"

"Upstairs," Paul said. "The master bedroom. It's upstairs, at the front of the hall."

"Upstairs! Of course, I'm sorry." She wrote. "And you were all here this weekend — how many of you? I had seven in my notes, but—"

"Seven." It came out of Joel before he could catch it. "Six now."

The room flinched. Six now. He heard it land — blunt, too precise, the public defender who couldn't turn it off even when he was the one hurting. Mallick looked at him, and her expression was either sympathy or measurement.

"Six. Of course. I'm sorry." She wrote something. "And you're..."

"Joel Beckman."

"Joel." The pen moved. "And you all come up here every year for this? For the fishing, or is it—"

"Fantasy football," Nate said. "It's a keeper league. Twenty-five years running. Well, twenty-five drafts, we started in—"

"Oh, that's wonderful. My nephew does that — he's always on his phone at Thanksgiving arguing about some waiver wire something. It drives his mother crazy." Mallick looked around the great room with an expression that made Joel think of a real estate showing — appreciative, curious, taking stock. "This is a beautiful house. That ceiling is something. Is that real cedar paneling?"

"Yeah," Paul said.

"My ex-husband always wanted cedar in our den, but we could never — well, that's a different story." She peered through the screen door at the deck. "And is that a hot tub I saw coming in? Nice one. Seats what, six? Eight?"

"Six," Tommy said. His voice sounded like he'd swallowed sand.

"Six. And you all used it last night? Or was that the night before?"

"Last night," Paul said, and Joel watched Paul calculate as he said it — the careful precision of a man who assessed risk for a living, choosing what to disclose and what to let pass. "Some of us. It was a late night."

"Sure, sure. Draft night. Like New Year's Eve but for football." She laughed, a short, warm sound that seemed genuine. "And what time did everyone turn in? Ballpark."

"Midnight, maybe one." Paul looked around for confirmation. He got shrugs, half-nods, the useless consensus of men who'd been drinking and weren't sure about much except that they wished they'd gone to bed earlier and that nothing about this morning made sense. "It was late."

"Of course. Of course." She wrote. "Now — and I'm sorry to ask, I know this is terrible timing — but is there coffee? I saw the pot going and I'm still trying to wake up after that drive."

Joel watched Paul pour her a cup. Because of course Paul would pour a cup for the detective investigating their friend's death. Paul managed situations the way he managed claims — assess the damage, contain the exposure, make sure the environment stayed functional. Joel had watched him do it at Martin's divorce dinner, at Nate's birthday when Nate drank himself into a fight with a bouncer, at a dozen weekends when somebody got sloppy and needed to be handled. Paul was a claims adjuster for human situations: not the cause, not the solution, just the man who made sure the paperwork was in order and nobody's premium went up too much.

Mallick held the mug under her nose and breathed in. "Oh, that's good. That's a nice dark roast. Martin — Mr. Kessler had good taste." She looked at the draft board. "Is that your fantasy football thing? Oh my goodness, that's elaborate. All those names and numbers."

"It's a keeper league," Nate started, and Joel could see the full explanation loading behind his eyes — the league history, the rules, the statistics, the twenty-five years of context that Nate would happily narrate for forty minutes if nobody stopped him.

"Nate," Joel said. "She didn't ask how it works."

Mallick smiled at Joel over the coffee. The smile lasted one beat longer than it needed to. Or one beat shorter than he expected. He couldn't tell which, and that bothered him more than the smile itself.

---

She worked the room for another forty minutes before she got to Joel directly. He watched her circuit the way he watched opposing counsel work a jury — tracking the questions against the answers, mapping what was asked against what was revealed, noting the rhythm.

She talked to Tommy on the deck. Joel couldn't hear most of it through the glass door, but he could see Tommy's body — hunched forward in an Adirondack, hands laced behind his neck, the specific posture of a man who'd just been told something he already knew but wasn't ready to hear. Mallick stood beside him with her coffee and her notebook and at one point she said something that made Tommy look up, and Tommy answered, and she nodded and wrote.

She talked to Cliff in the kitchen. Cliff was Cliff — shoulders back, good eye contact, the salesman's composure that Joel had always found slightly insulting because it meant Cliff was always working a room, even when the room was a kitchen where his friend had died. Mallick said something about the house — the layout, which room was where — and Cliff corrected her. Joel couldn't hear the specifics, but he saw the correction happen: Cliff's hand gesturing toward the hallway, Mallick's pen moving, her murmured thank you. Another wrong thing. Another correction. Another note.

She talked to Doug. Briefly. Doug gave her what Doug gave everyone — monosyllables, nods, the agreeable blankness of a man who had long ago learned that the less you said, the less anyone asked. Mallick didn't push him. She just wrote.

Then she refilled her coffee, walked back to the kitchen table where Joel was still sitting, pulled the chair across from him, set her notebook flat, and said: "So. Joel. You're the attorney."

"Public defender."

"Public defender. That's different, right? Different from—"

"Different from most things people think about when they hear lawyer. Yeah."

"You must see some things in that job."

"I do."

"My sister-in-law is a paralegal over in Fergus Falls. She's got stories that would — well." Mallick sipped her coffee. "How long have you known Martin?"

"Twenty-five years. Since undergrad. We were in the same fraternity."

"Twenty-five years. That's something. That's longer than my marriage lasted." She wrote. "And you drove up Thursday? From the Cities?"

"Hennepin County."

"Hennepin County, right. Close enough to the Cities."

"Close enough."

"Sure." She looked at her notes, or looked at the gesture of looking at her notes. Joel knew the difference. He'd watched prosecutors do it a thousand times — the performance of reviewing information you already had memorized. Or maybe she was actually looking at her notes. Maybe she was exactly what she appeared to be: a county detective from a department that handled property crimes and DUIs, who'd brought a casserole because that's what you did in Minnesota when someone died. He couldn't tell. And not being able to tell was making him crazy.

"Now," Mallick said, "someone mentioned — and I apologize, I don't remember who — but someone mentioned you and Martin had a disagreement? Thursday evening?"

There it was. Joel felt the muscles along his jaw go tight. He'd known it was coming from the moment she'd walked in with the Pyrex dish, but knowing a question was coming and hearing it asked were not the same thing, and the gap between them was where defendants got stupid.

"We had a conversation," he said.

"A conversation. OK." She wrote the word. "And was that Thursday? Or was it Friday — I might have the days confused."

"Thursday."

"Thursday. Thank you." She wrote. "And what was the conversation about?"

"It was private."

Mallick looked at him. The expression on her face was patient and slightly sad, the way a good teacher looks at a student who she knows can do better. Or the way someone looks when they're confused and trying not to show it. Both options fit. Neither felt right.

"Private," she repeated. "I understand. I do. It's just, with the situation, I'm trying to put together a picture of the last—"

"I understand what you're doing, Detective." Joel kept his voice level — the voice he used with judges, the one that said cooperation without offering compliance. "A man died in his sleep and you need a timeline. I'm telling you: we had a private conversation Thursday night, it was heated, and it had nothing to do with his death."

"You're probably right. Most arguments between old friends don't — well." She let it trail off with a small wave of her pen. "Can I just ask, for my notes — what time was the conversation?"

"Late. Eleven, maybe eleven-thirty."

"And where was this?"

"On the deck."

"The deck. OK. Good." She wrote. "And after the conversation, you..."

"Went to bed."

"What time?"

"Midnight. Probably a little after."

"And you stayed in bed until—"

"Until Nate woke me up. Around two."

"Two. And between midnight and two, you didn't get up at all? Bathroom, glass of water, check your phone, anything?"

"No."

"No." She wrote. The pen scratched against the paper. The refrigerator had cycled off and the kitchen was quiet, just the two of them and the scratch of the pen and the low murmur of one deputy's radio from the hallway. "You didn't hear anything? Footsteps, doors, someone moving around?"

"I had my door closed."

"Which bedroom are you in?"

"Upstairs. Second guest room, end of the hallway."

"Not the one near the bathroom?"

"That's Paul and Tommy's side. I'm at the other end."

"Oh — I always get these lake house layouts mixed up. So many rooms." She waved the pen vaguely. "So you didn't hear anything. The first thing you knew was Nate knocking on your door at two."

"Correct."

Mallick put her pen down. She looked at Joel with an expression that was either very honest or very carefully assembled, and the fact that he couldn't tell which was its own answer, or wasn't.

"Joel, can I be straight with you?"

"You can try."

"You're the calmest person in this house. By a distance. And I think part of that's because you're a lawyer — excuse me, public defender — and you're used to being around this kind of thing. I understand that, I really do. But here's where I'm at." She folded her hands over the notebook. "You had a heated argument with Martin the night before he died, you won't tell me what it was about, and you're the only person in this house whose account doesn't have a single loose thread. Everything lines up perfectly. Bed at midnight, door closed, didn't hear anything, woke at two." She paused. "And that's either because you've got nothing to hide, or because you're very, very good at this."

Outside, a loon called from the lake, the wavering cry carrying over the water and through the screen door.

"Nobody's a suspect here," Joel said. He used the word before she could.

"Oh, of course not. No, nobody's — this is just an unattended death, Joel, and I'm making sure I have the picture right." She picked up her pen. "You're right that it was probably a cardiac event. That's almost certainly what happened. A man his age, his habits — I'm sure you're right."

She let the word probably sit on the table between them. Joel let it sit there too. He knew the move. He'd watched prosecutors drop a probably into a silence and wait for the defendant to fill the space, to correct the probably into a certainly, to volunteer the detail that turned a question into a confession. It was a first-year technique, or it was genuine hedging from a county detective who wasn't sure about anything.

"Is there anything else?" Joel asked.

"Not right now. I appreciate your time, I really do." Mallick stood and pushed her chair back. "I might have some follow-up questions, if that's all right."

"I'm not going anywhere."

"Of course. None of you are — well, not today, anyway." She smiled again. "The coffee really is good. Thank you for that."

She walked toward the hallway, notebook under her arm, and Joel sat at the table with his cold mug and the specific hollow that settled after an interrogation that hadn't been an interrogation, and he realized what was bothering him.

She hadn't asked whether he was angry at Martin.

It was the most obvious question in the world. You argued with the deceased the night before he died — were you angry? Any first-year law student at William Mitchell would've asked it within the first three minutes. Joel had been ready. He'd had the answer prepared, the careful qualified response that acknowledged the argument's existence without opening the door to anything that could be repackaged as motive. He'd rehearsed it the way he rehearsed cross-examination questions — contingencies mapped, exits planned, the specific controlled language of a professional who knew how words worked in rooms like this.

She hadn't asked. Which meant she didn't think of it — possible, but hard to believe, even from a detective who brought casseroles and forgot names. Or it meant she already knew the answer and was more interested in watching how long he'd wait before volunteering it.

Joel sat with his cold coffee. On the wall, the draft board listed Martin's team in red Sharpie, the handwriting already starting to look like something from another era. Upstairs, Mallick's footsteps moved slowly from room to room, the floorboards announcing each step. Joel watched a deputy come down the stairs with an evidence bag, and the deputy skipped the third step from the top, the creak that everybody in this house had been avoiding for twenty-five years without ever fixing it.

---

Mallick went upstairs. The group drifted back to the great room the way they'd been drifting back to this room for a quarter century — gravitating toward their usual positions without discussion, dropping into their usual chairs, arranging themselves around the furniture in the specific configuration that twenty-five years of weekends had worn into habit. Martin's leather chair by the fireplace was empty. Nobody sat in it. Nobody looked at it, which was the same as looking at it.

"So," Nate said. He said it the way he launched podcast segments — loaded, building, ready to go somewhere.

"Don't," Joel said.

"Don't what?"

"Don't do the thing where you narrate what just happened while we're still inside of it."

"I'm not narrating, I'm — OK, fine. I'm processing. Some of us process out loud."

"Process quieter."

Tommy had come in from the deck and was standing by the kitchen island with his arms crossed, gripping his own biceps hard enough that his knuckles were white. He hadn't sat down. Hadn't been able to sit down since this morning, as far as Joel had noticed. "She ask you about Thursday night?" he said to Joel.

"Yeah."

"The argument."

"Yeah."

"What'd you say?"

"Nothing. I said it was private."

"Good." Tommy nodded once, the sharp definitive nod of a sideline call. "Good. Don't give her shit. Don't give any of them shit. You don't talk to cops — that's the first thing, that's rule one."

"Tommy—" Paul started.

"What? He knows. He's a goddamn lawyer, he tells his clients this every day. Don't talk to cops. Right?"

"What I tell my clients," Joel said, "is to answer questions truthfully and concisely and not to volunteer information beyond what's asked. There's a distinction."

"Fuck the distinction. The distinction is for courtrooms. This is a lake house and Martin's dead and she's upstairs right now going through his things. I'm saying don't talk."

Paul was sitting forward with his elbows on his knees, hands laced, staring at the floor between his feet. When he looked up, he wore the expression Joel had seen at claims adjustment meetings — the face that preceded an assessment nobody was going to enjoy.

"OK," Paul said. "We should probably coordinate. Before she comes back down."

"Meaning what?" Cliff said from his chair. He'd been quiet through all of this, watching, his coffee balanced on the armrest like a prop in a play he'd already read.

"Meaning if she asks us all the same questions, the answers should be consistent. The timeline especially."

"They'll be consistent," Cliff said. His voice was even and warm, the voice of a man explaining a deal's terms. "We were all here. We went to bed. Martin died in his sleep. That's the story, because that's what happened."

"Right, but the small things—"

"What small things, Paul?" Cliff uncrossed his legs and recrossed them the other direction. "Dinner at seven. Draft at eight. Drinks. Hot tub. Bed. Martin was alive when we went to sleep and dead when Nate found him. That's the story. Simple."

"When you say simple," Joel cut in, "you mean uncomplicated. Not easy."

"I mean both."

The room was quiet. Joel could hear Mallick's footsteps above them — slow, deliberate, the old floorboards creaking. She was in the upstairs hallway. Or Martin's bedroom. It was hard to tell from down here, and the not knowing made the ceiling feel lower than it was.

"She asked me about the hot tub," Tommy said. He'd started pacing — short laps between the kitchen island and the screen door, the agitated circuit of a man whose body needed to move when his mind couldn't. "What time we got in, who was in it, how long. She asked if the water was hot. If somebody adjusted the temperature during the night."

"What'd you tell her?"

"That I didn't remember exactly. We were drinking." Tommy's hand went to the back of his neck and gripped. "And she asked about — she called it the 'outdoor spa.' Like she didn't know what a hot tub was. Or she didn't—"

"She knows what a hot tub is," Joel said.

The room registered this. Tommy's pacing stopped. Nate's mouth opened, then closed. Paul took off his glasses and cleaned them on his shirt, the first normal Paul gesture Joel had seen all morning, and the normalcy of it — Paul cleaning his glasses in the middle of the worst day of all their lives — made something shift in Joel's chest that he chose not to examine.

"You think she's putting it on?" Nate said.

"I think I don't know," Joel said. "And I think that's exactly what she wants us to think."

"Or she's just a small-town detective from Becker County who doesn't deal with this very often and brought a casserole because that's what you do up here," Tommy said. "That's also a possibility. Not everything's a strategy."

"Both could be true," Doug said, and everyone turned because Doug talking unprompted was an event. He was still on the couch, still in the hoodie, but his phone was facedown on the cushion beside him. "She could be exactly what she looks like and still be — I don't know. Paying attention." He paused. "Every time someone corrected her, she wrote it down. She's been wrong about five or six things since she walked in, and she wrote down every single correction. That might be nothing."

Joel looked at Doug for a long moment. Doug looked at his own hands.

"OK," Paul said, pulling the room back. "So here's what I think. We answer her questions honestly, we don't volunteer anything beyond what she asks—"

"I already said that," Joel said.

"—and we don't contradict each other. If she asks about the timeline, we keep it simple. Dinner, draft, drinks, bed. Martin was alive when we turned in, dead when Nate checked on him. That's the truth and that's what we tell her."

"Why did Nate check on him?" Cliff asked.

Everyone looked at Nate.

"I wanted to make a side bet," Nate said. "About his first-round pick. It was a — we had a thing, a running thing about—"

"At two in the morning?" Cliff's eyebrows rose. The gesture was calibrated — just enough surprise to plant the question without making an accusation. Joel thought: this is why nobody in this room fully trusts you, Cliff. Because you're always positioning, even when you don't need to.

"We were all drunk," Nate said. "It didn't seem weird at the time. At the time it was just — it was normal. We do that."

"It's fine," Paul said. "Nobody's saying it's weird, Nate."

"Cliff literally just said it was weird."

"I asked a question," Cliff said. "Questions aren't accusations."

"OK, guys," Tommy said, and the coaching voice was on — the halftime voice, the third-quarter-deficit voice, the one designed to cut through panic and redirect. "Can we just — can we not do this right now? Martin's dead. There's a cop upstairs in his bedroom. Can we get through one day without this?"

The refrigerator kicked on with its loud, rhythmic cycling, the sound that had been the background of every midnight kitchen conversation for twenty-five years. From above, Mallick's footsteps moved from one room to another. A floorboard groaned, then another. She was in the hallway.

Joel looked at his five remaining friends and cataloged what he saw the way he'd cataloged a hundred courtroom galleries — Paul's controlled grief, the effort visible in his jaw and his too-clean glasses; Tommy's coiled, pacing energy, the body that couldn't find a position because every position was wrong; Nate's compulsive narration, the machine that wouldn't stop running even when the gears were grinding; Cliff's polished stillness, the composed surface that Joel instinctively didn't trust; Doug's retreat, the hood and the couch and the hands.

He had known these men for twenty-five years. He had drafted fantasy teams with them, gotten drunk with them, stood in their weddings and helped them through their divorces, and right now — sitting in this room where last night they'd argued about roster moves while Martin was still alive, still pouring scotch, still being Martin — Joel was certain of exactly one thing:

Every single one of them was lying about something.

He didn't know what. He didn't know why. But seven years as a public defender gave you a feel for the specific texture of a room where everyone had something to protect. It wasn't about evidence or inconsistencies — it was atmospheric, like the shift in pressure before a storm. The way people chose their words too carefully. The way they made eye contact at the wrong moments or avoided it at the right ones. The way Cliff's calm was too even and Tommy's agitation was too loud and Nate's talking was too much and Doug's silence was too complete.

The problem was that Joel had something to protect too. Not a tampering — he genuinely hadn't touched anything, hadn't moved through the house, hadn't done a single thing between midnight and Nate's knock except lie in the guest bedroom at the far end of the hall staring at the pine ceiling with the particular insomnia that comes from anger you can't discharge. He hadn't been thinking about Martin's health or the sound of footsteps in the hallway. He'd been thinking about Karen. About Martin and Karen. About eleven years of marriage that ended with a conversation at a Panera in Edina where she'd told him she wanted something different, and the eight months since when that something different had apparently been his fraternity brother, who was now dead in the room above their heads while a detective with a casserole and a leather notebook decided whether Joel's clean hands were the hands of an innocent man or a careful one.

He picked up his mug, realized the coffee was cold, and drank it anyway. It tasted like nothing.

"I'm going to sit on the deck," he said.

Nobody stopped him. He went outside and let the screen door close behind him and stood there in the September morning with the lake shining in the low gold light, loons calling somewhere out past the dock, the pine air cool enough that his breath wasn't quite visible but almost. Martin's house. Martin's lake. Martin's weekend that had outlasted Martin.

On the deck railing, someone had left a beer bottle from last night, the label peeled halfway off the way Tommy always peeled them when he was talking, his thick fingers working the paper while his mouth ran. Joel looked at it. The bottle was empty but it still smelled like beer, warm and stale, the ghost of a night that had felt normal while it was happening and would never feel normal again.

From inside, muffled through the screen door, he heard Mallick's footsteps coming down the stairs. She was done upstairs. He heard her voice — warm, chatty, asking one of the deputies something about the cedar paneling in the hallway.

Joel leaned on the railing and looked at the lake and thought: she's either an idiot or the most dangerous person in this house, and I can't tell, and that's the part that should be scaring me.
