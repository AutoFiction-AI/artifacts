# Chapter 12

Tommy was alone on the deck for the first time since Saturday morning. Monday afternoon, the sun low enough to turn the lake copper, and he had maybe fifteen minutes before someone came out to check on him or ask if he wanted another round of Mallick's questions or share their feelings about Martin or whatever the fuck else these guys couldn't stop doing.

He'd pulled one of the Adirondack chairs to the rail where he could put his feet up and watch the water. His hands were around a can of Busch Light that had gone warm twenty minutes ago. He wasn't drinking it. He was holding it the way he held a football before a snap drill — not for the thing itself but because his hands needed something to do.

It had been four days. Four days since Martin, and the house felt like a locker room after a loss that shouldn't have happened — everybody walking around like they'd forgotten how hallways worked, picking up cups and putting them back down, starting sentences and letting them die. Paul had been in Martin's study all morning doing whatever Paul did when he was processing, which meant reorganizing information into categories nobody asked for. Joel was somewhere being Joel, which meant radiating controlled fury at a man who couldn't receive it anymore. Doug was on the couch reading the same page of a magazine he'd been reading since breakfast. Nate was in the basement doing something with his phone that Tommy hoped wasn't podcast-related but probably was.

And Mallick had gone back to Detroit Lakes. Working the case, supposedly. Tommy didn't believe a goddamn word of it. That woman cataloged information the way Tommy cataloged opponent tendencies — every mistake filed, every tell indexed, waiting for the film review where it all made sense. She'd be back.

The screen door opened and banged shut behind him. Tommy didn't turn around.

"Hey." Cliff's voice. Easy, clean, the voice of a man who always sounded like he was about to offer you a deal on a time-share. "Brought you one."

A cold Busch Light appeared in Tommy's peripheral vision, held out at chest height. Cliff stood there in his pressed chinos and his collared polo — the guy had been at a lake house for four days and he still dressed like he was showing a property. His silver hair was combed. He'd shaved that morning. Tommy looked like a man who'd been sleeping in his clothes because he had been.

"Thanks." Tommy took the beer with his left hand and set the warm one on the deck. He cracked the new one. Cliff settled into the chair beside him, close enough that their armrests were six inches apart, which was closer than Cliff usually sat.

"Hell of a view," Cliff said.

"Yeah."

"Martin always had taste. I'll give him that. The man knew real estate, even if he didn't know—" Cliff paused, adjusted. "Even when it came to personal finance, the eye was there."

Tommy drank. The beer was cold enough to taste like something.

"You doing okay?" Cliff asked.

"I'm fine."

"Because you look like shit, if I'm being honest."

"I look like a guy who's been in the same house for four days with a dead friend and a detective who thinks I rearranged furniture in my sleep. So yeah. Shit."

Cliff laughed. Not his sales laugh — something closer to real. "Fair enough. Fair enough." He drank his own beer, some craft thing in a can Tommy didn't recognize. Of course Cliff brought his own beer to a lake house stocked with Busch Light and Miller High Life. The man couldn't drink a regular beer if you held a gun to his pressed chinos. "Listen, I wanted to talk to you about something."

"Okay."

"Not a big thing. Well — it's a thing. But I think it's a conversation we should have had a while ago, and the circumstances have kind of accelerated the timeline."

Tommy looked at him. Cliff was doing the thing he always did when he was working — leaning forward slightly in his chair, his beer resting on his knee, his posture communicating openness and accessibility. Tommy had watched Cliff do this at a hundred barbecues, a hundred draft nights, a hundred situations where Cliff was turning a person into a transaction. Cliff sold things. That was what he was. He sold real estate and he sold himself and right now he was selling something to Tommy on the deck of a dead man's house and he'd brought beer because Cliff always brought something.

"What conversation?"

"The money conversation. Martin's estate. The forty thousand he owed me."

Tommy shrugged. "Talk to a lawyer. That's not my thing."

"Right, and I will. Eventually. But here's what I'm thinking — and I'm just putting this out there, no pressure." Cliff paused long enough for the sentence to breathe, a technique Tommy recognized as the same one he used on anxious buyers. Let them sit in it. "You and Martin were tight. Tighter than I think some of the guys realize."

"We were friends."

"You were more than friends, Tommy. I mean — not like that." Cliff waved the implication off with his beer hand. "I mean Martin took care of you in ways that went beyond the regular friendship stuff. The way he always made sure you came back for the draft, even the years you tried to skip. Those little conversations you'd have, just the two of you. Out here, usually." He gestured at the deck, the chairs, the railing they were both resting their feet on. "Private conversations nobody else was part of."

"What's your point?"

"My point is you have weight with this group. If you say Martin acknowledged the debt, if you tell Paul and Joel that Martin intended to pay it back, that kind of thing — that puts social pressure on the process without anyone having to get lawyers involved. Martin owed me forty thousand dollars, Tommy. That's not a rounding error. That's real money from a real deal he walked away from."

"So talk to the estate."

"I will. But estates take time. Probate, lawyers, all of it. And I'm in a situation where time isn't something I've got a lot of." Cliff's voice didn't change, but something in his jaw did — a small tightening that Tommy recognized as the same look he saw in kids before they admitted they'd been the one who threw the punch. "My business is in a restructuring phase. And the forty thousand is — it's a real number. It's not theoretical money."

Tommy watched the lake. A breeze had come up from the north, putting small waves on the surface that caught the copper light and broke it into pieces. He could smell the water and the pine and, faintly, something cooking inside the house — probably Paul making pasta because Paul always made pasta when he was stressed, as if feeding people was a form of insurance.

"I can talk to Paul," Tommy said. "That's not hard. If Martin owed you the money, he owed you the money."

"He did."

"Then it's not a hard conversation."

Cliff nodded. He drank his beer. He looked at the lake. The silence held for maybe ten seconds, which was about eight seconds longer than Cliff usually let a silence sit.

"There's one other thing," Cliff said.

Tommy waited.

"I was in Martin's study. Sunday morning — I don't even know what day it is anymore. The safe was open. Martin left it unlocked, basically. The dial was one tick off, you could —" Cliff made a twisting gesture with his fingers. "One tick."

"Okay."

"I was looking for documentation. The loan, the forty thousand. Emails, contracts, whatever Martin had. And I found the financial stuff — that's all fine. But I also found something else."

Tommy's hand had stopped denting the beer can. His hand had stopped doing anything. It was resting on his thigh with the can balanced in his grip the way a quarterback holds a ball at the line of scrimmage — loose, or what looks loose, but the fingers know exactly where the laces are.

"What'd you find?"

"An envelope. Manila. Labeled 'RT.'"

The lake kept doing what the lake did. A loon called from somewhere past the dock, that long warbling sound that always made him think of his kids' first time up here, Denise wrapping blankets around them because she'd packed for July and it was September in Minnesota. Somewhere inside the house, the refrigerator cycled on with that loud mechanical groan that Martin had been saying he'd fix for fifteen years.

"Doesn't ring a bell," Tommy said.

"Yeah, I figured it might not." Cliff's voice had shifted into a register Tommy hadn't heard from him before — still smooth, still controlled, but with something harder underneath. Like the foundation of a building Cliff was showing you, where the finish was nice but you could feel the concrete through the carpet if you stood still long enough. "So I opened it. I figured it was real estate documents — RT, maybe a property code, an address. Martin was in real estate, I was in real estate, it made sense."

"And?"

"It wasn't real estate, Tommy."

The silence this time was different. Tommy could hear the blood in his own ears, which was a thing that happened to him on the sideline when a play went wrong — not a fumble, not a bad call, but the kind of wrong where a kid was lying on the field and not getting up and the whole stadium went quiet and Tommy could hear the hydraulics of his own body doing their work, pumping blood past his eardrums like a count that wouldn't stop.

"I don't know what you're talking about," Tommy said.

"It was a letter. Handwritten. Martin's handwriting — I've seen enough of his notes to recognize it. Dated August 2002." Cliff's voice was patient and level, the way it got when he was walking a buyer through a property's defects — not hiding them, just presenting them as facts that could be managed. "He wrote about a cabin fire. Washburn County, Wisconsin. A caretaker named Ray Toomey." Cliff let the name sit in the air between them. "He wrote about who started it. And he wrote about what he did afterward."

Tommy set the beer on the armrest. His hand was steady. His left forearm, where the scar was, had started to itch — a sensation he hadn't felt in years. He'd burned it on the door frame of the cabin in Washburn County twenty-two years ago and the scar tissue had settled into a smooth white ridge along the outside of his forearm that he wore long sleeves over in summer and that Denise had stopped asking about a decade ago.

"You read that," Tommy said. Not a question.

"I read it."

"And you — what. Took pictures."

Cliff looked at him. The smooth salesman face was still there, but the eyes were doing different work now — measuring, assessing, calculating square footage in a property he'd just realized was worth more than the listing price. "I'm a businessman, Tommy. I document things. It's habit."

"Fuck you it's habit."

"That's — look, let me put it this way." The reframe was instant, professional, almost admirable in its efficiency. "It's relevant. That's what it is. It's relevant to a conversation about Martin's estate, about the forty thousand, about how we all move forward from this weekend."

"You're blackmailing me."

"I'm not. I'm not even close to blackmailing you." Cliff held up a hand, palm out, the gesture of a man calming a situation that he believed he was in control of. "Blackmail would be 'give me money or I go to the police.' That's not what this is. What this is, Tommy, is two guys who both have a position here, and I'm saying let's find an arrangement that addresses both positions." He spread his hands, palms up. "You want that document to stay where it is. I want the forty thousand dollars that Martin owed me. These aren't incompatible interests."

Tommy stared at him. Cliff was sitting there with his craft beer and his pressed chinos and his leather loafers — the man wore leather loafers to a lake house, had been wearing them all weekend, and somehow Tommy had spent twenty-five years never letting this bother him — and his hands were smooth and manicured, the hands of a man who moisturized, who'd never done anything with those hands that didn't involve a pen or a handshake or the steering wheel of whatever German car he was leasing that year. He ran three miles a day on a treadmill and called it training. Tommy outweighed him by forty pounds and every one of those pounds had been put there on purpose, over years, in weight rooms and on practice fields.

The coaching part of Tommy's brain — the part that evaluated situations in terms of field position and time on the clock and what your personnel could handle — was doing something he couldn't quite control. It was calculating. Not the money. Not the negotiation. Something else. Something that had to do with the distance between this chair and the dock and the way sound carried over water at night.

The thought arrived and Tommy didn't push it away. He let it sit there the way a play diagram sits on a whiteboard — not a plan, just a shape, just lines and arrows showing where bodies could go if you told them to.

"Okay," Tommy said.

Cliff blinked. "Okay?"

"I'll talk to Paul. The forty thousand — yeah. I'll help move it along."

"Well, that's — I appreciate that. I do. That's a reasonable response to a reasonable situation."

"It's not about being reasonable. Martin owed you the money. He should've paid it."

"He should have. Two years ago, ideally, but—"

"So I'll help. Whatever you need. Pressure, conversations, whatever. I'll make it happen."

Cliff studied him. Tommy could see Cliff running the numbers behind those measured eyes — was Tommy too easy because he was angry, or too easy because he was planning, or too easy because he'd already decided to cooperate and was just letting Cliff talk himself out? Cliff would pick option three. Option three was the one that confirmed the deal, and Cliff always chose the interpretation that confirmed the deal.

"Good," Cliff said. He didn't stand up yet. He was still assessing, still working the close. "And the document—"

"Stays where it is."

"That's — right. Martin's business. Old history. Nobody needs to see it."

"Nobody needs to see it," Tommy agreed.

---

The memory came the way it always came — not as a story with a beginning but as a collection of sensory facts that his body had stored separately from his brain, as if the brain had refused delivery and the body had signed for it anyway.

The cabin. August 2002. He was twenty-five. Martin was twenty-five. They'd gone up to Washburn County for a weekend — a buddy's family cabin, or a buddy of a buddy, the chain of ownership so long that nobody checked because that was how it worked in your twenties, you knew a guy who knew a guy and the cabin was empty and you brought beer and nobody asked questions.

Friday night. The bonfire. Too much lighter fluid because Tommy had always been the guy who made fires too big, who couldn't just start a fire, he had to build one — the same thing that made him good at football made him bad at measured responses, the same physicality that coaches praised on the field was the thing that broke a screen door when he slammed it, that put a fist-sized hole in drywall when he lost an argument, that made every fire a performance of what fire could do if you let it.

The lighter fluid. The bonfire climbing higher than the tree line. Martin saying something — *that's enough, that's enough* — and Tommy squeezing more onto the base because the more Martin said enough the more Tommy wanted to show him that enough was a concept that didn't apply to Tommy Renaldi. Not at twenty-five. Not with eight beers in him and the Wisconsin summer dark around them and the feeling that the world was a thing you could grab if your hands were big enough.

Then the wind shifted. Or the fire found something it liked in the underbrush between the pit and the cabin's porch. Or the lighter fluid can was closer than Tommy thought. He didn't know. Twenty-two years and he still didn't know the mechanical sequence. He only knew the result: the fire jumped. Not toward the lake, not toward the clearing where Martin had set up the chairs. Toward the cabin.

The porch caught first. Dry cedar, old and desiccated, and it went up with a sound like someone crumpling heavy paper next to a microphone — that amplified crackling that wasn't like any fire Tommy had heard before or since, not the contained fires in a barrel or a pit but something alive, something that moved like it had an appetite and was glad to finally be feeding.

Martin yelled. Tommy ran toward the cabin, not away from it, because his first instinct was always to go to the problem — the coaching instinct, the football instinct, the thing that made him useful on a field and dangerous everywhere else. He grabbed the garden hose from the side of the cabin and it was dry, no pressure, the spigot handle spinning loose in his hand. He dropped it and ran around to the front where the propane tank was, thinking he could — he didn't know what he was thinking. He was twenty-five and the world was on fire and his thinking was an animal thing, all movement and no strategy.

That was when he saw the truck.

A green Ford pickup, dull paint, mud on the wheel wells. Parked in the gravel behind Martin's car. It hadn't been there when they arrived. Or it had been there and they hadn't looked, which was worse, which meant they'd been fifty yards from the bonfire they lit next to a cabin where someone was sleeping and they hadn't checked because checking wasn't something twenty-five-year-olds did.

Ray Toomey. The caretaker. Tommy didn't know the name then — didn't learn it until the newspaper report three days later, the one that said WASHBURN CO. CARETAKER DIES IN ELECTRICAL FIRE, the one Martin showed him in a McDonald's parking lot off Highway 53 with his hands flat on the steering wheel and his voice flat and his eyes doing something Tommy had never seen Martin's eyes do, which was nothing. They were doing nothing at all. Martin had turned off whatever part of himself was supposed to react and what was left was a man reading a newspaper headline to another man in a parking lot and saying, "They're calling it electrical. We don't talk about this again."

But that was three days later. In the moment, in the August dark with the cabin burning and the green truck in the driveway, Tommy ran to the front door and the handle was hot and he pulled it open and the heat came out like a physical force, like the thing heat becomes when it stops being temperature and starts being a wall that shoves you backward. Tommy's left forearm hit the door frame and the skin cooked — a half-second of contact, but the metal frame had already absorbed enough heat to do the job — and he jerked back screaming and the door swung shut and inside the cabin something fell. A beam or a section of ceiling. Something structural. The sound it made was the sound of a room closing itself off from the world, and under that sound, nothing. No voice. No movement. Just fire working.

He didn't go back in. He stood in the driveway with his forearm already blistering, the skin going waxy white where it should have been red, and Martin came around the side of the cabin and grabbed him by both arms and said, "We have to go. Now. Tommy. Now."

"There's someone in there."

"I know. We have to go."

"Martin—"

"There's nothing you can do. There's nothing — look at me. Tommy. Look at me." Martin's hands were on Tommy's shoulders, his grip tighter than Tommy had ever felt from a man who didn't do physical things, who hadn't played a sport since intramural soccer sophomore year. "There is nothing you can do for whoever's in there. We need to go."

And they went. Martin drove. Tommy sat in the passenger seat with his arm throbbing and his mind doing something it would never fully come back from, which was accepting that he had killed a man. Not on purpose. Not with malice or awareness. He hadn't known anyone was in the cabin, hadn't thought to check, had been too drunk and too young and too much himself to do the thing a careful person would have done, which was look at the goddamn driveway before pouring lighter fluid on a bonfire. But the result was the same. A man was dead in a burned cabin and Tommy's arm was burned and Martin was driving thirty miles an hour under the speed limit because Martin was always thinking about what came next while Tommy was still stuck in what just happened.

Martin took him to a clinic in Spooner the next morning. Grease burn, Tommy told the nurse. Deep fryer accident at a friend's place. She believed him or didn't care. The scar healed the way burns heal — slowly, badly, with a texture that announced itself to anyone who touched it. Denise asked about it on their third date. Tommy said, "Cooking accident. I was twenty-five and an idiot." Both parts were true even though neither part was the truth.

Twenty-two years. Every September, Tommy came back to the draft, and every September Martin would find a moment — on the deck, by the fire pit, during a walk to the dock — to check the seal. Not with words, usually. A hand on Tommy's shoulder. A look that lasted a beat longer than friendship required. Occasionally something spoken, always brief: "You good?" or "We're okay" or just Tommy's name in a tone that contained the whole history without breaking it open. And Tommy would nod or grunt or say "yeah" and that would be it for another year. The September ritual inside the September ritual. Martin was the valve that kept the pressure from blowing the pipe. Martin had written it all down and put it in a sealed envelope and put the envelope in a safe and left the safe one tick from locked, and Tommy had never asked why Martin wrote it down because asking Martin why he did things was like asking weather why it rained — the answer was beyond Tommy's operating level and always had been.

Now Martin was dead. The valve was gone. And Cliff Haywood, who sold houses for a living and brought craft beer to a lake house, was sitting on this deck with his moisturized hands and his leather loafers treating twenty-two years of managed disaster like a real estate opportunity with an attractive cap rate.

---

The screen door banged. Cliff came back with two more beers, even though Tommy hadn't finished the one he had. He set one on Tommy's armrest like a man topping off a client's water glass.

"So," Cliff said, settling back into his chair with the ease of a man who believed the hard part was over. "Logistics."

"Logistics."

"Paul's the one to start with — he's the mediator type, right? Insurance background, knows how estates work, at least roughly. If you tell him that Martin acknowledged the debt in conversation, that you heard him say he intended to pay it back — that creates social momentum. Gets the process moving without anyone pulling in attorneys."

"Mm-hmm."

"And there's probably some flexibility around assets. I'm not saying anything drastic. I'm saying there are mechanisms for settling debts before probate closes. Liens, purchase agreements on specific assets, that kind of thing. It can be clean."

"Mechanisms," Tommy repeated.

"Structures." Cliff glanced at him. "You're quiet."

"I'm listening."

"That's — okay. Good." But something in Cliff's face said it wasn't good, said that Tommy being quiet wasn't what he'd expected. Tommy could see Cliff running the numbers — was Tommy too quiet because he was angry, or too quiet because he was planning, or too quiet because he'd already agreed and was just letting Cliff finish? Cliff would pick the third option. The third option confirmed the deal, and Cliff always, always chose the interpretation that confirmed the deal.

"The thing is, Tommy, this doesn't have to be adversarial. I know how it sounded out there. Guy comes to you with information, asks for a favor — it sounds like a movie. But it's not that. Martin owed me the money. You know that's real. I've got emails from 2022 where he says, quote, 'I'll make it right by the end of the year.' End quote. He didn't. He died instead, which — I'm not blaming him for dying. But the debt didn't die with him."

"I get it."

"And the other thing — the document." Cliff's voice went careful here, calibrated, the tone of a man naming a defect while emphasizing the property's upside. "I've got no interest in that. It's old history. Your history and Martin's. I didn't know this Toomey—"

"Don't say his name."

The sentence came out flat and final. Not loud. Tommy hadn't raised his voice. But the temperature on the deck shifted by several degrees, and Cliff's mouth closed and stayed closed for three full seconds, which was the longest Tommy had ever seen Cliff Haywood go without following up on a silence.

"Fair enough," Cliff said quietly. "My point is, I've got no stake in whatever happened back then. What I've got is a financial position that needs resolution, and you're the person who can help resolve it. That's the full scope of what I'm asking."

Tommy looked at the beer on his armrest. Cliff had set it there the way he set things down in other people's houses — with the casual territorial confidence of a man who believed every surface was available to him if he was polite about using it.

"The photos are on your phone," Tommy said.

"Phone and cloud."

"So even if the letter disappeared—"

"It wouldn't change anything. That's correct." Cliff said this without triumph. He said it the way he'd tell a buyer that yes, the property had a lien, and yes, it would need to be cleared, but here's how they could work around it. "But I'm not holding it over you, Tommy. I want to be clear about that. It's just how I manage sensitive information. It's good practice."

Good practice. The phrase landed on Tommy like something physical — the weight of a man who thought documenting a twenty-two-year-old death was the same thing as saving a contract to the cloud. Cliff had filed Ray Toomey alongside his invoices and his property listings and his tax returns, and he didn't see anything wrong with that because Cliff didn't see categories — he saw positions. Leverage was leverage the same way a dollar was a dollar, regardless of what the dollar was made of.

"Okay," Tommy said.

"Okay."

"I'll talk to Paul this week. The forty thousand — you'll get it. I'll make sure."

"I appreciate that. I really do."

"And we don't talk about this again. You and me. This conversation didn't happen."

"Clean break," Cliff said. "Professional resolution. I couldn't agree more." He stood up, stretched, cracked his neck with a sound that made Tommy's jaw tighten. Then the smile — not the full-wattage Cliff smile, the one he used on clients and women and anyone whose agreement he needed. A smaller one. More private. The smile of a man who believed he'd just closed the best deal of a very bad year. "I think this is the right move. For both of us."

"Sure."

"I'm going to see what Paul's doing about dinner. You coming in?"

"In a minute."

"Don't sit out here too long. Gets cold when the sun drops."

"I know how September works, Cliff."

"Right. Yeah. Sure." Cliff went inside. The screen door banged shut. The sound of his loafers on the kitchen floor — two distinct steps, the leather soles on the old hardwood — and then the house swallowed him.

Tommy sat in the Adirondack chair. It was the same one he'd sat in every September for twenty-five years, the one closest to the rail, the one with the armrests worn smooth by two decades of men resting beers on them. Martin had bought these chairs the year he bought the house, which was the year after the fire, and Tommy had never let himself think about whether there was a connection between those two purchases because thinking about connections was thinking about the architecture of the thing between them, and the architecture was Martin's department. Martin built the structure. Tommy lived in it. That was the arrangement.

The scar on his forearm had stopped itching. His hands were flat on the armrests, palms down, fingers spread. He looked at them. Not the way he usually looked at his hands — with the casual ownership of a man who used them for everything, who coached with them and cooked with them and fixed things around the house with them. He looked at them the way he sometimes looked at his players during film review. Not evaluating their skill. Evaluating their capacity. What they could do if the play called for it.

Cliff's phone was in Cliff's pocket. The cloud backup was wherever cloud backups lived — some server, some building, some place Tommy couldn't reach. But Cliff wasn't a server. Cliff was a hundred and seventy-five pounds in pressed chinos who ran three miles a day on a treadmill and thought that was exercise.

Tommy thought about the dock. He'd walked it a hundred times. The wooden planks, the slight give under his weight, the swim platform at the end where the kids used to jump off before they got old enough to think jumping was uncool. The water beside the dock was three feet deep. Maybe less where the silt built up near the pilings. Enough to swim in if you were a kid, enough to wade in if you were an adult.

Not enough to drown in if you were conscious.

The thought sat on the whiteboard of his mind. Not a play yet. Just a shape. Lines and arrows showing where a body could go if someone told it to. He didn't push it away. He didn't draw it up. He let it sit there the way you let a bad idea sit on the board during a late practice, the kind of idea you're supposed to erase before the players see it, except Tommy didn't erase it because it wasn't a bad idea. It was a simple idea. It was the kind of idea Tommy was built for — physical, direct, one step, done.

The loon called again from somewhere out past the dock, and the lake had gone from copper to the flat gray it turned when the sun dropped behind the far tree line — the color change so gradual you couldn't point to the moment it happened, but the result was definitive. The golden afternoon was gone. September nights in Minnesota started warm and ended cold. You could go from shirtsleeves to shivering in an hour. The deck planks got slick when the temperature dropped and the dew settled. A man in leather loafers on wet wood in the dark could lose his footing. Might not even make a sound that carried eighty yards through pine and birch to the house, especially with the loons calling and the refrigerator running and nobody listening for anything because why would anyone be listening.

Tommy picked up the beer Cliff had brought him. The can was still full. He held it for a moment, then tipped it over the rail and watched the liquid catch the last gray light for a second before it hit the ground and disappeared into the pine needles below the deck.

He set the empty can on the armrest where Cliff had put it. He wiped his hands on his shorts.

Then he went inside.
