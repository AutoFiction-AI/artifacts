# Chapter 13

The draft board was still on the wall.

Nate had been looking at it all day, the way you look at something you should probably take down but haven't because taking it down would make it real. Martin's picks were circled in red Sharpie — Martin's red Sharpie, Martin's steady block capitals, each player name written with the same deliberate pressure Martin used for everything from grocery lists to lease agreements. The whiteboard was the erasable kind, and someone would eventually wipe it clean, and twenty-five years of data would disappear in one pass of a damp rag.

But not yet. Not today. Today the board stayed up and Martin's picks stayed circled and the great room still smelled faintly of the Lagavulin that Martin had been drinking Thursday night, and if Nate squinted at the room from the right angle it almost looked like any other year.

It was Joel who brought it up, because Joel was constitutionally incapable of letting a procedural question sit unresolved. They were in the great room — six men who should have been five and used to be seven — and Joel said, "So are we going to talk about the roster?"

Nobody said anything for a second.

"Martin's roster," Joel clarified, as if they might have thought he was talking about someone else's. "The season starts Thursday. His team's sitting there. Someone needs to decide what happens."

Tommy, from the far end of the couch, said, "His team plays."

"OK, but—"

"His team plays. That's his roster. Those are his guys. You don't mess with a man's roster."

"I'm not saying mess with it. I'm saying someone has to set lineups. Bye weeks, injuries, the waiver wire. You can't just let the app—"

"Auto-manage works fine."

"Auto-manage is dogshit," Nate said, because it was, and because saying things about fantasy football was the only behavior that still felt normal. "Auto-manage started Dalton over Herbert in week four last year. I did a whole segment on it. Auto-manage will bench your stud receiver on a Thursday game because the algorithm doesn't understand revenge narratives. Auto-manage is for people who don't care, and Martin cared more than anybody in this room."

That last part came out heavier than Nate had intended, and the room absorbed it.

"So someone manages it," Paul said. He was sitting in Martin's chair — Martin's leather recliner by the fireplace, the one Martin had claimed in 2006 and defended with the quiet immovability of a man who owned the house. Paul either hadn't noticed or had noticed and was doing it on purpose. "Someone takes over. Makes the waiver moves, sets the lineups. As a — I don't know."

"Tribute," Joel offered.

"That's a little Hunger Games."

"It's what you mean, though."

"It's what I mean, yeah. Someone runs his team the way he would've run it. Keeps it competitive. Honors the — I don't know. The work he put in."

"And who does that?" Joel leaned back. "Who among us thinks like Martin thought about fantasy football?"

"I do," Paul said.

Tommy made a sound from the couch. Not quite a laugh.

"I'm serious. I was his best friend for twenty-five years. I know how he thought about matchups, I know his waiver tendencies, I know—"

"Paul." Tommy sat forward. "You don't know shit about football."

"I know plenty about football."

"You took a tight end in the second round last year. The wrong tight end."

"Kelce went second round in every serious league that—"

"You took Kittle."

"Kittle had a great week one."

"Kittle had a great week one and then he had Kittle's season, which is what Kittle always has, which is why you don't take him in the second round, which is what Martin would've told you if you'd asked him, which you didn't because you thought you knew better."

Nate watched this play out with the part of his brain that was always watching, always noting, always filing things away in the folder marked *potential content*. Tommy's voice had the old rhythm — the coaching cadence, the halftime certainty, the way he built an argument by repetition until the other person either capitulated or got tired. It was the most normal Tommy had sounded in two days.

"OK," Nate said, "so Paul wants to manage it, Tommy thinks auto-manage, Joel's asking the procedural question nobody wants to answer. What about the rest of us? Cliff? Doug?"

Doug, who was sitting on the floor by the fireplace with a glass of water that Nate kept glancing at because it should have been a beer, shrugged one shoulder. "I think Paul should do it."

"Why Paul?"

"He'd actually do it. He'd pay attention." Doug took a sip of water and set the glass down with the careful precision of a man whose hands weren't entirely steady. "The rest of us would forget by week six."

"I wouldn't forget," Nate said.

"You'd turn it into a podcast arc."

Nate opened his mouth to argue and then closed it because Doug was right and the fact that Doug could see through him in four words while sitting on the floor nursing water was both impressive and slightly embarrassing. Doug saw things. That was Doug's whole deal — he was so quiet that people forgot to perform around him, and then he said something that made you realize he'd been watching the whole time.

"Fair," Nate said. "Cliff?"

Cliff was in the armchair by the window, legs crossed, holding a beer he hadn't drunk much of. He looked comfortable in the way Cliff always looked comfortable — settled, composed, the posture of a man who'd learned to take up space like he deserved it. "I think Tommy's right," he said. "Let the team play as-is. Auto-manage. Don't overthink it."

That was the second time in ten minutes that Cliff had agreed with Tommy without arguing about it first.

Nate clocked this. He clocked it the way a play-by-play guy clocks a coverage change — not with alarm, not with certainty, just a mental note that something in the defensive alignment had shifted and he should keep watching. Tommy and Cliff had fought about everything for twenty-five years. They'd fought about kicker scoring rules for forty-five minutes in 2019. They'd nearly thrown hands over a trade veto in 2016 — Nate still referenced it in his top-ten all-time episodes. Cliff and Tommy's default setting was opposition. It was how they related. It was their version of friendship, the friction that proved the connection.

And now Cliff was agreeing with Tommy quickly, smoothly, the way he agreed with a client. And Tommy was accepting it without the pushback that should have followed, the "damn right I'm right" or "since when do you agree with me, Haywood" that had been part of their rhythm since college. Tommy just nodded. Not at Cliff — at the space between them.

They were sitting on opposite ends of the room. They'd been on opposite ends of every room since yesterday.

"OK, new question," Nate said, because redirect and narrate and fill space was what he did, it was the skill that paid his rent and the skill that would probably get him killed someday, but at least he'd die talking. "What about trades? Can you trade with Martin's team?"

Joel's eyebrows went up. "You want to trade with a dead man."

"I don't want to. I'm asking if it's possible. If his team is auto-managed and someone offers a trade, and the algorithm accepts—"

"The algorithm doesn't accept trades. It rejects everything."

"Some platforms auto-accept if the trade improves the team's projected score."

"So you're asking whether a bot can consent to a roster transaction on behalf of a deceased owner." Joel looked at Nate with the expression he used in depositions, the one that said *I want you to hear what you just said.* "Is that the question?"

"When you put it like that—"

"That's how I'm putting it."

"It sounds weird when you put it like that."

"It is weird, Nate. The whole thing is weird. We're sitting in a dead man's house deciding what happens to his fantasy football team while a detective who may or may not be an actual detective is probably somewhere writing up a report about how five of us fucked with the crime scene. The whole thing is weird."

The room got quiet. Joel had that ability — the lawyer's talent for reducing a situation to its elements, stripping the cushion off the thing until you were sitting on the frame. Nate respected it and hated it in equal measure, because Joel's precision had a way of collapsing the bit, and the bit was the only thing between Nate and the actual shape of what was happening.

"No trades," Tommy said, quietly. "Nobody trades with his team. That's—no. You don't do that."

"Agreed," Paul said.

"Agreed," Cliff said, half a beat too fast.

The silence after that had a texture to it. Nate could feel the gears in the room — the things people wanted to say and the things people were choosing not to say and the gap between the two, which was wider than Nate had ever felt it in twenty-five years of weekends in this house. The refrigerator in the kitchen cycled on, its compressor kicking with the familiar clunk that had been the baseline hum of every late night they'd ever spent here, Martin standing at the counter at 2 AM making a sandwich, the fridge humming, the lake outside in the dark.

"We need a new commissioner," Nate said.

"You," Tommy said. "You've got the podcast. You're already tracking everything."

"I don't want to be commissioner."

"Nobody wants to be commissioner."

"Martin wanted to be commissioner," Paul said.

"Martin tolerated being commissioner because nobody else would do it right."

"For Martin, that's the same thing."

That landed and Nate let it sit, because Paul was right and because the truth of it — that Martin's love for them had always been expressed as control, as management, as the willingness to maintain the scoring system and enforce the trade deadlines and make sure everyone showed up every September even in the years when showing up was hard — was as close to a eulogy as any of them were going to manage on a Monday afternoon in the great room.

"Fine," Nate said. "I'll be interim commissioner. Of a league with no bylaws, no constitutional authority, a dead owner, and a scoring system from 2004 that still counts punt return yards because Martin thought punt return yards mattered and none of us ever had the balls to tell him they didn't."

"Don't touch the punt return yards," Tommy said, and his coaching finger came up, pointed at Nate across the room. "Martin put those in. You don't touch them."

"I'm not touching anything."

"You touch the scoring settings, I'm done."

"You're not done. Nobody's done. That's the whole—" Nate stopped. The bit was right there — *nobody's done, because the last person who was in is dead, and quitting would mean admitting the thing is over, and nobody in this room is ready to admit the thing is over* — and he could feel the shape of it, the rhythm, the way it would sound on the podcast, the thirty-second clip that would get shared. And then the bit collapsed, because he couldn't find the angle that made it funny without making it cruel, and the space where the bit should have been was just six guys in a dead man's living room who didn't know how to say they missed him without a scoring format.

"Nobody's done," Nate said, quieter. "We're playing the season. Martin's team plays. Auto-manage. Paul checks in on it. I'm commissioner. Punt return yards stay. Anything else?"

"Waiver priority," Joel said. "Martin had third priority. It redistributes — that's the only equitable ruling."

"Redistributes," Nate said. "Sure. Yeah. Done."

He looked at the draft board. Martin's picks in red. The names of players who would play the season for a man who wouldn't watch them.

Tommy got up and went to the kitchen. Nate heard him open the fridge, heard a bottle cap hit the counter, heard Tommy stand there for a second too long before coming back. When he came back he had two beers. He handed one to Cliff.

"Thanks," Cliff said.

"Yeah," Tommy said.

They didn't look at each other. The exchange took less than three seconds and told Nate nothing he could articulate and everything he could feel: something had happened between these two men, something that had replaced the friction with courtesy, and courtesy between Tommy Renaldi and Cliff Haywood was as wrong as silence in a room full of people who'd been talking over each other for a quarter century.

Nate filed it. He didn't know what to do with it yet, but he filed it.

---

They grilled steaks because that was what you did on the last night. Tommy manned the grill because Tommy always manned the grill — feet planted, tongs in his right hand, beer in his left, the posture of a man whose confidence began in his body and extended outward from there. He worked the propane burners with the easy precision of someone who'd spent his life managing heat and timing: the grill, the practice field, the weight room, the thing inside himself that Nate had always read as intensity and was starting to wonder if he'd been reading wrong.

The steaks were Martin's. Two-inch ribeyes from the chest freezer in the basement, purchased and portioned before the weekend, each one labeled in Martin's handwriting with a date and a cut. They were eating a dead man's groceries. Nobody mentioned it. The not-mentioning-it sat on the deck like a seventh person at a table set for eight.

The deck table had eight chairs. They took their usual seats, which left Martin's empty at the head and turned the arrangement into a thing with a gap in it. Nate sat facing the lake, next to Paul. Doug was across from him. Joel sat next to Doug. Tommy took the end nearest the grill. Cliff sat at the opposite end.

Three empty chairs between Tommy and Cliff. Nate counted them.

Three chairs between two men who, forty-eight hours ago, would have been shoulder to shoulder at the grill end, arguing about whether Tommy was ruining the sear by opening the lid every thirty seconds, the annual steak fight that had been as much a part of these weekends as the draft itself. Cliff would have said *you're letting the heat out, Coach*, and Tommy would have said *I've been grilling longer than you've been pretending to know what medium rare looks like*, and someone — Martin, usually — would have told them both to shut up and drink their beer.

Now Cliff sat three chairs away and cut his meat in small, careful movements, and said nothing about the sear.

The sunset was doing the thing it did in September on Elbow Lake — the light going from gold to amber to something Nate didn't have a word for, the color of the end of a weekend, the color of wanting to stay. The birches along the shoreline were turning at the tops, a yellow that hadn't decided whether it was autumn yet, and the lake was flat enough to hold the sky in it.

"Beautiful," Cliff said. To nobody. To the lake.

"Yeah," Doug said.

Cliff's fork paused. "Martin always said September was the only month that mattered."

"He said that about every month," Paul said.

"He said it most about September."

The conversation died. They ate. Tommy's steaks were good — medium rare, heavy pepper, the coarse salt from the grinder Martin had bought from some kitchen store in 2018 that everyone had mocked and then used every year since because it was Martin's grinder on Martin's deck for Martin's steaks.

Nate ate and watched.

Joel was doing the thing. The Joel thing. He'd look at Paul, hold the look for half a beat, then look away. Then again. Look, hold, away. It had the rhythm of a signal — not casual glancing but the deliberate eye contact of two people who had information to exchange and couldn't do it here. Nate had watched Joel do this in the courtroom once, the year he'd gone to see Joel argue a motion, the same silent choreography with co-counsel before a sidebar. Joel and Paul were building something. Nate didn't know what, but the body language was a conversation the rest of the table was excluded from.

Doug pushed his steak around his plate. He'd taken three bites that Nate had counted. His pint glass was water, still water, and he held it the same way everyone else held their beers but the camouflage wasn't working because the glass wasn't sweating the right way and there was no label and water catches light differently than pale ale. Doug had lost weight since last year — not good weight, not I-joined-a-gym weight, the kind that came off when food stopped being interesting and getting out of bed became the hardest thing you did each day. His hoodie was a size too big now. His face was thinner and the circles under his eyes had the permanence of something that wasn't going away with a good night's sleep.

He was forty-seven and he looked fifty-five.

"You doing OK?" Nate asked, quiet enough that it was just the two of them.

Doug looked at his plate. "Yeah. Fine."

"Yeah?"

"I'm good, Nate." He picked up the water and took a sip and set it back down. "Just tired."

"Sure."

"It's been a long weekend."

"It has."

Doug nodded and went back to pushing his steak and Nate let it go because Doug's voice had the quality of a lid being held down on something that was pressing upward, and Nate knew enough about men who were holding things down to know that pressing didn't help. You let them hold the lid. You just made sure they knew you could see the lid.

"Hey," Joel said, louder, to the table. "Anybody remember when Martin fell off the dock?"

"Oh, shit," Nate said, and the memory arrived fully formed. "2012."

"Was it twelve?"

"It was twelve because we were arguing about RG3," Paul said. "Whether you could trust him as a keeper with the knee."

"He just walked off the end," Joel said. "Not tripped, not slipped. Just walked. Like the dock was three feet longer than it actually was."

"He was making a point," Nate said. "Fumble return touchdowns. He wanted to add fumble return touchdowns to the scoring and he was up out of his chair waving his arms around and he walked backward right off the end of the dock."

"Was he drunk?"

"He'd had four scotches, but that wasn't — Martin with four scotches was baseline Martin. He wasn't drunk, he was making a point. He was making a point so hard that the dock ran out."

"And he went in," Paul said. He was smiling — the real smile, the one that came before grief interrupted it. "He went in and he came up coughing and the first thing out of his mouth—"

"Oh God—"

"The first thing out of his mouth, he's standing in three feet of water with his shirt soaked and his drink gone and his phone ruined, and he raises his hand"— Paul raised his own hand, the gesture involuntary, mimicking a dead friend from twelve years ago — "and he says—"

"'Motion still carries,'" Nate and Paul said at the same time.

"'I'm making a formal motion and it still carries,'" Joel added. "He said it like a goddamn parliamentarian. He's standing in the lake and he's citing Robert's Rules."

"And Doug went and got him a towel," Paul said.

"I got him a towel," Doug said, and for a second he was back, he was present, his face had the animation of a man remembering a thing he'd been part of. "He wouldn't take it. He said he wasn't leaving the water until someone seconded the motion."

"Did someone second it?"

"Tommy seconded it," Nate said. "Just to get him out of the water."

"I seconded it because he was right," Tommy said from the railing. He'd turned around to listen, his beer resting on the deck rail, and his face had something on it that wasn't quite a smile and wasn't quite grief but was the expression of a man looking at a place he couldn't go back to. "Fumble return touchdowns are a legitimate scoring category and Martin was right and the only reason we didn't add them is because Cliff voted it down."

"I voted it down because it would've blown up the historical comparisons," Cliff said, and for a moment — for one moment — the table sounded like itself. Like twenty-five years of arguments about things that didn't matter and meant everything. Like Thursday nights and Saturday mornings and the sound of six voices and a seventh that would never be part of it again.

They laughed. All six of them, together, and the sound traveled out over the flat water the way sounds do on Elbow Lake — further than it should, longer than it should, reaching distances where it had no business arriving.

Then the laughter trailed off. The silence after it was different from the silence before. Warmer and sadder.

Paul was looking at his plate. Joel was looking at Paul. Tommy was looking at the lake. Cliff was looking at Tommy. Doug was looking at his water. And Nate was looking at all of them, because that was his role and his curse and the thing that kept him in this group and the thing that kept him at a distance from it — the watcher, the narrator, the man who experienced his own life at a slight remove because somewhere in his brain a producer was always saying *good stuff, can we get that again from a different angle.*

He couldn't get this again. He knew that without knowing why he knew it. This particular configuration — six men on a deck, September light, the smell of grilled steak and pine and lake water, the sound of a shared laugh still ringing in the space above the table — was not going to happen again. The next time they sat on this deck, if they ever sat on this deck, something would have changed. Something was already changing. He could feel it the way you feel a weather system before it arrives, the barometric drop, the wrongness in the air that your body reads before your brain catches up.

Tommy turned back to the lake. The sunset had burned down to a thin line of orange along the tree line and the sky above it was going purple. The loons started — that sound, that specific sound that existed halfway between music and mourning, the sound of a lake in September when the summer people were gone and the water was getting cold.

"Gonna clean up," Paul said, and started stacking plates.

Joel helped. Doug brought the glasses inside. Cliff gathered the bottles — six empties and one glass of water — and carried them to the recycling bin by the kitchen door. Tommy stayed on the deck, resting his forearms on the railing, looking out at the dock at the far end of the stone path. The dock was a dark shape against darker water, the swim platform at the end just visible, the small bench where Doug had been sitting earlier in the weekend, alone, looking at the lake like a man who needed the lake to look back.

They filtered inside one by one. Doug first, with a quiet "night" that barely registered. Joel, with a nod to Paul that contained more information than any nod should be able to carry. Paul, who stopped at the sliding door and looked back at Nate on the deck and then at Tommy on the railing and seemed to decide something and went inside without saying it. Cliff said "Good night" to the deck generally — "Night, everyone" — and went to his room.

That left Nate and Tommy.

"Hey, Coach," Nate said.

Tommy didn't turn around. "Yeah."

"We're going to be all right. You know that, right?"

Tommy's left hand tightened on the railing. Nate could see the tendons in his forearm, the scar that ran along the outside of it — pale, old, the kind of scar you stopped asking about because the answer was always the same deflection. *Got it in high school. Doesn't matter.* Tommy's hands were the biggest hands in the group, built for gripping things, and right now they were gripping the railing like the railing was the only solid thing left.

"Yeah," Tommy said. "We're fine."

"Good."

"Night, Griggs."

"Night."

Tommy went inside. Nate heard his footsteps on the stairs, heard him skip the third step — everyone in this house skipped the third step, it had been creaking since 2003 and Martin had never fixed it because Martin said the creak was a feature, not a bug, it told you who was moving around at night — heard the upstairs hallway and then a bedroom door.

Nate sat on the deck alone.

The lake was black. The loons had stopped. Across the water, a single light — dock light or porch light, somebody else's house, somebody else's last night of something. The kitchen behind him threw his shadow long across the deck boards, out toward the stone path, out toward the dark pines and the dock beyond them.

He thought about the podcast. He thought about what he'd say, if he ever said anything, if this was his to say. The opening came to him the way openings always did — fully formed, the rhythm already there: *So here's the thing about a keeper league. It's not about the players you keep. It's about the people who keep showing up.*

He said it out loud. Quietly, testing. It was good. It was the kind of line that would land in the first thirty seconds and get clipped and shared and liked by people who'd never met Martin Kessler and never would.

He hated it. He hated that it was good and he hated that he'd thought of it and he hated that the part of his brain that made content never shut off, not for death, not for grief, not for the specific unbearable fact of an extra steak wrapped in foil in the fridge of a man who wouldn't eat it.

"Shit, Martin," he said, to the dark lake.

He went inside. He locked the sliding door. He went down to the basement where his pull-out sofa waited with his duffel bag and his podcast mic and his phone with Mia's last text — *Miss you. Come home safe* — still glowing on the screen. He brushed his teeth with the bathroom door open because the basement bathroom was small and the ventilation was bad and Martin had always said he'd fix the fan and never had. He set his alarm. He lay in the dark and listened to the old refrigerator kick on, its compressor clunking into its forty-minute rhythm, and the house above him settling with the sounds old houses make — pipes, wood, wind, or the weight of six men trying to sleep in a house that still belonged to the seventh.

He thought about Tommy's hands on the railing and Cliff three chairs away and Doug's water and Joel looking at Paul looking at Joel, and he thought: *something is wrong and I don't know what it is and I'm the guy who's supposed to know what it is.*

He filed the thought. He'd come back to it. He'd come back to all of it — the punt return yards and the draft board and Martin's steak and the loons and the lake and the sound of six men laughing at the same time for the last time.

He didn't know it was the last time. But his body knew. His body had been keeping score all weekend, and the score was bad, and the clock was running, and nobody had called the timeout.

He slept. Eventually, they all did. It was the last night all of them would sleep in this house, but nobody knew that yet, and the house held them the way it always had — old wood, lake damp, the pine smell and the dark water and the dock reaching out into the lake like a question that hadn't been answered.
