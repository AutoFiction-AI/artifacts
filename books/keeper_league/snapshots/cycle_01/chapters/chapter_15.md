# Chapter 15

The call came at 11:47 PM. Mallick was on the couch watching the ten o'clock weather — a front pushing in from the Dakotas, rain by Wednesday — and thinking about whether the tomato cages would hold. She picked up on the second ring.

Dispatch gave her the address and she knew it before the numbers finished. Martin Kessler's lake house. Elbow Lake. Second call to the same property in four days.

She wrote it down anyway. She always wrote it down.

She changed out of sweats. Blazer, polo, slacks, the low boots she wore for outdoor scenes. She clipped her reading glasses to the chain around her neck, took her leather notebook from the kitchen counter, and left through the garage. The casserole dish was in the cabinet above the stove. She did not take it.

The drive from Detroit Lakes took twenty-six minutes at that hour. No traffic on County Road 6, just her headlights cutting through the pines and the occasional pair of deer eyes flashing green at the tree line. She drove with the window cracked. September cold at this hour — low forties, the kind that reminded you October was two weeks away. She didn't turn on the radio.

She thought about Saturday. The green bean casserole. Getting Kessler's name wrong — Kessner, she'd called him, three times — and the way they'd all fallen over themselves to correct her. Paul Decker with his careful insurance-man precision. Cliff Haywood with his smooth real-estate courtesy. Each correction a gift she'd cataloged and filed.

Saturday was a different job. Saturday she'd been working a suspicious death that smelled like natural causes layered under five separate panic attacks. She'd built a map of who knew what, who'd been where, who corrected which detail about a house they all claimed to have slept through. That map was in her notebook. Thirty-seven corrections across six interviews, each one a small betrayal of knowledge the corrector didn't realize he was demonstrating.

Now someone was dead for real.

She turned onto the gravel drive at 12:13 AM. Two county cruisers were already parked at angles near the front door, light bars cycling blue and red against the pine-paneled A-frame. Every window in the house was blazing. She could see figures moving behind the glass — deputies, and the dark shapes of men standing or sitting in the great room.

She parked behind the cruisers and got out. The gravel popped under her boots. She could hear the lake — that constant low lapping that had been the background frequency of every conversation she'd had in this house. On Saturday it had sounded restful. Tonight it sounded like the thing it was: water moving against wood in the dark.

Deputy Arneson met her at the front door. Mid-thirties, big, competent. He'd been first on scene Saturday too.

"Detective. Same house."

"I noticed. What have we got?"

"Cliff Haywood, forty-eight. Found floating face-down in the water beside the dock by one of the other guests at approximately eleven-thirty PM. We pulled him out. Couldn't leave him in there — the other guest was hysterical, thought we could revive him." Arneson's jaw moved. "He was cold. Been in awhile."

"Who found him?"

"Doug Whitfield. The quiet one."

She remembered Doug. Monosyllables. Bags under his eyes. The kind of silence that made you listen harder.

"Where is everyone?"

"All five in the great room. We kept them together — didn't know if you wanted them separated."

"I do. Where's Kelleher?"

"With them."

She walked through the front hallway into the great room. The stone fireplace, the vaulted ceiling, the draft board still hanging on the far wall with Martin Kessler's picks circled in red marker. The smell of the house was the same — pine panels, old smoke, the ghost of Lagavulin — but the room was different. Four days ago it had the energy of a crisis being managed by men who thought management was possible. Now it had the energy of men who had stopped believing that.

She counted. Five.

Doug Whitfield was on the couch, wrapped in a fleece blanket that someone had put around his shoulders. His face was the color of the lake water he'd pulled his friend out of. He didn't look up when she entered.

Paul Decker was standing near the kitchen island, wire-rimmed glasses slightly crooked, hands folded in front of him the way men fold their hands when they want to look useful. He straightened when he saw her.

Joel Beckman was leaning against the fireplace mantel with his arms crossed, watching her. His eyes were doing the thing they'd done Saturday — tracking, assessing, reading her the way she read crime scenes. On Saturday, he hadn't been sure if she was incompetent or strategic. She saw the moment he decided.

Nate Griggs was pacing the length of the great room, podcast cap on, mouth already open before she'd said a word. Deputy Kelleher was standing near him with the expression of a man who'd been listening to a monologue for forty minutes and had stopped processing it sometime around minute twelve.

Tommy Renaldi was in the Adirondack chair closest to the deck door. He was sitting with his hands between his knees and his head down. He was still. That registered. Saturday he'd been a caged animal — pacing, gripping things, coaching-volume voice filling whatever room he occupied. The stillness was new.

Mallick addressed the room.

"I'm Detective Mallick. I was here Saturday investigating the death of Martin Kessler." She said the name correctly, without hesitation, the way she'd always known it. "I understand Mr. Haywood was found in the lake."

The name landed. She watched it land. Paul's eyes moved to Joel's. Nate stopped mid-pace. Even Doug looked up, briefly, and something crossed his face — not recognition exactly, but the awareness of something changed, something missing. The wrong-name detective who'd bumbled through their kitchen with a casserole and asked about the hot tub brand was gone. She'd walked in through the same front door and there was nothing left of the woman they'd corrected and dismissed and managed.

"Nobody leaves this property," she said. "Nobody touches anything. I need each of you in a separate room for the duration of the night. Deputy Arneson will get you situated."

Paul stepped forward. "Detective, is there anything we can—"

"Stay in the kitchen, Mr. Decker. There'll be coffee on by the time I get back." She turned to Arneson. "Mr. Beckman in the study. Coach Renaldi stays here. Mr. Whitfield stays where he is. Mr. Griggs in the basement."

Nate said, "I can tell you what happened. I mean, I didn't see it, but I can give you the whole evening, start to finish, everyone's movements, who went where—"

"That's helpful. I'll come find you." She said it without warmth and without the fluttery gratitude that had made Nate pour out his entire voicemail saga on Saturday. Nate's mouth closed.

She turned to Arneson. "Call dispatch. I need the BCA crime scene unit. Tell them homicide, not accidental drowning. They can stage out of Detroit Lakes and be here by first light." She paused. "Tell them the scene's been partially compromised — the body was removed from the water prior to my arrival."

Arneson nodded and stepped out. Kelleher began moving the men. Doug stood slowly, the blanket still around him, and shuffled toward the couch at the far end of the great room — not a different room, but far enough. Joel pushed off the mantel without comment and walked toward the study. His posture said he'd expected this. Tommy didn't move until Kelleher touched his shoulder, and then he just looked up, and the expression on his face was not the expression of a man startled by a hand. It was the expression of a man who'd been waiting for something and it had arrived.

Mallick went out the back door.

---

The stone path from the deck to the dock was eighty yards through a corridor of white pine and paper birch. At 12:30 AM in mid-September, with no moon and the cloud cover from the approaching front blotting out the stars, it was dark in a way that city people forgot existed. Her flashlight cut a narrow cone through the pines. The gravel crunched differently out here — smaller stones, some dirt, pine needles layering over the path in a way that would hold a footprint in daylight. She'd need the BCA to photograph the path before anyone else walked it.

The temperature had dropped since she'd left her car. Low forties, maybe high thirties at the water. Her breath showed. The lake smell was different at the dock — not the background note she caught on the deck, but the actual thing: cold water, decomposing vegetation, the mineral tang of lake bottom stirred up close to shore.

Arneson had left a battery-powered spot at the end of the dock, throwing a cone of harsh white light across the planks. Cliff Haywood's body was there, laid on his back on the dock's weathered cedar, arms at his sides in the approximate position of a man the deputies had straightened from a sprawl. His eyes were closed. She would've preferred them open — lividity in the sclera could tell you things. His mouth was slightly ajar. A thin line of dried frothy fluid traced from his left nostril across his cheek. More of it crusted at the corner of his mouth.

She crouched beside him and put on gloves.

His clothes first. The pressed chinos were dark with water, clinging to his legs. The collared polo — navy, she thought, though the spot washed out colors — was twisted slightly at the torso, the kind of displacement that happened when a body was dragged or pulled. His silver hair was plastered flat, lake water still beading on the strands that had begun to dry.

His shoes.

Leather loafers. Tasseled. Italian-looking. The kind of shoe Cliff Haywood wore inside the house — she remembered them from Saturday, clicking on the hardwood when he walked to his interview, the sound of a man who dressed for impressions even at a lake house. She'd noted it in her book: *Haywood — leather shoes indoors, boat shoes out.*

She remembered the boat shoes too. Tan Sperry-type deck shoes, broken in and salt-stained. He'd been wearing them Sunday when she'd seen him crossing the deck. Dock shoes. Outdoor shoes. The shoes a man wore when he walked eighty yards down a gravel-and-pine-needle path to the water.

These were not those shoes.

She lifted his right foot. The sole of the loafer was clean under the instep — no grit, no gravel dust, no pine needle residue. The toe was scuffed and wet, consistent with the foot being dragged or dangling. He hadn't walked to the dock in these shoes. Either someone had changed his shoes, or he'd been brought here wearing them, which meant he hadn't intended to be here.

She set the foot down and moved to the head.

The wound was on the left temple, slightly above and behind the ear. A laceration roughly two inches long, the edges relatively clean — a hard, flat surface, not a rock or branch. She looked at the dock railing. Cedar, splintered in places, but the splintering was old, weather-worn, not freshly broken. She looked at the bench at the end of the dock — smooth-worn pine seat, no blood, no hair, no indication of impact.

She stood and looked at the dock edge. If Cliff had fallen from a standing position on the dock, he'd fall forward or sideways into the water. A fall forward would produce a wound on the forehead, the bridge of the nose, the chin — frontal impacts. A sideways stumble might catch the railing, but the wound was above and behind the ear, which meant the force came from the side and slightly behind. You didn't get that angle falling down. You got it from someone standing next to you or behind you with something hard in their hand.

She looked at the water beside the dock. The continuity sheet in her memory was accurate: roughly three feet deep here, dark, the bottom a mix of sand and silted clay. Three feet of water didn't drown a conscious adult. A conscious adult stands up. An adult struck hard enough to stagger, to lose coordination, to go into the water face-first with the disorientation of a head blow — that adult might not stand up fast enough. Especially if someone was holding him down.

The frothy fluid confirmed what she suspected. Pulmonary edema. He'd breathed water. His lungs had tried to work and found lake instead of air. The autopsy would confirm it, but she'd seen enough drowning victims to know the difference between a dead man placed in water and a living man who died in it.

She stood up and took a step back. The spot threw Cliff Haywood's body into flat relief — every crease of his chinos, every tassel on his wrong shoes, the thin line of froth dried on his face like a trail someone had forgotten to wipe.

She turned her flashlight on the dock surface. Water marks. Scuffing. But the cedar planks were old and marked by twenty-five years of foot traffic, boat ropes, and weather. The BCA team would need to process all of it. She'd preserve what she could until they arrived.

She walked the length of the dock, checking the swim platform at the far end. Nothing. She walked back and paused at the spot where the dock met the shore. The stone path began here. She crouched and put her flashlight close to the ground. In the margin where gravel met grass, there was a partial boot print — larger than the loafer, a different tread pattern. It could be a deputy's. It could be Doug Whitfield's, from when he found the body. It could be someone else's.

She stood and looked back toward the house. Eighty yards through the pines. Every window still blazing. She could see the deck, the shape of the Adirondack chairs, the dark rectangle of the hot tub cover. From here, at night, the house was a distant constellation of warm light. From the house, the dock was invisible.

Someone had walked this path tonight with Cliff Haywood, or behind him, or carrying the evidence of what they'd done to him. Someone who knew the way in the dark.

Her assessment took shape. She'd write it down in the kitchen, but it was forming now, solidifying with each detail that didn't add up to accident:

Staged. Poorly. The head wound came from a weapon or a hard surface applied with force, not from a fall. The water killed him while he was alive and incapacitated. The shoes were wrong — the stager didn't know or didn't care which shoes Cliff wore outside, which meant it was someone who either didn't pay attention to Cliff or didn't have time to find the right pair. The body was in the right place — the dock — but dressed for the wrong place. Someone strong enough to handle a five-eleven man. Someone desperate enough to do this and sloppy enough to think leather loafers on a dock would pass.

She turned off her flashlight and stood in the dark for a moment. The lake lapped against the dock pilings. A loon called once from the far shore, low and carrying. The sound covered everything. Out here, at the end of the dock at night, you could do anything and the house would never hear it.

She walked back up the path toward the lights.

---

The kitchen table was where Mallick set up. The same table she'd sat at Saturday morning, accepting coffee, getting the name wrong, asking about the hot tub. The old refrigerator clicked on with its forty-minute wheeze while she opened her notebook and flipped to the section she'd labeled with a blue tab: *Kessler residence — corrections.*

Thirty-seven entries. Each one a moment where someone in this house had corrected a piece of wrong information she'd introduced, and in correcting it, had revealed something about what they knew and where they'd been. She ran her finger down the list:

*Decker — corrected body position (supine, left arm tucked). Knows original orientation. Was with body before police arrival.*

*Haywood — corrected study location ("second door past the bath, not the first"). Was in the study. Reason: financial documents? Safe?*

*Griggs — referenced voicemail he'd claimed not to have left. Tampered with phone. Compulsive talker — cannot withhold under conversational pressure.*

*Renaldi — corrected nothing. Did not volunteer spatial knowledge. Evasive about movements night of death. Was in bathroom (flushing) during initial arrival. Physically agitated Saturday.*

*Whitfield — corrected nothing. Spoke in monosyllables. Either concealing or overwhelmed. No spatial knowledge demonstrated.*

*Beckman — corrected timeline ("the argument was before midnight, not after"). Legal precision. Did not tamper. Did not conceal spatial knowledge. Angry, not evasive.*

She'd built this map to understand Martin Kessler's death — who'd panicked, who'd moved through the house, who knew what about a scene that five men had independently rearranged. She'd concluded, correctly, that Martin's death was natural and the tampering was a five-car pileup of individual guilt. Case headed for closure. Natural causes, contaminated scene, scared men, embarrassing but not criminal.

Now Cliff Haywood was dead on the dock and the map was something else entirely.

She flipped to a fresh page and wrote: *Haywood homicide — Tuesday, 12:30 AM assessment. Applying correction data from Kessler investigation.*

She started with what the map told her about Cliff. Cliff had been in Martin's study. He'd corrected her about the room's location — which meant he'd been in there, and he'd wanted her to know he knew where it was, or more likely he hadn't been able to stop himself from correcting her because his sales-trained mind couldn't let a factual error stand. Either way: Cliff had been in the study. The study had a wall safe. The safe had been open — she'd noted that Saturday, had assumed it was related to the financial dispute between Cliff and Martin. Forty thousand dollars owed.

But what if Cliff had found something else in the safe? Something that wasn't about money? Something that made Cliff a target for someone other than Martin's estate?

She wrote: *What was in the safe besides financial records? Revisit. Priority.*

She drew a line and started the interview notes.

Doug Whitfield came first. She went to the couch where he sat under his fleece blanket. He looked worse under the overhead light than he had when she'd arrived — the gauntness she'd noticed Saturday had deepened into something skeletal, and his hands, wrapped around a mug of water someone had given him, were trembling.

"Mr. Whitfield. I need to walk through what happened tonight, starting with when you went to the dock."

"Yeah." His voice was thin. "I went down around eleven-thirty. Maybe a little before."

"You've been going to the dock every evening?"

He nodded. "It's just — I like it down there. It's quiet."

She remembered. His dock routine. His chapter of the weekend — the quiet man who went to the water because the house was too full of other people's noise. She'd filed it under *Whitfield — habits* and moved on. Now the habit was the reason he'd found a body.

"What did you see when you got there?"

"He was in the water." Doug's hands tightened on the mug. "Face down. Next to the dock, on the — the left side, if you're walking out. I thought he was — I didn't know what I thought. I thought maybe he was swimming but it's cold, it's too cold to—" He stopped.

"What did you do?"

"I yelled. I think I — yeah, I yelled. And I went in. The water's only—"

"Three feet."

"Yeah. I pulled at him. I got him turned over and his face was—" Doug set the mug down on the cushion beside him. The water sloshed. "I dragged him onto the dock. His body was heavy. Water makes them heavy. I know that from — anyway. I got him on the planks and I yelled again and I ran up to the house."

"Did you notice his shoes?"

Doug blinked. "What?"

"What was Mr. Haywood wearing on his feet?"

"I don't—" He shook his head slowly. "I was looking at his face. I was trying to — I don't remember his shoes. I'm sorry."

She didn't push it. Doug's observation skills were precise when he was functioning — she'd noted that from her Saturday assessment. But he'd just pulled a dead man out of a lake. The precision was offline.

"Did you see anyone else on the path or near the dock before you found him?"

"No."

"Did you hear anything? Voices, movement, a splash?"

"No. Just the lake. The usual — the water sounds."

She thanked him. He pulled the blanket tighter and looked at the mug he'd set down, the water still trembling from where he'd placed it.

She went downstairs.

Nate Griggs was sitting on the edge of one of the pull-out sofas in the basement, Deputy Kelleher standing by the door with the same glazed expression he'd worn upstairs. The basement smelled like laundry detergent and the particular mustiness of a below-grade room near a lake. Nate stood up when she came down the stairs.

"Detective. Okay. So I've been going through it in my head and I think I can account for basically the whole evening, starting from when we finished dinner at like seven, seven-fifteen—"

"Mr. Griggs."

He stopped.

"I have specific questions. I'd appreciate specific answers."

"Yeah. Sure. Of course."

"What time did you last see Cliff Haywood alive?"

Nate's hand went to the bill of his podcast cap. He tugged it, resettled it. "Dinner. So that's — we ate around six-thirty, finished around seven-fifteen. Everyone was at the table. Cliff was there. After dinner some guys went to the deck and some went — I think Tommy went upstairs pretty early, like eight-thirty or nine. Cliff was on the deck for a while. I saw him out there when I came up to use the bathroom, which was maybe nine, nine-fifteen."

"Was he alone?"

"On the deck? I think — yeah, I think so. Maybe Paul was out there too? I'm not sure. I wasn't paying close attention, I was just passing through."

She wrote it down. "Mr. Griggs, you have recording equipment. A clip-on microphone you use for your podcast."

Nate went still in a way she hadn't seen from him before. The last time she'd mentioned his recordings — obliquely, on Saturday — he'd spiraled into the voicemail confession. Now something different crossed his face. Wariness, but also something else: the particular guilt of a man who knows his hobby has just become consequential.

"I had my mic going during dinner," he said. "I was recording the waiver wire discussion. For a segment. I do these — it doesn't matter. I had it clipped to my shirt and the phone was on the table recording."

"When did you stop recording?"

"After dinner. I turned it off when we cleaned up. So like... seven-fifteen, seven-thirty."

"Did you record anything after that? Later in the evening?"

"No." He hesitated. "Well — I had the mic plugged in down here. It's possible the phone picked up ambient — like, it wasn't recording-recording, but the app sometimes buffers if I don't close it all the way. I'd have to check."

"I'll need your phone."

"My — the whole phone?"

"The recording application and any audio files from this weekend. All of them."

Nate looked at Deputy Kelleher, then back at her. The podcaster's possessiveness — his content, his material, his equipment — was visible, and so was his understanding that possessiveness was not the right response when a man was dead on the dock. He reached into his pocket and handed her his phone.

"Passcode is 4421," he said.

"Thank you."

"Do you want me to explain what's on there? Because some of it is just — it's fantasy football stuff, it's not—"

"No. I'll listen to it myself."

She went back upstairs with the phone in an evidence bag.

Joel Beckman was in the study, sitting in Martin Kessler's desk chair with his hands on the armrests, not touching the desk surface. He'd positioned himself precisely: accessible but uninvolved. The posture of a man who understood chain of custody.

"Mr. Beckman."

"Detective." He stood. "You're not going to ask about the casserole this time."

She didn't acknowledge it. Joel was testing — the same way he'd tested her Saturday, looking for the seam between persona and person. He'd found it.

"When did you last see Cliff Haywood?"

"Dinner. Six-thirty to approximately seven-fifteen. After that I went upstairs to the guest room. I was reading. I didn't come down until I heard Doug screaming."

"What time was that?"

"Eleven thirty-two. I checked my phone when I woke up."

She wrote it down. His precision was genuine — not the rehearsed precision of a liar but the habitual precision of a man whose profession required him to know what time things happened.

"Did you notice any tension between Mr. Haywood and any other member of the group today?"

Joel's expression shifted. The legal armor came up — she could see it, the slight contraction around his eyes, the way his jaw set. He was deciding how much to give her.

"Cliff and Tommy," he said. "They were strange with each other all day. Since Sunday, actually. Too polite. Sitting apart at dinner. Not arguing, which — Tommy argues with everyone. The fact that he wasn't arguing with Cliff was the thing that stood out."

"Can you be more specific about the nature of the tension?"

"No. I noticed it but I don't know what caused it. Cliff was doing business with everyone all weekend — he's a deal-maker, that's his default setting. Something between him and Tommy went past business."

"Past business how?"

Joel's mouth thinned. "I don't have evidence for this. It's an impression. Tommy was afraid of Cliff. Not physically — Tommy's not afraid of anyone physically. But there was something Cliff had. Some kind of leverage. I could feel it at dinner."

She wrote it down. Joel watched her write.

"You're going to look at Tommy," Joel said.

She looked up. "I'm going to look at everyone, Mr. Beckman."

"Sure." He sat back down. "But you're going to start with Tommy."

She didn't confirm it. She closed the notebook and walked out.

She left Tommy for last.

He was in the same Adirondack chair, in the same position — hands between his knees, head down. When she pulled a chair over and sat across from him, he lifted his head. His eyes were red, but not from crying. From something else — the same flat exhaustion she'd seen in people who'd been carrying a weight for so long that the weight had become structural, and removing it would mean collapse.

"Coach Renaldi."

"Yeah."

"Where were you between ten PM and midnight?"

"In bed. Upstairs. I went up early. Nine, nine-thirty."

"Did you hear anything during that time? Movement in the house, someone going outside?"

"No."

"Did you leave your room at any point after nine-thirty?"

"No."

She watched him. His hands were still between his knees. The scar on his left forearm — she'd noticed it Saturday, a ropy line from wrist to mid-forearm, the kind of scar that came from a burn, not a cut. His sleeves were pushed up tonight, the scar fully visible. On Saturday he'd kept his sleeves down.

"How would you describe your relationship with Mr. Haywood?"

"He was a friend. We've been friends for twenty-five years."

"Any recent disagreements?"

"No."

The flatness of his answers was diagnostic. Saturday, Tommy had been a furnace — loud, agitated, physically restless, the coaching voice filling the room. Every answer had come with body language that could fill a paragraph: the neck-gripping, the pacing, the knuckle-cracking. Now he was giving her nothing. No heat. No volume. No movement. It was the most information he'd given her all weekend.

She didn't push. Not yet. The BCA would be here by morning. She'd process the scene, the path, the dock. She'd listen to Nate's recordings. She'd pull Cliff's phone records and find whatever was on his device. She'd revisit the safe in Martin's study and find out what Cliff had been looking at when he corrected her about which room it was in.

Tommy Renaldi was strong enough. Tommy Renaldi had been afraid enough — she could see it retroactively now, the fear she'd read as agitation on Saturday, the coaching-mode bluster that was containment, not personality. And Tommy Renaldi was careless enough, or distracted enough, or panicked enough, to put a dead man's indoor shoes on and think that would look right.

She stood up. "Thank you, Coach. I'll need to speak with you again in the morning."

"Yeah," Tommy said, and looked back at the floor.

She went to the kitchen.

The refrigerator clicked off — the end of one of its forty-minute cycles. The silence it left was the specific silence of a house where the machinery has paused and the people have stopped talking and there's nothing left but the building settling and the lake outside doing what it always did.

She sat down at the table. The casserole dish was visible through the glass refrigerator shelf — the green bean casserole she'd brought Saturday morning, covered in foil, untouched. It had been in there four days. Nobody had eaten it. Nobody had thrown it out. It sat there the way evidence sat in a locker, waiting for someone to decide what it meant.

She opened her notebook to the fresh page and wrote her preliminary assessment:

*Cliff Haywood. Homicide. Blunt force trauma to left temporal region, inconsistent with fall. Frothy fluid suggesting pulmonary edema — drowned while alive. Water depth at dock ~3 ft. Leather loafers, not victim's habitual outdoor shoes — staged. Body positioned at dock. Shoes wrong.*

*Manner: struck from behind/side, incapacitated, submerged and held. Killer: physically strong, emotionally desperate, logistically careless. Knew the dock. Didn't know the shoes.*

She drew a line and wrote below it:

*Correction map — Kessler investigation. Haywood was in the study. The study has a safe. Haywood found something in the safe. What he found got him killed.*

*Renaldi: no corrections Saturday (careful). Agitated then, silent now. Tension with Haywood since Sunday per Beckman. Physical capability: confirmed. Motive: TBD but present.*

*Griggs recordings: dinner audio, possible ambient buffer post-dinner. May contain timeline data — voices, movement, door sounds. Priority: listen before BCA arrival.*

She set down her pen. Outside the kitchen window, the first gray suggestion of dawn was touching the tree line across the lake — not light yet, just the darkness becoming slightly less absolute. The BCA team would be here within hours. She'd need to walk the stone path again in daylight, process Cliff's room for anything he'd left behind, and get into that safe.

She thought about Saturday morning. Walking up to this front door with a warm dish in her hands, getting the name wrong, asking about the bathroom. She'd liked these men, in the way she liked most people she investigated — they were scared and human and doing a bad job of hiding it, and the bad job was where the information lived. She'd felt almost fond of their collective bumbling. Five amateur cover-ups on top of a death that didn't need covering.

She didn't feel fond now. One of these men had hit Cliff Haywood hard enough to stagger him, put him in three feet of water, and held him there while he drowned. One of these men had stood in the dark on the dock and felt another person stop breathing under his hands and then walked back up the stone path to the house and gone to bed.

She picked up Nate's phone in its evidence bag. She'd start with the audio. The recordings a man had made for his small, precarious podcast — draft night banter, fantasy football arguments, the ambient sound of an evening among friends — were now the backbone of a murder timeline. The podcaster who couldn't stop recording had captured the sounds of a house where someone was about to die, and he'd done it for content.

She put on her reading glasses and opened the evidence bag.

The refrigerator clicked back on. The loon called once more from the lake, farther away now, barely audible through the kitchen window. Mallick pressed play.
