# Chapter 3

Tommy couldn't sit down.

He'd been standing for forty minutes, maybe an hour — he'd lost the sequence somewhere between Nate coming down the basement stairs saying Martin wasn't breathing and Paul running up to the master bedroom and coming back down with his glasses crooked and his shirt soaked in something Tommy didn't want to identify. Now it was — he checked the microwave clock — 3:14 in the morning, and he was standing in Martin's kitchen gripping the back of a chair so hard the wood was creaking, and he could not sit down because sitting down meant the situation was settled and the situation was not fucking settled.

Nate was on the couch in the great room, still in the shorts and T-shirt he'd been sleeping in, his face doing something Tommy had never seen on it — a kind of caved-in blankness, all the comedy evacuated. Paul sat at the kitchen table with both hands wrapped around a mug of coffee he hadn't touched. Joel was outside on the deck, which was either smart or suspicious — Tommy wasn't sure which. Doug had gone back to the basement, or maybe he hadn't. Cliff was somewhere. Cliff was always somewhere.

Martin was upstairs in the master bedroom, dead.

Tommy let go of the chair and walked to the refrigerator and opened it. He didn't want anything from the refrigerator. He closed it. Walked to the window over the sink. The lake was black. The pines were black against something slightly less black. A loon called — that idiot laugh they made, deranged and full-throated — and Tommy pressed his forehead against the cold glass and thought: even the goddamn birds.

He'd been doing this since Nate told them. Opening things and closing them. Picking things up and putting them down. Pacing the length of the kitchen, turning at the counter, pacing back. His body wanted to do something and his brain couldn't tell it what, so it was doing everything and nothing, the engine running in neutral while the car sat in the driveway going nowhere.

"We should call somebody," Tommy said. He'd already said this. He'd said it twenty minutes ago and Paul had said they should wait until morning, wait until they could think straight, and Tommy had said OK, and now he was saying it again because his mouth worked on loops when the rest of him broke down.

"In the morning," Paul said, same as before. His voice was flat. Insurance-adjuster flat. The voice of a man who assessed damage for a living, and the damage this time was his best friend dead in a lake house in September.

"We should call now."

"It's three in the morning, Tommy. Who are we calling? The guy's dead. He's —" Paul stopped. He pressed his thumb and forefinger against the bridge of his nose, under the wire rims, and held them there. "In the morning. We'll call in the morning."

Tommy turned from the window. He wanted to say something useful. He wanted to take the room by the shoulders and shake it into functioning. This was what he did — he walked into rooms full of seventeen-year-olds who were down by twenty-one at the half and he told them the play and they ran it. He'd done it for twenty years. He could do this.

"All right, listen," he said. He pointed at Paul. "Decker, you're the most put-together guy in this room right now. You handle the phone calls when the time comes. Family, police, whoever. Griggs —" He turned toward the great room. "Griggs, you good? You need water? Somebody should eat something."

Nate looked at him from the couch with an expression Tommy would later understand as pity but in the moment read as confusion.

"Tommy," Nate said.

"I'm serious. We can't just stand around with our dicks in our hands. We need a —"

"He's dead, man." Nate's voice cracked on the second word, and he said it again, quieter, to the floor. "He's dead."

Tommy's hands went to the back of his neck, both of them, fingers interlocking behind his head. The posture of a coach watching a bad play develop downfield in slow motion, the kind of play where the safety's been beat and the receiver is already gone and all you can do is stand there gripping the back of your own skull and watch. He held it there for five seconds, ten, squeezing until the tendons in his forearms burned.

"Yeah," he said. "I know."

He walked to the counter and braced both palms flat against the laminate and leaned into it, head down, shoulders loaded, the same stance he used calling timeouts he didn't have. The coaching thing had lasted ninety seconds. Ninety seconds of pretending this was a problem that could be organized, delegated, and resolved by the fourth quarter. It wasn't. Martin was dead and there was no play to draw up and the halftime talk had hit the room like a dropped pass in the end zone — a thing Tommy had reached for that his hands couldn't hold.

And then the thought came back.

It had been there since Nate first said the word "breathing." Not a fully formed thought at first, just a cold pressure below his ribs, a sensation that didn't belong to the grief or the shock but lived next to them — a tenant that had moved in while the other emotions were still unpacking. Now, standing in Martin's kitchen at 3:14 in the morning with his palms on the counter and the smell of Lagavulin still living in the air like Martin's ghost had stopped to have one more drink on his way out, the thought landed.

The pills.

Tommy had given Martin the Viagra on Friday evening. That was the joke. That was the bit. Twenty-five years of the same gag — every draft weekend, Tommy brought Martin something stupid. The inflatable woman that year they were all thirty. The Rogaine when Martin started losing his hair. The novelty jockstrap that said FANTASY STUD that Martin wore over his jeans for twenty minutes because Martin committed to bits the way other men committed to marriages. And this year: a sample pack of Viagra, four pills in a foil blister pack, purchased from a guy at Tommy's gym who sold them loose because Tommy was not about to walk into a CVS and explain that he needed a prescription he didn't have for a gift he was buying as a joke for a friend whose love life was none of his business.

He'd tossed the blister pack to Martin at the dinner table Friday night. "For the old man," he'd said, "since Karen's got the Kessler magic working overtime." Everybody had laughed. Martin had called him a goddamn idiot and slid the pills next to his water glass and that had been the end of it. The joke delivered, the joke received, the joke retired to Martin's nightstand where it could die of natural causes.

Except Tommy had seen Martin take one.

Friday night, after the hot tub, after Joel and Cliff had gone to bed and it was just Martin and Tommy and Paul on the deck finishing the Lagavulin. Martin had excused himself, gone upstairs, and when Tommy passed the master bedroom door on his way to the guest room he'd seen Martin standing at the nightstand pressing one pill through the foil with his thumb. Not for Karen — Karen was in Minneapolis, or wherever Karen was. Not for anyone. Just the bravado of it, the escalation of the bit. Martin had seen Tommy watching and given him a look — the look, the one that said "watch this" — and dry-swallowed the pill and winked, and Tommy had told him he was out of his mind and Martin had said "I've been told" and that was the last funny thing Martin Kessler ever said and Tommy was the only person in the house who understood what it might mean.

Viagra and hearts. Tommy was not a doctor. He was a high school football coach with a bachelor's in physical education from the University of Minnesota and twenty-two years of mandatory first-aid certifications that had taught him exactly enough to apply pressure and call 911. But he knew — everybody knew, it was on the commercials, it was the whole reason the joke had teeth — that you weren't supposed to take that shit if you had heart problems. The warning labels. The disclaimers. The part of the commercial where the voice got fast and the text got small. And Martin had been drinking all night and Martin had been in the hot tub for an hour and Martin's face had been ruddy and flushed since before the first beer hit and now Martin was dead at forty-seven of something that looked exactly like what happened when you gave a man a pill his heart couldn't handle and then watched him wash it down with scotch and soak in a hundred-and-two-degree hot tub.

Tommy pushed off the counter. He could feel his pulse in his palms where they'd been pressed against the laminate, the blood pounding in the flat of each hand like something trying to get out.

He looked at Paul. Paul was staring into his coffee.

"I'm going upstairs," Tommy said.

Paul didn't look up. "What for?"

"Bathroom."

Paul nodded. The universal masculine acceptance of the bathroom excuse, the social contract that said a man's stated reason for going upstairs at three in the morning was whatever the man said it was and nobody needed to verify it. Tommy went.

The stairs were dark. He skipped the third step — everybody who'd ever stayed in this house knew to skip the third step, the creak was a twenty-five-year-old hazard, Martin had been saying he'd fix it since Clinton was president — and his foot found the fourth step by muscle memory. The hallway upstairs was lit by a night-light plugged into the baseboard outlet near the shared bathroom, the kind shaped like a shell that Martin's mother had probably installed in 1994 and no one had ever replaced. Its glow made the pine-paneled walls look amber. Tommy could see the door to the master bedroom. It was open. Paul had left it open, or someone had.

Tommy didn't want to go in there. He wanted to go in there the way you wanted a root canal — not at all, with the understanding that the alternative was worse. The alternative was the three remaining Viagra pills sitting in a foil blister pack on Martin's nightstand, waiting for whoever came to deal with the body — the EMTs, the cops, the coroner, the guys whose job it was to catalog exactly what a dead man had in reach when he died — to find them and pick them up and start asking questions Tommy did not want to answer.

He went in.

Martin was on the bed. Paul had done something to the body — Tommy could tell it had been adjusted, straightened, though he didn't know the specifics and didn't want to look long enough to catalog them. Martin was on his back. His face was the wrong color — a grayish purple that bore no resemblance to the ruddy flush Tommy had spent twenty-five years drinking across from. His hands were crossed on his chest, which looked staged, which Tommy filed away and immediately buried because he could not think about what other people were doing tonight. He had his own problem.

The room smelled like Martin: the Lagavulin, the cedar from the closet, the humid funk of a man who'd been in a hot tub four hours ago and hadn't showered. The smell was so specifically, bodily Martin that Tommy's throat closed for a second and he had to stand there in the doorway and breathe through it.

He went to the nightstand. The lamp was off. In the light from the hallway he could see: a water glass, half full. Martin's reading glasses, folded. A paperback — something about the Civil War, a big heavy thing with a cracked spine — facedown with the pages splayed. And the blister pack — three pills still sealed in their foil bubbles, the fourth one punched through. Three small blue diamonds behind silver foil, sitting on a dead man's nightstand like a jury that had already reached its verdict.

Tommy picked up the blister pack. His hand was shaking — not the fine tremor of exhaustion but the big, visible, whole-hand shake of pure adrenaline, the kind his players got before state championship games, the kind he'd spent two decades teaching boys to breathe through. He was not breathing through it. He was shoving a blister pack of Viagra into the pocket of his sweatpants and walking out of the room without looking at Martin because if he looked at Martin he was going to make a sound and if he made a sound in this hallway at three in the morning someone was going to come up those stairs and ask why Tommy Renaldi was standing outside the dead man's bedroom with a prescription that didn't belong to either of them.

In the hallway his elbow caught the doorframe and the blister pack crinkled in his pocket, loud in the silence — unreasonably loud, the foil amplifying his exit like a snare drum — and he stopped and pressed both palms flat against the pine-paneled wall and waited. His heart was doing something that felt structural rather than rhythmic, a pounding that seemed to be testing the walls of his chest for weaknesses.

Someone was moving downstairs. He could hear footsteps on the main floor. The front door or the back door opened and closed — he couldn't tell which. One of the other guys going somewhere, doing something. Tommy didn't know what anybody else was up to tonight and he didn't want to know. He had the Viagra. That was his problem. Whatever they were doing was theirs.

He walked to the guest room — his room for the weekend, the one with the twin beds and the loon-patterned quilt that Martin's mother had sewn, the quilt Tommy had been sleeping under for twenty-five Septembers — and sat on the edge of the bed. The blister pack was in his pocket. He could feel the pills through the thin fabric of his sweatpants, the hard ridges of the foil pressing against his thigh.

He should flush them right now. Walk to the shared bathroom, punch the pills out, flush them, throw the empty pack in his duffel. A play. Three steps. He could execute three steps.

But the shared bathroom was twelve feet from the master bedroom and Paul had left that door open and Tommy could not make himself walk back down that hallway and listen to the toilet running and the pipes shuddering while Martin's body cooled on the other side of the wall. Not yet. Not right now.

He sat on the bed and gripped the edge of the mattress with both hands and stared at the wall — pine-paneled, a mounted fish that Martin's father had caught in 1987, a small watercolor of the lake — and thought about Denise and the kids and the coaching job and everything he'd built over twenty years that rested on the assumption that Tommy Renaldi was a simple man who did simple things and had never been responsible for anything worse than a losing season. He thought about what it would look like in a police report: the pills, the heart, the hot tub, the coach who brought a joke that killed his friend. He thought about whether there was a word for what he'd done and whether that word was manslaughter or negligent homicide or something else he didn't know because he wasn't Joel and had never needed to learn the law because the law was for people who had done things and Tommy — Tommy hadn't done anything, he'd told himself for years, decades, he hadn't done anything except what a friend does, what a guy does, what you do and then you don't talk about it and it stays where you left it — he'd just given a man a pill.

He waited for the sun.

The sun came in flat and pale through the curtains at something after six, and by seven-thirty Paul was calling the police on the landline in Martin's study because cell service inside the house was useless, and by eight-thirty two deputies from the Becker County Sheriff's Office were standing in the great room with their hats off and their notebooks out, and Tommy was sitting on the couch with the blister pack of Viagra in the right pocket of his jacket.

He'd put the jacket on when the deputies arrived — the morning was cold and pockets were deeper than sweatpants. The blister pack sat against his thigh like a second wallet, a thing he was aware of every time he shifted his weight.

The deputies were young, both of them. One was maybe twenty-six with a mustache he was clearly still auditioning, and the other looked like he'd started shaving within the last presidential administration. They asked questions — when did you last see the deceased, what time did you go to bed, any medical conditions you're aware of — and Tommy answered in the short declarative sentences that coaches used when the game was out of hand and the post-game press conference was a formality. "Yes." "No." "Around one." "I don't know." They wrote things down. They went upstairs. They came back down. One of them made a phone call on the deck where the signal was better, and the other stood in the kitchen drinking the coffee Nate had made, which was too strong because Nate made coffee like he was trying to punish it.

Tommy sat on the couch and the Viagra was in his pocket and the deputies were in the house and the thing he should have done at 3:14 in the morning — the flush, the disposal, the simple mechanical act of pressing a lever and letting the plumbing do what plumbing did — was now a thing he had to do with two sheriff's deputies twelve feet away eating Martin's granola bars and discussing whether the Vikings had a realistic shot this year.

The one with the mustache thought they could make the wild card. The other one was less optimistic. Tommy sat there and listened to them talk about Sam Darnold's arm strength and thought about the three blue pills in his jacket pocket and wanted to scream.

The deputies went outside. Something about waiting for the county detective, who was driving in from Detroit Lakes. Thirty minutes, maybe forty.

Tommy stood up.

He looked around the room. Paul was in the kitchen rinsing out the coffee pot with the careful, procedural motions of a man who needed something to do with his hands. Nate was at the table, his podcast cap pulled low, staring at his phone but not looking at it. Cliff was leaning against the counter with his arms crossed and a concerned expression that looked rehearsed — not fake, exactly, but positioned, like he'd found the right face in a catalog and was wearing it carefully. Joel was in the study with the door half-shut. Doug was in the basement or the bathroom or one of the other places Doug went when there were too many people in a room.

"Bathroom," Tommy said, to nobody in particular.

He walked down the hallway toward the half bath near the front door. The hallway was fifteen feet of pine floor and pine walls, the specific golden wood of a lake house that had been absorbing summer after summer since the seventies, and Tommy walked it with his right hand in his jacket pocket and his fingers wrapped around the blister pack. He could feel the foil under his thumb, the smooth bumps of the three remaining pills.

He pulled the blister pack out. His hands were worse than they'd been in the dark — a full night of adrenaline and no sleep had done something to his fine motor control that two decades of sideline composure couldn't fix. He pressed the first pill through the foil with his thumb. It popped out clean into his left palm, warm from his pocket. He pressed the second one, and it came out clean too. He was walking and pressing at the same time, which was a coordination task he'd have called basic if it weren't happening in the hallway of a dead man's house with deputies on the deck and a detective thirty minutes out.

The third pill didn't pop clean. The foil tore sideways and the pill shot out at an angle and hit the floor.

Tommy stopped walking.

The pill — small, blue, diamond-shaped, the exact color of a pharmaceutical commercial's reassuring sky, identifiable from ten feet by anyone who'd watched television in the last twenty years — bounced once on the hardwood and rolled. It rolled the way small objects rolled on old uneven floors: not straight, but with a wobble, a slow curving drift toward the baseboard that was both lazy enough to track and fast enough that Tommy's hand, when he lunged for it, closed on nothing but air and the warm space where the pill had been a half second earlier.

It came to rest against the baseboard, three feet ahead of where he was standing.

Tommy Renaldi — six-two, two-fifteen, head coach of the Eastview Lightning for twenty years, varsity record 147-89, three-time conference coach of the year — went down on his hands and knees on the hallway floor of his dead friend's lake house and crawled after a Viagra pill.

His kneecaps hit the hardwood and his palms hit the hardwood and the blister pack was still in his left hand, crumpled and empty, while his right hand reached for the baseboard. He could see the pill against the wood — a small blue diamond against honey-colored pine, absurdly bright, practically glowing, as if the pill itself wanted to be found. His fingers reached it. He felt the smooth coating under his index finger, the tiny rounded edge, and he picked it up and closed his fist around it and stayed there on his hands and knees with his forehead almost touching the baseboard and thought: this is what it looks like. Not the version you'd tell someone, not the version that sounds like anything — just a forty-seven-year-old man on the floor of a hallway, holding a pill, with his dead friend upstairs and a detective on the way.

He pushed himself up. His knees popped — the left one first, then the right, the specific sound of a body that had spent too many years on turf and was starting to complain about it. He looked at the floor where the pill had rolled. No powder, no residue, no blue smudge announcing what had happened here. He checked the baseboard where the pill had rested, and it was clean. He looked down the hallway in both directions — empty. Nobody had seen him. The kitchen was around the corner and the deputies were on the deck and the pill was in his fist and the hallway was just a hallway.

He walked the remaining four feet to the half bath and went in and closed the door.

It was a small room. Toilet, pedestal sink, a framed print of loons on the water that Martin's mother had hung before Tommy ever set foot in this house. A window with frosted glass that let in the mid-morning light — pale September gold, the kind that made everything in the room look like it was being remembered rather than seen. Tommy opened his fist over the toilet. Three blue pills in a sweating palm. He dropped them in. They made a small sound hitting the water, barely anything, and sat there on the white porcelain looking up at him.

He flushed.

The toilet was old. The flush was full-throated and violent — the kind of pipe-shuddering, wall-rattling roar that lake house plumbing produced, the original pipes Martin had been saying he'd replace since the nineties. The sound filled the small room and pushed through the door and probably reached the kitchen. Tommy watched the water swirl and the pills disappear and the bowl refill, clear, no trace of blue, and he thought: OK. They're gone.

He looked at the blister pack in his other hand. Empty foil with three pill-shaped holes punched through. He folded it twice and shoved it deep into his jacket pocket and turned on the sink to wash his hands because that was what a person did in a bathroom and he needed the thirty seconds of being a person who was just washing his hands.

Then he heard the gravel.

The sound came through the frosted window — tires on the driveway, the crunch of a car rolling up the slope to the house. Not the deputies' cruiser, which had been parked outside for an hour and a half — something new. Tommy shut off the water. He stood there with his wet hands dripping on the floor and listened. The engine cut. A car door opened and closed. Footsteps on the front porch — not heavy, not the boots the deputies wore, something lighter. The doorbell rang, the two-tone chime Martin had installed as a joke because the original bell sounded, Martin had told them, "like a dentist's office in hell."

A woman's voice, clear and warm, came through the front door and the hallway and the half bath door and arrived in the small room where Tommy was standing with water on his hands and empty foil in his pocket:

"Good morning! I'm looking for — oh, goodness, let me check my notes here — Mr. Kessner's residence? I'm Detective Mallick, from the county. I'm so sorry for the delay, 59 was just a mess this morning."

Someone opened the front door. Tommy heard the latch, the creak of the hinges.

"Kessler," someone said. Joel's voice. Precise and corrective, even now. "His name was Kessler."

"Kessler! Oh, I'm so sorry. I had it written down wrong — no, I had it right, I just — Kessler. Thank you. Goodness." There was a sound that Tommy couldn't identify — a transfer of something heavy, or ceramic, from one set of hands to another. "I brought this. It's a green bean casserole. It's not much but my mother always said you don't show up to someone's home empty-handed, even when — well. Is there somewhere I can set it down? The counter's fine, anywhere's fine."

More voices. Paul saying something polite. Nate saying nothing, which was wrong for Nate.

"And oh — I'm so sorry, before we get started, is there a powder room? I should have stopped on the way but there's nothing on 59 between Detroit Lakes and here, you'd think they'd put a gas station somewhere but I suppose that's the charm of — is it down this hall?"

Tommy heard footsteps in the hallway. Light footsteps. Coming toward him. Coming toward the half bath.

He looked at his hands. They were wet. He looked at the toilet. The water had refilled, clear and still. He looked at the door. The half bath door had no lock — he noticed this now, with a clarity that felt surgical, for the first time in twenty-five years of coming to this house, the half bath door near the front entrance had no lock — and his right hand found the knob and held it.

"I think that one might be occupied," Paul's voice said from somewhere in the hallway. Measured and careful, the way Paul said everything, the voice of a man who assessed situations for a living. "There's one upstairs, Detective. Second door on the left."

"Oh! Occupied. Of course. I'm so sorry." A small, genuine-sounding laugh. The footsteps stopped. "Upstairs? Second on the left? Thank you. You're a lifesaver." The footsteps reversed direction and headed for the stairs. The third step creaked under her weight — nobody had told her to skip it yet. "Oh! Does that always do that? What a great old house. Is this original woodwork? The banister is just beautiful."

Her voice carried down from the second floor, getting fainter, still talking — something about woodwork, something about how they didn't build them like this anymore — and then the upstairs bathroom door closed and Tommy was alone in the half bath with his hand on the knob and his wet fingers leaving prints on the brass and the detective's muffled voice overhead and the empty blister pack pressed against his thigh.

He thought about the seventeen times in his coaching career when the other team had scored with no time left and the scoreboard changed and the outcome was sealed and there was nothing to do but stand on the sideline and watch your season end — the specific helplessness of a man whose job was to control outcomes standing in a place where the outcome was already decided.

Those had been bad.

This was worse, because those seasons, at least, had ended clean. The buzzer went and the score was final and you shook the other coach's hand and you walked to the bus and you told your kids they'd played hard and the loss stayed on the field where it belonged. It didn't follow you into a bathroom. It didn't sit in your pocket.

He waited until he heard the upstairs toilet flush and the bathroom door open and the detective's footsteps on the stairs — she skipped the third step this time, somebody must have warned her — and then he let go of the knob and dried his hands on his jeans and opened the door and walked back to the great room and sat down on the couch and put both hands on his knees and stared at the draft board still hanging on the wall. Martin's picks were circled in red. Tommy's were in blue. The whole grid of names and numbers that had been a comedy Friday night was now the wallpaper of a room where a county detective was setting her leather notebook on the kitchen counter next to a casserole dish and saying something about how sorry she was and how she just had a few questions and would anyone like some of this casserole because there was plenty, she'd made way too much, she always made too much.

Nate looked at Tommy from across the room.

"You all right?" Nate said. It wasn't a bit. It wasn't a riff or a setup or the opening of a segment. It was just a question from a man who looked like he already knew the answer.

"Fine," Tommy said. "I'm fine."

His right hand was in his jacket pocket, his fingers resting on the folded foil. In the kitchen, Detective Mallick was asking Paul about the casserole dish, whether there was room in the fridge, whether that was the right shelf or would a different one work better, and her voice was warm and friendly and relentless and it filled every corner of the room like weather.

Tommy sat on the couch and did not move and did not speak. The pills were in the pipes. The pills were in the water. The pills were gone and there was nothing left to find and the blister pack in his pocket was just foil, just trash, meaningless.

Then Mallick turned from the refrigerator and looked at him — briefly, the way you checked a room to see who was in it, the way you counted heads — and she smiled, a quick polite smile that didn't seem to be aimed at him specifically, just a smile for the room, and Tommy's hands went cold inside his pockets and the morning light through the kitchen window was suddenly very bright and very specific and he sat there and received it.
