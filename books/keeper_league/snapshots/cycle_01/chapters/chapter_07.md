# Chapter 7

Doug took his pills in the basement bathroom with the door locked, which was unnecessary because Nate was upstairs — Doug could hear him through the floorboards, explaining something to someone, the specific pitch of Nate's voice when he wanted you to know he was handling it. The Lexapro first. He shook it out of the bottle and into his palm. Escitalopram oxalate, 10 milligrams, once daily with or without food, though the clinical data showed slightly better absorption with food and Doug hadn't eaten since yesterday's lunch. He swallowed it dry, the chalky scrape of it on the back of his tongue as familiar now as brushing his teeth. Three months. Same length of time as the divorce. The Lexapro arrived two weeks after Steph left, which meant his serotonin reuptake was being inhibited on roughly the same timeline as his marriage's dissolution, and he didn't know if the drug was working or if he was just getting used to the new shape of his life.

The Ativan was the other one. Lorazepam, 0.5 milligrams, as needed. It was needed. He'd sold both of these drugs for years — not these specific generics, the brand-names, back when the brand-names still mattered and pharmaceutical reps still got invited to lunch. He knew the dependency curve on benzodiazepines the way a bartender knows the ABV of draft beers: professional knowledge that had become uncomfortably personal. He swallowed the Ativan with a handful of water from the tap and studied his face in the mirror. Six feet of gaunt. Bags under his eyes that hadn't been there in June. The hoodie hanging off his shoulders like it was still on the hanger. He'd lost twenty-seven pounds since the divorce, which was the most effective diet he'd ever been on and the only one he couldn't recommend to clients.

Both bottles were in his duffel bag now, in the zippered side pocket, under a rolled pair of socks. Not in the shared bathroom upstairs, where he'd stowed them Friday evening when they'd all arrived and dumped their kits on the counter the way they'd been doing for twenty-five years — toothbrushes and razors and Tommy's industrial-sized bottle of Advil and Cliff's moisturizer that nobody was allowed to comment on. Doug's two prescription bottles had sat between Tommy's Advil and a tube of Paul's toothpaste, and they'd sat there for approximately fourteen hours before Martin died and Doug went to get them.

He'd gone upstairs around three in the morning, after Nate had shaken him awake to say Martin was dead. After the group had gathered in the great room. After the shock, the disbelief, Paul doing CPR that wasn't going to work, someone saying call 911, someone else saying wait. After all of that, while the house creaked and footsteps moved in the dark above him and a toilet flushed and a door opened and closed, Doug had gone upstairs. Skipped the third step because it creaked — everyone knew to skip the third step. Opened the medicine cabinet. Took both bottles, held them against his chest so they wouldn't rattle, and came back down to the basement and put them in his duffel.

Not because of Martin. Not because of evidence or detectives or what anything looked like. Because he'd pictured Mallick — the detective with the casserole who hadn't arrived yet but would — opening the medicine cabinet the way detectives in every show he'd ever watched opened medicine cabinets. Finding the bottles with WHITFIELD, DOUGLAS R. on the labels. Escitalopram. Lorazepam. And then asking about them. In front of everyone.

*Are you currently under psychiatric care, Mr. Whitfield?*

That was what he'd pictured. Not the question itself but the six faces behind it. Tommy's careful blankness, the way Tommy went neutral when something uncomfortable surfaced, like a coach who'd just been told a player was in trouble. Joel's quick eyes cataloging the information for future use, because Joel cataloged everything. Paul's measured concern, the insurance-adjuster kindness that was genuine and also an assessment. Nate's inability to not react — his face was a transcript of whatever he was thinking, and what he'd be thinking was *shit, Doug*. And Cliff, Cliff especially, who would file the information away and never mention it again and somehow make the not-mentioning worse than mentioning would have been, because Cliff's silences were transactions too.

Twenty-five years of being the easy one. The one who went along. The one who got more ice, who laughed at the right moments, who volunteered for the beer run. Doug Whitfield: doesn't cause problems. The Lexapro would be a problem. Not because any of them would judge him — they were better than that, mostly — but because it would change what he was in the group, and what he was in the group was the only thing about himself he hadn't had to renegotiate since the divorce.

So he'd taken the bottles. The most innocent tampering of the night. He was almost sure of that. Whatever else was happening in the house — the footsteps, the flushing, the muffled thump of something being set down in a room that should've been empty — Doug's contribution to the collective crime scene renovation was hiding the fact that he was sad.

He turned off the bathroom light and went upstairs.

---

The kitchen was bright with morning sun off the lake. Paul had made coffee, because Paul always made coffee — twenty-five years of mornings at this house and Paul always got up first, always found the filters, always made it too strong because Martin liked it that way and Paul deferred to Martin on things that didn't matter so he could push back on things that did. Martin's scotch — the Lagavulin — still haunted the great room, a smoky trace that seemed to have soaked into the pine paneling over decades of pours. The bottle itself was on the kitchen counter, two fingers left, the cap sitting next to it because Martin never put the cap back on. Martin would never put the cap back on.

Doug took a mug from the cabinet — the blue one with the chip on the handle, the mug nobody else wanted, which was why it was always available — and poured. Black. He'd stopped taking cream when the weight started falling off, not as a diet choice but because the taste of coffee with cream reminded him of weekend mornings with Steph, her feet on the couch, the specific way she held her mug with both hands and blew across the surface even when it wasn't that hot.

Tommy was on the deck. Doug could see him through the sliding glass door — pacing the length of the railing, cracking his knuckles, gripping the cedar and letting go, gripping and letting go. Tommy's body under stress was a coach's body: it needed a play to run, a formation to call, and when there was no play it just moved, burning fuel with no direction. His hands were huge and raw-knuckled and never still. Doug noticed the scar on Tommy's left forearm, the thin white line that caught the morning light when Tommy gripped the railing. Charcoal grill accident, Tommy had told them once, years ago. It looked older than that. But Doug didn't ask questions. Not asking questions was one of the things he contributed.

Joel sat at the kitchen table with nothing in front of him. No coffee, no phone, no book. Just Joel, his sharp features aimed at the middle distance, his jaw working like he was cross-examining a thought and the thought wasn't cooperating. He'd been like this since yesterday — wound tight, the lawyer armor so thoroughly deployed that Doug could practically see the seams. Joel had always been the prickliest of the seven, the one most likely to turn a casual observation into a line of questioning, but this was different. This was a man rehearsing something he wasn't ready to say.

"You eat?" Paul asked Doug, pouring his own refill.

"Not hungry."

"You should eat something."

"Yeah," Doug said. "Probably."

Paul studied him for a second — the adjuster scan, assessing the damage, calculating coverage — then let it go. Paul was good at letting things go. It was the thing Doug appreciated most about him and trusted least, because a man who let things go that easily was a man who was filing them for later.

Nate came down the stairs and straight into a monologue. Something about whether Mallick had asked anyone about the hot tub — had she asked about the hot tub? Because there was a whole — he'd been thinking about it and the hot tub chemicals, the chlorine or bromine or whatever Martin used, and whether that was relevant, whether that was something they should've mentioned or not mentioned, whether the hot tub itself was — and his hands were going, and the podcast cap was slightly crooked, and Doug wondered if Nate knew it was crooked or if the crookedness was part of whatever Nate was always doing.

"Nobody asked about the hot tub," Joel said, without looking up.

"Right, no, I know, but if she does—"

"She won't."

"How do you know that?"

"Because the hot tub isn't relevant."

"Everything's relevant, man. That's the whole — she's asking about everything. She asked me about the coffee maker."

"She asked you about the coffee maker because you were standing next to the coffee maker."

Nate looked at Doug, maybe wanting an ally, maybe just needing another face to talk at. Doug gave him a small nod. Nate accepted it as agreement and kept going, his voice climbing toward the register he used on his podcast when he was building to a take that he thought was important and that his co-host — if Nate had had a co-host, which he didn't, because the last one quit — would have cut off three sentences ago.

Cliff appeared from the hallway, showered, hair combed, wearing a fresh polo and pressed chinos as if he'd packed for a different weekend — one where nobody died and there were restaurant reservations. He poured coffee, thanked Paul for making it, and asked if anyone wanted toast. Cliff offering food was Cliff doing what Cliff always did: managing the room by being useful, positioning himself as the one who held it together, the reasonable presence. Doug watched him pull bread from the bag with his tanned, ring-less fingers — the second divorce had been two years ago but the tan line was finally gone — and thought about how Cliff was the only person in this house whose crisis response was grooming.

"Doug," Cliff said, holding up two slices. "Toast?"

"I'm good."

"You sure? It's sourdough."

"I'm fine."

Cliff put both slices in for himself. The toaster's coils glowed orange.

Doug took his coffee to the great room and sat in the corner of the sectional, close to the window where the morning light came in low and gold off the lake. The draft board was still on the wall. Martin's picks were circled in red marker — the shorthand they'd used for twenty-five years to mark the commissioner's selections. Martin had taken Kelce in the second round, which everyone had groaned about, and Lamar Jackson in the third, which was a reach that had sparked a five-minute argument, and in the seventh round he'd taken a rookie receiver nobody had heard of, and Nate had done a whole bit about it — who is this guy, where did you find this guy, are you drafting him or adopting him. Martin had just smiled. Martin's smile was the thing Doug missed most, which surprised him because he would have guessed he'd miss the conversations — Martin was the only one who asked questions that weren't setups for his own answers. But it was the smile. The gap between Martin's front teeth when he was pleased with himself, which was most of the time, because Martin moved through the world with the specific confidence of a man who'd inherited a lake house and built a life that fit inside it.

Now the lake house had a dead man's things in it and a detective in it and six men in it who couldn't look at the draft board without seeing the red circles and remembering Friday night, when Martin had been alive and confident and wrong about Lamar Jackson in the third round.

Doug drank his coffee and watched the light shift on the water outside. He was good at watching. It was the thing he contributed — not opinions, not comedy, not legal analysis, not money, not coaching authority, not mediation. He watched, and he didn't bother anyone with what he saw. The pharmaceutical sales career had trained him for this: fifteen years of reading rooms, reading doctors' body language during a detail, reading the micro-shift in a nurse practitioner's expression that meant the pitch was dead and it was time to pivot to the sample bag. He just applied those skills to his own friends now, and what he read this morning was not encouraging.

They were all performing. Every one of them. Tommy's performance was physical: movement as alibi, the body saying *I'm just a guy who can't sit still, nothing to see here*. Joel's performance was stillness: the lawyer playing it so carefully he'd turned himself into a statue, which was its own kind of tell. Nate's performance was the most obvious and the most genuinely him — narrating the disaster until it became material, because material was something you could use and using it meant you'd survived it. Paul was performing competence, the way Paul always performed competence, because competence was the thing Paul had instead of feelings he could show in public. Cliff was performing a Sunday morning.

And Doug was performing nothing, which was also a performance — the longest-running one of his life, the one he'd been rehearsing since college, the one where Doug Whitfield sits in the corner and watches and goes along and gets more ice and nobody ever wonders what Doug is thinking because Doug has made not-being-wondered-about into an art form.

He wondered sometimes if it was the depression that made him invisible or if the invisibility had made him depressed, and whether it mattered, and whether the Lexapro had an opinion.

---

Mallick found him on the deck after lunch, sitting in one of the Adirondack chairs, not reading the thriller he'd brought — a book Steph had left on the kitchen counter of the apartment when she came to pick up the last of her winter clothes, and Doug hadn't read a page of it but bringing it to the lake house had felt like something a person who was fine would do.

"Mr. Whitfield? Doug? Do you have a minute?"

He did. He had nothing but minutes.

She sat in the chair next to him, her leather notebook in her lap, her reading glasses on their beaded chain. She was shorter than he'd expected from her voice — five-six, stocky, the gray-streaked bun slightly loose on one side from a morning of conversations. She had a pen that she clicked, and the clicking was probably deliberate, the way everything about Mallick was either deliberate or the performance of not being deliberate, and Doug couldn't tell which.

"I know we chatted briefly yesterday," she said. "I just want to fill in a couple of gaps, if that's all right. How'd you sleep Friday night?"

"Fine."

"All the way through?"

"Yeah."

"Because some of the fellas mentioned hearing noises. Footsteps and whatnot. You're in the basement, right?"

"Yeah."

"Must be quieter down there."

"It is."

She wrote something. The pen's scratch on paper was louder than anything Doug had said.

"And Martin — how'd you two know each other? Were you close?"

"College. Same group." He paused. "We were friends."

"Good friends?"

"He was a good friend to me."

"That's nice. What kind of friend, if you don't mind my asking? Was he the type who checks in, calls on birthdays, that sort of thing?"

Doug thought about it. Martin had called him in July, after Steph. An actual phone call, not a text — Martin was the only person under sixty who still made phone calls that weren't about scheduling. He'd asked Doug how he was doing, and Doug had said fine, and Martin had said *bullshit*, and Doug had almost told him. Almost. He'd gotten as close as "it's been a little rough," which in Doug's vocabulary meant he'd spent three consecutive days on the couch in the apartment in Richfield without showering, eating peanut butter from the jar because grocery shopping required shoes and a destination, and Martin had said *come to the draft*, and Doug had said yeah, and Martin had said *I mean it*, and Doug had said I know.

"He checked in," Doug said.

"That's sweet. So Friday night — after the draft, after the hot tub, after the evening. You went to bed in the basement. What time was that?"

"Midnight, maybe. Twelve-thirty."

"And you slept straight through?"

"Yeah."

"Didn't hear Nate get up? He's down there with you, right?"

"He's a quiet — I didn't hear him. I sleep pretty hard."

Mallick nodded, wrote something else. She looked at him with that patient, warm curiosity that was either genuine neighborly interest or a trap so well-upholstered you'd be comfortable right up until it closed.

"Is there anything else you noticed over the weekend? Anything that seemed out of the ordinary before — before Saturday morning?"

"No."

"Nothing between any of the guys? Arguments, tension, anything like that?"

Doug could have said something. About Joel — Joel's white-knuckled silence at the draft table, the way he'd watched Martin all evening like he was preparing closing arguments. About Tommy's jittery energy that went beyond his normal coaching-mode restlessness. About Cliff being a little too interested in the house, asking Martin about the roof and the property taxes in a way that wasn't casual. He could have mentioned any of it. He saw more than anyone here, and Mallick, sitting there with her beaded chain and her notebook, was probably the first person in the room designed to actually receive what he saw.

"I don't think so," he said.

"No?"

"They're always like this. We've been coming here twenty-five years. There's always — you know. Tension."

"Sure. Fantasy football." She smiled. Doug didn't smile back, not because he was being cold but because the social energy required for a reciprocal smile wasn't available. The Lexapro handled some things but not that.

She asked two more questions — about the layout of the basement, about whether the basement door locked from inside — and he gave her the same flat answers. Yes. No. He didn't know. She thanked him and stood up and paused in the way she'd been pausing after every interview, that deliberate gap where she waited for the person to fill the silence with something useful. Doug let the silence sit. He was better at silence than anyone in this house, including Mallick, and she seemed to recognize it — a small adjustment behind her eyes, the expression of someone who'd reached for a tool and found it didn't grip. She gave a nod that was more recalibration than farewell and went inside.

He sat in the Adirondack chair for another minute. Then he got up and walked down the stone path through the pines and the birch, eighty yards of needles and duff underfoot, the light dappled and gold through the canopy, to the dock.

---

The dock was thirty feet of weathered planking extending into Elbow Lake. Martin had replaced boards over the years — Doug could tell the new ones from the old by color, the silvered gray of the originals versus the warmer brown of recent lumber that hadn't yet earned its weathering. A swim platform at the far end that none of them had used since their thirties. A bench bolted near the dock's edge, its green paint curling at the corners. Doug sat on the bench and let his legs hang over the side.

The water was clear enough to see the bottom. Sand and a few scattered stones and some beer caps that had sunk over twenty-five years of summers and lay there like small dull coins. Three feet deep, maybe less near the shore where the sand sloped up. Still enough today that the surface was almost a mirror, reflecting birch trunks and the underside of the dock's planking. A school of minnows held position near one of the pilings, twenty or thirty of them trembling in the same direction, catching light when they turned.

He sat there.

The sun was low enough in the afternoon to turn the lake gold. That specific September light that Doug associated with this place and nowhere else — softer than summer, warmer than what was coming, the kind of light that made everything look the way you remembered it rather than the way it actually was. The trees on the far shore were beginning to turn, patches of orange scattered through the green, and a motorboat buzzed somewhere out of sight, its wake arriving at the dock minutes later as a set of soft slaps against the pilings.

Doug thought about his apartment. The one-bedroom in Richfield with the beige carpet and the neighbor who watched *Jeopardy!* at a volume that suggested either hearing loss or hostility. He'd moved in the week after Steph said she was done, and the apartment had the specific emptiness of a place where no one had ever been happy — not actively miserable, just absent of any history that might soften the walls. He'd hung nothing on them. He'd bought a mattress and a coffeemaker and a set of towels. The towels were gray because gray was the color you bought when you didn't care about the color of your towels and wanted to stop standing in the aisle at Target.

Here, at least, the walls had things on them. The lake had history. The dock had twenty-five years of splinters and beer caps and the time Joel had cannonballed off the swim platform and come up bleeding from his shin, and Martin had called him an idiot, and Joel had called Martin a cheap bastard for not putting rubber bumpers on the platform, and they'd argued about it for the rest of the weekend, referencing it in draft trades like it was a war injury. *My client has a permanent scar from your negligent dockwork*, Joel had said. *I demand compensatory picks.* Martin had given him a sixth-rounder out of guilt and Joel had used it on a kicker, which Martin considered a personal insult.

Doug smiled. Not much of one. The kind that came without being asked.

He was glad he'd come. That surprised him, given everything — Martin dead, the detective, the pill bottles in his duffel bag like contraband. But he was glad. These men were exhausting and oblivious and at least half of them were lying about something, and being around them was still better than the apartment. Than the *Jeopardy!* neighbor and the gray towels and the mattress on the floor because he hadn't gotten around to buying a bed frame and at some point not getting around to it had become its own statement, a commitment to non-commitment that Steph would have had a word for. Steph had a word for everything. Her new boyfriend Todd — the physical therapist, the one with calves that could crack walnuts, according to their mutual friend Dana who didn't know when to stop sharing — probably had a bed frame. Todd probably had a headboard.

Doug's most dangerous secret: he thought about Todd's calves more than was healthy.

He came to this weekend every year for the same reason he took the Lexapro: not because it fixed anything, but because the alternative was worse. Twenty-five years of showing up to be the quiet one, the agreeable one, the one who got more ice, and in exchange he got a few days where his phone didn't ring with quarterly quotas and his apartment didn't close in on him and he could sit on a dock and watch minnows and feel, for a stretch, like a person who was part of something.

Martin had understood that. Martin hadn't known about the Lexapro or the Ativan or the three days on the couch or the gray towels, but he'd understood the shape of it. *Come to the draft. I mean it.* And Doug had come, and Martin was dead, and Doug was sitting on the dock where Martin's father had taught Martin to fish forty years ago, and the lake didn't know. The lake just did what it did — reflected the light, held the minnows, accepted the beer caps and the wake from distant boats. The minnows turned and caught the light and turned again.

A loon called from somewhere out on the water, that wavering cry that always sounded like a question that had been asked so many times nobody expected an answer anymore. Doug listened to it trail off and the silence come back, and the silence was fine. The silence was what he had.

He should eat something. The Lexapro worked better with food — he'd known that since before it was his, back when it was a product he detailed to physicians over catered lunches in clinic break rooms, back when depression was a market segment and serotonin was a talking point on a laminated card and the specificity of human unhappiness was something he packaged into a thirty-second elevator pitch for people who could prescribe their way out of someone else's problem. Now it was his problem and the elevator pitch was gone and the doctor who prescribed it had asked him, carefully, if he was having thoughts of self-harm, and Doug had said no, which was true. He wasn't. He was having thoughts of nothing. Long stretches of blank, like the beige carpet, where he didn't want to die but didn't particularly want to do anything else either. The Lexapro was supposed to fill those stretches in, give them texture, and maybe it was — he was here, after all. He'd packed a bag and driven four hours north and shaken hands and made small talk and eaten nothing and slept poorly and watched his friend die from a distance of one floor and the length of a hallway, and he was still here. On a dock. Watching minnows. Sitting in September light that made the lake look like something worth coming back to.

That had to count for something.

The wind picked up slightly, pushing small ripples across the surface. Doug pulled the sleeves of his hoodie over his hands and watched the ripples reach the dock and flatten against the pilings. He thought about going inside and decided against it and then kept deciding against it as the sun moved and the gold went amber and the amber went gray, which was a color he knew well.

When the light was mostly gone he went inside, ate a piece of the sourdough toast Cliff had left on the counter, and took the basement stairs down to the pull-out sofa. He lay there in the dark and listened to the refrigerator cycle above him, its asthmatic hum kicking on, running, clicking off, the forty-minute rhythm of a machine that had been keeping Martin's food cold for longer than Doug had known Martin. He didn't sleep. He didn't cry. He didn't do anything at all except exist in a house full of men who were hiding things, his own hidden thing the smallest and most ordinary of the lot — two pill bottles in a duffel bag, and a marriage that had ended, and the quiet fact of being alive when he wasn't sure what for, which wasn't a thing a detective would ask about and wasn't a thing his friends would notice and wasn't a thing that mattered to anyone but him, and only barely.
