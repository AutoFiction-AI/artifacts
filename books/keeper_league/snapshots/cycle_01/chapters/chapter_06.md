# Chapter 6

Nate Griggs had been holding his phone toward the basement ceiling for three minutes, tilting it at different angles like a man trying to divine water from a satellite that had better things to do. The search page loaded in pieces: Google logo first, then a white void, then the top result emerging letter by letter like a telegram from someone who wanted him to suffer.

*Can police recover deleted voicemails from a phone*

The first result was a legal advice subreddit, which was exactly the kind of source a serious person would not consult and exactly the kind of source Nate was going to read with the focus of a man studying his own biopsy results. He thumbed it open. The page loaded in strips, each paragraph arriving on its own schedule.

*Depends on carrier. Verizon keeps metadata (call time, duration, number) for 1-2 years. Actual voicemail audio is generally retained on their servers for 30-40 days after the user deletes it. Law enforcement can access recordings through a subpoena or court order—*

Thirty to forty days. He was on day one.

Nate sat down on the pull-out sofa. The mattress was thin enough that the metal frame bit into the backs of his thighs through the fabric. Doug's duffel bag sat on the other pull-out, zipped tight as a vacuum seal — Doug packed like a man trying to prove he took up no space in the world. The lake was audible through the small casement windows, a steady lapping that Nate's brain, helpfully, converted into a countdown.

The voicemail. The goddamn voicemail.

He'd left it around eight on Friday evening, after the draft was done and they were all on the deck with beers and Martin was doing his thing where he pretended to be above trash talk by simply not responding to trash talk, which was Martin's most infuriating move and had been for twenty-five years. Nate had taken his phone to the basement bathroom — the one with the shower that smelled like lake water and old PVC — and he'd called Martin's cell and left a voicemail because that was their thing, the bit, leaving long ranting voicemails about each other's draft picks because who leaves voicemails anymore, that was the joke, they were the last seven guys in America who used voicemail on purpose—

Six guys. Six.

He'd gone up to Martin's room two hours ago, thinking he could check the phone one more time, make sure the deletion had taken, see if there was a "recently deleted" folder he'd missed in the dark at three-thirty in the morning. The nightstand had been bare. Martin's phone, charger cord, watch, wallet — all of it bagged and taken by the county deputies who'd arrived at seven. The bedroom door was shut now with a strip of yellow tape across it that looked like it came from a hardware store. Martin's phone was in an evidence room somewhere in Detroit Lakes, and Nate couldn't check whether the deletion had actually worked, couldn't verify that the voicemail wasn't sitting in some purgatory folder waiting for a detective with a subpoena to bring it back to life.

Which brought him to the Quora answer.

*Great question! Even after a user deletes a voicemail, carriers typically retain the audio file for a minimum of 30 days. Standard law enforcement procedure involves serving a preservation request followed by a subpoena, with typical turnaround of 5-10 business days—*

Five business days. Next Friday. Mallick could have the voicemail playing through her car speakers by next Friday.

The voicemail. What he'd actually said. He could hear it in his own voice, the podcast cadence, the buildup rhythm he used when he was doing his weekly "Roster Autopsy" segment, and the word *autopsy* was another one he wished he could take back from the universe:

*Kessler. Martin. My guy. I've been staring at your roster for fifteen minutes and I am composing your obituary right now. This team is dead. You are dead to me, Kessler. Dead. You took Addison in the fourth — the fourth, Martin — when Nico Collins was right there, just sitting there, staring at you, begging you to take him, and you went with a guy who had six targets last week. Six. That's not a wide receiver, that's a bystander at the scene of a crime. And then — AND THEN — you took the Vikings defense in the seventh round. A defense. In the seventh. I'm at your funeral, man. I am standing at the podium delivering the eulogy and I'm telling people, 'He was a good man. A generous man. And he took a defense in the seventh round and deserved everything he got.' And I haven't even gotten to the Pacheco pick, which — I'll save that for the pod, because I want to do a full segment on how a man murders his own season in front of six witnesses. Your roster is a corpse, Kessler. A corpse. I'm performing the autopsy Monday morning. Rest in peace, your playoff hopes, 2024 to 2024.*

It went on. It went on for another ninety seconds after that. He'd riffed on Martin's backup quarterback as a "death wish," called the bench "a graveyard," and signed off with "this message will self-destruct, much like your season and your legacy." He'd said *dead* four times, *kill* twice, *murder* once, *funeral* once, *obituary* once, *corpse* twice, and *rest in peace* at the end like a sign-off for a man who was going to be dead in six hours.

Nate put his phone face-down on the mattress and pressed his palms against his eyes. He could still see the afterimage of the search results through his eyelids. Thirty to forty days. The voicemail was out there, sitting on a server in some Verizon data center in Iowa or wherever they kept their servers, and he had sent it there himself when he'd deleted it from Martin's phone in the dark at three-thirty in the morning, thinking that was sufficient, because he was a fantasy sports podcaster with twelve hundred subscribers, not a person with a working knowledge of telecommunications infrastructure.

What he'd done at three-thirty in the morning: he'd gone into Martin's room, where Martin was still in the bed, still dead, his arm hanging off the edge at the angle that had made Nate check on him in the first place. He'd picked up Martin's phone from the nightstand — the screen protector cracked in one corner, the home screen still a photo of the lake at sunset — and his hands had been shaking so badly he'd had to use both thumbs to navigate the voicemail app. He'd found the message, his own name next to it, 8:17 PM, three minutes forty-one seconds. He'd pressed delete. The phone had asked him to confirm. He'd confirmed. And then he'd stood there in the dark room next to his friend's body and thought, with the involuntary clarity of a man who cannot turn off the part of his brain that formats experience into content: *this would make an incredible episode.*

He hated himself for that thought. He hated himself with specificity and precision for that thought, and yet even the self-hatred had the shape of a podcast segment — the confessional beat, the "getting real with the audience" moment, the producer-Nate inside his head telling talent-Nate to lean into the vulnerability because vulnerability was engagement. He processed grief by narrating it. He processed fear by narrating it. He processed the fact of his friend dead in a lake house in Becker County, Minnesota, by reaching for the rhythms of a show that had a 2.3-star rating on Apple Podcasts and generated four hundred dollars a month before Mia's student loans, and if that wasn't the saddest thing about Nathan Griggs at age forty-six then he didn't want to know what was, because knowing would also become content.

His phone buzzed. A text from Mia, timestamped six hours ago: *How's the weekend going? Miss you.*

He hadn't answered. He hadn't answered because what was he going to say — *Martin's dead and there's a detective upstairs who brought a casserole and I committed a felony at three in the morning*? He left the phone on the mattress.

The ceiling above him creaked. Someone walking through the kitchen. The old refrigerator shuddered into its loud cycle — a compressor grinding to life with the shuddering complaint it made every forty minutes, a rhythm Nate had been unconsciously tracking for three days because that was what his brain did, it cataloged patterns and produced commentary and converted raw experience into material. Even now. Even here. He was filing the refrigerator's cycle under "ambient texture" and Martin's death under "season-ending injury" and his own terror under "character development," and he could not make himself stop.

He needed to go upstairs. He needed better signal. He needed to find out if Verizon kept the actual audio or just the call metadata, because if it was just metadata they'd see a three-minute call from Nate's number to Martin's at 8:17 PM and that was nothing, that was friends calling friends, but if they had the audio file with Nate saying *you are dead to me, Kessler* and *I'm at your funeral*—

He stood up too fast. Head rush. Six beers and a scotch last night, maybe ninety minutes of sleep total, and twelve hours of adrenaline working on him like a stimulant he hadn't consented to. His "Griggs on the Grid" cap was on the floor by the sofa, the logo that Paul once called "the saddest thing in podcasting." He picked it up, put it on, took it off. The cap was a prop, part of the show, and the show was the last thing he needed right now, but his hand wanted it the way his mouth wanted to narrate and his brain wanted to riff — it was all the same muscle, and the muscle did not know how to be still.

He went upstairs. The third step creaked. He always forgot about the third step and he always stepped on it, twenty-five years of stepping on that step, and now there would not be a twenty-sixth year of anything in this house.

Paul was in the kitchen, standing at the counter with a glass of water and the particular expression of a man trying to project that he was simply a man drinking water and not a man who had rearranged a crime scene nine hours ago. Nate recognized the expression because his own face was wearing something similar.

"Hey," Nate said.

"Hey." Paul took a sip. "You doing all right?"

"Yeah. No. I mean — yeah." He held up his phone. "Trying to get a hold of Mia, but the signal down there is — it's nothing. I don't know how Martin dealt with it." He heard himself use past tense and felt the podcaster in his skull whisper *great moment, the casual past tense, very usable*, and he wanted to walk into the lake.

"Deck's better," Paul said. "Or up by the road."

"Right. The deck. Yeah."

Nate didn't move toward the deck. He stood in the kitchen doorway and looked at Paul drinking his water, and he thought about asking Paul if Paul knew what carrier Martin used, and whether Paul thought voicemail servers retained audio files after deletion, and whether Paul had any professional opinion on the legal distinction between deleting a voicemail and destroying evidence, and then he thought about not asking any of those things, and then he thought about the segment he could do on the universal impulse to confess when you haven't technically done anything wrong, the way guilt manufactures itself out of proximity and bad timing, and then he thought about Martin, who was dead, who was actually dead, whose body was at a morgue in Detroit Lakes being examined by someone who would determine cause of death and whether the scene had been tampered with—

"I'm gonna go to the deck," Nate said.

"OK," Paul said.

The deck was getting cold. September in northern Minnesota did that — the afternoon had been golden and warm, the lake flat and bright, and then the sun dropped behind the white pines and suddenly you needed a hoodie and your breath was considering becoming visible. Nate sat in one of the Adirondack chairs and held his phone out and watched the signal bars flicker between one and two.

He loaded a new search: *Verizon voicemail retention policy law enforcement subpoena*

The results confirmed what the Quora answer had promised. All major carriers retained voicemail audio for a minimum of thirty days. Law enforcement access required a subpoena with a standard turnaround of five to ten business days. One article from an industry blog noted that some carriers had begun retaining data for up to ninety days as part of updated compliance protocols.

Ninety days.

Nate put the phone in his lap and looked at the lake. A loon called from across the water, the sound arriving across the flat surface with the clarity of something designed to be heard at distance. He thought about how the voicemail would sound played back in a courtroom. He thought about how it would sound on a true crime podcast — not his podcast, a real one, one with listeners, one that would play the recording with the key phrases captioned on screen:

*"You are dead to me, Kessler. Dead."*

*"I'm at your funeral, man."*

*"Your roster is a corpse."*

And a narrator, in that measured true-crime voice that every podcast had now: *Six hours later, Martin Kessler was found dead in his bed. The voicemail was discovered missing from his phone.*

Nate could hear the episode. He could hear the ad breaks. He could hear the Reddit thread.

The sliding glass door opened behind him. He flinched hard enough to knock his phone off the armrest.

"Mr. Griggs?" It was Mallick. She was holding a coffee mug and smiling like a woman who'd run into a neighbor at the co-op. "I was hoping we could have a little chat, if you're not in the middle of something. Oh — did you drop your phone? These chairs, they're beautiful but the arms are so narrow."

---

She'd set up at the kitchen table. Her leather notebook was open, her reading glasses were on the beaded chain around her neck, and the green bean casserole she'd brought that morning sat on the counter behind her, still covered in foil, untouched by anyone because nobody was going to eat a casserole brought by a detective to the house where their friend had just died. Also because it was green bean casserole and nobody actually liked green bean casserole, which was a thought Nate cataloged and filed under *the kind of observation your brain produces when it's using every available neuron to avoid thinking about the thing you can't stop thinking about*.

"Sit, sit," Mallick said, pulling out the chair across from her. "I'm so sorry to bother you — I know this has been just an awful day. Can I get you anything? I think there's still coffee in the — oh, shoot. I think I turned it off. Did I turn it off? I'm always doing that at the station, Debbie gets so mad at me."

"I'm good," Nate said. He sat down. The chair legs scraped on the floor and the sound was too loud for the room.

"So." She put her reading glasses on and looked at her notebook. "Nathan Griggs. You go by Nate?"

"Nate. Yeah. Just Nate."

"And you mentioned you're a podcaster? That's so neat. What's the show — 'Griggs on the Go'?"

"'The Grid.' 'Griggs on the Grid.' It's a fantasy football show — draft strategy, weekly matchups, start-sit decisions, that sort of thing."

"Oh, I'm sorry — 'the Grid.' Like the football field? The lines?"

"Sort of. More like the roster grid, the player — it's a spreadsheet thing, kind of." He watched her write something in her notebook. She'd gotten the name wrong and he'd corrected her and she'd written something down and Joel had told them all, two hours ago, that she did this with everyone, the wrong detail followed by the correction followed by the note.

"How fun," Mallick said. "My nephew Tyler does something like that, I think. The thing where you pick the players?"

"That's it. Yeah. So you draft real NFL players onto a fake roster, and then their real-game stats generate points for your team, and it's a whole — I mean, it's a huge industry. Thirty-three million Americans play. I've done a twelve-part series on the market size alone, not that — anyway." He was doing it. He was podcasting at a detective. He could feel the segment structure underneath his sentences, the setup-context-payoff rhythm that was as automatic as breathing, and Mallick was sitting there with her coffee and her notebook and her reading glasses and she was letting him go, which was the worst thing she could have done, because Nate Griggs in front of a permissive audience was a car rolling downhill with the parking brake off.

"Thirty-three million," Mallick said. "My goodness. And you all came up here to do that? To pick your teams?"

"Twenty-five years. Same weekend, same house, same guys. Martin set it up our first year out of college and it just — it became the thing. The one thing nobody missed." He stopped. "Well. Martin's the one who can't — who didn't." He stopped again. He was terrible at this. He was specifically, measurably terrible at talking about death in a way that wasn't a bit, because death had always been a bit for him — the weekly "Roster Autopsy" segment, the "Season Obituaries" episode in January, the "Rest in Peace, Your Playoff Hopes" tagline that fourteen people had bought on a T-shirt.

"Twenty-five years," Mallick said, and it sounded warm rather than evidentiary. "That's real loyalty. And how did the draft go? Thursday night?"

"Good. It was fun. The usual — guys arguing over picks, Tommy yelling, Joel trying to rules-lawyer every trade. Cliff doing Cliff stuff where he offers you something that sounds great until you realize you just gave up your first-round pick for a backup tight end and a promise."

"And Mr. Kessler? Did he have a good draft?"

Nate laughed before he could stop it — reflexive, genuine — and then heard it land in the kitchen and it was wrong, a man laughing about the draft performance of a man who was in a morgue. "No. He had a terrible draft. Martin always had a terrible draft and then somehow made the playoffs anyway, which — I've actually done a statistical analysis on the pod, his projected finish versus his actual finish over twenty-five years, and the variance is—"

"Oh, that's thorough. What made it so bad? His draft?"

Here was where Nate could feel it coming. The way you feel the back end of a car start to slide on a wet road — your hands still on the wheel, your foot already knowing the brake won't help, the physics of the situation decided before you got involved. Because the things that made Martin's draft terrible were the things Nate had ranted about in the voicemail. They were the same picks, the same complaints, the same language. And his mouth was already open and his podcaster brain was already cueing the segment and the only defense he had was silence, and silence was the one tool that Nate Griggs had never in forty-six years learned to use.

"He reached for guys. He always reached. He took Addison in the fourth when there was — look, there was clear value sitting there on the board, and he went with the name he recognized instead of the guy the numbers said to take. And then a defense in the seventh, which is — that's a cardinal sin. You don't draft a defense until the last two rounds. Everyone knows this. And the kicker in the tenth — I mean, we all gave him shit about it. That's the whole point. The draft's not just about picking players, it's about—" He caught himself. His brain had been accelerating toward *the trash talk is the game, the insults are the love language, when I said his team was dead that was just—* and he swerved. "—it's about the arguing. The competition. It's the only time most of us see each other."

Mallick nodded, sipping coffee. "Now, you mentioned you record things for your show. Were you recording Thursday night?"

"Parts of it. Not — it's not a hidden camera situation. Ambient draft audio, reactions, the group ripping each other. I always get some tape for the pod. Martin was fine with it. Everyone was fine with it." He was explaining things she hadn't asked about, defending a position no one had attacked, which was what he did when the anxiety was running ahead of the conversation by three or four sentences.

"Oh, that's wonderful. We might want to have a listen to that, if you wouldn't mind."

"Sure. Absolutely. It's just the draft. It's just us being us."

"Great." She made a note. "And your phone — was that the only way you'd be in touch with Mr. Kessler? Texting, calling, that kind of thing?"

"Yeah, texts mostly. We've got a group chat that's been going for — I mean, years. Thousands of messages. Draft talk, trades, the league standings. The group chat is basically the league and the league is basically the group chat."

"Mm-hmm. And phone calls?"

"Less. We're all texters mostly. Sometimes you'd call to say come out to the deck, or ask somebody to bring more ice up from the basement, that kind of thing."

"So no calls to Mr. Kessler that Friday? The day before?"

The question arrived clean and Nate could feel every door in it, the three or four ways he could answer that would be short, functional, and safe: *Maybe, I don't remember. Probably a quick one. Nothing important.* He had clean exits and his mouth walked past all of them because Nate's mouth did not take direction from the part of his brain that understood consequences.

"I mean — yeah, probably, at some point? We were all here, you call people. And voicemails, that's — we do this thing. A voicemail thing. Where you leave long, stupid voicemails about each other's picks, because nobody leaves voicemails anymore, right? So it's this ironic — it's a bit. Commentary on the medium. You leave a three-minute rant about someone's terrible roster because the format itself is funny, because voicemail is basically a dead—" He heard it, the word *dead* arriving in the sentence like a siren, and he tried to steer around it. "—a thing nobody uses. So we use it. As a joke."

"That is funny," Mallick said. She was looking at him with the warm, full attention of a woman who had absolutely nowhere to be. She was not writing anything, which was either because what he was saying didn't matter or because she was letting him build speed, and he didn't know which was worse.

"And the language — this is the thing I want to explain, because it sounds — if you heard it without context, the language we use, it's — you say things like 'your team's done,' 'you're finished,' 'rest in peace your season,' and that's not — when I said — look, when you say a guy's roster is dead, you don't mean—"

He stopped. His hands were frozen mid-gesture, both of them, in the air between him and the detective, shaped around a word he was trying to take back. He'd said *when I said*. Not *when you say*. Not *when someone says*. *When I said.* Past tense. First person. A man describing a thing he had done.

Mallick set her coffee mug down on the table. She picked up her pen.

"I'm sorry," she said. Her voice was the same voice it had been, the neighborly voice, the my-nephew-plays-that voice. "When you said — his roster was dead? To Mr. Kessler?"

"That's just how we — everyone says that—"

"Oh, sure, sure. I'm just — I want to make sure my notes are right. I didn't have any messages from you to Mr. Kessler that evening. But you're saying there was a voicemail?"

The kitchen was quiet. The refrigerator was between cycles. Through the window above the sink, Nate could see the last light on the lake, flat and amber and indifferent to what was happening at this table. He looked at Mallick's notebook, open to a page covered in her handwriting, small and even, and he thought about the eleven other guys at Hennepin County Public Defender who'd sat across a table from a client and said *don't talk to the police, just don't talk*, and he thought about how none of those clients had been fantasy football podcasters whose professional skill set was talking and whose personal pathology was talking and whose response to every stimulus the world had ever offered them was to open their mouth and narrate it until the stimulus became content.

"I left a voicemail," Nate said. "Friday night. On his phone. It was — I was doing a bit on his draft. How bad his picks were. And the language, it's — I said things like 'you're dead to me' and 'I'm at your funeral' and 'your roster is a corpse,' and those are all just — that's just how you talk about fantasy football. That's the dialect. Thirty-three million people talk like that."

"I understand," Mallick said. She was writing.

"And I deleted it."

He said it fast, the way you'd yank a splinter, with no strategy and no lead-up, because Nate Griggs had never been capable of strategic silence and wasn't going to start now. He was a man who narrated his own life, who turned every experience into a segment, who had built an entire career — such as it was, such as it barely was — on the principle that talking was the same as thinking and thinking was the same as understanding and understanding was the same as being safe.

"I went to his room. After we found him. Early, like three-thirty in the morning. And I went on his phone and I deleted the voicemail. Because I heard it back in my head after he died and it was — I'm saying 'you're dead to me' and 'I'm at your funeral,' and that's just sports, that's just the game, but if someone listened to it who didn't know—"

"Without the fantasy football context," Mallick said.

"Yes. Exactly. That's exactly it. Without context, it sounds like — I mean, it would sound insane. So I went in and deleted it. And that's — that's what I did."

Mallick's pen stopped moving. She looked at what she'd written, then looked at him over her reading glasses.

"Mr. Griggs, I just want to make sure I have this right. You entered Mr. Kessler's room after he was found deceased, you accessed his phone, and you deleted a voicemail you had left earlier that evening. Is that accurate?"

Put like that — her words, her rhythm, her clean restatement stripped of every piece of context Nate had spent five minutes building — it sounded like what it was. A man going into a dead man's room and tampering with a dead man's phone. The three-minute riff on draft picks and the twenty-five years of voicemail tradition and the thirty-three million Americans who talked the same way and the entire elaborate architecture of explanation that Nate had constructed, all of it collapsed into one sentence in a detective's notebook.

"It was a fantasy football voicemail," Nate said. His voice was smaller now. The performance was over — the pacing gone, the hand gestures gone, the building rhythm gone. The sentences were short and had nowhere to go. "Three minutes. Just me being an idiot about his picks. That's all it was."

Mallick closed her notebook. Not abruptly — she closed it the way you'd set down a book at the end of a chapter, the bookmark already placed, the stopping point chosen. She took off her reading glasses and let them hang on the beaded chain.

"Well," she said. "I really do appreciate you telling me that, Nate. Thank you."

She used his first name. She hadn't used his first name before. He wasn't sure whether that was better or worse and he couldn't ask because asking would also become information, and everything he said to this woman became information, and the only way to stop giving her information was to stop talking, and stopping was the one thing he had never learned to do.

"I think we might need to follow up on a few things," she said. She stood and took her mug to the sink. "Oh — can I warm you up some coffee? Shoot, I did turn it off. I'm sorry about that. I always do that."

Nate sat at the kitchen table. The refrigerator cycled back on, the compressor shuddering to life with its forty-minute complaint, and he stared at the foil-covered casserole on the counter and the draft board still hanging on the great room wall with Martin's picks circled in red marker, and he thought: *if I were doing a segment on this I'd call it something like 'The Voicemail' or 'The Correction' or 'How to Confess to Evidence Tampering in Front of a Detective Who Brought a Casserole,' and it would be about how a man's greatest skill can also be the thing that ends him, and it would be three parts funny and one part something else, and Mia would listen to it and say 'that was a good one, babe,' and twelve hundred other people would listen or they wouldn't, and none of it mattered now because the voicemail was on a server and the confession was in a notebook and Nate Griggs, for the first time in his adult life, had nothing left to say.*

His phone sat in his pocket. He still hadn't called Mia back. Through the kitchen window the lake was going dark, the amber light pulling off the surface in a long retreat, and the loons were starting up across the water, and the house was quiet except for the refrigerator and the settling of old wood and the scratch of a pen from wherever Mallick had gone, making notes about the man who'd talked himself into exactly the place he'd spent all day trying to talk himself out of.
