# Chapter 11

Paul woke at five-forty on Monday morning with the specific, organized thought that someone needed to go through Martin's things.

Not the bedroom — not again. He'd been in the bedroom once already, Saturday morning, the CPR, his palms flat against Martin's sternum pushing rhythmically into a body that gave back nothing. His hands had been sore for two days afterward, a deep bruise across the heels of both palms that he kept pressing absently, testing. The bedroom was police evidence now, or it had been, though the deputies had cleared out Sunday afternoon and Mallick hadn't explicitly sealed the rooms. She'd said to keep things as they were, a suggestion that was either casual or tactical and that Paul, after two days of watching her, could not determine.

What he meant was the practical work. The paperwork. Martin was divorced from Linda, no kids, self-employed. No HR department filing bereavement forms. No corporate life insurance policy with a named beneficiary triggering the right cascade of phone calls. Someone needed to find Martin's lawyer's contact information. Someone needed to locate the mortgage documents, the financial accounts, the insurance policies. Someone needed to figure out what happened next, procedurally, to a lake house on Elbow Lake when its owner died during a fantasy football draft and five of his oldest friends independently decided to redecorate the scene.

Paul was the right person for this. He'd been an insurance claims adjuster for twenty-two years. He knew the paperwork of death the way Martin had known the paperwork of money — intimately, professionally, without sentiment. He'd walked through forty or fifty houses where someone had died and assessed the property value of what remained. It was not a romantic skill. It was not something he mentioned at parties. But it was his, and this morning it was what he could offer.

He got out of the pullout couch in the basement — he and Joel had taken the basement beds after the deputies cleared the upstairs, neither of them wanting to sleep in the rooms adjacent to Martin's. Joel was still asleep, or pretending to be: on his back, one arm over his eyes, breathing with the measured deliberateness of a man who'd been lying awake for hours. Paul dressed quietly. He took the stairs. The third step creaked the way it had creaked for twenty-five years, and this morning he didn't bother skipping it. It didn't matter who heard him.

The kitchen smelled like old coffee and the faintly botanical sourness of the green bean casserole that Detective Mallick had brought on Saturday and that nobody had touched. Paul could see the edge of the casserole dish through the glass refrigerator door, sitting behind two six-packs and the container of Anne's potato salad. Martin had complimented the potato salad at Thursday's dinner — not generically, but with the specific, excessive praise that was Martin's way. He'd told Paul the ratio of mustard to mayonnaise was ideal, that Anne clearly understood the fundamentals, and that Paul should let her know Martin said so. Paul had relayed the message. Anne, on the phone, had gotten embarrassed and pleased, and Paul had felt the familiar, low-wattage irritation of a man whose wife was charmed by his best friend's effortless authority over small kindnesses.

He poured coffee from the pot Nate had set to auto-brew at five. Nate was up somewhere — Paul could hear the low mumble of his voice from the direction of the deck. Narrating, probably. Processing the weekend through the only mechanism Nate had: talking into a microphone, organizing experience into segments, finding the rhythm of it. Paul didn't blame him. Everyone had their mechanism. Paul's was paperwork.

He drank the coffee standing at the kitchen window. The fog was low and gray over the lake, the dock dissolving into it thirty yards from shore. A loon called from somewhere in the mist, and the sound carried the way lake sounds always carried — flattened and amplified at the same time, closer than the bird actually was. He put the mug in the sink and went upstairs to Martin's room.

The door was open. The bed had been stripped by the county deputies — just the bare mattress now, white with a yellowish stain near the center that Paul's brain cataloged automatically as old, pre-existing, unrelated to the death. Sweat, probably. Martin ran hot. He slept without a top sheet half the year and had never bothered with a mattress protector, which Paul had mentioned exactly once, in the context of resale value, and which Martin had received with the look he reserved for Paul's most insurance-brained observations.

Martin's suitcase sat on the floor by the dresser, unzipped, clothes erupting from it in the way that meant Martin had been digging for something specific and had not bothered to repack. Three polo shirts — the expensive kind that Martin bought to look casual, as if a sixty-dollar polo was casual — two pairs of khakis, boxers, a balled pair of running socks that Martin almost certainly hadn't run in. The dopp kit was on the dresser with the zipper half open. Paul looked through it: deodorant, a disposable razor, a bottle of ibuprofen, dental floss still in its original sealed packaging.

The floss made him stop. Not because it was significant but because it was Martin. Twenty-five years of the same running joke: Martin announcing at every draft that he'd finally committed to flossing regularly, Paul asking to see the receipt, Martin calling Paul the most tedious man in North America, and someone — Nate, usually — pointing out that this was, objectively, the least interesting recurring conversation in their twenty-five-year friendship. Martin's dentist knew he didn't floss. Paul knew. Martin knew Paul knew. The sealed package was the punchline to a joke that had been running since they were twenty-two years old and that would now, Paul understood, never have a new installment.

He set the floss down on the dresser. He didn't pocket it. He wanted to, and the wanting embarrassed him, because it was a three-dollar package of waxed floss and Martin was dead and Paul's grief was apparently finding its expression in the desire to steal dental supplies.

Martin's reading glasses were on the nightstand — the cheap drugstore magnifiers he'd been wearing for five years, the ones Paul had been telling him to replace with a real prescription. The thriller novel beside them had a folded page at about the two-thirds mark: Martin had been in the middle of something when he died. Paul picked up the book, looked at the cover, set it back. He didn't fold the page back. He left it where Martin had left it, at the last page Martin would ever read, marking a place no one would return to.

The room smelled like Martin's deodorant and something else — the Lagavulin, maybe, a whisper of peat smoke embedded in the curtains, the pillow, the years. Paul stood in the middle of the room and felt what it felt like to be in his dead best friend's space, going through his things, handling objects that were, technically, no longer Martin's but were not yet anyone else's either. It felt like adjusting a claim on a property where the owner had died. The comps changed. The history changed. You walked through the same rooms and they were not the same rooms, because the dying had restructured the value of everything inside them.

He left the bedroom and went down the hall to the study.

The door to the study was open. It had been open since the deputies went through on Saturday, and Paul had glanced in once or twice since then without going in, the way you pass an office that belongs to someone who isn't there yet. Martin's desk was the same organized chaos it had always been — papers in stacks that looked random but that Martin could navigate with a precision that made Paul's filing system look crude. The bookshelves held financial manuals, thriller novels with creased spines, and a set of lake maps dating back to when Martin's parents had bought the house in 1982. A coffee mug with a dried brown ring sat on a stack of papers. The room smelled like dust and paper and the faint turpentine whiff of old wood polish.

The painting was crooked.

It was a landscape of the lake — an oil painting Martin's mother had commissioned from a local artist two decades ago, competent but uninspired, the kind of art that existed to fill a wall rather than to be looked at. Martin had hung it behind the desk, and for twenty-five years it had hung straight, because Martin noticed when things were off-kilter and corrected them. Now it was tilted, maybe two inches to the left. And behind it, Paul could see the dark edge of the wall safe.

The safe was open. Not all the way — the door was pulled out maybe four inches, the combination dial set to what looked like its usual position, one tick from locked. Martin's habit. Martin had told Paul the combination once, years ago, during one of those conversations that Martin began with "if anything ever happens to me" and that Paul had received the way he received all such conversations: gravely, precisely, filing it away in the mental cabinet where he stored things that were probably unnecessary but whose absence would be catastrophic. The combination was Linda's birthday. June ninth, zero-six-zero-nine. Paul remembered it the same way he remembered policy numbers: automatically, permanently, without emotion.

Someone had been in the safe. The deputies, maybe. Or someone else. Paul pulled the painting aside — it swung easily on its hook, the wall anchor slightly loose — and opened the safe door the rest of the way.

Inside, a manila folder of financial documents: tax returns, quarterly statements, a copy of the house deed, correspondence from Martin's accountant. A smaller envelope with cash — Paul counted it automatically, the way he counted anything that could be itemized. Eight hundred and sixty dollars in twenties and fifties. A sealed manila envelope, letter-sized, with the letters "RT" written on it in Martin's handwriting. And beneath everything, in a clear plastic sleeve, a document.

Paul took the document out first.

It was Martin's will. *Last Will and Testament of Martin David Kessler*, dated March 14, 2023. Eighteen months ago. Six pages, typed, notarized, with Martin's signature at the bottom of each page — the firm, slightly oversized signature that Martin used for everything from contracts to birthday cards. Martin had used a real lawyer, a Minneapolis estate planning firm whose name Paul recognized from bus bench advertisements, the same benches where Paul's insurance office bought ad space.

He read it standing up, his back against the desk, the safe open behind him.

The first page was boilerplate — appointment of the personal representative, revocation of prior wills. The second page specified financial accounts and personal property: the investment portfolio went to Martin's sister Carol in Duluth, the car to a cousin Paul had met once at a barbecue, a donation to the University of Minnesota Athletic Department. Standard distribution. Generous but not surprising. Martin was organized even in death, which was exactly what Paul would have expected and exactly what Paul had always quietly admired — the precision of a man who ran his life the way Paul ran his claims: everything accounted for, every contingency addressed, every beneficiary named.

The third page was the lake house.

Paul read the paragraph once, quickly. He read it again, slower, with the attention he gave to coverage language when he suspected a gap — looking for the qualifier, the exception, the dependent clause that would make the plain text mean something other than what it appeared to mean on first pass. He read it a third time, word by word, the way he'd been trained to read exclusion riders.

There was no qualifier. The language was unambiguous.

Martin had left the lake house on Elbow Lake — all structures, land, and contents therein — to Thomas James Renaldi.

Tommy.

Not Paul. Not his best friend of twenty-five years, his best man, the person who had the combination to the safe and the spare key to the front door and the standing invitation to use the house any weekend between Labor Day and Memorial Day. Not Martin's sister Carol, who had spent childhood summers here. Not the university. Not the group collectively, which would have made a certain sentimental sense — the house as a trust for the annual draft, the tradition preserved.

Tommy Renaldi. High school football coach. Married to Denise. Three kids. A man who, as far as Paul had understood Martin's life, occupied the same tier of friendship as the rest of them — present, valued, one of the seven. Not first among equals. Not the heir to the thing Martin loved most.

Paul sat down in Martin's desk chair. The chair creaked under him, the wood-and-leather protest it had been making for years. The armrests were smooth where Martin's forearms had rested during decades of phone calls and calculations. Paul put his own arms on those smooth places and felt the shape that Martin's weight had worn into the leather.

He looked at the will again. Page three. *Thomas James Renaldi.*

What arrived was not grief. Grief he could have handled. Grief was appropriate, expected, a claims-adjuster response to total loss — you documented it, you processed it, you adjusted. What arrived instead was flatter and more humiliating: he was jealous. His best friend of twenty-five years was dead four days, and Paul's first response to the will was the petty, undeniable indignation of a man who had been assuming, for a quarter century, that he was Martin's person.

He had been Martin's best man. He had driven Martin to the lawyer during the divorce from Linda — five separate trips, because the proceedings dragged and Martin refused to handle it by email. He'd sat with Martin through two Christmases after the divorce when Martin was alone and drinking too much, Lagavulin in quantities that made Paul count ounces and then stop counting because counting wasn't helping and Martin wasn't listening. He'd lent Martin his truck three times. He'd listened to Martin talk about the consulting business for what amounted to probably a thousand cumulative hours over twenty-two years, and he had never, not once, said what he sometimes thought, which was: Martin, I have my own work, and my work is boring to you, and you have never once asked me about a claim the way I ask you about a client.

And Martin had left the house to Tommy.

Paul set the will down on the desk. He looked at the rest of the safe's contents. The manila envelope labeled "RT." He picked it up, felt the weight of it — several pages, the paper thick, something handwritten inside. He turned it over. No other markings. He put it back. Whatever else Martin had been keeping in this safe, whatever RT meant, Paul was not ready for it. He was not, at this particular moment, capable of processing additional revelations about his best friend's private architecture.

He was still, for the moment, adjusting to the fact that he was not the primary beneficiary of a dead man's loyalty.

He left the study and did not close the safe.

The chair Paul sat in on the deck was the one he thought of as Martin's, even though Martin had never claimed it. It was the Adirondack nearest the lake, angled to catch the afternoon sun, with a knot in the left armrest that Martin rested his scotch glass against — a natural coaster that Martin had discovered their first summer here and had used every year since, and that Paul knew about because Paul noticed things like that and Martin knew Paul noticed things like that and neither of them had ever mentioned it.

Paul sat in the chair now. Not claiming it. Just sitting in it and looking at what Martin looked at when he sat here.

The fog had lifted. The lake was silver and flat, and across the water a pontoon boat was cutting a slow, purposeless path, trailing a wake that took a long time to reach the near shore. The pines along the bank were dark against the lightening sky. A second loon called — farther away this time, the sound skimming the water's surface.

He thought about the time Martin showed up at his house with a chainsaw.

This was 2017, maybe 2018 — a spring storm had dropped a branch the size of a small tree onto Paul's garage. Not catastrophic, but the branch was too heavy for Paul to move alone and too awkward for a simple saw. Paul had scheduled a tree service for the following week. Martin had arrived Saturday morning without calling, a borrowed chainsaw in the bed of his truck and a look on his face that said he had already decided how this was going to go.

Martin had never used a chainsaw. This became evident quickly. He held it like a man who had watched a YouTube video once and retained the confidence but not the technique. He spent four hours cutting the branch into sections while Paul stood in the driveway offering corrections that Martin did not accept, and by the end of the afternoon Martin had gouged the garage siding in two places, nicked the gutter, and cut a shallow, bleeding line across his own forearm that he dismissed as cosmetic. Paul had pointed out, with the measured exasperation of a man who assessed property damage for a living, that the tree service would have cost a hundred and fifty dollars and the siding repair would run four hundred.

Martin had looked at him, sweating through his shirt, a piece of bark in his hair, the cut on his arm beading blood onto his watch, and said: "Yeah, but they'd have taken three weeks to schedule it, and you'd have spent every one of those nights calling me about the estimated damage to your garage. I know you, Deck. I did this for me."

He was right. Paul would have called. Probably not every night, but often enough that Martin's preemptive strike with a borrowed chainsaw was, in its stupid and destructive way, an act of genuine understanding. That was Martin. His generosity was aggressive and sometimes more expensive than the problem it solved, but it came from knowing people — knowing specifically what they needed, what they'd worry about, what would keep them up — and deciding to handle it before they could turn it into a project.

Paul sat in the chair and missed his friend with a force that made the jealousy feel petty and the pettiness feel correct, because Martin would have found it funny. Martin would have loved that Paul was sitting here, four days after Martin's death, furious about a will. He would have said something like: you're adjusting the claim, Deck. You're looking at the disbursement schedule and you don't like the numbers. And Paul would have said: the numbers don't make sense, Martin. They don't add up.

The numbers didn't add up.

Paul went back through the file. Twenty-five years of data points, organized the way he organized claims — chronologically first, then sorted by pattern, looking for the anomaly that would reclassify the whole account.

He started with what he'd never questioned. Martin was protective of Tommy. This was not, on its surface, unusual. Martin was protective of everyone. He'd covered Nate's rent once, disguising the payment as a podcasting fee. He'd called Doug every week after the divorce. He'd lent money to Cliff — though Cliff, as everyone now knew, considered the forty thousand a debt rather than a gift. Martin took care of his people. Martin held the group together. Martin was the gravitational center, and his protectiveness was the force that kept them all in orbit.

But with Tommy, the protectiveness had a different quality. Paul could see it now, the way you see a pattern in a claims history when someone finally gives you the piece that reframes the file.

2019. Tommy tried to skip the draft. He'd called Paul about it — some coaching conflict, a scrimmage he couldn't move. Paul had said he understood. Martin had driven to Tommy's house. Paul learned this months later, through Nate, in the middle of an unrelated conversation. *Martin went and got him. Picked him up like a goddamn extraction.* Paul had laughed. That was Martin. He wanted the full group, didn't tolerate opt-outs.

But Tommy had tried to skip before. 2013, the year Tommy's oldest was starting high school and Denise was working weekends at the clinic. Tommy called Martin directly that time, not the group. Paul didn't know what Martin said. He only knew that Tommy came, and that Martin, who could get loudly furious about things like Joel temporarily joining a different league, had handled Tommy's absence threat with a private conversation that nobody else was privy to. No loud objection. No guilt trip at the dinner table. Just a conversation, and then Tommy was there on Thursday like he'd never wavered.

2008. Tommy's daughter was born — his second kid. Tommy said he couldn't make the draft weekend. Martin rescheduled the entire event. Moved it a week later, ate the cost of a venue change when the original cabin wasn't available, told the group the shift was for "logistical reasons" that he didn't elaborate on. Paul had noticed, in the way Paul noticed everything, that Martin had not rescheduled when Nate's mother was in the hospital in 2011 or when Joel's divorce proceedings conflicted with the 2016 draft. Those years, Martin had expressed sympathy and said the draft would miss them and moved on. For Tommy, Martin moved the draft.

Paul thought about the smaller things. The refills. Martin was generous with drinks — everyone's glass stayed full, that was part of the host performance, the lake house as Martin's stage. But with Tommy, the attention was different. Not more generous, exactly. More watchful. Martin would notice Tommy's beer getting low before Tommy noticed it himself, and he'd get up to replace it without comment, without the usual "who needs another one" announcement that accompanied everyone else's refill. It was the difference between hospitality and monitoring.

And the conversations. Martin talked to everyone — that was his gift, the thing that made him the center. But his conversations with Tommy had a different architecture. They happened on the deck, usually. The two of them in adjacent chairs, voices low, the exchange private in a way that the draft weekend didn't ordinarily allow for. Paul had walked out there a few times over the years, and both times he'd noticed the same thing: a brief, involuntary pause in the conversation, the kind that happens when someone enters a room where a separate discussion is already underway. Martin would smile, invite Paul to sit, change the subject seamlessly. Tommy would lean back and look at the lake. The previous conversation would not resume.

Paul had never pushed. He'd assumed it was Martin being Martin — building individual relationships within the group, doing the one-on-one maintenance that kept the whole machine running. Martin had private conversations with Paul too. They talked about Anne, about Paul's work, about money and kids and the specific frustrations of being forty-seven and noticing that the life you'd built was exactly what you'd planned and somehow less than you'd imagined. Those conversations were real. Paul didn't doubt that.

But Martin hadn't left Paul the house.

Paul thought about the year Tommy's coaching staff got investigated for grade manipulation — 2015 or 2016, Paul couldn't pin it. Tommy had been distressed, louder than usual, pacing the deck. The group had ribbed him about it, the way they ribbed everyone about everything. Nate had done a whole bit about Tommy's team being the only squad in Minnesota history whose offensive line read at a fourth-grade level, and it was funny, and Tommy had laughed. But Martin had shut it down. Not immediately — he'd let the joke run its course, let Tommy take it and give it back. But when Nate circled back to it an hour later, Martin had said, quietly, that they could probably find something else to talk about. And Nate had stopped. They all had. Because when Martin said something quietly, it landed differently than when Martin said something loud, and everyone in the group understood the distinction even if none of them could have articulated it.

Martin protected Tommy from sustained examination. That was the pattern. Not from jokes — Tommy could take jokes, Tommy lived on jokes. But from anything that pressed too hard on Tommy's life, Tommy's choices, Tommy's past. Martin would let the conversation approach a certain depth with Tommy and then redirect it, not obviously, not clumsily, but with the practiced ease of a man who had been managing a particular boundary for a long time.

Twenty-five years. Martin had been managing Tommy for twenty-five years.

Paul considered the possibilities. The obvious one: Martin and Tommy had been involved. It was 2024 — the idea of two men in their late forties carrying on a concealed relationship was not outlandish, and it would explain the protectiveness, the private conversations, the will. But it didn't fit. Paul had been around Martin and women enough to know that Martin's attractions ran in one direction, and the Martin-Karen revelation from Sunday — Martin sleeping with Joel's ex-wife — confirmed it. And Tommy had three kids with Denise and a marriage that, as far as Paul could observe over twenty-five years of weekends, was tired but functional in the way that long marriages among men their age tended to be.

Not romance. Not sex. Something else.

Paul thought about the word obligation. It was an insurance word. A contractual duty. An enforceable commitment arising from an event or agreement that bound one party to another. Obligations had origins — something happened, a loss occurred, a liability was incurred, and the obligation attached. The obligation continued until it was discharged, settled, or extinguished by limitation.

Martin had been obligated to Tommy. The word fit the shape of what Paul was seeing. The protectiveness wasn't friendship operating at a higher frequency. It was management — careful, consistent, long-term management of a person and a situation that required ongoing attention. Martin had been managing Tommy the way Paul managed a long-tail liability: monitoring the exposure, keeping the file current, ensuring that the underlying claim didn't develop into something worse.

What was the underlying claim?

Paul didn't know. The lake house was worth — what, four-fifty? Maybe five hundred, the way lakeshore in Becker County had gone. Martin had left Tommy half a million dollars in property. A high school football coach's salary in suburban Minneapolis was not a lake house salary. Tommy hadn't contributed to the mortgage. He hadn't co-invested. If anything, the financial flow went the other direction — Martin had covered Tommy's draft expenses more than once, and Tommy had never objected in the proud, stiff way he should have if the payments were between equals.

So not a financial debt. Not a business arrangement. Something else, something older, something that had been running for twenty-five years and that Martin had managed until the moment he died, at which point the management transferred to the will — the final instruction from a dead man who had been protecting Tommy Renaldi from something Paul couldn't yet see.

Paul sat in Martin's chair and watched the lake. The knot in the armrest pressed against his forearm where Martin's scotch glass had sat ten thousand times. He could smell pine resin warming in the morning sun, and the mineral-wet scent of the lake, and somewhere inside the house the refrigerator cycled on with the low, grinding hum it had been making since the nineties.

He thought about going back to the study. The envelope labeled "RT." Whatever was in it might answer the question. Or it might open a worse one.

He wasn't ready. Not because he was afraid of what he'd find — Paul's professional life was built on opening files that other people didn't want to look at — but because he understood, with the clarity that came from twenty-two years of adjusting other people's losses, that some information restructured everything that came before it. You couldn't unread a document. You couldn't unfind a fact. Once you knew what was in the file, the file was yours, and the file's consequences were yours, and whatever action the file demanded was on your desk until you either processed it or closed it, and Paul Decker had never in his career closed a file he'd opened.

So he sat. He watched the pontoon boat complete its slow circuit of the far shore. He listened to the loon and the refrigerator and the distant murmur of Nate's voice from around the corner of the house, narrating something to no one. He thought about Martin's gap-toothed smile when he'd shown up with that chainsaw, proud and incompetent and absolutely certain he was helping.

Martin had been his best friend. Paul did not doubt this. Martin's friendship with Paul had been real — the conversations, the Christmases, the chainsaw, the potato salad compliments, the twenty-five years of showing up. All real. All genuine.

It just hadn't been the most important friendship in Martin's life. That distinction belonged to Tommy, and the will was the proof that it wasn't casual.

Paul sat in the dead man's chair and felt the specific, humiliating comedy of a man who had spent twenty-five years as the best friend and had only just learned he was second.
