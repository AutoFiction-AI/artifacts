# Chapter 9

Joel Beckman had been counting the hours since anyone last asked him about the argument. Fourteen. A personal best for this weekend, though the bar was roughly ankle-high. The previous record had been six hours, overnight, because even these people had to sleep eventually.

He sat at the kitchen table with a turkey sandwich he wasn't eating and watched Nate try to scrape the last of the mustard from the jar with a butter knife. Cliff was at the counter cutting an apple into mathematically precise slices. Tommy was on his second sandwich, or possibly his third — Joel had stopped tracking. Paul sat across from Joel with a cup of coffee he kept warming in the microwave, the same cup he'd been nursing since morning, which Joel found either disciplined or insane.

Doug was eating a bowl of cereal, because Doug had apparently decided that cereal was a valid Sunday lunch option, and nobody had the energy to argue.

The chair at the head of the table was empty. Nobody had mentioned it. Everyone had noticed.

"Mustard's done," Nate announced, holding up the jar. "We're officially post-mustard. I want that noted for the record."

"There's more in the garage fridge," Paul said.

"The garage fridge. Of course. Because Martin had a condiment annex." Nate set the jar down and took a bite of his dry sandwich. He chewed for a while. "His garage fridge had better beer than my actual fridge. That's not a bit. That's just true."

The kitchen was quiet except for the refrigerator's cycling hum and Tommy's methodical chewing. Afternoon light came through the windows at a low angle, catching dust in the air above the table. Martin's scotch — the Lagavulin — still sweetened the air in the great room beyond the kitchen, a smell that Joel had started to associate with the specific flavor of being accused of something he didn't do.

"So what happens now?" Tommy said. He said it to the table, to nobody specific, the way coaches posed questions they already had answers to.

"Mallick said she'd be back this afternoon," Paul said. "She's at the station going through her notes."

"I mean with us. The house. The — all of it."

"Martin's estate will handle the house," Cliff said, and Joel noted the smoothness of it, the way Cliff's voice flattened the question into real estate terms. Cliff knew things about Martin's finances that the rest of them didn't — or thought he did, anyway. Joel had been watching him for two days now, the way Cliff moved through the house like he was appraising it.

"I just want to go home," Doug said quietly. Then, catching himself: "Not that — I mean. We all do. Obviously."

"Mallick hasn't cleared us yet," Joel said.

Tommy looked at him. "She hasn't cleared anybody, Beck."

"She's cleared some of you more than others."

"What's that supposed to mean?"

Joel took a bite of his sandwich to avoid answering. The turkey was dry. Martin always bought the good deli turkey, the kind you had to ask for at the counter, and somehow it was already going stale. Two days. That was how long it took for the provisions to start outlasting the host.

Nate was watching him with the expression Joel had learned to recognize as the precursor to a bit — eyebrows up, mouth slightly open, the podcaster warming up. But Nate didn't launch into anything. He just chewed his dry sandwich and looked at Joel with something that wasn't quite sympathy and wasn't quite curiosity.

"Can I ask you something?" Nate said.

"You can ask."

"Are you — and this is just, I'm genuinely asking, not for anything, just between us — are you gonna tell us what you and Martin were fighting about?"

The table went quiet. Not silent — Tommy was still chewing, the fridge was still humming — but the conversational temperature dropped in a way Joel could feel against his skin.

"I told Mallick it wasn't relevant."

"You told Mallick it wasn't relevant to her investigation," Paul said, carefully. "That's not the same as it not being relevant."

"It's not relevant."

"Bullshit," Tommy said. Not angry — matter-of-fact. Coaching-mode. "You show up Thursday night already pissed off, you're in his face at midnight screaming loud enough that I could hear it from the hot tub, and Martin's dead twelve hours later. That's relevant."

"Martin died of a cardiac event."

"We don't actually know that yet," Paul said.

"We effectively know that."

"Joel." Paul set his coffee down. The microwave-warmed cup made a soft thud on the table. "We've been here for three days. Mallick keeps coming back. Nobody's been cleared. And you're sitting here telling us the argument that the entire investigation is centered on isn't relevant."

"The investigation isn't centered on the argument."

"It's centered on you," Cliff said, without looking up from his apple. "Which is the same thing."

Joel pushed his plate away. The turkey sandwich sat there, one bite taken, reproaching him with its dryness.

The truth was that Joel had been waiting for this conversation. Not wanting it — waiting for it, the way you wait for a dental appointment you've already rescheduled twice. The argument was the one piece of information that belonged to him and not to the investigation, and holding it had become physically exhausting in a way he hadn't expected. His jaw ached. His shoulders had been knotted since Saturday morning. He'd been sleeping three hours a night in a guest bedroom that smelled like Martin's fabric softener, lying in the dark rehearsing both the speech he'd planned to give Martin and the speech he now had to give these men, and the two speeches kept bleeding into each other until he couldn't tell which one he was rehearsing for which audience.

"It's personal," he said.

"We know it's personal," Nate said. "That's why we're asking."

"You don't know what kind of personal."

"Then tell us what kind."

Doug was looking at his cereal. He hadn't spoken since the "I want to go home" admission and its immediate retraction. But Joel could see Doug's eyes moving between the men at the table, the quiet surveillance that Doug had perfected over twenty-five years of being the one nobody watched.

"Look," Tommy said, leaning forward, elbows on the table. "I'm not trying to be a hard-ass here. But we're all in this. We're all stuck in this house with a dead friend and a detective who keeps asking us the same questions different ways, and you're the one piece of this that doesn't add up. If you've got something to say, say it. If you don't, fine. But stop acting like the rest of us are stupid for wanting to know."

Joel almost laughed. It was such a Tommy speech — the coach in the locker room at halftime, demanding effort and accountability, broadcasting certainty about the right play. Tommy Renaldi, who had flushed evidence down a toilet while a detective stood on the other side of the door, lecturing Joel about transparency.

"Okay," Joel said. The word came out smaller than he intended. "You want to know what the argument was about."

"Yes," said four voices, roughly simultaneously. Doug's mouth moved but no sound came out.

Joel looked at the empty chair at the head of the table. Martin's chair. The chair where Martin Kessler had sat for twenty-five drafts, running the league, managing the group, making sure everyone had a drink, making sure Tommy came back every year, making sure the whole machine kept turning. The chair where the most generous, most controlling, most thoughtfully selfish man Joel had ever known had presided over a weekend that was equal parts reunion and performance.

"Martin was fucking my ex-wife," Joel said.

---

The word sat on the table like a dropped plate.

Nate's sandwich stopped halfway to his mouth. Tommy's jaw paused mid-chew. Cliff set down his knife. Paul closed his eyes — not a flinch, more like a man bracing for an impact he'd been anticipating without knowing it.

Doug looked at Joel. Just looked at him, with an expression Joel recognized because he'd seen it in his own mirror for the past month — the face of a man processing a betrayal that rearranges everything he thought he knew about someone.

"Karen?" Paul said.

"That's the one."

"Jesus Christ," Tommy said.

"For eight months. Since January." Joel's voice was steady, which surprised him. He'd imagined this conversation a hundred times since Karen told him, and in every version he'd been shouting. Instead the facts came out flat and precise, the way he delivered opening statements — evidence first, emotion later, let the jury assemble the picture. "She told me a month before the draft. We were in the parking lot of Ellie's dorm, dropping off her stuff for fall semester. Karen waited until Ellie went inside and then she told me in the car. In the parking lot of Slichter Hall. She said she wanted me to hear it from her."

"Hold on," Nate said. "A month before the draft. So you came here knowing."

"I came here to talk to him."

"You came here to confront him," Cliff corrected.

"I came here to talk to him. I was going to be — I had a plan for how to be calm about it. I was going to wait until Saturday morning, when everyone was hungover and slow, and I was going to take Martin down to the dock and I was going to ask him why."

"Instead you screamed at him Thursday night," Tommy said.

"I didn't scream."

"Beck, I heard you from the hot tub."

"I raised my voice. There's a legal distinction."

"There's really not."

Joel rubbed his face with both hands. The legal precision was habit, the sarcasm was habit, and neither of them were doing what he needed them to do right now, which was to explain to five men how it felt to sit in a dormitory parking lot watching your daughter wave from a second-floor window while your ex-wife told you that your friend of twenty-five years had been inside her since New Year's.

"Martin and Karen," Paul said, still with his eyes closed. "How."

"How what?"

"How did it start? How long did you — how did Karen—"

"I don't know how it started. Karen said it started at a New Year's party. I wasn't there because I had Ellie that weekend. They ran into each other and one thing led to another, and apparently one thing kept leading to eight months of another." Joel heard his own voice going sharp and clipped, the cross-examination tone taking over. "She said it wasn't planned. She said she didn't mean for it to happen. She said all the things people say when they absolutely meant for it to happen."

"Did Martin know you knew?" Doug asked. His voice was so quiet that everyone turned to look at him, startled by the interruption from the man who never interrupted.

"Yeah. I called him. Three weeks before the draft. Told him I knew. He said—" Joel stopped. Swallowed. "He said he was sorry. He said it was over. He said he'd ended it in August."

"Had he?" Cliff asked.

"I have no idea. Karen says yes. Martin said yes. I can't exactly cross-examine a dead man."

"So Thursday night," Nate said. "The argument."

"Thursday night I got drunk and all the calm shit I'd planned to say turned into the actual shit I wanted to say. Which was louder and less articulate." Joel picked up his abandoned sandwich, looked at it, put it back down. "I called him a piece of shit. Several times. In several configurations. He stood there and took it because that's what Martin did — he absorbed things. He was good at absorbing things. He stood there with his hands in his pockets and his head tilted like he was listening to a weather report he found mildly interesting, and I wanted to hit him so badly I could taste it."

"Did you?" Tommy asked.

"No. I went to bed. I lay there for three hours thinking of every goddamn thing I should've said and didn't, and then Paul banged on my door at two in the morning and Martin was dead."

Silence. The refrigerator cycled off and the kitchen became genuinely quiet — the kind of quiet where you could hear the lake through the windows, the small insistent lapping that didn't care about anything happening inside this house.

Paul opened his eyes. He looked at Joel with an expression Joel couldn't fully read — grief and something else, something that might have been the recognition that his twenty-five-year image of Martin had just acquired a crack that would keep spreading.

"I'm sorry," Paul said.

"Don't be sorry for me. Be sorry that he died before I could finish telling him what I thought of him." Joel's voice caught on the last word, an involuntary crack that his legal training couldn't smooth over. "That's the part that — I had things to say to him. I had a whole — I had things prepared. And he just fucking died."

Tommy leaned back in his chair and blew out a long breath through his teeth. He looked at the ceiling, then at Joel, then at the empty chair.

"Shit, Beck," Tommy said.

"Yeah."

"That's — yeah. Shit."

---

What followed was, by Joel's professional assessment, the most structurally bizarre conversation he'd ever participated in, and he had spent sixteen years in Hennepin County criminal court.

They moved to the deck because the kitchen had gotten too small for what was happening, and because Nate said he needed air, which Joel suspected meant Nate needed space to process this at the volume his personality required. Tommy brought beers from the garage fridge — the good ones, Martin's IPAs, which added a layer of irony that nobody acknowledged. Paul brought his coffee. Doug followed the group the way he always did, taking the Adirondack chair farthest from the center of conversation, his cereal abandoned inside.

The September afternoon was golden and ridiculous. The lake was flat, the trees were starting to turn at their edges, and two loons were calling to each other from somewhere Joel couldn't see. It was the kind of afternoon that Martin had built this entire weekend around — the kind that made you forget you were forty-seven and your friend was dead and you were under suspicion for it.

"Okay," Nate said, settling into a chair with his beer. "I need to say something."

"Do you," Joel said.

"I need to say something that's gonna sound insensitive, and I want you to know I'm aware of that in advance."

"Noted."

"Was she — is Karen — I mean, was Martin..." Nate made a vague gesture with his bottle. "How serious was it? Because there's a difference between, like, a thing and a — like, was it a relationship, or was it—"

"Nate, I don't know the details. I didn't ask for the details."

"Right, right, no. I'm just trying to understand the, uh, the parameters."

"The parameters," Joel repeated.

"Of the violation," Nate said, and Joel could see the word coming, could see Nate's brain reaching for the only framework these men had for discussing ethics: "Because in league terms—"

"Oh, Christ," Paul said.

"No, hear me out. In league terms, this is — okay. An ex-wife is like a player you've traded. You don't own the rights anymore. The trade was completed. She's on a different roster."

"She's not on a roster, Nate."

"I'm using a metaphor."

"Use a different one."

"Fine. She's a free agent. Karen's a free agent. Joel released her — or she released him, I don't know who filed—"

"I filed."

"Okay, so Joel released Karen. She's a free agent. Technically, any team in the league can sign a free agent. There's no tampering rule for free agents."

Tommy pointed his beer at Nate. "There's a gentleman's agreement."

"Is there, though? Is there actually a gentleman's agreement about exes?"

"There's absolutely a gentleman's agreement about exes," Tommy said, with the moral certainty of a man whose own moral ledger was substantially more complicated than anyone at this table knew. "You don't go after your buddy's ex. Period. I don't care if she's been divorced ten years, I don't care if she's seeing someone else, I don't care if she's — look, I don't care. You don't do it."

"What about a statute of limitations?" Cliff said. He was leaning against the deck railing with his apple, the precise slices gone, eating the core now like a man who didn't waste anything. "Seriously. Is there a number? Two years? Five? At some point, doesn't the—"

"No," Tommy said.

"What if they were only married for six months?"

"Doesn't matter."

"What if the divorce was mutual and everyone's cool?"

"Doesn't matter. It's not about the math. It's about the principle. You don't touch your friend's ex. That's the play. That's the only play."

Joel sat in his Adirondack chair and listened to five men who had known him for a quarter century argue about whether Martin Kessler had committed a friendship violation by sleeping with his ex-wife, and something happened that he couldn't have predicted. The wire in his chest — the thing that had been tight since Karen told him in that parking lot, the tension that had been cinching for a month — eased by exactly one notch. Not because anyone was saying the right thing. Tommy's moral absolutism was blunt and performed. Cliff's statute-of-limitations framing was characteristically transactional. Nate's free-agent metaphor was offensive and also, Joel had to admit, not entirely without structural merit. But they were arguing about it. They were taking it seriously, in the only shared language they had, and Joel's pain was the text of the argument rather than the subtext, and for the first time in a month he felt like someone other than Karen and his therapist knew that it existed.

"Paul," Nate said. "You're quiet."

Paul was staring at his coffee. He hadn't touched his beer. His wire-rimmed glasses caught the afternoon light, and Joel could see Paul's jaw working slightly, the small muscle flex that meant Paul was editing his thoughts before releasing them.

"He was my best friend," Paul said.

"He was your best friend who was screwing Joel's wife," Tommy said.

"Ex-wife."

"See, and this is what I mean about the statute of limitations—" Cliff started.

"Shut up, Cliff," Tommy and Joel said at the same time, which was the first thing they'd agreed on all weekend.

Paul looked at Joel. The expression on Paul's face was the one Joel had been dreading — the look of a loyal man confronting the fact that the person he was loyal to had done something genuinely shitty. Paul Decker, claims adjuster, mediator, the group's designated adult, was sitting in an Adirondack chair trying to be fair to Martin and fair to Joel and realizing that this particular claim couldn't be adjusted.

"I didn't know," Paul said. "I want you to know that. If I'd known, I would've told you."

"Would you?"

Paul flinched. It was small, but Joel caught it and regretted the question immediately — not because it was unfair, but because it was the kind of question his legal training produced automatically, the reflex to test a statement even when the person making it was trying to be decent.

"Yeah. I would've."

Joel nodded. He believed Paul. That was the worst part — he believed Paul didn't know, which meant Martin had hidden it from his best friend too, which meant the secrecy wasn't about protecting Joel's feelings. It was about Martin wanting to keep his image intact across all his relationships simultaneously, managing each person's version of him the way he managed the fantasy league: quietly, thoroughly, with control that looked like generosity.

"You know what the fucked-up part is?" Joel said.

"Which fucked-up part?" Nate asked. "Because I've got a running list."

"The fucked-up part is that I came here to fight with him. I planned a whole weekend around getting to yell at him. I drove four hours with a speech in my head. And he died in his sleep like a — like he just opted out. Like he checked out of the conversation. Martin Kessler, who never let anyone else have the last word in twenty-five years, died before I could finish a sentence."

"That's..." Doug said. Everyone turned to look at him. Doug's face was doing something Joel couldn't quite parse — the bags under his eyes darker than they should be, his mouth open slightly, and something working behind his expression that Joel recognized not as sympathy but resonance. Doug understood this. Not the specifics — the shape. Being left without resolution by someone who was supposed to still be there.

"That's really fucked up," Doug said, quietly and completely, without trailing off, without walking it back, and the deck went silent because Doug finishing a sentence was an event.

"Yeah," Joel said. "It is."

Tommy cracked his knuckles — the sound sharp in the afternoon air, a physical punctuation. He leaned forward, elbows on knees, the posture of a coach about to draw up the final play.

"Here's what I think," Tommy said. "And I say this with — look, I loved Martin. We all loved Martin. But what he did was wrong. Full stop. There's no version of this where it's okay. I don't care that Karen was technically available, I don't care that they were adults, I don't care about any of that bullshit. You don't do that to a guy in your crew. Period."

Joel watched Tommy deliver this verdict with the clean moral certainty of a man who believed in rules, in codes, in the bright-line distinctions between what you did and what you didn't do. Tommy Renaldi, whose own relationship with Martin had apparently involved private conversations nobody else was invited into and twenty-five years of Martin making sure Tommy always came back to the draft. Tommy's certainty sounded right. It just didn't sound true — like a well-coached answer to a question about sportsmanship, technically correct and ringing off something hollow underneath.

"Martin wasn't a bad person," Paul said, and the qualifier was already bleeding in, the insurance-trained habit of reframing the damage. "He did a bad thing. Those aren't the same."

"That's a real estate distinction," Cliff said. "A house with termites and a bad house are functionally the same. You're still out the repair costs."

"Thank you, Cliff. That's very helpful."

"I'm just saying."

"What about Karen?" Nate asked. "I mean, she gets a say in this, right? She's not — she's a person. She made a choice too."

Joel looked at Nate. "Karen's choices are Karen's business. I'm not mad at Karen."

"You're not?"

"Karen didn't owe me anything. We're divorced. She can fuck whoever she wants." The word came out harder than Joel intended, the casual profanity suddenly carrying weight because of the specific image it forced into the room — Karen and Martin, Karen in Martin's apartment or Martin in hers, the domestic geography of the betrayal that Joel had spent a month trying not to map in his head. "Martin owed me something. Martin sat across from me at this table for twenty-five years and he owed me the basic goddamn courtesy of not putting his dick in my ex-wife."

"Okay," Nate said, both hands raised. "That's — yeah. That's fair."

"Fair," Joel said, and almost laughed. "What's fair? Is there a trade deadline for this? A waiver claim? What's the league precedent for your dead friend fucking your ex-wife — do I get compensatory picks?"

And there it was. The laugh. Not from Joel — from Nate, a short involuntary bark that Nate immediately tried to smother behind his hand. Then Tommy, a low rumble that built against his will. Cliff's thin smile, the developer's appreciation for a well-timed close. Even Paul's mouth twitched, the mediator's composure cracking for half a second before it reassembled.

Joel sat there in an Adirondack chair on a dead man's deck, holding a dead man's IPA, cracking jokes about a dead man's affair with his ex-wife, and the absurdity of it was so total that something in him gave way. Not dramatically — he didn't cry, didn't break, didn't deliver a speech. He just exhaled something he'd been holding for a month, and the wire in his chest loosened by one more notch, and he took a drink of Martin's beer and it was cold and good and he was going to finish it even though the man who bought it had been sleeping with Karen since January.

"Compensatory picks," Nate repeated, shaking his head. "Goddamn, Beck."

"I'm not joking. I want a grievance filed with the league."

"The league doesn't — the league doesn't have a rule for this."

"Then we need one."

"We're not drafting legislation about ex-wife — about—"

"The Kessler Rule," Tommy said, and there was something mean in it that Joel appreciated, the willingness to name the dead by their sin instead of their virtue. "No member of this league shall engage in romantic or sexual conduct with another member's former spouse. Penalty: forfeiture of all draft picks and permanent league shaming."

"Define 'former spouse,'" Cliff said. "Does this extend to ex-girlfriends? What about—"

"Cliff, I swear to God."

"I'm asking a legitimate question about scope."

Paul stood up. His coffee was cold again and he didn't go to the microwave this time. He stood at the deck railing and looked out at the lake, and Joel could see the tension in his shoulders — the straight line of a man who was managing his reaction the way he managed everything, carefully and thoroughly and with the growing suspicion that care and thoroughness weren't going to be enough.

"Martin was wrong," Paul said, to the lake. "I want to say that clearly. He was wrong, and I would've told him he was wrong, and he should've told Joel instead of hiding it. He should've told me." Paul's voice tightened, the qualifiers gone now, the way they went when Paul stopped adjusting and started meaning something. "He didn't tell me. Twenty-five years and he didn't tell me this, and I'm starting to think he didn't tell me a lot of things, and I don't—"

Paul stopped himself. Joel could see it happening — the claims adjuster putting the lid back on, filing the thought for later, recognizing that this was Joel's conversation and not his. But something in Paul's face had changed. The grief that had been clean and uncomplicated — best friend, twenty-five years, the worst loss of his life — was curdling into something messier. The suspicion that Martin's friendship had contained rooms Paul was never invited into.

"He loved you, Paul," Joel said, and was surprised to hear himself say it. "Whatever else he was doing, he loved you. I know that because he talked about you like you were the only person in this group who mattered. Constantly. Every draft, every phone call. Paul this, Paul that. If anything, I should be jealous of you."

Paul turned from the railing. His eyes were bright behind the wire-rimmed glasses and he nodded once — a short, hard nod that closed the subject.

"Thank you," Paul said.

Doug was sitting in his chair at the edge of the group with his hands folded in his lap, watching the exchange with that quiet attention that Joel was beginning to understand wasn't passivity but witness. Joel caught Doug's eye and Doug gave him a nod — not agreement, not sympathy exactly, just acknowledgment. I hear you. I've been somewhere like that. The nod of a man who had spent the past three months learning that the people you loved were capable of things you couldn't have prepared for.

"So where does this leave us?" Tommy said, because Tommy always needed the next play, the next whistle, some direction to run in.

Joel looked at the empty Adirondack chair — not the one at the kitchen table but the one out here on the deck, Martin's chair, the one he'd sit in with his Lagavulin and his scorecard, holding court from the best seat in the house. The afternoon sun was hitting it at the angle that would have warmed Martin's face if Martin had been alive to sit in it, and Joel felt the familiar surge of directionless rage at a dead man who had checked out of every conversation that mattered.

"It leaves us with a dead friend who was better than this and also exactly this," Joel said. "Same as it's always been."

Nate opened his mouth and closed it. For once, the podcaster had nothing to add. Tommy took a long drink. Cliff finished his apple core and looked out at the water with an expression Joel couldn't read — the smooth surface of a man whose thoughts were somewhere else entirely. Paul sat back down in his chair and wrapped both hands around his cold coffee.

The loons called from somewhere far across the lake. The afternoon kept happening, the way afternoons do when something has finally been said and nobody knows yet what to build on the ground it cleared. Joel drank Martin's beer. It was good. It was cold and bitter and good, and Martin was dead, and Karen was back in Minneapolis probably wondering whether Joel had told these men yet, and he had, and it hadn't fixed a single thing, but the wire in his chest was looser than it had been in a month.

That was something. It wasn't much, but it was something.
