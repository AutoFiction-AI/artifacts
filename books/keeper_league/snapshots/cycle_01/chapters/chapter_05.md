# Chapter 5

The casserole was still warming. Mallick had asked the tall one — Decker, the insurance man — to put it in the oven at two-fifty before she started with the lawyer, and now it was ticking away in there, the green bean smell curling through the kitchen like a guest who'd arrived too early and was making itself comfortable. She could smell it from the great room where she'd set up at the long pine table, her notebook open, her reading glasses pushed to the end of her nose, and the county's cheapest ballpoint in her right hand.

She'd finished with Beckman. That had been interesting — interesting the way a locked door was interesting. A public defender who knew exactly how much silence was worth. She'd thanked him and let him go and now she was looking at the draft board on the wall. Martin Kessler's picks circled in red marker. Somebody had pressed hard with that marker. She could see the indentation from here, the way the whiteboard surface dimpled under each circle. A man who cared about his fantasy football picks. A man who was dead upstairs in a room that looked like it had been tidied by a committee.

Mallick wrote: *Beckman — clean. Too clean? Or genuinely clean?*

She underlined *genuinely* and drew a small question mark next to it. Then she capped the pen and went to the kitchen.

The oven light was on. She bent to check the casserole through the glass — the cheese was browning nicely along the edges, and the fried onions on top had crisped into those little curls she liked. Her mother's recipe, technically, though Mallick had switched from canned green beans to frozen ones in about 2008 and hadn't told anyone. She straightened up, felt the pull in her lower back that she felt every time she bent at the oven, and poured herself a glass of water from the tap. The water here tasted like iron and pine. Lake house plumbing.

Paul Decker was standing in the doorway of the great room. He'd been waiting. She could tell because he was holding a coffee mug he'd already finished — the handle turned outward, gripped with both hands, the posture of a man who wanted to appear relaxed and had chosen the wrong prop.

"Oh, Mr. Decker," she said. "Is it Decker? I'm so bad with — hold on." She flipped her notebook back a page. "Paul Decker. Insurance, you said?"

"Claims adjuster," he said. "Mutual of Omaha, but the Minneapolis office."

"Claims adjuster." She wrote it down. She'd already written it down twice. "Now that's a job. My neighbor, Gary, he had a tree come down on his garage last March — big old ash, just split right down the middle — and his claims adjuster was just wonderful. Do you do homeowners, or is it more—"

"All lines," Paul said. "Homeowners, auto, commercial. Whatever walks in."

"Whatever walks in. Sure." She pulled out a chair at the kitchen table and gestured to the one across from her. "Do you want coffee? I think there's still some in the pot. It might be a little old by now, but I can make fresh if you—"

"I'm fine."

"You sure? I can't drink old coffee myself. My stomach. But some people prefer it stronger, so." She shrugged and sat down and opened her notebook to a fresh page. "Now, Mr. Decker. Paul. Can I call you Paul?"

"Sure."

"Paul. I just want to go over a couple of things, if that's all right. Nothing formal. I'm just trying to get the picture straight in my head because, honestly, I get confused sometimes with these — when there's a lot of people in a house and everyone's got a different version." She smiled. "I'm sure you understand. In your line of work."

"I do." He sat down across from her. His posture was good — straight-backed, hands flat on the table, the coffee mug set to one side. The posture of a man who'd conducted interviews himself. She noted this.

"So. Last night. You all had the draft — the football thing — and then drinks, the hot tub, and bed. Is that about right?"

"That's about right." He paused. "The draft went until about ten-thirty. Then we were on the deck for a while, maybe until midnight. Martin went to bed before the rest of us. Paul — sorry, I keep — Tommy went up maybe twelve-thirty, and I think I was one of the last ones. Maybe one, one-fifteen."

She wrote the times down. "And you slept where?"

"Upstairs. The guest bedroom on the right side, if you're facing the lake."

"The right side. And Martin was in the master?"

"Yes."

"And that's on the left?"

"That's on the left. At the end of the hall."

She nodded and wrote. "Now, Nate — Mr. Griggs — he said he found Martin at, oh, he said around two. Does that sound right to you?"

"I think it was a little after two. He woke me up. He was — he came to my room and he was standing in the doorway and he said Martin's not breathing, or something like that. Not breathing. I got up immediately."

"And you went to Martin's room?"

"I went to Martin's room. I tried — I did CPR. For several minutes. But he was —" Paul's jaw worked. Not for effect, she thought. She'd seen enough grief to know the mechanics of it, and this was the real thing — the muscle clenching because the words were in the way. "He was already gone. His skin was cool. Not cold, but. He'd been dead a while."

"I'm so sorry," Mallick said, and she meant it, in the way she always meant it, which was genuinely but with her pen still moving. "That must have been awful. I can't imagine. My husband passed in '09 and I wasn't even there for it — he was at work, just dropped — and I still sometimes think about whether it would've been better to be there or not. I go back and forth."

Paul looked at her. The mention of a dead husband had the effect she expected — a softening around his eyes, a slight dropping of the interview posture. People gave her things when she gave them something first. It was transactional and also true. Pete had dropped at work. She did go back and forth.

"I'm sorry," Paul said.

"Oh, it's been a long time now. But you don't forget how it feels." She flipped a page. "Now, when you went in and found Martin — Nate was already there, right? And you said you tried CPR. When you went in, was he on the bed?"

"He was on the bed."

"On his back?"

Paul paused. She watched the pause. In her experience, a pause before a factual answer meant the fact was being measured, not remembered.

"On his side," Paul said. "His left side. Facing the window."

"His left side. Facing the—" She frowned and flipped back in her notebook. "Oh, that's funny. I thought — the deputies' report said he was on his back when they got there this morning. Flat on his back, arms at his sides." She looked up. "But you're saying he was on his left side?"

"He was on his left side when I got there."

"Huh." She wrote it down slowly, her handwriting deliberately large so he could see she was recording it. "So between when you got there at two-something and when the deputies arrived at — what was it, eight-thirty? — he went from his left side to his back."

The silence in the kitchen was thick enough to hear the oven ticking and the old refrigerator starting its next cycle, that grinding mechanical sound that probably woke everyone in the house every forty minutes.

"I moved him," Paul said. "For the CPR. You have to — the person has to be on their back for chest compressions. So I rolled him onto his back."

"Oh, of course." She wrote it down. "That makes perfect sense. CPR. You'd have to, wouldn't you. And the arms at his sides?"

"I positioned him. For the compressions. You put the heel of your hand on the sternum and you—"

"No, sure, I understand. I took a course once. At the YMCA, if you can believe it." She smiled. "Although I think they've changed it since then. When I learned it there was the breathing part, but now I hear they don't—"

"They still recommend rescue breaths for people trained in it," Paul said. The insurance precision was coming through now, the need to have the right figure, the right protocol, the right claim. She could practically hear the form fields filling in. "I was trained. I did compressions and breaths for about four minutes. Then I stopped because there was no response and his — I could tell it wasn't going to work."

"Four minutes. That's very precise."

"I was looking at the clock on the nightstand. The digital one. It said two-fourteen when I started and two-eighteen when I stopped."

"Two-fourteen and two-eighteen." She wrote both numbers down, then drew a small box around them. "See, that's what I mean. Most people would say 'a few minutes.' You've got timestamps. That's your training, I bet."

"Probably."

"My husband was a roofer. When he estimated a job he'd measure to the quarter-inch and then add ten percent for waste. He said people who round up are lying and people who round down are selling you something." She wrote *clock on nightstand — verify position, verify model* in the margin of her notes and then drew a small circle around it.

"Paul, that's really helpful," she said. "I appreciate you being so specific. In my line of work, most people say 'a few minutes' or 'a while,' and that's not always helpful when I'm trying to put a timeline together." She paused, then added, as if it had just occurred to her: "You know, it's a little like claims work, isn't it? Getting the details right."

"I suppose it is."

"Do you mind if I ask — when you left Martin's room, did you close the door?"

"I closed the door."

"And you went back to bed?"

"I went back to bed."

The answer came too fast and too flat, and they both heard it. Paul's hands were still on the table but his thumbs had moved — tucked under his palms, a small compression that she filed away with the body position and the four minutes and the clock on the nightstand. A man who knew exactly how the body was oriented, exactly how long he'd performed CPR, and exactly what he'd touched. A man who'd adjusted a corpse and then gone back to bed and closed the door.

"Well," she said, standing up. "I think that's all I need for now. Would you send in — oh, who's next on my — " She checked her notebook as if she hadn't planned the order three hours ago. "Haywood? Is Mr. Haywood around?"

"Cliff," Paul said. "I think he's on the deck."

"Cliff. That's right. Cliff Haywood. Would you mind? Oh, and Paul — try the casserole if you get hungry. I always make too much."

"Thanks."

"The secret is the fried onions on top. French's. Don't let anybody tell you the fancy ones are better."

Paul nodded and stood and carried his empty coffee mug to the sink and rinsed it. She noted that too — a man who rinsed his mug in a dead friend's house, a man who cleaned up after himself automatically, a man whose instinct when holding a thing was to make sure it showed no trace of him.

---

Mallick moved the interview to the deck. The kitchen smelled too much like casserole now, and she wanted Haywood in a different setting than Decker — people behaved differently depending on where you put them. The kitchen made Decker precise. She suspected the deck would make Haywood expand.

She was right. Haywood came out with his own coffee, a fresh cup, and pulled one of the Adirondack chairs around to face her at a companionable angle rather than across from her. He'd changed his shirt since this morning — she'd seen him in a navy polo at seven, and now it was a sage green linen thing, open at the collar, the kind of shirt that cost more than it looked like it cost. She knew the type. Her second cousin sold commercial real estate in Fargo and dressed the same way — the studied casualness of a man who was always, at some level, making a pitch.

"Detective, I appreciate you taking the time with all of us," Cliff said, settling into the chair. "I know this can't be easy, coordinating — what is it, six interviews? Seven, if you count—"

"Oh, I lose count." She laughed. "I'm not very organized, honestly. My sergeant says I'd lose my own head if it wasn't attached, which, you know, he's probably not wrong." She flipped through her notebook, passing Decker's pages. "Now, you're Cliff — Cliff Haywood — and you do..." She trailed off and looked at him expectantly.

"Real estate development. Commercial and residential, mostly outer-ring suburbs. Plymouth, Maple Grove, that area."

"Oh, nice. Good market out there?"

"It's got its challenges." The smile was practiced. Not fake — practiced. The smile of a man who'd learned early that the appearance of confidence was a fungible commodity. "But we're positioned well."

"Positioned well. I love that." She wrote it down. She wrote down everything. Most of it was useless, but the act of writing put people on a tape delay — they watched her pen and forgot to watch her eyes. "So. Last night. The draft, the drinks, the hot tub. Same as everyone else?"

"Same as everyone else. Although I turned in a little earlier than the rest — maybe eleven-thirty? The hot tub does me in. My back."

"Oh, I hear you. My back is terrible. You know, this house — the deck is beautiful, by the way. Is this original cedar?"

"I think Martin had it redone maybe six, seven years ago. He mentioned it."

"It's gorgeous. And the view." She looked out at the lake. The September light was hitting the water at an angle that turned it amber near the shore and steel-gray farther out. A breeze was coming off the water, warm enough for September but with something underneath it — the first real suggestion that October was a thought the weather was having. Two loons were working the shallows by the dock, and she could hear one of them calling, that sound like someone laughing at a joke they didn't want to be laughing at. "I'd never leave."

"It's something."

"Did Martin use the place a lot? Year-round, or just—"

"Mostly summer and early fall. The draft weekend, obviously. Some winter weekends, I think, for ice fishing. He wasn't up here full-time."

"And he kept it nice. The inside — I was admiring the study. That little room off the hallway, is that the—"

"That's not the study," Cliff said. "The study is two doors past the half bath. Farther down the hall."

She stopped writing. Looked up. Let the look hold for exactly the amount of time it took Cliff's expression to adjust — a quarter-second of openness replaced by the smooth surface reforming.

"Two doors past the half bath," she repeated. "I thought it was that first room, the one with the—"

"No, that's a coat closet. Or a linen closet, I'm not sure what Martin used it for. The study is the next one. It's got a desk, bookshelves, a painting of the lake."

"A painting of the lake. Of course." She smiled and wrote it down and didn't look up. "Thank you for that. I always get turned around in these big houses. My place is eight hundred square feet, so anything with more than one hallway—" She laughed. "You've been to the study, then? During your visits?"

The pause was shorter than Decker's had been. Haywood was faster at recalibrating. "Over the years, sure. Martin and I had some business together. He'd show me paperwork sometimes. It's not — I mean, we're friends. Were friends. People go in each other's rooms."

"Of course they do. I wasn't suggesting — oh, goodness, no. I was just impressed you knew the layout so well. I've been to my sister's cabin a dozen times and I still can't find the bathroom." She shook her head, self-deprecating, and flipped to a new page. "Now. When you went to bed last night — eleven-thirty, you said — did you hear anything during the night? Anybody moving around, doors, that sort of thing?"

Cliff took a sip of his coffee. The sip lasted a beat too long. "I'm a heavy sleeper. Once I'm out, I'm out."

"Oh, I envy that. I haven't slept through a whole night since about 2004. The cats wake me up. I've got two, and they—" She stopped herself, waving a hand. "Sorry. Nobody needs to hear about my cats. So you didn't hear anything. And you woke up — when? What time this morning?"

"Six-fifteen, six-thirty. Around there."

"And Martin was — you knew already? Somebody told you?"

"Paul told me. He was in the kitchen when I came down. He said Martin was — he said Martin died during the night."

"Paul told you in the kitchen. Was anyone else there?"

"Tommy. Tommy was there. And I think Nate was somewhere — I could hear him on the phone, maybe in the other room."

She wrote it all down. Haywood had relaxed again. The study correction sat in her notebook like a seed she'd water later.

"Cliff, I really appreciate this. You've been so helpful. Can I ask you one more thing?"

"Of course."

"The casserole I brought — is it still in the oven? I can't remember if I asked someone to take it out."

"I think Paul put it in."

"Bless him. I hope it's not burning. My casseroles tend to — well, they're better a little overdone, honestly." She stood up. "I'll go check on it. Would you mind sending in — I wanted to talk to the quiet one. The one who was in the basement. What's his name — Whitney?"

"Whitfield. Doug."

"Doug Whitfield. That's right. Is he around?"

"He's been down by the water. The dock."

"The dock. Okay. Well, if you see him, would you ask him to find me when he's ready? No rush."

Haywood nodded and stood and shook her hand — he shook her hand, which nobody else had done, which told her everything she already suspected about the way he moved through the world. A handshake to end an interview. A deal closed. His grip was firm and calibrated, the handshake of a man who'd read an article about handshakes and absorbed it into his bones.

She returned the grip and smiled and went inside. On the way through the great room she passed the big one — Renaldi, the coach — coming out of the half bath. He was drying his hands on his jeans and he looked at her the way you'd look at a car coming the wrong way down a one-lane road: total focus, no expression. She said "Oh, excuse me" and he said "Yeah" and moved past her with his shoulders turned slightly, the way a large man does when he doesn't want to touch you in a hallway, and she thought about that for the rest of the afternoon — the shoulders, the single syllable, the hands still damp on the denim.

---

The casserole was fine. A little dark on the left side where the oven ran hot, but the structural integrity was sound, the green beans still distinct under the cheese. She pulled it out with a dish towel — she'd forgotten to bring an oven mitt, which was typical — and set it on the stovetop to cool.

Then she stood at the kitchen counter with her notebook open and her reading glasses pushed up on her forehead and she was not folksy anymore.

Decker knew the body's position. Left side, facing the window. The deputies found the body on its back with arms at his sides. Decker said he'd rolled Martin for CPR and positioned the arms, which was perfectly reasonable and also meant that between two and eight-thirty in the morning, Paul Decker had been close enough to the body to reposition it and had chosen to leave it looking like a man who'd died peacefully in his sleep, which was not how people looked after four minutes of chest compressions. She'd seen CPR aftermath. There should have been bruising on the sternum, displaced clothing, maybe vomit. The body upstairs was tidy. Too tidy for a man who'd been worked on.

Haywood knew the layout. Not just knew it — corrected her with the precision of a man who'd walked that hallway recently and with purpose. Two doors past the half bath. He hadn't said "I think it's" or "if I remember right." He'd said it the way you describe a route you drove this morning. Haywood was in Martin's study, and he was in there recently enough that the muscle memory of the walk was still warm.

And then there was Whitfield.

She'd seen him twice today and he hadn't said more than six words either time. Once in the kitchen this morning when the deputies were getting names — he'd given his name and his phone number in a voice so quiet the deputy had asked him to repeat both. Once on the deck an hour ago, when she'd passed him on her way to check the hot tub and said good morning and he'd said "morning" and looked at her and then looked at the lake and she'd felt the look land somewhere behind his eyes and stay there. Not evasive. Not guilty. Something else. A man who was watching the world from a distance he hadn't chosen.

He wasn't on her list yet. She'd interview him, probably tomorrow, and she'd be interested to see what a man who spoke six words in a day corrected her about. Sometimes the quiet ones gave you nothing. Sometimes the quiet ones gave you the thing the loud ones were burying.

Mallick sat down at the kitchen table and turned to a fresh page in her notebook. The refrigerator kicked on with its grinding cycle. The loons were calling from the lake — that wavering, unhinged sound that tourists found beautiful and that she'd grown up hearing as just the sound of evening in this part of the state.

She wrote:

*Decker — knows body position. Moved body. Says CPR. Scene too clean for CPR. Wiped?*

*Haywood — knows study. Two doors past bath. Definite. Was in there. When? Business with Kessler. Financial? Check debt/estate.*

*Beckman — lawyer. Won't explain argument. Didn't tamper (only clean hands?). Angry. Not at me. At Kessler? At himself?*

*Griggs — talked too much. Interview Monday. Will crack himself.*

*Whitfield — talked too little. Interview Monday. Won't crack at all.*

*Renaldi — haven't gotten to yet. Loud one. Coached voice. Shoulders turned in hallway. Note: was in the bathroom when I arrived. Long bathroom visit. Hands damp three hours later. Check.*

She drew a line under the names and then sat for a moment looking at the list. Six men. Five of them claimed they went to bed, slept through the night, and woke up to the news that their friend was dead. One of them — Beckman — was too angry to tamper and too proud to explain. The other five had each, as far as she could tell, moved through this house in the small hours of a Saturday morning doing things they now needed to explain away or avoid mentioning entirely.

She uncapped her pen and then capped it again and held it between her fingers the way she'd held cigarettes for twenty years before she quit — the muscle memory of thinking.

She thought about her mother's rule for casseroles, which was also her mother's rule for crime scenes: if you can see where one set of hands has been, you can feed a family. If you can't tell who touched what, somebody brought the wrong dish.

Five men. A body repositioned, a scene wiped, a study visited, a bathroom occupied, and — she was guessing now, but the guesses were the part of the job she was good at — at least one or two more things she hadn't found yet. A house rearranged between midnight and dawn by people who claimed to be sleeping.

Mallick looked at the draft board across the room. Martin Kessler's picks in red. Everybody else's in blue or black or green. She thought about the dead man's handwriting pressing into that whiteboard, the way the red circles dimpled the surface, and she thought about how you could learn a lot about a man from how hard he pressed.

She tore the page out of her notebook — she always tore the assessment pages out, folded them into her blazer pocket where they couldn't be read alongside the interview notes — and stood up. The casserole was cooling. She'd cut it later and offer some to whoever wanted it, because her mother was right: you didn't show up empty-handed, even to the worst places. Especially to the worst places.

She washed her water glass at the sink the way Decker had washed his mug, and she thought: everyone in this house is cleaning up after themselves. The question is what they're cleaning.

Outside, the late afternoon light was on the lake, turning it copper. She could see Whitfield sitting alone on the dock bench, sixty or seventy yards away through the pines and birch. He was sitting perfectly still, looking at the water. He might have been praying. He might have been sleeping with his eyes open. He might have been doing what she was doing — counting the things that didn't add up and coming up short of any total that made sense.

She put her notebook in her blazer pocket alongside the torn page and went to find her phone. She needed to call the BCA in St. Paul and ask about expediting the autopsy. She needed the cause of death, because everything she'd seen in this house said these men were hiding something, and she couldn't figure out what it was until she knew whether what they were hiding from was a murder or a natural death or just the specific unbearable thing that happened when your friend died in the night and you found yourself, at two in the morning, doing something with your hands that you couldn't explain.

The refrigerator cycled off. The house went quiet. Somewhere upstairs, the third step from the top of the stairs creaked — someone going up or coming down, she couldn't tell — and she wrote that down too.
