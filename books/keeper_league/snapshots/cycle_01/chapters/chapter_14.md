# Chapter 14

Doug folded the same flannel shirt for the third time. The sleeves wouldn't square right, the cuffs refusing to line up, and he gave up and stuffed it into his duffel. His other shirts were already in there, along with the prescription bottles he'd moved from the upstairs bathroom Saturday morning — the Lexapro and the Ativan, buried between a pair of jeans and his shaving kit, where they'd been since this whole thing started. He'd taken his evening dose an hour ago in the basement bathroom with the door locked, the way he'd taken every dose this weekend: quickly, quietly, the bottle opened and closed in a single motion so that the rattle of pills didn't carry through the thin walls. Twenty-five years of friendship and he still couldn't swallow an antidepressant without making sure the coast was clear.

The great room had the feel of a waiting room where the appointment had been cancelled. Paul sat at the kitchen table with his reading glasses and his phone, texting Anne about departure times, his thumbs moving with the deliberate patience of a man who answered every text in complete sentences. Joel had his laptop on the couch, answering the work emails he'd been ignoring since Thursday, the blue light catching the sharp angles of his face. The draft board hung on the wall above the fireplace. Martin's picks were still circled in red marker, and nobody had suggested taking it down. Nobody had suggested much of anything today. The day had passed in the half-speed of an airport delay — too awake to sleep, too tired to do anything useful, suspended in the lag between catastrophe and departure.

Mallick hadn't been back since Sunday evening. No follow-up calls, no uniformed deputies in the driveway, no casserole. The implication had settled over the house like weather: the investigation into Martin's death was winding down. Natural causes, suspicious tampering, no murder charge. Doug didn't know the legal specifics — he didn't have Joel's vocabulary for it — but he understood the shape of it. Martin died of a bad heart, five of his closest friends panicked in the dark, and the panicking had made a natural death look sinister, and now they were all going to go home and spend the rest of their lives knowing they'd made themselves look guilty of something that never happened. It was, if you tilted your head at it, almost funny. Doug didn't tilt his head.

He zipped the duffel. The sound was loud in the room and Paul glanced up.

"You're set?"

"Yeah. Early flight." This was a lie. Doug's flight wasn't until two-thirty tomorrow afternoon, but the lie gave him a reason to end the conversation, and ending conversations was what Doug did best. Twenty-five years in this group and his primary contribution had been his willingness to go get more ice.

Nate came in from the deck, sliding the glass door shut behind him. He'd been on the phone — Mia, probably, or his producer, or whichever version of his future he was negotiating with at any given moment. He went to the kitchen and poured himself a glass of water, then stood at the counter drinking it with the distracted air of a man whose body was in one room and whose brain was somewhere else entirely.

"Mia says hi," Nate said, to no one in particular.

"Hi back," Paul said, without looking up.

"She wants to know if we're all okay." Nate said it flatly, the performance drained out of it, which meant he'd been asked the question genuinely by someone who loved him and didn't know what to do with a genuine question. He drank the rest of the water and put the glass in the sink. "I told her we're fine."

"Are we fine?" Joel asked, from the couch, with enough edge that it could have been a real question or a lawyer's question, and Nate didn't answer either version.

"Where's Tommy?" Paul asked, and Doug heard the question carry more weight than Paul meant it to. Paul was asking about logistics — did Tommy need a ride to the airport? — but the name landed differently now. Everything landed differently now.

"Deck, I think," Joel said. "Or the firepit. I saw him walk that way twenty minutes ago."

"And Cliff?"

"Took his phone into the study." Joel closed his laptop and set it on the cushion beside him. "Calling someone. His office. His lawyer. Does Cliff have a lawyer? Everyone has a lawyer now, I guess."

That was a Joel observation — dry, self-aware, the sarcasm pointed at himself as much as anyone because Joel was a lawyer and the week had made lawyers of them all. Nobody picked it up. The room let the observation die of natural causes and moved on.

Doug sat on the arm of the stairs that led down to the basement and watched the room. He'd been watching rooms his whole life. Forty-seven years of reading rooms from the edges — the doctors' offices during his pharmaceutical sales calls, where you learned in the first three seconds whether the receptionist would let you wait or send you home. The living room of the apartment in Roseville where Steph had told him she wanted a divorce and he'd stared at the coffee table and cataloged every ring and scratch on its surface because cataloging surfaces was easier than hearing the words. The shared bathroom upstairs in this house, where he'd taken his prescription bottles out at four in the morning because the shame of other men seeing his medication was worse than any shame the investigation could produce.

The great room was lying to him. Everything in it said the weekend was over — the packed bags by the front door, Paul's careful logistics, Joel's email, Nate's glass of water where a beer would have been on Thursday. The draft board said it was over. Martin's empty chair at the kitchen table, still pulled back slightly from the table as if he'd just gotten up for a refill, said it was over. But Doug could feel something in the room that hadn't resolved. Not in any one person — it was in the distances between people. Tommy alone outside. Cliff in the study with his phone. The way Paul and Joel were careful with each other in a way that was new since Sunday, a carefulness that Doug couldn't identify as alliance or suspicion or something in between. The group's geometry had shifted, and Doug couldn't see the shape of it from where he sat, which was where he always sat, which was at the edge.

Cliff came back from the study with his phone in his hand and poured himself two fingers of Martin's Lagavulin. The bottle had been on the counter since Thursday night. Nobody had put it away. The scotch smell mixed with the general smell of the house — pine paneling, lake damp, the ghost of Martin's presence that lived in the walls the way old smoke does. Cliff drank standing up, the way he did everything — leaning forward slightly, one hand in his pocket, the posture of a man who was always about to leave for somewhere more important. He was wearing his pressed chinos and the navy polo and the leather loafers that Cliff wore indoors because he was the one person in the group who gave a shit about shoes.

"Paul." Cliff raised the glass an inch, a small toast or a small acknowledgment. "Joel. Doug."

"Hey," Doug said.

"We should talk about the house at some point," Cliff said. "Not tonight. But someone's got to deal with Martin's things. The estate, the property taxes, the—" He waved the glass in a small circle, the gesture doing the work the words wouldn't. "There's a process. I've gone through it with clients. I'm happy to help coordinate, if that's useful."

"Let's get through the drive home first," Paul said. The qualifier he'd swallowed — the _in my experience_ he'd left out, the _to be fair_ he hadn't added — made the sentence sound like a door being held politely shut.

Cliff nodded. He drank. He looked at the draft board and said, "He'd have won the league this year. Those picks were solid." Then he took his scotch and went out to the deck, and Doug watched him go — the chinos, the polo, the loafers making a soft sound on the hardwood as he crossed the room. The sliding door opened and closed. Through the glass, Doug could see him settle against the railing with his drink and look out at the lake where the last light of the day had gone and the moon was putting a silver path across the water.

Joel looked at Paul. Paul looked at his phone. Neither of them said anything about what Cliff had said or the way he'd said it, which told Doug that whatever was wrong in the room, they'd already discussed it between themselves and decided not to include him. This was fine. This was what always happened. Doug was the one you told things to when you needed someone to nod, not when you needed someone to act.

People drifted. Nate went down to the basement, said something about packing. Paul made a last round of the kitchen — washing the dinner dishes that nobody had finished, wiping down the counter, the caretaking that was Paul's way of grieving. Joel closed his laptop into his bag and said he was going to read upstairs. Tommy was still outside somewhere, a large, silent shape that Doug had lost track of.

The house settled into the sounds of its own machinery: the refrigerator cycling on with its forty-minute hum, the wind through the pine trees pressing against the walls, the third stair step creaking under Joel's weight as he went up. Doug sat on the arm of the basement stairs and felt the quiet accumulate. It was a different quiet than the dock quiet. The house quiet was full of people trying to sleep and failing, full of the sounds they made when they thought no one was listening — the floorboard, the sink, the muffled voice. The dock quiet was empty and that was what made it clean.

He went downstairs. Nate was on one of the pull-out sofas, phone in hand, thumb scrolling without purpose. He didn't look up when Doug came down. Doug sat on his own pull-out and took off his shoes and lay on top of the sheets in his sweatpants and hoodie and stared at the basement ceiling, which was low and paneled and had a water stain in the corner that looked like a map of somewhere he'd never been.

He couldn't sleep. The Ativan should have helped — it usually softened the edges enough that he could slide into a shallow unconsciousness that passed for rest — but tonight the edges stayed sharp. He lay there and listened to Nate's breathing slow into sleep and the lake lapping at the shore outside the basement window and the house making its noises above him, and he thought about Steph and about the apartment in Roseville that he'd moved out of three months ago and about the one-bedroom in Burnsville that he'd moved into and about the fact that he'd come to this weekend because he needed to be around people and the people he'd come to be around were either dead or going home in the morning and then he'd be back in the Burnsville apartment with the Lexapro and the Ativan and the pharmaceutical sales calls he couldn't seem to care about anymore.

He got up. Nate didn't stir. Doug put his shoes back on and pulled the hoodie tighter and went upstairs through the dark house and out the sliding door onto the deck. The deck was empty. Cliff's scotch glass sat on the railing where he'd left it, a finger of amber still in the bottom. The lake was out there, invisible except for the moonlight on its surface and the sound it made, which was the sound of water being patient.

Doug walked to the dock.

---

The stone path was uneven under his feet, the flagstones sunk at different depths from twenty years of Minnesota freeze-thaw. He walked it from memory in the dark. Eighty yards through pine and birch, the trees close enough on both sides to block the house lights after the first thirty feet. He could hear the lake before he could see it — the small persistent lapping that was Elbow Lake's answer to the wind, not waves but the water reminding itself that it was there.

The moon was three-quarters full and high enough to reach the path in broken pieces, the light cut by the tree canopy into bright shapes on the stone. September night in northern Minnesota. The warmth of the afternoon was gone and what replaced it was a damp cold that came off the lake and settled into the hoodie and found his skin. He shoved his hands in the front pocket. Pine needles underfoot, brittle and sharp. The smell of them — turpentine and sugar — was the smell of every September he'd spent at this house, and for a moment it was Thursday again, the arrival, the gravel driveway, Martin's voice from the deck: _Whitfield! You look like shit. Get up here._

Martin had noticed the weight loss. Martin had been one of the few — maybe the only one — who'd looked at Doug at the airport on Thursday and seen what was underneath the hoodie and the forced smile. Doug's weight was down twenty pounds from last September. His face was gaunt in ways that had nothing to do with a diet. Martin had noticed and Martin had said something about it, privately, on the deck that first evening: _You eating?_ and Doug had said _yeah, I'm fine_ and Martin had looked at him the way you look at someone who is not fine and you both know it and you let the lie stand because pushing it would make it real.

Martin was dead now. The man who noticed was dead.

The trees thinned and the dock appeared, extending thirty feet into the lake, the swim platform at the end a dark rectangle against the water. The bench was where he'd left it, facing the lake. Everything was where he'd left it.

Except there was something in the water.

Doug stopped where the stone path met the dock's first planks. The wood was pale in the moonlight, the grain visible, and the water on either side was dark and moved with the slow deliberation of a lake that had been moving this way for ten thousand years, and in the water to the left of the dock, six or seven feet from the edge, there was a shape that did not belong to the lake.

His first thought was a log. Deadfall from the north shore, stripped of bark and waterlogged, floating at the surface. Martin had complained about them — they fouled the swim ladder, they bumped against the dock at night and made sounds that woke people up. Doug's brain offered this explanation the way it always offered the explanation that hurt less. _Contraindicated_, his pharmaceutical vocabulary said, before the word dissolved, because the shape was not a log.

It was a person. Face-down. Arms extended at wrong angles, not floating so much as resting on the surface with a terrible limpness that was different from sleep or relaxation or any other state that a living body produces. The legs were extended at a slight downward angle, the feet nearly touching the bottom. The back of the shirt — a polo shirt, collared, light-colored — was just above the waterline, the fabric dark with water and clinging to the shape of a back and shoulder blades that were motionless in a way that motionless does not mean for a living person.

Doug took three steps onto the dock. The wood creaked under his weight. The sound felt obscene.

The silver hair. The width of the shoulders. The collared polo. It was Cliff. Doug knew it before he assembled the evidence, the way you know someone from behind by the set of their body, the particular proportion of shoulder to waist, the details you've absorbed across twenty-five years of draft weekends without ever deciding to learn them. Cliff Haywood was floating face-down in three feet of water beside the dock at Martin Kessler's lake house, and the lake was lapping at his sides with the same patient rhythm it had been using all week.

Doug knelt on the dock. The planks were damp and cold through his sweatpants. He leaned over the edge and reached down and his hand found Cliff's shoulder and the shoulder was rigid — not the give of sleeping muscle but the stiffness of flesh that has stopped being operated, the meat and bone of a body without a person in it. The water was cold. Minnesota-lake cold, September cold, a cold that went past his wrist and seized his forearm and held it, and he pulled.

Cliff's body moved sluggishly in the water, heavier than Doug expected or Doug weaker than he remembered, and the pull rotated Cliff's face half out of the water. The gash on the right temple was the first thing Doug saw. It was dark in the moonlight — blood reads black under moonlight, not red — and the wound was wide, the skin split in a way that looked like impact against something hard and angular, not the gradual scrape of a fall but the concentrated force of a single blow. The eye on that side was open. The other eye was still in the water. Cliff's mouth was open too, slightly, the jaw slack, and there was lake water in it.

Doug let go. His hand came back wet and smelling of the lake and something underneath the lake — something organic, something that was Cliff and the lake and the process that had already begun, the process that Doug's pharmaceutical training gave him clinical names for and that he did not want to name here on this dock in the dark with his hand dripping.

He looked at Cliff's feet. The loafers. Cliff's leather loafers — the indoor shoes, the ones he wore on the hardwood floors of the great room, the ones that had made that soft careful sound an hour and a half ago when Cliff crossed the room with his scotch and went out to the deck. Not deck shoes. Cliff had a pair of deck shoes — canvas, rubber-soled, the kind you wore on a dock because dock wood got slippery with dew and deck shoes gripped. Doug had seen them by the sliding door every day this weekend, kicked off on the mat. Cliff did not walk down to the dock in his loafers. The stone path was uneven. The dock was wet. Cliff cared about his shoes.

Doug stood up. His hands were shaking and the shaking had a quality he recognized from the first week on the Lexapro, before his body had agreed to the new chemistry — a tremor that seemed to come from somewhere deeper than his muscles, from the wiring underneath. But this was not medication. This was the thing that happened when his body understood what his mind was still refusing to assemble. His knees were wet from the dock planks. His right hand was wet from the lake. He was standing on a dock in the dark, alone, and Cliff was in the water, and the water was three feet deep, and there was a gash on his temple, and his shoes were wrong.

Doug opened his mouth and a sound came out that he did not recognize. It was not his voice. It was not any voice he had ever used — not the pharmaceutical sales voice, not the self-deprecating murmur, not the monosyllable, not the trailing-off half-sentence he used as social currency in a group where everyone else was louder and more certain and more willing to finish a thought. The sound was raw and enormous and it came from somewhere below his diaphragm, behind his ribs, and it crossed the water and hit the far shore and came back as an echo that sounded like someone else screaming from across the lake. It came again. And again. Three times Doug screamed on the dock beside Cliff Haywood's body, and by the third time his voice was breaking and the scream thinned into something hoarse and wrecked that was closer to the sound he actually made in the world, which was barely a sound at all.

The echo died. The lake absorbed it. The loons, if they were out there, stayed quiet. Somewhere across the water a dog barked twice and stopped. The silence that followed was not the dock silence he'd come here for — the clean, empty silence that asked nothing of him. This silence had a body in it. This silence was the kind that waits.

Doug stood on the dock and his legs felt strange beneath him — not weak exactly, but disconnected, as if his weight were a problem of engineering that his knees had decided not to solve. He sat down on the wet planks. The cold went through his sweatpants immediately. He sat and looked at Cliff in the water and waited for the sound of footsteps on the stone path, because they would come. They had to come. His scream had been the loudest sound he'd ever made in twenty-five years of friendship, the loudest sound this lake had heard all week, and somewhere in the house there were four men who had just heard the quietest person they knew make a noise that did not belong to him.

He waited. The water lapped. Cliff's silver hair moved slightly with the lake's pulse, a slow drift that was almost gentle, almost peaceful, like the hair of a person sleeping face-down in the water, which was not a kind of sleeping at all.

---

Nate reached the dock first. Doug heard him before he saw him — bare feet on stone, the slap and stumble of a man who'd run outside without shoes and was paying for it on the flagstones.

"Doug? Doug, what happened?"

Nate's phone light hit the dock and swung across the water and found Cliff and stopped.

"Oh, shit." Nate's voice dropped from his chest to his stomach. "Oh, shit. Oh—"

"He's dead," Doug said. His voice was back to its normal register, which was barely a sound, and the words came out flat and colorless after the screams that had produced them. He was still sitting on the dock. His sweatpants were soaked and his hands were in his lap, the right one still damp from the lake.

Nate stood at the foot of the dock with his phone light on the water. The light caught the silver of Cliff's hair and the fabric of his polo and the stillness of his arms, which were out at angles that suggested reaching — as if Cliff had been trying to grab something when he stopped trying. Nate's mouth was open and his face was the face of a man whose narration had crashed. The podcaster, the riff artist, the man who could produce forty minutes on any topic, standing barefoot on cold stone with nothing to say. His light hand trembled slightly and the beam wavered on the water, making Cliff's shape shift and blur.

"Is he—" Nate started, and didn't finish.

Paul arrived next, at a run, breathing hard, carrying a flashlight — a real flashlight, not his phone, because Paul was the man who knew where the flashlight was. The beam was brighter than Nate's and when it found the water and found Cliff it held steady with the practiced stability of someone who'd aimed flashlights at damage before. Insurance adjusters look at wrecked things for a living.

"Jesus Christ," Paul said.

He didn't wait. He stepped off the dock's edge and into the lake up to his shins, the water soaking his khakis to the knee, and he got a hand under Cliff's shoulder and turned him. Cliff's body rotated slowly in the shallow water — the face came up, white and slack, the eyes unevenly open, the gash on the right temple visible now in Paul's flashlight beam, the wound wider and more ragged than it had looked from above. Paul put two fingers against the side of Cliff's neck and held them there for what felt like a long time and was probably ten seconds. He moved his fingers. He tried again, a quarter inch to the left. Then he took his hand away.

"He's cold," Paul said. His voice was careful, calibrated, the voice he used for information that mattered. "He's been in the water for—" He stopped. Doug watched Paul's insurance brain start the calculation — water temperature, body temperature, rate of cooling, duration — and then watched something in Paul's face override the math and leave him standing in the lake with one hand on his dead friend's arm and no idea what the number was or whether the number mattered. "A while. He's been in here a while."

"Paul, come out of the water." Joel's voice, from the foot of the dock. He'd arrived without Doug hearing him — a deliberate walk, not a run, the pace of a man who'd been trained to approach situations with his brain before his body. His phone light found the water and held on it with the same steadiness as Paul's flashlight.

"I was checking—"

"I know. Come out of the water. Don't move him any more." Joel said this with the clipped authority that Doug had heard him use in the group only a few times in twenty-five years, the voice that belonged to the courthouse, not the lake house. "This is a crime scene."

The word settled over the dock. Nate looked at Joel. Paul, standing in the water, looked at Joel. Doug, sitting on the wet planks, looked at Joel.

"How do you know that?" Nate said. His voice was raw and smaller than usual and the question had no performance in it.

"Look at his shoes." Joel crouched at the edge of the dock and aimed his light at Cliff's feet. The leather loafers were visible beneath the surface, the dark leather taking on water, the thin soles entirely wrong for the stone path and the dock wood and every surface between the house and the lake. "Those are his indoor shoes. He didn't walk down here in loafers."

Nobody spoke. Joel's light stayed on the loafers. The water moved around them with gentle indifference.

"And the water's three feet deep," Joel continued. His voice was precise and quiet, the voice that assembled facts the way Paul assessed damage — not to understand but to contain. "A conscious person doesn't drown face-down in three feet of water. He was either unconscious when he went in, or he was held."

"There's a wound." Paul was still in the lake. He looked down at Cliff's face, half-lit by the flashlight beam. "Right temple. It's not a scrape. It's a gash. Something hit him, or—" Paul touched his own temple, demonstrating, and his hand was wet from the lake and left a dark streak on his face that Doug couldn't look at. "Something connected with force."

"Or something was used," Joel said.

The sentence ended and nobody added to it. Joel stood up from his crouch. Paul waded out of the water and onto the dock, his khakis dripping, his shoes ruined. He stood there breathing, not the hard breathing of running but the tight breathing of a man keeping himself in the measured range when the measurement was failing.

Tommy came down the path last.

His footsteps were heavier than the others — the solid tread of a big man in shoes, each step distinct on the flagstone. He arrived at the foot of the dock and stopped. His phone was in his hand but the light was off. He stood in the dark for a long moment, just the shape of him visible against the tree line, six-two and still built like the athlete he'd been, the crew cut and the big hands and the scar on his forearm that nobody had ever asked about in a way that produced a real answer.

Then he turned on his light and it found the water and it found Cliff.

"No," Tommy said.

One word. Not loud. Not the coaching bark, not the halftime volume, not the voice that could fill a gym and pin a sixteen-year-old to his locker. Just the word, at a speaking volume, and then nothing. He walked to the edge of the dock and stood next to where Doug was sitting and looked down at Cliff in the water and his hands were at his sides and they were closed — not fists, not threatening, just the closed hands of a man gripping something that wasn't there. His jaw was working slightly, a small clenching and unclenching that was rhythmic and private.

"Tommy," Nate said, and the name had the tone of someone reaching for a person who might not be reachable.

Tommy didn't respond. He looked at the water. He looked at Cliff. He was very still. Doug had seen Tommy still before — in the rare moments when the coaching bluster and the physical energy drained out of him and what was left was a man who was keeping something contained — but this stillness was different. This was the stillness of a man watching something he already knew about.

Doug noticed this. He filed it in the place where he filed things he saw and couldn't yet explain, the place that was full after twenty-five years of watching rooms from the edge.

"We need to call Mallick," Paul said. He was out of the water, standing on the dock, his flashlight now pointed at the planks rather than the body, giving Cliff the privacy that Cliff no longer needed. "Right now."

"It's midnight," Nate said, and the absurdity of the objection — as if there were a polite hour to report a body — registered on his own face the moment the words left him. "Yeah. Obviously. I know."

"I'll call," Joel said. He already had his phone out, his thumb finding the number, the efficiency of a man who'd called law enforcement in worse circumstances than this, from holding cells and courthouse hallways and the living rooms of clients whose lives were already over. He stepped off the dock and walked a few paces down the path and put the phone to his ear.

Doug sat on the dock and looked at Cliff. The moonlight had shifted or his eyes had adjusted, and he could see more clearly now — the color of the skin already different from the color of living skin, a pallor underneath the permanent tan that had always made Cliff look like he'd just come back from somewhere expensive. Cliff's watch was still on his wrist, the crystal intact, catching the moonlight. His hands were open. His polo was a pale blue — Doug could see it now, the color emerging as his night vision sharpened — and it was the polo Cliff had been wearing earlier that evening when he'd poured himself the Lagavulin and talked about the house and the estate and the process and said _he'd have won the league this year_ and gone out to the deck with his drink.

Two hours ago. Maybe less. Cliff had been alive in the great room two hours ago, drinking Martin's scotch, wearing those loafers on the hardwood floor, making plans for a future that now included neither Martin nor himself.

Behind him, Joel's voice, flat and clear: "Detective Mallick. It's Joel Beckman, at the Kessler house. We've got a second body."

The words carried across the dock and the water. _A second body._ Which meant that the first body — Martin, their friend, the man whose house this was and whose scotch Cliff had been drinking and whose draft picks were circled in red on the board in the great room — the first body was now just the first. A precedent. A number in a sequence that now had a second term, and the second term was in the water six feet from Doug's knees, and the weekend that had already been the worst of Doug's life had become something else entirely. Something that wouldn't end when they drove to the airport in the morning. Something that had just started.

Doug looked up from the water. The four men were arranged on and around the dock in positions that were, he realized, the same positions they'd occupied all weekend — Paul managing, Joel analyzing, Nate trying and failing to narrate, Tommy standing apart. But the positions meant something different now because the thing they were positioned around had changed. They weren't five friends dealing with a death anymore. They were four men standing over a murder.

One of them had done this.

The thought arrived without fanfare. Doug didn't gasp or stiffen or feel the ground shift. The thought simply appeared in the space where his brain stored things it had observed but not yet processed, like a prescription label he'd read without registering. One of them had done this. The shoes were wrong. The water was shallow. Cliff didn't walk to the dock in loafers. Someone had brought him here, or moved him here, or done something on this dock that ended with Cliff face-down in three feet of water with a gash on his temple and his indoor shoes still on. And someone meant one of them, because there was nobody else. There hadn't been anybody else all weekend. Just the seven of them, then six, and now five.

Doug wanted to say something. He opened his mouth and the thing that formed was too small for the size of what it had to carry, so he closed his mouth and said nothing, which was what he always did. What he'd always done. The quiet one. The one who watched. The one who saw everything and contributed nothing and let other people assemble the meaning from the evidence he'd been accumulating silently for twenty-five years.

But the scream was still in him. He could feel it in his chest, a vibration that hadn't fully dissipated, the residue of the loudest sound he'd ever made. He thought about the dock on Saturday evening — or was it Sunday, the days had blurred — when he'd sat on this bench and watched the lake and let the quiet be his instead of the group's. The dock had been the one place where his silence was not a symptom or a social failure or the thing that made Mallick suspicious. The dock was where silence meant peace. That was gone now. The dock was a crime scene. There would be tape and photographs and measurements and deputies with flashlights mapping the distance from the planks to the body and the angle of the wound, and the bench where Doug had sat would be evidence, and the path he'd walked would be evidence, and his footprints and his wet hand and his scream would be data points in whatever case was about to be built in this place where he'd come to not be data.

Joel came back to the dock, his phone call finished. "She's on her way. She said not to touch anything." He looked at Paul's wet khakis, at Doug's wet hand. "I told her we already touched things."

"I was trying to help him," Paul said, and his voice cracked on the last word — not a dramatic crack, not a sob, just the sound of a measured man's measurement failing at the seam. He cleared his throat and the measurement was back and the crack was gone. "I was trying to see if there was—"

"I know," Joel said. "It's fine."

It was not fine. Nothing about the dock or the water or the body or the shoes or the next several hours of their lives was fine. But _it's fine_ was the thing you said, the way _I'm good_ was the thing Doug said and _yeah, probably_ was the thing Doug said and _sure, that makes sense_ was the thing Doug said when nothing made sense and he was not good, and the language of male friendship was a series of lies told at close range by people who loved each other enough to let the lies stand.

Nate sat down on the dock next to Doug. Not close — three feet between them — but on the dock, which was where Doug was, which was a choice. Nate's bare feet dangled over the edge, inches above the water where Cliff was floating, and Doug saw Nate realize this and pull his feet back and fold them under him. They sat together on the wet wood and neither of them spoke.

Paul stood with his flashlight aimed at the planks. Joel stood at the foot of the dock, hands in his pockets, face unreadable in the bad light. Tommy stood where he'd been standing since he arrived — at the edge, not on the dock, not off it, a man on the margin of whatever this was. His phone light was off again. He was a shape in the dark.

The lake lapped at the dock and at Cliff and at the pilings and at the shore. The sound was the same sound it had been all week. The same sound it had been for twenty-five years of September weekends, the sound of water that didn't care what happened on its shore, that would be lapping at this dock long after every man standing on it was dead or in prison or back in his apartment in Burnsville taking his Lexapro with the door locked.

Doug thought: _I came here because I needed people._

He had. He'd packed his duffel in the Burnsville apartment and driven to the airport and flown to Detroit Lakes and ridden in the rental car to the house because the alternative was another weekend alone with the antidepressant and the anti-anxiety medication and the silence of a one-bedroom where the silence was not peace but absence, and he'd thought that being around his oldest friends would remind him that he was a person who existed in relation to other people and not just a man with two prescriptions and a divorce and a pharmaceutical sales quota he hadn't hit in three months. He'd come here because he needed people.

And now two of them were dead. And the dock where he'd found something like peace was a crime scene. And one of the men standing in the dark had killed someone tonight and was standing here in the aftermath pretending to be shocked, and Doug had screamed — Doug, who never raised his voice, who let other people finish his sentences, who said _it's fine_ when it wasn't and _I'm good_ when he wasn't and _yeah_ when he meant _no_ — Doug had screamed so loudly that dogs across the lake had answered, and the scream was still in his body, the one true sound he'd made all weekend, and it hadn't been a word or a sentence or even a thought. It had just been the sound of a man who came here because he needed people, finding one of them dead in the water.

Headlights appeared on the far end of the gravel driveway, distant, the crunch of tires audible before the light reached the house. Mallick. Coming back to the house she'd left two days ago when she'd decided this was probably natural causes. Coming back without the casserole.

Doug stood up. His sweatpants were soaked, his knees ached from the dock, his right hand still smelled like the lake. He stood and looked at the water one more time. Cliff's hair drifted in the moonlight. The loafers were dark shapes below the surface. The gash on his temple was a black line. He was forty-eight years old and he was dead in three feet of water on a Monday night in September, and the last thing he'd said to Doug was _hey_ in response to Doug saying _hey_, and that was it, that was the whole of their final exchange, two men who'd known each other for a quarter century saying _hey_ to each other across a room where another dead man's scotch was still on the counter.

Doug turned away from the water and walked up the dock toward the house and the headlights and whatever came next.
