# Chapter 17

Paul watched Tommy through the sliding glass door for a long time before he went out.

Tommy was in the Adirondack chair closest to the railing — Martin's chair, though nobody called it that anymore — with a beer balanced on the armrest he wasn't touching. The propane heater was off. The temperature had dropped into the low fifties after sunset, and Tommy was sitting there in a t-shirt and jeans like he couldn't feel it or didn't care. His hands were on his knees. He was looking at the lake.

Joel was in the study. He'd been there since they read the fire document, doing what Joel did — rereading Martin's careful handwriting for the third time, making notes on his legal pad, building the case against a man he'd known for twenty-five years with the same flat precision he used for people he'd never met. Joel had wanted to go straight to Mallick.

"We hand her this tonight," Joel had said. "The document, the photos on Cliff's phone, the timeline. She's got it."

"I know."

"Then let's go."

"Give me an hour."

Joel looked at him the way he looked at clients who were about to make their own worst decision. "Why?"

Paul didn't have a clean answer. He had the messy kind — because Martin was his best friend and Martin spent twenty-two years protecting Tommy and Paul needed to look at Tommy's face when Tommy heard the words. Because Paul had sat on that deck a hundred times and grilled steaks and argued about the Vikings' secondary and never once understood what was underneath the conversation. Because he'd read Martin's handwriting in the safe and it was the most Martin thing he'd ever encountered — specific, careful, nonjudgmental — and Martin never showed it to him. Martin, who told Paul everything. Martin, who apparently didn't.

"Because I need to," Paul said.

Joel didn't push it. Twenty years as a public defender had taught him the specific futility of arguing with someone who'd already decided. He'd filed the objection. It was on the record. "I'll be in the study. If he — "

"He won't."

"You don't actually know that, Paul."

"I know Tommy."

"You didn't, though." Joel's voice was flat. Not cruel. The observation of a man who had been wrong about everyone in this house and no longer pretended otherwise. "None of us did."

Paul left Joel with his legal pad and his cold coffee and went to the kitchen, where the refrigerator was in the loud phase of its cycle and the remains of somebody's sandwich sat on a plate nobody had washed. The kitchen smelled like old coffee and the faint cedar of Martin's cologne, which had somehow gotten into every room and refused to leave, the way Martin himself had gotten into every room and refused to leave, even now, even dead.

He stood at the sliding glass door. Through the glass, Tommy was perfectly still. The kitchen light threw a yellow rectangle on the deck boards behind him, and Tommy's shadow stretched long toward the railing, and Paul stood there looking at a man he'd known for twenty-five years and had apparently never known at all and tried to understand why his chest hurt the way it did. Not the Martin grief — that was older now, three days old, which in grief terms meant it had moved from the skin to the bone. This was a different hurt. This was the hurt of having to do the thing he was about to do, and knowing Martin would have done it differently, and knowing Martin's way was wrong.

The sliding door ground in its track. The sound carried.

Paul stepped onto the deck in his socks. The boards were cold and slick with dew. He'd forgotten shoes, which wasn't like him — Paul Decker, who noticed things, who spotted a staged claim at fifty feet, who remembered people's kids' names and showed up five minutes early and brought the right wine. Paul Decker, who'd missed the biggest thing in this group for twenty-two years because he was too busy being reliable to notice what reliability was for.

Tommy didn't turn.

Paul crossed to the chair next to Tommy's and sat. The Adirondack's armrests were rough with old paint. Martin had been saying for three summers that he'd sand and restain these chairs. The chairs were as they were.

"Hey," Paul said.

Tommy lifted his beer an inch. Set it back without drinking. "Hey."

The silence sat between them the way it had ten thousand times on this deck. Except it wasn't the same silence. Paul's silence had Martin's handwriting in it, careful and dated August 2002, and a dead man at the county examiner's in Detroit Lakes, and the specific sick knowledge that Tommy — Tommy who ran a football camp for sophomores, Tommy who made his daughters pancakes in the shape of footballs, Tommy who gave Martin that stupid Viagra as a joke four nights ago — had walked down to the dock eighty yards from where they were sitting and killed Cliff Haywood and held him under the water in three feet of lake until Cliff stopped moving. Tommy's silence was older. Twenty-two years older. Paul could see the shape of it now, in the way Tommy's shoulders pulled inward and his hands gripped his knees and his eyes stayed fixed on the dark water as if looking anywhere else might crack something he'd spent two decades keeping whole.

"I need to talk to you," Paul said.

"Yeah." Tommy's voice was flat. Not hostile. Not the coaching volume, not the bluster. Just flat. The voice of a man who'd been sitting in a cold chair waiting for the thing he'd been waiting for. "I figured."

Paul had planned an approach. He'd organized it the way he organized assessments — sequential, documented, fair to all sides, with room for the other party's account. He'd imagined starting soft. A question, maybe. Some context. An approach that was measured and reasonable and gave Tommy space.

He abandoned it the moment he opened his mouth.

"I know about the fire, Tommy."

Tommy's hand went rigid on his knee. The tendons stood out along his forearm, and the scar tissue above his left wrist — the scar Paul had seen a thousand times at barbecues and draft weekends and pool parties and never once asked about — caught the light from the kitchen window. That was all. His body didn't move. His face didn't change. His hand tightened and the scar shone white and the wind moved through the pines behind the house with its particular late-September whisper.

"I don't know what you're talking about."

"Washburn County. 2002. The cabin on the chain of lakes." Paul heard his own voice and it sounded like someone else's — the qualifiers gone, the hedging stripped, the insurance language abandoned. Just the facts. "Ray Toomey."

Tommy didn't move. The beer bottle trembled on the armrest. A small sound, glass on painted wood, barely audible.

"Martin wrote it down," Paul said. "He kept it in the safe for twenty-two years. His handwriting, his words. Joel and I read it this afternoon."

"Martin wouldn't — "

"He did."

"Martin wouldn't do that to me."

"He didn't do it to you. He wrote it down and he locked it up and he never showed a single person. Cliff found it. Cliff opened the safe and found the envelope and photographed what was inside and brought it to you on this deck, and you — "

"Don't."

" — and you killed him."

The word landed between them on the cold boards. *Killed.* The first time either of them had said it out loud, and it sat there with the weight of the body in the water and the wrong shoes and the gash on the temple and the shallow water that couldn't drown a conscious person and all the things Mallick was already assembling and all the things she wasn't.

Tommy stood up. The chair scraped back across the deck with a sound that traveled through Paul's sternum. Tommy's body did what it always did under pressure — went vertical, went wide, the coaching stance, chest forward, hands apart, feet planted, the posture of a man who controlled space for a living. He walked to the railing and grabbed it with both hands and stood with his back to Paul and his shoulders bunched under his t-shirt.

"You went through Martin's things." His voice was louder now. The coach. "You went in his study and went through his fucking safe and you read — "

"The safe was open. Cliff left it open."

"So you read some piece of paper Martin wrote twenty-two years ago and you think you know what — "

"I know what I read."

"You don't know shit, Decker." He turned. His face was working — the bravado trying to form and failing, the jaw clenched and the eyes unable to hold it, something underneath trying to break through the surface and finding no shape for it. "You read a piece of paper. You weren't there. You don't know what happened that night. You don't know what it — "

"You started the fire."

"I was twenty-five years old."

"A man died."

"I know a man died!" Tommy's voice cracked open on the word. Not a shout — the thing before a shout, the place where the coaching projection fails and what's underneath sounds like what it is. Scared. Raw. "You think I don't know that? You think I've gone a single fucking day — " He stopped. His mouth was open and his eyes were wet and his hands were still on the railing behind him and his chest was heaving. The kitchen light was reflected in his eyes — two small yellow rectangles in the dark of his face.

Paul waited. A bat crossed the gap between the pine canopy and the roofline, a small dark flickering thing, and neither of them watched it. Somewhere on the far side of the lake, a dock light blinked on and off and on again.

"Tell me," Paul said. Quietly. The voice he used with clients whose stories had collapsed and the only thing left was the version they'd been avoiding. "Tell me what happened."

Tommy breathed. In through the nose, out through the mouth — sideline breathing, the kind Paul had watched him use at games when a play went bad and he needed his body to be steady before his voice could be. His hands let go of the railing. He looked at his palms, turning them over, as if checking for something — splinters, burns, evidence of what those hands had done.

"We were up at the cabin," Tommy said. His voice was different. Smaller. Not the coach. Not the jock bluster. Something Paul had never heard from Tommy in twenty-five years, and he understood now that this was the voice that had been under the performance the whole time. "Martin's uncle's place, the one on the chain of lakes up in Washburn County. Weekend thing. We'd been drinking. I was — " He shook his head. "Hammered. Past hammered. There was a space heater in the back bedroom, the one with the bunk beds. I knocked it over getting into bed and I was too drunk to — I just passed out."

"And Martin — "

"Martin smelled the smoke. Martin got me out." Tommy's voice caught on the name. Not dramatically — the way a plank catches when you step on its weak spot, a single creak and then the surface holds again. "The caretaker was in the front cabin. Ray. The one closer to the road. The fire moved fast, Paul. Old wood. Dry pine that hadn't been treated in thirty years and it went up like — " He didn't finish. His hand came up and gripped the back of his neck, the fingers digging in, and Paul recognized the gesture now for what it had always been — not a stress habit but a memory, the hand going to where the heat went first, where the body remembered the fire before the mind did.

"Ray Toomey died," Paul said. Not a question. The way he'd write it on a form. *Insured's account of the loss.*

"Yeah." Tommy was looking at the lake. "Fifty-three years old. Three kids. Worked for the county parks department in the off-season." The details came out worn smooth, the way memorized things do — rehearsed over twenty-two years of carrying them. "Martin called it in as electrical. Faulty heater, old wiring, no one's fault. He looked at what was left of the cabin and he looked at me and he said we call it in now and we say the heater was bad and nobody's going to dig deeper. And nobody did."

"Until Cliff."

Tommy's face changed. The rawness that had been surfacing — the twenty-five-year-old kid speaking for the first time about the worst night of his life — closed over. Something harder took its place. His eyes went flat and the wetness in them stopped being grief and became something Paul recognized from the sideline at rough games, when a play turned dirty and Tommy's body went still before his voice went loud.

"Don't."

"Cliff came to you with the document."

"I said don't."

"On this deck. Sunday. Two beers and a business arrangement."

Tommy's hands were fists at his sides. Not aimed at Paul — Paul was aware, in the distant calculating way of a man whose job required risk assessment, that he was sitting in the dark on a deck with a person who had killed someone thirty-six hours ago. But the fists weren't for Paul. They were turned inward, clenched against something inside Tommy's own chest that had been clenched for years.

"He wanted forty thousand dollars," Tommy said. "Not from me. From Martin's estate. He wanted me to make sure the estate paid him back what Martin owed. Plus interest. Plus — he called it goodwill. Cliff sat right there with a beer and called it a goodwill adjustment. Like he was closing on a property."

"And Monday night."

Tommy's jaw moved. His eyes were fixed on a point past the railing, past the trees, past the lake, somewhere Paul couldn't follow.

"He came down to the dock. He wanted to talk about the arrangement. About how we were going to manage it." Tommy's voice dropped. The profanity was gone, the bluster was gone, the volume was gone. What was left barely carried over the wind. "Manage. He kept saying manage. Like Martin used to — " The sound he made wasn't a word. It was the space where a word tried to form and the thing behind it was too large. "I didn't plan it. He was standing there in those fucking loafers he always wore, talking about managing me, and I — "

He stopped.

Paul sat in the Adirondack chair and listened to the wind in the pines and the small lapping of the lake against the shore and the silence where the rest of that sentence would have been. Eighty yards away, down the stone path through the pines and birch, the dock extended thirty feet into the black water. Monday night, the path would have been dark. The water beside the dock was three feet deep. Cliff was wearing the wrong shoes.

Tommy's breathing was ragged but slowing. His forehead was resting against his forearms on the railing. He looked like a player in a penalty box. A man serving time he hadn't been assigned yet.

"Martin spent twenty-two years on this," Paul said. His voice was hoarse. The insurance cadences were gone — the measured phrasing, the professional distance. Just Paul, talking to a man he'd known since they were twenty-two and never known at all. "Twenty-two years making sure you came back every September. Making sure nobody looked too close. Making sure you were — "

"Managed."

"Protected." Paul's throat ached, and the ache was the specific kind that comes from holding words back for too long and then releasing them into air that isn't warm enough to receive them. "He protected you, Tommy. His whole life. And he never told me."

Tommy lifted his head. He looked at Paul. For the first time since Paul sat down, Tommy's face was fully open. No bluster, no coaching bravado, no defensive volume. A man's face, forty-seven years old, with the lines of carrying something heavy for too long visible in a way they'd never been before, because the thing holding them in place — the performance, the simplicity, the loud uncomplicated surface — was gone.

"He loved you too," Tommy said. "He did, Paul."

"He loved you more."

The sentence was out before Paul could qualify it. He would normally have softened it — *to be fair, it's not that simple, I don't mean it like that.* But the qualifiers were burned off and the sentence stood in the cold air between them and it was true. Martin loved Tommy more. Not more cleanly, not more simply, but more urgently — with the specific weight of a bond made in a burning cabin with a dead man in the next room. Paul's friendship with Martin was clean. Martin's bond with Tommy was something else entirely — love and guilt and obligation, twenty-two years of showing up for the person you saved or the person who owed you the saving, and neither of them ever being sure which was which.

"Yeah," Tommy said. Not arguing. "He did."

Something moved across Tommy's face then — not an expression Paul could name, exactly, but a shift, a loosening, as if a muscle that had been contracted since 2002 had released. It looked like exhaustion. It looked like grief. And underneath both of those, barely visible, it looked like relief. The relief of being seen. Twenty-two years of performing bluntness and simplicity, of making himself small because smallness was safe when you were hiding the thing Tommy was hiding, and now someone was looking at him and seeing all of it — the fire and the man who died in the fire and the man who covered it up and the twenty-two years of managed guilt and the Monday night at the dock when the management failed — and Tommy didn't have to hold the shape anymore. The shape was held by someone else's knowing.

---

The wind was picking up off the lake. Paul could feel it on his face, cold and smelling of the water — organic, mineral, the specific September smell of a lake turning over, life decaying in the shallows as the temperature dropped. His socks were soaked through from the dew on the boards. He should have gone inside for shoes. He should have done a lot of things.

"Martin left you the house," Paul said.

Tommy went blank. Not the blankness of a controlled face — the blankness of a blown fuse. Everything that had been visible on his face a moment before — the grief, the anger, the fragile relief of being known — went dark.

"What?"

"The will's in the safe. Same place as the fire document. Martin left you this house. The lake house. Everything. Not me, not Joel, not the group. You."

Tommy looked at the deck beneath his feet. At the railing his hands had been gripping. At the outline of the A-frame roof above them, cutting the stars in a familiar triangle he'd been looking at for twenty-five Septembers. His eyes moved across these things like a man seeing them for the first time or the last time or both.

"He left me the — " Tommy's mouth worked. He looked at Paul. "Not you?"

"Not me."

"You were his best friend."

"I thought so too."

Tommy's hands slid off the railing. He took a step back, and then another, with the careful testing motion of a man who'd discovered the surface under him might not hold. He backed into the Adirondack chair and sat down hard — not a controlled sitting but a giving way, his hips forward and his head back and his legs extended, the beer bottle on the armrest knocked loose by his elbow. It rolled across the boards and came to rest against Paul's foot. Neither of them picked it up.

"He left me the house," Tommy said. Not to Paul. To the sky above the lake, to the darkness above the pines, to the stars that didn't care. "This house."

"It's in the will."

"This fucking house." Tommy pressed his palms against his eyes. His fingers were trembling — not the gross tremor of adrenaline but the fine, involuntary kind, the body's wiring shorting against a load the circuits can't carry. "The house where we came every year. The house where he kept the document. The house where I sat on this deck and drank his scotch and he — he kept me coming back here, Paul. Every year. Every time I said maybe I'd skip this one, he'd call. He always called."

"I know. I noticed."

"Did you?" Tommy took his hands from his face. His eyes were red — not crying, somewhere past or before it, the physiological response running ahead of the thing that would make it into tears. "Because I noticed too. I noticed every time he called. I noticed every time he made sure I was included, every time he poured me a drink or pulled me aside on this deck for one of his little talks about how I was doing, whether I was all right, whether the kids were good, whether Denise was — " His voice lost shape. He pulled in a breath that shuddered. "He was checking on me. That's what it was. Twenty-two years of check-ins. Making sure I was still holding up. Making sure I wasn't going to — "

He didn't finish. He didn't need to.

"And now he left me the house," Tommy said. His voice was barely audible. The coaching volume was so far gone it was hard to remember it had ever been there. "He left me the house because that's what you do when you've spent your whole life watching someone. You leave them the place where you watched them. You leave them the — "

His sentence collapsed. He was sitting in Martin's chair on Martin's deck looking at Martin's lake and his hands were on his knees and his shoulders were folded inward and he was not a loud man or a dangerous man or a coach or a killer or any of the things the surface had said he was. He was a man who had started a fire when he was twenty-five and had been kept whole ever since by another man's attention, and the attention was over.

Paul felt the cold deep in his feet now, up through his ankles, a physical thing that had nothing to do with the conversation and everything to do with the fact that he'd been sitting on a wet deck in September in his socks for twenty minutes. His hands were stiff on the armrests. The rough paint under his fingers was flaking, small chips coming away and falling to the boards. Martin's paint. Martin's chairs. Martin's deck. Martin's.

"That's the thing," Tommy said. Quieter still, but steadier. The trembling in his hands had stopped. His body had settled into the chair the way a structure settles after the shaking stops — not repaired, not whole, but done moving. Bearing what it was going to bear. "Martin kept me. That's what it was. Twenty-two years on the roster. Making sure I showed up, making sure I didn't disappear, making sure I sat on this deck and acted like a guy who was still part of something. And I hated it and I needed it and without it — " He looked at Paul. His eyes were clear. "Without that, Deck, I'm just the guy who killed somebody. That's all I am."

Paul opened his mouth. Closed it. He wanted to say something — that Tommy was more than that, that the twenty-five years of friendship were real, that the coaching and the pancakes and the daughters and the loud stupid life Tommy had built was real. But he couldn't say it because he didn't know if it was true, and he'd spent three days learning that the things he thought were true about people he loved were not the things that were true, and he was done saying things he didn't know. So he sat there in the cold and said nothing, and the nothing was the most honest thing he'd given Tommy all night.

Tommy looked at the lake. His face was the most complicated thing Paul had ever seen — not one expression but all of them, moving through like weather, rage and grief and exhaustion and the persistent awful relief of a secret finally being held by someone else's hands. The Viagra guilt and the fire guilt and Martin's death and Cliff's death and the house he'd just learned he owned and the years of being kept and the years of being watched and the understanding, arriving quietly, that the chain holding him to this group and this weekend and this deck was also the only thing that had kept him recognizable as a person and the chain was broken.

"I'm not going to tell you it's OK," Paul said. "None of this is OK."

"I know."

"I mean it. Ray Toomey. Cliff. The fire, the — none of it."

"I know, Paul."

---

The loons had started again. The tremolo — the alarmed call, the one that sounded like laughing if you didn't know what it was and sounded like warning if you did. Paul had heard it every September for twenty-five years. He'd hear it differently now.

"Mallick knows about the tampering," Paul said. His voice had recovered some of its shape — not the full insurance register, not the measured professional phrasing, but enough structure to hold what he needed to say next. "All of ours. She's been building the picture since Saturday. She has Cliff's phone — his texts to his lawyer about leverage, the photos of the fire document. She doesn't have Martin's original yet, or she does and she hasn't told us. Either way she's going to put it together. Joel says she already has enough for the forensics on the dock to narrow it down."

Tommy was looking at the lake. His face had settled into something Paul almost recognized from insurance work — the expression of a person who has lost the house, the car, the marriage, and is sitting in Paul's office signing forms for a payout that won't cover what was lost. A stillness that isn't peace. The stillness of having nothing left that can be protected.

"What do you want me to do?" Tommy asked.

"Go to Mallick."

Tommy's hands tightened on his knees. A reflex, not a decision.

"In the morning. Go downstairs and sit at that kitchen table and tell her what happened at the dock."

"The fire — "

"I'm not talking about the fire. That's — " Paul lost the sentence, found another one. "That's twenty-two years ago and it's between you and a lawyer and I don't know what it is. But Cliff. Tell her about Cliff."

"And if I don't?"

Paul looked at him. Tommy in the Adirondack chair. Martin's chair, Martin's deck, Martin's paint peeling on the armrests that Paul had rested his arms on a hundred times while they argued about the roster and drank too much and talked the way men talk when the drinks have been flowing for hours and the lake is flat and the night is doing the thing nights do up here, where the darkness is bigger than the darkness in the city and the silence is a physical presence and the years of friendship accumulate like sediment until you can't see the bottom anymore. All those nights. All those conversations on this deck about nothing and everything and the Vikings' chances and whose marriage was holding and who looked rough this year. And underneath all of it, underneath every one of those nights, Tommy carrying the fire and Martin carrying Tommy and Paul carrying nothing, knowing nothing, sitting in the chair next to the biggest secret in the group and thinking the biggest problem was Martin's draft strategy.

"Then I tell her," Paul said. "In the morning. Six AM."

Tommy looked at him for a long time. The wind off the lake pressed at their faces and Paul could smell the water and the pines and the cold coming in from the north, the specific weather that meant September in Minnesota was about to become October.

"You're not going to do what he did," Tommy said. Not a question.

"No."

"Martin would've figured something out."

"Martin would've given you the rest of your life." The sentence left Paul's mouth with a weight he hadn't expected, and he felt it land — not on Tommy but on himself, in the place where his grief for Martin had been quietly transforming for three days into something harder and more clear. He was not going to be Martin. He was not going to spend the next twenty-two years managing Tommy's guilt and the body at the dock and the name of a man who died in a fire in Washburn County while the group gathered every September on this deck and pretended the foundation underneath them was solid. Martin did that because Martin loved Tommy, and because Martin was the kind of man who, once he decided to carry something, carried it until it killed him. His heart gave out on Saturday at one-thirty in the morning and Paul would never know whether the twenty-two years of carrying Tommy's secret had anything to do with the specific way Martin's heart was built, but he knew it had something to do with the kind of life Martin lived — the vigilance, the management, the generosity that was always, always covering something. Paul loved Martin. But he was not Martin. He was Paul Decker, claims adjuster, forty-seven years old, a man who believed in the basic architecture of assessment and resolution and who could not look at what Tommy had done and call it an open claim that could be managed indefinitely. The claim was due. The loss was totaled.

"I'm giving you the night," Paul said.

Tommy nodded. Not agreement. Registration. The nod of a man hearing terms he has no power to negotiate.

"That's — " Tommy's mouth twitched. Not a smile. The ghost of something that might have been humor on a different night, in a different life, in any of the twenty-five Septembers that came before this one. "That's generous, Deck."

"It isn't." Paul stood up. His feet ached — a deep, dull cold that had moved up through the wet cotton into his bones. He steadied himself on the armrest and his hand came away with flakes of paint on his palm, small white chips that fell to the deck between them. "Six AM. That's when she starts."

Tommy didn't answer. Paul looked at him one more time. Tommy in the Adirondack chair, the lake behind him, the last of the kitchen light on half his face. The other half in shadow. He looked like what he was — a forty-seven-year-old man sitting on the deck of a house he'd just learned he'd inherited from the friend who'd loved him the way you love the person whose worst moment you witnessed and whose life you rearranged your own to protect. The gift that was also a sentence. The house that was also a monument to a bond that was never friendship, not really, or was friendship but the kind that grows in the soil of something terrible and takes the shape of the terrible thing even as it reaches for the light.

Paul went inside. The sliding door ground shut behind him.

He walked down the hallway past the half bath and the photographs on the wall — Martin at twenty-two with the group at the first draft, Martin at thirty with a bigger gut and a better grill, Martin at forty-five standing on the dock with his arm around Paul and both of them squinting into a sun that made them look younger than they were. Paul stopped at that one. Martin's hand on his shoulder. Paul's hand holding a beer. Behind them, out of focus, the lake, the sky, the end of a summer they hadn't known was ending.

He went into the study. Joel looked up from his legal pad.

"He's got until six," Paul said.

Joel put his pen down. He didn't ask if Tommy had admitted it. He didn't need to ask. Joel had known since this afternoon, since the crease pattern and the fire document and the shape of the evidence assembled in this room. What he'd been waiting for was Paul to come back, and Paul was back, and the expression on Paul's face said everything the words didn't.

"OK," Joel said.

Paul sat in Martin's desk chair. The leather was cracked and cold and the chair smelled like cedar, the cologne Paul had given Martin for his birthday two years ago. The bottle must have been in the drawer. The smell of Martin in the chair where Martin used to sit, in the room where Martin kept his secrets. The safe was behind the lake painting on the far wall, ajar, the way Cliff had left it and they'd left it after, because the safe didn't matter anymore. What it had protected was loose in the world now. What it had contained was sitting on the deck in the cold.

Paul closed his eyes. Through the study window, if he'd looked, he would have seen the deck, and on the deck he would have seen Tommy, still in the chair, still facing the lake, his hands on the armrests of the chair he now owned in the house he now owned on the lake where his best friend had died and his blackmailer had died and his twenty-two years of managed silence were about to end.

But Paul didn't look. He sat in Martin's chair with his eyes closed and his wet socks leaving marks on the hardwood floor and he listened to the loons calling across the water in the dark. The wail this time — the long, rising one, the sound that carried over the lake like grief looking for a place to land. Joel turned a page on his legal pad. The refrigerator clicked off. The house went quiet, the way the house had been quiet at one-thirty on Saturday morning when Martin's heart stopped, the way it would be quiet tomorrow at six when Tommy walked down the stairs and sat across from Detective Mallick in the kitchen and said whatever he was going to say.

Paul listened to the quiet. He listened for a long time.
