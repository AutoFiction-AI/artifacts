# Chapter 2

Nate was standing in the doorway of the guest room in his boxers and a podcast-branded T-shirt, and Paul knew before Nate said anything because Nate wasn't performing. Nate was always performing — at the draft table, at the grill, on the phone to his eleven listeners, in his sleep for all Paul knew. But the man in the doorway was small and still and his mouth was working without sound, and that was how Paul Decker learned that his best friend was dead.

"Something's wrong with Martin." Nate's voice came out at a register Paul had never heard from him — flat, airless, like the words had been squeezed through something too narrow. "I went up to — I was gonna ask him about the side bet, and he's — Paul, I think he's —"

Paul was already moving. Bare feet on the cold hallway floor, the bathroom nightlight throwing that sick yellow glow Martin always left on for guests who drank too much and needed a beacon to piss by. Martin's bedroom door was open at the end of the hall. Nate must have left it open. The light from the nightstand lamp spilled into the hallway in a thin stripe.

Martin was in bed. On his back, one arm hanging off the mattress, the other folded across his chest like he'd arranged himself for a photograph. The sheets were pulled up to his waist. His mouth was open. His eyes were closed — Paul would be grateful for that later, grateful he didn't have to see whatever the eyes looked like, but right now he wasn't thinking about later. He was looking at the color of Martin's face, which was wrong. Martin was always ruddy. Flushed from lake sun, from scotch, from yelling about someone's draft pick, from the blood pressure he joked about and never treated. Now his skin was a grayish yellow that Paul had never seen on a living person, and the seeing of it was what told his body before his brain caught up.

He put his hand on Martin's neck and felt nothing. Warm skin, slack muscle, no pulse. The warmth was the cruelest part — it meant recent, it meant close, it meant that somewhere in the last hour Martin Kessler's heart had stopped and the heat was still leaving his body and Paul had been asleep twenty feet down the hall.

"Call 911," Paul said.

"I don't have — my phone's downstairs, I left it on the —"

"Go get it. Go."

Nate didn't move. His face was doing something Paul had never seen from him, a kind of involuntary disassembly, the muscles pulling in directions that had nothing to do with comedy or performance or any expression Nate had practiced. His eyes were wet and he was gripping the doorframe with one hand like it was the only thing keeping him upright.

"Nate. Phone. Now."

Nate turned and went. Paul heard him on the stairs — heard the third step creak, the one everyone knew to skip, because Nate was not thinking about stairs right now — and then Paul was alone with Martin.

He started CPR because you start CPR. That was what you did. You didn't check for a pulse a second time or stand there making calculations or weigh the odds like a man who reviewed actuarial tables for a living. You put your hands on your best friend's chest and you pushed. Paul's training was a weekend course twelve years ago, mandated by his insurance company for field adjusters who might encounter a medical emergency at a claim site, and what he remembered was: thirty compressions, two breaths, don't stop, don't second-guess the depth, and the practice mannequin had smelled like new rubber sneakers and the instructor had said "you can't make it worse" with the cheerful certainty of a man who had never done this on someone he loved.

He laced his fingers and set the heel of his right hand on Martin's sternum and pushed. The first compression produced a sound that was not the cinematic crack of breaking ribs but a soft wet yielding, a thud that Paul felt travel through his palms and up his forearms and into a place behind his eyes where it would live for months. He pushed again. Again. The counting was supposed to help — one, two, three, four — but what the counting actually did was give his brain something to hold while the rest of him processed the fact that the body moving under his hands was the person who had driven four hundred miles to pick him up from a motel in Fargo when Paul's rental car died during his father's funeral trip. Who had shown up at Paul's house the night after Anne's miscarriage with a bottle of Maker's Mark and a roll of trash bags and had cleaned the kitchen without being asked and without saying a single thing about why the kitchen needed cleaning. Who had been Paul's best man and given a toast that made Paul's mother cry, and Paul's mother did not cry, and Martin had known exactly which story would do it — the one about Paul falling asleep in the library junior year and Martin covering him with his own coat and leaving a note that said "you looked cold, you looked stupid, I'll be at the bar."

Fifteen, sixteen, seventeen. Paul tilted Martin's head back and sealed his mouth over Martin's and breathed. The air went in and Martin's chest rose and the air came back tasting like Lagavulin and something underneath the Lagavulin that was metallic and intimate and terrible. He breathed again. He went back to compressions. Twenty-five years of friendship being expressed as a rhythm Paul could not maintain because his arms were already burning and his breath was already ragged and the counting was already losing itself in the gap between what he was doing and what it was accomplishing.

Because he knew. Paul Decker, who had reviewed eleven wrongful death claims in his career, who could cite out-of-hospital cardiac arrest survival rates to two decimal places, who had once described to a jury the difference between a body found within the resuscitation window and a body found after it — Paul knew that what he was doing was not medicine. It was not even hope. It was community theater: a forty-seven-year-old man in flannel pajama pants performing chest compressions on another forty-seven-year-old man whose heart had stopped long enough ago that the warmth was leaving and no amount of rhythmic pressure was going to reverse whatever had already been decided.

He pushed anyway. He pushed until his shoulders felt like they were separating and the count dissolved into meaningless numbers and the sound of his own breathing was louder than anything the body was producing. He pushed until the effort became its own reason — not to save Martin but to not be the person who stopped trying.

Then he stopped.

He sat back on his heels and looked at Martin's face, which had not changed, which would never change again, and he said, "Fuck."

Quiet. Almost conversational. Not addressed to Martin or to God or to the obscenity of dying alone in bed at forty-seven with a half-finished scotch on the nightstand and your friends sleeping down the hall. Addressed to the room. To the lamp and the sheets and the gap between Martin's front teeth that Paul could see because Martin's mouth was still open and there was nothing dignified about death, nothing composed, nothing like the version Paul had been selling to insurance claimants for twenty years when he described their covered family member's passing.

He leaned forward and put his forehead on the edge of the mattress. The sheets smelled like Martin's laundry detergent — the blue bottle, the cheap one, the same brand Martin had used since their freshman dorm at the University of Minnesota. Paul had seen that blue bottle under every sink Martin had ever owned. Apartments, the first house, Linda's house, this lake house. A stupid detail, a nothing detail, and Paul pressed his face into it and his eyes burned and his throat closed and his nose ran onto the cotton, and he stayed there with his forehead on the bed and his hands on the mattress three inches from his dead best friend and he did not move for what felt like a very long time.

---

Nate came back with his phone and Tommy and Joel behind him, all of them half-dressed and blinking in the hallway light. Tommy in coaching shorts and nothing else, the scar on his left forearm visible, his crew cut flattened on one side from the pillow. Joel in boxers and a University of Minnesota T-shirt that had to be twenty years old, its collar stretched and the maroon faded to something closer to brown. They filled the doorway and looked past Paul to the bed, and Paul, who was standing now, who had wiped his face with the back of his hand and straightened up because that was what you did, shook his head.

"Jesus Christ," Tommy said. He said it the way he said everything — loud, definitive — but his face was wrong for the volume. His face was the face of a man who had just been told something he already feared.

"Is he —" Joel started.

"Yeah."

Tommy stepped into the room. He put his hand on Martin's chest, his enormous hand flat on the sternum Paul had just been compressing, and held it there like he was checking for himself, like the determination required a second opinion from a man who coached high school football for a living. He held it there and then he pulled it back and looked at his own hand and the fingers curled slightly and Paul watched something move behind Tommy's eyes that was not grief. Not yet. It was closer to recognition, like a man hearing a sound he'd been listening for.

"Did you call?" Paul asked Nate.

"I tried — there's no signal in the basement. I came back up and got these guys first."

"There's signal on the deck," Paul said. "Or by the road."

Nobody moved toward the deck.

Doug appeared in the hallway behind them, gaunt and blinking, his sweatshirt on inside out, the tag sticking up from his collar like a small white flag. He didn't ask. He looked past the bodies in the doorway at the bed and at their faces and back at the bed, and his mouth made a small shape that didn't become a word. He stepped back against the wall to make himself smaller, which was what Doug did when rooms got too full.

Cliff was last. He arrived in pressed pajama pants and a T-shirt that still had a crease from the fold — because Cliff packed his T-shirts in tissue paper, because Cliff was Cliff — and he stood at the back of the group and looked at Martin's body on the bed and then his eyes moved to the nightstand and the scotch glass and the room's layout, and Paul recognized the expression. He'd seen it on adjusters and attorneys and the kind of people who walked into a situation and immediately started sorting it into categories of liability. Cliff was calculating. Someone was dead in the room and Cliff Haywood was already doing math.

"We need to call," Paul said. He heard the word again — again — and the repetition made it sound less like a plan and more like something a man says to a room that isn't listening.

"He's gone, Deck." Tommy's coaching voice, the one for halftime when a kid was hurt and the injury was real and the ambulance was already on its way and what mattered was keeping everyone else from falling apart. Flat, declarative, controlled. "You can see he's gone. What's the ambulance gonna do?"

"That's not the point. We're supposed to —"

"He's not wrong," Joel said. Joel's voice had the careful, mortared quality it got when the legal architecture engaged, each word placed and weighed before delivery. "We call now, we get an ambulance in thirty minutes that can't help him and a county deputy in forty who takes statements until dawn. We call at seven, we get the same deputy during business hours and the result for Martin is identical." A pause. "The result for Martin is identical either way, Paul."

"Except that a five-hour delay is going to look like something."

"It's going to look like six men who were drinking were woken up at two in the morning and handled it badly. Which is true."

The room was quiet. The refrigerator hummed downstairs in its forty-minute cycle. A loon called from the lake — that sliding, deranged sound that tourists recorded on their phones and locals tuned out, a bird screaming into the dark for no reason a human could use.

Paul looked around the room. At Tommy, who was staring at the nightstand now with an intensity that didn't match the moment. At Joel, whose legal reasoning was sound and whose reasons for wanting it sound were his own. At Cliff, who had stopped calculating and was now looking at the doorway like he was measuring how long it would take to get through it. At Doug, pressed against the hallway wall, arms crossed over the inside-out sweatshirt, saying nothing. At Nate, who was holding his phone with both hands like it was something that had been taken away from him and given back wrong.

Paul wanted to argue. He wanted to pick up the phone and call himself and let the county handle it. But the truth was that Joel was making sense, or making something that sounded enough like sense at 2 AM when you were standing in your dead friend's bedroom with scotch still metabolizing in your blood and your CPR sweat drying on your best friend's chest. The truth was also that Paul did not want to call. Not yet. Not because he didn't want to do the right thing but because he had spent three minutes pressing his hands into Martin's sternum and breathing into Martin's mouth and his saliva was on Martin's lips and his tears were on Martin's sheets and his DNA was on Martin's skin, and some small irrational part of his insurance-trained brain was filing a claim it did not want to file: Paul Decker, forty-seven, present at scene. Physical contact with deceased. Bodily fluids transferred. Close personal relationship, twenty-five years. Only witness to time of discovery: Nate Griggs, who left the room before CPR commenced.

It wasn't rational. He knew it wasn't rational. He was an insurance adjuster, not a suspect, and CPR residue on a dead man's body was evidence of exactly one thing, which was that someone had tried to save a life. He could have written the risk assessment in his sleep. He could have explained it to a jury in four sentences.

But standing in that room, looking at Martin's face — which was settling now into something that was not peaceful or pained but simply vacated, like a house after the tenants have gone and the lights are off but you can still see where the furniture was — standing there, Paul could not stop the calculation. The scene looked wrong. The body was not where it had been when he walked in. One arm was at an angle Martin hadn't put it in. The sheets were twisted. The pillow was half off the bed. CPR did that, of course CPR did that, any responding paramedic would recognize the displacement pattern and note it and move on. But Paul's brain wouldn't let it go. The scene looked like a man whose sleep was interrupted. By someone. By Paul.

"OK," Paul said. "Morning."

The room held its shape for another minute — six men and a dead man in a bedroom at 2 AM, none of them knowing what to do with their hands. Then Doug said, quietly, that he'd make coffee, and the word "coffee" was enough to break whatever was keeping them there, because it was specific and practical and had nothing to do with the body on the bed. The group moved to the kitchen in the useless, shuffling procession of people who have been told there is nothing to do and have not yet accepted it.

Paul stayed. He told them he wanted a minute. They gave it to him.

The minute lasted twenty-five.

What Paul did in Martin's bedroom in those twenty-five minutes was the most irrational thing he had ever done, and he did it with the methodical calm of a man filling out a claims form. He repositioned the body. He moved Martin's arm back across his chest, or close to where it had been — he couldn't remember exactly and the trying to remember was making him sweat in a way that had nothing to do with the room's temperature, a cold sweat that started at his hairline and ran down behind his ears. He smoothed the sheets. He straightened the top sheet across Martin's waist the way he thought Martin had had it, but the truth was that Paul had barely looked at the sheets when he'd started compressions and now he was guessing, and the guessing was worse than not knowing because each adjustment felt like it was adding distance from whatever the original configuration had been.

He put the pillow back. He set the scotch glass upright on the nightstand — it had tipped during compressions, not quite spilling, but it was at an angle that said someone had bumped the nightstand and Paul didn't want the nightstand to say that. He looked at Martin's face, at the moisture around the mouth where Paul's breath and Paul's saliva had been, and he wiped it with the corner of the top sheet. That was the worst thing he did. Worse than the repositioning, worse than the sheets, because wiping a dead man's mouth with a bedsheet was something from a crime show, something a person did when they had done something to the mouth, and Paul had done something to the mouth — he had breathed into it, he had tried to restart the life behind it — but the wiping transformed an act of love into an act of concealment and Paul understood this even as he was doing it and he did it anyway.

Then he wiped down the surfaces he'd touched. The hem of his flannel shirt, wrapped around his index finger, moving across the nightstand top, the bedframe where he'd gripped it for leverage during compressions, the doorknob on both sides. Like something from a network procedural he would have mocked from his couch at home, Anne beside him, a bag of pretzels between them, Paul saying, "Nobody actually does that. Nobody wipes down a scene when they haven't committed a crime."

He was halfway to the hall bathroom to wash his hands before the absurdity of it hit him — he was going to wash off evidence of a thing he didn't do, evidence that proved he'd tried to help, in order to make it look like he hadn't been there, even though five people had watched him walk into the room and stay behind. The logic was broken. The risk assessment, if you could call it that, was the worst he'd ever conducted. But the water was running and his hands were under it and the soap was Martin's soap — Ivory, the bar, the same soap Martin had used at every apartment, every house, because Martin was a man of habits so specific and unchanging that the constancy of his soap had become one of the small fixed facts of Paul's life, and now the soap was making Paul's hands smell like Martin's hands and Martin's hands would never smell like anything again.

He dried his hands on the towel. He turned the bathroom light off. He went downstairs to the kitchen, where Doug had made coffee that was strong enough to strip varnish.

The kitchen was lit by the fluorescent tube over the sink that nobody liked and nobody replaced because it was Martin's kitchen and Martin got to decide what was harsh. Paul sat at the table with both hands around a mug of Doug's punishing coffee and looked through the sliding glass door at the deck.

Tommy was outside, pacing. Even from behind glass, even in the dark, Tommy was unmistakable — the size, the athletic restlessness, the way his hands gripped the back of his own neck and then dropped and then gripped again, a man whose body could not process whatever his brain was running and whose only solution was locomotion. Back and forth across the deck. Back and forth.

Nate was in the great room, visible through the kitchen doorway, sitting on the couch with his phone on his lap and his hands on his knees. Not looking at the phone. Not performing. Just sitting there.

Joel had taken the chair by the dead fireplace and was already asleep, or pretending to be — his legs stretched out, his chin on his chest, his breathing even. Joel's ability to shut down under pressure was either a lawyer's skill or a dissociative gift, and Paul had never been able to tell which.

Cliff was somewhere. Paul could hear him in the hallway for a moment, and then he couldn't.

Doug sat across from Paul with his own mug of terrible coffee, and they looked at each other over the table, and neither of them said anything for a long time. Doug's face was the same face it had been all weekend — hollowed, the bags under his eyes almost purple in the kitchen light, the weight loss that nobody was asking about because the asking would require an answer and the answer might be worse than the not-knowing. Doug looked like a man who hadn't slept well in months, and the not-sleeping had nothing to do with tonight.

"You OK?" Paul asked, because that was the question you asked in kitchens at 3 AM even when the answer was obvious and the asking was performance.

Doug looked at him. "No." A beat. "Are you?"

"No."

"OK."

That was the whole conversation, and Paul would remember it later as one of the most honest exchanges of the weekend — two men admitting the obvious and then sitting with the admission in silence, drinking bad coffee in a dead man's kitchen while the refrigerator hummed and the lake lapped at the shore fifty yards away and a loon called for the second time, that territorial wail, a sound that meant nothing except I am here and this is mine and I am afraid.

The coffee maker gurgled through its cycle. Paul sat there and he listened.

That was when it started.

The first sound was a creak from the stairs. Not the third step — everyone knew about the third step, everyone had been skipping the third step for years, it was one of the house's shared facts, as known and automatic as which burner ran hot on the stove and which bathroom door you had to lift to latch. This was the fourth or fifth step, higher up, and the creak had weight to it — someone heavy, someone who'd remembered the third step and skipped it but whose mass was enough to make the next one protest.

Paul checked. Tommy was still on the deck. Joel was in his chair. Doug was across the table. Nate had been on the couch — Paul leaned back to look through the kitchen doorway, and Nate was still there, hunched forward now, phone in his hands, the screen casting a blue wash across his face.

Someone else was on the stairs. Or the stairs were settling.

Then the upstairs hallway. Footsteps — heavy, deliberate, the stride of a big man trying to walk quietly and failing because big men walk heavily even when they try not to. A pause. Then the sound of Martin's bedroom door, which Paul had left almost closed, swinging open on its hinges. That specific hinge — the top one always gave a faint metallic moan that Martin had said he'd oil and never did.

Someone was in Martin's room.

Paul's first thought was flat and practical: someone's checking on him. Paying respects. Having their own version of the moment Paul had just had. His second thought arrived with the sour taste of Doug's coffee climbing back up his throat: someone's doing what I just did.

He almost got up. Almost went to the stairs and called out. But that would mean admitting he was listening, and listening meant he was alert to the possibility that someone else had a reason to be in that room at 3 AM, and being alert to that possibility meant acknowledging that Paul himself had had a reason, and Paul's reason was nothing, his reason was love and panic and bad risk assessment, and he did not want to establish a precedent of vigilance in this kitchen at this hour.

He sat. He drank his coffee.

Over the next two hours, the house moved. Not the way houses move when they settle — the pine beams adjusting to the temperature drop, the A-frame groaning against the wind off the lake, the sounds you stopped hearing after the first night. The house moved the way a house moves when people are trying to move through it without being heard and are not quite succeeding.

At 3:22 — Paul noted the time because noting times was what his brain did, it was what insurance adjusters' brains did, the filing and cataloging as automatic as breath — at 3:22, a toilet flushed upstairs. Not a casual flush, not the half-pull you give at night when you're trying to be quiet. The handle held down hard, the full-tank flush, the kind of flush you commit to when you need everything to go down on the first try. The plumbing rattled in the walls for a beat afterward, the old copper pipes that Martin was always going to replace.

At 3:48, the study door. Paul knew it was the study because the study had an old-fashioned thumb latch instead of a knob — a wrought-iron piece that Martin's parents had installed in the seventies — and it made a specific metallic snap that was different from every other door in the house. Distinct as a fingerprint. Paul was sitting twelve feet from that latch and he heard it click open and he did not get up because getting up would have meant he'd heard it and hearing it would have meant he cared and caring would have meant there was something in the study worth caring about at 3:48 in the morning.

At 4:10, footsteps overhead again. Lighter this time — not the heavy tread from earlier but someone moving with the particular caution of a person carrying something, their weight redistributed, their steps shortened. Then the soft rattle of the shared bathroom's medicine cabinet, the hinge on that mirror-door that squeaked in a way Martin's mother had probably complained about before Paul was born. Something small and hard met the tile floor — a bottle, a container — and was picked up quickly, the tiny percussion of plastic on ceramic and then the scrabble of fingers retrieving it. The footsteps retreated, lighter than they'd come, moving with the careful rhythm of someone who now had something in their hands they didn't want to drop.

At 4:30 or 4:40, Paul couldn't pin it exactly — his timekeeping had started to fray at the edges, the way precision frays when you've been awake too long and the only clock is the coffee maker's cycle — a phone screen glowed in the hallway outside the kitchen. Not a flashlight, not an overhead light. The specific blue-white of a phone screen, held close to someone's body, the glow moving and pausing and moving again and then going dark. Someone standing in the hallway doing something on a phone that could not wait for daylight.

Paul cataloged all of it. He did not mean to catalog it. His brain did what his brain had done for twenty-three years at Midwestern Casualty: it filed. It cross-referenced. It noted the discrepancies and the patterns and the things that did not match. The heavy footsteps on the stairs were not the light footsteps near the bathroom, which meant at least two different people. The toilet flush was upstairs, consistent with the guest bedroom wing. The study latch was ground floor, twelve feet from the kitchen — and Paul had not seen or heard anyone pass through the kitchen to reach the hallway, which meant they'd come through the great room or down the back stairs from the basement and approached the study from the opposite direction. The phone screen in the hallway could have been anyone, but the glow was low, waist-height, held by someone who was standing still and deliberating, and the deliberation had a quality Paul associated with deletion — the specific attention a person gives to a screen when they are making something go away.

He sat with all of it. He did not discuss it with Doug, who was still across the table, who was on his third cup of terrible coffee, and whose inside-out sweatshirt was now right-side-out, which meant Doug had taken it off and put it back on at some point when Paul was listening to the house and not watching Doug, and Paul thought about that and then set the thought aside in the same mental drawer where the rest of the night's observations were accumulating.

By 5 AM the house was quiet. Or the version of quiet a house achieves when you've been listening so hard that the absence of human sound feels like its own kind of noise. The refrigerator cycled. The pines moved outside the kitchen window. The lake lapped at something — the dock, the rocks, the shore.

The first gray light came through the window and Paul realized he had been sitting in the same chair for two hours, both hands on a mug of cold coffee, running a claims assessment on a house full of his oldest friends.

He stood up. His knees cracked. His lower back had locked into the shape of the chair and protested the new configuration. He walked down the hallway.

The study door was ajar. He had walked past it on his way to the kitchen hours ago and it had been closed. Now it was open two inches, and through the gap Paul could see Martin's desk and the landscape print on the wall — the one with the lake and the pines, the one Martin's parents had bought at a craft fair in Duluth in 1983 — and the print was slightly crooked. Tilted a quarter inch to the right, as if someone had bumped the wall behind it or moved it and tried to put it back and misjudged the angle. Paul knew the angle because he'd sat in Martin's study dozens of times over twenty-five years, talking about nothing, and the print had always hung straight.

He continued to Martin's room. The door was as he'd left it. He pushed it open.

Martin was on the bed, in the position Paul had arranged. The arm across the chest, the sheets smooth, the pillow centered. Paul's work. Paul's theater. But the nightstand was different. The scotch glass was there, the lamp was there, but something was absent, and Paul stood in the doorway staring at the nightstand the way you stare at one of those spot-the-difference puzzles, knowing something has changed but unable to isolate it until the absence resolves itself into a shape.

The pill case was there — Martin's small brown leather supplement case, fish oil, B-12, the vitamins Martin took every morning with the earnest optimism of a man who believed he could drink like a twenty-two-year-old and compensate with capsules. But next to where the pill case sat, there was a gap. A circle of dust-free nightstand where something had been and was not. Paul had seen it during CPR without registering it — a small orange prescription bottle, the standard pharmacy kind with the white child-safe cap, sitting next to the pill case. He hadn't read the label. He'd been busy trying to restart a heart.

The bottle was gone. Someone had taken it.

He backed out of the room and went to the shared bathroom at the other end of the hall. The medicine cabinet was open — not swung wide, just unlatched, the mirror-door floating a quarter inch forward like a drawer that hadn't been pushed fully home. Paul didn't open it further, but he could see the shelves. His own toiletries kit on the second shelf, a tube of toothpaste, Nate's brand of mouthwash that no one else would use. The third shelf, where there had been — Paul was almost certain — other things. More things. He couldn't itemize what was missing because he hadn't inventoried what was there, but the shelf had a cleaned-out quality, a sparseness that felt deliberate.

He went back to the kitchen. Doug was still at the table, in the same position, as though he had not moved since Paul sat down. But his mug was empty and the coffee pot was lower and the sweatshirt was different and the silence was different, and Paul looked at Doug and Doug looked at Paul and neither of them said any of the things that the looking contained.

Tommy was no longer on the deck. Paul could hear the shower running upstairs — the pipes shuddering the way they shuddered when you turned the hot water past halfway, a sound so specific to this house that it had become part of the weekend's ambient texture, as regular as the loons and the refrigerator. Tommy was showering at 5:15 in the morning.

Joel was still in the chair by the fireplace, chin on chest, eyes closed. His breathing was too regular for real sleep — Joel was playing a role, or Joel had genuinely found a way to turn off, and either possibility was equally Joel.

Cliff was in the great room now, sitting where Nate had been, scrolling through his phone with an expression of focused calm that Paul recognized from twenty years of watching Cliff work rooms and deals and dinners: the face Cliff put on when the numbers were bad and the other party hadn't figured it out yet. Cliff looked up when Paul entered the kitchen and gave him a nod that contained nothing — no grief, no solidarity, no meaning that Paul could read. A nod that was a closed door.

Nate was gone from the couch. Paul looked around. Not on the deck, not in the hallway, not in the kitchen. Gone somewhere. Doing something. At 5 in the morning.

Paul poured himself another cup of Doug's devastating coffee and stood at the kitchen window. The lake was visible through the pines, iron-gray in the predawn light, flat and still except for the small lapping at the dock that was the lake's constant, the sound that you stopped hearing after the first hour of every visit and that Paul was hearing now as if for the first time. The dock reaching out into the water. The Adirondack chairs on the deck, wet with dew. The propane grill where Martin had made burgers eighteen hours ago, scraping the grates with the perfectionist's attention he gave to every physical task, scolding Tommy for leaving grease on the drip tray, and Tommy had called him a psychopath and Martin had said "I'm a psychopath with clean grill grates" and everyone had laughed and the burgers were perfect. Eighteen hours ago.

"Deck."

Tommy was in the kitchen doorway. Showered, dressed in jeans and a polo shirt that was wrong for Tommy — too neat, too deliberate, the outfit of a man who had made decisions about his appearance at 5 AM that were not about appearance. His face was scrubbed and raw. His hands were in his pockets, and Tommy's hands were never in his pockets. Tommy's hands were always doing something — holding a beer, gripping a shoulder, gesturing at a point he was driving home. Hands in pockets was a man containing his hands.

"We should call," Tommy said. "Should've called hours ago."

"Yeah."

"I'll do it. I'll call from the deck, there's better signal."

"OK."

Tommy stood there another beat. He looked at Paul with an expression that Paul would spend weeks trying to classify — guilt, or something that wore guilt's face but moved differently behind the eyes, something that used guilt as a shelter for whatever the actual feeling was. Then Tommy went out the sliding door and Paul heard his voice on the deck, his coaching voice, reporting the death of Martin Kessler to the Becker County 911 dispatcher with the clipped factual cadence of a man who had called in injuries before: name, location, situation, time discovered. No emotion in the delivery. All the emotion in the hands that Paul could see through the glass, gripping the deck railing until the knuckles went white.

Paul looked around the kitchen. The fluorescent tube buzzed. The refrigerator cycled on. The morning was arriving whether any of them were ready for it, the light coming through the pines in thin gold lines that hit the lake and turned the gray to something brighter that had no right to be beautiful and was.

He thought about what he'd heard in the night. The footsteps, the flush, the latch, the cabinet, the phone. At least four separate events, produced by at least three different people — probably more, probably four or five, because the weights were different and the locations were scattered and the timing was spread across two hours in a pattern that was too irregular for one person and too purposeful for coincidence.

Each of them had done something. That was what the night added up to. Each of them had gotten out of bed and moved through the house and touched or taken or adjusted or deleted something, and none of them had coordinated with any of the others, and the result — Paul could feel this in his insurance brain, the part that understood how small individual decisions compound into systemic exposure — the result was that the scene upstairs no longer matched the scene Paul had found at 2 AM. A prescription bottle was gone. The study had been entered. Something had been flushed. Something had been done on a phone. And Paul himself had repositioned the body, wiped the surfaces, and cleaned Martin's mouth.

Five separate adjustments to the same death. Five independent decisions, each one probably as irrational as Paul's, each one driven by some private panic that had nothing to do with murder and everything to do with whatever each man was hiding from the others and from himself. And the composite — the sum of all their small, scared, separate acts — looked nothing like a man who died in his sleep. It looked like something designed. Something coordinated. A committee's work.

Except the committee had never met.

Paul Decker, insurance claims adjuster, twenty-three years with Midwestern Casualty, stood in the kitchen of a house on Elbow Lake and assessed the total exposure. That was the phrase his brain produced, automatic and grotesque: total exposure. As if Martin's death were a claim. As if the liability could be quantified and the deductible applied and the payout calculated and everyone could go home with a check and a sense of resolution. He could write the report. He could see the form. He could fill in every field except the one that mattered, the one at the top that asked for the determination, and the determination was: loss. Total loss. Not covered under any policy that existed or would ever exist. Not adjustable. Not fixable. Not a thing that would resolve into a number and a signature and a file closed and stored.

His best friend was dead. His best friend's house had been rearranged in the night by the men who loved him. And when the county came — the deputy, the detective, whoever showed up — they would walk into a scene that looked, to anyone who hadn't lived through it, like something planned. Something calculated. Something that a group of men had agreed to do.

The sun cleared the tree line. The lake turned from gray to gold. Paul put his cold coffee in the sink and rinsed the mug and set it on the drying rack the way Martin would have wanted, because Martin was particular about his drying rack, and that was the thought that finally broke Paul — not the death, not the CPR, not the night of sounds, but the drying rack, the stupid wooden drying rack that Martin had bought at a kitchen supply store in Fargo and been unreasonably proud of, and Paul stood at the sink with water running over his hands and cried without making a sound while the sun hit the lake and Tommy's voice carried through the glass door, talking to the dispatcher, giving directions to a house that none of them would ever come back to the same way again.
