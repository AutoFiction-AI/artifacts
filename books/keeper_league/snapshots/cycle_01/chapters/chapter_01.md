# Chapter 1

Nate Griggs heard the house before he saw it. The driveway was a quarter-mile of gravel through pines, and somewhere up ahead Tommy Renaldi was laughing — that big, open-throated laugh that carried across practice fields and parking lots and apparently through woods in rural Minnesota, because Nate could hear it over his engine and the podcast ad for a mattress company he hadn't bothered to pause.

He parked behind Cliff's Audi. The Audi was new, or new-looking — Cliff's cars were always new-looking even when they weren't. Nate grabbed his duffel, his mic bag, and the thirty-rack of Hamm's that was his annual contribution to the weekend. Martin stocked the house with Lagavulin and craft beer, but Nate brought the Hamm's because he'd brought the Hamm's since 2001, and some bits were sacred even when the beer wasn't.

The lake house was an A-frame on Elbow Lake, pine-paneled, with a deck wrapping around the lake side. Martin's parents had bought it in the eighties, and Martin got it in the divorce five years back — Linda never contested the house because she'd always hated the drive from the Cities. Twenty-five Septembers they'd been coming here. The same nail held the draft board on the wall, the same third stair creaked, the same smell of wood smoke and lake damp lived in the pine. The league was the excuse. The house was the thing.

Nate let himself in and walked into noise. Tommy was in the great room with a beer in one hand and Doug Whitfield's shoulder in the other, telling a story about a cornerback who'd transferred to his school. Paul Decker was at the kitchen table with a scotch, overnight bag squared against the wall like it was awaiting inspection. Martin was at the counter unwrapping deli paper from a stack of sandwiches he'd driven to Dent to get, because Martin always drove to Dent, because Martin could not allow a person to be hungry in his house.

"Griggsy," Martin said, pointing with a mustard-smeared knife. "Thirty-rack goes in the fridge, your room's the same, towels are on the bed."

"I didn't even get to put my bag down."

"Towels on the bed, room's the same, thirty-rack in the fridge. Different order, same information."

Nate dropped the Hamm's on the counter. Martin was already rearranging the fridge to accommodate it, moving bottles with the focus of a man who believed refrigerator organization was a moral issue. He was six-one, stocky, with a ruddy face and a receding hairline he'd stopped fighting about three years back. He wore a flannel that cost a hundred and fifty dollars and looked like it cost twelve, which was Martin's whole approach to possessions — expensive things made to seem careless, because caring visibly about what you owned was a vulnerability Martin didn't permit himself. Big hands. Gap between his front teeth when he smiled, which he did now, reaching for the Hamm's.

"Tommy," Nate said, nodding toward the great room. "What inning are we in?"

"The cornerback. The one who can't catch." Paul sipped his scotch. "I think we're approaching the father who calls the superintendent."

"Classic."

Tommy hadn't noticed Nate yet, or had noticed and didn't care. The shoulder he was gripping belonged to Doug, who was standing in the kitchen doorway with the patience of someone who'd been caught in Tommy's gravity and knew the only exit was to wait it out. Doug had lost weight. More than the usual. His face was thinner, the hoodie hung wrong on him, and the bags under his eyes looked permanent. But he was leaning into the room rather than away from it — tilted toward the noise, smiling at Tommy's story with his whole face, the posture of a guy who'd driven three hours specifically to be near people.

Nate caught his eye and raised a hand. Doug waved back.

"You need anything?" Doug asked.

"A beer and a time machine."

"I can do one of those."

"He needs both," Tommy said, finally breaking off. He clapped Nate on the back hard enough to move him. "Griggs. Holy shit, that hat. You're still wearing that hat?"

The podcast-branded cap. GRIGGS ON THE GRID in white block letters. His listeners had collectively paid for a hundred of them, which meant he'd sold twenty-three.

"Content is content."

"It's a garbage hat is what it is. You look like you're sponsoring yourself." Tommy grinned — six-two and still built like something functional, crew cut graying at the temples, hands that were always gripping something. A beer, a shoulder, the back of his own neck. "I'm kidding. Kind of. Not really. It's a bad hat."

Cliff Haywood came downstairs in pressed chinos and a collared polo, like he'd confused the lake house with a client dinner. His handshake was firm enough that Nate felt it in his elbow. He had silver hair and a permanent tan and the kind of posture that made you aware of your own.

"Nate. Good drive up?"

"Cliff. Are those new pants?"

"These are pants."

"Those are pants the way your car out there is just-a-car."

Cliff smiled — the automatic one, the sales-floor one, the one that activated before his face decided anything — and moved to the counter. "Kess, tell me you went to Lindemann's. Tell me you got the turkey club."

"Two turkey clubs. You ate one and a half last year."

"I ate one. Paul had the other half."

"To be fair," Paul said, "that's accurate."

"You had one and a half." Martin tapped his temple. "I remember everything that happens in this kitchen."

Joel Beckman was the last one Nate registered, and only because Joel wasn't doing anything registrable. He was leaning against the wall near the hallway, arms crossed, beer untouched in his hand. He looked the same — lean, wiry, sharp features, those quick eyes that had made him the best poker player in the group for a decade — but something was wrong. His jaw was set. He smiled when someone looked at him, but the smile came late by half a second, like it was being translated from a language Joel wasn't currently speaking.

Nate couldn't place it. Joel was always a little wound up — the public defender in him always running cross-examination in the background — but this was different. This was a man who'd already had a fight nobody else had been invited to.

"Beck," Nate said. "You good?"

"Fine." Joel pushed off the wall. "You want a beer?"

He was at the fridge before Nate could answer, and the question was dead.

Martin caught Nate's eye across the kitchen. A small shrug. Don't push it.

The house filled up the way it always did over the next hour. Duffel bags dropped in rooms, the third stair creaked under everyone who went up, Martin circulated making sure drinks were topped off and food stayed accessible. Nate clipped his podcast mic to his phone and propped it against the chip bowl on the kitchen counter. He wasn't recording yet — just testing levels. He'd grab audio during the draft later: the arguments, the trash talk, the ambient sound of seven guys treating a fantasy football league like it mattered more than their mortgages. His listeners expected the draft-night episode. All hundred and twelve of them.

"You bringing that thing again?" Tommy said, eyeing the mic.

"Content is content."

"Last year you put my voice on there and some shithead kid in Ohio said my zone defense was garbage."

"He wasn't wrong."

"He was twelve, Griggs."

"Wise beyond his years."

Tommy threw a chip at him, and the evening had started.

---

The draft board went up at six. Martin hung it on the same nail in the great room wall he'd been using since 2004, when the nail had replaced the thumbtack that had replaced the masking tape that had replaced the year they just wrote picks on the back of a pizza box. The board was laminated whiteboard now, with columns for each team and rows for rounds. Martin had pre-printed the player rankings on a separate sheet and taped it beside the board. He'd brought color-coded markers.

He brought color-coded markers every year since the incident of 2016, when Tommy and Cliff had both used blue and the third round became unreadable and the argument about it lasted, intermittently, until April.

"Red's mine," Martin said, uncapping a marker and writing KESSLER at the top of the first column. "Pick order's in the bowl."

He set a ceramic bowl on the table with seven folded slips of paper inside. Martin had cut the paper, written the numbers, folded them identically, because Martin folded things identically. The draft order was random, but the randomness was Martin's — he produced the system, ran it, and never submitted it to review. Nobody had ever accused him of rigging it. Nobody had ever verified it either.

Tommy drew first pick and bellowed. Cliff drew second and said nothing, which meant he was doing math. Doug drew third and said, "Sure, that works." Paul drew fourth and nodded. Nate drew fifth and made a sound that Tommy later described on the podcast clip as "a walrus being told bad news." Joel drew sixth without comment. Seventh was Martin's by default — the last slot, the host's slot, the one that looked like sacrifice — and nobody pointed out that the man who controlled the paper and the bowl had landed there again.

Nate hit record on his phone.

"All right," Martin said. "Tommy's on the clock."

"Don't need to look." Tommy grabbed his green marker and wrote LAMAR JACKSON on the board. "Done. Let's go."

"You're keeping Lamar again," Joel said.

"Damn right I'm keeping Lamar."

"Four years in a row, Tommy."

"Because he's a goddamn cheat code. You just said he's great."

"I said he's great. I'm saying at a certain point you're paying a premium for loyalty over value."

"What premium? He's a top-five quarterback. What the fuck premium are you talking about?"

"Moving on," Martin said. "Cliff."

Cliff studied the ranking sheet for ten seconds. He uncapped his black marker, then turned to Paul. "Quick question. What are you thinking at four?"

"I'm thinking about my pick at four."

"Right, but directionally. Running back? Tight end? Because if you're going running back, I don't need to reach at two, and we can—"

"Cliff. I'm not telling you my strategy."

"I'm not asking your strategy. I'm asking your direction. Those are different things."

"The difference is the same information in a nicer package," Joel said.

"Here's what I'm proposing," Cliff said, ignoring Joel entirely. "An understanding. Not a trade. An understanding where, if you're going running back at four, I take the tight end at two, the running backs fall to you, everybody wins. It's an arrangement that benefits both—"

"A trade," said five people.

Cliff held up his hands and wrote CeeDee Lamb on the board.

The draft went the way it always went: slowly, then all at once, then Tommy calling someone an idiot. By the fourth round the table was louder than the house could hold. Paul was making a measured case for drafting a defense early — citing bye weeks, strength of schedule, the kind of structural argument that made his insurance-adjuster brain happy — and Tommy was telling him that it was the most chickenshit strategy he'd heard in twenty-five years of a league that had seen some profoundly chickenshit strategies, and Cliff was quietly negotiating a sixth-round swap with Doug that Doug clearly didn't follow but had agreed to because Cliff asked nicely and Doug agreed to things.

"You're getting fleeced," Joel told Doug.

"Probably," Doug said.

"You understand what you just gave up?"

"Not entirely."

"Then why'd you agree to it?"

Doug shrugged. "He was very persuasive."

Cliff pointed at Doug. "See? A reasonable man. This is what a reasonable man looks like."

"A reasonable man who doesn't know what he agreed to," Joel said. But he was almost smiling — the closest to relaxed he'd been since arriving.

Nate was narrating pieces of the draft in his broadcaster voice, the one that made Tommy groan and Martin shake his head and that Nate couldn't turn off because the bit was how he processed the evening. But under the bit was the real observation: these guys, this table, this board, this lake going copper through the window in the late September light. Twenty-five years of exactly this. He'd keep recording. At least one of them would say something usable.

Martin overrode a disputed pick in the seventh round. Joel had selected a running back Tommy claimed was injured, and the table split — Tommy pulling up stats on his phone, Joel citing the player's own Instagram post from that morning, Cliff proposing a compromise that was transparently another trade, Paul asking whether the league had ever actually established an injury-report protocol, and Doug saying he thought the guy played last week but wasn't sure.

Martin listened to all of it. Then he said, "The pick stands."

No vote. No debate. No appeal. Joel's pick stood because Martin said it did, and nobody pushed back because Martin's authority in this league was the kind that came from twenty-five years of driving to Dent for sandwiches and hanging the board on the nail and bringing the markers and opening his house every September and never once asking anyone to acknowledge any of it. You couldn't argue with that kind of authority. The argument would require naming the debt, and the debt was too large and too old and too deeply woven into the weekend to bear naming.

"Thank you," Joel said. Flat.

"Don't thank me. I just read the rules."

"You're the rules," Tommy said.

"Somebody's gotta be." Martin reached across the table and topped off Tommy's beer from the pitcher without being asked.

The draft ended at nine-thirty. Martin's team was, by consensus, terrible. Seventh pick every round, the scraps nobody else wanted. He circled his picks on the board in red marker and shrugged.

"Worked out last year."

"Last year you finished fourth," Cliff said.

"Top half."

"Fourth isn't top half. Fourth out of seven is exactly the middle."

"Middle's the new top half. Nate, tell your listeners."

"I'm sure all twelve of them agree," Nate said, and Paul actually laughed, which was generous.

The evening opened up after the draft. The board stayed on the wall — Martin's red circles already looking like the saddest column — and the talk shifted from picks to the upcoming season, from the season to coaching, from coaching to Tommy's cornerback story again, and from the cornerback to the kind of stories that twenty-five years of friendship accumulated like lake silt: the wedding where the DJ played "Dust in the Wind" during the first dance and nobody stopped him. The Super Bowl party where Doug fell asleep on the couch and nobody noticed until the fourth quarter. The year Cliff brought Megan to the draft and the group became so aggressively polite that Martin pulled Cliff aside and said he'd start talking about bowel health if she came back.

Tommy pulled a small ziplock from his jacket pocket and tossed it on the table in front of Martin. Four blue pills rattled inside the plastic.

"Happy birthday, old man."

"My birthday was in June."

"Happy belated. Happy early. Happy whatever you want. Open it."

Martin held the bag up. "This is Viagra."

"It's a wellness product."

"It's four Viagra, Tommy."

"One for each night. Plus a spare, in case you meet a nice lady at the Dent gas station."

"I'm not meeting a lady at the gas station."

"You don't know that. I'm saying be prepared. Denise's friend's husband takes 'em. Says they changed his goddamn life."

"His life needed changing. Mine doesn't."

"You're forty-seven and you've been single for five years, Kess. Take the pills. Stick 'em in your medicine cabinet. Use 'em, don't use 'em, I don't give a shit. It's a gag gift."

"Some gag."

"Next year I'll get you a prostate exam. At least this year's gift is fun."

The table was laughing — Paul with his hand over his mouth, Cliff tilting back in his chair, Nate already building the podcast bit in his head, Doug grinning from his corner of the table. Joel had walked out to the deck five minutes earlier and hadn't come back.

Martin scooped the pills back into the bag and put it in his shirt pocket. "I'm keeping these as evidence."

"Evidence of what?"

"Evidence that you're an asshole."

"That's never been in dispute, buddy."

Martin was already pouring the next round of Lagavulin — the good stuff, the bottle he replaced every year because it never survived the weekend. The hot tub was humming on the deck. Nate could hear its pump through the glass door like a low mechanical purr under everything else.

He and Doug went out to smoke. Doug had started again since the divorce, and Nate smoked when Doug smoked, which was the closest either of them got to a buddy system.

"Steph still not talking to you?" Nate asked.

"She texts about the furniture. That counts, right?"

"Barely."

"Yeah." Doug exhaled into the dark. The lake was flat and black beyond the deck railing, and a loon called from somewhere past the dock. "I'm glad I came up."

"You say that every year."

"I mean it every year." He looked at Nate with an expression that was too open for the joke Nate had been building toward. "I needed this one. You know?"

Nate let the joke go. "Yeah, man. I know."

They stood there for a minute, smoking, not saying anything, listening to Tommy's voice through the window arguing about something that didn't matter with someone who didn't care.

Martin had gone to the far end of the deck — Nate could see him through the glass door, standing at the railing with Tommy, the two of them in what looked like a private conversation. Martin's hand rested on Tommy's shoulder for a moment. Tommy nodded at something. It lasted thirty seconds, maybe less. Martin came back inside first. Tommy followed a beat later, running the back of his hand across his face in a way that could've been the lake air or could've been something else entirely. Nobody mentioned it.

In the kitchen, Cliff had cornered Paul. Not about the draft. "I've been trying to get Martin to close out our position on the Burnsville thing for two years. He keeps deferring."

Paul sipped his scotch and offered nothing.

"I'm sure he's good for it. I'm just saying it'd be nice to see some movement. He's generous as hell when it's his terms — the sandwiches, the house, all of this." Cliff gestured at the room. "Try to collect on the other end and the guy's a wall."

"Cliff." Paul set his glass down. "It's draft night."

"I know it's draft night."

"Then let it be draft night."

"Sure. Yeah, absolutely." Cliff looked at his drink, then back at Paul, and produced the same automatic smile from the afternoon. "Great night."

The evening got late. Nate lost track of the time around eleven, three scotches past good judgment and one past the ability to notice. He was in the basement setting up his cot when he heard raised voices through the floor. One was Martin's. The other was Joel's. They weren't the kind of raised voices that came from arguing about fantasy football picks. They came from somewhere older and meaner and the tone cut through thirty years of un-insulated floorboards without any trouble at all.

He went upstairs. Tommy was in the hallway, leaning against the wall outside Martin's study.

"What's going on?"

"Don't know. Don't want to know."

"Is that Joel?"

"I said I don't want to know, Griggs."

The voices were coming from behind the study door. Nate couldn't make out words — just tone. Martin's was low and controlled. Joel's was climbing toward a register Nate had never heard from him in twenty-five years.

Paul appeared at the top of the stairs. "What the hell is that?"

"Joel and Martin," Tommy said. "Been going at it for ten minutes."

The study door opened. Joel came out like he'd been launched — face white, jaw locked, eyes registering the three men in the hallway without any change in expression whatsoever.

"What was that about?" Paul asked.

"Nothing."

"Joel. That wasn't—"

"I said it's handled." Joel went straight for the front door and out onto the porch. The screen door slapped shut behind him.

Paul looked into the study. The door was still open. Martin was standing behind his desk with both hands flat on the surface, breathing through his nose.

"Martin?"

"Go to bed, Paul."

"You need anything?"

"I need you to go to bed."

Paul went. Tommy went. Nate went back down to the basement. The house settled into the specific quiet of men who had decided to stop talking to each other. The refrigerator cycled on somewhere above him. A loon called from the lake.

Nate lay on his cot and stared at the ceiling and thought about the Viagra bit and whether he'd caught Martin saying "evidence that you're an asshole" on mic and whether Joel was okay and whether it was any of his business.

He fell asleep with the recorder still running on his phone, the mic propped against the chip bowl in the kitchen three floors up, picking up nothing but the refrigerator and the click of the front door opening and closing at twelve-fifteen.

---

Friday was the lake day. They took Martin's pontoon out, fished badly, drank adequately, came back sunburned and quiet the way a full day of water and sun makes people quiet. Joel and Martin didn't speak to each other — or didn't speak where anyone else could hear — and nobody tested the silence. Doug sat in the back of the boat and trailed his hand in the water and looked more at peace than Nate had seen him in a year.

The evening was supposed to be the easy one. The draft was done, the league had structure, and the drinking could be pointless instead of competitive. Martin fired up the hot tub at eight. By nine-thirty all seven of them were in it, which was two bodies past the tub's comfortable capacity and exactly the right number for the kind of conversation that only happens when middle-aged men are mostly submerged and can't pretend to have dignity.

"I'm touching someone's foot," Cliff said.

"That's my foot," Doug said.

"Your foot is on my thigh."

"There isn't room."

"Kess, you said this seats six."

"It seats six people who aren't Tommy."

"I'm a large man," Tommy said.

"You're a large man in a medium tub, and you're displacing everybody else's personal space."

"You want me to get out?"

"Don't get out," Martin said. "You get out and the water level shifts and it gets worse."

Tommy shifted anyway. Water slopped over the edge onto the deck. Nate, wedged between Paul and the jet controls, felt something give in his lower back and decided to address it never.

"This is disgusting," Joel said. He was perched on the rim with only his calves in the water. "This is seven guys making soup."

"Don't say soup," Paul said.

"Man soup. Guy broth. Whatever this is."

"It's a bonding experience," Tommy said.

"It's a health violation."

"Twenty-five years and he's still a snob about the tub," Tommy announced to no one in particular. "Twenty-five goddamn years."

"I'm not a snob. I'm hygienic."

"You're dry. Your feet are wet and you're calling that participation. That's bullshit, Beck. Get in the soup."

Joel lowered himself in with the deliberation of a man who did not want to be there and had decided to be there anyway. Water rose. Cliff swore. Doug pulled his legs in. Martin, at the far end with his arms spread along the rim, watched the whole operation with the quiet satisfaction of a host whose party was proceeding on schedule.

"Now we're in business," Tommy said.

"Now we're in a petri dish," Joel said, but he settled in.

They told stories. The same ones, mostly, refined by twenty-five annual retellings into something between history and myth. The year Paul got food poisoning and spent the entire draft in the bathroom, calling his picks through the closed door. The year Nate accidentally included thirty seconds of Tommy screaming about a referee in a podcast episode and Tommy's AD called him on Monday morning. The year someone — nobody could agree who — pissed off the end of the dock at three in the morning and the neighbor across the lake turned on a spotlight.

"That was Cliff," Tommy said.

"That was absolutely not me."

"It was Cliff. I saw the whole thing. You were standing on the dock with your dick out talking to the moon."

"I wasn't on the dock. I was asleep."

"You were having a full conversation with the moon about interest rates. I remember because it was the most Cliff Haywood thing I've ever witnessed in my life."

"None of that happened."

"Some of it happened," Doug said quietly, and the tub erupted.

Doug almost never contributed stories. But when the laughter hit, his whole body went into it — shoulders shaking, sinking deeper into the water, eyes squeezed shut. He was sunburned across the nose, hair plastered to his forehead, and he looked for the first time that weekend like someone who had no problems at all.

"You know what I love about this?" Doug said.

"The foot contact," Cliff said.

"Shut up. I love that this hasn't changed. Twenty-five years and this part is still the same." His sentence faltered. He moved his hand across the surface of the water, a gesture that tried to hold something the words couldn't. "It's good. This is good."

Nobody argued with that.

They stayed in the tub until the sky was full dark and the water had started to cool and moths were throwing themselves against the porch light. Martin climbed out first, wrapped a towel around his waist, and went inside. The loons were going. Somewhere across the lake a motorboat was coming in late, its running light a white pinprick moving slow against the trees.

"I'm done," Martin called from the doorway half an hour later. He was in sweats and a thermal, last scotch of the night in hand. "Lock up when you're done. Not you, Beckman — you don't know where anything locks."

"I know where the door is."

"That's a start. Good night."

He went upstairs. The third step creaked under him like it always did.

The rest of them stayed up another hour. Tommy and Cliff had a conversation on the deck that Nate caught the edge of — Cliff leaning in with a beer, that particular pitch in his voice that meant he was making an ask, Tommy shaking his head and going inside. Joel sat in an Adirondack chair staring at the lake, his beer going warm in his hand. Nate thought about asking him what the fight with Martin had been about and decided against it. Whatever it was lived between Joel's silence and Martin's closed study door, and it wasn't Nate's.

Paul was the last one to head up. He stood in the kitchen rinsing glasses, the way he always did — the clean-up man, the one who made sure Martin's house looked the same in the morning as it had in the evening.

"You good down there?" he asked Nate.

"Fine. Gonna stay up a bit."

"Don't record me sleeping."

"No promises."

Paul went upstairs. The house emptied out.

Nate went down to the basement, brushed his teeth, and lay on his cot. He couldn't sleep. The day had been too long, or the scotch was too much, or his brain was doing what it always did after a full day of performing — running the highlight reel, cutting the bits, deciding what was content and what stayed in the room. The Viagra was a cold open. The hot tub could be a full segment. Martin's "evidence that you're an asshole" was a closer, if Nate had caught it on mic, which he probably hadn't.

He checked his phone. One-thirty. He should sleep.

He didn't sleep.

At quarter to two he decided he wanted one more thing from the weekend. The bet. He and Martin had been making the same wager since the second season of the league: five bucks on who'd finish last. It was a nothing bet, a ceremony. They shook on it every year in Martin's kitchen at some ungodly hour while the house slept.

Nate went upstairs. Basement stairs, main stairs, the third step that creaked under his weight. The upper hallway was dark. Martin's door was at the end. Nate knocked twice, soft.

Nothing.

He knocked again. "Kess. You up?"

The door wasn't latched. It swung inward a half-inch from the knock. Nate pushed it open.

The room was dark. Martin was in bed on his back, and the room was wrong.

Nate couldn't have said what told him — not immediately, not in words he could've put on a podcast or in a conversation or anywhere else. It was the hand. Martin's left hand was lying on top of the blanket, palm up, fingers loosely curled but not gripping anything, not twitching, not doing any of the small involuntary things a sleeping hand does. The mouth was open. The chest was still.

Nate stepped closer. The scotch smell hit first — scotch and sweat and something underneath both that he couldn't name but that his body understood before his brain caught up, because his hands went cold and his legs stopped wanting to hold him.

"Martin."

He touched Martin's arm. It was warm. Warm and absolutely still.

"Martin. Hey. Martin."

Nate stood there. He didn't know for how long. The Lagavulin bottle was on the nightstand with a finger left in it, and the glass beside it had a dried ring where the scotch had evaporated, and the pine smell of the room was the same smell it had always had for twenty-five years, and the loons were going on the lake, and the refrigerator was humming somewhere below him, and Martin's hand was open and his chest was still and Nate was not narrating this. He was not building the bit. He was not doing any of the things he always did when the world handed him something he didn't know how to hold. He was standing in a dark room next to his friend and his friend was not breathing and the room was wrong.

He sat on the edge of the bed. He picked up Martin's hand and held it for a moment. It was heavy in a way a living hand is not. He set it back on the blanket.

He got up. He left the room. He walked down the hall to Paul's door and knocked until it opened. Paul stood there in a T-shirt and boxers, blinking, and Nate said, "Martin," and that was all he could get out — the name, everything the name meant — and Paul's face changed, and they went back down the hallway together.

The weekend was over.
