# Chapter 8

The house was worth four hundred thousand, maybe four-fifty in this market. Cliff had been appraising it since Thursday, the way he appraised everything — automatically, compulsively, the numbers ticking behind his eyes like a meter he couldn't shut off. Cedar siding, original but maintained. Wraparound deck, probably eight years old, the boards starting to silver where the stain had worn through. Stone fireplace, real stone, not the manufactured veneer he'd been selling clients for the past three years because real stone cost four times as much and most people couldn't tell the difference. Vaulted A-frame ceiling that photographed well and was a nightmare to heat. Lake frontage, eighty feet, mature pines and birch, dock in decent shape. He'd built his career on knowing what things were worth before anyone told him, and Martin Kessler's lake house on Elbow Lake was worth four hundred to four-fifty, which was not what Cliff needed it to be worth, because what Cliff needed was not a house.

What Cliff needed was forty thousand dollars.

The number had been living in his chest since March of last year, a low-grade infection that flared whenever he opened his laptop or checked his bank balance or drove past the duplex on Dale Street that had eaten his capital and his partnership with Martin and most of his ability to sleep through the night. Forty thousand dollars. Not forty thousand in the abstract, the way people said "forty thousand" when they meant "a lot of money" — forty thousand specific dollars that Martin Kessler had owed him for twenty-three months from a joint real estate deal on a duplex in Roseville that had gone sideways in the particular way that only real estate went sideways: slowly, expensively, and with both partners telling different stories about whose idea the purchase had been.

Martin had brought the property lead. Cliff had put up the capital. The property had needed a new foundation, a new roof, and remediation for asbestos that the inspection had somehow missed — the inspector being a guy Martin knew, a guy Martin had used before, a guy who had apparently inspected the duplex the way Martin inspected the refrigerator in this lake house, which was to say he'd looked at it, decided it was fine, and walked away. The duplex was gone. Sold at a loss that split unevenly because the capital had been Cliff's. Martin's half of that loss was forty thousand dollars, and Martin had been making sounds about repayment for twenty-three months — the right sounds, the "I know, I know, I'm working on it" sounds, the sounds that Cliff recognized from a career of dealing with people who owed more than they could pay and had learned to substitute conversation for checks.

Now Martin was dead, and dead men didn't write checks, but dead men had estates, and estates had obligations, and somewhere in this house there was documentation that would make Cliff's forty thousand an obligation rather than a memory.

Sunday morning. The kitchen smelled like bad coffee and the lake. Cliff stood at the counter with a mug he wasn't drinking from, watching the others move through their versions of a morning routine in a house where the host was dead. Paul was at the kitchen table in the wire-rimmed glasses that made him look like a disappointed accountant, thumbing through something on his phone with the particular concentration of a man doing work to avoid doing grief. Insurance claims, probably. Paul adjusted claims the way other people bit their nails — compulsively, under stress, as if the act of evaluating other people's losses could insulate him from his own. He'd been wearing the same button-down since Saturday and hadn't noticed.

Joel was on the deck in one of the Adirondack chairs, facing the lake with the focused blankness of a man who'd been told not to leave the property and was trying to decide if that was a request or an order. He had a coffee too. He hadn't touched it. The steam had stopped rising ten minutes ago and Joel hadn't noticed that either.

Doug was somewhere. The basement, probably. Doug had retreated to the basement the way a cat retreats under a bed — not because the basement was comfortable but because it was the place in the house where the fewest people would look for him. Cliff had noticed Doug's weight loss on Thursday, the way the hoodies hung on him now, the jawline that used to be soft and was now sharp in a way that looked medical, not disciplined. He hadn't said anything. It wasn't his business, and Cliff had learned a long time ago that commenting on another man's body was a conversation that went nowhere good.

Nate was near the front door, pacing the three steps between the coat hooks and the doormat that got the best cell signal, talking to someone with the desperate energy of a man who processed reality by narrating it. "No, I'm fine, I'm fine," Nate was saying, his free hand conducting an invisible orchestra. "It's just — no, listen, it's — they haven't said we can leave yet. The detective, the — yeah, the one with the casserole. I know. I know it sounds — yeah."

Tommy was on the deck too, in the other Adirondack chair, not talking to Joel. The two of them sat six feet apart facing the same lake, and the silence between them was the particular silence of two men who had nothing to say to each other and couldn't leave because the chairs were there and the lake was there and going inside would require a decision they hadn't made yet. Tommy's hands were on the armrests, big hands, the knuckles white from gripping. His crew cut was growing out unevenly, the gray at the temples catching the morning light. He looked like a man waiting in a doctor's office for results he already knew.

Mallick had left an hour ago. She'd told Paul — Paul being the one she treated as the group's designated adult, which Paul accepted the way Paul accepted everything, with a small nod and the air of a man adding another claim to his caseload — that she'd be back in the afternoon and that nobody should leave the property. She'd said it with a smile that was supposed to make it sound like a suggestion rather than an instruction. Cliff didn't buy the smile. He didn't buy anything about Mallick, because Cliff sold for a living and he recognized performance when he saw it, the same way a magician recognized another magician's setup. She was good. The casserole, the wrong name, the bathroom before the body — all of it was choreographed to look like it wasn't. Whether she was good enough to matter was a separate question that Cliff didn't have time to answer right now.

What mattered was that she was gone. What mattered was the study.

Cliff had been in Martin's study once already — Saturday night, in those scrambled hours between Martin's death and dawn, when every man in this house had apparently decided, independently and for private reasons, to creep through the darkness doing something stupid. Cliff's stupid thing had been this: he'd gone to the study looking for the promissory note Martin had signed. Or not signed — that was the problem. Martin had acknowledged the debt in texts, in emails, in conversations that Paul and Tommy had both overheard, but acknowledgment was not documentation, and if Martin's estate was going to settle debts, it would settle documented debts first, and Cliff's forty thousand existed primarily in Martin's word, which had depreciated to a market value of zero as of approximately 1:30 AM Saturday.

He hadn't found the promissory note. What he'd found was Martin's desk, Martin's filing cabinet — locked, a keyed lock, and Cliff hadn't wanted to force it at three in the morning with the house full of sleepless men — and Martin's wall safe. The safe was behind a framed print of a lake that looked nothing like Elbow Lake, a stock image in a frame that cost more than the print, which was Martin all over: invest in the container, neglect the contents. The safe was a SentrySafe, wall-mounted, combination dial. And the dial had been sitting one tick off from the final number — not unlocked, but one turn from unlocked, the way you'd leave a door on the latch when you were coming right back.

That was either Martin's trust or Martin's laziness, and in twenty-five years of friendship Cliff had never determined which one it was, because the thing about Martin was that his carelessness and his generosity looked identical from the outside. Martin left the safe one tick off. Martin left the front door unlocked. Martin left forty thousand dollars unrepaid. It was all the same gesture — a man who believed that things would hold together without being secured, and who had been right about that up until the moment his heart stopped.

Saturday night, Cliff had opened the safe, seen the contents — folders, envelopes, cash — and then heard the third stair from the top creak and closed the safe and left the study and gone back to the pull-out sofa in the basement where his heart had hammered against the mattress springs for twenty minutes. He'd folded. Cliff Haywood, who had sat across from bank loan officers with underwater properties and city zoning boards with rejected permits and told them things that were not entirely true with a resting heart rate of sixty-two, had folded because a stair creaked.

Now it was Sunday morning and Mallick was gone and the staircase was empty and Cliff's heart rate was currently seventy-four — he'd checked in the bathroom, two fingers on his wrist, because he was the kind of man who wanted data points even when the data was his own body failing to be calm. Seventy-four was fine. Seventy-four was a man about to go for a walk, not a man about to break into a dead friend's safe for the second time in thirty-six hours.

He rinsed the mug. Set it in the sink. The refrigerator cycled on with its loud, asthmatic hum, the compressor grinding through a rhythm it had probably been grinding through since 1998. Martin had never replaced it. Martin had never replaced anything in this house except the deck, and only because the old one had literally rotted through. This was one of seven reasons the Roseville duplex had been a disaster — Martin's conviction that maintenance was optional, that things could be sustained indefinitely through inattention, was a philosophy that worked for a cabin on a lake and failed catastrophically for rental property in a market that punished deferred maintenance with vacancy and code violations.

"Gonna make a call," Cliff said to the room. Paul nodded without looking up from his phone. Nate, still pacing near the door, gave a thumbs-up that nobody had requested.

Cliff walked down the hallway. Past the half bath with its nautical-themed hand towels that Martin's ex-wife Linda had probably bought and that Martin had probably not noticed in the five years since the divorce. Past the framed photographs — the annual draft weekends, twenty-five of them, a time-lapse of seven men aging in the same room. Cliff glanced at the 2003 photo without stopping. They were all twenty-three, twenty-four in that one. Tommy had a full head of dark hair and a sunburn and was grinning with his arm around Martin, and Martin was leaning into it, and the two of them looked like what they were — young men at a lake with nothing behind them and everything ahead. A year after the fire. Whatever showed on their faces, it wasn't guilt.

Martin's study was the last door on the left. Cliff turned the knob. Stepped inside. Closed the door behind him and stood still for three seconds, listening. The house gave him nothing — coffee sounds from the kitchen, the refrigerator, the barely audible murmur of Nate's voice. Nobody was coming.

The study smelled like Martin's desk — printer ink, paper dust, the specific mustiness of a man who kept documents he should have shredded. Desk, filing cabinet, bookshelves stacked with financial planning guides, Tom Clancy novels, and the college textbooks Martin had kept because Martin had kept everything. The window faced the front of the house, the gravel driveway, and Cliff checked: Mallick's county-issue sedan was not in the drive. Just their cars — Paul's Accord, Joel's Volvo, Tommy's F-150, Nate's Subaru with the podcast bumper stickers, Doug's Camry, and Cliff's Lexus. The Lexus was leased. The lease payment was fourteen hundred a month. Cliff was currently three weeks late on it, which put him in the grace period, which was a term that meant the same thing in car loans as it did in religion: you were forgiven right up until you weren't.

He pulled the lake print away from the wall. The safe was where he'd left it, which was where Martin had left it, which was where it had probably been since Martin's parents had installed it sometime in the eighties. Cliff turned the dial that single remaining tick. The handle gave. The door opened.

Inside: three manila folders, one business-size envelope, one legal-size manila envelope, and a stack of bills held with a binder clip. Cliff took the bills first. Counted without removing the clip — twenties and fifties, roughly two thousand dollars. He put them back. Two thousand wasn't the answer to anything. Two thousand was three weeks of the answer.

The first folder was labeled HOUSE. Deed, tax records, homeowner's insurance with a carrier Cliff recognized as midrange. The house was paid off — no mortgage. That was good. An unencumbered asset in the estate.

The second folder was INVESTMENTS. Edward Jones brokerage statements, a Vanguard index fund, some municipal bonds. Cliff scanned the most recent statement: total portfolio value around two-forty. Not rich. Comfortable. The kind of money that looked like enough until you subtracted a divorce settlement, two decades of property taxes, and forty thousand dollars in unrepaid debts.

The third folder was PERSONAL, and here Cliff's hands slowed. PERSONAL in a dead man's safe could be anything — love letters, medical records, the specific secrets that men kept in locked boxes because they couldn't keep them in their heads. What Cliff needed it to be was a signed promissory note.

He opened it. Birth certificate, Martin Kessler, born January 14, 1977, Hennepin County. Passport, expired. Military discharge papers — Martin had done two years in the Army after high school and before college, a fact that Cliff always forgot because Martin never mentioned it. Divorce decree, Martin A. Kessler and Linda M. Kessler, five years ago, Becker County District Court. And at the bottom, a single handwritten page on loose-leaf paper:

*I acknowledge that I owe Cliff Haywood $40,000 (forty thousand dollars) for his share of the capital loss on the Roseville property at 1847 Dale St. I have not yet repaid this and I intend to repay it in full by end of calendar year.*

No date. No signature. No witness line, no notarization, no language that any lawyer would call binding. Just Martin's square, neat handwriting and Martin's intention, which was worth exactly as much as Martin's heartbeat.

Cliff read it twice. It was something. It was evidence of intent. It was the kind of document his lawyer would look at and say "it's helpful" in the specific tone that meant "it's not enough," and then bill him four hundred an hour for the distinction.

He put the folder back. Picked up the business-size envelope — it was Martin's will, two pages, printed, witnessed by a notary in Detroit Lakes. Cliff skimmed it. The will was the estate's problem and the estate would be Paul's problem, because Paul was the executor, because of course Paul was the executor. The relevant line: the lake house was left to — Cliff stopped. Read it again. The lake house was left to Thomas Renaldi.

Tommy. Martin had left the house to Tommy.

Not to Paul, his best friend. Not divided among the group. Not to Linda, his ex-wife. To Tommy. Cliff let that sit for a moment, the way he let surprising numbers sit when a deal showed him something he hadn't expected. It was unusual. It was a data point he didn't yet know what to do with. He put the will back.

Which left the manila envelope.

It was thick, legal-size, sealed with a metal clasp that had been bent open and reclosed enough times that the metal was fatigued — the clasp wobbled when he touched it. The tab was labeled in Martin's handwriting, two letters in black Sharpie:

**RT**

Cliff's pulse ticked up. RT. His first thought was mechanical, automatic, the way his brain categorized letters: RTI Holdings, the LLC Martin had used for the Roseville deal. RT could be real estate transaction. RT could be a property code, a reference number, something connected to the duplex, to the money, to the paper trail that would make forty thousand dollars into a legal obligation instead of a dead man's vague handwritten promise.

He bent the clasp open. Pulled the contents out.

Not a contract. Not a partnership document. Not anything Cliff's brain had built in the three seconds between reading the tab and touching the paper.

It was a letter. Handwritten, Martin's handwriting again, four pages on yellow legal pad paper, blue ballpoint ink. The pages were soft at the edges, the particular softness that paper developed when it had been handled and then left alone for a long time — taken out, read, put back, taken out, read, put back, over years. The ink had faded slightly at the creases where the pages had been folded.

The first page began:

*August 17, 2002*

*I'm writing this down because somebody should, and because I don't trust myself to remember it right if I don't write it now while it's still clear. This is what happened at the cabin on Wolf Creek Road, Washburn County, Wisconsin, on the night of August 11, 2002.*

Cliff shifted his weight against the edge of Martin's desk. From the front of the house, he could hear Nate's voice, fainter now, and the clink of a mug in the kitchen. The lake lapped at the shore through the open study window. He read.

The first page was context. Martin and Tommy had driven up to a cabin outside Minong that belonged to a friend of Tommy's uncle, a man named Gunderson. They were closing it up for the season — draining pipes, shuttering windows, the kind of favor you did in your twenties when someone asked and you had a free weekend. The cabin had a caretaker, a man named Ray Toomey, who lived in a single-wide on the adjacent lot and maintained the property in the off-season. Toomey was supposed to have cleared out before they arrived. His truck — Martin specified a blue Ford pickup, the detail of a man who wanted to get this right — was in the driveway when they got there Friday night.

Second page. Tommy had been drinking since they'd left Minneapolis. Cliff could see it without effort — twenty-five-year-old Tommy, the same Tommy from the 2003 photo in the hallway, big and sunburned and careless, a twelve-pack riding between his feet in the passenger seat of whatever car Martin was driving in 2002. They'd found Toomey's truck but no Toomey. The cabin was dark. Tommy went in first. Martin wrote that he heard Tommy banging through the kitchen, swearing at the furniture, the particular noisy gracelessness of a big drunk man in a dark unfamiliar room. Then the sounds changed. A crash, then a different quality of silence, and then brightness in the kitchen window, and Martin smelled smoke before he could see the fire through the glass.

Third page. Martin's handwriting got smaller here, tighter, the letters compressing the way handwriting did when the hand was trying to keep pace with a memory that didn't want to be slowed down.

Tommy had knocked over a kerosene heater. The spill had caught on the pilot flame and the kerosene had splashed across the kitchen floor and the kitchen floor was pine, old pine, dry from a drought summer, and the fire had moved faster than either of them could think. Martin ran in. Found Tommy standing in the front room, not moving, watching the kitchen burn with the particular stillness of a man who understood what he had done but could not yet understand what it meant. Martin grabbed his arm. Tommy didn't resist. They got out the front door as the kitchen ceiling came down behind them, a sound Martin described as "like someone dropping a piano from a second floor." Four minutes. The cabin was fully involved — Martin's phrase, "fully involved," a firefighter's term that he must have looked up later — within four minutes.

Ray Toomey had been asleep in the back bedroom.

They didn't know he was there. His truck was in the driveway, but they'd assumed he'd walked to a bar or gotten a ride with a neighbor or any of the other things you assumed when you were twenty-five and a building was on fire and your brain was looking for reasons not to go back inside. The volunteer fire department found what was left of Ray Toomey in the wreckage the next morning. The county coroner's report — Martin didn't include the full report, but he referenced it — listed the blood alcohol at .14. Toomey had been drunk and asleep with a dead smoke detector battery and hadn't woken up, and the fire had done the rest.

Fourth page. This was the part that made Cliff read standing still, not leaning, not shifting, the air in the study suddenly specific and close.

Tommy had wanted to call 911 from the road. Martin had talked him out of it. Not because Martin was cold — the letter made that clear, the agitation of the handwriting, the crossed-out words and restarts — but because Martin had done the math the way Martin always did the math: Tommy was drunk. Tommy had entered the cabin without invitation. Tommy had knocked over the heater. A man was dead. The math on Tommy's future came out to involuntary manslaughter at minimum, and Martin had made a decision in the dark on a county road outside Minong that he'd then spent twenty-two years living inside.

Martin drove back to the cabin site at three in the morning. Alone. He wore gloves — work gloves, from the trunk. He repositioned the kerosene heater to look like it had malfunctioned in place rather than been knocked over by a person. He adjusted the spill pattern, as much as he could with the char. The fire investigation ruled it accidental: faulty heater, unattended cabin, intoxicated caretaker, dead smoke detector. Case closed. Ray Toomey was buried in Minong. Nobody asked Martin or Tommy anything, because nobody knew they'd been there.

The last paragraph:

*Tommy is not a bad person. He was drunk and it was dark and he didn't know Toomey was there. But a man died because of what Tommy did and what I did after, and I need to write it down. I don't know what I want done with this. I don't know if I want anything done with it. I need it to exist somewhere outside my head. If it's only in my head then it's just a thing I think about, and it has to be more than that. It has to be a thing that happened.*

*— Martin Kessler*

Cliff set the pages down on the desk. The study was the same study it had been six minutes ago. Filing cabinet, bookshelves, Tom Clancy novels, the window facing the empty gravel drive, the lake print leaning against the wall where he'd moved it. The refrigerator hummed in the kitchen. Someone — Paul — was running water in the sink. The loons were making their low, warbling call from somewhere out on the lake.

He stood there for maybe thirty seconds without thinking anything. Just standing in the room with the pages on the desk and Martin's handwriting in front of him and the name Ray Toomey in his head, a name he'd never heard before, a man he'd never met, a man who had been dead for twenty-two years because Tommy Renaldi had been drunk in a dark kitchen and knocked over a kerosene heater.

Then the thirty seconds ended and Cliff's brain did what Cliff's brain did. It started running numbers.

Tommy Renaldi. Forty-seven. High school football coach. PE teacher. Married to Denise, three kids — two in high school, one at St. Cloud State or maybe Mankato. Head coach of the varsity program for eleven years, the kind of tenure that made you a permanent fixture in a suburban Minnesota community, the kind of man whose name was on a field or a gymnasium or at minimum a glass case of trophies in the school lobby. Cliff didn't know which school, but he knew the type. He'd sold houses in those suburbs. He knew what a coaching career was worth in a town where Friday night football was the social calendar.

The statute of limitations on involuntary manslaughter in Wisconsin was six years. Cliff wasn't a lawyer, but he'd sat through enough deal closings and contract disputes to know when a clock had expired. Tommy couldn't be prosecuted. Probably. The concealment was a different question, and Martin's involvement was a different question, but Martin was dead and the criminal exposure was, in Cliff's assessment, limited.

But the criminal exposure was not the kind that mattered. The kind that mattered — the kind that had no statute of limitations, no expiration date, no appeals process — was what happened when a high school football coach's community learned that he had started a fire that killed a man and then let his friend cover it up and then spent twenty-two years coaching other people's children while a dead caretaker's family believed it was an accident. That kind of exposure didn't have a price because it was total. It was the end of the coaching career, the marriage, the reputation, the version of Tommy Renaldi that existed in the world. It was everything.

And everything was worth more than forty thousand dollars.

Cliff was not proud of the arithmetic. He would have preferred to be the kind of man who read a dead friend's twenty-two-year-old confession and felt moral weight, the appropriate gravity that Paul would feel, the legal complexity that Joel would catalogue, the quiet grief that Doug would absorb without comment. He was not that man. He was a man whose business had three projects stalled and one in active default. A man whose second ex-wife's lawyer had filed a motion to increase alimony that Cliff's lawyer couldn't contest because Cliff owed his lawyer eleven thousand dollars in unpaid fees. A man whose leased Lexus was forty-five days from repossession. A man who had put suits and restaurant tabs and client gifts on a credit card that was now at its limit, making minimum payments that didn't cover the interest, watching the balance grow like something alive and hungry.

A man inside those numbers did not read leverage and think about morality. He thought about arithmetic.

He wasn't going to use it crudely. He wasn't an idiot. He wasn't going to walk up to Tommy and say "I know about the fire, give me forty thousand dollars." That was blackmail, and blackmail was a crime, and more importantly blackmail was a bad deal — it was a one-time transaction with no repeat business and maximum risk. What Cliff was going to do was have a conversation. He was going to help Tommy understand that there was an arrangement available — Tommy's influence with the group, with the estate, with whatever mechanism existed to make Martin's debt an enforceable obligation. Tommy could make the forty thousand happen. Tommy had relationships with Paul, with the executor, with the people who would administer Martin's affairs. A word from Tommy, some pressure applied in the right places, and Cliff's money would surface through the estate process the way it should have surfaced two years ago. Tommy wouldn't even have to write a check himself. He'd just have to make sure the right conversations happened.

It wasn't blackmail. It was a restructuring.

Cliff took his phone from his pocket. He photographed each page, front and back — four sheets, eight images. The camera shutter sound was off, had been off since 2019 when he'd started photographing competing agents' listing sheets at open houses, a practice that was frowned upon by the ethics board of the Minnesota Association of Realtors and practiced by every agent Cliff had ever met. He checked each image for focus and legibility. Martin's handwriting was clear. "Tommy" was clear. "Ray Toomey" was clear. "Washburn County" was clear. The date was clear. The signature was clear.

He put the pages back in the manila envelope. Bent the clasp closed, careful to match the angle it had been at when he'd opened it. Put the envelope back in the safe — behind the folders, in front of the cash, exactly where it had been. He closed the safe door. Turned the dial back one tick from locked, Martin's habitual position, the position Cliff had found it in, the position it would be in when the estate eventually sent someone to inventory the contents and they found what they found.

He rehung the lake print. Adjusted it until the frame sat level on its hooks. Stepped back to check. The print really was terrible — a generic lake, generic pines, the kind of image you'd find in a dentist's office. Martin could have had a local artist paint Elbow Lake itself, the actual view from the actual window three feet away, and instead he'd hung a stock photograph of someone else's lake. That was Martin. Why fix a thing when you could leave it slightly wrong and live with the wrongness for twenty years.

Cliff opened the study door. The hallway was empty. He could hear Paul and Joel in the kitchen now, their voices low, one of those careful conversations about logistics and next steps that men had when the emotional conversation was too large to start. He walked to the half bath. Washed his hands — not for fingerprints, his prints were already on the safe from Saturday night, and if Mallick dusted for prints she'd find six men's fingerprints throughout the house because they'd all been in this house for three days. He washed his hands because they were shaking and the hot water helped and because a man washing his hands in a bathroom after using the bathroom was the most invisible man in any house.

He looked at himself in the mirror above the sink. Silver hair that he paid sixty dollars to maintain at a barber in Edina. The permanent tan that came from spending fifteen years walking property lines and standing on half-built decks pointing at lake views. The collared polo he'd bought on a credit card that was now maxed. The watch — a Tag Heuer, a gift to himself in 2017 when the business had been good, the last year the business had been good. He looked like a man who sold lakefront property and tipped well at dinner. He looked like a man who had his affairs in order.

He dried his hands on the nautical towel. Went back to the kitchen. Poured a fresh mug of the bad coffee. Paul was still at the table. Joel had come in from the deck and was leaning against the counter, arms crossed, looking at nothing.

"Mallick say what time she's coming back?" Cliff asked. Even voice. Two hands on the mug.

"Afternoon," Paul said. "Didn't specify."

"Okay." Cliff nodded. Sipped. The coffee was genuinely bad — Martin had never figured out the ratio, always used too much water, and the can of Folgers in the cabinet was the same brand Martin had been buying since college. Twenty-five years of bad coffee. The man had a two-hundred-forty-thousand-dollar investment portfolio and couldn't buy decent beans. "Think I'm gonna take a walk. Clear my head a little."

"Sure," Paul said. His eyes were back on his phone.

Joel said nothing. Joel had said almost nothing since Saturday, and when he did speak it was in short, precise sentences that sounded like deposition answers. Cliff nodded to him anyway and Joel gave a nod back that was more acknowledgment than greeting, and Cliff took his coffee and walked out the front door.

He sat on the porch step where no one inside could see him. The gravel drive was bright in the morning sun. The birches across the road were just starting to turn — a few yellow leaves among the green, the first signal of a Minnesota September tipping toward October. The air smelled like pine sap and lake water and the faint sweetness of leaves beginning to decompose. It was a beautiful morning. It was the kind of morning that sold properties — Cliff had used mornings like this in listing photos, had positioned his camera to catch the golden light on the water, had written copy about "peaceful lake living" and "your own private retreat" for properties that were forty minutes from the nearest grocery store and had septic systems that cost eight grand to replace.

His phone was in his pocket with eight photographs on it. RT. Not real estate transaction. Not RTI Holdings. Ray Toomey. Caretaker. Dead at whatever age he'd been — Martin hadn't included that, just a name and a truck and a man asleep in a back bedroom who didn't wake up.

From the deck, Cliff could hear Tommy. Not words — Tommy wasn't talking. Just the sound of a big man in an Adirondack chair, the creak of the wood when he shifted, the particular heavy breathing of someone who was sitting still but not at rest. Cliff had noticed it before without understanding it. The way Tommy got quiet when the group conversation moved to anything before 2005 or so. The way Tommy came to the draft every year without fail, even the year his daughter was born, even the year his team made the state tournament, even the year Denise had asked him not to go and he'd gone anyway. The way Martin always made sure Tommy came, called him, included him, protected the seat even when other members of the group had suggested maybe this year Tommy should sit it out.

Twenty-two years. Martin had been managing Tommy's guilt for twenty-two years, and Tommy had been showing up to be managed, and now Martin was dead and the management had ended and Tommy was sitting on the deck of a lake house that Cliff had just learned Martin had left to Tommy in his will, gripping the armrests with hands that were white at the knuckles.

A loon surfaced about fifty yards out, its black head slick and shining. It dove without ceremony, slipping under so cleanly it left barely a ring on the surface. Gone.

Cliff drank the coffee. It was still bad.

He'd have the conversation with Tommy soon. Not today — today was too raw, too close to Saturday, too close to Mallick. He'd wait for the right moment. A quiet moment on the deck, maybe. Two beers. A casual opening, the way you opened any negotiation: establish common ground, acknowledge the difficulty, present the proposition as a solution rather than a demand. Cliff had been having conversations like this for twenty years. He knew how to frame a restructuring so the other party felt like they were participating in a decision rather than receiving one.

He sat on the porch step and finished the bad coffee and watched the light move on the lake and planned the conversation he was going to have with Tommy Renaldi, a conversation about money and influence and the reasonable resolution of a financial obligation, and he believed — as completely as he had ever believed any deal was sound, any listing was priced right, any client would close — that the conversation would go the way he planned it.
