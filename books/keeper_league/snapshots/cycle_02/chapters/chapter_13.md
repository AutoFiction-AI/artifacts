# Chapter 13

If Nate looked at the great room from the right angle, Monday almost passed for any other draft weekend cleanup. The football was on mute. Empty bottles crowded the coffee table. Six men argued about rules Martin used to pretend he hated settling.

Joel ended that in under a minute.

"So are we dealing with the roster or what?"

Nobody answered.

"Martin's roster," Joel said. "Season starts Thursday. His team's sitting there. Somebody has to decide what happens."

Tommy was stretched out on one end of the couch, one ankle on his knee, beer balanced against his stomach.

"His team plays."

"That doesn't answer the question."

"It answers it fine."

Paul said, "I think Joel means bye weeks, injuries, waiver claims. The team still needs a manager."

Cliff lifted his beer from the armchair by the window.

"Let it auto-manage. Let the asset keep running. No need to get cute."

"Auto-manage is dogshit," Nate said. "Auto-manage started Dalton over Herbert last year. I did twelve minutes on it because I was furious and because no one in my real life respects boundaries."

Tommy pointed the bottle at him.

"Also because no app gets to finger-fuck a dead man's lineup."

Joel stared at him.

"That is a revolting phrase."

"It's the right phrase."

"On the merits," Joel said, "I unfortunately agree."

Nate put a hand to his chest.

"Finally. Constitutional law I can use."

Paul was in Martin's recliner by the fireplace, which felt wrong even before Nate decided whether Paul knew it.

"I'm not saying the app should do it," Paul said. "I'm saying one of us does it. As a tribute, maybe."

"Tribute," Nate said. "That sounds like a district in a dystopian YA book."

"You know what I mean."

Tommy sat up.

"No. No fuckin way. Not you."

Paul blinked.

"Why not me?"

"Because you took Kittle in the second round because you got horny off one preseason clip of him running without a limp."

"I did not get horny."

"You absolutely did," Nate said. "Deck, you drafted thighs. It was a body-based evaluation."

Paul looked around the room for backup and got nothing.

"That is not what happened."

Tommy kept going.

"I wouldn't let you pick a stripper with somebody else's cash. I'm not letting you run Martin's flex."

Joel said, "The court would like it noted that those are two separate skill sets."

Doug laughed into his water, quick and quiet, then looked like he wished he hadn't. The pint glass in his hand was plain water, no label, no sweat, nothing to hide behind.

Nate said, "Doug, you're on the floor with hydration and judgment in your eyes. What do you think?"

"Paul should do it," Doug said.

"Why Paul?"

"Because he'd do it." Doug took a sip. "The rest of us would forget by week six."

"You think I'd forget?"

"You'd turn it into content."

That one got the room. Nate opened his mouth, closed it, and pointed across the coffee table.

"Cheap shot. Fair. But cheap."

Cliff took another drink.

"Tommy's right, though. Let the team play. Let the app handle the maintenance."

That made two quick agreements with Tommy in less than ten minutes. Nate kept his face still and logged it anyway. Tommy and Cliff usually called timeout the second they landed on the same side of anything.

Nate said, "All right, new issue. Trades. Can you trade with Martin's team?"

Joel turned and gave him the full Beckman deposition stare.

"You want to trade with a dead man."

"I don't want to. I'm asking whether it's possible."

"Those are not materially different questions."

"If the platform accepts it automatically," Nate said, "then technically nobody is exploiting grief. They're exploiting code."

Joel said, "We already have a detective asking why five of us touched a crime scene. I don't think we need to add dead-man trades to the pile."

Tommy rubbed a hand over his mouth.

"No trades. That's corpse-fucking with projections."

Joel shut his eyes for a second.

"Again, appalling phrasing. Again, correct."

Paul said, "No trades."

Doug nodded.

Cliff said, "No trades," so fast it sounded rehearsed.

The room went still on that one. Not the peaceful kind. Cliff had that answer loaded too fast, and everybody heard it.

"We need a commissioner," Nate said, because silence had always felt like a personal challenge.

"You," Tommy said. "You've already got the bullshit vocabulary."

"I don't want the job."

"Nobody wants the job."

Paul said, "Martin did."

"Martin wanted control," Joel said.

"For him that was love," Paul said.

Nobody laughed, but nobody argued either.

Nate rubbed his face.

"Fine. I'm interim commissioner of a league with no bylaws, a dead owner, and scoring settings imported from the Bush administration."

Tommy leaned forward and pointed.

"Don't touch punt return yards."

"I wasn't going to."

"You touch that, I'm driving across this room."

"Noted."

"Seriously, Griggs."

"I'm serious too."

Joel said, "Waiver priority has to redistribute. Martin had third. That's the only fair ruling."

"Fine," Nate said. "Waiver priority redistributes. Martin's team stays alive. Paul babysits it without touching anything gross. I do commissioner paperwork. Punt return yards stay because Coach is threatening domestic violence. Anything else?"

No one answered.

Nate looked up at the red circles over the fireplace. Martin's picks sat there in Martin's handwriting, a whole season waiting on a guy who was already out of it.

That was when Nate realized he could not remember the last time Tommy and Cliff had gone a full room without needling each other.

---

They grilled steaks because it was Monday night and because no one in the house knew what else to do with meat Martin had already bought.

Tommy worked the grill in his usual stance, feet planted, tongs in his right hand, beer in his left. He swore at flare-ups, checked the sear too often, and told Nate to quit hovering before Nate had moved within six feet.

"I wasn't hovering."

"You were preparing to hover."

"I was scouting."

"You scout with your eyes, not your breath. Back up."

Nate backed up.

"You know, in some counties that's verbal abuse."

"In some counties your podcast is a cry for help."

That got a real laugh out of Paul. Even Cliff smiled.

By the time the steaks came off, the table on the deck had gone from bright to amber. Eight chairs around it. Martin's chair at the head stood empty, and Tommy and Cliff took opposite ends without discussion. Three open seats lay between them. Nate counted because he counted everything.

He took the side facing the lake. Paul sat on his left. Doug sat across from him with the same water glass. Joel took the chair beside Doug. No one mentioned that they were eating a dead man's ribeyes off a dead man's deck with a dead man's salt grinder in the middle of the table.

Tommy set the platter down.

"Anybody asks for sauce can eat my whole ass."

Joel cut into his steak.

"Your hospitality has really matured."

"Steak sauce is ketchup for cowards."

Paul said, "I wasn't going to ask."

"You looked like you were thinking about it."

"I was thinking about potatoes."

"Same face."

Doug made the little huff he used instead of a laugh. It was good steak and a good line, good enough for a minute.

Beyond the deck, the lake held the last of the light. Birches on the far shore had gone yellow at the tips. Somewhere in the reeds a bird made one thin sound and stopped.

Cliff kept his eyes on the water.

"Nice night," he said, in his open-house voice.

Tommy said, "You sell the sunset and the dock comes free."

Cliff gave him half a smile.

"You want me to write up the listing, Coach, I can do that."

Tommy cut his steak.

"House comes with six alcoholics and one felony interview. That's a hard sell."

That clipped the smile off Cliff's face for a beat. Joel looked down at his plate. Paul drank. Three bites in, Doug quit making progress and started cutting the same piece smaller and smaller. Water caught the porch light in the wrong way.

Across the table Joel kept flicking looks at Paul. Paul gave him the smallest nod.

Then Joel looked up and said, louder than he needed to, "Anybody remember when Martin walked off the end of the dock?"

"Oh, shit," Nate said. "Twelve. It was twelve."

"RG3 year," Paul said immediately. "We were arguing keeper value."

Joel pointed with his fork.

"He did not slip. He did not trip. He just kept backing up like the dock still had real estate behind it."

"He was making a speech," Nate said. "Not even a good one. He wanted fumble return touchdowns in the scoring like he was introducing civil rights legislation."

"Four scotches deep," Paul said.

"That's not drunk for Martin," Tommy said. "That's warmed up."

Doug said, "Boat shoes too."

Joel started laughing before he finished chewing.

"Boat shoes with no socks. Christ."

Paul put his fork down.

"The khakis went clear the second he came up."

Nate slapped the table once.

"Everybody at that table saw more Martin than they ever asked for."

Joel said, "I want that sentence out of the record."

Tommy said, "Looked like somebody vacuum-sealed a kielbasa."

Doug bent over his plate laughing. Even Paul laughed hard at that, the kind with no manners on it.

Nate pointed across the table.

"No, the worst part was his face. He came up mad. Not embarrassed. Mad. Like the lake had interrupted him."

"It had," Joel said. "He surfaced already arguing."

"He raised one hand," Paul said, lifting his own without thinking, "and he goes, 'Motion still carries.'"

Nate and Doug said it with him.

"'Motion still carries.'"

Joel wiped his mouth.

"Standing in three feet of Minnesota lake water with his phone drowned in his pocket, and he's citing Robert's Rules like a goddamn parliamentarian."

Doug said, "I brought him a towel."

"You did," Paul said.

"He wouldn't take it."

"Because he wanted a second," Nate said.

Tommy pointed with his fork.

"I gave him the second because his balls were trying to climb back into his torso."

Joel put a hand over his eyes.

"That is medically disgusting."

"It's medically true," Nate said.

Doug was still laughing.

"He told me to keep the towel warm till the vote closed."

Paul said, "Then he climbed back up and blamed Cliff for the whole thing."

"I was protecting statistical continuity," Cliff said.

"You were being a cheap, nitpicking asshole," Tommy said.

"I was preserving the historical record."

"It's fantasy football, not the fuckin Dead Sea Scrolls."

That one broke the whole table again. Six men were laughing on a darkening deck, loud enough to carry over the water.

Cliff stayed with it a second longer than the rest. He wiped his mouth with his napkin, looked down the table at Tommy, and said, "You were halfway out of your shorts before Martin even came up. Fastest I've ever seen you move."

The old version of the night had an answer there. Nate could hear it, the automatic fuck you, the insult, the pile-on that would follow. Tommy even looked up, ready for it.

It didn't.

Tommy set down his fork.

"Anybody need another beer?"

No one said yes.

Tommy carried two empties inside and came back with two fresh ones anyway. One went to Nate. The other went to Cliff. Tommy set it beside Cliff's hand without looking at him.

"Thanks," Cliff said.

"Yeah."

Nobody reached for the joke again. Plates resumed. Knives on ceramic. Doug's water glass touched the table a little too carefully. Joel looked at Paul, held the look, then let it go.

By the time Paul said, "I'm going to start clearing this," the sun was down to a strip behind the trees.

Joel stood and stacked plates. Doug collected glasses. Cliff took the empties, plus the lone water glass, and carried them inside to the recycling bin by the kitchen door. Tommy stayed at the railing with both hands flat on the top board, looking out toward the dock.

They peeled off a man at a time after that. Doug first, with a quiet good night. Joel next, one more glance at Paul on his way in. Paul stopped at the slider with a hand on the frame, thought better of it, and went upstairs. Cliff said, "Night, boys," to the general air and disappeared into the house.

Nate stayed on the deck with Tommy.

"Hey, Coach."

Tommy kept his eyes on the lake.

"Yeah."

"We're all right?"

A lone bird called out in the dark. Tommy's fingers tightened on the railing.

"Sure," he said.

Nate waited.

Tommy said, "We're fine, Griggs."

It ended the conversation whether Nate believed it or not.

"All right," Nate said. "Night."

"Night."

Tommy went in. A few seconds later the third stair gave its usual crack, then the hall above the great room carried his steps away.

Alone, Nate looked out at the black water and the one porch light burning across the lake. It was somebody else's dock, somebody else's house, somebody else's ordinary Monday night.

After locking the slider, Nate went downstairs to the basement. The pull-out waited there with his duffel, his mic, and his phone still lit with Mia's last text telling him to come home safe.

The opener hit him anyway. It was habit, reflex, brain damage.

"So here's the thing about a keeper league," he said to the empty room.

That was as far as he got.

Back upstairs, a floorboard moved. Then the house went still again.

Nate opened Voice Memos, stared at the red record button, and tapped it. The waveform jumped when he breathed. He listened to himself breathe for two seconds, hit stop, and deleted the file.

The phone went facedown beside the mic. In the dark, the refrigerator kicked on and kept running.
