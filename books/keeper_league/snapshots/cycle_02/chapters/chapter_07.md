# Chapter 7

Doug took his pills in the basement bathroom with the door locked. Unnecessary. Nate was upstairs, talking through the floorboards in that pitch he used when he wanted other people to hear how calm he was.

Lexapro first. Escitalopram oxalate, 10 milligrams, once daily with or without food. Clinical data said absorption ticked up a little with food, and Doug hadn't eaten since yesterday's lunch. Dry swallow. Chalk on the tongue. Three months now, same as the divorce. The prescription had arrived two weeks after Steph left. Maybe it was working. Maybe he was just getting used to waking up in a different life.

Ativan next. Lorazepam, 0.5 milligrams, as needed. It was needed.

For years he'd sold both classes of drug, back when brand names still mattered and lunch got you into the back office. Now he tipped one into his palm and took it from the tap with the same hand he'd once used to pass out sample cards. The mirror showed nothing new except the number on the scale back in Richfield: twenty-seven pounds gone since Steph said she was done. Best diet he'd ever been on. Hard to recommend it with a straight face.

Both bottles were in his duffel now, zipped into the side pocket under a rolled pair of socks. They'd started the weekend in the shared upstairs bathroom, tucked between Tommy's industrial bottle of Advil and Paul's toothpaste after everyone dumped their gear there on Thursday evening. Toothbrushes, razors, Cliff's moisturizer nobody was allowed to mention, the same clutter they made every year. Doug's bottles had sat there through Thursday night and all of Friday. Around three Saturday morning, he went up and got them.

That was after Nate shook him awake to say Martin was dead. After the six of them stood around in the great room not believing it. After Paul pounded on a chest that wasn't coming back. After somebody said call 911 and somebody else said wait. While the house creaked and men moved in other rooms and a toilet flushed overhead, Doug took the stairs, skipped the third step because everyone knew better, opened the medicine cabinet, and lifted both bottles out with his thumb over the labels so they wouldn't click together.

Not because of evidence. Not because of detectives. Because he could already see the question arriving in a stranger's mouth and six old friends' faces behind it.

*Are you under psychiatric care, Mr. Whitfield?*

Tommy would go flat and careful. Joel would store it. Paul would do that insurance face that looked like concern and audit at the same time. Nate's whole face would say *shit, Doug*. Cliff wouldn't say anything at all, which would be worse.

Twenty-five years with this group had taught Doug how to stay harmless in public. He gave up the chair with the lake view. He took the beer run nobody remembered assigning. He laughed before a story stalled out. The bottles would change that. So he took them. If that counted as tampering, it was tampering by shame.

Upstairs, the kitchen was bright with lake light. Paul had made coffee, strong as ever because Martin liked it that way. Martin's Lagavulin still hung in the great room, smoke and iodine caught in the paneling. The bottle stood on the counter with two fingers left in it, cap off because Martin never screwed the cap back on.

Doug poured into the blue mug with the chipped handle and skipped the cream. Cream in coffee still brought Steph back whole: her feet tucked under her on the couch, both hands around the mug, blowing once across the top even when it didn't need it.

Tommy was already on the deck, working the railing with both hands, pace-turn-pace, knuckles cracking in little bursts. Morning light caught the scar on his left forearm. Grill accident, Tommy had said years ago. Maybe. Doug had never asked. Not asking was part of what he did for people.

At the table, Joel sat with nothing in front of him. No mug, no phone, no newspaper, just his jaw moving like he was trying a case in his own head and the witness kept changing the answer.

"You eat?" Paul said.

"Not hungry."

"You should anyway."

"Yeah. Probably."

Paul gave him the usual once-over and let him go.

Nate came down the stairs in the middle of a sentence and kept going. Mallick hadn't asked about the hot tub yet, right? Because the chemicals had to matter somehow. Chlorine or bromine or whatever Martin used, and maybe that was nothing, but what if it wasn't nothing, because she'd asked him about the coffee maker and who asked about a coffee maker unless they were building toward something.

"Nobody asked about the hot tub," Joel said.

"Not yet."

"She won't."

"How do you know that?"

"Because it isn't relevant."

Nate looked at Doug for backup. Doug gave him a small nod, meaning nothing in particular, and Nate took it as permission to keep spiraling.

Cliff came in from the hallway showered and pressed, fresh polo, chinos, hair combed, the kind of Sunday morning you wore when nobody had died. With his mug in hand, Cliff topped off his coffee, asked if anybody wanted toast, and set bread in the toaster with careful tanned hands. Doug watched him do it and thought, not for the first time, that Cliff met a crisis the way other men met a restaurant reservation.

"Toast?" Cliff asked him.

"I'm good."

"It's sourdough."

"I'm fine."

Orange coils lit behind the toaster slots.

Coffee in hand, Doug moved to the corner of the sectional by the window. The draft board still hung on the wall. Martin's commissioner picks were circled in red, same as always. Kelce in the second, which made everybody groan. Lamar in the third, which started a five-minute argument Thursday night. In the seventh, a rookie receiver nobody had heard of, followed by Nate asking if Martin was drafting him or adopting him. Martin had smiled through all of it. That was what Doug kept catching on now, not the jokes or the arguments, just the smile and the little gap between Martin's front teeth when he liked his own move.

The board turned the room into a before-and-after picture. Thursday night, Martin had been alive and needling everybody. Now the same red circles looked like somebody had marked evidence.

He wasn't the joke guy or the fixer or the lawyer or the loud one. He was the guy people forgot to guard themselves around. Fifteen years in pharmaceutical sales had trained him to catch the half-second before people finished lying to themselves, and he still did it on weekends for free. What he saw that morning wasn't subtle. Tommy was trying to wear panic out through his hands. Joel had gone so still it looked deliberate. Nate kept talking because silence would make him hear himself think. Paul was making coffee and acting like competence could keep a body in the room. Cliff was dressed for an open house.

If Doug stayed quiet long enough, people stopped editing.

Sometimes he wondered whether the invisibility came first or the depression. Sometimes he wondered if the order even mattered.

---

After lunch Mallick found him on the deck with the paperback he'd brought from Richfield open on his knee and unread.

"Mr. Whitfield? Doug? You got a minute?"

He had nothing else to do.

She settled into the next Adirondack chair with the leather notebook in her lap and the click-pen she'd been worrying all morning. The glasses on the beaded chain bumped lightly against the notebook when she wrote.

"We talked a little yesterday," she said. "I just had a couple things I wanted to make sure I had right. How'd you sleep Friday night?"

"Fine."

"All the way through?"

"Yeah."

"Some of the other fellas mentioned noises. Footsteps and a door and all that. Basement might've been quieter, though."

"It was."

Her pen scratched.

"And Martin. How'd you two know each other?"

"College. Same group."

"Were you close?"

"We were friends."

"A good friend?"

"He was good to me."

That made her look up. Not pounce, exactly. Just look up.

"In what way?"

Doug thought of July. Martin calling instead of texting. Martin saying *bullshit* when Doug said he was fine. Martin telling him to come to the draft and meaning it enough that Doug heard it.

"He checked in," Doug said.

"That's nice." Another note. "So Friday night, after the hot tub and everybody finally shut up, what time did you go downstairs?"

"Midnight. Maybe later."

"And then you slept straight through."

"Yeah."

"Didn't hear Nate get up?"

Doug almost said *he's quiet* and stopped there, because even that sounded like more than he wanted on paper. "Didn't hear him."

Mallick nodded as if she'd been handed something useful, though all he'd given her were three or four flat stones.

"Anything else over the weekend strike you funny before Saturday morning?" she asked. "Arguments, bad blood, somebody acting off?"

Doug could have given her plenty. Joel's shut jaw on Thursday night. Tommy's extra voltage. Cliff asking Martin about roof age and property taxes like he was already halfway into an appraisal. Even Nate, working too hard at being Nate. He carried those scraps around because nobody ever asked him to do anything with them. Mallick, with her casserole manners and notebook, was probably the first person in the house who would.

"I don't think so."

"No?"

"They're always like this." Doug shrugged. "Twenty-five years. There's always something."

"Fantasy football," she said, smiling.

Doug didn't return it. The energy wasn't there.

She asked about the basement door. The lock. Whether anybody else stored things down there. Same patient, meandering voice, every question wrapped in house talk. Doug answered in the fewest words available and let the gaps stay open after each one. Most people rushed to fill silence. Mallick didn't. She just let it sit between them, then put down one more line in the notebook.

When she stood, she paused the way she'd paused after every interview all weekend, leaving a little space for a last-minute correction or confession. Doug gave her nothing. Silence was the only thing in the house he trusted himself to handle better than she did. Something in her expression shifted, not annoyance, not surprise either. Recalculation. Then she nodded and went back inside.

Doug stayed on the deck until the sliding door shut behind her. Then he took the stone path through the pines toward the dock, dry needles giving under his shoes.

---

The dock pushed thirty feet into Elbow Lake, boards silvered by weather except for a few newer ones Martin had replaced over the years. A bench sat near the end. The swim platform was beyond it. Clear water below, sand and stones and a scatter of old beer caps sunk into the bottom. Near one piling a school of minnows held steady against nothing visible, all of them facing the same direction as if there'd been an instruction Doug had missed.

Doug sat on the bench with his feet hanging over the side.

The place still knew how to be itself. Soft September light lay on the water. Trees were just starting to turn on the far shore. A motorboat droned somewhere out of view, and its wake reached the dock late and gentle. Doug thought about the apartment in Richfield with the beige carpet and the neighbor who watched *Jeopardy!* loud enough to feel personal, then about the gray towels from Target, the mattress on the floor, the blank walls. The lake house pushed back with its own inventory: splinters in the dock, old beer caps under the water, Joel once coming up from a cannonball with blood running down his shin and suing Martin for negligent dockwork until Martin paid him off with a sixth-round pick.

That got a smile out of him.

The surprise was that he was glad he'd come. Martin dead, detective in the house, pill bottles hidden in a duffel, and he was still glad. These men were oblivious and exhausting and at least half full of shit, but the weekend gave him what the apartment didn't: noise, history, other bodies in the rooms. Martin had understood that much when he called in July. *Come to the draft. I mean it.* Doug had come because Martin said it like an instruction from somebody who'd noticed he was slipping.

A loon called out on the lake. By the time the sound thinned away, the light had gone from gold to gray.

Doug went back up the path, let himself into the house, and ate one cold piece of Cliff's sourdough standing at the counter. After that he took the basement stairs to the pull-out sofa and lay in the dark with the duffel beside him. Above his head the refrigerator kicked on, rattled through its cycle, clicked off. Before it started again, he still hadn't slept.
