# Chapter 16

Joel sat in the Adirondack chair that had been Cliff's two nights ago and watched a BCA tech in nitrile gloves walk the stone path from the dock toward the house with an evidence bag held away from his leg. From this distance, through pine and birch, the tech looked like a man cleaning up after a picnic. Careful. Methodical. The kind of man who didn't miss what other people wished he'd miss.

Tuesday afternoon. September light came low through the trees and threw gold across the lake. The water was flat. A motorboat hummed somewhere past the far shore. The loons hadn't started yet.

He hadn't slept. All night the house had kept making Martin noises: the refrigerator clicking on, the third stair complaining under somebody's weight at three in the morning, the hot tub pump running its maintenance cycle for nobody. Joel had lain in the guest bed listening and setting one fact against the next until a pattern started to hold. Except there was nowhere clean to put it. Just a house where two men had died in four days and five others were still moving through the rooms like nobody had told the furniture what happened.

Paul came out through the slider with two mugs. He'd made coffee four times since dawn. Joel had quit trying to decide what that meant. Paul set one mug on the chair arm and sat down, not in Martin's chair. Nobody had sat in Martin's chair since Saturday morning.

"She talked to Nate again," Paul said. "Forty minutes, maybe a little more."

"About what."

"The audio. Cliff's phone. Monday night."

Joel took the mug. Too hot. Too weak. Paul had been making the same bad coffee for twenty-five years and still hadn't improved.

"She asked me something else," Paul said.

"What."

"Whether Martin kept papers in the study."

Joel looked at him. "And?"

"And whether there was a safe."

"You told her."

"She already knew there was a painting hanging wrong." Paul took a sip, put the mug down, then corrected himself. "No, that's not right. She knew there was something behind it. She didn't say safe until I did."

"Did she open it?"

"Not yet. Saturday the deputies gave the office a quick pass because Martin looked like a heart attack, not a homicide. Desk, file cabinet, obvious paper. Since Cliff, they've been on the dock, Cliff's room, the path, the phone extraction. Mallick said she'll do the safe after that."

"Today."

"Before dinner, was the phrase."

Joel watched a gull cut low over the water and disappear behind the trees. "When you found the will Monday morning, was the safe already open?"

"One tick off locked. Same as always."

"And you looked through everything."

Paul's mouth tightened. "I found the will."

"That's not what I asked."

Paul let that sit for a second. "There were folders. Cash. The will. A yellow envelope with RT on it. I saw it. I left it."

"You didn't open it."

"No."

"Why."

"Because I was looking for the will, Joel. I found it and stopped. That's what happened."

Joel nodded. He believed him. Paul was too exact to lie casually, and too upset to dress a truth up pretty. Still, the detail sat there now, bright and ugly.

"You told me Tommy gets the house," Joel said.

"Yeah."

"And it surprised you."

"It did."

"Because you expected—"

"I expected me." Paul rubbed a thumb against the mug handle and started again. "Or split, maybe. Something that looked like twenty-five years, not Tommy getting the whole place."

He looked out at the lake. Joel let him. When Paul finally went on, it came the way most of Paul's real things came, by circles first.

"I've been going back over Martin and Tommy," Paul said. "Not just this week. All of it. Every year he made sure Tommy came. Every year. Even the years Tommy tried to skip. Denise was sick in 2011 and Martin called him four times. Four. I remember because Anne said it was insane."

"Protective."

"More than that." Paul shook his head. "Tommy was late in 2019 by, what, an hour and ten? Martin didn't say a word. Nate's five minutes late and Martin rides him to the driveway. Tommy got patience nobody else got."

Joel remembered the dock talks after dinner, Martin's hand closing on Tommy's arm and keeping him back a minute too long, the way he'd always read it as closeness because that was the easiest answer available. Paul was right. It hadn't been loose affection. It had been management.

"And there's Cliff," Joel said.

Paul turned. "What about him."

"Sunday morning he came out of the study wearing that sales look. He had something."

Paul went quiet. Joel could see him run it forward, consequence by consequence.

"Cliff and Tommy were wrong after Sunday," Paul said. "At the waiver wire. At lunch. Every time they were in the same room."

"Yeah."

"You think Cliff found whatever was in the safe."

"I think Cliff found the thing that explains the will, and twenty-two years of Martin acting like Tommy was made of thinner glass than the rest of us."

Paul gave a short breath through his nose. "The safe is part of an active murder scene."

"The safe is about to stop being available to us."

"That is not the same thing as permission."

"I know."

Paul looked back at the house. Through the kitchen window Joel could see movement, a blue jacket, somebody leaning over the table where Mallick had probably spread out Cliff's life and Monday night in neat piles.

"If we go in there," Paul said, "and we're wrong, then what."

"Then we lose ten minutes and I get to hear you tell me I talked myself into this because I haven't slept."

"And if you're right."

"Then Cliff didn't die over forty thousand dollars."

Paul stared at the deck boards for a long moment.

"Ten minutes," he said.

"Ten minutes."

They went inside.

---

The study smelled like Martin. Leather from the desk chair. Pine from the paneling. Old scotch sunk into the room over years with the door shut. Joel hadn't been in here since Thursday night, when Martin had done the tour he always did, look at the shelves, look at the lamp, look at the trim he'd redone, as if the house were a project that might one day be finished.

The first deputies had disturbed the room without really searching it. Dust was broken across the desk. One drawer sat open half an inch. Two rectangles on the blotter showed where something had been bagged on Saturday and removed. Nobody had gone behind the painting yet.

It hung a little crooked on the wall, a lake scene Martin's parents had bought at a craft fair in Duluth in 1983 and kept because the frame matched the paneling. Paul lifted it off the hook and set it carefully against the shelves.

The safe door was shut.

"See?" Paul said. "Not opened."

He put a hand on the knob. It turned without the dial being spun. One tick off, just like he'd said.

Inside were folders, a bank envelope, a thick stack of documents bound with a rubber band, and the yellow RT envelope sitting in the back where Paul had left it Monday.

Paul pointed. "That one."

Joel took it out. Old paper smell. Office-supply glue. On the front, in Martin's block letters, two initials.

"I saw it and left it there," Paul said. "I've replayed that about fifty times already, so you don't need to add anything."

"Wasn't going to."

"You were."

Joel didn't argue because he had been.

The envelope flap was tucked, not sealed. Inside were three sheets of yellow ruled paper folded into thirds. Joel started to open them, then stopped.

"What."

"Look."

He held the top page out. The old folds were soft and darkened from time. A second fold pattern crossed them, sharper and a little off. Somebody had opened the document and put it back without matching the original creases.

Paul stared. "Cliff."

"Sunday morning, yeah."

"Or me."

"No. You would've matched it."

That got no answer. Paul had already gone pale.

Joel unfolded the pages the rest of the way and read.

*August 14, 2002.*

*I'm writing this down because somebody should, and because I don't trust myself to remember it right if I don't write it now while it's still clear. This is what happened at the cabin on Wolf Creek Road, Washburn County, Wisconsin.*

*Tommy and I had been there since Friday. Tommy was drinking heavy on Saturday, worse than usual, and usual was already a lot. Around 9 PM he went outside to smoke. I heard the shed door. He came back in around 9:15 and I could smell gasoline on his hands. He said he'd knocked over a can trying to get to a fishing rod.*

*I woke up at about 1:30 AM to the smell of smoke. The shed was fully engulfed. Tommy was not in the cabin. I went outside and found him standing in the driveway watching it. He wasn't moving. He wasn't trying to help. His left arm was burned from the elbow to the wrist.*

*Ray Toomey's truck was in the driveway. I didn't know Ray Toomey was in the shed and I don't think Tommy knew either. Tommy told me later he went back out around midnight because he couldn't sleep. He said he knocked the gasoline can near the space heater and the thing went up. He said he tried to get back in and that's how his arm burned. He said he didn't know about Ray. I believe he didn't know about Ray. I'm less sure about the gasoline.*

*The fire department ruled it electrical. Tommy told them what he told me. I didn't contradict him. I didn't tell them about the gasoline smell on his hands at 9 PM. I didn't tell them Tommy was outside when I couldn't find him. I said nothing.*

*Ray Toomey was 54. He had been caretaker at the property for eleven years. He had a wife in Shell Lake. I learned that from the news.*

*I didn't start the fire. I didn't lie to the fire department. I also didn't tell the truth in any useful way. A man died because Tommy was drunk and careless and I've been protecting Tommy from the consequences ever since.*

*I don't know what this document is. It's not a confession because I didn't kill anyone. It's not evidence because I'm not giving it to anyone. It's a record.*

*Tommy is my friend. I'm going to keep protecting him. I'm writing this down so that at least one true account exists somewhere.*

Joel read it once, then again, slower. The second time the pieces locked where they'd been slipping all day. The will. The weird patience. Martin making sure Tommy kept showing up. Twenty-two years of ordinary-looking behavior with a dead man standing behind it.

He handed the pages to Paul.

Paul read in silence. Halfway down the second page he took his glasses off, cleaned them on his shirt, put them back on, and started that paragraph again from the top as if cleaner lenses might change it.

"Twenty-two years," he said.

"Yeah."

"He wrote it down and put it in a wall." Paul swallowed. "He didn't tell me. He left it here."

Joel said nothing. There wasn't a useful answer available.

Paul looked back at the fold lines.

"Cliff saw this Sunday," he said.

"He saw it, and he put it back wrong."

"Then Monday he gets Tommy alone."

"And after that Cliff starts acting like forty thousand isn't enough."

Paul's eyes stayed on the page. "Jesus Christ."

"Cliff didn't need Tommy for cash. Tommy doesn't have cash. He needed Tommy leaning on the group, leaning on the estate, making sure Martin's money moved where Cliff needed it to move. He thought he had leverage." Joel rubbed a hand over his mouth. "What he had was a bomb."

Paul refolded the sheets carefully, using Martin's old lines, not Cliff's. He slid them back into the envelope but kept it in his hand.

"If Cliff read it, he photographed it."

Joel looked up. "Yeah."

"He wouldn't trust memory with something like this."

"No. He'd put it on his phone."

Which meant the clock was even shorter than Joel had thought. If the extraction had already run, the paper in Paul's hand was only the first version of this story Mallick was going to get.

"Tommy killed him," Paul said.

Joel started testing that sentence for holes on reflex. Not because it was wrong. Because it was early.

"This gets Mallick motive," he said. "It doesn't get her all the way there."

"Joel."

"I'm serious. If Tommy shuts up, this is a dead man's account and a fold pattern and a bad week between two men. It's strong. It's ugly. It's still not the whole case."

Paul cut him off, which Paul almost never did. "I'm not asking for jury instructions."

Joel shut up.

The room went quiet. Somewhere down the hall somebody opened the refrigerator and let it bang shut again.

Part of Joel kept insisting the document was motive, not proof. The rest of him was already settled. Cliff found it. Cliff used it. Tommy answered with the dock.

"Okay," Joel said.

Paul waited.

"Okay," Joel said again. "Yeah. Tommy killed him."

Paul let out a breath, but it didn't make him look better.

"So what now."

"We go to Mallick."

"No."

Joel blinked at him. "No."

"I need to talk to him first."

"That's a terrible idea."

"Probably, yes."

"Paul."

"I'm not protecting him."

"That isn't what I said."

"It's what you're thinking."

Joel took a step toward him. "What I'm thinking is that if Cliff put photos of that document on his phone, Mallick may have them before we finish this argument."

"Then we're already late."

"Which is why we stop making ourselves later."

Paul looked down at the envelope again.

"Martin spent twenty-two years deciding what Tommy got to know and when. I'm not doing that. But I'm not letting Mallick be first either. Not if I can get to him first."

"You think that matters."

"I think I need it to."

Joel opened his mouth, then stopped. The truth was ugly and simple: if Paul went out there alone Joel wasn't going to tackle him in the hall, and Paul knew it.

"One hour," Joel said.

"Joel—"

"One hour. That's me compromising with a plan I hate. You do not take the paper out there. You do not start inventing versions. You tell him we know, and you tell him he walks into that kitchen himself or we do it for him."

Paul nodded once. "Fine."

"If he starts lying?"

"Then I stop listening."

Joel almost laughed at that, not because it was funny, because it was so thoroughly Paul.

Paul set the envelope on Martin's desk instead of putting it back in the safe.

"Leave it," Joel said. "I'm reading it again."

"I figured."

They went out into the hall. The late light had turned amber across the great room. The draft board was still on the wall, seven columns of names in different colors from Thursday night when Martin had been alive and Cliff had been alive and Tommy had just been Tommy, loud and obvious and impossible to miss.

Paul stopped at the slider.

Tommy was already outside on the deck in Martin's chair, a beer on the armrest warming untouched in the last of the light.

"One hour," Joel said.

Paul kept his eyes on Tommy. "I know."

Joel went back into the study, pulled the document out of the envelope again, and reached for a yellow pad.
