# Chapter 11

Paul woke at five-forty on Monday morning with the organized thought that someone needed to go through Martin's things.

Not the bedroom, not again. He'd been in the bedroom once already, Saturday morning, the CPR, his palms flat against Martin's sternum pushing rhythmically into a body that gave back nothing. His hands had been sore for two days afterward, a deep bruise across the heels of both palms that he kept pressing absently, testing. The bedroom had been police evidence, or close enough, though the deputies had cleared out Sunday afternoon and Mallick hadn't actually sealed anything. She'd told them to keep the rooms as they were. Paul still couldn't tell if she'd meant that gently or as instruction.

What he meant was the practical work. Paperwork. Martin was divorced from Linda, no children, self-employed. No HR department filing bereavement forms. No corporate life insurance packet with a named beneficiary and a list of next steps. Someone needed to find Martin's lawyer, the mortgage documents, the account information, the insurance policies. Someone needed to figure out what happened next, procedurally, to a lake house on Elbow Lake when its owner died during a fantasy football draft and five of his oldest friends had each, for reasons of their own, made the scene worse.

Paul was the right person for this. He knew the paperwork of death the way Martin had known the paperwork of money: professionally, intimately, without romance. He'd walked through enough houses after enough losses to know how fast grief turned into forms, signatures, inventories, appraisals. It wasn't a noble skill. It wasn't something he mentioned at parties. But it was what he had.

He got out of the pullout couch in the basement. He and Joel had taken the basement beds after the deputies cleared the upstairs because neither of them wanted to sleep in the rooms next to Martin's. Joel was still asleep, or pretending to be, on his back with one arm over his eyes, breathing with the measured deliberateness of a man who'd been lying awake for hours. Paul dressed quietly. He took the stairs. The third step creaked the way it had creaked for twenty-five years, and this morning he didn't bother skipping it. It didn't matter who heard him.

The kitchen smelled like old coffee and the faint botanical sourness of the green bean casserole Detective Mallick had brought on Saturday and that nobody had touched. Paul could see the edge of the casserole dish through the glass refrigerator door, sitting behind two six-packs and the container of Anne's potato salad. Martin had complimented the potato salad at Thursday's dinner, not generically, but with the excessive precision that was Martin's way. He'd said the mustard-to-mayonnaise ratio was ideal, that Anne understood the fundamentals, that Paul should tell her Martin said so. Paul had relayed the message. Anne, on the phone, had gotten embarrassed and pleased, and Paul had felt the familiar low-wattage irritation of a man whose wife was charmed by his best friend's effortless authority over small kindnesses.

He poured coffee from the pot Nate had set to auto-brew at five. Nate was up somewhere. Paul could hear the low mumble of his voice from the direction of the deck. Narrating, probably. Processing the weekend through the only mechanism Nate had, which was talking into a microphone, organizing experience into segments, finding a rhythm for it. Paul didn't blame him. Everyone had a mechanism. Paul's was paperwork.

He drank the coffee standing at the kitchen window. The fog was low and gray over the lake, the dock dissolving into it thirty yards from shore. A loon called from somewhere in the mist, and the sound carried across the water, closer than the bird actually was. He put the mug in the sink and went upstairs to Martin's room.

The door was open. The bed had been stripped by the deputies, just the bare mattress now, white with a yellowish stain near the center that Paul's brain cataloged automatically as old, pre-existing, unrelated to the death. Sweat, probably. Martin ran hot. He slept without a top sheet half the year and had never bothered with a mattress protector, which Paul had mentioned exactly once, in the context of resale value, and which Martin had received with the look he reserved for Paul's most insurance-brained observations.

Martin's suitcase sat on the floor by the dresser, unzipped, clothes erupting from it in the way that meant Martin had been digging for something specific and had not bothered to repack. Three polo shirts, the expensive kind Martin bought to look casual, as if a sixty-dollar polo was casual. Two pairs of khakis. Boxers. A balled pair of running socks that Martin almost certainly hadn't run in. The dopp kit was on the dresser with the zipper half open. Paul looked through it: deodorant, a disposable razor, a bottle of ibuprofen, dental floss still in its original sealed packaging.

The floss made him stop. Not because it mattered but because it was Martin. Twenty-five years of the same running joke: Martin announcing at every draft that he'd finally committed to flossing regularly, Paul asking to see the receipt, Martin calling Paul the most tedious man in North America, and somebody, Nate usually, pointing out that this was objectively the least interesting recurring conversation in their friendship. Martin's dentist knew he didn't floss. Paul knew. Martin knew Paul knew. The sealed package was the punch line to a joke that had been running since they were twenty-two and would now, Paul understood, never get another round.

He set the floss down on the dresser. He didn't pocket it. He wanted to, and the wanting embarrassed him, because it was a three-dollar package of waxed floss and Martin was dead and Paul's grief was apparently finding its expression in the urge to steal dental supplies.

Martin's reading glasses were on the nightstand, the cheap drugstore magnifiers he'd been wearing for five years, the ones Paul had been telling him to replace with a real prescription. The thriller novel beside them had a folded page about two-thirds in. Martin had been in the middle of something when he died. Paul picked up the book, looked at the cover, set it back. He didn't unfold the page. He left it where Martin had left it, at the last page Martin would ever read, marking a place no one would return to.

The phone charger had been taken with the phone when the deputies bagged it, but the empty outlet beside the bed looked wrong anyway, as if the cord should have still been there, draped down the side table, part of the room's usual clutter. Paul's eye kept going to the blank spot and trying to correct it.

He opened the top dresser drawer because that was what a person doing practical work did. Sunglasses. Two hotel pens. A folded map of Becker County from some gas station promotion years ago. Receipts Martin had shoved in loose, including one from Thursday afternoon for the Busch Light and the ice. Paul looked at the date, the time, the total, then set the slip back down because this was how his mind worked and he knew it and this morning he wasn't interested in correcting it.

The room smelled like Martin's deodorant and something else, the Lagavulin maybe, a whisper of peat smoke caught in the curtains and the pillow and the years. Paul stood in the middle of the room and felt what it felt like to be in his dead best friend's space, going through his things, handling objects that were technically no longer Martin's but were not yet anyone else's either. Same lamp, same glasses, same half-read paperback, and the room had gone wrong anyway. It wasn't waiting for Martin to come back from the bathroom or from downstairs with coffee. It had already started becoming a room other people would empty.

He left the bedroom and went down the hall to the study.

The door to the study was open. It had been open since the deputies went through on Saturday, and Paul had glanced in once or twice without crossing the threshold, the way you pass an office that belongs to someone who isn't there yet. This morning he stepped inside and saw at once what was wrong. The frame behind the desk hung crooked. The safe was already visible in the gap.

The door of the safe stood open about four inches, the dial one tick off locked, Martin's usual habit. Martin had told Paul the combination once, years ago, during one of those conversations Martin began with "if anything ever happens to me" and that Paul had received the way he received all such conversations: gravely, precisely, filing them away in the mental cabinet where he stored things that were probably unnecessary but whose absence would be catastrophic. The combination was Linda's birthday. June ninth. Zero-six-zero-nine. Paul remembered it the same way he remembered policy numbers, automatically and forever.

Someone had been in the safe. Maybe the deputies. Maybe Mallick. But if Mallick had found a will, she would've asked who Martin had named personal representative. She would've said the word estate three times and written it down in that little notebook with the casserole grease on the cover. She hadn't. Which meant either the deputies had handled private papers without noting it, which Paul did not believe, or one of the men in the house had gone into Martin's wall and kept it to himself.

Paul pulled the frame aside, felt the loose wall anchor give a little, and opened the safe the rest of the way.

Inside were three manila folders with Martin's block capitals on the tabs: HOUSE, INVESTMENTS, PERSONAL. A stack of twenties and fifties pinched under a black binder clip. Paul counted without taking the clip off. Nineteen hundred and sixty dollars. A small cream law-office envelope from a Minneapolis estate-planning firm. A legal-size manila envelope with RT written across the front in Martin's hand.

That was all. No missing compartment. No dramatic hidden key. Just records, cash, an estate-planning envelope, and the legal-size thing with two letters on it that meant nothing yet and too much on sight.

Paul took the law-office envelope first.

The flap had been slit cleanly and tucked in years ago. Inside was a clear plastic sleeve holding six folded pages, the notary stamp faintly visible through the top sheet. Paul slid them out and unfolded them carefully, flattening the creases against Martin's desk blotter.

It was Martin's will. *Last Will and Testament of Martin David Kessler*, dated March 14, 2023. Eighteen months ago. Six pages, typed, notarized, with Martin's signature at the bottom of each page, the firm, slightly oversized signature Martin used for everything from contracts to birthday cards. A real lawyer had done it. Not some half-assed internet template. Martin had paid somebody in Minneapolis to make his death legible.

Paul read it standing up, his back against the desk, the safe open behind him.

The first page named a personal representative and revoked prior wills. The second page handled financial accounts and personal property: the investment portfolio to Martin's sister Carol in Duluth, the car to a cousin Paul had met once at a barbecue, a donation to the University of Minnesota Athletic Department. Standard enough. Generous but unsurprising. Martin was organized even in death, which was exactly what Paul would've expected and exactly what Paul had always quietly admired. The man hated loose ends. Of course he'd paid somebody to tie them off while he was still alive.

The third page was the lake house.

Paul read the paragraph once, quickly. He read it again, slower, looking for the comma or buried clause that would change the meaning on a second pass. He read it a third time, word by word, because sometimes a sentence really was as bad as it looked.

There wasn't a second meaning waiting in it. There was just the sentence.

Martin left the lake house on Elbow Lake, all structures, land, and contents therein, to Thomas James Renaldi.

Tommy.

Not Paul. Not the best friend of twenty-five years, the best man, the person with the spare key and the combination and the standing invitation to use the place any weekend between Labor Day and Memorial Day. Not Carol, who'd spent childhood summers here. Not the group collectively, which would have at least made sentimental sense, the house held in some informal trust for the annual draft and the lie that traditions stayed put if enough men kept showing up.

Tommy. The one Martin made room for. The one he covered for in ways Paul had always filed under temperament, affection, group management, any explanation that let the pattern stay small.

Paul flipped to the end of the paragraph, looking for a condition. Maintain the property for the annual draft. Permit the others reasonable use. Consult Paul on major decisions. There was nothing. Tommy got the house and everything in it. Clean sentence. No side letter. No hidden little courtesy clause that said Paul, at minimum, understood the place better than anybody else alive.

He sat down in Martin's desk chair. The chair creaked under him, the wood-and-leather protest it had been making for years. The armrests were smooth where Martin's forearms had rested during decades of calls and calculations. Paul put his own arms there and felt the shape Martin's weight had worn into the leather.

He looked at the will again. Page three. *Thomas James Renaldi.*

What arrived was not grief. Grief he could have handled. Grief was appropriate, expected, the kind of feeling that should have made him quieter. What arrived instead was flatter and more humiliating: jealousy. His best friend of twenty-five years had been dead four days, and Paul's first response to the will was the petty, undeniable indignation of a man who had been assuming, for a quarter century, that he was Martin's person.

He had been Martin's best man. He had driven Martin to the lawyer during the divorce from Linda, five separate trips, because the proceedings dragged and Martin refused to handle it by email. He'd sat with Martin through two Christmases after the divorce when Martin was alone and drinking too much, Lagavulin in quantities that made Paul count ounces and then stop counting because counting wasn't helping and Martin wasn't listening. He'd lent Martin his truck three times. He'd listened to Martin talk about the consulting business for what amounted to probably a thousand cumulative hours over twenty-two years, and he had never, not once, said what he sometimes thought, which was: Martin, I have my own work, and my work is boring to you, and you have never once asked me about my day the way I ask you about yours.

He was nowhere else in the will, either. Not as backup personal representative. Not in the scotch or the draft binders or any keepsake at all a man might leave the friend who knew how the house worked. Martin had managed, with six pages and legal witnesses, to turn Paul into an omission.

And he had left the house to Tommy.

Paul set the will down and looked back into the safe. The three folders were stacked in clean order. HOUSE. INVESTMENTS. PERSONAL. He opened HOUSE first because his hands needed something to do.

Inside were exactly the documents he would have expected Martin to preserve: deed, title insurance, property tax statements, dock permit paperwork, the septic inspection from two summers ago that Martin had complained about for forty straight minutes because the county fee had doubled. The neatness of it infuriated Paul. Martin had organized even his absence.

He checked INVESTMENTS next and then PERSONAL, not because he thought either would explain the will but because Paul's brain hated an unresolved file. Statements, beneficiary forms, a photocopy of Martin's passport, an old Army discharge document, a handwritten list of passwords that looked three years out of date. No letter saying *Deck, here's why.* No confession. No apology. No paragraph that turned the bequest into anything other than what it was.

He copied the phone number from the law-office envelope onto a yellow legal pad from Martin's desk. The office wouldn't be open yet. It was six-fifteen. By the time somebody answered, Paul would either have decided to be practical about this or he wouldn't. The number stared up at him in his own handwriting, tidy and accusing.

Then he looked at the legal-size envelope.

The letters RT had been written with the kind of pressure Martin used when he was labeling something he intended to keep a long time. The flap was still sealed. Several pages, maybe more than several. Thick paper. Handwritten, maybe. Paul picked it up, felt the weight shift, then turned it over once in his hands.

If he broke that seal now, alone, with the safe already disturbed and the will still hot in him, he became one more man in this house handling a dead man's record in private. Later there would be no clean way to say what had been in the envelope before Paul touched it, or what shape his reading of it had taken with jealousy already in the room. If somebody else had opened the safe before him, the chain was already dirty. He did not need to make it dirtier.

He put the RT envelope back. Then the will, back into the clear sleeve, back into the law-office envelope. He returned the folders in their order and slid the cash stack into place under them. When he was done, the safe looked the way it had looked when he opened it, except that now Paul knew what was in it and the knowledge itself felt like a disturbance.

He left the frame hanging crooked. He left the safe door cracked the same four inches. There was no point pretending the room had not already been entered. He tore the sheet with the lawyer's phone number from the pad, folded it twice, and put it in his pocket.

---

The chair Paul sat in on the deck was the one he thought of as Martin's, even though Martin had never claimed it out loud. It was the Adirondack nearest the lake, angled to catch the afternoon sun, with a knot in the left armrest where Martin rested his scotch glass, a natural coaster he'd discovered their first summer here and used every year since. Paul knew about it because Paul noticed things like that, and Martin knew Paul noticed things like that, and neither of them had ever seen a reason to mention it.

Paul sat in the chair now. Not claiming it. Just sitting in it and looking at what Martin looked at when he sat here.

The fog had lifted. The lake was silver and flat, and across the water a pontoon boat was cutting a slow, purposeless path, trailing a wake that took a long time to reach the near shore. The pines along the bank were dark against the brightening sky. The scrap of paper with the lawyer's number pressed against his thigh each time he shifted.

He thought about the time Martin showed up at his house with a chainsaw.

It was 2017, maybe 2018. A spring storm had dropped a branch the size of a small tree onto Paul's garage. Not catastrophic, but the branch was too heavy for Paul to move alone and too awkward for a simple saw. Paul had scheduled a tree service for the following week. Martin arrived Saturday morning without calling, a borrowed chainsaw in the bed of his truck and a look on his face that said he had already decided how this was going to go.

Martin had never used a chainsaw. This became evident quickly. He held it like a man who had watched a YouTube video once and retained the confidence but not the technique. He spent four hours cutting the branch into sections while Paul stood in the driveway offering corrections Martin did not accept, and by the end of the afternoon Martin had gouged the garage siding in two places, nicked the gutter, and cut a shallow bleeding line across his own forearm that he dismissed as cosmetic. Paul had pointed out, with the measured exasperation of a man who assessed property damage for a living, that the tree service would've cost a hundred and fifty dollars and the siding repair would run four hundred.

Martin had looked at him, sweating through his shirt, a piece of bark in his hair, the cut on his arm beading blood onto his watch, and said, "Yeah, but they'd have taken three weeks to schedule it, and you'd have spent every one of those nights calling me about the estimated damage to your garage. I know you, Deck. I did this for me."

He was right. Paul would have called. Probably not every night, but often enough that Martin's preemptive strike with a borrowed chainsaw was, in its stupid and destructive way, an act of genuine understanding. That was Martin. His generosity was aggressive and sometimes more expensive than the problem it solved, but it came from knowing people, knowing what would keep them up, what they'd worry into a project if left alone too long, and deciding to handle it before they could.

Paul sat in the chair and missed his friend with a force that made the jealousy feel petty and the pettiness feel correct, because Martin would have found that funny. Not the will exactly. Paul, in his chair, pissed off enough to sulk about it. He could see Martin on the chainsaw day with bark in his hair and blood drying under his watchband, grinning at Paul's driveway like he'd personally saved the structure. That same grin would have shown up here too. It didn't make the hurt smaller. It just made Martin more himself inside it.

Then Paul's mind, because it would not sit still, went back through the years and landed on 2008.

Tommy's second kid had been born the week of the draft. Paul could still see Martin's email because Martin almost never used email for personal decisions unless the decision was already final. Subject line: `MOVE THE DRAFT`. The body was four lines. Tommy's kid came early. We do next weekend. If the deposit gets weird, that's on me. Sorry for the scramble.

There hadn't been a vote. Nate complained about flights. Joel said the new weekend conflicted with court. Paul had already rearranged things with Anne. Martin held the line and moved it anyway.

Three years later Nate's mother was in the hospital, and Martin said he'd save him a seat if he could make it. In 2016 Joel's divorce hearing collided with the weekend, and Martin told him to send his keepers and stop acting like the world was ending. Only Tommy got the date itself changed.

At the time Paul had read it as loyalty with extra horsepower, Martin protecting the tradition by protecting the one friend most likely to miss it. Sitting in Martin's chair with Thomas James Renaldi still burned into his eyes, it looked different. It looked as if Tommy had always been operating under a separate set of terms.

Another memory surfaced right behind it. The keeper deadline in 2014. Noon on Friday, clear rule, same as every year. Tommy forgot because Eastview had an away game the night before and one of his kids split a chin on the driveway when they got home and the whole day got away from him. At twelve-oh-seven Tommy texted Martin the names with a `shit` in front of them. Paul had texted back that noon was noon. Martin responded to the whole group before Paul had finished typing his second sentence.

He's in. Nobody's getting bounced over seven goddamn minutes.

Nate had called bullshit. Joel, for once, had agreed with Nate. Paul had not enjoyed finding himself on the same side as either of them, but rules were rules and Martin had written half of them himself. Martin took the heat, reopened the window, and told everyone to stop acting like federal judges. Two seasons later Nate missed a waiver deadline by nine minutes because he had "bad cell service at a Buffalo Wild Wings," and Martin told him tough shit, deadlines mattered, set an alarm next time.

At the time Paul had laughed. Nate's excuse deserved to die. Martin's hypocrisy had seemed ordinary, the kind of thing that happened in long-running friend groups where some guys irritated you in one key and some in another. Now it looked like another carve-out. Another place where Martin's fairness bent in the same direction.

There had been other moments, smaller and somehow louder in retrospect. The year Tommy nearly drove home Saturday night after Joel rode him too hard about a first-round pick and Martin disappeared down to the dock after him. The two of them sat out there forty minutes in the dark, talking too low to hear. Tommy came back mean-eyed and embarrassed and stayed the rest of the weekend. The time Martin quietly switched bedrooms so Tommy could have the one with the better fan because Tommy said his room was too hot and he wasn't sleeping. Martin told the rest of them the breaker was acting up upstairs. Paul had believed him until now.

Those moments had always felt like Martin doing what Martin did, managing the room, preserving the weekend, keeping the seven-man machine from losing a part. That was the story Paul had told himself because it was a generous story and because Martin's generosity toward Paul had been real. The Christmases after the divorce were real. The chainsaw was real. The potato salad compliments, the hundred little phone calls, the way Martin would text Paul a photo of some cracked retaining wall and ask, *Total loss or repairable?* all of that was real.

It just had not been the first loyalty in Martin's life.

Paul thought about the safe again. Not the will, exactly. The fact that somebody had gotten there first. The fact that Martin had kept a legal-size envelope in there under two initials Paul didn't recognize. The fact that the one thing Martin had chosen to spell out cleanly and officially, with witnesses and notary stamp and no ambiguity at all, was Tommy.

He knew enough about claims to know when separate data points wanted to become a pattern. Most of his career was people handing him isolated facts and asking him not to connect them. Here the facts were embarrassingly small. A moved weekend. A reopened deadline. A room switch. A man named in a will. Nothing criminal in any of it. Nothing even necessarily secret. But the cumulative shape was wrong. The numbers no longer balanced.

The screen door opened behind him.

Joel came out carrying coffee in one of Martin's blue mugs, barefoot, his hair still flattened on one side from sleep or from lying awake and refusing to admit it. He stopped when he saw Paul in the chair.

"You been up since five?" Joel asked.

"Something like that."

Joel looked toward the lake, then back at him. "You find anything useful?"

There it was. The easy opening. Tell Joel. Pull the folded paper from his pocket, say Martin had a will, say Tommy got the house, say the safe had already been disturbed, say there was another envelope in there and Paul didn't know what it meant but it felt bad in his hands. Joel would listen. Joel would parse the language. Joel would probably get angry on Paul's behalf, which Paul might even have enjoyed.

Instead Paul said, "House paperwork. Financial stuff. I'm sorting it."

Joel took a sip of coffee. "That's not a no."

"No," Paul said.

Joel let that sit. He was too good a lawyer not to hear the gap between the words Paul used and the words he didn't. But he also knew Paul well enough to recognize when pushing would only make him retreat farther.

"All right," Joel said. "Just don't sit on whatever it is until Cliff turns it into a breakfast conversation."

That made Paul look at him. "Why Cliff?"

Joel shrugged. "Because Cliff turns everything into a breakfast conversation. Or a real estate seminar. Same thing."

It was a cheap line, not quite a joke, but it carried enough truth to land. Paul almost told him then. The safe had already been touched. Cliff had been weird all weekend. The connection was flimsy and maybe unfair and maybe exactly the kind of leap Paul professionally distrusted. Still, his mind had already made it.

But the will was too fresh. The jealousy was too fresh. He didn't trust himself yet, and he trusted himself most in precisely those moments when he should not have.

"I'll tell you if it's something that needs telling," Paul said.

Joel nodded once, accepting the phrasing without endorsing it. "Okay."

He stayed another minute, standing at the rail with the mug in both hands, looking out over the water like a man trying on silence and finding it itchy. Then he went back inside. The screen door banged shut behind him.

Paul sat a while longer.

Nate's voice started up again somewhere around the side of the house, lower now, maybe not recording after all, maybe just talking because the alternative was thinking in full sentences. A truck moved along the county road out front and was gone. The pontoon on the far shore completed its lazy circuit and disappeared behind a stand of birches. The lawyer's phone number stayed folded in Paul's pocket, a practical next step he could take whenever he wanted and therefore, for the moment, did not take.

Martin had been his best friend. Paul did not doubt that. Martin's friendship with Paul had been real, the conversations, the Christmases, the chainsaw, the twenty-five years of showing up. All of that was true.

It just had not been the first loyalty. That belonged, somehow, to Tommy, and the will made it impossible to keep calling that casual.

Paul sat in the dead man's chair with the lawyer's number in his pocket and felt the humiliating comedy of a man who had spent twenty-five years thinking he knew the load-bearing facts of a friendship and had only just learned he was standing second to something he couldn't yet name.
