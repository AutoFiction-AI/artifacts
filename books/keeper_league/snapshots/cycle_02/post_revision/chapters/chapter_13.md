# Chapter 13

If Nate looked at the great room from the right angle, Monday almost passed for any other draft-weekend cleanup. Football on mute. Bottles on the coffee table. Six men arguing about rules Martin used to pretend he hated settling because pretending he did not love power was part of his whole thing.

Joel ended that fiction in under a minute.

"So are we dealing with the roster or what?"

Nobody answered.

"Martin's roster," Joel said. "Season starts Thursday. His team's sitting there. Somebody has to decide what happens."

Tommy was stretched out on one end of the couch, one ankle on his knee, beer balanced against his stomach.

"His team plays."

"That doesn't answer the question."

"It answers it fine."

Paul said, "I think Joel means bye weeks, injuries, waiver claims. The team still needs a manager."

Cliff lifted his beer from the armchair by the window.

"Let it auto-manage. Let the asset keep running. No need to get cute."

"Auto-manage is dogshit," Nate said. "Auto-manage started Dalton over Herbert last year. I did twelve minutes on it because I was furious and because no one in my real life respects boundaries."

Tommy pointed the bottle at him.

"Also because no app gets to finger-fuck a dead man's lineup."

Joel stared at him.

"That is a revolting phrase."

"It's the right phrase."

"On the merits," Joel said, "I unfortunately agree."

Nate put a hand to his chest.

"Finally. Constitutional law I can use."

Paul was in Martin's recliner by the fireplace, which felt wrong even before Nate decided whether Paul knew it.

"I'm not saying the app should do it," Paul said. "I'm saying one of us does it. As a tribute, maybe."

"Tribute," Nate said. "That sounds like a district in a dystopian YA book."

"You know what I mean."

Tommy sat up.

"No. No fuckin way. Not you."

Paul blinked.

"Why not me?"

"Because you took Kittle in the second round because you got horny off one preseason clip of him running without a limp."

"I did not get horny."

"You absolutely did," Nate said. "Deck, you drafted thighs. It was a body-based evaluation."

Paul looked around the room for backup and got nothing.

"That is not what happened."

Tommy kept going.

"I wouldn't let you pick a stripper with somebody else's cash. I'm not letting you run Martin's flex."

Joel said, "The court would like it noted that those are two separate skill sets."

Doug laughed into his water, quick and quiet, then looked like he wished he had not. The pint glass in his hand was plain water, no label, no sweat, nothing to hide behind. He still had that old pharmaceutical-sales reflex of checking the room before he answered, as if a sentence could have side effects.

Nate said, "Doug, you're on the floor with hydration and judgment in your eyes. What do you think?"

"Paul should do it," Doug said.

"Why Paul?"

"Because he'd do it." Doug took a sip. "The rest of us would forget by week six."

"You think I'd forget?"

"You'd turn it into content."

That one got the room. Nate opened his mouth, closed it, and pointed across the coffee table.

"Cheap shot. Fair. But cheap."

Cliff took another drink.

"Tommy's right, though. Let the team play. Let the app handle the maintenance."

That made two quick agreements with Tommy in less than ten minutes. Nate kept his face still and logged it anyway. Tommy and Cliff usually called timeout the second they landed on the same side of anything.

"All right," Nate said. "New issue. If nobody likes Paul and nobody likes the robot, what about a committee? Weekly text chain. Somebody says start the backup running back, somebody else says no, Martin would never, we all feel bad on our phones."

Joel looked over.

"That is not a committee. That is grief with notifications."

"It's participatory democracy."

"It's seven men trying to ventriloquize a dead friend in a group chat. That sounds miserable."

Paul said, "It also turns every lineup decision into another argument."

"And we are famously so rested for more arguments," Nate said.

Cliff said, "Then keep it simple. Auto-manage or Paul. Bright line."

Tommy rubbed a hand over his mouth.

"You don't get to make him a project. That's the part you keep not hearing."

Paul's voice stayed even.

"I am hearing it."

"No, you're hearing the words. You are not hearing the thing."

Nate let that sit a second because Tommy did not usually bother separating words from the thing unless he was already pissed.

Joel said, "Then articulate the thing."

Tommy looked at him.

"No one gets to improve Martin now. That's the thing."

Nobody said anything.

Tommy leaned forward, elbows on knees.

"You leave his guys in. If somebody's on a bye, fine. If somebody tears an ACL, fine. You don't start getting cute with matchups like you're saving him from himself. He picked that team. That's his team."

Paul nodded once.

"Okay. I can see that."

Cliff said, "Inactive-only management."

Nate turned to him.

"You said auto-manage forty seconds ago."

Cliff gave a small shrug.

"I'm adjusting."

That was not really an answer either, but it was the answer Cliff was offering, polished and easy, so everybody let it through.

Nate said, "What about waivers, though? Are we allowed to improve the team or are we preserving his last known bad decisions like they're constitutional text?"

Joel said, "Injuries and bye weeks force moves. Aesthetic corrections do not."

"Aesthetic corrections," Nate said. "You make fantasy football sound like zoning."

"I make everything sound like zoning. Stay with me."

Paul said, "Martin would hate auto-manage more than death."

That got them all looking at him.

Paul spread a hand.

"I'm serious. He checked weather for kickers. He benched a defense once because the opposing quarterback had 'good shoulders,' which still means nothing to me. The man did not outsource."

"That was not his reasoning," Nate said.

"It was part of his reasoning."

Tommy said, "He also held grudges against entire franchises. Guy wouldn't draft a Charger for six years."

"Because they were spiritually unserious," Nate said.

"Because Marty was a lunatic," Joel said.

"Both can be true."

The room loosened for a minute. Nate could almost feel everybody leaning toward the old shape of it, the one where Martin would bark from the kitchen that they were all wrong and then somehow win anyway.

Then Cliff said, "So Paul handles injury subs, Nate handles commissioner settings, and the team stays basically as drafted. Clean enough."

Tommy looked at him.

"You in a hurry to close this file?"

Cliff smiled without much showing.

"I like a decision."

"Bullshit," Nate said before he could stop himself. "You like selling the feeling of a decision. Different product."

Cliff tipped his bottle at him.

"That's hurtful, Griggs."

"It's market research."

Doug said, "He is right, though."

Everybody turned toward him because Doug using a complete sentence was enough to quiet a room.

Doug stared into his water.

"About leaving it mostly alone, I mean. Martin would've hated the app doing it. He would've hated us pretending we knew better too."

He took another sip and shrank right back out of the spotlight.

"Fine," Nate said. "No app. No full necromancy. Limited intervention. We are preserving competitive integrity and honoring the dead and also, somehow, still taking this incredibly seriously."

Joel pointed at him.

"We should also freeze trades."

Nate turned.

"Can you trade with Martin's team?"

Joel gave him the full Beckman deposition stare.

"You want to trade with a dead man."

"I don't want to. I'm asking whether it's possible."

"Those are not materially different questions."

"If the platform accepts it automatically," Nate said, "then technically nobody is exploiting grief. They're exploiting code."

Joel said, "We already have a detective asking why five of us touched a crime scene. I don't think we need to add dead-man trades to the pile."

Tommy rubbed his face again.

"No trades. That's corpse-fucking with projections."

Joel shut his eyes for a second.

"Again, appalling phrasing. Again, correct."

Paul said, "No trades."

Doug nodded.

Cliff said, "No trades," so fast it sounded rehearsed.

The room went still on that one. Not the peaceful kind. Cliff had that answer loaded too fast, and everybody heard it.

Nate said, "Okay. Cool. Great. Love the way a sentence can die in here."

No one helped him.

"We need a commissioner," he said, because silence had always felt like a personal challenge.

"You," Tommy said. "You've already got the bullshit vocabulary."

"I don't want the job."

"Nobody wants the job."

Paul said, "Martin did."

"Martin wanted control," Joel said.

"For him that was love," Paul said.

Nobody laughed, but nobody argued either.

Nate rubbed his face hard enough to feel stubble.

"All right. I'm interim commissioner of a league with no bylaws, a dead owner, and scoring settings imported from the Bush administration."

Tommy leaned forward and pointed.

"Don't touch punt return yards."

"I wasn't going to."

"You touch that, I'm driving across this room."

"Noted."

"Seriously, Griggs."

"I'm serious too."

Joel said, "Waiver priority has to redistribute. Martin had third. That's the only fair ruling."

"And free agency?"

"Injury and bye-week replacements only," Paul said. "Not optimization."

"No one gets to decide Martin finally believed in tight ends," Tommy said.

"That was never going to happen anyway," Nate said.

Doug said, "Could make one exception."

They all looked at him again.

Doug's mouth twitched.

"If a guy gets arrested."

That got a bigger laugh than it should have. Nate pointed at him.

"There he is. Quiet assassin."

Doug ducked his head.

"I'm just saying."

Joel said, "Inactive, bye, injury, incarceration. There's our standard."

"That is an insane sentence," Nate said.

"And yet a legally coherent one."

Paul said, "Then that's the rule."

Cliff nodded.

"Clean."

There it was again. Not agreement exactly. Eagerness for the meeting to end. Nate felt it and did not know what to do with it, so he did what he always did and turned it into noise.

"Fine. Waiver priority redistributes. Martin's team stays alive. Paul babysits it without touching anything gross. I do commissioner paperwork. Punt return yards stay because Coach is threatening domestic violence. You can replace a player if he's hurt, on bye, or in handcuffs. Anything else?"

No one answered.

Nate looked up at the red circles over the fireplace. Martin's picks sat there in Martin's handwriting, a whole season waiting on a guy who was already out of it.

That was when Nate realized he could not remember the last time Tommy and Cliff had gone a full room without needling each other.

---

They grilled steaks because it was Monday night and because no one in the house knew what else to do with meat Martin had already bought.

Before they went out, Nate went downstairs for a sweatshirt and came back with his phone and clip mic looped around his wrist. The basement got bad service and too much silence. The deck at least gave him one bar and other people's voices.

Tommy saw the wire.

"What the fuck is that."

"Commissioner's tape," Nate said. "If one of you claims tomorrow I changed waiver order in the dark, I want the record."

Joel said, "That is not what records are for."

"It is what my records are for."

He set the phone near his plate and clipped the little mic to the wire basket in the center of the table beside Martin's salt grinder. The red timer started climbing.

"Jesus Christ," Tommy said. "You recording us eat now?"

"I'm recording governance."

"You're recording steak noises."

"And history."

Tommy looked like he wanted to tell him to shove the thing in the lake, but he turned back to the grill instead. Nobody else objected either. Martin had let Nate turn draft weekends into scraps and cold opens and voicemails nobody asked for for years. Half this friend group probably lived in Nate's cloud without consent already.

Tommy worked the grill in his usual stance, feet planted, tongs in his right hand, beer in his left. He swore at flare-ups, checked the sear too often, and told Nate to quit hovering before Nate had moved within six feet.

"I wasn't hovering."

"You were preparing to hover."

"I was scouting."

"You scout with your eyes, not your breath. Back up."

Nate backed up.

"You know, in some counties that's verbal abuse."

"In some counties your podcast is a cry for help."

That got a real laugh out of Paul. Even Cliff smiled.

Nate looked toward the table. His phone sat beside the basket, screen dimming, little red bar still there. He had told himself it was for notes and because Mia's texts only came through on the deck. The truth was thinner than that. He had not wanted the night to go unrecorded. Not because he thought anything was going to happen. Just because Martin was dead and Monday still kept trying to look like Monday, and the mismatch of that felt worth trapping somewhere.

By the time the steaks came off, the table on the deck had gone from bright to amber. Eight chairs around it. Martin's chair at the head stood empty, and Tommy and Cliff took opposite ends without discussion. Three open seats lay between them. Nate counted because he counted everything.

He took the side facing the lake. Paul sat on his left. Doug sat across from him with the same water glass. Joel took the chair beside Doug. No one mentioned that they were eating a dead man's ribeyes off a dead man's deck with a dead man's salt grinder in the middle of the table and Nate's mic clipped to the basket like it had been invited.

Tommy set the platter down.

"Anybody asks for sauce can eat my whole ass."

Joel cut into his steak.

"Your hospitality has really matured."

"Steak sauce is ketchup for cowards."

Paul said, "I wasn't going to ask."

"You looked like you were thinking about it."

"I was thinking about potatoes."

"Same face."

Doug made the little huff he used instead of a laugh. It was good steak and a good line, good enough for a minute.

Nate raised his beer.

"All right. To Martin. Patron saint of impossible scoring settings and meat bought in quantities that suggest psychosis."

"That is a terrible toast," Joel said.

"Improve it, then."

Paul lifted his glass.

"To Martin."

That went around cleaner. Even Tommy did it. Even Cliff. Doug raised his water last, almost apologetic about it. They drank, and for one second Nate had the stupid, physical certainty that Martin was about to come through the slider asking who overcooked the steak.

He did not, obviously. The phone kept recording. Somebody's knife touched ceramic. The lake moved against the shore below them with that quiet slap it did when the air started cooling off.

Beyond the deck, the last of the light held on the water. Birches on the far shore had gone yellow at the tips. Somewhere in the reeds a bird made one thin sound and stopped.

Cliff kept his eyes on the lake.

"Nice night," he said, in his open-house voice.

Tommy said, "You sell the sunset and the dock comes free."

Cliff gave him half a smile.

"You want me to write up the listing, Coach, I can do that."

Tommy cut his steak.

"House comes with six alcoholics and one felony interview. That's a hard sell."

That clipped the smile off Cliff's face for a beat. Joel looked down at his plate. Paul drank. Three bites in, Doug quit making progress and started cutting the same piece smaller and smaller. Water caught the porch light in the wrong way.

Across the table Joel kept flicking looks at Paul. Paul gave him the smallest nod.

Nate reached for the salt grinder and saw the red timer before the screen dimmed again. Forty-three minutes. Forty-three minutes of grill hiss, cutlery, Tommy saying ass like it was a food group. He left it alone.

Then Joel looked up and said, louder than he needed to, "Anybody remember when Martin walked off the end of the dock?"

The whole table took that like a life raft. Nate did too. Better Martin wet and furious than another ten minutes of them pretending waiver priority was a moral question.

"Oh, shit," Nate said. "Twelve. It was twelve."

"RG3 year," Paul said immediately. "We were arguing keeper value."

Joel pointed with his fork.

"He did not slip. He did not trip. He just kept backing up like the dock still had real estate behind it."

"He was making a speech," Nate said. "Not even a good one. He wanted fumble return touchdowns in the scoring like he was introducing civil rights legislation."

"Four scotches deep," Paul said.

"That's not drunk for Martin," Tommy said. "That's warmed up."

Doug said, "Boat shoes too."

Joel started laughing before he finished chewing.

"Boat shoes with no socks. Christ."

Paul put his fork down.

"The khakis went clear the second he came up."

Nate slapped the table once.

"Everybody at that table saw more Martin than they ever asked for."

Joel said, "I want that sentence out of the record."

"Too late," Nate said. "We are on tape, counselor."

Tommy said, "Looked like somebody vacuum-sealed a kielbasa."

Doug bent over his plate laughing. Even Paul laughed hard at that, the kind with no manners on it.

Tommy was laughing too, chin tucked, shoulders loose for once, and for a second Nate thought maybe that was all this night was going to be: six assholes being disgusting about a dead friend in the way that would've made him happiest. Then Tommy looked back at his plate before Cliff could catch his eye.

Nate pointed across the table.

"No, the worst part was his face. He came up mad. Not embarrassed. Mad. Like the lake had interrupted him."

"It had," Joel said. "He surfaced already arguing."

"He raised one hand," Paul said, lifting his own without thinking, "and he goes, 'Motion still carries.'"

Nate and Doug said it with him.

"'Motion still carries.'"

Joel wiped his mouth.

"Standing in three feet of Minnesota lake water with his phone drowned in his pocket, and he's citing Robert's Rules like a goddamn parliamentarian."

Doug said, "I brought him a towel."

"You did," Paul said.

"He wouldn't take it."

"Because he wanted a second," Nate said.

Tommy pointed with his fork.

"I gave him the second because his balls were trying to climb back into his torso."

Joel put a hand over his eyes.

"That is medically disgusting."

"It's medically true," Nate said.

Doug was still laughing.

"He told me to keep the towel warm till the vote closed."

Paul said, "Then he climbed back up and blamed Cliff for the whole thing."

"I was protecting statistical continuity," Cliff said.

"You were being a cheap, nitpicking asshole," Tommy said.

"I was preserving the historical record."

"It's fantasy football, not the fuckin Dead Sea Scrolls."

"He made us vote again," Paul said. "That was the part that killed me. Full recount from the dock."

"Because the first vote happened under adverse conditions," Joel said.

Nate could feel how badly he wanted the room to stay right here, in this dumb safe pocket where the worst thing Martin ever did was fall in and come up arguing.

"Because he was insane," Nate said.

That one broke the whole table again. Six men laughing on a darkening deck, loud enough to carry over the water, and the phone in the middle of them taking it all down like this was worth preserving.

Cliff stayed with it a second longer than the rest. He wiped his mouth with his napkin, looked down the table at Tommy, and said, "You were halfway out of your shorts before Martin even came up. Fastest I've ever seen you move."

The old version of the night had an answer there. Nate could feel it climb into his own throat before Tommy even looked up, the automatic fuck you, the insult, the pile-on that would follow.

Tommy even looked up, ready for it.

It did not come.

Tommy set down his fork.

"Anybody need another beer?"

No one said yes.

Tommy carried two empties inside and came back with two fresh ones anyway. One went to Nate. The other went to Cliff. Tommy set it beside Cliff's hand without looking at him.

"Thanks," Cliff said.

"Yeah."

Nobody reached for the joke again. Plates resumed. Knives on ceramic. Doug's water glass touched the table a little too carefully. Joel looked at Paul, held the look, then let it go.

Paul said, "What time are people heading out tomorrow?"

"After breakfast, probably," Joel said.

"I should get on the road by nine," Paul said. "If I leave later than that, the Cities turns into a parking lot."

"I'm in no hurry," Cliff said. "I've got calls to make either way."

Tommy chewed, swallowed, drank.

"I'm staying till after lunch."

"To clean up?" Nate said.

"To not drive tired."

"Very responsible. Very adult. Hate this version of all of us."

Paul gave him a look.

"You should probably leave early too."

"What, because my life in Minneapolis is so organized?"

"Because you look like shit."

"That is friendship, right there. Honest feedback."

Doug said, "He's not wrong."

"Brutal. Absolutely brutal table."

That got another small round, not the full break this time, just enough to keep the thing breathing.

By the time Paul said, "I'm going to start clearing this," the sun was down to a strip behind the trees.

Joel stood and stacked plates. Doug collected glasses. Nate reached for the basket to unclip the mic and saw the red line still moving across the top of the screen. An hour and change gone already. He let it keep running while he gathered forks into a pile.

Cliff took the empties, plus the lone water glass, and carried them inside to the recycling bin by the kitchen door. His loafers made a soft indoor sound on the hardwood. Tommy stayed at the railing with both hands flat on the top board, looking out toward the dock.

The slider kept opening and shutting. Plate stack. Sink water. Somebody laughing once inside and stopping quick.

Nate had the phone half lifted when Cliff came back out with a dish towel in his hand. Instead of heading for the table, he stopped beside Tommy at the railing.

"Coach," he said, low but not low enough. "Ten-thirty. At the dock. Give me ten minutes."

Tommy did not turn.

"For what."

"To finish the conversation from this afternoon."

"No."

Cliff folded the dish towel once.

"You don't have to enjoy it. Just give me ten minutes."

"We're not doing business tonight."

"Then don't call it business." Cliff's voice stayed light, smooth, too smooth. "Ten-thirty. Dock. We talk, then we're done."

Nate could see the red timer on the phone screen still climbing between his fingers.

Tommy's jaw moved once.

"Ten minutes," he said.

Cliff nodded like they had agreed on a tee time.

"Appreciate it."

Then he turned back into the house with the same easy pace he used for everything, like he had just asked for more ice.

Nate looked at Tommy. Tommy was still staring toward the water.

"What the hell was that," Nate said.

"Nothing."

"That sounded pretty specifically like something."

Tommy picked up his beer.

"Go inside, Griggs."

They peeled off a man at a time after that. Doug first, with a quiet good night. Joel next, one more glance at Paul on his way in. Paul stopped at the slider with a hand on the frame, thought better of it, and went upstairs. Cliff said, "Night, boys," to the general air from inside the great room.

Nate stayed on the deck with Tommy, phone and mic still in his hand.

"You and Cliff doing real-estate detention later?"

Tommy kept his eyes on the lake.

"No."

"That sounded like a scheduled no."

"Drop it."

Nate waited a beat longer than he should have.

"We're all right?"

A lone bird called out in the dark. Tommy's fingers tightened on the railing.

"Sure," he said.

Nate did not move.

Tommy said, "We're fine, Griggs."

It ended the conversation whether Nate believed it or not.

"All right," Nate said. "Night."

"Night."

Tommy went in. A few seconds later the third stair gave its usual crack, then the hall above the great room carried his steps away.

Alone, Nate looked out at the black water and the one porch light burning across the lake. It was somebody else's dock, somebody else's house, somebody else's ordinary Monday night. In his hand the phone screen had gone dark again, still warm from recording.

He hit stop. The file saved without a title.

After locking the slider, Nate went downstairs to the basement. The pull-out waited there with his duffel and the sweatshirt he had forgotten to put on. His phone lit with Mia's last text telling him to come home safe, the new long memo sitting above it in Voice Memos like any other stupid scrap he would probably never use.

The opener hit him anyway. It was habit, reflex, brain damage.

He set the mic on the blanket, opened a fresh memo instead of the dinner one, and looked at the red record button.

"So here's the thing about a keeper league," he said to the empty room.

That was as far as he got.

Back upstairs, a floorboard moved. Then the house went still again.

Nate stared at the tiny new waveform jumping with his breathing. He listened to himself breathe for two seconds, hit stop, and deleted only that file.

The longer dinner recording stayed where it was, unnamed. The phone went facedown beside the mic. In the dark, the refrigerator kicked on and kept running.
