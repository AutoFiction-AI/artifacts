# Chapter 12

Tommy was alone on the deck for the first time since Saturday morning. Monday afternoon, sun low enough to turn the lake copper, and maybe fifteen minutes before somebody came out to check on him or ask if he wanted another round of Mallick's questions or start talking about Martin in that careful tone everybody had found since the body came out of the bedroom.

He had dragged one of Martin's Adirondack chairs to the rail so he could put his feet up and look at the water. A warm can of Busch Light sat in both hands. He wasn't drinking it. He was palming it the way he palmed a football before a snap drill, not because the beer mattered but because empty hands felt worse.

It had been two nights since Martin. Not even three full days, and the house already felt like a locker room after a loss nobody knew how to explain. Men kept picking up cups, setting them down, starting sentences, dropping them halfway through. Paul had spent the morning in Martin's study doing whatever Paul did when he was processing, which usually meant turning grief into categories. Joel was somewhere in the house, still angry at a man who couldn't catch any more of it. Doug had been on the couch with the same magazine page since breakfast. Nate was in the basement with his phone, which Tommy was trying not to think about.

Mallick had gone back to Detroit Lakes. She was working the case, supposedly. Tommy didn't buy it. That woman filed people the way Tommy filed opponent tendencies. She filed tells and mistakes and weak side and strong side. She'd be back.

The screen door opened and banged shut behind him.

"Hey." Cliff's voice. Smooth as ever. "Brought you one."

A cold Busch Light came into Tommy's peripheral vision. Cliff stood there freshly shaved and too put together for a lake house, like he'd stepped out to show a shoreline lot instead of sit on a dead friend's deck.

"Thanks." Tommy took the cold can, set the warm one on the boards, and cracked it. Cliff lowered himself into the chair beside him, closer than he usually sat.

"Hell of a view," Cliff said.

"Yeah."

"Martin always had taste. I'll give him that. The man knew property. The rest of life, maybe less."

Tommy drank. It was cold enough to register.

"You doing okay?" Cliff asked.

"I'm fine."

"You look like hell."

"I look like a guy who's been trapped in this house since Saturday with a dead friend and a detective who thinks I redecorated in my sleep. So yeah. Hell."

Cliff laughed, not the showroom laugh, an actual short bark. "Fair. Listen, I need to talk to you about something."

"Okay."

"It should've been a simple conversation. Now it's not."

Tommy turned toward him. Cliff had gone into work posture, shoulders pitched forward, beer on his knee, listening face already in place. Tommy had watched him do this at cookouts and draft nights and open houses. Cliff sold things. That was the center of him. He sold condos, he sold confidence, and right now he was trying to sell Tommy a version of what came next.

"What conversation?"

"The estate. The forty Martin owed me."

Tommy shrugged. "Call a lawyer. That's not me."

"Sure, and I will if I need to. But if it gets formal, I go to the back of the line behind family and paper and everybody else's feelings about a dead man. I don't need a hearing. I need the room to understand the debt was real."

"We all know you two did deals."

"Doing deals and owing forty grand aren't the same sentence." Cliff took a drink. "You've got weight with this group, whether you like hearing it or not. If you tell Paul Martin meant to square me up, if you tell Joel this isn't me inventing a debt over a corpse, that changes the temperature."

"We were friends."

"You were close in a way the rest of us weren't." Cliff lifted a hand. "Not that. I mean Martin watched out for you. Everybody saw it. He made sure you came back every year, even when you tried to skip. You two had your own side conversations. Out here, usually."

"What's your point?"

"My point is you say Martin meant to pay me, people believe it faster." Cliff looked back at the lake. "I've got three projects stalled and one note already in default. Forty grand matters right now."

Tommy watched the water. A north breeze had come up and chopped the copper light into pieces. The air smelled like pine and lake water and garlic from inside. Paul was probably making pasta again, because feeding people was how Paul pretended he wasn't panicking.

"I can talk to Paul," Tommy said. "If Martin owed you the money, he owed you the money."

"He did."

"Then it's not a hard conversation."

Cliff nodded, but silence sat between them longer than Cliff usually let silence sit. He rolled the cold can between both palms and stared at it like he was reading numbers there.

"There's one more piece," he said.

Tommy waited.

"Sunday morning I went into Martin's study. The safe was practically open. Dial one click off."

"Okay."

"I was looking for paper on the debt. Found some. Found an envelope too."

Tommy tightened his grip on the can. "What envelope."

"Manila. Marked RT."

The lake kept moving. Somewhere past the dock, a loon called. Inside the house the refrigerator kicked on with that old mechanical groan Martin had been promising to fix for fifteen years.

"Doesn't mean anything to me," Tommy said.

"Yeah. I figured you'd say that." Cliff's voice stayed smooth, but the polish had a harder backing now. "I opened it because I thought RT was a property code or an address. It wasn't."

Blood beat in Tommy's ears, the same thick rush he used to hear on the sideline when a kid stayed down too long and the whole field got quiet.

"I don't know what you're talking about."

"It was Martin's handwriting. August 2002. Washburn County. A caretaker named Ray Toomey." Cliff spoke in the careful, even tone he used when he was telling buyers about rot under a porch. "It wasn't hard to figure out why you two never discussed it in front of the group."

Tommy set the beer on the armrest. His hand stayed steady. The scar along his left forearm had started itching for the first time in years.

"You read it."

"I did."

"All of it."

"Enough."

"You took pictures."

Cliff met his eyes. The salesman face was still there. The math behind it had changed. "I document things. Habit."

"Fuck you."

"Call it whatever you want." Cliff spread one hand. "It's relevant."

"You're blackmailing me."

"No. Blackmail is cash or cops. I'm talking about an arrangement." He kept his voice mild, almost patient. "You want that letter to stay buried. I want the money Martin owed me. Those goals don't have to fight each other."

Tommy looked at the craft beer, the leather loafers, the hands soft enough to look moisturized. He outweighed Cliff by forty pounds, and every pound had been built on purpose, in weight rooms and on practice fields. Across from him sat a man who thought leverage was cleaner than force because leverage left his hands looking nice.

The coaching part of Tommy's brain had already stopped hearing the sales pitch. It was doing field-position math instead. It marked the distance to the dock, what sound carried over water, what kind of body could do what to another body and how long it would take. The shape came into his head and stayed there.

"What exactly do you want?" Tommy asked.

"Simple version? I want you to move the room." Cliff leaned back, relieved to be on terms he trusted. "You tell Paul the debt's real. You tell the others Martin meant to make me whole. You keep this from turning into six guys litigating whether I deserve to get paid."

"That sounds like litigating."

"No, it sounds like not wasting a week on performative outrage while my business bleeds out." Cliff took another drink. "I'm not asking you to lie about a number. I'm asking you not to let people make it personal because they always make it personal where money's concerned."

"It's already personal. Martin's dead."

"Exactly. Which means reason's in short supply." Cliff kept his voice low. "You've got the only vote in that room that can make this feel settled instead of ugly."

Tommy stared at him.

Cliff mistook the stare for uncertainty and kept going. Salesmen hated silence the way linemen hated open space.

"Look, I'm not trying to hurt you here. I found something I wish I'd never found. But I did find it, and now we're both living in the world where I found it. So let's be adults about that."

"Adults."

"You know what I mean."

"Yeah," Tommy said. "I know exactly what you mean."

Cliff nodded, hearing agreement where there wasn't any yet. "Good. Then we're in business."

Tommy picked the cold can back up. The metal bit at his palm. He let Cliff sit in the confidence for one more breath.

"Okay," he said.

Cliff blinked. "Okay?"

"I'll talk to Paul. I'll help move it."

"That's a smart answer."

"It's not about smart. Martin owed you. He should've paid you."

"He should've, yeah."

"So I'll help."

Cliff studied him for a beat, deciding which version of Tommy he was looking at: angry Tommy, cooperative Tommy, or the too-still one. Then some private ease came over his face. He'd heard yes, and yes was all Cliff ever really listened for.

"Good," he said. "And the letter stays where it is."

"Nobody sees it."

"Right."

---

The memory never came back as a story. It came back as smell first, then temperature, then small pieces his body had kept after his mind refused them.

It was August 2002 at a cabin in Washburn County. He was twenty-five. Martin was too. The place belonged to somebody's family, or somebody's cousin, or somebody's friend from Eau Claire, and Tommy had stopped trusting himself on that part years ago. What he remembered cleanly was the feeling of being twenty-five and invincible enough to treat borrowed property like scenery.

They were outside Friday night with a bonfire going and too much beer already in them. Tommy remembered the lighter-fluid can in his hand because Martin kept telling him that was enough and Tommy, being twenty-five and full of his own strength, took that as an instruction to squeeze harder. After that, the sequence went soft around the edges. Maybe the wind shifted. Maybe dry brush carried it. Maybe the can flashed and he never saw the flash because he was laughing when it happened. However it moved, the fire was on the porch before Tommy had caught up to what he'd done.

The porch was old cedar, dry enough to go up in a hungry run. It caught with a crackle so loud it sounded amplified, not like a barrel fire or a pit fire but like something finally getting fed.

Martin yelled. Tommy ran toward it because that had always been his first instinct. Go at the problem. He snatched the hose from the side of the cabin and got nothing, no pressure, just a loose handle spinning in his hand. Then he dropped it and ran for the side yard where he thought the propane shutoff might be, because movement still felt like action even when it wasn't.

That was when he saw the truck.

A blue Ford pickup sat behind Martin's car, dull paint gone almost green in the firelight, mud dried around the wheel wells. It hadn't registered when they arrived. Maybe it had been tucked under the trees. Maybe they had been too busy hauling beer and talking shit to notice. Either way, there was a truck in the driveway, and the truck meant what the truck always should've meant. Somebody might be inside.

He did not know the name Ray Toomey yet. He only knew there was a cabin on fire and a stranger's truck in the dark.

He ran to the front door, grabbed the handle, and got hit by heat so hard it felt like a man shoving him backward. His left forearm clipped the metal frame and the skin cooked there on contact. He screamed, dropped back, and the door slammed shut. Inside, a beam or part of the ceiling came down. The room sealed itself. After that there was no voice. No movement. Just the fire climbing.

He tried once more from the side window and got smoke in his face so thick he gagged. Martin came around the corner then, eyes wide in a way Tommy had never seen on him before, and grabbed him by both arms.

"We have to go. Now. Tommy. Now."

"There's somebody in there."

"I know. We have to go."

"Martin."

"Look at me." Martin's hands dug into his shoulders. "There's nothing you can do for him. We need to go."

And they went. Martin drove. Tommy sat in the passenger seat with his arm throbbing and his mind doing the one thing it would never fully undo, which was accept that he had killed a man. It was not on purpose. He had not known anybody was inside. He had still killed him. He had been drunk and loud and stupid and too pleased with force to bother checking a driveway. The result was a dead man in a burned cabin and Martin driving thirty miles under the limit because Martin had already moved to what came after while Tommy was still inside the first fact.

Tommy remembered the drive in pieces too. He remembered burned skin and spilled beer. He remembered the window open because the cab smelled like smoke and melted nylon and his own arm. Martin's jaw locked so hard the muscle jumped at the hinge. He remembered a gas station somewhere outside Spooner where Martin bought bottled water and a towel and told Tommy not to touch the burn with lake water again if he wanted to keep the arm.

At the clinic the next morning, Tommy told the nurse it was a grease burn. Deep fryer accident at a friend's place. Martin stood beside him and did not blink. The nurse either believed them or didn't care. Tommy never found out which. The scar healed into a white ridge that always announced itself to a hand before it announced itself to an eye.

Three days later, in a McDonald's parking lot off Highway 53, Martin held up a local paper and read the headline in a flat voice: WASHBURN CO. CARETAKER DIES IN ELECTRICAL FIRE. That was when Tommy learned the name. Ray Toomey. Martin's eyes had gone dead in a way Tommy had never seen before. No anger. No panic. No grief. Just shutdown.

"They're calling it electrical," Martin said.

Tommy was still staring at the paper. "It wasn't."

"I know."

"We need to tell somebody."

Martin folded the paper once. Carefully. "And say what."

Tommy had no answer ready for that because every answer led straight back to his own hand on the lighter-fluid can.

"Tommy."

"We left him there."

"I know."

"We left him there, Martin."

Martin looked out the windshield. Cars slid past on Highway 53, families and contractors and people who had no idea two men were parked under a McDonald's sign trying not to become the worst thing they'd ever done.

"We don't talk about this again," Martin said.

Tommy waited for more. There wasn't any. Martin was already in the part of his mind where bad things became files. Tommy was still in the part where they were flames.

At some point after that, Martin wrote his own account and sealed it in an envelope marked RT. Tommy knew that much because Martin told him there needed to be a record in case anything ever came apart. Tommy never asked to read it. He knew what his own body remembered and what it could not line up cleanly anymore. He did not want Martin's version climbing into his head and taking over the parts he had spent years trying not to replay.

Denise asked about the scar on their third date. Tommy said, "Cooking accident. I was twenty-five and an idiot." Both parts were true. Neither part was the truth.

For twenty-two years Martin checked the seal. The first few Septembers he did it bluntly, pulling Tommy aside by the shed or the dock path and asking if he was sleeping, if he was drinking too hard, if he needed to get his shit together. Later it got quieter. Martin would put a hand on Tommy's shoulder when a bonfire climbed too high. He would take the lighter fluid away without making a joke of it. He would turn off a news story about a cabin fire halfway through the weather. Sometimes it was only Tommy's name said low, with that extra beat under it. Tommy would nod. Martin would nod. Another year would go by.

Martin was the valve. He kept the pressure sealed, kept the thing contained, kept Tommy from having to find out what kind of man he was if the seal ever broke. Tommy had never asked why Martin took that job on for twenty-two years. Why Martin did things was Martin's department.

Now Martin was dead. The valve was gone. Cliff Haywood was sitting on the same deck, talking about twenty-two years of ruin like it was a debt stack he could restructure if everybody stayed calm.

---

Cliff rolled the cold can against his knee. "One housekeeping item."

Tommy looked at him.

"The photos from Sunday are on my phone, and my phone backs up automatically. I'm only saying that because I don't like misunderstandings."

"So if the letter disappears, you've still got it."

"That's right."

"Anybody else have it?"

"Not the images." Cliff held his gaze. "I'm trying to keep this contained."

Tommy let that sit there. It meant the phone, the cloud, maybe a lawyer too, maybe not. Cliff was the kind of man who believed a paper trail was what separated professionals from morons.

"You'll get your money," Tommy said. "This week I talk to Paul. Then we're done."

Cliff nodded once. "That works for me."

"You don't say Toomey's name again. You don't come back to me with this again. No second conversation."

"Clean break."

"Yeah."

Cliff stood, stretched, and set his empty can on the deck rail for a second before thinking better of it and putting it on the boards. Even now he was staging surfaces. "I appreciate you being reasonable."

Tommy didn't answer.

"I'm going to see what Paul's doing about dinner," Cliff said. "You coming in?"

"In a minute."

"Don't sit out here too long. It drops fast once the sun goes."

"I know how September works, Cliff."

Cliff gave Tommy a quick smile, small and private and satisfied. Then the screen door banged and the house took him back.

Tommy stayed where he was. The chair itself was newer than the ritual. Martin had bought these Adirondacks the year after the fire, but Tommy had claimed this rail-side spot every September for twenty-five years, armrests worn smooth by beer cans and forearms and men pretending a lake view could absorb whatever they didn't want to say.

The scar on his forearm had stopped itching. Both hands lay flat on the armrests, palms down, fingers spread. Tommy looked at them the way he looked at his players in film sessions when he wasn't judging technique but capacity. What could this body do if the call asked for it.

Cliff's phone lived in Cliff's pocket. The backup lived somewhere Tommy couldn't reach, in a server or a building or some room full of humming lights and people he'd never meet. Cliff, though, wasn't abstract. Cliff was here. Cliff was flesh and bad shoes and a habit of getting close once he thought a deal was done.

Tommy looked toward the dock through the trees. It lay thirty feet out, boards with a slight give under them, wood that would go wet once the air dropped. The stone path to it ran through enough pine and birch to make anybody private before they even reached the water. He didn't let the rest of the thought finish. He only needed to know the problem had knees and shoulders and a pulse.

He picked up the beer Cliff had brought him. The can was still half full. He tipped it over the rail and watched the spill flash once in the last gray light before it vanished into the pine needles.

Then he went inside.

The kitchen had shifted into one of those draft-weekend meals that existed because Martin used to refuse real planning and Paul refused chaos. There was pasta on the stove, garlic bread from Dent in the oven, and jar sauce improved by sausage and whatever red wine had been open since Friday. The football was on mute in the great room. Nate's phone sat faceup by the salt and pepper, charging off the wall with a cord stretched across the counter like somebody had started wiring the kitchen for surveillance and gotten bored halfway through.

"There he is," Nate said from the island. "Coach decided to rejoin civilization. You want a bowl or are you doing one of those weird guy things where grief makes you basically keto."

"Bowl's fine," Tommy said.

"Thank God," Paul said without turning around. "I made enough for eight because I forgot Martin's dead for half a second and then had to keep going."

The room went still for a beat, just long enough for the sentence to land all the way through them.

"Sorry," Paul said.

"You don't have to apologize for pasta math," Joel said. He was posted at the far end of the island with a beer and Martin's commissioner binder open in front of him, as if he might file an objection to the noodles. "That's the first thing anybody's said all day that made actual sense."

Doug was grating Parmesan over a cutting board with the severe focus of a man grateful for a job nobody else wanted. He glanced up at Tommy, nodded once, and went back to the cheese.

Cliff stood by the sink with a fresh beer already open, sleeves pushed neatly to the forearms. He had come in off the deck and gone right back into the room like nothing had happened out there. That more than anything made Tommy want to hit him. The ease of it.

Paul handed Tommy a bowl. "Can you grab the bread?"

Tommy pulled the tray from the oven and set it on the stove. Butter and garlic rolled into the room. For a minute everybody moved around one another with the old choreography, the kind you only got after years of standing in the same kitchens opening the same beers. Joel found plates. Doug carried bowls. Nate stole a piece of bread and yelped because it was too hot. Paul swore at him on instinct, then looked startled at himself for having done something normal.

They sat where they always sat when Martin wasn't there to assign people without admitting he was assigning them. Tommy took one end of the couch. Doug dropped into the recliner nearest the lamp. Nate stayed half perched on a barstool until Paul told him to either sit or start helping. Joel took the dining chair he liked because it let him swivel and make points with his whole torso. Cliff chose the armchair by the window, of course, the one with a view and no need to share an armrest.

Nobody said grace. Nobody had ever said grace at the lake. They ate in the ugly quiet of hungry men who had spent three days forgetting to be hungry.

Nate broke first. "This is deranged," he said through a mouthful of bread. "Not the pasta. The vibe. The vibe is deranged. We're six guys eating grief spaghetti while ESPN runs a Chiefs segment nobody can hear. If I described this on air, people would think I was reaching."

"Don't describe it on air," Joel said.

"I said if."

"You shouldn't even have the phone out."

"It's charging."

"It's always charging when it's also recording."

"That's slander and also technologically confused."

Paul rubbed his eyes. "Can we not do this tonight."

"We're not doing anything," Nate said. "I'm sitting here respectfully charging a device in the presence of multiple witnesses."

Tommy looked at the phone. The screen was lit. A little red waveform moved almost too small to see.

Nate caught him looking and pulled the phone closer with one finger. "Ambient only. I wasn't kidding. Silence in this house is making me itchy."

"Then turn on the game," Doug said quietly.

Everybody looked at him. Doug shrugged, still staring at his bowl. "I'm just saying. Mute makes it worse."

Nate reached for the remote and clicked the volume up two notches. A studio crew started arguing about preseason usage. The sound was bad and too bright and exactly what the room needed.

Paul set his fork down. "Before this turns into another night of everybody talking over everybody, there are a couple things we probably do need to figure out. Not legal stuff. Just practical stuff. Food tomorrow. Who's staying. If Mallick comes back."

"She'll come back," Joel said.

"Probably."

"Definitely."

Paul exhaled. "Okay. Definitely. That too."

Cliff dabbed sauce from the corner of his mouth with a napkin. "Since we're on practical stuff, I do need to put one item on the table."

Joel closed his eyes. "Jesus Christ."

"I'm not asking for a vote right now." Cliff kept his tone careful, almost reasonable enough to be insulting. "I'm saying I don't want the money Martin owed me getting lost because everyone feels bad and then three weeks from now somebody pretends they never heard about it."

Paul looked at him across the table. "Tonight?"

"When would you like me to start having money problems, Paul."

"If this is about the duplex again," Joel said, "I need everybody to know in advance I'm hostile."

"I'm aware you're hostile as a baseline." Cliff didn't look at him. "Forty thousand isn't gossip. It's a real number. I'm just trying to keep it from becoming a future misunderstanding."

Paul's face tightened. Tommy could see the calculation there, the claims-adjuster part of him already building folders and columns whether Paul wanted it to or not.

"I found some paperwork in the study," Paul said. "I haven't sorted what any of it means yet."

Cliff nodded like they were discussing weather delay on a job site. "Understood. I'm not asking you to sort it mid-pasta. I'm asking you not to treat it like I made it up because Martin isn't here to answer."

Nobody said anything.

That was the moment Cliff had bought with the deck conversation. Tommy felt it arrive. The room waited. Paul's eyes slid to him because Martin's name and Cliff's tone had both dragged him into it.

"He's not making it up," Tommy said.

Paul kept looking at him. "You know that."

"Yeah."

"How."

Tommy shoved another bite of pasta into his mouth and chewed before answering. A little stall, just enough to feel normal.

"Martin talked about it before. Not numbers every time, but enough." He set his fork down. "If he owed Cliff, he owed Cliff."

Joel leaned back in his chair. "That's not exactly documentary support."

"Nobody asked for a fucking affidavit," Tommy said.

"No, but since we're talking about money owed by a dead man, some level of specificity wouldn't be crazy."

"Specificity is Martin borrowed forty and didn't pay it back."

"According to Cliff."

"According to me too."

The room changed on that one, not loudly but enough.

Cliff lowered his eyes at the right second, like he was embarrassed to be defended. Tommy hated him for knowing the exact second.

Paul picked his fork back up, then put it down again. "Fine. I hear you. We can deal with it once I know what I'm looking at."

"That's all I'm asking," Cliff said.

"No," Joel said. "That is not all you're asking. You're asking us to preload sympathy before we've seen a page."

"I'm asking not to get buried in grief theater while October eats my ass alive."

That got Nate's head up. "Well, now we're talking like men."

Doug made a sound that might've been a laugh.

Paul said, "Can we maybe not say 'grief theater' at dinner."

"Fair," Cliff said at once. "Bad phrase."

"Terrible phrase," Joel said.

"I said fair."

Tommy kept eating. He didn't taste any of it now. He just tracked voices, positions, hands. Joel's leg kept bouncing. Paul's thumb rubbed the edge of his fork. Cliff relaxed enough to lean back and cross one ankle over a knee, loafer hanging there like the room owed him softness.

The conversation drifted because drift was what this group did when the alternative was admitting they were scared. Nate started talking about auto-draft settings. Joel told him auto-draft was an insult to sentient life. Paul asked if anybody knew where Martin kept extra trash bags even though it had nothing to do with anything. Doug said they were under the sink, voice so low they made him repeat it. For five or six minutes the room almost found its old stupid shape.

That was the dangerous part, not the shouting but the almost.

Tommy took his bowl to the kitchen when he was done. Paul was already at the sink running water over plates with more force than the plates required.

"You okay?" Tommy asked.

Paul gave a short laugh that wasn't one. "Not especially."

"Yeah."

Paul kept spraying sauce from a plate. "Did Martin really talk to you about Cliff's money?"

Tommy dried his hands on a dish towel he didn't need. "He mentioned it."

"Mentioned it how."

"Like it existed."

"That is a dodge, Tommy."

Tommy leaned a hip against the counter. "What do you want from me."

"An answer."

"I gave you one."

Paul shut the water off. In the sudden drop of kitchen noise, Tommy could hear the TV crew in the great room and Nate laughing too hard at something Joel hadn't meant as a joke.

"Okay," Paul said finally. "Then here's mine. Cliff can wait until tomorrow. I'm not having a debt meeting tonight."

"Fine."

"If he pushes, that's still my answer."

"Fine."

Paul looked at him the way coaches looked at tape they knew they were going to have to revisit later. "You don't have to say fine like I'm assigning sprints."

"I'm saying it like I heard you."

Paul nodded once and turned the water back on. Conversation over. Or paused. With Paul it was hard to tell.

Tommy dried two bowls because standing there with a towel looked more harmless than going back to the couch too fast. From the island he could feel Cliff watching the kitchen without seeming to. Tommy did not look back.

When he returned to the great room, Joel had the commissioner binder open on his lap and Nate had moved his phone to the coffee table beside a stack of coasters, still screen-up, still listening. Doug was pulling at the label on his beer. Cliff had traded the kitchen chair for the armchair by the window, already settling in like he knew where the night was headed.

Cliff caught Tommy's eye just long enough to tip his beer toward the hall. It wasn't dramatic, just a tiny motion that said later.

Tommy sat down.

Nate said, "I would like it noted for the record that if we kill the league now, Martin wins from beyond the grave, because nothing would make him happier than controlling the room after death."

"You say that like it's a joke," Joel said.

"It's half a joke. Maybe sixty-forty."

Paul came in with a dish towel over his shoulder and lowered himself into the chair Martin used to call the bad-back chair even though nobody's back actually improved in it.

The football kept talking at low volume. Outside, the lake went from gray to black without anybody watching it happen.

Cliff stood to get another beer. On the way back he paused behind Tommy's shoulder like he was waiting for somebody to move a foot.

"Later tonight," he said, quiet enough that it should've passed for nothing. "Ten-thirty. Dock. Let's finish the conversation where it's calm."

Tommy didn't turn around. "Fine."

Cliff kept moving.

Tommy watched the dark window over the television. In the glass he could see the room doubled. Six men, a dead friend's house, a coffee table crowded with bottles, Nate's phone glowing red to show it was still running, and the black shape of the dock path beyond the reflection like the house had grown a second hallway straight into the trees.
