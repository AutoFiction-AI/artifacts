# Chapter 7

Doug took his pills in the basement bathroom with the door locked. Unnecessary. Nate was upstairs, talking through the floorboards in that pitch he used when he wanted other people to hear how calm he was, as if calm could be proved by volume.

Lexapro first. Escitalopram oxalate, 10 milligrams, once daily with or without food. The white sticker carried his name in hard black print, Douglas Whitfield, apartment number in Richfield beneath it like an address somebody else had borrowed. Clinical data said absorption ticked up a little with food, and Doug hadn't eaten since yesterday's lunch. Dry swallow. Chalk on the tongue. Three months now, same as the divorce. The prescription had arrived two weeks after Steph left. Maybe it was working. Maybe he was just getting used to waking up in a different life.

Ativan next. Lorazepam, 0.5 milligrams, as needed. It was needed.

For years he'd sold both classes of drug, back when brand names still mattered and lunch got you into the back office. Now he tipped one into his palm and took it from the tap with the same hand he'd once used to pass out sample cards. The mirror showed nothing new except the number on the scale back in Richfield: twenty-seven pounds gone since Steph said she was done. Best diet he'd ever been on. Hard to recommend it with a straight face.

Both bottles were in his duffel now, zipped into the side pocket under a rolled pair of socks and a phone charger he'd brought for Nate and already forgotten to hand over. They'd started the weekend in the shared upstairs bathroom, tucked between Tommy's industrial bottle of Advil and Paul's toothpaste after everybody dumped their gear there on Thursday evening. Toothbrushes, razors, Cliff's moisturizer nobody was allowed to mention, the same clutter they made every year. Doug's bottles had sat there through Thursday night and all of Friday. Around three Saturday morning, he went up and got them.

That was after Nate shook him awake to say Martin was dead. After the six of them stood around in the great room not believing it. After Paul pounded on a chest that wasn't coming back. After somebody said call 911 and somebody else said wait. While the house creaked and men moved in other rooms and a toilet flushed overhead, Doug took the stairs, skipped the third step because everybody knew better, opened the medicine cabinet, and lifted both bottles out with his thumb over the labels so they wouldn't click together.

Not because of evidence. Not because of detectives. Because he could already see the question arriving in a stranger's mouth and six old friends' faces behind it.

*Are you under psychiatric care, Mr. Whitfield?*

Tommy would go flat and careful. Joel would store it. Paul would do that insurance face that looked like concern and audit at the same time. Nate's whole face would say *shit, Doug*. Cliff wouldn't say anything at all, which would be worse.

Twenty-five years with this group had left him with habits. He gave up the chair with the lake view. He took the beer run nobody remembered assigning. He laughed before a story stalled out. He volunteered for cigarettes, ice, garbage, all the little errands that let a man leave a room without anybody asking why. The bottles would change that. So he took them. If that counted as tampering, it was tampering by shame.

Upstairs, the kitchen was bright with lake light. Paul had made coffee, strong as ever because Martin liked it that way. Martin's Lagavulin still hung in the great room, smoke and iodine caught in the paneling. The bottle stood on the counter with two fingers left in it, cap off because Martin never screwed the cap back on. Somebody had moved the foil-covered casserole to make room for the coffee maker. The casserole was still there anyway, the whole house smelling faintly of onions and browned cheese under the coffee and the lake damp and the old scotch in the wood.

Doug poured into the blue mug with the chipped handle and skipped the cream. Cream in coffee still brought Steph back whole: her feet tucked under her on the couch, both hands around the mug, blowing once across the top even when it did not need it. She used to leave a half-inch in the bottom every time. Said the last swallow was always punishment.

Tommy was already on the deck, working the railing with both hands, pace-turn-pace, knuckles cracking in little bursts. Morning light caught the scar on his left forearm. Grill accident, Tommy had said years ago. Maybe. Doug had never asked. Not asking was part of what he did for people.

At the table, Joel sat with nothing in front of him. No mug, no phone, no newspaper, just his jaw moving like he was trying a case in his own head and the witness kept changing the answer.

"You eat?" Paul said.

"Not hungry."

Paul slid the bread bag six inches closer anyway. "You should anyway."

"Yeah. Probably."

Paul gave him the usual once-over, the one that started as friendship and drifted too easily into triage. Martin's version had always been cruder. Martin would have shoved toast at him and told him to quit looking like a nineteenth-century orphan. Paul just let the bread sit there like a quiet recommendation and turned back to the stove.

Nate came down the stairs in the middle of a sentence and kept going. Mallick hadn't asked about the hot tub yet, right? Because the chemicals had to matter somehow. Chlorine or bromine or whatever Martin used, and maybe that was nothing, but what if it wasn't nothing, because she'd asked him about the coffee maker and who asked about a coffee maker unless they were building toward something.

"Nobody asked about the hot tub," Joel said.

"Not yet."

"She won't."

"How do you know that?"

"Because it isn't relevant."

Nate looked at Doug for backup. Doug gave him a small nod, meaning nothing in particular, and Nate took it as permission to keep spiraling.

Cliff came in from the hallway showered and looking ready for a client meeting, coffee mug in hand, the only man in the house who seemed to believe Monday still existed. He topped off his coffee, asked if anybody wanted toast, and set bread in the toaster with the brisk competence of a man who thought order might still be available if you moved fast enough.

"Toast?" Cliff asked him.

"I'm good."

"It's sourdough."

"I'm fine."

Orange coils lit behind the toaster slots.

Coffee in hand, Doug moved to the corner of the sectional by the window. The draft board still hung on the wall. Martin's commissioner picks were circled in red, same as always. Kelce in the second, which made everybody groan. Lamar in the third, which started a five-minute argument Thursday night. In the seventh, a rookie receiver nobody had heard of, followed by Nate asking if Martin was drafting him or adopting him. Martin had smiled through all of it. That was what Doug kept catching on now, not the jokes or the arguments, just the smile and the little gap between Martin's front teeth when he liked his own move.

The board turned the room into a before-and-after picture. Thursday night, Martin had been alive and needling everybody. Now the same red circles looked like somebody had marked evidence.

Paul reached past him for the bread bag, then the spoon. Nate checked Doug's face for a signal and took a nod for permission. Cliff asked about toast because he was asking everybody, not because he expected Doug to complicate the morning. Out on the deck Tommy kept cracking his knuckles. Joel sat at the table with his jaw working and let his face say whatever it wanted.

From the corner of the sectional Doug had a clean view. Tommy was trying to wear panic out through his hands. Joel had gone so still it looked deliberate. Nate kept talking because silence would make him hear himself think. Paul was making coffee and acting like competence could keep a body in the room. Cliff looked like he had already arranged tomorrow.

---

After lunch Mallick found him on the deck with the paperback he'd brought from Richfield open on his knee and unread.

"Mr. Whitfield? Doug? You got a minute?"

He had nothing else to do.

She settled into the next Adirondack chair with the leather notebook in her lap and the click-pen she'd been worrying all morning. The glasses on the beaded chain bumped lightly against the notebook when she wrote.

"We talked a little yesterday," she said. "I just had a couple things I wanted to make sure I had right. How'd you sleep Friday night?"

"Fine."

"All the way through?"

"Yeah."

"Some of the other fellas mentioned noises. Footsteps and a door and all that. Basement might've been quieter, though."

"It was."

Her pen scratched. She looked back toward the house.

"I keep looking at that board in there," she said. "All those names and the little red circles — somebody told me Martin did those himself? The commissioner, is that right?" She flipped a page back. "How'd you two know each other, you and Martin?"

"College. Same group."

"Were you close?"

"We were friends."

"A good friend?"

"He was good to me."

That made her look up. Not pounce, exactly. Just look up.

"In what way?"

Doug thought of July. Not the vague idea of July. The actual call. Wednesday afternoon, too hot outside, blinds shut in the apartment because the sun on the beige carpet made the whole place look temporary. He had been half asleep on the mattress on the floor when Martin called instead of texting.

*Did I wake you?*

*No.*

*Whit, it's three-forty.*

Doug had laughed then, too fast. Said he worked in the field and the field was very flexible. Martin had let that go by without even the courtesy chuckle. Then he asked what Doug had eaten. Doug said coffee. Martin said coffee was not food. Doug said divorce had been good for portion control. Martin said bullshit, flat and immediate, and told him to get up and open the refrigerator.

Now Doug said, "He called me in July because I sounded half asleep in the middle of the afternoon. Asked what I'd eaten. Made me go look in my fridge."

Mallick waited.

"There wasn't much in it," Doug said. "I made a joke about the divorce diet."

"And he?"

"Said bullshit. Told me to come to the draft."

She wrote that down slower than the rest.

"That's a pretty specific kind of friend."

Doug looked out at the water and let that sit there.

"That sliding door," she said after a second, nodding toward the house. "Does it always rattle like that? I keep thinking somebody's coming in and it's just the wind working at the frame." She made a note. "Anyway. Friday night, after the hot tub and everybody finally shut up — what time did you go downstairs?"

"Midnight. Maybe later."

"And then you slept straight through."

"Yeah."

"Didn't hear Nate get up?"

Doug almost said *he's quiet* and stopped there, because even that sounded like more than he wanted on paper. "Didn't hear him."

Mallick nodded as if she'd been handed something useful, though all he'd given her were three or four flat stones.

"Anything else over the weekend strike you funny before Saturday morning?" she asked. "Arguments, bad blood, somebody acting off?"

Doug could have given her plenty. Joel's shut jaw on Thursday night. Tommy's extra voltage. Cliff asking Martin about roof age and property taxes like he was already halfway into an appraisal. Even Nate, working too hard at being Nate. He carried those scraps around because nobody ever asked him to do anything with them. Mallick, with her casserole manners and notebook, was probably the first person in the house who would.

"I don't think so."

"No?"

"They're always like this." Doug shrugged. "Twenty-five years. There's always something."

"Fantasy football," she said, smiling.

Doug didn't return it. The energy wasn't there.

She asked about the basement door, the lock, whether anybody else stored things down there. Doug answered in the fewest words available and let the gaps stay open after each one.

"Just you and Nate sleeping down there?"

"Yeah."

"Anybody use that little bathroom besides you two?"

"Not much."

"Storage room too, though? Laundry, bags, extra towels, all that?"

Doug felt the question in his ribs before he felt it in his face. "Some bags."

"Mm-hmm. People always spread out in places like this. You set down a duffel Thursday and by Saturday it lives there." She made another note. "Funny how fast a weekend gets itself organized."

Most people rushed to fill silence. Mallick didn't. She just let it sit between them, then put down one more line in the notebook.

When she stood, she paused the way she'd paused after every interview all weekend, leaving a little space for a last-minute correction or confession. Doug gave her nothing. Silence was the only thing in the house he trusted himself to handle better than she did. Something in her expression shifted, not annoyance, not surprise either. Recalculation. Then she nodded and went back inside.

Doug stayed on the deck until the sliding door shut behind her. Then he took the stone path through the pines toward the dock, dry needles giving under his shoes.

---

The dock pushed thirty feet into Elbow Lake, boards silvered by weather except for a few newer ones Martin had replaced over the years. A bench sat near the end. The swim platform was beyond it. Clear water below, sand and stones and a scatter of old beer caps sunk into the bottom. Near one piling a school of minnows held steady against nothing visible, all of them facing the same direction as if there'd been an instruction Doug had missed.

Doug sat on the bench with his feet hanging over the side.

The place still knew how to be itself. Soft September light lay on the water. Trees were just starting to turn on the far shore. A motorboat droned somewhere out of view, and its wake reached the dock late and gentle. Doug thought about the apartment in Richfield with the beige carpet and the neighbor who watched *Jeopardy!* loud enough to feel personal, then about the gray towels from Target, the mattress on the floor, the blank walls. The lake house pushed back with its own inventory: splinters in the dock, old beer caps under the water, Joel once coming up from a cannonball with blood running down his shin and suing Martin for negligent dockwork until Martin paid him off with a sixth-round pick.

That got a smile out of him.

The July call kept coming back because Mallick had made him say enough of it out loud to wake the rest. He had gone to the refrigerator that day because Martin would not let him stay horizontal and wiseass his way out of it. Mustard. Two beers. Soy sauce. A bagged salad gone wet. Half a takeout carton that had started to fur over in one corner.

*Read it to me,* Martin had said.

*I'm not doing inventory for you.*

*Then give me the highlights.*

Doug had read it because Martin had already heard too much. The slow voice. The air conditioner rattling in a room with no other sound. The way Doug kept trying to turn the call into a bit and Martin kept refusing to laugh. When Doug got to the takeout carton, Martin said, *Jesus Christ, Whit. That's not a fridge. That's evidence.*

Doug smiled despite himself now, remembering that. At the time he had leaned his forehead against the refrigerator door and said Martin sounded like a narc. Martin said good, maybe a narc was what Doug needed. Then he said, in the same plain irritated voice he'd use to tell somebody the grill was out of propane, *Come to the draft. You can sit in the basement and say six words all weekend. I don't care. Just come. I'm not doing this with you from Richfield.*

Doug had tried one more deflection. He said he had territory meetings. He said the timing was lousy. He said maybe next year, after things settled down.

Martin had cut straight through that too. *You're a forty-seven-year-old man sleeping on a mattress on the floor and living on mustard. Don't tell me about timing.*

No speech after that. No soft landing. Just the truth said in Martin's ordinary voice, which was worse because it meant he had not reached for drama to make the point. Doug had laughed because he wanted the call over. Martin stayed quiet until the laugh died on its own.

Then he said, *Tell me now if you're coming.*

Doug had come because Martin noticed things other people let pass. The weight loss, yes. The midafternoon sleep, yes. But more than that, the cheap way Doug kept trying to make his own deterioration easier to look at. Martin would let a joke breathe if it earned the air. He would not let one do the work of an answer.

That was the surprise now, sitting on the dock with the water knocking softly against the pilings. Martin dead, detective in the house, pill bottles hidden in a duffel, and Doug was still glad he'd come. These men were oblivious and exhausting and at least half full of shit, but the weekend gave him what the apartment didn't: noise, history, other bodies in the rooms. Martin had understood that much before Doug did.

A loon called out on the lake. By the time the sound thinned away, the light had gone from gold to gray.

Doug went back up the path and let himself into the house. The kitchen windows had gone blue. He ate one cold piece of Cliff's sourdough standing at the counter, heel of bread, no butter.

From the great room came Mallick's voice, easy and apologetic.

"Well, now, I keep mixing up what was where in this place. Those little basement windows face the lake, right? I just want to look one more time. Somebody told me that downstairs bathroom lock sticks and then somebody else said no, that's the upstairs one, and I'd hate to go writing down the wrong bathroom. My mother would rise from the dead just to tell me to label things better."

Paul said, "Basement one sticks sometimes."

Doug stopped chewing.

The duffel was still beside his pull-out. Side pocket half unzipped from the morning. He saw it all at once without seeing it: orange cap, white label, his name in block print, Mallick's eyes settling there for half a second and never leaving it alone again.

"I can show you," he said.

The words came out too fast. Paul and Mallick both looked over.

"You don't have to," Paul said.

"It's fine." Doug set down the crust and was already moving. "I'm heading down there anyway."

He took the basement stairs two at a time. The lower level smelled like detergent and old lake towels and the stale sweetness of the basement bathroom cleaner Martin bought in bulk. Nate was on his own pull-out in socks, holding his phone up near the little lake-facing window as if two extra inches might convince the service bars to return.

"Any luck?" Nate said without looking over.

"No."

Doug crossed to his sofa and yanked the duffel toward him. The side pocket caught on the blanket. He pulled harder. The zipper gave, and one orange bottle nosed up through the gap.

He slapped his hand over it before it could hit the floor.

Nate looked over then. "You good?"

"Yeah."

"What're you doing?"

"Moving my stuff."

Nate frowned at the bag. "Now?"

"Apparently."

Mallick was on the stairs before Nate could ask anything else.

"Don't mind me," she called. "I just wanted another peek down here, and then I promise I'll stop traipsing through everybody's sleeping arrangements."

Doug had time for one choice. Shove the duffel into the laundry closet and look panicked, or pick it up and look like he'd meant to move it all along. Planned was closer to innocent. He zipped the side pocket as far as it would go, slung the bag by one strap, and stood.

Mallick came down with the notebook tucked under one arm and her reading glasses low on her nose. Her gaze touched the duffel, left it, came back to it for the length of a blink, then moved on to the room.

"Oh, am I sending you packing?" she said.

"No. Just getting it out of the way."

"Mm-hmm."

She went first to the bathroom door and worked the lock once, twice, a little rattle of metal. Then she stepped to the window by Nate's sofa and looked out toward the darkening lake.

"You boys really can hear the water from down here," she said. "Funny what carries and what doesn't."

Nate still had his phone lifted toward the window. "Cell service doesn't carry worth a damn, if that helps."

"That does help." She wrote something down. "Now, Friday night, if somebody was walking around upstairs, from this sofa maybe you hear it and maybe you don't. Hard to know. Refrigerator kicks on, pipes do their thing, everybody's had a few. Houses tell on you and cover for you at the same time."

Doug stood with the duffel hanging from his hand. The strap cut into his fingers. Setting it down would have made it a normal thing. Not setting it down made it worse. He kept standing there anyway.

Mallick turned and gave the room another slow look. Pull-outs. Folded blankets. Laundry closet. Doug with his overnight bag clenched like he expected somebody to frisk it.

"Folks keep most of their luggage down here?" she asked mildly.

"Some of it," Doug said.

"Any reason somebody besides you and Nate would've been in and out during the night? Towels, medicine, toiletries, whatever people forget they need till two in the morning?"

The word *medicine* hit him hard enough he thought Nate would hear it.

"Maybe towels," Doug said. "Not much else."

Her pen paused. "Mm-hmm."

She asked Nate two more questions about what he had heard and when. Nate answered in fits and starts, phone still in his hand, giving her three possible times and a theory about bad acoustics that he did not really believe himself. Doug barely heard him. Every shift of the duffel brought a soft plastic knock from inside the side pocket. Tiny sound. To him it was a church bell.

Mallick finally tucked the notebook shut against her palm.

"Well. Thank you, gentlemen. I appreciate it. And I'm sorry again for the marching around. Pretty soon you'll all be rid of me."

Nobody said that seemed likely.

She went back upstairs. Her steps faded. The house settled around them again.

Nate lowered his phone.

"You leaving?" he asked.

"No."

"Okay."

That was one of the reasons Doug could stand Nate in doses. Nate could talk for an hour when he was scared, but if he heard the floor drop out under a person he usually stopped.

Doug set the duffel on his sofa. For a second he thought about moving the bottles again, somewhere smaller, somewhere dumber, the laundry hamper or behind the extra paper towels or under the sink in the bathroom. Every option felt more suspicious than the last. He unzipped the side pocket just far enough to make sure both bottles were still there and zipped it shut again.

Nate looked away on purpose and gave him that much privacy.

"You want a smoke later?" Nate said.

"Maybe."

"All right."

Doug kicked off his shoes and lay back on the pull-out fully dressed, the duffel against his hip because now that Mallick had seen him moving it, putting it anywhere else felt like another decision he would have to make for a reason. Above his head the refrigerator kicked on, rattled through its cycle, clicked off. Before it started again, he still hadn't slept.
