# Chapter 17

Paul watched Tommy through the sliding glass door for a long time before he went out.

Tommy was in the Adirondack chair closest to the railing, Martin's chair whether anybody called it that or not, with a beer balanced on the armrest he was not touching. The propane heater stood dark beside him. The temperature had dropped into the low fifties after sunset, and Tommy was sitting there in a T-shirt and jeans like cold had quit mattering. His hands were on his knees. He was looking at the lake.

Joel was in the study with the RT envelope open on Martin's desk and a yellow legal pad filling up in his square public-defender block print. He had been at it since they read the document and pulled Cliff's phone extraction. Fourteen photos of Martin's pages. Texts to a Minneapolis lawyer about leverage. Times. Dates. A clean line from a dead man opening a safe on Sunday morning to another dead man going under at the dock on Monday night.

Joel wanted to walk it straight into the kitchen and wait for Mallick to come back from Detroit Lakes.

"We hand her this tonight," he had said.

"I know."

"Then let's stop pretending there's a reason not to."

Paul had been standing by the lake print, the one that hid the safe, still holding the frame because he had not bothered rehanging it yet. His fingers were on the wire backing. He could feel dust from the wall in the creases of his skin.

"Give me an hour," he said.

Joel looked at him over the top of the legal pad. "For what."

"To talk to him."

"About Cliff?"

"About all of it."

"Paul, this isn't an exit interview. This is a homicide."

"I know what it is."

Joel set the pen down. "Then act like it."

Paul had wanted to answer cleanly, the way he answered clients when they wanted reasons a policy did not cover what they needed covered. He had not had a clean answer. Martin was dead. Cliff was dead. Tommy was sitting on Martin's deck in Martin's chair with Martin's beer within reach and a house full of Martin's arrangements all around him. Paul needed to look at him when the arrangement ended.

"Because I need to," he said.

Joel studied him for another second. "If he goes sideways, I am not waiting politely in the study while he decides whether to run."

"He won't run."

"You don't know that."

"I know Tommy."

Joel's mouth tightened. "No. You knew the version that worked."

That had been the hurt inside everything else since they opened the envelope. Martin being dead was one piece. The other piece was finding out there had been a second life running beside the one Paul thought he knew, and that Martin had built part of it in the same house where Paul had spent a quarter century drinking bourbon, arguing about running backs, sleeping off hangovers, and believing proximity was knowledge.

He had left Joel in the study with the legal pad, the cold coffee, and the pages Martin had kept hidden for twenty-two years. He had gone to the kitchen and stood at the slider and watched Tommy not drink his beer.

The kitchen smelled like old coffee, stale bread, lake damp, and the cedar note of Martin's cologne that had somehow worked itself into every room. The refrigerator clicked into one of its arthritic cycles behind him. A bug hit the glass and fell away. Beyond the yellow spill of kitchen light, Tommy was a dark shape against darker water.

Paul had known him since they were twenty-two. He had seen Tommy hung over, Tommy laughing so hard beer came out his nose, Tommy in shoulder pads, Tommy holding court in three different basements, Tommy asleep facedown on a sectional at five in the morning with ESPN playing to nobody. He had seen him loud, stupid, cruel, generous, scared, petty, loyal, and bored. He had not seen this. Or he had and had not understood what he was looking at.

Martin always had Tommy's terrible beer in the garage fridge before he even arrived. Not beer people actually wanted, not the IPA Joel brought or the twelve-year bourbon Martin saved for Paul, but the plain silver cans Tommy drank by the dozen and pretended tasted better cold off the dock. One year Paul had laughed at the sight of two cases already stacked by Thursday noon.

"You stocking for a siege?" he had asked.

Martin had grinned without looking up from the grill. "I'm stocking for Tommy. Different emergency category."

Everybody laughed. Tommy came in an hour later, saw the beer, and pointed at Martin like he'd pulled off a magic trick.

Another year Tommy had gotten too loaded Friday night and decided at one-thirty that he was absolutely fine to drive to Dent for cigarettes. He had been standing by the mudroom bench patting every pocket in increasing outrage because his truck keys were gone. Martin had leaned against the counter with a bourbon and let him get halfway to accusing Nate of stealing them before reaching into the bread bowl and dangling the keys by one finger.

"Found them," Martin had said.

"You're an asshole."

"You're welcome."

Tommy had barked a laugh, called him worse, and stayed. Paul had thought it was Martin doing what Martin always did, running the room, managing the idiots, keeping the weekend from tipping over. He had not seen the quieter part, the way Martin kept doing it long after the joke landed. Later that night, when Paul got up to piss, Martin was in the downstairs hall setting a bottle of water, two Pepcid, and a wastebasket inside the guest room Tommy used. He had looked up once, caught Paul there, and shrugged like it was nothing.

"Guy eats like he's mad at his organs," he had said.

Paul had laughed and gone back to bed.

There had been Sunday mornings too. Martin at the kitchen island with coffee and his phone tucked to his ear, saying, "Yeah, Denise, he's here. He's ugly, but he's here," while Tommy slept through whatever hour a grown man with three kids should already have been awake. Paul had heard those calls and taken them for bossy hospitality. Now, with the RT pages on the desk behind him and Tommy on the deck in Martin's chair, they looked different. Not hospitality. Attendance.

It was not even subtle attendance. It was a habit with twenty-two years under it.

Paul had gone back to the study after a few minutes because the will was still out on the desk under the RT envelope. He had already read it once on Monday morning. He did not need to read it again. He read the same line anyway.

I give my lake property in Becker County, Minnesota, and all contents therein, to Thomas Renaldi.

No explanation. No side note. No letter in the envelope. Martin had left the biggest object in his life to the man he'd spent two decades quietly keeping upright.

Joel had been on page three of his notes by then.

"You look like hell," Joel said without glancing up.

"Thanks."

"Did reading the will again fix anything."

"No."

Joel made another note. "I need you clear about something before you go out there."

Paul waited.

"If he admits Cliff, he doesn't get to fold that into grief. He doesn't get to make Martin the subject so he can disappear inside Martin. You know what he's going to do."

"Yeah."

"Do you."

Paul looked at the open envelope, at Martin's handwriting on the first page beneath it. Martin printed when he needed control. The letters were too neat, too even, each line beginning in a margin that might as well have been ruled with a blade. Paul had spent twenty-three years in insurance and had seen enough bad statements to know what panic looked like on paper. This was the opposite. Martin had been containing something while he wrote. That might have been the worst part.

"I'm not going out there to make him feel better," Paul said.

Joel finally looked up. "Then what are you doing."

Paul thought of Tommy outside with the untouched beer. He thought of Martin hiding truck keys, stocking the wrong beer, calling Denise, resetting the same room year after year like Tommy was a son who could only be kept alive by repetition.

"I'm ending Martin's part in it," he said.

Joel's face changed just a little at that. Not softer. More resigned.

"One hour," he said.

"I know."

The door ground in its track when Paul opened it.

He stepped onto the deck in his socks because he had forgotten shoes. Dew soaked through almost at once. The boards were slick and cold under his feet. Somewhere down the shoreline a pontoon stereo was dying from volume to static.

Tommy did not turn.

Paul crossed to the chair beside him and sat. The armrests were rough with old paint. Martin had been saying for three summers that he would sand and restain them. He never got around to it.

"Hey," Paul said.

Tommy lifted the beer an inch and set it back down. "Hey."

Paul still had Martin's handwriting in his head from the study, those blade-straight lines putting Cliff back on the steel table at the examiner's and Tommy back at the dock, bent over water shallow enough a child could have stood up in.

Paul had planned an approach on the way out, something sequential and fair. He dropped it as soon as he opened his mouth.

"Joel and I read the fire document this afternoon," he said. "We pulled the phone extraction too. I know about Washburn County. August of 2002. Ray Toomey."

Tommy's hand went rigid on his knee. Tendons stood out along his forearm, and the pale burn scar above his left wrist caught the light from the kitchen window. The rest of him stayed still.

"I don't know what you're talking about."

"Yes, you do."

"No."

"Martin wrote it down. Cliff photographed fourteen pages off that safe pile and started texting a lawyer about leverage. Joel and I read all of it."

Tommy's jaw flexed once. "Martin wouldn't."

"He did."

"Martin wouldn't do that to me."

"He didn't do anything to you. He wrote it down and locked it away and sat on it for twenty-two years. Cliff found it. Cliff brought it to you. Then Cliff died."

Tommy stood up so fast the chair legs barked across the deck.

"You went through Martin's things." His voice came up hard, the coach voice, the one that made fourteen-year-olds run suicides and assistant coaches stop explaining themselves. "You went into his study and his safe and read his shit like you had a right."

Paul stayed seated. "The safe was open. Cliff left it open."

"That isn't your point."

"No."

"Then say the point."

Paul looked up at him. "You killed Cliff because he knew what happened in 2002 and Martin wasn't here to manage it anymore."

The word left a metallic taste in Paul's mouth. Tommy's shoulders locked against the railing.

Tommy turned away and put both hands on the railing. His shoulders spread. When pressure hit, his body always tried to become larger than the thing pressing on it.

"You think you know the whole play because you got one pile of paper," he said. "That's what you think."

"I think two men are dead."

"You weren't there."

"No."

"You weren't there for any of it."

"Then tell me."

Tommy gave a short laugh with no humor in it. "Tell you what."

"What happened in Washburn County. What Cliff said to you Monday night. What you did on the dock. Pick one and start."

Tommy gripped the railing harder. "You always do this."

"Do what."

"Make it sound simple enough to total up."

That landed because it was true enough to sting. Paul spent his days turning catastrophe into categories, exposure into amounts, loss into paperwork someone could sign. He was good at it. He hated that Tommy knew it.

"Nothing about this is simple," he said. "That doesn't change the part where I need the truth."

Tommy's shoulders did not come down, but some of the noise went out of him. He stared over the lake. Wind moved dark strips across the surface. The beer on the armrest stayed untouched.

"Martin should've burned that thing," Tommy said at last.

"He didn't."

"I know."

Paul waited.

Tommy rubbed one thumb along the scar on his wrist. Not absentmindedly. Like checking whether it was still there.

"We were up there for two nights," he said. The words came reluctantly, dragged rather than offered. "Washburn County. End of summer. You know that wet-cold cabins get after midnight, where the walls sweat a little and everything smells like old coffee and dead bugs. It was one of those places. Not nice. Knotty pine, cheap blinds, floor that sloped toward the door. We were drinking cards and beer and whatever else we'd dragged in. I was drunk enough the room tilted when I stood up."

He stopped.

Paul said nothing.

Tommy kept his eyes on the lake. "I wasn't even in a real bed. It was a foldout shoved too close to a heater because the place never held heat after dark. Blanket half on the floor. Ashtray on a milk crate. My boots somewhere I couldn't have found if you put a gun on me. I remember lying there with the metal bar from the foldout in the middle of my back and thinking I should move and not moving."

His breathing changed. Not louder. Thinner.

"I woke because something was wrong with the air. Not because I was smart. Because it was in my mouth. Bitter. Sweet too. Gas or plastic or varnish or all three. I don't know. I still don't know. I know I opened my eyes and there was orange down by the blanket, low and fast, and for one second I thought I was dreaming because it looked too clean. Then the heat hit my face and I tried to slap it out and burned my palm on the blanket and rolled right onto the floor."

Paul saw the muscles in Tommy's forearm jump.

"Smoke got me before I was fully awake. I took one breath and it went down mean, all the way down, and then I was coughing so hard I pissed myself a little. I can still feel that. The floor was cold on one side of my face and hot on the other. My chest felt stapled shut. I was trying to push up and my hands kept slipping because there was beer on the plank or melted something or I was just that fucked. I don't know. I keep getting pieces. That's what I get now. Pieces."

He took his hands off the railing and looked at them.

"I know I dropped the cigarette earlier. I had one in my hand in bed because I was drunk and stupid and twenty-five and thought bad shit happened to other people. After that, I don't trust all of it in order. It moves around on me."

"What do you hear," Paul asked.

Tommy shut his eyes.

"Martin yelling my name like he was pissed at me for sleeping through practice." His mouth twitched once and failed at becoming a smile. "He came in with his shirt over his mouth. I remember that. White T-shirt with the Twins logo on it. He had one hand up and one hand out, low, because he couldn't see. He hit the table with his shin and swore. Then he got hold of me right here."

He touched the scar on his left forearm.

"He grabbed me so hard I thought he broke it. I was too dead in the head to help. I was coughing and gagging and trying to suck air where there wasn't any. He dragged me backward. My heel kept catching on the strip where the linoleum met the wood and every time it caught I thought we were done. I could hear the blinds crackling. The plastic smell. My own hair. Jesus Christ."

He bent forward, elbows on his knees now, staring at the deck boards between his shoes.

"He got me to the door and threw me out. Not gently. Threw me. I landed in gravel in my socks. Couldn't stop coughing. Snot everywhere. Eyes running so bad I couldn't see the trees right away. And then I heard Ray once."

The words came smaller.

"One shout. Not even a full shout. More like the start of one."

Paul did not move.

"I turned back and Martin was already going the other way. Back in. That's what he did. No pause. No speech. He just went back in. I got up on one knee and tried to stand and the whole place tipped on me again. I saw him in the doorway and then not in the doorway because the smoke swallowed it. The screen banged. A second later one of the little windowpanes popped and spit glass into the dirt. I still hear that sound. That little pop. Not the big movie explosion bullshit. Just that one stupid pane going out and me knowing if the glass was going out, the rest of it was gone."

He swallowed hard enough Paul could hear it.

"By the time I got to the truck Ray's boots were on the ground by the step. Rubber boots with blue tape around one top where it had split. That's what I looked at. Not fire. Boots. I stood there in wet socks and stared at those boots and knew nobody was coming out wearing them."

Wind came off the lake and moved the deck chair beside Paul a fraction of an inch.

"Martin came back out half carrying part of the door screen with him. He wasn't carrying Ray." Tommy's voice flattened. "He wasn't carrying anybody. He hit the dirt on both knees and threw up. Then he crawled the last bit to me because he couldn't get air either. He kept saying stay down, stay down, stay down. Like I was the one trying to run in."

Paul looked at Tommy's hands. They were shaking now, visibly, fingers opening and closing against his jeans.

"What happened after," Paul said.

Tommy laughed once, ugly and tired. "After. You mean the part where it kept going."

"Yeah."

"Sirens took forever. Maybe they didn't. It felt forever. Martin got me on the hood of the truck because the gravel was cold enough I was shaking so bad my teeth hit each other. He dumped a cooler bottle over my face and kept telling me to breathe through the coughing. He burned his hands. Both of them. Had blisters by morning." Tommy stared at his scar again. "He told me not to talk until I could keep the story straight."

Paul's eyes lifted to him.

"What story."

Tommy met his look for the first time since standing up. "The one where I woke to smoke. The one where I was too drunk to know anything useful. The one where nobody needed to hear about a cigarette because Ray was dead either way and Martin had already decided one dead man was enough."

"He said that," Paul said.

"Not like that. Not neat. He was coughing his lungs up and I was half naked and both of us smelled like burned plastic. But that's what it was. He told me shut up and let him handle it."

"Then he spent twenty-two years handling it," Paul said.

Tommy turned back to the water. "Yeah."

Paul let a beat pass. Then another.

"And Cliff."

Tommy's shoulders tightened again, though not the same way. The old rage did not come back full force. What came back was fatigue with edges on it.

"Monday afternoon he corners me in the mudroom with his phone out like he's got fucking listing photos," Tommy said. "Zooming in on Martin's pages. Date, Ray's name, my name. He says he's not trying to be a bad guy, he just wants to get made whole. Forty thousand dollars Martin owed him, plus interest, plus what he called goodwill for the inconvenience."

"He used that word with me too," Paul said.

"Of course he did. Cliff loved a clean word for dirty work."

Paul almost said yes to that. He did not want agreement to feel like comfort, so he kept quiet.

"I told him Martin was dead and there wasn't forty grand sitting in a cigar box for him. He said then we get creative. He said maybe Tommy Renaldi, family man, educator, youth mentor, could decide what keeping his name out of a dead-man file was worth." Tommy's mouth pulled sideways. "He kept smiling like we were negotiating cable."

"Did he say he had a copy."

"Said the photos were enough. Said the lawyer already had the timestamps. He wanted me scared. He got that part."

Tommy bent, picked up the beer, and finally took a swallow. He made a face and set it down again.

"Then Monday night he came to the dock."

Paul waited.

"You know the broad strokes."

"Tell me your version."

Tommy scrubbed a hand over the back of his neck. "He was already half in the bag. Loafers on. No socks. One of Martin's bourbons in a rocks glass because of course he wasn't going to bring a can down there like the rest of us. He starts in again with the same shit. Manage it. Keep it clean. Maybe sell one of Martin's accounts. Maybe I sign something. Maybe he takes a piece of the house before anybody sees a courtroom. He used the word asset while we were standing by a dead man's dock."

The words got faster. Not louder. Faster.

"I told him stop talking like that. He didn't. He stepped closer. Said Martin spent twenty-two years covering my ass and I could spend one night covering his note. Then he put his hand on my arm like we were partners in some ugly little business."

Tommy looked down at his own wrist, the same arm Martin had dragged him by.

"I shoved him off me. Not off the dock. Off me. That's what I meant to do. He was wearing those stupid house shoes. He slipped on the wet edge, hit his temple on the bench, and went in sideways."

Paul had heard the outline already. Hearing Tommy say it did not make it feel more theoretical. It made it worse.

"He came up fast," Tommy said. "Way faster than I thought. Spitting and cussing, hand on the dock board, trying to pull himself up. There was blood right away from the temple. Dark in the water. He had one hand on the dock and one hand got my wrist. Hard. Like if he could get enough of me he was safe."

He looked up and past Paul, beyond the glass, toward the house.

"And I saw the whole thing. Not Cliff. The other thing. The truck. The boots. Martin dragging me. Twenty-two years of him making space for me in this house and calling my wife and taking my keys and never saying the one thing out loud. And Cliff had his hand on me and was still talking. Still fucking talking."

Tommy's mouth worked once.

"I got down on my knees and pushed his head back under."

Nothing moved on the deck for a few seconds but the loose edge of the grill cover.

"He fought," Tommy said. "Of course he fought. Kicked the dock post. Shoes scraping wood. Tried to turn his face sideways and get a breath off the gap. I put my weight on him and held him there. That's it. That's the piece that doesn't get to move around on me. I know that one in order. I held him there until he stopped."

Paul felt the cold boards through his socks again, immediate and simple.

"You could've pulled him out," he said.

"Yeah."

"You made a choice."

"Yeah."

Tommy did not dress it up. That was almost harder to sit with than denial would have been.

"Then I sat there for maybe thirty seconds, maybe three minutes, I don't know. Water slapping the posts. My hand shaking. Then I started thinking again and that made it worse. Got him up, got the shoes straight, started the whole dumb accident version. By then it was already murder. The rest was just cowardice on top."

Paul looked at him for a long time.

"Why didn't you tell Martin when Cliff came to you."

Tommy laughed through his nose. "Tell Martin what. That the thing he'd spent twenty-two years tamping down had come back because Cliff couldn't keep his hands out of a safe. He was dead, Deck."

"Before he died."

"I didn't know Cliff had the pages before Martin died." Tommy rubbed both palms on his jeans. "And after Friday there wasn't a before anymore. There was just the next mess."

Paul waited. The deck smelled like old cedar and cold water, with the faint metal tang of the cold propane heater under it.

"Martin spent twenty-two years on this," he said. "Not in the abstract. Not just inside that document."

Tommy said nothing.

Paul went on because he needed Tommy to hear it said by somebody else.

"He had your beer in the garage fridge every year. He took your keys. He set that room up for you like you were—" Paul stopped. His throat was tight. "He called Denise on Sunday mornings before you woke up. Every year, Tommy. I thought that was just Martin. I thought he did that because he liked running the place."

Tommy's head lowered a little. Not quite a nod. More like he could not hold it up the same way.

"He did like running the place," Tommy said.

"That's not all it was."

"No."

"So tell me what it was."

Tommy stayed quiet long enough Paul thought maybe he would not answer. Then he sat down again, slow this time, all the air gone out of the movement. He did not sit like a man gearing up for a fight. He sat like a man whose legs had finally admitted they were part of the problem.

"You remember the green room off the hall," he said.

"Yeah."

"Every year he put me in there and told everybody it was because I snored and because if I was in the bunk room I'd talk all night and ruin the children." Tommy wiped his mouth with the side of his hand. "That wasn't why. Not really. It was right by the slider. Right by the kitchen. If I got up in the middle of the night, he heard it. If I went outside, he heard the door. If I started drinking again at five-thirty in the morning, he was already there making coffee."

Paul pictured it. The room off the hall on the first floor. Easy to get to from the deck. Easy to hear from the kitchen. Easier to monitor than the basement bunks or the upstairs room Nate sometimes used.

"One year," Tommy said, "I left a charger in that room. Cheap black Android charger. I came back the next September and it was in the same drawer, wrapped up neat with a twist tie like a hotel maid had done it. Another year I blew through a bottle of Pepcid in one weekend because Denise had me on some all-protein bullshit and Martin gave me hell for it. Next year there was Pepcid in the bathroom before I got there. He never asked. It was just there. Toothbrush once. Razor once. Athletic tape for my knee. My coffee, the bad hazelnut stuff, always in that cabinet nobody else touched."

He laughed once, a cracked sound.

"I thought he was busting my balls. He'd make a whole show of it. 'Coach needs his supplies, clear the lane.' Everybody laughs. Then I'd come back and the same shit was there again. Ready."

Paul looked toward the house, toward the dark hallway behind the kitchen and the little room off it.

"He wasn't just being nice," Tommy said. "He was—" He stopped. Started again. "Same room every year. Same drawer. Same chair. Same call to Denise. If I showed up and ate and slept and drove home, he got to—" Tommy shook his head. "He got another year."

"And if you didn't show up," Paul said.

Tommy looked at him. His eyes were red now, and not from cold.

"He called. Then he called again. Not always me. Sometimes Denise. Once my oldest kid. Asked about practice schedule like he gave a shit about varsity football. He wasn't asking about football." Tommy swallowed hard. "He was counting heads."

Paul felt something turn over inside him, something ugly and grieving at the same time. He had loved Martin for a quarter century and had not known the scale of what Martin had been doing. Or maybe he had known it in pieces and never laid the pieces flat.

"He should've told me," Paul said.

"Yeah."

"He should've told somebody."

"Yeah."

"He'd do these little checks like they weren't checks," he said. "How's Denise. Kid still liking baseball. Knee holding up. Half the time I'd tell him to fuck off and he'd laugh and ask again the next month." Tommy pressed his thumb into the scar on his wrist. "I thought it was guilt. Maybe it was. But it never stopped. It just kept showing up."

"He loved you too," Tommy said after a minute. "I know you need me to say that. He did. You know he did."

Paul stared at the wet boards between his feet. "That's not the problem."

"No, Deck. You thought you were the one he—" Tommy looked at him. "You thought he told you everything."

Paul's head came up.

Tommy did not look away. "I know that face, Deck."

The words hit because they were accurate enough to be cruel. Paul had spent years being the person Martin called after a bad quarter, after the Linda paperwork, after his mother went into memory care. There had been a whole other level under that, and Martin had kept Paul on the floor above it.

"You don't get to make me feel sorry for you right now," Paul said quietly.

"I'm not." Tommy looked back at the house. "I'm saying I know what this is doing to you. That's all."

They sat in the wind for a few seconds. Then Paul said the thing he had come out to say from the moment he re-read the will.

"Martin left you the house."

Tommy turned so fast the chair legs squealed.

"What."

"The will was in the safe. I found it Monday morning. He left you the lake house. The house and everything in it."

Tommy stared at him blankly, as if the words had arrived in a language he only partly understood.

"No."

"Yes."

"No, he didn't."

"He did."

"He wouldn't do that."

"He did."

Tommy shook his head once, twice, hard enough his face changed with it. "To me."

"To you."

"What about Carol."

"Portfolio. Other stuff. The house is yours."

"What about you."

Paul could have lied there, could have softened it, could have said what does it matter. He did not.

"Nothing changes what was in the will," he said. "The house is yours."

Tommy sat back like something had struck him in the chest. The beer bottle tipped off the armrest, rolled across the boards, and tapped against Paul's foot.

He did not bend to get it.

"Jesus Christ," Tommy said.

Paul did not reach for the bottle or say anything.

Tommy's face folded in on itself in a way Paul had never seen on him. Not theatrical. Not wild. Just stripped.

"The green room," he said after a while, almost to himself. "The charger. The fucking Pepcid."

Paul said nothing.

"The old Vikings hoodie on the hook. You know the one. I left it here in, I don't know, 2016 maybe. He washed it and hung it back up and every year after that he'd say, 'Your formalwear is where you left it, coach.' I thought he was doing a bit." Tommy pressed the heels of his hands to his eyes. "He left all of it. Every—" His breath caught. "All of it."

"Every year," Tommy said through his hands. "Every year he made sure I came back. And now he left me the fucking building."

His hands dropped.

"What am I supposed to do with that."

Paul's throat hurt. "I don't know."

"Live in it?" Tommy laughed once, ugly and disbelieving. "Raise my kids in the place where he kept me on a leash."

"He wasn't keeping you on a leash."

"What was it then."

Paul almost said protection. The word felt too clean for the room. He tried again.

"He was making sure you stayed where he could see you."

Tommy nodded before Paul finished. "Yeah."

"And maybe making sure he stayed where you could see him."

That hit too. Tommy's face twitched. "Yeah."

The lake slapped softly against the dock far below them. Somewhere across the water a dog barked twice and quit.

"Without that," Tommy said, "I'm just the guy from the cabin. I'm the guy from the dock." He looked at his hands. "That's it."

Paul had nothing that would not sound false if he said it too quickly. Anything he added now would come out packaged and false, the kind of sentence he used at work when there wasn't any coverage left.

Tommy bent and picked the beer bottle up from where it had rolled against Paul's foot. He held it without drinking.

"Did he tell you why," he asked.

"No."

"Not once."

"No."

Tommy stared at the bottle. "I don't know if he even knew anymore. Maybe after enough years it quit being one reason. Maybe it was just—Tuesday. And then September. And then the next year." Tommy wiped his nose with the back of his hand. "I don't know what to call that."

Paul did not answer.

"It was love too," he said. "I'm not saying it wasn't. I'm saying it was twisted up with the rest. That's different."

Paul looked at him. Tommy's face was wet now. He had not noticed him start crying. The tears were just there, moving down into the stubble at his jaw while he kept sitting upright like he could still coach his way through it.

"Yeah," Paul said.

"He left me the house."

"Yeah."

Tommy nodded to himself once, a tiny exhausted movement. "That's mean."

Paul almost flinched at the accuracy of it.

"Maybe," he said.

"No, it is. It's kind too. That's the worst part." Tommy wiped his face roughly. "Kind and mean at the same time. Only Martin."

"I'm not telling you this so you can call it punishment and be done with it," Paul said.

Tommy let out one wet breath that might have been a laugh.

"I know."

"I'm telling you because Martin is dead. Cliff is dead. The thing he built to keep you upright is over."

Tommy looked at the house again, the deck light, the dark hallway beyond the kitchen.

"Yeah," he said.

"And I'm not doing what he did."

That got Tommy's full attention.

"Meaning what."

Paul had thought about this all the way from the study to the deck and still found the saying of it harder than he'd expected.

"Meaning I am not spending the next twenty years adjusting around what you did," he said. "I am not taking your keys and making calls and helping you keep a lid on it and pretending that's friendship. Martin made his choice. I'm making mine."

Tommy's eyes dropped to the bottle in his hands.

"So that's it," he said.

"No. Not yet."

Paul sat forward, forearms on his knees now, looking directly at him.

"Mallick already has the tampering. All of ours. She has Cliff's phone. She has the photos. She has the texts to the lawyer. She will get to the dock with or without help. What happens next is whether you walk into that kitchen yourself or whether I tell her before breakfast."

Tommy did not answer right away.

"The fire too," he said finally.

"The fire is twenty-two years old. You need a lawyer for that. Cliff is now."

"You think those stay separate."

"No. I think one of them is what you can still choose how to tell."

Tommy rubbed his thumb over the wet label on the bottle until the paper started to peel.

"And if I don't."

Paul looked at him. Not at the big body and the scar and the hands that had pulled boys up by shoulder pads for half their lives. At Tommy, stripped of motion, Martin's chair under him, Martin's house behind him, Martin gone.

"Then I tell her. Six o'clock. I will give you that long because after tonight I know exactly what waiting costs."

Tommy nodded once.

"You'd really do it."

"Yes."

"You won't find a way around it."

"No."

"He would've."

Paul looked at the lake. "Yeah. He would've."

"And you won't."

"No."

Tommy sat back. "Then I guess that's the difference between you two."

Paul let that stand. Maybe it was the difference. Maybe it was one of many. He was too tired to sort them.

"I am giving you the night," he said. "That is all I am giving you."

Tommy swallowed. "That's generous, Deck."

"It isn't. Six o'clock."

He stood. Tommy did not.

At the slider Paul glanced back once. Tommy was still in Martin's chair, beer in hand, staring at the house like it had shifted on its foundation while he sat there.

The door ground shut behind Paul.

---

The kitchen felt too warm after the deck. Paul stood inside the slider with water dripping from his socks onto the hardwood and let the room settle around him. The refrigerator kicked off. The house made its usual small night noises, only now every one of them sounded like a person trying not to speak.

Joel looked up from the study doorway. "Well."

"Six o'clock," Paul said.

Joel watched him for a beat. "Did he admit it."

"Yes."

"Both."

"Enough."

Joel's face did not change much, but he leaned back from the desk as if giving the fact physical room.

"Do you need me to call her now," he asked.

"No. Not yet."

"Paul."

"I said six."

Joel opened his mouth, then shut it. "Fine. I want that hour on the clock, though. Not in your head where it turns into mercy."

The line irritated Paul because it was aimed well.

"It's 9:58," he said, looking automatically at the microwave. "Six is six."

Joel nodded once. "Good."

Paul went down the hall to find dry socks and stopped at the green room instead.

The door was open a few inches. Light from the hall cut across the bed. He pushed it wider and went to the dresser.

Black phone charger, wrapped with a twist tie.

Travel bottle of Pepcid.

Fresh toothbrush.

Half roll of athletic tape.

Nothing dramatic. No letters. No confession. Just maintenance.

On the back of the door hung the old Vikings hoodie Tommy had mentioned, washed clean and rehung for another year. Paul stood looking at it and felt the whole arrangement go plain in front of him. Martin had not been improvising kindness. He had been running a system.

"Jesus Christ, Martin," Paul said.

When he came back to the study with the wet socks in one hand, Joel looked up once.

"You found the Tommy annex."

"Yeah."

Paul sat in Martin's chair. The RT pages were squared beside Joel's pad. The will was underneath them.

"He said the memory moves around on him," Paul said. "The fire. He knows he dropped the cigarette. After that it's pieces. Smell. Heat. Martin dragging him out. Ray's boots by the step. Martin telling him to keep the story straight."

Joel listened without interrupting.

"That tracks," he said.

"You sound impressed."

"I sound like a lawyer hearing a cover-up that lasted because the first two men in it loved each other in different wrong ways."

Paul looked at him, then away.

"Is he coming downstairs in the morning," Joel asked.

"I don't know."

"Do you think he wants to."

Paul thought of the charger, the Pepcid, the hoodie on the hook.

"No," he said. "I think he wants Martin back."

Joel capped his pen. "I'm setting an alarm for 5:45. If he hasn't done it by six, we call."

"Fine."

Paul took dry wool socks from Martin's dresser, put them on, and came back to the kitchen. Joel was writing again. Outside, Tommy was still on the deck, a shape in Martin's chair, the lake behind him black as roofing tar.

Old wood ticked in the walls. Lake damp clung to the kitchen. Outside, Tommy stayed on the deck while Paul listened to Martin's house and counted down to six.
