# Chapter 15

The call came at 11:47 PM. Mallick was on the couch watching the ten o'clock weather, a front pushing in from the Dakotas, rain by Wednesday, and thinking about whether the tomato cages would hold. She picked up on the second ring.

Dispatch gave her the address and she knew it before the numbers finished. It was Martin Kessler's lake house on Elbow Lake, the second call to the same property since Saturday morning.

She wrote it down anyway. She always wrote it down.

She changed out of sweats and into a blazer, polo, slacks, and low boots. She clipped her reading glasses to the chain around her neck, took her leather notebook from the kitchen counter, and left through the garage. The casserole dish sat in the cabinet above the stove. She left it there.

The drive from Detroit Lakes took twenty-six minutes. County Road 6 was empty. Deer eyes flashed at the tree line and vanished. She drove with the window cracked. The cold had that September edge that told you October was already practicing. She didn't turn on the radio.

Saturday kept replaying anyway. There had been the green bean casserole, Kessner instead of Kessler, and men lining up to correct her. Paul had the careful insurance cadence. Cliff had the smooth courtesy of a man who sold people the future for a living. There had been thirty-seven corrections across six interviews, thirty-seven small disclosures from men who thought they were fixing her mistakes instead of showing her where they'd been.

Saturday had been a suspicious death with a natural heart under it and five separate panic attacks on top. She had walked away with a contamination map and a case she could close.

Now somebody had put a second man in the lake.

She turned onto the gravel drive at 12:13 AM. Two county cruisers were already there, light bars washing the A-frame blue, then red, then blue again. Every window in the house was lit. Figures moved behind the glass.

The lake was loud from the yard. On Saturday it had been background. Tonight it sounded like pressure.

Deputy Arneson met her at the door. He was the same big, competent deputy she'd dealt with on Saturday, and his face was set a little tighter now.

"Detective. Same house."

"What do we have?"

"Cliff Haywood, forty-eight. Found face-down beside the dock around eleven-thirty. Doug Whitfield pulled him out before we got there. There's a cut on the right side of the head."

"How bad?"

"Hard to say in this light. Good-sized split. Also..." Arneson glanced past her toward the dark out back. "He was wearing tassel loafers."

"On the dock."

"In the water beside it."

"How deep?"

"Three feet. Maybe a little more right off the edge."

That kept accidental drowning off the easy list.

"Who found him?"

"Whitfield. He's in shock. Other four are in the great room. Kelleher's with them."

"Any phone on Haywood?"

"Not that we saw. Wallet, yes. Keys, yes. No phone."

She filed that and walked in.

The room she'd worked on Saturday was still crowded with men and light. No one in it looked in charge of anything.

Doug Whitfield sat on the couch with a fleece blanket around his shoulders, face drained gray by shock and overhead light. Paul Decker stood near the island with his hands folded, already arranged into usefulness. Joel Beckman leaned against the mantel and watched her come in, all the lawyer sarcasm gone. Nate Griggs paced in one narrow lane across the rug, cap still on, words building in his throat. Tommy Renaldi sat in the Adirondack chair by the deck door with his hands between his knees.

Saturday Tommy had been all movement. Tonight he looked bolted down.

Mallick stopped where all five could see her.

"I'm Detective Mallick. I was here Saturday investigating Martin Kessler's death." She let the correct name sit there. "I understand Mr. Haywood was found in the lake."

They heard it. Paul looked at Joel. Nate stopped walking in mid-step. Doug raised his eyes for a second, long enough to register the missing casserole, the missing apologies, the missing act. Tommy didn't move at all.

"Nobody leaves this property," she said. "Nobody touches anything. I want each of you separated for the rest of the night."

Paul took a step toward her. "Detective, if there's anything we can do..."

"Stay available, Mr. Decker."

She turned to Arneson. "Mr. Beckman in the study. Mr. Griggs in the basement. Coach Renaldi stays here. Mr. Whitfield stays on that couch. Keep Mr. Decker in the kitchen."

Nate lifted both hands. "I can give you the whole night, start to finish. I can walk you through dinner, who went where, who was outside, all of it."

"I'll come get you."

There was no warmth in it, no neighborly flutter. Nate's mouth shut.

Mallick looked back to Arneson. "Call dispatch. I want the BCA crime scene unit headed this way. Tell them possible homicide and a compromised water scene. Nobody goes near the dock until I see it."

Arneson nodded and stepped out.

Kelleher started moving the men. Doug got up slowly, blanket still around him. Joel went toward the study without complaint. Nate muttered once under his breath and headed for the stairs. Paul stayed near the island, hands still folded, waiting for instructions that weren't coming.

Kelleher touched Tommy's shoulder. Tommy lifted his head, looked at Mallick, and gave her a flat stare, mouth set hard.

She went out the back door.

---

The route to the dock vanished into the trees ten feet off the deck. Her flashlight found wet gravel, pine needles, roots, the dark shine of cedar rail at the bottom. It was good enough for a person who knew the property. It was good enough to carry a man down without wanting to be seen. She would need the BCA to photograph the ground before daylight traffic ruined whatever it was holding.

The air by the water was colder than the yard. Her breath showed. Lake smell came up strong: mud, weeds, cold mineral water, churned-up silt.

Arneson had left a battery spot at the end of the dock. Cliff Haywood lay on his back in that hard white circle, arranged into more order than death usually allowed. Deputies had straightened him after pulling him out. She could see it in the hands, in the shoulders, in the shirt twisted across his chest.

Froth had dried from one nostril and the corner of the mouth. His eyes were closed. She would have preferred them open.

She crouched, pulled on gloves, and started with the shoes.

They were leather loafers, tasseled and expensive. Cliff wore them in the house. She remembered the click of them on Martin's hardwood on Saturday. Even his worst weekends had a dress code. Sunday afternoon she had seen him cross the deck in beat-up boat shoes with wet-darkened seams and soft rubber soles.

These were not dock shoes.

She lifted one foot. The sole was clean through the arch. No grit clung to it, no wet sand worked into the edge, and no pine needles stuck to the leather. There was only a scuff at the toe and the drag-smear of somebody else's handling.

Either Cliff hadn't walked down here in those shoes or somebody had changed them after he was dead.

She lowered the foot and moved to the wound.

The split sat on the right temple, a little above and behind the ear, about two inches long. It was clean enough to be hard-edge impact, not rock and not branch. She looked at the railing. The wood showed old splinters, weathered gray, nothing fresh. She looked at the bench at the end of the dock. The bench was smooth, with no blood, no hair, and no scrape she trusted.

If he had fallen on his own, the wound should have argued for it: forehead, chin, bridge of the nose, maybe a shoulder. This one sat too far back, on the side and behind, not where a simple fall would land.

She shined her light over the edge of the dock. The beam reached bottom fast, finding pale sand close in, then a seam of churned silt, then darker water beyond. It was three feet, maybe a little more where the dock dropped off. It wasn't enough to drown a sober grown man who wanted to live. It was enough to drown a man who'd been stunned first. It was enough if somebody kept a hand on the back of his neck and waited out the fight.

The froth mattered too. It wasn't definitive without the autopsy, but it pushed the same way. He had taken water in alive or close enough to it.

It wasn't proof yet, but it was plenty to take accident off the easy list.

She stood and backed up a step. In the white light, Cliff's body looked split between two stories. The dock said slip, stumble, bad night, cold water. The loafers said indoors. The wound said help.

She walked the planks to the far end and back again. Nothing looked obvious on the bench, and nothing lay loose on the swim platform. At shore, where gravel gave up and grass started, her light caught half a boot print. It was bigger than Cliff's shoe. It was clear enough to note, not clear enough to own. It could be Doug. It could be a deputy. It could be the man she was out here for.

From the dock, the house was mostly windows and glow through branches. From the house, after dark, the dock disappeared. The trees and water did the rest.

A person could hit a man out here. A person could hold him down. A person could walk back to the house wet to the elbows and never be heard over the lake.

She took one last look at Cliff's face, then turned back toward the lights.

---

The kitchen table was where she set up. It was the same chair as Saturday, under the same cheap overhead fixture. The old refrigerator kicked on with its forty-minute wheeze as she opened her notebook to the blue-tabbed section marked *Kessler residence, corrections.*

She did not need to reread the whole page. The tags were enough.

Decker had corrected the body position.

Haywood had corrected the study location.

Griggs had exposed the voicemail.

Renaldi had said little, corrected nothing, and kept his hands busy.

Whitfield had shut down.

Beckman had known the timeline clean.

Saturday the map had told her who touched what after Martin died. Tonight it might tell her who had access, who had leverage, and who was stupid enough to panic twice in the same house.

Cliff's correction still mattered most. He had known Martin's study too fast because he had been in it. At the time she had filed it under money. Martin owed Cliff forty thousand dollars. A dead man with an unsecured safe could make an idiot out of anybody.

Money still might explain it.

Paper could explain it better.

She wrote one line on a fresh page. *Haywood was in the study. Reopen that path.*

Then she started with Doug Whitfield.

Doug sat back down with the blanket pulled high and both hands around a mug of water he wasn't drinking. Up close the gauntness in his face looked worse than it had on Saturday. The lake had left a dark line halfway up his sweatpants.

"Mr. Whitfield. Start with when you went down there."

Doug swallowed. "Around eleven-thirty. Maybe a little before."

"You go every night?"

"Most nights."

"Why."

He looked at her over the rim of the mug. "It's quieter."

"To smoke?"

"Sometimes. Mostly just to sit."

"Who knows you do that."

Doug gave a tiny shrug under the blanket. "Everybody, probably. I disappear a lot."

"What did you see?"

"He was in the water." Doug stared at the mug as he said it. "Face down. Left side if you're walking out. I thought maybe he was swimming and then I saw it wasn't that."

"What wasn't."

"The way he was just there." Doug's fingers tightened around the paper cup. "No movement. No sound."

"What did you do?"

"I yelled. I went in. Water wasn't deep. I got him turned over and his face..." Doug stopped, mouth open for a second, then shut it. "I dragged him up. Then I yelled again and ran back."

"Did you notice his shoes?"

"No."

"Anyone on the path? Any voices? Splashing?"

Doug shook his head. "No. Just the lake."

"Did you see Coach Renaldi after dinner?"

Doug thought about it longer than the question deserved. "Inside. On the deck once maybe. I don't know what time."

"Was Haywood with him?"

"Maybe earlier. Not when I saw him." He took a breath and stared at the blanket on his lap. "I don't know. I know that's what everybody says when they know more than they're saying. I really don't."

She believed that part. Shock had narrowed him down to the size of a nail.

"All right," she said.

Doug nodded once and looked back at the mug.

Nate Griggs was downstairs with Kelleher. The basement smelled like detergent, damp carpet, and a house built too close to water. Nate stood as soon as she came in.

"Detective. I've been going through it and I can pretty much give you the whole night if you want the director's cut."

"I don't."

Nate stopped.

"What time did you last see Cliff Haywood alive?"

"Dinner. For sure dinner. Six-thirty to, I don't know, seven-fifteen, seven-twenty. Everybody was there. After that it broke up. Some guys stayed upstairs, some went out back. I saw Cliff on the deck later when I came up to use the bathroom. Maybe nine. Nine-fifteen."

"Who was with him?"

Nate tugged the bill of his cap. "I think he was alone. Maybe Paul had just come in. I can't swear to it."

"How was he acting at dinner."

"Normal Cliff, which means if you hate salesmen he'd make you want to hit him with a chair. Real smooth. Real helpful. He kept doing that thing where he talks low to one guy like he's giving out state secrets and really he's just cornering him."

"One guy being Tommy."

"Yeah." Nate's mouth twisted. "Tommy was weird all day, though. Quiet weird. Not mad weird. Which is worse."

"You were recording dinner."

His face changed at once. The performance pulled back. What remained was the man under it, smaller and more alarmed.

"Yeah."

"How long did you record?"

"Dinner. Waiver wire talk. Mostly bullshit." Nate rubbed his palm on his shorts. "I usually let it run a little after because sometimes somebody says one useful thing when they think we're done."

"Did you stop it yourself?"

"I think so. I mean, I thought so. I'd have to look at the file."

"I'd like the phone."

"The whole thing?"

"The whole thing."

Nate winced, then reached into his pocket and handed it over. "Passcode's 4421."

"Any other audio from tonight?"

"Probably. Maybe. If there is, it's because I forgot to kill the app all the way. That's not me hiding anything. That's me being bad at software and worse at boundaries."

"I'll sort it out."

Nate nodded. "Okay."

"Did you notice any trouble between Cliff and Tommy?"

"Trouble like yelling? No. Trouble like one guy talking too low and the other guy acting like his skin didn't fit? Yeah."

"When?"

"All day. Worse at dinner."

She wrote that down, bagged the phone, and went back upstairs.

Joel Beckman was in the study, sitting in Martin's desk chair with his hands on the chair arms instead of the desk. He looked careful without making a show of being careful.

"Mr. Beckman."

"Detective." Joel stood. "No casserole this time."

"No."

That answer seemed to satisfy him more than silence would have.

"When did you last see Cliff Haywood?"

"Dinner. Roughly six-thirty to seven-fifteen. I went upstairs after that. I came down when Doug screamed."

"What time?"

"Eleven thirty-two. I checked my phone."

The precision sounded native to him, not built for her.

"Any tension today between Cliff and anyone else?"

"Tommy."

"How so?"

Joel considered it. "Too polite. That's the first part. Cliff gets smooth when he wants something. Tommy gets louder when he wants out of a conversation. Today Cliff was smooth and Tommy was quiet."

"All day?"

"Since Sunday, really. Tonight it got hard to miss."

"Did you hear an argument?"

"No. I heard the absence of a normal one." Joel shifted his weight. "I don't have proof of anything. I have an impression. Cliff had leverage. Tommy knew it."

She wrote that down too.

"Leverage about what?"

"If I knew that, I'd tell you. Or I'd bill for it." Joel's mouth tightened for half a second. "But yes. Start with Tommy."

"I'm starting with everybody."

"Sure."

He didn't believe her, which was fine. She didn't need belief from Joel Beckman. She needed time stamps and clean observations, and he'd just given both.

Paul Decker was waiting where she had left him, by the sink with his sleeves rolled once and his hands braced on the edge of the counter. He had found something to do while he waited. Four clean glasses sat upside down on a towel by the dish rack, lined into a row that nobody needed.

"Mr. Decker."

"Sure."

"Walk me through dinner and after."

Paul nodded immediately, almost with relief. "Dinner started a little after six-thirty. Doug grilled the steaks. Nate talked almost the whole time. Cliff had two bourbons, maybe two and a half. Tommy drank beer. Joel barely touched anything. By seven-fifteen people were done eating. Seven-twenty, maybe. I was clearing plates."

"You know the time."

"The microwave clock said 7:19 when I started loading the dishwasher." He glanced at the appliance as if it might still back him up. "I remember because I was thinking if I cleaned up right away maybe the house would feel normal for five minutes."

"Did it."

"No."

That was the first blunt sentence he had given her all night.

"Tell me about Haywood and Renaldi."

Paul exhaled through his nose. "Cliff kept finding him. That's the simplest version. By the grill. On the deck. In the hallway by the downstairs bath. He'd come over quiet and Tommy would look like he was bracing for weather."

"Did you hear what was said."

"Not all of it. Money once. Martin's name more than once. I heard Cliff say 'this place' at one point. Tommy told him not now."

"What time was that."

"Around eight on the deck. I think." Paul rubbed a thumb against the counter seam. "Then again later."

"Later meaning."

"Ten twenty-two. Maybe ten twenty-three."

"That's specific."

"I was standing right here deciding whether it was too late to make coffee." He gave the ghost of a humorless smile. "That sounds ridiculous, but that's what I was doing. Tommy went out the slider with no jacket. Empty hands. A minute or two later Cliff followed with his drink and his phone."

"You saw the phone."

"Yes. He had it at dinner too. Set it next to his plate. The screen kept lighting up and he kept turning it facedown."

"Did you see either man come back in."

"I saw Tommy later in the great room. I can't give you the minute. Cliff, no." Paul's eyes moved toward the dark glass of the deck door. "I didn't clock the gap when it mattered. I should have."

"What about disagreement. Yelling, threats, anything more obvious."

"Not obvious. That was the trouble." He straightened a little. "Cliff was being reasonable on purpose. Tommy was trying not to explode. It had that feel."

"What kind of feel."

"The kind where one person already knows too much."

That sounded closer to Joel than to Paul, which made her trust it more.

"You knew Cliff said Martin owed him money."

"Forty thousand from the Roseville duplex. He'd mentioned it before Martin died and after. More after." Paul looked toward the study, then back at her. "He thought the group should treat the debt as real. He wasn't wrong."

"Would Renaldi care about that debt."

"Not enough to kill a man over it." Paul heard how fast he answered and corrected himself. "Not that I know of."

She wrote a few short lines. Decker saw Renaldi go to the deck at 10:22. Haywood followed with his phone. The pressure had stayed quiet all night. The forty-thousand-dollar debt was still live.

"One more thing," she said. "What was Renaldi wearing."

"Dark sweatshirt. Jeans. Work boots, I think. Mud on them earlier from outside."

That matched the half print on the shore closely enough to make the skin across her shoulders tighten.

"Thank you."

Paul nodded. "Do you want the others' phones on the table? I can get that done."

"No. I'll get it done."

He accepted the correction without arguing. That was how frightened he actually was.

Tommy was where she'd left him, in the same chair and the same posture. His hands were still jammed between his knees like he didn't trust them loose. His sleeves were shoved up. The pale scar along the left forearm showed clean under the light.

"Coach Renaldi."

"Yeah."

"Where were you between ten and midnight?"

"In bed."

"What time did you go up?"

"Nine. Nine-thirty."

"Did you leave the room after that?"

"No."

"Did you talk to Cliff Haywood after dinner."

"No."

"Did you hear Cliff go out? Anybody go out?"

"No."

He didn't hedge, didn't add, and didn't perform indifference. Every answer came flat and clean. Tommy was usually louder than this or already leaving the room. This version of him was wrong.

"How would you describe your relationship with Mr. Haywood?"

"He was a friend."

"Any disagreement today?"

"No."

Mallick watched his face while he said it. There was no shift, no heat, no coaching bark. Saturday he had been half-motion even standing still. Tonight every muscle was locked.

"What shoes were you wearing tonight."

His eyes flicked to hers for the first time. "Boots."

"Where are they."

"Mudroom."

"What kind."

"Brown work boots."

"And what were you wearing when you went upstairs."

"This." He looked down at himself like the answer annoyed him. "Jeans. Sweatshirt. Same thing."

She asked a few more narrow questions and got the same dead-flat answers back: no witnesses, no movement, no conflict, in bed by nine-thirty, nothing to add.

He had the size for it, and he was holding himself so still it looked painful. Neither one was evidence. The case wasn't going to hold on an old scar and a bad feeling.

"All right," she said. "I'll speak to you again in a little bit."

That got a reaction. Tommy looked up.

"Thought you said morning."

"I changed my mind."

He stared at her another second, then looked at the floor again. "Yeah."

When she got back to the kitchen, the first BCA van was coming up the drive. Tires crunched on gravel, doors thudded shut, and clipped practical voices carried in from outside. The house heard it too. The rooms seemed to contract around the sound.

Arneson came in from the porch pulling on fresh gloves. "Techs are here."

"Good. I want all five living phones on this table, logged and bagged. I want Haywood's noted as missing, not overlooked. Nobody goes to a bedroom, a bathroom, or a sink without one of us on them."

"Already told Kelleher no washing up."

"Tell him again."

Arneson nodded.

For the next five minutes the kitchen turned into a collection point. Joel set his phone down without comment. Paul did it gently, like it belonged to someone else. Doug had to be reminded twice before he patted his pocket and produced his. Tommy held his for a second too long before putting it on the wood. Nate's was already in evidence.

Five phones sat on the table. There was no sixth.

Mallick looked at the row of evidence bags.

"Nobody found Haywood's on him."

"No," Arneson said. "I checked again with the deputy who helped pull him out. Wallet and keys in the front pockets. No phone."

"Then somebody handled it."

Paul said, "Cliff always had that thing on him."

Tommy didn't look at anybody.

Mallick took Nate's bag back, opened it, and sat down with the phone and her notebook.

The audio app opened to a list of files. Most of them had Nate all over them: `WEEK3_SLEEPERS`, `MAILBAG_FINAL_v2`, `MON_DINNER_RAW`, `HOT_TUB_IDEAS`, `NO_USE_1`. The dinner file was longer than he'd implied. It ran twenty-nine minutes.

She tapped it.

At first it was exactly what he'd promised: beer bottles on wood, somebody arguing about waiver priority, Nate laughing too hard at his own line, someone calling someone else an idiot with real affection under it. It was the group before the second death, the group when it could still pass for itself.

Then plates started moving. Chairs scraped back. Voices spread out, farther from the phone.

Cliff's voice came in close and low, not meant for the whole table but caught anyway.

"Ten-thirty. Dock. You and me."

There was a pause. Three seconds of silverware and chair legs.

Tommy answered from farther off, rough enough that she would have known him without the weekend of interviews.

"Not in here."

Cliff again, just as low. "Ten-thirty."

There was another pause. Then Tommy, flat and final.

"Fine."

Mallick hit pause.

It wasn't a confession. It was more than a hunch. Tommy had lied about the disagreement. Paul had placed both men moving toward the deck. Cliff's phone was gone. The night had finally done her the courtesy of starting to line up.

"Mr. Griggs," she said without looking up.

Nate had drifted as far into the doorway as Kelleher would let him. "Yeah."

"Are these timestamps native to the app or did you rename anything."

"Native. I mean, the stupid file names are me, but the time stamps are the app. I don't know how to fake metadata if that's what you're asking. I can barely get the sponsor reads level."

"Any recordings after dinner."

"A couple accidental ones maybe. I set the phone down and forget it exists. Sometimes it records my pocket. Sometimes a table. Sometimes forty-five seconds of me breathing like a pervert because the mic's right there." Nate saw her face and flinched. "Sorry. Wrong moment."

"Stay awake. We'll go through all of them."

"Okay."

She handed the bagged phone to Arneson. "Get a charger on that and keep it with me."

Then she stood.

"Kelleher."

The deputy appeared at the kitchen entry.

"Bring Coach Renaldi to the study. He doesn't stop at the bathroom, he doesn't stop at the mudroom, and he doesn't put his hands in his pockets on the way."

Tommy's head came up from the great room. "What's this."

"It's me changing my mind."

Arneson said, "On your feet, Coach."

Tommy stood slowly. The chair legs scraped across the floorboards and then the room was quiet again.

Mallick picked up her notebook and went to Martin's study. Tommy came in between the deputies and stayed standing until she pointed him at a chair.

"Sit."

He sat.

She took Nate's phone back from Arneson long enough to cue the file, then set it on the desk and hit play.

Cliff: "Ten-thirty. Dock. You and me."

Tommy on the speaker, unmistakable: "Not in here."

Cliff again: "Ten-thirty."

Tommy: "Fine."

She stopped the playback and let the silence sit there.

"You told me you didn't talk to him after dinner," she said.

Tommy looked at the desk, not at her. "He wanted to talk."

"That is a conversation, yes."

"I meant no argument."

"You also said you were upstairs by nine-thirty and never left."

His jaw worked once. "I said I went up around then."

"No. You said you were in bed."

He didn't answer.

"Mr. Decker saw you go out the slider at 10:22. He saw Haywood follow with his phone."

Tommy's eyes snapped up then, not at the mention of the dock but at the mention of Paul.

"So start again," she said.

He breathed through his nose. In, out. "He wanted to talk money."

"What money."

"Martin owed him."

"Forty thousand from Roseville."

"Yeah."

"And he set that meeting at dinner."

"Yeah."

"You went."

Tommy's fingers locked together so hard the knuckles blanched. "I went outside."

"To the dock."

"For a minute."

"What happened."

"He wanted me to back him up with the house. With the estate. Said the group would listen if I said Martin owed him and it had to get handled." Tommy swallowed and started again, less clean this time. "He kept coming at me with it all day. I told him I wasn't doing that. I told him to take it up with Decker or the lawyer or whoever the hell he wanted. Then I came back in."

"You came right back in."

"Yeah."

"Why lie about the meeting."

For the first time some heat showed through the dead flatness. It wasn't much. It was enough.

"Because two guys were dead in the same damn house and I wasn't handing you one more thing to hang on me."

That at least sounded like him.

"What happened to Haywood's phone."

"I don't know."

"Did you touch it."

"No."

"Did you touch him."

Tommy looked past her to the dark window, where his own reflection sat over the lake. "No."

She watched his hands. They had gone very still again.

"Your boots are in the mudroom," she said.

"Yeah."

"And the clothes you're wearing are the clothes you wore outside."

He hesitated.

"Yeah."

That one was different. It wasn't big. It was enough.

Mallick didn't move her eyes off him when she spoke to Arneson. "Bag the boots from the mudroom. Bag the sweatshirt and jeans when we're done here. If there's anything in the washer, the dryer, the shower drain, or the trash that looks like it was handled after eleven, I want it photographed before anybody touches it."

Tommy leaned forward in the chair. "You don't have shit."

"Not yet," Mallick said. "But I have your lie, I have your meeting, and I have a dead man with the wrong shoes and no phone. That's enough to keep me interested."

He stood so fast the chair legs barked against the floor.

Arneson took one step into the room. "Sit down."

Tommy looked at him, then at her. For a second the coaching voice threatened to come back. She could see it building, the instinct to get louder, bigger, more certain than the room. It died before it reached his mouth.

He sat.

Mallick closed the notebook.

"Here's what happens next," she said. "You stay where a deputy can see you. You don't touch your room, your boots, or a sink. Mr. Griggs and I go through every file on that phone until daylight if we have to. And before this night is over, somebody is going to tell me what Haywood had that made him think he could call you to the dock like that."

Tommy's face changed at the last part. It wasn't enough for court. It was plenty for her.

She stood and opened the study door.

"Arneson. Sit on him."

"Yep."

"Kelleher, bring Griggs upstairs in five minutes. I want Cliff Haywood's phone found before sunrise. Search the yard, the path, the shoreline, the trash, the couch cushions, the whole damn house."

"Copy."

Outside, down by the trees, another flashlight came on and started moving toward the dock.
