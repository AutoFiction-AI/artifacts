# Chapter 18

The kitchen light was on when Tommy came downstairs.

It had been on since 4:38, when Paul Decker let Mallick in through the side door in his socks and said, "He's still upstairs," in a voice that had already used up most of itself for the night.

She'd gotten his call at 4:07 in Detroit Lakes. No hedging, no claims-adjuster qualifiers, no little pads of fairness around the edges. Just: "He said six. If he's going to tell you, it'll be at six."

She'd said, "I'll be there in half an hour," pulled on yesterday's blazer, and driven the lake road north with her notebook on the passenger seat and the coffee going bitter in a thermos cup she never quite remembered to wash right away. The dark had that wet September thickness to it, cold enough that the ditches held mist and the low places looked smoked over. By the time she turned into Martin Kessler's gravel drive, the trees were beginning to separate from one another. Not daylight yet. Just shapes admitting they were shapes.

Paul stood aside to let her in. He'd changed his shirt sometime after midnight, but not into a clean one. Yesterday's button-down was wrinkled up the front and damp at one cuff. His glasses were on. His hair wasn't. He smelled like lake water that had dried on a tired man.

"Joel's awake," he said. "Study."

"The others?"

"Nate and Doug are downstairs, I think. Tommy's in Martin's room. Or he was."

She nodded. "Coffee still works?"

"Yeah."

That was the full exchange. Paul led her to the kitchen, filled the machine, and went back out into the great room without asking what she knew. A different man would have asked right away. Paul had already moved past wanting a forecast. He wanted the weather to arrive and stop pretending it might not.

So Mallick had been at Martin Kessler's kitchen table since four-thirty, her leather notebook open, her second cup cooling beside eight evidence photos she'd printed in Detroit Lakes the night before. Cliff Haywood's hands had taken those photos on Sunday morning: four sheets, front and back, eight images, the whole RT document laid out in the quiet light of a dead man's study. The refrigerator worked through its arthritic cycle behind her. Beyond the sink window, the lake was still black.

She'd arranged the case in two stacks because that was what it had finally become.

On the left: Martin.

Natural death. Hot tub heat, sildenafil, whiskey, a bad heart nobody knew enough about until it quit. After that, panic. Five men making separate little repairs to a thing that didn't need their help and getting all over it in the process.

On the right: Cliff.

Temple wound, water in the lungs, loafers put on after the body was already wet, mud in the cuffs where a man had fought to get his feet under him in three feet of water and never made it.

The trick, once the lab reports came back and Nate turned over the dinner audio and Cliff's lawyer-texts came off the phone, was not proving the house had lied to her. The house had been lying from the moment she arrived with the casserole. The trick was sorting which lie belonged to which death.

She'd spent Tuesday afternoon drawing a line down the middle of a yellow legal pad and moving every correction, every volunteered detail, every reflexive no, not like that to one side or the other.

Paul correcting her about which side Martin had been on when he found him. That mattered because only the man who'd turned Martin over for CPR would answer that fast and that clean. It was guilt from Saturday, not violence from Monday.

Cliff correcting her about the study off the main hallway. That mattered because she had not said study. She'd said office. Cliff's mouth had done the rest. Sunday morning, then, not Monday night: the safe, the document, the photos on the phone, the first place leverage got born.

The deleted voicemail. The missing pills. The borrowed certainty around things that men only knew because they'd been in rooms they later denied being in. Every one of those slips reached backward toward Martin's bed, Martin's bathroom, Martin's phone, Martin's safe. Humiliations. Petty thefts. Panic. None of them required Cliff dead.

Only one set of facts crossed the line she'd drawn on the pad.

Cliff had photographed the RT document and texted a Minneapolis lawyer about leverage.

Nate's dinner recording put Cliff on the record setting a 10:30 dock meeting with Tommy.

The BCA report said Cliff took the blow to the temple before he inhaled water, which meant the lake had not surprised him and the drowning had not happened clean.

And Tommy, unlike the other four, had a reason that did not belong to Martin's natural death at all. Martin had spent twenty-two years holding the lid down on the Ray Toomey fire. Cliff had found the thing under the lid, and Cliff had started talking to Tommy the way men talk when they believe they have a mortgage on somebody else's life. Debt on one side. Secret on the other. Then a late meeting at the dock in the cold.

That was enough for an arrest. The county attorney would have taken that much and asked for more later.

It was not enough for Mallick.

Three days of this house had given her versions. Softened versions. Defensive versions. Versions with the shame rearranged so the speaker didn't have to be the man he'd actually been for those ten minutes at the lake or those twenty-two years before it. She wanted the ugly center without the house cleaning around it. She wanted the second choice, the one after Cliff was already in the water and calling Tommy by name. A shove in anger was one thing. Kneeling down and forcing a man under until the sound changed was the murder, and paper could only take her so far into that.

The kitchen held the layered smell of this week: old coffee, refrigerator cold, lake damp in the trim, whiskey worked into the pine, a sweet canned-onion note from the casserole she'd left in the fridge on Saturday morning because her mother had raised her to bring food to bad houses and not show up empty-handed. The foil pan was still in there between a half bottle of Lagavulin and three cans of Grain Belt, untouched for four days. A good dish waiting on the wrong catastrophe.

At five-twenty Paul came in and poured himself coffee.

He didn't sit. He stood by the sink with both hands around the mug, looking not at her but at the dark pane over the sink. In the glass his face floated over the black lake.

"He say anything after you called?" she asked.

"No."

"Did you sleep?"

He gave that one a look over his shoulder, not offended, just too tired to pretend it was a real possibility. "Not exactly."

Mallick tapped the legal pad with one finger. "I'm not going to play dumb with him this morning."

"I figured not."

"If he comes down and decides to bullshit for fifteen minutes before he gets there, I'll let him do some of that. But not much."

Paul nodded once. "If he doesn't come down?"

"Then I go up."

He drank. The refrigerator clicked off. The room went quiet enough that the lake made a low, steady slap somewhere beyond the dock line.

"He'll come down," Paul said.

There was no faith in it. Just knowledge. He set the mug in the sink without rinsing it and walked back out, his shoulders tighter than when she'd arrived. Not hopeful. Resolved.

Mallick went back to the notes.

She had the correction grid in the notebook itself now, recopied neat from the yellow pad because she hated losing the shape of a thought to her own bad penmanship. Twelve pages. Names down the left. Topics across the top. Body. Phone. Study. Bathroom. Pills. Dock. Shoes. Timeline. Under each heading she'd written not what the men meant to tell her, but the thing they'd shown by correcting her too fast or too hard.

Most of the pages were now history. The Saturday mess had resolved into exactly what it looked like once pride and fear stopped smearing it around: one man hiding an affair-related humiliation, one man hiding a voicemail, one man hiding a debt document, one man hiding pills, one man hiding pills of a different kind, all orbiting the same dead friend and all convinced for one stupid hour that if they adjusted the evidence they could save themselves from being seen as ridiculous. Men in their late forties protecting their own little shames while a corpse cooled upstairs. Human as hell. Pathetic too.

Monday was meaner.

Monday had a path, a dock, a meeting time, a lawyer text, and a body staged badly because the killer had done the final thinking with adrenaline in his teeth.

She looked again at the BCA notes she'd copied over in the margin:

water in lungs

blunt-force injury temple pre-submersion

wrong footwear for scene

When she'd first read the wrong-footwear line she had thought of accident staging, plain and obvious. By midnight she'd stopped thinking accident. Cliff's loafers weren't just the wrong shoes for the dock. They were the wrong shoes in a husband's panicked imagination of what another man ought to have on when he died outside. The first pair to hand. The sort of correction a guilty man made because the body in socks looked too naked, too domestic, too much like a bedroom death instead of a lake one.

Tommy had always been the most physical of the group. Biggest hands. The coaching stance. The habit of taking up doorways without seeming to notice. Mallick had seen the sort before in bars, at school-board meetings, in court hallways where men explained themselves with open palms and leaned too close while saying they weren't threatening anyone. Most of the time the loudness was the point. With Tommy, the loudness had started to look by Tuesday like a lid. Martin had spent twenty-two years helping him keep it on. Then Martin died in bed, Cliff opened the safe, and the pressure moved.

She turned back to Cliff's texts.

No poetry there. Cliff's money troubles had squeezed the varnish right off him. The messages kept circling the same three words in different forms: leverage, contained, protect. A salesman vocabulary collapsing into a frightened man's vocabulary one line at a time.

Protect myself from what, Mallick had written in the margin. The answer sat on the evidence table and in Tommy's silence both.

She heard movement overhead then: not a man's full wake-up routine, not drawers, not the bathroom, just a floorboard and stillness after it.

She closed the notebook. Opened it again. Set her pen parallel to the spine.

At five-fifty-three Joel crossed the great room toward the hallway in pressed khakis and yesterday's shirt, already belted, already wearing the expression of a man who'd put his face on before the rest of him caught up. He paused in the kitchen doorway.

"You have enough?" he asked.

Lawyer wording. Not do you know. Not is it him. Enough.

"Yes," she said.

Joel took that in, gave the smallest nod, and looked toward the ceiling once. "He'll try to stop at the exact point he thinks helps him most."

"Most people do."

"He's worse at it than most people."

Then he went on toward the bathroom, maybe to piss, maybe just to move. Mallick watched him go. The house was full of men who had discovered this week that intelligence and decency did not cancel out cowardice. They only made it more articulate.

The third stair creaked.

Everybody in the house knew to skip it. Tommy had probably skipped it a hundred times over twenty-five Septembers. This morning he hit it anyway. Mallick sat still and listened to him pause at the bottom, hand scraping the wall, then start down the hallway toward the kitchen light.

Tommy came around the corner in gray sweatpants and a faded Eastview Lightning football t-shirt. Sleep hadn't touched him. Neither had the sort of shower men take when they think hot water can buy them a different face. The coaching posture was gone. His shoulders had rolled inward and his hands hung empty at his sides.

He stopped when he saw her at the table. His eyes went to the notebook, then to the evidence photos, then to the coffee maker.

"So he called you."

"He did."

Tommy breathed out through his nose. Not surprise. More like confirmation that the last remaining door in the building had the lock he'd expected.

Mallick nodded at the cabinet. "Mugs are above the sink."

He poured himself coffee in a blue mug that said WORLD'S OKAYEST FISHERMAN, filled it too high, and sat across from her at the scarred pine table. Steam rose between them. She left the silence alone. The first sentence belonged to the person who needed it.

"I know why you're here," Tommy said.

"I'm here because Cliff Haywood died on that dock."

Tommy looked at the table. "Yeah."

Outside, a loon called once across the water. The note hung there and went flat.

"I did it," he said.

Mallick didn't reach for the notebook yet. If she touched paper too soon, men like Tommy started speaking to the record instead of the room. "Tell me from when you stepped onto the path."

His fingers tightened around the mug. "I'd been sitting in Martin's room for maybe twenty minutes. Maybe longer. Paul had gone in on me and then Joel had gone in and then it was just me and that fucking room and all his stuff still sitting there like he was coming back for it." He swallowed. "I kept thinking maybe I just don't go. Maybe I let Cliff stand out there and freeze and go to sleep and deal with him in the morning. But if I did that he'd just come back louder."

He wasn't starting with Cliff at the dock. He was starting upstairs, with the minute he still could've stayed in Martin's room and let the whole thing slide to morning. That mattered.

"So you went."

"Yeah."

He rubbed his thumb along the mug handle once, then again. "Path was wet. Cold enough the stones were slick. He was already down there. Sitting on the bench at the end with a beer like it was some normal talk. He heard me and stood up before I got all the way out."

"What did he say?"

"The same shit he'd been saying since the deck. That we could handle it. That he wasn't trying to ruin me. That nobody needed to get dramatic." Tommy's mouth pulled tight. "He said he was trying to be decent about it."

That helped more than if Tommy had made Cliff sound crueler. He was repeating the pitch the way Cliff had given it, all reasonable surfaces and money underneath.

"And you?"

"I told him to stop saying that word."

"Decent."

"Yeah. I said don't fucking say decent to me." He looked up for the first time. His eyes were bloodshot and dry. "He kept doing the sales pitch anyway. Forty grand. Paper. He could make it go away if I helped him make it go away. He kept talking like Martin had left him a messy file and we were two guys closing it up."

Mallick let that sit for a beat. "Did he mention the document?"

Tommy's jaw moved once. "He didn't have to."

"But he did."

"He said he had what he had." Tommy looked back down. "He said if I was smart I wouldn't make him use it."

That was as close as he was going to come on the fire, at least on the first pass. Mallick filed it and moved on.

"How close were you when you put your hands on him?"

"Arm's length. Less." Tommy planted both palms on the table, showing her without seeming to know he was doing it. "He stepped in like he was calming me down. One hand out, beer in the other, and I—" He stopped. "I shoved him. Right here, in the chest. Hard enough the bottle went first."

"And Cliff?"

"He went back crooked. His heel—the wet board, his heel just went. Then his head hit the dock beam on the way down."

That tracked with the body. Beam first, then water. It was an accident for one second, maybe two. The rest was where the charge lived.

He stopped. The muscles in his jaw bunched.

"What did you hear?" Mallick asked.

"The bottle busting under him. Then the hit. Wood, then him. Then the splash."

"Did you know right then he was hurt bad?"

"I knew he hit hard." Tommy stared at his own hands. "I didn't know if it was bad-bad until he came up."

"He came back up."

"Yeah. That's the—yeah." Tommy shut his eyes for a second and opened them again. "It was shallow. He didn't go all the way under. He landed on his side and got—he got up on one knee. One hand on the beam. Blood going down his face. He was coughing, trying to get the water out of his—trying to stand up at the same time."

He kept returning Cliff to one knee, one hand, one breath, alive enough to need a decision. Mallick let him keep saying it.

"What did he say?"

Tommy nodded once. "My name. He said, Tommy, what the fuck. Then he said, help me up."

Help me up. That was the hinge she'd come for. Cliff was alive, Cliff was asking, and Tommy was standing over the one clean chance he still had.

The refrigerator kicked back on. Its hum filled the room and kept going.

Mallick let the sound ride a second, then cut through it. If she gave him accident language now, he'd live in it. "The shove put him in the water. The murder happened after. Tell me that part clean."

Tommy's face changed when she said murder. Not surprise. Relief, almost. Relief at a word that didn't pretend.

"He had one hand on the dock," he said. "One foot under him. He wasn't gone. He was still there. Still talking. Still gonna come up and keep at me."

"So what did you do?"

"I got down and shoved him back."

"Once?"

"More than—no."

"Tell me."

He swallowed again. "He slipped. Came up again. He kept—" Tommy's hand went to the back of his own neck. "He got his fingers over the edge."

He wasn't blurring the mechanics yet. That mattered. Men looking for mercy usually got vague right here.

"And then what."

"I grabbed him. Back of his neck. Between his shoulders." He was talking to the table now. "I couldn't do it standing. I went down on my knees to get my weight over him."

Mallick said nothing. Silence was cleaner than any summary she could offer him here, and usually meaner.

"He kept trying to get his feet under him. Kicking mud. Pushing on the bottom. Every time he got his face out I pushed him—" Tommy's voice had gone very quiet, quieter than his normal quiet, all the coaching stripped out of it. "He got one breath at me. Not a full one. More like—" He shook his head once. "Like a bark. Then he was drinking lake."

"You could've stopped."

"Yeah."

"You didn't."

"No."

"Why?"

Tommy looked toward the window but not through it. "Because if I pulled him up, he was still Cliff. He was still standing there with that thing over me. Still talking. Still wanting paper. Still wanting me to take care of his goddamn—" He dragged a hand over his mouth. "Martin had been dead twelve hours. Twelve hours and Cliff's already out there acting like he owns the whole—because he found a folder. A fucking folder in a dead man's safe."

Mallick kept her face still. He wasn't talking about getting caught. He was talking about Cliff continuing to exist after the shove. That was better for the case and worse for everybody in the room. "So you held him down."

"Yeah."

"How long?"

"Long enough."

"That's not time."

"I don't know time." Tommy's voice sharpened, then dropped again. "Long enough that he stopped—at first he was going for the beam. Pushing with his feet. Using his hands. That was him still trying to get out. Then it was just grabbing at whatever. My wrist. My sleeve. The posts." His jaw worked once. "He kicked one shoe off trying to get up. I didn't even know that till after. Then it was just thrashing."

There it was again: not panic about police, not even panic about the body, but fury that Cliff had kept reaching for him. Tommy was narrowing his own motive for her without knowing it.

"What changed?"

"The sound."

"What sound?"

"When a guy's yelling through water and then he can't anymore." The words came out flat, almost disgusted at themselves. "After that it was his heels hitting the posts and the water slapping under the boards and me breathing like I'd run hills."

Mallick waited. The sound mattered. He'd put the change there on his own.

"Then he stopped reaching for the beam," Tommy said.

He lifted the mug, realized his hand was shaking, and set it back down untouched.

"Was he dead when you let go?" she asked.

"I don't know the exact second." Tommy stared at the coffee spilling a little against the rim. "He wasn't coming back. That's what I knew."

"What did you do next?"

"I sat there for a second. Maybe ten seconds. Maybe a minute." He shook his head at himself. "I was on my knees with both hands wet and muddy and there was blood on the beam and I kept waiting for him to come up mad instead of dead. Like there was still another version coming."

He breathed in through his nose. Out.

"Then I pulled him—I got him farther out. Off the side, by the end. Where the water drops deeper."

He was moving into staging now, which was fine. Men often got more precise once the killing itself had already been said out loud.

"Why?"

"Because right next to the dock looked wrong. Too easy."

"And then?"

"Then I went back up to the house. Upstairs to his room."

"For what?"

"Shoes."

"Why shoes?"

Tommy laughed once without humor. "Because dead in socks looked wrong too."

The laugh was useless and honest. So was the answer. Dead in socks looked wrong to him because it looked like house, not dock.

"And the loafers didn't."

"No, they did. I know they did." Shame moved across his face, raw and stupid and late. "I wasn't picking from a menu. I was shaking. I knew the ones on him were wrong or there weren't any, I don't even—" He closed his eyes. "It looked like house, not dock. I went by his bed, grabbed the loafers, came back down, jammed them on him in the water, shoved him out again, washed my hands in the sink, and went to bed."

"You put his house shoes on him."

"I didn't know what he wore out there."

"Did you think anyone would buy it?"

"I wasn't thinking that far." He looked at her then, suddenly angry in a tired, used-up way. "People say that after, like panic's supposed to have a strategy. I was trying to get one thing to stop becoming ten things."

Anger didn't cancel the admission. If anything, it cleaned it up. He wasn't claiming strategy. He was claiming the mess and his hand in it.

"But you did think enough to change the scene."

He held her eyes for two seconds, maybe three. "Yeah. I did."

"Did anyone see you?"

"No."

"Did anyone hear anything?"

"If they did, nobody came."

"Were your clothes wet?"

"Knees. Sleeves. Front of the shirt some." He glanced down at his sweatpants like he'd expected the wetness to have kept its shape for two days. "I hung the shirt in the bathroom for a while. Put it back on. It dried."

His knees were wet. So were the sleeves. The shirt had hung dry in the bathroom. Those were the small household details juries tended to believe because nobody bothered to invent them well.

"And then you slept."

"Not really." Tommy rubbed the heel of his hand against his chest. "I laid there. Every time I shut my eyes he was still coming up."

That one he said like a fact, not a request for mercy.

Mallick reached for the notebook then, flipped it open, and wrote while he watched her do it. Not much. Just enough to fix his words in place. Dock. Beam. One knee. help me up. back of neck. between shoulders. shoes.

She saw Cliff there as Tommy talked: one hand on the beam, blood cutting a dark track through lake water, still using Tommy's first name like they were men in the same deal. Then the separate thing. Tommy making the second choice and keeping at it long after accident had run out as an excuse.

"Mr. Renaldi," she said. "I need to read you your rights."

Tommy nodded once.

She read Miranda from memory. He listened without interruption, eyes on the mug, face gone loose now that he'd said it and couldn't unsay it.

"Do you understand these rights as I've explained them?"

"Yes."

"Are you willing to keep talking to me?"

"About Cliff, yeah."

"About Monday night at the dock."

"Yeah."

She called county dispatch and requested a unit to the Kessler property, reporting that she had a suspect in custody and needed transport. Custody sounded borrowed in a kitchen that still smelled like old coffee, lake damp, and onion soup mix.

They waited in the refrigerator hum and the slow arrival of morning.

Tommy sat with both forearms on the table now, hands hanging between his knees. Without the coffee mug in them his hands looked even bigger.

"Martin owed Cliff forty grand," he said after a while. "From some deal."

"I know."

"Cliff thought he could use that, plus what he found in the..."

The sentence died there. Tommy's mouth closed hard after it. Somebody had told him where the line was, or he'd found it himself in the dark sometime before dawn.

"We have Cliff's phone," Mallick said. "We have the photographs. We have the texts to his lawyer. I found the document in the safe."

Tommy went very still.

"I'm not asking you about the fire right now," she said. "I'm asking about Monday night."

"I told you Monday night."

"You did."

"That's what happened."

"At the dock, yes."

He lifted the mug with both hands this time. Coffee spilled over the rim onto the table before it reached his mouth. Tommy stared at the spill and set the mug back down without wiping it.

"You know what the worst part is?" he asked.

Mallick waited.

"It wasn't the push." He looked at the coffee spreading toward his wrist. "The push was me being mad. Me being stupid. The worst part was hearing him ask for help and knowing I still had a clean out if I wanted one. That's the part that keeps replaying."

She wrote one more note without letting him see the words: knew clean out / declined.

"Why tell me now?" she asked.

Tommy gave a short, tired laugh. "Because Paul was right."

"About what?"

"That there wasn't another version coming."

Something moved in the hallway.

It wasn't the staircase. Someone was already on the main floor.

Mallick looked past Tommy to the archway. Paul stood there in the same wrinkled shirt, glasses on, face drawn so tight it looked held together by habit. He looked at Tommy's back, then at Mallick, and gave away the rest of last night without saying a word.

Joel appeared behind him, fully dressed, chin up, already in courtroom mode. His eyes went straight to Mallick's belt, then the notebook, then Tommy's hands on the mug. Nate came up from the basement in a hoodie and socks, bareheaded for once, all the usual motion drained out of him. Doug arrived last with a fleece blanket around his shoulders and stopped in the archway without speaking.

Tommy never turned around. His shoulders tried to square themselves once and failed.

"It's all right," Mallick said. She wasn't sure who she meant it for.

The county unit came eleven minutes later, gravel crunching outside before the headlights swept the kitchen window.

Mallick stood. Tommy stood with her.

She stepped around the table and took the cuffs off her belt. She'd been carrying them since Tuesday morning.

"Hands behind your back, please."

Tommy obeyed. Steel clicked shut around his wrists.

She checked the fit. Not too loose. Not too tight. Skin already cold from the house and the hour.

She walked him through the hallway. The four men had flattened themselves against the room's edges without discussing it. Joel watched the cuffs and the transport grip with automatic legal attention. Paul's eyes stayed on Tommy's face. Nate stood with both hands in his hoodie pocket and looked like he was holding himself in place by force. Doug watched Tommy's feet pass and then lifted his gaze to the door.

At the threshold Tommy stopped for one breath, two.

"Tell Denise I called," he said to nobody in particular.

Paul shut his eyes once. Joel looked away. Mallick gave no promise she couldn't keep.

Then Tommy walked outside without looking toward the lake.

The air had dropped into the forties. Dew darkened the gravel. Deputy Anderson waited by the cruiser with the back door open, one hand resting on the frame. Tommy ducked into the seat. The door shut.

The cruiser backed down the drive and disappeared through the pines. After the engine noise went, the house gave itself back to the lake and the trees. A loon called again, farther out this time.

From somewhere inside, the refrigerator clicked off.

---

Mallick went back in.

The four men were still in the great room, standing where she'd left them in their mismatched morning clothes. Nobody spoke until Paul did.

"What happens now?"

His voice was controlled and stripped of its usual qualifiers.

"He'll be booked at the county jail in Detroit Lakes," Mallick said. "Arraigned within thirty-six hours. He'll want a lawyer before then."

"He should call Denise first," Paul said. "Before she hears it from somebody else."

"He'll get a phone call."

Joel cleared his throat. "And the rest of it?"

He didn't have to name Martin or Cliff. The house had done enough naming for one week.

Mallick looked from one face to the next. "Martin Kessler died in his bed. Cliff Haywood was killed at the dock Monday night. Those are not the same event, and they weren't committed by the same kind of panic."

Nate's mouth opened and closed once. "Jesus."

It was the first word he'd contributed all morning. No joke structure around it. No tag. Just the word itself, dropped in the middle of the room like something heavy and breakable.

"So all that other shit," he said, then started over. "All that stuff everybody was lying about after Martin. That was just us being us."

"It was you all making things worse for yourselves," Mallick said. "But yes. Cliff's death was separate."

Nate looked at the floorboards as if one of them might open and let him out of the sentence.

Joel's lawyer face stayed on, but only just. "You know about the document in the safe."

"I know."

"Do you know what it means?"

Paul cut in before she could answer. "Joel."

That interruption landed harder than if he'd shouted. Joel shut his mouth. He wasn't done wanting the answer, but he heard the warning in Paul's voice and let it stand.

Doug moved first. He went to the kitchen, filled the kettle, and set it on the stove with the sort of quiet competence people only notice when everyone else has stopped knowing what hands are for.

Nobody thanked him.

Mallick stayed where she was. From this angle she could see the blanket hanging off Doug's shoulders, one corner dragging the floor; Nate with his hoodie strings gone uneven where he'd been worrying them; Joel with one thumb hooked in his pocket like he was trying to keep himself from cross-examining the walls; Paul standing too straight, as if posture could keep him from falling into whatever came after this hour.

"Will you be charging anybody else?" Joel asked.

Mallick looked at him. "Not for Cliff."

That was precise enough to satisfy the lawyer in him and vague enough to leave the rest of the week where it belonged for the moment. Joel accepted it with a small movement of the jaw.

Paul sat down heavily in Martin's recliner, then seemed to realize what chair he'd chosen and stood back up again almost at once. He rubbed a hand over his face.

"I gave him the night," he said, not looking at anybody. "That was my big idea."

No one answered.

After a few seconds Nate said, "What else were you gonna do?"

Paul laughed once without humor. "A lot of things, probably."

"No," Nate said. "I mean it. What else were you gonna do?"

His voice was thin and frayed, not quite steady enough to count as comfort. It was still the closest thing in the room to it.

Paul looked at him and then away. "I don't know."

Doug came back with four mugs balanced on a cutting board and set them on the coffee table with the kettle and a box of tea bags. Chamomile, green tea, peppermint, all the little domestic lies people buy when they want to believe hot water can fix a week. Paul took one. Joel took one. Nate stared at the options for a long moment before picking peppermint as if the choice contained information. Doug took the last mug and sat on the couch arm.

Mallick watched them make tea in silence.

Steam rose into the great room and vanished before it reached the beams. The house had that aftermath feel she'd known since her first death call twenty years ago: the noise gone out of a place so completely that every ordinary movement sounded borrowed. Ceramic on wood. Kettle lid ticking. A throat cleared behind a hand. Somebody sniffing because crying in front of other men still required a disguise.

Nate blew on his tea and said, almost to the mug, "He was out there asking Tommy for help."

The room tightened around the sentence.

Mallick didn't ask how Nate knew. He knew because anyone with ears and a stairwell had heard enough.

"Yeah," Paul said.

No elaboration. No correction. Just the word, and then the room had to live inside it.

Joel took off his glasses, pinched the bridge of his nose, and put them back on. "He stopped at the document on purpose."

"Of course he did," Paul said.

"He confessed to one homicide and lawyered up around the other crime before breakfast."

"Joel."

"What?" Joel's temper came out fast and bright, less legal now than simply tired. "I'm not asking you to feel sorry for Cliff. I'm asking whether we're all going to keep doing Martin's work for him after he's dead."

Doug looked up over the rim of his mug. "Maybe not right this second."

It was the most Doug had said, and because it was Doug the room obeyed it.

Joel exhaled through his nose and leaned back. "Fine."

Paul sat down this time on the edge of the sofa instead of Martin's chair. "Mallick."

"Yes."

"Tommy said enough to make a case?"

"Yes."

"Enough that Denise won't hear some watered-down version from a public defender and think we're inventing it?"

"Yes."

That answer seemed to steady him more than anything else had.

He wrapped both hands around the mug and stared into it. "All right."

Nate looked toward the kitchen. "I can't believe that casserole's still in there."

The line would've been a bit in any other chapter. Here it came out stunned and almost childlike.

Mallick glanced toward the refrigerator. "Neither can I."

"Martin would've eaten half of it standing up," Nate said. "Burned his mouth and still done it."

Doug gave the smallest nod. Joel looked down. Paul's mouth twitched once and stopped.

"He would've complained there weren't enough onions on top," Doug said.

That got them one thin breath of something that wasn't a laugh but remembered the shape.

Mallick let it pass through the room without helping it or hurting it.

"I'll need each of you available for follow-up interviews," she said after a moment. "Don't leave the state without calling my office."

Four nods. Almost in unison.

"Today especially," she added. "Nobody drives anywhere they don't need to. Nobody starts deciding for anybody else what version of this house gets told."

That one was aimed at all of them, but Joel wore it most visibly.

"Understood," he said.

Paul looked at Nate and Doug. "Just stay put."

"Yeah," Nate said. "No road trip plans."

The effort at normal phrasing collapsed before it became a joke. He stared at the tea in his hands instead.

Mallick left them there for a moment and went back into the kitchen.

Tommy's mug still sat on the table. WORLD'S OKAYEST FISHERMAN. The coffee had spread to the edge of the pine and stopped there. She wiped the spill with a paper towel, rinsed the mug, and set it in the dish rack next to her own.

Then she opened the refrigerator.

The casserole sat on the second shelf between the half-empty bottle of Lagavulin and the Grain Belt, foil still crimped tight, MALLICK written across the tape on top in thick black marker. She'd made it Friday night in her kitchen in Detroit Lakes, before the call from Elbow Lake, before the safe, before the second body, before she knew these men as the sum of their evasions. Cream of mushroom soup, frozen green beans, french-fried onions from the can, cheddar folded through because her mother had believed in improving the church-basement classic when the family deserved it.

She touched the cold foil with the backs of her fingers.

Her mother had brought casseroles to car wrecks, layoffs, heart attacks, barn fires, a child with leukemia in Audubon, a man who'd lost three fingers to an auger in Callaway. Bad news produced the same first question in her mother's house every time: What can go in a pan. Mallick had made fun of that for years, right up until widowhood taught her how a dish on the counter could stand in for language when language had no usable shape.

This one had sat untouched because nobody in this house had ever arrived at the clean form of grief that made a casserole useful. Martin died and the men started tampering. Cliff died and the house turned from stupid to vicious. By the time there might have been room for food, the week had moved on to handcuffs.

She took the dish out and set it on the counter.

In the great room the tea mugs clicked softly. Someone cleared his throat. Someone else walked three steps and stopped. The living were doing the small awkward chores of remaining alive in the same building.

Mallick peeled the foil back, checked the beans, saw they were still good, and smoothed the foil down again. She found a Sharpie by the phone stand and wrote HEAT 45 MIN / 350 on the tape below her own name, then left the dish by the stove instead of carrying it out.

When she stepped back into the great room, all four men looked up.

"There's food in the kitchen," she said. "Eat it or don't. But don't leave it there till Sunday and call it a plan."

Nate gave one small nod. Paul looked like he might thank her and thought better of it. Doug said, "OK." Joel only watched.

Above the fireplace, the draft board was still hanging where Martin had left it on Thursday night. Seven columns, eighteen rounds, names written in different inks and markers because men who claimed not to care about aesthetics always cared once they were labeling things. Martin's column sat in the final slot on the far right. Every pick in it had been circled in red in Martin's own hand, his old habit from every draft: mark the choice, argue with himself in the margin, move on like conviction was just another game he knew how to play.

The red circles looked different now. Not sentimental. Not mystical. Just a dead man's handwriting still instructing a room that no longer had seven men in it.

Paul followed her gaze to the board. "I should take that down."

Nobody answered him.

He didn't move to do it.

Mallick stood with the draft board in front of her and the four men quiet at her back, steam lifting from tea none of them wanted. The league had ended without anyone saying the sentence out loud. That was probably how it had to end. Not with a vote. Not with a ritual. Just with a dead man in the final slot, a murderer on his way to county, and four friends too tired to pretend there'd be another September they could walk back into as the men they were last Thursday.

She picked up her bag and notebook from the hall table.

Cold air caught in her nose and the back of her throat when she stepped outside. Wet gravel snapped under her shoes. The lake smelled metallic and still, and the pines gave off that damp September smell that always hit harder before full light. She stood for a second with the house behind her and the dock somewhere beyond the trees and then got in the car and drove back toward Detroit Lakes.
