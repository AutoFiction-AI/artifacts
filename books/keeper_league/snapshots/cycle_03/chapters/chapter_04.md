# Chapter 4

The Becker County deputies had made seven trips up and down the stairs. From the kitchen table, Joel counted every one. He'd been planted there since quarter to eight with a mug Paul kept refilling, and on each pass the deputies skipped the third step near the top. Paul had warned them about the creak before Joel even thought to mention it.

Cup four sat half-finished in front of him. Too much coffee on top of no sleep and no food had his hands shaking in short fast bursts, and twice he'd caught himself doing what his clients did in arraignment: sitting very still while people with authority moved through a place that used to feel safe. In twenty years as a public defender in Hennepin County, he'd never once considered that side of the room. Now he knew. It felt like shit.

The great room still smelled like Martin's scotch, the Lagavulin he'd poured too generously last night, because Martin never knew how to pour lightly, host reflex still firing even when people told him to sit down. The draft board stayed on the wall, Martin's picks circled in red Sharpie. Nobody had touched it because touching it would mean deciding what it was now: a record, an artifact, evidence. Morning light came through the A-frame windows in thick gold slabs, postcard light, the wrong light for this.

Tommy stood on the deck with both hands around a coffee mug like it was the only solid thing left. A gray clammy cast had settled over his face, the look Joel had seen on defendants listening to charges. In the kitchen doorway, Nate was explaining the lake house's history to a deputy who had not asked and did not care. Doug sat on the couch in the corner with his hood up and his knees pulled in, looking at his phone with the blank concentration of a man reading absolutely nothing.

By the fireplace, Cliff occupied an Adirondack chair with his legs crossed and his coffee balanced on the armrest. He looked delayed-flight calm. Everyone else looked like they'd been in a wreck.

Paul came back from the hallway and sat across from Joel. His wire-rimmed glasses were smudged and still smudged, which was how you knew Paul Decker was not all right. Under normal conditions the man cleaned them every forty minutes, same as the refrigerator cycle.

"Medical examiner's been called," Paul said. "They said a couple hours."

"From Detroit Lakes?"

"Yeah." Paul rubbed the bridge of his nose where the glasses sat. "The deputies have been professional. Thorough. I told them Martin had some health concerns."

"Did he?"

Paul looked at him. "He was forty-seven and he drank like he was twenty-two. He didn't exercise. You saw what he ate last night."

"That's not a health concern, that's a lifestyle."

"Joel."

"I'm not being a lawyer."

"You're always being a lawyer." Paul said it without malice. He'd been saying it for twenty years.

Joel let that one go because it was true. The mug came up and went back down untouched. "Has anyone called Linda?"

"I've got her number. I've been waiting until... I don't know what I'm waiting for. There's no protocol for this."

"No."

"Yeah." Paul's eyes went to the draft board. Martin's first-round pick sat there in red, with the neat projected-points note Martin always added, the numbers that didn't matter anymore. "I know."

Gravel popped under tires out front. Both deputies turned toward the windows. A maroon Ford Explorer rolled in behind the sheriff's cruiser, and the woman behind the wheel leaned across the passenger seat for a Pyrex dish with foil stretched tight across the top.

So that was what she'd reached for.

She came through the front door carrying it with both hands, already talking. "Oh, good morning. I'm Detective Mallick with the Becker County Sheriff's Office. I'm so sorry about your friend. Is this the Kessner residence?"

Silence took the room by the throat. Six men had heard the same wrong name and were waiting for somebody else to fix it.

"Kessler," Paul said. "Martin Kessler."

"Oh, Kessler. I'm sorry, I had it wrong in my notes somewhere." She shifted the casserole to one arm and offered her free hand toward the room in general. She was stocky, maybe five-six, with gray in her low bun and reading glasses on a beaded chain. She looked like the neighbor who'd come over after seeing too many cars in the driveway. "Now, where can I set this down? It's green bean, my mother's recipe, and I know everybody says that, but she really did make it better than anybody. Needs about twenty minutes at three-fifty if you want it later."

Nate moved first. He took the casserole like you took a dish from an aunt at a funeral, automatically, before your brain had time to register how insane the gesture was. "Yeah, the fridge is right here."

"Thank you so much. And before anything else, I'm sorry, this is embarrassing, but could someone point me toward the restroom? The drive from Detroit Lakes, two cups of coffee, and I just, well." Her smile came out warm and faintly sheepish. Joel felt the skin on the back of his neck tighten. "Anyway."

"Half bath's by the front door," Paul said. "Just past the hall closet."

"I'll find it. Thank you." She set her leather notebook on the counter and disappeared down the hallway with the unhurried ease of a woman looking for the bathroom at a friend's house, not at a death scene.

Joel looked at Paul. Paul looked back at him.

"What the fuck," Tommy said from the deck doorway. He had come inside without Joel noticing and was standing there with his mug aimed at the hallway. "Did she just bring us a casserole?"

"A detective," Nate said. You could hear the podcast gears starting up, already sorting this into a story he could tell later if later still existed. "A county detective brought a green bean casserole to a... I mean, is that standard? Is that a Minnesota thing?"

"It's a casserole thing," Doug said from the couch without looking up.

"Right, thanks, Doug. Great distinction. A casserole thing. At a death investigation. From a woman who called Martin the wrong name."

"Keep your voice down," Joel said.

"I'm just..."

"I know what you're doing. Keep it down."

Mallick came back from the bathroom drying her hands on the sides of her blazer. She picked up the notebook, clicked open a pen, and got a quick nod from the younger deputy with the crew cut, the one who'd been asking about the draft board earlier. Then he stepped back and let her take the room.

"Now," she said, pulling up a chair at the kitchen table as if she'd been invited. "I'm sorry about getting here late. Road work on 59 had everything backed up, if you can believe that on a Saturday. Deputy Sievert and Deputy Kohn have been taking good care of things." She opened the notebook to a fresh page. "So I understand Mr. Kessler was found earlier this morning?"

"Around two AM," Paul said. "Nate found him."

"Nate." Her eyes moved around the room. "That's..."

"Me. Yeah. Nathan Griggs." Nate stepped forward like he'd been called on in class. "I found him at, well, closer to one-fifty. I looked at my phone right when I..."

"One-fifty. OK." She wrote it down. "And he was in the downstairs bedroom? One of the..."

"Upstairs," Paul said. "Master bedroom. Front of the hall."

"Upstairs. Of course. I'm sorry." More writing. "And you were all here this weekend, how many of you? I had seven in my notes, but..."

"Seven." It came out of Joel before he could stop it. "Six now."

The room flinched. Even Joel heard how it landed, too clean and too exact, public-defender phrasing arriving when he ought to have sounded like a friend. Mallick looked at him for a beat with an expression that could have been sympathy or simple attention.

"Six. Of course. I'm sorry." Her pen moved again. "And you're..."

"Joel Beckman."

"Joel." She wrote the name down. "And you all come up here every year for this? For fishing, or is it..."

"Fantasy football," Nate said. "It's a keeper league. Twenty-five years running. Well, twenty-five drafts. We started in..."

"Oh, that's wonderful. My nephew does that. He's on his phone every Thanksgiving arguing about waiver wire nonsense and it drives his mother crazy." She peered through the screen door at the deck. "And is that a hot tub I saw coming in? Nice one. Seats what, six? Eight?"

"Six," Tommy said. His voice sounded scraped raw.

"Six. And you all used it last night, or was that the night before?"

"Last night," Paul said. Joel watched the calculation happen in real time, that insurance-adjuster precision kicking in while he decided what to disclose and what to leave alone. "Some of us. It was a late night."

"Sure, sure. Draft night. Like New Year's Eve for football." She laughed, short and easy, and kept writing. "What time did everybody turn in, ballpark?"

"Midnight, maybe one." Paul looked around for confirmation and got shrugs, half-nods, the useless consensus of men who had been drinking and now wished they hadn't. "It was late."

"Of course." She wrote that down too. "And is there coffee? I saw the pot going and I'm still trying to wake up after that drive."

Paul got up and poured her a cup. Of course he did. Paul managed situations like claims: assess damage, contain exposure, keep the room functional. Joel had seen him do it at Martin's divorce dinner, at Nate's birthday when Nate drank himself into a fight with a bouncer, at a dozen weekends when somebody got sloppy and needed handling. He wasn't the cause and he wasn't the cure. He was just the man making sure the paperwork stayed in order and nobody's premium jumped too high.

Mallick held the mug under her nose and breathed in. "Oh, that's good. Nice dark roast. Martin, Mr. Kessler, had good taste." Her attention drifted to the draft board. "Is that your fantasy football thing? Oh my goodness. That's elaborate."

"It's a keeper league," Nate started, and Joel could see the whole explanation loading behind his eyes, league history, rules, scoring quirks, twenty-five years of context ready to pour out whether anyone wanted it or not.

"Nate," Joel said. "She didn't ask how it works."

Mallick smiled over the rim of the mug and said nothing, which shut Nate down faster than another question would have.

---

For another forty minutes she worked the room before she got to Joel. From his chair, he tracked her circuit as if he were watching opposing counsel work a jury: not for any single question but for rhythm, sequence, the moment the soft stuff ended and the real question showed up.

Out on the deck she talked to Tommy while he sat hunched forward in an Adirondack with his hands laced behind his neck. Joel couldn't hear the words through the screen door, only see Tommy's face lift once in surprise before Mallick wrote another note and nodded like she'd been told where to find the extra towels.

Next came Cliff in the kitchen. Cliff being Cliff, shoulders back, good eye contact, selling composure even here. Mallick asked about the house layout. Cliff corrected her on which guest room sat where. She thanked him, wrote it down, and moved on.

Doug got only a few minutes. He gave her nods, monosyllables, that agreeable blankness he gave anyone who pushed too close. Mallick didn't lean on him. She just wrote.

On her way back through the kitchen, she paused beside Paul and asked if the oven ran hot and whether he'd mind putting the casserole in around two-fifty. Paul said yes. Then she refilled her coffee, crossed back to the table, pulled out the chair opposite Joel, set the notebook flat, and said, "So. Joel. You're the attorney."

"Public defender."

"Public defender. That's different, right? Different from..."

"Different from most things people picture when they hear lawyer. Yeah."

"You must see some things in that job."

"I do."

"How long have you known Martin?"

"Twenty-five years. Since undergrad. Same fraternity."

"Twenty-five years. That's a long time." Her pen moved again. "And you drove up Thursday? From the Cities?"

"Hennepin County."

"Right. Close enough."

She checked her notes, or gave the impression of checking them. Joel had seen both versions often enough in court. Either way, the pause did what it was meant to do.

"Now," Mallick said, "somebody mentioned, and I apologize, I don't remember who, but somebody mentioned you and Martin had a disagreement Thursday evening?"

There it was. Joel felt his jaw tighten. He'd known the question was coming from the moment she walked in with the Pyrex dish, but waiting for it and hearing it were different things. Defendants got stupid in the space between those two events.

"We had a conversation," he said.

"A conversation. OK." She wrote the word. "And was that Thursday? Or was it Friday? I may have the days mixed up."

"Thursday."

"Thursday. Thank you." More notes. "And what was the conversation about?"

"It was private."

Mallick looked at him with patient concern on her face, or maybe only patient attention. There was enough warmth in it to be sincere and enough room in it to work as technique.

"Private," she repeated. "I understand. I do. It's just, with the situation, I'm trying to put together a picture of the last..."

"I understand what you're doing, Detective." Joel kept his voice in the register he used with judges, cooperative on the surface, locked underneath. "A man died in his sleep and you need a timeline. I'm telling you we had a private conversation Thursday night, it got heated, and it had nothing to do with his death."

"You're probably right. Most arguments between old friends don't, well." She gave a small, apologetic lift of the pen. "For my notes, what time was the conversation?"

"Late. Eleven, maybe eleven-thirty."

"And where was this?"

"On the deck."

"The deck. OK. Good." She wrote again. "And after the conversation, you..."

"Went to bed."

"What time?"

"Midnight. Maybe a little after."

"And you stayed there until..."

"Until Nate woke me up. Around two."

"Two. And between midnight and two, you didn't get up at all? Bathroom, glass of water, check your phone, any of that?"

"No."

"No." She wrote it down. The pen scratched over the paper while the refrigerator sat quiet for once and a deputy's radio muttered low from the hallway. "You didn't hear anything? Footsteps, doors, anybody moving around?"

"My door was closed."

"Which bedroom are you in?"

"Upstairs. Second guest room, end of the hall."

"Not the one by the bathroom?"

"That's Paul and Tommy's side. I'm at the other end."

"Oh. I always get these lake-house layouts mixed up. Too many rooms." She waved the pen as if embarrassed by herself. "So you didn't hear anything. First thing you knew was Nate knocking on your door at two."

"Correct."

Mallick put her pen down. She picked up her coffee instead and sat back.

"Joel, can I be straight with you?"

"You can try."

"You're very calm." She took a sip. "I don't mean that in a bad way. You're just — calmer than everyone else here. And I think part of that's because you're a public defender and you're used to this, and I get that."

"OK."

"It's just —" She put the coffee down and looked at her notebook. "Thursday night. You had this argument with Martin, and you won't tell me what it was about. And that's fine, Joel, I'm not pushing on that." She ran a finger down what she'd written. "But yours is the cleanest account in the house. Bed at midnight, door closed, didn't hear a thing, woke at two." She looked up. "Everybody else has got something out of place. A time they're not sure about, a trip to the bathroom, something. You don't have any of that."

She sat with her hands folded over the notebook.

Outside, a loon called from the lake, that wavering sound carrying across the water and in through the screen door.

"Nobody's a suspect here," Joel said.

"Of course not. This is an unattended death and I'm making sure I have the picture right." She picked up her pen again. "You're right that it was probably a cardiac event. That's almost certainly what happened. A man his age, his habits, I'd expect the same thing."

The word probably sat on the table between them. Maybe it was a move. Maybe she truly meant it. Joel couldn't sort that out, and the uncertainty worked on him anyway.

"Is there anything else?" he asked.

"Not right now. I appreciate your time, I really do." Mallick stood and pushed the chair back in. "I might have some follow-up questions later, if that's all right."

"I'm not going anywhere."

"No," she said, with a soft little smile. "I suppose none of you are, not today anyway. And the coffee really is good."

She headed for the hallway. Joel stayed where he was with the cold mug in front of him and the empty feeling those almost-interrogations left behind. One question never came.

The missing question wasn't whether he'd been angry at Martin. Any first-year law student at William Mitchell would have asked that inside three minutes. Joel had an answer ready. Yes, he was angry. No, he wasn't going to explain why the anger had gone private and humiliating before it ever got loud. He'd built that answer like a cross-examination: contingencies mapped, exits marked, language tight enough to say less than the truth without becoming a lie.

She hadn't asked. Maybe she missed it. Maybe she'd already found a better question and was waiting to see whether he'd volunteer the wound on his own.

Upstairs, her footsteps moved from room to room. Joel stared at Martin's draft board until the handwriting started to look older than yesterday.

---

Once Mallick disappeared upstairs, the group drifted back into the great room by reflex. Twenty-five years of these weekends had worn grooves into all of them: Cliff in his chair, Doug in the corner, Paul pitched forward on the couch, Tommy near the kitchen island because sitting still had become impossible. Martin's leather chair stayed empty. Nobody took it. Nobody looked straight at it either.

"So," Nate said, already leaning into the word.

"Don't," Joel said.

"Don't what?"

"Don't narrate what just happened while we're still in it."

"I'm not narrating. I'm processing. Some people process out loud."

"Process quieter."

Tommy still hadn't sat down. He stood by the island with his arms crossed so hard his knuckles had gone white. "She ask you about Thursday night?" he said.

"Yeah."

"The argument."

"Yeah."

"What'd you say?"

"Nothing. I said it was private."

"Good." Tommy gave one hard nod, sideline-call decisive. "Good. Don't give her shit. Don't give any of them shit. You don't talk to cops. Rule one."

"Tommy," Paul started.

"What? Joel knows. He's a goddamn lawyer. He tells people that every day."

"What I tell my clients," Joel said, "is answer questions truthfully and concisely and don't volunteer extra."

"Same rule."

"Not the same rule."

"Close enough."

Paul sat forward with his elbows on his knees and his hands laced together, staring at the floor between his shoes. When he finally looked up, he had the face Joel had seen at claims meetings right before a number nobody liked got read out loud.

"We should coordinate," Paul said. "Before she comes back down."

"Meaning what?" Cliff asked from his chair. He had barely spoken for the last hour.

"Meaning if she asks all of us the same questions, the answers should line up. Timeline especially."

"They will line up," Cliff said. His voice came out even and warm, the sound of a man explaining terms. "We were all here. We went to bed. Martin died in his sleep. That's what happened."

"Right, but the small stuff matters."

"What small stuff, Paul?" Cliff uncrossed his legs and crossed them the other way. "Dinner at seven. Draft at eight. Drinks. Hot tub. Bed. Martin alive when we turned in, dead when Nate found him. That's the story."

"When you say story," Joel said, "you hear yourself, right?"

"You know what I mean."

Quiet settled over the room. Above them, floorboards complained under Mallick's steps. She was in the hallway maybe, or the master bedroom, or a guest room. Hard to tell. That was the point, or maybe just old wood.

"She asked me about the hot tub," Tommy said. He had started pacing again, short ugly laps between the island and the screen door. "What time we got in, who was in it, how long, whether the water was hot, whether anybody touched the controls."

"What'd you tell her?" Paul asked.

"That I didn't remember exactly. We were drinking." Tommy grabbed the back of his neck and squeezed. "Then she called it the outdoor spa. Outdoor spa. Like she didn't know the word hot tub."

"Maybe she talks like that," Nate said.

"Maybe," Joel said. "Maybe not."

The room took that in. Tommy stopped pacing for a second. Nate opened his mouth and shut it again. Paul took off his glasses and cleaned them on his shirt, the first normal Paul move Joel had seen all morning, and the normalcy of it hit Joel harder than it should have.

"So you think she's putting it on?" Nate asked.

"I think I don't know," Joel said. "That may be the point."

"Or she's a county detective from Becker who actually brought a casserole because that's what people do up here," Tommy said. "That could also be true. Not everything's a play."

"Both could be true," Doug said.

Everyone turned. Doug had put his phone facedown on the cushion beside him and was staring at his own hands.

"She could be exactly what she looks like," he said, "and still be paying attention. Every time somebody corrected her, she wrote it down. Could just be note-taking. I don't know. It stuck out."

Nobody jumped on that. Paul nodded once and pulled the room back toward business.

"Answer what she asks. Don't volunteer past the question. And don't contradict each other." He looked around at all of them. "If she asks for the timeline, keep it simple. Dinner, draft, drinks, bed. Martin alive when we turned in, dead when Nate checked on him. That's true."

"Why did Nate check on him?" Cliff said.

All eyes went to Nate.

"I wanted to make a side bet," Nate said. "About his first-round pick. It was a running thing with us."

"At two in the morning?"

Cliff didn't raise his voice. He didn't have to. The question hung there anyway.

"We were drunk," Nate said. "It didn't feel weird then. We do stupid shit late all the time."

"It's fine," Paul said. "Nobody's saying it's weird."

"He literally just asked it like it was weird."

"I asked a question," Cliff said. "That's all."

"Can we not do this right now?" Tommy's coaching voice snapped on for real then, halftime voice, bad-loss voice, the one he used when panic needed a wall put in front of it. "Martin's dead. There's a cop upstairs in his bedroom. Can we get through one day without tearing into each other?"

The refrigerator kicked on, loud and rhythmic, the same old cycling noise that had underscored every midnight kitchen conversation for twenty-five years. Mallick crossed overhead from one room to another. A board groaned, then another.

Joel looked at his five remaining friends and fell into the old courtroom habit of cataloging the room: Paul's controlled grief, all that effort visible in the set of his jaw; Tommy's pacing body, unable to find one position that didn't look guilty or scared; Nate's reflex to fill silence before it could settle; Cliff's polished stillness, always a little too polished; Doug sunk down in his hoodie, saying almost nothing and noticing almost everything.

Twenty-five years with these men, and Joel was certain of exactly one fact: every one of them was hiding something.

He didn't know what yet. But twenty years in arraignment rooms and interview rooms had given him a feel for what secrecy did to air. It wasn't evidence yet. It was pressure: word choice going too careful, eye contact showing up late or not at all, Tommy loud in a way that felt defensive, Cliff calm in a way that felt rehearsed, Nate talking too much, Doug far too little.

Joel had his own fact boxed up too, and it wasn't tampering. He hadn't touched the scene, hadn't moved through the house, hadn't done anything between midnight and Nate's knock except lie awake in the guest room staring at the pine ceiling and trying not to go back out to the deck. What kept him there was Karen, and Martin refusing on Thursday night to deny the one thing Joel had driven up to ask him. It was the Panera in Edina, Karen with both hands around a paper cup, saying she wanted something different. It was the month since, every little detail in his life quietly rearranging itself into a shape he hated. Martin died before Joel decided whether he wanted an apology, a confession, or just the chance to hit him.

The coffee in his mug had gone cold. Joel drank it anyway. It tasted like nothing.

"I'm going to sit on the deck," he said.

Nobody stopped him.

Outside, September air came off the lake cool enough that his breath almost showed. Low sun laid gold across the water. Loons called somewhere past the dock. It was Martin's house, Martin's lake, Martin's weekend, still here without Martin in it.

A beer bottle sat on the railing from last night, its label peeled halfway off where Tommy had worried at it while he talked, thick fingers working the paper while his mouth kept moving. Warm stale beer clung to the glass. Joel stood there looking at it and thinking about how normal last night had felt while it was happening, which meant it would never feel normal again.

From inside, through the screen door, Mallick's footsteps started down the stairs. A moment later her voice carried into the kitchen, warm and chatty, asking one of the deputies about the cedar paneling in the hallway.

Joel leaned on the railing and watched the lake and thought: she's either an idiot or the most dangerous person in this house, and I still can't tell which.
