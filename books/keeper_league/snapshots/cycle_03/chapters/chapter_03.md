# Chapter 3

Tommy couldn't sit down.

For forty minutes, maybe an hour, he'd been on his feet. The sequence had gone missing somewhere between Nate coming up from the basement saying Martin wasn't breathing and Paul coming back down from the master bedroom with his glasses crooked and his shirt wet with whatever had come out of Martin. The microwave said 3:14. Tommy stood in Martin's kitchen with both hands on the back of a chair, squeezing until the wood complained. Sitting down meant the situation was settled. It wasn't fucking settled.

Nate was on the couch in the great room, still in the shorts and T-shirt he'd slept in, with his face gone hollow. Paul sat at the kitchen table with both hands around a mug of coffee he hadn't touched. Joel had taken himself out to the deck, which was either smart or shady. Tommy couldn't decide. Doug had disappeared back downstairs. Cliff was somewhere in the house, doing whatever Cliff did when things went bad.

Martin was upstairs in the master bedroom, dead.

Tommy crossed to the refrigerator and opened it. Tommy didn't want anything. Instead, he shut it again, went to the sink, and stared out at the lake. The water was black, the pines were blacker, and the sky was maybe one shade lighter than either. A loon let out that idiot laugh from somewhere across the cove, and Tommy pressed his forehead to the glass and thought, even the goddamn birds.

Since Nate told them, he'd been opening doors, closing them, lifting things, setting them down, pacing from table to counter and back. His body wanted a job. His brain kept handing it static.

"We should call somebody," Tommy said.

He'd already said it once, maybe twenty minutes ago. Paul had told him to wait until morning, wait until they could think straight, and Tommy had said fine because the word had come out before the argument did. Now his mouth had looped back around.

"In the morning," Paul said.

His voice was flat, flat in that insurance way of his, the voice of a man who spent his life looking at wreckage and assigning numbers to it.

"We should call now."

"It's three in the morning, Tommy. Who are we calling? The guy's dead." Paul pinched the bridge of his nose and held it there. "We'll call in the morning."

Tommy turned from the window. What he wanted was a useful sentence. What he wanted was for somebody to move, for the room to stop sitting there like this was already a story they would tell later. This was what he did. He pointed, barked, made people pick a job, and the next thing happened.

"All right, listen," Tommy said. He pointed at Paul. "Decker, when the time comes, you handle the calls. Family, police, whoever. Griggs, drink some water. Somebody eat something. Somebody do anything except sit there like a dead fucking goldfish."

Nate looked at him from the couch with an expression Tommy read as confusion and would later understand as pity.

"Tommy," Nate said.

"I'm serious. We can't just stand around with our dicks in our hands."

"He's dead, man." Nate's voice cracked. Then it dropped to the floor with the rest of him. "He's dead."

Both of Tommy's hands went to the back of his neck. He squeezed hard enough to feel the tendons jump in his forearms. Nate was still on the couch. Martin was still upstairs dead. Nothing in the room changed.

"Yeah," he said. "I know."

At the counter, he planted both palms on the laminate and leaned in until his shoulders hurt. The coaching version of this had lasted maybe ninety seconds. Long enough to bark assignments and act like volume could put order back in the room. There was no fixing this. Martin was dead. The halftime-talk bullshit had hit the walls and dropped.

Then the thought came back.

It had been in him from the moment Nate said breathing. It hadn't even been a whole thought at first, just pressure under the ribs, cold and ugly and not the same as grief. Now it pushed all the way through: the pills.

Friday night's joke had been old-man dick medicine. Actually, the joke had started years earlier. Every draft weekend Tommy brought Martin something stupid. He'd brought an inflatable doll when they were thirty, Rogaine when Martin's hairline started backing out, and once a novelty jockstrap that said FANTASY STUD and got worn over jeans for twenty loud minutes because Martin never knew when to leave a bit alone. This year Tommy had bought a sample pack of Viagra off a guy from the gym who sold them out of his locker like contraband protein powder.

At dinner he'd winged the blister pack across the table and told Martin it was so Karen didn't have to spend the whole weekend pretending to be patient. That line had started ten minutes of the usual garbage. Better stretch first, old man. Hope she signed a waiver. Somebody asked if Martin needed a Gatorade and a whistle. Martin called Tommy a goddamn asshole, laughed so hard he had to wipe his eyes, and set the pills beside his water glass. Everybody at the table had gotten one crude shot in. That was the point of a gift like that.

Tommy had seen him take one.

Late Friday, after the hot tub, after Cliff and Joel had gone to bed, after the Lagavulin bottle on the deck had dropped low enough for people to start making sentimental noises, Martin went upstairs. Tommy passed the open master door on his way to the guest room and caught him at the nightstand, pushing one blue pill through the foil with his thumb. Karen was in Minneapolis, or somewhere not here. There was no audience except Tommy. That almost made it worse. Martin saw him looking, gave him that watch-this grin, tossed the pill dry into his mouth, and winked like a teenager about to sneak off and jerk off on principle. Tommy had told him he was out of his goddamn mind. Martin said he'd been told. That was the last funny thing he ever said.

Viagra and bad hearts did not belong together. Tommy wasn't a doctor. Tommy was a high school football coach with a PE degree and enough first-aid classes to know how to count compressions and get actual help on the way. But he knew the one medical fact every man in America knew about Viagra, because the commercials never shut up about it. Old guys with bad hearts weren't supposed to take boner pills, not if they were on blood pressure meds, not if they'd had too much booze, and probably not after a long soak in a hot tub either. Martin had been drinking since dinner. Martin had spent an hour stewing in hot water. Martin had looked red as hell before Tommy ever tossed him the pack. Now Martin was upstairs dead at forty-seven, and Tommy couldn't stop doing the ugly math.

From the counter, Tommy looked at Paul, who was still staring into untouched coffee.

"I'm going upstairs," Tommy said.

"What for?"

"Bathroom."

Paul nodded. Men accepted bathroom lies on instinct. Tommy went.

The stairs were dark. On the way up Tommy skipped the third step because everybody skipped the third step. Martin had been promising to fix that creak since Clinton. Upstairs, a shell-shaped night-light glowed by the shared bathroom and turned the hallway amber. The master bedroom door stood open.

Nothing in Tommy wanted to go through it. But the alternative was worse. The three remaining pills were still on Martin's nightstand in a foil pack anybody with a badge or a clipboard could pick up and start asking questions about.

Tommy went in.

Martin lay on his back on the bed. Paul had done something to him. Tommy could tell that much right away. The body had been straightened. The hands were crossed on the chest. Martin's face had gone a gray-purple color that had nothing to do with the ruddy flush Tommy had spent twenty-five years drinking across from.

The room smelled like Martin: Lagavulin, cedar closet, old lake-house air, wet skin, and chlorine still clinging to the place. The smell caught Tommy in the throat so fast he had to stop in the doorway and breathe once before moving.

The lamp on the nightstand was off. In the hall light Tommy saw a half glass of water, Martin's reading glasses, a thick Civil War history lying facedown, and the blister pack. Three pills were still sealed. The fourth bubble was empty.

Tommy picked it up. His hand was shaking hard now, not a little tired tremor but a whole-arm shake that started in his shoulder and ran clear into his fingers. The foil crackled. The pack went into his sweatpants pocket, and Tommy left the room without looking back at Martin because he knew if he looked back he was going to make a sound.

In the hallway his elbow clipped the doorframe. The blister pack crinkled again, loud as a starter pistol in the quiet house, and Tommy flattened both palms against the wall and waited. Footsteps moved on the main floor. A door opened and shut. One of the other guys was up and doing whatever dumb panicked thing he thought he needed to do. Fine. Let him. Tommy had his own problem.

The guest room sat two doors down, twin beds under one of Martin's mother's loon quilts. Tommy sat on the edge of the mattress and felt the pills through the thin fabric of his sweats, three hard bumps against his thigh. Right now he should flush them. He needed to walk twelve feet, punch them out, flush, and throw the empty foil in his duffel. It was three steps. Three steps were still three steps.

But the shared bathroom was right beside the open master door, and Tommy could not make himself stand in there with the toilet roaring while Martin cooled on the other side of the wall.

So he sat with both hands locked around the edge of the mattress and stared at the paneled wall, at the mounted fish Martin's dad had caught in 1987, at the watercolor of the lake gone pale in the night-light. His mind kept landing on Denise, the kids, the school-board parents, the booster-club assholes, his job. All of it sat on the idea that Tommy Renaldi was a simple, decent man who did simple, decent things and had never been anywhere near the kind of trouble that got written into reports. In his head he pictured the words anyway: pills, cardiac arrest, hot tub, joke gift. Maybe there was a law word for it, manslaughter or negligent homicide or some other word Joel would know. Tommy didn't. Tommy knew only that he'd handed a friend a box of boner pills and that friend was dead upstairs.

Somewhere down the hall, boards ticked. Then the shared bathroom door eased shut and the medicine-cabinet mirror tapped the wall. Tommy went still. Another cabinet door clicked, plastic rattled, and then a zipper ran.

Of course. Jesus Christ. He wasn't the only asshole in the house doing arithmetic at four in the morning.

He opened the guest-room door an inch. Doug came out of the shared bathroom in a gray hoodie with his duffel hugged high against his ribs, head down, face washed clean of color. He didn't look toward Tommy. He moved past the shell night-light and down the hall in socks, a man trying to weigh nothing.

Tommy almost said something. What the fuck are you doing, Whitfield, was up first, and under it was the worse question, which was what do you think you're hiding. He didn't want either answer. Everybody had a thing. Everybody had some little private grenade they were trying to get out of sight before daylight. Fine. Great. Beautiful. Tommy shut the door before Doug reached the stairs.

He could have used the bathroom then. The hall was empty again. Instead he sat back down harder than before. If Whitfield had just cleaned out the medicine cabinet and somebody came up two minutes later and found Tommy standing over the toilet with a fistful of blue pills, that stopped being bad luck and started looking like a committee.

Tommy waited for the sun.

What the sun bought him was witnesses.

Pipes knocked in the walls. Coffee started downstairs. Somebody opened the slider and shut it too hard. Tommy came out of the guest room with the blister pack in his sweatpants pocket and found the kitchen lit ugly and yellow, all six of them back inside a morning they clearly did not want. Paul was measuring coffee with the concentration of a bomb technician. Nate sat at the table in yesterday's T-shirt with both hands around an empty mug. Joel stood on the deck in a hoodie, looking at the lake like he was preparing an argument for it. Cliff had somehow shaved. That felt obscene.

"We're calling now," Tommy said.

Paul nodded once. "Yes."

"No, I mean now now. Nobody wanders off. Nobody starts saying weird shit before we know who's coming out here."

Joel came in off the deck and slid the door shut behind him. "What does weird shit mean?"

"It means don't freelance."

Cliff let out a breath that wasn't a laugh. "That is a hell of a phrase to use before anybody calls the police."

Tommy turned on him. "You know what I mean."

"Unfortunately, I do," Joel said. "And stop talking like we're in a locker room. This isn't a team meeting."

Nate scrubbed both hands over his face. "Can we maybe not do rules right now? Jesus Christ."

Tommy felt himself getting louder and hated it while it was happening. "Somebody's got to do something. We got six guys walking around like they forgot how a phone works."

Paul set the coffee scoop down. The metal click against the counter was small. It shut Tommy up faster than shouting would've.

"I'll make the call," Paul said. "You go outside where you can get a signal and talk to dispatch if they kick us over to county. That's useful. The rest of us can stay out of your way for thirty seconds."

Paul had called it useful, and that was enough. There it was: a job.

Tommy grabbed Martin's fleece off the hook by the mudroom without asking. It still smelled like cedar and Scotch and a little chlorine. At seven he stood on the deck with the fleece pulled over his T-shirt, cell phone lifted toward the lake because that was where the signal held, repeating directions to the dispatcher every time the wind chewed half the sentence away. Twice he gave the address. Three times he described the turn off 59. By the time he came back inside his hands were numb and Paul was in the study on the landline answering the callback questions Tommy couldn't stand there for, spelling names, giving Martin's date of birth, explaining that yes, the man was cold and no, they had not moved him. That last part made Tommy look away. The deputies from Becker County showed up around eight-thirty. By then the blister pack was in the right pocket of Tommy's jacket.

The morning was cold enough to justify the jacket and the jacket had deeper pockets than sweatpants. Every time Tommy shifted on the couch, the empty foil scratched lightly against his thigh.

The deputies were young. One had a mustache that looked borrowed. The other looked like he'd started shaving during the Obama years. They asked their questions in the careful voice people used around fresh bodies: when did you last see him, what time did you go to bed, did he have any medical conditions you knew about. Tommy answered in short hard sentences. He'd last seen Martin around one. No, he didn't know. No, he wasn't aware of any medical conditions. They wrote it down, went upstairs, and came back down. One of them took a call on the deck where the signal was better. The other stood in the kitchen drinking Nate's coffee, which tasted like punishment.

The worst part was how long Tommy had waited. At 3:14 he could have flushed the pills in peace. Now it was daylight, there were deputies in the house, and the three blue diamonds in his pocket had become three tiny sirens.

They stayed, made more trips upstairs, took more notes, and did another round through the bedroom. By the time the deputies finally stepped outside to wait for the county detective, they'd been in the house long enough to empty half the coffee pot and learn Martin's middle name. The wall clock over the stove said 9:52.

When the screen door slapped shut behind them, the kitchen breathed once and tightened right back up.

"County detective's ten minutes out," Nate said, staring at his phone like it might turn into a different morning if he stared hard enough.

Paul nodded. "That's what they said."

Tommy looked at the front hall, then the stairs, then the five men in the kitchen with him. "All right. Listen to me. When she gets here, answer what she asks and not the extra. Don't start wandering off into side stories because you're nervous. Don't joke. Don't speculate. Don't volunteer."

Joel lifted his head from the study door. "Jesus Christ."

"What?"

"What you've just described is coaching witnesses."

"I'm describing not acting like idiots."

Cliff pushed off the counter. "Maybe let's avoid football speeches and legal seminars in the same room."

Nate gave one short laugh with no humor in it. "Cool. If she asks, we'll let her know Coach already did the pregame."

Paul turned from the sink. He didn't raise his voice. He never had to. "Tommy. Stop."

That was worse than a fight. A fight at least had motion. Tommy looked at the hall again, at the narrow strip of pine leading to the half bath, and realized he had one thing left to do before a county detective came in carrying a casserole and started asking the kind of questions that stayed in writing.

Tommy stood.

Paul was at the sink rinsing the coffee pot with the blank concentration of a man who needed a job for his hands. Nate sat at the table in his podcast cap, staring at his phone without seeing it. Cliff leaned against the counter with his arms crossed and his concern dialed in just a notch too carefully. Joel had the study door half shut. Doug was nowhere Tommy could see.

"I'm going to the bathroom," Tommy said to the room.

Down the front hall, old pine glowed yellow in the morning light. Tommy kept his right hand buried in his jacket pocket around the blister pack and felt the three smooth bumps under the foil.

Walking, Tommy pulled it out. His fingers were worse now than they'd been in the dark. He'd had no sleep, too much adrenaline, too much coffee smell, too much dead body in the air. The first pill punched through clean and dropped into his left palm. So did the second.

The third one tore sideways through the foil and shot out.

It hit the floor, bounced once, and rolled.

The thing was instantly recognizable, a small blue diamond in that cable-commercial sky blue. Anybody with cable and a pulse would know exactly what Tommy Renaldi was crawling after if they saw it.

The pill wobbled toward the baseboard and Tommy lunged too late. His hand slapped empty floor. The pill kept going and came to rest against the pine three feet ahead of him.

Tommy Renaldi went down on all fours in the hallway of his dead friend's lake house and crawled after a Viagra pill.

His knees cracked against the hardwood. The empty blister pack got crushed in one fist while the other hand reached for the baseboard. There it was, bright against the wood, like the house itself had spit the thing back at him. Tommy pinched it between his fingers and stayed there for one extra second with his forehead almost against the wall, a grown man kneeling on a lake-house floor because he couldn't let anyone find a stray dick pill.

He got up again. The left knee popped, then the right. Tommy checked the floor. There was no powder, no blue streak, no witness mark. The baseboard was clean. The kitchen around the corner stayed loud with ordinary kitchen noise. Nobody had seen him.

Four more feet and he was inside the half bath with the door shut.

The room was small: toilet, pedestal sink, framed loon print, frosted window turning the light a flat September gold. Tommy opened his fist over the bowl. Three blue pills sat in his palm, damp from sweat. Tommy dropped them in and flushed.

The toilet answered with the full old-house roar, pipes shuddering in the walls, the kind of flush you felt in your teeth. Tommy watched the pills spin, flash blue once, and vanish down the throat of the bowl. Clear water climbed back into place.

The empty blister pack stayed in his other hand. Tommy folded it twice, jammed it deep into his jacket pocket, and turned on the sink because a man in a bathroom needed to be washing his hands if anybody asked.

Then tires hit the gravel.

The sound came through the frosted glass as a car climbed the driveway, cut its engine, and shut its door. Light quick steps came up the front porch. Then the doorbell rang, the stupid two-note chime Martin had once described as a dentist's office in hell.

A woman's voice came through the hall.

"Morning. County detective. Am I at Mr. Kessner's house, or have I dragged this casserole to the wrong place?"

The front door opened.

"Kessler," Joel said. He was precise even now. "His name was Kessler."

"Right. Kessler. That's what I wrote down. My mouth took a detour." Her voice stayed warm, easy, busy. "I'm sorry. And yes, this is a green bean casserole. It seemed rude to show up with only a notebook. Could I set it somewhere before my arms give out?"

More voices followed, Paul being polite and Nate not saying a thing, which was wrong enough to make Tommy's skin tighten.

"And before I start bothering people, do you have a bathroom on this floor? I made a bad decision with gas-station coffee between Detroit Lakes and here."

Footsteps turned down the hall toward him.

Tommy looked at the toilet. The bowl was clear. His wet hands caught his eye. Then the door. Twenty-five years coming to this house and he'd never clocked the fact that the half-bath door had no lock. Now his hand clamped the knob like it was the only hardware in Minnesota that mattered.

"I think that one's occupied," Paul said from the hall. He sounded calm and careful, doing his adult voice. "There's one upstairs. Second door on the left."

"Oh goodness. Of course it is." A small laugh. "Upstairs is perfect. Thank you."

The footsteps turned away and went to the stairs. The third step barked under her weight.

"Lord, that's a loud stair," she said, still cheerful, voice drifting upward. "Pretty banister, though. Your friend had a nice house."

Her talking moved to the second floor and blurred behind the closing bathroom door. Tommy stayed where he was, hand on the knob, wet fingerprints drying on brass, heart kicking at his ribs.

Upstairs, the toilet ran, then cut off. A cabinet door clicked. Water hit porcelain. Tommy kept his hand on the knob and stared at the damp crescents his fingers had left in the brass until he heard the bathroom door open again.

Then the stairs, quicker this time and skipping the creak because somebody had warned her. Tommy dried his hands on his jeans, opened the door, and went back to the great room.

The draft board still hung on the wall. Martin's picks were in red. Tommy's were in blue. Friday it had been the whole weekend. Now it was a bright grid behind a county detective setting a casserole dish on the kitchen counter and setting her leather notebook beside it.

Nate looked over from the table.

"You all right?" he asked. There was no bit in it, no setup, just the question.

"Fine," Tommy said. "I'm fine."

His right hand stayed in his jacket pocket, thumb worrying the folded foil until the corners pressed into his skin. In the kitchen Mallick was asking Paul where she'd be least in the way, whether the fridge had room, whether that shelf would work or if another one made more sense. Her voice was warm and neighborly and relentless.

Tommy sat on the couch and kept still. The pills were in the plumbing. The pack in his pocket was folded twice, warm from his hand, sharp at the corners.

Then Mallick turned from the refrigerator and looked at him. She didn't look long, just long enough for Tommy to stop breathing. She gave the room a quick polite smile and turned back to the casserole. Tommy stayed on the couch with both hands buried in his pockets and waited for his lungs to start again.
