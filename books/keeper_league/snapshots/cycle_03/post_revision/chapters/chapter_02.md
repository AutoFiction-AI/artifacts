# Chapter 2

Nate was standing in the doorway of the guest room in his boxers and a podcast-branded T-shirt, and Paul knew before Nate said anything because Nate wasn't performing. Nate was always performing, at the draft table, at the grill, on the phone to his eleven listeners, in his sleep for all Paul knew. But the man in the doorway was small and still and his mouth was working without sound, and that was how Paul Decker learned that his best friend was dead.

"Something's wrong with Martin." Nate's voice came out at a register Paul had never heard from him — flat, airless, like the words had been squeezed through a slit. "I went up to — I was gonna ask him about the side bet, and he's — Paul, I think he's —"

Paul was already moving. Bare feet on the cold hallway floor, the bathroom nightlight throwing that sick yellow glow Martin always left on for guests who drank too much and needed a beacon to piss by. Martin's bedroom door was open at the end of the hall. Nate must have left it open. The light from the nightstand lamp spilled into the hallway in a thin stripe.

Martin was in bed, turned onto his left side, facing the window. The sheet had twisted down around his hips. His left arm was trapped under him. His right hand hung off the mattress, fingers open toward the floor. His mouth was open. His eyes were closed. Paul would be grateful for that later, grateful he didn't have to see whatever the eyes looked like, but right now he wasn't thinking about later. The color of Martin's face was wrong. Martin was always ruddy. Flushed from lake sun, from booze, from yelling about someone's draft pick, from the blood pressure he joked about and never treated. Now his skin was a grayish yellow that Paul had never seen on a living person, and the seeing of it was what told his body before his brain caught up.

Paul put his hand on Martin's neck and felt nothing. Warm skin, slack muscle, no pulse. The warmth was the cruelest part: it meant recent, it meant close, it meant that somewhere in the last hour Martin Kessler's heart had stopped and the heat was still leaving his body and Paul had been asleep twenty feet down the hall.

"Call 911," Paul said.

"I don't have — my phone's downstairs, I left it on the —"

"Go get it. Go."

Nate didn't move. His face was coming apart in a way Paul had never seen from him, the muscles pulling in directions that had nothing to do with comedy or performance or any expression Nate had practiced. His eyes were wet and he was gripping the doorframe with one hand like it was the only thing keeping him upright.

"Nate. Phone. Now."

Nate turned and went. Paul heard him on the stairs, heard the third step creak, the one everyone knew to skip, because Nate was not thinking about stairs right now, and then Paul was alone with Martin.

CPR came next because you start CPR. That was what you did. You didn't check for a pulse a second time or stand there weighing odds the way Paul did at a desk all day. First he got one hand under Martin's shoulder and one at his hip and rolled him off his left side onto his back. The body came over heavy and slack, the pillow bunching under one shoulder, the sheet snarling around Martin's legs. Then you put your hands on your best friend's chest and you pushed. Paul's training was a weekend course twelve years ago, mandated by his company for field adjusters who might encounter a medical emergency at a claim site, and what he remembered was: thirty compressions, two breaths, don't stop, don't second-guess the depth. The practice mannequin had smelled like new rubber sneakers, and the instructor had said "you can't make it worse" with the cheerful certainty of a man who had never done this on someone he loved.

Paul laced his fingers and set the heel of his right hand on Martin's sternum and pushed. The first compression produced a sound that was not the cinematic crack of breaking ribs but a soft wet yielding, a thud that Paul felt travel through his palms and up his forearms and into a place behind his eyes where it would live for months. Again, he pushed. The counting was supposed to help: one, two, three, four. What it actually did was give his brain a count to hold while the rest of him processed the fact that the body moving under his hands was the person who had driven four hundred miles to pick him up from a motel in Fargo when Paul's rental car died during his father's funeral trip. Who had shown up at Paul's house the night after Anne's miscarriage with a bottle of Maker's Mark and a roll of trash bags and had cleaned the kitchen without being asked and without saying a single thing about why the kitchen needed cleaning. Who had been Paul's best man and given a toast that made Paul's mother cry, and Paul's mother did not cry, and Martin had known exactly which story would do it, the one about Paul falling asleep in the library junior year and Martin covering him with his own coat and leaving a note that said "you looked cold, you looked stupid, I'll be at the bar."

Fifteen, sixteen, seventeen. Paul tilted Martin's head back and sealed his mouth over Martin's and breathed. The air went in and Martin's chest rose and the air came back tasting like stale whiskey and metal. Air went in again, and then Paul went back to compressions. Twenty-five years of friendship being expressed as a rhythm Paul could not maintain because his arms were already burning and his breath was already ragged and the counting was already losing itself in the gap between what he was doing and what it was accomplishing.

Because he knew. He knew the ugly small percentages, knew how quickly a room turned from emergency to aftermath, had once had to explain that difference to twelve jurors staring back at him. By the third set he knew he was just putting force into a closed room, a forty-seven-year-old man in flannel pajama pants driving his weight into another forty-seven-year-old man's chest after the real chance was gone.

Still he pushed. The count dissolved into meaningless numbers. His shoulders felt like they were separating. Soon the effort became its own reason, not to save Martin but to avoid becoming the person who stopped trying.

Then he stopped.

On his heels, Paul looked at Martin's face, which had not changed, which would never change again, and said, "Fuck."

Then he leaned forward and put his forehead on the edge of the mattress. The sheets smelled like Martin's laundry detergent, that cheap blue bottle he'd been buying since freshman year at the University of Minnesota. Paul had seen it under every sink Martin had ever owned: apartments, the first house, Linda's house, this lake house. A stupid detail, and Paul pressed his face into it until his eyes burned and his throat closed and his nose ran onto the cotton. He stayed there with his forehead on the bed and his hands on the mattress three inches from his dead best friend and did not move for what felt like a very long time.

---

Nate came back with his phone and Tommy and Joel behind him, all of them half-dressed and blinking in the hallway light. Tommy in coaching shorts and nothing else, the scar on his left forearm visible, one side of his head mashed flat from the pillow. Joel in boxers and a University of Minnesota T-shirt that had to be twenty years old, its collar stretched and the maroon faded toward brown. They filled the doorway and looked past Paul to the bed, and Paul, who was standing now, who had wiped his face with the back of his hand and straightened up because that was what you did, shook his head.

"Jesus Christ," Tommy said. It came out the way Tommy said everything, loud, definitive, but his face was wrong for the volume. His face was the face of a man who had just been told the news he already feared.

"Is he —" Joel started.

"Yeah."

Tommy stepped into the room and put his hand on Martin's chest, his enormous hand flat on the sternum Paul had just been compressing, and held it there like he was checking for himself, like the determination required a second opinion from a man who coached high school football for a living. The hand stayed there a beat and then came back, and as Tommy looked at his own palm the fingers curled slightly and Paul watched recognition move behind Tommy's eyes that was not grief. Not yet. It was closer to the look of a man hearing a sound he'd been listening for.

"Did you call?" Paul asked Nate.

"I tried — there's no signal in the basement. I came back up and got these guys first."

"There's signal on the deck," Paul said. "Or by the road."

Nobody moved toward the deck.

Doug appeared in the hallway behind them, blinking, his sweatshirt on inside out, the tag sticking up from his collar like a small white flag. Doug didn't ask. His gaze went past the bodies in the doorway to the bed and to their faces and back to the bed, and his mouth made a small shape that didn't become a word. Then he stepped back against the wall to make himself smaller, which was what Doug did when rooms got too full.

Cliff was last, arriving in pressed pajama pants and a T-shirt that still had a crease from the fold, because Cliff packed his T-shirts in tissue paper, because Cliff was Cliff, and he stood at the back of the group and looked at Martin's body on the bed and then his eyes moved to the nightstand — the Lagavulin, the glass, the room's layout — and Paul recognized the expression. Paul had seen it on men who walked into a bad room and went straight to angles instead of grief. Cliff was calculating. Someone was dead in the room and Cliff Haywood was already doing math.

"We need to call," Paul said. The word came back to him, and the repetition made it sound less like a plan and more like the line a man throws at a room that isn't listening.

"He's gone, Deck." Tommy's coaching voice, the one for halftime when a kid was hurt and the injury was real and what mattered was keeping everyone else from falling apart. Flat, declarative. "You can see he's gone."

"That's not the point. We're supposed to —"

"What's the ambulance gonna do?" Tommy scrubbed a hand over his face. "It's a thirty-minute drive from Detroit Lakes and he's — Paul, he's gone. You know he's gone."

"We still have to call."

"He's not wrong." Joel, from the far side of the doorway. His voice had shifted into that careful, mortared register — the legal architecture engaging. "We call now, we get a county deputy in forty minutes taking statements till dawn. Six guys in their underwear who've been drinking, trying to —"

"Yeah, and if we don't call, it looks like —"

"We call at seven, it's the same deputy." Joel's hands came up, the narrowing gesture. "Business hours. Same result. The only difference is —"

"The only difference is five hours where it looks like we sat on it."

"It looks like six men who were woken up at two in the morning and handled it badly." Joel held Paul's look. "Which is what this is."

The room was quiet. The refrigerator hummed downstairs in its forty-minute cycle. A loon called from the lake, that sliding, deranged sound that tourists recorded on their phones and locals tuned out, a bird screaming into the dark for no reason a human could use.

Paul looked around the room. At Tommy, who was staring at the nightstand now with an intensity that didn't match the moment. At Joel, whose legal reasoning was sound and whose reasons for wanting it sound were his own. At Cliff, who had stopped calculating and was now looking at the doorway like he was measuring how long it would take to get through it. At Doug, pressed against the hallway wall, arms crossed over the inside-out sweatshirt, saying nothing. At Nate, who was holding his phone with both hands like it had been taken away from him and given back wrong.

Paul wanted to argue. The impulse was to pick up the phone and call himself and let the county handle it. But Joel was making enough sense to sound like permission, and Paul's chest and forearms were still wet from the compressions. His saliva was on Martin's mouth. His tears were on Martin's sheets. Some part of his brain, traitorous and trained, started laying the pieces out anyway: Nate gone for the phone. Paul alone with the body. Martin's arm knocked out of place. Wet marks on the nightstand. His own spit on Martin's mouth.

Martin's body was nowhere near where Paul had found it. He had rolled Martin off his left side for CPR and left him canted across the mattress, one arm trapped under his ribs, the sheets twisted, the pillow half off the bed. Paul looked at all of it and saw interruption. What he saw was a room he had touched too hard.

"OK," Paul said. "Morning."

The room held its shape for another minute, six men and a dead man in a bedroom at 2 AM, none of them knowing what to do with their hands. Then Doug said, quietly, that he'd make coffee, and that was enough. The group drifted to the kitchen in a slow, useless shuffle.

Paul stayed and told them he wanted a minute. They gave it to him.

The minute lasted twenty-five.

What Paul did in Martin's bedroom in those twenty-five minutes was methodical and sickening. First he moved the body again. He got his hands under Martin's shoulder and hip and hauled him out of the diagonal CPR sprawl and into the middle of the mattress on his back. He pulled the trapped arm free, laid one arm by Martin's side, folded the other across the chest, then hated himself for how much that looked like intention. He straightened the pillow under Martin's head and smoothed the sheet back over Martin's waist. Every adjustment was a guess, and each guess felt like dragging the room another inch away from what Paul had actually found at 2 AM: Martin on his left side, facing the window, as if sleep had simply tipped over into death.

Then came the pillow and the glass, tipped sideways against the lamp base after CPR. The glass went upright. At the wet shine around Martin's mouth, he hesitated and wiped it with the corner of the top sheet. The cloth caught on Martin's lower lip. Paul stopped with the sheet still in his fist, seeing what the gesture had become, then finished wiping because half-done felt worse.

Next the surfaces: nightstand, bedframe, doorknob, the hem of his flannel shirt wrapped around one finger. The motions were small and practiced-looking, which made them worse. A man on television did this after murder. Paul did it after trying to save his friend.

By the time he reached the hall bathroom, the tap was already on. At the sink, he scrubbed his hands with Martin's Ivory soap until the smell changed, sweat and old whiskey replaced by that flat clean bar smell Martin had carried from apartment to apartment for twenty-five years. Water ran over his wrists. Foam slid into the drain. When he dried his hands on the towel and turned off the light, the room upstairs was tidier and less true.

The kitchen was lit by the fluorescent tube over the sink that nobody liked and nobody replaced because it was Martin's kitchen and Martin got to decide what was harsh. Paul sat at the table with both hands around a mug of Doug's punishing coffee and looked through the sliding glass door at the deck.

Tommy was outside, pacing. Even from behind glass, even in the dark, Tommy was unmistakable, the size, the athletic restlessness, the way he kept reaching the edge of the deck and turning hard as if somebody had given him a direction he couldn't follow. Back and forth across the deck. Back and forth.

Nate was in the great room, visible through the kitchen doorway, sitting on the couch with his phone on his lap and his hands on his knees. He wasn't looking at the phone. The performance was gone. He just sat there.

Joel had taken the chair by the dead fireplace and was already asleep, or pretending to be, his legs stretched out, his chin on his chest, his breathing even. Joel's ability to shut down under pressure was either a lawyer's skill or a dissociative gift, and Paul had never been able to tell which.

Cliff was somewhere. Paul could hear him in the hallway for a moment, and then he couldn't.

Doug sat across from Paul with his own mug of terrible coffee, not drinking it, turning the handle back and forth with two fingers as if that small motion was all he could tolerate. They looked at each other over the table, and neither of them said anything for a long time.

"You OK?" Paul asked.

Doug looked at him. "No." A beat. "Are you?"

"No."

"OK."

The coffee maker gurgled through its cycle. Paul sat there and he listened.

That was when it started.

The first sound came from the stairs, a creak from higher up than the usual third step. Everyone knew that third step. Everybody skipped it without thinking, same as avoiding the hot burner or lifting the bathroom door to latch it. Whoever was climbing had remembered. Their weight landed on the fourth or fifth instead and made the wood complain.

Paul checked. Tommy was still on the deck. Joel was in his chair. Doug was across the table. Nate had been on the couch. Paul leaned back to look through the kitchen doorway, and Nate was still there, hunched forward now, phone in his hands, the screen casting a blue wash across his face.

Either someone else was on the stairs or the house had chosen that moment to settle.

Then footsteps crossed the upstairs hallway, heavy and deliberate, the stride of a big man trying to be quiet and failing because big men still walked heavy when they tried not to. A pause, then Martin's bedroom door swung wider. The top hinge gave its faint metallic moan. Martin had been meaning to oil it for years.

Someone was in Martin's room.

Paul's first thought was practical: someone's checking on him. His second came sour and fast: someone's doing what he'd just done.

Paul almost got up. Instead he stayed in his chair and kept his mug.

Over the next two hours, the house kept giving people away. Its ordinary night noises were still there, pine beams adjusting to the temperature drop, the A-frame taking wind off the lake, the sounds you stopped hearing after the first night. Over that came another layer: people trying to move through the place unheard and missing by inches.

A little after three, the front hall toilet flushed. Somebody held the handle down hard and let the tank empty all the way, the kind of full-tank rush you used when you needed everything gone on the first try. The plumbing rattled in the walls afterward, Martin's old copper pipes objecting the way they always did.

Later the study announced itself with the thumb latch. Martin's parents had put that wrought-iron thing on the door in the seventies, and when it snapped back it made a hard iron-on-iron click no other room in the house could make. Paul heard it and stayed in his chair. Getting up would've meant he'd heard it, and hearing it would've meant he cared, and caring would've meant there was any reason to think about the study at that hour.

Later still, footsteps crossed above him again, lighter now. Whoever it was had shortened their stride the way people did when they were carrying something they didn't want to drop. Then came the soft rattle of the shared bathroom's medicine cabinet. The mirror hinge squeaked. Martin's mother had probably complained about that exact squeak before Paul was born. Something small and hard hit the tile, then got scooped up fast. The footsteps retreated in short careful beats.

A little later, after his sense of time had started to fray, blue-white light washed across the hallway outside the kitchen. A phone screen, held close to someone's body. It moved, paused, moved again, then went dark. Whoever was out there needed to work a phone before daylight.

Paul kept the sounds unsorted: the heavy steps on the stairs, the hard flush, the study latch, the bathroom mirror, plastic on tile, the blue-white hallway glow. By the end of it the whole house felt handled. Doug sat across from him on his third cup of terrible coffee, sweatshirt right-side-out now, which meant Doug had changed something too while Paul was listening and not looking.

By five the house had gone still again, or still enough that Paul could hear the lake at the dock and the pines outside the kitchen window. First gray light was coming through.

When he finally stood, his knees cracked. His lower back had locked into the shape of the chair and protested the new configuration. Then he walked down the hallway.

The study door stood two inches open. On his way to the kitchen hours earlier, Paul had passed it closed. Now the gap showed Martin's desk and the lake print on the wall, tipped a quarter inch to the right. Paul noticed because that print had hung straight for as long as he'd known Martin.

From there he continued to Martin's room. The door was as he'd left it. Paul pushed it open.

Martin was on his back in the middle of the bed, exactly where Paul had put him: arm across the chest, sheets smoothed, pillow centered. But the nightstand had changed. The Lagavulin bottle remained. So did Martin's phone, the glass, and the lamp. Beside Martin's small brown supplement case, though, a clean patch of wood the size of a matchbook showed where the foil-backed sildenafil sample pack had been. Tommy had passed it around on the deck like a dirty joke; by the time Martin set it beside the vitamins, one bubble was already punched flat. Paul had registered it during CPR without knowing he'd registered it.

Backing out of the room, he went to the shared bathroom at the other end of the hall. The medicine cabinet stood just unlatched, the mirror-door floating a quarter inch forward. Paul didn't open it further. He could see his own toiletries kit on the second shelf, a tube of toothpaste, Nate's mouthwash. The third shelf looked wrong. Too bare.

Doug was still at the table when Paul came back. His mug sat empty. The coffee level in the pot had dropped. He'd turned the sweatshirt right-side-out. Doug looked at Paul. Paul looked back. Neither of them said it.

Tommy was no longer on the deck. Paul could hear the shower running upstairs, the pipes shuddering when the hot water went past halfway and sending a hollow bang through the walls. Tommy was showering at 5:15 in the morning.

Joel hadn't moved from the chair by the fireplace.

Cliff was in the great room now, sitting where Nate had been, scrolling through his phone with an expression of focused calm that Paul recognized from twenty years of watching Cliff work rooms and deals and dinners: the face Cliff put on when the numbers were bad and the other party hadn't figured it out yet. Cliff looked up when Paul entered the kitchen and gave him the sealed-off nod he used in bad meetings, polite on the surface and impossible to read.

Nate was gone from the couch. Paul looked around and didn't see him on the deck, in the hallway, or in the kitchen. Gone somewhere at 5 in the morning, busy with whatever had pulled him off the couch.

Paul poured himself another cup of Doug's devastating coffee and stood at the kitchen window. The lake showed through the pines, iron-gray in the predawn light. The Adirondack chairs on the deck were wet with dew. The propane grill sat where Martin had made burgers eighteen hours ago, scraping the grates clean, scolding Tommy for leaving grease on the drip tray, everybody laughing. Eighteen hours ago.

"Deck."

Tommy was in the kitchen doorway. Showered, dressed in jeans and a polo shirt that was wrong for Tommy, too neat, too deliberate. His face was scrubbed raw. His hands were jammed in his pockets so deep the denim pulled tight, and he stood too still for a man that size.

"We should call," Tommy said. "Should've called hours ago."

"Yeah."

"I'll do it. I'll call from the deck, there's better signal."

"OK."

Tommy stayed there another beat, gave Paul a look Paul would spend weeks failing to name, then went out the sliding door. Paul heard his voice on the deck, his coaching voice, reporting the death of Martin Kessler to the Becker County 911 dispatcher with the clipped factual cadence of a man who had called in injuries before: name, location, situation, time discovered. He'd stripped the emotion out of it. Through the glass Paul could see Tommy's knuckles whitening on the railing.

Paul looked around the kitchen. The fluorescent tube buzzed. Morning came through the pines in thin gold lines and laid them across the lake.

The sun cleared the tree line. Paul poured out his cold coffee, rinsed the mug, and set it on the wooden drying rack Martin had once hauled back from Fargo like it mattered. Then he stood at the sink with water running over his hands and cried soundlessly while Tommy's voice carried through the glass door, giving directions to a house none of them would ever walk into the same way again.
