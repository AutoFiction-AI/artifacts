Seven college friends — all men, the host plus six — at the same lake house every September for twenty-five years. The excuse is the fantasy football draft, the debts and grudges are older than the league. The host dies on the second night. Natural causes, but five of the six tamper with the scene before morning. Some think they killed him. One just needs to delete what he said about the host's draft picks six hours ago. The sixth didn't tamper but looks the most suspicious. By dawn it looks like a murder designed by a committee that never met. The detective shows up late with a casserole, gets the host's name wrong, asks where the bathroom is before the body. The guests keep correcting her. She remembers every correction. Then a second guest dies, and this one the detective does not greet with a casserole. The host had been quietly protecting one of them for decades, and the investigation just pulled that thread loose. The clues to the real murder were in the comedy the reader was enjoying.

Murder mystery. Self-contained. Columbo meets Knives Out meets The League. The comedy does not stop when the investigation starts. The investigation IS the comedy: watching smart people lie badly, the detective's bumbling questions detonating secrets, five cover-ups tangling into each other, the absurdity of arguing about fantasy football while someone is dead. Character depth lives INSIDE the comedy, not alongside it. The tone only breaks when the second death happens. The dialogue should be crude, raunchy, and profane — the way old male friends actually talk when no one is listening. Do not clean this up. The death should happen early — by the end of chapter 1 or start of chapter 2. The detective should be on the page by chapter 3. Do not spend multiple chapters on pre-death setup. Rotate POV aggressively — no single character should carry more than 3-4 chapters. The reader will have been solving the wrong crime.
