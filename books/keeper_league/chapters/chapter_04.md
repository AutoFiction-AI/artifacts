# Chapter 4

The Becker County deputies had made seven trips up and down the stairs. From the kitchen table, Joel counted every one. He'd been planted there since quarter to eight with a mug Paul kept refilling, and on each pass the deputies skipped the third step near the top. Paul had warned them about the creak before Joel even thought to mention it.

Cup four sat half-finished in front of him. Too much coffee on top of no sleep and no food had his hands shaking in short fast bursts. His stomach had gone sour an hour ago. Every time a deputy passed behind him, the muscles between his shoulders locked. He'd seen clients go that still in arraignment. He had never thought about what it cost them. Now he knew. It felt like being made small in public.

The great room still smelled like Martin's scotch, the Lagavulin he'd poured too generously last night, because Martin never knew how to pour lightly, host reflex still firing even when people told him to sit down. Empty glasses still crowded the stone hearth where people had set them after the draft, nobody willing to decide yet what counted as trash and what counted as evidence. Morning light came through the A-frame windows in thick gold slabs, postcard light, the wrong light for this.

Tommy stood on the deck with both hands around a coffee mug like it was the only solid thing left. A gray clammy cast had settled over his face, the look of a man bracing for numbers he already knew were bad. His right hand kept drifting to the pocket of his zip jacket and coming back out empty. In the kitchen doorway, Nate was explaining the lake house's history to a deputy who had not asked and did not care. Doug sat on the couch in the corner with his hood up and his knees pulled in, looking at his phone with the blank concentration of a man reading absolutely nothing.

Nobody held still for long. Nate shifted from one foot to the other and filled every silence the deputy left him. Tommy came in from the deck, went back out, came in again. Doug's thumb moved over a black screen without ever waking it. Even Cliff's calm had a showroom shine to it, too polished to trust. The whole room felt arranged instead of inhabited, like six men had been told to wait in a place they recognized and now couldn't figure out how to sit in it.

By the fireplace, Cliff occupied an Adirondack chair with his legs crossed and his coffee balanced on the armrest. He looked delayed-flight calm. Everyone else looked like they'd been in a wreck.

Paul came back from the hallway and sat across from Joel. His wire-rimmed glasses were smudged and still smudged, which was how you knew Paul Decker was not all right. Under normal conditions the man cleaned them every forty minutes, same as the refrigerator cycle.

"Medical examiner's been called," Paul said. "They said a couple hours."

"From Detroit Lakes?"

"Yeah." Paul rubbed the bridge of his nose where the glasses sat. "The deputies have been professional. Thorough. I told them Martin had some health concerns."

"Did he?"

Paul looked at him. "He was forty-seven and he drank like he was twenty-two. He didn't exercise. You saw what he ate last night."

"That's not a health concern, that's a lifestyle."

"Joel."

"That's not me lawyering this."

"You're always being a lawyer." Paul said it without malice. He'd been saying it for twenty years.

Joel let that one go because it was true. The mug came up and went back down untouched. "Has anyone called Linda?"

"I've got her number. I've been waiting until I know what to say. I still don't. There's no protocol for this."

"No."

"Yeah." Paul's eyes went to the draft board. Martin's first-round pick sat there in red, with the neat projected-points note Martin always added, the numbers that didn't matter anymore. "I know."

Joel nearly asked what the deputies had found upstairs, then stopped himself. Paul would answer if he could. If he couldn't, the question would just thicken the room.

Gravel popped under tires out front. Both deputies turned toward the windows. A maroon Ford Explorer rolled in behind the sheriff's cruiser, and the woman behind the wheel leaned across the passenger seat for a Pyrex dish with foil stretched tight across the top.

So that was what she'd reached for.

She came through the front door carrying it with both hands, already talking. "Oh, good morning. I'm Detective Mallick with the Becker County Sheriff's Office. I'm so sorry about your friend. Is this the Kessner residence?"

Silence took the room by the throat. Six men had heard the same wrong name and were waiting for somebody else to fix it.

"Kessler," Paul said. "Martin Kessler."

"Oh, Kessler. I'm sorry, I had it wrong in my notes somewhere." She shifted the casserole to one arm and offered her free hand toward the room in general. She was stocky, maybe five-six, with gray in her low bun and reading glasses on a beaded chain. She looked like the neighbor who'd come over after seeing too many cars in the driveway. "Now, where can I set this down? It's green bean, my mother's recipe, and I know everybody says that, but she really did make it better than anybody. Needs about twenty minutes at three-fifty if you want it later."

Nate moved first. He took the casserole like you took a dish from an aunt at a funeral, automatically, before your brain had time to register how insane the gesture was. "Yeah, the fridge is right here."

"Thank you so much. And before anything else, I'm sorry, this is embarrassing, but could someone point me toward the restroom? The drive from Detroit Lakes, two cups of coffee, and I just, well." Her smile came out warm and faintly sheepish. Joel felt the skin on the back of his neck tighten. "Anyway."

"Half bath's by the front door," Paul said. "Just past the hall closet."

"I'll find it. Thank you." She set her leather notebook on the counter and disappeared down the hallway with the unhurried ease of a woman looking for the bathroom at a friend's house, not at a death scene.

Joel looked at Paul. Paul looked back at him.

"What the fuck," Tommy said from the deck doorway. He had come inside without Joel noticing and was standing there with his mug aimed at the hallway. "Did she just bring us a casserole?"

"A detective," Nate said. You could hear the podcast gears starting up, already sorting this into a story he could tell later if later still existed. "A county detective brought a green bean casserole to a death investigation. I mean, is that standard? Is that a Minnesota thing?"

"It's a casserole thing," Doug said from the couch without looking up.

"Right, thanks, Doug. Great distinction. A casserole thing. At a death investigation. From a woman who called Martin the wrong name."

"Keep your voice down," Joel said.

"I'm just saying."

"I know what you're doing. Keep it down."

Mallick came back from the bathroom drying her hands on the sides of her blazer. She picked up the notebook, clicked open a pen, and got a quick nod from the younger deputy with the crew cut, the one who'd been asking about the draft board earlier. Then he stepped back and let her take the room.

"Now," she said, pulling up a chair at the kitchen table as if she'd been invited. "I'm sorry about getting here late. Road work on 59 had everything backed up, if you can believe that on a Saturday. Deputy Sievert and Deputy Kohn have been taking good care of things." She opened the notebook to a fresh page. "So I understand Mr. Kessler was found earlier this morning?"

"A little after two," Paul said. "Nate found him."

"Nate." Her eyes moved around the room. "That's you, then?"

"Me. Yeah. Nathan Griggs." Nate stepped forward like he'd been called on in class. "It was after two. Two-oh-three maybe. I looked at my phone right when I saw him."

"Just after two. OK." She wrote it down. "And he was in the downstairs bedroom? One of the guest rooms?"

"Upstairs," Paul said. "Master bedroom. Front of the hall."

"Upstairs. Of course. I'm sorry." More writing. "And you were all here this weekend, how many of you? I had seven in my notes, but maybe I'm counting wrong."

"Seven." It came out of Joel before he could stop it. "Six now."

The room flinched. Even Joel heard how it landed, too clean and too exact, like he'd trimmed the soft parts out before it left his mouth. Mallick looked at him for a beat with an expression that could have been sympathy or simple attention.

"Six. Of course. I'm sorry." Her pen moved again. "And you're Joel?"

"Joel Beckman."

"Joel." She wrote the name down. "And you all come up here every year for this? For fishing, or is it football?"

"Fantasy football," Nate said. "It's a keeper league. Twenty-five years running. Well, twenty-five drafts. We started in college."

"Oh, that's wonderful. My nephew does that. He's on his phone every Thanksgiving arguing about waiver wire nonsense and it drives his mother crazy." She peered through the screen door at the deck. "And is that a hot tub I saw coming in? Nice one. Seats what, six? Eight?"

"Six," Tommy said. His voice sounded scraped raw.

"Six. And you all used it last night, or was that the night before?"

"Last night," Paul said. Joel watched the calculation happen in real time, that insurance-adjuster precision kicking in while he decided what to disclose and what to leave alone. "Some of us. It was a late night."

"Oh, sure. Draft night. Like New Year's Eve for football." She smiled down at her notebook. "And Mr. Kessler was out there with the rest of you till close to midnight?"

"No," Tommy said, too fast. "Not that long."

Paul turned his head.

Tommy kept going anyway. "He got in, he got out. Ten minutes maybe. He said it was too hot."

Mallick looked up. "Too hot."

"That's what he said," Tommy replied. He squeezed the mug once, hard enough to flex the lid. "Something like that."

Paul cleared his throat. "It was late. Everybody was moving in and out."

"Of course." She wrote again. "I swear those things are always either lukewarm or boiling. No middle on them. And that deck door still catches unless you lift it first, or did he finally fix that?"

Nate answered first. "You just give it a shove with your hip and it goes."

From the chair by the fireplace, Cliff said, "Lift and slide. If you shove it, it jumps the track."

Mallick turned to him as if grateful for the help. "Lift and slide. Thank you."

Cliff gave her a small smile meant to say he was the kind of man who noticed houses.

Joel took another swallow of coffee. Tommy volunteering the hot-tub timing. Cliff volunteering the door. Maybe both meant nothing. Maybe both were only the way men sounded when a stranger sat down and started writing.

"Is there coffee?" Mallick asked. "I saw the pot going and I'm still trying to wake up after that drive."

Paul got up and poured her a cup. Of course he did. When things went bad, Paul started taking care of whatever sat nearest his hands. Coffee. Plates. Somebody's coat over the back of a chair. A sentence calm enough to lay over whatever ugly thing had just been said. Joel had seen him do it at Martin's divorce dinner, at Nate's birthday when Nate drank himself into a fight with a bouncer, at a dozen weekends when somebody got sloppy and needed handling. It was how Paul kept the room from coming apart while he avoided looking straight at the worst thing in it.

Mallick held the mug under her nose and breathed in. "Oh, that's good. Nice dark roast. Martin, Mr. Kessler, had good taste." Her attention drifted to the draft board. "Is that your fantasy football thing? Oh my goodness. That's elaborate."

"It's a keeper league," Nate started, and Joel could see the whole explanation loading behind his eyes, league history, rules, scoring quirks, twenty-five years of context ready to pour out whether anyone wanted it or not.

"Nate," Joel said. "She didn't ask how it works."

Mallick smiled over the rim of the mug and said nothing, which shut Nate down faster than another question would have.

She kept them at the table another fifteen minutes, not really interviewing anybody so much as asking the sort of house questions that sounded too dumb to matter. When was the addition put on. Was the upstairs bathroom the one with the slow drain. Did the dock light work if you hit the switch twice or was that the old place on Pelican Lake she was thinking of. Every answer got a thank-you and a note. Every correction sat somewhere behind her reading glasses and stayed there.

When she asked whether the master had its own bathroom or if everybody used the hall one, Paul said the master had its own. Tommy said, "Hall bath's ours." Then Doug, still looking at his dead phone, said, "Nate and I are downstairs." Mallick nodded like she'd just learned who took cream with their coffee.

"That has to be a traffic jam after midnight, then," she said. "Six men, one hall bath, scotch and hot-tub heat on board."

"Three men," Tommy said.

Paul said, "Four if you count Martin's bathroom as off-limits to the rest of us."

"Right. Of course." She smiled and wrote it down. "I only ask because old houses like this keep a little record of people in the night. Toilet lid, bad floorboard, somebody clomping around for aspirin."

Nobody answered right away.

Nate tried first. "I mean, sure, people get up. We're not nineteen."

"Did you?" Mallick asked.

"Me?" Nate pointed at his chest like she might mean another Nate. "I was downstairs. I don't think so. Maybe once. For water. No, that was earlier."

Mallick wrote something down.

Joel watched Paul's face go flat and Tommy's right hand disappear back into his jacket pocket. He had not seen Tommy pull anything out of it all morning.

When she asked whether Martin had always taken the front bedroom, Doug said, "Always," without looking up from his dead phone screen.

When she wondered if the deck faced east, Nate said west, no, southwest, actually straight at sunset this time of year, and Joel watched him hear himself talking too much without being able to stop.

When she said the stairs were steeper than she'd expected, Paul said Martin's father had built the place in the eighties and left more room for windows than common sense. Paul smiled when he said it, and for half a second it was the right smile, the old one, the one you got before Martin followed it with something filthy. Then it was gone.

When she asked whether people used the front door or the deck more after midnight, three men answered. Nate said deck. Cliff said whichever was closer. Paul said it depended on weather. Mallick thanked them all and wrote another line. Joel had the sour little thought that if she asked what color the fucking moon had been, somebody in the room would correct somebody else about it.

The younger deputy watched all this from the hallway without pretending not to. He never wrote anything down in front of them. He just leaned a shoulder to the wall and looked from speaker to speaker as if the whole room might sort itself into categories if he stayed patient long enough. Joel kept looking for some soft place in it, the point where this turned back into a weekend with one terrible thing at the center instead of a whole house gone wrong. He could not find one.

---

For another forty minutes she worked the room before she got to Joel. From his chair, he watched her make the slow circle and felt each stop bring her closer. By the time she turned away from Paul, the back of his neck was damp.

Out on the deck she talked to Tommy while he sat hunched forward in an Adirondack with his hands laced behind his neck. Joel couldn't hear every word through the screen door, only pieces. "After midnight," Mallick said at one point, mild as ever, and Tommy answered too quickly with, "No. Before. I was done before that." He rubbed at the back of his neck until the skin there went red. When she asked something else Joel missed, Tommy glanced toward the hallway instead of at her and said, "I didn't go back in there."

She did not lean forward at that. She didn't pounce or sharpen or change tone. She only said something Joel could not make out and let Tommy fill the silence for her. Tommy looked over his shoulder at the lake as if the answer might be written there, then said, louder, "I went back to my room. I shut the door. That was it." Mallick nodded and wrote, and Tommy's mouth stayed open a second after the words were finished, like he'd realized too late that every extra word was making it worse.

Next came Cliff in the kitchen. Cliff being Cliff, shoulders back, good eye contact, selling composure even here. Mallick asked about the house layout. Cliff corrected her on which guest room sat where, then corrected her again when she called Martin's study the little office downstairs. "Upstairs," he said. "Left side off the hall. Pocket door." She thanked him, wrote it down, and moved on while Cliff drank his coffee like he'd closed something.

Then she asked whether Martin always kept the study shut. Cliff said usually. She asked whether the room had its own window AC or only the central air. Cliff said only central, unless Martin still hauled the portable unit up in July. He caught himself halfway through that answer, maybe hearing how far into the room he'd just wandered, and added, "I mean, I don't know if he did this year." Mallick thanked him again. Cliff smiled, but it came slower that time.

Doug got only a few minutes. He gave her nods, monosyllables, that agreeable blankness he gave anyone who pushed too close. Mallick didn't lean on him. She just wrote. Once, when she asked if he heard people moving overnight, Doug said, "Heard stairs," then seemed to wish he hadn't said even that.

"More than once?" Mallick asked.

Doug stared at the floorboards. "I wasn't counting."

"Sure," she said. "But more than once?"

Doug dragged a hand over his mouth. "Maybe. I don't know. House made noise."

"Old houses do." She stood without another word, which seemed to bother Doug more than if she'd kept after him.

She took Nate next, maybe because he had become impossible to ignore even while trying to disappear. He stayed standing until she asked him twice to sit, then perched on the edge of a chair as if full contact with it might count as commitment. Joel could hear most of that one because Nate forgot to lower his voice.

"I didn't go up there because something was wrong," Nate said. "I went up because I was awake. I mean, I was already awake. Basement couch, weird angle, my back was cooked, so I went looking for, not looking. I was just up."

"For the bathroom?" Mallick asked.

"Maybe water. Maybe the bathroom. Maybe both. That's the glamorous answer."

"And your phone was in your hand because?"

Nate laughed once. "Because I'm forty-six and I don't go anywhere in the dark without that stupid flashlight. Also service is better by the stairs."

"Mm-hm."

"That sounds rehearsed. It isn't rehearsed. I just know where the bars are in this place."

She wrote that down. Nate saw her do it and talked faster, explaining which corner of the basement ate calls, how Martin always complained the carrier map was bullshit, how nobody ever fixed lake-house internet because half the point of coming up was pretending the world couldn't reach you. By the end of it he sounded like a man testifying against a cell tower. Mallick thanked him with the same little nod she used on everybody else, and Nate sat back like he'd somehow talked himself into a hole he could not locate.

She circled back to him two minutes later from the doorway and asked, easy as anything, "When you found him, was the hall light on already or did you switch it on?"

Nate blinked. "Hall light."

"Already on."

"No. I turned it on. I think. Wait." He looked at the ceiling as if the answer might still be there. "Maybe the bathroom light was on and I hit the hall switch after. I don't know."

Mallick nodded. "Those old switches are never where you expect them."

Nate let out a breath like she'd just spared him something.

On her way back through the kitchen, she paused beside Paul and asked if the oven ran hot and whether he'd mind putting the casserole in around two-fifty. Paul said yes. Then she asked whether Nate had been downstairs with Doug all night.

"Mostly," Paul said.

Nate let out a laugh that died halfway. "Mostly is such a guilty word, man."

Paul didn't look at him. "It means mostly."

Mallick wrote that down too, like it was as useful as the deck door or the oven temperature. Maybe it was.

She did not let Paul go after that.

"Did you hear anyone up after you went to bed?" she asked.

Paul took off his glasses, wiped them on the hem of his shirt, and put them back on. "Not clearly."

"Not clearly."

"It's an old house. Things carry. Pipes, steps, people settling in."

"Sure. And you yourself stayed put?"

"As far as I recall."

That one made Joel look up. Not because it was damning. Because it was Paul, and Paul did not usually leave himself three exits in one sentence unless he needed them.

"Did Martin mention chest pain, indigestion, anything like that before bed?" Mallick asked.

"No," Paul said.

Then, after half a beat, "Not to me."

Mallick thanked him. She did not change expression. She only wrote a little longer than she had before.

Then she refilled her coffee, crossed back to the table, pulled out the chair opposite Joel, set the notebook flat, and said, "So. Joel. Let's talk."

He nodded.

"Twenty-five years with Mr. Kessler too?"

"Since undergrad."

"And you drove up Thursday? From the Cities?"

"Hennepin County."

"Right. Close enough." She gave him a small apologetic smile. "I'm sorry. I've been in Detroit Lakes too long. Anything south of St. Cloud starts feeling like the same trip."

She checked her notes, or gave the impression of checking them. Either way, the pause did what it was meant to do.

"Now," Mallick said, "somebody mentioned, and I apologize, I don't remember who, but somebody mentioned you and Martin had a disagreement Thursday evening?"

There it was. Joel felt his jaw tighten. He'd known the question was coming from the moment she walked in with the Pyrex dish, but waiting for it and hearing it were different things. While she worked her way around the room, he'd had time to build the answer and sand the roughest edge off it. Now his mouth tasted like old coffee and coins.

"We had a conversation," he said.

"A conversation. OK." She wrote the word. "And was that Thursday? Or was it Friday? I may have the days mixed up."

"Thursday."

"Thursday. Thank you." More notes. "And what was the conversation about?"

"It was private."

Mallick looked at him with patient concern on her face, or maybe only patient attention. There was enough warmth in it to be sincere and enough room in it to work as technique.

"Private," she repeated. "I understand. I do. It's just, with the situation, I'm trying to put together a picture of the last several hours."

"I understand what you're doing, Detective." Joel kept his voice level, cooperative on the surface, locked underneath. "A man died in his sleep and you need a timeline. I'm telling you we had a private conversation Thursday night, it got heated, and it had nothing to do with his death."

"You're probably right. Most arguments between old friends don't, well." She gave a small, apologetic lift of the pen. "For my notes, what time was the conversation?"

"Late. Eleven, maybe eleven-thirty."

"And where was this?"

"On the deck."

"The deck. OK. Good." She wrote again. "And after the conversation, you went where?"

"Went to bed."

"What time?"

"Midnight. Maybe a little after."

"And you stayed there until Nate knocked?"

"Until Nate woke me up. Around two."

"Two. And between midnight and two, you didn't get up at all? Bathroom, glass of water, check your phone, any of that?"

"No."

"No." She wrote it down. The pen scratched over the paper while the refrigerator sat quiet for once and a deputy's radio muttered low from the hallway. "You didn't hear anything? Footsteps, doors, anybody moving around?"

"My door was closed."

"Which bedroom are you in?"

"Upstairs. Second guest room, end of the hall."

"Not the one by the bathroom?"

"That's Paul and Tommy's side. I'm at the other end."

"Oh. I always get these lake-house layouts mixed up. Too many rooms." She waved the pen as if embarrassed by herself. "So you didn't hear anything. First thing you knew was Nate knocking on your door at two."

"Correct."

Mallick put her pen down. She picked up her coffee instead and sat back.

"Joel, can I be straight with you?"

"You can try."

"You're very calm." She took a sip. "I don't mean that in a bad way. You're just calmer than everybody else in here."

"OK."

"And you've got the cleanest account in the house." She looked at her notebook. "Thursday night, you had this argument with Martin, and you won't tell me what it was about. That's your business. But your timeline's neat. Bed at midnight, door closed, didn't hear a thing, woke at two." She looked up. "Everybody else has got something that wanders. A time they're not sure about, a trip to the bathroom, a detail they want to fix after the fact. You don't have any of that."

"Maybe I sleep better than they do."

"Middle-aged men who've had scotch, hot-tub heat, and bad lake-house mattresses don't usually sleep straight through," she said. "One of them gets up to piss. One of them goes looking for antacids. One of them opens the wrong door and swears at the dark. That's just how these weekends work."

"Maybe I got lucky."

"Maybe you did." She did not smile. "Or maybe you're better than your friends at sorting what sounds bad from what sounds true."

That hit closer than Joel wanted. Heat moved up his neck. Thursday night came back in one ugly flash: Martin on the deck rail, lake black behind him, saying the thing calm enough to make it worse. Joel had gone to bed with his molars aching from how hard he'd clenched them. They ached again now.

"I'm telling you what happened."

"I know you are," she said. "I'm just still deciding whether you're telling it in the order it happened or the order that helps you most."

She sat with her hands folded over the notebook.

A loon broke across the lake and Joel's head came up before he could stop it, the cry close enough to a person hollering from the dock to raise the hair on his arms.

"Nobody's a suspect here," Joel said.

"Of course not. This is an unattended death and I'm making sure I have the picture right." She picked up her pen again. "You're right that it was probably a cardiac event. That's almost certainly what happened. A man his age, his habits, I'd expect the same thing."

The word probably sat on the table between them. Maybe it was a move. Maybe she truly meant it. Joel couldn't sort that out, and the uncertainty worked on him anyway.

"Did somebody tell you I was the last one he fought with?" Joel asked.

Mallick tipped her head. "Somebody told me there was a disagreement."

"That's not what I asked."

"No," she said. "It isn't."

He hated that he'd given her even that much.

"Is there anything else?" he asked.

"Not right now. I appreciate your time, I really do." Mallick stood and pushed the chair back in. "I might have some follow-up questions later, if that's all right."

"I'm not going anywhere."

"No," she said, with a soft little smile. "I suppose none of you are, not today anyway. And the coffee really is good."

She headed for the hallway. Joel stayed where he was with the cold mug in front of him and the scraped-out feeling she'd left behind. One question never came.

The missing question wasn't whether he'd been angry at Martin. Anybody with ears could've asked that inside three minutes. Joel had an answer ready. Yes, he was angry. No, he wasn't going to explain why the anger had gone private and humiliating before it ever got loud. He'd worked that answer over carefully enough to say less than the truth without quite turning it into a lie.

She hadn't asked. Maybe she missed it. Maybe she'd already found better splinters to work at.

Nate had choked on the minute mark like there was more attached to it than the time. Then he'd nearly talked himself into a story about flashlights and cell bars and which light switch he'd hit first. Tommy had jumped in to say exactly how long Martin stayed in the hot tub when nobody had blamed the hot tub for anything, then told Mallick twice he hadn't gone back in there. Cliff knew which room mattered and which door stuck and whether the study ever got a portable air conditioner. Paul kept sanding everybody's answers down half a second after they came out, including his own.

Maybe that was only shock. Maybe a dead friend turned every sentence in the house strange. But Joel stopped feeling singled out. If Mallick was building a map, she had more than one houseguest on it.

She could still be exactly what she looked like: county casserole, wrong name, bathroom before body, all of it. Joel would have paid good money to believe that version. But fools did not get four grown men to volunteer the shape of a house they were supposedly just sitting in. Fools did not make a clean timeline look dirty by setting it next to five messy ones.

Maybe she was an idiot. Maybe she was the smartest person in the house. Joel couldn't tell, which was its own problem.

Upstairs, her footsteps moved from room to room. Joel stared at Martin's draft board until the handwriting started to look older than yesterday.
