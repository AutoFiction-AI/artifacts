# Chapter 9

At Sunday lunch, Joel Beckman sat at the kitchen table with a turkey sandwich he wasn't eating and the minor satisfaction of having gone fourteen hours without anyone asking about the argument. A personal best for this weekend, though the bar was roughly ankle-high. The previous record had been six hours, overnight, because even these people had to sleep eventually.

Nate was trying to scrape the last of the mustard from the jar with a butter knife. Cliff was at the counter cutting an apple into mathematically precise slices. Tommy was on his second sandwich, maybe his third. Joel had stopped tracking. Paul sat across from him with a cup of coffee he kept warming in the microwave, the same cup he'd been nursing since morning, which Joel found either disciplined or insane.

Doug was eating a bowl of cereal, because Doug had apparently decided cereal was a valid Sunday lunch option and nobody had the energy to argue.

The chair at the head of the table was empty. Nobody had mentioned it. Everyone had noticed.

"Mustard's done," Nate announced, holding up the jar. "We're officially post-mustard. I want that noted for the record."

"There's more in the garage fridge," Paul said.

"The garage fridge. Of course. Because Martin had a condiment annex." Nate set the jar down and took a bite of his dry sandwich. After a while he said, "His garage fridge had better beer than my actual fridge. That's not a bit. That's just true."

The kitchen was quiet except for the refrigerator's cycling hum and Tommy's methodical chewing. Afternoon light came through the windows at a low angle and caught dust above the table. The room smelled like stale coffee, mustard, and the wet brown edge of Cliff's apple, a Sunday-house smell that said the weekend had gone on too long.

"So what happens now?" Tommy asked the table. It came out in the tone coaches used when they already had an answer and wanted the room to arrive there on its own.

"Mallick said she'd be back this afternoon," Paul said. "She's at the station going through her notes."

"I mean with us. The house. All of it."

"Martin's estate will handle the house," Cliff said, and Joel heard the smoothness in it, the way Cliff's voice could flatten any question into terms of property and transfer. Cliff knew things about Martin's finances that the rest of them didn't. Or thought he did, anyway. For two days now Joel had watched him move through the house like he was appraising it.

No one had said suspect out loud since Saturday, not after Paul snapped that it was a hell of a word to use in Martin's kitchen. But the house had built a quiet little safety plan around Joel all the same. If he stepped onto the deck, somebody else drifted out there. If he took too long in the downstairs bathroom, Tommy hollered through the hall asking if he was alive. Even Doug kept surfacing in whatever room Joel was in, as if by accident. It was amateur surveillance, obvious enough to insult him. Joel might have been insulted if he hadn't also understood it. Anger made people nervous. Anger plus a dead body made them start taking attendance.

"I just want to go home," Doug said quietly. Then he caught himself. "Not that. I mean. We all do. Obviously."

"Mallick hasn't cleared us yet," Joel said.

Tommy looked at him. "She hasn't cleared anybody, Beck."

"She's cleared some of you more than others."

"What's that supposed to mean?"

Instead of answering, Joel took a bite of the sandwich. The turkey was dry. Martin always bought the good deli turkey, the kind you had to ask for at the counter, and somehow it was already going stale. Two days. That was how long it took for the provisions to start outlasting the host.

Nate was watching him with the expression Joel had learned to recognize as the lead-in to a bit: eyebrows up, mouth slightly open, the podcaster warming up. This time Nate didn't launch. He just kept chewing and looked at Joel with something that wasn't quite sympathy and wasn't quite curiosity.

"Can I ask you something?" Nate said.

"You can ask."

"Are you, and this is just me genuinely asking, not for anything, just between us, gonna tell us what you and Martin were fighting about?"

The table went quiet. Not silent. Tommy was still chewing. The fridge was still humming. But the conversational temperature dropped in a way Joel could feel against his skin.

"I told Mallick it wasn't relevant."

"You told Mallick it wasn't relevant to her investigation," Paul said carefully. "That's not the same as it not being relevant."

"It's not relevant."

"Bullshit," Tommy said. Matter-of-fact, not angry. Coaching mode. "You show up Thursday night already pissed off, you're in his face at midnight screaming loud enough that I could hear it from the hot tub, and Martin's dead twelve hours later. That's relevant."

"Martin died of a cardiac event."

"We don't actually know that yet," Paul said.

"We effectively know that."

"Joel." Paul set his coffee down. The microwave-warmed cup made a soft thud on the table. "We've been here for three days. Mallick keeps coming back. Nobody's been cleared. And you're sitting here telling us the argument everybody keeps circling isn't relevant."

"The investigation isn't centered on the argument."

"It's centered on you," Cliff said without looking up from his apple. "Which is the same thing."

Joel pushed his plate away. The sandwich sat there with one bite missing, reproaching him with its dryness.

The truth was that Joel had been waiting for this conversation. He hadn't wanted it, but he'd been waiting for it, the way you waited for a dental appointment you'd already rescheduled twice. The argument was the one piece of information that still belonged to him and not to the investigation, and holding it had become physically exhausting in a way he hadn't expected. His jaw ached. His shoulders had been knotted since Saturday morning. For three nights he'd slept maybe three hours at a time in a guest room that smelled like Martin's fabric softener, lying in the dark and rehearsing both the speech he'd planned to give Martin and the speech he now had to give these men. Lately the two speeches kept bleeding into each other until he couldn't tell which one belonged to which audience.

What he hadn't rehearsed was saying Karen's name in this kitchen. Once he said it here, in Martin's house, it stopped belonging to him and Karen and Martin and became group property, something these men could pass around, argue over, and fit into whatever rules they used to make sense of themselves. That was probably childish. It was still the bargain he'd been trying to make all weekend: let them suspect the anger, keep the humiliation private.

"It's personal," he said.

"We know it's personal," Nate said. "That's why we're asking."

"You don't know what kind of personal."

"Then tell us what kind."

Doug was staring at his cereal. He hadn't said anything since the I want to go home admission and the quick retraction after it. Joel could still see Doug's eyes moving from one man to the next, the quiet surveillance Doug had perfected over twenty-five years of being the one nobody watched.

"Look," Tommy said, leaning forward, elbows on the table. "We're all stuck here. Dead friend, detective, same questions. And you're the piece that doesn't add up."

"I'm not the—"

"Yeah you are. You know you are." Tommy tapped the table once. "So say it. Whatever you got, just say it, because the rest of us sitting here pretending we're not wondering? That's bullshit."

Joel almost laughed. Pure Tommy — the halftime challenge, the coaching certainty. Tommy Renaldi, who had flushed evidence down a toilet while a detective stood on the other side of the door, lecturing Joel about transparency.

"Okay," Joel said. The word came out smaller than he intended. "You want to know what the argument was about."

"Yes," said four voices at roughly the same time. Doug's mouth moved but no sound came out.

Joel looked at the empty chair at the head of the table. Martin's chair. The chair where Martin Kessler had sat for twenty-five drafts, running the league, managing the group, making sure everyone had a drink, making sure Tommy came back every year, making sure the whole machine kept turning. The chair where the most generous, most controlling, most thoughtfully selfish man Joel had ever known had presided over a weekend that was equal parts reunion and performance.

"Martin was fucking my ex-wife," Joel said.

---

The sentence hit the table like a dropped plate.

Nate's sandwich stopped halfway to his mouth. Tommy's jaw paused mid-chew. Cliff set down the knife. Paul closed his eyes and sat very still through one full breath.

Doug looked straight at Joel. It was only a look, but Joel recognized it because he'd been seeing it in the mirror for a month now: the face of a person trying to understand a betrayal big enough to rearrange the furniture in his head.

"Karen?" Paul said.

"That's the one."

"Jesus Christ," Tommy said.

"For eight months. Since January." Joel heard how steady his own voice was and found it strange. In all the versions of this conversation he'd rehearsed, he'd been shouting by now. Instead the facts came out flat and precise, the way he delivered an opening statement, leading with the evidence and leaving the emotion for later. "She told me a month before the draft. We were in the parking lot of Ellie's dorm, dropping off her stuff for fall semester. Karen waited till Ellie went inside and then told me in the car. Slichter Hall parking lot. She said she wanted me to hear it from her."

He could still see the fluorescent August light flattening the inside of the car, Ellie's bedding receipt crumpled in the cup holder, the campus map folded wrong between the seats because Karen had been using it ten minutes earlier and then just dropped it. Karen kept both hands on the steering wheel though the engine was off. She told it straight, eyes forward, like if she looked at him she'd have to stop. Joel had watched a kid drag a box fan across the parking lot and spent one stupid second trying to convince himself he'd misheard the noun.

"Hold on," Nate said. "A month before the draft. So you came here knowing."

"I came here to talk to him."

"You came here to confront him," Cliff said.

"I came here to talk to him. I had a whole plan for being calm about it. I was going to wait till Saturday morning, when everybody was hungover and slow, take him down to the dock, and ask him why."

"Instead you screamed at him Thursday night," Tommy said.

"I didn't scream."

"Beck, I heard you from the hot tub."

"I raised my voice. There's a legal distinction."

"There really isn't."

Joel rubbed his face with both hands. Legal precision was habit. Sarcasm was habit. Neither one helped him explain what it felt like to sit in a dorm parking lot watching your daughter wave from a second-floor window while your ex-wife told you that your friend of twenty-five years had been inside her since New Year's.

"Martin and Karen," Paul said. "How."

"How what?"

"How did it start? How long did you know? How did Karen even..."

"I don't know how it started. Karen said it started at a New Year's party. I wasn't there because I had Ellie that weekend. They ran into each other and one thing led to another and apparently one thing kept leading to eight months of another." Joel could hear the cross-examination tone taking over, sharp and clipped. "She said it wasn't planned. She said she didn't mean for it to happen. She said all the things people say when they absolutely meant for it to happen."

"Did Martin know you knew?" Doug asked.

His voice was so quiet everybody turned to him. It was the first interruption Doug had made all day.

"Yeah." Joel swallowed. "I called him three weeks before the draft and told him I knew. He said he was sorry. He said it was over. He said he'd ended it in August."

"Had he?" Cliff asked.

"I have no idea. Karen says yes. Martin said yes. I can't cross-examine a dead man."

"Then why'd you still come?" Paul asked.

Joel gave a short laugh. "Because not coming would've been letting him keep it clean. He gets eight months with my ex-wife and then I politely skip draft weekend so nobody has to feel weird? Fuck that. I came because he was going to look at me while I knew."

"So Thursday night," Nate said. "That's what the argument was."

"Thursday night I got drunk and all the calm shit I'd planned to say turned into the actual shit I wanted to say. Which was louder and less articulate." Joel picked up the abandoned sandwich, looked at it, and put it back down. "I called him a piece of shit. Several times. In several configurations. He stood there and took it because that's what Martin did. He absorbed things. Hands in his pockets. Head tilted. Like he was listening to a weather report he found mildly interesting. I wanted to hit him so badly I could taste it."

"Did you?" Tommy asked.

"No. I went to bed. I lay there for three hours thinking of every goddamn thing I should've said and didn't, and then Paul banged on my door at two in the morning and Martin was dead."

The refrigerator cycled off. In the sudden absence of it, the house went properly quiet. Joel could hear the lake through the windows, that small steady lapping that didn't care what was happening inside.

Paul opened his eyes. Grief was still there. So was something new. Joel couldn't name it yet, but he could see the crack forming.

"I'm sorry," Paul said.

"Don't be sorry for me. Be sorry that he died before I could finish telling him what I thought of him." Joel's voice tightened. "That's the part that gets me. I had things to say to him. I drove up here with them loaded and ready. And then he just fucking died."

Tommy leaned back and blew air through his teeth. His gaze went to the ceiling, then to Joel, then to the empty chair.

"Shit, Beck," he said.

"Yeah."

---

By Joel's professional assessment, what followed was the most structurally bizarre conversation he'd ever participated in, and he'd spent sixteen years in Hennepin County criminal court.

They moved out to the deck because the kitchen had gotten too small for it, and because Nate said he needed air. Joel suspected Nate mostly needed room to think out loud without clipping the counter on his way around. Tommy came back from the garage fridge with beers, the good IPAs Martin hoarded. Paul brought his coffee. Doug took the Adirondack chair farthest from the center of things, his cereal still sitting inside and going soft in its bowl.

The September afternoon was golden and ridiculous. The lake was flat. The trees were just starting to turn at the edges. Two loons called somewhere out past the dock. It was exactly the kind of afternoon Martin built the whole weekend around, the kind of clean expensive weather that could almost make this feel like a bad weekend instead of damage.

Nate settled into a chair with his beer. "Okay. I have to ask a gross question."

"That's how we know you're alive," Joel said.

"No, I'm serious. Was this like... were they just fucking, or were they doing couple shit too? Because those are different categories of offense."

"Jesus Christ," Paul said, at the same time Tommy said, "Doesn't matter."

"What? They are. One is a drunken horrible thing. The other is your friend sticking his dick into your life for eight months."

"Please don't phrase it like that," Paul said.

"How would you like it phrased?"

"I'd like not to hear 'sticking his dick into your life.'"

"I don't know the details," Joel said. "I didn't ask whether he left a toothbrush there."

Joel had made the mistake of thinking the hardest part would be saying it out loud. It turned out the harder part was sitting here while they sorted it into categories, as if Nate might actually draft an adultery bylaw if you gave him a legal pad. He took a swallow of Martin's beer and tasted grapefruit, bitterness, and the stale little insult of drinking someone else's stash on someone else's deck.

Tommy pointed his bottle at Nate. "Here's the rule. You don't fuck your friend's ex-wife. Period. I don't care if they've been divorced six months or six years."

"They're divorced, Tommy," Cliff said.

"I know they're divorced. I said friend."

Cliff leaned against the railing with his apple. The careful slices were gone. He was down to the core now, scraping fruit from around the seeds with his front teeth. "She's his ex-wife, Tommy. She's a grown woman who made a—I'm saying Joel doesn't own her."

Nate held up both hands. "I'm not saying own. I'm saying there should still be a waiting period. Minimum."

"That's not better," Paul said.

"It's worse, actually," Joel said.

Tommy kept chopping the air with his bottle like a second gesture might turn outrage into policy. Cliff had worried the apple down to seeds and stem. Nate only looked straight at Joel when Karen came up; the rest of the time he aimed his arguments at the lake. None of them had language worth a damn for it. Still, the knot under Joel's sternum eased a notch. The thing was out here now, ugly and specific, and he wasn't carrying it by himself anymore.

"What about Karen?" Nate asked. "She gets a vote in this. She's not just the field everybody's arguing over. She chose it too."

"Karen's choices are Karen's business," Joel said. "I'm not mad at Karen."

Nate blinked. "You're not?"

"No. We're divorced. She doesn't owe me loyalty. She can sleep with whoever she wants. Martin was the one who sat at my table for twenty-five years and then put his cock in my ex-wife anyway."

Paul made a low sound in his throat and took a drink of coffee that had to be cold by now. Tommy stared out at the lake like he was trying to find somewhere to throw the whole conversation. Even Cliff lost his smoothness for half a second.

"Okay," Nate said. "Yeah. When you say it like that, the hearing gets very simple."

Joel laughed before he meant to. It surprised all of them, him most of all. "Hearing. Great. Good. Do I get compensatory picks or what?"

That got Nate first, a short bark that escaped before he could stop it. Tommy followed with a low laugh of his own. Even Paul's mouth twitched. Cliff smiled thinly. For half a second the whole thing had rules again, and Joel could see how much he liked that. Joel folded over Martin's beer and laughed into the neck of the bottle, brief and helpless. Foam slicked his thumb. He wiped it on his jeans and took a swallow while the deck kept going, because that was easier than acting like the joke hadn't gotten him.

Cliff's smile disappeared first. "And it still isn't murder."

Paul turned to him. "No. But it's why Joel was in his face Thursday night, and I'm done pretending otherwise."

The hearing joke had gotten laughs. Paul's sentence knocked the deck flat. Paul set the mug on the rail with a small hard click and stayed standing.

"He was my best friend," Paul said. "I've spent two days trying to be fair to him. I don't think I've been fair to you." That last part was to Joel. Then Paul looked back at the lake. "Next time Mallick asks about Thursday night, I'm telling her why."

Joel should've felt vindicated. Instead the back of his neck went hot. Being believed and being written down were about to become the same thing.

Nobody spoke for a second. The deck seemed to pull tighter around the sentence.

Cliff straightened from the railing. "You're going to hand her motive because suddenly everybody wants to feel morally clean?"

"This isn't about clean."

"It's about—" Nate waved a hand. "It's about not feeding her a different fake answer every time she comes back, Cliff." No joke on either side of it. The air changed again.

Cliff ignored him. "The minute you say affair, she stops hearing cardiac event and starts hearing reason."

"She already hears reason," Doug said.

All five of them looked at him.

Doug shifted in the Adirondack chair. "Sorry. I just mean she keeps asking the same thing because the answer keeps wobbling. This doesn't wobble."

Doug almost never spent that many words in a row on somebody else's business. Hearing him do it made Joel straighten in his chair before he caught himself.

Tommy pointed at him. "That's exactly right."

"It's not exactly right," Cliff said. "It's a dead man sleeping with a divorced woman. Ugly, yes. Not some capital crime."

Joel felt the laugh go out of him. "Could everybody stop grading the offense like this is instant replay? That's not the problem."

Cliff looked at him. "Then what is?"

"The problem is everybody in this house has been acting like I blew up at him over nothing. That's the problem." Joel heard the rawness in his own voice and let it stay there. "You want to say divorced woman like that cleans it up, fine. It doesn't clean up him sitting at my table for twenty-five years and doing it anyway."

Tommy nodded once. Hard. "Exactly."

Paul didn't raise his voice. "I'm not—nobody's calling him a monster. I'm saying Thursday night wasn't just some random blowup, and she already knows it, Cliff. She knew it Saturday."

Cliff let out a short breath through his nose. "And Karen? You want her in a police notebook because the rest of you need the story to feel balanced?"

There it was. Not Martin. Not fairness. Ellie hearing it sideways from somebody with a badge.

Joel looked out at the water so he wouldn't have to look at any of them while he answered. A boat moved somewhere beyond the trees, just enough engine noise to remind him the rest of the lake still had ordinary people on it. "I don't want Ellie hearing this from somebody else," he said. "I don't want Karen getting a call from a county detective on a Sunday because Martin couldn't keep his dick in his pants. I don't want any of that."

Paul turned now. "I'm not volunteering Ellie. I'm not telling her anything Joel doesn't want out there. But the next time she asks me about Thursday night—" He exhaled. "I'm telling her why. Not everything. Just why."

Nate rubbed a hand over his face. "Yeah. Same. I can't do the old-friends-had-words version anymore. It's bullshit."

Tommy took a swallow of beer. "Tell her. She wants motive, give her the real motive. Beck yelled at him because Martin was fucking his ex. That's cleaner than half the crap we've already fed her."

"That's exactly why you don't tell her," Cliff said. His smoothness was starting to show the seams. "We've already got a detective circling the house. You want to narrow the beam onto Joel even more?"

"Maybe she narrows it the right way, then," Doug said, almost to his lap.

"What?"

Doug didn't look up. "At the actual reason. Not some made-up one."

Nate stared at him. "Whit, you've said more in the last two minutes than the last two days."

Doug gave the smallest shrug. "I know."

Doug kept staring at the deck boards like the sentence might crawl back into the crack he pulled it from. Joel knew that feeling too well. Say the one true thing in the room and suddenly everybody acted like you were the one who'd broken it.

Paul kept his eyes on Cliff. "I'm not asking permission. I'm telling you what I'm doing."

That cleared the last of the joking out of the air. Cliff looked from Paul to Tommy to Nate, taking the count. For the first time all afternoon he wasn't steering the room; he was checking how many of them he'd already lost.

Then he turned to Joel. "That's what you want? Karen's name in a notebook, Ellie maybe hearing about it sideways, all because everybody out here suddenly decided honesty matters?"

Paul stayed squared to the lake. Tommy stood with the bottle hanging from two fingers, jaw working. Nate had folded in on himself, elbows on knees now, no bit left in him. Doug still wasn't looking anybody straight in the face, but he hadn't taken back the sentence. Cliff saw the same thing Joel did a second later: whatever story they'd all been handing Mallick had died right here on the deck boards.

"I want it to stop sounding like I came up here looking for blood for no reason," Joel said. "After that, I don't really give a shit how tidy it feels."

For the first time all afternoon, Cliff had nothing smooth ready. He grabbed his beer off the arm of the chair and went back inside without offering to get anyone else one.

Nobody called after him.

A cabinet door banged in the kitchen. Then another one banged. Cliff was done pretending not to be rattled.

Paul stayed at the rail. "If she asks, I'll give her Thursday night. The affair. That's all."

Joel let out a breath that felt like it'd been living under his ribs since the dorm parking lot. "If she asks me, I'm not using Karen's name unless I have to."

"Fair," Paul said.

"And don't lawyer it," Tommy said. "Just say it straight."

Joel looked at him. "You saying that to me is offensive on about six levels."

"It can be offensive and right."

That got a brief sound out of Nate, not quite a laugh. "He's got you there, guy."

Paul rubbed at the bridge of his nose. "We need one version. Not a coordinated one. Just the real one."

Doug lifted his head. "None of us have told her the real one yet." He looked at his hands. "Just saying."

Nobody had anything to say to that.

Joel kept the bottle in his hand and let the quiet sit there. The deck ticked in the sun. The lake knocked softly at the dock posts. Nobody asked him another question.

The loons called again, farther out this time. Whatever happened when Mallick came back, it wasn't going to be the same six men handing her the same story.
