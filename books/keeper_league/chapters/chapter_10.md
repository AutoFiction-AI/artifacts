# Chapter 10

Her leather notebook lay open on Martin Kessler's kitchen table to a page that would've embarrassed her if any of her colleagues at the county office saw it. Across it she'd drawn a grid. The columns were labeled with initials: PD, CH, NG, DW, TR, JB. The rows were labeled with the physical evidence. Check marks, question marks, and, in two cases, small x's that meant confirmed absent crowded the page. The grid looked like a badly designed crossword puzzle, and it told her less than she wanted and more than she expected.

By her count of the refrigerator cycles, it was close to ten o'clock. Since seven-thirty she'd been sitting there and she was no closer.

At last she took off her reading glasses and set them on the table. The beaded chain caught on her coffee mug and she untangled it with the patience of a woman who'd been untangling the same chain from the same kinds of things for a decade.

Martin Kessler was dead of cardiac arrest. The medical examiner had been clear: hypertrophic cardiomyopathy, undiagnosed, aggravated by alcohol, sildenafil, and prolonged heat exposure from the hot tub. It was a natural death. People died like this. Healthy-looking men in their late forties dropped dead and their friends stood around looking stunned, and the medical explanation was usually sufficient.

But Martin Kessler's house had been rearranged while his body cooled.

With the glasses back on, she looked at her grid.

The body. Martin had been found in bed, and by the time the deputies arrived Saturday morning, he was on his back with his arms at his sides. Tidy. Composed. People who died in their sleep did not usually end up tidy and composed. They ended up in whatever position the last involuntary movement left them: twisted, half off a pillow, one arm thrown, a leg bent. Paul Decker, bless his insurance-adjusting heart, had corrected her on Saturday about which way Martin's head had been turned. "I think it was more to the left, actually, toward the window." The correction told her two things. Paul had seen the body in its original position. The body was no longer in that position when the deputies arrived. Someone had moved Martin and straightened him out.

The wiping had to wait until Sunday morning for the BCA team to drive up from St. Paul, which was its own little joke on county resources. They found partial cleaning on the bedroom doorframe, the edge of the mattress, and the headboard. Partial. Whoever wiped got the right side of the headboard and missed the left. He got the doorframe at shoulder height and skipped the handle entirely. This was not professional cleaning. This was somebody on hands and knees at three in the morning, wiping surfaces he'd touched, probably crying. Same room as the body work. Same panic. Probably the same man.

The safe. The wall safe in Martin's study was hidden behind a lake landscape print, which was exactly where a lake house wall safe would be hidden. Martin had left the combination dial one tick off from locked, so anyone who jiggled it would find it open. Cliff Haywood had corrected her on Saturday about which room was the study when she'd pretended to confuse it with the half bath. "The study's the second door, not the first." She hadn't confused anything. Cliff's correction placed him in a room he had no stated reason to enter, and it placed him there with enough familiarity to know the order of the hallway doors. Financial records were inside the safe. Cliff was owed forty thousand dollars by the dead man. It did not take the county's best detective to draw that line. There was no county's best detective. There was the county's only detective, which was a different thing.

The voicemail. Nate Griggs had spent forty-five minutes telling her about his podcast, his audience metrics, his recording setup, his microphone preferences, and the exact linguistic conventions of fantasy football trash talk, and in the middle of minute thirty-eight he'd referenced "the thing I left on Martin's phone, the voicemail, which, I mean, not that it matters, because it was just draft stuff." He'd said it with the desperation of a man who had rehearsed not mentioning a detail and then mentioned it anyway. Martin's phone, now in evidence, showed a deleted voicemail from Nate's number, received at 8:47 PM Friday and deleted at approximately 3:15 AM Saturday, after the death and before the call to police. Nate had deleted a message from a dead man's phone because the message contained the words "dead to me" in the context of fantasy football, and he was terrified a county detective in rural Minnesota would not understand the genre.

A sip of coffee confirmed it had gone lukewarm, and she drank it anyway.

The medications. The shared upstairs bathroom had a medicine cabinet that was too clean. Six men in their late forties sharing a bathroom for a weekend and the cabinet held a tube of toothpaste, a disposable razor, and a bottle of Advil. No prescription bottles, no allergy medication, no heartburn tablets, no sleep aids. Nothing. Saturday afternoon she'd opened the cabinet and clocked the emptiness the way she'd clock a rug that had been freshly vacuumed in a house where nobody admitted to vacuuming. Someone had cleared that cabinet out, and the only reason to do that was if it had held pills somebody didn't want found. Given the age and habits of the men in this house, the missing bottles most likely said depression, anxiety, blood pressure, or sleep. They said struggle in a language the group did not like speaking out loud.

In the notebook she wrote "meds: shame, not guilt" and underlined it.

And the Viagra. Sildenafil in Martin's blood, and no Viagra anywhere in the house. Not in his bedroom, not in his bathroom, not in his luggage. The ME had confirmed the level was consistent with a single dose taken four to six hours before death. Whoever got rid of the rest had not started in the half bath where she'd heard the flush Saturday morning. He'd started in Martin's room, because that was where the pills would have been before they became a problem. Tommy Renaldi had come out of that bathroom looking like he'd run a half mile in a sauna. On Saturday she'd asked if he was alright and he'd said, "Fine, yeah, fine," in the way people said fine when they were not fine and couldn't think fast enough to build a better lie.

Five.

Back at the grid, the thing that mattered was no longer the names. Five separate acts of tampering. More than one person for sure. Probably five completely different motivations.

Some boxes wanted to fill themselves in. Nate belonged to the voicemail unless Martin's phone had learned his passcode on its own. Cliff leaned hard toward the safe. The missing prescriptions narrowed to one man who wanted his bottles back in a bag before a deputy opened the cabinet. Tommy stood too close to the missing sildenafil. The body and the wipe marks still looked like one panic in one room, but suspicion was not proof.

Her glasses came off again. The chain clinked against the table.

For thirty-six hours she'd been working this on the assumption that somebody had either killed Martin Kessler or helped cover up his killing. Every instinct she'd developed over twenty years told her that a scene this contaminated pointed to one plan, one motive, one person leaving his fingerprints all over the aftermath.

The grid said otherwise: five separate panics layered over each other so sloppily they looked coordinated, with half the men in the house probably never knowing who else had padded down that hallway before dawn.

For a moment the idea sat there by itself, and then she laughed. It came out louder than she'd expected in the empty kitchen, a genuine helpless sound that she covered with her hand out of habit. There was no one to hear her, but the habit persisted, the way her cats still knocked things off the nightstand even though she'd stopped keeping things on the nightstand three years ago.

This wasn't a conspiracy. It was a committee that never met.

Five middle-aged men with bad knees, reading glasses, weak bladders, and private humiliations, sneaking through the same lake house between two and six in the morning. Somebody in the bedroom straightening Martin's body while Nate worried over a voicemail that had seemed funny at eight-forty-seven and threatening by three-fifteen. Cliff in the study with the safe. One man slipping bottles out of the upstairs cabinet. Tommy carrying a problem out of Martin's room and into the half bath. All of them hearing the same floorboards, the same doors, the same third stair from the top. All of them freezing when they heard somebody else in the dark. All of them deciding, with the confidence unique to frightened men, that the sound had to be nothing.

It was the most absurd case she'd ever worked. Once she'd investigated a snowmobile reported stolen in Richville that turned out to have been repossessed by the dealership, reported stolen by the owner who was six months behind on payments, and then actually stolen by the dealership owner's nephew, who rode it across Otter Tail Lake in March and went through the ice forty yards from shore.

This was better. Panic made people fix only the part with their fingerprints on it. It meant the lies wouldn't line up. One man would remember a door, another a flush, a third would swear the hallway had been empty because he needed it to have been empty.

She opened the refrigerator and looked at the casserole dish she'd brought Saturday morning, after the call came in about a death at a lake house on Elbow. Somebody died, she cooked something, she brought it. The foil was still on top, crimped neatly at the edges. Nobody had touched it. Twenty years of bringing casseroles to grief and she could count on one hand the number that actually got eaten, but she kept making them because the gesture mattered, or because she liked the cooking, or because it was easier to walk into a room full of suspects holding a Pyrex dish than holding a badge.

The fridge door closed again. It was probably better the second day. It usually was.

Back at the table, she wrote in her notebook: "Natural death. Separate tamperings. Rebuild bedroom/hall traffic." She underlined "bedroom/hall traffic" twice, then stopped with the pen still touching the paper.

Phone, safe, cabinet: those were starting to settle. The bedroom wasn't.

The body work and the wipe marks belonged to one panic. Nate's voicemail was deleted at 3:15 AM, after Martin died and before anyone called police. The remaining sildenafil had started in Martin's room before it ended in a toilet. Three separate errands touched the same space around the same dead man, and no one she'd spoken to had given her a clean account of who saw whom in that room, or in the hallway outside it, while the house was awake and hiding from itself.

She needed sightlines: the bed, the bathroom door, the top stair, the stretch of hall a kneeling man could hear better than see.

Notebook closed, blazer on, she went to find someone precise.

---

Mallick found Paul Decker on the back deck, sitting in one of the Adirondack chairs with a beer he wasn't drinking. The September evening had turned cool enough to see breath, and Paul had a fleece pullover zipped to his throat. The lake was a flat dark sheet beyond the tree line. A loon called once from far across the water, and the sound hung there until Mallick let the screen door bang shut behind her.

"Oh, Mr. Decker. I'm sorry. I didn't realize anyone was still out here."

Of course she had. He'd been visible through the kitchen window for the last twenty minutes.

"Just getting some air," Paul said. He didn't look at her.

"I don't blame you. It's close in there tonight." Mallick settled into the Adirondack chair beside his, which required a small negotiation with the chair and with gravity. "Would you look at that lake, though. My mother had a place up near Park Rapids when I was growing up. Not as nice as this. An A-frame, though. She loved an A-frame."

Paul glanced at her. Calculation crossed his face: whether this was a conversation or an interview, whether Park Rapids had wandered in here honestly or tactically. Paul Decker was the kind of man who calculated because his profession ran on risk assessment and the accurate categorization of incoming information.

"Martin's parents built this one in the seventies," he said.

"You can tell. The paneling's got that warm look. Pine, is it?"

"Knotty pine."

"Knotty pine. There we go." With a tug at her blazer against the chill, she said, "I should let you sit in peace. I just had a question, and it's probably nothing. You know me and my questions."

Paul set his beer on the armrest. His posture shifted by a degree somebody less attentive would've missed. The shoulders squared. The spine straightened against the chair's slant. "Sure."

"I was looking back over Saturday morning, the sequence of events. I wanted to clarify one thing about Martin's phone. Did you happen to notice anyone near it after you found him?"

Paul's brow creased. It was not the crease of a man building a lie. It was the crease of a man searching an honest memory and finding nothing there. "His phone? No. I wasn't thinking about his phone. I was with him. With Martin. I was trying to—" He stopped. "I was in the bedroom."

"Of course you were. You were right there with him. So if anybody picked it up after that, you didn't see it."

"No." He rubbed his thumb against the wet label on the bottle. "No, I didn't."

Mallick nodded and looked out at the lake as if the answer had put a pleasant little check mark in her notebook.

"While I'm bothering you, the upstairs bathroom. The one you all share."

"What about it?"

"Did you happen to notice what was in the medicine cabinet before Saturday morning? Friday, say."

He frowned. "No. I don't think so. We've got our own stuff in our bags, mostly. The cabinet's more for communal things. Tylenol. Toothpaste. That kind of stuff."

"Sure. And you wouldn't know if somebody took prescription bottles out of there after Friday night."

"No." He paused. "Why? Is there something about the bathroom?"

"Probably not. I'm just checking boxes. You know how it is. Paperwork. My office wants every little thing accounted for."

Paul sat with that. A gust moved through the pines and then was gone.

"Can I ask you something?" he said.

"Of course."

"The safe in the study. Did somebody open it?"

She looked at him. This was useful. She hadn't asked about the safe, and he'd brought it up on his own.

"What makes you ask about the safe?"

"Martin always left the dial one click off. He did it every year. He'd close it and then not quite spin it all the way. We all knew." Paul took a breath. "If somebody was going through the house that night, they would've found it. I should've mentioned it before. I didn't think of it until today."

"And you didn't go into the study that night."

"No."

"But you knew about the safe."

"Everybody knew about the safe. Martin showed it to us years ago. He was proud of it. The combination, the mounting, the whole thing. He'd show somebody and say don't tell anyone, and then he'd show the next person. We'd all pretend we hadn't seen it before." The faintest smile touched his mouth and disappeared. "That was Martin."

"That's nice," she said, and she meant it.

"So you didn't go in the study. You didn't notice the medicine cabinet. And you weren't near Martin's phone."

Paul looked at her then. He was still holding the beer, but his hand had stopped moving. Condensation ran over his knuckles and he didn't seem to notice.

The pattern reached him a beat later. Phone, medications, safe: three separate questions in three separate parts of the house.

"Detective," he said.

"Mm-hmm?"

"You're asking about different parts of the house."

"I am."

"Because you don't think it was one person."

She let the silence hold.

"Mr. Decker." She was looking at the lake. "I think you straightened Martin."

Paul's hand tightened on the bottle.

"The headboard. The doorframe. The mattress edge. You wiped those."

"I moved him," he said. "His arm was hanging off the bed. The sheet was twisted around his—I straightened him out. And I wiped what I'd touched." He swallowed. "Not well."

"Why?"

"Because my DNA was all over a—" He stopped. "Exposure. I wasn't thinking straight. I was thinking like an adjuster instead of a—" He rubbed his face. "My best friend was dead and I was on my knees worrying about my prints."

"And the phone?"

"No."

"The medicine cabinet?"

"No."

"The safe?"

"None of that was mine." His voice had gone flat. "You already knew that."

"I did."

"So other people were in there. Doing their own—"

"At least."

He stared at the water. His jaw worked once. The beer was still in his hand. He hadn't drunk from it.

"I heard people that night," he said. "Moving around. Doors."

"And you told yourself it was the house."

"Yeah." He let out a breath. It hung in the cool air for a moment and dissolved. "It was a natural death. The ME said so."

"The ME said so."

"Then why are you still—"

"From where you were kneeling, how much of the hallway could you actually see?"

Paul blinked. "Not much. The bed blocks part of it."

"The bathroom door?"

"If it was open, maybe the edge."

"The stairs?"

"The top of them. Not the landing."

"So you get sound before shape."

"Yeah." He swallowed. "Yeah, that's right."

"Martin's phone was handled at 3:15." She let that sit. "And the pills had to come out of his room before they ended up in the half bath."

Paul stared at the water.

"You were in that bedroom. Or just outside it."

"I know where I was."

"So either people were moving through that space without you seeing them, or somebody's lying to me about when they were there."

Paul said nothing.

"I still think it was a natural death," she said. "But I don't close a file with that much traffic around the body unaccounted for."

He looked back out at the lake. Somewhere deeper in the house, a floorboard popped and settled.

"The casserole," Mallick said. "Did you try it?"

"What?"

"My casserole. The green bean. It's in the fridge."

"No." He blinked. "No, I didn't."

"It's better the second day," she said. She stood from the Adirondack chair with a small graceless push off both armrests that she'd stopped being embarrassed by years ago. "You should try it. There'll be a third day too, the way things are going."

She went back inside. The screen door banged behind her. Paul stayed on the deck with his warming beer.

In the kitchen Mallick sat back down at the table and opened her notebook. Under body/surfaces she wrote: "Paul admitted move + wipe after CPR." On the next line: "Phone/safe/meds not his. Bedroom traffic still doesn't close."

One square had gone from question mark to check mark. Under it the rest of the bedroom stayed messy: phone at 3:15, pills out of Martin's room, footsteps in a hallway Paul could hear better than see.

Through the kitchen window the yard had gone black and the dock was mostly guesswork.

She looked at the line again. To make it work she had to picture Nate slipping in and out while Paul stayed bent over the bed, Tommy palming the pills and getting downstairs without clipping a doorframe, grief shrinking Paul's field of vision to Martin's face and nothing else. Too many moving parts to leave as a guess.

Notebook in hand, she went upstairs.

The upper hall smelled like old pine, damp towels, and the flat medicinal note that had settled over the house since Saturday morning. Martin's bedroom door stood open. The shared bathroom door at the far end of the hall stood open too. A plug-in night-light near the baseboard threw a weak amber wedge over the carpet.

From Martin's doorway the room gave her the bed, the near nightstand, a strip of hallway, and not much else. First with the hall light on, then off, then with the bedroom lamp switched on and the hall left dim, she tested the sightlines. The room kept changing what it offered. With light behind you, the hall flattened into shadow. With light in front of you, the room flattened into shadow instead. If somebody stepped out of the shared bathroom and kept moving, Paul could hear him before he really saw him. If somebody came up the stairs and stopped at the top, all Paul would get was outline.

At the bedside she knelt where Paul would've knelt. Both knees complained. The bed frame erased most of the hallway. From that height the bathroom door disappeared completely. The landing disappeared too. One hand went to the mattress edge where the BCA had found the wiped strip. Martin's chest would've been directly in front of him. Martin's face would've been the whole assignment. A man trying to pump life back into his best friend at two in the morning was not running security. He was looking at a body and trying to make it not be one.

From the shared bathroom to Martin's door took six seconds. Eight if you were trying not to be heard. Heel to toe, she walked it and listened to the floor answer her. One board near the bathroom clicked dry and quick. One outside Martin's room sighed. The stair spoke once, low, and then shut up. A frightened person who knew the house could choose quiet badly and still think he was invisible.

That was enough time for whoever cleared the cabinet. Nate could've leaned in for the phone and back out before Paul looked up. Those fit.

The Viagra removal still snagged. Somebody had to go into Martin's room, find the pills, get back out, get downstairs to the half bath, and flush them before the house fully woke. That was more movement. More noise too. More chances to brush the doorframe, catch the light, or make Paul look up at exactly the wrong second. The picture of Tommy trying to make himself narrow enough for the house not to notice almost made her laugh again.

At the top of the stairs she listened. Old houses lied about sound. A tick in the pipe could be a footstep. A settling pop from downstairs could sound like a door on the hall. One bad sound, sure. Not a whole chain of them.

Back in the kitchen she wrote under bedroom traffic: "Can miss one in the dark. Hard to miss the rest." Then she circled "rest" until the paper roughened under the pen.

This time the notebook stayed shut. After rinsing her coffee mug and drying it, she set it on the counter next to the coffeemaker because she'd want it first thing and it was already clean and why open the cabinet twice. Then she turned off the kitchen light and went upstairs to the guest bedroom the deputies had cleared for her.

The third step from the top creaked under her weight, thin and accusing in the dark.
