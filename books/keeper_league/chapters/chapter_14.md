# Chapter 14

Doug shoved the last flannel into his duffel and felt the prescription bottles knock once against his shaving kit. He'd already hidden them once this weekend. Now he zipped the bag and listened to the low voices in the great room and the way everyone kept acting like tomorrow was normal.

The great room looked half-packed. Paul sat at the kitchen table with his reading glasses and his phone, texting Anne about departure times, pausing after every line to read it back before his thumbs moved again. Joel had his laptop on the couch, answering the work emails he'd been ignoring since Thursday, the blue light catching the sharp angles of his face. The draft board was still there, and nobody had suggested taking it down. Nobody had suggested much of anything today. They'd spent the day in the same stalled rhythm, too awake to sleep, too tired to do anything useful, checking flights, rinsing plates, waiting to leave.

Mallick hadn't been back since Sunday evening. No follow-up calls, no uniformed deputies in the driveway, no casserole. The implication had settled over the house like weather: the investigation into Martin's death was winding down. Natural causes, suspicious tampering, no murder charge. Doug didn't know the legal specifics. Joel did. Doug understood the shape of it anyway. Martin died of a bad heart, five of his closest friends panicked in the dark, the panicking made a natural death look sinister, and now they were all going to go home and spend the rest of their lives knowing they'd made themselves look guilty of a murder that never happened. Tilt your head and it was almost funny. Doug didn't tilt his head.

Doug zipped the duffel. The sound was loud in the room and Paul glanced up.

"You're set?"

"Yeah. Early flight." This was a lie. Doug's flight wasn't until two-thirty tomorrow afternoon, but the lie gave him a reason to end the conversation, and ending conversations was what Doug did best. Twenty-five years in this group and his primary contribution had been his willingness to go get more ice.

Nate came in from the deck, sliding the glass door shut behind him. He'd been on the phone, Mia probably, or his producer, or whichever version of his future he was negotiating with at any given moment. At the kitchen counter he poured himself a glass of water, took a couple of distracted swallows, and stared through the dark glass as if the call had kept going out there.

"Mia says hi," Nate said, to no one in particular.

"Hi back," Paul said, without looking up.

"She wants to know if we're all okay." Nate said it flatly, the performance drained out of it, which meant someone who loved him had asked the question genuinely and he hadn't known what to do with that. He drank the rest of the water and put the glass in the sink. "I told her we're fine."

"Are we fine?" Joel asked from the couch, with enough edge that it could have been a real question or a lawyer's question, and Nate didn't answer either version.

"Where's Tommy?" Paul asked, and Doug heard the question carry more weight than Paul meant it to. Paul was asking about logistics, whether Tommy needed a ride to the airport, but the name landed differently now. Everything landed differently now.

"Deck, I think," Joel said. "Or the firepit. I saw him walk that way twenty minutes ago."

"And Cliff?"

"Took his phone into the study." Joel closed his laptop and set it on the cushion beside him. "Calling someone. His office. His lawyer. Does Cliff have a lawyer? Everyone has a lawyer now, I guess."

That was a Joel observation, dry and self-aware, the sarcasm pointed at himself as much as anyone because Joel was a lawyer and the week had made lawyers of them all. Nobody picked it up. The comment died there.

Doug sat on the arm of the stairs that led down to the basement and watched Paul keep typing, Joel keep pretending his inbox still mattered, Nate stand at the sink with the empty glass still in his hand.

The room looked finished. Bags stood by the front door. Paul was still working departure logistics. Joel had gone back to email. Nate was drinking water where he'd have had a beer on Thursday. Martin's chair at the kitchen table was still pulled back slightly, like nobody had wanted to put a hand on it. All of that should have meant the weekend was over. It didn't feel over. Doug could still feel the tension in the gaps between people. Tommy alone outside. Cliff in the study with his phone. The way Paul and Joel were careful with each other in a way that was new since Sunday, a carefulness Doug couldn't identify as alliance or suspicion or the sour space between them. The group's geometry had shifted, and from the edge Doug still couldn't see the shape of it.

Cliff came back from the study with his phone in his hand and poured himself two fingers of Martin's Lagavulin. Peat smoke mixed with the general smell of the house, pine paneling, lake damp, the ghost of Martin's presence that lived in the walls the way old smoke does. Cliff drank standing up, the way he did everything, leaning forward slightly, one hand in his pocket, already half turned toward the next room. The leather loafers he wore indoors shone under the kitchen lights.

"Paul." Cliff raised the glass an inch, a small toast or a small acknowledgment. "Joel. Doug."

"Hey," Doug said.

"We should talk about the house at some point," Cliff said. "Not tonight. But someone's got to deal with Martin's things. The estate, the property taxes, all that. There's a process. I've gone through it with clients. I'm happy to help coordinate, if that's useful."

"Let's get through the drive home first," Paul said. The qualifier he'd swallowed, the _in my experience_ he'd left out, the _to be fair_ he hadn't added, made the sentence sound like a door being held politely shut.

Cliff nodded. He drank. He looked at the draft board and said, "He'd have won the league this year. Those picks were solid." Then he took the glass out to the deck, and Doug watched him go, the loafers making a soft sound on the hardwood. The sliding door opened and closed. Through the glass, Cliff settled against the railing with the drink and looked out at the lake where the last light of the day had gone and the moon was putting a silver path across the water.

Joel looked at Paul. Paul looked at his phone. Neither of them said anything about what Cliff had said or the way he'd said it, which told Doug that whatever was wrong in the room had already been discussed somewhere without him. Doug let it sit. If they wanted him in it, they'd ask.

People drifted. Nate went down to the basement and muttered about packing. Paul made a last round of the kitchen, washing the dinner dishes nobody had finished, wiping down the counter, the caretaking that was Paul's way of grieving. Joel closed his laptop into his bag and said he was going to read upstairs. Tommy was still outside somewhere, a large silent shape Doug had lost track of.

Doug waited for the slider to open again. Waited for Cliff to come back in with one more practical suggestion nobody wanted or for Tommy to stomp through the house loud enough to make normal irritation possible. Neither happened. Paul shut cabinets softly. Upstairs, a toilet flushed. Then the refrigerator kicked on and took over the room. The night felt less settled than suspended, as if the house had stopped between breaths.

The house settled into the sounds of its own machinery: the refrigerator cycling on with its forty-minute hum, the wind through the pine trees pressing against the walls, the third stair step creaking under Joel's weight as he went up. Doug sat on the arm of the basement stairs and felt the quiet accumulate. It was a different quiet from the dock quiet. House quiet was full of people trying to sleep and failing, full of the sounds they made when they thought no one was listening. The dock quiet was empty. That was what made it clean.

Downstairs, Nate was on one of the pull-out sofas, phone in hand, thumb scrolling without purpose. He didn't look up when Doug came down. Doug lay on top of the sheets in his hoodie and sweatpants and waited for the pill to do what it had done every other night this weekend.

Doug tried the breathing count the app had suggested back in March. In for four, hold for four, out for six. By the third round he was counting other things instead: the bills on the counter in Richfield, the half-unpacked box Steph had left because it contained nothing worth forwarding, the refrigerator light on mostly condiments. In July Martin had called and, after two minutes of Doug saying he was fine, started asking what was in the fridge. Doug had lied once, then given up and admitted the answer was basically nothing. Martin had stayed on the phone until Doug ordered food. Doug had come to the lake house because Martin kept asking after that. Now the only place in the house Doug could imagine breathing was the dock.

It didn't. After an hour of Nate's breathing, the lake at the basement window, and Richfield waiting for him tomorrow night, Doug got up. Nate didn't stir. Doug put his shoes back on and pulled the hoodie tighter and went upstairs through the dark house and out the sliding door onto the deck. The deck was empty. A wet ring marked the railing where Cliff had left his drink, but the glass itself was gone. Beyond the railing the lake was mostly black, broken only by moonlight and the small slap of water against the dock.

Doug walked to the dock.

---

The stone path was uneven under his feet, the flagstones sunk at different depths from twenty years of Minnesota freeze-thaw. Doug walked it from memory in the dark, through pine and birch, the trees close enough on both sides to block the house lights after the first stretch. Before the dock came into view he could hear the water under it, a steady slap against the posts.

The moon was three-quarters full and high enough to reach the path in broken pieces, the light cut by the tree canopy into bright shapes on the stone. September night in northern Minnesota. The warmth of the afternoon was gone and what replaced it was a damp cold off the lake that worked through the hoodie to his skin. Doug shoved his hands in the front pocket. Pine needles underfoot, brittle and sharp. Their smell, turpentine and sugar, was the smell of every September he'd spent at this house, and for a moment it was Thursday again, the arrival, the gravel driveway, Martin's voice from the deck: _Whitfield! You look like shit. Get up here._

Martin had noticed right away. One of the few, maybe the only one, who'd looked at Doug at the airport on Thursday and seen what was underneath the hoodie and the forced smile. On the deck that first evening Martin had asked, privately, _You eating?_ and Doug had said _yeah, I'm fine_ and Martin had looked at him the way you look at someone who is not fine and both of you know it and let the lie stand because pushing it would make it real.

He'd done it in July too. Doug had answered on the fifth ring from his kitchen and Martin, hearing the echo in the apartment or the drag in Doug's voice, had started asking what was in the fridge. Doug had lied once, then stopped. Half a lime. Mustard. One beer. Martin had stayed on the phone until Doug ordered takeout. That was how Doug ended up here, three months later, walking to the dock because the one man who'd noticed was now the first dead man.

Martin was dead now. The man who noticed was dead.

The trees thinned and the dock appeared, the swim platform at the end a dark rectangle against the water. The bench at the far end sat crooked to the boards, one leg hanging half off the edge. Near the swim ladder, a tumbler had shattered into moonlit grit, amber still slick on the planks, and two pale gouges cut through the weathered gray where rubber soles had skidded.

Then Doug saw the shape in the water.

Doug stopped where the stone path met the dock's first planks. The wood was pale in the moonlight, the grain visible, and the water on either side was dark, shifting in short ripples under the moon. In the water to the left of the dock, six or seven feet from the edge, there was a shape that did not belong to the lake.

His first thought was deadfall from the north shore, bark-stripped and waterlogged, hung at the surface. Martin had complained about those. They fouled the swim ladder. They bumped against the dock at night and made sounds that woke people up. Doug's brain offered log because it always offered the explanation that hurt less. _Contraindicated_, the word arrived before the water turned the shape enough to show him a shoulder.

It was a person. Face-down. Arms extended at wrong angles, resting on the surface with a terrible limpness beyond sleep or relaxation or any other state a living body can produce. The legs were extended at a slight downward angle, the feet nearly touching the bottom. The back of the pale shirt sat just above the waterline, the fabric dark with water and clinging to a back and shoulder blades with the awful stillness only a dead body has.

Doug took three steps onto the dock. The wood creaked under his weight. The sound felt obscene.

The silver hair. The width of the shoulders. The pale shirt. It was Cliff. Doug knew it before he assembled the evidence, the way you know someone from behind by the set of their body, the particular proportion of shoulder to waist, the details you've absorbed across twenty-five years of draft weekends without ever deciding to learn them. Cliff Haywood was floating face-down in three feet of water beside the dock at Martin Kessler's lake house, and the water kept slapping softly against his sides.

Doug knelt on the dock. The planks were damp and cold through his sweatpants. He leaned over the edge and reached down until his hand found Cliff's shoulder. The shoulder was rigid, flesh stiff and heavy under his grip, the meat and bone of a body without a person in it. The water was cold. Minnesota-lake cold, September cold, a cold that went past his wrist and seized his forearm and held it while he pulled.

Cliff's body moved sluggishly in the water, heavier than Doug expected or Doug weaker than he remembered, and the pull rolled Cliff toward him until the right side of his face broke the surface. The gash on Cliff's right temple was the first thing Doug saw. It was dark in the moonlight. Blood reads black under moonlight, not red. The wound was wide, skin split around one deep impact, the kind metal or dock hardware would leave. The right eye was open. The other eye was still in the water. One hand came with him, palm up, two knuckles flayed raw and a crescent of black dock grit packed under the thumbnail. Cliff's mouth was open too, slightly, the jaw slack, and there was lake water in it.

Doug let go. His hand came back wet and smelling of lake rot and the sweet-sour beginning underneath it, a smell with names he did not want in his head here on this dock in the dark with his hand dripping.

Doug looked at Cliff's feet. Leather loafers, already taking on water. Indoor shoes. The ones that had made that soft careful sound on the hardwood an hour and a half ago. The canvas deck shoes Cliff kept by the slider on the mat all weekend were somewhere else. He didn't walk the stone path and this wet dock in these. Cliff cared too much about shoes.

Doug stood. His hands were shaking. The tremor had a quality he recognized from the first week on the Lexapro, before his body had agreed to the new chemistry, a vibration that seemed to come from somewhere deeper than his muscles, from the wiring underneath. His knees were wet from the dock planks. His right hand was wet from the lake. Cliff was in the water. The water was three feet deep. There was a gash on Cliff's right temple. His shoes were wrong. His body understood it before his mind would.

When Doug opened his mouth, a sound came out that he did not recognize. Raw and enormous, it came from somewhere below his diaphragm, behind his ribs, bigger than any voice he actually used in the world, and it crossed the water and hit the far shore and came back as an echo that sounded like someone else screaming from across the lake. It came again. And again. Three times Doug screamed on the dock beside Cliff Haywood's body, and by the third time his voice was breaking and the scream thinned into a hoarse, wrecked noise closer to the sound he actually made in the world, which was barely a sound at all.

The echo thinned out over the water. The loons, if they were out there, stayed quiet. Somewhere across the lake a dog barked twice and stopped. After that Doug could hear only the small slap under the dock and his own breathing. It wasn't the clean empty quiet he'd come down here for. Cliff was in it now.

Doug stood on the dock and his legs felt wrong beneath him, loose and unreliable. He sat down on the wet planks. Cold went through his sweatpants immediately. Doug sat and looked at Cliff in the water and waited for footsteps on the stone path, because they would come. They had to come. The scream had carried across the lake and into the trees, and somewhere in the house four men had just heard the quietest person they knew make a noise that did not belong to him.

Water slapped lightly against the dock. Cliff's silver hair shifted a little with each slap. From a distance it could have looked gentle. Up close there was no missing what it was.

---

Nate reached the dock first. Doug heard him before he saw him, bare feet on stone, slap and hiss and another slap as the flagstones bit him.

"Doug? Doug, what happened?"

Nate's phone light hit the dock and swung across the water and found Cliff and stopped.

"Oh, shit." Nate's voice dropped from his chest to his stomach. "Oh, shit. Oh—"

"He's dead," Doug said. His voice was back to its normal register, barely a sound, and the words came out flat and colorless after the screams that had produced them. Doug was still sitting on the dock. His sweatpants were soaked and his hands were in his lap, the right one still damp from the lake.

Nate stood at the foot of the dock with his phone light on the water. The beam caught the silver of Cliff's hair and the fabric of his polo and the stillness of his arms, which were out at angles that suggested reaching, as if Cliff had been trying to grab hold before he stopped trying. Nate's mouth stayed open, but nothing came after the first oh, shit. Usually he could fill any silence with stats, bullshit, a whole panicked halftime show. Barefoot on the cold stone, he had nothing. His light hand trembled slightly and the beam wavered on the water, making Cliff's shape shift and blur.

"Is he—" Nate started, and didn't finish.

Paul arrived next, at a run, breathing hard, carrying a real flashlight because Paul knew where the flashlight lived. The beam was brighter than Nate's and when it found the water and found Cliff and the shattered tumbler on the planks it stopped wavering, pinned there the way Paul's hands always went still around broken things. Insurance adjusters look at wrecked things for a living.

"Jesus Christ," Paul said.

Paul didn't wait. He stepped off the dock's edge and into the lake up to his shins, the water soaking his khakis to the knee, and he got a hand under Cliff's shoulder and turned him. Cliff's body rotated slowly in the shallow water. The face came up, white and slack, the eyes unevenly open, the gash on the right temple visible now in Paul's flashlight beam, wider and more ragged than it had looked from above. The same light found the hand Doug had dragged up with him, knuckles stripped, and the collar twisted hard around Cliff's throat. Paul put two fingers against the side of Cliff's neck and held them there for what felt like a long time and was probably ten seconds. He moved his fingers. He tried again, a quarter inch to the left. Then he took his hand away.

"He's cold," Paul said. His voice was careful, calibrated, the voice he used for information that mattered. "He's been in the water for..." He stopped. Doug watched Paul's insurance brain start the calculation: water temperature, body temperature, rate of cooling, duration. Then grief overrode the math and left him standing in the lake with one hand on his dead friend's arm and no idea what the number was or whether the number mattered. "A while. He's been in here a while."

"Paul, come out of the water." Joel's voice came from the foot of the dock. Doug hadn't heard him arrive. He'd come down the path with careful, placed steps, fast enough to matter but not fast enough to slip. His phone light found the water and held on it with the same steadiness as Paul's flashlight.

"I was checking."

"I know. Come out of the water. Don't move him any more." Joel said it with the clipped authority Doug had heard from him only a few times in twenty-five years, the voice that belonged to the courthouse, not the lake house. His light moved from Cliff to the shoved bench, the broken tumbler, the fresh skid marks near the ladder, then back to the body. "This is a crime scene."

The word settled over the dock. Nate looked at Joel. Paul, standing in the water, looked at Joel. Doug, sitting on the wet planks, looked at Joel.

"How do you know that?" Nate said. His voice was raw and smaller than usual and the question had no performance in it.

"Look at his shoes." Joel crouched at the edge of the dock and aimed his light at Cliff's feet. The leather loafers were visible beneath the surface, the dark leather taking on water, the thin soles entirely wrong for the stone path and the dock wood and every surface between the house and the lake. "Those are his indoor shoes. He didn't walk down here in those."

Nobody spoke. Joel's light stayed on the shoes. Water moved around them in short black ripples.

"And the water's three feet deep," Joel continued. His voice was precise and quiet, the voice that assembled facts the way Paul assessed damage, lining them up so panic couldn't outrun them. "A conscious person doesn't drown face-down in three feet of water. He was either unconscious when he went in, or he was held."

"There's a wound." Paul was still in the lake. He looked down at Cliff's face, half-lit by the flashlight beam. "Right temple. It's a gash. He hit metal, or got driven into it."

"Either way, this wasn't a fall," Joel said.

The sentence ended and nobody added to it. Joel stood up from his crouch. Paul waded out of the water and onto the dock, his khakis dripping, his shoes ruined. He stood there breathing through his nose in short, counted pulls, like he was trying to keep a gauge needle from slipping into the red.

Tommy came down the path last.

His footsteps were heavier than the others, each one landing hard on the flagstone. At the foot of the dock he stopped with his phone in his hand and the light still off. No question came out of him. He just stood there, a dark shape against the tree line, breathing through his nose like he'd run harder than the short walk from the house required.

Then Tommy turned on the light and found Cliff.

"No," Tommy said.

Low. Stripped of the coaching bark. Just the word. Tommy came as far as the first wet plank and no farther. His free hand flattened once against his thigh and stayed there. When Joel said _held_, the muscle in Tommy's jaw jumped hard enough for Doug to see it in the bad light.

"Tommy," Nate said, and the name had the tone of someone reaching for a person who might not be reachable.

Tommy didn't answer. His light dropped from Cliff to the broken glass and the boards and then went dark again. Everybody else had asked some version of what happened. Tommy hadn't asked anything. Doug didn't know what that meant. It only sat wrong.

"We need to call Mallick," Paul said. He was out of the water now, standing on the dock, his flashlight pointed at the planks rather than the body, giving Cliff the privacy Cliff no longer needed. "Right now."

"It's midnight," Nate said, and the absurdity of the objection, as if there were a polite hour to report a body, registered on his own face the moment the words left him. "Yeah. Obviously. I know."

"I'll call," Joel said. He already had his phone out, his thumb on the number before the sentence was finished. He stepped off the dock and walked a few paces down the path and put the phone to his ear.

Doug sat on the dock and looked at Cliff. The moonlight had shifted or his eyes had adjusted, and the skin was already a different color from living skin, a pallor underneath the permanent tan that had always made Cliff look like he'd just come back from somewhere expensive. Cliff's watch was still on his wrist, the crystal intact, catching the moonlight. His hands were open. The polo was a pale blue, the same shirt Cliff had been wearing earlier that evening when he'd stood in the great room talking about the house and the estate and the process and said _he'd have won the league this year_ before taking his drink outside.

Two hours ago. Maybe less. Cliff had been alive in the great room two hours ago, wearing those leather shoes on the hardwood floor, making plans for a future that now included neither Martin nor himself.

Behind him, Joel's voice, flat and clear: "Detective Mallick. It's Joel Beckman, at the Kessler house. We've got a second body."

The words carried across the dock. _A second body._ Martin had died. Cliff had been put here. Wrong shoes. Shallow water. Broken glass. Skid marks. Gash on the right temple. Somebody had done this, and the somebody was one of the men breathing in the dark with him.

Doug looked up from the water. The four men had sorted themselves without discussing it. Paul on the dock with the flashlight angled down, as if he could keep doing one useful thing. Joel at the foot of the dock, phone still in his hand. Nate lowering himself beside Doug because sitting beside the body-finder was the only help he had. Tommy one step back from the wet boards, his phone dark again, his face turned toward the lake instead of Cliff. Even the way they were standing had started to matter.

Joel came back to them. "She's on her way. She said not to touch anything." His glance went to Paul's soaked khakis and Doug's wet right hand. "I told her we already touched things."

"I was trying to help him," Paul said, and his voice cracked on the last word. It wasn't a sob. It was the sound of a measured man's measurement giving way for a second.

"I know," Joel said.

Nate sat down on the dock next to Doug. Three feet between them, but on the dock because Doug was on the dock and that was the point. Nate's bare feet dangled over the edge, inches above the water where Cliff was floating. He realized it, muttered "shit," and pulled them back under him.

The wait settled over them in separate pieces. Paul kept the flashlight on the planks. Joel stood at the path, listening for tires. Tommy stayed where he'd stopped, too still, his hands opening and closing at his sides only when he thought nobody was looking. Doug kept seeing Cliff as he'd been in the great room two hours earlier, dry shoes on hardwood, a glass in his hand, talking about taxes and process like the future still existed.

Headlights appeared at the far end of the gravel driveway, distant at first, then the crunch of tires reached the house. Joel straightened. Paul's flashlight dropped to his side. Nate stayed seated for another second before pushing himself up.

Doug stood. His sweatpants were soaked, his knees ached from the dock, and his right hand still smelled like lake water and Cliff's skin. One last look at the water: hair drifting in the moonlight, dark shoes below the surface, the gash on the right temple a black line. Then Doug turned away and walked up the dock toward the house and the headlights.
