# Chapter 5

By November the library windows had gone black by half past four, and the blown-out north bay held the evening cold like a cistern. Owen sat on the floor between two displaced cases with Binns's old *History* across his knees and his ash-linen notebook open beside one shoe. He had come up there because the damage still made a pocket no prefect bothered with, a wedge of cracked shelving and dust where the draught reached first and people reached last. Also because his wand had turned sulky after the greenhouse business, and writing with a pen had lately been less humiliating than trusting a charm to do what he asked.

The burn across his right palm had learned the weather before the rest of him. It pulled tight whenever the stone went cold. Tonight it felt as if a thin hot wire had been stitched under the skin and was being drawn in small patient increments each time he flattened his hand to steady the page.

He had copied the same blank table three times and disliked all three copies. The original in the Grinlow Leaves was not properly blank anymore, not to him. It had pressure in it. The page answered his burned hand with the sense of ruled spaces and something almost written just beyond them, as if the text was standing in another room with the door not quite shut. On paper from the stationer it collapsed back into ordinary failure.

A shadow crossed the floorboards and then held still.

"Owen."

He looked up too fast and knocked his pen. Ink dotted the floor, his sock, the cuff of his robe. Prue Hollis stood over the gap between the shelves with three books braced against her chest and her scarf knotted badly enough to suggest she had done it while walking. Her spectacles had fogged at the edges from the change in temperature. She pushed them up with the back of her wrist rather than her fingers, which were occupied by the books and by one orange fingerless mitten with the thumb seam split open.

"The library's closing," he said, because it plainly was, and because literal truths came quicker than useful ones.

"In twenty minutes." She nudged one of the loose boards with her shoe. "Move your notebook. Unless you want ink all over Bagshot."

"It's not panic."

"Fine. Your method, then."

She crouched before he could stop her and laid the books out in a row on the floorboards with a care that looked brisk only until one watched it. She lined the spines, squared the lower edges, then took a folded paper parcel from under one arm and set it between them. The parcel smelled of bread and cold onion. Only after that did she sit back on her heels and look at him properly.

Owen shut the notebook by reflex. It was too late for dignity. She had seen enough of the page to know he had been copying something and hiding it badly.

"I said one thing to you," he said. "At the Room. One citation."

"You said a great deal at the Room. Most of it was appalling." She tucked a curl behind her ear and failed; it sprang loose at once. "The citation was the interesting bit."

He ought to have left. The corridor beyond the damaged bay was empty. Madam Pince's tread had gone off toward the front desk. Owen could have taken the books as obstruction, Prue as risk, the whole thing as a reason to go to supper late and alone. Instead he stayed where he was and watched her open the first volume.

They were all copies of *A History of Magic*, but they were not identical. One had the old classroom binding with the boards wrapped in green cloth so drab it had gone almost brown. Another was a later school issue with newer buckram and a repaired hinge. The last was so over-handled the title on the spine had worn off completely.

Prue turned them all to the same page and pointed with a bitten thumbnail.

"Look there," she said.

"I'm looking."

"No, you're waiting to tell me it proves nothing. Look first."

Above the footnotes, the body text matched line for line. He could see that much at a glance. The paragraph break was the same. The hyphen fell in the same place. The word municipal carried the same tiny blot where a damaged bit of type had filled with ink. But the bottom of the page changed as it went across the row. In the oldest copy the footnotes ran six deep. In the next there were still six, but the lines sat tighter and the final one cramped itself into the lower margin. In the latest there were five, neat and even, and the line where his impossible citation ought to have been had simply gone.

"The page was reset," he said.

"The bottom third was reset," Prue corrected. "Badly. Everything above the paragraph mark is from the old standing type. You can see the nick in municipal, and the broken r in wizardry, and the little tilt on the numeral three. Then in this one" - she tapped the middle copy - "someone has squeezed the notes. In the later school issue they set the lower block afresh and tidy the numbering. Which would be normal if they'd revised the page. They didn't. They revised one citation."

Owen leaned in despite himself. The pages smelled different. The old cloth copy smelled of dust, the middle one of glue and old damp, and the newest of the sour paper left by too many student hands. The missing line was not absence anymore once she had pointed to it. It was surgery.

"That still might be authorial," he said, though the words came out thin.

"No." She opened the second book further, flattening it with the heel of her hand. "Because the footnote numbers in the running index at the back are off by one until chapter nineteen, where whoever did it noticed and overcorrected. And because this copy" - she touched the middle volume, almost fondly - "is a mixed one. Water damage, then repair. Two replacement leaves from a later issue bound into an earlier impression. Sloppy library work, bless them. One of the replaced leaves carries the tidy reset. The original one didn't. That means the citation survived in the earlier printing line and disappeared later. It was there, not just in Professor Binns's fossil but in the lineage."

She said the last word quietly, as if lowering her voice made the evidence less indecent. It did not.

Owen felt the small, mean wish that she had made some mistake. It wasn't exactly that he wanted her wrong. If she was right, the footnote had ceased to be his private, ridiculous disaster. It had roots outside him.

"How long did that take you?" he asked.

"Most of yesterday and all of this afternoon."

"Why?"

Prue looked at him as if the question itself was a way of wasting her time.

"Because you quoted a shelf mark that can't exist in any school issue after 1883. Because you looked ill enough that I thought you might actually have found something and then made a complete hash of explaining it. Because I was curious. Pick one."

From the corridor came the thud of a distant door and the faint scrape of a trolley wheel. Neither of them moved. The cold had got under Owen's robe and was working methodically through his knees.

Prue nudged the paper parcel toward him.

"Take half before I change my mind."

It held two thick slices of bread, a wedge of hard cheese wrapped in waxed paper, and an apple that had bruised brown at one shoulder. It wasn't much, but it was more than he had expected after missing most of supper. He broke the bread without thanking her quickly enough and felt rude too late.

"There were currant buns," she said. "I left them. Sultanas in bread are an offence."

That, absurdly, made him look at her again. She had spoken with the same severity she had used about print lineages. One of the curls at her temple had flattened where her hat or hood had pressed it; the rest were escaping in damp little hooks around her face. Her cuffs were inked through in two places. One lens of her spectacles carried a pale smear where she had tried and failed to polish it on the scarf.

Owen ate. The bread was dry and the cheese cold enough to squeak against his teeth. It was still excellent.

Prue waited until he had swallowed before she said, "So. Is the thing real?"

He could feel the notebook under his left hand, the copied tables trapped inside it like an accusation.

"That's a large question."

"You can answer a smaller one first, then. Did you find something at the shelf mark?"

"Yes."

"Can you show me?"

"No."

She bit into the apple, considered him over the sound of it, and said nothing for a while. The silence lengthened without sharpening. Somewhere in the front half of the library Madam Pince murmured a locking spell. A book settled in the damaged bay with a papery sigh. Owen finished the bread and looked at the bruised wood grain between his shoes.

"Do you go home for Christmas?" Prue asked.

He looked up.

"What?"

"Christmas," she said. "In December. Everyone pretends to be surprised by it and then it arrives anyway. Do you go home?"

"Usually."

"On the full train?"

"Yes."

"God." She took another bite of apple. "King's Cross in winter makes people forget they were ever civilized."

It was so plainly not the conversation he had prepared for that he answered without much guard.

"My mother wraps the sandwiches too tightly," he said. "Every year. Greaseproof paper folded under itself about six times. You have to tear it open with your teeth by Peterborough."

Prue's mouth twitched, not quite to a smile.

"What sort?"

"Egg. Or cheese and pickle if she's in a temper."

"That's a train sandwich," she said. "My father insists on buying them on the platform so he can complain about the price. He does it every year. He says if he doesn't complain they win."

"Who wins?"

"The railway, presumably."

Owen snorted before he could stop himself. The sound startled him almost as much as it pleased her. She used the moment to steal the cheese paper back and divide what was left into two more exact pieces than his had been.

"I hate the bit after York," she said. "Everybody goes to sleep upright and then wakes offended by it. My aunt always brings boiled sweets for her ears and passes them round as if she's distributing medicine to the condemned."

"We change at Nottingham sometimes. If the connection goes wrong my father reads the timetable aloud as if that helps."

"Does it?"

"No."

They ate the rest without much talk. The apple went last. Prue cut away the bruised part with a blunt little knife she produced from nowhere and offered him the cleaner half first. Owen took it and wondered, not for the first time, how many habits of someone else's he had missed in the Room of Requirement simply because he had been busy collapsing in public.

When she spoke again her tone had altered only slightly, enough for him to hear the question underneath it.

"That page you were copying," she said. "The one you shut. Was it from the thing?"

He hesitated.

"Partly."

"It looked wrong."

"Wrong how?"

"It didn't look like a table." She wiped the knife on the waxed paper and folded it away. "You'd ruled it as a table because the lines repeat, but the spacing wasn't clerical. It was sky spacing. Or it wanted to be."

Owen's hand tightened on the notebook.

"You saw that from one glance."

"Yes." She brushed crumbs from her skirt with the flat of her palm. "I had Astronomy after supper. I've still got the overlays in my bag. Monthly transparencies. Sinistra makes us do them because she enjoys other people's finger joints freezing off. If your page really is built on declination bands, or on some older version of them, we'd see it at once."

"You wouldn't see anything at once."

"No," she said, accepting the correction without offence. "Fair. We'd spend half an hour being cold and annoyed first. But then maybe we'd see something."

He should have said no again. He had already said yes to too much: yes, he had found something; yes, the shelf mark had led somewhere; yes, his ridiculous story had matter outside himself. The manuscript had stayed his partly because he never had to watch someone cleverer than he was put their hands near it.

But the copied pages remained stupidly blank in his notebook. His own efforts had got him as far as ruined latches, a worsening burn, and the knowledge that he was now being out-thought on printing history by a girl with one mitten and a violent opinion about sultanas.

"Where?" he asked.

Prue blinked once. "Where what?"

"If you were going to try it. Where?"

"Somewhere with glass." She gathered the books back into a stack. "Somewhere not watched. A greenhouse would do, if there's one you haven't already catastrophically improved."

He stared at her.

"That was a guess," she said. "Your cuffs smell faintly of tomato stems and burnt copper. I don't actually know what you did."

He rubbed at one sleeve, which made the smell no less real.

"Half an hour," he said. "After evening study."

"All right."

"One page."

She rose with the books braced back against her chest.

"One page," she agreed, in the tone of someone agreeing because more argument would only slow the next part.

At the end of the bay she stopped and turned back.

"Owen."

"What?"

"It isn't panic," she said. "Whatever you've done with it after that might be. The footnote isn't."

Then she went out between the shelves and the library took back its ordinary sounds: the faint clack of locks, the long wooden settling of old cases, the draught moving through the damaged north wall. Owen sat with his closed notebook under one hand and the ache in his palm bright as a coal. Keeping her out had been the rational thing. He could still tell himself that. It had just stopped being the easier one.

* * *

The greenhouse Prue chose was not the one Owen had misused, though it stood only two down the line and carried some of the same smell in its bones: damp soil, old sap, the mineral breath of watering cans left half-full too long. This one had been emptied for winter save for two benches of dormant bulbs and a row of cracked terracotta pots stacked by the door. Frost had started along the lower panes and worked upward in ferny white fans. The heating pipe clicked without conviction under the staging.

Owen reached the latch and hesitated before touching it. Since October every hinge had felt faintly personal to him.

The door opened normally. That ought not to have been a relief.

Prue was already there. She had commandeered a stubby hurricane lamp from somewhere and set it on the central bench between a bag of tools and a tray of dead labels. Her scarf was off now, wound around the base of the lamp to stop the draught licking the flame. She had rolled her transparencies into a cardboard map tube tied with black ribbon, and she was rubbing a clear patch in the condensation on one pane with the heel of her hand.

"You're late," she said.

"By three minutes."

"I was cold for all of them."

He set his satchel down. The Grinlow Leaves were wrapped inside his spare shirt at the bottom, with the ash-linen notebook on top as decoy and working copy. He had brought a second candle as well, because his wand had refused a steady *Lumos* in the corridor and he was not in the mood to be shamed by it in front of her.

"Did anyone see you?" he asked.

"No. I came round the back by the potting sheds."

She jerked her chin at the bench. "Put the page down. If we're going to do this, let's not waste the cold."

He opened the notebook first. The copied table sat in the middle like all the worst bits of homework he had ever done: painstaking, ugly, and still not right. He had traced the repeating lines, the blind little pinpricks at the corners, the faint curve he could only see when the page lay almost flat to the light. Prue bent over it with her hands braced either side and went still in that total way some people did when reading. Her curls slid forward. One caught in the orange wool of the mitten and stayed there until she blew it away.

"You haven't copied the depth," she said after a moment.

"What depth?"

"Exactly." She glanced at him. "You copied where the marks are, not what they do under light."

"I know that."

"I know you know that. I'm saying it aloud because that's the problem."

She slid the notebook nearer the lamp and untied the ribbon round the tube. Four sheets of translucent paper came out, each larger than foolscap and stitched with inked lines so fine they might have been cracks. She had pricked the brighter stars through with a needle and numbered them in cramped pencil. In the lamplight the pinholes showed white.

"These are this term," she said. "September through December. Monthly sky paths for the southern view from the tower. Don't laugh at my November one. My hands froze."

"I wasn't going to laugh."

"Good."

The paper was colder than it ought to have been. Owen held one corner while she laid October over the copied table, then November, aligning the lower edge to his ruled lines. Nothing happened. She rotated the sheet ninety degrees. Still nothing showed. The lamplight shone through ink, paper, and Owen's bad copy without the smallest hint of answer.

Prue frowned and adjusted again, narrower now, moving by increments no thicker than a fingernail. Owen could feel impatience stirring in him, not at her exactly but at surfaces, at the insufficiency of copies, at the way the whole business kept presenting itself as nearly legible and then refusing the last inch.

"If you're going to say it isn't a table after all," he said, "that's not actually helpful."

"I'm not saying that." She shifted the sheet left. "I'm saying your copy is obedient in a way the original probably isn't. Which is inconvenient and very rude of it."

The heating pipe clicked again. Wind dragged softly across the glass above them. Beyond the roof the night was clear in patches, clouds moving thin and fast.

Prue tried the November sheet upside down. The pinpricks of the larger stars crossed the drawn lines in his copy and stayed meaningless.

"This is boring," she said.

"Yes."

"Good. It would be suspicious if it wasn't."

They worked through another stretch in the same cold silence. Prue lifted the sheet, turned it, and lowered it again. She tried December, aligning not the edges but the corner pinpricks to the faint blind marks he had copied from the margins. That gave them a pattern for half a second - or Owen thought it did - then lost it.

Prue sat back, flexing her fingers. "How exact is the copy?"

"Exact enough."

"That isn't an answer."

"It does if the question is whether I copied what I could see."

"And if the question is whether what you could see is the thing itself?"

Owen looked at the notebook. The page lay there with all his effort in it and none of the original's stubborn pressure. The right answer was no. The safe answer was also no. He disliked the way those had ceased to match.

"The original behaves differently," he said.

"Yes, I thought it might."

She did not pounce on that. She only rubbed at the split seam of her mitten with her thumb and waited. It was, Owen realized, a particularly vicious kind of patience. He could feel the satchel against his leg like another presence in the room.

"Did you bring it?" she asked.

He should have lied. Instead he said, "Yes."

"May I see the page, then? I don't mean the whole thing. I just mean the page."

The not the whole thing was kind enough that it nearly annoyed him more. He crouched by the satchel, unbuckled it, and lifted out the notebook to keep up the appearance of choosing. Beneath it lay the spare shirt, rolled around the leaves. He unwound the cloth with hands that did not feel attached to the rest of him. The burn in his palm sharpened as soon as the outer fold came away, a quick bright pull, as if the manuscript recognized the cold and objected.

Prue did not lean in. She stayed where she was, though her breathing changed.

The leaf he chose was one of the bad ones, or what he still thought of as bad: mostly blank to anyone else, restless under his hand, ruled in faint pressure and scattered marginal punctures that might have been damage if they repeated less exactly. The surface was not parchment and not paper. It had the dry, dense feel of fabric treated until it forgot softness.

"Can I touch it?" Prue asked.

She hadn't asked to keep it, and she hadn't asked what it was. She had only asked to touch it.

"One finger," he said. "At the edge."

She gave him a brief look that might have been amusement and rested her forefinger on the lower corner. Her nail was bitten square. She touched the page as if testing heat from a stove.

"That's horrible," she said quietly. "It's like starched skin."

Owen had thought something similar and hated hearing it said aloud. He set the leaf on the bench instead and flattened the corner himself. The burn in his palm lit hard under contact. The blind lines in the page pressed up against his skin from below.

Prue took the November transparency again.

"Let's not waste time pretending your notebook matters," she said. "Hold the top edge."

They carried the leaf and overlay to the cleared pane she had rubbed free of condensation. Beyond the glass the grounds were a blur of dark lawn and paler frost, with a slice of sky above the black line of the greenhouses. There was no full moon, just winter stars when the cloud unstitched enough to allow them.

Prue held the transparency over the leaf and began to turn it in minute adjustments. Owen kept the page steady against the glass with his left hand and the lower margin pinned under his burned right palm. The cold from the pane went straight through the leaf and into the bones of his fingers.

"Lower," she murmured.

He lowered it.

"No, the other lower. Sorry. I hate words with directions in them."

The sheet moved again. One of the pricked stars on her overlay sat suddenly over a marginal hole in the manuscript and vanished into it. Then another. Then a thin line, brown-black and mean, came up between two of the blind rulings as if damp had struck through from the back. Owen stopped breathing.

Prue saw it too. He knew she had because the paper trembled once in her hands and then steadied.

"Don't move," she said.

"I wasn't-"

"I know. I mean it kindly."

More cloud moved off the slice of sky. Through three pinholes the real stars burned white. Under them the page answered.

The blind ruling darkened into columns. A heading surfaced along the top, not all at once but letter by letter, the strokes seeping into visibility with reluctant patience:

*FIRST WARD TABLES*

Below it, narrower lines rose and locked into place. He saw symbols he did not know and a repeated mark like a hooked gate. Four short columns sat under four quarter-signs. A note in a different hand ran up the side: *read against the winter sky; confirm at anchor; do not trust copied distances.*

Prue made a sound deep in her throat, not wonder exactly, more the involuntary sound of a person whose argument had just been vindicated by the furniture.

"Owen," she said. Then, because she had sense, "No. Never mind. Keep holding it."

He wanted all his hands and had only two. "Notebook."

"Right." She let go of the overlay for half a second, caught it again when it slipped, swore once under her breath with enough force to surprise him, then dragged the notebook open on the bench beneath the window. "Read."

"I can't read it all."

"Read what you can."

He did. He read the title, the side note, and two of the column headers once he had the angle. One might have been burden, or weather, or a word near enough both that translation did not help. Another repeated a sign he had seen elsewhere in the manuscript beside exercises that involved edges, thresholds, places where one state admitted another.

Prue wrote fast and ugly when she was in a hurry. That, too, humanized her abruptly. Her ordinary notes were compact and even; these sprawled where the lamp and the changing sky forced them. Ink caught on the paper fibers. She blew on one line and smudged it with the edge of her hand.

"Again," she said.

He read again, narrower, and corrected himself twice. She accepted the revisions without ceremony. Together they got enough down to matter before the clouds thickened and the lower lines sank back into blind pressure.

When the page went quiet again the greenhouse seemed louder. Wind worried the far door. Water ticked somewhere from a cracked pipe. Owen became aware that his right hand hurt fiercely. He took it off the lower margin and saw the burn had gone the angry red of fresh sealing wax.

Prue set the pen down. Her fingers were stained black to the second knuckle. "That wasn't a code," she said. "Or if it was, it wasn't just a code."

"No."

"It's an instrument."

"Probably."

She looked from the page to him. "How long have you been doing this alone?"

He flexed his hand once. The cold made the skin feel split without actually breaking.

"Since September," he said.

Prue swore again, softer this time and without weight enough to count as anger. More like a person placing a fact somewhere she disliked it.

"And the burn?"

"From the box it was in. Mostly."

"Mostly."

He said nothing.

She lifted the notebook page they had just filled and looked at the sprawl of copied words, the scratched columns, the crude little quarter-signs she had drawn to catch the structure before it disappeared. Then she looked back at the leaf on the bench, dull and blind again in the lamplight.

"You weren't mad," she said. "You were just impossible."

"I didn't think I was mad."

"You stood up in front of Neville Longbottom and proposed what sounded very much like a library haunting. I left room for the possibility."

It was a fairer account than he wanted. He sat on the edge of the bench opposite her and found that his knees were shaking slightly from cold. The lamp made a small island between them. Around it the greenhouse remained all old glass, dark soil, and winter air sneaking through putty gaps.

Prue took off the orange mitten with her teeth and rubbed her cramped hand. The fingers were ink-stiff and reddened with cold. "My mother does this thing with the gas bill," she said abruptly. "Every January. She carries it into the kitchen as if it's a telegram from the front and then spends half an hour turning down fires we already aren't using."

Owen blinked.

"What?"

"Nothing. I just..." She flexed her hand again. "It's stupid. This feels very large and I suddenly thought of my mother bullying the boiler as if moral force will make it cheaper."

He looked down at the page with *FIRST WARD TABLES* half-copied across it in Prue's hurried hand. The hidden lines, the sky, the impossible footnote with its own ancestry of removal. It was large. That was the problem. That was also, increasingly, the attraction.

"My father mends plugs at Christmas," he said before thinking better of it. "Nothing's wrong with them. He just lines them on the newspaper and says if he doesn't do it now he'll never do it. He never finishes. There are always screws left."

Prue huffed a laugh and pressed the heel of her bare hand briefly against her eye.

"That's worse than the gas bill."

"Yes."

They sat with that for a bit. There was no revelation in it. They were just two cold people in a school greenhouse with bad food still sitting heavily in their stomachs and a page between them that had no business existing.

Prue was the first to return to the actual trouble.

"The side note said confirm at anchor," she said. "Do you know what that means?"

"No. Not exactly."

"And the quarter-signs?"

"No."

"Ward tables, then. They aren't spells."

"No. They aren't."

She nodded once, mostly to herself. Her private voice, Owen noticed, was not warmer than her public one. It was only less defended. He could hear the small repairs in it where she chose the truer sentence over the cleaner one.

"If we're doing this," she said, "I need to know whether that was one lucky page or whether the whole thing is like this."

He heard the if and the need. It wasn't dramatic or even particularly forceful. It was simply exact. She had earned the exactness.

The manuscript lay on the bench beside his satchel, wrapped leaf over wrapped leaf inside the spare shirt. He could put it away now. He had the proof he needed. More than proof: a heading, a method, another mind capable of seeing what the pages wanted. If he thanked her and sent her back to her tower charts and school issues and platform sandwiches, he could still pretend this had been a single use of a useful person.

He imagined doing that and knew, with a cold clarity he disliked, that it would be a lie not just to her but to the work. Prue had already become part of the conditions under which the manuscript answered. She had proved the footnote belonged to the world and not only to his panic. She had brought the sky to a page he could not open alone. Keeping the rest hidden would no longer be caution. It would be vanity.

His hand hurt. The lamp guttered once and recovered. Somewhere outside, very far off, a door slammed in the castle proper and the sound came through stone and glass as a dull, ordinary fact.

"All right," he said.

Prue waited.

He reached into the satchel, drew out the wrapped bundle, and untied the shirt completely this time instead of teasing out a single leaf. The Grinlow Leaves settled on the bench between the dead labels and the lamp base with the weight of something both lighter and more serious than any schoolbook had a right to be.

Prue looked at the packet, then at him.

"So it isn't just one page?" she asked.

"No." He swallowed. The answer felt less like generosity than stepping off a curb in fog and discovering the road was already under him. "If you're staying, you may as well see how it starts."

For the first time that evening she seemed to lose a word, though only one. Then she pulled the notebook and the November sheet nearer, turned to a fresh page, and said, very carefully, "All right, then. Show me from the shelf mark."
