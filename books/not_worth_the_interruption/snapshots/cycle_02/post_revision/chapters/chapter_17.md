# Chapter 17

His mouth kept working.

The chamber had no proper weather of its own, but battle made one above it. Dust kept sifting out of the seams in the roof in thin grey veils. The stone under Owen's knees answered distant impacts a half second late, as if the castle were taking blows somewhere else and deciding whether to pass them on. Every time the anchor caught a fresh flare from the fighting overhead, the carved ribs around it lit fox-red and the open pages of the Grinlow Leaves showed more script than they had any right to hold. He had been living for hours inside that ugly bargain: let the school break hard enough, and the deeper work became legible.

He knelt in the founder-cut ring with his right hand laid flat on the anchor stone and his left braced against the floorboards of notation he had scratched over the flags in chalk, soot, and blood. Blood had dried under his fingernails in dark crescents and then opened again. His nose had stopped on its own and started twice more. The front of his shirt was stiff where he had wiped his face without thinking. Every breath brought mineral damp, old lake-cold, and the singed-metal smell that came when the ward edge rubbed against his amendment and failed to tear.

Mended was too generous a word. It was merely widening more slowly.

Only narrow phrasing held. He did not ask the world to heal, or protect, or save. Those were theatrical words and the First Art disliked them for the same reason good tools disliked vague hands. He asked for continuance at the seam. For dimness. For a lower answer where the breach had begun to shine. Each clause had to be braced against the next before the previous one slipped. He could feel the whole lattice as a cold drilling through the split skin around his nails and up into his wrist. When his attention blurred, the stone under his palm went hot enough to make the old burn wake and pulse like a second mouth.

Something in the passage scraped.

At first he sorted it with the other noises. Overhead collapse. A fallen suit of armour. One of the service grates knocking loose in the draft. Then it came again: the particular drag and halt of a person turning sideways in the narrow run between the cracked lintel and the old boiler flue. He knew that sound because he had once gone first with a lamp and Prue behind him swearing softly into the back of his jumper every time the stone caught her spectacles.

He looked up too quickly. The chamber tilted, then settled.

Prue came through the low opening with one hand on the wall and the other still clenched around her wand. Dust had turned the dark curls at her temples almost grey. One lens of her spectacles was milked over with ash except for the thumbprint arc where she had tried to clear it. She had kept, absurdly, a short library pencil hooked behind her right ear, as if there might still be a table somewhere she meant to work at. Her cardigan was gone. Her shirt-sleeves were rolled unevenly, one to the elbow and one halfway, and her left cuff was black with soot where she had wiped blood or smoke from her mouth. She shut the stone hatch behind her by feel, not because stealth mattered down here now, but because she had learned the route properly and habit survived panic.

For a second she only stared at the chamber. The chalk grid. The Leaves. Owen inside the ring with his hand fixed to the anchor like something nailed there.

"You found it in the dark," he said, because his mind offered route before motive.

"I found it because you brought me here the night we did the four-anchor tables, and I haven't forgotten the route just because you'd rather I had." Her voice came rougher than usual, smoke-scraped, but still careful on the edges. She took one step forward, saw the live line cut across the floor, and stopped. "Don't be stupid. I can see it's active."

Another impact sounded somewhere far above. Dust struck the pages. The anchor flared under Owen's palm and a cold pain went up his arm to the base of his neck. He swallowed against it.

"You shouldn't be here," he said. "The route isn't stable when the castle's taking this much load."

"I know. I came anyway." She was breathing too fast and trying to hide it by speaking on the out-breath. "I couldn't find you in the hall and Tallow wouldn't answer a direct question, which, frankly, was answer enough."

At Tallow's name a small practical corner of Owen's mind pictured the elf in the kitchen with a ladle still in his hand, pretending not to look at the service door while smoke came down the chimney. It made him feel, absurdly and briefly, ashamed of the clean circle of work around him. He set the shame aside because the seam moved when he did.

"How bad is it?" he asked, and heard how narrow the question was. He had stripped the battle down to load, pressure, scale, as if names and bodies were a later problem.

Prue heard it too. Something in her face altered as a guess hardened into proof.

"Bad," she said. "The front stairs are gone in one section. The courtyard keeps changing hands. There are children down there, Owen. There are people trying to move the injured on dining benches because nobody can keep the corridors clear long enough for anything clever. Neville's still standing. I saw him ten minutes ago, unless time is doing something odd on me. The rest..." She shook her head once, impatient with the uselessness of summary. "It's still a battle. That's the point. It's not over."

He nodded. The ward seam answered that news before he did. A pulse ran through the chamber wall, outward and back again, as if the school had taken a breath through cracked ribs. The notation nearest his knee rearranged its own soot shadow and showed him the dormant local branch once more: a buried adjunct nested under the house-protection tables, old as the foundations, waiting for authority and enough catastrophic stress to wake.

Prue followed his glance.

Her eyes dropped to the line he had been telling himself was a matter of sequence. The clause sat half above and half within the stone, its house-scope markers plain now that the battle had made them answer: not the small bounded amendments he had managed in corridors or punishment rooms, but castle-wide condition, hostile instrument, denial of ignition. Even exhausted, even half-blind with chalk grit, he could feel how much authority it would take and how quickly it would bite.

Prue stepped sideways for a clearer view, then went very still. When she spoke, her voice had lost the smoke-scraped raggedness and gone thin with exactness.

"That isn't just a shield."

"No."

"It suspends wandwork."

"Hostile wandwork," he said automatically. "Locally. Within the house. There's a duration problem and the boundary language is old."

She looked at him. "Owen."

"That matters."

"It matters to the wording, yes. I know that. I just, it doesn't make this a different thing."

A laugh tried to happen in his throat and came out as a cough. Fresh blood salted the back of his mouth. He turned his head and spat red into an old drainage groove cut beside the ring. It ran pink for a moment and vanished under the anchor.

"The seam is open," he said when he could talk again. "Not fully, not like the lens showed under the lake, but enough. This is what the battle is doing. If I spend the hold on the house clause now and lose the edge, the whole point of coming down here goes with it."

Prue took the pencil from behind her ear and, without seeming to know she was doing it, rolled it between thumb and forefinger like a worry bead. "You said if the battle peaked, the node would answer. You didn't say it would answer with a way to stop the battle."

"I didn't know it would."

"But now you do."

The chamber shuddered under something heavier and nearer than the ordinary concussion of curses traded overhead: a stone strike that made dust pour in sheets from the left wall and sent a hairline crack through one of Owen's outer chalk braces. He swore and reached with his free hand to redraw the broken segment before the branch-language bled into the repair. The floor bit cold through his trousers. By the time he closed the gap his fingers were shaking hard enough to blur the last mark.

Prue flinched at the sound but held her ground. "Can I touch anything?"

"No."

"Can I hold the left margin open?"

"No."

"You didn't even look."

"I don't need to. The surface is live and keyed through the scar. If you put your hand in the ring it'll answer you as noise."

"Then tell me what you need."

There was something almost unbearable in that question because it was practical, because it came from the part of the year when practical had still meant shared work and not judgment. His eyes stayed on the anchor. "Less interference," he said.

Prue closed her eyes once. When she opened them again she had gone quieter, which with her was worse than shouting.

"Upstairs," she said, "people are dying in bursts while you sort which word goes where."

He nearly said that dying in bursts was still dying. He nearly said that if the breach announced itself beyond the planetary mask those bursts would become meaningless. He nearly said several accurate things, and accuracy stood there between them with its hands folded like a school prefect.

Instead he said, "I know what it's doing."

"No, you know what the outer seam is doing." She crouched carefully outside the ring, keeping her skirt and sleeves clear of the marks. Her knees cracked on the stone and she winced, annoyed at the body for being audible. "I'm talking about the clause in front of you. The one that would stop hostile wandwork in the castle. Not perfectly, perhaps, not forever, I heard you, but long enough. Long enough for the battle to break the other way. Long enough for children to stop burning in corridors while you decide the other disaster matters more."

She grimaced almost before she'd finished it. "Sorry. No. It is what I mean. I just hate how tidy that sounded."

Owen pressed harder into the anchor until the heat climbed his arm. Pain made the notation cleaner. The repaired seam narrowed by a fraction. He could not tell whether the fraction came from his work or from some turn upstairs. He hated that uncertainty. He hated, more quietly, that her imprecision matched the bodies upstairs better than his exactness did.

"If I switch branches now," he said, "I may not get this hold back. The clause isn't free. Nothing here is. The whole reason it woke is that the school's under catastrophic load and the stone is answering too much at once. If I divert that answer into the local condition and then try to resume the seam, there is no reason to think the seam will still take. It might. I don't know. I really don't know. But if it doesn't, we've spent the only window on the wrong... on the smaller question."

Prue listened without interrupting, which was how he knew she had already decided what he was saying.

"Smaller to who?" she said.

He swallowed. "You know what I mean."

She gave a short, unbelieving sound. "No. I do. That's the problem."

The chamber settled into a brief lull. No impact. No shout through stone. Just water ticking somewhere in the wall and Owen's own breath rasping in and out. In that quiet the dormant clause glowed faintly inside the anchor, patient and available. It did not press him. The First Art never pressured in the human sense. It merely made options legible and then waited to see which damage you preferred.

Prue saw it too. Her gaze kept snagging on that lit branch and returning.

"You could do it and then come back," she said, but even as she spoke she was reading the brace structure around the seam and knew what the answer would be. "No. You'd have to unhook the hold. You'd lose the continuity."

"Yes."

"How long to finish this part?"

He almost said minutes. That would have been a lie offered in good style. He looked at the moving edge, at the place where the world kept failing to keep its old promise.

"I don't know," he said. "Less than from the start. More than anyone upstairs would call acceptable."

She sat back on her heels. Her spectacles slid down with sweat and dust. She pushed them up with the heel of one hand and left a fresh ash streak across her cheek. When she spoke again there was less urgency in the volume and more in the repetition, the way someone checked a latch already tested three times.

"So you can stop it."

His thumb kept tracing the seam in the stone. "Prue-"

"So you can stop it."

"I can invoke a local condition with unknown edge failure and bounded duration."

"That means yes."

"Yes," he said, because narrowing further would only make him sound sly, and perhaps he was. "Yes."

Above them something screamed. Not a person exactly; stone, timber, magical structure. The sound went through the chamber in a low animal bellow and set the water in the drainage cuts trembling. Prue's head jerked toward the ceiling as if she could see through it by force. Owen felt the battle answer in the anchor: hostile load cresting, the whole castle bright as a beacon to things no one upstairs even knew enough to fear.

"Then do it," she said.

He did not.

There was no gesture to mark the refusal. No heroic stillness, no dark revelation arriving in him whole. The hand on the anchor did not move. He followed the seam through another small correction. He redrew a failing bracket. He breathed through the hot nail driven up the middle of his arm and let the house clause remain dormant and available and unused.

Prue made a noise then that he had not heard from her before in all the months of libraries and cold rooms and quiet work, too ragged to sort cleanly as sobbing or anger. It was the sound a person made when thought and revulsion met without enough time to arrange themselves.

"You mean it," she said.

He did not look at her.

"You're going to let them finish it without you."

"I mean to keep the breach from brightening past recall."

"That isn't what I asked."

"It's what I can answer."

She laughed once, breathlessly. "Course it is."

Another impact. Nearer. One of the old lamp-hooks broke loose from the chamber wall and shattered on the floor outside the ring. Prue flinched back, then immediately looked ashamed of the flinch, as if being startled in a battle were a personal indulgence. She picked up one splintered bit of iron, turned it in her fingers, and set it carefully aside so it would not roll into the marks. Even now she would not make his work sloppier than it already was. That small obedience hurt more than if she had kicked the whole circle to pieces.

"You brought me in here," she said after a moment. "That night with the overlays, when we were mapping the four anchors. You remember."

"Yes."

"You showed me the service route and the keyed braces and the way the stone took the old notation because you needed another mind for the tables, and because for one hour you forgot to shut me out on purpose." Her voice shook once and steadied. "Don't now pretend I don't know what I'm looking at."

"I haven't."

"Then say it plainly." She leaned forward, not into the ring but toward him, demanding the line with her whole body. "Not the seam. Not the wider field. The other part. Say what you're choosing."

His mouth dried. The chamber tasted of wet stone and old pennies. A fresh thread of blood had worked loose from somewhere high in his nose and was making its slow way down the back of his throat.

He could have lied. Not well, perhaps, not to Prue, but enough to blur the edge. He could have spoken about probabilities, about branch instability, about catastrophic visibility, about the unnamed thing leaning toward any bright magical wound in the planetary mask. All of it would have been, in pieces, true.

Instead, because the clause glowed there between them and because she had earned the shape of it, he said, "I'm choosing not to stop the battle."

Prue stared at him.

The words, once spoken, did not improve. They sat in the chamber like spoilage. Outside the ring, water kept ticking in the wall.

"When I came off the west stair," she said, so quiet he had to lean toward the sound, "Martha was down on the bad knee again and still trying to keep two first-years behind the banister while Neville got a dining bench full of injured round the turn. There was a boy from Ravenclaw with his sleeve over his mouth and the cloth kept going dark anyway. I told myself if you were down here, it was because you had something none of us had."

Owen's thumb slipped on the anchor. The house markers on the left face surged silver and then thinned at the bottom edge, one old founder stroke guttering toward invisibility. He saw at once what the battle had been hiding by sheer noise: the clause was keyed not only to siege but to balance. Once the castle tipped hard enough toward victory or rout, the branch would sink again.

Without quite deciding to, he leaned one fraction of pressure sideways to test the reading. The local branch came up sharp and eager. On the western side of the repair a white line opened where he'd banked the seam to ember. Not failure. Not yet. Just the cost, immediate and ugly. He shoved the hold back into continuance before the white could widen.

Prue watched his hand, not his face. "So that's it, then. It isn't that you can't. You're waiting for the window to shut."

"I heard you," he said.

"No. You heard the cost. That's not the same."

There was, and because there was, he had no defense that did not prove her point.

The battle above changed.

He knew it before the next sound reached them. The anchor lost a whole band of pressure at once, as if a hand the size of the castle had been clamped around his throat for hours and now eased two fingers away. The hostile load did not vanish, but it ceased behaving like escalation and started behaving like aftermath: scattered bursts, collapsing vectors, magic no longer converging on victory but breaking into pockets and routs.

A second later the stone carried down a muffled concussion so deep it felt not heard but remembered. Dust billowed from the roof. Somewhere very far off, through floor and wall and earth, rose a noise that might have been shouting.

Prue went white.

Owen kept his hand on the anchor.

The dormant house clause dimmed as the moment passed out from under it, the battle tipping beyond the exact kind of undecided catastrophe that had woken it. He felt the availability narrowing and with it, stupidly, a wash of relief so filthy he wanted to retch. The choice had not been taken from him. Other people's work had simply carried it past its sharpest edge.

"It's turning," he said before he could stop himself.

Prue stared at him with such naked disgust that the words fell dead in the air.

"Yes," she said. "Up there. They did that without you."

Something like cheering reached them then, thin through the stone, torn by distance and masonry until it sounded almost like crying. Another voice shouted. Another. Then a scatter of noises too blurred to sort: grief, triumph, orders, disbelief. Human sound after a decision.

Owen's amendment still needed him. The seam had not sealed because Hogwarts won a battle. The planetary ward did not care about school heroics. He knew this. He kept working because the work remained. Yet the chamber no longer felt separate from upstairs. Prue had broken that insulation by arriving. Every careful stroke of attention now stood beside the image she had forced into plain speech: people dying in bursts while he sorted questions.

His vision fuzzed at the edges. He blinked grit out and steadied the last brace. The stone under his palm cooled by degrees, not kindness but compliance. The seam answered lower. The raw shining at its edge dulled from knife-metal to banked ember. He followed it through the taper until the amendment could stand without constant pressure and then, only then, lifted his hand.

The absence hit him hard. His arm went numb to the shoulder. He folded forward at the waist and caught himself on blood-slick fingers before his face struck the stone. For a few seconds the chamber moved in long slow swings. He tasted chalk.

Prue did not cross into the ring. She knew better. She also, he thought, knew that if she touched him now he might mistake it for pardon.

When he could sit back, the anchor was only stone again. Old, damp, ribbed with founder cuts and the residue of too much answer. The house clause had sunk below visibility. The Leaves lay open to nonsense tables and blank margins. Above them the castle continued making victory and grief in ordinary human proportions.

Prue stood.

One of her knees had stiffened from crouching. She put a hand on it, massaged the joint once with brisk annoyance, and straightened fully. The pencil was still in her fingers. She looked at it as if wondering why, then snapped it in half without seeming to mean to and dropped the pieces beside the broken lamp-hook.

"I'll go back up," she said.

Owen wiped his mouth with the back of his wrist. "Prue-"

She cut across him, not loudly. "Don't explain. If you explain it smaller you'll only make it worse."

He stopped.

She adjusted her spectacles with soot-black fingertips. The ash streak on her cheek had dried into something almost childlike and thereby unbearable. When she spoke again, her voice was level from effort.

"I needed to know whether you'd misread it or whether you meant it. I know now. No, that's not— I know what happened. I know you saw it and left it there."

He wanted, very briefly, to say that he had done something. That the seam was banked, the brightness lowered, the breach not lost tonight. But even in his own skull the defense arrived in the wrong units.

So he said, "All right."

She gave him a look so tired it nearly counted as pity and then took the lamp from the wall niche with a hand that shook only on the first attempt. At the hatch she paused, not turning back yet.

"Martha will ask where you were," she said.

The name moved through him like grit in a wound. "You don't have to tell her."

Prue looked over her shoulder. "I know I don't have to."

He could not tell from her face whether that meant she would or would not. Perhaps she did not know yet either.

Another muffled shout came through the stone, closer to laughter this time. Dawn was not in the chamber, but some paler quality had entered the cracks around the hatch, a less absolute dark. Smoke threaded down after it.

Prue lifted the latch.

"Come up when you can stand," she said. "Or don't. But if you stay below, don't start telling yourself the seam made the choice for you, or the conditions, or— any of that. There was a human thing in front of you. You know there was."

Then she went into the passage, bending automatically at the low turn, lamp cupped against the draught. The light moved along the narrow stone for a moment, caught on the repaired wire at the hinge of her spectacles, and vanished.

Owen stayed where he was.

Above him, somewhere in the enormous body of the school, people were finding each other alive or not alive. Names travelled down through the stone in bursts. Boots hammered a corridor, then slowed. Something heavy scraped over flagstones. Once a laugh came apart halfway through and turned into retching. He pictured benches shoved straight, faces covered with whatever cloth came to hand, runners breaking toward the Great Hall while victory started hardening into the version of the night other people would someday understand. The castle held all of that in its upper rooms.

Below, the anchor stone cooled under his ruined hand. The seam did not glow. The wider dark, for now, had been taught to keep its distance.

After a while he shut the Grinlow Leaves, wrapped them back in the tea towel, and set them into his satchel without looking at them.

He sat in the chalk wreckage until the last tremors left the floor and the sounds above resolved from battle into aftermath, and what remained in the chamber with him was the exact shape of the thing Prue had witnessed: help unused, made useless by decision.
