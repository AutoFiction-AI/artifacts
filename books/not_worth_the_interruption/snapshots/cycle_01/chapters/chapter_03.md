# Chapter 3

The Room of Requirement had stopped pretending to be mysterious. By late September it had settled on the look of a school storeroom after a small war: camp beds shoved too close together, trunks underfoot, laundry strung between bookcases, steam from a kettle going nowhere because the windows it had invented were only a kindness and not a real route for air. It smelled of damp wool, onion soup, old socks, and the sharp medicinal green of dittany. Somebody had bled on one of the cushions and flipped it over. The blood had come through anyway.

Owen stood just inside the door with the Grinlow Leaves wrapped under his jumper and had the immediate sense that he had brought the wrong object into a room that needed five dozen other things first.

Neville Longbottom was at the centre of it, not elevated, not making a speech, just standing with a scrap of parchment in one hand and saying names in the flat voice of somebody who had already had to repeat himself too many times that day.

"If you're sleeping here tonight, you don't leave after lights-out unless somebody sends you," he said. "I don't care if you hear your own dormitory door. I don't care if you think it's your own dormitory door. Ginny, can you take the first-years on the left side? Dean, I still need two lookouts for the fourth-floor turn. No, not both of you, because if both of you get grabbed we're worse off than before."

A younger Hufflepuff on the nearest bed had her school tie tied around her wrist above a curse welt that shone wet and red. Seamus was trying to pry a splinter of broken frame out of his thumb with his teeth. At the back wall a tall Ravenclaw girl Owen knew only by sight was sorting salvaged library books into two stacks, dry and ruined, and muttering a separate apology to each ruined one before she set it down. Her wire-rimmed spectacles kept sliding to the end of her nose. She pushed them back with the back of her wrist because both hands were full of warped paper.

Martha saw him first.

"There you are," she said, low and exasperated, as if he had wandered off during washing-up rather than vanished into his own head for the better part of two days. Her dark plait was loosening down one shoulder and there was a smear of something grey on her cheek that might have been dust or soot. "If you've come to stand in the doorway, shift. You're making everyone squeeze round you."

He moved because three people immediately needed the space he had been occupying. The room accepted them and not him. His right palm pulsed under the handkerchief he had wound round it. The burn had quieted enough during the walk that he had nearly convinced himself it was ordinary. Here, in the crush and heat, it had started needling again, as if the manuscript disliked crowds.

"Sorry," he said.

Martha was tying a bandage round a third-year's forearm one-handed while she talked. "Take that kettle over if you're useful. If you're not useful, sit somewhere narrower."

He took the kettle. It was hot through the handle and slopped weak tea over his knuckles. Nobody thanked him, which was fair. The tea was not for him. It was for a first-year Gryffindor whose mouth still shook after every swallow. Owen carried it across and nearly trod on a pair of shoes drying by the fire, stuffed with newspaper. One of the soles had been patched with cardboard cut from a Potions essay.

By the time he got back to the edge of the room Neville was crouched over a rough floor plan of the fourth floor scratched in charcoal on the top of an old wardrobe laid flat.

"They've started checking the suits of armour again," Neville said. "Not all of them. The two by the tapestry of Barnabas are still blind if you pass close. Amycus knows about the broom cupboard now, so stop using the broom cupboard. If anyone is still hiding notes in the flour bins, stop doing that as well. Tallow nearly had my head off."

There was a tired ripple of laughter at that, real enough to count as relief.

Martha came up beside Owen with a basin that smelled of pennywort and burnt cloth. "What happened to your hand?"

He folded the burned one in against the manuscript under his jumper before she'd properly seen it. "Nothing. I caught it on a hinge."

She looked at him for a moment that was too long for the lie and too short for a fight. "Right."

He should have told her then. Or if not her, Neville, because Neville at least would know what to do with new danger if it had a shape and a corridor attached to it. That was why he had come. The manuscript had spent the afternoon on his bed like a thing that had chosen him badly. When he touched the resolved pages the diagrams had settled into legibility for a few seconds at a time, enough for him to understand that he did not understand them, and underneath that, worse, enough to understand that the claim was not a metaphor. Magic was not the machinery itself. It was only what happened at the surface. There was something lower down, old and structural and written into the world like load-bearing grammar, and the manuscript treated Hogwarts as one local problem inside a much larger one.

He had tried, alone, to think what a sensible person did with that.

The sensible answer had appeared to be: tell other people immediately.

The flaw in that answer was the other people.

"Owen," Martha said, because Neville had paused to hear from a fifth-year Slytherin about patrol changes and Owen was still standing there like a badly placed chair. "Did you need something?"

Half the room did not look at him. The half that did looked in the way people looked when any small interruption threatened to become a larger one.

"I found something in the library," he said.

Neville looked up, waiting. He had a bruise yellowing along one cheekbone and the particular worn expression of somebody who had stopped expecting good news but still had to leave a space for it in case it arrived by accident.

"Is it a route?" Neville asked.

"Not exactly."

"Is it about patrols?"

"No."

Martha shut her eyes briefly. "Just say it, then."

Owen pulled the manuscript free enough to show its wrapping, then more because stopping halfway looked furtive and going on looked mad, and there was not, apparently, a useful degree between those two conditions. The vellum leaves slid against one another with a dry, insect-thin noise. Several of the pages facing outward were blank. One held a smear of diagram in rust-coloured ink that looked less like writing than a map of cracked riverbeds.

The younger students nearest him leaned away first and then, seeing it was only paper, leaned back.

"This was in a hidden shelf behind the north stacks," Owen said. "In a lead coffer. The footnote was in an old classroom copy of *A History of Magic*. Pre-1883, I think. I checked the shelf numerals after the blasting and they still match the old arrangement, or enough of it does, and the note said `North Stacks C-13, sealed by founders' agreement,' which shouldn't have still been there at all, and it wasn't just a book hidden there, it's something older than that, and I think it means the castle has-"

"Slow down," Neville said.

Owen stopped, swallowed, and discovered he had already lost the order of what mattered.

"It means," he said, smaller, "there's a kind of magic under the usual sort."

There was a silence, but it was not the silence of interest. It was the silence of a room deciding whether the interruption had been stupidity or panic.

Seamus stared at the nearly blank page. "Under the usual sort."

"Not bigger," Owen said quickly. "Lower down. Structural. Conditions, not spells. I know how that sounds. I know it sounds-"

"Like you've not slept," said someone near the back.

That got a few grim smiles, and because they were grim they hurt more.

Neville straightened. "All right. Start again. What does it do?"

"I don't know yet."

"What can we use it for tonight?"

"Not tonight exactly. I mean, perhaps eventually, if it's what it says it is, because there are diagrams about the castle and maybe not just the castle. There are references to warding on a scale that doesn't make sense if they're only talking about one building. More like the country, or-" He heard himself and tried to cut the sentence smaller before it finished humiliating him. "Or larger than the country."

Martha stared at him. "Owen."

"I'm not making it up."

"Nobody said you were," Neville said, and he was trying, Owen could see that, trying not to let the room turn cruel just because he did not have time for this. "I'm asking what, exactly, you want from us."

It was a simple question. Owen should have had an answer ready before he'd opened the manuscript in public.

"Help," he said, and hated the word the moment it left his mouth because it sounded childish and because it was true. "I need another look at it. Or someone who knows old references. Or just-" He shifted the leaves and a resolved scrap of text ghosted up the margin and then slid away before he could force it to stay. "This cites things that don't fit the editions we have. It says the old magical civilisation didn't die out, it left, and that what we've got now is-" He looked round the room at cots, bruises, steam, a first-year trying not to cry into weak tea. "Not the whole of it."

Nobody laughed. That would almost have been kinder.

Dean said, "Brilliant. So while Amycus is skinning us alive we've also got a secret civilisation to worry about."

"It's not that," Owen said. "It's not another war, I don't think. It's more that there are protections, or were, and they're-"

"Failing?" Seamus said. "Excellent. Lovely. Put it on the list."

That did make people laugh, short and exhausted. Neville did not.

"Enough," he said, and the room obeyed him because it trusted him more than it liked the joke. He looked back at Owen. "Do you have a hidden room the Carrows don't know? A route we can use? A way to stop them dragging people out of bed? If the answer to any of that is yes, say yes and we'll sort the rest later."

Owen's palm burned harder. Sweat slicked the handkerchief. The manuscript seemed suddenly indecent, like he had brought in an old saint's fingerbone and wanted applause because it glowed.

"I don't know if it's that direct," he said. "I haven't had time."

"We've all had time," Martha said, too sharp now to be only tired. She set her basin down on the floor with a wet slap. "We've had exactly the same amount."

He felt heat climb his face. "That isn't what I meant."

"Then what did you mean?"

He could have lied. He could have said the room needed warning because the castle sat on something older and stranger than anyone knew. He could have said the manuscript might matter later. Both statements were true, and both would have sounded like a person trying to make his terror nobler than other people's terror.

Instead he said the truest and worst version.

"I think this is bigger than the Carrows."

The room changed around him. Not dramatically. No intake of breath, no shouted outrage. It was smaller than that, which was why it was impossible to answer. Shoulders shifted. Somebody at the fire gave a disbelieving little snort through the nose. A first-year actually looked afraid, not of the manuscript, but of him, of the possibility that one more older student had finally cracked and started speaking in useless grand statements while the rest of them still needed supper and lookout rotations.

Martha's voice dropped. "Don't say that in here."

"I don't mean they're harmless, I mean scale. I mean if the castle itself is part of-"

Neville cut across him for the first time. "No."

It was not loud. It did not need to be.

"Listen to me," Neville said. "If you've found something real, then good. Hide it properly and keep yourself alive long enough to work out what it is. But I can't pull people off moving children and covering corridors because you've found a mostly blank book that says the world is bigger than we thought. The world is already bigger than we thought. That's why half this room is sleeping in here."

Nobody moved. Neville went on, plain and tired and absolutely right.

"If you want a bed, have one. If you want someone to check whether you're hexed, we'll do that. If you want me to tell everyone here that their actual problem tonight is less important than your theory, I won't. I don't have that luxury, and neither do they."

Owen opened his mouth. Nothing came out that was fit to say.

Martha rubbed at the grey smear on her cheek with the heel of her hand and only spread it wider. "Have you even told me where you found it?"

"North stacks. Behind them. Through the old service run by the kitchens."

"Can we use the run?"

"Not for this. I mean, yes, physically, probably, but that's not-"

"Then say the useful bit first next time."

The words landed harder because she had not raised her voice. She sounded fed up, embarrassed for him, embarrassed for herself for having expected better.

At the back, the Ravenclaw girl with the ruined books had stopped sorting. She was looking at Owen now with her head slightly to one side, not in pity, not in agreement either. More like she had heard a shelf creak behind a wall and was trying to decide whether the building plan allowed it.

Dean let out a breath through his teeth. "Can we get back to not dying till breakfast?"

"Yes," Neville said at once, without taking his eyes off Owen. "We can."

That was the dismissal. It was kinder than Owen deserved and somehow worse for that reason. If Neville had told him to shut up, Owen could have disliked him for half a minute and gone away cleaner. Instead Neville simply turned his body a fraction, enough to include everybody else and exclude Owen, and asked Ginny whether the first-years on the left side had eaten yet.

The room resumed around him. Not smoothly. A few glances still snagged on the manuscript and then slid off in a hurry, the way people looked away from vomiting. Martha went to the third-year's bandage. Seamus found his splinter again. Someone by the fire said they had one heel of bread left and another voice said cut it thinner, then. The ordinary stupidity of surviving reassembled itself and left Owen standing outside it while still technically in the middle.

He wrapped the leaves up with fingers that had gone clumsy.

His burned palm had dampened the handkerchief through. When the cloth shifted, the skin beneath flashed up angry and ridged, not a simple blister now but the dark outline of a branching mark he had no name for. He covered it again at once. If anyone had seen, nobody said.

He did not know where to look. Anywhere in the room was wrong. At the beds made him feel theatrical. At Neville made him feel younger than eleven. At Martha made him feel false. He ended up looking at the floorboards, where somebody had spilled soup earlier and half wiped it, leaving a pale onion crescent in the grain.

For one shabby second he considered saying sorry to the whole room.

That would have made it about him again, which was apparently a skill he had discovered a fresh aptitude for.

He waited until nobody was directly watching and edged backwards toward the door. The Room, perverse as ever, had put a narrow table in his way since he'd come in. On it sat three labelled jars of salve, a stack of folded socks, and a chipped china dog with its head broken off. He had not seen the dog before. He had the absurd impression that the room itself had added it simply to improve the quality of his humiliation.

"Phipps."

He stopped with his hand on the latch.

It was not Neville. It was the Ravenclaw girl from the back wall. Up close she was older than he'd thought from a distance and more tired around the eyes. One cuff of her jumper was ink-black where she had clearly been using it as a handkerchief for weeks. She had tucked two bent quills behind one ear as if she'd forgotten they were there. In one hand she still carried a ruined book, held carefully away from her body so the warped pages could dry.

"Sorry," she said, though she did not sound sorry. "What printing was the *History of Magic*?"

Owen stared at her.

The room noise carried on behind them. Neville was assigning another corridor pair. Somebody swore softly because a bandage had stuck.

"What?" Owen said.

"The edition." She pushed her spectacles up with one knuckle. "The footnote you quoted. `North Stacks C-13, sealed by founders' agreement.` Which printing had that? Because the classroom text drops the founder shelf marks before the 1883 clean issue, and after the 1894 recatalogue nobody writes `C-13` for anything in the north stacks. So either you've had a breakdown in a very specific bibliography, or somebody cut that note out of every copy that ordinary people can still find."

For a moment Owen only looked at her. He had spent two days with a discovery so large it made the school year feel like a bad local arrangement of furniture. It had not occurred to him until then that the first person who might believe him would do it because the citation style was wrong.

"The title page was half gone," he said. His voice came back rough. "But it was old. Binns's classroom shelf. Not library stock."

The girl's gaze sharpened, not with excitement exactly, but with the grim concentration of someone seeing a filing error big enough to imply deliberate damage.

"Right," she said. "I thought so."

"Prue," Martha called from across the room, distracted and irritated. "If you're done courting mildew, I need the dry bandages."

The girl did not look away from Owen. "In a minute."

Then, quieter, to him: "Don't wave that about in here again."

It was not disbelief. It was worse and better than that.

It was advice.
