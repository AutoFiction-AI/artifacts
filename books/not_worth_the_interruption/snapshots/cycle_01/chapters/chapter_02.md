# Chapter 2

North Stacks C-13 did not exist in any arrangement the library still admitted to using.

Owen proved that to himself three different ways before supper and twice after it, because there was always the chance that he had made one small stupid transcription error and built the whole thing out of that. He checked the current shelf index by hand until Madam Pince told him to stop breathing on it. He whispered the location charm fourth-years were taught for lost essays and watched the spell shiver uselessly over every respectable part of the north wall except the blasted section, where it went dull and dropped like wet thread. He measured the exposed stone numerals again, fingers in the cracks left by Amycus's last fit of disciplinary enthusiasm, and found the same thing he had found the night before: the modern shelving stood over an older system whose numbers had been cut straight into the stone, and C-13 belonged to that older system, not this one.

By nine o'clock the library had that late, watchful quiet it got under occupation. It wasn't peaceful. It was empty in the wrong way. A room could keep hundreds of books and still feel as if it had been searched for valuables. The hanging lamps were turned low. Dust sat in the broken joints of the north stacks where blasting had shifted the cases half an inch off true. Owen was supposed to be checking spines for damage on the nearest trolley. He had checked six. The rest sat untouched while he crouched in the gap between two leaning cases with the old Binns copy open on his knee and a second book balanced against the shelf.

The second book had been the useful one.

It was a prefect guide from 1881, bound in cracked school-green cloth and written in a tone that assumed rule-breaking was a regrettable but permanent branch of natural history. Owen had found it shelved among obsolete discipline records because nobody at Hogwarts now needed instructions for how to patrol after curfew without upsetting the house-elves, and nobody in any century had wanted to read it for pleasure. The useful part was not the advice about confiscating dungbombs or the outraged paragraph on courting couples behind armour. It was the foldout plan in the back, a cross-section of the castle's service levels and blind passages, annotated for prefect rounds in a tidy dead hand.

The modern library ended at the north wall.

The 1881 plan carried on past it.

Behind the wall, above a drop marked kitchen runs, an older line of storage spaces had been drawn in pale green ink: retired shelf-chases, too narrow for ordinary traffic, lettered by chase and numbered by span. C-13 sat there, behind what had once been the Restricted Section and what was now a bank of respectable history shelves with a blast crack through them.

Owen looked from the plan to the exposed numeral carved low in the stone. C-11, half hidden under a shifted case. Two spans along, if the old measurements still held. Another crack. Another offset. Then C-13, presumably on the other side of a wall no catalogue charm could see through because it was no longer part of the library as the library understood itself.

His hand hovered over the foldout's margin note.

"Access by service aperture only. Not for broad-bodied patrol use. Report disturbances to kitchen steward."

He read it again, because the line was the difference between a hidden shelf and a hidden shelf he might actually reach.

The route was not for broad-bodied patrol use.

The phrase would have been insulting if it had not been so helpful.

He was still looking at it when shoes clicked two aisles over. Owen shut the guide at once, not loudly, slid it under the trolley cloth, and reached for the nearest damaged book. Madam Pince came into view between the shelves with a candle balanced so steadily in her left hand that it looked nailed there. Her mouth had the pinched professional bitterness of a woman forced to babysit a war inside a room that had once obeyed rules.

"If you are shelving by smell, Mr. Phipps," she said, "you will be here all night."

"Sorry," he said. "This one had torn boards."

"Then you may put it on the torn-boards trolley instead of staring at it as if remorse will mend it."

"Yes, Madam Pince."

She moved on. The keys at her waist made a dry little rainfall against her skirt. Owen waited until the sound had passed into the west end of the room before he let out the breath he had been flattening in his ribs.

Curfew was in twelve minutes.

The guide gave him a route in theory. Theory was not enough. Half the castle's routes had been blocked since the year began, and the other half were usable only if you liked explaining yourself to people with authority. He needed to know where the service aperture actually opened now and whether it still opened at all.

There was only one person he could ask who might know the answer and might not immediately hand him over for being out after hours.

Owen finished the six books he was supposed to have shelved, stacked the rest exactly as he had found them, and signed out with Pince a minute before the bell. Then he took the long turn down the Hufflepuff-side stair, cut through a laundry passage that smelled of damp wool, and slipped into the kitchen corridor just as the castle settled into its curfew noises: doors thudding, distant shouts, the drag of benches somewhere overhead. The stone changed temperature down there. Library chill gave way to banked heat and old steam caught in the walls.

The kitchens were still working. They were always still working. Trays were being scrubbed. Cauldrons were cooling. Someone had dropped a spoon and not yet found it, which mattered immensely if you judged by the muttering.

Tallow stood on an upturned crate by the scullery sink, polishing a dent out of a copper pan with furious concentration. He was small even for a house-elf, all singed ears and knotted wrists, with soot worked so deep into the creases of his knuckles it looked permanent. He had a tea towel thrown over one shoulder and three bent soup spoons lined on the sill in front of him like patients waiting to be told how bad it was. Every few strokes he stopped polishing long enough to check whether the pan's loose handle had worsened.

He saw Owen without appearing surprised.

"Young sir is late for not being late," he said to the pan. "Tallow is busy with a thing that used to be round."

"Sorry. I just needed to ask one thing."

"One thing grows elbows. Then it wants another thing and another thing and the gravy skins over while everyone talks."

Owen came closer anyway, lowering his voice because even here the castle felt as if it had ears now. "Do you know where the old kitchen runs meet the north stacks?"

Tallow kept polishing.

"Lots of places meet lots of places. That's castle work."

"The retired shelf-chases," Owen said. "The ones above the kitchen side. There was an old patrol map and I think C-13 comes off the library north wall, only the aperture isn't marked properly and the catalogue doesn't see it and I can't tell if it's sealed or just forgotten."

That got Tallow to stop.

He looked up with the expression of someone who had hoped a schoolboy was asking for bread and discovered instead that the schoolboy had opinions about masonry. One of the bent spoons slid from the sill and clinked into the sink. Tallow did not take his eyes off Owen.

"Young sir has been reading in shelves that don't want company."

"I've been reading a guide."

"Mm."

Owen waited. Tallow set the pan down, picked up the fallen spoon, bent its neck back by hand with a soft hateful click, and only then spoke again.

"C-chase runs behind the old north books, yes. It drops from the library wall to the hot side through an ash throat nobody uses because it was built for smaller times. The opening is behind the flour bins, second panel after the cracked blue tureen. Don't lean on the right jamb. It gives."

"It still opens?"

"It still pretends to be stone. That isn't the same as being shut."

Owen felt his pulse move, not faster exactly but harder, as if something inside him had put its shoulder to a door.

"Could an adult fit?"

Tallow gave him a look which, on a human face, would have been almost rude.

"A grown wizard?" he said. "In robes? With the sort of dignity they're always carrying? No. A very thin one might get his arm in far enough to injure himself and blame the house. A prefect boy your size, if he takes off his jumper and has the sense to exhale before the second bend, can manage. That's what the passage is for now. Not that it's for you. It's for nobody."

"Right," Owen said. "I only meant generally."

"Young sir means specifically and badly."

Owen started to deny it, stopped, and tried a narrower truth. "If something was hidden there, and nobody could get at it physically, wouldn't a Summoning Charm bring it out?"

Tallow's mouth tightened. He began setting the bent spoons in a straighter line.

"If the box is what the old north stewards called a hush box, no. Lead in the walls, lead in the seams, old library spite all round it. It sits off the list. It doesn't answer pulls. The catalogues slide round it like grease round cold porridge. That was the point."

"A hush box," Owen repeated.

"A nasty little thing for books people meant to have and not have. Too hidden for lending, too important for burning. There were more once. Then there were fewer. Then everybody forgot why fewer did not mean none."

The cracked blue tureen sat on a high shelf by the flour bins, its hairline fracture stitched with old silver. Owen had passed it a hundred times without thinking about the wall behind it.

"You know what is in C-13?"

Tallow snorted softly and took up the copper pan again.

"If Tallow knew what all the old hidey-holes kept, Tallow would spend his life disappointing clever children. Tallow knows routes. Tallow knows which stones feel wrong. Tallow knows the north throat gives him a pain behind the eyes and a taste like pennies. That's enough knowledge for work."

He scrubbed hard at the dent. The pan squealed under the cloth.

"Don't take a lamp," he added. "Air goes mean in there. Use wandlight if you must, but keep it low or the shine will leak at the books. And if the wall starts humming, come out. I mean come out, young sir. Not in five minutes. Not after a look."

"Does it do that often?"

"Often enough for manners."

There was a pause in which the kitchens sounded suddenly immense: water thrown into a sink, someone chopping stale crusts for tomorrow's pudding, the distant rush of enchanted taps shutting themselves off. Tallow inspected the pan handle again, grimaced, and muttered something to it that Owen did not catch.

"Why tell me at all?" Owen asked.

Tallow did not look up.

"Because if you go blundering after curfew without the left-stone, you will put your foot through the ash throat and then I will be the one scraping you out before breakfast. Because boys who think in maps are worse than boys who think in fists. Because the house keeps enough mess. Pick one."

It was not permission. It was enough.

Owen nodded. "Thank you."

"Young sir can thank Tallow by putting the tureen back exactly where it sits if he starts touching things. That crack bites flour."

Owen almost smiled. He did not trust smiling just now; it felt like something that could make noise.

"I will."

Tallow had already gone back to the pan. "Mm. And wash the blood off the stone if there is blood. The grout stains."

That made Owen stop in the doorway.

"Why would there be blood?"

Tallow turned the pan to the light, judging the dent with offended attention.

"Because old work was not made for being found by boys after bedtime," he said. "Off with you."

Owen went.

He did not go straight back to the cellar. He waited in the passage beyond the kitchens until the last of the service noise had lowered and the curfew hush settled properly over the lower corridors. Then he counted two patrol cycles by the castle clock and came back in stocking feet, shoes slung over one shoulder, jumper knotted round his waist. He moved the cracked blue tureen first because Tallow had been irritatingly clear about that. Behind it the stone panel looked no different from the rest of the wall until he pressed the left edge and felt a hidden give.

The right jamb shifted under his testing hand exactly as Tallow had said. Rotten, but in stone rather than wood. Owen put his weight elsewhere and pulled.

The panel opened inward on a slit of old heat and dust.

The aperture was shoulder-high and immediately narrower than he had hoped. Not impossibly narrow. Just honest. He put his shoes aside, flattened himself, and slid in feet first until his hips jammed. For one blank second he had the stupid, complete conviction that this was it, that he would be found halfway into a wall after curfew with his trousers full of mortar dust and no explanation in English or any other language. Then he remembered to let the air out of himself. His ribs narrowed. The stone let him through another few inches. He twisted, found the left side with his hand, and dropped onto a sloping run of brick that shed cold ash against his palms.

The panel settled behind him with a soft closing cough.

Darkness took the passage whole.

Owen got his wand out carefully. "Lumos."

The light came up at the tip in a pinched blue bead, meaner than usual, but steady. The passage pitched down at once, no more than three feet across and less in places, old brick on one side and the dressed stone back of the library wall on the other. It smelled of soot, dead heat, and the dry metallic tang of places shut up for years. Ahead, the crawlspace bent sharply left. The floor was a mixture of ash, cinders, and grit fine enough to get into his sleeves and between his teeth.

He went on hands and knees after the first yard because there was no dignity to be had from pretending otherwise. The walls closed and opened unpredictably. Sometimes his shoulder slid through with room to spare; sometimes a jut of stone found the soft part under his arm and made him suck air through his nose until the pain passed. Above him, at irregular intervals, he could hear the library in fragments: a muffled footstep through planks, the creak of a settling case, once the tiny hard ring of Pince's keys. The whole structure seemed to be listening sideways.

C-11 had been visible from the library. He counted spans as he went, palming the old cut numerals where they emerged from soot and brick: C-12 on a recessed block, then a cracked support where part of the chase had slumped, then another low shelf of stone that forced him flat enough to feel his shirt drag on every grain.

At the second bend the passage tightened brutally.

He stopped, cheek against cold brick, and tried to send calm into parts of himself that had no interest in being calm. There was no room to turn back from here, not properly. He would have to inch backward blind if he failed to get through, shoulders burning, heels catching. He pictured that too clearly. Tallow's warning about manners and humming and blood came back in a stupid useless rush.

He took another breath, let it out, and reached forward.

His hand found empty space.

He dragged himself through the constriction and came into a pocket just large enough to crouch in. He sat back on his heels, blinking sweat out of his eyes. The wandlight lifted a narrow chamber built between the back of the library shelving and the older exterior wall: shelves long removed, iron brackets left in place, a drift of shredded cloth and mouse droppings in one corner. The stone here had a different feel from the crawlspace, less domestic, more deliberately secret. Someone had meant this gap to remain a usable volume long after the library forgot it.

C-13 was cut into the wall at knee height.

Nothing obvious sat beneath it.

For a few dreadful seconds Owen thought he had done all this for an empty niche and a dead numbering system. Then he saw the line in the stone above the numeral: a rectangular seam so fine he only caught it because the blue wandlight flattened differently on metal than on rock.

It was lead.

It was not painted or plated. It was a whole inset box driven into the wall and disguised with stone dust until even at arm's length it nearly vanished.

His heart gave a nasty, eager kick.

He tried the smallest thing first, because trying the biggest thing first was how people ended up with eyebrows missing. "Accio box."

Nothing happened.

Not even the courtesy twitch of resistance.

He swallowed and altered the angle. "Accio contents of C-13."

Still nothing happened.

The silence after the spell had a thick, almost personal quality, as if the box had listened and chosen not to answer.

Owen edged closer and laid two fingers against the seam. The metal was cold enough to sting. His locating charm in the library had not failed because he had cast it badly. The guide had not been misfiled by chance and the citation had not survived because the catalogue had overlooked it. The thing had been hidden from every ordinary library habit on purpose. There was lead in the walls, lead in the seams, no Summoning range, and no catalogue hold.

That was why it had stayed lost.

Any grown wizard with authority would have tried the same clean spells he had just tried, found nothing, and concluded there was nothing there. Adults did not generally take their jumpers off and crawl through ash for a footnote. Owen was not sure whether that made him cleverer or merely less well brought up.

The seam ran round three sides. On the fourth, low down, there was a pressure catch disguised as a bit of damaged tooling. He got his nails into it and failed. He tried the tip of his wand and felt the catch give half a line before the wood jerked in his hand like something alive. The bead of light at the end spat sideways into the wall. Blue sparks cracked over the stone and went out with a smell like singed wool.

Owen stared at the wand.

Nothing about that had been a normal recoil. It had felt less like a miscast than like the space itself had slapped his wrist.

"Right," he whispered. "Fine."

He set the wand between his teeth for light, braced his left hand against the wall, and used his right thumb to work the catch again. This time it depressed. A second catch, higher. Then a third, hidden under the lip. The box door shuddered but did not swing free. He had to put his palm flat to the face of it and shove.

The brand took him so fast he did not at first understand it as pain.

Heat struck through the centre of his right hand, flat and white and total, as if the lead had become a stove plate. He made a noise into the wand, half choke and half swear, yanked away, and knocked his head off the wall hard enough to fill his eyes with sparks. The wand dropped. Blue light spun across the cramped chamber, flashed once against the ceiling, and landed in the ash.

For one impossible instant the mark on his palm shone brighter than the wandlight.

Then the pain arrived properly.

It went up his arm in a grinding wave. Owen folded over it, forehead to his knees because there was nowhere else to go, sucking air through his teeth and trying not to vomit in the crawlspace. The skin across his palm had gone bone-pale where the heat had hit, not blistered yet, just raised in a shape he could not make sense of through the tears in his eyes: a hooked line, two branching cuts, something almost like a diagram burned into flesh.

"Fuck," he said, softly and sincerely.

The box door had sprung open.

Inside lay a stack of thin folios wrapped in oilskin so old it had gone stiff as bark. The covering had split where the latch released, and under it the manuscript showed itself in edges and corners: pale leaves, narrow and long, with green-black stitching through one margin and a title strip darkened by age.

The strip read Grinlow Leaves.

He knew the title before he had fully read it. The letters had that brute founder-era confidence old school objects sometimes kept, as if they had never expected a later century to exist long enough to mishandle them.

His palm throbbed with each beat of blood. He picked the wand up with his left hand, relit it, and reached back into the box more carefully, using the cuff of his shirt between his right hand and the folios. Even through the cloth, the packet made the small hairs on his arm rise. It was neither warmth nor cold. It was a pressure, like standing too near a dropped note that hadn't stopped ringing.

The first leaves were partly blank.

They were not empty. They had been worked over. Ink had once been there and had gone down into the page the way old pond water goes dark under weed. When he tilted the folios, faint geometry moved under the surface: ruled circles, cross-hatched margins, strokes too deliberate to be decoration. Then a page halfway through resolved suddenly into legible writing, not because the light changed but because his burned palm, hanging useless and hot at his side, seemed to pull the page into answer.

Owen sat very still.

The script was older than any school text he had handled and yet disturbingly practical in tone, like notes for competent people who had no patience for being impressed. He could not read every word. Some of the language slanted into archaic forms, and some of the notation was not any runic hand he knew. But the argument was plain enough to punch through the gaps.

This was not wandwork.

It was not incantation.

It was not a spell to be cast at an object.

It was a condition to be amended before any spell, any threshold, or any named category could behave.

The page set out examples in parallel columns: open, closed, held, permitted, witnessed. It was not teaching the reader how to throw force at the world. It was teaching the reader how to alter the rule under which force counted. Every spell Owen had ever learned assumed the world came pre-agreed, its permissions already fixed. These leaves were about working beneath the ordinary surface of that agreement, at whatever layer made Hogwarts magic possible in the first place.

He turned another page with the back of his left knuckle.

The next page went blank again, then offered a diagram of nested circles and a later English note squeezed into the margin: amendments proceed from ground conditions, not visible effects.

The next legible leaf was stranger still.

It carried no instructions he could understand, only a table and a sphere drawn with four marked points around its middle, each point linked to a line that went outward beyond the page edge. Around the sphere ran the familiar grammar of warding, but at a scale no school ward ought to need. The heading used a term he had to sound out twice before it meant anything at all: Meridian Commonwealth. Beneath it, in a thinner hand, a later note cross-referenced departure houses, continuation masks, terrestrial concealment.

The word terrestrial sat there in plain ink.

He looked at the drawn sphere again.

It had to mean Earth, then, or the magical world represented as Earth whether the diagram meant the whole planet or only a part of it. Four marked sites ringed the sphere, and lines led away from each of them. The wards were being held in place not around a room or a vault or even a school, but around something planetary and left active after some organised departure. It did not suggest extinction or ruins abandoned in panic. It suggested a leaving organized enough to keep the wards running afterward.

His scalp prickled under the dust.

Another page, half legible, showed a lattice over coastlines he recognised only because Hogwarts taught geography badly enough that Britain always looked lonelier than it should. Another page stayed stubbornly blank no matter how he angled it. Then another answered in a burst, text rising dark from the fibres while he watched, and he nearly dropped the folio at the sight of it.

The manuscript was not passively revealing itself to any reader with time.

It was answering a condition in his hand.

The burn seared again, as if to underline the point.

Above him, somewhere beyond a wall and several centuries of changing floorplans, a case shifted with a soft wooden groan. Owen froze. The library, the patrols, and the curfew all came back at once, insultingly local after what lay in his lap and still absolutely capable of ruining him.

He wrapped the folios back in the split oilskin as carefully as he could one-handed and looked into the lead niche again. There was nothing else inside except a bed of grey felt and, under that, the imprint where the packet had sat untouched for so long the fabric had learned its edges. Returning it would be the sensible thing. Returning it would also mean trusting a wall to keep behaving now that the box had burned him open.

He was not putting it back.

He slid the Grinlow Leaves under his shirt, flat against his stomach. The packet was colder than the air and somehow heavier than its size allowed. He shut the lead door with the heel of his left hand. The catches did not re-seat cleanly. One remained sprung, leaving the tiniest black line at the seam.

It would stay forced open, then.

He would have to live with that.

Getting back through the crawlspace with one functional hand was worse than getting in. He had to lead with his left arm and drag the rest of himself after it while trying not to crush the manuscript against the brick or scrape the burn on the wall. Twice his right palm brushed stone and the pain turned his vision grainy. Once the wand flared without warning, a sharp blue spit that lit the passage all the way to the far bend and made him lie still, breath stopped, until he was sure nobody in the library had seen.

By the time he pushed the kitchen panel open again his hair was wet at the roots and his shirt stuck to him with soot. The corridor beyond was empty. Warmth from the banked ovens pressed against his face. A pot somewhere in the dark gave a sleepy settling tick.

He set the cracked blue tureen back exactly where it had stood.

Then he leaned one shoulder against the wall and finally looked properly at his hand in the wandlight.

The burn had darkened from white to angry red. In the middle of it the branded shape stood out sharper now, a small angular figure with one branching stem and a hooked cross-line, neither letter nor rune he knew. The skin around it had already started to swell.

He touched his wand to the edge of the mark, meaning only to test whether a cooling charm might be worth the risk.

The wand jerked.

A thread of blue fire spat sideways, singed a line across the flagstone, and vanished under the flour bin with a smell like hot dust.

Owen snatched the wand back and stared at the scorched mark on the floor. Ordinary magic had never done that in his hand before. A misfire after pain, perhaps. Except it had not felt like pain. It had felt like interference. As though the burn had shifted some invisible tolerance and the wand no longer sat in his grip at the angle the world expected.

From the scullery came a soft clink. Tallow, somewhere beyond the doorway, still working.

"Did you bleed?" the elf called without raising his voice.

Owen looked at the flagstone, the singed flour dust, the sweat on his own wrist.

"No."

There was a pause.

"Mm," said Tallow, in a tone that meant he did not believe him but could inventory the lie later.

Owen rinsed the worst of the ash from his left hand at the kitchen tap, not daring to put the right under water yet, and slipped back into his shoes. The manuscript pressed flat and impossible under his shirt. He could feel each folio edge when he breathed. Outside the kitchen corridor the castle kept up its occupied-school business: patrol routes, shut doors, boys whispering in dormitories, girls pretending not to listen for steps in the passage, one whole frightened year trying to get to morning by routine.

He climbed toward the cellar with a founder-era manuscript tucked against his skin and the shape of a burn changing on his palm.

At the turn below the common room he stopped under a lamp and pulled the top folio half free again, just enough to make sure he had not imagined the sphere, the table, the buried instructional tone. The page that had been blank in the chase was still mostly blank now, but not entirely. Along the inner margin, as if surfacing through fog, a line of script had come dark enough to read.

It was not about a charm.

It was about permission.

He slid the leaf back in at once, hand shaking not with fear exactly but with the effort of fitting the thing he knew into the thing he had just found. Magic at Hogwarts had always arrived as craft and vocabulary and rule-bound effect. It depended on speaking correctly, moving correctly, and meaning it. These pages suggested that all of that sat on a deeper arrangement somebody could alter if they knew where to press.

And if the pages with the sphere meant what he thought they meant, then somebody had once used that deeper arrangement not to open doors or mend cups or curse enemies, but to hide the whole world.

The common-room barrels were a few yards ahead, warm and absurdly ordinary.

Owen tucked the manuscript back under his shirt, closed his burned hand around nothing, and went in without any idea how he was supposed to sleep with the universe suddenly reduced to a condition someone had written down.
