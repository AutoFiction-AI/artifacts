# Chapter 11

By the time the actual parchment reached him, Owen had already been told twice that the Headmaster wanted him.

The first warning came from a narrow portrait on the second-floor landing, a witch in tarnished blue silk whose painted pearl earring had been scratched off years ago so that one bare ear looked oddly accusatory. She had been darning a stocking in her frame with short offended jabs. As Owen came up from the cellar after dawn, satchel dragging at his shoulder and the whole night with Prue still caked under his eyes, she looked up and said, "Mr Phipps. Do stop behaving as if staircases can protect you. The Headmaster has sent for you."

He had kept walking because portraits repeated things for pleasure and because he still had the Grinlow Leaves in his satchel, wrapped under his spare shirt beside the ash-linen notebook. He had not trusted the manuscript to the dormitory after the night's work, not with the map of four failing anchors still half alive in his head and Prue's last, flat silence following him down the corridor more cleanly than anything she might have said.

The second warning came outside the Great Hall. Edith Wren, a Hufflepuff sixth-year with a voice made papery by a winter cough, caught his sleeve as he went in. Her badge had been sewn back on with blue thread because she lost pins constantly and refused to admit it.

"Filch was asking after you before breakfast," she said. "And one of the Slytherin prefects. Proper asking, not corridor gossip."

"Right," Owen said.

Edith let go of his sleeve and studied his face for a second too long. "You look dreadful."

"Didn't sleep."

"That'll help."

She moved on, coughing into her fist. Owen stood one beat longer in the doorway while the Hall noise struck him full on: plates, chairs, the soft public thunder of a few hundred people pretending school still had a shape. Then he went to the Hufflepuff table because not going would only have made him more visible.

The parchment waited under his spoon.

It was folded once, no seal, only his name written in a severe hand that managed to look disappointed by the existence of ink. His bowl of porridge steamed around it. Somebody had placed it there after he sat down or before he arrived; either possibility was bad in a different direction.

Across from him, Martha looked from his face to the note without pausing in the work of buttering bread. Two of her knuckles were split and yellowed with ointment. She had started favoring her left leg after the corridor fiasco, not enough to call it a limp if one was being polite, more than enough to notice if one knew her old stride.

"Open it," she said.

Owen unfolded the parchment.

`Mr Phipps will attend the Headmaster immediately.`

Nothing else. No threat, no explanation, only the command and the signature below it: `S. Snape`.

Martha tore her bread in half with more force than necessary. "Well."

"Yes," Owen said.

"If you're going to say you don't know what it's about, don't. Nobody's that unlucky by accident."

"I don't know which thing it's about," he said before he could stop himself.

That got a short look out of her, not surprised exactly, more disgusted that he had managed to confirm there were multiple candidates. Three places down the bench a fourth-year stopped talking to his friend and glanced over. At the Ravenclaw table Prue was bent over her tea as if it required classification. One lens of her spectacles was clouded where she had wiped it badly on her cuff. She did not look up.

Owen folded the note smaller and slid it into his pocket. His satchel strap had wound itself round his ankle under the bench. He thought, briefly and very stupidly, of leaving it there. But the table was full, people moving in and out, Amycus somewhere in the middle aisle with his mug and his appetite for interruption. There was no route back to the cellar that did not pass other eyes first. The manuscript was safer on him than off him, which was another way of saying there was no safe version at all.

Martha had already started on her porridge. "Are you going to eat?"

"Not now."

"Fine."

She did not tell him good luck. He did not wait for her to.

The walk to the office should have been long enough to think. It was not. Everything in the castle seemed arranged to remove thought and leave only awareness.

Two suits of armour stood outside the Hall doors. Usually they watched the room with the bored vacancy of school fixtures. This morning one of them had its visor angled fractionally down, enough that Owen felt inspected while he passed. On the third-floor landing a pair of Slytherin girls broke off talking when they saw him and stepped aside too quickly, as if proximity itself might make them part of whatever was happening. A staircase that normally preferred dithering students over punctuality settled under his feet at once and carried him straight upward without the usual malicious pause. He was so tired that each small convenience felt worse than obstruction would have done.

His right palm had been hot since dawn. Not the clean old burn from the coffer now, but the newer cross-lines that had risen after corridor work and undercroft strain. He kept the hand in his pocket and the pocket clenched.

At the headmaster's staircase the stone guardian admitted him before he had fully stopped. The concealed door folded back on a draft of colder air, and the spiral stair began to turn with the faint grinding sound of old machinery deciding it had waited long enough.

Owen put one hand on the rail and rode upward with the satchel pulling his shoulder crooked. He had imagined, in the worst moments since breakfast, that he might be sick on the stair. Instead he was too frightened for that. Fear had gone dry on him somewhere between the portrait and the Hall. It sat now as a series of practical recognitions. The manuscript was in the bag. His wand was unreliable. If Snape searched him, the rest became fast. If Snape did not search him, that might be worse.

At the top, the office door stood open.

He had never been inside under Snape. The room still had the circular authority of the place under Dumbledore, but whatever softness had once kept it from feeling administrative had been pared away. The windows let in a washed grey morning. Most of the silver instruments on the side tables had gone still, as if their owners had been told not to chatter. Several portraits of dead headmasters appeared to be sleeping with theatrical innocence. Two others were plainly listening. The air smelled of beeswax, cold ash, and old cloth that had been shut away from daylight too often.

Snape stood beside the desk, one hand on a stack of parchments he was not reading. He looked more tired than Owen had ever seen him at a distance, which was to say he looked exactly as severe as usual with a little more of the skull showing through. His black hair hung straight and damp at the temples, as if he had come in from weather that only he could detect.

"Close the door, Mr Phipps."

Owen did.

"Put your satchel on the chair."

The words landed low and clean. Owen obeyed because there was no form of hesitation that improved them. He set the bag on the hard wooden chair opposite the desk and sat nowhere near it.

Snape watched the movement. "You may stop looking at it. If confiscation had been my immediate object, you would not still be carrying it."

The line went through Owen like a nail drawn down glass. He kept his face as blank as he could manage, which was not very blank at all this tired.

Snape went on as if he had merely corrected posture. "Within the last several months this school has acquired a greenhouse with impaired ideas about closure, a punishment platform that briefly mistook contact for permission, and fresh stress marks in wards old enough to predate standardized wand instruction. Your own spellwork has degraded in parallel. Shall we continue pretending these are separate accidents."

It was not phrased as a question. Owen answered anyway.

"Things have gone wrong," he said.

"Yes." Snape's mouth moved by less than a smile. "That is what I have just summarized."

Owen looked at the desk because looking at Snape made him too aware of the satchel at his side. The desk surface was almost bare. A school ledger, a quill, two sealed memoranda, and a fine grey film of dust in the carved groove at the back where even house-elves could not be bothered with ceremonial furniture this year.

"I haven't done anything to the office," he said, and heard at once how childish it sounded.

Snape's eyes narrowed not with anger but with the concentration of a man finding a smaller and more exact disappointment than the one he had expected. "No. Fortunately for both of us, you have so far confined yourself to less politically complicated sections of the building."

He came round the side of the desk, not quickly. Owen was suddenly aware of how little the room offered in the way of useful movement. The portraits, the door, the stair below it, the still instruments, all of it had the same quality as the summons had carried up the corridor: surveillance that no longer needed to hide it was surveillance.

"Your right hand," Snape said.

Owen did not move.

"Mr Phipps."

He took the hand out of his pocket and laid it palm up on his knee. The old coffer burn crossed the center in its dark glossy ribbon. Over it and off it ran the newer red-brown branches, finer and less regular, as if some second hand had tried to write through the first in a language skin was unsuited to.

Snape did not touch. He bent slightly, studying the palm with the same expression he might have given a contaminated potion sample.

"A primary brand," he said after a moment, mostly to himself. "Then load response. Crude, but not random." He straightened. "What you have been using is older than wand discipline and less forgiving. Older scholarship usually calls it amendment."

The word hit harder than any shouted accusation could have done. Owen had not realized, not really, how much of his courage depended on the possibility that no one else in Hogwarts possessed even the right nouns.

"I don't know what you mean," he said.

Snape looked at him with frank contempt. "Then your standards for lying have worsened along with your wandwork."

Owen shut his hand again.

Snape returned to the desk but did not sit. "Let me save us both time. I do not know which manuscript, table, ruin, or founder-era leftover you have put your hands on. If I did, this conversation would be shorter and your morning markedly worse. I do, however, know the traces left by pre-wand amendment traditions when they are practiced by someone with more access than discipline."

Owen's mouth had gone dry enough that swallowing felt public. "You've seen this before."

"I have read around it," Snape said. "Which is not the same thing and should not reassure you."

The room seemed to contract by a measurable amount. One of the supposedly sleeping portraits shifted under its painted blankets and then went still again.

Snape's voice lost a degree of its classroom edge and became colder for that loss. "Ordinary spellwork applies force within agreed boundaries. Amendment does not. It asks a prior question. What counts as shut. What counts as contact. What belongs within a wall and what does not. The older notes sometimes describe these as questions of the world." He let the phrase sit. "The world's chief defect, in this context, is that it answers literally."

Owen heard the greenhouse latch, the punishment dais, the stupid comic burst of doors and toilets and armor in the corridor sabotage, all of them suddenly classified inside a vocabulary that was not his alone.

"I wasn't trying to do any of that," he said.

"Of course you were not. Intent is often the least interesting part of the damage."

Owen looked up despite himself. "If you know this much, why haven't you taken it."

Snape's face altered by almost nothing. It was enough.

"An admirably direct question," he said. "For you." He turned one parchment over, though there was nothing on the back worth seeing. "Because the Carrows notice broken children, missing food, and overt insubordination. They do not notice stress in old ward seams unless somebody educates them. I prefer to preserve that ignorance."

The answer was not an answer. It admitted strategy and nothing underneath. Owen tried another angle.

"So you want me to stop."

"I want you," Snape said, "not to make the school structurally more ridiculous than it already is."

For a second Owen almost laughed, not because the line was funny but because it struck the exact scale at which he had been trying and failing to think since the undercroft. School, planetary masking ward, Carrows, manuscript: all of it had been refusing proportion. Snape had reached into the mess and selected a sentence small enough to function.

"I can't promise that," Owen said before good sense caught up.

Snape's head tilted. "No. I had not expected promise from you. Only information, which I am also apparently not to have."

That was a mistake, Owen knew it while making it, but fear had gone on hardening and had taken some of the ordinary manners with it.

"If you already know the traces," he said, "then you know enough without the source."

One of the portraits gave a tiny sound that could have been a smothered cough or a laugh. Snape did not take his eyes off Owen.

"Do not mistake my current ignorance for your safety," he said.

"I'm not."

"Good."

He moved to the window and looked out over the grounds, though the tone of the room did not suggest scenery had much to do with it. From where Owen sat he could see only a slice of pale lawn and the black lines of wet branches.

"There is another matter," Snape said.

Owen waited.

"Hogwarts is not inert stone. The founders wrote conditions into the place because they trusted architecture more than descendants. Stairs, thresholds, classroom limits, permissions, exclusions, old service seams, all of it. Most of those conditions have been layered over by centuries of ordinary magic and adolescent misuse. They are still there." He turned back. "If you keep worrying the same old work, the castle will not remain scenery to your experiments. It will begin answering on its own terms."

Owen felt the undercroft in his teeth again, the metal-spoon tapping through the floor, the sense of the building as machinery instead of backdrop.

"What's that mean."

"It means," Snape said, each word clipped clean, "that a school may decide what counts as inside. Or safe. Or hostile. Or admitted. It means doors may become arguments. Corridors may remember the wrong rule. Rooms may begin completing your imprecisions in ways you did not intend and cannot reverse. The castle is a fixed frame large enough to mistake for a world if one is young and foolish. Do not ask it questions you cannot survive having answered."

The warning went through Owen more coldly than the threat had. For the first time since he entered the office he forgot the satchel for a full second.

"You know about the undercroft," he said.

Snape's expression shut at once. "I know enough to recognize when the school has acquired a new source of strain below the public level. Do not flatter yourself by assuming omniscience where attention suffices."

There it was again, the gap. He knew traces, not site. He knew tradition, not scale. He had vocabulary, not the whole map. Owen did not have the luxury of relief; even partial knowledge in Snape's hands felt like standing near a blade while someone else tested its weight.

"Who else have you told," Snape said.

Owen thought of Prue's face over the table, ink on her cuffs, the hurt in her silence when he would not promise human use for human danger. He thought of Martha. Neville. The Room in September. He answered the most literal version available.

"No one who can do anything with it without me."

Snape's gaze sharpened. "That was not reassurance either."

"It wasn't meant to be."

For the first time something almost like genuine irritation crossed Snape's face. "No. I see you have reached the age at which boys confuse exclusivity with competence."

He came back to the desk and finally sat, which somehow increased rather than reduced the pressure in the room. Seated, he looked less like an administrator and more like a knife somebody had remembered to fold.

"Listen carefully," he said. "If Amycus or his sister develop curiosity about your hand, your wand, or the bits of corridor damage that coincide suspiciously with your movements, your life will become shorter and louder. If you are foolish enough to continue this work, keep it off public routes. Keep it off moving architecture. Keep it away from children who have already got sufficient cause to scream. And if the building starts answering before you have finished asking, you will stop. Immediately. I will know the difference."

Owen's throat felt lined with grit. "How."

Snape looked at him as if the question had failed an examination before it was fully set down. "Because I am Headmaster," he said. "Because every damaged ward in this place eventually reports as inconvenience to someone. Because there are very few categories of disaster left to surprise me."

The last sentence was flatter than the rest. It did not invite sympathy. It did not even make sense as confession. It only sat there as fact, a hard little object nobody touched.

Owen heard himself ask, "Are you going to tell anyone."

Snape's answer came without delay. "No useful person."

Again it was not quite an answer, or only the answer of a man who had arranged his whole year around speaking at slants. Owen had no way to test it and no power to require a straighter version.

Snape picked up one of the sealed memoranda and set it down again. It was dismissal, almost.

"You may go, Mr Phipps."

Owen stood.

"And Mr Phipps."

He stopped with the satchel strap in his hand.

"Whatever you are carrying," Snape said, "the fact that it has chosen a fifteen-year-old boy as its current custodian should not be taken as endorsement by history, providence, or this office."

There was no reply to that that was not either stupid or adolescent in a way Owen could not afford just then. He lifted the satchel, felt its familiar bad weight drag at his shoulder, and left.

The spiral stair carried him down too smoothly. By the time he reached the corridor his knees had started shaking with a delay that felt insulting.

He had walked into the office with the manuscript, the notebook, and every obvious fear laid out in advance. He had walked out with the manuscript and the notebook still in his possession. The fear had altered shape instead of lessening. It had picked up detail.

* * *

By supper the meeting had gone common.

Nobody came up and said so. That would have required a level of leisure the year did not provide. But the knowledge moved through the school in the way useful dirt always did: under doors, through lines, across tables, inside jobs people were already doing with their hands.

A second-year Ravenclaw who normally borrowed ink from anyone looked at Owen's bottle in Charms and asked the person two seats over instead. On the stairs after lunch two Gryffindor boys stopped a murmured conversation the moment he rounded the bend, one of them crushing a folded scrap of parchment into his fist so fast the edges cut his palm. In the library Madam Pince took back a stack of indexes from him without comment and then, after a beat, locked the damaged north bay with a key rather than the usual charm. That one hurt for reasons too particular to explain.

At the Hufflepuff table the gap around him was only half a seat wider than usual. It still registered. Martha sat farther down with two younger girls and a loaf she was slicing too thin because that was the only way there would be enough. She did not look up once while he ate.

Prue was absent entirely. Owen found himself checking the Ravenclaw table three times anyway.

Halfway through the meal a first-year Gryffindor stopped beside him with a folded note. She had sewn stars in white thread onto the frayed edge of her cuff, uneven little five-pointed things that made the school robe look briefly like something she had chosen.

"Neville says after," she muttered.

"Where."

"Barnabas tapestry."

She put the note down and went before he could ask anything else. The paper was blank inside. It did not need words.

He knew, walking there after supper, that the worst part would not be accusation. Accusation could be answered, even badly. The worst part would be arithmetic.

Neville had chosen a disused classroom off the seventh-floor turn, not the Room itself. That was sensible. The corridor smelled of damp stone and cabbage from somewhere below. When Owen pushed the door open, Neville was inside with a crate of pilfered supplies on the desk: bandages, soap, three apples, and a jar of peppermints that had probably been taken from some office meant to keep them for guests. Martha stood by the windowsill tearing an old sheet into strips with both hands and her teeth when the fabric refused the line. She kept working when he came in.

Neville shut the door behind him.

"How long were you with him," he said.

There was no greeting and no theatrics. Owen almost preferred that.

"I didn't time it."

"Long enough that people saw you go in and come out."

"I was summoned."

"Yes," Neville said. "I know how offices work."

Martha ripped another strip and wound it into a roll. "Shut up and answer him properly."

Owen looked from one to the other. Martha's plait had come half out and been redone in a hurry. There was dried soap at one wrist where she had clearly tried to wash something off and been called away before finishing. Neville looked as if he had slept in sections no longer than ten minutes each for a month. A bruise had worked out along his jaw in yellow and green. He still stood with the steadiness of somebody forcing the room to borrow it from him.

"He'd noticed damage in the wards," Owen said. "And my hand."

Neville held out a hand. "Let's see."

Owen hesitated, then showed him. Neville did not touch either. He just looked, jaw tightening once.

"That from Snape."

"No."

"From your private project."

"Yes."

Martha snorted softly without amusement. "At least we're saying it plain now."

Neville's eyes stayed on the hand a second longer, then moved to Owen's face. "What did he want."

There it was, the question with no good answer.

"He knows some of it isn't ordinary magic," Owen said. "Old stuff. Older than wands, or older than how we use them. He knows enough vocabulary to recognize traces."

"What traces."

"Stress marks in the castle wards. Places things went wrong."

Martha dropped a bandage roll into the crate. "Places you made them go wrong."

"Not always on purpose."

"I know," she said. "That's one of the less comforting bits."

Neville took that in and let it sit. "Did you tell him anything he didn't know already."

Owen thought of the undercroft, the anchor stone, the four dim nodes on the table. Thought of Snape saying manuscript and not book, old work and not system, enough but not all. He answered carefully.

"Not much."

"Not much isn't a measure."

"No locations. No other names. Nothing that lets him use it."

Neville's face did not change. "Use what."

Owen knew at once he had stepped in it.

"The work," he said. "Whatever the old system is."

"Right." Neville rested both hands on the desk. "So here's the problem. You've got some hidden bit of magic you've been keeping from everyone. It leaves marks on you and on the building. Snape notices, calls you in private, and you come out with your bag still on your shoulder and no blood on your face. Then you tell me not to worry because he can't use it. Do you hear how that sounds."

"I didn't say don't worry."

"You didn't need to."

There was a short silence. From somewhere down the corridor came running feet, then laughter cut off too quickly. Martha kept tearing sheet into lengths and winding them, tear, pull, fold, wind, as if the work licensed her not to look directly at him.

Owen tried again because the alternative was standing there and watching the arithmetic close.

"He warned me," he said. "About the castle. If old work gets pushed too hard, the place can start answering on its own. Doors, rooms, rules. He knows that part."

Neville's expression stayed flat, but something in it cooled another degree. "And you thought this helped your case."

"It means he's dangerous."

"He was dangerous before breakfast."

"Not like this."

"Fine." Neville's voice sharpened for the first time, not louder, just cleaner. "Then he's dangerous in one more direction. What exactly do you want me to do with that."

Owen opened his mouth and had nothing except the wrong scale again, the same failure as the Room months earlier, only now with worse evidence and less trust.

Martha supplied it before he could.

"He wants us to understand the special horrible thing on top of the ordinary horrible thing," she said. She still was not looking at him. "And maybe thank him for the update."

"That isn't fair."

She turned then. "No. It isn't. None of this is."

The plainness of it shut him up better than shouting would have done.

Neville straightened from the desk. "Listen. I don't know whether Snape's using you, watching you, or just cataloguing one more school disaster for later. Maybe you don't know either. What I do know is that I can't have someone in planning who goes in and out of his office on private business and then tells me not much when I ask what happened."

"It wasn't private business. I was hauled in."

"That doesn't make it safer."

"I didn't choose it."

"You chose everything before it."

That landed. Not because it was entirely fair, not because it accounted for every turn the year had taken, but because it was near enough to true that arguing over percentages would only make Owen sound exactly like himself at his worst.

He heard the thin scrape of his own breath and hated it. "So what. You're throwing me out."

"I'm keeping you off routes, meetings, and message work until I know what this is." Neville nodded once toward the satchel at Owen's side. "And until I know whether Snape does."

"He doesn't."

"You don't get to certify that."

Martha dropped the last roll of bandage into the crate and wiped her hands on her skirt. "You really don't."

Owen looked at her. "You think I told him."

"I think you keep making your own mess and then act surprised when the rest of us are standing in it." Her voice was tired rather than hot now, which hurt more. "You came out of Snape's office carrying the same bag you went in with. Good for you. From where anybody else is standing, that looks filthy."

He wanted to say he had been protecting them by not naming Prue, by not naming the undercroft, by not dragging anyone else into Snape's vocabulary. He wanted to say the work under the school had become large enough to deform every ordinary decision around it. He wanted, stupidly, to say that Snape had not taken the manuscript, as if that constituted innocence instead of one more problem.

None of those sentences belonged in Neville's room, with the apples on the desk and the torn sheets and the smell of soap. They were all the wrong scale again.

So he said, "Right."

Neville rubbed a hand over his face. "If you need somewhere to sleep, you still know where the room is. If there's immediate danger to students and you know it, you tell me. Quickly. Plainly. Beyond that, stay out of the work."

"Your work," Owen said, and heard the pettiness too late.

Neville dropped his hand. "Yes," he said. "Mine, because somebody has to keep count."

No one spoke for a moment after that.

Then Martha picked up the crate. "I've got to get these downstairs."

Neville moved to open the door for her. She went out without brushing past Owen, though the room was small enough that avoiding him took a visible angle.

At the threshold Neville paused and looked back once.

"This isn't a punishment," he said. "It's me not having room for doubt."

Then he left too.

Owen stood alone in the disused classroom with the torn-thread dust from the sheets still drifting in the late light. On the desk one peppermint had rolled out of the jar and come to rest against a crack in the wood. He stared at it for a second as if he had been assigned the task of accounting for sweets and had failed that as well.

When he finally went downstairs to the Hufflepuff common room, the round room was full in the ordinary way: homework, wet shoes by the fire, somebody arguing over a Transfiguration essay, bread smell coming faintly through the stones from the kitchens. The scene should have been a relief. Instead it felt arranged.

At the long central table two third-years were bent over a hand-drawn map of corridor turns. One of them, a boy with a patched sleeve and a habit of chewing the inside of his cheek, saw Owen come in and put his hand over the paper at once. He did it efficiently, not guiltily.

The other boy folded the map and slid it under his Potions text.

Neither of them said anything.

Owen crossed the room to his usual chair and set the satchel down between his shoes. The manuscript was still there. The notebook was still there. So was everything Snape had not taken, everything Neville would not now let near his own work, everything large and hidden and structurally wrong.

Around him the common room kept being warm, round, and Hufflepuff in the old stubborn way. Somebody laughed by the hearth. Somebody else asked for the butter. No one asked him to pass it.
