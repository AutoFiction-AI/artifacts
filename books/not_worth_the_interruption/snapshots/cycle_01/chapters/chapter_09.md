# Chapter 9

By February the castle's cold had stopped behaving like weather and become one more school habit. It found the wet band above a sock, the strip of wrist between cuff and skin, the spot at the back of the knee where stone got in and stayed. Even in the kitchens there was no proper defeating it, only heat from ovens on one side and the draught from the service arches on the other, as if Hogwarts meant to keep every comfort half-argued.

Owen sat at the end of a scrubbed table with his ash-linen notebook under one hand and a heel of bread under the other, pretending to copy Herbology notes while he stared at the same three crooked marks for the fourth time. The marks had come out of the Grinlow Leaves that morning only after he pressed his burned palm flat across the margin and waited through the now-familiar wrongness in his teeth. South warming passage, fourth hinge from scullery arch. The English gloss in his own notebook looked childish beside the older notation, but he had not found a better one.

Tallow was on a stool by the nearest range, sorting spoons into piles with the concentration of a man handling bones. He muttered numbers under his breath, stopped to sniff one of the spoons in suspicion, then polished it on his apron anyway. The kitchens smelled of onions, wet stone, yeast, and a pan of something sweet catching at the edge.

Martha came in through the fruit portrait with sleet in her hair and a tea towel over one shoulder. Her dark plait had half come loose. One of her knuckles was split and freshly mended, the skin still shiny. She had a crate of potatoes braced against one hip as if she had forgotten it was there.

"You're hiding in plain sight again," she said.

Owen looked up. "I'm in the kitchens."

"Yes. Exactly." She set the crate down by Tallow's stool. "Have you eaten?"

"Some."

She pushed the bread heel nearer him with two fingers. "That doesn't count."

Tallow clicked his tongue without looking round. "Miss Sorrell is correct, and Tallow dislikes repeating an obvious fact in his own kitchen."

Martha pulled a face at the range, affectionate enough that Tallow allowed it. Then she turned back to Owen, and the quick ordinary irritation in her expression thinned into purpose. "Need you tonight."

He glanced at the notebook before he meant to. Martha noticed everything practical and most things that were not. Her eyes went there and back.

"For what?"

"Lower west corridor. After curfew." She hooked the tea towel into her waistband. "We've got two first-years stowed in the disused classroom by the staff lavatory, and Hester with them trying not to make herself useful. They bolted after Dark Arts this afternoon and somebody needs them out before Amycus does his late sweep and finds them where they ought not to be. Neville's putting a noise together at one end, we move them through the other, and then everybody gets downstairs before anyone important starts feeling clever."

Owen kept his face still. So it was lower west. The service line in the margin note had to be part of that run. The burn in his right palm gave one small hot throb, enough to make him close his hand.

"Why me?"

Martha stared at him as if the question itself were rude. "Because you know the kitchen side better than half the people doing this. Because you can get through a narrow gap without sounding like a wardrobe falling over. Because there are three twelve-year-olds in a room that smells of bleach and mop water and fear, and I'm short a person." She wiped her wet hand on the towel. "And because I asked you, Owen. That one was meant to matter a bit."

He took a bite of the bread to buy time. It was still warm in the middle. Across the table Tallow lifted a spoon, frowned at a bent handle, and put it in a different pile as if the castle's fate might yet be decided by cutlery.

"What sort of noise?" Owen said.

"The ordinary sort. Armor, lavatory, corridor mess, a few things where they shouldn't be." Martha leaned against the table. "Nothing grand. We don't need grand. We need four minutes and nobody fainting."

"If it's only that, why short a person?"

"Because people keep getting caught, or hexed, or moved to detentions, or sent home in bits." She said it flatly, not for effect. "Because one of the sixth-years from Ravenclaw can't put weight on his foot after Tuesday. Because normal reasons."

Owen nodded. He could already see the corridor in the notebook's margin, not the real one but the older line beneath it, founder stone dressed over and over under newer doors. If he got there under cover of somebody else's operation, he could test the warming passage without having to invent a reason for being out after curfew. He could be down to the service run and back before Amycus finished shouting at the toilets.

He ought to have said no.

The Leaves had been clearer with every error: divided attention was not harmless merely because it felt efficient. Category did not care what he had meant. The corridor would be full of doors, latches, metal plates, old stone. They were shared structures, the exact sort of place where a careless amendment travelled farther than his nerves.

Martha was still watching him, though not hard, not yet. There was more hope in it than she would have liked him to notice.

"All right," he said.

Her shoulders changed first, dropping by about half an inch. Then the rest of her face followed. "Right. Good."

"I'm not promising I'm useful."

"No, I've met you before." For a second she nearly smiled. "Just turn up and do what you're told. That'll already be an improvement."

Tallow slid off the stool and crossed to a shelf without wasting movement. He handed Martha a folded cloth parcel that had not been there a moment ago, or at least not where Owen had seen it. The parcel smelled of cheese and pepper.

"For the little ones," he said. "And if anyone puts muddy feet on the west passage wall again, Tallow will know."

"They won't." Martha took the parcel with more care than she had shown the potatoes. "Thanks."

Tallow gave Owen a look from under singed brows. "The drain by the fourth arch is backing up. Mind where you stand if you don't want the castle on your stockings."

The fourth arch.

Owen said, too quickly, "Which fourth arch?"

Martha's eyes narrowed a fraction. Tallow only sniffed.

"From the scullery side," the elf said. "The one that always smells of cold iron when the weather turns. If you had been listening to the house instead of your notebook, you would know it."

"I was listening."

"Mm."

Martha picked up the potato crate again. "Second bell after lights-out. Old Charms storeroom, the one off lower west. Don't be late."

She started for the portrait, then stopped and looked back. Her voice, when she spoke, was lighter on purpose. "And do try not to bring that face with you."

"What face?"

"That one. The one that says every other person's emergency is interrupting a timetable only you can see."

He opened his mouth, shut it again, and Martha went through the portrait before he found an answer that was not either defensive or true.

Tallow resumed sorting spoons. The kitchen noise filled back in around the gap she had left: pans shifting, water hissing on hot iron, some distant rattle from the scullery where another elf was losing patience with a tray.

Owen put his hand over the notebook until the page stopped looking exposed. The bread had gone cooler. The line in the margin had not changed. South warming passage, fourth hinge from scullery arch.

He told himself he could do both. He could help with the corridor. He could use the corridor. He could keep the amendment narrow. He could keep everyone moving.

That was the sort of sentence the Leaves liked hearing before they proved him wrong.

* * *

The old Charms storeroom had not held charms equipment in years. It held broken desks, two boxes of warped copybooks, a coat stand missing three hooks, and eight students trying not to breathe loudly.

Neville stood in the middle of them with a bit of chalk and the back of a cabinet door laid across two chairs. He had the corridor drawn on it in blunt white lines: one turn from the stair, the lavatory, the disused classroom, the fruit portrait passage. He looked more tired every time Owen saw him now, not in a noble way, only in the way of somebody whose sleep kept getting stolen by practical failures. There was a yellowing bruise along his jaw.

"Again," Neville said as Owen came in. "We're not here to win a war. We're here to get Hester and the two first-years out of a stupid place before Amycus finds them there. If anything goes wrong, the younger ones go first and the rest of us become scenery."

One of the younger ones, a second-year girl with one mitten safety-pinned to her sleeve so she would not lose it, raised her hand without waiting to be asked. "What if the scenery's already on fire?"

Martha, crouched by a canvas bag of junk at the wall, said, "Then run round it, Hester."

Hester nodded as if this was useful and final.

Beside her a first-year boy in a jumper two sizes too big kept smoothing his fringe down with spit and failing to make it stay. The third child, another first-year, was holding his wand and a wrapped sandwich in the same hand because he seemed unable to decide which mattered more. His spectacles had been repaired with thread so dark it looked almost blue.

Len, lanky and hollow-eyed, kept winding and unwinding a bit of string round his wrist until the skin went pink. By the door Jo worried at the sticking-plaster round both thumbs with her teeth, then caught herself and shoved her hands into her sleeves.

Owen had not expected sandwiches. That, more than anything, made the thing feel like Martha's work as much as Neville's.

Neville tapped the chalk map. "Len, stop worrying that string and trip the left-hand armor when Amycus comes round from the stair. Hester, you pull the spoon from the cistern brace only when Martha says, not before. Don't improve on the plan because you suddenly feel inspired. Martha takes the younger ones from the classroom across to the fruit portrait. Jo, keep those bandaged thumbs out of sight and stay on the portrait. Once they're through, everyone peels off separate."

He looked at Owen.

"East end," Neville said. "You keep the service press clear and the passage dark. If somebody comes up from the scullery side early, you cough twice and we all pretend we're idiots for another reason."

"Right," Owen said.

Neville held his stare for another beat, checking for whatever it was he had wanted from Owen back in the Room and had still not found. Then he went back to the cabinet door.

Martha lifted things out of the canvas bag and handed them round as she talked: a spoon bent nearly in half, string, a bottle of ink, three Filibuster scraps no bigger than cigarettes, a rag that smelled of vinegar, two rusted screws, one lump of soap shaved into slivers. It was not glamorous sabotage. It was school sabotage, the sort made by children with access to cupboards and grudges.

"The armor only needs to start moving," she said. "It doesn't need to kill anybody, so don't overdo it. The ink goes under the lavatory seat, not in the bowl. If it goes in the bowl, it just looks sad. Jo's got the portrait. Neville's got the stair. Owen's got the east end." She glanced up at him. "Have you got the east end?"

"Yes."

"Good."

The wrongness began before he left the storeroom. It was not much, only a pressure in the fillings of his teeth and a faint hum in the bones behind his ears. The lower west corridor was old enough, then. It was old in the useful way.

When they filed out two at a time and flattened themselves into the stone shadows, the corridor looked narrower than his memory of it. There were six doors in the space of thirty feet if he counted the lavatory stalls as doors, which he probably ought to have done sooner. The walls sweated faintly. One suit of armor stood by the lavatory corner with a dent in its breastplate and a halberd none of the original owners could have meant it to keep. Another stood opposite the service press at Owen's end, smaller and more decorative, its visor wired on years ago and badly polished.

The fourth arch from the scullery side smelled, exactly as Tallow had said, of cold iron. It was exactly the fourth arch. Owen stopped under it as if he were only checking sightlines. The service press set into the wall there was newer oak on older stone, the frame built into a seam that went down farther than the joinery had any right to know. Someone had patched the lower hinge in brass. The patch sat over scratch marks that were not accident.

His palm burn woke all the way.

Martha had gone to the far end with the two first-years. Neville was at the stair turn. Hester was kneeling by the cistern brace with the expression of a child trying very hard not to sneeze. Everyone else's attention was fixed where it ought to have been.

Owen took the notebook from inside his jumper and opened it against the wall. The corridor torchlight was bad for the older marks, but he had already copied what he needed. Three small signs, a narrower joining line, then the human note beside them so he would not lie to himself later about what he had intended: admit on first pressure, suppress complaint.

He should have stopped at the first sign.

He should have gone and stood where Neville had put him and done the stupid useful thing in front of him.

Instead he rubbed soot and tallow together with spit on the pad of his thumb, pressed the paste into the groove beneath the brass hinge-plate, and traced the signs no bigger than nail crescents. The stone answered at once, too quickly, with that deep unpleasant sense of a room taking notice.

He froze.

Nothing else happened yet.

From the far end Martha gave him the smallest possible jerk of the chin, telling him to get into position.

Owen slid the notebook away and put his fingers on the service press handle. It loosened under his hand so beautifully that for a fraction of a second he was stupid enough to feel pleased. There was no squeal and no resistance. The latch yielded like cloth.

Then, three doors down, another latch clicked.

He looked up.

The disused classroom door opposite opened itself an inch.

At the lavatory the nearest stall banged back hard enough to hit tile.

The little decorative armor at his shoulder lifted its visor on a wire snap that sounded exactly like a mouse trap going off.

Owen took his hand off the press. "Oh, for fuck's sake," he whispered.

* * *

The corridor did not explode all at once. It developed opinions.

The classroom door that had opened an inch decided an inch was cowardice and flung itself full wide. A mop bucket inside toppled over and rolled into the passage. Hester jerked round toward the sound, lost hold of the spoon brace in her other hand, and the lavatory cistern gave a liquid cough before she had touched it properly. Across from Owen, the service press yawned open to blackness. The small armor suit, offended by sudden passage on all sides, took one mincing step forward and discharged the Dungbombs wired into its greave directly into its own feet.

The smell hit half a second later.

"No," Owen said, already moving.

He reached for the classroom door. It shut under his hand, then sprang back open again as soon as he let go. The pressure in the stone thickened, spreading under the flags and up the frames. It was not one threshold. It was the whole run. Of course it was the whole run. The founder seam went uninterrupted under the corridor, and he had written into the line, not the hinge. Threshold was not a door. It was any made place where one condition passed into another: hinge, latch, visor, sluice, seat, bolt, valve. It was anything the castle understood as passage.

At the far end someone yelped. It was the younger ones. Owen turned just in time to see the disused classroom where Martha had hidden them spit its door open so hard the handle cracked the plaster. The boy with the thread-mended spectacles stumbled out clutching his sandwich. Behind him the boy in the too-big jumper slid in spilled water and nearly lost both shoes.

"Move," Martha snapped. "No, this way. This way, don't stand there looking at it."

Neville came off the stair in three strides. "Kids first. Go."

Then the toilets joined in.

Every cistern along the lavatory wall gave up on containment in the same ugly instant. Chains rattled against porcelain. Lids jumped. Brown-grey water surged over the back lip of the nearest one and slapped across the floor, carrying ink from the rigged seat with it in long purple-black fans. The stall doors slammed open and shut as if a dozen invisible people were too polite to pick one side. Somewhere behind tile a pipe knocked like somebody punching wood.

The large armor suit by the corner woke under Len's string before Len had pulled it, because the changed corridor had decided any suggestion of passage counted as enough. It lurched forward with murderous dignity, halberd lowering, and walked straight into the lavatory doorframe. The blow tore the halberd free, spun the suit sideways, and sent the cistern brace Hester had been meant to use skittering under Neville's feet.

For one impossible second the whole business was merely humiliating. It was filth, ink, armor, children in wet socks, and a corridor behaving like a drunk cupboard. One of the first-years laughed in pure fright and then burst into tears in the same breath. Hester, still on her knees, shouted, "I didn't pull it yet," as if anyone might still blame technique.

Then the second classroom door flew open and exposed the rest of the route.

Two third-years who had been waiting there with blankets and contraband bread stared into the corridor, stunned, their cover gone. One of them had flour on his sleeve from the kitchens. The other was barefoot and holding a tin of dittany against his chest like a rescued pet. Any hope of keeping the operation small vanished with the open doorway.

"Shut them," Neville said.

Owen had his wand out already. "Colloportus."

The spell came off the wand sideways, a thin spray of red sparks that stitched harmlessly across the opposite wall and knocked a framed fire notice crooked. His ordinary magic never failed in large dignified ways. It failed like this, embarrassingly, as if the wand were offended by the company it kept.

"Owen," Martha snapped.

"I know."

He dropped to one knee in the dirty water and went for the stone with his bare hand instead. A countermark might have worked if he had had a clear surface, if the corridor had been still, if First Art respected panic less than it did. The flagstones throbbed under his palm with the same sick answering pressure he had felt in the greenhouse and under the undercroft arch. There was too much motion and too many interrupted lines. His own pulse kept slipping into the shape.

He scratched the first half of a narrowing sign with his thumbnail. The service press beside him answered by swinging wider, and a rush of colder air breathed out of the dark beyond it. For a bright contemptible instant he could smell deep stone and old damp below the kitchens. The route had opened.

Martha had one arm round the crying first-year and the other shoved against the disused classroom door, trying to force it closed by body weight alone. "Neville, take Hester. Go. Go now."

Neville did not waste words on objection. He caught Hester by the sleeve, snatched the sandwich boy with his other hand, and drove them toward the fruit portrait. Len, swearing, wrestled the big armor suit by the halberd stump long enough to send it crashing into the opposite wall. The smaller decorative suit took another two furious ballet steps and planted both feet in Dungbomb sludge, where it remained vibrating with thwarted intent.

The corridor might still have been saved into something only ridiculous if Amycus had taken even thirty seconds longer.

He did not.

His boots hit the stair turn with speed enough to show he had been running. He came in half belted, robe collar crooked, one bootlace tucked into the top of his boot instead of tied. There were tiny shaving cuts on his jaw and the sweet rotten smell of drink under whatever mint he had sucked on to hide it. For one beat he simply stopped and looked.

The corridor looked back at him in sewage, ink, and cheap sabotage.

"What the fuck," he said, almost admiringly.

Then he lifted his wand and blasted the large armor suit apart.

Steel rang off stone. A greave skimmed past Owen's shoulder and hit the wall hard enough to take plaster with it. The first-year with the spectacles screamed. Martha shoved him bodily toward Neville's end and turned back because she was Martha and there were still too many bodies in the corridor.

"Run," she said to the third-years in the exposed room. "Now. Move."

Amycus saw her say it. That was enough.

He crossed the distance in four heavy strides and caught the back of her robe before she could turn. The cloth bit into her throat. She went backward, boots skidding in lavatory water, and hit the tiles on one hip so hard Owen felt it in his own teeth.

"You," Amycus said, delighted now. "There you are."

Martha twisted, got one foot under herself, and nearly made it. Amycus kicked the back of her knee. She went down again. The corridor seemed to recoil around the impact, doors still banging and water still spilling and all the while the actual human damage narrowing to a single body on stone.

Owen pushed up from the floor, filthy hand slipping on the flag. He could say it. He could say enough. Not all of it, not the Leaves, not the undercroft, but enough to pull Amycus's attention off her and onto the thing he had actually done.

He opened his mouth.

Amycus dragged Martha upright by her plait.

Owen did not speak.

* * *

Amycus held her there long enough for the corridor to quiet by degrees around them. One stall door still knocked. Water kept running somewhere behind the wall. From the other end came the muffled slap of the fruit portrait closing and the hard-breathed retreat of the children Neville had got out. The operation had shrunk to aftermath and selection, to deciding who would be left with the blame and who would not.

Martha's face had gone white under the wet. Strands of hair had pulled free from the plait and stuck across her mouth. She spat one away and looked straight at Amycus, not bravely exactly, only refusing to help him by pretending.

"Funny little mess," Amycus said. He was breathing hard through his nose. "Funny little rebels. Doors opening all over, piss everywhere, children out after curfew. You people do like making work."

He wrenched her head back by the hair and looked past her at the corridor as if the answer might be written on the wall. His gaze snagged on Owen.

"Phipps."

Owen did not move.

"Still here," Amycus said. "That was my first surprise tonight. Come on then. Use your eyes for once. Which one set it off?"

Owen's notebook felt enormous inside his jumper. The chalk paste under his nails had gone gritty with dirty water. He could still smell the colder air from the opened service press behind him, faint now under sewage and scorch. The route was still there. If he gave Amycus the whole thing, the route would go with it. The manuscript would go with it. The hidden work below the kitchens would go with it. Everything that mattered at the scale he had finally begun to understand would go with it.

Everything that mattered at the scale in front of him was in Amycus's fist.

"I don't know," Owen said.

Amycus barked a laugh. "You don't know. You were standing in the middle of it."

"I saw the armor go first."

It was the most literal answer. It was also true.

Amycus's face changed, not into belief but into the special contempt reserved for students who imagined truth could be arranged like cutlery. He let go of Martha's hair only to hit her across the mouth with the back of his hand. Her head snapped sideways. Blood appeared immediately, thin and startlingly bright against all the dirty water.

"Wrong answer," he said.

Owen flinched half a step without meaning to. Amycus saw and enjoyed it.

"Ah," he said softly. "So you do know something."

Martha made a short rough sound that might have been a laugh if it had not come through blood. "He doesn't know his own timetable half the time."

Amycus turned and drove her down to her knees with a hand on the shoulder. Broken porcelain from one of the cistern lids crackled under her robes. She sucked air through her teeth but did not cry out. He set his boot on her left hand, not hard enough yet to break anything, only enough to make the threat precise.

"Let's try the girl," he said. "Who else is here?"

"No one."

He put more weight on the hand. Owen heard the tiny ugly grind of skin against porcelain before he heard Martha's breath go wrong.

"Who else."

"No one."

Amycus looked up at Owen again as if inviting him into the lesson. The corridor light shone wetly on his shaving cuts. "See, Phipps, this is why everybody hates group work. One stupid child decides to decorate the school and then another one has to explain it with her fingers."

Owen said nothing.

He could feel the pressure of the old line under the flags, still faintly active, still waiting for a cleaner command. If he dropped to the stone again and tried to change it, he would do it under Amycus's eyes with no preparation and no guarantee except that the castle was already wounded enough to answer too loudly. If he confessed, Amycus would have the corridor and then, very quickly, anything else Owen had touched. If he did neither, Martha would be the one taken.

He chose neither loudly enough for anyone to mistake it. He simply stayed where he was.

Martha's eyes found his for one second. There was pain in them, and fury, and an exhausted disbelief that was somehow worse than either. Then Amycus hauled her up again and the look broke.

"Right," Amycus said. "You can be tomorrow's example. We've all earned that, haven't we."

He shoved her toward the stair. She caught herself on the wall with her good hand, smearing blood and dirty water over the stone. Amycus yanked her onward by the arm.

"Anybody I find out after curfew from this minute," he called over his shoulder, "can join her and bring their own teeth in a bag."

The threat ran down the corridor into the walls. Then his boots and Martha's stumbling steps receded toward the turn and were gone.

For a moment nobody moved.

The little armor suit finally settled with a metallic sigh. Water dripped from the broken cistern in fat slow taps. Owen could hear people breathing in the classroom, behind the wall, inside the whole damaged hush of the thing.

Neville came back first from the portrait passage, wet to the knees and breathing through his mouth. He took in the corridor in one sweep, ending on Owen. Len appeared behind him carrying the tin of dittany that had survived somehow. Hester, pale and furious at herself, clutched her pinned mitten with the free hand like it might keep the rest of her attached.

"The little ones are down," Neville said. He looked at the blood on the wall, then at Owen again. "Was that you?"

Owen knew better than to ask which part.

"Part of it," he said.

Len swore under his breath.

Neville's face did not change much. That was the worst of it. If he had shouted, Owen might have answered back, or at least known where the line was. Instead Neville nodded once, as if a piece on a board had moved exactly as its shape required.

"For what?" Neville asked.

Owen said, "I was opening the press."

"For us."

The opened service press behind him breathed cold from the dark.

Owen did not answer in time.

Neville looked away first. "Right."

That single word carried more rearrangement than a speech would have. He crouched by the broken cistern, snapped a bit of plaster off the floor, and pressed it to the running crack as if damage still deserved practical attention even in the middle of betrayal.

"Len, get the wall. Hester, help him. Jo can come back up for the bucket. Nobody says press, sabotage, corridor, any of it if they're spoken to." He stood and faced Owen again. "And you don't come near another job of mine if you can't tell which side of it you're on."

Martha's blood was drying already in a crooked handprint where she had caught herself.

Owen said, because it was weak and because it was true, "I didn't mean this."

Neville's mouth went tight with something flatter than anger. "No. That's the problem."

He turned away and started giving people tasks. Hester fetched rags from the classroom. Len scraped soot and dung off the armor's decorative foot with the disgust of somebody cleaning up after a dog he did not own. Jo slipped back through the portrait with a bucket and would not meet Owen's eye. The corridor filled with work, quick and low-voiced and competent.

No one asked Owen to do any of it.

He stood by the still-open press with dirty water drying on his socks and the notebook heavy under his jumper while the others mopped, lifted, wiped, hid, and repaired around him. The cold from the service dark kept touching his ankle. Somewhere down the stair Amycus shouted, and farther off than that, almost below hearing, Martha made no sound at all.

By the time the last of them went back down to the kitchens in twos and threes, nobody had asked Owen to carry so much as a bucket.
