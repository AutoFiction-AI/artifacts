# Chapter 10

Cold pulled Leela the rest of the way awake. June's bare foot was gone from her ankle; sometime before dawn she'd kicked the duvet halfway off, and the strip of hotel sheet across Leela's thigh felt damp where sweat had dried after the sex and then cooled under the vent. When she moved, the skin along the outside of her hip reminded her about the carpet. From the bathroom came toothbrush noise, then running water, then June clearing her throat like she resented even that.

On the desk, both laptops were open where they'd gone dead in the middle of the night. June's still displayed the thumbnail pane of `ruderal_pitch_v3.pdf`, frozen at a slide with a blue-orange benchmark plot that Leela knew too well now to look at casually. Yesterday, in the bad fluorescent hallway outside Ballroom C, finding the deck had felt like one of those conference accidents people later retold as fate. This morning it looked uglier and more ordinary. It was a PDF with a date stamp, a thing that would either survive contact with policy or get rinsed down to misunderstanding.

The bathroom door opened. June came out in yesterday's T-shirt and hotel socks, her blunt-cut hair shoved back with wet fingers. There was a bright scrape on her collarbone that Leela knew she had made. June saw her see it and did not pull the shirt up.

"You're awake," June said.

Leela pushed herself up against the headboard. "Barely."

"Good." June picked up the room-service notepad from the nightstand, tore off the page with the breakfast menu printed on the back, and sat cross-legged at the foot of the bed. "Let's figure out whether this is real or whether he's just such a fucking parasite that everything feels criminal by default."

That was June's version of not mentioning last night.

Leela reached for her laptop. The plastic was cold. "I thought we already knew it was real."

"We know it feels real." June clicked the trackpad hard enough to make the loose hotel desk shiver. "I want the part that would still be real if Gloria looked at it with that dean face she does. The calm one. The one that means you're about to be turned into process."

Leela almost smiled, then didn't. The phrase had the right cruelty in it.

June enlarged slide eight until the caption fuzzed. The plot compared four model variants across municipal complaint datasets, the exact chart June had spent most of October trying to stabilize after the seminar disaster. Mohit had put it on a white background with Ruderal's green logo in the corner and a title that read `Reliability Under Distribution Shift for Public-Sector Decision Pipelines`. He had shaved off the caveats and the entire point of the work with one line of copy.

"This y-axis," June said. She tapped the screen with her nail. "I stopped calling it failure rate on October third. After the seminar. He said failure sounded defensive."

"You changed it to worst-case transfer error."

"Yeah." June opened Finder, then a folder called `baltimore_talk`, then a graveyard of versions. `v2`, `v2_fix`, `v4_REAL`, `v4_REAL2`, `v5_use_this`, `v5_use_this_final`, `v5_use_this_finalforreal`. The naming alone made Leela's chest hurt with recognition. June clicked through them until an earlier export appeared. Same plot, same colors, same heavy blue line. Same y-axis label as the deck.

"There." June rotated the screen toward her. "October second, 11:48 p.m. The old label. And see the gray band? I took that out after Omar said the bootstrap intervals made the variance look cleaner than it was."

Leela leaned in. June smelled like hotel soap over sweat and nicotine gum. The gray band was in Mohit's deck too, translucent enough to miss if you were not looking for it.

"He didn't recreate the figure," Leela said. "He lifted this version or something exported from this version."

"From my laptop?"

"Maybe from the shared drive. Maybe from an attachment. Maybe from somewhere older."

June looked at her. "Older how?"

Leela held the look for half a beat too long and turned back to the deck. She knew older how. She knew there was a private directory on Hades with cloned repositories and Ruderal nested under it like rot under paint. She also knew she had kept that knowledge to herself in September, then only handed Omar the softest corner of it in October, and that last night had not made her cleaner.

"Older than Baltimore," she said. "This didn't start at the conference."

June kept looking. Leela could feel it. Then June let it go, or parked it.

"Fine," June said. "Let's keep making him a timeline."

They moved slide by slide.

Slide eleven used one of Leela's robustness plots from the paper she had submitted in September. Mohit had retitled it `Adversarial Resilience With Minimal Retraining Cost`, which would have been funny if it were not her work and not a lie. The tell there was smaller: the legend still said `budgeted perturbation`, a phrase Mohit himself had later crossed out in red on a printout because he thought it sounded "too defensive, too academic." In the deck, the old wording remained, along with the slight dip in the orange line from a failed rerun she had fixed later.

June noticed it a second after Leela did. "That yours?"

"Yeah."

"Can you date it?"

Leela clicked into the folder she'd synced locally before Research Integrity had frozen her access. Her cache was incomplete. A few filenames had the small cloud icon that meant they now lived somewhere Haviland controlled and she did not. Others had actually made it onto the laptop. She found the September draft, then an August export she had forgotten existed. There it was: the same legend, and below it the same small asymmetry in the orange line. She had flattened it later. Mohit's deck had not.

"August twenty-ninth," she said.

June did not look at the date stamp first. She looked at Leela. "August?"

"Yeah."

"That isn't conference scavenging."

"No."

"It's before Baltimore. Before the seminar even finished blowing back on us."

Leela kept her eyes on the screen.

June hooked two fingers under the collar of her T-shirt and tugged it sideways just enough to show the scrape on her collarbone and the darker mark lower on her shoulder. "Then don't do this to me."

Leela looked up. "Do what."

"Don't fuck me on the carpet and come back at daylight acting like I'm cleared for the edited version." June's voice stayed low. That made it worse. "You left marks. If you know this goes back further, stop talking to me like I'm a committee."

"I'm not cleaning it up."

"No? Because from here it looks like you want the deck, the theft, and last night in three different folders."

The hit landed because it was exact. Leela said, "Last night wasn't separate."

"Then say what it was."

Anger moved first in Leela, then the harder thing behind it. "It was me wanting you. It was me being angry enough to stop pretending that standing next to you counted as neutrality. It was not me deciding Haviland would suddenly hear us correctly."

June held her gaze. "And the rest?"

Leela looked back at the plot. "I know this didn't start in Baltimore."

"How?"

"Not in a form I can use yet." The answer sounded evasive even to her. She made herself keep going. "If I tell it wrong before I can prove it, he gets to fold the deck, the theft, and us into one story about bad boundaries and unstable people."

June's mouth flattened. Hurt moved across her face, then calculation over it. "Fine," she said. "You get an hour. After that, no more careful half-truths to my face. We either build something real or we stop pretending last night made us allies."

Leela clicked `Get Info` on the PDF itself. She had not done it yesterday because by the time they'd found the deck they had both been too angry to manage the parts of anger that required menus. The properties window opened.

Created: November 12, 2025, 2:14 a.m.  
Modified: November 12, 2025, 7:06 a.m.  
Author: Mohit Saran.

June read over her shoulder. "He skipped my prep slot because he was up till two building this thing."

"Or editing it."

"He left me out there to take that panel question by myself because he was polishing seed-copy."

The hotel's vent hit the same register as the panel-room mic, and the room snapped back into Leela's ears: the squeal, the polite throat-clearing before the question, June saying thank you with her teeth set hard enough to count as a bite. Yesterday she had thought the worst part of Baltimore was the talk landing flat after all the work June had put into recovering from October. This was somehow worse. It converted rudeness into allocation. Mohit had been elsewhere on purpose.

June reached past her and scrolled to the title slide. `RUDERAL ANALYTICS`. White type. Green weed-shoot logo, tasteful enough to look expensive. Underneath, smaller: `Decision infrastructure that survives the real world.`

"He thinks he's clever," June said. "Ruderal. Weed that grows through concrete. Real fucking subtle."

Leela searched the properties window for something more than the author field. There was nothing useful beyond the creation data: Keynote, Mohit's name, and not enough.

"This still isn't the thing we forward," she said.

June gave her a look that was half warning now, half invitation to be precise. "Because?"

"Because if Ruderal only exists inside his file, he calls it exploratory bullshit. Faculty show unpublished slides to potential partners all the time. He says Haviland owns the work. He says we found a rough deck and panicked."

"And if Ruderal exists outside the deck."

"Then it stops being bad boundaries and starts being a paper trail." Leela touched the logo with one finger. "If there's an actual company behind that name, the filing date matters more than this PDF. A state record is harder to talk over."

June looked at the green sprig again. The meanness in her face settled into something flatter and more useful. Then she dragged the notepad back. "Okay," she said. "Then we make a list."

She wrote in block letters that looked almost architectural when she was pissed:

`1. SOURCE FILES / EXPORT DATES`  
`2. PDF METADATA / VERSION HISTORY`  
`3. WHAT THE FUCK RUDERAL ACTUALLY IS`  
`4. KEEP OFF Haviland systems`

Under the fourth line she drew so hard the pen scored the paper.

Leela said, "We should probably not talk about it on lab Slack."

"We should probably not say the word out loud anywhere in Keating," June said. "Honestly we should maybe not say anything in that building that we don't want attached to a faculty meeting agenda."

The room had gotten brighter without warming. November Baltimore light, thin and metallic, sat on the dresser mirror and the empty plastic water cups and the heap of June's clothes by the desk chair. June reached for the coffee Leela had let go cold, made a face, and drank it anyway.

"Do we tell Omar?" she asked.

Leela thought of Omar's careful face, of how badly he needed any information that might explain Mohit's shifting cruelty, of how clean it would feel to widen the circle, and of how every widened circle in this lab had become leverage for someone else.

"Not yet," she said. "Not until we know what we're saying."

June nodded once, not agreeing so much as storing the decision for later argument. Then she stood, stretched, and winced very slightly when her shoulder rolled back.

Leela had left a bruise there too.

June caught her looking and said, "Don't start apologizing with your eyes. We have checkout in forty minutes."

The line came barbed, but it sounded more like June than the silence had. Leela took it.

By the time they left the room, the notepad page was folded into June's back pocket and the deck existed in three places that were not Mohit's laptop. The outline of a plan had started to harden. Panic had finally been forced into sequence.

* * *

Lakebridge had turned from bright November to damp November while they were gone. The sidewalks between the station and campus held a skin of old rain that kept trying to become ice but had not committed. By Monday afternoon, Keating carried damp coats, radiator heat, and the basement's usual dust-baked breath. Students had lined boots under radiators that never quite dried them. Somewhere below, the server closet whined through the floor.

Leela found June in the ground-floor coffee alcove, under the bulletin board where half the flyers had curled off the cork from humidity. Ben was already there.

He stood by the industrial urn in a cream parka that probably cost half a month of Leela's rent, stirring cinnamon into a jar of overnight oats gone gray from chia seeds. He had managed to snap the bowl off the plastic spoon and was using the handle like a dowel, concentrating on the task with the earnest seriousness of someone who still believed adulthood might be a series of correct purchases.

When he saw them, he brightened too fast. "Hey. Welcome back. I was actually going to email, which is annoying, I know, but Mohit wanted everybody from Baltimore to dump materials into the shared drive today just so the external versions are aligned. Not, like, urgent urgent. Just cleaner."

June took a paper cup from the stack without looking at him. "Aligned with what?"

Ben's eyes flickered once. "Just, you know, if anybody has handouts or sponsor-facing things or slide exports. There was some confusion after the side meetings."

"The side meetings you weren't at?" June said.

He laughed a little, the sound of someone checking whether laughter still counted as charm. "No, right, obviously. I just mean that's what he said. About avoiding version noise."

Leela poured coffee. The coffee was burnt, bitter, and thin. Ben always made her feel the full age gap in strange directions: sometimes older than he was, sometimes much younger, like he had skipped straight from being the kind of undergraduate professors remembered fondly into performing the grad student he thought faculty wanted. His hair was still damp at the temples from the cold outside. A pharmacy receipt stuck out of his pocket with the top line folded over and over by his thumb.

June said, "We'll manage our own files."

"Totally." Ben nodded too many times. "Totally. I just figured I should flag it because he sounded pretty specific about provenance, and if there are, like, multiple unofficial exports floating around, people can get weird."

There was that word again: `Provenance`. Mohit had it now too.

Ben went back to his oats with furious concentration, stirring the gray chia sludge until the spoon handle clicked the jar. June blew across the coffee and said, quietly enough that only Leela heard, "That's new."

Leela did not answer until they were in the stairwell, the concrete walls amplifying their footsteps. "The word?"

"The whole sentence. He sounds like a junior admin memo."

"He always sounded like that."

"Not at eight in the morning with breakfast slime in a jar."

On the sixth floor the graduate office was half full and therefore unusable. Two first-years from another group were arguing about package versions at the long table. Someone had opened the lake-facing window a crack and then regretted it, so the room held both radiator heat and hard outside air at once. Leela led June past it to the narrower alcove by the printer, where the lake came through the glass in flat steel strips between buildings.

"This is still too public," June said.

"It's two minutes."

"That's how people get caught in documentaries."

"We're not in a documentary."

June looked at the cubicle farm, the half-dead fern on the filing cabinet, the whiteboard with three layers of erased marker staining through one another. "We're in a worse thing. We're in a university."

Leela opened her laptop anyway and tethered it through her phone instead of touching campus Wi-Fi.

They searched `Ruderal Analytics` first and got a landscaping company in Ohio, a weed-management startup in Colorado, and a venture blog using the word metaphorically. June muttered through all of it, impatient enough that the trackpad clicks started sounding like a small mechanical weapon. Then Leela narrowed the search with Lakebridge, then New York business records, then Mohit's full name. On the third try the result appeared.

`RUDERAL ANALYTICS LLC`  
Domestic limited liability company  
Filed: August 14, 2025  
Principal office: Water Street, Lakebridge  
Managing member: Mohit Saran

The office address was a coworking suite above a wine bar Leela had passed a dozen times without remembering it. The filing agent used a service address in Albany. There was no Haviland affiliation anywhere on the page. That omission did more than a logo could have done. The page stripped away every academic euphemism: no translational outreach, no lab-side exploratory shell, just Mohit naming himself and filing the lie where the state would treat it as a structure.

June read the page once, then again. "August," she said.

Leela nodded.

"Before the semester."

"Yeah."

"Before he told all of us the lab had to toughen up because the center bid died."

"Yeah."

June put her hand flat on the desk and leaned into it, not dramatic, just containing force. "So he reorganized the whole lab after he already had a company."

Leela heard the sentence click several prior chapters into alignment inside her own head: the summer loss, the adversarial pairings, the contradictory promises, the abrupt obsession with "robustness" not as research question but as brand. She had known pieces. Knowing the filing date gave the pieces edges.

"He didn't pivot because things got hard," June said. "He pivoted because he already had somewhere else to take the work."

Before Leela could answer, a shadow crossed the printer glass. Ben leaned around the divider holding a pastry box from somewhere far nicer than campus coffee.

"Oh, sorry," he said, already continuing. "I didn't mean to interrupt. The faculty candidate breakfast had leftovers, and I thought people up here might want them before they get murdered by air." He lifted the box a little. Inside, three croissants and something glazed sat on parchment paper. "Also Mohit asked if either of you still had the November sponsor deck, because he wants to make sure nothing from Baltimore is being read out of context."

June's head came up so sharply it made Ben take one step back.

"The what?" she said.

Ben blinked. "The sponsor deck. Or side-meeting deck. I don't know what he called it exactly. He just said there were exploratory materials and he didn't want weird circulation."

He had heard something, or seen something, or Mohit had sent him to sweep the hallway for heat. It did not matter which.

Leela said, carefully, "Why would he ask you that?"

Ben opened and closed the pastry box once, buying himself time with the rustle of parchment. "He didn't, like, deputize me. He just mentioned it when I dropped off the visitor badges. I thought if you had it and there was some confusion, better to clean it up early than let it become a whole thing."

June laughed, low and ugly. "You thought that."

Ben's face changed. The performance fell a fraction and the twenty-four under it showed. "I'm trying to be useful," he said, then grimaced slightly, hearing the sentence land in Mohit's voice instead of his own. "I'm trying not to have everyone act like I'm radioactive every time I open my mouth."

There it was, human and not strategic. The admission sounded needier than slick. He wanted in. He wanted the room to stop punishing him for trying to belong to the wrong person too visibly. In that naked bid for approval, Leela saw how Mohit must have built the habit in him: praise one errand, one forwarded screenshot, one careful hallway update at a time, until reporting ceased to feel like reporting and started feeling like competence.

June did not soften. "Then stop carrying his weather for him."

Ben stared. "What?"

"Nothing," Leela said before June could keep going. "We're working."

Ben lingered there, hurt and uncertain and still somehow eager to recover. "Okay," he said. "Anyway. Pastries are here if you want them."

He set the box on the filing cabinet and left with the same quick, front-loaded walk he brought to every errand, a gait that made him seem always a little late to the adulthood he was auditioning for.

June waited until his footsteps had vanished into the office noise. "He said November sponsor deck."

Leela nodded.

"I never said sponsor. I never said November. Did you?"

"No."

"Then how did he know it wasn't just random Baltimore trash?"

Leela closed the business-records tab and opened nothing in its place. Her coffee had gone lukewarm again. Below them a stairwell door banged and the office printer started its dry mechanical coughing. The whole building sounded exposed.

"He heard enough," she said. "Or Mohit asked enough."

"Same outcome."

Leela's phone buzzed on the desk.

The sound stopped both of them cold.

Then she turned it over.

It was Mohit.

`Good to have you both back. Quick reminder that exploratory external materials tied to ongoing lab work are not for circulation outside approved channels. Easy for context to get lost. Happy to discuss later today if helpful.`

June read it over her shoulder. "There. Done. He felt the temperature change."

Leela looked past the screen to the pastry box Ben had left behind. One croissant had shed a confetti of flakes onto the wax paper. The glaze on the danish had already started to set cloudy in the office air.

"He's not subtle enough to be a mole," June said.

"No."

"He's an app notification with shoes."

Despite everything, Leela almost laughed. June pressed on before the laugh could land and drain the room.

"We can't talk in here anymore," she said. "Not this, not Omar, not anything remotely like a noun."

Leela nodded. Her body had already agreed before her mind finished the sentence. The sixth floor, the basement lab, the coffee alcove, the hallway outside Mohit's office where people used to debrief after meetings like they were still in one shared story: by evening each place seemed built for the same second task, watching your mouth and lying clean.

"We also can't use university storage," she said. "If he starts asking about circulation, he's already thinking about access logs."

June's eyes cut to her. "You think he can see that much?"

Leela thought of the Hades audit trails, of what system administrators could see, of the fact that she no longer had privileges and Mohit still did. "I think we should behave like he can."

"Great." June picked up the notepad page she had tucked into her laptop sleeve at the hotel and flattened it on the desk between them. "Rules, then."

Her pen moved fast.

`No lab Slack.`  
`No Haviland drive.`  
`No talking in Keating unless it's fake.`  
`Tell Omar only in person, off campus.`  
`Keep copies on personal machines.`  
`Assume Ben reports vibe, if not content.`

Under that she wrote, in smaller letters: `Find prior deck versions if they exist.`

Leela said, "We should keep a timeline too. Filing date. Deck creation. Slide export dates. The conference schedule. His missed prep slot."

"Yeah." June handed her the pen. "You do the depressing part."

Leela wrote:

`08/14/25 Ruderal filed`  
`08/29/25 Leela plot export w/ old legend`  
`10/02/25 June fig export w/ old label`  
`11/12/25 deck created 2:14 a.m.`  
`11/12/25 Mohit misses prep / off-program meeting`

The act of writing it by hand, on cheap hotel paper in a sixth-floor alcove that smelled faintly of toner and radiator dust, made the dates feel less like impressions and more like a structure one could lean weight against.

June looked down at the list. "He didn't get sloppy," she said. "He got greedy."

"Greedy looks sloppy after enough timestamps."

June folded the page once, then again. "We should tell Omar tonight."

"At your place?"

"Not mine first. Too obvious. The noodle place on Alder, then upstairs if needed."

Leela nodded. The plan made immediate sense because it assumed the old habits were dead. They would not loop Omar in casually between machines anymore. They would not do hallway check-ins after lab meeting. If they needed to say a real sentence, they would go downhill to Alder and pay for a bowl of noodles first.

June snapped her laptop shut. "Come on."

"Where?"

"Outside. Before Professor Helpful comes back with a fresh bulletin."

They took the stairs instead of the elevator. On the landing between third and second, Leela heard voices below and June stopped with one hand on the rail, listening. The building gave them its usual layered sounds: pipe knock, distant laughter, the metal hush of the HVAC turning over. June waited through all of it before moving again, as if any one of those noises might separate into a name.

They exited through the side door near the loading dock where the smokers stood in all weather. No one was there now. The air hit hard and damp. Across the alley the brick wall of the next building sweated dark streaks. June tucked her hands into the pockets of her coat and looked back at Keating with plain distrust now. The basement windows showed nothing but fluorescent blur.

"I keep thinking about how normal this would've looked a year ago," she said. "Ben hovering, Mohit doing side meetings, deck versions everywhere. I would've just called it bad boundaries and gone home."

Leela zipped her coat higher. "It was bad boundaries."

"Yeah." June looked at her. "And now it's evidence."

They did not touch. Last night stayed in the alley with them anyway, mixed now with filing dates and deck versions. If they wanted either thing to survive contact with Mohit, they would have to separate the facts by hand.

Leela said, "If we do this, we do it on purpose. No blurting. No hoping the right person will happen to care."

June's mouth tightened in something that was almost approval. "I know."

Leela waited a beat, then said the harder part. "And if Ben asks, we don't punish him with honesty until we know what we need from him."

June looked back toward the building. "That's a very ugly sentence."

"It's true."

"It's also true that I want to shake him until his fillings fall out."

"I know."

June breathed steam into the wet cold and rubbed it away with the back of her glove. "Fine. We treat him like a leak, not a villain."

"Yeah."

"And Mohit?"

Leela thought of the August filing date, the 2:14 a.m. timestamp, the old exports, and the polite text on her phone that had arrived minutes after Ben's interruption, already dressed in institutional language about context and approved channels. She thought of the rooms Mohit preferred: the office, the conference suite, the committee table, every place where composure could do violence without leaving visible marks. She thought too of the thing she still had not told June, the older proof sitting in the dark behind this newer one like a second foundation.

"Mohit," she said, "we treat him like somebody who's already started building his explanation."

June considered that and nodded. "Okay."

For the first time all day, the answer held still long enough to sound like a plan.

They stood there another moment in the alley cold, not touching. Traffic hissed on the wet street beyond the engineering buildings. Somewhere inside Keating a door alarm chirped and shut off. June pulled the folded note from her pocket, looked at it once more, and then tucked it into the inside compartment of her wallet behind an old MetroCard and three receipts.

"Tonight at seven," she said. "Text me only if you get delayed. Nothing else."

Leela said, "Okay."

June started walking toward the immigrant-commercial strip without waiting to see if Leela followed, confident now in a way that had nothing to do with optimism. It was simply direction. Leela fell in beside her after a step, matching pace.

Behind them, Keating kept humming, fluorescent and server-hot and falsely neutral. June's folded note rode in the clear wallet slot behind an old MetroCard and three receipts. Leela kept Mohit's text in her coat pocket, the phone warming against her palm, and kept the older secret where she had kept it for months, behind her teeth. At the corner, both of them turned downhill toward Alder without discussing it.
