# Chapter 15

Blue, salted morning leaked through the blinds and across the defense folder on Leela's desk while the radiator hammered the pipe behind it. The noise had started at six and never settled back into mere noise. She had given up on sleep, shoved the blanket aside, and crossed the studio barefoot, the floorboards cold enough to bite. Her right hand had curled during the night. She pulled the wrist brace tighter with her left, listening to the Velcro rasp in the dark.

The folder held exactly what she had left there: dissertation abstract, chapter summaries, projected timeline, the one-page defense calendar she had built and rebuilt until every week between March and June looked occupied by progress instead of panic. She had printed the calendar on better paper than the rest because the department copier made ordinary sheets feel temporary. If today went the way it was supposed to go, she would email the graduate coordinator by four, hold a room for late April, and stop opening the graduate-housing portal just to stare at the line about summer vacancy. If today went well, the credit-card minimum on the eighteenth would remain what it had been for months, a nuisance rather than a referendum. If today went well, the money her mother had sent in November could stay filed under bridge instead of beginning to look like subsidy.

She boiled water for coffee she did not really want, then drank it because her body no longer recognized the difference between ritual and appetite. The mug warmed her palm through the chipped glaze. She ate half a banana gone soft at the stem and swallowed an antacid that turned to chalk at the back of her tongue. Her phone, faceup beside the sink, gave her nothing from June. The last exchange sat there from two nights earlier: `committee tomorrow at two, unless he moves it again` from Leela, then silence long enough to count as its own sentence.

Omar had written at 7:12.

`Good luck. Text when you're out.`

That was all. It was the correct amount. Anything larger from him would have turned into strategy or apology, and he could not afford either one before noon. She typed `thanks` and deleted it. She typed `Will do` and deleted that too. In the end she locked the phone and put it in her bag unanswered. Leaving the message in its neat, limited kindness felt safer than touching it.

She dressed the way she always dressed when she needed people to mistake endurance for order: black jeans, clean socks, the good blouse she saved for committee rooms, the navy blazer she had bought for Baltimore and never quite trusted as armor, small gold studs she had not thought about since Diwali. Her hair was too oily to leave loose, so she braided it fast and clipped the tail up. Concealer softened the purple under her eyes without removing it. There were limits to what concealer and ten minutes could do. She slid the folder into her tote, added her laptop, charger, a tube of lip balm, the orange extension cord she carried out of habit, and the protein bar that had been living in the bag long enough to qualify as emergency planning rather than food.

By the time she stepped outside, the sky over Lakebridge had flattened into that white winter color that made every building look overlit from above. The walkway behind the library was striped with old salt and the damp black prints of boots. Wet cold worked itself up through the soles of her shoes. She walked fast enough to heat her face and not fast enough to call it nerves. Keating sat ahead of her in its usual sealed-off ugliness, all concrete confidence and dark windows that treated damp air and consequence alike as things to keep outside.

What kept returning was how calm Mohit had been all week, not kind or generous or even conciliatory, just calm. He had replied to her slide deck within nine minutes on Monday with three copyedits and one note about framing the contribution more independently. He had smiled at her in the lab on Tuesday and asked whether she had enough time. Yesterday he had sent `Looking forward to a constructive conversation.` He had not once tried to rattle her, which meant he no longer needed the warm-up.

The elevator to the sixth floor smelled faintly of wet wool and somebody else's breakfast sandwich. Two undergrads got in at three with a plastic prototype between them, a hacked-together drone arm or lamp or something equally sure it would matter. One of them held the thing wrapped in his scarf so the epoxy would not stick to the cardboard box. They looked at Leela's folder and laptop and slid politely to make room. She wondered, not for the first time, how old twenty-two had begun to look. Less like youth every year and more like unspent time.

The committee room was smaller than the word committee deserved. A narrow oval table, six chairs, a wall monitor that never centered anything properly, one window facing the lake and offering only a flat sheet of gray light. Burnt coffee, radiator dust, and the sweet chemical wipe somebody had used on the table hung in the air. Gloria Fisk was already there with her permanent paper folder and a cardigan the color of old oyster shells under her blazer. Up close, as always, she looked less reassuring than she did in departmental newsletters. Her skin had the flattened fatigue of somebody who had spent the morning turning crises into agenda items.

"Leela," she said. "We're just getting set up."

"Thanks for making time," Leela said, because any committee started with that kind of sentence or not at all.

Professor Alan Weiss sat two chairs down, uncapping a fountain pen and capping it again with the practiced impatience of a man already stuck in a waiting room. The side of his middle finger was stained green from the ink. He taught systems and liked to ask questions that sounded curious until the third clause, when they hardened. Opposite him sat Professor Elena Reyes from information science, who kept tucking the end of a red scarf back under her jacket collar even though the space was overheated. She had set a little tin of ginger lozenges next to her notebook and arranged them in a line neat enough to suggest either discipline or a need not to think with her hands.

Leela plugged in her laptop. The wall monitor mirrored her first slide crooked, then corrected itself. Title, subtitle, her name, date. The simple institutional holiness of it almost made her laugh. She had spent six years converting the most humiliating parts of her life into deck format. Objectives, methods, limitations, next steps. She had once believed that was adulthood.

Mohit came in last.

He did not look rushed. He had his coat folded over one arm and a thick paper packet under the other, cream stock clipped with a black binder clip. His glasses flashed once when he turned toward the window. He apologized for traffic between buildings though he had clearly walked over from the office downstairs, then set the packets in front of each chair with the gentle, distributive competence of a man handing out an agenda for a donor lunch.

"I wanted to circulate a little supplementary context before we start," he said. "Nothing dramatic. I just thought it would help ground the discussion."

Leela looked down at the cover page.

PRE-DEFENSE REVIEW: SUPPLEMENTAL MATERIALS FOR COMMITTEE CONSIDERATION.

Her own name sat underneath in departmental serif.

No one moved, but the air around the table cinched tight. It was too immaculate. That was the first insult. Not even stapled crooked or rushed off the printer. He had tabs on the side: scholarship, provenance, compliance. On the second page she could already see the header from the Research Integrity notice that had suspended her server privileges in October. Under compliance, in bold, the phrase unresolved data-handling concerns.

She had not been asked for a response memo. She had not been warned there would be materials beyond her dissertation summary and slides. She had been told pre-defense, which in Haviland language meant developmental, collegial, a room full of people finding a procedural way to say yes while protecting themselves from blame if your final draft later embarrassed them.

Mohit sat down and gave her the same small smile he used on first-year admits and visitors from industry. It showed no teeth and all the damage.

"Why don't you go ahead," Gloria said.

Leela did.

The first minute was all mechanics. Clicker in left hand because the brace made her right thumb clumsy. Slide advance late by half a beat. Her own voice too dry on the opening sentence. Then the body remembered its cheaper talents. She had been talking her way through rooms for years now, and the skill had outlived almost everything else. She walked them through the dissertation's frame: machine-learning triage systems used by cities and public agencies, the way they failed not in the abstract but at the exact points when demand spiked and language got ugly and human. Storm-week complaint logs. Tenant hotlines. Crisis tags that drifted under stress because people in trouble did not speak in benchmark English. A model could look robust on clean data and still misroute the worst day of somebody's month.

By slide four, her breathing had evened out. She stopped seeing the packet for stretches at a time. She talked about the error taxonomy she had built from cross-city logs, the ablation studies that showed where supposedly resilient models fell apart, the human-review interventions that mattered more than another decimal point of leaderboard performance. She did not oversell. She had learned that if you offered Mohit anything shinier than the truth he would later invoice you for it with interest.

Professor Reyes leaned forward at the slide on multilingual drift. "On the Newark and Bridgeport samples," she said, touching one lozenge tin with a fingertip without opening it, "did the hand-labeled error categories transfer, or did you have to rebuild them city by city?"

"Mostly transfer with ugly edges," Leela said. "The high-level categories held. The trouble was at the level that mattered operationally. People describe a flooded basement differently from a heat outage, and they describe both differently when they're writing on no sleep from a borrowed phone. The model reads that as noise unless you teach it what desperation sounds like in each local corpus."

Reyes gave one short nod and wrote something down.

Weiss asked about generalization across domains, and Leela answered that too. She was good here. That, more than anything, made the whole exercise cruel. If she had been messy or shallow, if the work had actually collapsed under a few ordinary questions, she could have hated herself cleanly. Instead she could feel them following her. Even Gloria, who had mastered the administrative expression of listening without promising anything, looked up from the packet and kept looking.

When Leela finished, there was a brief, decent silence, cooler than praise but long enough to let the work sit in front of them as work and not as a student body making requests. She let herself feel one stupid inch of hope.

Mohit folded his hands.

"Thanks, Leela," he said. "That was clear, and there's real intelligence in the project. From where I sit, this isn't a question of effort. It's whether the dissertation is stable enough, on authorship and provenance, to go into a defense room without blowing open."

There it was, polished enough to reflect the lights.

Gloria turned one page of the packet with a careful finger. "Walk us through it."

Mohit inclined his head, as though he had hoped not to be asked and was prepared anyway.

"Three things are tangled together for me," he said. "First, authorship. Several evaluation pieces in the dissertation grow out of shared lab benchmark infrastructure, and right now I can't point an outside reader cleanly to where June Park's systems work ends and Leela's dissertation contribution begins."

Leela opened her mouth. Gloria lifted a hand, not quite to stop her, just enough to sequence her.

"Let him finish," Gloria said.

"Second, data provenance. Parts of chapters two and three run through a pipeline and repository history that were already under scrutiny in October. Even without a final finding, that leaves weak spots an external committee will push on. Third, the chapter-two robustness result changed on rerun after the fall workshop submission. I'm not calling that fraud. I'm saying a central claim had to be narrowed after submission, and that's too shaky a base for a defense date."

He said support a defense date the way other people said support a local business.

Leela could hear the fluorescent hum again. It had dropped out while she was presenting and returned the moment he began translating her year into process nouns.

"I need to answer that now," she said.

Gloria looked at her and gave the smallest nod.

"The October review was not a finding that I mishandled data," Leela said. She heard herself starting in the register she used for faculty, measured enough to be mistaken for calm. "It was a temporary suspension on inherited admin privileges because my credentials were attached to repositories I used to maintain for the lab. Those were shared systems. The review did not conclude that my dissertation data was fabricated or that the analysis here is invalid."

Mohit glanced down at the packet, performing the courtesy of checking whether his own document agreed with him.

"I don't believe I said either of those things."

"You tied provenance concerns to the dissertation and talked like it was settled."

"I said the record is unstable."

"Because the lab record is unstable," Leela said, and now the clauses started adding themselves. "Which is not the same as my work being unstable. The repository structure was not something I designed for my dissertation. It was inherited infrastructure, and I lost access to the audit trail after the suspension, so if what you're asking for is a more defensible history than the one the lab currently has, that's not something I can produce alone in this room."

Weiss looked up from his green-stained finger. "Set the politics aside for a second. If you had to annotate chapter two for an outside examiner, which analyses still depend on that flagged pipeline, and which ones can you regenerate from mirrored copies and local logs?"

"Yes," Leela said. "Most of the analysis can be rerun from the mirrored dataset copies and the local experiment logs. What matters isn't whether the results exist. It's that the original chain of custody sits inside a lab system I no longer administer."

"And on authorship," Reyes asked. She did not sound hostile. That made it worse. Hostility would have given Leela something to push against. "If an external reader landed cold on chapter one, what sentence would tell them where June Park's infrastructure work stops and your dissertation claims start?"

June's name at the table landed like a thumb on a bruise. Leela felt it before she had language for it.

"June built the benchmark infrastructure layer," she said. "I was never claiming otherwise. My dissertation uses that infrastructure the way everybody in the lab used shared infrastructure. My contribution is the failure taxonomy, the cross-city stress tests, and the intervention analysis built on top of it. Those are not the same project."

Mohit steepled his fingers more tightly. "The problem is that they don't yet read as separate enough. Not for a defense. Maybe for an internal draft conversation, but not for the department's final threshold."

"They read that way because the lab was organized that way," Leela said.

Nobody interrupted. The fluorescent hum came forward. She had not raised her voice. She had, however, allowed a truth in without furniture around it.

Mohit did not flinch. "Say more."

It was the kind of invitation he liked because it could always later be described as generosity.

Leela's hand went into her tote before she had fully decided. Her fingers found the hot edge of her laptop.

"In September I found cloned copies of student repositories under a private directory labeled Ruderal," she said. The words were out now, flat and specific. "My credentials were still attached to part of the admin structure, which is part of why the October review landed on me. The provenance problem didn't appear out of nowhere. It was built."

Gloria straightened. "Leela, is that a formal allegation in this room?"

"I'm saying the lab architecture was manipulated in ways that now make my work look contaminated by design."

Weiss leaned forward. "Do you have the underlying logs in the materials we actually have today, or anything that would let us test that claim without starting a second proceeding?"

"No."

Reyes asked, "Did Research Integrity receive them, or are they nowhere in the file we're discussing?"

"Not for this review," Leela said. "This was supposed to be a pre-defense meeting, not a hearing on why the lab got arranged this way."

Gloria's voice stayed level, which was what made it final. "Then we're not doing that here. If there are additional materials, they need to come through the committee in advance."

Mohit kept his eyes on the packet. "I don't think it helps Leela to turn a hard, messy lab history into the whole basis for today's decision."

Gloria's pen moved into the margin. Weiss sat back. The three faculty at the table let his narrower question replace everything that had come before.

The flash of courage that had opened her bag died there under the fluorescent hum and three faculty faces arranging themselves around procedure. She thought of the log export she had kept hidden in September and June's yellow legal pad in an apartment she could not currently walk into. If she kept pushing now, out of order, in defense-mode desperation, Gloria would call it a separate matter, Weiss would ask for commit hashes, Reyes would ask why none of it had been attached beforehand. All reasonable. That was the trap.

"The projects in the lab overlapped by design," she said finally. "Methods, evaluation scaffolds, sometimes data access. That was presented as collaboration and robustness work. It made authorship and independence harder to narrate cleanly after the fact, but it doesn't make the dissertation derivative."

Reyes asked, "Was any of that overlap written down in a project memo, a methods note, an authorship statement, anything an outside reader could inspect?"

Leela gave one helpless half-laugh she hated the instant it escaped. "Not in a way that would make this easier."

Mohit shifted one page of the packet toward the center of the table. "This is why I'm asking for a pause. Leela's talented. The lab was sloppier than it should've been, and some of that's on me. But if we send her into a defense before the authorship boundaries and data history are cleaned up, we're asking her to survive a room that can pull the whole thing apart."

Reyes stopped touching the lozenge tin. Gloria wrote `disentangle` on her pad in block capitals.

Gloria's face did not change much, but her voice lost some of its cushioning. "Do you have documentation the committee should've had before today, and would it materially change this picture?"

The question tightened the air around the table. It was fair in the way institutions loved best: fair if you ignored time.

She thought of the log export on her laptop and the page in June's legal pad that matched Mohit's promises against the server timestamps. She thought of Omar's letter, Ben forwarding the grievance draft like a child running to tell, the night in the library when June had looked at the timeline gap and waited for Leela to step all the way into the truth, and Leela had still chosen the version of courage that left a door open for herself.

If she brought any of that here now, out of order, in defense-mode desperation, Mohit would call it retaliation. Gloria would call it a separate matter. Weiss would ask for dates and commit hashes and who else had seen the files first. Reyes would want to know why none of it had been formally attached to the pre-defense materials. All of those would be reasonable questions. The devastating part was that reasonableness was not on her side anymore.

"I have files and context," she said. "I don't have a packet before the committee."

Mohit looked almost sad for her. That expression on his face had once worked on her like a hand at the back of her neck.

"And that's why I don't think we should pin down a date today," he said softly.

She turned to the others before she could answer him directly and make the committee about temperament instead of content.

"The nonreplicating result from the fall workshop wasn't carried forward unchanged," she said. "I reran those analyses, cut the claim back, and rewrote the chapter around failure concentration under stress conditions. If you want the exact diff between the submission and the dissertation version, I can send it this afternoon."

Weiss flipped to a page she had not yet read. "This says the revised chapter still leans on the same evaluation slice, even if the wording around it is narrower."

"It relies on the same dataset family," Leela said. "Not the same claim."

Mohit touched the workshop tab without looking up. "And that's the difficulty. The prose is narrower, yes, but the underlying file history still shows a central result getting corrected after submission."

Reyes looked at her over the top of the packet. "If I were the outside reader next month, could I stay inside the argument, or would I spend the defense unwinding provenance and authorship first?"

That was the honest question on the table, which was why it hurt the most.

Leela looked at the window. The lake beyond it was the color of printer paper left in sunlight too long. She thought of saying yes and letting the lie bloom in front of everyone. She thought of saying the provenance questions were manufactured, which was truer and still unusable.

"I could defend the argument," she said. "I couldn't keep the defense from turning into a hearing about the lab first."

No one wrote that down. Weiss's pen hovered above the packet without landing. Gloria pressed her thumbnail into the binder clip. Reyes shut the lozenge tin with a soft click.

Gloria closed the packet and rested both hands on it. "Then here's the practical piece. If we don't schedule today, what do you revise first, Leela?"

Her palms stayed on the closed packet as she said practical, pressing the mess into the version you could minute and circulate.

Leela knew the answer because she had already built it into the better-paper timeline.

"I'd rewrite the benchmark framing in chapter one so the shared infrastructure is explicitly bounded and the original contribution is carried by the failure taxonomy and intervention sections," she said. "I'd append a data-provenance memo separating the mirrored dataset copies from the lab-admin pipeline that triggered the October review. I'd also include the rerun tables from the workshop analysis as an appendix so the narrower claim is transparent."

Gloria started writing before Leela finished the second sentence. By the time she said appendix, the chair's notes had turned six months of theft and improvisation into three neat deliverables.

Weiss tapped his capped pen once on the table. "How long is that, realistically, if access holds and if someone besides you has to check the disentangling?"

There was the trap again, tucked into the assumption that access still belonged to her.

"A few weeks for the writing," Leela said. "Longer if access questions slow down the provenance memo."

"Who verifies the separation from June's benchmark work?" Gloria asked.

Leela nearly said June. But June had not answered her in days. June had taken Mohit's meeting and then stepped sideways out of the coalition they had built badly in the first place. June was not going to walk into this room and rescue her with a yellow legal pad and an uncomplicated conscience.

"I can document my own commits, drafts, and notebooks," Leela said.

The silence that followed was small and ruinous. Weiss's pen stopped moving. Gloria did not add a fourth line. Across from Leela, Mohit folded one cuff over the other and waited.

Leela turned to him then. She had been avoiding that direct line, the one between them that could still rearrange the whole room if either of them used it correctly.

"You know the work isn't derivative," she said.

His face remained almost tender. "I know the work came out of collaboration, pressure, and some preventable ambiguity. But I can't ask the department to act on what I believe in the abstract or what I feel for you. It has to be plain on the page and durable in review."

Gloria uncapped her pen. Weiss went back to the compliance tab. Reyes watched Leela instead of him.

"The record isn't a weather system," she said.

Nobody spoke for a beat.

Mohit looked at her as if he wished she had chosen a different sentence. "No," he said. "But it's the surface the institution can actually act on."

Gloria intervened before the line could sharpen any further. "We're at a decision point. Approve now, approve conditionally, or defer. Does anyone support conditional approval tied to the revisions Leela just described?"

Reyes opened the tin at last, took out a ginger lozenge, and set it on the table without unwrapping it. "I don't," she said. "Right now I can't separate the dissertation's substantive claims from the provenance fight around them. I'm sorry."

There was no comfort in the apology. It had too much intelligence in it.

Weiss cleared his throat. "I don't either. With the October compliance shadow still hanging there, and with the contribution boundaries still this muddy, I would not send it to defense. That's timing, not a judgment on the intellectual project."

Gloria looked from face to face and waited the extra beat institutions used when they wanted caution to look collaborative. No one filled it.

She pulled the committee form toward her and wrote as she spoke. "Then the committee isn't approving scheduling today. The recommendation is deferral pending further revision and review, with a reconvened pre-defense once the provenance questions are clarified and the independent contribution is more cleanly stated."

Leela's hands had gone very still on the table. She could feel the edge of the packet biting the pad of her thumb through one sheet of paper. If she moved too quickly she would either cry or say something that would become a new document by five o'clock.

"What's the timeline for reconvening?" she asked, and heard how disciplined she sounded, which made the question lonelier.

Gloria did not give her the kindness of pretending there was one. "I can't give you a date today."

Mohit leaned slightly forward. "Let's take a week, regroup, and turn this into an actionable revision plan."

The phrase hit her harder than the decision. Build a revision plan was what people said when a project slipped scope, not when an adult life broke cleanly along the seam he had been scoring for months.

In the instant after Gloria's sentence, whole adult futures she had been living toward simply disappeared. The April room reservation. The summer move out of graduate housing before the rent laddered again. The postdoc applications she had half drafted under the fantasy that being late by a few years could still be narrated as seriousness once the degree existed. The dentist appointment Meera had nagged her to make. The possibility of telling her mother a real month instead of another evasive update. Even the humiliations had been organized by the assumption of an end. She had accepted debt, delay, Mohit's moods, six years of basement light, because the line still pointed forward. Now the line stopped in a committee room with a view of dirty ice.

"Can the recommendation specify that there is no finding of misconduct attached to the dissertation itself?" she said.

Weiss and Reyes both looked at Gloria, not at Mohit, and the tiny shift barely helped.

Gloria chose her wording carefully enough to make each word feel purchased. "The recommendation can say the committee is deferring on grounds of revision and record clarity. It won't make any finding beyond that."

Which meant every person who heard about it secondhand would supply their own finding anyway.

Mohit nodded like this was a fair accommodation. "I think that's the cleanest way to state it."

Leela began sliding her papers into the folder before anybody had formally dismissed her. The motion forced an end to the meeting. Her summary pages had gone damp at one corner from her hand. She could not remember touching water.

"Thank you all," she said. She hated herself for saying it and hated herself more for saying it steadily.

Reyes gave her a look that was not pity exactly. It was closer to professional sorrow, the feeling smart women in institutions learned to substitute for outrage when outrage would not clear a room. Weiss recapped his pen. Gloria said, "We'll follow up in writing." Mohit stood with one hand half-extended toward the cables.

"I've got it," Leela said.

She packed the charger badly, shoving the orange cord into the tote without wrapping it. Mohit's packet went in too. She did not know why except that leaving it behind felt like consenting to the version of events that had just won.

The hallway outside the room was full of ordinary afternoon life. A graduate student from materials science laughed too loudly near the printer. Somebody had left a half-dead peace lily on the windowsill with a note that said FREE TO GOOD HOME. The copy room door stood open on its humming blue light. Every building liked itself best at the scale where other people's disasters registered as background acoustics.

Leela walked to the bathroom at the end of the corridor because the elevator bank would have people and the stairwell would have too much air. The women's room was empty. The light came on in staggered strips above the mirrors, one fluorescent tube after another finding the courage to admit where she was.

Industrial soap and old paper towels baked dry by the vent hung in the bathroom. One stall door did not quite latch. There was a curl of black thread on the counter by the sink, maybe from somebody's scarf, maybe from the edge seam of her own blazer. She set her bag down hard enough that the packet corners inside thudded against the laminate.

Then her body, which had been willing to behave in public for exactly fifty-three minutes, quit the arrangement.

She bent over the sink and tried to take one careful breath. Another one. The first breath shuddered and came back sharp. The second hitched halfway in. She pressed the heel of her left hand against the counter edge until it hurt. Her right hand, strapped and mostly decorative in moments like this, hung useless at her side.

During the meeting she had been biting the inside of her cheek without noticing. Now she found the torn place with her teeth and bit down deliberately, hard enough to make the pain separate from everything else. It was an ugly, bodily impulse, the kind a body chose when it needed one sharp signal. Skin gave. Warm salt flooded her mouth over the chalk of the antacid she had taken that morning.

She spat into the sink. Pink first, then darker.

The automatic faucet took a second to recognize her hands. Water jumped out too fast, cold enough to sting. She rinsed and spat again. A thin clot clung to the white basin before the stream worried it toward the drain.

Her phone vibrated in the bag. Once. Then again.

She did not look.

She took Mohit's packet out and laid it on the counter because the bag suddenly felt too full of paper, too much like an office she was carrying on her shoulder. The top page was already bent where she had jammed it in. Pending revision and further review, the recommendation line said. A drop of blood fell from her mouth before she could turn away and stippled the words further review. Toner feathered outward in a tiny gray bruise.

The words blurred under the blood, and with them went the last hope that the right phrasing might still save her.

She bit the cheek again. Harder this time. The fresh pain flashed white behind her eyes. When she spat, the sink caught a red string and thinned it to nothing.

Her phone buzzed a third time and went still.

Leela rinsed once more, folded the damp packet shut, and shoved it back into the tote without checking the phone. She stayed there facing the mirror while the torn place in her cheek kept filling and emptying, filling and emptying, and the fluorescent lights hummed over the sinks like a machine too small to care what it was preserving.
