# Chapter 7

The next morning, the email from Research Integrity arrived while Leela sat at her desk in Keating trying to coax one dead benchmark run into becoming a paragraph she could still someday call a dissertation. Outside the sixth-floor window, Lakebridge had gone the color of dishwater, the wet part of cold rather than snow, a steady damp that climbed into coat sleeves and stayed there. The message carried a cheerful blue flag beside it, bright enough to feel hand-placed and intimate in the worst way.

Subject: Required Attendance - Research Integrity Intake

Dear Ms. Krishnan,

Please report to Baines Hall, Room 414, at 2:30 p.m. today regarding a data-handling concern associated with privileged access credentials under your stewardship.

The message was signed by Mara Sloane, Associate Director, and copied an Information Security address that looked automated enough to absolve everybody in advance.

Leela read it twice, then a third time with her hand flat on the desk so the tendons in her wrist would stop twitching under the brace. The phrase privileged access credentials under your stewardship sat in her head like a stain. Nobody in Keating said things like stewardship unless they were already arranging blame into folders.

Across the cubicle wall Omar said, "You made the face."

He had a pharmacy bag open on his desk and was eating pistachios one by one, setting the empty shells in a neat line along the edge of a conference flyer. Even hungry, even frightened, he organized his mess before it existed. He leaned back enough to see her over the partition, his beard line still too careful for a man who had slept in the department three nights the week before.

"What face?"

"The one you make when the machine has decided it knows more than you do."

She turned her laptop so he could read the email. He was silent longer than she liked. Outside, somebody in the hallway laughed too loudly at something faculty-adjacent, the sound carrying the false confidence that came from not being the one in trouble.

"This isn't routine," Omar said.

"Everything here calls itself routine until somebody loses a year."

He looked at the subject line again. "Do you know what it is?"

She did. The words themselves were vague, but her body had already sorted them into the correct directory: root access, audit trail, the cloned repositories from September, the ones she had found under the private namespace and then hidden from everybody because the clean version of that discovery had lasted maybe twelve seconds before it turned into the dirty version, the one where she still needed Mohit for money, timing, recommendations, permission to exist in the building as something other than an error.

"Probably the cluster," she said.

"Probably," Omar repeated, in the tone of a man laying a cracked dish gently back on the shelf.

Her phone buzzed before either of them spoke again.

Mohit: Heard RI may reach out today about some inherited access-governance issues. Annoying but manageable. Keep your answers tightly factual and don't speculate beyond what you can document.

He sent it like weather. He sent it three minutes after the summons, fast enough to make the whole building feel wired back to him. Leela stared at the screen until it dimmed. Omar did not ask to see the message. He only watched her face become something he could already interpret.

"He knows," he said.

"Apparently."

"Did he say why?"

"No. He said not to speculate." She gave a small laugh that had nothing in it. "Which is generous, because speculation is currently all I own."

Omar pushed the pistachio bag toward her. She took one and hated him a little for the kindness of it.

"Will you tell them he had access?" he asked.

The question was clean enough that she could not answer it cleanly. She had configured Mohit's laptop herself in August, kneeling on the thin rug in his office while he stood over her with one shoe off because he'd stepped in something outside and didn't want it on the carpet. He had talked the whole time about maturing the lab's infrastructure, about not being bottlenecked by central IT, about how much he appreciated that she understood urgency at a systems level. She had pasted his public key where he wanted it because he had said appreciated in the tone that really meant remember who decides your timeline.

"I don't know what I'll tell them yet."

Omar cracked another shell with his thumb. "That usually means you know exactly what the choices are and hate all of them."

"Useful."

"I can walk over with you."

She looked at him. He kept his expression neutral, but his left hand had started flattening and refolding the corner of the conference flyer until the paper went soft. There was always some tiny object taking the damage on his behalf.

"You have class at three."

"I can be late."

"And then if they ask why you came, what do I say? That the lab brought a witness because we run our misconduct reviews communally?"

"That's not what I meant."

"I know."

He let that sit. He was one of the few people in the department who could let silence stand without rushing in to explain himself. Under pressure it read less like calm than like expensive discipline, something he had built because being visibly scrambled in institutional rooms cost him more than it cost everybody else.

"Text me when you come out," he said at last.

"I will."

"And Leela."

"What."

"If they're asking yes-or-no questions about how the lab actually works, don't let them make you sound stupid just because the truth sounds embarrassing."

The inside of her cheek caught between her teeth. The embarrassing part was how much of the lab only worked because fear had been dressed up as initiative. She nodded instead, and Omar looked away first, giving her back the privacy of her panic.

She did not work between eleven and two-thirty. She opened the policy page on privileged systems access and closed it. She opened the log screenshots she'd hidden in a folder called tax_receipts and closed those too. She drafted three versions of an explanation in her head, each one stable until it reached Mohit. Past him, language turned to paste: advisor, supervisor, PI, mentor. None of the nouns held the right ratio of dependence to fear. None of them explained why she had known enough to be culpable and not enough to be safe.

At two-ten she left Keating. The air across the quad smelled of wet wool and leaf rot and the campus coffee carts grinding through beans they charged too much for. Baines Hall sat ahead of her in its black-glass self-importance, near enough to the engineering complex that a summons felt immediate, far enough that the walk still gave her time to imagine running. She did not run. She kept going because most academic disasters were built exactly this way, from people continuing to walk toward rooms they should have set on fire.

Omar matched her at the curb without comment. He had put on the charcoal coat he wore to anything that smelled faintly official, and the neatness of him made her feel both steadier and more doomed.

"You didn't have to," she said.

"No," he said. "But I wanted the exercise of crossing campus with an indictment in the weather."

"It's not an indictment."

"I know. It's an intake. Much more relaxing."

The automatic doors opened before they touched them. The lobby smelled unmistakably of artificial citrus, all solvent and rind with none of the sweetness. Somebody had polished the stone floor recently enough that the overhead lights lay across it in obedient rectangles. A student worker at the front desk wore a pumpkin-patterned cardigan and looked seventeen and terminally bored.

"Fourth floor," she said without looking up. "Sign in on the tablet."

Leela typed her name with the plastic stylus chained to the stand. Visitor reason flashed in a drop-down menu. Procurement. Sponsored Programs. Appeals. Research Integrity Intake. She chose it and watched the words settle under her own name as if they had always belonged there.

The elevator was lined in brushed metal that gave everyone the complexion of bad fish. Omar kept his eyes on the floor numbers.

"If they suspend your access," he said, "that's not neutral."

"You have a gift for comfort."

"I'm serious."

"So am I."

The doors opened on four. The carpet began immediately, a sound-eating neutral gray that made her wet shoes seem obscene. Room 414 was already open. Omar stopped outside it.

"I shouldn't be visible in there," he said. "Not for my sake. For yours."

That was true and ugly and not worth pretending otherwise. Anybody in Baines could convert a student's need for company into evidence of instability in two sentences flat.

"Text me," he said again.

"Go teach."

"I am not teaching. I'm pretending first-years can derive asymptotic notation before caffeine."

She glanced at him. The uncontracted form landed as deliberate precision, his way of holding himself together when the edge came close. Then he gave her a brief, crooked look, the private sardonic one he never used in seminar rooms.

"Fine," he said. "I'm going to stand in front of eighteen undergrads and lie about recurrence relations. Text me after."

Then he left, one hand already in his pocket feeling for the cheap click pen he always carried to class like a talisman.

The conference room had no windows, only a frosted glass panel beside the door and a long table too shiny for the light above it. A speakerphone sat in the middle with the mute button lit red, performatively inactive. Mara Sloane stood when Leela came in. She was maybe in her late forties, wearing a navy sweater set under a blazer and the expression of a woman who had once been pretty and still resented administrative lighting for taking liberties. A Sunday crossword half-finished in blue ink lay tucked under her yellow legal pad, nine-down abandoned at the edge. Next to her sat Brendan Yu from Information Security, damp at the cuffs from the weather and with a bicycle helmet clipped to his messenger bag like he had come straight from the real world and been trapped here by accident.

"Ms. Krishnan," Mara said. "Thank you for coming over on short notice."

"I got the sense it wasn't optional."

Mara gave a polite almost-smile, the administrative version that claimed credit for hearing tone without engaging content. "Please. Have a seat. This is Brendan Yu from Information Security. We're doing a preliminary intake regarding privileged systems usage on the Hades cluster and related repository replication events. This is not, at present, a formal misconduct hearing."

At present, related, hearing: each word arrived scrubbed and dry.

Leela sat. The chair cushion hissed under her with trapped air. Somebody had left the room too cold, or maybe buildings like Baines believed temperature itself could count as professionalism.

"Do I need a lawyer?" she asked.

Brendan blinked. Mara folded her hands.

"You're welcome to seek advice at any point," Mara said. "For today's purposes we're collecting factual information about account stewardship and access control. If that changes, you would be notified."

"Okay."

"Would you like water?"

"No."

"All right." Mara clicked her pen. "Can you confirm that from August 2024 through the present you have been the designated custodian of the `hades-admin` credential set for Civic Signals Lab infrastructure?"

There it was. Not her name, not exactly. Something older, shared, deniable in the way only longstanding disasters were deniable.

"Designated by who?" Leela asked.

"By the lab's internal access documentation and by Professor Saran's annual systems attestation."

The phrase made her picture Mohit signing some web form with his expensive glasses halfway down his nose, annoyed by the interruption, writing her into responsibility because she was the senior student and because she said yes too often in the years when yes still looked like progress.

"I had the credential set," she said. "Custodian makes it sound more orderly than it was."

Brendan opened a folder and slid three printed pages toward her. They were log excerpts, timestamped and annotated in yellow highlighter. She recognized the paths immediately. `/srv/projects/ruderal_sync/`. Mirror clones of June's benchmark repo, Omar's baseline code, her own work, all hauled sideways into a private directory tree under the authority of `hades-admin`.

The room seemed to contract around the yellow marks. She had seen these lines on a screen in September at two in the morning, with server fans screaming on the other side of the closet door and a vending machine burrito turning in her stomach. On paper they looked cleaner, almost reasonable. Paper removed the heat from a crime.

"These replication events occurred under that credential set," Brendan said. "Do you dispute that?"

"No. I dispute that that tells you who did it."

"We'll get there," Mara said. "First I need to establish baseline governance. Were these credentials shared with anyone else?"

Leela looked at the pages again, buying time. The truthful answer depended on which part of the system anyone wanted to claim was normal. There was official policy, under which the answer was supposed to be no. Then there was the lab, which had run for years on a more embarrassing answer: yes, but only when urgent, yes, but everybody knew, yes, but don't write it down, yes, but never in a way a university office could quote back to you later.

"Informally, yes," she said.

Brendan wrote something. Mara's face changed almost imperceptibly, not surprise exactly, more like the small settling motion of a shelf receiving the object it had expected.

"Shared with whom?"

"Professor Saran had access."

"Direct credential access?" Brendan asked.

"He had enough access to use the systems when he wanted something done quickly."

"I'm asking whether he knew the password, possessed the key material, or otherwise had the ability to authenticate as `hades-admin`."

"Yes."

That felt good for half a second. Then Mara asked, "Was that approved through central IT?"

Leela laughed before she could stop herself. Nobody else did.

"No," she said.

"Was it documented anywhere?"

"Only if you count the lab wiki and a lot of people pretending not to notice how the lab wiki worked."

"I don't think we can count that," Mara said.

"That's convenient."

Mara let the sentence pass as if she were being generous rather than tactical. "Can you describe how Professor Saran came to have access?"

Leela rubbed the edge of the first log page with her thumb until the paper began to fuzz. She could tell them the clean version. Professor Saran needed emergency weekend access during a server migration, and she provisioned it under time pressure. That version made her sound merely sloppy. The dirtier version was closer to true: Mohit had wanted direct access because waiting made him feel subordinate to infrastructure, and she had been too close to graduation, too dependent, too flattered by being treated as the grown-up in the room to tell him no.

"He asked me to set up access on his machine last summer," she said. "He said central IT was slow, and if we wanted the lab to function like a real research group we couldn't open tickets every time something broke."

"Did you raise concerns about policy compliance at that time?"

"To him?"

"To anyone."

"No."

"Why not?"

Because she was thirty and still living in graduate housing with cheap blinds and a credit card balance she fed minimum payments to like a sick stray. Because Mohit could delay her defense by three months without ever phrasing it as punishment. Because he could call her mature when he wanted labor and unstable when he wanted leverage. Because by the time you had words for coercion in academia you had usually already helped build the machine that used it.

"Because saying no to your advisor isn't the same as saying no to a colleague," she said. "It doesn't fit the formal vocabulary, but it's real."

Mara's expression softened in a trained way that made Leela want to flip the table. "I understand there may have been supervisory pressure."

"No," Leela said. "That's you making it sound survivable."

"What are you saying?"

Leela heard the trap open under the question. If she said control, Mara could recommend a different office, a different form, a different useless corridor. If she said fear, it would become affect. If she said he made it functionally impossible to refuse without threatening my funding and my degree timeline, she would sound like every graduate student cliche faculty rolled their eyes at in closed meetings.

"I'm saying the person who controls compute, authorship, letters, travel, defense timing, and whether I get to graduate doesn't make casual requests," she said. "He makes requests that arrive with consequences already attached."

Mara nodded once, already translating. "I can note that you experienced pressure within the supervisory environment."

The sentence was so thin Leela could see daylight through it.

"That vocabulary is too small for what I mean," she said.

"It may be the most precise language available to this office."

"Then your office is useless."

Brendan shifted in his chair, not offended, just wary of splash damage. Mara put her pen down very carefully on top of the crossword.

Her anger had arrived too early, before it could be made useful. Mara let the sentence cool between them, then said, "I'm trying to determine who used privileged credentials to duplicate restricted repositories into an unsanctioned project namespace. If you want to make a broader complaint about your advisor, there are channels for that. They aren't the same channel."

There. The architecture of the whole place, laid out with the quiet pride of competent bureaucracy. If she wanted to describe the way Mohit occupied every future tense in her life, she needed one office. If she wanted to describe the way he had put a startup directory on university hardware and routed it through access she nominally controlled, she needed another. If she wanted any of those descriptions to matter, she needed documents she had not created when the truth was still only dangerous, not yet expensive.

"Fine," she said. "Then ask the narrow question."

Mara did.

"Did you personally initiate the repository replications shown in front of you?"

"No."

"Do you know who did?"

Leela looked at the highlighted path where the private directory name sat in plain text: `ruderal_sync`. The word itself was almost funny, startup-anxious and vaguely botanical, the sort of brand name a man like Mohit would think meant resilience because it sounded scrappy in a well-funded way. She had the screenshots from September. She had the memory of seeing his laptop connected. She had enough to start something and not enough to control where it would spread.

"I know who had motive and access," she said.

"That isn't what I asked."

"Then yes and no."

"Ms. Krishnan."

"I can't prove it in the format you want."

Brendan tapped the log pages. "Do you have documentary evidence that someone else authenticated with the credential set?"

"Not in a way that would make you happy."

"Do you have documentary evidence, full stop?"

This was the point. The room had been moving toward it since she sat down. She could put the screenshots on the table. She could say Mohit's name without softening it and watch the whole thing widen immediately, not into justice but into exposure. They would ask when she discovered the activity. They would ask why she didn't report it. They would ask why the same credential set remained active afterward, why she continued acting as senior student, why she had not preserved the chain of custody cleanly, why her own compromised paper had later used the same compute environment. She could feel those future questions lined up behind the current one like faculty outside a seminar door.

"No," she said.

The lie was technical, which made it academic enough to survive a minute in the room.

Brendan wrote again. Mara's tone stayed patient. "Were you aware, prior to today's meeting, that these replications had occurred?"

Leela looked at her.

Here it was again, the second trap under the first. The truthful answer would move time itself against her. If she said yes, she became the student who sat on a known access violation. If she said no, she became either incompetent or naive. Mohit had built the options so elegantly over months that even now, absent from the room, he was choosing the furniture.

"I was aware there had been unusual replication activity on the cluster," she said. "I did not understand the full purpose of it."

Mara took that in. "Did you report the unusual activity to anyone?"

"No."

"Why not?"

Because she had seen catastrophe and called it maybe, then called it later, then called it after the deadline, after the seminar, after she had some other piece that would keep the first piece from costing her everything. Because cowardice in graduate school rarely felt dramatic. It felt like scheduling. It felt like I need one more week.

"Because I thought I could resolve it internally," she said.

"How?"

"By understanding whether it was backup behavior, export behavior, or something else."

"Did you resolve it?"

"Obviously not."

Brendan cleared his throat. "Just to confirm: you allowed shared use of privileged credentials, you did not document that sharing through approved channels, you became aware of unusual replication activity, and you did not report it."

He read it back in a tone almost free of accusation, which made it land harder. It sounded like a case summary already. It sounded like the version of events a committee would believe because committees loved lists.

"Add one more line," Leela said. "Professor Saran had direct systems access because he asked for it and because I gave it to him."

"I have that you reported shared use," Mara said. "I do not have documentary support assigning the replication events to any other individual."

"Right," Leela said. "So on paper I'm the part you can prove."

"Can you provide any emails, messages, or written instructions documenting that Professor Saran asked for or used the `hades-admin` credentials?"

Leela thought of Mohit's text from three hours earlier. Keep your answers tightly factual. It was almost artful, how a warning could arrive dressed as advice. But the message did not admit what mattered. He would say access-governance issues because he knew there were access-governance issues. He would say don't speculate because mentors always told students not to spiral. He had spent years building sentences with emergency exits already installed.

"No," she said.

"All right." Mara folded the log pages back into the folder, the yellow highlights vanishing under her hand. "Based on what we have today, I'm not opening a formal misconduct action against you at this time."

Relief came so fast it embarrassed her. It lasted less than a breath.

"However," Mara continued, "Information Security will place an interim restriction on your privileged access to Hades while we review the broader access-governance failure. This means admin rights, shared-key authority, credential rotation authority, and direct repository replication permissions will be suspended effective today."

Leela stared at her. "Suspended for how long?"

"Pending review."

"Pending review isn't a timeline."

"No," Mara said. "It isn't."

Brendan slid a different paper toward her. It was one page, university letterhead, checkbox language. The notice called itself an interim access restriction, not disciplinary, not a finding, an administrative safeguard. There was a place for her signature acknowledging receipt. At the bottom, in smaller text, distribution: Research Integrity, Information Security, Department Chair, Faculty Supervisor, Graduate Programs.

"You're copying Mohit?" she asked.

"Professor Saran is the faculty supervisor for the affected infrastructure."

"He's the private namespace on the logs."

"That's your position," Mara said. "It's not established by today's intake."

"If I need my own checkpoints or figures, I go through him?"

"If you need research materials retrieved during the interim period, those requests can be coordinated through your PI or, if necessary, through your chair."

"So until review, the man I named gets to meter my dissertation back to me."

"And I noted that."

"In what words."

Mara did not answer immediately. Then she turned the legal pad half an inch so Leela could see the line she had written in compact blue script: Student reports feeling pressured by supervisory environment; no documentary support provided at intake.

It was obscene in its tidiness.

"That's your life in there?" Leela asked.

Mara's expression changed at last, not to guilt, nothing so dramatic, but to the brief weariness of someone who had long ago decided not to feel individually responsible for structural cruelty. "It's what I can defend on the record."

Leela picked up the pen. The university used the cheap kind with a translucent barrel and a cap that never stayed on. Her wrist brace caught on the cuff of her sweater as she bent to sign. For a wild second she considered refusing. Then she imagined the record that would produce too, the note that she had declined acknowledgment, the tiny extra proof that she was difficult when handled administratively. She signed.

Brendan took the page back. "Your access change should propagate within the hour."

"Should."

"Usually faster."

"Great."

Mara gathered her folder. The crossword corner peeked out again, nine-down still unsolved, something four letters long. "You'll receive a summary email before end of day," she said. "If you later locate supporting documentation relevant to your concerns about Professor Saran's involvement, you may submit it as an addendum."

"And if I do, what happens?"

"We evaluate it."

"No," Leela said. "What happens to me."

For the first time all meeting Mara looked directly at her without administrative cushioning. "I can't answer that in advance."

"Because it depends who believes him."

"Because it depends on the evidence."

Which was another way of saying the same thing. Evidence was never just evidence here. It was timing, hierarchy, polish, who had an office, who had already been entered once into the system as a concern.

Mara stood. The meeting was over because the office had decided it was over.

In the hallway, Leela took out her phone before she had even cleared the door.

No formal case, but I lost admin. They copied the chair and Mohit.

She sent it to Omar and immediately hated the telegraphic ugliness of it. Her entire afternoon reduced to plus signs because complete sentences would have made it feel more real.

He replied while she waited for the elevator.

I'm sorry. That's a weapon with committee stationery.

She almost laughed. Then the elevator opened and she stepped into her own distorted reflection again.

Outside, the rain had thinned to mist, fine enough to soak her hair while pretending it wasn't rain at all. She walked back toward Keating too fast, carrying the folder they had let her keep. The paper had already gone soft at one corner from her grip. Every time she loosened her hand, the distribution line flashed back at her: Department Chair, Faculty Supervisor, Graduate Programs. By the time she crossed the quad, her access problem had become everybody else's careful copy.

When she pushed into the basement lab the familiar air hit her all at once: burnt coffee, hot dust, old couch fabric, server heat. The fluorescent hum felt almost intimate after Baines. June was at the long table near the whiteboard, in a washed-out green work jacket with nicotine gum wrappers tucked under her keyboard because she never seemed to find a trash can at the exact moment trash existed. She looked up, took one read of Leela's face, and pulled a fresh piece of gum from the packet in her coat pocket.

"Well?" she said.

Leela set the folder down. "No formal finding."

"Which means there was definitely a punishment."

"They suspended admin on Hades."

June stopped with the gum halfway to her mouth. "Jesus."

"Yeah."

"For how long?"

"Pending review."

June put the gum in, chewed once, hard. "Pending review means whenever they feel like it. That's a hostage note."

Leela sat at her machine and woke the monitor. Her inbox was already filling with perfectly normal department garbage, colloquium reminders, an undergrad asking for extension mercy, a mailing-list message about pumpkin-carving in student affairs. The ordinary world had continued with such commitment it bordered on insult.

"Did you tell them it was him?" June asked.

Leela did not answer quickly enough.

June's chewing slowed. "You didn't."

"I told them he had access."

"That's you ducking his name."

"I know."

June looked back at her screen, not out of delicacy, more because contempt was easier to manage at a forty-five-degree angle. "They make you sign something?"

"Acknowledgment."

"Which they absolutely won't treat like guilt later."

Leela let out a short breath. "Can you not do that right now."

June was quiet. When she spoke again the meanness had gone flatter, more useful. "What do you need off Hades before the lock hits?"

The question landed deeper than comfort would have. Leela typed `ssh hades.cs.haviland.edu` with hands that felt suddenly too large for the keys. The terminal printed `Passwordless auth attempted`, then stalled for a second, the cursor blinking with useless courtesy.

Permission denied (publickey).

She stared at the line. It was not dramatic. No alarm sounded. The server closet kept humming ten feet away, heat breathing through the slats. Somewhere a long-running experiment of hers was probably still alive on a GPU node, counting through the afternoon without her, no longer hers in any way that mattered.

"Already propagated," she said.

June muttered, "Motherfuckers," so quietly it almost counted as prayer.

"I can maybe grab some of your stuff from my side," she said after a moment. "User-level, not admin. Assuming they haven't decided we're all diseased."

"Thanks."

"Don't thank me. Just make a list before they get clever."

Leela opened a blank text file and found she couldn't immediately remember the names of the directories that mattered most. Not because she had forgotten them, exactly. Because every project she had touched in the lab was braided through with somebody else's timelines, somebody else's permissions, somebody else's informal promise that it would all sort itself out once the paper was in, once the review came back, once the defense got scheduled. She was not losing a password. She was losing the fiction that competence had ever translated into ownership.

June stood. "I'm getting coffee before I commit a misdemeanor. Want one?"

"No."

"Suit yourself."

At the door she paused without turning around. "For what it's worth," she said, "that's not a small thing."

Then she left, jacket rubbing the frame, leaving behind the faint medicinal mint smell of her gum.

Leela remained at the desk with the dead terminal window. The lab sounded exactly as it had that morning, fans and fluorescent buzz and an undergrad somewhere down the hall laughing too hard. She opened the interim notice again. Her signature sat at the bottom like a confession written by someone else. Three lines of retrieval instructions told her what the dead terminal already had: every figure, checkpoint, and half-trained model she needed now had to come back through Mohit first.

Her phone buzzed with the summary email from Research Integrity.

Dear Ms. Krishnan,

Thank you for meeting with us this afternoon. This message confirms that no formal misconduct proceeding is being initiated at this time. As discussed, privileged access associated with the `hades-admin` credential set has been temporarily suspended pending review of lab-wide access governance practices. This interim measure is administrative in nature and should not be construed as a finding regarding intent.

Relevant parties have been notified to ensure continuity of research operations.

Relevant parties. She did not need to scroll to know the cc line would include Gloria Fisk and Mohit Saran. She scrolled anyway.

It did.

Thirty seconds later another email appeared in the thread.

From: Mohit Saran

Thanks, Mara. I appreciate the quick clarification.

Leela, let's use this as a chance to simplify process and protect your momentum. For any materials you need during the review window, send requests through me and I'll help coordinate with the team. We should be able to keep dissertation work on track if we're disciplined about scope.

Best,
Mohit

She read it twice. Protect your momentum. On track. Disciplined about scope. He had already converted the afternoon into mentorship.

The server closet kept humming behind its locked metal door, the same pitch it always held when the GPUs were hot. For years that sound had meant she was still central, still useful, still the one who knew where things lived and how to restart them when they failed. Now it was just a machine continuing without witness. On paper she had lost nothing worse than a credential set. In practice Mohit had just been copied onto the terms of her dependency. The only version of distance she could imagine now lived off campus.
