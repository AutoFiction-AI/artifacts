# Chapter 18

The memorandum called it a change in supervisory circumstances.

Leela read the phrase three times because the first two had not felt insulting enough. The PDF sat open on her laptop beside the stripped-down dissertation, both windows full of tracked changes and institutional blue headers, both pretending language could make scale disappear. Change in supervisory circumstances. As if Mohit had taken a sabbatical. As if he had developed a polite rash and not spent a year turning his students into weapons against one another, then funneled their work into a startup deck with his soft little mentor's smile still warm on it.

Her studio was hot in the stale, boxed-in way graduate housing got hot once the radiators finally shut up and the building remembered it had no idea what to do with summer. The cheap blinds clicked against the frame. A fan pushed the air from one side of the room to the other without improving it. On the mattress behind her sat three stacks of papers she kept meaning to sort: committee forms, credit-card statements, and the dissertation drafts from before her project had been reduced to something survivable.

The current file was one hundred sixty-eight pages if she counted the bibliography and the appendix she no longer respected. In January it had been two hundred forty-two. The missing pages had not vanished cleanly. She had cut away the benchmark chapter whose figures had shown up, translated and polished, in `ruderal_pitch_v3.pdf`. She had cut the section with the robustness result that had not replicated cleanly and had looked so beautiful in a conference template that she had nearly believed it herself. She had cut every paragraph that still carried Mohit's phrasing in the joints. What remained was individually attributable work, which was the department's phrase for the part of the wreckage they could process without having to confess how the wreckage had been built.

The memo listed the conditions of her provisional submission:

1. revised committee composition
2. narrowed research claims
3. external review to follow defense scheduling
4. no use of disputed laboratory materials beyond already archived public artifacts

Disputed laboratory materials was another good one. The year had apparently been composed of materials and circumstances, weather systems and office supplies, not Omar in the graduate office with both hands flat on the desk because if he picked up his phone too fast it shook, not June in the Baltimore bathroom with conference makeup gone gray under fluorescent light, saying say it plain, if he stole it then say stole, and later, hours later, June's hand over Leela's mouth on the hotel-room floor because the walls were thin and wanting anything that badly felt louder than grief, not the smell of the basement lab after midnight, hot dust and old upholstery and the sweet rot of coffee left too long on a warmer, all of it mixed with the intimate panic of realizing the room where you had spent six years had been training you to accept smaller and smaller humiliations as ordinary.

She highlighted the phrase external review to follow defense scheduling and replaced follow with inform. It was useless and made her feel better for six seconds.

Her phone buzzed with a calendar reminder: Baines Hall, 10:00 a.m., Fisk.

The walk across campus took eight minutes if she cut behind the library. June sun had reached that early point where it looked generous from a distance and mean once it hit concrete. Students in commencement leftovers still moved through the quad with their families, all of them carrying flower paper and shiny envelopes. Leela felt wrong among them in the way she had felt wrong among undergraduates since she was twenty-six. Too old for ceremony, too unfinished for pride, carrying not flowers but a manila folder with the memo, the revised title page, and a printout of the Montrose State College posting Gloria had forwarded the week before with the subject line Worth considering.

Baines Hall smelled like artificial citrus and vacuumed carpet. It always did. The building had the acoustic quality of a place already preparing its own transcript.

Gloria Fisk was in the conference room with her paper folder and a coffee so weak it looked medicinal. Her silver hair was perfect from ten feet away and more tired up close. Today she had added a yellow butterscotch candy in a twist of cellophane, set beside her legal pad as if she had packed herself a treat for surviving a meeting she did not want.

"You found the place," Gloria said.

"I have actually been summoned here before."

Gloria gave a quick breath that might have been a laugh if she had trusted the room more. "Fair enough. Sit."

Leela sat. The chair was upholstered in the exact fabric Haviland must have bought by the acre in 1998 and never questioned again. Gloria opened the folder, arranged the papers with small precise taps, and for one second looked less like a chair and more like a woman trying to keep paper from replacing blood entirely.

"I've reviewed the revised draft," she said. "This is workable."

Workable was the little promotion from circumstances and materials. Leela folded her hands under the table so she would not touch her face.

"That's not the same as good," Leela said.

"No," Gloria said. "It isn't. But it is defensible, which is what the degree requires at this point."

At this point was another administrative miracle. It packed a whole year into four words and left the damage outside.

Gloria pushed the memo across. "You will submit under substitute supervision. Professor Halliday has agreed to chair the committee on a provisional basis. The defense itself will likely be late summer. Possibly early fall, depending on external review timing."

"And the review?"

Gloria looked at the papers instead of at Leela. "Continues."

"That's an elegant verb for not saying anything."

"It is the accurate verb."

Leela almost said accurate according to whom, but Gloria was tired in a way she recognized from mirrors, and the question would not improve the answer. Through the glass wall she could see an administrator walking past with a box of catered sandwiches from some event already better funded than the end of her degree.

Gloria took off her glasses, polished them on the hem of a handkerchief, and put them back on. "I want to be direct about one thing. The final report is not going to use the words you would use."

"Which words are those?"

"You know which words."

She meant words like stole, threatened, and coerced, words that would also have had to include the fact that Mohit built a private company out of student labor while teaching them to call the arrangement realism. Leela let the list pass through her without speaking it. There was no pleasure in making Gloria hear it.

"The current findings language is about disclosure failures, authorship misrepresentation, and inappropriate entrepreneurial overlap." Gloria said the phrase as if she had not authored it but could not deny custody. "There may also be findings on advising conduct."

June would have laughed out loud. Omar would have become so precise his sentences would start clicking at the joints. Leela only stared at the candy on the table and thought entrepreneurial overlap sounded like two strollers bumping on a sidewalk.

"He cloned our repositories," she said.

"I know."

"He used my credentials to route part of it."

"I know."

"He held Omar's visa letter until it was almost too late."

For the first time Gloria's face tightened in something less administrative than annoyance. "I know what happened. Knowing and writing a report are not the same function."

There it was, finally, the private register slipping through. Not kinder. Truer.

Leela sat back. The chair made a sound like a cough. "So what's the report for?"

Gloria's eyes moved toward the door, then back to Leela. "Containment, if you want the vulgar answer. Process, if you want the donor-safe one. A record, if you want the answer I can say without hating myself."

The room went quiet except for the vent. Leela had not expected honesty from Gloria and disliked the brief urge to reward it.

"All right," she said.

Gloria nodded once and turned a page. "The Montrose position is still open."

The words landed harder than anything about the report, not because they were cruel but because they were practical. The one-year teaching fellowship at Montrose State College was in New York but not near anything that would count as leverage. It meant four courses a semester, advising, committee service if needed, and a salary just high enough to be called humane by people who had not graded eighty first-year coding assignments in a week. Gloria had sent the ad with two sentences: They need someone quickly. You would be good at this.

"I spoke with the chair there," Gloria said. "Not at length. He has a son with celiac disease and spent ten minutes describing bread to me, so I can vouch for nothing except that he is a real person. But they can move fast. If you want it, I believe the offer will be formal by this afternoon."

Leela blinked. The detail about the bread was so unnecessary it made the whole thing feel more real, which was probably why Gloria had included it. Or maybe Gloria had simply been unable to help herself. Administrators also wanted proof that the people at the other end of their phone calls had kitchens.

"Why?" Leela asked.

"Why can they move fast?"

"Why did you call them?"

Gloria held her gaze. "Because your advisor cannot write a letter. Because this department can offer sympathy more easily than bridge funding. Because you need an income in August whether the external review has found its preferred nouns or not. Choose whichever answer offends you least."

Leela looked down at the Montrose printout in her folder. She had circled the salary last week and then written `Hudson Federal 18th` in the margin like a joke so bad only she would understand it. Four thousand eight hundred dollars of card debt did not care whether her future felt narratively satisfying. Graduate housing ended thirty days after a successful defense or sixty days after loss of student status, depending on which office she asked and how badly they wanted her gone. A one-year fellowship was not what she had thought six years in a PhD was for. It was, however, a thing with health insurance and a date certain.

"I'd have to move."

"Yes."

"I'd be teaching intro Python to nineteen-year-olds who still think Git is a social problem."

Gloria's mouth twitched. "Almost certainly."

"And machine learning ethics to business majors."

"Probably that too."

Leela thought of Mohit telling her, years ago, that she had a research temperament. He had said it with such proprietary warmth that she had carried the sentence around like a reference letter before learning that he handed different versions of the same sentence to whoever needed feeding. Research temperament. Now she had a one-year teaching fellowship at a state college she had never once imagined while constructing the grand theoretical life she had supposedly been building in a basement without windows.

"You'd be good at it," Gloria said again, and this time Leela heard the other clause inside it: it is also what there is.

She nodded. "Tell them yes."

Gloria wrote the word on her pad. No ceremony. No congratulations. A brief scratched yes, then a line under it. "Good. The formal offer should come today. Sign the submission memo before you leave."

Leela signed in three places, beside Candidate, Provisional committee approval, and Acknowledgment of revised scope. The university pen dragged at the loops in her name. By the time she slid the papers back across the table she felt neither relief nor defeat, only the blunt metabolic drop that came when a decision stopped being hypothetical and started billing you.

"For what it's worth," Gloria said, stacking the pages, "smaller isn't always lesser."

Leela stood. "That's a line you should workshop before giving it to students."

"I wasn't planning to put it in a brochure."

"Good."

Gloria picked up the butterscotch candy and turned it once between her fingers. "No. Probably not."

Outside Baines, Leela stood in the heat and checked her phone. There was already an email from Montrose Human Resources with the subject line Visiting Teaching Fellowship Offer Letter. She opened the PDF in the patchy shade of a campus maple while a family nearby took graduation photos in matching linen. The PDF listed salary, start date, courses assigned as needed by departmental demand, the contingency of degree completion or documented ABD status, and benefits beginning September 1.

She accepted before she could stage a better emotional response.

An hour later Montrose sent a faculty ID setup link, a payroll packet, and a draft fall schedule for Intro Python, Data Ethics for Managers, and a lab section nobody had mentioned in the posting. That was how the fellowship began, not with a clean escape but with logins.

Back in her studio she opened the dissertation portal. The site had confetti-colored icons and a banner that read Congratulations on this milestone. Leela laughed once, out loud, a short ugly sound that made the room feel smaller. She uploaded the PDF, the memo, the scope statement, the committee revision form. She checked a box confirming that the work was her own to the extent described in accompanying materials, which was apparently how Haviland preferred to phrase a year of theft, panic, silence, and reciprocal contamination.

When the progress bar hit one hundred percent, she expected something bodily, maybe nausea or tears or the simple animal drop of terror. Instead she noticed the fan clicking every seventh rotation and the sharp imprint of the desk edge against her forearm. The body was always a little humiliating that way. It refused the big theater and delivered a minor detail.

Submission complete, the portal said.

Below that: Thank you for your contribution to the university's scholarly community.

Her phone buzzed.

June: you alive

Another message before Leela answered.

June: last tuesday dinner before i go become a corporate evaluation ghoul

June: bring beer or contempt, i have enough plates for exactly neither

Leela typed `I can do both` and sent it before she had time to decide whether the line sounded too much like their old selves.

* * *

June's apartment looked as if somebody had tried to move out and then gotten distracted by the problem of having once lived there. Half the books were boxed and half still leaned above the folding table. The good knives were wrapped in a dish towel on the counter with PACK LAST written across the towel in black marker. A roll of packing tape hung from June's wrist like a bracelet she hated. Her blunt-cut hair had fresh uneven edges, which meant she had trimmed it herself in a bathroom mirror and not cared if the back came out wrong.

"You actually brought both," she said when Leela held up the beer and the takeout bag.

"I respect the brief."

June took the beer, popped the cardboard top, and called over her shoulder, "Door's open if that's Omar. If it's a murderer, tell him to wait until after dumplings."

It was Omar. He came in with a white bakery box balanced on one hand and looked, as usual, as if he had dressed carefully in order to argue with the day's humiliations before they could begin. Even in June's heat-trap kitchen he still had the collar buttoned on his shirt. The box had gone soft with syrup in one corner.

"The baklava suffered in transit," he said. "You will have to accept it as a moral rather than structural offering."

June snorted. "Perfect. That's also what the university gave us."

Omar set the box down, glanced around the apartment at the open cartons and missing chairs, and for one raw second looked too young for his careful beard. "You're really leaving."

"Tomorrow morning." June pulled a nicotine-gum blister pack out of her pocket with the same motion someone else might have used for cigarettes. "If I stay any longer, Haviland will figure out a way to award me a certificate in resilience."

"Frame it," Leela said. "Hang it next to your employee badge."

"I'm not getting a badge. I'm getting a laptop and a Slack invitation to a company with a name like ScalarForge or moralpanic.ai. I told you, it's bleak."

It was not called either of those things. It was called Mercer Metrics, which sounded like a consulting firm built entirely out of beige carpet. June had described the job over text as model evaluation for companies too embarrassed to admit they had built wrappers around other people's systems. The pay was good enough to say yes quickly and not so good that the yes felt clean.

They ate on paper plates because the real plates were already boxed. June had packed the tablecloth but not the cheap folding table itself, as if the table had earned more loyalty by being ugly in public. The dumplings sweated through their containers. Outside the open window somebody on Alder Street was revving a motorcycle with the lonely commitment of a man who badly wanted witnesses.

June cracked a beer and slid another to Leela. "You first. Did the empire let you submit your corpse dissertation?"

"I submitted."

"And?"

Leela shrugged. "Now I wait for them to decide whether survival counts as scholarship."

"It never has before."

"Gloria got me the Montrose job."

June stopped halfway through peeling the paper off a pair of chopsticks. "Seriously."

"Seriously."

"Jesus. Four-four?"

"Four-four."

"That's not a fellowship. That's witness protection."

Omar laughed, then looked guilty for enjoying it and laughed harder. "I'm sorry. That is very cruel and also exactly right."

"It's fine," Leela said. "They also might give me first-year ethics for computer science."

"Even funnier."

"I know."

June leaned back in her chair and looked at Leela for a long second, the joke thinning around the edges. "You taking it?"

"I accepted it outside Baines before I could remember my pride."

"Good." June tore open the nicotine gum. "Pride is a luxury product. We don't stock it here."

Omar wiped syrup from his thumb with a napkin. "Gloria finally sorted out my status too. Or perhaps sorted is generous. There is now a path, which is not the same thing as ground."

Leela looked at him. "What kind of path?"

"Provisional co-advising with Rina Patel in applied math, if the dean approves summer bridge funding. If not, they are prepared to discuss what they keep calling an alternative completion option."

"Terminal master's exit," June translated.

"Yes." He smiled without humor. "I appreciate how our institution always chooses the phrase least likely to alarm a donor at lunch."

"What did the visa office say?"

Omar took a drink before answering. "They said my documentation is currently sufficient. The word currently did a great deal of work."

There it was again, the grammar of managed injury, with words like currently and workable and circumstances doing the worst labor. Leela looked down at her paper plate, at the soy sauce soaking through one corner, and felt the whole year gather in her chest not as grief exactly but as compression, like somebody had been tightening straps around a box and had only now stopped.

June reached behind her and tugged a stapled packet from one of the boxes. "Speaking of language crimes. Gloria sent me the preliminary findings summary."

"Of course she did," Leela said.

"I think as a treat."

June handed the packet across. The first page was full of headings and restrained nouns. Findings regarding disclosure practices. Findings regarding supervisory climate. Findings regarding external commercial entanglement. Leela read three paragraphs and had to put it down.

"Supervisory climate," June said. "Like the lab got mold."

"Climate is a useful word," Omar said. "It suggests weather. It lets everyone behave as though they merely forgot an umbrella."

Leela found the line about inappropriate entrepreneurial overlap and felt actual laughter rise in her throat, ugly and involuntary. "Overlap. He stole our work and the report sounds like two circles on a whiteboard touching by accident."

"Don't forget advising conduct," June said. "Maybe they'll put him in timeout and ask him to complete a module."

"With reflection questions," Omar said. "Name a time when a student's immigration status was not an appropriate mentoring lever."

That got all three of them. The laughter went longer than the joke deserved because the alternative was the report itself. June bent over the table, coughing into her elbow. Omar took off his glasses to wipe his eyes. Leela laughed until the room blurred and then hated the blur because she did not want to cry in June's half-packed kitchen with dumpling grease on her hands.

When the laughter died, June tapped the packet flat against the table.

"I don't know what I expected," she said. "Not justice. I'm not twelve. But this thing doesn't even have enough nouns to be insulting properly."

"It can't use the nouns," Leela said. "If it did, they'd have to explain how long everybody watched."

June looked at her. Not hostile. Not soft either. Just direct enough that Leela had to keep going.

"They'd have to explain why I sat on the logs," she said. "Why you took the meeting. Why Omar touched the baseline. Why Ben kept hovering until hovering became forwarding. The clean version is that Mohit did it to us. The dirty version is that he built a room where every bad choice came with an invoice."

Omar's jaw shifted, that tiny sign that fear and pride used the same muscles on him. "The report has no use for the dirty version."

"No," Leela said. "And I don't know what to do with the part that stays."

June got up, crossed to the counter, and came back with her yellow legal pad, the one she had been carrying through the grievance months like a second spine. The cover was bent, the top page scarred with pen pressure from older notes. She put it beside the packet, then looked at Leela.

"We decide that tonight," she said. "Before I go. What happens to `holdout_set`?"

Leela had brought the drive in her bag without telling herself she had decided anything. It was small enough to fit in her fist, matte black, no bigger than an old phone. Inside it sat the logs, the deck versions, Omar's letter, June's notes, the contradictory project briefs, and enough timestamps to make an administrator break into hives. Also inside it sat their own uglier residues, the parts the official report had no use for because institutions liked victims best when they came pre-separated from compromise.

She set the drive on the table.

"The lawyer still has a copy," Omar said.

"I know."

"Then we could delete ours."

June's expression flattened. "Do you want to?"

Omar thought. He always did. Even now, with beer on the table and syrup drying at the corner of the bakery box, he gave the question full intellectual dignity before he answered. "Some days, yes. Not because I think forgetting is possible. Because I'm tired of evidence as a way of living."

Leela felt that in her back teeth. The months of folders and hashes and forwarding chains, the careful naming of files so panic did not eat chronology, the sense that even friendship now required metadata.

"But?" June said.

"But if we delete it," Omar said, "then the report becomes the whole available memory. And the report is a coward."

The room went still. Not generic stillness, not one of those novel-room silences that could happen anywhere. This one had Alder Street in it, the motorcycle outside finally gone quiet, the refrigerator clicking on, the packing tape tacky against June's wrist when she lifted her beer.

"I don't want to send it wider," Leela said. "Not now. Not because I'm noble. Because the next people who get it will flatten it in a different direction. They'll want one monster and three deserving students and maybe a cautionary op-ed. That's not what happened either."

"No," June said. "It isn't."

She was looking at the drive, not at Leela. The side of her thumb still had a little ridge of adhesive from the tape. Leela remembered that thumb inside her mouth in Baltimore and then, immediately after, remembered June not answering three of her calls in March. The body did not sort chronology into what was useful.

"I'm not asking for a retroactive happy ending," June said, still looking at the table. "For us, I mean."

Omar looked politely away.

Leela let out a breath she had been holding for months under different names. "Good. I don't have one."

June nodded once. "Okay."

The simplicity of it hurt more than a fight would have. Not because it closed anything beautifully. Because it didn't. Because the truth between them had finally gotten small enough to carry without performance. They had wanted each other. They had also failed each other in sequence and in detail. Both things would remain true in Brooklyn, in Montrose, in whatever office Omar ended up pleading his future inside.

June picked up the drive and turned it over in her hand. "We keep calling it `holdout_set`, right?"

"I think so," Omar said. "The name still fits."

"Say why," June said.

He gave her an annoyed look that was mostly cover for thought. "Because a holdout set is the portion you don't let the model train on if you actually want to know whether it understands anything. You keep something back. Otherwise it only learns the house answers."

June set the drive down again and looked at Leela.

"And this?" she said.

Leela looked from the drive to the packet from Gloria to the legal pad with its dented cardboard cover. The official record would keep the sanctioned pieces: leave status, policy violations, mentorship reforms, whatever donor-safe surgery the department performed on the year to keep the patient nominally alive. `holdout_set` held the rest. The stolen files and the frightened improvisations. The parts that made none of them innocent enough for a brochure and did not stop mattering because of that.

"This is what they don't get to train on," she said. "The part they can't absorb without admitting what the system actually rewarded."

June smiled, quick and humorless. "Gross. Correct, but gross."

"My specialty."

"No argument."

Omar reached for the legal pad and tore off a clean page. "Then we keep it privately. Encrypted. With all three of us."

"Not Ben?" June asked.

It was the first time his name had entered the room. Leela thought of Ben's expensive winter jackets, his appalled little-boy face at demo day when events had finally outrun audition. He had sent an apology the length of a fellowship statement the week before. She had read the first six lines and archived it.

"I don't know," she said.

"He should not have sole custody of a houseplant," June said. "I'm not giving him this."

"Agreed," Omar said. "No offense to houseplants."

The joke landed lightly and stayed light, which was maybe the best available mercy.

Leela unzipped her bag and took out two more drives. June looked up, surprised.

"I made copies yesterday," Leela said. "Same volume, same checksum. I thought maybe if I had them ready we wouldn't pretend not to be making a decision."

"Jesus," June said softly.

"What?"

"Nothing. That's just the most you thing I've ever heard."

Omar took one drive and weighed it in his palm as if surprised by the lack of heft. "Almost no mass at all."

"Like every other catastrophic object in this book," June said.

They wrote down the recovery instructions separately, not the passphrase itself, just the route to it: union lawyer if one of us disappears, do not put in email, do not store in cloud notes, do not get sentimental and open it drunk. June added the last line and underlined it twice. Omar folded his page with absurd neatness and tucked it into his wallet behind his transit card. Leela slid hers into the Montrose offer folder, which felt bleak but appropriately honest. Her future job and the evidence of her ruined one were in the same pocket.

By the time they finished, the takeout had gone cold and the room had darkened toward evening. June switched on the kitchen light. It made the apartment look temporarily more itself, the old Tuesday-dinner version with too many people and not enough chairs, before the boxes ruined the illusion again.

"I should say something wise because I'm leaving," June said.

"Please don't," Leela said.

"Fair. Then something practical. Send me your Montrose address when you have it. If you let your undergrads bully you into making Discord channels, I'll come upstate and put you down."

"I would deserve it."

June looked at Omar. "And you. If they try to sell you the terminal master's as a favor, call me before you sign anything."

"Why?"

"Because I would enjoy being professionally rude to them."

Omar's smile this time was real. "Thank you. I will treasure the offer and hopefully never use it."

He stood first. Of course he did. He had an office to return to, an inbox capable of ruining a night, a whole provisional future balanced on other people's calendar invites. At the door he turned back, fingers already on the frame.

"For what it's worth," he said, careful and unsentimental, "I'm glad the year did not end with the university being the only witness."

Then he was gone, footsteps quick on the stairs.

Leela helped June tape one more box because leaving right away would have made the room too conscious of itself. The box was full of cables, three chipped mugs, and a pan June refused to abandon though its handle wobbled and one side had burned permanently black.

"You could buy a new one," Leela said.

"I could. This one knows me."

Leela pressed the tape down. "That's disgusting."

"Yeah."

June set the box by the door and leaned against it for a second. Up close she smelled like tape adhesive, dish soap, and the peppermint bite of nicotine gum. It was not the hotel and not the lab. It was something current and unromantic and therefore harder to sentimentalize.

"I'm glad you took the job," June said.

"I'm glad you took yours."

"No you're not."

"No," Leela said. "But I'm glad you got out."

June looked at her long enough to make lying impossible in either direction. "You too," she said. "Sort of."

That was the shape of it. Not absolution. Not a reopened wound. A damaged sentence with enough grammar left to stand.

When Leela left, the night air on Alder Street had gone soft and city-warm. She walked uphill with the beer buzz already fading, one copy of `holdout_set` in her bag beside the Montrose paperwork, another kind of future knocking lightly against the first every time she moved. Behind her, June's apartment windows stayed lit while boxes shifted in shadow. Ahead, campus rose in its familiar blocks and institutional facades, all of it still standing, all of it already rewriting.

The official findings would become a memo, then a personnel action, then a cautionary paragraph in some committee archive nobody opened without a reason. They would call it advising climate and entrepreneurial overlap and revised scope. The nouns would harden. They would get cited. They would become the version of the year that fit in folders.

The drive in her bag held the remainder. Not the innocent version. Not the marketable one. Just the part that had to stay outside the training loop if anyone wanted an honest test of what the institution was.

It weighed almost nothing. She kept touching the bag anyway, as if the fact of carrying it could keep one last piece of the plural from being processed into weather.
