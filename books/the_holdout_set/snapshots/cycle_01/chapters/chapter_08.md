# Chapter 8

By the time Leela got to Edison, the rangoli outline was already on the front step, half finished and feathered at one corner where somebody had opened the storm door too hard. The chalk loops were Meera's, neat enough to look premeditated and a little too symmetrical for holiness. A string of orange lights had been tested and abandoned in a coil by the planter. Through the glass she could hear the pressure cooker and the television from next door and her mother's voice talking to herself in the kitchen in the firm, efficient tone she used for recipes and incompetent doctors.

She set her duffel down on the crowded shoe rack and stood there for a second in the familiar crush of the entryway, coat half unzipped, train stink still in her hair. The townhouse smelled like hot oil, toasted cashews, sandalwood, and the damp wool of too many winter things drying in one place. Framed school photos climbed the wall by the stairs in a chronology that had once felt flattering and now mostly felt like evidence. Meera in her white coat. Leela at seventeen with an overachieving smile she wanted to slap off her own face.

From the kitchen Anita called, "Shoes off. Wash your hands. Don't bring Penn Station onto my clean floor."

Leela laughed before she meant to. "Hi to you too, Amma."

Anita turned just long enough to look at her over reading glasses that had slid halfway down her nose. She was frying ribbon pakoda in a dented kadai and keeping the good roasted cashews in an old Danish butter-cookie tin because every storage container in the house had lived at least two lives already. "If I say hi first, you think the rules have changed. They haven't. Go."

It was a pleasure, instantly and embarrassingly deep, to be told what to do in language that had nothing to do with deliverables or remediation or whether a committee might someday decide that her future qualified as real. She took off her shoes, washed the train grime off her hands, and came back to the counter where Anita had already poured tea into a steel tumbler and slid it to the one open patch of space between the curry leaves and the box of matches.

Anita looked her over with quick billing-clerk accuracy, the same glance that could find a missing ICD code in a doctor's dictation and now found the wrist brace, the under-eye shadows, the sweater that had once been black. "You've lost weight."

"I've been eating."

"I didn't ask that question."

The tea was too hot. Leela burned her tongue and felt absurdly grateful for it. She stood at the counter drinking while Anita turned strips of dough in the oil with a slotted spoon, shook water off more curry leaves into the sink, and kept up a running commentary on the cardiology practice's latest software disaster.

"They changed the patient portal again," Anita said. "Now nobody can find where to pay and they all call us as if I designed the website myself. One man this morning told me his blood pressure went up because of the login screen. I said, good, then at least you know why you're coming to cardiology."

"You said that?"

"I thought it. Same thing."

Leela smiled into the rim of the tumbler. The house was full of small competent noises: oil ticking in the pan, the cabinet hinge that always clicked before shutting, the hum of the old refrigerator, the faint pop-pop-pop of neighborhood firecrackers starting too early. At Haviland every sound lately had seemed to carry obligation. Here even the pressure felt older and more decent. It asked only that she bring the steel plate from the second shelf, not that she defend her adulthood in the language of institutional process.

Her phone buzzed in her coat pocket on the chair.

She ignored it.

Anita did not. "Take it out, at least. If it keeps vibrating on the wood, I can hear it."

Leela pulled the phone free and saw Mohit's name before she could stop herself.

Hope you made it home safely. When you have a quiet ten minutes tonight, send me a short chronology of how the legacy root access passed to you. The office will ask again Monday, and it's better if we're clean and consistent.

For a second the black glass administrative conference room in Baines Hall rose inside her with such clarity that she could smell the fake citrus cleaner again. She turned the phone facedown on the counter hard enough that Anita looked up.

"Work?" Anita asked.

"Lab stuff."

Anita made a small dismissive sound. "Today of all days."

That was all. No speech about balance, no inquiry into whether professors had the right to text on Diwali, no helpful escalation. The dismissal itself was a form of protection. In this kitchen Mohit was merely rude, which was not the same as powerless but came close enough to be briefly soothing.

Anita lifted a piece of fresh pakoda onto a paper towel and then, because she had never believed in serving food at a safe temperature, tore one piece in half and put it straight into Leela's hand. Oil hit her skin. Chili, ajwain, salt. The burn on her fingertips matched the one on her tongue. It was perfect.

"Too much spice?" Anita asked.

"No."

"Good. Your sister said I made it old-people mild last year."

"I said it was edible by cardiologists," Meera called from the doorway, arriving with the exact timing of someone who had always expected to be quoted in absentia.

She came in carrying a bakery box against one hip and a reusable grocery bag full of tea lights and spare batteries, still wearing charcoal slacks under a navy scrub top with the elastic marks of an N95 stamped faintly into her cheeks. When she shrugged out of her coat a handful of individually wrapped floss picks spilled from the pocket and skittered across the floor. She looked down at them with ordinary annoyance, as if her job was physically incapable of ending at the office.

"Those are clean," she said. "Don't make that face."

"I wasn't making a face," Leela said.

"You were. Move, let me put this down. Amma, Mrs. Bhatia sent another sweets box to the practice. If we eat all this, every one of us deserves our A1C."

Anita took the bakery box without gratitude. "People bring sweets to a dentist like they have never once thought a thought through to the end."

"Exactly. Also, one patient bit through his temporary crown because apparently ice is a civil right." Meera bent to gather the floss picks, stuck them back in her pocket, and nudged the bag of tea lights toward Leela with her foot. "Can you check which of the diyas are cracked? Last year half of them leaked oil all over the tray."

Again that drop in the body, the relief of being conscripted into something finite. Leela took the bag, sat at the table, and sorted clay lamps onto newspaper while Anita and Meera moved around her in the kitchen's narrow practiced choreography, never colliding because they had been not colliding in this room for decades. Meera washed grapes and talked about a hygienist who wanted Thursdays off because the middle-school pickup time had changed. Anita complained about the HOA newsletter's latest war on front-yard clutter. Between them they discussed whether the cardiology office holiday potluck could possibly be improved by anyone's idea of quinoa salad. The conversation had no thesis. Nobody was building toward an insight. It was made entirely of recurring local burdens that could be survived by telling the story properly.

Leela had missed whole categories of adulthood while sitting in one basement for too long. She had opinions about benchmark contamination and compute bottlenecks and which faculty members used the word community only when donors were present. Meera had opinions about autoclave maintenance contracts, payroll tax deadlines, and the woman in unit twelve who kept trying to reserve both guest parking spots with patio chairs. Anita knew exactly which doctor in the practice would forget to sign a form on a Friday and which insurance company would pretend not to receive a fax until the third attempt. Their lives generated inconvenience, but it was the kind of inconvenience that at least admitted the existence of a next step.

Her phone buzzed again on the counter.

Meera glanced at it. "You can just turn it off, you know."

"I know."

"Then that's a personality issue."

"Thank you, doctor."

"Different kind."

She smiled, but she still picked the phone up. A second text.

Also, let's not widen this unnecessarily. These reviews tend to become narrative very fast. A simple factual chronology from you will help.

He had a gift for making the administrative voice sound less like bureaucracy and more like a private warning. The word narrative sat on the screen with a smugness that made her want to throw the phone into the sink. Instead she slid it into her back pocket, where it felt warm and invasive as a hand she had not invited.

Outside, the light had thinned to the blue-gray that made the whole block look briefly provisional. Anita sent them both out with the tray of diyas and a steel bowl of oil, then remained inside to rescue something from the stove. Meera crouched on the concrete in her work clothes and adjusted the rangoli border with the concentration she probably gave a difficult bite wing.

"You made this?" Leela asked.

"Part of it. The rest looked childish, so I redid it."

"Whose?"

"Mine."

Leela laughed, genuinely enough that the sound surprised her. She knelt beside the tray and pinched cotton wicks between oily fingers. The front step was cold through her jeans. Across the street two boys were trying to light sparklers from the wrong end while their father shouted uselessly from the porch. Somewhere farther down the block someone had started frying onions. Home, it turned out, was partly smell and partly being insulted by people who would still hand you a shawl if the wind picked up.

Meera placed the last pinch of magenta powder with the tip of her little finger and sat back on her heels to inspect it. "How bad is the wrist?"

Leela flexed the brace. "Fine."

"That means it's not fine."

"It means I type too much."

"Then stop typing like you're digging a trench."

"Extremely actionable advice."

"I've got more if you want it. Most of it involves posture and accepting that your spine is attached to the rest of you."

This, too, had continued while Leela had been elsewhere: Meera becoming the kind of person who carried her competence around lightly enough to make it look innate. She had not become smug. That would have been easier to hate. She still squinted in the wind and cursed under her breath when a diya tilted. She still had the old family habit of treating care as a form of correction. But there was a settledness to her that made Leela feel not judged exactly, more like she had stayed too long at a bus stop after everybody else had admitted the route was wrong.

They lit the lamps one by one. Oil darkened the clay. The little flames steadied faster than they had any right to in the cold. Anita came out wiping her hands on a dish towel and made a dissatisfied circuit of the steps, moving two diyas half an inch apart because symmetry, apparently, was also a moral value.

"Don't put that one so close to the door," she told Leela. "You'll kick it coming in."

"You have a lot of faith in me."

"I remember."

The block brightened in pieces. Orange lights in hedges. Blue LEDs strung badly across a garage. Actual flame at thresholds that made the houses look, for once, as if someone lived inside them on purpose. For ten whole minutes Leela's phone stayed silent. She stood on the step with oil on her fingers and watched the rangoli catch the low light, the chalk colors deepening as the concrete dampened underneath. Her stomach was warm from tea and fried dough. Her mother's shoulder pressed briefly against hers as Anita reached past her to straighten the tray. It was enough, more than enough, and because it was enough it made her almost immediately wary.

Dinner happened in the old family way, with no one admitting there had been a decision to sit down. One moment they were still moving through tasks, and the next there was rice, cauliflower roast, chole, raita, the bakery sweets ignored in favor of homemade ones, and Anita telling Meera to stop serving herself like a guest. The dining table was the same one from high school, small enough that knees negotiated with each other under it. The crowdedness felt merciful. It meant nobody could retreat into a graceful posture.

Meera talked about the practice because practice was what had happened to her all day and because she had never learned the academic habit of pretending work was an embarrassment in domestic space.

"I had a man today insist that if flossing matters so much, why don't we give it away free. I said, we do. It's literally in my pocket right now. He still acted like I'd revealed a secret menu."

"Did you at least shame him properly?" Anita asked.

"Of course. I'm a professional."

"You enjoy that too much," Leela said.

"I enjoy being right in a chair that reclines."

Anita snorted. "One day somebody will punch you."

"Then I'll bill it as oral trauma."

The rhythm of them was so old it did not need performance. Leela found herself adding little comments at the correct places, not because she was especially present but because the pattern still knew her shape. Anita asked whether graduate housing had fixed the heat. Meera reminded her, without looking up, to book an actual dental cleaning and stop waiting until something hurt. Anita described a patient who had screamed at the practice because his copay existed. Meera described the office landlord's new obsession with changing the parking lot striping right before winter. Somewhere between the raita and the second round of chole, time took on the blunt domestic quality Leela had come home for. It moved by serving spoon and interruption, not by countdown.

Then Anita said, "Tell me which month the defense is."

It was a clean sentence. Not suspicious. Not theatrical. She said it while spooning more rice onto Leela's plate, as if exact months belonged in the same category as who needed seconds and whether there was enough yogurt left.

Leela looked up too slowly.

"What?"

"Which month," Anita repeated. "If it's March, I need to put my time off in early. If it's April, I can wait a little. Meera also needs to know if she has to move patients."

Meera nodded with her mouth full. "If it's a Friday, tell me soon. I can shift fillings. Sedation cases are a whole drama."

The shame arrived so fast that for a second it felt physiological, like missing a stair. Not because the question was cruel. Because it was ordinary. Because ordinary people asked for dates when events were real.

"It should be spring," Leela said, and heard at once how useless that sounded. She kept going because stopping was worse. "I mean, the committee part is mostly calendar coordination, and there's this annoying compliance review because of some inherited server permissions, but that's separate, it's not really about the dissertation as such, so it depends on when they want all the paperwork lined up."

Meera swallowed. "That was a lot of words for no month."

"We're still figuring it out."

Anita's face did not change. She had the kind of self-command that made stronger emotions look like a narrowing of practical focus. She set down the rice spoon. "Did your advisor give you a season or not?"

Leela drank water to give herself a second. Her own voice, when it came, had the careful collaborative softness she hated in herself. "He thinks spring is realistic."

That was perhaps not yet a lie. Or not fully one. Mohit thought many things were realistic when realism cost him nothing.

Meera said, "Okay. Fine. Text me when you have an actual date. I need notice."

There it was again, that life of verbs that terminated. Need notice. Move patients. Put time off in. Nothing in the lab ever ended that cleanly. Everything there was both urgent and provisional, and the provisional part was always somebody else's fault until it became hers.

Her phone vibrated in her pocket against the chair.

She did not have to look to know.

Anita was saying something about whether the cardiology office was closing early on Christmas Eve. Meera was describing, with professional disgust, a patient who used her front teeth to open pistachios. The room continued around Leela with insulting competence. She put down her fork and went to the kitchen on the excuse of getting more water.

Mohit again.

If there's any chance you've described the review more broadly, let me know before Monday. These things escalate through misunderstanding.

Under it, as if he had earned casualness by writing two procedural messages first:

Enjoy the holiday.

The obscenity of that almost made her laugh. She stood at the sink with the faucet running too long, reading the text twice as if the second pass might reveal a version less intimately irritating. He had never been inside this house. He had no claim on the crowded shoe rack or the cheap orange lights or the old table with the water ring no one could remove. Yet his language had entered anyway, with its aligned facts and misunderstandings and cozy little threats.

From the dining room Meera called, "You drown in there?"

"Coming."

She put the phone away and went back. The rest of dinner held. That was almost the worst part. No catastrophe arrived to justify the pulse of dread she carried back to the table. Anita cut the milk sweet into exact squares. Meera took a call from the office because a patient was panicking about post-procedure bleeding and came back rolling her eyes. "He spit after the extraction even though I told him not to, so now the clot's gone and suddenly I'm a witch." Anita said, "You are, but that's separate," and for a moment all three of them laughed so hard Leela had to wipe her eyes.

It should have been enough to repair something. Instead it made the later damage more precise.

By nine thirty Meera had packed leftovers into two containers, checked tomorrow's schedule on her phone, and stood in the entryway zipping her boots.

"Text me when you get the defense date," she said. Then, with the affectionate impatience she used when she wanted concern to look like task management, "And book a cleaning before I drag you in by your hair."

"You say the nicest things."

"I save the really nice things for patients with insurance."

She hugged Anita first, then Leela in the quick solid way siblings do when they have spent too much of life pretending not to need ceremony. During the hug she slipped three floss picks into the outer pocket of Leela's duffel without comment. When she pulled back, she said, "Those are for your own good. Don't act persecuted."

"I haven't said anything."

"Your face has." She leaned down to fix one of the diyas at the threshold, because even leaving required an adjustment, then straightened. "Drive safe doesn't apply, so just text when you get back Sunday."

After she left, the house went quieter by a whole dimension. Anita put away the extra food, wiped the table, and turned the television down to a murmur. Outside, intermittent firecrackers kept cracking open the dark. Leela carried plates to the sink and rinsed them because it was easier than sitting still. Her mother did not send her to bed. That itself was a form of notice.

The kitchen after guests or near-guests was always smaller. Steam had dried off the windows. The floor was sticky in one patch by the stove where sugar syrup had splashed earlier. Anita stood at the sink in her old house T-shirt, bangles pushed up her forearm, scrubbing the kadai with more force than the job required. The reading glasses were gone now; without them her face looked less official and more tired.

Leela dried plates and stacked them. Neither of them spoke for long enough that the silence became specific.

Then Anita said, "How bad is it?"

Leela kept her eyes on the plate in her hand. "What?"

"Your professor texting on a holiday. Your face all evening. The answer at dinner that went around the block and came back with no address." Anita turned off the faucet and looked at her directly. "How bad is it?"

Here it was, the point in the evening where truth might still be chosen. She could say: my admin access is suspended because he routed his own repository theft through credentials that technically used to be mine. She could say: I sat in a conference room that smelled like disinfected fruit and watched an officer take notes as if coercion were a workflow preference. She could say: I'm thirty years old and still vulnerable to a man who can hurt me by sounding reasonable. She could say: I don't actually know if spring means spring or if it means never.

And then what.

Anita would get practical. Of course she would. She would ask whether the stipend was safe, whether housing depended on enrollment, whether there was someone higher up, whether Leela had saved anything, why she had not said this sooner, what exactly the advisor's name was again, whether this was racism or ordinary professor evil or both. None of those would be bad questions. That was the problem. They would all be the right questions, and each one would make the whole arrangement sound as degraded as it was.

Leela put the dry plate into the cabinet and heard her own answer arrive in the falsely calm tone she used in meetings when she was trying to preempt other people's alarm.

"It's not that bad. It's stupid and procedural and I should've just said that better at dinner. The review is about old permissions, not research fraud or anything dramatic. It doesn't change the defense. We're still on track."

Anita watched her. "On track for what month."

There was a final chance even then to step sideways into honesty or at least vagueness. Leela missed it deliberately.

"Probably March," she said. "Maybe early April, depending on calendars. But this academic year. That's still the plan."

The lie entered the room without fanfare. No thunderclap, no maternal intuition sharp enough to cut it in half. It simply sat down between them with the dish soap and the damp steel bowls and became, for the moment, another household fact.

Anita turned back to the sink. "Okay."

Relief came first, hot and ugly.

Then her mother said, "How much is on the card now?"

Leela stared. "What card."

"Don't be insulting."

"Amma."

"How much."

This was another one of her mother's gifts, if that was the word: the refusal to let shame select the nouns. The card was the card. Debt was not an atmosphere. It was a number that could be named or ducked, and ducking it only made Anita more exact.

Leela set the dish towel down. "It's fine."

"That's not a number either."

"I'm making the minimums."

"That also isn't a number."

The kitchen felt suddenly overlit. She could hear the refrigerator motor kick on, the neighbor's television through the wall, a child outside begging for one more sparkler. The whole block seemed to be practicing ordinary life at high volume while she tried not to tell her mother the balance on a credit card she had run up buying groceries and train tickets and the occasional decent meal on weeks when the lab ate every available hour.

"Around five," she said finally.

Anita's hands stopped moving in the sink. "Five hundred?"

Leela laughed once, without humor. "No."

"Five thousand?"

"A little under."

Anita closed her eyes, not theatrically, just for one second as if filing that number somewhere she did not want it loose. When she opened them she did not scold. Of course she did not. Scolding was for correctable mistakes. She reached for the dish towel, dried her hands, and picked up her phone from beside the fruit bowl.

"Amma, no."

"I didn't ask your opinion yet."

"You don't have to do that."

"Apparently I do."

She tapped into her banking app with the same unromantic speed she used to pay utility bills. Leela could see the hairline crack in the corner of the phone case, the one Anita had been meaning to replace for months and had not because the old one still held. That, almost more than the number, made the next minute hard to stand upright through.

"Take this and pay the card down," Anita said. "Not all the way. Keep some for groceries and whatever else you've been pretending doesn't cost money."

"Amma, seriously, keep it."

"For what. A nicer funeral?" She kept tapping. "You're close enough to the end that I'm not going to watch you do something stupid because of pride."

Close enough to the end. The lie thickened in Leela's throat.

"I can manage," she said, and heard how weak it sounded even to her. Not because it was false; she had managed, technically, for years. Because the sentence belonged to the version of herself that was still trying to keep family love from becoming family accounting.

Anita looked up. "Can you?"

It was not cruel. That made it worse.

Leela said nothing.

Anita's voice gentled but did not soften. "Listen to me. I know the difference between needing help once and building a whole life out of other people's rescue. I'm not confused. This is the last push, that's all. Finish properly. Then we'll talk about the rest."

Finish properly. As if the degree were still a road instead of weather. As if March existed because they had now said it aloud twice.

Leela's phone buzzed on the counter before she could answer.

For one stupid second she thought it was Mohit again and felt a flash of murderous clarity so clean it was almost pleasurable. Then she saw the bank notification.

ANITA KRISHNAN sent you $1,200.00.

The amount sat there with brutal legibility. Not festival money in an envelope, not a vague promise, not dinner pressed into her hand on the way out the door. Twelve hundred dollars. Enough to knock the card down. Enough to breathe through next month. Enough to make the lie feel functional.

"Amma."

"Pay it tonight before you decide to become noble," Anita said. She turned back to the sink and picked up the last steel bowl. "And don't send it back. I will only send it again and be irritated."

Leela did not say thank you right away because thank you would have made the whole transaction sound cleaner than it was. She stood at the counter with the notification still bright on the screen, the digits reflected faintly in the dark window over the sink. Behind her, the diyas at the front step burned low and steady. In the sink Anita rinsed soap from the bowl, set it on the rack, reached automatically for the next thing that needed doing.

Leela opened her banking app. Relief moved through her first, plain as hunger. After that came humiliation, and after that something meaner: the knowledge that her body could not entirely distinguish between being loved and being bailed out. She did not return the money. She did not even hover over the option. She looked at the new balance, thought of the minimum due on the eighteenth, thought of groceries, train fare back, the little strip of life that twelve hundred dollars could hold in place, and let the transfer settle.

On the counter her phone lit once more with Mohit's name, another text arriving into the house as if he owned some corridor there. Leela turned the screen facedown without reading it.

"Put the laddus away before they dry out," Anita said.

Leela slid the sweets back into the steel tin with her oiled fingers and kept the money.
