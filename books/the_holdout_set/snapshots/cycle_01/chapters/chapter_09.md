# Chapter 9

By nine in the morning the conference hotel had taken everybody down to badges.

That was the first thing Leela noticed about Baltimore, or at least the part of Baltimore the Harbor East Conference Hotel had been willing to admit existed. Men who would have looked merely tired on campus now looked senior because their lanyards were printed in a darker color. Women in black blazers moved through the lobby carrying tote bags with sponsor logos and cups of coffee that cost more than a decent lunch in Lakebridge. The carpet had the thick, muffling softness of money spent to keep panic respectable. The air-conditioning was set for an audience of menopausal donors and had no interest in November.

June stood at the registration table with one hand on her rolling suitcase and the other digging through her backpack for the confirmation email she had already pulled up twice. She had cut her hair again sometime after Diwali; the blunt edge sat sharper at her jaw now, as if she had decided even her face needed less excess. She wore black trousers, a white button-down, and the dark green blazer Leela had only ever seen hanging on the back of June's apartment door like a threat of adulthood neither of them trusted. The blazer fit, technically. It still looked like June was trying it on for irony and had run out of time to change.

The volunteer at the table had a Ravens scarf looped three times around her neck and a fountain-pen stain on the side of her hand. "Professor Saran already checked in," she said, sliding over two folders and not looking up from the alphabetized stack. "He asked if his students got the reduced parking voucher."

"Of course he did," June said.

The volunteer gave her a quick, tired grin, the kind conference workers saved for people who were clearly not the problem at the current moment. "It's clipped inside."

Leela found her own badge, Haviland University printed under her name in a font that made the institution look cleaner than it had ever been in life. She clipped it on. June did the same. Instantly they were no longer two women who had taken a too-early train with bad coffee and laptop chargers tangled together in a tote bag. They were attendees. Presenters. People who could be judged before speaking.

Mohit had texted at 8:11.

Running slightly behind. Please rehearse without me. I have a breakfast conversation with external folks that may be useful to all of us later.

Useful to all of us later had become one of his favorite phrases that semester. It usually meant useful to him immediately and harmful to somebody else on a delay.

June looked over Leela's shoulder at the message and started chewing one of her nicotine gums with the clipped violence she usually reserved for people speaking too slowly at her. "He doesn't even bother to lie attractively anymore."

"He might actually be with a sponsor."

"Then he can sponsor his own advisee's fucking talk."

Leela should have laughed. On campus she probably would have. The hotel had changed the proportions of June's anger. It looked less like a joke here and more like something she was trying to keep inside the lines of her face.

They took the escalator up to the meeting floor, past a glass case full of fake sailboats and a bar that would not open for another hour but was already displaying oranges pierced with cocktail picks. The room reserved for their prep slot, Patapsco B, was colder than the hallway and smelled faintly of dust baked out of the projector. Someone had left a half bowl of peppermints at the lectern from a breakfast panel on grant partnerships. June set her laptop down, plugged in the HDMI cable, and the first slide appeared: title, institutional logos, the clean set of fonts she used when she wanted the work to look more finished than her life felt.

"Do you want me to time it?" Leela asked.

"I know how long my own talk is."

"I know."

June rubbed at the red mark the lanyard had already made on the side of her neck. "Sorry. Yes. Time it."

Leela sat in the front row with her phone in her lap and watched June begin. This, more than almost anything else in the lab, had always made Leela feel briefly superstitious about competence. June at dinner was funny because she could not bear an earnest silence. June in the lab was funny because contempt saved time. June in front of slides became something tighter and cleaner. She didn't waste words. She didn't need to perform how clever she was because the structure of the talk already assumed she had done the reading and the work and the stupid, necessary cleanup no one praised until it failed.

She moved through the opening framing quickly, one hand in her pocket, the other tapping the clicker against her thigh. Evaluation drift in public-service NLP systems. Benchmark contamination. Error propagation when institutions reused supposedly robust models in places where the human cost of a miss was not abstract. Practical, bodily, anti-ornamental. When June trusted the material she almost sounded gentle.

Leela checked the time. Good pace.

Then June hit the middle section and stalled for half a beat at the slide with the main benchmark comparison, the one Omar had gone after in the seminar. It was barely visible if you didn't know her. Leela knew her. The stall lived in the room like a skipped stair.

She finished the rehearsal anyway. Eleven minutes forty seconds.

"You're fine," Leela said. "If anything, you can slow down on the third section."

"That's reassuring. I love being told I can savor my own public damage."

The joke should have put things back where they belonged. Instead it landed between them with its edges exposed.

Leela said, "June."

June unplugged the laptop and wound the cable too tightly around her hand. "No. It's fine. I'm not doing this before the talk."

What Leela heard under it was not dismissal but triage. June could not afford to open the wrong thing at 9:20 in the morning in a hotel ballroom whose walls could be moved with hidden latches. Leela let it go.

At 10:03 Mohit still was not there.

At 10:09 he sent another message.

May need to miss the session start. Unexpectedly productive conversation. You know the material. Trust the room.

June read that one directly on her own phone and gave a little breath of laughter that had no humor in it. "Trust the room. He talks like a man who has never once in his life trusted a room."

"Do you want me to call him?"

"For what? So he can tell me this is good practice?"

Leela looked at the rows of empty chairs, the draped table for panelists, the sweating pitcher of water already collecting a ring on the linen. On campus the damage between them had a basement, a seminar room, doors, a geography. Here it had followed them into a generic hotel that had no memory of them and therefore gave nothing back.

"If anybody asks a bullshit version of the seminar question," Leela said, "I'll step in."

June's eyes lifted to her face. They were very dark when she was tired enough. "Do that because you think it'll help now," she said. "Not because you suddenly figured out what one sentence costs."

Then she picked up her laptop and headed for the ballroom.

* * *

The session chair wore silver running shoes under a navy dress and kept unwrapping the same cough drop, then deciding against it and rewrapping it again. Her badge said ELENA RITCHIE, Carnegie Mellon. When she introduced June she pronounced Haviland correctly and the project title correctly and still managed to make the whole thing sound like a provisional inconvenience before lunch.

The room filled to a little over half. Enough bodies to hurt. Not enough to disappear in.

Leela sat near the aisle with her notebook open and the deck title written at the top of the page in case June needed a visible ally in the audience, something as stupid and ancient as eye contact. The panel before June's had gone long on a healthcare triage model that nobody trusted and everybody clapped for anyway. People were hungry. Two faculty in the back were already packing their bags on their laps so they could leave quickly for whatever sponsored buffet waited downstairs. A man in a red quarter-zip opened his laptop before June reached slide two and left it open the entire talk like a private insult.

None of that would have mattered if June had caught the room early.

She didn't.

It wasn't that she was bad. Leela had seen bad talks. Bad talks had flailing jokes and apology sweats and the terrible impulse to re-explain every slide as if volume were clarity. June was sharp, fast, and entirely herself. The problem was that she walked into the room carrying the shape of the earlier injury, and some part of the room could smell it even without knowing the story. Her voice tightened on the benchmark section. One transition came half a beat too soon. She made a joke about everyone in evaluation quietly praying their datasets were less haunted than they were, and only one person laughed, a little too late.

When she reached the central result, Leela could feel the audience deciding whether this was a talk about infrastructure or a talk about a flaw the speaker was trying to outtalk.

Then the question came.

It came from a woman in the second row with athletic tape around one thumb and a legal pad covered in tiny, square handwriting. She waited until Q and A, smiled as if she were about to ask for a citation, and said, "Could you say more about how you're separating adversarial brittleness from benchmark construction error here? Because if the perturbation set is partly surfacing annotation mismatch, then the system may not be failing in the way you're claiming. It may just be inheriting noise your benchmark invited."

The wording was not Omar's. The wound was.

June answered immediately, which was one of the reasons she survived as well as she did in academia: speed could look like confidence to people who had not seen you break. She explained the held-out slices, the ablation checks, the reason they had included exactly the noisy subsets the questioner was worried about. She was right more often than not. But the question had done its work. It had pulled the seminar back into the room and laid it over her slides like cheap acetate.

The woman nodded in a way that meant thank you for confirming I should remain unconvinced.

Leela lifted her hand before she had fully planned the words. Elena Ritchie pointed to her with visible relief, the way chairs did when they thought a friendly question might rescue timing.

"Could you say a little more," Leela asked, keeping her voice level, "about why the failure mode matters even if some of the noise is benchmark-level? My understanding is that the institutional use case here is exactly that nobody deploying these systems gets to pretend the mess is elsewhere."

June looked at her. For a second the room reduced to that look, which was not gratitude and not anger and not forgiveness either. Then June turned back to the audience and answered the version of the question Leela had handed her instead of the accusation underneath it. She was good there. Brief, specific, unsentimental. For thirty seconds the talk was the talk it should have been.

It still ended to polite applause.

Not warm. Not interested enough to become hostile. Just the sound a regional conference made when it believed it had behaved decently.

In the hallway outside the ballroom, people did the soft, awful thing conference people did when they thought they were being kind.

"Tough room."

"Important problem space."

"I liked your point about deployment assumptions."

A doctoral student from Maryland stopped to tell June she had appreciated the slides' color palette. She wore a pearl barrette shaped like a fish skeleton and kept pushing it back into place with the side of her wrist because both her hands were full of boxed salad and a charger brick. June thanked her with perfect public politeness. As soon as the student was gone she said, "If one more person tells me the problem space is important, I'm going to put my head through the ice machine."

Leela said, "You answered well."

June turned on her. Not loudly. That would have been easier. "You don't get to grade the recovery."

Leela felt the point land because it was accurate. The conference folder dug a paper edge into the crook of her elbow. "I wasn't trying to."

"You asked the question you should've asked in October."

"I know."

"Do you?"

The hallway kept moving around them. A sponsor banner for responsible AI bent slightly where somebody had assembled it wrong, so the word responsible had a permanent wave through the middle. Two men walked past arguing cheerfully about compute budgets as if those were weather systems that happened to everyone equally.

June looked exhausted suddenly, not theatrical about it, just used up along the edges. "Where is he?"

Leela did not need to ask who.

She checked her phone. No message from Mohit asking how the talk had gone. No Are you okay. No strategic interpretation of the room. Nothing.

"I'll call."

He sent the new text before she could.

Need a favor. I left a print packet at the business center under my name. Could one of you grab it and bring it to Harbor C by 3:15? I'm tied up in a conversation that's running long. Will regroup afterward.

June read it over her shoulder and went very still. It was different from anger. Anger at least had movement in it.

"He wants us to courier his little side hustle," she said.

"He didn't say what it is."

"He didn't ask how it went."

There wasn't a defense for that. Leela did what she had spent years doing around Mohit and looked automatically for a version of the situation that would cost the least if spoken aloud. External folks. Productive conversation. Something useful later. Every interpretation came dressed as management and ended in degradation.

"Let's get the packet," June said.

"You don't have to."

"No. I really fucking do."

* * *

The business center sat off the lobby behind a frosted glass wall that made everybody at the computers look guilty regardless of what they were printing. A man in a clipped vest staffed the counter. His tie clip was crooked by almost an inch, and every few minutes he pressed the pad of his thumb against the bare pale groove where a wedding ring had once sat, as if checking whether the skin had fixed itself. He looked up when Leela gave Mohit's name.

"Professor Saran?" he said. "Yeah. He's been in and out all morning with those decks. Said two students might pick up the last set."

June's mouth flattened. "Decks."

"Color double-sided, nicer stock." The man reached below the counter and brought up a rubber-banded packet with a print receipt paper-clipped to the top. "He was pretty specific about the paper weight."

Of course he had been.

Leela thanked him and took the packet. The cover sheet showed a hotel logo, print charges, and the file name.

`ruderal_pitch_v3.pdf`

User: `msaran@haviland.edu`

11:07 AM

24 copies

June saw it at the same time she did. "Give me that."

They moved out into the hall before June tore off the rubber band. The packet was still warm from printing. It had that faint sweet-plastic smell of toner and thick paper. On top sat a title slide:

RUDERAL ANALYTICS  
Decision Support Under Adversarial Conditions

No Haviland logo. No lab name. Just a clean green sprig of a logo that made the whole thing look like a climate-tech company until you actually read it.

June flipped to the next slide, then the next.

On slide six she stopped.

Leela knew the figure before she consciously understood it. A clustered bar chart, blue against rust, error rates across perturbation categories. June had shown a version of it in the seminar. June had shown a cleaner version of it ten minutes ago. This one still carried the old typo in the x-axis label, the one June had corrected in October after swearing for five straight minutes at matplotlib.

`sythetic code-switch`

The missing n sat there like a fingerprint.

Slide seven was worse. A heat map from June's internal benchmark slices, not the public draft. The tiny title at the top still read `eval_holdout_oct3b`, because Mohit had apparently taken the screenshot directly from the version June had exported for lab feedback and never meant to circulate outside Keating. Slide nine had one of Leela's robustness curves with the ugly orange confidence band she had used only in a late-night internal draft because the prettier palette broke on the department printer. The caption underneath called it a proprietary generalization stress test.

Leela touched the edge of the page and felt the slick tooth of the paper against her fingertip. Her own bad September choice looked strangely expensive in color.

"No," June said.

It was almost quiet. That made it worse.

She flipped farther.

Slide twelve: pilot partnerships.

Slide fourteen: market opportunity in municipal compliance and triage systems.

Slide sixteen: defensible moat through exclusive benchmark access.

Slide eighteen: timeline to seed round.

In the footer of the final slide, smaller than the thank-you line, sat a general contact address and a web domain: `contact@ruderal.ai` and `ruderal.ai`

June gave a short laugh that sounded like something breaking inside a wall. "He made a fucking moat out of my benchmark."

Leela took the packet and forced herself to look again, page by page, because the first shock wanted to turn everything abstract and she could not afford abstraction. Their figures were here. Their labels. Their phrasing. June's benchmark, which had just been publicly reopened as maybe flawed, was being sold downstairs as proprietary infrastructure. Leela's robustness curve, built partly on a result she still did not trust enough to speak about cleanly, had been pulled out of the lab and dropped into a deck with market sizing and a clean little plant logo.

This was not intuition. This was not a mood. It was a print receipt and a filename and toner still warm on the page.

"We should photograph all of it," Leela said.

June was already taking out her phone. "I know."

She shot the receipt first, then each slide, her thumb steady now in the practical way it became when panic got too expensive. They worked through the packet leaned against the wallpaper between the business center and the elevators, two women in conference badges doing documentary labor in public while a dentist from somewhere in Connecticut wheeled a suitcase past them and did not look twice.

At slide nine June paused again. "Did you give him this?"

Leela looked at the figure. Not the version from the submitted paper. Earlier. Internal. The orange band. She had forgotten it existed outside the folder structure in her own head.

"No," she said, which was true and not enough.

June studied her face for a second too long, then went back to photographing.

When they finished, she shoved the papers back into order with a violence that left one corner bent. "Harbor C," she said. "Let's see what the productive conversation is."

Harbor C was at the far end of the meeting floor, past the sponsor lunch buffet with its little silver labels and its trays of food sweating under heat lamps. The door to the room was closed. On the easel outside, somebody had slid a plain white sheet into the holder where conference signage usually went.

PRIVATE MEETING

No logo. No program listing.

From inside came the softened hum of a projector and Mohit's voice, unmistakable even through hotel wood because he had a way of making every sentence sound like a calm solution to a problem he had not caused. Leela couldn't make out the words, only the rhythm: pause, reassurance, controlled enthusiasm.

June stood with the packet tucked under her arm and looked at the door.

If she went in now, Leela thought, the scene would belong to him within ten seconds. He would smile, introduce them as brilliant students, say there had been an unfortunate misunderstanding about draft materials, make them look unstable in a room full of people who had already arrived prepared to invest in his competence.

June knew it too. You could see the knowledge hardening in the way she shifted her weight and did not move closer.

"He used my worst month to do a sales pitch," she said.

"Yes."

"And your figure's in there too."

"Yes."

"Do you know what the clean version of this is going to be? University policy, broad rights, collaborative assets, everybody's confused, nobody remembers where a slide came from."

Leela did know. The wording was practically already on Gloria Fisk's paper folder.

June pressed the heel of her hand against her mouth once, hard enough to flatten it, then dropped it. "I want five minutes where I don't have to hear him explaining my own work back to me."

"Come upstairs," Leela said.

June looked at her, then at the closed door, then back down the hall toward the elevators. "My room."

That was all.

Leela followed her.

* * *

June's room looked exactly like Leela's room would have looked if a committee had designed loneliness by consensus. One king bed with a white duvet too thick for the season, a desk bolted under the television, a print over the bed of boats in a harbor painted in colors no actual harbor had ever admitted. The window showed a slice of gray water and three office buildings that could have been in any city willing to finance itself with glass.

June dropped the packet on the desk so hard the cheap wood shuddered. She took off her blazer, missed the back of the chair with it, and let it fall. Then she stood with both hands braced on the desk and her head down for so long that Leela thought, stupidly, of the night in the third chapter of her dissertation when her code had corrupted and she had spent forty minutes simply staring at the monitor because rage required calories she did not have.

"Say something useful," June said.

Leela shut the door behind her. The room clicked sealed. "The receipt matters."

"Yes."

"The typo matters. The draft labels matter. The fact that he's using your benchmark as proprietary access matters."

"I know the evidence matters." June straightened and turned around. Without the blazer she looked younger and more tired, the white shirt wrinkled at the waist from sitting through other people's talks. "Say something useful about him. About us. About why I gave that talk while he was downstairs trying to sell the blood still in it."

Leela felt the dry hotel air catch at the back of her throat. Not a generic panic. The room's climate control had been pushing cold through a vent above the window all afternoon, and now it made every breath feel borrowed.

"He thinks," she said carefully, "that if he keeps the language professional enough, then the actual theft becomes a matter of interpretation."

"That's about him."

"June."

"No." June shook her head. "Not this thing where you give me the institutional version first because it's neater. I'm not asking for neat."

Leela set her conference folder on the nightstand and looked at the packet spread open on the desk. June's work. Her own work. Mohit's fonts. Ruderal's stupid sprig logo. Everything in the room had edges suddenly. The stitched hem of the duvet. The plastic seam on the ice bucket. The bend in the bent slide corner June had made in the hall.

"Useful," Leela said, making herself keep going before caution could dress it up. "I should've backed you harder in the room today, and I know why that sounded disgusting coming from me after October."

June gave a single nod, not kind and not cruel either, and Leela understood it as permission to continue.

"Useful is that this isn't him being careless," Leela said. "This is work. He printed twenty-four copies. He paid extra for paper weight. He made a plan. And useful is that if he planned it, he thinks he can survive it."

"He probably can."

"Not if we have enough."

June laughed once. "We. That's ambitious."

Leela looked at her. "Do you want me to leave?"

June went quiet.

From somewhere down the hall came the brief thud of an ice machine emptying itself. Someone laughed in the neighboring room and then coughed for a solid ten seconds in the bathroom, the kind of ugly hotel cough that told you conferences were just trade shows for overwork.

June reached into her pocket, took out a stick of nicotine gum in a crumpled wrapper, then looked at it and put it back. "I don't know what I want you to do," she said. "That's part of the problem."

It was the plainest thing she'd said all day. In the lab or at dinner June usually hid feeling inside velocity. Here the velocity was gone.

Leela moved closer, slowly enough that June could have stopped her without difficulty. She could smell June now: cold air, train wool, the faint medicinal mint of gum.

"I know I missed the sentence that mattered," Leela said.

June's eyes flicked up. "Yes."

"I keep hearing it anyway."

"That doesn't help me retroactively."

"I know."

Another step. They were close enough now that Leela could see the shallow red groove the lanyard had rubbed into June's neck. She wanted, absurdly, to press her mouth to that exact irritated strip of skin. The wanting had been in her for longer than she had language she trusted for it. That was part of what made the seminar so awful: not just that she had failed June publicly, but that she had failed her while knowing too well how June's face changed when she was trying not to show damage.

June said, "Why are you still here?"

The question had more than one right answer and at least three wrong ones.

Leela did not let herself choose the prettiest version. "Because I didn't want to leave you alone with that deck," she said. "Because he spent all day treating us like labor and I'd like one hour that isn't organized around him. Because I wanted to come upstairs with you before I had a good reason."

June looked at her a long time.

"That's more useful," she said softly.

Leela could have stopped there. She knew that. Could have turned it into a conversation about evidence handling, tomorrow's train, the version of solidarity that lived comfortably on paper. Instead she lifted a hand and touched the mark on June's neck with the backs of her fingers. June didn't move away.

The contact was so slight it should not have changed anything. It did.

June caught Leela's wrist, not hard, just enough to keep the hand there. "If this is guilt," she said, "go back to your room and write me a memo."

The line was mean in structure and wrecked by how low her voice had gone.

"It isn't."

"Not even a little?"

Leela thought about lying. Decided against it. "I feel guilty about plenty. This isn't that."

June's mouth twitched. "Good. I'd hate to get pity all over the duvet."

Then she let go of Leela's wrist and kissed her.

It was not tentative. June did not know how to do tentative without sounding sarcastic. Her hand came up to the back of Leela's neck and the first kiss landed almost angry, hard enough that Leela's teeth clicked lightly against her own. The second was slower. By the third June had stepped them backward until the backs of Leela's knees hit the bed.

Leela kissed her back with the relief of finally being allowed the obvious. The room, the deck, the day, the conference floor below them with its buffet labels and donor sentences, all of it stayed present, which was part of why this did not feel like escape. June's shirt was cool from the air-conditioning. The skin at her waist, when Leela got her hand under it, was warm.

June broke the kiss just long enough to tug the lanyard over Leela's head and throw it onto the lamp. It caught there, swinging. "That thing was making me homicidal."

Leela laughed into June's mouth. June made a sound that could have been a laugh too and then it was teeth and breath again, June's blunt-cut hair against Leela's cheek, June's palm flattening over the small of her back as if she had been meaning to place it there for months.

Leela got one hand under the white shirt, then both, and June let her push it up. The conference camisole underneath had a tiny snag near the strap, ordinary and specific enough that it made Leela want her more, not less. She kissed down the side of June's throat and felt June swallow.

"Tell me if you want me to stop," Leela said against her skin.

"I won't."

That could have been arrogance. From June it was trust with the paint stripped off.

Leela pulled the shirt over her head. June stood still to help, then immediately reached for the hem of Leela's sweater and dragged it up harder than necessary. The soft lining of it caught briefly on Leela's wrist brace. June stopped at once.

"Shit. Sorry."

"It's fine."

June looked at the brace, then at her. "Does it hurt?"

The question was so practical it nearly undid her.

"Sometimes."

June sat her on the edge of the bed and peeled the brace open carefully, undoing the Velcro one strip at a time so it wouldn't rip loud through the room. Underneath, the skin of Leela's wrist was indented and pale where the fabric had pressed all day. June put her thumb there, lightly, then bent and kissed the mark.

Tenderness, Leela thought wildly, had always been the more dangerous thing.

She touched June's face, the blunt line of haircut, the warm shell of her ear, then leaned in and kissed her again. June pushed her back onto the bed this time, climbing over her with a competence that made Leela's stomach go tight in a way no theoretical language had ever helped. The duvet was too smooth; they slid on it until June swore and shoved the whole top layer half off the mattress.

"God, I hate conference hotels."

"You don't have to say that like a warning label."

"I'm setting expectations."

Leela reached for her and June's answer came all at once: mouth on hers, knee between her thighs, one hand working the button of Leela's jeans with impatient concentration. The room's cheap art knocked once against the wall when the headboard shifted. Both of them froze, then listened. Nothing. The people next door were still talking too loudly about a grant review panel. June grinned against Leela's mouth, sudden and young and a little feral.

"Good," she murmured. "I wanted one thing in this building to belong to us."

Leela pulled June down harder by the waist. There wasn't anything neat about the way it went after that. June had to spit the nicotine gum, finally, into the empty conference coffee cup by the bed. Leela's jeans stuck at one ankle because she had kept her shoes on too long. June's bra clasp took two tries in the bad lamp light. None of it made the wanting smaller. If anything it made it more exact.

When June slid her hand between Leela's legs, Leela grabbed at the fitted sheet so hard it snapped free from one corner. The exposed mattress pad rasped against her calf. June watched her face with a seriousness that was almost stern, then kissed her once, once more, and pushed two fingers inside her with the same direct competence she brought to every task she had decided was worth doing. Leela bit down on the heel of her own hand to keep quiet.

June pulled the hand away. "No."

"The walls are thin."

"Then bite me instead."

Leela did, lightly, at the slope where shoulder became neck. June's breath broke. The sound of it went through Leela like a current. She had spent months, maybe years, not letting herself imagine specifics because specifics asked to be answered for. Now June was above her in a hotel room with the deck on the desk and the afternoon going gray at the window, and the answer turned out to be not abstract at all. It was June's wrist flexing. It was the scratch of the blanket at Leela's thigh. It was the little involuntary curse June said when Leela got a hand under her underwear and found her already wet enough to make June jerk and laugh at once.

"Fuck," June said, forehead dropping briefly to Leela's shoulder. "Okay. Fine."

Leela rolled them partly over, wanting to see her. June let her, then didn't let her stay in charge for long. That was one of the things that made this feel less like a soft collapse and more like an event: leverage kept changing. June went rough when she wanted the day out of her body. She went careful the second Leela winced on the bad wrist. Leela, who lived so much of her life translating herself into acceptable precision, found she did not have precision left. She had hands, mouth, the stunned pleasure of June making room for her and then asking for more.

By the time Leela went down between June's thighs the room smelled like hotel detergent, sweat, and the citrus soap the housekeepers used on every bathroom surface in the country. June had one arm flung over her eyes and the other hand in Leela's hair, not pushing, just holding. When Leela brought her to the edge June's whole body tightened under the thin conference-hotel light, then came apart with a sound she buried in the pillow too late to matter.

Afterward they stayed half tangled on the stripped bed, both breathing too hard for conversation. The deck still sat on the desk in a crooked stack. One of June's socks had landed on slide fourteen, market opportunity obscured by black cotton.

June turned her head on the pillow and looked at Leela with an expression Leela had not seen on her before: not armored, not exactly soft, but unhidden. "Come here."

Leela already was. Still she moved closer until June's bare leg slid over hers and the cool part of the room no longer mattered.

For a minute it might have passed for peace if you did not know anything about them.

Then June said, "This doesn't fix October."

Leela stared at the ceiling print of fake harbor boats. "I know."

"And it definitely doesn't fix that deck."

"I know."

June traced the indentation from the wrist brace again, lightly, as if testing whether the skin had memory. "Okay," she said.

She didn't say what okay meant. Leela did not ask.

When June finally got up to pee, she took the packet with her to the desk on the way back and photographed every page again under the lamp, this time slower, making sure the slide numbers showed. Leela watched from the bed, still naked under the twisted sheet, and felt how little of the day had actually left them. Desire had clarified something. It had not simplified anything.

Leela said, "We should make backups."

June snorted softly. "There you are."

"That wasn't a retreat."

"I know. It was you."

The difference mattered enough that Leela let it stand.

* * *

In the morning the room had the stale cold of overworked climate control and not enough sleep. Leela woke before the alarm because the vent above the window had kicked on with a mechanical cough. For a few blank seconds she forgot where she was. Then she felt the strange mattress under her, the crease in the pillowcase against her cheek, June's shin warm over her ankle, and remembered all of it at once: the talk, the receipt, the closed door outside Harbor C, the way June had said bite me instead.

Gray harbor light leaked around the blackout curtains.

June was awake already. Not fully out of bed, just propped on one elbow looking at her with that serious, post-sleep face people rarely showed unless you had earned something from them or taken it. Her hair was flattened on one side. There was a faint mark on her shoulder where Leela had bitten her.

"You snore a little when you're exhausted," June said.

Leela closed her eyes. "You could've taken that to the grave."

"I could've. I chose transparency."

The joke was dry enough to be June again and low enough to be something else too.

Leela opened her eyes. "Was it awful?"

"No. It was almost cute, which is annoying."

She lay back down, then sat up again a second later as if stillness made her suspicious. The sheet slid to her waist. She reached for the hotel notepad on the nightstand, tore off a page covered in her own handwriting, and handed it to Leela.

It was a list of three file names and a newly created email address.

"I sent the photos to that account from the hotel Wi-Fi at two in the morning," June said. "Then I deleted the sent mail and the local copies from my phone. I'm going to forward them somewhere else once we're off this network."

Leela read the page. June's handwriting was quick and ugly, letters leaning like they were trying to escape each other. "You slept at all?"

"Some."

Leela put the page down. The tenderness of the night remained in the room, but thinner now, stretched over the return of consequences. The deck had not vanished. Mohit still existed. The conference still had a second day. Their bodies had changed relation to each other. The rest of the story had not courteously rearranged itself to match.

"About last night," Leela started.

June looked immediately irritated. "Don't make it worse."

"I was going to say it didn't feel like a mistake."

June's face changed, not much. Enough. "It wasn't."

Leela waited.

June pushed the sheet back, got out of bed, and crossed to the desk for the packet. She stood there in her underwear and one sock, flipping the receipt to the top as if evidence required an outfit more than she did. "That isn't the same thing as me suddenly trusting you in every possible setting," she said. "I need you to understand the distinction."

Leela sat up. The sheet pooled in her lap. "I do."

"Do you?"

"Yes."

June looked at her over the packet. Morning made her plainer, which on her read as more dangerous because there was less performance between thought and speech.

"I wanted you," June said. "I still do. I also watched you freeze when it cost you something to speak for me. Both things are true. If one of them starts eating the other, I want to know before it turns stupid."

Leela felt the sentence in her body like a clean cut. Not because it was cruel. Because it was exact.

"Okay," she said.

June nodded once. She set the packet down, then bent to pick up her discarded blazer from the floor. "Good. Also Mohit texted at 6:40 asking if we'd found the print packet and saying sorry he missed us."

"Fuck."

"Yeah." June slipped her arms into the blazer and rolled her shoulders into place. "I told him yes, sorry we missed him, long day."

Leela stared. "You already answered?"

"Of course I answered. I'm not giving him free alarm bells before breakfast."

That, too, was June: under real fear, humor gone, logistics first.

Leela got out of bed and crossed to her jeans, stepping over the abandoned lanyard still hanging from the lamp. When she reached for her wrist brace, June took it from the desk before she could.

"Give me your hand," June said.

Leela did.

June fastened the brace snugly, practical again, fingers sure on the Velcro. Not gentle for show. Not rough either. When she finished, she kept hold of Leela's hand for one extra second, then let go.

"We need coffee," she said. "And we need to stop looking like people who spent the night inventing a new problem."

Leela glanced toward the desk, where the deck receipt sat on top of the packet like a charge sheet. "Too late on the second one."

June's mouth pulled, almost a smile. "Then let's at least get the coffee right."

They dressed in the overbright silence of hotel morning, each of them moving around the other with a new knowledge that did not simplify the old ones. When they stepped into the hallway, the conference had already resumed its bland machinery: badges, carpet, elevators, the smell of burnt coffee moving up from the lobby. Mohit's text sat in both their phones. The evidence sat in June's bag. Last night sat in Leela's body with enough tenderness to hurt.

At the elevator June reached out and straightened Leela's badge where it had flipped backward against her shirt. The touch lasted no longer than the adjustment required.

"After the morning panel," June said, eyes on the descending numbers above the door, "we go through the slides again."

"Okay."

"And Leela."

"What?"

June looked at her then, fully. "Don't make me regret trusting you with two different things at once."

The elevator arrived. The doors opened on a mirrored box full of strangers and conference tote bags.

Leela stepped in beside her and said, "I won't," knowing as soon as the words left her mouth that the promise was too clean for the year they were living, and knowing June had heard that too.
